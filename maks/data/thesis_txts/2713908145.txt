Abstract
The nonôÙinvasive nature of blood sampling have made the plasma proteome an ideal canôÙ
didate for getting a snapshot of an individuals proteomic state. Changes to the plasma
proteome have been investigated in disease states, but subôÙclinical changes are not well
elucidated. A recent report found that half of the Danish population have used prescripôÙ
tion drugs within the last 14 days. Investigating the effects of prescription drugs on
the human plasma proteome is therefore highly relevant, as the usage of prescription
drugs could have unknown or unwanted consequences for a high number of people.
Here, we investigated the influence of prescription drugs on the plasma proteome in 250
participants from the Danish Blood donor study (DBDS). First, cohort characterisation
was performed to give an overview of prescription drug usage in the DBDS, and to asôÙ
sess the representativeness of the 250 people selected for analysis. Using differential
abundance analysis oral contraceptives were found to significantly alter the abundance
of proteins associated with blood coagulation, fibrinolysis, and endopeptidase activity.
Furthermore, plasma protein modules estimated using coôÙexpression analysis showed
low module preservation for participants using oral contraceptives and a heightened
modular correlation within modules associated with inflammation and immune response.
Furthermore, the study showed that the DBDS, a prospective cohort with strict inclusion
criteria, can be used to investigate the effects of prescription drugs in an exploratory
manner.

Introduction
A recent report estimates that 50 % of all Danes have used prescription medicine within the last
14 days [1]. The functional nature of proteins make them ideal drug targets and as a result it
has been estimated that up to 75 % of all FDA approved drugs act on the human proteome [2].
A proteome may be defined as the set of proteins expressed by the entire organism, a tissue,
or a cell at any particular time [3]. Considering the high amount of prescription drug usage,
and that the majority of these drugs target proteins, makes mapping the effect of drugs on the
proteome highly relevant. So far, studies have focused on investigating proteome changes
for disease states [4, 5] (to name a few), but effects of subôÙclinical changes have not been
properly elucidated. While understanding the changes occurring in a disease state can provide
valuable information for determining disease etiology, detecting early preôÙclinical changes hold
the potential for prevention of the disease altogether. In this paper we have focused on the
changes caused by common prescription drug usage. It is crucial that robust methodology for
investigating these changes is developed. Preferably, this methodology would take advantage
of the vast amount of health data available from existing cohorts and population based registers,
as this is both faster and cheaper than creating data deôÙnovo.
One important aspect of proteome investigation is proteome quantification. Quantifying proôÙ
teomes can be difficult as tissue samples often have to be surgically removed [6, 7, 8]. Utilizing
blood plasma has been proposed as a nonôÙinvasive way to circumvent this problem, and to get
an informative portrait of the patients proteomic state [9] (see fig. 1 B). The Danish Blood Donor
Study (DBDS) is an existing cohort that possess both electronic health data, and blood plasma
measurements for multiple participants.
Changes to the plasma proteome was investigated in a subset of the DBDS cohort. By comôÙ
bining data from the danish prescription registries, the DBDS and a stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart mass specôÙ
trometry (MS) based workflow (detailed section on the basis of MS protein quantification and
the nature of protemic data in supplementary section) we set out to investigate the effect of
commonôÙprescription drugs on the plasma proteome. Understanding the conclusions made in
this study requires a brief introduction to the DBDS cohort and the analytical approach used in
analyzing the plasma proteome.
Prescription drug usage and the Danish Blood Donor Study
The DBDS cohort is a prospective cohort of around 150.000 danish blood donors created to
utilise samples collected from danish blood banks. Using the blood samples as well as additional
information collected from the blood donors, researchers can study both donor specific and nonôÙ
specific conditions. Due to the strict inclusion criteria of Danish blood donors, serious disease
1
==PAGE:14==
is very rare in active blood donors. This gives an unique opportunity to investigate the effect of
prescription drugs in a large cohort of participants that have all cleared these criteria.
By crossôÙreferencing the DBDS with the Danish prescription drug registries, we are able to
map drugs that have been taken around time of donations (see supplementary section on ATCôÙ
codes, the prescription drug registries and the Danish blood donor study for a more thorough
explanation). As proteome quantification is expensive, time consuming, and not readily availôÙ
able in all participants in the DBDS, 250 (125 men and 125 women) participants were chosen
from the full cohort. To be included, blood donors had to be active in 2018 and have their latest
sample stored at ôÙ80 ô¯ C. To justify the method applicability as an exploratory method, particiôÙ
pants were not selected based on known prescription drug usage. This allowed us to not only
assess the changes to the human plasma proteome, but also investigate if it is possible to do
it in a prospective manner. The plasma proteome changes were investigated using classical
abundance based methods, as well as systems biology and network analysis. This next secôÙ
tion argues why it is important to use network concepts when investigating proteomes (A more
thorough explanation of central network concepts and theory can be found in the supplemental
theory).
Network analysis and rewiring in the human plasma proteome
Historically, proteins have been investigated using the reductionist methodology [10]. The reôÙ
ductionist approach aims to explain a system by investigating its individual parts. Inferring corôÙ
rect behaviour of proteins in the reductionist way is particularly easy when the interaction is
simple (Mendel found evidence of inheritance [11] almost 100 years before Watson and Crick
found the chemical basis of inheritance [12]). However, when the interaction is not simple,
the reductionist approach can be infeasible or even impossible. Systems biology aims to look
holistically at all components comprising a biological system [13]. Take the human cell as an
example. As illustrated in fig. 1, A, interaction between DNA, RNA and protein is not always
unidirectional. The central dogma theory correctly identified the transformation of DNA to RNA
to protein [14] but today we realize that these three molecular species have extensive crossôÙ
talk. Examples of this includes protein histones binding DNA to regulate transcription, protein
mediated degradation of RNA, and alternative splicing of RNA by protein complexes. By reducôÙ
ing the size of the system under investigation these interactions would be hard to interpret in a
high throughput manner. By applying a systemsôÙwide approach they can be observed. This is
often done using network analysis, a discipline that is intrinsically linked with systems biology.
Like systems biology, network analysis aims to map molecules, their interactions, and potential
changes within them [15]. This is done by displaying biological entities as simple vertices (also
known as nodes) and their interactions as edges between these vertices. The full set of vertices
and edges is known as a network. Displaying biological entities as vertices and interactions
as edges comes with suppression of biological detail [16], but it opens up a vast toolbox of
analytical methods developed for analyzing networks (see supplementary section on network
concepts for a more thorough explanation of networks and network terminology). One of these
is coôÙexpression analysis (I use the term coôÙexpression analysis as it is the name it is given in
literature but it also applies to protein abundance measures).

Proteome coôÙexpression analysis is dependent on precise quantification of proteins (see supôÙ
plementary section on coôÙexpression analysis for a more detailed review of the topic). Multiple
methods exist for quantifying protein, but MSôÙbased methods remain the gold standard. After
assessing protein levels, coôÙexpression analysis infers relationships between proteins based
on the similarity in expression. Although not direct proof of interaction, correlation has been
shown to be overôÙrepresented in interacting proteins [17]. The inferred relationships between
proteins can be used to generate protein networks, a data representation that display all protein
interactions simultaneously. As mentioned above, the human molecular network spans multiôÙ
ple entities, not just proteins. However, this thesis focuses only on the analysis of the plasma
proteome network.
The mathematical field of graph theory has developed a number of analytical methods for netôÙ
works. These methods can be utilised for analyzing protein networks. One of these methods is
rewiring.
Rewiring is a term used to describe changes in networks as a result of perturbations (see suppleôÙ
mentary section on rewiring for a more detailed description ôÙ a section on differential abundance
analysis is also presented). Perturbations are simply external or internal events affecting a sysôÙ
tem. The nature of perturbations are manyôÙfold, but in this paper the focus is on prescription
drug usage. In Denmark, prescription drug usage is tracked through central databases and by
using the personôÙspecific CPR number, the data can be cross referenced with other databases.
With our 250 participant subsample we set out to investigate the effect of common prescription
drugs on the plasma proteome. However, the first step was to assess representativeness of
the 250 people. Conclusions drawn from an unrepresentative sample cannot be used to draw
overall conclusions on the applicability of the methodology on the rest of the cohort. A thorough
cohort characterization was needed.

Across all samples, 596 proteins were quantified, 164 were present in all samples. 422 were
present in more than 66 % of the samples (See fig. 5 B). Low abundance proteins also had the
highest amount of missingness (see fig. 5, A). This indicates that some missingness could be
due to quantification depth. Predicted protein locations were found using the Human Proteome
Atlas. Proteins with high average abundances were often secreted. There is a clear pattern
that proteins that have a low abundance exhibit high missingness and are not secretory. As a
result, proteins that were present in less than 66 % of samples were removed as inferring them
from the other samples is problematic. The remaining proteins were inferred (see methods on
proteomic data preprocessing). This creates another potential problem, as the signal we are
looking for might indeed be the proteins that show up as a stress response. As the network
generation is dependent on correlations, inferring values would create a number of proteins
with high correlations and therefore the uncommon proteins were discarded.
Protein abundance levels not directly inferable from age and sex
General trends in the abundace based on age and sex was investigated. PCA analysis showed
no clear pattern in abundance between participants based on age or sex (see fig. 6 A). PreviôÙ
ous publications have shown a partitioning in plasma protein abundance based on sex [9] (see
fig. 6 C). This trend is not observeable in our data. However, our study contains 25 times more
participants than the previous studies. As the sample size increases more confounding variôÙ
ables are likely included. As more confounding variables are included more potential sources of
variance follow. This could explain why no principal component explains as large a proportion
of the variance as the earlier study (see fig. 6 B).
Although age and sex does not explain the majority of the variability in the data, these variables

correlations to infer relationships between proteins, which in turn can be used to create modules

the abundance of every gene as a function of age and sex of the study participants. Initial
network generation showed five distinct protein modules (see fig. 8). The module stability was
found by repeating network generation and modularization 50 times (see fig. S2) and calculating
the proportion of times each protein was grouped into the same module (table 3, left module
column). The modules containing only the proteins with a stability above 0.5 were visualized
and named (see fig. 8). However, for subsequent analysis all proteins were kept in the modules.
Calculating the average stability of every module showed one very consistent module (M1) and
a number modules with lower consistency (M2ôÙM5) (table 3).

genes for any of the modules (after Bonferroni multiple testing correction) (results not shown).
DA proteins in modules M1ôÙM5 are represented in red in fig. 8.

Discussion:
To recap, it was shown that the 20 minute MSôÙruns could be used on a prospective cohort
to find evidence of DA proteins in the coagulation cascade of study participants taking oral
contraceptives. No siginificant changes were foud for participants taking penicillin. The rewiring
analysis showed that most generated modules had low reproducibility for participants taking
oral contraceptives. However, it was also apparent that the modules with low preservation had
a low average consistency in the original cohort. One module in particular (M5) showed a high
increase in intraôÙmodular correlation.
Proteomic networks can be made using MS based proteomics
While multiple prior studies have investigated changes in the blood plasma proteins, what distinôÙ
guishes this investigation from these previous works is the use of short runtime MS methodology
on such a large cohort, and the lack of a pathology as the external source perturbation.
Other recent studies utilizing network analysis in plasma proteomes have been done based on
microchips (e.g., see [25, 26]). Conceptually, microchips would hold a different bias than MS
based methods and using MS data is therefore a good supplement to the existing literature. One
crucial difference between microchips and MS workflows is that quantifying proteins with chips
can often lead to a larger number of quantified peptides (e.g. [25] who uses a 4300 protein chip
assay). This could also be achieved by increasing MSôÙrun times [9], but this is both expensive
and would not yield proteomes of a similar size to the chips (ã¥1000 proteins quantified by
increasing MS runtime manyfold [9]). To my knowledge, no direct comparison of chipôÙbased
quantification and MS based quantification exist.
Another aspect of the analysis that became clear was the large dissimilarity between proteins,
even within modules (no dissimilarity score below 0.8). When WGCNA is used, pairwise dissimôÙ
ilarities often get below 0.5 [27, 28]. WGCNA carried out on expression data seem to generate
higher similarities between objects. The times were WGCNA has been carried out on MS data
([29, 30] to name a couple) the dissimilarity sizes are quite similar to what we found.
Most MSôÙbased investigations contain far fewer participants. As we saw in fig. 6, previous invesôÙ
tigation using similar methodology have covariates as sex explaining a far greater proportion of
the variance. As we include more study participants the amount of confounding factors increase
and the signal can become noisier. However, this is a balancing act, as increasing sample size
also offers obvious strengths. Increasing sampling size renders methodology more robust to
outliers, and increases the likelihood of getting a sample representative of the underlying disôÙ
tribution. Furthermore, the conclusions that can be drawn from studies with large sample sizes
are oftentimes more applicable to the rest of the population, as small samples are often only
representative of the few people in the sample. However, the fact remains that the variation is
often larger in large samples.
One especially important factor for this variation could be genetics [31]. Quantified protein
levels have been shown to be associated with a number of quantitative trait loci. The analysis

Adverse effects of prescription drugs show mixed results
Our findings showed very little effect on the plasma proteome by penicillin. Although pencillin
have been showed to affect the gut microbiome on a time scale similar to similar to the preôÙ
scriptions used in this paper[32], the effects were largest in the weeks following the antiobiotic
treatment. As quarantine periods are in place for penicillin, the main effects of them might not
be visible. Futhermore, unlike oral contraceptives, antibiotic treatment is only given once, and
the changes to the proteome could be fleeting.
Possible adverse effects of hormonal contraceptives have been known almost as long as they
have been prescribed [33]. Especially the influence of hormonal contraceptives on the carôÙ
diovascular system and the coagulation cascade has been under investigation in recent years
[34]. Clinically this often manifests as increased risk of venous thrombosis. The conducted
DA protein analysis showed upôÙregulation of three specific pathways: fibrinolysis, regulation of
coagulation and affected endopeptidase activity. fibrinolysis, endopeptidase activity, and regôÙ
ulation of coagulation could all potentially influence thrombosis. Our ability to replicate known
adverse effects in plasma protein measurements shows promise for using differential protein
abundance analysis in the DBDS.
The results of the rewiring analysis showed mixed results. Firstly, the modules that showed little
evidence of preservation also had the lowest stability when generating modules in full cohort.
As a result, it is hard to argue that lack of module preservation is caused by drug perturbations
and not simply low module stability. Other studies performing rewiring analysis in blood plasma
have been able to show effects of pertubations like Alzheimerãs disease, brain trauma or type 2
diabetes mellitus [29, 26, 4]. These studies find upwards of 50 different modules ranging in sizes
between 20 and 500 proteins in every module. That our study only finds 5 distinct modules could
be due to methodology. The original purpose of the shorter MS protocol used here was to be
applicable in the clinic [9]. Although of very high quality, for the purpose of network generation,
it may have been better use of resources to sequence fewer samples deeper.
The intraôÙmodular correlation changes of M5 are particularly interesting, since the increase in
correlation is very large compared to the changes in the other modules. Almost all proteins
in the module are part of the immune system, and the increase in correlation could show an
increase in immune response. If the time had permitted, this phenomenon would have been
interesting to investigate, but for now the underlying reason remains hidden.
A question that is worth considering is whether the interesting modules are those with high
consistency or low consistency. High consistency modules could show rewiring, but maybe the
16
==PAGE:29==
reason why they are consistent is that their function is highly conserved. In the DBDS study
participants have declared themselves healthy and we might not expect crucial blood modules
to change much as this would cause disease states. The proteins that fall outside the modules
require more investigation.
Cohorts with strict inclusion criteria shows potential for investigation
prescription drugs
The Danish blood donor study has published multiple papers on the general demographics
and genetic data of its participants [19, 18]. The joining of the DBDS and national prescription
drug registries is more recent, and a limited number of publications have been generated using
this data (to my knowledge only [35, 36, 37] have published papers based on the prescription
drug history of the Danish blood donors). All these papers share a common methodology:
Investigating overôÙrepresentation of drugs in a particular ãdiseasedã subset of the Danish blood
donors. Our approach has been different as we have let patterns in prescription drug usage
drive data analysis.
We took the initial steps of a larger explorative characterization of cohort prescription drug usôÙ
age (see fig. 3). This has previously been done for the entire Danish population [38], but to
my knowledge it has not yet been published for the DBDS. One of the central requirements for
the analysis undertaken in this paper was the presence of a sufficient number of people taking
drugs that could reasonably be assumed to cause common system perturbations. The cost and
time used in estimating proteome profiles of participant makes unfeasible to carry out several
thousand of MS proteome quantifications. Even with a methodically realistic subsample size of
randomly selected participants (random on the basis of drug usage) we found patterns in drug
usage that could be used for analysis. All reason tells us that the statistical power when investiôÙ
gating drug specific changes would only increase if the investigated subset of study participants
had been chosen based on prescription usage.
The fact that all major ATC groups are represented in almost all of the subôÙsampled patients
has another interesting takeaway. In an attempt to eliminate variance in measurements caused
by external factors, most highôÙthroughput methods have historically modelled away factors like
age and sex. However, recent studies show that factors like circadian rhythm [39] have an effect
on blood composition (on an RNA level at least). As more and more data becomes available,
the number of external factors we know influence our measurements increases. As our results
show, plasma protein abundance is significantly altered when taking hormonal contraceptives.
Considering this in other similar studies could be important for limiting noiseôÙtoôÙsignal ratio.
Investigating prescription drugs could contribute to online network ecosystem:
In the age of big data and highôÙthrougput technologies it is worth taking some time to conôÙ
sider how our results might fit into the current public access ecosystem of proteomics. Online
resources of the human proteome [40, 23, 41] and more specifically drugôÙprotein interaction alôÙ
ready exist [42]. However, for these databases to evolve, multiple different high quality studies
investigating protein interactions have to be made. As mentioned earlier, the context of which
the prescription drugs have been investigated in this paper is rather unique due to the strict inôÙ
clusion criteria of the DBDS. These kind of investigations provide new information that should be
17
==PAGE:30==
incorporated into online databases. Expanding knowledge of protein interactions might even be
close to having clincial applicability. Streamlined methodology to incorporate network concepts
into clinical proteomics is being developed [43]. Adding the effects of prescription drugs to these
methods is could provide valuable information. Our own analysis pipeline could might also have
been improved by incorporating existing knowledge en plasma proteome modularization.
A recent study of all danish prescription usage showed statistically significant trajectories beôÙ
tween prescription drug usage [38]. That is specific combinations of drugs occur sequentially
more often than what you would expect to be the case. Although the DBDS obviously does not
contain the same number of prescription drugs as the entire danish population, it does contain
blood samples with regular intervals. It would be interesting to see investigate similar trajecôÙ
tories in the DBDS, and see if blood donations match times when medicine was changed. By
estimating the network state (it does not have to be the proteome) before and after changes in
prescriptions, trajectory specific rewiring might be elucidated.
In summation, this study shows that even in a cohort with strict health inclusion criteria like
the DBDS, prescription drugs are taken by the majority of participants. That we are able to
see differences in plasma proteins based on hormonal contraceptives shows us two things:
1) Side effects of drugs that are already known can be found in discovery cohorts 2) healthy
cohorts can be used for discovery of an array of subôÙclinical changes. It is not necessary for
clinical manifestations of adverse drug effects (e.g., blood clots in the example of hormonal
contraceptives) to be present for us to notice changes in healthy patients. This methodology
opens up new ways of investigating the effects prescription drugs have, and incorporating these
findings into the exisiting omicsôÙplatforms could have uses beyond the plasma proteome.
Methods:
Cohort characterization:
125 male and female participants were randomly selected (N = 250) from the DBDS participants
with the following exclusion criteria: 1) They had to be active donors in 2018, and 2) their last
sample has been kept at ôÙ80 ô¯C. An initial visual cohort characterization was done to assess
distribution of age. Density distributions of age estimated using a Gaussian kernel filter. Weight
and height are self reported through the DBDS questionnaires and as a result they are someôÙ
times omitted. A detailed explanation of the DBDS can be found in the methods section on the
DBDS.
Sample preparation:
Blood samples were collected from venepuncture of the elbow joint vein (antecubital fossa).
After sampling, samples were treated with EDTA, centrifuged, the red blood cells removed, and
kept at ôÙ80 ô¯C. At the time of analysis, the samples were thawed and sample preparation done
according to the methods presented by Geyer et al. [9]. The blood was donated with informed
consent with the general inclusion criteria matching that of the Danish blood donors [21].
The overall pipeline for plasma protein preparation can be briefly described as: 1) reduction
and denaturing, 2) digestion into peptides, 3) isolation of peptides, and 4) solubilizing peptides
18
==PAGE:31==
in buffer fit for HPLC.
To reduce and denature proteins, blood plasma was added SDC reduction and alkylation buffer
(24 ôçL per ôçL blood plasma) and boiled for 10 minutes. For protein digestion a mix of lysin and
trypsin was added (1:100 ratio between digestive peptides and plasma protein assessed by
tryptophan flourescense emission assay), to the reduced/denatured protein mix and the digesôÙ
tion performed at 37 ô¯C for an hour. The digested peptides were then centrifuged in specialized
StageTips, and all the nonôÙpeptide parts washed away using washôÙbuffer. The remaining pepôÙ
tides were eluted using elution buffer, dried and suspended in LCôÙbuffer (2 % acetonitrile, 0.1
% TFA).
HPLC MS:
HPLCôÙMS was likewise done according to the method described in [9]. The suspended peptides
were transferred to a chromotography column (EASY nLC 1000 ultraôÙhigh pressure system,
Thermo Fisher scientific) and eluted using buffer A. A linear 15 min gradient of buffer B (60 %
acetonitrile) 10ôÙ50 % was then added. Finally, the column was washed using 98 % B buffer.
The elution was ionized using an electropsray (nanoôÙelectrospray ion source, Thermo Fisher
scientific) ionizer and analyzed on an Oribtrap MS (Thermo Fisher scientific). Data was acquired
using dataôÙdependent MS/MS scan method for optimization of MS spectra parameters.
The corresponding MSôÙraw files were quantified using MaxQuant software and peptide lists
were searched against a UniProt database. Protein quantification was based on amount of
peptides mapping to the reference database.
Proteomic data preprocessing:
Label free quantification values were generated using MaxQuant software. Some samples had
multiple distinct MS runs, in these cases LFQ was the average of the two runs. Every samôÙ
ple was adjusted to library size by multiplying each protein with a sample specific factor. The
sample specific factor is the sum of LFQ values in the sample divided by the highest sum of
LFQ values across samples. If a protein is present in less than a third of the samples it is
discarded. Proteins with missing values in 45ôÙ66 % of samples are sampled from a normal
distribution under detection limit. The detection limit is set to be the smallest measured value of
the libraryôÙnormalized proteins. Proteins with missingness between 0ôÙ45 % was imputed using
kôÙnearest neighbours. Different imputation techniques was used depending on the proportion
of missing values. If less than 45 % of values were missing that protein was assumed missôÙ
ing completely at random (MCAR), otherwise the value was deemed missing not at random
(MNAR). The method applied for MCAR is KNN and MNAR is minprob imputed [44].
After value imputation every sample was log2ôÙtransformed and ZôÙscore normalised. A linear
model (age and sex as independent variables) was fitted for every protein and the residuals
used for downstream analysis.
Protein exploration:
Initial screening of proteins was performed. To estimate the tissue of origin for every protein
the Human Protein Atlas database was used [23]. The webpage version was scraped (using a
custom BeautifulSoup [45]ôÙbased python script) for information on tissue specificity, predicted
19
==PAGE:32==
cellular location, and immune cell specificity. The predicted cellular location was narrowed down
to whether the protein was secreted, intracellular, or both. The information gathered from the
Human Protein Atlas was crossôÙreferenced with all the protein measured in the samples.
Prescription drug usage: Quarantine periods
ATC codes for every drug was downloaded from the WHO database [46]. The corresponding
ATC codes for every drug was crossôÙreferenced to the prescription drug registries of all DBDS
participants. Strict drug quarantine periods are in place for blood donations [21]. However, the
quarantine information is not readily accessible in table form. To assess quarantine periods for
each drug, proôÙmedicin.dk was web scraped for quarantine periods. Some drugs that share
ATC codes can differ in severity and be used for different subset of patients. As a result, the
same ATC code can have multiple quarantine lengths depending on the drug. In cases where
an ATC code had multiple quarantine lengths the longest was chosen. For severe disease some
ATC codes have associated drugs that require the donor to be removed as a blood donor. If an
ATC code has both a quarantine period for one drug and removal of patient for another drug,
the quarantine period was selected. It was assumed that persons would not be a part of the
blood donor population if they are severely sick.
Prescription drug usage: Sample representativeness and selecting drugs for
analysis
Assessing sample representativeness was done by bootstrapping 250 random DBDS particiôÙ
pants 1000 times and looking at the distribution of ATC group 1 classes. Prescriptions within
each ATC anatomical subgroup of every subsample was summed up and the density estimated
using a Gaussian density kernel. The full sample was then investigated by determining how
many of the 1000 subsamples that were more extreme. If the original sample was larger than
the median of the 1000 subsamples, the pôÙvalue was estimated as the amount of subsamples
that were even larger than the original sample. This was done to emulate a oneôÙsided statistical
test.
To select the subset of drugs for further analysis the top 3 ATC anatomical subgroups were
looked at. A prescription was included if it had been redeemed within 12 months of the blood
donation used for proteome quantification. The next exclusion criteria was that at least 50
prescriptions had to be given within a single pharmacological subgroup within the last year.
Lastly, the two most common chemical subgroups were chosen for analysis. This included
progesterones and penicillin.
Because of the large amount of oral contraceptives taken, it was possible to only use study
participants that have been shown to receive a prescription within 90 days before AND after
donation date. This yielded 40 participants. Penicillin was less commonly used and further exôÙ
clusion criteria was therefore deemed too costly when considering loss of power. We therefore
included 37 participants that had received treatment up to 12 months prior to the blood donation.
Differential protein abundance analysis:
Differential protein analysis was performed using limma. Limma utilises counts, and raw LFQ
count data was therefore used. The first step was to create a design matrix. Two separate
20
==PAGE:33==
matrices were made: one for contraceptions and one penicillins. As this part of the analysis
does not use model residuals, the design matrix includes an intercept, age in years, sex, and one
variable containing information on usage of penicillin or contraceptions. For oral contraceptives
two analysis were performed. One that includes all participants and one that includes only
women.
After generating a design matrix voom was used to log2ôÙtransform and to infer weights of each
protein in each sample based on overall meanôÙvariance trend. These normalized data with
added weights were then used used to fit an array of linear models (lmfit) and used in the
empirical Bayes function (bayes) to model variances for DE gene assessment. This information
borrowing is one reason why two separate analysis were performed for oral contraceptives. On
one hand including men could artificially increase statistical power by adding participants that
provide no additional information. However, by adding more gene measurements, the meanôÙ
variance trend could be improved, and as we have no reason to believe that protein quanôÙ
tification meanôÙvariance trend is sexôÙdependent, including men could improve model variance
estimation.
GOôÙterms were investigated using the online PANTHER tool (the GOôÙterm specific pipeline)
and a reference of blood proteins extracted from the Human Protein Atlas. OverôÙabundance
of specific biological GOôÙterms was tested using a Fisherãs exact test and the pôÙvalues were
BenjaminiôÙHochberg corrected.
Network creation:
Creating coôÙabundance networks was done using the workflow presented by Steve Horvath
and colleagues [47]. First a correlation matrix was calculated using the biweight midcorrelation
(see WGCNA and biweight midcorrelation supplementary theory). The correlation matrix was
converted into an adjacency matrix using the power function (xö£). The ö£ was estimated using a
scaleôÙfree topology criterion (R squared > 0.8). The adjacency measure was converted into a
dissimilarity measure using topological overlap dissimilarity. The topological overlap measure
dissimilarity was then used to generate generate modules.
A hard adjacency matrix was also created for visualizing networks. Soft adjacency matrices
have lines between all the nodes, so to only visualize the important edges, a hard adjacency
matrix was used. The hard adjacency matrix was created by thresholding the correlation maôÙ
trix with a thresholding parameter chosen so that the network connectivity follows a scaleôÙfree
distribution.
Generating protein modules and assigning module consistency:
Protein modules were estimated using the dynamical tree cutting algorithm with a deepsplit
of 3, a minimum cluster size of 10 and mediods option enabled [48]. After module generation,
module correlation was investigated using the first eigengene (the sample projected onto the first
principal component of PCA of the protein abundance). If the RôÙsquared value of the correlation
between eigengenes was larger than 0.8 the modules were merged. Unassigned genes were
not used for subsequent analysis.
Consistent modularization of every protein was estimated using subsampling. The above analôÙ
21
==PAGE:34==

Quantification of protein
Understanding the nature, strengths, and limitations of the method used to quantify protein
levels is crucial for further analysis. There has been a revolution within highôÙthroughput genetic
sequencing in recent years, but unfortunately protein levels are not directly inferable from their
preceding molecular stages, RNA and DNA [50]. The quantification of protein has turned out
to be a lot more challenging than RNA and DNA and only recently has it become possible to
quantify proteins in a highôÙthroughput manner. Although different analytical methods for protein
quantification exist, the most common way of performing highôÙthroughput proteomics is MassôÙ
spectrometry (MS) [51, 52]. This section will briefly review MSôÙbased proteomic methodology.
The emphasis will be on the general concepts, not the inner workings of the physics underlying
MS since this is beyond the scope of this thesis.
All proteomic MS utilise the different massôÙcharge ratios (m/z) of proteins and peptides have
upon ionization [53]. This difference in mass charge ratio can be used by a mass analyser to
separate and quantify proteins. Proteins can be analyzed in a topôÙdown or bottomôÙup (also
known as shotôÙgun) manner (see fig. Supp. Theory 1 A). In a topôÙdown approaches, the m/z of
entire proteins are used, whereas bottomôÙup approaches proteolytically degrade proteins into
smaller peptides prior to analysis. Distinguishing native proteins based on their m/z can be
challenging, as they can be quite similar. As a result, topôÙdown proteomics are often preceded
by a pullôÙdown of relevant proteins [54]. This performs well for assessing presence or amount
of clinically relevant proteins, but for exploratory analysis there is an inherent bias in limiting
the measured proteins to those targeted by the pullôÙdown. BottomôÙup approaches are therefore
prefered in explorative analysis.
The first steps modern bottomôÙup proteomics protocols are protein isolation and digestion. MS
can be performed on multiple biological entities (i.e., lipids, proteins, and metabolites) and proôÙ
tein isolation is necessary to get a clean proteome readôÙout. Protein digestion is done to inôÙ
crease the sensitivity of the MS measurement, as MS is best suited to extract sequence informaôÙ
tion in shorter peptides [55]. Performing proteomic analysis in this way eliminates the selecting
bias of topôÙdown approaches, but introduces new challenges e.g., detecting low concentrationôÙ
proteins.
One way of overcoming this is through an initial peptide sorting. This can be done on a high
pressure liquid chromatography (HPLC) column to minimize the range of peptides being ionized
at any given time. Furthermore, immunodepletion is sometimes used in samples with a big
difference between the abundance of the protein with the lowest and the highest abundances
[9]. Immunodepletion utilises antibodies to remove some of the high abundance proteins before
transferring the sample to a HPLC column. However, immunodepletion also creates a bias, as
proteins partaking in protein interactions can be removed. In blood, proteins often associate with
e.g., albumin, which is also one of the most abundant proteins in blood and one that is often

Network nomenclature
Network analysis (also known as graph theory) is said to have its origins with the Swiss mathôÙ
ematician Leonard Euler in 1735 (as reviewed in [72]). Euler attempted to solve a problem
revolved around the most efficient route over seven bridges connecting four pieces of land in
the city of KûÑningsberg. He approached this problem by eliminating all other factors than land
masses and bridges, effectively displaying them as vertices and edges respectively. Since then,
the field of graph theory has expanded, and understanding its application in this thesis requires
a short introduction to some central graph concepts.

Weighted Gene CoôÙexpression Network Analysis (WGCNA)
An old saying in neurology goes: ãWhat fires together wires togetherã [77]. Putting the obvious

function. CoôÙexpression analysis is based on the idea that similarity in expression patterns conôÙ

proteins can be converted into protein adjacencies and a network can be made. Certain design

also applies to protein abundances.

WGCNA is therefore to construct an adjacency matrix using expression data [78]. The steps
in doing this are as follows: 1) Choose measure of similarity and create similarity matrix from
expression data, 2) choose adjacency functions to convert similarity measure into adjacency
measure, 3) determine optimal adjacency function parameters, and 4) convert similarity matrix
into adjacency matrix [78].

and logôÙtransform them. After transformation, a linear model is fitted to every feature and the
variance of the residuals is calculated. A LOWESS (nonôÙparametric) fit is then fitted to the
varianceôÙmean abundance of every feature. Since every point has an abundance, variance of

is achieved by fitting additional hyperôÙparameters to all geneôÙwise models. HyperôÙparameters
are parameters that are shared between all geneôÙwise models, and are preôÙdefined before the
geneôÙwise linear model coefficients are calculated. Weighted least squares regression is then
performed based on the Voom weights.
The final step of limma is the testing for differential abundance based on the linear model coôÙ

limma also utilises information sharing in this part of the pipeline. The tôÙtest is dependent on the
sample variance, in the case of DE testing this variance is the variance of the geneôÙwise model

residual sample variance. As the sample variance decreases, the statistical power increases.

uses the BenjaminiôÙHochberg pôÙvalue correction [87].
Rewiring:
The purpose of the following section is to specify what rewiring means and cover how it can
be quantified. Biological rewiring can be defined as: ãthe inherent restructuring of interactions
between biological units owing to conditional changeã [88]. However, this definition only makes
sense when considering the otherwise stable structure of networks.
Most organisms have a number of core functionalities that are needed to sustain them and as
a result, biological systems are constant [89]. A systemãs inherent ability to resist stimuli/perôÙ
turbations and remain constant is called system robustness. The more robust a system is the
more resistant it is to lasting change and change in functionality. As a result of system robustôÙ
ness, networks are believed to exist in states, platforms of equilibrium where protein levels and
interactions remain constant (constant within a certain interval). The concept of system states
have been experimentally demonstrated in bacteria and yeast [90], but as is often the case, the
situation in humans seems more complex.
When the system changes state it is known as rewiring. Rewiring analysis has historically been
investigated for pathologies [91, 4]. The reasons are obvious ôÙ mapping pathological network
states could lead to novel ways of treating the conditions. However, there could be a number
of rewiring events prior to pathological network states, priming the pathological rewiring event.
Investigating these hold great value in mapping underlying network structure and getting a step
closer to creating the first dynamical networks.
Conceptually, rewiring can be measured on different timescales resulting in dynamical netôÙ
42
==PAGE:55==
works or differential networks. The most common approach is differential networks. A difôÙ
ferential network is simply the accumulated differences between two networks. This approach
is often the only one that is feasible, but the downside is that multiple rewiring events can have
occurred between two measured states.
A dynamical network provides a continuous measurement of each vertex and edge in a network.
In other words, it maps all rewiring events gradually. This requires temporal measurements,
which is often not possible. As a result, almost all rewiring analyses are based on differential
networks.
In the case of coôÙexpression networks, the rewiring events are quantified based on changes in
correlation. Here, I have used two distinct ways of quantifying rewiring. The first way is to check
for module preservation. If the proteins in a novel cohort are no longer preserved, we can asôÙ
sume that rewiring has occurred. More specifically, this module preservation is summarized with
a summary statistic called Zsummary. The Z summary statistic is an average of several different
zôÙscores. Z scores are calculated for a couple of the central network metrics mentioned above
(e.g., average connectivity) [49]. A distribution of each network metric is estimated by subsamôÙ
pling. Participants taking prescription drugs are subsampled 50 times. For every subsampling
modules are made by randomly assigning proteins to modules with the same size as the origôÙ
inal modules. The easiest way to explain is using an example. A module with 50 proteins has
been estimated using all the individuals not taking prescription drugs. Central network metrics
of a module containing exactly these proteins are calculated in the cohort taking prescription
drugs. 50 subsamplings then occur in the prescription drug cohort. For each subsampling a
module containing 50 random proteins is made, and the same network metrics estimated. The
mean and standard deviation of the central network metrics from the 50 subsamplings is used
in a normal distribution. From the normal distributions of the different metrics the zôÙscore of the
original module in the prescription drug cohort can be calculated.
Another method of quantifying rewiring is by using biweight midcorrelation directly. The average
midcorrelation of every module and all the pairwise midcorrelations are estimated. This is done
for both the discovery and test cohorts. The difference between the estimated correlations is
then calculated as a measure of intraôÙmodular correlation change [49]. Conceptually, this can
be used in a number of different ways. The mean difference in correlation as an overall pattern
of change within the modules, the pairwise correlation changes to find particular genes driving
the rewiring or the most changed paths (i.e., neighbouring edges) symbolising possible pathway
changes.
