Abstract
This report investigates the dynamics of two parts coupled through a contact interface,
focusing on the behaviour arising from nonlinear vibrations. The two studied parts are
small beams that may potentially represent two parts of a hearing aid device. The motiva-
tion behind this study is to analyze the vibration transmission between parts and identify
the nonlinear behaviours, in attempt to combat the occurrence of mechanical feedback
in hearing aids. While the theoretical framework is based on a simplified beam problem,
the findings lay the groundwork for extending the analysis to more complex structures and
contact geometries. Through a comprehensive analysis, both experimental and numerical,
it is revealed that nonlinear phenomena, including harmonic distortion, aperiodic motion,
bifurcations, and hardening effects, are apparent in the contact interaction between vibrat-
ing parts. The prominence of these phenomena depends on the geometrical configuration
of the two parts and the excitation parameters. The study identifies three cases that exhibit
significantly distinct vibration behaviours: strong contact, weak contact, and occasional
contact case. It is found that the case of strong contact between the parts can to some
extent be modelled using a linear contact model, rather than the nonlinear one, which
simplifies the problem. The contact force between the parts and the resulting response to
excitation depends primarily on the initial gap distance between the parts, and the magni-
tude of excitation. In summary, this paper contributes to understanding the challenges of
nonlinear vibrations in contact interfaces, proposing a modeling approach, combined with
experimental validation and areas for future research to enhance accuracy and predictive
capabilities in practical applications.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Hearing aids are devices designed to restore and improve the user’s ability to hear, and
play a significant role in the lives of individuals with hearing impairments. These small
but complex devices use advanced technologies to amplify and deliver sound to the users’
ears, however, the size and complexity of hearing aids (HA) introduce limitations and
challenges to their performance that call for advancing research and development. One of
the issues that can compromise the hearing aid perfomance is the occurance of feedback,
which arises from the interaction between the receiver (loudspeaker) and the microphone.
Feedback is generally understood as unwanted amplification of resonant frequencies in
the receiver-microphone loop, resulting in unpleasant howling sound. It is a significant
hinder to the effective use of HA. This does not only result in a poor sound quality ex-
perience for the user, but also decreases the usefulness by limiting the safe amplification
range of the hearing aid, known as the feedback margin. One of the scenarios in which
feedback can occur is when sound vibrations generated by the receiver are mechanically
transmitted through the hearing aid structure and eventually reach the microphone, that
feeds this signal back into the receiver. This type of feedback is referred to as mechanical,
or structure-borne feedback. A possible feedback path in a hearing aid is shown in Fig-
ure 1.1 To address this issue, one needs methods and models that capture the behaviour of
hearing aid components under vibrational loads. Models that can simulate the interaction
between hearing aid parts are an important tool in the design and development process
of new hearing aid solutions, as they can be used to predict and reduce occurrences of
feedback. The contact between parts introduces uncertainties into the problem in sev-
eral ways. These uncertainties stem from design features, manufacturing inaccuracies,
mechanical loads, and external factors, and are associated with contact forces, frictional
effects, and potential wear.
The primary objective of this thesis is to investigate the dynamics of two parts coupled
through a contact interface, focusing on the behaviour arising from nonlinear vibrations,
in order to examine their significance in vibration analysis of parts in contact. This is
achieved by developing a mathematical model that represents this problem. The two parts

1.2
State of The Art
To confirm the relevance of this study’s subject, and emphasize its significance, some
literature on the topic of feedback and vibrations in hearing aids (HA) has been reviewed.
This section presents the key findings.
Feedback in HA, as well as in other audio systems is a well-known and documented prob-
lem, that has been apparent ever since a microphone was first connected to a loudspeaker
[2]. Hearing aids are an example of an audio system where the microphone and the re-
ceiver are placed close to each other, which is the main cause of feedback. It is observed
both during tests in the development phase and in daily use, by the HA-users. “The prob-
lem of feedback in hearing aids” [3] gives a clear picture of feedback in HA and gives
some examples of its occurrence and prevention, although many new feedback reduction
methods emerge, as it is a topic of high interest and relevance. Feedback can stem from
various components, mechanisms, and circuits within a hearing aid, and is generally cat-
egorized into internal or external, or acoustic or mechanical. Acoustic feedback is the
case when the sound produced by the receiver in the ear leaks out and back to the HA mi-
crophone. Mechanical feedback involves vibration generated by the receiver membrane
and by the air pressure accumulating inside the hearing aid tubes and conducts, leading to
vibration in the parts .

Since the phenomenon of feedback is complex and can stem from many sources, the avail-
able solutions and methods address different aspects and stages of design and development
of a hearing aid. Reducing feedback in the design phase of a HA is an area of great focus,
and many studies propose design solutions that minimize feedback.
“Reducing acoustic feedback in hearing aids” [4] presents several methods on how to ac-
tively reduce acoustic feedback, using cancellation and filtering methods. Similarly, [5]
proposes adaptive feedback reduction algorithms that use adaptive gain reduction, notch
filtering, and phase cancellation strategies, as the most reliable and efficient methods used
in the industry. Although methods in this and other similar papers suggest valid solu-
tions, they do not address the source of the problem, namely the acoustic and mechanical
feedback paths themselves. Investigating and minimizing potential feedback sources and
paths in the design stage prevent some feedback from occurring at all.
A broadly studied approach to combat mechanical and acoustic feedback is to carry out a
vibro-acoustic analysis of a hearing aid model already in the prototype stage, as presented
in “Vibro-acoustic analysis and optimization of hearing aids” [6]. The study investigates
vibrations transmitted due to the HA receiver, which creates an internal feedback path. The
research led to improving the overall performance and reducing the feedback caused by
those vibrations. The vibro-acoustic model developed in the study improved the vibration
isolation mounts which effectively reduced the vibrations transmitted through the hearing
aid, without sacrificing the performance accuracy.
A crucial element of feedback prevention is to be able to predict and consider the feedback
already in the design phase of a HA. Many methods used in the industry help to achieve
this. One obvious approach is to use numerical methods like finite element method to
analyse the HA model, as suggested in “Structural feedback analysis of a hearing aid using
finite element and lumped parameters methods”[7]. Here, the HA model is reduced to the
main components associated with the structural feedback mechanism which are: case,
receiver and receiver suspension. Using finite element method, the researchers perform a
modal analysis of the HA case and simulate its response to the operation of the receiver. In
this way, one can model the response of the hearing aid to sound produced by the receiver,
and predict the occurrence of a feedback loop by investigating the resonant frequencies at
specific gain stages.
”Balanced inlet design for microphone vibration sensitivity cancellation in hearing aids”
[8] proposes a new inlet design that reduces the total vibration sensitivity, by taking into
account the feedback caused by both, sound-leakage through air, and internal air pres-
sure building inside of sound tubes. The newly designed inlet makes the two feedback
signals cancel each other out. The effectiveness of the design proposal was examined
and improved using finite element analysis on vibro-acoustic models of hearing aids, and
validated through experimental measurements.
Reviewed literature indicates that structural vibrations are a significant cause of internal
feedback, therefore it is mostly relevant to study the dynamic properties of a hearing aid.
The literature also points in the direction of vibration analysis of HA systems, already in
the design phase, to predict and prevent potential feedback issues. Tools like finite element
method and other numerical methods may aid in performing such an analysis. It seems
that interfaces between components like receiver-to-case interface are often in focus, as
it is where the vibrations are transmitted, therefore vibration in contact interfaces is a
significant focus area. The review of the literature shows that the subject of non-linear

vibrations in hearing aid assemblies is somewhat lacking, and not extensively explored.
As for the problem of this study, theoretical methods in the fields of non-linear vibrations
and contact mechanics are required to develop a useful model. Literature in those fields is
collected and reviewed as well, to extract the essential tools and apply them to the proposed
problem.
Beams are structural elements commonly discussed in literature. Numerous theories are
available to aid in studying vibrations of beams. ”Dynamics of transversely vibrating
beams using four engineering theories” [9] provides an excellent overview over the four
most common beam theories, all for different applications and degrees of accuracy. Ad-
ditionally, textbooks like [10], [11] and [12] provide extensive methods on deriving and
studying the vibrational models of continuous systems, including beams.
To further study the system, mathematical tools for qualitative and quantitative analysis
are necessary. One commonly used technique for a wide range of nonlinear vibration
problems is harmonic balance method (HBM). [13] gives an excellent guide on how to
formulate, solve and study a harmonic balance problem based on a dynamic system. Many
studies follow the HBM approach making it a well-documented method.
[14] shows how the HBM can be applied to non-smooth dynamic systems, just like the
system of this study. It suggests using HBM combined with the Galerkin method, to derive
the harmonic balance equations, and then using a Newton-Raphson solving procedure to
solve the equation system. In the process of solving complex vibration problems, nonlin-
ear equations often appear naturally. Solving nonlinear equation systems often requires
numerical solution methods like for example the Newton-Raphson method, and similar
iterative methods [15, p. 231].
Problems discussed in [16] and [17] are both on cantilever beams under harmonic exci-
tation with a nonlinear force, due to contact with an obstacle, similarly to the problem of
this study. They both indicate that the resulting response of a system with contact may
exhibit discontinuities in the frequency and time domain.
The ideas and methodologies found in the literature mentioned above serve as a reference
to develop a mathematical model for the problem of this study. Some approaches and
methodologies are adapted into this study to create a framework for the analysis of the
given problem.

CHAPTER 2
Mathematical Model
To analyze the vibrations of the beam system, one needs a mathematical system based
on the equations of motion (EOM) of the beams. The mathematical system consists of
differential equations expressed in terms of beams’ displacements and its derivatives with
respect to time. The EOM’s of the system in this study are derived by combining Newton’s
second law, theory of beam vibrations, and contact mechanics.
The system in its mathematical form is expected to comply to a set of assumptions, which
allow to employ known vibration theories. Firstly, Euler-Bernoulli beam behaviour is
assumed since the beams are considered slender, and the system fulfills the Euler-Bernoulli
assumptions [9, p. 938]
The contact interaction between beams is not easily modelled, therefore the system fol-
lows another simplification. When beams come into contact, a pressure arises across the
area of contact. In a dynamic interaction, both the contact area and the pressure distribu-
tion vary with respect to time, and are unknown. First assumption is to consider the two
beams rigid, so that they do not deform due to or change shape when subjected to external
force. Additionally, when beams come into contact, friction forces arise. In this model,
the friction is neglected, and the pressure is approximated by an equivalent point force FN
at some point xN. By doing this, the contact force can be incorporated into the equations
of motion in form of a point force. Although this is a big simplification, it allows a more
straightforward investigation of the dynamics. Other contact models that account for dis-
tributed contact forces and friction exist„ like for Hertz contact theory [18], or pressure
distribution models [19] and can potentially be incorporated into the model presented in
this study.

2.3
Harmonic Balance Method
The system of equations established above represents a combined system of two beams
exhibiting non-linear behaviour because of the contact coupling. In fact, the system can
be considered piece-wise linear — it may behave linearly when there is no contact, or
strong contact between beams, and nonlinearly when the contact is weak. To capture the
nonregular behaviour one has to deploy a method for approximating the response of a
non-linear system. The harmonic balance method (HBM) is a mathematical technique
used to analyze the steady-state vibrations of non-linear systems. To apply the method,
one assumes a harmonic solution in the form of a Fourier series with a truncation of H
lowest harmonics. Then, it balances out the harmonic terms by applying the Galerkin
method on the governing equations with assumed harmonic solutions, which transforms
them into a set algebraic equations. When solved these equations provide the unknown
amplitudes of the harmonic response. Choosing the appropriate number of harmonics to
account for requires some prior knowledge of the system, and higher number of harmonics
usually gives higher accuracy, however a single-harmonic approximation may often yield
informative results [14]. To derive the equations needed to calculate the amplitude in the
frequency domain, the HBM is applied to the system in question. For that, it is useful to
distinguish the linear terms, from the nonlinear terms, so 2.36 is rewritten, and defined as
the residual equation R.

2.3.1
Smooting Function
In the case of a nonsmooth nonlinear force like the contact force discussed in this study, the
nonlinear force does not change smoothly in the time period T — the force can be either
0 or fc, based on the geometrical configuration of the system at given time, as stated in
Equation 2.3. Therefore the residual function R cannot be directly integrated in the time
domain. One can address this issue in a few ways, e.g integrate by parts, to calculate the
unknown transition time, that is the time point at which the nonsmoothness occurs, and the
force changes its state. This approach is considered most accurate, given that one accounts
for all transitions during one period, and separate the integrals for each transition (there
can be more than one transition) [14]. Another technique of handling the non-smoothness
is by using a threshold function to smooth the nonlinear force in time [24].
A threshold function distributes the state transition over time rather than letting it be in-
stantaneous. This allows to integrate the residual equation with the smoothed nonlinearity
to obtain the algebraic equations. Many known functions are commonly used as a thresh-
old function [25]. For the system discussed here, a hyperbolic tangent function is used
because when properly scaled it approaches the characteristics of the Heaviside function
as the steepness β approaches infinity, and it is smooth and differentiable everywhere on
its curve. The smoothing function has the form
h(ζ) = 1 + tanh (β(ζ −η))
2

where β is the steepness, η is the threshold, and ζ is the input variable, which in this case
corresponds to the gap function of the beams g(x, t). See Figure 2.2 for the illustration of
the threshold function. Now, the smoothed nonlinear force is
fc = Q(q, p, t) · h(−gj(t)),
(2.45)

the contact constraint is not strictly enforced, as the force may gradually increase or de-
crease as the gap approaches 0 from positive or negative side, rather than changing step-
wise as it is assumed in the mathematical derivation. This is a simplification that may
deviate from the physical system, however with a high β, one approaches the behaviour
of a step function. Since the area of contact is approximated by a point, the exact state-
transition of the contact force is not reflected in the mathematical model. In reality, the
contact force may in fact be initiated gradually as the area of contact changes with time,
rather than changing its state abruptly.

2.4
Numerical Solver
To efficiently solve the HBM equations established above, and compute the time- and fre-
quency responses of interest, a numerical solving and processing procedure is needed. As
mentioned earlier, the HBM equations pose a nonlinear equation system. Solving such
system with a Newton-based solver requires an initial guess which is relatively close to
the local solution. To obtain a frequency response, the amplitudes and the Fourier coeffi-
cients in Equation 2.52 and 2.53 need to be computed for a series of frequency points in
the desired range. Coming up with an good initial guess for a nonlinear system without
prior knowledge about it, for every frequency step is difficult and impractical, therefore
a typical approach is to use path continuation techniques that use previous solution point
to approximate the next. The numerical procedure used to solve the equation system and
obtain results presented in this report uses a solver called solve_and_continue, provided
by Krack and Gross [13]’s MATLAB - toolbox. The solver takes the equation system in
Equation 2.50, defined in a function handle, as input, and solves the system for the Fourier
coefficients a and b the desired frequency range. It combines MATLAB’s native function
fsolve with a predictor-corrector continuation technique to be able to work through the
frequency range and find the correct solution path even in the case of turning points and
multiple solution branches, which are often seen in nonlinear systems. The solver is only
used to solve the equation system for the unknown Fourier coefficients for the desired
frequency range, and they are then further processed by the author to generate the results
shown in this report. The solving procedure, and the implementation of the harmonic bal-
ance method is validated against a problem with known solution — the Duffing oscillator.
The result comparison is shown in Appendix A.2.

3.1
Experiment Procedure
Input parameters that control the experiment are set beforehand and consist of: sweep rate
and direction, frequency range, excitation amplitude voltage (which converts to amplitude
displacement A), initial gap g0, and contact area ∆. These parameters correspond to the
ones in the mathematical model, such that any combination of input parameters can be
simulated in the numerical analysis.
The shaker is supplied by a signal generated by a controller unit through the dedicated
software corresponding to a chosen excitation function. A controller unit is programmed
to generate an input signal that activates the shaker, which then vibrates with amplitudes
and frequencies corresponding to the input voltage. Simultaneously, the input signal is
sent back to the controller unit, to establish a phase reference for the measurement.
Several different functions can be used as input signals to shakers. One commonly used
signal in frequency response analysis, is periodic chirp, because it can cover a broad fre-
quency range in relatively short time as it continuously sweeps through the frequency
range and then extracts the steady-state response, rather than reaching a steady-state at
every frequency step. This signal type is more efficient than other conventional input sig-
nals like for example a linear sweep [26]. In this study, the periodic chirp is used for the
acquisition of frequency responses of system A.
For the complete problem — system B, it is desired to capture the system’s response to
a linear, step-wise change in frequency, in a manner similar to the mathematical model,
where the response is calculated at the every sequential frequency step. This is necessary
to capture the nonlinearity in the response. Therefore, in the measurements of system B,
the system is excited with a sine wave sweeping through the frequency range, either by
increasing the frequency (upsweep) or decreasing it (downsweep).
The first stage of the experiment is dedicated to measuring the vibration data of the sys-
tem. Second, when the measurement is done, the output data is exported and processed

3.2
Validation of the Experiment
For the measurements to be reliable, it is crucial to confirm that the experiment is valid,
and that it reflects the assumptions made in the mathematical model. This involves defin-
ing and controlling the experiment conditions and system behaviour. For the system of
this study, apart from the independent input parameters, there is a set of conditions to be
examined: 1) boundary stiffness degree, 2) shaker output signal, 3) initial contact force
between beams and 4) frequency sweep rate.
1) Boundary stiffness is the stiffness of the support of each beam. The goal is to achieve a
stiffness as close as possible to a ”clamped” support, which is assumed in the theoretical
model. In reality however, the fixed boundary condition is expected to act as something
between ”clamped” and ”hinged”. To roughly determine the ”clampedness degree”, the
ratio between the measured natural frequencies and the theoretical frequencies can be ex-
amined for a few lowest natural frequencies of the beams, similar to methods to estimate
the boundary stiffness in [27]. For this specific problem, the support only needs to be stiff
enough to effectively transfer the vibration input to the beam. Therefore, it is not strictly
required of the beam to be fully clamped.
2) The contact between the beams is expected to behave as a strongly nonlinear spring,
which will likely add higher harmonics to the shaker output. The result is that a single-
frequency harmonic input (cos(Ωt)) to the power amplifier will not necessarily generate a
corresponding input force to the system, due to potential distortions caused by the higher
harmonics (3Ω, 5Ω, 7Ω...). The shaker output is therefore measured and used as reference
to be verified. The higher harmonics in the force input can be considered insignificant if
their amplitude is much lower than the fundamental harmonic (< 40dB).
3) The initial gap or contact force between the beams will influence the behaviour of the
system significantly. A big benefit would be to be able to control these quantities for every
repeated measurement. Before the measuring the vibration, the initial gap or contact force
should be adjusted by moving the beams closer/further from each other.
4) To achieve stationarity at each tested frequency, one should use a sufficiently slow
sweep rate. If the sweep is too fast, the obtained frequency response function may not
be true, because of the transient response – during up-sweeps, resonances will happen at
higher frequencies with lower peak maxima, and conversely for down-sweeps. The time
required to reach stationarity depends on damping [10, p. 35]. Lower damping results
in longer transients and more time to reach stationarity, hence the need for slower sweep
22
==PAGE:30==
rates. To identify a suitable slow sweep rate, an up-sweep test is performed at a specific
rate, then repeated at half the sweep rate. If this changed the FRF, the sweep rate is halved
again, continuing until the FRF remains the same for the new sweep rate. Moreover, for
verification purposes, sweep rate should be halved once more.
3.3
Uncertainties
It is important to notice that due to the assumptions and simplifications in the theoretical
model, and the practical choices in the experiment, some discrepancies are expected be-
tween the results of the two. Those are minimized where possible and are not expected to
influence the general qualitative observations in both analyses. However, some uncertain-
ties may lead to noticeable discrepancies between the empirical and theoretical results.
One significant limitation of the experimental setup is the fact that the laservibrometer,
pointed at the top surface of the beams, cannot reach the exact contact point because of
the beams overlapping (see Figure 3.2). The contact point on beam 2 is therefore not
available for measurement, and has to be calculated from the response of other points on
the beam.
Material uncertainty and sample variation may lead to different dynamic properties of the
samples. The sample is printed with a finite accuracy, and some variation in the printing
process is unavoidable. Therefore, exact dimensions of the samples may differ from the
model. Furthermore, the boundary stiffness of the beam fixture may not fully reflect the
”clamped” condition used in the mathematical model. This discrepancy is reflected in
the frequency response, as the natural frequencies depend heavily on the boundary condi-
tions. Damping ratios of the beam samples are calculated just once, based on a measured
response of one sample using the half-bandwidth method. The damping ratios, as well as
the response may differ slightly from sample to sample, and ideally should be calculated
for every single sample.

System B — Frequency Response Analysis
This study primarily considers three cases of system B, each with a different initial gap
size, g0. The gap sizes for the three cases are case 1) g0 = 0 mm, 2) g0 = 0.027 mm and
3) g0 = 0.050 mm. The reason for choosing three distinct gap distances is to classify the
configurations and look into the influence of the gap distance on the system’s dynamics.
Frequency response analysis examines the physical system’s characteristics in attempt to
discover and identify any nonlinear phenomena in the frequency domain.
Although a lab measurement can give a meaningful picture of the frequency response,
it is important to consider the fact, that sweep measurements are only partially inform-
ing due to the following aspects of nonlinear vibrations [10]. Firstly, one should ex-
pect hysteresis i.e. frequency response during upsweep and downsweep may be differ-
ent from each other. Secondly, the measured response may not reach the theoretical
amplitude/frequency-peaks, because of the tendencies to jump, or drop to another branch
before reaching a peak. Lastly, since a system is either in a stable state, or in its way
to one, only the stable response branches can be observed in a typical sweep measure-
ment, meaning that the unstable branches are impossible to measure. Some techniques,
however, are available to overcome these limitations, and are briefly discussed in the dis-
cussion-chapter.

==PAGE:34==
The numerical simulation model is validated against the lab measurements, so that it can
be used to investigate the areas that are not seen in the experiment results. It is fitted to
replicate the three cases as accurately as possible. Some uncertainties make it difficult to
exactly fit the mathematical model to the physical system. Apart from the simplifications
and assumptions discussed in chapter 2, the material- and geometrical properties of the
sample beams are a source of error, as they can only be measured with a finite precision.
Therefore, the inaccuracies in dimensions, mass and stiffness of the samples, resulting in
discrepancies in the response is expected. Furthermore, the damping coefficients calcu-
lated from a measured response may be a source of error. Additionally, the most crucial
parameter of the contact model is the contact stiffness ϵ. It can not be calculated from the
known quantities of the system, and has to be tuned to match the experiment results in a
manner of trial and error.
Considering the above, the parameters of the numerical simulation are tuned to reflect
the experiment results as accurately as possible, which in itself may introduce sources of
error. The physical uncertainties can not be treated separately, since they all contribute to
the combined uncertainty that is reflected in the measurements. Therefore, independently
tweaking the simulation parameters to match the results may on one side increase the
agreement of the results for specific study cases, but on the other side introduce errors in
the simulation’s representation of physical characteristics.

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
The analysis methods used in this study — experimental and numerical, were successful
in yielding insightful results that characterize the behaviour of the system and its nonlinear
phenomena. The analysis methods employed in the study qualitatively describe the vibra-
tion of the system. As expected, there are discrepancies between the experimental and
numerical findings. This chapter discusses the crucial aspects of the analysis, emphasiz-
ing their significance in physical and practical contexts, and identifies the most important
takeaways. Additionally, the variances and discrepancies between between the theory and
practice are identified, and their underlying sources are discussed. Furthermore, this chap-
ter reflects upon the methodology, analysis and results, and provides a critical evaluation
of the work done in the process. Suggestions for future research and continuation of this
study are given, with a perspective of improving the accuracy and quality of the results
and extending its area of application, eventually bridging the outcomes of this study to the
design process of hearing aid.
5.1
Interpretation of Results
The results of the analysis reveal that the vibration behaviour of the two beam configura-
tion depends primarily on how efficiently the vibrations is being transmitted from the ex-
cited beam to non-excited beam. This again comes down to factors such as: the excitation-
magnitude and frequency, and the initial gap size or initial contact force. Larger excita-
tion magnitude results in a larger vibration, but also, as anticipated in resonant systems,
the closer to resonance the excitation frequency is the larger the vibration amplitude be-
comes. For the beams to initially come into contact, the amplitude of vibration has to be
at least as large as the gap in between them. Once the beams reach contact, the vibration
of the excited beam is transmitted in the other beam in various degrees and patterns.
The cases studied experimentally present three combinations of input parameters that re-
sult in three distinct scenarios, that are in focus of the discussion. One other known sce-
nario — large initial gap, and low amplitude of beam 2, in which there is no contact
between the beams — is not discussed as it is considered trivial. This is a linear vibration

case which does not exhibit any nonregular behaviours and this configuration acts like a
single, base-excited beam, very close to system A described earlier. The only trace motion
of beam 1 that can be measured in that case is due to air pressure from the vibrating beam,
or measurement noise from environment.
The scenario described in case 1) — no initial gap, where the beams maintain contact at
lower amplitudes is referred to as the ”strong contact case”. Here, the beams vibrate in a
regular motion at weak, non-resonant excitation maintaining contact most of the time. For
strong, resonant excitation the beams occasionally separate from each other, which leads
some nonlinearities in form of impacts and higher harmonics. Second, case 2) describes
a scenario where the gap is still relatively small compared to the vibration amplitude of
B2, and the beams remain in contact for a wide frequency range. This is referred to as
”weak contact case”. Lastly, case 3) with a relatively large initial gap is when the beams
occasionally come into contact, only around the peak frequency, that is during strong,
resonant excitation, referred to as ”occasional contact case”.
5.1.1
Strong Contact
The beams in strong contact exhibit a regular vibration motion while maintaining mostly
continuous contact. Both beams vibrate with a similar amplitude. This behavior is ex-
pected, as the motion transmission between the beams remains efficient due to the initial
strong contact. Both the time response and frequency response show mostly linear be-
haviour, with no higher harmonic components nor aperiodic motion. Since the two beams
are strongly coupled, they exhibit a behaviour typical to coupled oscillators. In fact, in
this scenario, the contact between the beams can be compared to a close-to linear spring
attached at the point of contact between the beams. As long as the contact between the
beams remains, the force from beam 1 on beam 2 is linearly proportional to the deflection
of the beams by a stiffness coefficient, as in

analysis of the connected parts, as it may cause amplification of unwanted frequencies.
5.1.2
Weak Contact
In contrast to the strong contact, the weak contact case is characterized by a relatively
large initial gap between the beams, requiring higher amplitude for the contact to occur.
During weak, non-resonant excitation, the beams might not come into contact at all since
the amplitude of B2 will be too small. When the beams come into contact, the transmission
of motion between the beams is less predictable compared to the strong contact scenario,
resulting in reduced coherence in vibration patterns.
Under strong, near resonant excitation, the amplitude of the excitation force becomes suf-
ficiently high to overcome the initial gap and the beams come into contact. This leads
to intermittent impacts between the beams, along with periodic regions of contact and
separation. The intermittent contact introduces additional complexities into the vibration
behavior, resulting in non-regular motion patterns.

Chapter 5. Discussion
The weak contact scenario highlights the sensitivity of the two beam system to changes
in excitation conditions, particularly when it comes to the initiation of contact between
the beams. As shown in figure, beam’s velocity decreases as it approaches the extrema of
motion, since restoring force of the beam’s elasticity decelerates the beam as it moves away
from the equilibrium position. Therefore, the further away beam 1 is, the less velocity
does beam 2 have upon impacting beam 1. From that one can deduce that larger initial
gap results in less vibration transmitted from B2 to B1, and therefore smaller amplitude
of B1.
One significant effect observed in the weak contact case is the hardening effect due to
which the system becomes stiffer, as the amplitude increases. The hardening effect occurs
because of the relationship between the displacement of the two beams and the contact
force between them, remembering that the contact force is a function of the displacements
of both beams, and is only present during contact.
Considering the nature of this case, it is not a desired scenario for the connection of two
parts, because of the less predictable behaviours caused by the nonlinear effects in the
system. The hardening effect causes the resonances to shift, compared to the linear re-
sponse. Additionally, resonances and antiresonances that appear in the nonlinear system
are harder to predict, as they cannot be observed in the linear system.
5.1.3
Occasional Contact
In the case of occasional contact, the parts come into contact only during strong, reso-
nant excitation, but even then remain separated for most of the time period. The result
of this is multiple contact/separation-transitions, resulting in strong harmonic distortion
and incoherent response of the beams. In fact the harmonic distortion could already be
observed in the lab measurement, because of the noise coming from the colliding beams.
In a linear case, the noise is expected to correspond to the excitation frequency, however
in this scenario, other noise components were clearly audible. This scenario again should
be avoided when designing parts in contact for similar reasons to the weak contact case.

Reflection
In hindsight, the execution of the project has provided valuable insights into nonlinear
vibration in contact problems. The methodology employed, including harmonic balance
method along with numerical solving techniques, and experimental measurements yielded
notable results in discovering nonlinear effects in the system’s response. However, a crit-
ical evaluation of the work also reveals areas that could have been improved upon for
a more complete and accurate analysis. Various improvements to the methodology and
analysis are possible and further development of the tools presented in the report may po-
tentially extend the applications and accuracy of this study. Multiple challenges occurred
during this study, both in the derivation of the mathematical model, the numerical imple-
mentation and the experimental analysis and while the project proposes valuable tools for
modelling vibration of parts in contact, it is important to acknowledge their limitations,
as well as areas that could’ve been improved upon to achieve better results more effi-
ciently. The challenges of the study include simplifications and approximations put upon
the initial problem, limitations of the numerical solver, and practical restrictions in the ex-
perimental analysis of the problem. In this section, some of the notable accomplishments
and drawbacks of the study are discussed.

Mathematical Model
The primary challenge during the preliminary phase of the project was to formulate the
mathematical model outlined in chapter 2, as well as to develop a numerical tool capable
of efficiently and accurately solving that model. To define the problem statement clearly
and translate it into a mathematical framework, it was essential to refine and adjust the
conceptual issues through multiple consultations with the supervising experts from the
company and the academic supervisors. Formulating the mathematical model required
extensive research into various aspects of the topic, including theory of vibration, contact
mechanics, and nonlinear vibration analysis. A series of simplifications and assumptions
had to be made to develop the mathematical model using common theories and methods.
All of which influence the accuracy and validness of the model. Although all models
are only approximations of reality, the simplifications limit the model’s ability to reflect
reality. However, some simplifications make the model more general, so that it can be
improved and expanded to fit to other problems.
Once the problem could be expressed mathematically, it was then transformed into a con-
crete mathematical model with the assistance of selected theories and resources. An im-
portant accomplishment here was to express the entire problem, including arbitrary num-
ber of mode shapes or harmonic order, in a compact, matrix notation, suitable for numeri-
cal implementation. Final ODE-system in 2.36 and the HBM-equation system in 2.50 are
both one-line expressions that contain all the coefficients and variables in matrices and
vectors, ready to be directly rewritten into code inside the numerical solver. Furthermore,
once the system was implemented numerically, the code allows to choose set number of
modes N and/or harmonics H, and assembles the system accordingly, which is extremely
useful for performing convergence studies, and makes it easy to adjust the approximation
order of the analysis.
One important aspect of the mathematical model is the harmonic order of the harmonic
balance method. Harmonic order determines the number of harmonic terms included in the
solution. Ensuring the appropriate harmonic order is crucial for achieving accurate results.
Conducting a convergence study is a way of achieving this, as it involves systematically
increasing the harmonic order and observing the convergence of the solution. By assessing
how the solution changes with varying harmonic orders, one can pinpoint the optimal
order that balances computational efficiency with accuracy. Increasing the harmonic order
results in a less balanced system, leading to poor convergence and difficulties related to
the continuation technique, which limits the ability to obtain the full solution. During this
study, the harmonic order was chosen carefully and most likely too low in some cases.
As seen in the time response measurements, higher harmonics appear up to 7Ωand likely
above. Based on that, the chosen harmonic order of 3, for the some results in the report is
likely too low
Important to address, the most significant simplification on the model of this study is re-
ducing the contact force to a point force, along with neglecting the varying contact area and
friction. In reality one should assume that the contact force is distributed across the area
of contact resulting in a surface pressure on that area. As the parts vibrate, the surface area
and the pressure’s magnitude varies. Adding parallel motion results in friction between the
two parts. Neglecting these components takes away from the accuracy of the model. The
significance of these components was not examined in this study. To increase the accuracy
of the contact model, one should take both these components into account. Several contact
models that include friction are proposed in [20]. Specifically for beams, [19] proposes
41
==PAGE:49==

approximate methods for contact modelling. Including those methods would approximate
the varying contact area between the beams, so the contact pressure wouldn’t have to be re-
duced to a point force, supposedly making the model closer to reality. Another significant
simplification is the use of smoothing function on the nonlinear forcing term. Applying
a hyperbolic smoothing function smoothes out the discontinuity of the forcing term at the
contact incident, which does not reflect the true nature of this transition accurately. This
can lead to a loss of fidelity in representing the underlying phenomena. Additionally, the
degree of smoothing depends on two parameters: steepness of the hyperbolic function β,
and the sample size of the time integration domain. Both of these two parameters should
ideally be as high as possible. A higher steepness in the smoothing function improves
accuracy by approaching the characteristics of a step-function, effectively capturing the
abrupt changes in the system, while introducing a level of continuity. A high time resolu-
tion is essential when numerically integrating a smoothing function to accurately capture
changes in the data, preserve temporal dynamics, and prevent aliasing. It allows for a
more precise detection of changes and ensures that the integration accurately represents
the underlying phenomena. One could elliminate the dependance on these parameters with
the approach of parting the time integration of the forcing term at the unknown transition
times mentioned earlier.
Experiment
The experiment is limited to technical possibilities, especially when it comes to assem-
bling configuration, exciting the system and measuring the system’s response. The ability
to control the initial conditions and input parameters is important, as it allows to perform
repeated measurements without introducing uncertainties in the initial conditions and in-
puts. The uncertainties of the experiment setup in this study emerge from, among others,
beams’ position and alignment, including the gap between them.
To match the desired setup the two beams must be parallel along all three axes. The mount-
ing fixture does not allow for precise alignment in those directions, hence the parallelism
of the beams can not be controlled by means other than visual inspection. Additionally,
the distance between the beams is not explicitly adjustable in the mounting fixture. The
linear guide allows to adjust the position of beam 2, but there is no way of establishing a
zero-reference between the beams other than visually. The setup designed for this study
however, allows for an easy and secure assemble of the samples to their desired positions,
and to some degree ensures a rigid fixation.
Another issue in the experiment is the fact that the setup of the experiment limits the
possibility to measure the desired points on the samples. The vibrometer can only measure
what is in reachable by the laser in a clear line. Since the beams’ tips overlap each other,
beam 2 is not reachable and therefore can not be measured at the exact point of contact
like it is calculated in the mathematical model. To overcome this, one needs to measure
closest possible to the contact point, and from that measurement calculate the vibration of
the contact point using the mode shape expansion.
One major challenge discovered in the experiment process is the fact that the input am-
plitude of excitation does not directly correspond to the input voltage. This results in a
varying amplitude across the frequency range, something that was not initially accounted
for in the mathematical model. The excitation amplitude in the experiment was declining
as the frequency increased, likely because of the reaction forces on to the shaker’s base
that distorted the excitation signal. To make up for this, the amplitude signal is used as the
input amplitude for the mathematical model. The drawback here is that the sweep mea-

surements do not reflect a constant amplitude across the frequency range, which would’ve
been more informative about the system’s response.
An important takeaway from the process of experimental analysis is to prepare a proce-
dure in which the measured data can be examined and evaluated immediately after the
measurement, so that in case of any error, the measurement can be repeated straight away.
During this project, several measurements turned out to be faulty because of erroneous
settings, bad setup etc, while the time and availability to repeat the measurements was
limited.
5.3
Future Directions
This study embarks on the topic of vibration of parts in contact, and creates a fundamen-
tal understanding of the theoretical problem of two beams in contact. Further study of
this topic should aim to extend the understanding, and areas of application by conducting
experimental parameter studies and building upon the numerical model, to simulate the
problem more accurately and efficiently, as well as continue the vibration analysis of the
observed phenomena, with a goal to be able to classify a system and simulate it with high
confidence. The beam problem is helpful to establish a working procedure to develop and
analyze vibrating contact problems, however, it should be a future goal to apply similar
studies to other, more complex models, may it be other structural elements like plates,
tubes and prisms, or eventually 3D models of real parts.
Based on the results presented in this report, one natural continuation of this study is
to analyze the system further in frequency response. The frequency response curves of
the system reveal intriguing behaviour, that calls for further investigation. The numerous
types of bifurcations observed in the frequency response analysis call for a deeper analysis,
to classify the different bifurcation points. Bifurcation analysis concerns why and how
they occur, and leads further into stability analysis, where one analyzes the stability of
stationary points and branches. The stability analysis based on theory from [13] and [10]
can be implemented into the current numerical model, by evaluating each frequency point
for stability, and producing a graphical representation. This can be especially useful in
predicting which regions of the frequency response are stable, and thereby which can be
observed in a practical setting or in a lab measurement.
One other potential direction for continued study is to extend the excitation signal to in-
clude a wider band of frequencies, rather than a single frequency. This study focuses solely
on the primary resonant excitation, which lies just above 100 Hz. Typical frequency range
in a hearing aid device however, is 200—8000Hz, and the audio signal usually contains a
wide frequency band. Studies on how the system responds to a multi-frequency excitation
are an important step towards applying the analysis tools and results to realistic hearing
aid parts.
As mentioned earlier, lab measurements have their limitations when it comes to study of
nonlinear systems and bifurcations specifically. However, there are experimental tech-
niques that allow experimental bifurcation- and stability analysis, such as control-based
continuation [30], and may potentially be employed in the lab setting for this and other
contact problems. The technique combines mathematical modelling, experimental mea-
surements and signal processing to be able to work around with unstable equilibria and
branches.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
In conclusion, the process towards the objective of the thesis, which was to investigate
the the behaviour arising from nonlinear vibrations of parts in contact reveales significant
challenges that demand careful consideration. It is clear that nonlinear vibrations appear
naturally in a contact interaction between two vibrating bodies and manifest themselves
as harmonic distortion, aperiodic motion, bifurcations and hardening effects. The promi-
nence of the nonlinear phenomena depends strongly on the geometrical configuration of
the system and excitation parameters. Moreover, the dependence of system behavior on
initial parameters underscores the importance of precise control in both experimental and
numerical studies.
Since many contact theories exist, the right choice of a mathematical contact model is
crucial as it may simplify the system substantially while maintaining the important force
characteristics and the ability to reflect the physical behaviour, and furthermore allows to
address nonlinear behavior. However, it would be naive to settle on one, simple contact
model, as it certainly omits important aspects of the contact interface, such as friction,
pressure distributions and material deformation.
The complexity of the model, and the computational effort increases along with the order
of approximation necessary to increase the accuracy. Nonlinear problems tend to exhibit
abrupt changes in the system, which are often difficult to handle by the mathematical
models, primarily due to discontinuities in the vibration response.
Frequency responses of the system obtained in this study are an efficient way to iden-
tify and communicate the system’s qualities and changes thereof related to amplitude and
frequency. On the other side, time response of the system under single frequency excita-
tion is an informative way of tracking the exact motion of the part, where any harmonic
distortions and nonregular motion are visible. The analysis of the system under different
geometrical configurations attempts to classify the system into recognizable scenarios that
may be a used as reference when considering design of a contact interface. Some scenar-
ios, like for example the strong contact case described in this study, may be preferable for

certain applications, because of their ability to simplify the model without losing the phys-
ical aspects of the system. Other scenarios described in the study give clear indications of
what to avoid and look out for when designing a contact interface.
While this report provides a foundation presented on a theoretical beam problem, there
remains potential for further refinement and extension to apply the analysis tools to other,
more complex structures and contact geometries. Moreover, there are numerous tools to
capture the full range of vibration behaviors, that can be added to the analysis of the results
presented in the report, specifically bifurcation and stability analysis.
Comparisons between experimental and numerical results reveal qualitative agreement
in system behavior, yet discrepancies occur, indicating areas where further adjustments
are necessary to enhance accuracy, such as precise conversion of material properties, di-
mensions, boundary conditions, and excitation parameters. The efforts to improve the
alignment between experimental observations and numerical simulations will be crucial
for advancing the understanding and predictive capabilities in modeling parts in contact
and the nonlinear vibrations.
