Abstract 
Listeria monocytogenes is a gram-positive, psychrotroph, facultative anaerobic 
foodborne pathogen that grows in various environments under a wide range of conditions 
such as temperature, pH and salinity. L. monocytogenes causes listeriosis which is severe to 
pregnant women, the elderly and people with compromised immune system. Even though the 
infection rate is low the fatality is high. In the food processing plans, L. monocytogenes can 
be proved hard to be killed. The application of biocides and most specifically of 
benzalkonium chloride is being used for the disinfection of the processing plans. However, 
there have been considerations about the adaption of the bacteria to the excessive use of 
biocides and benzalkonium chloride. This study aims to investigate the adaptation of L. 
monocytogenes when it is exposed to sub-lethal concentrations of benzalkonium chloride, but 
also how the adapted strains react to different conditions. For this study, the minimum 
inhibitory concentration of L. monocytogenes was studied. The successful adaptation of the 
strains LT16, PD4, L45 and A36 was conducted. Moreover, a BioScreen assay, a bactericidal 
suspension test and desiccation test of the L. monocytogenes parent and adapted strains were 
performed. Overall, the extensive use of biocides in sub-lethal concentrations should be 
avoided as L. monocytogenes can gain resistance. However, even though the bacteria strains 
gained a resistance to benzalkonium chloride, their growth rate and resistance to desiccation 
did not have any significant changes compared to their parent strains.

1. 
Introduction  
1.1. Taxonomy of L. monocytogenes 
L. monocytogenes belongs in the Genus Listeria in the sub-group Listeria sensu 
stricto, as seen in Figure 1. Species that belong to Listeria sensu stricto are commonly found 
in the environment and the intestinal tracts of healthy animals and animal-derived foods. 
Species from the Listeria sensu lato group can be found in the environment and are unable to 
infect animals. Between these species only L. monocytogenes is pathogenic towards humans., 
whereas L. ivanovii is an animal pathogen and rarely infects humans (Dortet et al., 2014; 
Olaimat et al., 2018). 
 
Figure 1: Genus Listeria and its sub-groups. (Olaimat et al., 2018) 
1.2. Characteristics of L. monocytogenes 
L. monocytogenes is a ubiquitous gram-positive, psychrotroph, facultative anaerobic 
foodborne pathogen, with the ability to grow and survive in a variety of environmental 
conditions, as shown in Table 1. Its optimum growth conditions are at neutral pH and 30-37 
ºC (Matthews et al., 2017). L. monocytogenes is a non-spore forming, rod shaped bacilli, of 
0.4 – 1/1.5 μm, with no capsule. It is motile at temperatures 10-25 ºC but non-motile at 37 ºC, 
due to the lack of flagellin expression at these temperatures (Dortet et al., 2014; Vázquez-
Boland et al., 2001).

Due to the wide range of survival conditions, L. monocytogenes poses a persistent 
problem in the food industry especially in Ready-to-Eat (RTE) foods. L. monocytogenes 
contaminations can prove hard to deal with but also threatening to the consumer’s health, by 
causing severe symptoms (Olaimat et al., 2018). 
1.2.1. Flagella and biofilm formation 
L. monocytogenes has 4-6 peritrichous flagella which are dependant on the 
temperature. More specifically, at temperatures below 30 ºC the bacterium is motile, 
however, as the temperature increases to 37 ºC the bacterium’s phenotype changes. The 
transcription of the protein responsible for the flagella expression, flagellin flaA, is being 
inhibited, making L. monocytogenes non-motile. Flagella has an important role in the early 
attachment of the bacterium on surfaces such as stainless steel at < 30 ºC, by acting as 
adhesins to surfaces (Schirm et al., 2004). 
L. monocytogenes has the ability to form biofilms when immotile and attached to 
surfaces in the food processing plans, such as stainless steel, glass etc. Biofilms are 
formations or structures formed by communities of bacteria that are attached on a surface, 
surrounded by extracellular polymeric substances (EPS). Biofilms are composed in a large 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
percentage of EPS which in turn are composed of polysaccharides, proteins and DNA. This 
formation enables the bacteria to survive and resist harsh environments, like desiccation, and 
the use of biocides or other media and treatments (Chew & Yang, 2016; Lee et al., 2019; Lee 
et al., 2020). 
1.2.2. Desiccation 
In biology, desiccation is defined as the drying or the removal of water from an 
organism (Alpert, 2005). As water has an important role in the survival and growth of 
microorganisms, a humid environment is necessary. However, in many cases desiccation 
frequently occurs naturally or not, especially in a food processing plan. For example, air 
dehumidification treatments are being used in the industry after disinfection.  
According to Esbelin et al., in order to survive desiccation many bacteria have 
different survival mechanisms (Esbelin et al., 2018). A previous study from Vogel et al., on 
the survival of L. monocytogenes to desiccation showed that the bacteria can survive three 
months on stainless steel surfaces (Vogel et al., 2010). In the case of L. monocytogenes, the 
formation of biofilms occurs which allows the bacteria to survive. Moreover, as a gram-
positive bacterium L. monocytogenes has a thicker peptidoglycan that also contributes to its 
survival to desiccation. However, mechanisms behind this are not fully understood yet 
(Esbelin et al., 2018). 
1.3. Listeriosis  
The U.S. Food and Drug Administration has identified raw meat, raw vegetables, 
dairy products from unpasteurized milk and RTE food that are not heat treated, as the main 
sources of food-borne infections cause by L. monocytogenes. This bacterium can cause 
listeriosis, which can prove life-threatening to the elderly, pregnant women and their fetus, 
and people with compromised immune system which is why it is described as an 
opportunistic pathogen (Borucki & Call, 2003; Matthews et al., 2017). 
L. monocytogenes enters the host’s body mainly through the gastrointestinal tract 
(GI), where it enters the cells, grows and tranfers to the nearby cells. From there it can 
infiltrate and enter the placenta and brain (Matthews et al., 2017). The infection starts 
manifesting symptoms after 20 h of the ingestion in the cases of gastrointestinal symptoms 
such as gastroenterities, nausea or diarrhea. However, if the clinical systoms are that of 
invasive illness such as bacteremia, sepsis etc. then the incubation time is 20-30 days after the 
consumption of contaminated food (PROFEL, 2020; Vázquez-Boland et al., 2001).

According to Vázquez-Boland et al., in vivo studies have shown that  L. 
monocytogenes has two forms, as shown in Figure 2. Both infect the central nervous system 
or CNS (Vázquez-Boland et al., 2001). 
The human exposure to L. monocytogenes is high due to how widespread the 
bacterium is in the environment. However, listeriosis cases are low in frequency but have 
high fatality (19 %) according to the ECDC (ECDC, 2018; Wallecha et al., 2013). A report 
by WHO/FAO, states that the incidence of listeriosis in EU was 0.3 to 7.05 per million 
people (FAO/WHO, 2004).

1.3.1. Epidemiology and related outbreaks  
Among the identified serotypes of L. monocytogenes, see Figure 3, 1/2a is the most 
common food isolate whereas serotype 4b isolates have been identified as the cause of most 
human outbreaks (Borucki & Call, 2003; Vázquez-Boland et al., 2001). 
The Foodborne Disease Outbreak Surveillance System of CDC (Center for Disease 
Control and Prevention), in the United States, has identified serotype 4b as the isolate that 
caused the most outbreaks, from 1998 to 2008, with 10 numbered outbreaks which led to 
70% of the cases to be hospitalized, followed by 1/2a isolate with 8 outbreaks and only 45% 
hospitalizations (Cartwright et al., 2013). 
A Danish epidemiological study by Gerner-Smidt et al,  reviewed a total of 299 cases 
from 1994 till 2003 and concluded that serotype 4b was more severe than 1/2a and 1/2b 
together. Out of these cases, univariate analysis showed that serogroup 1/2 caused 174

1.4. L. monocytogenes in the food processing environment 
L. monocytogenes enters and contaminates the food-processing plans through various 
pathways, as depicted in Figure 4, mainly through cross-contamination from the workers, 
soil and water. The food-processing environment supports the growth of the bacterium, as it 
is rich in nutrients combined with high humidity (Matthews R.K. et al., 2017).

As already mentioned, L. monocytogenes has the ability to attach on surfaces and 
create biofilms in hard to reach and hard to clean areas such as crevices, cracks etc. which is 
why specific regulations and rules concerning manufacturing and hygiene should be followed 
(see 1.4.2.) (Matthews et al., 2017). 
1.4.1. Hygiene and the use of biocides in the food industry 
According to the Biocides Directive (98/8/EC) biocides are biological or chemical 
substances used to destroy, prevent or control the action of any harmful organisms, like pests 
e.g. rats, insects etc. or microorganisms such as bacteria, virus, fungi etc. Such products are 
disinfectants, 
antiseptics, 
pesticides, 
herbicides, 
fungicides, 
and 
insecticidesm(EU 
Commission, 2008; GreenFacts & Scientific Committees, 2009). 
In the food industry, biocides are mainly used as disinfectants to the facilities and to 
the equipment that has direct contact with the food products. They can also be used to 
disinfect drinking water or on the animal carcasses. (GreenFacts & Scientific Committees, 
2009) 
Holah et al reports that there are many cases where the disinfectants are being used 
longer than the instructions suggest or they are not being rinsed at all from the surfaces, in the 
products where the hygiene Directives permit it, if the disinfectants are not toxic (Holah et 
al., 2002). The excessive use or bad practices of biocides, however, could lead to the 
development of resistant bacteria. According to the EU Commission, resistance is defined as 
the ability of a strain to survive a repeated concentration of biocide that is used in disinfection 
plans, when this concentration is sufficient to kill susceptible strains (EU Commission, 2009).   
1.4.1.1. 
Benzalkonium Chloride 
Benzalkonium Chloride (BC) belongs to the Quaternary Ammonium Compounds 
(QACs), which are non-corrosive and can be used for example on conveyors to avoid 
corrosion (Holah et al., 2002). BC is commonly used as a disinfectant in the food industry in 
order to ensure microbiological safe products.  It acts by disrupting the lipid bilayer 
membrane of the bacteria, which makes it effective towards Gram-positive bacteria, such as 
L. monocytogenes (Rodríguez-Melcón, Riesco-Peláez, et al., 2019).  
As already mentioned, the excessive use of biocides could lead to resistant strains, 
which is also the case for BC. More specifically, previous studies have shown that L. 
monocytogenes can adapt to BC and gain resistance to it (Lundén et al., 2003; To et al., 2002; 
Yu et al., 2018). Adaptation to BC and therefore to QACs are a result of the expression of the 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
efflux pump (Yu et al., 2018).  Efflux pumps are transporters that help bacteria by removing 
toxic substances from their internal environment  (Soto, 2013).  
L. monocytogenes’ resistance to BC can cause a problem in the disinfection of the 
food processing plans, as the bacteria may not be killed and therefore could cross-
contaminate the products.

Moreover, it is stated that the food business operators are responsible for the testing, 
sampling and the compliance of the microbiological criteria set by the commission. The 
sampling should also be taken from machines, equipment and processing areas. EFSA 
recommends the planning of monitor programs for L. monocytogenes and the hygienic 
conditions of the processing environment (Koutsoumanis et al., 2020). 
According to the European association of fruit and vegetable processors or PROFEL, 
good hygiene practices include cleaning plans and disinfection activities of the areas and 
equipment, in order to prevent bacterial accumulation and the formation of biofilms 
(PROFEL, 2020). Moreover, the food productions should follow good hygiene practices and

proving they follow an effective HACCP plan and the specific regulations (EU Commission,

A water management plan is also necessary. Water testing is important for the 
monitoring of L. monocytogenes due to its ubiquitous nature. Therefore, frequent sampling 
and maintenance of the water and irrigation system (pipes, water tank etc) is necessary 
(PROFEL, 2020). 
Finally, personal hygiene and personnel awareness are crucial in a food processing 
plan. Due to the wide transmission routes of L. monocytogenes, the bacterium can easily 
transfer through workers to surfaces. That is why the personnel should be trained accordingly 
(PROFEL, 2020). 
1.5. Aim and objectives of the study 
Considering the above, the aim of this study was to evaluate whether L. 
monocytogenes’ continuous exposure to sub-lethal concentration of BC could lead to the 
adaptation of the bacteria to the biocide. The variants that have become resistant were 
exposed to conditions such as desiccation, to determine if they have become cross-resistant 
based on their phenotypic traits. The phenotypic traits of the adapted strains were compared 
with that of their parents to determine their cross-resistance to other conditions. Moreover, in 
order to ensure that the adaptation that took place is stable and not temporary, the adapted 
strains were exposed to a higher concentration of BC for a short amount of time using a 
suspension test.

For the susceptibility testing, 96-well plates were used. Distribution of 133 µL dTSB

2.5. BioScreen assay of L. monocytogenes strains 
2.5.1. Inoculum preparation and procedure 
To study and monitor the growth rate of both parent and adapted strains BioScreen 
assay was used. For the experiment, fresh colonies of the adapted and parent L. 
monocytogenes strains were grown from the frozen stocks kept at -80 ºC in TSA (VWR, 
granulated Soybean Casein digest Agar) plates at 37 ºC overnight, before being inoculated in 
4 mL dTSB) (Kumpulan Saintifik, Soybean Casein Digest Medium, Oxoid), incubated for 48 
h in 15 ºC. The inoculum was then diluted 1000x to correspond to 105 CFU/mL.  
Moreover, 50 µL of the diluted sample was used to inoculate 250 µL dTSB in the 
BioScreen plate. The total volume of each well was 300 µL with a level of 2-7x104 CFU/mL. 
The optical density OD540 was measured every 10 min whereas the temperature T was 
measured every 15 sec (T was constant) at 15 ºC. The duration of the assay was 3 days. Only 
1 biological replicate was taken with 4 technical replicates. 
2.5.2. Data analysis 
For the BioScreen assay, Bioscreen C MBR instrument and software were used. The 
average of each replicate was taken and plotted into a growth curve diagram using GraphPad 
Prism 9.

4. 
Discussion 
The study focused on the susceptibility of L. monocytogenes to BC and on the 
adaptation of various strains to BC.  The phenotypic characterization of the strains that were 
originally resistant and the adapted resistant was carried out. 
In order to simulate the conditions found in a food processing plan diluted TSB and 
15 ºC were used throughout the experimental process.  
4.2. Susceptibility testing and strain adaptation 
 
Out of the 8 strains that were analyzed, strains PD33, L42, LT17, PD11 were shown 
to be initial resistant to BC, where LT16, PD4, L45 and A36 were shown sensitive. Based on 
the MIC values after a series of exposure to sub-MIC values during the adaptation method, 
==PAGE:36==
36 
 
the initial sensitive strains obtained resistance to BC, whereas the original resistant strains did 
not increase their resistance to BC.  
Other studies, such as Yu et al., were also able to adapt L. monocytogenes to BC (Yu 
et al., 2018). According to Piercey et al. and Yu et al., the presence of reserpine determines 
the sensitivity or resistance to Quaternary Ammonium Sanitizers and therefore BC (Piercey et 
al., 2017; Yu et al., 2018). Low MIC values were linked to the presence of reserpine on 
adapted L. monocytogenes strains to BC (Yu et al., 2018). Based on this grounds, further 
study should be conducted on the MIC of the LT16, PD4, L45 and A36 strains after exposure 
to reserpine. 
Furthermore, the adapted resistant strains were frozen down in cryogenic tubes at -80 
ºC and the rest of the experiments had these frozen stocks as the starters for the adapted. MIC 
testing after the freezing showed that the strains had maintained their MIC concentrations and 
were still resistant to BC. 
The results indicate that in a food processing plan, residues of BC due to bad 
disinfection and hygiene practices, such as insufficient rinsing of the biocide or over-
exposure of L. monocytogenes to BC, could lead to the development of adapted strains 
(Holah et al., 2002). A research by Rodríguez-Melcón et al., experimented on the exposure of 
L. monocytogenes to sub-MIC concentrations of BC. The results revealed that the biofilm 
formation of the bacteria was enhanced, allowing them to survive the biocide exposure and 
cross-contaminate the food chain (Rodríguez-Melcón, Capita, et al., 2019).  
 
According to Holah et al., in order to prevent the continues use of the same biocides, 
rotation is suggested for the use of Quaternary Ammonium Compounds and BC especially 
with an oxidative biocide such as hypochlorite (Holah et al., 2002). This is exclusively 
effective in the case of cleaning conveyor belts, as they tend to be hard to clean. 
Overall, Roeden et al., claim that there is a lack of susceptibility testing methods that 
can represent the industrial conditions on L. monocytogenes, which could be explained by the 
limited information that MIC testing provides on the resistance to biocides (Roedel et al., 
2019).  
4.3. Cultivating of L. monocytogenes 
The plating of the bacteria was conducted in order verify if the strains that were 
adapted on a media surrounded by the biocide were also adapted when cultivated on TSA and 
BC plates. Results showed that we cannot transfer the MIC from broth to plates containing 
==PAGE:37==
37 
 
BC. It was noticeable that the colonies of the adapted strains needed 2 days to grow at 37 ºC, 
on both normal plates and plates containing BC, compared to their parents. Moreover, the 
adapted strains formed less and smaller colonies in both types of plates. This shows how the 
stressed cells needed time to adapt to the new conditions but were still able to form colonies.  
Currently, there are not relatable studies where agar plates are being used to isolate or 
preserve adapted strains. 
4.4. Bactericidal Suspension Test 
The bactericidal suspension test was intended to simulate cleaning in a food 
processing plan. The test was designed to measure the effectiveness of the biocide when 
parent and adapted strains were exposed to a higher concentration of BC. The data obtained 
showed that the pre-exposed strains had higher survival numbers than the non pre-exposed 
strains or the adapted and parent strains, where the pre-exposed adapted had similar number 
with the non pre-exposed adapted. This suggests that L. monocytogenes besides resistance 
can gain temporary tolerance to the biocide from an incubation of 48 ºC to BC. This means 
that continuous exposure with BC in a food processing environment might make the 
subsequent disinfection using BC less effective. 
It is worth mentioning that the replication of the results was difficult. This type of 
experiment needs practice, standardization, materials. Because the replication of the results 
was difficult it means that further studies should be done to verify the findings. 
4.5. BioScreen assay 
In general, the growth rate of the adapted strains followed that of their parents. 
However, for strains LT16, PD4 and A36 the growth curves showed differences between 
parent and adapted strains. For PD4 and A36, the adapted strains grew faster than their 
parents, but for LT16 the opposite was observed. This could mean that the adapted PD4 and 
A36 could compete with their parent strains in a food processing environment.  
This difference in the assay could also be interpretated as a result from manually 
spotting or the expression of the efflux pump.  
Manually spotting and bias depending on the technician could be potentially culpable 
for this difference. A Finish study by Aalto-Araneda et al., examined 400 L. monocytogenes 
strains and their growth under salt stress. Their results showed that the calculated growth 
rates were clearly affected by which of the two technician that incubated the strains (Aalto-
Araneda et al., 2020). 
==PAGE:38==
38 
 
A study by Romanova et al. went into details about the effect and role of efflux 
pumps in the adaptation of L. monocytogenes to BC. More specifically, the expression of the 
efflux pump affects the resistance of the bacteria to BC due to the genes. Efflux pump Mdrl 
levels determine the sensitivity to BC as it was revealed that in naturally sensitive strains its 
levels were low (Romanova et al., 2006).   
4.6. Desiccation 
With the stimulation of L. monocytogenes from the glucose and dTSB solution a 
higher initial cell concentration was ensured. The data obtained showed that all strains 
behaved similarly as there was a clear reduction of 3-log after 10 days of desiccation. This 
indicates that even though the strains got adapted, they did not obtain cross-resistance to 
drying. Also, none of the strains became more sensitive to desiccation, meaning that the 
obtained resistance to BC did not come with a fitness cost in terms of desiccation survival. 
A desiccation study by Yu et al., concluded that BC adapted L. monocytogenes strains 
did not appear to have resistance to desiccation when exposed to 6% NaCl based on 
comparison of their growth curves and there was small difference between the reduction in 
the cell numbers of the adapted and parent strains (Yu et al., 2018). In most cases the results 
indicated a 2-4-fold increase in the MIC to BC, that follows the data of this study. 
Another possible explanation on the small difference could be that there are different 
populations incubated on the coupons, with a different resistance to the desiccation 
conditions. 
 
There appears to be a connection between the biofilm formation and the survival to 
desiccation conditions on BC adapted strains, however this field was not looked further on 
this study (Piercey et al., 2017).  
5. 
Future studies 
Based on the above findings, it is apparent that further study on the effect of efflux 
pumps should be conducted, as they play an important role in the adaptation of L. 
monocytogenes to BC. More specifically the use of reserpine that inhibits the efflux pumps to 
the adapted strains LT16, PD4, L45 and A36 could be investigated according to (Romanova 
et al., 2006). 
As this study focused mainly on the phenotypic traits of the L. monocytogens the 
nucleotide sequences of the adapted strains could be compared with the parent by using Fasta 
files on online databases such as ResFinder and PlasmidFinder, in order to compare any 
==PAGE:39==
39 
 
possible plasmids responsible for the adaptation to BC. In addition, whole genome small 
nucleotide polymorphism analysis could be done to detect any possible changes between the 
parent and adapted strains. Moreover, a phylogenetic tree could be built from the whole 
genome sequence data. 
The connection of biofilms and BC resistance of the adapted strains LT16, PD4, L45 
and A36 should also be studied further. Lastly, it is important that the BSTesting should be 
repeated as it is apparent that the results could not be replicated well enough.  
6. 
Conclusion 
In conclusion, the strains LT16, PD4, L45 and A36 were successfully adapted to BC 
with the use of repeated expose to sub-MIC in vivo. This indicates that L. monocytogenes can 
adapt to harsh environments that contain BC and could pose a threat for potential infections 
of listeriosis, due to insufficient or improper disinfection of the food processing plans. 
However, it was not possible to see any increase in resistance to BC in strains that were 
originally resistant to BC. Moreover, no significant difference was found in the survival cell 
count during the desiccation, which shows that the BC adapted strains did not have any cross-
resistance to desiccation compared to their parent strain. The comparison of the growth 
curves of the adapted resistant with their parents showed similarities which indicates that 
both grow similarly. The results from the broth suspension test and the cultivation in BC agar 
plates showed that the adaptation was permanent, and that the strains did not revert to their 
original form. Lastly, it should be mentioned that the suspension testing was hard to duplicate 
and give accurate results and therefore should be repeated.
