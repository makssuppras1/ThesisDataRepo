Abstract
The European Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (ESPR) is driving the adopยญ
tion of Digital Product Passports (DPPs), which require traceable, machineยญreadable inยญ
formation about products across their entire lifecycle [1]. However, DPP data collected
in practice is typically heterogeneous, fragmented across organisations, and only parยญ
tially timestamped. This makes it challenging to apply process mining directly and to
link concrete lifecycle behaviour to environmental performance without additional logging
conventions.
This thesis investigates how process mining can be embedded into a working DPP imยญ
plementation so that sustainabilityยญrelevant behaviour can be detected, quantified and
surfaced to users. Concretely, this project designs and implements a single architecture
split into two views inside the Prduct platform: (i) an upstream pipeline where brands and
suppliers model material, processing and logistics steps via an environmental footprint
builder, and (ii) a useยญphase pipeline where end customers claim products through a moยญ
bile DPP label and log usage, repair, transfer and endยญofยญuse events. Both pipelines write
to DPPยญstyle event schemas and are mined in dedicated views (Material Process Discovยญ

performance indicators (KPIs).
The implementation is evaluated using synthetic event logs calibrated with realistic patยญ

based verification shows that the pipelines produce mineable logs, detect selected deviยญ

hotspots and lifetimeยญextension opportunities at activity level. The prototype also demonยญ
strates how DPP features such as origin, disposal and resale guidance can be backed by
explicit event evidence.
Remaining limitations include uncertainty in emission factors and incomplete coverage
of upstream and useยญphase events. While the prototype records timestamps for logged
events, timestamp quality and provenance remain a practical constraint: in the useยญphase
pipeline, the system relies on userยญentered event dates (with serverยญside capture time as

Future work includes tighter timestamp governance (source attribution, validation, and auยญ

and โlostยญandยญfoundโ flows, and extending the framework with rightยญtoยญrepair specific indiยญ

2.4
Process Mining and Digital Product Passports
Process mining is about learning how a process actually runs by looking at the digital
traces it leaves behind in IT systems. In most information systems, every important step
creates an entry in a database or log file. Process mining takes these entries and reconยญ
structs the flows, so that we can see which paths are common, where cases get stuck,
and where the real behaviour differs from the expected process model [5].
2.4.1
Event logs and process discovery
The basic input to process mining is an event log. An event log is usually described
as a collection of events, where each event belongs to a case (also called trace), has
an activity name, and a timestamp. Events can also have extra attributes, such as the
country, facility, or CO2e emissions for that step.
In a simple view, an event log row contains at least:
โข a case identifier, which tells us which events belong to the same process instance
(for example a specific product, lot, or serial number),
โข an activity or event type, such as โSpinningโ, โDyeingโ, or โOwnership transferredโ,
โข a timestamp indicating when this activity happened,
โข optional attributes, such as location, country code, impact metrics, or quantities.
Process discovery is the task of taking such a log and automatically building a process
model from it. At a high level, the discovery algorithm looks at which activities tend to
follow each other in the same case, how often they appear, and in which order. Based
on this, it constructs a process model (for example, a directlyยญfollows graph or a Petri net)
that shows the typical paths cases take, including loops and branches [5]. In this thesis,
discovery is mainly used to:
โข get an overview of the upstream lifecycle of products (from raw materials to endยญofยญ
use),
โข understand how customers actually use, wash, repair, and resell products.
Once a model is discovered, it can be used for conformance checking (comparing obยญ
served behaviour with the expected one) and for attaching performance and sustainability
metrics to different parts of the process.
2.4.2
Digital Product Passports as event sources
The Digital Product Passport (DPP) is introduced by the EUโs Ecodesign for Sustainable
Products Regulation (ESPR) as a digital record that travels with the product and exposes
machineยญreadable information along the value chain [1]. Industry initiatives and vendors
describe the DPP as a structured data container that holds product identifiers, compoยญ
sition, supplyยญchain information, certificates, and endยญofยญlife guidance, and that can be
accessed via technologies such as QR codes and GS1 identifiers [8, 9, 10, 11].
From a process mining perspective, a DPPยญenabled system is a natural place to emit event
logs. Each time a product moves, is transformed, used, repaired, or resold, the system

Methodology
The development of this project utilised agile development with three monthly deliverables
with set objectives, to ensure validation of the approach, early feedback on each iteration,
and a clear link between intermediate results and the final research questions. During
thesis there were biยญweekly meetings with supervisor and biยญweekly meetings with work
supervision ensuring that the project is on track and that the development of it also.
3.1
Methodological Approach
The methodological approach in this thesis is hybrid. It combines design and engineering
work inside an existing DPP/PIM system at Prduct ApS, a case study of that system in
a realistic setting, and quantitative process mining analysis on DPP style event logs. As
outlined at the beginning of this chapter, the work is organised into three agile iterations
(D1โD3) with fixed monthly deliverables, each iteration running the full loop from schema
design to evaluation.
The design and engineering part focuses on extending the Prduct platform so that it can
actually emit mineable event logs suitable for process mining. This includes shaping
a minimal DPP ready event schema, wiring logging into existing product, supplier and
lifecycle data, and making sure that both upstream lifecycle logs and useยญphase logs (as
introduced in Chapter 2) can be produced with a consistent case identifier, activity names
and impact attributes. In this sense the thesis follows a designยญscience approach: an
artefact (the logging and mining pipeline) is built and refined to address the practical gap
identified in the introduction.
3.2
Data Management and Ethics
Data used in this thesis comes from two main sources, synthetic event logs that I genยญ
erated to mirror realistic DPP scenarios, and selected exports from Prductโs own enviยญ
ronment. Synthetic logs are calibrated with literature values for emissions and lifecycle
behaviour rather than customerยญspecific information. When using company data, identiยญ
fiers for customers, suppliers and internal products are anonymised or aggregated so that
no commercially sensitive or personal information is exposed in the thesis.
All datasets are stored in private, accessยญcontrolled locations. The platform necessarily
stores personal data (e.g. owner email and coarse location) to support ownership workยญ
flows, but the derived event logs used for analysis and reporting contain only anonymised
case identifiers and do not include direct personal identifiers. Case identifiers refer to
products and lots, not to individual customers, the clients of platform never get access to
the customers data that claimed the products. This keeps the work aligned with GDPR
and with internal confidentiality expectations at Prduct ApS, while still allowing the conยญ
struction and analysis of meaningful DPP style event logs.
3.3
Risk Assessment
The main methodological risks were missing or immature logs, changing ESPR/DPP reยญ
quirements, and uncertainty in emission factors and KPIs. These were mitigated by startยญ
ing with synthetic datasets, keeping the event schema minimal but extensible, and verยญ
sioning mappings so that changes in DPP expectations could be absorbed without breakยญ
ing the whole pipeline. Where emission factors were uncertain, the analysis focuses on
plausibility ranges and relative comparisons rather than exact point estimates.

7
Discussion
This chapter discusses what the prototype means beyond the verification already preยญ
sented in Chapter 6. Rather than repeating the observations, the focus here is on interยญ
pretation (what the system can and cannot be used to claim), limitations that matter in
practice, and concrete directions that would make the approach more reliable and more
valuable in real deployments.
7.1
Interpretation of Results
A practical interpretation of the prototype is that it turns a DPPยญstyle system from a passive
data repository into something closer to an evidence surface, it can highlight missing
information, suspicious patterns, and potential hotspots in a way that is fast to inspect
and easy to communicate. That is already useful, because many organisations do not fail
due to lack of ambition, but due to lack of visibility and operational followยญup.
At the same time, the prototype should be understood as a decisionยญsupport tool, not an
automatic verifier of truth. The conformance and KPI panels are best read as โsignals
worth investigatingโ rather than final judgements. If a phase is missing, an ordering looks
wrong, or a risk threshold is exceeded, the right next step is typically to ask, is the data
missing, is the mapping wrong, or is the process actually problematic?
For CO2e , the most important takeaway is that the process view is a good interface for
finding where impact concentrates, but it does not yet explain why or provide a guaranteed
best action. In other words, the system can help a user notice that certain transport legs
or stages dominate the footprint, but translating that into interventions still depends on
context (e.g., product mass, functional unit, packaging, customer expectations, and serยญ
vice life). This is especially important when comparing very different product categories,
โfurniture has higher total CO2e than a shirtโ may be true and still not actionable unless
the impact is normalised or linked to a comparable service outcome.
Finally, for the useยญphase pipeline, the interpretation should be conservative, the system
can measure lifetime and transfer signals if people log events, but it cannot turn selfยญ
reported activity into strong causal claims about realยญworld impact reduction without field
data and careful handling of bias and missingness. In practice, the product value is still
real, even imperfect signals can be enough to support repair/resale programs, customer
engagement, and internal learning but the claims must match the evidence strength.
7.2
Threats to Validity
โข Construct validity The evaluation relies on synthetic event logs and proxy KPIs,
CO2e values depend on factor assumptions and calibration choices, so observed
โhotspotsโ reflect the constructed signal model.
โข Internal validity Results are sensitive to logging completeness (missing phases),
derived timestamps, and (in the useยญphase) selfยญreported events, deviations may
reflect data quality issues rather than true process behaviour.
โข External validity Findings may not generalise across product categories and brands
with different lifecycle granularity, data availability, and process variability, rule proยญ
files likely require categoryยญ and customerยญspecific tuning.

7.3
Limitations
Timestampยญfirst discovery is a deliberate baseline, but limited by data quality The
current mining logic is intentionally timestampยญfirst because product lifecycles can be
categoryยญ and supplierยญspecific and the platform cannot assume a single ordering. In
practice, real supplyยญchain data often has missing or inconsistent timestamps. In those
cases, discovered structure can reflect logging artefacts and coverage gaps rather than
the true chain of activities. This limitation motivates hybrid discovery as future work, using
timestamps when reliable, and falling back on structural constraints (e.g., footprintยญgraph
precedence, billยญofยญmaterial constraints, or explicit business rules) when timestamps are
weak.
Limited representation of โhow it should beโ (beyond time) Many deviations that matยญ
ter for sustainability and compliance are not purely temporal. They depend on structural
constraints and domain rules, required manufacturing subยญsteps for specific materials, alยญ
lowed/forbidden processยญcountry combinations, certificate obligations, or conditional reยญ
quirements triggered by product composition. A phaseยญlevel conformance layer cannot
express these cases well, so it will inevitably miss some meaningful deviations.
Conformance does not generalise across mixed product portfolios A single company
can span categories with very different lifecycles (e.g., furniture and apparel). If rules are
kept generic, they become too weak to be useful, if rules are made strict, they produce
false violations. Without categoryยญaware rule profiles (and ideally customerยญspecific tunยญ
ing), the system cannot reliably support a broad portfolio under one configuration.
CO2e output is only as credible as the factor input The prototype can display CO2e
values cleanly, but it does not solve factor governance. If factors are static, outdated, or
poorly matched to the actual activity, the resulting hotspots may be misleading. The same
issue applies to risk overlays, coverage and provenance matter as much as the UI.
Onยญdemand log derivation has scaling and reproducibility tradeยญoffs Deriving logs
from JSON snapshots at query time keeps the architecture simple, but it can become
expensive at scale and makes historical reproducibility harder unless snapshots/logs are
versioned explicitly (e.g., โshow the model as of last quarterโ).
Selfยญreported events and nonยญcertifying CO2e values Both pipelines should be interยญ
preted as decisionยญsupport rather than certified audit trails. In the useยญphase pipeline,
events such as wash, maintenance, or end_of_use are userยญentered and can be backยญ
dated or entered incorrectly, the system records and mines what was reported, but it does
not independently verify that the action occurred. In the upstream pipeline, CO2e values
may be supplierยญprovided or manually entered and can differ in assumptions, boundaries,
or data quality across organisations. Accordingly, the CO2e lenses and conformance
outputs are presented as inspectable evidence cues (hotspots, deviations, and coverยญ
age gaps) that support investigation and comparison under stated assumptions, not as
assurance or certification of environmental claims.
