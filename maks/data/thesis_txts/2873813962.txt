Abstract 
Gaseous pollutants pose a significant and often undetected threat to cultural 
heritage collections within galleries, libraries, archives, and museums (GLAMs), 
causing at parts-per-billion (ppb) concentrations. Despite this risk, practical 
monitoring remains limited, as most commercial sensors lack the necessary 
sensitivity, real-time capability, or heritage-specific standardization. As part of 
the SIMIACC project, this study aimed to bridge this gap by identifying and 
validating commercially available gas sensors for acetic acid, nitrogen oxides, 
formaldehyde, and hydrogen sulfide at heritage-relevant low concentrations. 
Through market mapping and controlled laboratory experiments, commercially 
available gas sensors were evaluated for price, lifetime, stability, sensitivity, 
response time and selectivity. Results indicate that electrochemical sensors for 
acetic acid, designed for industrial safety, lack the sensitivity and selectivity 
required for GLAM applications. Formaldehyde sensors offer cost and 
sensitivity advantages but are affected by cross-interference, which could be 
mitigated through sensor arrays or data interpretation strategies. A specific 
nitrogen dioxide sensor lacked sufficient ppb-level sensitivity and stability, while 
a nitric oxide sensor showed promise for monitoring nitrogen oxides trends but 
required humidity and hydrogen sulfide correction. A hydrogen sulfide sensor 
performed well but needed complementary formaldehyde sensing to isolate 
interference. The findings confirm that no single commercial sensor can be 
directly matched to one target gas without calibration and environmental 
characterization. A recommended configuration combines formaldehyde 
sensors and hydrogen sulfide sensors for organic compounds and hydrogen 
sulfide monitoring, with nitric oxide sensors for nitrogen oxides, enabling 
continuous monitoring supplemented by traditional sampling analysis when 
unclear alerts occur. Ultimately, accurate monitoring in GLAMs necessitates 
moving beyond simplistic one-sensor-per-pollutant approaches towards 
integrated, calibrated sensor systems tailored to specific heritage environments.

1. Introduction 
1.1. Motivation: Threat of Gaseous Pollutants 
Gaseous pollutants significantly threaten indoor cultural heritage collections, 
causing corrosion, fading, and embrittlement at concentrations far below human 
detection thresholds. Findings from the MEMORI project, which surveyed over 
170 heritage institutions, identified airborne pollutants as responsible for 36% of 
recorded damage[1], highlighting the urgent need for improved indoor air quality 
management. Alarmingly, only 13% of institutions actively monitor pollutants, 
revealing a critical gap between recognized risks and preventive action.  
The framework for managing environmental factors on cultural heritage has 
evolved considerably since Garry Thomson's foundational work on relative 
humidity, temperature, light and air pollution effects [2]. Subsequent studies 
have contributed substantially to the advancement of environmental monitoring 
technologies for cultural heritage. While temperature, humidity and particulate 
matter monitoring have become standard practice [3], [4], the practical solutions 
for monitoring specific gaseous pollutants such as nitrogen oxides and acetic 
acid remain underdeveloped [5]. Current approaches to the gaseous pollutant 
monitoring in heritage environments face three critical limitations:  
Inadequate Sensitivity: While commercial sensors exist for most pollutants [6], 
they are primarily designed for industrial safety standards (ppm ranges), failing 
to meet the sensitivity requirements for cultural heritage applications, where 
damage can occur at parts-per-billion (ppb) or even parts-per-trillion (ppt) 
concentrations [7].  
Lack of Real-Time Capability: Although laboratory-grade analytical methods 
can achieve the necessary detection limits, solutions like passive dosimeters 
and air samplers typically require off-site analysis and extended measurement 
periods (days to months) [8], preventing real-time and sustainable monitoring.  
==PAGE:11==
 
2 
Absence of Standardized Solutions: The lack of heritage-specific solutions 
forces institutions to develop custom-built devices that often experience cross-
sensitivity to interfering gases, leaving conservators with inadequate real-time 
options or cumbersome lab methods. 
Consequently, portable devices combining several metal-oxide, electrochemical 
or optical gas sensors have received particular attention in heritage monitoring 
strategies [6], despite ongoing sensitivity challenges. Recent field applications 
of sensor prototypes in Rome conservation institutions demonstrated potential 
for heritage air quality monitoring. The low-cost device integrated multiple 
commercial sensors [9], successfully captured distinct diurnal trends and traffic-
related variations, despite the NOx sensor exhibiting a noise standard deviation 
of 7.5 ppb. Apart from sensor-based approaches, the MEMORI technology 
offers a well-established solution using two small dosimeters to monitor acidic 
gases and nitrogen oxides [10]. Rather than measuring the concentration of 
individual pollutants, it assesses risk based on cumulative exposure levels of 
acidic and oxidizing air pollutants, which are then compared to material-specific 
damage thresholds. However, it requires a three-month exposure period and 
lacks compensation for temperature and humidity variations, which calls for 
cautious interpretation.  
Clearly, these solutions each involve trade-offs between real-time capability, 
accuracy, and interpretability, highlighting the need for further optimization in 
heritage-specific pollutant monitoring tools. 
1.2. Objectives: Bridging Monitoring Gaps 
Recent developments clearly indicate a growing momentum in the heritage 
sector toward integrated, sustainability-oriented approaches to preventive 
conservation. A prime example of this trend is the Horizon Europe-funded 
project, SIMIACC [11], which seeks to design and implement 20 innovative 
prototype solutions across six galleries, libraries, archives, and museums 
(GLAMs). Accurate contaminant monitoring and forecasting are essential for 
optimizing indoor air quality. As a subtask of the SIMIACC, this study explores 
==PAGE:12==
 
3 
practical environmental monitoring solutions for heritage environments by 
selecting and validating commercially available gas sensors capable of 
detecting acetic acid, nitrogen oxides, hydrogen sulfide and formaldehyde at 
extremely low levels.  
The objectives of this study are to: 
 Investigate sensor technologies and identify commercially available gas 
sensors suitable for heritage environments. 
 Evaluate sensor performance at low concentration levels relevant to 
conservation needs, through literature reviews and laboratory experiments. 
 Provide recommendations for sensor selection in GLAMs applications based 
on the data analysis. 
1.3. Scope and Limitations  
While this study explores practical solutions for heritage air quality monitoring, 
certain technical and operational boundaries influence its applicability. The 
limitations detailed below serve as essential context for interpreting the results 
and as directions for future innovation in heritage monitoring systems. 
 
Limited testing sample size 
The study's scope was constrained by project costs and time, limiting both the 
variety/quantity of sensors evaluated and the range of gas species tested. 
Evaluation focused exclusively on SIMIACC's four target gases (acetic acid, 
nitrogen oxides, formaldehyde, and hydrogen sulfide), with supplemental testing 
of isobutylene and methanol for certain sensors. Interference from other gases 
was not assessed. Moreover, acetic acid sensors failed during experiments; this 
occurrence may not be interpreted as indicative of overall product quality. 
 
Limited testing duration and environmental scope 
Based on controlled testing using a custom-built platform over two months, the 
results may not reflect long-term environmental variations or material-specific 
interactions. 
==PAGE:13==
 
4 
 
Measurement accuracy and uncertainty 
During the testing process, indoor environmental conditions were controlled, 
instruments were calibrated, and multiple repeated measurements were 
performed. Also, data processing methods such as smoothing and curve fitting 
were applied to improve result accuracy. Nevertheless, some degree of 
measurement error remains. A detailed uncertainty analysis is provided in 
Chapter 3. 
1.4. Structure of the Thesis 
The introduction establishes the research context by highlighting the critical 
threat posed by gaseous pollutants to heritage collections, current monitoring 
gaps, and the specific objectives and scope of this study. Chapter 2 then 
provides the essential background knowledge, introducing target contaminants 
and reviewing existing monitoring technologies with a particular focus on 
sensor-based approaches. Building on this foundation, Chapter 3 details the 
process of selecting commercially available gas sensors capable of detecting 
acetic acid, nitric oxide, nitrogen dioxide, formaldehyde and hydrogen sulfide at 
heritage-relevant concentrations. Following the market mapping and sensor 
selection, Chapter 4 describes the experimental design, specifically presenting 
the multi-sensor testing platform developed for evaluation and explaining the 
methods for generating controlled pollutant gas sources. Calibration procedures 
and error control measures will also be discussed. Chapter 5 presents and 
discusses the experimental results, systematically evaluating sensor 
performance for five pollutants in dedicated subsections, followed by an 
integrated overview comparing performance across all pollutants. Finally, 
Chapter 6 summarizes the main findings, discusses their implications for 
practical heritage monitoring, and suggests future research directions.

2. Technical Background 
Effective indoor air quality management in heritage spaces begins with two 
foundations: a clear understanding of pollutant behavior and the capability for 
accurate detection. Following an introduction to target pollutants defined in the 
SIMIACC project, a literature-based review of monitoring technologies is 
presented. 
2.1. Target Pollutant Profiles: Risks to Heritage Collections 
According to the SIMIACC project, the target pollutants in this study are acetic 
acid, formaldehyde, nitrogen oxides, and hydrogen sulfide. Both acetic acid and 
formaldehyde are classified as polar volatile organic compounds (VOCs) that 
readily volatilize at room temperature. Among nitrogen oxides, nitrogen dioxide 
and nitric oxide are of particular concern in heritage conservation contexts. 
Hydrogen sulfide, in turn, represents the reduced sulfur gases known for their 
corrosive effects. Summaries of pollutant profiles are provided in this section, 
based on data from Tools for Conservation: Monitoring for Gaseous Pollutants 
in Museum Environments [12]. A common feature of these pollutants is that 
their damage thresholds for heritage collections vary significantly depending on 
material type and microclimatic conditions. For instance, field measurements at 
the British Museum (BM) demonstrate that formaldehyde concentrations 
reaching 480 ppb, which is 24 times higher than the recommended 20 ppb 
threshold for general collections[8], did not cause detectable material 
degradation when relative humidity remained strictly below 45% [13]. As a 
result, establishing absolute concentration thresholds or universal monitoring 
standards remains challenging in conservation practice, since the underlying 
reaction mechanisms may be more complex than currently understood. 
2.1.1. Acetic acid 
As an organic aid, acetic acid (HAC) exhibits relatively high vapor pressure of 
20.3 hPa at 25°C [14], facilitating its ready off-gassing from common heritage

metal corrosion and cellulose hydrolysis. Organic acids like acetic acid require 
monitoring protocols specific to heritage conservation, distinct from general 
occupational health standards. The permissible exposure limit (PEL) for acetic 
acid is 10,000 ppb for human health protection [15], whereas the guideline for 
GLAMs recommend maintaining concentrations below 400 ppb for one-year 
preservation targets [16].

2.2. Monitoring Technologies: From Lab to Field 
2.2.1. Literature review 
Methodologies for monitoring airborne pollutants have been a defining agenda 
at the International Conference on Indoor Air Quality in Heritage and Historic 
Environments (IAQ Heritage) since 1998. Analysis of fifteen conference 
proceedings (1998-2024) (Figure 2.1), reveals that 78% of presentations 
addressed monitoring-related research, with air sampler applications accounting 
for 48% of these studies [17]. While air samplers remain prevalent due to their 
operational accessibility and result reliability, their extended sampling durations 
and dependence on external laboratory processing hinder timely preventive 
interventions in heritage conservation. 
 
Figure 2.1 Adoption of air samplers, passive dosimeters and sensor technologies in 
heritage monitoring. (Data based on 536 reports across 15 conferences, 1998-2024) 
The need for detecting facilities that institutions can operate independently, 
without reliance on specialized laboratories, was explicitly raised by the 
Germanische Nationalmuseum in 2001. Their presentation noted that,  
“Independent and reliable detecting at low costs must be the first step.” [18] 
A fully operational prototype for on-line real-time monitoring system based on 
metal sensors was featured at the Seventh Conference (2006) [19], with 
wireless sensor networks subsequently dominating technical presentations by

2020 [20]. Although generic sensor network architectures are well-established 
in other fields, realizing their full potential for heritage-specific monitoring 
demands cross-disciplinary efforts in: 


and reliability; 
 
Data-to-decision frameworks for transforming monitoring data into 
conservation actions; 


Devices that offer true real-time measurements are generally based on sensor 
technologies. Most portable air quality sensors operate as passive samplers, 
offering direct-read, quantitative measurements, whereas stationary analytical 
instruments typically rely on active sampling, drawing air continuously or at set 
intervals to achieve higher accuracy and consistency. Gas sensor technologies 
can be further classified into electrical, thermochemical, optical and advanced 
types. Electrical sensors detect gases through changes in resistance, such as 
those in metal oxide semiconductors and electrochemical cells. Optical sensors 
measure how gases interact with light, including methods like non-dispersive 
infrared and photoionization detection. Thermochemical sensors rely on heat-
driven reactions, as seen in catalytic bead devices. Advanced technologies offer 
high precision, including approaches like photoacoustic spectroscopy and 
sensors based on carbon nanotube arrays. Figure 2.5 qualitatively compares

3. Sensor Selection 
Building upon the technical foundation established in Chapter 2, this chapter 
focuses on the identification of commercially viable sensors using a three-stage 
selection methodology. As illustrated in Figure 3.1, the process begins with the 
definition of pollutant-specific selection criteria, followed by systematic market 
mapping, and concludes with a multi-criteria decision analysis. 
 
Figure 3.1 Three-stage sensor selection workflow: from criteria filtering to final decision. 
3.1. Selection Criteria: Technical and Operational Factors 
Taking into account both the characteristics of the target and practical design 
constraints, a set of initial screening criteria for heritage sensor selections is 
defined, as shown in Table 3.1. The screening framework prioritizes three 
critical dimensions to the sensor selection: technical feasibility, operational 
viability and sustainable economics. A minimum lifespan of one year is set to 
support sustainable, long-term monitoring without frequent replacement. To 
facilitate compact system design and scalability, sensors must sensors must 
feature a miniaturized form factor, support standard communication protocols 
(UART/I²C), and preferably provide an open software development kit to allow 
flexible data access and integration into wireless sensor networks. Furthermore, 
for this project, sensors were excluded unless they were commercially available 
off-the-shelf with a delivery time of 30 days or less and unit costs of €200 or

below, ensuring feasible implementation within the budget and timeline 
constraints.

3.2. Market Mapping: Commercial Products Screening 
Potential sensor candidates were identified through a comprehensive market 
survey utilizing a multi-source approach: literature mining, targeted screening of 
online manufacturer databases, and direct supplier engagement (see Appendix. 
A. Tab. 1 to Tab. 5 for full candidate list and key metrics).  
3.2.1. Literature Mining 
Applying the pollutant damage thresholds established in Chapter 2 as a primary 
filter, literature mining identified and extracted validated sensor models 
documented in technical literature and case studies for their proven efficacy in 
monitoring pollutants critical to heritage conservation.  
3.2.2. Online Searches 
Online screening of manufacturer resources applied initial filters based on target 
gas pollutant, high resolution requirements (≤100 ppb), and miniaturized form 
==PAGE:24==
 
15 
factor. A pivotal observation emerged: sensors meeting these core 
technical/physical requirements universally satisfied baseline operational criteria 
for integration, delivery and lifetime. While certain models offered advantages 
beyond these thresholds, such as extended lifespans or additional 
communication interfaces, their marginal utility in heritage applications was 
deemed low. Given the lack of empirical evidence quantifying sensor 
degradation under actual long-term heritage monitoring conditions, claims of 
long-term savings from extended-lifespan sensors lack credibility. Stakeholders 
therefore rationally prioritize minimizing initial investment over hypothetical long-
term savings. Given the stable indoor museum environment and well-defined 
wireless network architecture, the benefits of features like multi-interface 
support offered negligible functional gains while potentially inflating costs.  
3.2.3. Supplier engagement 
Direct supplier engagement provided critical validation, securing verified 
performance specifications, updated datasheets, technical clarifications, binding 
quotations, and confirmed delivery timelines. Notably, electrochemical sensors 
dominated supplier recommendations, accounting for approximately 98% of the 
proposed solutions. The limited alternatives included photoionization detector 
(PID)-based total volatile organic compound (TVOC) sensors and metal oxide 
semiconductor (MOS)-based H2S detectors. PID sensors provide broad 
detection capabilities at high cost and poor selectivity. MOS sensors offer a 
cost-effective alternative but lack the resolution required for precise 
environmental control in cultural heritage applications.

lifetime requirements, the final selection phase prioritized the two remaining 
criteria: unit cost and detection resolution. Figure 3.2 presents a resolution–

3.3.1. HAC Sensors 
In the context of acetic acid detection, the market lacks dedicated sensors. Most 
available devices are general-purpose TVOC sensors, which require specific 
calibration to accurately quantify acetic acid. Therefore, the best resolution, 
listed as 1 ppb, reflects measurements based on standard calibration gases 
(e.g., isobutylene) and may not be representative of actual acetic acid 
performance. In contrast, the poorest resolution, at 1000 ppb, corresponds to 
electrochemical sensors, which is unlikely to satisfy the sensitivity criteria. 
Faced with this constrained availability and the critical need for sub-ppm



4. Experimental Methodology 
4.1. Experimental  Overview 
All experiments were conducted inside an indoor environmental chamber (3.6 m 
× 2.5 m × 2.5 m) (Figure 4.1), which utilized displacement ventilation with an air 
exchange rate of 1400–1500 m³/h. Room temperature and relative humidity 
were adjustable as needed. Standard test conditions were set at 25 °C, in 
alignment with typical sensor operating specifications. Indoor humidity was 
continuously monitored but, in most cases, not actively controlled. 
 
Figure 4.1 Interior view of the environmental chamber with the experimental setup 
During a 67-hour dynamic monitoring campaign in May, the return-air 
temperature was set at 25.0 °C, while sensor-local air temperature remained 
highly stable, averaging 26.77 ± 0.07 °C, with a relative humidity of 29 ± 3%. 
This thermal offset was expected and attributed to internal heat generation by 
sensor electronics and supply fan. In addition, the atmospheric pressure for all 
==PAGE:30==
 
21 
calculations in this study was set at 1.01 bar, corresponding to the average 
atmospheric pressure recorded in Lyngby during the experimental monitoring 
period (April-June 2025). 
The experimental setup consists of three main components. 
 
Test Subjects: A set of eight selected sensors (Table 3.2). 
 
Exposure Platform: Parallel evaluation system for multiple gas sensors. 
 
Pollutant Delivery: Controlled generation systems. 
With the selected sensors already detailed in Chapter 3, the following sections 
focus on the underlying experimental principles of the multi-sensor testing 
platform, the controlled pollutant generation systems and relevant calibration 
procedures.  An associated uncertainty analysis is elaborated in each section. 
4.2. Parallel Multi-Sensor Testing Platform 
The multi-sensor testing was conducted within a flow duct system designed to 
introduce precise target gas mixtures. All sensors were positioned within a 
downstream section of the duct. A removable transparent glass tube enclosed 
this sensor section, facilitating visual inspection and maintenance access 
(Figure 4.2). A reduced duct diameter intentionally increased flow velocity, 
generating turbulent conditions that enhanced gas homogeneity across the 
sensor array. Independent data interfaces connected each sensor, ensuring 
signal integrity and eliminating electrical cross-talk between channels.

4.4. Controlled Pollutant Generation 
The generation of target gas can be divided into two categories: static and 
dynamic methods. Static gas generation involves evaporating a fixed liquid 
volume or thermally decomposing a known polymer mass within a sealed 
container of known diluent volume. However, adsorption and condensation on 
==PAGE:34==
 
25 
the container walls make it difficult to calculate the exact concentration of the 
released gas [23]. Another major limitation is that only a finite amount of test 
gas is produced in each batch. By contrast, dynamic methods, which are more 
suitable for this study, rely on a continuous flow of diluting gas passing through 
a generation unit where it mixes with the vapor at a known generation rate.  
This study comparatively evaluated four dynamic methods for controlled 
pollutant release (standard gas dilution, injection, evaporation, and reactive 
generation), with their applicability to specific gaseous pollutants detailed in the 
following subsections. 
4.4.1. Standard Gas Cylinder 
Standard gas cylinders function as primary reference sources for sensor 
calibration and validation. These pressurized containers hold precisely 
quantified gaseous mixtures at certified concentrations, traceable to 
international metrological standards. Due to both safety and accuracy 
requirements, certified standard gas cylinders purchased from Linde were 
employed as the pollutant sources for NO, NO2, and H2S, with specifications 
and purities summarized as follows: 
 
Nitric Oxide 2.5: ≥99.5% at 33 bar 
 
Nitrogen Dioxide 2.0: ≥99.0% at 1 bar 
 
Hydrogen Sulfide 2.3: ≥99.3% at 17.9 bar 
In addition, the NO2 / N2O4 equilibrium system requires critical consideration in 
standard gas applications. These species exist in temperature-dependent 
equilibrium: 
2𝑁𝑂2(𝑔) ⇌𝑁2𝑂4(𝑔) 
Under typical cylinder storage pressures (1–3 bar), liquid N2O4 predominates at 
ambient temperatures. NO2 exhibits a characteristic brownish-red color, while 
N2O4 is colorless. Therefore, special attention must be paid during gas release, 
as any change in color may indicate a shift in the equilibrium.

4.6. Sensor Validation Principles 
Measurement data were automatically recorded by each device with a unified 
timestamp at millisecond, second, or minute intervals. After preprocessing, the 
data were imported into MATLAB and analyzed using different scripts for 
processing, analysis, and visualization (Appendix. C. ). 
The sensor validation focused on the following key aspects: 
1) Zero drift represents evaluation of baseline signal stability over time in 
the absence of target gases. 
 
Under standard conditions 
 
Temperature-induced drift 
 
Humidity-induced drift 
==PAGE:45==
 
36 
2) Sensitivity reflects the ability to detect small changes in pollutant 
concentration. 
 
Digital resolution: the smallest change reflected in the digital 
output, determined by instrument design.  
 
Repeatability uncertainty: the degree of fluctuation in sensor 
output when exposed to a constant gas concentration, typically 
quantified by the standard deviation (SD) around the mean value.  
 
Detection limit (LOD): the lowest concentration that can be 
distinguished from zero with statistical confidence, typically 
estimated as three times of the standard deviation measured 
under clean-air conditions [25]. 
3) Response characteristics 
 
Linearity assesses how well the sensor output corresponds to the 
actual gas concentration across a wide range. A perfectly linear 
sensor has a proportional response throughout its operating 
range. 
 
Response time describes how quickly the sensor reacts to a 
sudden change in gas concentration. It is commonly quantified by 
the time required to reach 90% of the final response (T90). For 
sensors with first-order dynamics, this behavior can be modeled 
using a time constant (τ), which corresponds to the time needed to 
reach approximately 63% of the final value [26]. 
 
Hysteresis effects refer to the difference in sensor response 
when comparing ascending and descending concentration steps. 
4) Selectivity/cross-sensitivity evaluates how the sensor responds to 
other gases present in the environment, which may interfere with 
accurate detection of the target gas. 
5) Additional parameters 
 
Warm-up time: the time required after power-on before the sensor 
output becomes stable. 
 
Baseline recovery after high-concentration exposure: how quickly 
and reliably the baseline returns to normal after being exposed to 
high levels of target or interfering gases.

5.1. Acetic Acid Sensors 
Electrochemical sensor A1 requires several days to reach a relatively stable 
baseline after initial power-up, primarily due to its circuit design with a positive 
bias voltage. In contrast, sensor A2, based on PID principles, was not a brand-
new unit; after prolonged power-off storage, it took several hours to stabilize 
upon reactivation. Notably, A2 had not been frequently used and had previously 
been sent for repair during its last usage. However, both HAC sensors 
eventually failed after several days of testing. A1 rapidly lost responsiveness to 
acetic acid after initial operation. A2 experienced a firmware storage error about 
one week into the experiment, requiring factory maintenance.

5.2.3. Response Characteristics: Linearity and Dynamic Response 
The raw data used for fitting came from experiments conducted over two 
separate days. On Test 1, both B1 and B2 sensors were used simultaneously 
for measurements. B1’s measurements were calibrated based on the results 
from air sampling analysis, yielding a linear relationship between readings and 
the actual concentrations (Figure 5.7). The resulting linear function was then 
used as a reference to estimate formaldehyde levels during the Test 2 
experiments, where air sampling was not performed. Among three sensors, B1 
exhibited rapid response to concentration changes but showed measurable 
hysteresis: at identical concentration levels, its readings during ascending 
phases consistently exceeded those during descending phases. Therefore, 
averaged values from multiple measurement points were used for analysis to 
reduce the hysteresis effects.

Furthermore, these fitted calibration formulas were validated through 
subsequent concentration tests. For example, at a known gas flow rate q1=0.99

ccs, the predicted concentration c2 was 155±8 ppb. The predicted B1 reading

The results indicate that none of the sensors’ readings perfectly align with the 
actual measurements, but all three sensors exhibited increasing readings with 
higher concentrations. Moreover, each exhibited strong linear correlations, as 
reflected by high R² values. However, baseline measurements for B1 and B2, 
which were generally located at the origin (0, 0), deviated substantially from the 
main trend and were excluded from the fitting.  
Possible reasons for these deviations include: 
 
Sensors may not respond to concentrations below a certain threshold, so 
a strictly linear relationship hold only above the detection limit. 
 
Sensors may produce negative responses at low concentrations, which 
are suppressed by design and thus do not represent the true signal. 
Although accurate measurements below 20 ppb remain challenging, B1 and B2 
are sufficient to alert risks at formaldehyde levels above 10–20 ppb, which is the 
threshold for general collections in GLAMs. 
These results suggest that for precise concentration measurements, the 
sensors must be recalibrated before using under the specific conditions of their 
intended application, otherwise the readings may be much higher or lower than 
the actual concentrations.

The algebraic calculations presented here provide a constructive approach to 
component recovery, but they are not a final practical solution. Experimental 
validation is required as performance is constrained by several real factors: 
 
Sensor-Specific Detection Limits 
Detection limits and resolution differ across sensors. For example, B3 may 
not respond below ~20 ppb for formaldehyde, and its 10-ppb resolution 
substantially increases uncertainty in computed concentrations. 
 
Environmental Interference 
Some sensors exhibit environment-dependent baselines. For example, D1’s 
baseline shifts with humidity, so accurate concentration retrieval requires 
detailed humidity compensation. 
 
Unmodeled VOCs 
Untested VOCs beyond the target gases may also cause cross-interference, 
violating the linear model and degrading reconstruction accuracy. 
 
Matrix Degeneracy 
When sensor responses to different gases are highly correlated, the 
resulting sensitivity matrix becomes near-singular. In such cases, even small 
measurement noise can be greatly amplified during calculations, leading to 
unstable or unreliable estimates.

6. Conclusion 
Accurate monitoring of gaseous pollutants in GLAM environments remains 
challenging due to low concentrations and sensor cross-sensitivity. A set of 
radar charts (Figure 6.1) provide a qualitative overview of each tested sensor’s 
strengths and weaknesses, illustrating their suitability for the target gases.
 
Figure 6.1 Qualitative assessment of the sensor performance 
==PAGE:69==
 
60 
Electrochemical sensors are not ideal for acetic acid monitoring in GLAM 
environments, as industrial designs only meet high-concentration alarm 
thresholds. PID sensors can detect acetic acid under isolated conditions using 
scaling factors, but co-existing VOCs often mask the signal. However, since 
portable TVOC sensors are already widely used and well-established in 
standard indoor environments, existing sensors in GLAMs, even if not originally 
intended for acetic acid monitoring, can be adapted using calibrated conversion 
factors to track acetic acid levels. This approach remains viable only when the 
pollutant composition of the specific gallery is thoroughly characterized. 
Formaldehyde sensors offer favorable sensitivity and cost, but their 
performance is limited by cross-interference. Using a combination of sensor 
arrays allows more accurate determination of major gas components, although 
minor interferences may persist. 
For nitrogen oxides, sensor C1 lacks sufficient sensitivity and stability for 
practical monitoring at ppb levels. In contrast, sensor D1 can track trends in 
both NO and NO₂ without interference from organic compounds, but readings 
must be corrected for humidity and hydrogen sulfide effects.  
Sensor E1 provides moderate cost and superior response performance, yet 
requires complementary use with a formaldehyde sensor to isolate 
formaldehyde-related interference. 
Overall, no single commercial sensor can be strictly assigned to one target gas. 
A practical configuration combines B1 for organic compounds and hydrogen 
sulfide detection, with D1 dedicated to nitrogen oxides monitoring. This sensor 
array enables continuous long-term monitoring and can be supplemented by 
triggered checks or traditional sampling. If finer resolution of individual gas 
components is required, additional sensors can be added to form a larger array. 
Linear algebra provides a principled framework for designing such arrays, but 
real-world deployment still requires iterative validation to address sensor-
specific detection limits, environmental variability, and unmodeled effects. Only 
through this refinement can a theoretically sound system become a reliable 
operational solution.
