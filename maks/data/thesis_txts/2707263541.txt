ABSTRACT 
On March 11, 2020, the World Health Organization declared a pandemic when the 
highly contagious COVID-19 virus had spread across the globe. As a result of the 
measures taken to slow down the spread of the virus, in-person social contact 
was limited to a minimum. This has pushed many companies to adapt 
immediately by implementing telework. As a result of working from home and 
splitting the workforce, management practices were put to the ultimate test, as 
managing a virtual team in many cases demands a different approach than 
managing a traditional face-to-face team. 
Project management success is typically measured by a project team's capacity 
and effectiveness to complete the project on time, within budget, and within the 
client's scope and quality requirements. This approach generally focuses on 
technical areas. However, since projects are performed and delivered by people, 
research suggests that human factors, such as trust, motivation, and the 
willingness to collaborate play a significant role in project management success. 
Nevertheless, developing and maintaining these factors in dispersed teams 
appears to be more difficult than in co-located ones. The purpose of this thesis is 
to explore the roles of trust, motivation, and collaboration in a virtual team setting, 
as well as to propose actions for developing, maintaining, and enhancing these 
human factors as a project manager at Skanska Sweden in light of the increasing 
prevalence of virtual teamwork. 
This research has mainly taken a qualitative approach. However, quantitative 
data has also been used in some cases to supplement the context with valuable 
information. A literature review and a case study have been conducted. The case 
study included a questionnaire sent to project managers at Skanska Sweden. 
Based on previous research and the findings of this study, suggestions on actions 
have been proposed. 
The findings of this study show that trust, motivation, and collaboration, among 
other factors, play a significant role in the success of virtual projects. As a result, 
project managers must use their leadership skills to a greater extent when leading 
their teams from a distance to develop, sustain, and enhance these human-related 
factors. The findings indicate that there are a number of human-related aspects 
to consider in order to achieve a successful virtual project. However, many of 
these aspects seem to be interconnected.

1 INTRODUCTION 
This chapter gives a background to the problem addressed in this report while 
also presenting the study's purpose and research questions. 
 
1.1 Background 
On March 11, 2020, the World Health Organization declared a pandemic when the 
new, highly contagious COVID-19 virus had spread across the globe (WHO, 2022). 
Intending to slow down the spread of the virus, governments worldwide 
undertook various measures, including mobility restrictions, socio-economic 
restrictions, physical distancing, hygiene measures, communication, and 
international support mechanisms (Bruinen de Bruin, et al., 2020). Due to the 
significant changes caused by the measures, in-person social contact, among 
others, was limited to a minimum, pushing several companies to adapt 
immediately by implementing telework and home-office arrangements 
(Belzunegui-Eraso & Erro-Garcés, 2020). Face-to-face meetings became virtual 
meetings for many, and collaboration with others shifted from physical 
encounters to virtual platforms, leaving employees across all industries with the 
challenge of working on or managing virtual teams (Swift, 2022). As a result of 
working from home and splitting the workforce, several managers found 
themselves in unfamiliar territory in terms of team management (Newman & 
Ford, 2021). While organizations have long accepted and acknowledged virtual 
and flexible working, the COVID-19 pandemic has hastened its adoption and 
necessity (Newman & Ford, 2021). This has put management practices to the 
ultimate test, as managing a virtual team in many cases demands a different 
approach than managing a traditional face-to-face team (Contreras, Baykal, & 
Abid, 2020). 
In project management, success is commonly measured by a project team's 
capacity and effectiveness to achieve the triple constraints of completing the 
project on time, within budget, and within the scope and quality requirements of 
the client (Levin & Rad, 2006). This approach generally focuses on technical areas. 
Nevertheless, it is crucial to keep in mind that projects are carried out and 
delivered by people. Research suggests that the human-related aspect of projects 
plays a significant role in achieving project management success (Bond-Barnard, 
Fletcher, & Steyn, 2018). Without high-performing people devoted to the project's 
objectives and the organization's strategic vision, it is challenging to accomplish 
==PAGE:10==
 
9 
Zahra Al-Mosawi                Virtual Team Management with Focus on Trust, Motivation & Collaboration 
the project's scope, time, and cost targets (Levin & Rad, 2006). A few examples of 
human project success factors that have been investigated in literature are trust, 
motivation, and collaboration (Bond-Barnard, Fletcher, & Steyn, 2018). Although 
these factors are essential for all types of teams, developing and maintaining 
them in dispersed teams appears to be more difficult than co-located ones 
(Newman & Ford, 2021). 
Now that the COVID-19 pandemic has pushed many organizations to work fully 
or partially in virtual settings, Skanska Sweden is interested in exploring how 
project managers can achieve project management success by developing, 
maintaining, and enhancing trust, motivation, and collaboration among their 
teams through virtual and flexible working, as this way of working is expected to 
be more common in the future. 
 
1.2 Purpose  
The purpose of this thesis is to gain better knowledge and understanding of the 
roles of trust, motivation, and collaboration in a virtual team setting. Furthermore, 
based on previous research, the experiences and measures of Skanska project 
managers during the pandemic, the goal is to propose actions on how to develop, 
sustain, and enhance trust, motivation, and collaboration as a project manager in 
light of the increasing prevalence of virtual teamwork. 
 
1.3 Objectives/Research Questions 
This research aims to answer the following questions: 
  
▪ What are the roles of trust, motivation, and collaboration in a virtual team 
setting? 
 
▪ How can project managers develop, sustain, and enhance trust, motivation, 
and collaboration in virtual teams? 
 
▪ What difficulties have project managers at Skanska Sweden encountered 
when they needed to manage virtual or hybrid teams due to the COVID-19 
pandemic?

3 THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK 
This chapter of the report presents and describes the theoretical framework that

3.1 Projects 
3.1.1 What is a Project? 
According to the PMBOK® Guide, a collection of standard terminology and 
guidelines for project management published by The Project Management 
Institute, a project is "a temporary endeavor undertaken to create a unique 
product, service, or result" (Project Management Institute, 2017). The reason why 
projects are carried out is to achieve objectives by delivering deliverables. An 
objective may have several definitions, and in the PMBOK ® Guide, it is defined as 
"an outcome toward which work is to be directed, a strategic position to be 
attained, a purpose to be achieved, a result to be obtained, a product to be produced, 
or a service to be performed". On the other hand, a deliverable is described as "any 
unique and verifiable product, result, or capability to perform a service that is 
required to be produced to complete a process, phase, or project".  
Each project is unique, although many of them may share similarities in 
numerous aspects. The differences in projects may be in the provided 
deliverables, the influencing stakeholders, the used resources, the constraints, 
and how the processes are adjusted to provide deliverables (Project Committee 
ISO/PC 236, Project Management, 2012). Some project deliverables and activities 
may contain repetitive parts. However, this repetition does not affect the project's 
essential and unique characteristics (Project Management Institute, 2017). 
Being temporary indicates that a project has a start and an end (Project 
Management Institute, 2017). When one or more of the following conditions is 
met, the project may be considered complete: 
▪ The objectives of the project are fulfilled; 
▪ The objectives, for some reasons, will not or cannot be achieved; 
▪ The project's funding has been depleted or is no longer available; 
▪ The project is no longer needed; 
▪ There are no longer available human or physical resources; or 
==PAGE:15==
 
14 
Zahra Al-Mosawi                Virtual Team Management with Focus on Trust, Motivation & Collaboration 
▪ The project has been cancelled due to legal reasons or for the sake of 
convenience. 
Although projects are temporary, their deliverables may last beyond their 
completion. The deliverables that projects may result in could have a social, 
economic, material, or environmental nature (Project Management Institute, 
2017). 
A project may be limited by a variety of constraints, which are usually 
interdependent. Therefore, project deliverables should meet the project's needs 
while also considering any constraints, such as scope, quality, time, resources, 
and cost (Project Committee ISO/PC 236, Project Management, 2012). 
 
3.1.1.1 The Project Life Cycle 
The sequence of phases that a project goes through from start to finish is referred 
to as the project life cycle (Project Management Institute, 2017). It establishes the 
framework for the overall management structure of the project and is applicable 
regardless of the type of project activities involved. In each phase, a range of 
actions should be conducted in order to manage the project efficiently throughout 
the whole project life cycle (Project Committee ISO/PC 236, Project Management, 
2012). The phases may be sequential, iterative, or overlapping and be determined 
by a variety of factors such as (Project Management Institute, 2017): 
▪ Management requirements;  
▪ Project nature;  
▪ Specific organizational, industrial, or technological characteristics; 
▪ Project elements, such as technology, engineering, business, process, or 
legal; and  
▪ Decision-making points. 
Utilizing several phases may give better insight into the management of the 
project (Project Management Institute, 2017). In addition, it may allow for an 
assessment of the project performance and the implementation of appropriate 
corrective or preventive actions in later phases. 
The project management team is responsible for determining the optimal life 
cycle for each project (Project Management Institute, 2017). The project life cycle 
must be flexible and adaptive enough to the project's diverse factors. By 
Identifying the processes that need to be carried out in each phase, executing the

3.1.2 What is Project Management? 
Project management refers to the process of applying knowledge, skills, tools, and 
techniques to project activities to meet the requirements and objectives of a 
project (PMI, Learn About PMI, 2021). The primary goal of project management is 
to ensure that a project is delivered within the agreed-upon scope, on time, on 
budget, and to the required level of quality (APM, 2021). 
 
3.1.2.1 Project Management Processes 
The project life cycle is managed by carrying out a systematic series of project 
management activities referred to as project management processes (Project 
Management Institute, 2017). By using suitable project management tools and 
techniques, each project management process generates one or more outputs 
from one or more inputs. The output may be a deliverable or an outcome, which 
is a result of a process. The project management processes are connected by the 
outputs they generate in a logical manner, and throughout the project, they may 
include overlapping activities.

The number of iterations and interactions across processes varies according to 
the project's needs and requirements. Processes can, in general, be divided into 
three categories:

▪ Processes that are employed only once or at specific times during the 
project. 
▪ Processes that are carried out on a regular basis as needed. 
▪ Processes that are executed regularly during the duration of the project. 
By appropriately applying and combining logically grouped project management 
processes, effective project management can be achieved (Project Management

3.1.2.2 Project Management Process Groups 
The PMBOK ® Guide divides the project management processes into five groups 
known as project management process groups. The project management process 
groups are defined as “A logical grouping of project management inputs, tools and 
techniques, and outputs” (Project Management Institute, 2017). Included in these 
groupings are Initiating, Planning, Executing, Monitoring, and Controlling and 
Closing. These are described briefly below: 
 
▪ Initiating Process Group: The group of processes carried out in order to 
define a new project or a new phase of an existing project by receiving 
approval to begin the project or phase. 
 
▪ Planning Process Group: The group of processes needed in order to define 
the project scope, refine the objectives, and to identify the activities 
required to accomplish the project's objectives. 
 
▪ Executing Process Group: The group processes utilized to accomplish the 
work specified in the project management plan in order to meet the 
project's objectives. 
 
▪ Monitoring and Controlling Process Group: The group processes necessary 
to monitor, review, and control the project’s progress and performance, 
identify any areas requiring plan adjustments; and implement the 
necessary changes. 
 
▪ Closing Process Group: The group processes needed to finish or close the 
project, phase, or contract in a formal manner.

▪ Project Resource Management: The processes for identifying, acquiring,

▪ Project Communications Management: The processes necessary to ensure

3.1.2.4 The Role of the Project Manager 
A project manager is a project management professional in charge of the 
organization, planning, and execution of a project (Coursera, 2021). It is the 
responsibility of the project manager to lead the project team, define project 
objectives, interact with stakeholders, and manage the project to its completion 
(Coursera, 2021). Many project managers are involved in a project from its start to 
its end. However, in some businesses, a project manager may be involved in 
evaluation and analytical operations prior to the start of a project (Project 
Management Institute, 2017). The function of a project manager varies depending 
on the organization. However, just as project management processes are adapted 
to the project, the project management role is adapted to the organization (Project 
Management Institute, 2017).

3.1.3 Leadership vs. Mangagement 
According to research on leadership and management, the current and the future 
organizations need both leaders and managers (Toor & Ofori, 2008). They require 
leaders who are capable of managing and managers who possess leadership 
qualities. This is in order for the organizations to be more competitive, but also to 
==PAGE:21==
 
20 
Zahra Al-Mosawi                Virtual Team Management with Focus on Trust, Motivation & Collaboration 
be better at problem-solving and to ensure overall functioning teams (Toor & Ofori, 
2008). As a result of this fact, the following question arises:  
"What is the difference between leaders and managers in the first place?" 
Although the terms leadership and management are frequently used 
interchangeably, they do not have the same meaning (Project Management 
Institute, 2017). Management leans more towards leading an individual from one 
point to another by utilizing a predefined set of actions and behaviors (Project 
Management Institute, 2017). On the other hand, leadership leans more towards 
guiding people from one point to another by working with them through 
discussion and debate. Capowski (1994) means that leaders are more about the 
soul or heart, whereas managers are more about the mind (Capowski, 1994). 
Generally, managers tend to be problem solvers and more scientific in nature, and 
their energies are directed toward resolving issues involving organizational goals, 
resources, structures, and people (Toor & Ofori, 2008). As a result, managers tend 
to be more scientific in nature, have a more structured and deliberate approach, 
an authoritative and stabilizing behavior, and a persistent and strict routine (Toor 
& Ofori, 2008). Leaders, in contrast, tend to be more candid, open, communicative, 
and collaborative. As a result, they tend to promote the development and 
application of innovative ideas to solve problems (Toor & Ofori, 2008).

3.1.3.1 Project Management Methodologies  
Project management methodologies are a collection of guiding principles and 
procedures employed to plan, manage, and carry out projects (Project 
Management Methodologies, 2021). Utilizing project methodologies can help 
increase the likelihood of fulfilling project objectives, regardless of the industry or 
project size (Chin, Yap, & Spowage, 2010). The project management methodology 
selected determines how the work within a project is prioritized and completed 
(Project Management Methodologies, 2021). Other than the PMBOK® Guide that 
has been used a lot in this report, some other popular project management 
methodologies are briefly described below (Coursera, 12 Project Management 
Methodologies: Your Guide, 2022):  
 
▪ Waterfall 
The waterfall methodology was first developed for software development 
by Winston W. Royce in 1970 (Coursera, 12 Project Management 
Methodologies: Your Guide, 2022). It is considered a more traditional, linear 
approach to project management and guides a project via a sequence of 
steps or phases. In general, each step or phase must be finished before 
proceeding to the next. The waterfall methodology's logical flow makes it 
==PAGE:23==
 
22 
Zahra Al-Mosawi                Virtual Team Management with Focus on Trust, Motivation & Collaboration 
a great choice for short, predictable projects with a clear vision of the final 
product and set requirements that are unlikely to alter. 
 
▪ Agile 
Agile is a project management approach that focuses on incremental and 
iterative steps to project completion (Coursera, What Is Agile? And When to 
Use It, 2022). Short-term development cycles are used to complete 
incremental elements of a project. Instead of top-down management and 
sticking to a fixed plan, the approach promotes speedy delivery, 
adaptability to change, and collaboration. Agile processes provide 
continuous feedback, allowing team members to respond to challenges as 
they arise and stakeholders to communicate regularly. Although the agile 
approach was initially established for software development, it is now 
widely employed in the execution of several different types of projects and 
the management of various organizations. 
 
▪ Scrum 
Scrum is an Agile methodology dedicated to developing products in an 
environment that is susceptible to change (Coursera, Agile vs. Scrum: 
Which Should You Use, and Why?, 2022). The delivery cycles in Scrum are 
referred to as "sprints" and typically run one to four weeks. The work is 
done incrementally, building on earlier work. Scrum teams are generally 
small, ranging from three to nine persons, including a Scrum master and a 
product owner. Communication with team members and stakeholders is 
continuous, allowing for consistent feedback and adjustments to be made 
as needed. 
 
▪ Kanban 
Kanban is an Agile project management methodology that enables 
workflow visualization in order to increase efficiency (Coursera, 12 Project 
Management Methodologies: Your Guide, 2022). The approach first gained 
traction in the Japanese manufacturing industry before spreading to other 
sectors. In Kanban, a physical or digital tool called Kanban board is used. 
The Kanban board splits the workflow into columns categorized by 
development stage (to-do, in-progress, done). This contributes to the

▪ PRINCE2

▪ Critical Path Method 
The Critical Path Method (CPM) defines the longest sequence of

3.2 Teams 
3.2.1 What is a Team? 
A team is a group of individuals that work on related tasks to accomplish a 
common goal (Lumen, 2021). Teams are employed to complete tasks that are too 
large or complex for a person to accomplish alone or that demand a wide range of 
skills and knowledge (Lumen, 2021). In a team, every member is accountable for 
their contributions, but the group as a whole is accountable for the success of the 
team (Lumen, 2021). In organizations, teams may be temporary or permanent 
(Lumen, 2021). 
When talking about teams, the term teamwork is frequently used. Teamwork is 
defined as a shared commitment to the team's process and the product or result 
that the team aims to achieve (MIT, 2021). Teamwork is generally based on 
common norms and rules, a willingness to contribute and pay attention to the 
overall team process, and a shared responsibility towards the team members as 
individuals but also the team all together (MIT, 2021). When it comes to teamwork, 
a few conditions must be met to ensure that everyone's contributions and the 
team's overall effort will be successful (Lumen, 2021). The characteristics of 
effective and successful teams are as follows: 
▪ Shared values: A shared set of values and principles governing how and 
why team members will collaborate. 
 
▪ Mutual trust: Confidence among team members that each prioritizes the 
team's interests over their individual ones. 
 
▪ Inspiring vision: A well-defined goal that encourages commitment to a 
collective effort.

▪ Skills: The ability to integrate skills and expertise to complete tasks and 
collaborate effectively with others 
 
▪ Rewards: The recognition of the achievements towards the team’s goals. 
These five characteristics may assist individuals in collaborating with others by 
directing their efforts in a common direction and obtaining an outcome that can 
only be achieved through teamwork (Lumen, 2021). 
 
3.2.1.1 Types of Teams 
There are different types of teams including permanent teams, temporary teams, 
task force, committee, organization/work force, self-managed teams, cross-
functional teams, and virtual teams (Management Study Guide, 2021). These are 
briefly described below: 
▪ Permanent teams: Teams that work together on a permanent basis and are 
not disbanded after the task is completed. 
 
▪ Temporary teams: Teams that work together until the task is completed 
and are disbanded afterwards.  
 
▪ Task force: Teams that are formed for a particular purpose such as working 
on a specific project or solving a critical problem. 
 
▪ Committee: Committees are usually created to work on a specific project, 
either permanently or temporarily. People with similar interests, 
backgrounds, and attitudes get together on a single platform to create a 
committee and work on any issue. 
 
▪ Organization/Work force: Teams that are formed in organizations where 
team members work together under the leadership of an expert leader. The 
leader, together with his/her team, work hard to achieve a shared goal. 
 
▪ Self-managed teams: A group of people who work together for a common 
goal but without the guidance of a leader. In this kind of team, each 
individual is responsible for their own performance.

▪ Cross-functional teams: Teams that bring together people from many 
disciplines or fields to solve a problem or achieve a goal. 
 
▪ Virtual Teams: Teams that are made up of people dispersed by distance but 
are linked by a computer or any other digital device. The team members of 
a virtual team communicate online through communication technology. 
 
3.2.2 What is a Virtual Team? 
Virtual teams are growing in popularity as more organizations do business from 
remote locations and as more people telecommute (Ferrazzi, 2014). Organizations 
are increasingly reliant on virtual teams due to a more competitive global market, 
the benefits of merging the work of geographically dispersed specialized 
personnel, and the need to save time and travel expenses (Hantula, D'Arcy, & Kock, 
2004). 
Virtual teams are functioning teams with interdependent members who are 
geographically dispersed, have shared outcomes, and rely on technology-
mediated communication to complete their tasks rather than face-to-face 
interaction (B. Gibson & G. Cohen, 2003). The geographical dispersion of virtual 
team members means they are not co-located, and their primary work locations 
are distinct from one another (B. Gibson & G. Cohen, 2003). They could, for 
example, be located in different buildings, cities, states, countries or even 
continents. The members of a virtual team may belong to the same organization 
or several different organizations. In order to communicate and get their work 
done, virtual teams mainly make use of electronically mediated communication 
(B. Gibson & G. Cohen, 2003). They use different kinds of technologies, such as 
telephone, e-mail, teleconferencing, videoconferencing, and collaborative tools.  
From time to time, virtual teams may meet in person, however, they are generally 
not able to get their work done or effectively coordinate their activities without 
technological support (B. Gibson & G. Cohen, 2003). Co-located teams may also use 
technologies like telephones, e-mail and computers; however, it is the degree of 
reliance on electronic communication that differs between the two types of teams 
(B. Gibson & G. Cohen, 2003). Virtual teams often have no choice but to 
communicate electronically. On the other hand, co-located teams usually have 
more flexibility in terms of deciding whether or when to use technology. 
The degree of virtuality in virtual teams varies from slightly virtual to extremely 
virtual (B. Gibson & G. Cohen, 2003). It is the reliance on electronically mediated 
==PAGE:28==
 
27 
Zahra Al-Mosawi                Virtual Team Management with Focus on Trust, Motivation & Collaboration 
communication and the degree of geographical dispersion that determines the 
level of virtuality of a team. For example, a team that works entirely through e-
mail, text messages, and teleconferencing, never meeting in person, is more 
virtual than the one that meets in person once a month. The same is valid in the 
case of a team that spans countries and time zones compared to one with 
members in the same city. However, increasing virtuality introduces additional 
complexity that must be managed (B. Gibson & G. Cohen, 2003). Because virtual 
teams are infinitely variable, they necessitate management that is based on their 
uniqueness, as well as what is more widely recognized about virtual teams and 
their characteristics (Brewer, 2015). 
There are certain similarities between virtual and face-to-face teams, but there 
are also significant distinctions (Brewer, 2015). Virtual and face-to-face teams 
both have a shared vision of the work or project, they organize activities around 
key capabilities and competencies, collaborate in groups, and process information 
swiftly through systems in real-time (Brewer, 2015). In contrast to co-located 
physical teams, virtual teams typically cross boundaries of location, culture, time, 
organizations, and hierarchies. While physical teams mostly communicate face-
to-face, virtual teams mainly communicate through technology (Brewer, 2015).  
Coordination of tasks in face-to-face teams is straightforward and done by the 
team members collectively; in virtual teams, however, activities need to be 
considerably more structured (Nader Ale, Shamsuddin , & Zahari, 2009). 
 
3.2.2.1 Benefits and Drawbacks of Virtual Teams 
One of the key benefits of virtual teams is that they enable enterprises to pool the 
talents and expertise of their own personnel and those of trading partners and 
consulting firms from various locations to compete in today’s hypercompetitive 
global environment (Greenberg, Greenberg, & Antonucci, 2007). Virtual teams also 
allow for incorporation of employees who work from home offices, including 
individuals with mobility limitations and disabilities (Project Management 
Institute, 2017). Employee flexibility is another benefit of virtual teams  (Hantula, 
D'Arcy, & Kock, 2004). Members of a virtual team often have more flexibility with 
their schedules and are not bound by regular workdays or workplaces. Individuals 
may also be a member of more than one team at the same time and have the 
ability to switch between them quickly (Hantula, D'Arcy, & Kock, 2004). Virtual 
teams, in general, enable organizations to reduce travel expenses and save travel 
time, resulting in financial savings and possibly increased team member

also allow for a continuous workflow during a day (Hantula, D'Arcy, & Kock, 2004). 
While there are major benefits to using virtual teams, there are also a number of 
significant drawbacks. When team members are co-located, they often become 
acquainted with one another both informally and through task-related activities 
(Greenberg, Greenberg, & Antonucci, 2007). By being co-located, managers and

3.3 Trust  
3.3.1 What is Trust? 
To guarantee performance and collaboration among a team, trust is crucial 
(Holton, 2001). Although there are numerous definitions of trust, almost all of them 
have one thing in common: the trusting party must willingly place themselves in 
a position of vulnerability to or risk from the trusted party (Gallivan, 2001). This 
indicates that the trusting party potentially makes themselves vulnerable to 
opportunistic behavior by the trusted party, regardless of their ability to monitor 
==PAGE:33==
 
32 
Zahra Al-Mosawi                Virtual Team Management with Focus on Trust, Motivation & Collaboration 
or control the trusted party's behavior (Gallivan, 2001). Several studies have been 
conducted over the last few decades to examine the nature of trust from a variety 
of perspectives (Zhan & Xiong, 2008). Trusting others appears to be a natural 
human tendency that can change depending on the circumstances. The 
establishment of trust is dynamic and distinct in character at various stages of 
any relationship, and trust will evolve over time (Zhan & Xiong, 2008). 
Trust can be built through regular and meaningful interaction, in which 
individuals develop a sense of security and openness in sharing their insights and 
concerns, in which ideas and assumptions can be challenged without fear of 
repercussion, and in which diversity of opinion is valued over conformity or 
commonality (Holton, 2001). Social communication, including personal 
conversations, caring talk, and storytelling, is a type of discourse that can create 
a supportive environment and foster self-disclosure and emotional sharing 
(Holton, 2001). It can also increase group trust and allow for more critical 
conversations. In a team, trust can be established and enhanced by incorporating 
factors that depend heavily on the interaction of task-related and social 
communications (Meixner, 2018). A lack of trust among team members is a 
concern since it is often related to added cost, resulting in lower team 
effectiveness (Wilson, Straus, & McEvily, 2006). When team members lack trust in 
one another, they are more likely to spend additional time and energy monitoring, 
backing up or duplicating each other's work, as well as recording problems. All 
this may result in decreased productivity among team members (Wilson, Straus, 
& McEvily, 2006). 
Based on the works of Lewis and Weigert (1985), a study by Kanawattanachai and 
Yoo suggests that trust is built upon two basic foundations: cognitive and affective 
trust (Kanawattanachai & Yoo, 2002). Cognitive trust is referred to as the 
calculative and rational attributes demonstrated by the trusted party, including 
reliability, integrity, competence, and responsibility (Kanawattanachai & Yoo, 
2002). People usually evaluate this type of trust depending on a variety of factors 
such as profession and level of familiarity. Trust based on cognition is more 
dependent on information and develops through communication between 
individuals. In contrast to cognitive trust, affective trust refers to the trusted 
party's emotional and social abilities, such as caring, benevolence, and emotional 
connection to others (Kanawattanachai & Yoo, 2002).  Although affective trust is 
generally associated with close social relationships, McAllister (1995) discovered 
that it also has an effect on team performance and well-being in the sense that 
one takes someone else's problem as his own and is willing to lend a helping hand 
==PAGE:34==
 
33 
Zahra Al-Mosawi                Virtual Team Management with Focus on Trust, Motivation & Collaboration 
to the one in need even if they did not ask for help (McAllister, 1995). Previous 
research found that the importance of cognition- and affect-based trust varied 
depending on the type of social connection, circumstance, and the considered 
system (Lewis & Weigert, 1985). For instance, the affective trust would be higher 
than cognitive trust in close social relationships such as those of couples and 
family members. In contrast, the cognitive trust would be more important in a 
group that is less familiar with each other, such as a workgroup. 
 
3.3.2 Trust & Leadership 
Fostering a trusting environment is one of a leader's most important 
responsibilities (Fairholm & Fairholm, 2000). While it is crucial for the team 
members to trust each other, it is also crucial for them to trust their leader. The 
trust that leaders place in those they manage enables both themselves and their 
team members to thrive (Mineo, 2014). Leaders who communicate openly and 
honestly while adhering to the organization's core values most likely build group 
trust (Fairholm & Fairholm, 2000). Higher degrees of communication openness 
and transparency have, among others, been associated with improved 
relationships between leaders and their team members, increased team 
motivation, role clarity, more favorable peer relationships, and trust (Norman, 
Avolio, & Luthans, 2010). A leader's ability to be transparent is measured by how 
open and clear they are in their actions towards others, by sharing the needed 
information for decision making, being accepting towards others' input, as well 
as, revealing their personal values, motives, and sentiments in a way that enables 
followers to more accurately assess the leader's competence and morality in the 
context of their actions (Norman, Avolio, & Luthans, 2010). 
 
3.3.3 Trust in Virtual Teams 
People generally trust those who perform in a reliable and competent manner 
while also showing concern for the well-being of others (Greenberg, Greenberg, & 
Antonucci, 2007). Being a part of a team entails a certain level of risk, as members 
rely on one another to successfully complete the team's common task (Greenberg, 
Greenberg, & Antonucci, 2007). If one of the team members fails to keep promises 
or attempts to take advantage of the others, the team's overall workload may 
increase, and the performance may suffer (Greenberg, Greenberg, & Antonucci, 
2007). The fact that members in virtual teams do not have to face one another 
makes them more vulnerable to lack of commitment and taking advantage of 
==PAGE:35==
 
34 
Zahra Al-Mosawi                Virtual Team Management with Focus on Trust, Motivation & Collaboration 
each other than co-located ones (Greenberg, Greenberg, & Antonucci, 2007). 
Zaccaro and Bader (2003) argue that developing and maintaining trust is one of 
the most critical criteria in contributing to the success of virtual teams (Zaccaro 
& Bader, 2003). According to research, there are two interconnected factors that 
make building trust in virtual teams more difficult than in typical co-located 
teams (Greenberg, Greenberg, & Antonucci, 2007). These factors are various 
locations and technology-based communication. For example, members of 
traditional teams have the opportunity to create relationships easily through face-
to-face interactions and have their reactions conveyed both through verbal and 
non-verbal interactions. During these interactions, both social connections and 
professional respect that leads to trust can be formed (Greenberg, Greenberg, & 
Antonucci, 2007). People who work in different locations can have different 
working contexts and situations, which can cause problems, misinterpretations, 
and misunderstandings (Greenberg, Greenberg, & Antonucci, 2007). Forming 
relationships of cohesion that can lead to affective trust can be more challenging 
when team members are dispersed (Zhan & Xiong, 2008). This is because there are 
no conversations, for example, over coffee, at lunch, or after a meeting, that help 
teams create a shared identity and group norms (Greenberg, Greenberg, & 
Antonucci, 2007). Furthermore, because the team is only visible electronically, 
members may struggle to experience a sense of belonging (Greenberg, Greenberg, 
& Antonucci, 2007). Despite the use of videoconferencing, the social aspects of 
working together remotely are not implemented in the same way as when co-
located, making the establishment of a professional relationship more difficult 
(Greenberg, Greenberg, & Antonucci, 2007). The utilization of technology also 
limits communication among virtual team members (Greenberg, Greenberg, & 
Antonucci, 2007). Face-to-face communication allows for a greater range of 
emotions and reactions than technology-based communication, meaning that 
managers and team members do not have as many visible indicators to express 
behavior and attitude (Greenberg, Greenberg, & Antonucci, 2007). This also means 
that more straightforward communication is required in virtual teams, as team 
members cannot see each other's eyes rolling, heads nodding in agreement, or 
heads shaking in disagreement (Greenberg, Greenberg, & Antonucci, 2007). What 
constitutes a proper written response to substitute body language may be 
unknown to team members, and it may vary geographically (Greenberg, 
Greenberg, & Antonucci, 2007). These factors underline the importance of 
concentrating on communication content and patterns. Therefore, it is essential 
that team managers actively encourage social communication along with task-

3.3.3.1 Swift Trust  
On some occasions, there is not always enough time to form a trusting 
relationship, such as when a group of individuals are brought together and are 
required to start working right away. In such cases, a different type of trust called 
swift trust may be necessary. The term "swift trust" refers to a presumptive type 
of trust and was first used by Meyerson and his colleagues (1996) to explain the 
trusting behavior displayed by members of new temporary teams (Meyerson, 
Weick, & Kramer, 1996). In these teams, members who had never worked together 
before were able to build trust instantly and started behaving in a trusting manner 
(Robert, Denis, & Hung, 2009). Since these individuals did not have any prior track 
record, it was suggested that they brought trust with them from other past 
situations and experiences (Robert, Denis, & Hung, 2009). Based on the study of 
Kramer (1999), Robert, Denis, and Hung (2009) believe that five bases of trust are 
what individuals bring from other contexts to impact their development of swift 
trust. These five types of trust include role-based trust, rule-based trust, third-
party recommendation-based trust, dispositional-based trust, and category-based 
trust (Robert, Denis, & Hung, 2009). Role-based trust is based on heuristics 
regarding the roles of the other team members without knowing many personal 
facts about them (Robert, Denis, & Hung, 2009). Rule-based trust is built on 
elements like situational normality, organizational and team structures, as well 
as explicit and implicit awareness of transaction norms, interaction routines, and 
exchange practices (Robert, Denis, & Hung, 2009). Third parties can also contribute 
to the development of trust by enabling team members to transfer their trust from 
well-established relationships to people about whom they lack sufficient 
knowledge to build their own judgements (Robert, Denis, & Hung, 2009). A person's 
general tendency to trust or distrust others is referred to as dispositional-based 
trust (Robert, Denis, & Hung, 2009). It is usually formed through past personal 
experiences with fulfilled and unfulfilled promises, as well as firsthand 
observation of people's behavior. Dispositional trust is suggested to have a higher 
impact on an individual's trust belief prior to the availability of relevant 
knowledge about a team member's past behavior (Robert, Denis, & Hung, 2009). 
Finally, category-based trust is built on information about a trustee's membership 
==PAGE:37==
 
36 
Zahra Al-Mosawi                Virtual Team Management with Focus on Trust, Motivation & Collaboration 
in a social or organizational category, which might unknowingly impact others' 
trustworthiness assessments (Kramer, 1999). 
A significant advantage of swift trust in virtual teams is that it can be utilized to 
foster trust among team members when there is limited time to create trust or 
when team members have limited interactions with one another (Kwaye, 2018). 
However, as swift trust is developed quickly, it is also fragile and easily destroyed 
(Gallivan, 2001). 
 
3.4 Motivation 
3.4.1 What is Motivation? 
Motivation refers to the desires or needs that drive conduct towards a specific 
goal (Lumen 2, 2021). It is an impulse to act or behave in a manner that fulfils 
particular conditions, such as wishes, desires, or goals. Earlier theories of 
motivation suggested that human motivation was guided by rational thought and 
reason (Lumen 2, 2021). However, psychologists today believe that motivation 
may be rooted in basic impulses to maximize well-being and pleasure and 
minimize physical pain (Lumen 2, 2021). 
Motivations are often divided into drives and motives (Lumen 2, 2021). Drives 
mainly refer to biological factors, such as thirst, hunger, and sleep, that direct us 
to seek out and perform certain activities. It is thought that drives originate within 
an individual and may not necessitate external cues to encourage action (Lumen 
2, 2021). On the other hand, motives are driven mainly by social and psychological 
factors, like work, family, and relationships. Unpleasant or aversive situations or 
events, for example, can stimulate motivation, while attractions to positive or 
pleasurable situations or events can do the same (Lumen 2, 2021). 
There are two types of motivation; intrinsic motivation that develops from 
internal factors and extrinsic motivation that develops from external factors 
(Hennessey, Moran, Altringer, & Amabile, 2015). Intrinsically motivated actions 
are carried out because they provide a sense of personal fulfillment and are done 
for the sake of the enjoyment of the activity itself (Hennessey, Moran, Altringer, & 
Amabile, 2015). On the other hand, extrinsically driven actions are carried out to 
achieve some external purpose or meet a constraint that is imposed by an 
external factor (Hennessey, Moran, Altringer, & Amabile, 2015). Examples of that 
could be to earn a reward or avoid punishment. In real life, motivations are usually 
a combination of intrinsic and extrinsic factors that may vary over time (Lumen 
==PAGE:38==
 
37 
Zahra Al-Mosawi                Virtual Team Management with Focus on Trust, Motivation & Collaboration 
2, 2021). However, in some cases where intrinsic motivation is combined with 
extrinsic motivation, the intrinsic motivators can be suppressed (Hennessey, 
Moran, Altringer, & Amabile, 2015). This phenomenon is known as the 
overjustification effect and might result in the loss of the intrinsic motivators and 
a reliance on external rewards to maintain performance (Hennessey, Moran, 
Altringer, & Amabile, 2015). 
 
3.4.2 Motivation at Work 
Work motivation is the process by which goal-directed performance is initiated 
and sustained (Clark, 2003). Work motivation fuels the mental work required to 
put our knowledge and skills to use. It precludes or nudges us to transform 
intention into action and initiate or restart a previously undertaken task. It also 
influences our decisions to persist with a certain work-related goal in the face of 
distractions and competing agendas (Clark, 2003). Furthermore, work motivation 
drives us to expend more or less cognitive effort to improve our work 
performance's quality and quantity. It is necessary to keep in mind that 
motivation does not directly impact work performance. On the other hand, 
motivation drives us to effectively apply our knowledge and abilities to work 
tasks (Clark, 2003).  
The majority of employees are in need of motivation in order to feel good about 
their work and perform at their best (Ganta, 2014). While some employees are 
driven by money, others are motivated by recognition and rewards or by personal 
goals and accomplishments (Ganta, 2014). Employees who are motivated and 
enthusiastic about their work perform to the best of their abilities and contribute 
to the organization's overall productivity (Ganta, 2014). 
According to Hackman and Oldham's (1976) job enrichment model, a job can be 
made more motivating by increasing the following (Ganta, 2014): 
 
▪ Skill variety: The variety of skills needed for the job. 
 
▪ Task identity: The extent to which the job generates something 
meaningful. 
 
▪ Task significance: The importance of the work included in the job. 
 
==PAGE:39==
 
38 
Zahra Al-Mosawi                Virtual Team Management with Focus on Trust, Motivation & Collaboration 
▪ Autonomy: The degree to which the employee has the freedom and control 
over how the task is performed. 
 
▪ Feedback: The extent to which the employee receives continuous feedback. 
 
Another aspect that can increase motivation and empowerment among 
employees is the opportunity to develop professionally and also use individual 
strengths and knowledge (Peterson, 2007).  
 
3.4.3 Motivation & Leadership 
Motivation is a critical element of effective leadership (Schaffer, 2008). Without 
the ability to motivate followers, it is unlikely that leaders of organizations can be 
successful. A project manager, for instance, may have the required skills to lead a 
project team through several project stages and life cycles, but team motivation, 
which is much less tangible, is crucial for the project to be successful (Schmid, 
2008). The leadership and management styles that leaders utilize can be inclusive 
or exclusive and are assumed to influence the intrinsic or extrinsic sources of 
team motivation (Schmid, 2008). Peters and Waterman (1983) discovered that 
what differentiated large successful organizations from others were that they 
focused on the individual employee and the development of intrinsic motivation 
(Peters & Waterman, 1983). Since it is not always possible to directly control 
people's motivation in their work, a leader may facilitate that process by fostering 
an environment that encourages employees to become more intrinsically 
motivated (Schmid, 2008). Employees or team members who are intrinsically 
motivated will most likely do what is required to make the project a success since 
the project's outcomes provide them with personal satisfaction (Schmid, 2008). To 
create an environment in which most project participants are motivated, project 
managers must exercise caution throughout the early stages of a project (Schmid, 
2008). Having clear and open communication at the early stages of a project is 
believed to be the key to developing and maintaining high motivation throughout 
the whole project (Schmid, 2008). In addition, it is important for project managers 
to be aware of the team members' individuality. By knowing what motivates each 
team member, the project manager will be able to connect them to suitable 
environments, tasks, responsibilities, and objectives that promote personal 
motivation (Peterson, 2007). 
==PAGE:40==
 
39 
Zahra Al-Mosawi                Virtual Team Management with Focus on Trust, Motivation & Collaboration 
Since employees may be motivated both by intrinsic and extrinsic factors, it is 
crucial for managers to stimulate these to increase and sustain the motivation 
among their team members (Schmid, 2008). This can be done through consistent 
team recognition of accomplishments (intrinsic) and by providing rewards for 
outstanding work (extrinsic) (Ganta, 2014). 
In brief, fostering team motivation and high team performance requires a 
manager to use a range of interpersonal skills, such as good communication, 
training others, decision-making, leading by example, and establishing a positive 
and motivating environment through an understanding of and association with 
essential elements of motivation (Peterson, 2007). 
 
3.4.4 Motivation in Virtual Teams 
Regardless of whether a team is co-located or virtual, the team manager must 
focus on motivation as it is a vital component of every high-performance team 
(Levin & Rad, 2006). A virtual team's sophistication is generally assessed by how 
well it meets the client's expectations in terms of scope, quality, cost, and time 
(Levin & Rad, 2006). Nevertheless, the team's success in accomplishing these 
project outcomes is dependent on how well the team members connect with each 
other and the project as a whole (Levin & Rad, 2006). To ensure project success, 
project managers must, therefore, utilize various motivational approaches 
tailored for each team member, even in virtual environments. Virtual teams 
typically have less motivators for performance than traditional face-to-face 
teams (Nunamaker Jr, Reinig, & Briggs, 2009). For instance, virtual teams may 
generate less social comparison among team members due to fewer nonverbal 
cues, making it more difficult for team members' enthusiasm to inspire and 
motivate others. However, studies have shown that motivation in virtual teams 
can be increased through regular communication and feedback (Geister, Konradt, 
& Hertel, 2006). 
 
3.5 Collaboration 
3.5.1 What is Collaboration? 
Cambridge Dictionary describes collaboration as "the situation of two or more 
people working together to create or achieve the same thing" and as "the act of 
working together with other people or organizations to create or achieve 
something" (Cambridge Dictionary, 2021). Collaboration begins with a group's 
==PAGE:41==
 
40 
Zahra Al-Mosawi                Virtual Team Management with Focus on Trust, Motivation & Collaboration 
ability for in-depth communication and the potential to establish trust and open 
communication (Holton, 2001). It is widely acknowledged as a core competency 
of a learning organization. As the foundation for effective organizational team 
building, collaborative learning demands a degree of personal familiarity, 
closeness, and trust that allows team members to listen to and understand one 
another (Holton, 2001). When working in collaborative teams, we typically achieve 
creativity and innovation via the respectful support and consideration of diversity 
(Holton, 2001). Once a team develops the ability to recognize, accept, and utilize its 
diversity, it can anticipate achieving the optimal interdependence attributes of a 
high performing team (Holton, 2001). 
The basis of collaboration is that all team members see the value of working 
together, accountability and comprehending the collaboration culture (Hao, 2012). 
For that to happen, a team must commit to a shared goal that is clear for everyone 
involved. Other critical factors to foster collaboration are the team members’ 
combined skills and competencies (Lumen, 2021), as well as the knowledge 
sharing that occurs between them, which all together contribute to the shared 
meaning and purpose of their work (Malhotra, Arvind; Majchrzak, Ann, 2004). 
 
3.5.2 Collaboration & Leadership 
One of the primary responsibilities of a leader is to manage collaboration activities 
(Hao, 2012). When leaders encourage supportive social relationships, display 
collaborative behavior, and emphasize the importance of interaction between 
employees, leaders and other colleagues, teams are most likely to perform well 
(Gratton & Erickson, 2007). According to Gratton and Erickson (2007), clear roles 
and responsibilities and less formal processes are essential to increase 
collaborative behavior among team members. Furthermore, they suggest that 
strengthening an organization's capacity for collaboration necessitates a variety 
of long-term investments in developing relationships and trust, in cultivating a 
culture in which leaders serve as role models of collaboration, and well-thought 
short-term decisions about team formation, role definition, and communication 
of challenges and tasks (Gratton & Erickson, 2007). As a result of a study that 
Gratton and Erickson conducted, they also found that the most effective and 
innovative teams were generally led by both task- and relationship-oriented 
leaders, especially those who adapted their leadership style during the project 
depending on the situation (Gratton & Erickson, 2007).

41 
Zahra Al-Mosawi                Virtual Team Management with Focus on Trust, Motivation & Collaboration 
Keeping team members involved and engaged in collaborative activities will 
most likely improve the overall efficiency (Team Collaboration, 2021). Leaders can 
achieve that by identifying and maximizing the strengths of each team member, 
as each individual contributes something useful to the project. 
 
3.5.3 Collaboration in Virtual Teams 
As we continue our journey to a global, knowledge-based economy, fostering 
effective collaboration becomes crucial (Holton, 2001). In a virtual environment, 
the challenge is to provide ways and opportunities for team members to have the 
degree and depth of communication required to create a shared future (Holton, 
2001). Notable is the importance of allocating appropriate time to strategies and 
processes for fruitful interactions, and creation of shared purpose, and a 
commitment to a collaborative culture (Holton, 2001). The choice and effective use 
of communication technologies specifically aimed at facilitating the initiation 
and development of meaningful communication is a starting point in introducing 
virtual team members to the level of genuine conversations and deep dialogue 
required for establishing trust and effective collaboration (Holton, 2001).

6 DISCUSSION 
This chapter discusses the results while also linking them to the knowledge 
obtained through the literature and case study. 
 
6.1 Team Management During COVID-19 
According to the results, it appears that all project managers who responded to 
the questionnaire have managed their teams both virtually and face-to-face 
during the COVID-19 pandemic 2020/2021. That could be due to the nature of 
construction projects where some roles have to be on-site for things to happen, 
while others can usually complete their tasks from any other location. Since 
project managers are responsible for the project as a whole, they fall somewhere 
in the middle of these two groups of their teams. They have to ensure that all team 
members, regardless of their location, walk in the same direction and that the 
work is coordinated and performed in an effective manner. According to the 
questionnaire, it is apparent that project managers have not always been able to 
meet their team members all at once. An example of a likely scenario could be 
that when a project manager visits the project site office, they might only meet 
the construction manager and supervisor because the rest of the team is working 
from home or any other location. While this situation may be easier for the project 
manager than managing a fully dispersed team from a leadership perspective, 
maintaining consistent contact with each team member may still be challenging. 
This may not only be a challenge for the project managers but also for the team 
members themselves by them not having the same contact with each other. 
Although scheduling regular face-to-face meetings to which all team members 
are invited is an excellent solution to this problem, it may not be applicable for all 
teams, especially in a pandemic where employees are recommended to work 
from home as much as possible or to have a limited number of attendees in 
physical meetings. While the latter may not be an issue for smaller teams, it may 
be an issue for larger teams, such as those with more than ten members. However, 
based on the results, it is clear that the respondents met their teams in person 
weekly or monthly, regardless of the team size. 
When it comes to the technology utilized when working remotely, the results 
show that all respondents used e-mail, telephone, and meeting and 
videoconferencing tools. This could be due to the familiarity and convenience of 
use of the first two mentioned, as well as the fact that Skanska encouraged its 
==PAGE:71==
 
70 
Zahra Al-Mosawi                Virtual Team Management with Focus on Trust, Motivation & Collaboration 
employees to use digital meeting platforms such as Microsoft Teams even though 
many were not acquainted with them since before. If the project managers' 
leadership styles were based on in-person communications and personal 
interactions prior to the pandemic, the new way of managing virtual or hybrid 
teams would necessitate managers learning and employing new communication 
technologies and management practices in order to maintain the leadership and 
team culture. 
 
6.2 Challenges when Managing a Team from a Distance 
The questionnaire results indicate that the challenges encountered by the project 
managers when managing virtual teams are primarily related to communication, 
leadership, performance, involvement, and issue identification and monitoring. 
These challenges seem to have arisen as a result of team members being 
dispersed across space for most of the time and being pushed into a new manner 
of working in a short period of time. Both factors have been significant changes 
for most project managers and teams at Skanska, who typically would work from 
the same location. When previous research on trust, motivation, and collaboration 
in virtual teams and teams in general, is examined, it becomes clear that most 
areas in which Skanska’s project managers have struggled have a significant 
impact on how trust, motivation, and collaboration are established, sustained, and 
enhanced. Here are some examples taken from the theoretical framework of this 
study indicating that: 
▪ Higher degrees of communication openness and transparency have, 
among others, been associated with improved relationships between 
leaders and their team members, increased team motivation, role clarity, 
more favorable peer relationships, and trust (Norman, Avolio, & Luthans, 
2010).  
 
▪ The majority of employees are in need of motivation in order to feel good 
about their work and perform at their best (Ganta, 2014).  
 
▪ Fostering team motivation and high team performance requires a manager 
to use a range of interpersonal skills, such as good communication, training 
others, decision-making, leading by example, and establishing a positive 
and motivating environment through an understanding of and association 
with essential elements of motivation (Peterson, 2007). 
==PAGE:72==
 
71 
Zahra Al-Mosawi                Virtual Team Management with Focus on Trust, Motivation & Collaboration 
▪ Studies have shown that motivation in virtual teams can be increased 
through regular communication and feedback (Geister, Konradt, & Hertel, 
2006). 
 
▪ Collaboration begins with a group's ability for in-depth communication and 
the potential to establish trust and open communication (Holton, 2001).  
 
6.3 Trust 
The results indicate that most respondents agree that building trust in a virtual 
team is more challenging than a traditional team. However, research suggests 
that trust is the most critical criteria in contributing to the success of virtual teams 
(Zaccaro & Bader, 2003). This means that managers of virtual teams need to pay 
special attention to aspects that contribute to the development of a trusting 
environment among their teams to succeed. 
In order to maintain a trusting environment, project managers at Skanska have 
had regular and continuous contact with their teams during the pandemic. This 
has been done, among others, through phone calls, follow-up meetings, and 
regularly visiting the project site office. Phone calls and virtual meetings may not 
provide the same opportunity to interact with others and create or maintain a 
trusting relationship between managers and their teams or amongst team 
members as if they were all co-located. However, combining phone calls and 
virtual meetings with weekly or monthly physical meetings may allow the team 
to interact and pick up what they have missed when working remotely. 
Two of the respondents had some interesting responses to how they have 
maintained trust among their teams while working virtually. One of them noted 
that they had previously known their teammates and had created trust and 
confidence in them prior to working virtually. After working together for some 
time and continuing to deliver and perform effectively, they have been able to 
confirm their trust and, that way, have been able to maintain it. This statement is 
in line with Greenberg, Greenberg and Antonucci's (2007) suggestion that people 
typically trust those who perform reliably and competently. This also presents the 
idea that building a trusting relationship before working remotely may facilitate 
the maintenance of trust in a virtual environment. The other respondent 
mentioned that they maintained trust partially by establishing clear roles and 
responsibilities. While there is not much mention about it in the reviewed 
literature on trust in teams, it is an interesting point. It seems to be linked to 
==PAGE:73==
 
72 
Zahra Al-Mosawi                Virtual Team Management with Focus on Trust, Motivation & Collaboration 
cognitive trust, which is usually evaluated based on factors such as profession 
and level of familiarity (Kanawattanachai & Yoo, 2002). In this case, it would imply 
that team members develop trust in one another based on the roles and 
responsibilities that each of them has. 
Most project managers appear to agree that social communication, team 
members' reputations, and transparent leadership are critical components in 
establishing trust in virtual teams. Previous research used in the theoretical 
framework confirms these assertions as follows: 
▪ Social communication 
Social communication, including personal conversations, caring talk, and 
storytelling, is a type of discourse that can create a supportive environment 
and foster self-disclosure and emotional sharing (Holton, 2001). It can also 
increase group trust and allow for more critical conversations. 
 
▪ Team members’ reputation 
Third parties can also contribute to the development of trust by enabling 
team members to transfer their trust from well-established relationships to 
people about whom they lack sufficient knowledge to build their own 
judgements (Robert, Denis, & Hung, 2009). 
 
▪ Transparent leadership 
Leaders who communicate openly and honestly while adhering to the 
organization's core values most likely build group trust (Fairholm & 
Fairholm, 2000). Higher degrees of communication openness and 
transparency have, among others, been associated with improved 
relationships between leaders and their team members, increased team 
motivation, role clarity, more favorable peer relationships, and trust 
(Norman, Avolio, & Luthans, 2010). 
 
6.4 Motivation 
The respondents had slightly different views on whether establishing motivation 
was more challenging in virtual teams or traditional ones. However, most of them 
agree that it is more challenging to create a motivating environment virtually. 
This could be because, as Nunamaker Jr, Reinig, & Briggs (2009) mention in their 
study, virtual teams typically have fewer motivators for performance than 
traditional face-to-face teams.

73

Skanska's project managers have taken different actions to ensure a motivating 
environment for their team members during the pandemic. Examples of these 
actions have been meeting their teams regularly, setting milestones, having daily 
follow-ups, 
sharing 
important 
information, 
having 
clear 
roles 
and 
responsibilities, and having fun tasks. Nevertheless, it is difficult to tell whether 
these actions have been able to keep every team member motivated, especially in 
a virtual setting. Work motivation varies from individual to individual, making it 
a complex topic. Therefore, Levin and Rad (2006) suggest that team leaders utilize 
various motivational approaches tailored for each team member to ensure high 
performance. Instead of making assumptions about the preferred motivational 
strategy, it may be a good idea if project managers consult with the team members 
involved.  
One responder noted that they had the opportunity to work with individuals who 
have a great drive to deliver in their positions and were, that way, motivated 
themselves. This is an excellent illustration of how team members may transmit 
motivation among each other and be inspired. However, this instance may be 
more difficult in other virtual teams. This is because virtual team members may 
elicit fewer social comparisons due to fewer non-verbal cues, making it more 
difficult for team members to inspire and motivate each other with their 
enthusiasm (Nunamaker Jr, Reinig, & Briggs, 2009). 
Project managers seem to agree that open communication, leadership style, 
recognition and appreciation, and a positive work environment are key factors 
that influence motivation in virtual teams. Geister, Konradt and Hertel (2006) 
confirm that motivation in virtual teams can be increased through regular 
communication and feedback. On the other hand, Schmid (2008) means that 
leaders' leadership and management styles are assumed to influence the intrinsic 
or extrinsic sources of team motivation. Ganta (2004) suggests that employees’ 
intrinsic motivation can be increased through consistent team recognition for 
accomplishments. Lastly, Peterson (2007) means that a positive environment can 
help foster team motivation. 
It is noteworthy that although most respondents agree that flexible work 
schedules could be a strong motivating factor, there are still a few who seem to 
disagree with this. This suggests that flexible working is not for everyone, which 
is also reflected in the responses to the question whether the project managers 
want to work virtually more often or not. In this case, it appears to be a good idea 
that Skanska wants to provide that opportunity to employees who wish to work 
part of their working hours remotely.

6.5 Collaboration 
The results show that almost all respondents consider the establishment of a 
collaborative environment to be more challenging in virtual teams than a 
traditional teams. Holton (2001) points out that the challenge in a virtual 
environment is to provide ways and opportunities for team members to achieve 
the level and depth of communication needed to create a shared future. 
The project managers at Skanska have been able to sustain a culture of 
collaboration within their teams through regular meetings (both digital and face-
to-face), phone calls, collaborative tools, a frequent presence at the office, and a 
willingness to collaborate. Holton (2001) highlights the importance of 
communication in fostering a collaborative environment by stating that 
collaboration begins with a group's ability for in-depth communication and the 
potential to build trust and open communication. 
The findings suggest that clear and shared goals, trust, great communication, 
great leadership, defined roles and responsibilities, skills and competence, and 
engagement and involvement are critical to creating a collaborative virtual team 
environment. This goes in line with the literature reviewed as follows: 
Holton (2001) emphasizes the essence of devoting adequate time to strategies and 
processes for fruitful interactions, creating a common purpose, and committing 
to a collaborative culture. Furthermore, Holton explains that a collaborative 
organization requires a certain level of personal familiarity, closeness, and trust 
that allows team members to listen to and understand each other. Lumen (2021), 
on the other hand, explains that another crucial factor for teamwork is the 
combined skills and competences. Malhotra and Majchrzak (2004) add that the 
sharing of knowledge among team members contributes to a common goal and 
thus would help to create a collaborative environment. Finally, it is possible to 
improve the overall efficiency of a team by involving and engaging the members 
in collaborative activities (Team Collaboration, 2021). 
The results show that diversity is the least rated by the project managers. Holton 
(2001) disagrees, suggesting that when working in collaborative teams, we usually 
achieve creativity and innovation through respectful support and consideration 
of diversity.  
Another point worth noting is that the project managers had different opinions 
about how important collaborative tools are for virtual teamwork. A few 
==PAGE:76==
 
75 
Zahra Al-Mosawi                Virtual Team Management with Focus on Trust, Motivation & Collaboration 
assumptions on this would be that collaborative tools were not used to the same 
extent by the different teams, that some team members may have lacked 
knowledge and experience in using the tools, and that the right tools may not 
have been used. According to Holton (2001), selecting and effectively utilizing 
communications technologies geared toward facilitating the initiation and 
development of meaningful communication is a good place to start when 
introducing virtual team members to the level of genuine conversations and deep 
dialogue required for trust and effective collaboration. 
 
6.6 Critical Factors for Virtual Project Success 
According to the project managers, trust, clear roles and responsibilities, frequent 
communication, and collaboration seem to be crucial factors for the success of 
virtual projects. This is followed by motivation and leadership competence. While 
there were differing opinions on how critical each factor is to the success of a 
virtual project, all project managers seem to agree that the ability and willingness 
to collaborate with others is a very important component of a successful virtual 
project. The results confirm that there are a number of human factors to consider 
in order to achieve a successful virtual project. However, many of these factors

7 SUGGESTIONS 
This chapter presents suggestions on how trust, motivation, and collaboration 
can be developed, sustained, and enhanced in a virtual team environment from a 
project manager’s perspective. 
 
7.1 Suggestions for Project Managers & Team Leaders 
Based on the literature review, suggestions, and lessons learned by the project 
managers, this section of the report will recommend ways to build, maintain, and 
enhance trust, motivation, and collaboration in a virtual or hybrid project team 
environment. Currently, it appears that Skanska employees will continue to work 
in a virtual setting, at least to some extent in the future, even after the pandemic. 
Although most employees were probably trained to work remotely during the 
pandemic and have become accustomed to it, it is important to remember that 
this way of working was forced by the pandemic and was not free-will for many. 
Even though many may have realized that this way of working suits them better, 
there will always be employees who prefer the "old" way of working, depending 
on their roles and personal preferences. However, both parties should accept each 
other's preferred way of working and be willing to collaborate. The nature of 
construction projects does not make it possible to work entirely virtually; some 
roles still need to be at the site office, ensuring that things are happening there 
and that they are happening correctly. In the future, it will likely depend on 
whether office staff at Skanska choose to work from home a few days a week or 
if they choose to be physically present in the office as they were before the 
pandemic. The following suggestions will, therefore, be primarily for project 
managers who will partially manage their project teams remotely and have team 
members who will work remotely. 
 
7.1.1 Allow Team Members to Choose How They Want to Work  
While many seem to like working virtually and flexibly for several reasons, it is 
crucial to be aware that this might not be the case for everyone. Therefore, 
managers should allow team members to choose how they want to work as much 
as possible, as long as it is appropriate for the project and adheres to Skanska's 
governance system. As a result, team members are more likely to find the work 
style that best suits them, feel motivated and be productive. 
 
==PAGE:78==
 
77 
Zahra Al-Mosawi                Virtual Team Management with Focus on Trust, Motivation & Collaboration 
7.1.2 Adapt Leadership Style 
Managing virtual or hybrid teams can be different from managing traditional co-
located teams. Therefore, managers and leaders need to be aware of this and 
adapt their management and leadership style according to their team's situation 
and needs. Doing that may facilitate the process of leading, motivating, and 
directing project teams in achieving the set objectives for the project. 
 
7.1.3 Decide Which Meetings Should be Held Online and In-Person 
Some meetings can, with an advantage, be held digitally. While other meetings 
would work much better if held in person. Getting the team members involved in 
deciding which meetings should be virtual and which ones should be face-to-face 
should create a sense of trust and involvement. By having a transparent and open 
leadership, involving team members in decisions that would impact their work 
and allowing shared decision-making, team members would most likely feel as if 
their leader values their inputs and is willing to do what is best for the team, which 
leads them to trust their leader. 
The team's efficiency is something to keep in mind when deciding where the 
different meetings should take place. Skanska suggests that it may be more 
efficient to hold digital meetings or perform certain individual tasks remotely, 
whereas joint work in the office is likely to be more efficient. 
 
7.1.4 Hold Regular Team Meetings 
Teams with members working from different locations may find it more difficult 
to meet up all at one than co-located teams. Therefore, regular team meetings 
become necessary to ensure that everyone is up to date on their tasks and that 
misunderstandings are minimized. Meeting up regularly as a team makes it 
possible to keep the confusion to a minimum while also ensuring that the team 
members are on the same page sharing the same goals. Additionally, it would help 
maintain the collaborative culture. 
 
7.1.5 Communicate & Encourage Communication 
Managers should be able to recognize that communication approaches that have 
proven effective in a face-to-face environment must be adapted or improved to 
satisfy the communication needs of virtual or hybrid personnel. To show respect 
for the team members and responsiveness to their needs, it is important to 
==PAGE:79==
 
78 
Zahra Al-Mosawi                Virtual Team Management with Focus on Trust, Motivation & Collaboration 
communicate in a timely manner. This means that the managers should be 
present for their team members when they need. 
 
Employees who work away from the office often tend to feel excluded from the 
communication flow. If the manager does not actively communicate and 
encourage communication, misunderstandings and confusion are likely to occur. 
Communicating frequently and openly with the team members will not only 
show that they are not forgotten but also provide them with an opportunity to 
establish or maintain trusting relationships with the manager and the other 
members. Research suggests that social communication can help increase group 
trust. As a result, it may be beneficial to encourage team members to engage in 
informal conversations and share their feelings when possible. Another crucial 
point is to communicate clearly and ensure that the team members understand 
the message. 
 
7.1.6 Ensure Clear Roles & Responsibilities 
To increase the collaborative behavior among team members, it is essential for 
them to have clear roles and responsibilities. It can be useful to delegate tasks 
based on the team members' skills, competences, development opportunities, and 
personal motivations. That way, team members can use their skills and 
competences while also feeling motivated and developing professionally. 
 
7.1.7 Schedule Individual Meetings with Each Team Member 
From time to time, it might be beneficial to set up individual meetings with each 
team member. That way, they can ask questions, talk about their job, open up 
about problems, and get the support they need. At the same time, managers can 
use this time to get feedback and perhaps also ask about the team member’s 
motivations and similar topics. This would give team members the impression 
that they are being heard and supported by the manager, and the manager would 
receive feedback and input that would assist them in adapting their leadership 
style and leading their team more effectively. 
 
7.1.8 Provide Appropriate Communication & Collaboration Tools 
When working virtually, it is important to select appropriate communication and 
collaboration tools in order for the team to perform their work effectively. Thus, 
managers need to ensure that the right tools are made available to the team 
==PAGE:80==
 
79 
Zahra Al-Mosawi                Virtual Team Management with Focus on Trust, Motivation & Collaboration 
members and that team members are educated and trained to use them. If the 
team members use the right tools for collaboration and communication 
effectively, it will likely be reflected in their performance and relationships with 
one another. 
A project management forum where management and leadership-related 
activities can be handled is another tool that can be useful in managing remotely 
and leading virtual team members. If the forum is accessible to a certain level for 
team members, it will also allow for open leadership, which will likely lead to trust 
in the leader. 
 
7.1.9 Give Feedback  
In a traditional face-to-face office environment, much of the feedback happens 
organically and informally, for example, at the employees' desks or after a 
meeting. However, that may not be the case in a virtual setting, although it is still 
necessary. Therefore, managers need to find ways to provide feedback even when 
working virtually. By doing that, the team motivation is likely to increase or at 
least be sustained. 
 
7.1.10 Recognize and Celebrate Accomplishments  
One of the motivating factors for many is to be recognized for their 
accomplishments. Recognition and celebration of the team's accomplishments is 
likely to lead to increased motivation and performance by team members as they 
realize that their hard work is valuable and is seen by the manager and the overall 
organization.

8 CONCLUSION 
The aim of this thesis has been to better understand the roles of trust, motivation, 
and collaboration in a virtual team setting, as well as suggest actions on how to 
develop, sustain, and enhance them as a project manager considering the 
increasing prevalence of virtual teamwork, especially after the COVID-19 
pandemic.  
Previous research, literature, and the case study presented in this report indicate 
that managing geographically dispersed teams is a significant challenge 
regardless of the industry. While project managers' primary goal is to complete 
the project on time, within budget, and within the scope and quality requirements 
of the client, the human-related aspects of a project should not be overlooked, 
especially when leading a virtual or hybrid team.  
According to the findings of this study, trust, motivation, and collaboration, 
among other factors, play a significant role in the success of virtual projects and 
project management in general. As a result, project managers must use their 
leadership skills to a greater extent when leading their teams from a distance to 
develop, sustain, and enhance these human-related factors among their teams. It 
is also critical for project managers to tailor their leadership styles to the needs of 
their teams, as well as keep in mind that managing a virtual team is different from 
managing a traditional one.  
The findings also indicate that frequent and open communication between 
managers and their teams, as well as between team members, is a critical element 
in building trust, motivation, and collaboration in a virtual environment. 
Based on previous research, literature, and the case study, a few action 
suggestions have been offered to assist project managers in establishing, 
maintaining, and improving trust, motivation, and collaboration among teams 
operating in a virtual setting. These suggestions are as follows: 
▪ Allow team members to choose how they want to work. 
▪ Adapt leadership style. 
▪ Decide which meetings should be held online and in-person. 
▪ Hold regular team meetings 
▪ Communicate and encourage communication 
▪ Ensure clear roles and responsibilities 
▪ Schedule individual meetings with each team member 
▪ Provide appropriate communication and collaboration tools 
==PAGE:82==
 
81 
Zahra Al-Mosawi                Virtual Team Management with Focus on Trust, Motivation & Collaboration 
▪ Give feedback  
▪ Recognize and celebrate accomplishments  
 
The results confirm that there are a number of human factors to consider in order 
to achieve a successful virtual project. However, many of these factors seem to be 
interconnected and can be affected by the same actions. For example, frequent 
communication may result in trusting relationships among team members and 
in a collaborative team environment where members are motivated to perform at 
their best.
