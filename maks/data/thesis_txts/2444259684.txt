Abstract
The scope of this project has been to develop and optimize a sensitive and selective
LC-MS/MS analytical method to detect the secondary metabolites, Tropodithietic
acid (TDA) and Methyl thiotropicin (MeTDA) in natural biological samples. This
included optimization of sample extraction and preparation protocol as well as the
optimization of the various LC and MS parameters, which was the main focus of the
project.
Experiments were conducted to optimize the current sample preparation protocol
into a simpler and more efficient extraction protocol. This was done by comparing
recovery rates of the three different methods, Liquid-liquid extraction (LLE), Solid-
phase extraction (SPE), and QuEChERS (quick, easy, cheap, effective, rugged, safe)
extraction that are the current standards for these analyses. However, the preparation
and analysis of the real natural biological sample source, seawater, revealed a problem
with the sample preparation. The new protocol was optimized using 300 µL - 1 mL of a
media grown dense bacterial culture of Phaeobacter inhibens (P.inhibens), sample size
depended on the method used. The real bacterial samples from seawater are not as
dense as media derived ones and therefore extraction needs to be performed on much
larger sample volumes, 1 L of seawater. It turned out that the extraction protocol was
not optimal for extraction of a large volumes of biological samples. This issue should
be investigated further, for example with regards to sample concentration in the
beginning and salt removal. The optimization process for the analytical method itself
was focused mainly on optimizing the ion source settings of the mass spectrometer and
the separation ability of the LC by changing the stationary phase and composition
and gradient of the mobile phase. The optimized analytical method was found to
be specific and linear over almost the full calibration range when using standards.
Moreover, method sensitivity in terms of limit of detection (LOD) and quantitation
(LOQ) was found to be significantly improved in comparison to the original method.
LOD for TDA went from being 78.30 nM to below 0.9 nM and the LOQ for the same

In conclusion, a more sensitive and selective analytical method was developed

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The ocean, which covers more than 2/3 of the surface of the Earth, offers a biolog-
ically rich community of organisms. Howevever, to this day this massive resource
still remains almost unexplored. Marine bacteria, for example, have had to adjust to
survive the hostile environment, including fast changes in salinity and pressure, ex-
treme temperatures, and constant competition with predators, and maintain survival
adaptations that are different from bacteria that survive on land. Therefore more
researchers are now turning to the sea for novel metabolites produced by marine bac-
teria. Marine bacteria produce small organic molecules, or secondary metabolites,
that provide many important adaptive functions including communication as well as
protective and defensive functions.
Secondary metabolites produced by Pheaobacter inhibens (P.inhibens) are of great
interest due to the bacterium potential as a probiotic in aquaculture. One of the
metabolites, Tropodithietic acid (TDA), has antibacterial properties, and inhibits
both human and fish pathogenic bacteria. TDA can, for example, prevent vibriosis in
cod and turbot. Probiotics have great advantages over antibiotics, mainly because of
the risk of developing and transfer of antibiotic resistance. To explore the metabolites
further, researchers have to be able to detect, identify, and quantify the different
metabolites in a complex biological sample.
Due to the variety of metabolites, which in many cases may have very low concen-
tration, different types of analytical techniques are needed for their analysis. For this
study Liquid chromatography/mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) is applied as a very
efficient analytical method to analyze and separate analytes in mixed samples. Liq-
uid chromatography (LC) is the preferred separation technique and is often combined
with detection techniques such as mass spectrometry (MS) or UV/Vis detection. Due
to the increasing demands for sensitivity, selectivity and structural information MS
has become the preferred detection technique. And because the compounds analyzed
with LC are in a solvent electrospray ionization (ESI) is the preferred interface for

LC-MS/MS.
Challenges when analyzing biological samples when coupling LC to a MS include
the issue of non-volatile mobile phase and the high concentration of non-volatile ionic
species such as sodium, which can lower the sensitivity, lead to ion suppression, and
may clog the MS.
Optimization of the MS is needed and although the instruments may differ, com-
mon parameters such as nebulizer gas, capillary voltages, dry gas flow, dry gas tem-
perature, ion type and collision energy are among the parameters that need to be
investigated. In order to improve efficiency, method development and optimization
of instruments and processes is a very common practice. The goal is to increase pro-
ductivity without compromising the quality. It is, therefore, important to define the
best approach to optimization while the quality is determined and maintained.
1.1
Aim of the project
The primary goal of the project was to develop a sensitive and selective trace analysis
method, LC-MS/MS method for detecting TDA. The TDA is a secondary metabolite
that has been shown to have antibiotic properties in natural biological samples like
seawater. The aim is to develop a sensitive method to detect TDA in samples with
extremely low concentration, not only in dense bacterial culture.

CHAPTER 2
Theory
2.1
Marine bacteria
The ocean contains a vast number of microbes, a vast bacterial diversity that remains
undiscovered and uncharacterized [1]. All bacteria, and in fact, all living organisms
can be classified in a specific classification system called a taxonomy system. The
taxonomy system is used to classify organisms based on their anatomical similarities
and shared characteristics; it is meant to help researchers to learn from what has
already been discovered and classified and further classify unknown organisms. The
classification can be categorized, divided into levels, the kingdom being the highest
level and species being the lowest, see Figure 2.1 [2, 3].
Figure 2.1: The taxonomy system with added information on the bacteria used in
this project (modified from [2]).
Bacteria can be said to be ”masters of changes” as they have developed means to
communicate, respond, and adjust to continuously changing environments, especially
marine bacteria [4].
Therefore, it is not surprising that marine bacteria, bacteria
adapted to life in the ocean are both diverse, genetically distinct, and maintain sur-
vival adaptations that are different in many ways from bacteria that survive on land.
==PAGE:22==
2 Theory
For example, they need to survive in hostile environments, fast changes in salinity
and pressure, extreme temperatures, as well as constant competition with predators,
like other microbes and viral pathogens [5, 6]. It has now been discovered and docu-
mented that bacteria use small organic molecules, or secondary metabolites, for many
critical adaptive functions including communication, both inter- and intraspecies com-
munication, while other metabolites have protective and defensive functions against
microbial life forms [4, 7].
2.2
Secondary metabolites
All organisms convert the nutrients available to them into the metabolites that are
essential for their growth, primary metabolites, and their survival, secondary metabo-
lites [8]. While the primary metabolites, carbohydrates, proteins, and lipids, are sim-
ilar between organisms, the secondary metabolites are usually produced by narrow
taxonomic groups of organisms and are usually strain-specific. This means that a
certain secondary metabolite is specific to a group of organism, and there is often a
reason for the production of a certain compound, for example being essential for their
survival [4, 8]. Secondary metabolites of marine bacteria are extremely heterogeneous,
representing all molecular classes such as fatty acids, peptides, polyketides, terpenes

2.3
Sample preparation methods
Sample preparation methods are defined as all the chemistry done with the sample
before the injection of the sample into the analytical instrument. In other words, all
that is done to the sample to make it compatible with the analytical method to be
used [15]. When working with biological samples, the right sample preparations are
probably the most crucial part of developing a method for detecting natural products
in the samples.
Biological samples are often very complex and therefore can not
be injected straight into an analytical instrument, for example like the LC-MS/MS
used in this project. These complex samples usually contain one or even more matrix
substances that can either suppress the signal, ion suppression, or give a false positive
response, have interference on the signal of the analyte in question, some matrix
components might even elute at the same time as the analyte. Also, it can contain
substances that can affect and deteriorate the instrument itself or be incompatible
with the instrument if no sample preparations are performed. The appropriate sample
preparation is also essential as the concentration levels of the analyte in biological
samples are often very low, which means that the analysis has to be very sensitive

and selective [15, 16].
The sample preparation methods relevant for this report are Liquid-liquid extrac-
tion (LLE), QuEChERS extraction, and Solid-phase extraction (SPE).
2.3.1
Liquid-liquid extraction
LLE has been the most traditional sample preparation method for years, but it has
advantages and disadvantages. LLE involves a water phase and an organic phase
and one or more solutes/analytes in the sample that needs to be separated and an-
alyzed. LLE is intended to transfer the analyte from the biological sample to an
organic solvent, but at the same time leave all other matrix compounds that can
have interference in the water phase. The selection of the organic phase or so-called
extraction solvent, should be based on the molecular structure of the analyte, and it
should also be immiscible with water to get separation of the phases [15, 17, 18]. A
control of pH is important to make sure that the analyte will be in the right phase,
for example, in acidic solution, acidic analytes are neutralized, and therefore their
water solubility is poor, but their solubility in an organic solvent is relatively good.
Thus, the analyte will in principle be retained in the organic solvent. However, other
factors should be taken under consideration when choosing the right extraction sol-
vent. First, as mentioned before, the solvent has to be immiscible with water to get
adequate separation of the two phases and that requires a polarity index of the solvent
4–5 or less. Secondly, it should be volatile since most of the times the extract has
to be evaporated and redissolved in another more compatible liquid before analysis.
Third, the solvent should preferably be less dense than water, to form the upper layer
after phase separation, as that can be more convenient when the extract is collected.
Finally, the organic solvent to be chosen should also be acceptable for use in terms
of safety and environmental aspects, it should not be toxic, flammable, or explosive.
To achieve this, it is sometimes necessary to use a mixture of two solvents together
to get the desired outcome [15, 18].
With very complex biological samples, containing many matrix components at
high concentration, a single extraction may not be enough as some of the matrix
compounds also can be extracted into the organic solvent. In such cases, it can be
necessary to perform a so called back extraction, a second extraction step, to clean
the matrix components that are in the organic phase [15]. Advantages of LLE is

that inorganic salts are easily removed and optimization of the method is not time-
consuming, but at the same time it is labour intensive, difficult to automate, and it
uses large volumes of organic solvents [19].
2.3.2
Solid-phase extraction
SPE is a frequently used sample preparation method when working with biological
samples, especially when large amounts of samples are needed to be ”cleaned”. SPE
is based on use of an extraction column which is filled with a specific stationary phase
that can be easily changed for each analysis and is chosen based on the analyte [19].
SPE is a four step method, where the first step in SPE is the conditioning/preparing
step. It involves conditioning of the column, which is done by flushing certain liquids
through the column. The second step is loading of the sample and flushing it through
the column. During this step, the analyte is retained in the column because of interac-
tions with the stationary phase in the column. Whilst the other matrix components,
the salts, and all other unwelcome compounds are flushed out of the column straight
away, as those compounds have no interactions with the stationary phase. In the
third step, the washing step, one or even more washing solutions are flushed through
the column for the purpose of sample clean-up. This can be important to eliminate all
potential interference components before the analytical analysis. In the elution step,
which is the final step, a small volume of a certain liquid, which is called the eluent,
is flushed through the column. That specific eluent/solvent breaks the interactions
between the analyte and the column and releases the analyte from the column. What
comes out of the column in this step is then collected for analysis, only the analyte
should be in the solvent, free of major matrix compounds from the sample. SPE is
normally a very efficient extraction method, and extraction recoveries are close to
100%. The last step, the elution step, can be done many times with different solvents
to get fractions of eluents to analyze [15, 16, 19].
Advantages of SPE are clearly that it can be very selective, and has high recoveries
and reproducibility. Disadvantages are that it can be very complex and therefore dif-
ficult to master and it can be both costly and time-consuming to optimize. There are
many opportunities in using different types of stationary phases and as well different
types of eluents [19].

2.3.3
QuEChERS extraction
QuEChERS extraction or quick, easy, cheap, effective, rugged, and, safe extraction
method is a novel sample preparation method. This method was originally devel-
oped for detection of pesticide residues in food, mainly vegetables and fruits. As the
name uncovers the QuEChERS method involves a simple extraction with acetoni-
trile (ACN) followed by another extraction step after the addition of a specific salt
mixture, a mixture of sodium chloride (NaCl) and magnesium sulfate heptahydrate
(MgSO4 · 7 H2O). When adding the salt mixture the ACN becomes immiscible with
the saturated salty water and two separated phases becomes visible.
Just like in
LLE, buffers can be added to help control the pH during the extraction step and thus
optimize the recovery of basic or acid sensitive analytes. The final extract, upper
layer, being solved in ACN, is compatible with determinative analysis based on LC
and/or GC. Great advantages of the QuEChERS method is that it effectively covers
a very broad analyte scope, including highly polar molecules as well as highly acidic
and basic ones with high recovery rate. It has been shown to be faster and use less
solvent compared to the SPE and LLE, and also to be safer, greener and less costly
[20, 21].
2.4
Chromatography methods
Chromatography is an example of a separation technique that utilizes two phases to
achieve separation. The analytes will partition between the two phases, which results
in a separation because of different interaction with the two phases. Chromatography
in principle consists of a mobile phase, also called eluent, which can either be a liquid
or a gas, and a stationary phase, which is bound on a surface, most commonly within
a column. The column can then also either be packed with stationary phase particles
inside, or be open tubular with stationary phase bonded on the sides of a hollow
capillary with a very small diameter.
Both types of phases will be discussed in
further details.
At this point it may be useful to define important terms that explain different
characteristics of chromatographic methods.
• Qualitative vs. Quantitative analysis: The aim of a qualitative analysis is

to answer the question ‘What’ is in the sample, while a quantitative analysis,
is based on relationship between the instrumental responses, for example peak
height and area, and the concentration/amount of the analyte in the sample

• Selectivity: The capability of an analytical method in quantifying analytes
in the presence of other matrix or interfering compounds.
It describes the
difference in interaction of different analytes with the stationary phase under
specific conditions. A requirement for a selectivity can be that there should be
baseline separation of analytes [15, 22].
• Sensitivity: The slope of the calibration/standard curve [23].
• Precision: The consistency among independent results obtained under the
same operating conditions [24].
• Recovery: Is the estimation, in percentages, of the amount of the analyte

signal. There is always some noise around, noise results from the electronics of
an instrument, and thus noise is not only present between signals but also in

samples with known concentrations of analyte with those of blank samples and
establishing the minimum concentration at which the analyte can be reliably
detected [16].

HPLC systems, also the early ones, contain all the same components that are still
being used today but only with more sophisticated execution. An LC system consist

As mentioned briefly above, in LC, the mobile phase is a liquid forced through
a column or a stationary phase, packed with a material that can interact with the
analytes and retain the analytes introduced into the system. The analytes are injected
into the flow of mobile phase just in front of the column, and the outlet of the column
is directly connected to the detector. Most importantly, only eluted substances are
detected [15]. After the analytes are eluted from the column, they reach a detector,
which, together with a computer system that reads the data from the detector, creates
a chromatogram. A chromatogram is a graph showing the detector responses as a
function of time. Each peak in the chromatogram represents a response from the
detector for a different analyte. The time it takes an analyte to pass through the
system, the time it takes the sample to reach the detector from when it is injected, is
called the retention time [22].

2.4. Chromatography methods
2.4.1.1
The mobile phase
The mobile phase can either be isocratic or gradient, both phases have their own
advantages and limitation. An isocratic mobile phase is when the composition of the
mobile phase does not change during the analysis, the composition is kept constant.
Several problems are associated with the use of isocratic mobile phase for analysis. For
example, when the range of analyte polarities is broad, some analytes may be poorly
retained, and resolution is lost with peaks eluting very early. Also, other analyte
components may have significantly more interactions with the stationary phase and
therefore show unacceptably long retention times. Due to the various band broadening
processes, these late eluting peaks will be broad, have tailing and show reduced
sensitivity due to reduced peak height. The main reason for a narrow peak shape
is the velocity of the peak as it leaves the column. Another possibility is that some
components will be irreversibly adsorbed on the column and cause contamination,
called a carry-over. Many of these problems mentioned above that are associated
with isocratic analysis can be overcome by using a gradient. In this mode the mobile
phase composition is progressively changed during the analysis, by increasing the
amount of the organic solvent. The initial composition is chosen so that the strength
is appropriate to retain and resolve the early eluting components. The organic solvent
composition, and therefore the polarity, is increased in a predetermined manner to
elute analytes with maximum resolution and peak height. Whereas the final mobile
phase composition is chosen to ensure elution of all compounds of interest from the
column with an acceptable retention time. And it should be possible to increase the
organic solvent concentration to wash strongly retained and potentially contaminating
components from the column [22, 27].
To be able to use gradient mobile phase specific pumps are needed that allow for
online mixing of the mobile phase solvents. During gradient elution, all compounds
accelerate through the column and thus elute at a high velocity.
The difference
between analytes in retention time is directly related to the proportion of the or-
ganic solvent and the velocity of the change of the proportion. The goal is that all
compounds should have approximately the same speed when they leave the column,
otherwise broadening of the peaks, tailing, or fronting of peaks is a possibility. A
reason for bad peak shape is the fact that the front and tail of a peak is being eluted
in different concentrations, proportion of organic solvent. But bad peak shape such
Page 13
==PAGE:32==
2 Theory
as asymmetric peaks is not a frequent problem in gradient elution. In fact the narrow
peaks obtained in gradient elution provide better detection limits when compared to
isocratic elution [16, 22].
Gradient method is though not perfect. For example it can be hard transferring
the gradient method between instruments. And it can sometimes lead to inconsistent
results. One reason for inconsistency is that instruments can have different dwell
volumes. Every instrument has its own dwell volume, which is the volume between the
point at which the gradient is mixed to the point at which that specific composition
enters the HPLC column. Differences in the system dwell volume can make large
differences to not only the retention time of the analyte but also to the selectivity
and therefore the resolution. The more complex the sample is, the more likely it is a
good idea to use a gradient for analysis. When using a gradient method, it is crucial
to recondition the stationary phase back to the initial mobile phase composition as
soon as the solvent strength gradient has reached its final composition level. This
is to ensure that the mobile and stationary phase are back in so-called equilibrium
state, which is a prerequisite for stable retention times run after run [16, 22, 27].
2.4.1.2
The stationary phase
The stationary phase is the other phase of the two phases forming a chromatographic
system. The stationary phases can have different characteristics based on the use of
different packing material. Ideally the packing material should be chemically stable,
and stable against acids, base, and different type of solvents used as mobile phases.
It should also be mechanically strong to be able to withstand the high pressure. A
stationary phase or column can be described with the following parameters; particle
form and size, surface area, pore size, and functional groups. The mode of an HPLC
method is defined by the difference in polarity of the mobile phase and the stationary
phase. The mostly used modes are called normal phase and reversed phase, other
modes are for example ion-exchange and size exclusion.
The normal phase mode
is based on mobile phase that is non-polar while the stationary phase is polar. A
reversed phase mode uses a system where the mobile phase is more polar than the
stationary phase, polar analytes will elute first and the most non-polar will elute in
the end. The reversed phase is the most commonly used mode, and is the one used
in this project. The most used materials in reversed phase mode column are particles
Page 14
==PAGE:33==

of porous silica that have carbon chains attached to the silica surface. On the surface
of bare silica are primarily silanol (SiOH) groups that makes them polar, but with
addition of carbon chains it becomes non-polar and therefore well fitted for reversed
phase HPLC. Most common side chains are octadecyl (C18) and octyl (C8). Other
types are, for example, biphenyl, phenyl-hexyl, and perfluorophenyl (F5 or PFP). All
offer different selectivity based on their interactions with the analyte, the so-called
retention mechanism, which can be quite complex and is not entirely clear [16, 25,

Length of columns are typically between 50 and 250 mm, and internal diameters
between 1 and 10 mm, both depends on the required resolution. The particle size of
the packaging material is also of importance, the smaller the particles, the better the
resolution and therefore better separation. Resolution is used to describe the efficiency
of separation, and good resolution is obtained when two peaks do not overlap and are
separated with baseline [25, 27].

Detection methods
Different types of detectors are compatible with and can be coupled to an HPLC,
such as UV/Vis, MS detector, fluorescence, conductivity, etc. [15, 16]. The detection

2.5.1
Mass spectrometry detector
A mass spectrometry detector (MS) consists of three parts, an ion source, a mass
analyzer, and a detector, see Figure 2.4. A great variety of ion sources, mass analyzers,
and detectors is available on the market, and all have their advantages and limitations.
Though all mass analyzers use electric and magnetic fields for separation, the basis
for the various kinds of mass analyzer is in how these two fields are used [23, 28].
Though the mass analyzer is important the appearance of a mass spectrum is mostly
depended on the ion source, that is the method that is responsible for forming the
ions [27, 28].

An MS works on the principle that it measures the mass to charge ratio (m/z)
of ions formed during the ionization in the ion source, and separates the ions based
on the difference in m/z with a mass analyzer. The ions formed travel through the
mass spectrometer, moreover, the mass analyzer part that separates them, which
makes them arrive at different times or at different parts of the detector, all based
on their m/z [27, 29]. After the ions are detected signals are generated and recorded
by a computer/data system.
The computer displays the signals graphically as a
mass spectrum showing the relative abundance of the signals according to their m/z
ratio. The primary goal of an MS is to identify an analyte from the molecular mass
of its constituents.
Information delivered by mass alone can be sufficient for the
identification of elements and to determine the molecular formula of an analyte. Then
the relative abundance, intensity, of isotopic patterns helps to decide which elements
contribute to the molecular formula and also to estimate the number of atoms of

2.5.1.1
Agilent jet stream electrospray ionization (AJS-ESI)
Electrospray ionization (ESI) is a method to generate/form the ions which are neces-
sary for the detection of the analyte with MS. ESI uses electrical energy to transfer
ions from solution into gaseous phase, making it a perfect match for use with HPLC
[27, 30]. ESI can be divided into three steps. First the nebulization of the sample into
charged droplets. Secondly, freeing of ions from those droplets, solvent evaporation,
and finally the transportation of those ions into the mass analyzer part of the MS [31].
The sample is injected straight from the LC column as a liquid into the ion source,
where charged droplets are formed at the needle tip in a so-called spray chamber,
Page 17
==PAGE:36==
2 Theory
where there is a strong electric field. Nebulizing gas flows around the sample in the
needle, in the form of nitrogen (N2) gas. The strong electric field at the needle tip
combined with the N2 gas flow forms a fine mist of charged droplets, see Figure 2.5.
Figure 2.5: Schematic diagram of Agilent jet stream electrospray ionization, the ion
source that is used in this project. The figure also shows the steps of the ESI process
as the source for the MS. This figure is adapted from [32]. See full page size figure in

The AJS-ESI has an extra feature making it more superior then a regular ESI.
It has superheated sheath gas, also N2, that confines the ion spray, and thus creates
higher ion density right at the entry of the capillary [32]. The sign of the electric
field determines the charge of the droplets, positive or negative. In general, a positive
mode will ionize most analytes, and is therefore used more often. Due to elevated
temperatures and drying gas flow, the charged droplets get smaller and smaller as
the solvent evaporates. The droplets keep getting smaller until they reach a critical
point, where the surface tension can no longer sustain the charge, called the Rayleigh
limit. When that happens the droplet bursts, ”Coulomb Explosion”, and ions at the
surface of the droplet are ejected into the gaseous phase. The ions are then collected
with a sampling skimmer cone and lenses and then accelerated into the mass analyzer.
As this is a soft ionization method, the ions that get to the mass analyzer usually do
not have enough energy to fragment into smaller pieces without help, and therefore
the molecular ion is most apparent, [M + X]+ where X is H, Na or K [27].

The ion beam shaper and ion slicer are positioned before the ion pulser, or the accel-
erator. It is unique for the Agilent 6545 Q-TOF used in this study [33]. Both shape
the ion beam into a flat profile before the acceleration. This improves the resolution
and the mass resolving power of the Q-TOF even more and makes it one of the most
accurate mass analyzers available today. The charged ions are then accelerated by
applying a high voltages pulse, hence the name ion pulser, theoretically giving the
ions all the same kinetic energy (K) and therefore allowing the detection of charged
ions with different m/z. In practice, during the acceleration, ions with the same mass
can have a slight difference in energy due to their positioning within the ion pulser.
The kinetic energy dispersion can be corrected with ion mirror (reflectron) placed in
the upper end of the flight tube. Ions with more kinetic energy and hence with more
speed will go higher into the reflectron than ions with lower energy. Consequently,
the faster, high energy ions will spend more time in the reflectron and will therefore
in the end reach the detector at the same time as slower ions with the same mass. As
a result the Q-TOF can be considered a high resolution MS detector [15, 28, 29, 33].
2.5.1.3
Triple Quad
A Triple Quad (QqQ) is a mass analyzer that consists of three quadrupole mass
analyzers in a row, and is based on trajectory stability of the ions from the ion source
in oscillating electric field [28]. It plays an increasingly important role, especially
in a targeted analysis of complex samples. A quadrupole uses an electric field to
separate/filter the ions based on their m/z value. It consists of four cylindrical parallel
poles that serve as electrodes. Adjacent poles are connected electrically, applying both
a direct current and a radio frequency, creating an oscillating electrical field. When
applying both a constant voltage and a radio-frequency oscillating voltage the electric
field deflects ions, allowing only ions with certain m/z through. The electrical field
Page 20
==PAGE:39==
2.5. Detection methods
causes the ions to travel forward in the z direction with oscillatory motion in the
x-y plane. The ions that enter this field, either fly through to the detector or, if not
travelling on stable trajectory, the ions will hit the poles and therefore will not be
detected. It is possible to change the voltage and radio frequency and therefore only
let specific ions through to the detector, see Figure 2.7. In a single quadrupole mass
analyzer fragmentation is not possible [15, 28, 29].
Figure 2.7: Schematic diagram showing how a single quadrupole works. To the left,
black line shows a stable oscillating ion moving in the z-direction and reaching the
detector, while the grey line shown an unstable ion colliding with one of the poles.
To the right an x/y view of the poles, representing the electrical field (U) and radio
frequency (V cosωt) [15].
A triple quadrupole functions are in principle the same as for a single quadrupole
but has also the advantage of being able to perform fragmentation within the analyzer
itself. A QqQ consists of two quadrupole mass analyzers and a collision cell between
the two, see Figure 2.8.
A QqQ can be operated as a single MS or as a tandem MS (MS/MS). When it is
operated as a single MS, no fragmentation is carried out, and either the first or the last
quadrupole is used as an mass analyzer. However, QqQ is rarely used as a single MS,
as the main advantage of a QqQ is its ability of fragmentation and second filtering,
tandem MS. Fragmentation in a QqQ is performed as follows. In the first quadrupole
(Q1), the ions to be fragmented are filtered and transferred to the collision cell (Q2).
This can be a quadrupole (hence the name) or a hexapole that contains an inert
gas, usually argon (Ar) or nitrogen (N2). By accelerating the ions in the z-direction
toward the exit of the collision cell, ions collide with the gas molecules present in
the tube and fragment, called collision-induced dissociation (CID). Collision energy

In the collision some of the
kinetic energy is converted into internal energy, which will result in fragmentation
of the molecular ion into smaller fragments.
The fragmented ions are transferred

possible to get different types of scans [16, 29]. The types of scans that are relevant

• Product ion scan: Q1 is fixed, thus only allowing one ion with specific m/z

This mode can be used for studying

• Multiple reaction monitoring: Both Q1 and Q3 are fixed for a pre-determined

[29].

2.5.2
UV/Vis detector
A UV/Vis detector is a detector based on that the analyte absorbs light, either from
the visible region (400-750nm) or Ultra Violet light (UV-light) region (190-400nm),
which means that only analytes that can absorb light can be detected with this
detector. The analyte must contain a chromophore, a functional group that has at
least one double bond, conjugated system. Chromophore is the part of the molecule
that is capable of absorbing ultraviolet/visible radiation. The analyte contains π-
electrons or non-bonding electrons (lone pairs) that can absorb energy in the form of
ultraviolet or visible light and excite these electrons to higher anti-bonding molecular
orbitals. Absorption of light, whether it is in the visible region (compound often
has a color), or UV light region, the principle is the same. Absorbance is a result
of a transition from a lower state of energy to a higher energy state, π →π∗. For
a molecule to perform this transition, the applied energy has to equal the exact
difference between the energy levels. Light sources are used to irradiate the compound
and give it the necessary energy to reach the transition. Typically, a deuterium lamp
is used for the UV region and a tungsten lamp for the visible region.
In an LC
system, the light from these two lamps is merged and shone onto the eluent. The
light that comes through is split into several beams which are detected by a diode
array detector (DAD). DAD has a series of photodiode detectors adjacent to each
other, where every diode records a small interval of the UV/Vis region. Thus, a full
spectrum of the UV/Vis region is recorded. The obtained spectrum will be a total
wavelength chromatogram (TWC), which has absorbance as a function of time. If a
particular wavelength is of interest, an extracted wavelength chromatogram (EWC)
can be formed. If a specific peak within one of these chromatograms comes from an
analyte of interest, the peak can be integrated and an absorption chromatogram for
this time interval can be extracted. This will display absorbance as a function of

wavelength and is called a UV/Vis spectrum, see Figure 2.9 [16, 27].
Figure 2.9: UV/Vis Spectrum of TDA [14].
A UV spectrum of a certain compound can provide useful information, it can
suggest which functional groups, common chromophore systems, might be present.
Comparison between two different UV spectra can be used to predict if two molecules
are likely or unlikely to have the same chromophore. The wavelengths of the peaks
can be correlated with the types of bonds in a given molecule and are valuable in
determining the functional groups within a molecule, one top equals one possible
excitation etc. The most used application for an UV-detector is quantitative analysis
of known compounds, based on a standard curve [16, 27].

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
A more sensitive and selective method using an LC-MS/MS was developed and applied
to detect two important bio-active secondary metabolites, TDA and MeTDA, in a
dense bacterial culture. Furthermore, it was tested on natural biological samples. The
original LC-MS/MS method gave broad peaks with a tail with a high limit of detection
(LOD) and quantification (LOQ). To improve the detection and quantification was
the driving force for the development of an optimized method. Narrower peaks with
minimized tailing give increased sensitivity, improved resolution and better separation
between analytes.
The mass spectrometer used was a Triple Quad (QqQ) which, in theory, is very
fitting for targeted analysis like the one discussed here. The analytes were known
and there was no need to identify unknown compounds making the QqQ an obvious
choice for the detector. The optimization of the MS revealed some advantages of the
detector which was equipped with a built ion funnel. Due to the ion funnel, the ion
beam is better-focused as it enters from the ion source into the mass analyzer, making
it even more sensitive. Due to time restriction the ion focusing was not thoroughly
investigated or optimized.
A High-pressure liquid chromatography (LC) is a very commonly used separation
method for MS instruments. This separation method was optimized in this project.
The Kinetex Biphenyl column was found to be optimal for the analytes under investi-
gation (TDA and MeTDA). Furthermore, the separation abilities of the LC combined
with the sensitivity and the ability targeted analysis of the MS was well suited for
the analytes. Some problems arose during the development process of the LC, for
example, the optimized column started to deteriorate after 300 injections making it
necessary to finish the analyses of samples using another column.
A quantitative analysis was made on extracts of algae and bacterial co-culture
and seawater. In all these samples the background noise was higher than desired,
indicating that more sample preparation might be needed before analysis. The sam-
==PAGE:72==
5 Conclusion
ple preparation method used was an optimized liquid-liquid extraction (LLE). The
optimization was done on pure and simple samples of dense cultures and then tested
on matrix enriched samples (seawater samples).
The results showed high level of
interference and it was therefore concluded that LLE is not appropriate when using
large volume samples.
This project describes only the initial steps in the development of an analytical
method using LC-MS/MS for the detection of TDA and MeTDA in biological sam-
ples. In spite of some of problems that arose, the results showed that the new and
improved method is a promising analytical tool for analysis of these specific secondary
metabolites.
5.1
Perspective
As mentioned briefly in the conclusion, there were some problems that arose, but due
to time restrictions and delays of sample delivery it was not possible to do a thorough
investigation. For example, further research on various sample preparation methods
are necessary in order to make sure that only TDA and MeTDA are being purified
from biological samples. It would have been interesting for example, to see if adding
one single liquid extraction with water, before the concentration step with seawater,
would have had the desired effect or if further cleaning procedures were necessary.
Another option is to look further into why the method was not transferable to the
Q-TOF that has the exact same ion source, Agilent jet stream electrospray ionization.
Is it only due to the difference including the ion funnel in the QqQ or were there other
factors responsible.
With regards to recovery calculations in the sample preparation optimization,
it would have been interesting to use more than only one spiking level and more
repetitions in order to get more reliable results. However, since the main goal of
the project was to focus on optimizing the analytical method but not the sample
preparation, this was set aside.

C.1.1
Concentrations and dilution series of standards
Concentrations or amounts of solute and volume of solution are linked by this follow-
ing equation:
C =
m
V ∗MW
(C.1)
Where, C is the concentration [mol/L], m is the mass [g] of the solute, V volume [L]
of solvent used to dissolve the solute and finally MW is the molecular weight [g/mol]
of the solute [16].
To make a fixed amount of a dilute solution from a stock solution, the dilution equa-
tions:
Cinitial ∗Vinitial = Cfinal ∗Vfinal
(C.2)
Where Cinitial is the concentration [mol/L] of stock solution and V initial the volume
[L] of stock solution needed to make the new diluted solution. While Cfinal and V final
are final concentration and final volume of the new diluted solution [16].

C.1.2
Recovery calculations
Recovery calculation is used to determine losses that can occur during the sample
preparation process. Two runs are performed, one with the sample and a second with
the sample that was spiked prior to the sample preparation with a known concentra-
tion of analyte. Quantitation of the data from the two sample runs allow the recovery
factor of the sample preparation to be calculated.
% Recovery =
Sspiked
Sstandard
∗100
(C.3)
Where Sspiked is the peak area for the spiked sample after sample preparation treat-
ment, and Sstandard is the area for a standard with the same concentration as spiked
sample before sample preparation treatment [15].
C.1.3
Uncertainty calculations
To calculate the uncertainty of the quantification of the real samples the propagation
of uncertainty method was used, see (C.4).
Uncertainty in x (= sx) = sy
|m|
√
1
k + 1
n +
(y −y)2
m2Σ(xi −x)2
(C.4)
sy is standards deviation of y, |m| is the absolute value of the slope, k is the number
of replicates in measurement, n is the number of data points for the standard curve,
y is the mean value of y and x is the mean value of x for the points in the standard
curve [16].
