Abstract 
Intracellular sterol transport proteins are not a fully explored area and are interesting drug target 
candidates. By creating a pseudo-natural product compound collection, the chemical space can be 
investigated for potential inhibitors of these proteins. In this project, a cis-decalone is chosen as the 
scaffold to build the collection from. It was synthesized in six steps and brominated to activate the 
ketone for synthesis of privileged heterocycle analogues. Five thiazoles and three quinoxalines were 
synthesized and fully characterized and the synthesis of an imidazopyridine was attempted. Three of 
the thiazoles were tested in a fluorescence polarization assay for activity against the Aster-proteins 
and no hits were detected.

Resumé 
Intracellulære sterol transport proteiner er et ikke fuldt udforsket område og er interessante kandidater 
som mål for medicin. Ved at lave en pseudo-naturproduktforbindelsessamling, kan man afsøge the 
kemiske rum for potentielle inhibitorer af disse proteiner. I dette projekt, er en cis-decalon valgt som 
skelettet samlingen bygges ud fra. Den blev syntetiseret i seks trin og bromineret for at aktivere 
ketonen til syntesen af privilegerede heterocykelanaloger. Fem thiazoler og tre quinoxaliner blev 
syntetiseret og fuldt karakteriseret og syntese af en imidazopyridin blev forsøgt. Tre af thiazolerne 
blev testet i et fluorescenspolarisationsassay for aktivitet imod Asterproteinerne, og der blev ikke 
registreret nogen udslag.

1 Introduction 
Cholesterol is known mostly for its involvement in disease. In 2021, 31.7% of adults in the United 
States had hypercholesterolemia (high cholesterol) which doubles the risk of contracting heart disease 
because of plaque formation.1 Yet, cholesterol is an essential component of cell membranes and 
required for the biosynthesis of sterols and any errors in the biosynthesis or transport of cholesterol 
may lead to equally deadly diseases.2 
Because of cholesterol’s significant involvement in cardiovascular disease, a large effort has been 
placed in researching extracellular cholesterol. However, some diseases can be treated via 
intracellular sterol transport proteins (STPs).3 In this project a sterol-inspired library will be 
synthesized and tested for inhibition against three major classes of STPs: Asters, ORPs and STARDs. 
First the theoretical background for cholesterol, STPs and the overall synthesis strategy is described. 
Next each synthesis is presented followed by an evaluation and discussion of assay results. 
2 Cholesterol 
Cholesterol is a sterol lipid that consists of four rings and a hydroxy moiety functioning as a small 
polar head group. The hydroxy head group combined with the aliphatic tail helps orient cholesterol in 
the membrane. The four ring-system is consistent across sterols and have letters assigned to them as 
shown in Figure 1.4 
 
Figure 1: The structure of cholesterol and the sterol four-ring skeleton. 
Cholesterol is incorporated into the cell membrane to maintain its liquidity. Cholesterol breaks up 
the phospholipids, preventing them from packing tightly and neatly. If cholesterol was not present it 
would result in crystallisation. If cholesterol levels are too high, the membrane starts becoming rigid. 
The correct balance for cell membranes is having 25-40% of lipids as cholesterol. Certain parts of the 
cell membrane called lipid rafts have elevated cholesterol content. Lipid rafts are groups of 
transmembrane protein complexes.4 Lipid raft complexes enable cell signalling and transport in a way 
that free floating membrane proteins would not be able to.5  
Cholesterol can be either biosynthesized or taken in via diet. Most of the cholesterol in the body is 
biosynthesized in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) of liver cells. The biosynthesis starts with acetyl-
coenzyme-A, which is transformed into squalene, a linear terpenoid. Oxidation of squalene forms 2,3-
==PAGE:15==
2 
 
oxidosqualene, which contains an epoxide used for ring closure, forming the hydroxy group of 
cholesterol.3,6 
Dietary cholesterol is absorbed with the help of either Niemann-Pick type C1-like 1 (NPC1L1) 
protein or the low-density lipoprotein receptor (LDLR). NPC1L1 is present in intestinal cells 
(enterocytes) and absorbs cholesterol directly from the diet. LDLR is used by other cells to get access 
to cholesterol. The receptor binds LDL in the bloodstream and absorbs it into the cell by endocytosis. 
The cholesterol in LDL is esterified while NPC1L1 binds unesterified cholesterol. When cholesterol 
is imported to the cell, it is first delivered to the lysosome. From the lysosome the cholesterol is then 
distributed to other parts of the cell.3 
LDL is a particle of fatty acids with a lipoprotein keeping it soluble in blood. It stays in the 
bloodstream until absorbed by a cell. When a cell needs to take up cholesterol from blood it will 
present LDLR. Hight-density lipoprotein (HDL) is similar to LDL but removes cholesterol from the 
blood supply by transporting it to the liver. Thus, high levels of LDL and low levels of HDL will lead 
to accumulation of cholesterol which leads to plaque formation.2 
In the body, there are ways cholesterol can be removed if the levels are too high. The ATP-binding 
cassette (ABC) proteins can transport cholesterol to apolipoprotein A1 which forms HDL. HDL 
transports the cholesterol to the liver where it is excreted. Cholesterol can also be stowed away in 
lipid droplets where it remains until a need for cholesterol arises. This is done by acyl-Coenzyme A: 
cholesterol acytransferase 1 and 1 (ACAT1 and 2). Cholesterol is esterified as part of this process. 
The biosynthesis of cholesterol can be regulated as well. ER-bound sterol regulatory element 
binding protein 2 (SREBP2) is the most significant cholesterol regulator, being involved in both 
activating and deactivating cholesterol biosynthesis. NPC1L1 and LDLR are also regulated by other 
mechanisms. If the cholesterol level is too high and must be treated, bile acid binding resins can be 
used. Cholesterol is used in bile acid biosynthesis, so when bile acid is removed, the liver must use 
more of the cholesterol reserves for its synthesis.4 A second option is targeting the biosynthesis. There 
are 30 enzymes that are involved in the biosynthesis of cholesterol and thus are potential targets. One 
way to narrow down is to go for the rate limiting step because inhibiting that step has the most overall 
effect. In cholesterol biosynthesis this step is catalysed by 3-hydroxy-3-methylglutarul-coenzyme A 
reductase (HMGR) and one of the best-selling drugs in history targets this enzyme: atorvastatin.2

2.1 Statins 
The majority drugs with cholesterol lowering effects are statins. The first statins (Type I statins) were 
isolated from microorganisms that did not have HMGR. These microorganisms were investigated 
because they might produce HMGR inhibitors as a defence mechanism against microorganisms that 
do have HMGR. Later statins (Type II statins) are synthetic, optimised and use other ring systems. 
The generalized structure of Type I and type II statins is shown in Figure 2. The fact that statins 
inhibit HMGR is not the only reason for their effectiveness. HMGR is one of the most well-regulated 
enzymes so when inhibited and the cholesterol level lowers, the cells react by producing more HMGR 
quickly. The real lasting effect is the increased level of LDLR introduced into the cell membrane 
caused by the low cholesterol. The receptors then begin clearing out the LDL-cholesterol in the 
bloodstream, reducing plaque formation.2,4  
 
Figure 2: Generalized structure of Type I statins (left) and Type II statins (right). 
The focus of cholesterol-lowering drugs has generally been on LDL which is part of extracellular 
transport. The mechanisms of intracellular cholesterol and transport thereof is not completely 
elucidated and there are no approved drugs targeting the main families of intracellular sterol transport 
proteins. The proteins and their function will be introduced in the following section. 
3 Intracellular Sterol Transport Proteins 
Three kinds of STPs are covered here: the oxysterol-binding protein (OSBP)-related protein (ORP) 
family, the steroidogenic acute regulatory protein-related lipid transfer domain (STARD) family and 
the Aster-domain containing family. They cover most of the non-vesicular intracellular sterol 
transport. In each family, some protein isoforms only contain a sterol-binding domain (SBD) while 
others have other domains as well. The other domains define the proteins’ location in the cell and 
mechanism of cholesterol transport. The proteins only consisting of an SBD are spread out in the 
cytosol and/or nucleus. 
Lipid selectivity has not been completely elucidated for the STPs, but some are known to transfer 
other lipids than cholesterol. Aster proteins have been observed to transfer carotenoids.7 ORP- and 
STARD proteins have been observed to transfer a variety of lipids including phosphatidylcholine, 
==PAGE:17==
4 
 
ceramide, and phosphatidylinositol phosphates. A big portion of cholesterol transport happens from 
the ER where cholesterol is synthesized. An overview of intracellular cholesterol transport is shown in  
Figure 3.3

3.1 The Oxysterol-Binding Protein-Related Protein Family Proteins (ORPs) 
The ORPs all share the same OSBP-related domain (ORD), except for ORP3. OSBP, ORP1, ORP2, 
ORP4 and ORP9 are the ORPs that transport cholesterol and have been found in mammals. 
An incomplete β-barrel gives the ORD a tunnel shape that works as a lipid binding pocket. The 
crystal structure for ORP1 in complex with cholesterol has been solved (Figure 4) and comparison 
with the sequences for other ORPs reveals that the binding mode of cholesterol is conserved. 
Cholesterol binds the ORD with the head-group pointing inwards because of the ORDs strong 
hydrophobic interactions with the rest of the molecule. The sequence of ORPs vary in the section that 
binds the head group of cholesterol. This section may be what determines the differences in ligand 
affinities.3 
 
Figure 4: (Left) Structure of the ORP1-ORD (PDB: 5ZM5 in complex with cholesterol (blue)), consisting of an incomplete 
β-barrel forming a hydrophobic tunnel, which serves as a binding pocket for one lipid molecule. Enclosed to the pocket 
entrance reside the EQVSHHPP signature (orange) and the N-terminal lid (green), containing the helix α1 (pink). (Right) 
Representation of the cholesterol binding mode in ORP1. The rigid cholesterol backbone and the hydrocarbon tail interact 
with multiple hydrophobic residues at the tunnel wall. The 3-OH of cholesterol forms direct (Q724) or water-mediated 
hydrogen bonds (Y583) at the bottom of the binding tunnel. Reproduced from Depta et. al.3

3.2 The Steroidogenic Acute Regulatory Protein-Related Lipid Transfer 
Domain Family Proteins (STARDs) 
The STARDs are subdivided into 6 sub-families. STARD1/D3 are the sterol-specific binding and 
membrane targeted subfamily. STARD4/D5/D6 are the sterol-binding and soluble subfamily. 
STARD2/D7/D10/D11 are the phospholipid/ceramide-binders. Other STARDs have no identified 
ligands and are therefore not known to bind cholesterol and not included here. 
All STARD proteins contain the steroidogenic acute regulatory (StAR) lipid transfer domain. The 
binding domain consists of a curved β-sheet gripped by two α-helices in a so-called α-helix/β-grip 
fold. The β-sheet works as a bowl and the C-terminal α-helix works as the lid. 
The structure of a STARD1, STARD3 and STARD5 with cholesterol have been proposed based 
on simulated docking and molecular dynamics. Therefore, no inhibitor structure is present in Figure 5. 
Based on the simulations, cholesterol is suggested to bind “head-first”. The cause of the differences in 
ligand specificity is still unknown for the STARDs.3 
 
Figure 5: (Left) Structure of the STARD1-START domain (PDB: 3P0L), folding into an α-helix/ β-grip fold globular 
structure, comprising of a curved β-sheet gripped (green) by two α-helices (pink and orange). The concave face of the β-
sheet and the C-terminal α-helix enclose a hydrophobic cavity for sterol binding. (Right) Representation of the sterol 
binding pocket in StAR (blue). Amino acids E169, F184, R188, L199, H220 and F267 seem to define the size of the pocket. 
Reproduced from Depta et. al.3

3.3 The Asters 
The Asters have three members: Aster-A, Aster-B and Aster-C. They all have an N-terminal GRAM 
domain, a StAR-like ASTER domain and a C-terminal transmembrane domain. The GRAM domain is 
used for detecting cholesterol and anionic lipids in membranes. The ASTER domain consists of seven 
β-sheets that curve around the binding site. A long α-helix works as a lid, similar to the StAR-domain. 
There is an extra volume in the pocket which is occupied by a glycerol. This pocket can be exploited 
when designing inhibitors. The Asters have been shown to have an important role absorbing HDL 
from the bloodstream8, so they might be useful targets for treatment of high cholesterol even though 
they are intracellular. 
The crystal structure of Aster-A and Aster-C has been solved with different ligands. Aster-A is the 
one showed in Figure 6 with the ligand 25-HC. Aster-C has been solved with a cholesterol derived 
inhibitor called AI-1l9. The structure of Aster-A is close to that of the StAR-domain but the subtle 
differences make the ligand bind in a rotated mode compared to what is expected in the StAR-domain. 
The exact amino acids making this difference are F405, Y524 and F525. They bind to the head group 
of the ligand, so similarly to ORPs, this pocket is important for selectivity.3 
 
Figure 6: (Left) Structure of the Aster domain of Aster-A in complex with 25-HC (PDB: 6GQF), consisting of a curved 
seven-stranded β-sheet that forms a hydrophobic groove for sterol binding, two short N-terminal α-helices and a long C-
terminal α-helix that closes the hydrophobic cavity. (Right) Representation of the 25-HC binding mode in Aster-A. F405, 
Y524 and F525 determine the ligand orientation, which are conserved in all three mammalian Aster proteins. Reproduced 
from Depta et. al.3

3.4 The State of STP Small Molecule Inhibitors 
The challenge of making useful STP modulators lies in the fact that STPs are structurally similar and 
thus it is challenging to develop selective inhibitors. Many of the current STP modulators have been 
discovered by chance and were not actively searched out. The inhibitors mentioned in this section are 
shown in Figure 7. 
Cephalostatin 1, ritterazine B, schweinfürthins and OSW-1 are natural products that were found to 
inhibit OSBP and ORP4L by Burgett et al.10 They named the group of inhibitors “ORPphilins”. The 
ORPphilins were previously known to inhibit tumour cell growth, thus inhibition of OSBP or ORP4L 
should reduce tumour growth. Later, Zhong et. al11 developed LYZ-81 based on OSW-1. LYZ-81 
specifically inhibits ORP4L and was demonstrated to eliminate leukemia stem cells. They compared 
the toxicity of OSW-1 and LYZ-81 towards normal cells and hypothesized that LYZ-81 was less toxic 
because it did not target OSBP. OSBP may therefore be unrelated to tumour formation. However, 
Péresse et. al12 found that selectively targeting OSBP inhibits cell growth. They used schweinfürthin 
G which has a 40-fold preference for OSBP over ORP4L. SWG also has fluorescent properties which 
makes it an excellent tool compound for tracking OSBP in cells. 
The amount of known small molecule modulators for STARDs is scarcer. That does not mean 
STARD inhibitors would not be useful. Many STARDs are for example known to be involved in 
cancer.13 One STARD3 inhibitor, VS1, identified by Lapillo et al.13 is the only inhibitor that targets a 
cholesterol transporting STARD. 
In The Asters it was mentioned that the crystal structure of Aster-C was solved with AI-1l. This 
inhibitor was presented by Xiao et. al9 and was found to be selective against Aster-C. They also found 
a pan-Aster inhibitor in the same work named AI-3d. The Laraia group found an inhibitor of Aster-A 
and (-)-Astercin 1 as an inhibitor of Aster-C by synthesizing a sterol-inspired library in recent work.14 
This is described in more detail in the Previous Work section. Finally, Laraia et. al15 used phenotypic 
screening to identify two acyl aminothiazole-based compounds (autogramins) as inhibitors of Aster-A 
and used them to demonstrate Aster-A’s role in autophagy.

While finding effective and specific inhibitors of STPs has potential for drug development, they

4 Library Synthesis Strategies 
When pharmaceutical companies are in the earliest stages of drug discovery, they study a 
pharmaceutical target and look for compounds that bind or modulate the activity of the target. With 
the rise of high throughput screening (HTS) the number of compounds that could be screened within a 
timeframe increased enormously. However, when more compounds were screened the expected 
amount of hits did not increase as expected. It was found that the size of the library screened was not 
nearly as important as diversity in the core structures.16  
When synthesizing a library of compounds it is usually advantageous to cover the chemical space 
which is defined as “all possible small organic molecules”.16 The amount of molecules that fulfil this 
criteria has been estimated to be ca. 1063.17 The chemical space can be described by principal moment 
==PAGE:23==
10 
 
of inertia (PMI) which orders compounds according to their overall shape with the extremes being 
benzene (disc), adamantane (sphere) and diacetylene (rod). An example of a PMI-plot is shown in 
Figure 12. Most synthetic compounds are on rod/disc side of the plot because the three-dimensionality 
of a sphere is difficult to synthesize. The difficulty stems from the sp3 carbons needed to introduce 
three-dimensionality into a compound. Inclusion of sp3 carbon centers often leads to chiral centers 
which make synthesis more challenging because a certain level of stereoselectivity is required in the 
reactions used.18 
To meet the demand for extended libraries a few strategies have been developed. These strategies 
are natural product (NP)-derived compound collections, diversity-oriented synthesis (DOS), biology-
oriented synthesis (BIOS) and pseudo-NP (PNP) compound collections. An important concept in all 
three methods is “privileged” scaffolds or structures. Privileged scaffolds are promiscuous yet 
selective when decorated with appropriate substituents. Different variations of decorated scaffolds 
results in products that bind to different target proteins.19 
4.1 Natural Product-Derived Libraries 
Natural products were once the only source of medicinal treatments.20 They are still an excellent 
source of drugs because they have evolved to have specific biological activity. NP-libraries can be 
made by using extracts from natural sources that are separated in fractions with columntography, 
called prefractionated libraries.20 Each fraction can then be tested directly for activity. If any hits show 
up, an effort is made to purify the fraction and deduct the structure of the compound.16 Another way to 
make a library is with purified NPs. The advantage is that it can be used in HTS directly, but it takes a 
very long time to set up and is resource intensive. If the compounds must be isolated, the amounts 
isolated are often very low. The compounds can alternatively be bought from external providers.20 
The problem with NP-inspired collections is that the resulting library is closely related to the 
source of inspiration and that NPs are difficult to synthesize. Even though all NP-structures are 
considered privileged, any analogues will only explore a fraction of the chemical space. This can still 
lead to a good hit rate on proteins that have the original NP as substrate because they have conserved 
structures. Some proteins have functions that are so essential that any significant change to their 
secondary structure would be detrimental.17 
4.2 Diversity-Oriented Synthesis 
Diversity oriented synthesis (DOS) seeks to spread the compound collection out in the chemical 
space. Where target-oriented synthesis (TOS) seeks to make one complex structure, DOS seeks to 
make many. As a result, TOS is convergent with synthetic paths meeting to make the target while 
DOS starts from one or more simple starting materials and spreads out.17 The concept was introduced 
by Stuart Schreiber and was inspired by the way random mutations are used in genetics.21 The 
build/couple/pair (B/C/P) approach (Figure 8) is the commonly used method for DOS.18 The build 
==PAGE:24==
11 
 
phase involves synthesis of fragments which contain functional groups that enable the next phases. In 
the couple phase the fragments are combined into one molecule. In the pair phase the functional 
groups are coupled in various ways. The result is a set of skeletons with diverse shapes.22 
 
Figure 8: How the B/C/P-method can yield diverse scaffolds. Adapted from Yi et. al.22 
To judge the diversity of a collection principal component analysis and cluster analysis can be 
employed to see how compounds group up. While DOS was initially used in drug discovery it has 
since found use in materials science.18 
4.3 Biology-Oriented Synthesis (BIOS) 
BIOS is an alternative to DOS that was introduced by Waldmann et. al.23 BIOS leads to more focused 
libraries with fewer compounds because it uses few NP scaffolds where DOS would use many 
synthetic scaffolds. This selectivity also often leads to similar activity as the source of inspiration, 
which can be an advantage if that is the objective. There exist two methods for finding a suitable NP 
scaffold to start from. One comes from structural classification of natural products (SCONP), called 
scaffold trees, and the other is protein structure similarity clustering (PSSC).17 
Scaffold trees are crafted from a large database such as the dictionary of natural products (DNP). A 
set of rules is set to filter through thousands of compounds. Then each compound is simplified, 
following another set of rules to create “parent” compounds. Each parent is compared, and one is 
chosen. This may result in multiple compounds from DNP having the same parent. These compounds 
are branching out from the same parent compound in the final tree. A section of a scaffold tree of the 
DNP is shown in Figure 9. Special rules can be added to for example maintain macrocycle 
structures.17 
==PAGE:25==
12 
 
 
Figure 9: Scaffold tree of NP-scaffolds. The scaffolds shown here only represent around 0.2% of the DNP. Reproduced 
from Kumar et. al.17 
In PSSC the PDB or similar is searched for similar proteins to the target protein. The search 
specifically targets the binding domain of proteins. Any interesting hits, especially ones with different 
amino acid sequences are closely inspected and added to the cluster. Then any proteins in the cluster 
can be investigated for known substrates and those substrates can inform the synthesis of a library. 
The two methods can also be combined. The known substrates from PSSC can be simplified in a 
scaffold tree to yield the central core of active compounds. 17 
4.4 Pseudo-Natural Products (PNPs) 
PNP libraries try to combine the best of NP-inspired libraries with DOS and/or BIOS. The term of 
PNP or NP-like spawned from the opportunities DOS, BIOS and other new methods provided in 
organic synthesis.20 As soon as the library primarily has compounds that are not present in biology yet 
shares characteristics with them, it can be called a PNP library. To achieve this either DOS or BIOS 
can be used as the underlying method. Synthesizing a PNP library gives the opportunity to combine 
NP-fragments that are not seen together in biology to achieve new types of privileged scaffolds.24 
==PAGE:26==
13 
 
There is a difference between PNP-derived and PNP-inspired compound collections. Derived 
collections use the same scaffold as the NP. The potential size of the library thus depends on the 
reactivity of the NP-scaffold. The inspired collection is, as the name suggests, only inspired by the 
NP, so the scaffold can have modifications. These modifications can enable more reaction types and 
thus a larger library.20 
In this project, a PNP-inspired library is synthesized with STPs as the intended target. Usually, it is 
advantageous to design three-dimensional structures instead of planar ones. However, since 
cholesterol itself is planar (Figure 10), it is meaningful to design the library compounds to have 
similar shape.

7.1 Scaffold Synthesis 
The scaffold must be synthesized first and from commercially available fragments. The best tool for 
figuring out a pathway is retrosynthetic analysis, shown in Scheme 1. A good starting point is 
realizing than 11 is not far from the product of a Robinson annulation. Before the Robinson 
annulation, a simple 1,4-addition will lead to commercially available fragments. All that is needed is a 
double bond and a ketone instead of an alcohol. The resulting annulation product is the Wieland-
Miescher ketone, known as an easy way to obtain a structure that imitates the AB sterol ring system. 
After the annulation one of the ketones must be reduced to an alcohol in a stereoselective manner. 
Here a protection and deprotection step must be introduced because the other ketone must be 
maintained. An acetal is a suitable protection group because it is stable against nucleophiles and 
bases. If the protection is performed before reducing the alkene, it can help the acetal with selectivity 
towards the correct ketone by stabilizing the other.28

7.1.1 Conjugate Addition with Enolate 
This procedure and the two following were presented by Whitmarsh-Everiss et. al14. The reaction was 
run at room temperature for 19 hours and resulted in a quantitative yield of 3 (Scheme 2). 
 
Scheme 2: Conditions used for conjugation with enolate. The yield was above 100% because the crude was used in the next 
reaction without further purification. 
This reaction is also called 1,4-addition or Michael addition. It is called 1,4 because the electrons 
go through four atoms on the electrophile. The alternative is 1,2-addition where the ketone carbon is 
attacked directly, and the electrons thus only move through two atoms. Direct addition is the kinetic 
product because the carbonyl carbon has a larger partial positive charge, which attracts the negative 
charge. The thermodynamic product is more stable than the kinetic product because the C=O bond is 
maintained and C=O bonds are stronger than C=C bonds. Therefore, it is important that the reaction is 
thermodynamically controlled. In this case the reaction was run at room temperature along with only 
catalytic amounts of triethylamine over 16 hours. Direct addition forms the kinetic product and will 
happen anyway, but that pathway is reversible, so over time all the starting material will have been 
converted to the desired, thermodynamic product.28 The mechanism is shown in Figure 14. 
 
Figure 14: Mechanism of conjugate addition with enolate28 
The enolate formed is stabilized by the ketones, so only a relatively weak base, here Et3N, is 
needed for deprotonation. The reaction is pulled towards the thermodynamic product by HSAB-
effects. Both carbons are soft and thus prefer each other. If the hard enolate oxygen attacked to 
carbonyl carbon it would form the unfavoured, reversible product. The reaction is also possible under 
acidic conditions.28

7.1.2 Synthesis of Wieland-Miescher Ketone via Robinson Annulation 
The reaction was run at 111°C for two hours and resulted in a 93% yield of 4 (Scheme 3). 
 
Scheme 3: Conditions used in the Robinson annulation 
The Robinson annulation is an intramolecular aldol reaction, resulting in cyclisation. The Wieland-
Miescher ketone was synthesized this way by Robinson himself and demonstrates the usefulness of 
the reaction since the ketone is an equivalent structure to the AB ring system of steroids. The 
mechanism is shown in Figure 15. 
 
Figure 15: Mechanism of the Robinson Annulation28 
Since the starting material has a symmetric ring, it does not matter with which ketone the enolate 
forms the ring. The reaction could be stopped at the tertiary alcohol but proceeds by E1cB elimination 
to form the conjugated ketone because of the conditions. Normally the elimination of hydroxide is not 
possible because of its high energy, making it a bad leaving group. However, the high stability of the 
formed conjugated system, pushes the elimination forward. Stronger bases and higher temperatures 
also push the reaction forward. In this case a Dean-Stark trap was also used. The Dean-Stark trap 
captured the water formed during the reaction, pushing the reaction to complete conversion. 
The aldol reaction could happen intermolecularly, but the 6-membered ring is small enough that 
the reaction partners are not too far from each other and large enough to be stable. The reaction also 
uses enamine as an intermediate instead of an enolate. Enamines are less reactive than enolates, so 
this also helps avoid any intermolecular reactions.28

7.1.3 Acetal Protection 
The reaction was run at room temperature for two hours and resulted in a 66% yield of 6 (Scheme 4). 
 
Scheme 4: Conditions used for acetal protection. 
The reaction is acid catalysed and reversible. The equilibrium is pushed to the right by the 
molecular sieves and the fact that a large equivalence of 5 was used (22.5 equiv.). The mechanism is 
shown in Figure 16. 
 
Figure 16: Mechanism of acetal protection28 
The protection is regioselective towards the unconjugated ketone because protecting the 
conjugated ketone would break the conjugated system. However, it is still necessary to use short 
reaction time and mild conditions to avoid double protection and protecting the wrong ketone. The 
slight selectivity is the reason why the protection group is placed before the C=C bond gets reduced. 
Acetals are resistant against nucleophiles, such as L-selectride. This reaction could also have been 
performed with a Dean-Stark trap as in the previous reaction. In this case, molecular sieves and large 
equivalence of ethylene glycol were used instead.

Palladium on carbon is a heterogenous catalyst where Pd sits on the surface of carbon particles. 
Ammonium formate is added to the reaction because it decomposes to NH3, CO2 and H2.30 The 
molecular hydrogen is adsorbed onto the palladium surface, creating a plane of hydrogen atoms. This 
makes the reaction stereoselective towards syn addition. However, in the case of 6 one should also 
consider the resulting orientation compared to the methyl group. The conformation will always have 
the saturated ring and methyl group pointing in opposite direction. The ring provides more steric 
hindrance, so the alkene will be reduced on the same side as the methyl group. One conformation of 6 
is shown in Figure 17. This explains the selectivity towards the cis-fused ring.28

7.1.5 Reduction with L-selectride 
The procedure was presented by Boev et. al.31 The reaction was run at -78°C for one hour followed by 
room temperature for one hour and resulted in a quantitative yield of 9 and 10 (Scheme 6). 
 
Scheme 6: Conditions used for the reduction of the ketone with L-selectride. 
Lithium tri-sec-butylborohydride (L-selectride) is a boron-based reduction agent. It performs the 
same reactions as sodium borohydride (NaBH4) except the isobutyl groups makes it bulkier. L-
selectride will prefer to attack the least hindered side of the ketone because of the substituents 
whereas NaBH4 would have little preference. The resulting relative stereochemistry of the alcohol is 
the opposite of the methyl and hydrogen in the joined rings. The cis-decalin will have a convex and 
concave face, illustrated in Figure 18. The less hindered side of the ketone is the convex face. In  
smaller molecules the selectivity of L-selectride can be explained by Felkin-Ahn models32, but 
here the molecule is large and bends enough to influence accessibility. 
==PAGE:34==
21 
 
 
Figure 18: L-selectride (left) prefers attacking the less hindered convex face 
The selectivity cannot be explained by equatorial/axial positioning because the rings are cis-fused. 
In trans-fused rings, each functional group is locked in its position as equatorial or axial. If the rings 
are trans-fused, both substituents where the rings meet must be axial and thus locks in the rest of the 
molecule. For cis-fused, one is axial, and the other is equatorial, and they can switch.

7.5 Fluorescence Polarization Assay 
The fluorescence polarization (FP) assay is a competitive binding assay53 used in high throughput 
screening (HTS).54 It uses the polarization of light to detect the inhibitory constant (Ki). If polarized 
light is shined on a fluorophore, the polarization of the emitted light is inversely proportional to the 
Brownian motion of the fluorophore. Brownian motion describes the rotation and speed thereof. Large 
complexes rotate slower in solution and thus polarize light less. This is used to detect binding between 
protein and substrate.53,54 The principle behind the assay as used in this project is shown in Figure 28. 
Cholesterol is bound to the fluorophore and when cholesterol is bonded to the transport proteins 
the light remains polarized. If the added analogue can compete with cholesterol, the polarisation will 
increase due to higher concentration of cholesterol in solution. The fluorophore is bound to 
cholesterol rather than the potential inhibitor because the fluorophore may influence the Ki.53 
 
Figure 28: The principle of the FP assay. A: When no competitor is present, cholesterol binds to the STP and the light is 
polarized. B: Cholesterol is outcompeted resulting in a loss of polarization. Green = fluorophore, orange = cholesterol, grey 
= STP, blue = compound. 
The results of the assay are shown in Table 6. Each result is a comparison of activity between 
cholesterol and the tested compound. A result of 1 means that there was no change in the amount of 
polarized light. Above 1 means the amount of polarized light decreased because cholesterol is 
outcompeted. Below 1 means the amount of polarized light increased. Any compounds giving results 
above 1 should be investigated for innate fluorescence. If they are not fluorescent, they bind to the 
protein in a way that improves cholesterol binding. The compounds that turn out as hits are tested at 
multiple concentrations to calculate IC50.

8 Conclusion and Future Work 
In this project a cholic-inspired cis-fused decalone scaffold was synthesized in six steps with 17% 
cumulative yield and a total of eight novel analogues were synthesized for a pseudo-natural product-
inspired compound library. Specifically, five thiazoles and three quinoxalines were made. 15e was 
unfortunately found to contain impurities to such a degree that a resynthesis will be necessary. 
It was attempted multiple times to synthesize an imidazopyridine but all reactions either failed or 
the product was lost during purification. Yet, the attempts at the imidazopyridine have laid the 
groundwork for future syntheses. As the next step, more solvents will be tested to see if a sweet-spot 
temperature can be found where the product does not break down and the reaction is not too slow. 
After that, alternative synthesis methods can be used. For example, imidazopyridine synthesis has 
been performed under no-solvent and no-catalyst conditions at 60°C with 91% yield.55 
A few more quinoxalines and other compound classes should be synthesized. After quinoxalines it 
was planned to synthesize benzofuranes and imidazothiodiazoles. Synthesis of some tetramic acid-
based analogues is also an option. Many of the compounds that contain tetramic acid combined with 
decaline are antibiotics.16 
No activity was found against the Aster proteins for the thiazoles. The quinoxalines still need to be 
tested against the Asters. However, it will be very interesting to see the results for the STARDs 
because cholic acid, the inspiration for the scaffold, was found to bind to STARD5. An assay will also 
be developed for testing the library against the ORPs. Once any hits are found, the project can move 
forward with those compounds. Once enough analogues are synthesized and tested valuable SAR-
information for the proteins will be revealed, hopefully leading to the development of useful drugs or 
tool compounds.
