Abstract
This thesis investigates the potential of Machine LearningВӯGuided Directed Evolution (MLDE)
in protein engineering, with a particular focus on enzymatic activity prediction. A compreВӯ
hensive exploration of MLDE workflows is presented, examining initial library construction
using zeroВӯshot models and testing multiple predictive sampling strategies. The signifiВӯ
cance of sequence representations and inductive biases in constructing machine learnВӯ
ing models is analyzed, identifying promising avenues for maximizing predictive power.
Results further emphasize the advantage of model ensembles over individual models,
providing a robust strategy for future protein engineering endeavors. The insights offered
by this work contributes to the emerging role of MLDE in advancing biotechnological and
sustainable applications by optimizing protein functions.

1
Introduction
Enzymes are versatile biocatalysts that play vital roles in biological systems and have
broad applications in industrial processes [1]. However, the deployment of enzymes in
industrial applications often faces limitations due to factors such as their stability, activity,
and functionality under nonВӯbiological conditions. Therefore, enzyme engineering, particВӯ
ularly the design, and modification of enzymes to perform specific functions or operate
under certain conditions, is an important area of research [1].
Despite significant progress in the field, predictably engineering enzymes with desired
properties remains a challenging task. This difficulty arises from the complex relationship
between an enzymeвҖҷs sequence, structure, and function. Fortunately, machine learnВӯ
ing, known for its capability to identify and learn patterns from large datasets, provides a
promising approach to address this issue [2].
In this thesis, we explore the integration of machine learning into enzyme engineering
through Machine LearningВӯGuided Directed Evolution (MLDE). MLDE combines machine
learning with directed evolution, aiming to enhance enzyme functions and thereby improve
their application in biotechnological processes [3]. In this work various stages of the MLDE
workflow will be investigated, from the initial library construction using zeroВӯshot models,
to variant sampling, and model training. Our goal is to identify strategies that can enhance
the predictive power and efficiency of machine learning models within this context.
The following questions will be addressed: What are the prevalent challenges in enzyme
engineering and how can machine learning assist in overcoming these? Which factors
influence the performance of machine learning models in predicting enzyme activity, and
how can these models be optimized for improved results? We will delve into these quesВӯ
tions, providing a thorough examination of machine learning applications in enzyme enВӯ
gineering and offering valuable insights that could inform future work in this field.
By exploring the potential and intricacies of MLDE, we aim to contribute to the field of
enzyme engineering and provide a foundation for further studies. We anticipate that the
insights and methods outlined in this thesis will serve as a steppingВӯstone toward harnessВӯ
ing the full potential of enzymes through machine learningВӯguided engineering. Through
this work, we take a step closer towards a future where the realms of machine learning
and biotechnology intertwine, to enable more efficient and versatile use of enzymes.

Theoretical Background
2.1
Enzymes
Enzymes, lifeвҖҷs catalysts, are biomolecules, typically proteins, that accelerate biochemical
reactions in living organisms. They function by reducing the activation energy barriers of
chemical reactions, thereby facilitating biochemical processes that would otherwise be
too slow to support life [4]. All biological reactions within human bodies, plants, animals,
bacterial cells, and viruses are mediated by enzymes. Hence, they play a pivotal role
in life processes, from simple ones like the digestion of food to complex ones like DNA
replication and energy production [5]. This section provides a highВӯlevel understanding
of enzymes, focusing on their structure, function, importance to life, and relevance in
biotechnological applications.
The basic building blocks of enzymes are amino acids, the sequences of which determine
the proteinвҖҷs unique structure and function. Protein sequences are like unique fingerВӯ
prints for proteins Вӯ even a small alteration can result in a completely different structure
and thus, possibly, a different function [5]. Protein structures are primarily divided into
four levels: primary, secondary, tertiary, and quaternary. The primary structure is the seВӯ
quence of amino acids, while the secondary structure refers to localized folding patterns
such as alphaВӯhelices and betaВӯsheets. The tertiary structure represents the overall threeВӯ
dimensional shape of the protein, and the quaternary structure, if present, describes the
assembly of multiple protein subunits [5]. The relationship between protein structure and
function, also known as the structureВӯfunction relationship, suggests that the function of a
protein is directly linked to its threeВӯdimensional structure [5]. This relationship is critical
as the intricate folding of a protein enables the formation of the active site, the region
of the enzyme where substrate binding and catalysis occur. Any alteration in this strucВӯ
ture can influence enzyme function. Thus, understanding this relationship is paramount
to protein engineering and enzyme catalysis. Enzymes are instrumental in maintaining
life. They mediate every metabolic process in an organism, from the breakdown of food
for energy production to the synthesis of DNA in cells. Enzymes work with exceptional
specificity, often interacting with only one or a few substrates, which they convert into
specific products. Their speed and specificity make them an irreplaceable component
in every cellular process [5]. The unique properties of enzymes make them valuable in
many biotechnological applications. Enzymes are used in various sectors, such as food,
pharmaceuticals, textiles, biofuels, and waste treatment. They are attractive for industrial
applications due to their high catalytic efficiency, selectivity, and ability to function under
mild conditions [4]. Besides, enzymes are biodegradable and nonВӯtoxic, making them a
sustainable alternative to traditional chemical catalysts. Their usage contributes to the
development of green technologies, thus aligning with the United NationsвҖҷ Sustainable
Development Goals [6]. Biotechnological applications often require tailored enzymes that
can function under specific conditions or catalyze reactions with improved efficiency. This
is where protein engineering, and machine learningВӯguided protein engineering, steps in
[7]. The understanding of the protein structureВӯfunction relationship and the prediction of
enzyme activity based on sequence information can lead to the design of novel enzymes
with desired characteristics, ultimately contributing to sustainability [1].

2.2
Principles of Catalysis
The study of catalysis can be divided into three domains Вӯ thermodynamics, kinetics, and
mechanism. The thermodynamics of a reaction describes the energetics of a chemical
reaction and is used to describe the system at equilibrium. Kinetics describes the change
of a system with respect to time. Both thermodynamics and kinetics are used to describe
the macroscopic aspects of a catalytic system. The mechanism describes the molecular
and electronic structure of a system and how it changes over time. This section is devoted
to understanding how enzymes work, how the properties of enzymes can be modeled, and
how they can be engineered.

2.3.1
Protein fitness landscape
The protein fitness landscape is a conceptual model that provides a comprehensive underВӯ
standing of the relationship between protein sequences, their corresponding structures,
and the biological function or вҖқfitnessвҖқ they confer to an organism. The protein fitness
landscape is envisioned as a vast, multiВӯdimensional space. Each point within this landВӯ
scape represents a unique protein sequence. The altitude of each point is determined by
the fitness of the protein, which can be measured in terms of its stability, and its ability to
carry out a particular function. One of the key features of protein fitness landscapes is their
ruggedness. This means they contain many local peaks and valleys. This ruggedness
reflects the complex interplay between the different amino acids in a protein sequence. A
mutation that is beneficial in one context may be detrimental in another, depending on the
rest of the sequence. Mutations in a protein sequence can be thought of as steps taken
on this landscape. Some mutations may lead uphill, towards higher fitness, while others
may lead downhill. The steepness of the slopes represents the magnitude of the fitness
effect of the mutations. Most sequences in the sequence space are completely inactive
and most mutations that can be introduced have detrimental effects on the protein fitness.
As a consequence, the protein fitness landscape is very difficult to model which makes
their optimization very difficult (figure 2.4) [12, 13].
Figure 2.4: Illustration of a protein fitness landscape (left) versus a more simple smooth
fitness landscape (right).
The fitness landscape of proteins is not only very rugged but also very vast. At any given
position in a protein sequence, one out of 20 amino acids can be chosen (when only natВӯ
ural amino acids are considered). This means, even for small proteins with a chain length

generate all possible amino acid combinations which would lead to the desired physical,
chemical, or biological properties 2.1.

many computational and experimental strategies have been developed to address this
challenge, which will be introduced in the following sections.
2.3.2
Directed evolution
Directed evolution is a method that is used in protein engineering, which mimics natural
evolution but aims to optimize the protein toward an artificial objective. Directed evolution
consists of iterative rounds of mutagenesis followed by screening and selection. In muВӯ
tagenesis gene variant libraries are usually created through random mutagenesis, either
over the entire length of the gene or a target area in the gene. In screening and selection,
the bestВӯperforming variants are identified and then further improved. Directed evolution
can be performed in living cells (in vivo) or with the enzyme in isolation (in vitro).
Directed evolution (DE) is a very effective method to optimize proteins and enzymes for
a target property, however, it also has its weaknesses. The goal of directed evolution is
to improve a single existing individual enzyme [14]. A drawback of the method is, that
it has not primarily been designed to generate large datasets for machine learning, and
collecting in large quantities would be expensive. Therefore, it is extremely difficult to use
the wealth of information that has been generated but not collected to build predictive
models for future experiments [7]. In directed evolution experiments usually, a few of the
bestВӯperforming variants are sequenced in each round, leading to low amounts of data
and a strong bias in the data to highВӯperforming variants [14].
2.3.3
Deep Mutational Scanning
Deep mutational scanning (DMS) is a method that aims to overcome the challenge of
data scarcity, by systematically mutating each position to every other possible amino acid

and measuring the effect of the mutation. Thus, deep mutational scanning provides a deВӯ

substitutions [15]. Deep mutational scanning is a technique requiring a coupled genotypeВӯ
phenotype platform like cellВӯbased assays or in vitro systems. This process begins with
synthesizing a library of gene variants which are then expressed in cells. The proteinвҖҷs
function in these cells is then selected for, promoting the enrichment of active variants
and depletion of inactive ones. After selection, the library is recovered, and each variВӯ
antвҖҷs frequency is determined by highВӯthroughput DNA sequencing. A variantвҖҷs function

read counts [15].
2.3.4
Computational protein engineering
Experimental methods for protein engineering have proven successful in improving the
activity of enzymes, however, experimental methods still suffer from the overwhelming
complexity of the protein fitness landscape. It is practically impossible to measure all
possible enzyme variants, due to the high number of possibilities [16]. Additionally, most
variants which can be created, are dysfunctional. As a consequence, randomly mutating
sequences will lead to a high proportion of variants being inactive, which makes the search
inefficient [12]. Ideally, we would like to be in a situation, where many of the variants we
assay are either better than the wild type or at least functional to a degree that we can
measure any activity. To do so we need to restrict the search space, by predicting which
mutations are likely to fulfill the aforementioned criteria [17]. In other words, we want to
create a variant library that both optimizes learning outcomes and the desired function
of our protein [18, 19]. Many different computational methods have been developed to
address the challenge of narrowing down the search space and predicting the mutational
effects on the function of a protein [20]. The methods can be divided into three paradigms,
which are physicsВӯbased methods, evolutionaryВӯbased methods, and machine learningВӯ
based methods. It is noteworthy, that most modern methods rely on a combination of two
or more paradigms.
PhysicsВӯbased methods
The most commonly used physicsВӯbased method in protein engineering is molecular dyВӯ
namics (MD) simulations [21]. In MD simulations, proteins are represented as a collection
of atoms or pseudo atoms, which follow the laws of classical Newtonian physics. The inВӯ
teractions of atoms in an MD simulation follow the laws of attraction, repulsion, and the
laws of motion as defined by Newtonian physics. Each atom in an MD simulation is paВӯ
rameterized with a force field, which governs the interactions of the atoms. Force fields
are usually parameterized through experimentally determined constants and theoretical,
quantum mechanical simulations. [22] The goal of MD simulations is to get insight into
the function of proteins on the basis of their dynamical properties [23]. Other physical
methods include quantum mechanical simulations, like density functional theory (DFT),
where quantum mechanical wave equations are approximated and solved to determine
the energy profiles of reactions. [24]
Evolutionary based methods
EvolutionaryВӯbased methods for protein engineering are concerned with identifying the
evolutionary patterns of an enzyme. The general assumption of evolutionaryВӯbased methВӯ
ods is, that if something occurs in nature very often, it is likely for a reason. The hypothesis
is, that residues which never or rarely change in evolutionary history are likely to be imВӯ
portant to function [25]. In evolutionary approaches, the choice to mutate a residue is
guided by information about evolutionary conservation and entropy of a position [26].

Combined methods: Rosetta

evolutionary methods. Structural patterns which occur many times likely have beneficial
features and future occurrences are also to possess the same properties [29].

BLOSUM (BLOcks SUbstitution Matrix) encoding is another commonly used form of enВӯ

knowledge into the encoding might therefore change the model performance.

tionarily variable regions, like surface exposed residues, have higher entropies, while
functionally important sites like ligand binding domains cluster in regions of the proteins
with low entropy [68].
ZeroВӯshot protein models are trained just as most other protein language models using the
masked language modeling objective [54] as described in chapter 2.4.5. ZeroВӯshot modВӯ
els have demonstrated comparable performance as specialized models for the prediction
of mutational effects [68]. In the context of machine learningВӯguided directed evolution,
we hope that zeroВӯshot models can be used to generate an initial library that either maxВӯ
imizes the proportion of functional or improved protein variants in the library or at least
maximizes the learning outcomes.

2.6.1
Sampling methods for library design
As mentioned in the previous section, zeroВӯshot models could be used to bias the library
design process toward a higher proportion of more active variants and a lower proportion
of inactive variants. This could be done by preferably sampling mutants with better zeroВӯ
shot scores.
The choice of sampling method plays a critical role in determining the quality of the initial
library. In the simplest approach, scores could be generated by a zeroВӯshot model, ranked
by their scores and then mutants could be picked from the top of the list. Another approach
could involve stochastic sampling, where all samples are represented as a probability
distribution. This could be done by applying a вҖқsoftmaxвҖқ function on the zeroВӯshot scores.
The bias during the sampling from this probability distribution could be controlled with a
temperature factor [70]. The latter approach will be referred to as temperature sampling.
In temperature sampling the zeroВӯshot scores are converted to a probability distribution
by applying a вҖқsoftmaxвҖқ function (eq. 2.35)
qi =
exp(zi/T)

(2.35)
Here, the probability qi вҲҲp(q) of a mutant zi is adjusted based on a temperature factor
T, which is usually set to T = 1 [70]. The temperature factor T plays a crucial role in
controlling the diversity of the sampled variants. When T is close to zero, the softmax
function essentially becomes a max function, and the variant with the highest score will
almost always be chosen. This makes the sampling process deterministic and reduces
the diversity of the sampled variants. Conversely, as T increases, the distribution beВӯ
comes more uniform, and the probability of sampling lowerВӯscoring variants increases,
thus increasing the diversity of the sampled variants.
Therefore, by adjusting the temperature factor, the tradeВӯoff between exploitation (biasing
the sampling process towards higherВӯscoring variants) and exploration (biasing towards
a more diverse set of variants) can be controlled. This allows us to tune the diversity of
the initial library, enabling a broader search of the sequence space while still maintaining
a preference for potentially more active variants as predicted by the zeroВӯshot model.

5
Discussion
The goal of this study to explore ML methods to predict the mutational effects on enВӯ
zyme activity and the use of zeroВӯshot models for library design. For the mutational effect
prediction, it was hypothesized that different encoding methods have different inductive
biases leading to different performances. The different sequence representations that
were used in this study can be divided into two categories: The latent space represenВӯ
tations or densityВӯbased methods such as the VAEВӯ and the LLMВӯbased representations,
and the individual amino acid encoding methods OHE and BLOSUMВӯencoding. These
sequence representations were analyzed and used to train SVR models to predict mutaВӯ
tional effects on enzyme activity. Additionally, model ensembles were built to utilize the
benefits of combining different models and sequence representations.
Finally, the predictions of zeroВӯshot models were analyzed for their application in library
design. The main points of the investigation were how zeroВӯshot prediction scores correВӯ
late with experimental results and how to sample variants based on zeroВӯshot scores to
construct libraries.
5.1
Amino acid representations
The amino acid encodings methods, OHE, BLOSUM50, and BLOSUM62, were analyzed
and visualized using tВӯSNE projections 4.4, which is a common method for visualizing
highВӯdimensional data. The method is used to identify patterns in data and to spot abnorВӯ
malities. The OHE includes no inductive bias about evolutionary conservation, while the
BLOSUM encodings reflect the exchangeability of residues. When analyzing the encodВӯ
ings using the dimensionality reduction method tВӯSNE, the encodings overall had similar
appearances. The representations seemed ordered, with points arranged in a circular
structure around a center. The biggest difference in the representations could be obВӯ
served between the BLOSUMВӯencoded projections and the OHEВӯencoded projections.
In the BLOSUM encodings have a jittering of points which is not present in the OHE
representations. This is likely due to the orthogonality of different mutants in the OHE
representations, compared to the more nuanced BLOSUM encodings. The orthogonality
of mutations in the OHE projection could lead to multiple or all mutations at a single poВӯ
sition to be projected onto a single point in the tВӯSNE plots. This would be different for
the BLOSUM encodings where all substitutions are different. The overall circular shape
of BLOSUM representation projection is likely due because, the strongest differentiating
signal in the data stems from the location of the mutations.
5.2
VAE training and representations
The VAEs were built to capture the inductive biases of an enzyme familyвҖҷs evolutionary hisВӯ
tory in the latent space representations, in contrast to the LLM representations which capВӯ
ture a wide evolutionary range across different enzyme families. The predictions based
on the VAE representations were worse than the other methods, although literature sugВӯ
gests that they should be at least equivalent to the other tested methods [18, 88]. This
is surprising because the model architecture, hyperparameters, and optimizers were only
slightly different from the implementation by Frazer et. al. The differences in this work
were in the hidden layers, and in the embedding dimension. The default parameters of
Frazer et. al for the hidden layers were 2000, 1000, and 300 hidden layer neurons for the
encoder and decoder and 50 neurons for the latent space representation. The dimensions

used in this study were 2048, 1024, and 256 hidden layer neurons in the encoder and deВӯ
coder and 64 neurons for the latent space representations. The remaining conditions, like
the optimizer and learning rate were identical. The training curves for the VAEs indicate
that more training steps would likely not significantly increase the performance. Due to
the flattening of the training curves, it is unlikely to expect that large performance gains
by training for more steps. However, it should be noted, that the final saved checkpoints
for the BLOSUMВӯencoded VAEs were saved in the late stages of the training. Therefore,
longer training might have led to slight improvements for the BLOSUM VAE models. The
small distance between the train and validation error also suggests no strong overfitting
of the models, and that the models generalize beyond the training dataset. However, the
total training loss per epoch for the BLOSUM VAEs are in the order 102, while the trainВӯ

magnitude in the loss, with the same model architecture and input dimensions, it is reaВӯ

more model parameters. The tВӯSNE projections of the representations also indicate a
loss of information in the BLOSUM encodings, which can be inferred from a loss of strucВӯ
ture in the visualization, as indicated by the strong overlap and absence of clusters in the
representation (figure 4.5).
5.3
ESM representations
LLMs were used to capture inductive biases over a wide evolutionary history across diВӯ
verse families of proteins. Using the LLMs protein sequences were transformed into fixedВӯ
sized sequence representations by passing the sequences through the LLM, extracting
the final layer output, and averaging the output over the sequence length. It was hypothВӯ
esized, that by utilizing general patterns observed across diverse protein evolutionary
history, nonВӯtrivial connections might be extracted from the sequence that could beneВӯ
fit mutational effect predictions. The LLM representations were also visualized using a
tВӯSNE projection. The projection revealed clusters in the tВӯSNE projections of shapes
and sizes that were not observed in the other representations. To investigate if these
clusters are connected to enzyme activity, the clusters were analyzed using a DBSCAN
algorithm. The analysis of the clusters did not indicate that the clusters are connected to
enzyme activity. Therefore, it was concluded, that clusteres are likely not connected enВӯ
zyme activity. Although some cluster tilt to higher scores than others, the differences were
generally small. Furthermore, no clear trend could be observed that could be generalized
across studies.
5.4
Enzyme activity prediction
5.4.1
Model training
SVR models were trained to predict mutational effects on enzyme activity using different
sequence representations. The models were trained in a grid search to find optimal model
parameters for each case. In the grid search the regularization term and the kernel of the
SVR models were modified. The largest differences in model parameters was observed
between the ESM representations and all other representations. The models trained on
ESM representation used only linear kernels, compared to the other representations which
mostly used nonВӯlinear kernels. This indicates that information about enzyme activity can
be linearly interpolated from LLM representations.
5.4.2
Comparing representations
The goal of using different sequence representations was to identify which inductive biВӯ
ases are most useful for predicting the mutational effects on enzyme activity. The differВӯ
ences in model performances based on different sequence representations suggest that

different inductive biases lead to differing results, although the differences are small. As
mentioned above, the VAEВӯbased encodings perform the worst out of all the tested repreВӯ

datasets that were used in this work. Out of the remaining four representations, the OHE
representations performed worst, however, the difference in performance between the

5.5
Model ensembles
Because models trained on different representations they can learn different patterns.
Therefore, it was explored if combining different models in an ensemble can increase
performance compared to using individual models. The model ensembles were created
using a simulated annealing algorithm, which randomly selects and deselects models
for inclusion or exclusion to the ensemble, followed by a probabilistic decision to either
accept or reject the change. A temperature constant was used in the algorithm to allow for
a slow change from more exploration in the beginning to more selective toward the end of
sampling. In the simulated annealing algorithm, the ensemble was validated on validation
data, which was not used for training, and the resulting ensembles were tested on test
data, which has neither been seen during training nor during validation. Interestingly the
ensembles outperformed the individual models in every case, suggesting a strong benefit
for using ensembles over individual models. Although, all previously mentioned individual
models were part of the sampling the VAE based models and the OHE based models were
never selected.
5.6
ZeroВӯshot predictions and library design
ZeroВӯshot predictions are used to estimate the mutational effects on protein activity withВӯ
out the need for prior data collection and training [68]. This makes zeroВӯshot models an
interesting method for designing initial libraries in MLDE workflows. In this work LLMs
were used to generate the zeroВӯshot scored based on masked sequence modeling. MuВӯ
tants were scored using the masked marginal probability (MMP) of a mutation according
to equation 3.3 proposed by Meier et al. [68]. It was observed, that the zeroВӯshot scores
had a positive correlation with experimentally determined scores. Further characterization
of the predictions revealed more detailed strategies for library design.
5.6.1
Sampling and library design
Here, different sampling methods were compared to determine the best strategies for difВӯ
ferent circumstances. In this work random sampling, semiВӯrandom sampling, and deterВӯ
ministic sampling methods were compared. It was shown, that random sampling generally
leads to worse libraries than libraries informed by zeroВӯshot models. However, it was also
observed, that the best performing variants can be found across a wide range of zeroВӯshot
Prediction of enzyme sequenceВӯactivity relationship using directed evolution guided by machine learning 49
==PAGE:60==
scores, which are often the most interesting for later applications. Therefore, sampling
methods should be flexible enough to sample as many of the top performing variants as
possible. At the same time, reducing the number of inactive variants, the effective library
size is increased. Because the top performing variants are distributed across a wide range
of zeroВӯshot scores, which favours some random sampling, and the goal of increasing the
overall activity of the library a balance between random sampling and a to strict sampling
bias was investigated. The strongest sampling bias was deterministic sampling, where
mutants were scored, sorted by their score and the libraries were constructed by taking
the top N predictions. The balanced sampling was done by transforming the zeroВӯshot
prediction scores into a probability distribution to sample from using a softmax function.
By varying the temperature factor in softmax function, the randomness (bias) of the distriВӯ
bution could be varied. It was shown that the strong bias approach leads to the most active
libraries, judging by the mean activity of the libraries and the distribution indicated by the
boxplots in figure 4.16. For the balanced sampling approach two different results were
obtained, by either including or excluding a study, which could be regarded as an outlier.
The excluded study was concluded to be a potential outlier due to discrete experimental
scores and a strongly different behaviour druting sampling (figure 3.4). By including the
outlier study during sampling, a temperature factor greater than 1 was optimal, indicating
that some stochasticity is beneficial. However, when excluding the the outlier study, high
bias was preferred as indicated by a temperature factor below 1. This trend indicates,
that a strong bias based on zeroВӯshot scores is beneficial. Because it is unclear if the
study should be regarded as an outlier or not, both results were provided. The biased
strongly biased sampling approaches lead to more active libraries, but potentially miss
critically important variants. This makes the sampling approach exploitative, with the goal
of finding as many positive hits as possible. The more random sampling method on the
other hand is a more explorative approach, risking an overall less active variant for the
possibility of finding outliers. How and when to use any of the two sampling methods
should be decided on a project to project basis.
Finally, it is important to mention the practical limitations of library synthesis, although they
have not explicitly been addressed in this work. Synthesizing specific genes is expensive
and can only be accomplished for very small libraries. Libraries for directed evolution are
therefore usually synthesized using either random mutagenesis, combinatorial methods
of library synthesis that offer more control [2]. An example of how optimal library design
under the constraints of library synthesis can be achieved is addressed by Yang et al.
[19].

Conclusion
The focus of this work was the exploration of machine learningВӯguided directed evoluВӯ
tion (MLDE) methods, which can be devided into four stages: Initial library design and
sytnhesis, library testing, model training, and modelВӯguided library design. Here, all MLDE
stages exceot the experimental library testing stage were addressed. The methods of this
work were tested on enzymatic DMS datasets. The first stage of the MLDE workflow Вӯ the
initial library construction Вӯ was addressed by using zeroВӯshot models to predict mutational
effects without the need for experimental data. Here the goal was to explore how these
predictions can be used to generate more active libraries and to develop sampling strateВӯ
gies. The important theme of this stage was to strike a balance between exploration and
exploitation, using highВӯ, mediumВӯ and lowВӯbias methods. It was concluded, that random
sampling is likely not a good strategie to follow, because to many inactive variants will be
sampled. Depending on the project constraints stronger bias should be considered during
the library design stage. Genereally, the sampling bias should be decreased if the goal
of the project is exploration and kept high if the main goal is exploitation. After the initial
library design stage, data can be collected by testing variants of the library. The data is
then used to build ML models, which take the function of the zeroВӯshot for subsequent
library design. It was determined, that the performance of these models differ for different
mathematical representations of protein sequences. It was found that both models trained
on density based representation and on encodings of individual amino acids can perform
similarly well. The best performing methods were the LLM representations and the BLOВӯ
SUM encodings, and the VAEВӯbased representations and OHE performed worst. The low
performance of the VAEВӯbased representations, was surprising due to literature accounts,
which might be explained due to subtle differences in the model implementation or data
used in this work. However, these results at least suggests, that it can be more difficult
compared to other methods. In addition, it was explored if the combination of different
models and representations in an ensemble leads to higher perfomrances, compared to
using individual models. It was found that, model ensembles outperformed the individual
models in every case tested in this work. This suggests, that using model enselbles is
favourable over using individual models. It was observed that the LLM was used in every
ensemble, BLOSUM encodings were used in some ensembles and OHE and VAE based
methods were entirely absent. This suggests, that it might not be necessary to train these
models in future projects.

Outlook
The results of this work suggest, using zeroВӯshot models can be used to filter out inactive
variants which increase the effective library size. Additionally it was demonstrated that
combining different encoding methods and models in ensembles boosts the performance
of mutational effect predictors. The application of zeroВӯshot models and mutational effect
predictors should benefit future MLDE projects. However, future work should focus on
both on improving the predictive capabilities of mutational effect predictors, and to address
practical limitations of library synthesis. In the last years, the improvements in predictive
algorithms has increased only very slowly [18, 91]. Although a wide range of different ML
methods have been tested over the years, it seems that all of the tested methods perform
very similarly. In my hypotheses for this apparent lack of disruptive progress is discussed,
in addition to hypotheses on how to overcome these challenges.
In some regard, it is unintuitive to assume that zeroВӯshot models are capable of predicting
mutational effects on enzyme function, because the models are agnostic to the engineerВӯ
ing goal envisioned by the designer. Because, the models are unaware of the desired
new function, it is reasonable to assume that the improvements that were correctly preВӯ
dicted are not directly related to the proteins function, but instead improvements of general
properties. These could for example be improved folding, expression levels or stability.
It would therefore be interesting to collect data which measures multiple properties, inВӯ
stead of reducing activity to a single number. The additional information could lead to an
increased understanding of mutational effects on a finer level of detail, by connecting the
mutational effect to more isolated, simpler properties. For example, a recent review article
for example investigated the effects of loop dynamics in enzyme evolution and its effects
on enzyme activity [92]. Predicting mutations which increase or decrease the flexibility of
loops could for example be more easily predicted than macroscopic and complex properВӯ
ties like enzyme activity. Such simpler properties could also greatly benefit from physical
and chemical simulations, reducing the requirement for experimentation. For example,
by understanding the equilibrium conformations of enzymes, for example by catalytically
active conformations could be determined [93, 94, 95]. ML algorithms could then be used
to sample the mutational space which stabilizes the active conformations over the inactive
conformations.
In summary, ML methods for mutational effect prediction seem to have reached a plateau.
Advancing our fundamental understanding of the enzymes and catalysis could lead to
better performing models, by breaking down the complex problem of enzyme activity into
more isolated, easier problems. Such approaches could be especially successful due to
the vast array of physical and chemical simulation methods for data generation.
