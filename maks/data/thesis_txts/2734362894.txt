Abstract
With an increasing demand to find novel alternatives to traditional fossil­based pathways of pro­
ducing various materials, chemicals and other products, fermentation has been gaining a lot of
attention in recent years. This work aims to contribute to the research in the area of fermenta­
tion by investigating a numerical CFD model of two most common bioreactor set­ups ­ stirred
tank reactor (STR) and bubble column ­ and validating the simulation outcomes against experi­
ments. A numerical model of a 24 L two­phase bioreactor with a working volume of 15 L of water
sparged with air was created for both reactor set­ups.The following parameters were monitored
throughout the simulations: residuals, Courant number, torque, air inflow and outflow, air hold­
up, mixing time, liquid velocity profile and the air volume fraction profile. Furthermore, mesh
and timestep studies were carried out on the STR configuration, as well as the investigation of
different turbulence models, bubble size distributions and interphase force models. Moreover,
both reactor configurations were also used in lab experiments where the observed parameters
were: bubble size, volumetric mass transfer coefficient, air hold­up and mixing time. For the
STR configuration, the chosen impeller speed values were 300, 400, 500 and 600 rpm and
the aeration rates were 4 and 5 NL/min. For the bubble column configuration, two different dif­
fusers with a different pore size were used, and the aeration rate of 2 and 4 NL/min was applied.
The CFD model generated in Ansys CFX which predicted the flow behavior the best was the
one with 167,984 cells for the rotor domain and 781,522 cells for the stator domain, timestep of
0.004 s, standard k −ϵ turbulence model, Ishii­Zuber drag force model, and a constant bubble
size of DB = 3.9 mm determined in the experiments. The simulation was in agreement with
visual observations of the STR in terms of the velocity profile and the air volume fraction distribu­
tion profile. Moreover, the reported mixing time of 4.2 s was comparable to the experimentally
determined mixing time of 3.2 s. Likewise, the simulation air hold­up of 0.0078 also corre­
sponded to the experimental hold­up of 0.0079 measured by a modified inclined tube method.
Other simulations were typically in agreement in most criteria with the experiments except for
the mixing time which was either underestimated for the bubble column set­up or overestimated
for the STR set­up. At the end, the limitations of both the simulations and the experiments are
discussed and possible improvements and future work are proposed.

1
Introduction
Bioprocesses such as fermentation, microbial biocatalysis or enzymatic catalysis have been
utilized by humans for thousands of years. The first documented use of bioprocesses is the
production of fermented food products such as bread, cheese and alcoholic beverages, eg.
beer. Naturally, with the scientific progress in the fields of enzymology, molecular biology, bio­
catalysis and many others, the bioprocesses have become more complex allowing their use in
other industries than exclusively food production and preservation, namely for example: phar­
maceutical or chemical industry. Let us just consider that a non­negligible portion of commercial
products such as alcohol, organic solvents, fermented food products or smaller, more expen­
sive chemicals like vaccines or antibiotics could not be produced in such variety and amounts
nowadays without fermentation and other bioprocesses. [1], [2], [3]
Besides the traditional uses of bio­based approaches and technologies, it is believed that fer­
mentation and other bioreactions could also prove to be very useful in a partial or full replace­
ment of established fossil­based pathways of synthesis of chemicals, fuels and materials. The
aspiration to bypass the optimized and established fossil­based routes in chemical engineering
is driven by the increasingly alarming climate change­related issues and the consequent need
to develop more sustainable alternatives to many of the current processes and technologies.
[1], [4]
In the following chapters, main focus will be on the fermentation rather than other bioprocesses
such as biocatalysis. The most important difference that distinguishes fermentation from other
bioprocesses is the dependence of the production of desired molecules as well as byproducts
on the cell growth. Conversely, in biocatalysis, the growth of the catalyst (cells) is separated
from the catalytic reaction itself. [4], [5]
Some fermentation processes have already reached industrial scale. Examples are brewing,
cheese and yoghurt production, wastewater treatment, manufacture of bulk organic chemicals
such as acetone and butanol or vitamines (B12) and antibiotics (eg. penicillins) and many others.
Nevertheless, there are still many challenges that prevent even larger spread of fermentation
on the industrial scale nowadays. One of the biggest obstacles is the scale­up. In many cases,
the efforts to reproduce the otherwise promising lab­scale experimental results on the industrial
scale often fail. Typically, the problem lies in the trade­off between power input and mixing effi­
ciency. As discussed later in more detail, efficient mixing is absolutely crucial in many industrial
fermenters in order to distribute nutrients as well as heat uniformly and in fast manner across
the reactor. In lab­scale reactors, efficient mixing is often achievable without significant power
requirements, however, when the same mixing times and efficiency is expected in larger reac­
tors, the power requirements may very easily become unrealistic. Therefore, it is of the utmost
importance to scrutinize the processes on the smaller scale before upscaling them, and then try
to find solutions which reduce the power inputs while maintaining the required mixing criteria.
[3], [6], [7], [8], [9]
One of the most commonly used bioreactor configurations is the stirred tank reactor (STR).
The difference between STRs and bubble columns (the other most common bioreactor config­
uration) is the presence of mechanical stirrers in the case of STRs. On the contrary, in bubble
Numerical simulations of multiphase reactors and their experimental validation

==PAGE:18==
columns, there is no mechanical agitator and the mixing and aeration takes place simultaneously
by means of gas sparging. In both cases, when sparged with air, the presence of multiphase
flow makes the hydrodynamics inside the bioreactors complicated. [3], [6]
Addressing the issue of complex multiphase flow hydrodynamics in STRs and the related mixing
efficiency and gas­liquid mass transfer has been difficult due to the lack of precise and reliable
measurement and validation techniques. However, in recent years, thanks to the progress in
numerous scientific fields such as optics, physical and analytical chemistry or computer science,
new methods and techniques that may help us gain more knowledge on the multiphase flows
in STRs have been developed. One of the innovative and novel approaches is the coupling of
in situ measurements related to the mass transfer parameters (eg. bubble sizes and their dis­
tribution, dissolved oxygen concentration, gas holdup etc.) with computational fluid dynamics
(CFD). CFD has proven to be particularly practical in determination of fluid dynamics in STRs
and its characteristics, mixing and bubble dispersion and break­up efficiency as well as in im­
proving the design of STRs and their up­scaling or down­scaling. [6], [7], [10], [11]
The goal of this project is to firstly summarize the basics of the current knowledge in the topics
of fluid flow, common STR configurations, mass transfer and the fundamental CFD theory. Sec­
ondly, an experimental set­up of two most common bioreactor configurations ­ STR and bubble
column ­ is presented with the intention to introduce the ways of measuring some of the flow
parameters such as the air hold­up, characteristic bubble size, volumetric mass transfer coef­
ficient, and the mixing time. The focus is on the STR configuration; nevertheless, the shown
bubble column measurements serve as additional complementary experiments aiming to intro­
duce an alternative to STRs rather than a thorough investigation of the flow behavior in bubble
columns. Thirdly, the CFD simulations run in CFX Fluent are presented. CFD simulations for
both bioreactor configurations are proposed as a possibility to replace or complement often
cumbersome and time­consuming experiments. Finally, the two methods of acquiring informa­
tion on fluid behavior in lab­scale bioreactors ­ experiments and simulations ­ are compared
to one another with the aim to validate and assess the feasibility of CFD simulations. Further­
more, the advantages and limitations of both the experimental approach and CFD simulations
are discussed and possible pathways for future work are discussed.

2.1
Fluid flow and stirred tank reactor configurations
In this section, firstly, the basic theory of fluids in motion, turbulence and viscosity will be dis­

2.1.1
Characteristics of a fluid flows
When describing and modelling fluid flows, the basic concepts related to viscosity, momentum
transfer and its analogy with heat and species transfer, hydrodynamic boundary layers and their
separation cannot be avoided. However, firstly, we should discuss what the main characteris­
tics that distinguishes fluids from solids is. A conventionally agreed parameter which draws
the line between solids and fluids is their reaction to a shear force. By definition, unlike solids,
fluids undergo a certain degree of continuous deformation upon the application of a shear force
without retaking their original shape. [3]
To get better understanding of how fluids react to a shear force, let us consider the following
situation: a fluid is filling up the space between two parallel plates. One of the plates is stationary
and the other one is set to motion with velocity v. We will observe a development of a fluid flow
between these two plates. If we assume a laminar flow (turbulence is described later in this
section), after reaching steady state, the velocity profile as shown in Figure 2.1 is achieved. [3],

as the flow in STRs is typically of turbulent character. [3], [12]
There are several characteristics that distinguish the laminar flow from the turbulent flow. Unlike
laminar flow, turbulent flow is in its nature a disordered, irreproducible and three­dimensional
phenomenon accompanied by a formation of eddies of various scales. Eddies are flow struc­
tures which contain most of the kinetic energy of the turbulent flow. Typically, the initially formed
large eddies are broken down into smaller and smaller ones. At the end, when the eddies are
too small to keep their swirling rotational movement, their energy is dissipated into heat through
friction. [3], [14], [15]
In STRs, there is a large number of eddies of various sizes ­ the biggest ones are of the order
of magnitude of the diameter of the vessel, whereas, the smallest ones, depending on the fluid
viscosity, have their characteristic lengths typically in the µm scale. This means that in STRs,
there is a coexistence of eddies ranging in their sizes over up to five orders of magnitude. Ini­
tially, the largest eddies gain their kinetic energy from the bulk flow, and then they transfer it
through the process called energy cascade to the smaller eddies. This process involving vortex
stretching is responsible for the energy transfer from the largest eddies down to the smallest
ones which in turn dissipate the received energy in form of heat. Note that the smallest ed­
dies are assumed to be fully independent of the mean flow and any boundary conditions of the
system. The assumptions of energy cascade, heat dissipation on the smaller scales and the in­
dependence of small eddies on the external conditions gave rise to the so­called Kolmogorov’s
universal equilibrium theory. This theory postulates that neglecting the energy losses of the
energy cascade, the power supplied by the impeller in STRs is at the end dissipated from the
smallest eddies. Based on this theory, the following equation for the size of the smallest eddies
was proposed [3], [14], [15]:

One very important parameter which describes the transition between the laminar and turbulent
flow is the dimensionless criterion called the Reynolds number. It is defined as the ratio between
inertial forces and viscous forces in fluids. When the Reynolds number is small, the viscous
forces are relatively dominant compared to the inertial forces meaning that fluid particles have a
tendency to maintain their streamline motion without changing the direction. On the other hand,
when the Reynolds number is relatively large, the inertial forces are predominant, meaning that
fluid particles have a tendency to change a direction when their path is even slightly disturbed.

because of primary disturbances in the flow caused by, for example, increased stirrer speed,
velocity starts to fluctuate in various directions. These fluctuations are accompanied by the cre­

transition regime. As the Rei increases, the vortices become more and more unstable and start
to interact with one another leading to local turbulence regions with unpredictable presence of
unstable eddies. Consequently, these turbulent regions become more and more frequent with
increasing Rei up to the point that the turbulent flow is developed. [3], [14], [15]

Another phenomenon associated with a fluid flow and its relevance for STRs is the concept of
the hydrodynamic, boundary layers and their separation. In fluid flows in presence of a solid
object, such as the case for STRs, a hydrodynamic boundary layer is an imaginary thickness of
the fluid where the flow is ’affected’ by the presence of the solid object. If we assume no −slip
conditions, meaning that the fluid particles adhere to the solid surface, due to the viscous drag,
the fluid layers adjacent to the wall are slowed down. With increasing distance from the solid
surface, the flow field is becoming less affected by its presence and the flow velocity is practi­
cally the same as the one of the bulk flow. [14] [16], [17]
When the boundary layer if formed, it has typically a laminar character, however, as the flow
develops, depending on Re, it can evolve into a turbulent boundary layer. In both cases, its
thickness is growing as the flow develops until it reaches its final thickness which is dependant
on the Re. The formation of boundary layers and the associated velocity gradient throughout
its thickness plays an important role in heat and mass transfer processes, as is expressed in
some of the dimensionless criteria such as the Sherwood number which relates the overall mass
transfer to the mass transfer through the boundary layer. Analogy to the Sherwood number for
the heat transfer processes is the Nusselt number. It is hence apparent that there is high rele­
vance in boundary layers when studying fermentation processes. [14] [16]

large enough, the boundary layer separation occurs, as is shown in Figure 2.3. It is associated
with an energy­expensive generation of a wake and related eddies and vortices. Due to the
losses of energy of the bulk flow, boundary layer separation is often considered an undesirable
phenomenon. On the other hand, in STRs, it promotes turbulence and the associated enhanced
mass transfer and may be in fact of advantage. [14], [16]

2.1.2
Mixing and typical stirred tank reactor configurations
Mixing is one of the crucial unit operations not only in fermentation but also in other chemical
and biochemical production processes. Very often, mixing takes place in STRs and its purpose
is, among others, to [14], [19]:
• Blend homogeneous solutions of sugars, oils and many other chemicals.
• Disperse gas in liquids for fermentation, absorption, stripping, oxidation, etc.
• Suspend solids and maintain the suspension of solids such as cells, etc.
• Homogenize, blend or emulsify miscible, partially miscible or immiscible liquids.
• Promote and enhance the heat transfer.
The use of STRs is a common practise in fermentation processes which explains why they were
the main focus are in this work. Typical STRs are vertical cylindrical vessels, even though, hor­
izontal vessels are also sometimes used. Mixing typically takes place by means of an impeller
mounted on an impeller shaft. The shaft is connected to a stirrer motor. Another important
component of STRs are the so­called baffles which are metal stripes attached to the side walls
of the tank. Their purpose is to prevent a creation of a vortex structure called the fluid swirl
and promote vertical flows in the liquid. And at last but not least, when gas is introduced to the
liquid system, sparger is installed in STRs in order to serve as the main gas inlet. [14], [19], [20]
In figure Figure 2.4, the conventional STR geometrical configuration is shown. The bottom of
the mixing vessels is typically dished or round rather than flat in order to prevent creation of
stagnant zones around sharp edges. The volume of STRs can vary significantly, ranging from
units of litres to tens of m3. Nevertheless, there are some geometrical specs which are com­
mon for most of the STRs regardless of the volume. Typically, the ratio between the impeller
diameter Di and the tank diameter DT is 1/4 to 1/2. Moreover, the liquid height HL is ideally
reaching between 1.0 to 1.25 of DT to ensure efficient mixing. In the cases where, for practical
reasons, this aspect ratio (HL/DT ) is too high (typically approx. HL/DT > 5), more than one
impeller is installed to maintain good­quality mixing. [14], [19]

2.2
Mass transfer theory
In the following chapter, mass transfer and the physics behind is discussed with particular focus
on two­film theory, convective mass transfer, volumetric mass transfer coefficient and oxygen
transfer from bubbles to liquid broths. Furthermore, the equipment and methodology used to
measure bubble flows, mass transfer coefficient, gas holdup and mixing time in STRs is de­
scribed.
Mass transfer is defined as a net transport of species in a mixture from one place to another
by virtue of a concentration gradient. This inherently implies that mass transfer only occurs
when there is a certain degree of concentration variation in a mixture. Furthermore, in fermen­
tation processes, mass transfer is frequently taking place over interphases between two or more
phases. Typical example of mass transfer over an interphase in a fermentation is the oxygen
supply to cell cultures. [14], [21]
There are two different common mechanisms of a mass transfer in bioprocesses: molecular
diffusion and convective mass transfer. Unlike the convective mass transfer, molecular dif­
fusion occurs in a presence of concentration gradients, irrespective of the fluid bulk motion,
moreover, it is a very slow process. Therefore, in most situations, diffusion is not sufficient to
ensure effective mixing in bioprocesses. For this reason, agitation, increased turbulence, dis­
persion etc. related to convective mass transfer are typically necessary for satisfactory mass
transfer within fluid mixtures. [14], [21]
2.2.1
Molecular diffusion
Despite the fact that diffusion is usually accompanied by the convective mass transfer, it has
also its importance in bioprocesses. One example is a mass transfer within the smallest eddies
on the Kolmogorov’s scale. The smallest turbulent eddies follow the streamline laminar motion,
therefore, the mass transfer on the scale of those smallest eddies is typically taking place due
to the molecular diffusion. Another example of the molecular diffusion in a fermentation is the
mass transfer across liquid­liquid, solid­liquid or gas­liquid interphases. The velocity of fluid par­
ticles very close to interphases is typically low ­ as mentioned in subsection 2.1.1, sometimes
even assumed to be zero in the case of a no­slip condition ­ therefore, the mass transfer across
those phase boundaries also takes place by virtue of the molecular diffusion. [14]

the net mass transfer of a species A occurs due to the presence of a concentration gradient of

2.3
Computation fluid dynamics theory
Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) is a relatively new tool used in applications related to fluid
flows. It is an alternative to fluid­related physical experiments since the full experimental de­
termination of the fluid flow behaviour can be an extremely challenging task. The relevance of
CFD has increased significantly with an improved computational efficiency and accessibility of
high­speed computers in recent years. In essence, CFD simulates numerically fluid motion as
well as other variables related to heat transfer, species transport, etc. and even though, it is of­
ten used to replace experiments, when complemented by them or validated against lab results,
it has a large potential in many scientific and industrial fields. [6], [14], [28]

2.3.2
Numerical solver
Navier­Stokes equations are partial differential equations in space and time and even though
they describe a continuous fluid motion, in order to provide a numerical solution, they need to be
discretized both in space and time. CFD software breaks down the investigated fluid region into
smaller control volumes and transforms continuous equations into discontinuous. This process
is called a discretization and it is done prior to all CFD simulations. [19]
The discretization of the fluid region into control volumes sometimes also referred to as subdo­
mains or cells, a grid is used. The process of grid generation is called meshing and the final grid
is called a mesh. Mesh elements can take different shapes ­ typically tetrahedral, hexahedral,
prism or pyramid. Very often, the mesh is not consisting of one element shape but of multi­
ple. Such meshes are called hybrid meshes. Two further mesh classes can be distinguished:
structured and unstructured meshes. The former are characterized by their uniformity and reg­
ularity of the repeating grid pattern, the later are less uniform and the grid pattern is arbitrary. [19]
The mesh should be fine enough to resolve the flow as much as possible but not unneces­
sarily too fine to increase the computational time excessively. Typically, the mesh density is
non­uniform, meaning that at certain specific fluid regions, there are more mesh elements com­
pared to the remaining part of the fluid. For laminar flow, the mesh should be finer around the
boundaries and for turbulent flows, the mesh should be finer in the near­wall regions. On top of
that, for turbulent flows, the wall functions are typically used. These functions are based on the
assumption that directly adjacent to the wall, there is a narrow viscous sublayer next to which is
the broader fully developed turbulent layer. Moreover, in the case of STRs, it is advisable that
the grid is more refined and finer in the inlet regions, around the impellers and near baffles. [19]

pressure­based solver, the pressure field is calculated directly from the continuity and momen­

field and the pressure field is calculated from the equation of state. [13]

until the error is as small as predetermined, then we call the result a converged solution. [19] [34]

2.3.3
Turbulence models
One of the biggest challenges in most of the CFD studies is an accurate simulation of turbu­
lence. The difficulty lies in the unsteady, 3D behavior of turbulent flows with a variety of different
time scales and length scales. It is particularly difficult to resolve those scales in time and space
accurately and the demands on the computational power are too large for most of the turbulent
flows. There are two ways of dealing with turbulence ­ one is not to resolve the smallest eddies
in Navier­Stokes equations and the second is the time averaging of the governing equations.
Both approaches will be discussed in the following paragraphs with a focus on time­averaged
governing equations. [3] , [13], [19], [34]
In certain situations with high geometrical simplicity and low Re, the Navier­Stokes equations
can be resolved fully in time and space in the so­called Direct numerical simulation, DNS.
The solutions from DNS are the most accurate, nevertheless, due to extreme demands on com­
putational power of today’s computers, it cannot be used in most cases, including STRs. [3] ,
[13], [19], [34]
Another approach is the so­called Large eddy simulation, LES. Unlike DNS, LES only solves
the governing equations for the larger scales. The smaller scales are compensated for in the
equation of motion by additional terms. Such simplifications make LES less accurate than DNS,
however, the demands on the computer CPU are way lower. LES is particularly suitable for sim­
ulations where boundary layers are not extremely relevant for the overall flow behavior. The
reason is that LES operates with coarser grids and when the mesh is too fine in certain regions,
even LES becomes too much computationally demanding. [3] , [13], [19], [34]
Even though, both DNS and LES represent high­accurate turbulence models, in most CFD mod­
els with higher Re, further simplifications are usually necessary to solve the governing equations
in a reasonable time. One big group of turbulence models which is commonly used in CFD sim­
ulations with turbulence are Reynolds−Averaged Navier−Stokes equations models. They are
particularly useful when all of the turbulent fluctuations do not need to be fully resolved, hence
when only the mean flow behaviour is of interest. The meshes can therefore be coarser since
only the changes in the mean flow are expected to be captured. [3] , [13], [19], [34]

accurate results for free shear flows such as eg. the jet flow. The reason behind is the high

boundary layers. A part of this obstacle is overcome in the baseline (BSL) k −ω model which
implements the standard k −ω model in the boundary layers and then slowly transitions into
the k −ϵ model further in the free stream region. Even more advanced is the shear −stress
transport, SST, k −ω model which is just a further improvement of the BSL model by the mod­
ification of the term for turbulent viscosity µT so that it also includes the turbulent shear stress
transport. [13]

simulations, etc. Nevertheless, the above­mentioned two­equation RANS models are typically
the most often used in simulations related to multiphase flows in STRs. [13], [19]

In the case of flows which consist of more than one phase, an appropriate multiphase model has
to be applied in CFD. The one most commonly used in aerated STRs is the Eulerian multiphase
model which falls under the category of inhomogeneous Euler−Euler models. The fact that the

in case of multiphase bubble flows in aerated STRs as the relative slip between the gas and

The group of Euler−Euler methods under which the Eulerian multiphase model falls handles all
the phases as continua. This is in opposition to the so­called Euler −Lagrange methods which
consider only the continuous phase to be a continuum and hence the Navier­Stokes equations
are only solved for this phase. The dispersed phase is then computed by particle tracking. Such
approach is typically applied only in cases when the dispersed phase does not exceed 10 vol.%.
[13], [19]
Eulerian multiphase model solves Navier­Stokes equations for each of the incorporated phases
which makes it one of the most complex multiphase models. This is unlike the other two alter­
natives proposed in ANSYS Fluent and other commercial software. One of those alternatives
is the volume of fluid model (VOF) which solves only one set of governing equations. In this
model, the phases do not interpenetrate and each control volume contains exclusively only one
of the phases or the interface. VOF is particularly useful for free surface flows and slug flows.
Another option is the mixture model which also uses just one set of governing equations sim­
ilarly to the VOF model. However, unlike VOF, it allows for volume fractions of each phase
in each control volume. Moreover, since it can calculate the interphase slip velocity from an
algebraic equation, the individual phases are allowed to have different velocities. It is apparent
that the mixture model presents a good alternative to the more computationally demanding Eu­
ler multiphase model which solves momentum equations for each phase. Nevertheless, when
only two phases are used, Eulerian model is a preferred option due to its higher accuracy. [13],
[19]
One challenge that arises solving flow problems using Eulerian fluid multiphase model is the
coupling of phases by means of interphase exchange coefficients. This interphase coupling is
already shown in Equation 2.28 and Equation 2.27 in terms of interphase momentum transfer
Mgl. As mentioned earlier, there are number of forces which can contribute to the interphase
momentum transfer ­ ie. the drag force, the turbulent dispersion force, virtual mass force, lift
force or the wall lubrication force. Typically, only some of the forces are considered, not all of
them due to their low relative importance compared to the others. For all of the forces which
contribute to the momentum transfer, there are numerous models proposed in the commercial
CFD software based on the data from literature. One can name eg. Ishii­Zuber drag force
model, Tomiyama model for the lift force or Burn’s model for the turbulent dispersion force. [13]

Mixing time measurements
For investigating the mixing time, the conductivity meter WTW inoLab® Cond 7110 was used.

a target in all of the mixing time measurement. Since the experimental tank was 3x bigger than
the calibration volume, 60 ml of the salt solution was used in all STR experiments. To sum up,

g/dm3 will be used for mixing time measurements. The assumed probe response time was 3.40
s.

For most simulations, values for both the locally scaled and globally scaled residuals (de­

the whole tank volume are typically presented. When the mesh and timestep studies were con­

the torque received by the stationary parts of the tank (walls, baffles and the air inlet) were not
experimentally measured, the precise values of the torque were not important as such. How­
ever, monitoring the torque was still a good indicator of the flow stability and mesh quality. In
the case of one­phase simulations, once the flow is stable, the torque should converge towards
a certain value. If the mesh is good, this value should no longer change significantly with further
refinements of the mesh. If, on the other hand, the torque notably changes, it means that the
mesh is not fine enough, at least not in some of the fluid regions. Conversely, for two­phase
flows, due to the presence of the gas phase and related phenomena such as oscillating cavi­
ties behind the blades, the torque will not stabilize as easily. Therefore, the torque alone is not
an ideal indicator of a good­quality mesh in such situations. When comparing different simula­
tions, the average torque value for the last 1 s was typically taken. In Listing C.2 in Appendix,
an example of the code used for postprocessing of the torque data from CFD .csv files is shown.

in the liquid for higher aeration rates. This is also shown in the results of the hold­up experi­

4.2.3
Turbulence, bubble size distribution, interphase forces
In the following section, the impact of some of the turbulence models, intephase forces and the
bubble size distribution on the fluid characteristics was investigated and compared to the base
case scenario described in subsection 3.2.2.
Turbulence models
Out of the proposed turbulence models, some of them resulted in convergence difficulties with
residuals not stabilizing but diverging. Namely, RNG k −ϵ and realizable k −ϵ were facing such
difficulties. Solving the arising issues would require further refinements of the mesh, timestep
or adjusting of the underrelaxation factors. However, since the goal was to change exclusively
one parameter compared to the base case and observe the impact of such an adjustment,
the unconverged simulations were not further investigated. Consequently, only two turbulence
models, namely k −ω standard model and k −ω SST model, were compared to the base case
simulation which used k −ϵ standard model. Out of the used metrics for comparison, a special
attention is paid to the residuals, gas hold­up, mixing time and the air volume fraction profile.
For the remaining characteristics: Courant number, mass outflow, torque and velocity profile,
the simulations led to very similar results.
In terms of the residuals, both k −ω simulations evinced more stability. In all cases, the limiting
residual (both local and global) was the continuity residual oscillating around 10−3. Neverthe­
less, for the base k −ϵ turbulence model, a residual representing the turbulent dissipation rate
ϵ had a tendency to spike for some of the iterations. However, this was typically accompanied
by a sharp decrease in the residual. The residuals for all three cases are shown in Figure D.11,

Interphase forces
Various models of interphase forces were testested, however due to convergence difficulties,
only three of them were run successfully and hence can be compared to one another. Namely, it
is the Ishii­Zuber drag force (FD) model (base case), Grace drag force model and at last but not
least, a combination of Ishii­Zuber drag force and Burns turbulent dispersion force (FT ) model.
A simulation using a combination of Ishii­Zuber drag force and Tomiyama lift force (FL) model
did not converge and therefore, it is not presented in the following text. Note that all simulations
were run with the k −ϵ standard turbulence model, and the model using only Ishii­Zuber drag
force corresponds fully to the base case k −ϵ model presented in the previous chapter. The
focus when comparing interphase models is on the residuals, air outflow, air hold­up, mixing
time and the velocity and air distribution profiles.

limiting residuals were both continuity and the turbulent dissipation rate ϵ residuals.

Bubble size distribution

Firstly, with regards to the residuals, both for the base case and the simulation of 300 rpm

simulation and Qout = 7.70 · 10−5 for the last 5 s of the simulation.

Bubble column
The last set of simulations run on two bubble column set­ups is presented in the following chap­

predicts the flow behavior in the case of a bubble column. Special attention when scrutinizing
the results was paid to the residuals, air outflow, air holdup, mixing time and the velocity and
air distribution profiles. Two cases of the bubble column set­up were investigated. First was
a bubble column with the air inflow of 2 vvm and the experimentally determined bubble size
DB = 0.94 mm (Enviro diffuser). Second was a bubble column with the air inflow of 4 vvm and
the experimentally determined bubble size DB = 2.80 mm (Hailea diffuser).
The local residuals for both bubble column set­ups are shown in Figure D.21 and Figure D.22
in Appendix. The residuals for both cases were very similar. For both aeration rates, the limit­
ing residual was the continuity residual which, unlike other residuals, did not drop below 10−3
(locally scaled). Interestingly, one difference compared to the STR simulations was the devi­
ation between the locally scaled residuals and globally scaled residuals. In the case of STR
residuals, the limiting residual typically oscillated in the orders of magnitude around the same
value, typically 10−3. However, in the case of both bubble column set­ups, there was a signifi­
cant difference between locally scaled residuals and globally scaled residuals. For example the
limiting continuity residual which did not drop below 10−3 for the locally scaled residuals, was
10−1 for the globally scaled residuals. We may assume that such high global residuals could be
caused by a relatively good initial guess since the global residuals always compare the residual
for the given iteration with the first 5 iterations. Overall, even though the global continuity resid­
uals were high and full convergence was never reached, we may assume that the simulations
provide reliable results in terms of residuals since the local residuals were relatively low.

kg/s. Therefore, in both cases, the air inflow was very close to the air outflow which is in ac­

5
Discussion
In the following paragraphs, the comparison of the experiments and the simulations is presented
and the accuracy of both methods is assessed. Furthermore, the difference between the STR
and bubble column set­up is discussed. And at last but not least, the social and environmental
impact as well as the possible future work are considered.
5.0.1
Accuracy and limits of the experiments and the numerical model
Firstly, concerning the bubble size measurements, the novel experimental method using the
endoscopic probe provided sufficiently accurate data. The biggest limitation was filtering out
cases where two or more bubbles overlapped when the picture was taken. On the other hand,
since the captured bubbles were mostly circular, the most feasible way of reducing the error was
to define the maximum allowed eccentricity. Nevertheless, even though a large set of various
bubble sizes was obtained, only one characteristic size ­ the Sauter diameter ­ was chosen to
represent the set. The only exception was when the bubble distribution was considered as dis­
cussed in subsection 3.2.5. Then, up to 4 different bubble sizes were included in the analysis.
Nevertheless, in all cases, the large number of various bubble sizes was always reduced sub­
stantially for the CFD simulations. In the future, representing the experimentally observed set
more accurately and in its whole in the CFD simulations could lead to the improvement of results.
With regards to the accuracy of the experimental measurements, the bubble size distribution
measurements were fairly accurate, even though, one should consider that the endoscope was
always placed at the same position in the tank which had a considerable impact on the mea­
surements. The position of the endoscope was not selected randomly, but rather so that the
endoscope was as close as possible to the impeller where the bubbles were broken down.
Considering different placements of the endoscope and evaluating its impact on the final results
could provide another interesting opportunity for future research. Similar experiments where the
impact of the position of the measurement device on the flow characteristics were performed
by Haider and Solvik, 2020 and 2021 [7], [10].
Similarly to the bubble size measurements, the experiments conducted with the goal to de­
termine the kLa value also evinced a high degree of accuracy. Moreover, as can be seen in
Figure 4.2, the results were also reproducible which manifested itself in a very low standard
deviation. Except for the error caused by the measuring device, some error on the final results
was also imposed by the postprocessing. One may notice that the DO profile is reaching its
limit when approaching the saturation levels. However, this contradicts the use of Equation 2.24
where the assumed profile is fully logarithmic, therefore limitless. In this work, this issue has
been solved by considering only some of the values in the time series which evinced the log­
arithmic behavior. Nevertheless, this choice had undoubtedly impact on the final kLa value.
One should note that kLa was only determined in the experiments but it was not investigated
in the CFD models. This opens an opportunity for future work on the CFD simulations as there
is an option to determine kLa in the simulations. However, the use of user­defined functions
is necessary in such cases. This presents a whole variety of new obstacles that should be
addressed when the kLa value is obtained from the simulations. On the other hand, if the kLa
was determined from the simulations, it could provide another validation criterion similarly to the
mixing time or the air hold­up.

Regarding the mixing time measurements, the biggest error was imposed by the response time
of the conductivity probe. It is beyond question that in our experiments, it would be more fea­
sible to use a conductivity probe with a lower response time and with online data acquisition.
If the mixing time was considerably higher the used probe would suffice; however, for some of
our experiments, the mixing time could not be determined as it was simply to close to the probe
response time. Moreover, even for the impeller speed of 300 rpm, 400 rpm and the bubble col­
umn experiments, where the mixing time was the longest, the measurements were burdened by
a relatively significant error. Another issue with the mixing time experiments was the acquisition
of concentration profiles by recording the concentration changes on a phone camera and then
analyzing the obtained video. In this case, online data acquisition would be more suitable. Nev­
ertheless, even despite the discussed obstacles, which could be resolved by more appropriate
experimental equipment, the experimental results and the simulations gave in some cases simi­
lar results. In particular, the difference was relatively low for the base case simulation. In all STR
simulations, the determined mixing time was higher than in the case of experiments. On the
contrary, for the bubble column measurements, the experimental mixing time was higher than
the one given by the simulations. It should be a matter of further investigations why the CFD
model underestimates the experimental results in one case and overestimates them in the other.
At last but not least, the air hold­up measurements which were conducted using two methods
both carried a considerable error. This was already apparent by the difference in the values
acquired by the two methods. Measuring the water level rise by a ruler and deducing the air
hold­up from that is a rather first­order air hold­up estimation. More advanced techniques such
as the pressure difference measurements or the local hold­up measurements should be con­
sidered if more accurate results were to be acquired. On the other hand, what deserves the
reader’s attention is that in the orders of magnitude the simulations corresponded to the exper­
iments.
Another aspect of this work which deserves attention are the CFD model choices. More specif­
ically, the decisions with respect to the overall simulation time, timestep, mesh, number of inner
iterations and convergence. Firstly, concerning the simulation time, most simulations were run
for 65 ­ 71 s which corresponded to approximately 14 days of real time. When deciding on
what time was sufficiently long enough so that it could be said that the flow is stable enough,
the monitoring of the torque, hold­up, air outflow and the concentration profiles was used. Most
of the mentioned criteria were stabilized before the simulations were stopped. However, this
was not the case for the torque for most two­phase simulations. The question that arises is
whether the torque monitoring would evince oscillatory unstable behavior no matter how long
the simulation was run, or if there is some point where the torque would not oscillate anymore.
Thanks to the one­phase simulations where the torque reached stable values after less than
40 s, it was assumed that the oscillatory behavior was related to the two­phase nature of the
flow and hence the flow was considered stable before the simulations were stopped. Neverthe­
less, running the simulations for longer time could potentially lead to new outcomes in the future.
On a similar note, it was described in the previous chapters that for most of the simulations, after
20 inner iterations for the given meshes and timesteps, the convergence defined as the state
when the residuals drop either below Rlocal = 10−4 or Rglobal = 10−3 was not reached. Note
that the mentioned thresholds are rather a common practise in CFD and we may encounter
very different convergence criteria for different flow problems. Nevertheless, due to the com­
plicated nature of the two­phase flows in STRs, it may be very challenging to satisfy even the
relatively benevolent criteria mentioned above. For example, in our case, even reducing the
Numerical simulations of multiphase reactors and their experimental validation
77
==PAGE:93==
number of inner iterations, refining the mesh and reducing the timestep did not result in any
significant reduction of the residuals. In future work, some improvements should be considered
in order to assure better compliance with the convergence criteria. One way could be running
the simulations only on 1/4 of the geometry using symmetry in CFD. This could allow the use
of more mesh elements for a smaller volume. Another option could be to reduce the timestep
even more drastically below 0.001 s for the STR simulations and below 0.0001 s for the bubble
column simulations. However, this would increase the simulation real time drastically, therefore,
more computationally powerful systems would have to be applied in order to keep the simulation
time in the range of days rather than weeks.
To sum up, regarding the experimental set­up, the accuracy of the air hold­up and the mixing
time experiments was limited by the chosen measurement methods. Therefore, it is necessary
to interpret the results rather as estimates than accurate and precise values. On the other hand,
the measuring equipment for the determination of the bubble sizes and kLa values was suffi­
ciently accurate. Nevertheless, for both cases, further development of the data postprocessing
could lead to improved result interpretation. Regarding the CFD model, in order to achieve more
accurate results, one should consider further refinements of the mesh, reduction of the timestep
and longer simulation time. On the other hand, the limiting factor in such cases is typically the
available computational power.
5.0.2
Comparison of the stirred tanks and bubble columns
One notable comparison which arises when interpreting the results of this work is the difference
between the bubble columns and the stirred tank reactors. Firstly, we may notice that the dif­
fusers used in our work produced smaller bubbles compared to any of the STR configurations.
This may be an advantage when a high degree of aeration is required without extensive power
inputs.
Smaller bubbles had a positive impact on the mass transfer coefficient as was shown when
the Enviro diffuser with small pores was used instead of the Hailea diffuser. Interestingly, the
Enviro diffuser was comparable in terms of the kLa value with the STR under the impeller speed
of 300­400 rpm. That means that when the diffuser can produce bubbles of very small sizes,
high power inputs related to the impeller rotation can be avoided. On the other hand, on the
industrial scale, one should always consider the trade­off between the high power input related
to STRs and a high pumping power and pressure demands related to the generation of very
small bubbles in bubble columns.
Another aspect which plays a role when deciding on the experimental or production set­up in
fermenters is the mixing time. On the lab­scale, the STR resulted in significantly lower mix­
ing time compared to all bubble column configurations. This may be an advantage especially
when a fast homogenization of the fermentation broth is required. On the other hand, in order
to achieve such low mixing times on the industrial scale, several impellers and a high impeller
speed would be required. This is not only a problem in terms of the power input but also a po­
tential issue when working with cells which are prone to shear damage. In such instances, one
should decide which of the following is the priority: either the homogeneity of the fermentation
broth or the protection of the cells used for the fermentation.
Regarding the accuracy of the simulation results, the STR simulations were closer to the exper­
imental results compared to the bubble column simulations. This could be due to many factors.

One of the most important ones is the fact that the focus was more on the STR simulations
and there were no mesh and timestep studies conducted on the numerical model of the bubble
column. Better refinements of the mesh used in the bubble column simulations and a different
timestep choice should be the first investigated parameters when improving the simulations.
5.0.3
Advantages and disadvantages of simulations and experiments
One of the questions which may arise when reading this work is whether the experiments are
a better tool than simulations when observing the flow behavior of multiphase reactors. The
answer is that both approaches have their advantages and drawbacks. The apparent advan­
tage of the experiments is the fact that when a sufficiently accurate and appropriate measuring
method is used, the experimental results may be of a very good quality and give valuable infor­
mation on the flow behavior.
On the other hand, it is apparent that experimental measurements come with a lot of difficulties
associated with the demands on the equipment. It is undoubtedly not feasible to purchase lab
equipment which is only rarely used. Furthermore, as could be seen in our work, even very sim­
ple measurements can be inaccurate due to very basic but essential problems. An example is
the long response time of the used conductivity probe. Other issues are related to the required
location of the measurements. For example, on the industrial scale, one might be interested
in measuring the pH at several different locations in the fermenter. However, it is practically
impossible to install tens of pH meters at various locations in the tank. On top of that, many
measurement techniques require permanent structural changes to the fermenters, for example,
drilling holes in order to install the monitoring device.
Numerical models and simulations, on the other hand, present a very elegant alternative to the
experiments. The issues related to the number, cost and locations of the measuring devices are
prevented in the case of simulations. Moreover, the simulations may come in handy when the
process is supposed to be up­scaled or down­scaled. Nevertheless, as could be seen in this
work and other works, many design choices have to be taken prior to running CFD simulations.
Moreover, the credibility of the results should be always verified and at last but not least, CFD
simulations may run for days to weeks.
The ideal trade­off between experiments and simulations was outlined in this work. On the lab­
scale, the experiments may be used to validate numerical models which can be then used to
predict the flow behavior on larger scales. In this way, high costs related to expensive equip­
ment used on the industrial scale may be avoided.

chemicals, food, fuels or materials. The issue with the existing established technologies is that
they commonly rely on the use of fossil fuels. Thanks to fermentation, parts of the above­
mentioned processes could be performed in a significantly more sustainable way. It is without
any doubt that nowadays, there is a high demand for novel alternatives to fossil­based produc­
tion pathways driven by the climate change and other related issues.

Future work

Firstly, it is the improvement of the numerical models and experimental measurements. Sec­

on building and testing costly equipment and furthermore, it could help us assess the feasibility
and appropriateness of different procedures and apparatuses.

6
Conclusion
The main goal of this project was to design CFD simulations of multiphase flows in STRs and
bubble columns and validate these simulations against experimental data. The first step was to
review and summarize the current knowledge in the fields of fluid flow, mass transfer and the
CFD theory. Secondly, the aim was to set­up experiments which can be used as inputs for the
simulations as well as for their validation. And at last but not least, CFD simulations were run
with the intention to give reader an information on some of the typical flow characteristics.
Different sets of experiments were carried out in order to determine the following flow parame­
ters of the STR and bubble column: bubble size, volumetric mass transfer coefficient, air hold­up
and the mixing time. For the STR, the used impeller speed was 300, 400, 500 and 600 rpm,
and the aeration rate was 4 and 5 NL/min. For the bubble column experiments, two different
diffusers (brands Heilea and Enviro) were used, the first with the aeration rate of 2 NL/min and
the second with aeration rates of 2 and 4 NL/min.
The most important findings were that the bubble size decreased with the increasing impeller
speed reaching its minimum at D2,3 = 3.12 ± 0.31 mm at 600 rpm and 4 NL/min. The maximum
bubble size was D2,3 = 4.11±0.45 mm at 300 rpm and 4 NL/min. For the bubble column exper­
iments, Enviro diffuser at 2 NL/min generated the smallest bubbles of D2,3 = 0.94 ± 0.38 mm.
With respect to the volumetric mass transfer coefficient, it increased with the impeller speed
reaching its maximum of kLa = 76.2 ± 2.1 h−1 at 600 rpm and 5 NL/min. Both the vigorous
mixing and smaller bubbles associated with higher impeller speed contributed to the increase
in the volumetric mass transfer coefficient. The effect of the bubble size on the mass transfer
coefficient was also apparent in the case of the bubble column where the Enviro diffuser pro­
ducing the smallest bubbles also resulted in the highest value of the mass transfer coefficient
of kLa = 39.7 ± 1.3 h−1 compared to the second used diffuser.
The findings with respect to the air hold­up should be interpreted rather as order­of­magnitude
estimates than very accurate values. Nevertheless, the trend of an increased hold­up with
higher impeller speed and aeration rate was clear except for one set of measurements at 400
rpm. It can be assumed that the higher hold­up values are related to smaller bubbles due to
their lower rising velocity. Out of the two techniques used (observing the water level rise and the
modified inclined tube method) and out of all of the observed operating conditions for the STR
configuration, the highest hold­up of αG = 0.0168 was measured for 600 rpm and 5 NL/min by
the inclined tube method. Analogously, for the bubble column experiments, the highest hold­up
of αG = 0.0091 was also measured for the smallest bubbles produced by the Enviro diffuser. At
last but not least, the mixing time measurements could be only conducted for the bubble column
set­up and the STR set­up at 300 and 400 rpm because for the remaining operating ranges of
the impeller speed, the mixing time was too close to the response time of the used conductivity
probe. Overall, for the STR configuration, the mixing time was significantly impacted by the
turbulence related to the impeller speed. That is why the longest mixing time in the case of STR
of tm = 7.8 ± 0.2 s was reported for 300 rpm and 4 NL/min. Even higher mixing time values
were reported for the bubble column experiments with the maximum of tm = 24.4 ± 0.9 s for
Hailea diffuser at 2 NL/min.

==PAGE:97==
Secondly, CFD simulations were run with the goal to assess if they can be validated against
the experimental results. For the STR set­up, as a first step, a one­phase mesh study was
carried out. Then the timestep and mesh study were conducted on the two­phase flow study
case as well. At last, it was investigated which turbulence model, interphase force model and
bubble size distribution would provide the outcome closest to the experimental results. Out of
the tested cases, the simulation based on the k −ϵ turbulence model, Ishii­Zuber FD model and
constant bubble size model of bubble distribution with one bubble size bin corresponding to
DB = 3.9 mm obtained from the experiments, was the most in agreement with the experimental
results. For all of the flow characteristics such as the flow velocity profile, air flow volume frac­
tion profile and gas outflow, the above­mentioned simulation provided reliable results supported
by visual observations and experiments. On top of that, in terms of the reported mixing time of
tm = 4.2 s and the air hold­up of αG = 0.0078, the results were close to the experimental values.
The simulations conducted on the bubble column set­up were overall not as thorough as for the
STR and future work should be considered in order to provide more credible results. Neverthe­
less, the results in terms of the air outflow, and the air and velocity profiles were credible.
To sum up, the goal of the study was to investigate whether CFD simulations can be verified
against experiments. Overall, we may conclude that to some extent, it is possible to find agree­
ment between the simulations and experiments. Nevertheless, improvements of the numerical
models as well as the experiments should be made in future studies in order to increase the
robustness and reliability of the proposed methods and allow their use on a larger scale.

A.1
Non­Newtonian fluids
Fluids which do not obey the Newton’s law of viscosity are referred to as non −Newtonian
fluids. For non­Newtonian fluids, for the given temperature, pressure and fluid composition,
viscosity µ is not constant. Conversely, it is either time­dependent, i. e. µ = µ(t), where t
represents time (rheopectic and thixotropic fluids), or it depends on the shear stress exerted on
the fluid, i. e. µ = µ(τyx). When classifying non­Newtonian fluids, one important parameter
arises ­ apparent viscosity µa ­ defined as the ratio of the the shear stress (τyx) and shear rate
(dvx/dy) as follows [3], [12]:
µa =
τyx
dvx/dy
(A.1)
In Table A.1, different fluid types with regards to their behavior under varying shear rate are
shown, moreover, Figure A.1 presents the flow curves of different fluid types. Even though, in
our experiments and CFD models, we focus on water­air multiphase system where both fluids
are Newtonian, the basic knowledge of non­Newtonian behaviour and the impact of viscos­
ity is particularly important in fermentation processes where the broths typically evince some
form of non­Newtonian behavior. Moreover, the rheology and related viscosity of fermentation
broths are typically dependant on the cell concentration, morphology and product and substrate
concentrations. [3]
