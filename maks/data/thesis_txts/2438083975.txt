Abstract
Through the Paris agreement a record number of countries have come together and
agreed on striving towards lowering global emission. As the world’s CO2 budgets are
being exceeded, more radical measures needs to be taken in order to limit the average
global temperature increase to 2◦C, and preferably 1.5◦C above pre-industrial levels.
This is believed, in the Nordic countries, covering Denmark, Norway, and Sweden, to be
done by becoming net CO2 negative. This study focuses on how the Nordics can become
net CO2 negative by 2040, by among other, using the CCS technology combined with the
assumption of bio-energy being carbon neutral. The approach used covers a definition
of possible CCS technologies, which are implemented into energy system models, using
a three step approach - capture, transport, and storage. The energy system models, the
Balmorel and TIMES-Nordic model, are used to define a possible layout of an energy
system containing the CCS technologies. The results are analysed by comparing three
cases: a base case, a renewable case, a and CCS case. The cases are affected differently
as the CCS technologies are only available for the base and CCS case. Furthermore, the
renewable and CCS cases emissions are lowered through emission bounds, whereas CO2
taxes are introduced for the base case.
The use of CCS technologies increase the possibility of reaching the temperature
targets. However, without incentives, it is not the most viable solution. In the base case,
renewable energy sources are a far more favourable. Whereas in the CCS case there
is an increased usage of CCS. Mostly the heavy industry is affect, where up to 90% of
the production is covered by CCS technologies. Moreover, it is also implemented in the
power and heat sector covering 5% of the power production. Although, CCS capacities
are larger in the industry, more is captured in the power and heat sector, covering 88%
of the total CO2 captured. Most of the CO2 captured from power and heat production
is from biomass, which allows the overall system to become net negative.
The largest share of CCS costs is the costs of capture, accounting for approximately
91 %, therefore, by lowering them the CCS is utilised more. However, with increased
costs, the renewable energy system case seems as a more appropriate option. The Nordics
cannot become CO2 negative without using CO2 removing technologies, such as CCS in
combination with bio-energy and remove CO2 at a higher rate than it is being emitting
in the system. Thus, to lower the load on the CCS bio-energy capture it is recommended
that the energy system transitioned simultaneously to a non- or low emitting renewable
system. Additionally, subsidising CCS could make it more attractive.
Keywords: CCS, net CO2 negative, carbon neutral, Balmorel, TIMES-Nordic

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Today the world stands for a series of challenges that effects the very existence of the
human idea of the modern global community. One of those is the climate change. The
average temperature continues to rise each year effecting the climate on numerous points
such as the degradation of ecosystems, rising sea levels, and enhancing extreme weather
conditions – threatening human ability to adapt.
The main cause of the temperature rise is found in the release of Greenhouse gases
(GHG). Some occur naturally, but it is the anthropogenic emissions which are causing
high concentrations in the atmosphere and consequently leading to elevated temperatures
[1]. The member states in the Unites Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change
(UNFCCC) agreed in 2015 to fight climate change and adapt to its effects, under one
single agreement “The Paris agreement”. The central goal of the agreement is to keep
the global temperature development in 2050 well below 2◦C, favourable under 1.5◦C,
compared to the pre-industrial levels. The agreement states that each member state
should be able to deal with the impacts of the climate change on their own terms, while
striving to lower GHG emissions. Therefore, to meet these goals each of the member
countries must mitigate the changes e.g. by setting more ambitious goals and changing
energy systems into lower emitting systems.
The Intended Determined Contributions (INDCs) states each of the member states
actions in terms of preventing the climate change after 2020 [2]. The EU states are
in UN perspective seen as developed countries, and therefore more ambitious measures
are expected in terms of lowering GHG emissions. The EU has, in order to meet the
demands of the Paris agreement, agreed to implement a series of more specific measures
related to both lowering of emissions and adaption of climate changes.
EU’s overall long-term goal is to cut of the emissions by 80-95% by 2050, compared to
a 1990 level. A target they believe should be reached by using more energy efficient
and low-carbon technologies, including the aspect of trapping of CO2 from energy and
industrial related production [3].
On the other hand, according to Rogejli et al. [4] approximately 2/3 of the available
carbon budget, to keep the temperature below the 2◦C, has already been used. Therefore,
Rogejli et al. states that to ensure that the budget is kept, an increase in ambitions
over time is needed, by taking more radical measures to achieve the goals. Rogejli et al.
lists an amount of measures that could be implemented being more ambitious, where
the possibility of storing CO2 is stated.
There is today an increased interest in the use of CCS, since in combination with energy
or industrial production could make emitting technologies CO2 neutral or even in special
cases CO2 negative. A conference in 2018 at Chalmers University in Gothenburg [5]
==PAGE:26==

1 Introduction
about the aspect of negative emissions, presented among other things, how negative
emission could postpone the effects of GHG emissions and increase the possibility of
changing energy systems to low emitting systems in time.
In line with this, according to the Global Status of Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS)
report [6], there are currently 17 large scale CCS plants operating worldwide. However,
in order to reach the 2◦C degree target there is a need to implement more than 2000 of
fully operational large scale CCS plants by 2040, which have to remove 14 % of worlds
the cumulative emissions.
1.1
The scope
In relation to the INDC’s and the European goals, the Nordic have also set ambitious
goals to reach low emissions. Denmark, Norway and Sweden have committed themselves
to reducing GHG emissions by either 85-90 % or being fully carbon neutral. This is
done through multiple focus points, covering fossil fuel independence, supplying energy
demands through renewable sources, renewable transportation, increased energy efficiency
etc. [7, 8, 9].
While having used over 2/3 of the global carbon budget, more drastic measures should be
considered. Therefore, the Nordic countries are investigating the possibility of reaching
net negative emissions, as a response to increasing ambitions. Thus, instead of becoming
neutral by 2050, the Nordic countries are interested in becoming net negative by 2040.
Considering these objective, the aim of this study is to investigate how the Nordic
countries can become CO2 negative by 2040.
Here the use of CCS becomes interesting, due to its ability of creating negative CO2
emissions in the atmosphere.
The Nordic countries’ main GHGs emitted is Carbon Dioxide (CO2). Considering the
numbers found from European Environment Agency (EEA) data viewer [10], it can be
seen that CO2 covers 79 % of total national emissions for the whole Nordics. Emissions
mainly stems from the transport sector (50 % of the total), where power and heat
production and industry sector come in second and third respectively (21 % and 17
% of the total). The used numbers can be seen in appendix A. The Nordics emission
sources in the power and heat and industrial sector come from mainly fuel combustion
and industrial productions i.e. Combined Heat and Power (CHP), iron and steel etc.
Thus, by implementing CCS onto these technologies, it could be possible to achieve net
neutrality or even net negativity using existing technologies. Furthermore, the need of
rapid transition to a renewable energy system, could be postponed.
Energy and industrial production creates large, CO2 rich, gas streams [11, 12]. Therefore,
implementing CCS technologies into these productions could be a promising alternative
to renewable and efficient solutions.
As mentioned implementing CCS technologies
with energy and industrial production using biomass can result in a negative emission
contribution to the atmosphere.
Renewable energy systems are a more sustainable
solution. However, with them alone, it is not possible to reach a neutral or negative
state. Therefore, the CCS can serve as a bridge to transition to more renewable energy

systems in the Nordic countries.
As conversion of the energy system is relatively complex both in terms of technical
aspects and economical and environmental impacts on society [13].
This process is
made possible by using energy system models, such as the TIMES-Nordic and the
Balmorel models. Therefore, energy system models are used in this study to analyse the
possibilities and effects of implementing CCS technologies in the Nordic energy systems.
This is done by analysing three cases. A base case which resembles the energy system
case by the Nordic Energy Technology Program (NETP) report of 2016 [14] and is
controlled by a CO2 tax. A CCS case where CCS technologies utilised controlled by a
CO2 bound. A renewable case, where only renewable energy sources are available, also
controlled by a CO2 bound.
Hypothesis
CO2 capture is seen as an energy intensive and costly technology. Moreover, the need
for transportation and permanent storage in geological formations both on- and offshore,
under high pressures increases the total costs of CCS even more [15]. The technology
would have to be implemented into processes, existing in the power and heat and industry
sector i.e. into CHP plants, boilers and carbon-intensive industries. In the power and
heat sector, there are many alternatives to low emitting technology e.g.
renewable
energy sources, increased efficiency etc. The renewable sources have a huge potentials
in the Nordics and are seen as the most cost efficient solution for the transition to a
low emitting system in regard to energy production [16, 17, 18]. On the other hand,
the industry sector has fewer options on the transition, as most of the technologies are
dependent on extreme temperatures. Then a transition which is only possible using
more efficient solutions or creating a solution involving the use of bio-energy.
Based on this information, three hypotheses are established. The first hypothesis is that
under current conditions the CCS technology is too expensive and therefore, not the
most attractive solution in terms of lowering CO2 emissions. The second hypothesis
states that when chosen, CCS technologies or more applicable in the industry sector,
rather than in the power and heat sector. The third hypothesis states that, without
any legal and regulatory frameworks, renewable alternative is a more attractive solution
than implementing CCS in Nordic energy systems [19].
1.2
Literature review
In this section a litterateur review is conducted.
It describes mainly the use and
implementation of CCS into existing energy models. Furthermore, the expected costs
from CCS implementation is described.
The first study covers an integrated assessment of CCS in the German power sector
in comparison with development of renewable energies, made by Samuel et al. [20].
The investigation takes basis in the CCS technology and its availability in the German
==PAGE:28==
4
1 Introduction
power and heat sector, exploring it through different scenarios.
It is stated that the CCS is not favourable in the German system. This is mainly due to
the large-scale availability expected for renewable energy sources. In general, it is shown
in the study that renewable technologies are less expensive compared to CCS. Another
aspect is the fact that renewable energy and electrification of the transport system reduce
the fossil fuel share considerably - eliminating the areas where CCS can be implemented.
Moreover, they state that if technologies expected efficiency improvement and renewable
energy source potentials weren’t as large, it could also affect the implementation of the
CCS.
Finally, they show that CO2 storage potential is paramount to the CCS potential. As if
storage capacities are too low, the investments related to them becomes too high, and
is therefore not used.
Due to the large competition in the power and heat sector, they suggest switching the
CCS focus onto the industrial sector. As the selection for low emission technologies are
limited to either increasing of efficiency or switching to a more sustainable production
processes. Another possibility is to make the CCS available for biomass energy producing
plants, as it could result in negative emissions.
Another study covered by Gouveia et al. [21] covers the implementation of CCS into
the Iberian Peninsula’s (Spain and Portugal) energy system.
Using the TIMES_IBERIA model, they investigate several factors such as climate
policies, renewable energy supply, CCS cost curves and gas prices etc.
The CCS
technology is implemented into both the power and heat and industry sectors.
They discover that the CCS becomes attractive only after the renewable energy supply
potential has been fully exploited. This is reasoned through lower costs for implementing
renewable technologies compared to CCS technologies.
However, when the CCS technology is implemented in the industry i.e. in steel and
cement production, it seems to be the most cost-effective solution. Compared to the
power and heat sector, the industry sector has a smaller range of possibilities to lower
emissions. Which can be solved by improving efficiency, utilising renewable energy, or
shifting fuel. Further investigations showed that a larger renewable energy source cost
lead to a higher usage of CCS.
It is concluded from their study that the policies in the EU and on the Iberian Peninsula
points in the favour of renewable energy and energy efficiency, which lowers the interest
of CCS.
Strachan et al. [22] investigated with the focus on implementing CCS as being a part
of the power generation mix. This was done using both the national techno-economical
linear optimisation MARKAL (MARKet ALlocation) developed for the Netherlands,
Norway and the UK, and the regional Pan European TIMES model, including all member
states of EU-27 (Including also Norway, Iceland, and Switzerland).
The models consider the availability of capturing CO2 in both the power and heat sector
and the industry sector. The models also includes the transportation and storage of any
captured CO2. Here only pipeline transportation is considered. In the study CCS is
==PAGE:29==
1.2 Literature review
5
tested in the aspect of limiting CO2 emissions, furthermore, the potential capacities are
investigated through sensitivities.
They depict that MARKAL/TIMES are good tools in terms of modelling the role of
CCS in the energy system. Furthermore, they find that lowering the fuel prices could
increase the use of CCS, and increase the use of long distance storage locations. It was
also shown that the Utsari formation had a capacity large enough to store the emissions
from both the UK, the Netherlands, Norway, Denmark, and Germany altogether.
Ramírez et al. [23] did another similar investigation to define the benefits and bottlenecks
of using CCS in the energy system. The countries included in this study are Norway,
Denmark, Germany, the Netherlands and the UK. Using the same models as stated in
the Strachan et al. investigation, under the same assumptions.
During their study they find that transportation is dependent on volumes transported,
transport distances and terrain type. Storage is more dependent on location, potential
injection rate, and possibility of reuse of infrastructure. Furthermore, they find that
the general ordering of costs of CO2 transport and storage is: Enhanced Oil Recovery
(EOR), nearby aquifers and oil and gas reservoirs, and long distance storage locations.
It is seen that countries with onshore storage capacity firstly utilises these until exhaustion.
Therefore, the availability of onshore storage capacity determines the competitiveness of
offshore storage locations.
Another point is that there is seen a difficulty of CCS penetration for countries with
large renewable energy source potentials. They state that a reduction in costs can have
a significant role in the implementation of CCS.
In relation to modelling of CCS transportation many studies have been made.
A study conducted by Kjärstad et al. [24] investigates the costs of ships and pipeline
transportation from specific storage locations. They determine that the costs of these
transportation technologies are dependent on volumes and distances of the transported
CO2. From this the effects of choice of reservoir, i.e. hydrocarbon fields or saline aquifers,
choice of transportation option i.e., pipelines or ship, was studied. This was done with
the focus of transporting from various locations placed around in Norway, Denmark,
Sweden and Finland. Furthermore, the study covers the possibility of transporting from
a single source or a cluster of sources to a cluster of many storage locations.
It was found that the clustering of the sources makes pipeline transportation the most
cost-efficient option. However, for most single emission solutions the ship was shown to
be a more economical solution, or during ramp-up of the cluster location.
It is also depicted that the cost for ship transport increases modestly with larger distances.
On the other hand, the pipeline has significant scale benefits as the costs of pipelines
decrease rapidly with rising transportation volumes.
Therefore, they conclude that the clustered sources are needed in order to make the
pipeline transportation more interesting, and for smaller to modest emission locations
ship transport is expected to be most cost effective.
Another investigation showing the deployment of CCS infrastructure is conducted by
==PAGE:30==

Kanudia et al. [25]. They identify and asses the most cost effective CO2 transport
and storage infrastructures available in the west Mediterranean (Spain, Portugal, and
Morocco).
The investigation itself is made using the TIMES-COMET model, and
Geographical Information System (GIS), used to determine the distances considered.
The focus is set on emitters with emissions larger than 0.1 Mtpa, covering both the
power and heat sector and the industrial sector.
Therefore, new CCS technologies are considered for implementation in the model. These
emission sources are aggregated into clusters in predefined areas, the same applies to
the storage potentials. Transportation is modelled assuming that it happens through
pipelines. They consider using the layout of the transportation grid, through already
existing gas pipelines, but also the point of having free routes in terms of transportation.
The scenarios built into the model covers aspects of economic growth, national mitigation
levels, CCS availability in transport and storage infrastructures, storage capacity, national
CO2 pipeline networks, and possibility of transporting across borders.
Running the scenarios in the model, they find that CCS contributes considerably to the
mitigation, and that if the CCS was not available it would increase the cost of mitigation
of up to 4.5%. Furthermore, they discover that the industrial sector is more stable in
terms of maintaining the CCS in their portfolio as compared to the power and heat
sector, as the scenarios becomes stricter in terms of emission targets.
They explain
it through the power and heat sector having more options in terms of cost effective
mitigation, than the industrial sector.
Another assumption of using free routes compared to existing is deemed negligible, as
the stored amount changed with less than 2% and also lowered the total system costs
by much less than 0.1%.
The option of making CO2 available for cross border transport is found interesting, as
it would increase the use of CCS in countries where storage capacities is less available.
However, it was concluded that the CCS is generally competitive, and exploited to its
maximal technical potential under the assumptions stated.
Considering the industrial processes in relation to the CCS technology, a study has
been conducted by Skagestad et al. [26], where they asses the feasibility of the identified
CCS cases based on final technical and economical evaluation, made by the NORDICCS
project.
The study mainly covers CCS in industrial processes such as oil and gas, cement, iron
and steel, aluminium, and paper and pulp production. CHP plants are also considered
in the investigation. The aspect of transport and storage is also covered.
They investigate 6 different cases around the Nordic region covering from Iceland to
Finland.
In terms of transportation, they depict that the least costly type of transportation is
ships, but is very much dependent on the assumptions applied to the specific transport.
They also find that the storage closest to the emission location is utilised first. It is
further found that transportation becomes more cost effective for larger volumes of CO2
transported. On the other hand, an aspect of the cost effectiveness for the ship transport
is their higher flexibility, as ships are assumed to have a reuse potential as compared to

pipelines. Making the ship transport more interesting, as this flexibility is likely to be
important due to large uncertainties regarding time frame of implementation of CCS in
plants and uncertainties on storage locations availability.
In terms costs for of use of CCS in the power and heat sector and industrial sector,
some analysis has been covered. These mostly cover the reviews of known litterateurs.
The Energy Technology System Analysis Programme (ETSAP) [27] released a report
covering the aspect of CO2 capture, transportation, and storage. In this report it is
stated that both the power and heat sector as well as the heavy industrial (I.e. Iron and
Steel, Cement, Chemical and Petrochemical, Pulp and paper, and refinery industries)
sector are well suited to get CCS implemented into their production.
In terms of costs they discover that, for a storage location that lays within 200 km, the
capture costs cover over 70 % of the total CCS costs, whereas transport and storage
cover 15 % each. They state that the capture costs might decrease as an effect of the
technologies learning rate. On the other hand, transport and storage costs might increase
explained through distance, cost of pipelines, regulatory and safety-regulated costs.
The costs related to energy production capture ranges from approximately 30 EUR/tCO2
captured to 90 EUR/tCO2 capture, which is dependent on the fuel used in the production.
If a new power plant is built an increase of 50-100 % in costs compared to a plant
without CCS is expected. Industry CCS costs ranges from 13 EUR/tCO2 captured to
97 EUR/tCO2 captured.
It is furthermore stated that the costs of retrofitted CCS are undoubtedly going to be
considerably larger than a newly built plant with CCS already implemented. However,
there is a larger uncertainty for costs related to industrial processes, whereas for energy
production plants it is lower.
Costs connected to transportation of CO2 can be very varying as it is depending on
the volumes transported, but also the distance of transportation, and terrains i.e. on-
or offshore transportation.
Storage costs depends on the well drilling, compression,
injection, Operation and Maintenance (OM), and monitoring are included. It is stated
that onshore costs are lower than offshore.
To summaries the CCS technology can be implemented into an energy system, where it
seems reasonable, meaning that there is a potential for it i.e. having available storage
locations, and technologies in where the capture can be implemented. In general, in
the power and heat sector the renewable energy sources seems more cost efficient in
terms of lowering emissions, and CCS mostly becomes interesting when the renewable
technologies are fully utilised. In relation to the industry sector CCS gains more ground,
as there are less options for lowering emissions, moreover, the CCS seems possible to
implement in many industrial processes.
In the transport aspect of the CCS the costs are mostly related to distances and volume
of CO2 transported, where the specific route to the storage location didn’t have that
large of influence. Usually pipelines are used for very large volumes of CO2 transported,
and ships for small to medium volumes.
Finally, CCS effects any technology by increasing costs both in terms of investments,

1.3
Justification and approach
CCS has been investigated in multiple regions around in European countries, most
notably in Denmark and Norway [28]. Therefore, it is of interest to study the implementation
of CCS into the Nordic energy systems. The technology is traditionally implemented in
a stable energy system whereas modifications are only applied to the technology itself.
Putting it in relation to other technologies through energy system analysis is therefore
an appropriate approach to validate the technology in context of an actual developing
energy system.
The energy models used are the TIMES-Nordic model and the Balmorel. Energy system
model analysis is an acknowledged approach to validate technologies in a large energy
system.
The approach used is by considered the two models together, utilising the best aspects
of both models. Implementation of scenarios to simulate the reduction of emissions,
and then forcing carbon neutral technologies into the models. It can make the CCS a
part of the model consideration. As TIMES-Nordic is used to the aspect of emission
budgets and demand developments, whereas Balmorel is used for its detailed built-up in
the power and heat sector, and the probability to generate important endogenous data
e.g. power prices. These numbers are then moved between the models until outputs are
harmonised. Then TIMES-Nordic is used for the final output, and results.

2.1
Capture technologies
An easy way to understand the CCS capture technology is as a “flue gas cleaner” inserted
into any emitting process found in either industrial and power and heat production to
separate the CO2 before releasing it into the atmosphere [30]. This means that this gas
==PAGE:34==
10

cleaner would have to be added as extra capacity for any type of process in order to
capture the CO2. Thus, implying that an extra cost is added to any technology and to
the energy system using this technology.
There exist three different well-known types of capture technologies [29]; Post-, Pre-, and
Oxyfuel-combustion. These three are now elaborated for the purpose usage in Power
and Heat production. The industrial related capture processes is explained later. All
of these technologies, are assumed to be available for all sorts of combustion processes,
using the fuels coal, gas, or biomass.
In the post combustion the capture of the CO2 happens under a separation process
after the combustion. Most of the CO2 is removed before the flue gas is let out into the
atmosphere. The capture rate of the post-combustion technology is expected to be high,
which can be seen in table 2.5.
Today the most advanced commercially applied technology in post-combustion capture
is the wet scrubbing process with the use of aqueous amine solutions. In this process
CO2 is removed from the flue gas (Absorbed into the amine solvent), at relatively low
temperatures. The amine solvent is then regenerated for later use, where under this
process the CO2 is released again. The separated CO2 is then dried (Wet CO2 can be
very corrosive), compressed for transportation purposes [31].
The post-combustion process, is considered to be the simplest of the capture technologies
available. As small alterations would be needed as to redirect the flue gas from the
combustion chamber, to the capture facility. The boundary dam power plant in Canada
is today the worlds largest post combustion plant. A picture of the plant can be seen in
figure 2.2. As stated by Michael Monea at the 2018 Gothenburg conference on negative
CO2 emissions [32], the biggest complications of implementing the CCS technology on
the existing plant involved dimensioning of the heat exchanger between the power and
capture plant. Therefore, it is assumed that this process, can easily be retrofitted onto
the existing process. However, comparing it to building a completely new plant with
CCS already implemented the retrofitting is assumed as a more complicated process. To
understand more how the CCS is implemented into a conventional power production, a
sketch of the principle layout can be seen in seen in figure 2.3.

The pre-combustion capture is a different method, where the CO2 is captured before

This process builds on the principle of fuel gasification. The gasification happens at
high pressures (70-80 bars) to create a “synthetic gas” mostly consisting of CO and H2.

shift reactions”. Afterwards, the CO2 is separated to have a pure H2-based fuel gas
ready for combustion. In the separation process, the CO2 is dissolved (Using physical
solvent)[31].
Same as the post-combustion process, the pre-combustion process has high capture rate.

The final well-known capture process is the oxyfuel-combustion capture technology. In
relation to the post-combustion the capture phase happens after the combustion. This
is the bottom process seen in figure 2.3.
In the process O2 is at first separated from the ambient air, using an energy intensive Air

used in the combustion chamber. Fuel is recycled to create a more efficient combustion,
as mass is removed when only O2 is used in the combustion.
However, by using only pure oxygen in the combustion it creates flue gas which mainly
consists of CO2 and condensable water vapour. Afterwards, it is cleaned, dehydrated,
and separated during a compression process.

2.3
Storage technologies
Today there are multiple ways of storing CO2. According to the IPCC [29], the most
famous covers three main groups: Underground geological storage, ocean storage, and
mineral carbonation.
Ocean storage is based on the idea of releasing CO2 in depths where CO2 has a larger
density than water and can therefore be stored in a so called ”CO2 lake”. This is not
covered in the study mainly due to lack of studies covering the potential in the Nordic
countries.
Mineral carbonation is a process that turns CO2 into insoluble carbonates by reacting
with metal oxides. Today many studies are covering this aspect, where there seems to
be a large potential in Iceland, due to large amounts of basalt rocks. This is not covered
since the potential is found very limited for the countries considered in the study, and
data is furthermore limited on the case of defining it.
From this a focus is put on the geological storage in the Nordic region.
There are
several possibilities, the ones considered in this study is [29]:
• Hydrocarbon fiels (I.e. oil and gas fields)
• Saline aquifers
The hydrocarbon fields have been studies for many years, and behaviours of these are
well known. Today there exists projects where CO2 have been injected into these fields
e.g. the Sleipner project. Usually these fields are used for Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR),
however, they can also be used for storage in depleted fields.
The saline aquifers are deep sedimentary rocks saturated with brines, containing high
concentrations of dissolved salts. The CO2 is then injected into the formation beneath
multiple layers, ensuring the storing of the CO2. Other possibilities of storing geologically
are for this study not covered, since the potential is either very small or none existing
in the Nordic area.
Figure 2.13 shows that there is a large possibility to store CO2 in the Nordic countries.
The areas cover multiple saline formations and hydrocarbon fields, which are available
for storage. It should be noticed that onshore storage is only available in Denmark.
From investigations made by Anthonsen et. al. [39], they found that the total storage
capacity in Europe is around 358 Gt, where as 212 are found in the Nordic region
(Including Finland, and Iceland), covering 59 % of it. Hydrocarbon fields and saline
formations have a total potential capacity of 103 Gt [39, 28]. Therefore, the potential
for storing CO2 is huge.

Real life energy systems are very comprehensive and hard to model in every detail;
therefore, energy system models are used, which are based on simplifications and assumptions.
Due to this the models’ simulations are given a very simplified view, meaning that not

For this study, two different energy system analysis models have been used to investigate
the implementation of CCS into the Nordic Energy system i.e.

The two energy system models used, are as explained built to cover at least the Nordic
energy system i.e. Denmark, Norway and Sweden. The main purpose of using both
models is to describe the implementation of the CCS-technology in the energy system
as extensive and realistic as possible. The amount of literature on the two models is
scare, and therefore the assessment of Balmorel has been made by the studies of Weise
et. al. and TIMES is based on a lecture by Balyk [41], a study by Loulou et. al. [42],

3.1
Bamorel
The Balmorel model is a linear partial equilibrium model (Mixed integer is also possible).
Optimisation is done simultaneously on generation, transmissions, and consumption of
power and heat, in regard of the power and heat sector, under the assumption of perfect
market competition. Through constrains the model expands existing energy systems
and simulates future events, to define the cost impacts on energy systems, and give an
indication of how future energy systems could be developed.

TIMES-Nordic
The TIMES-Nordic model is a multi-regional model, technology-rich, and techno-economic
partial equilibrium model-generator.
TIMES-Nordic can work on different levels of
planning going from international/National to regional or city planning considering a

The analysis itself starts in a base year, and takes time-steps which are on a yearly basis,

Through the assumption of perfect foresight and market
conditions, it maximises welfare by minimising the total system costs.
Unlike Balmorel, TIMES-Nordic is driven through the TIMES model generator source

energy system, which is seen in figure 3.2. Boxes represent the processes, whereas vertical

3.3
Comparison and combination
Comparing Balmorel and TIMES-Nordic to each other the major difference is the coverage
of the sectors.
Balmorel currently only covers the power and heat sector, whereas
TIMES-Nordic covers both supply, power and heat, industry, residential and transportation.
This means, in terms of TIMES-Nordic it is possible to investigate future aspects onto
different sectors in the model and analyse the influence of one sector across different
sectors. Whereas Balmorel is limited to influences within the power and heat sector
itself. Therefore, TIMES-Nordic has the advantage of modelling an implementation of

e.g. large shifts from fossil fuel driven vehicles onto an electrical driven in the transport
sector, affecting the power and heat sector. In Balmorel it would have to be simulated
through a manual driven exogenous input into the demand, increasing it.
On the other hand, in Balmorel power and heat prices are endogenous defined in the
mode1, which is dependent on the current generation mix. Therefore, as the model
develops, and invests in new capacity, transmission lines etc., it effects the prices of
energy. These prices are however added exogenous in the TIMES-Nordic model, where
changes in capacity do not affect the power or heat costs. In this way Balmorel has a large
advantages, due to is detailed description of the power and heat sector. Furthermore,
power transmission capacity and investment in these is also much more detailed, which
also give an advantage in terms of power trade.
Overall, it seems that Balmorel is much more comprehensive concerning production,
investment and power trade and prices regarding the power and heat sector. On the
other hand, the TIMES-Nordic model covers all sectors considered in the energy system.
Therefore, it seems much more beneficial to consider the Balmorel for the power and
heat sector, where it is thorough on the energy production, trade and new investments.
Whereas TIMES-Nordic due to its wide range in the energy system, it can be used to
investigate among others policy inputs cover all sectors e.g., a forced shift to renewable
energy sources in a country.
The idea of the project is to utilise the best of both models. To do so, the two models
are used in series related to each other, by feeding needed information from one model
to another, to update each model for further investigations.

CHAPTER 4
Main assumptions
To implement CCS into both Balmorel and TIMES-Nordic, there are a number of
assumptions which have to be stated, for a realistic output.
This chapter describes the main assumptions used in the models. Firstly, the assumptions
related to the generating technologies, which is used for the carbon capture is integrated.
Then the emissions related to biomass usage is clarified, as the models currently consider
biomass as being carbon neutral. Next the overall model assumptions are covered, where
specifically the CO2 flow is considered. This follows a detailed explanation of the main
model assumptions on the capture, transport, and storage technologies.Finally, the data
handling of the costs in the model is covered.
4.1
Fuel technologies and biomass emissions
The study is limited to consider only coal, natural gas and biomass powered technologies.
The main reasons for this is that the literature used to define the technologies only covers
these technologies.
The studies from ETRI, Ramirez et.al. and Koornneef et.al. [35, 23, 44] have been used
to define technical parameters and costs etc. for the power and heat sector whereas the
industry sector is based on the studies of Kuramochi et.al. and Onarheim et.al. [11, 12].
Since TIMES-Nordic is limited in terms of industry technologies, in regard to the
possibilities of CCS integration, the assumptions are made from a basis on the power
and heat sector. The technologies covered in the industry sector are the heat production
technologies i.e. large-scale boilers (considered only for temperatures above 150◦C). In
terms of the power and heat sector, mainly CHP plants are considered. The retrofitted
capacities are assumed to be practicable for most thermal processes using the fuels
considered i.e. coal, natural gas and biomass. Furthermore, large scale heat boilers
are also considered practical for CCS capture implementation. To clarify the following
technology groups have been covered in the power and heat and industry sector:
• CHP plants
• Large scale heat boilers
A more detailed description for which technologies that are covered for TIMES-Nordic
can be found in tables H.2 and H.3 in appendix H for the power and heat and industry
sector respectively. For Balmorel the same can be seen in table G.2.
Another aspect is that biomass is considered a carbon neutral fuel in both models. This

modelling flow is sketched in figure 4.1.

4.2.1
Capture assumptions
The first step in the CCS part of models covers the capturing of the CO2. At the moment
both models have a way of calculating the CO2 created from any process but neither of
them have a direct way of handling the created CO2.
This is done through an approach, which can be seen in figure 4.2. For simplification,
the models distinguish between retrofitted and new technologies with CCS already
implemented. Moreover, retrofitted processes are only applied for energy production
while new capacity is applied for both energy and industrial processes.
Retrofitted
energy processes involve implementing capture technologies onto existing and newly
built energy producing capacities without CCS. Whereas, the CCS in the newly built
capacities are represented through the technology parameters itself.
Figure 4.2: Capture model layout for retrofitted and new capture technologies.
The CO2 flow is as shown in figure 4.2. For the retrofitted capture technologies the
model considers the accumulated emissions based on the used fuel, and decides whether

it wants to either capture all, a certain amount, or none of the CO2 emissions. If the
model chooses to capture CO2, it has to match a capacity of a specific capture technology,
connected to the emissions from the specific fuel.
This approach is slightly different for the new capacities. The newly built capacities
only consider emissions from the technologies with the specifically implemented capture
technology e.g. emissions from a coal power plant, with post-combustion implemented.
There is no middle step, in relation to the retrofitted, as all the CO2 emissions generated
from these technologies are captured.
This is assumed because it would be too expensive to build a new power plant with the
capture technology implemented and not to use it.
In this study the retrofitted capacities consider two types of capture technologies, namely
the post-, and oxyfuel-combustion. Whereas the newly built capacities have the possibility
of using post- and oxyfuel-combustion and CLC for capturing, where the latter is only
for biomass in the power and heat sector. However, this has only been done for the
power and heat sector, whereas the industry sector is limited to showing only that a not
specified capture capacity is added.
In chapter 2 it is stated that the technologies use energy to perform the capturing.
This is in reality both heat and electricity, however, to simplify and make the capture
technologies more comparable to each other the required energy has been assumed to
be only electricity. The main reason is that it has not been possible to divide the total
energy consumption of the capture technologies into heat and electricity.
The energy consumption for capture technologies is modelled differently for the two ways
of capturing. For the retrofitted, it is included as an input, for each CO2 captured. Thus,
reflecting the efficiency penalty of the plant, as e.g. a power plant needs to produce more
for the same delivered output.
For the newly built CCS plants it is embedded into the efficiency of the technology to
which they are connected, lowering it according to the efficiency penalty.
The capture rate from the different capture technologies, are shown through a capture
efficiency. As the model is introduced to the emissions, it captures a certain rate of
the emissions related to the capture technology chosen. This applies to both types of
capturing technologies. Figure 4.2 shows demonstrates this aspects by emitting a small
amount of CO2 from the capture technology, as the remaining is sent to the next step
in the CCS model.
The capture technologies are then able to capture any CO2 from the processes they are
considered in. It is furthermore assumed that the CO2 that is captured, is compressed
and dehydrated to make it ready for transportation. All of these aspects are included
into the CCS model’s parameters i.e. efficiencies, costs etc.

4.2.3.1
Storage length and leakage
The idea behind storing CO2 is that it is put in the ground permanently, furthermore,
the IPCC [29] claim that the storage is likely to maintain at least 99 % of the storage
capacity over a 1000 years, and may be able to store this amount of up to a million
years.
However, an investigation is conducted to find the influence of the storage leakage, to
see if it is able to assume that none of the storage is lost during a span of e.g. 30 years
(This corresponds to a simulation from 2020 to 2050).
According to Streit et. al. [49] the losses from geological storage locations are explained
by permeable zones and permeable faults. These losses are then induced by the following
factors: buoyancy forces, injection-related over pressures, and over pressures due to
earthquakes at the Richter scale of 5 or higher.
A more detailed explanation of the points of leakage from a geological storage, and the
calculation of the probability of losses are located in appendix D.1.1.
Comparing the upper statements with the investigation by Streit et al., and this studies
investigation, it can be stated that the leakages occurring would most likely be happening
as fault leakage, where it is primarily due to buoyancy forces. Streit et al. give an
estimate of expected leakage to be of 6-60 tonnes per year, giving an average loss of of
33 tonnes per year. Comparing this to the total storage available (in table 4.9, which
is 103.33 Gt) it is approximately a loss of 0.00003% per year. Therefore, losses from
storage are assumed negligible.

5.1.1
Carbon budget and negative emissions
A carbon budget is added to the model through the add-on, which is given as CO2
budget. The model is then able to emit a certain amount of CO2 limited by the budget,
which is considered in the process CO2 emitted in figure 5.2. The inputs into this proces
are for the model seen as the emissions from other fuels not considered for capture
technologies, the emissions which have been chosen not to be capture, the emissions
not captured in the capturing process, and a negative contribution from the stored
biomass emissions. As stated in section 4.1, through the assumptions of biomass being
carbon neutral when used in the models, it is evident that when removing CO2 from the
combustion process, would lead to a negative contribution to the atmosphere due to its
neutrality and emissions during combustion. Therefore, it is assumed that all emissions
captured from biomass gives a negative contribution to the emitted CO2.
The following equation is therefore used in Balmorel, for it to utilise the negative
contributions from the CCS in the system:
CO2Bud ≥CO2NOTCCS + CO2CCSEmit + CO2CCSNotCap −CO2Sto,Biom
(5.1)
Here CO2Bud is the stated CO2 budget, where CO2NOTCCS are the emissions not
considered for the CCS technologies. CO2CCSEmit are the emissions considered for the
CCS technologies, but have been chosen to be emitted. CO2CCSNotCap are the emissions
which are not captured during the capturing process, and finally CO2Sto,Biom are the
biomass emissions which are stored, and can therefore be assumed to have a negative
contribution to the total emissions.
Equation 5.1 shows a simplified layout of the contributions in relation to the CO2 budget.
The model did not before hand control emissions in a similar way, as emissions where
measured through emission taxes e.g. the M_POL parameter. However, the model is
now able to consider a carbon budget from which it is allowed to ”spend” emissions,
and with the CCS add-on it is furthermore allowing to subtract some of the created
emissions and e.g. postpone termination of emitting technologies.
A detailed description of the upper equation 5.1 can be seen in appendix G.1.

The implementation into TIMES-Nordic is different as compared to Balmorel.
The
CCS technologies are not added as an ”add-on”, but as part of a spectrum available

is able to add into the energy system. TIMES-Nordic compared to Balmorel has the

available for all regions, however, some of the information is added in the SubRes-Trans-files
considering specific regional information.
For TIMES-Nordic the SubRes_CCS_Techs contains definitions and data specifications
of the new CCS technologies, thus, containing the actual CCS technologies themselves.
Whereas the SubRes_CCS_Techs_Trans contains mapping and transformation operations
to control the specific regional data.
As known the transport sector uses the same
technologies, however, costs deviate due to different transported volumes and distances,
which the Trans-files is used to control.

the CO2 is handled in the model and how the three step modelling is included into the
model. Then the CO2 budget as well as the negative emissions is explained.

The model starts with having an overview to the CCS technologies existing in the
SubRes-file. Furthermore, the model is affected by constraints such as the one from

The flows, shown in the figure as arrows, represents demands, commodity- and costs
flows. The demands determine the technologies needed in later expansions, which moves
information around from inside the CCS SubRes-file to the TIMES-Nordic model. The
commodity flows are the CO2 going through the CCS technologies i.e. the three model

negative emissions.
The cost flows are then represented as the costs related to the
technologies, as capacities are invested.
The dark grey boxes, in the figure, are in the model seen as overall processes, meaning
that some sort of process is found within, explicitly shown is the CO2 emissions bound

for the model e.g. the capture technologies in the capture step etc. The surrounding
dotted boxes defines either one of the three steps in the CCS expansion.
TIMES-Nordic considers the capture technologies in two different ways, just as described
for Balmorel in chapter 5 the retrofitted and the newly built capacities are differentiated
in the same manner.
The retrofitted technologies considers the accumulated regional emitted CO2, and matches
it to capture capacities by the model if wanted.
Therefore, in TIMES-Nordic they
represent only the capture plant themselves.
Costs are represented by the specific
unit, and efficiency penalties are represented by an electrical input into the capture
technology. The capture efficiencies are added as efficiencies attributes controlling the
emission commodities going in and out of the process. A more elaborate clarification
can be seen in appendix H.
The newly built capacities are as in Balmorel existing technologies having the capture
plant implemented into the technology when invested. These new technologies have as in
Balmorel increased costs and lowered efficiencies to replicate the implementation of the
capture technology. However, they now use fuels which are not emitting. This is done
by e.g. for coal used in the power and heat sector changing the name from ELCCOA
to ELCCCSCOA and include that no emissions are created when using this commodity.
The new capacities are then the only that can use these commodities when in operation.
This means that the CO2 emitted and captured from the new capacities are controlled
by creating them as an output from them, which is done in accordance with the capture
rate of the capture technology added. For a more in depth explanation appendix H
should be considered. Moreover, the specific technologies which are included for the new
technologies can also be seen in tables H.2 and H.3 for power and heat and industry
sector respectively.
Then through a techno-economical optimisation the TIMES-Nordic model selects a
number of appropriate capture technologies. Compared to Balmorel, there are capture
technologies available in both the power and heat sector and the industry sector, regarding
the new capacities, whereas only the retrofitted are available for the power and heat

sector, and CLC available in the new capacities as CHP plants.
The next step in the CCS expansion is the transport step. When the model selects to
capture CO2, the model is then through bounds forced to transform the captured CO2
into CO2 transported. Therefore, the model has to invest in transportation technologies
corresponding to the captured amount. As for the Balmorel model, the TIMES-Nordic
considers technologies which are both on- and offshore, available at different distances for
all regions. Furthermore, there is the inclusion of options between ships and pipelines.
The transformation of capture CO2 to transported is assumed to be happening under
a 100 % efficiency, meaning that none of the CO2 is lost during transport, the same is
assumed upon delivery.
The transportation costs are added differently as compared to the rest of the technologies.
Since the transportation technologies costs are different between regions due to various
distances and volumes transported. These costs are therefore regional dependent and
are added from the Trans-files to the SubRes-files, which can also be seen in figure 6.1.
The costs included in these files are the investment and FOM.
The final step in the CCS expansion is the storage technologies. It is here the captured
CO2 ends, after being transported and is transformed into stored CO2. The storage and
transport technologies are very similar in the ways of modelling. It is seen by e.g. the
options on- and offshore with saline aquifers and hydrocarbon fields offshore, and only
saline aquifers onshore.
Specifically for the storage step, the storage locations are according to regions given a
maximum storage potential for different distances, which matches the capacities shown
in 4.10 in chapter 4. This simulates the maximum capabilities of storage in the Nordic
region, for each of the investigated countries e.g. onshore storage is only considered for
Denmark.
Therefore, the model determines the best suited technologies for the specific situation,
and sends technology information back to the model itself, where it calculates system
costs, productions, and emissions etc.

6.1.1
Carbon budget and negative emissions
A CO2 budget is simulated in the TIMES-Nordic model by setting a total bound on the
emissions allowed in 2040 and onward. Thus, showing how the energy system could look
like by complying with this bound.
The bound is added to the model ensuring at least zero emissions by the simulation year
2040. This ensures that the model aims to reach the at least neutral and possibly a
negative emissions state, by this year. Therefore, if e.g. the capture capacity of biomass
in either the power and heat or industry sector is increased it could lead to net negative
emissions in the system. This bound stated in the workbook is shown in figure 6.2. The
new aspect of the model is the ability of applying a negative contribution to the total
emissions. The negative contribution bound sets a capacity on the total emissions to
zero in 2040. The bound is also shown in figure 6.1, as the negative emissions moving
into the process CO2 Emitted.
Figure 6.2: Bound built-up in TIMES-Nordic model.
Figure 6.2 shows an equation of emission flows in 2040, which is set to be zero by then.
From the upper the following equation 6.1 is formulated.
CO2ELC+CO2IND+CO2RES+CO2SUP+CO2TRA+CO2ELC,W+CO2NotCap−CO2Sto,Biom ≤0
(6.1)
The upper CO2 first six terms covers the different sectors in the model such as power and
heat (ELC), industry (IND), residential (RES), supply (SUP) and power and heat,
waste (ELC, W). The next CO2NotCap covers the emissions which are not captured in
the capturing process, due to the capture rate. The final CO2Sto,Biom covers the stored
biomass in the system, since this is assumed give a negative contribution when captured
and stored, it is added with a negative to the equation. It should be stated that all of
the upper variables in equation 6.1 are also variables in the model. This means that
it can choose to set them different from zero by themselves, but is not forced to do
so. Furthermore, if it sets some different from zero apart from the biomass stored, it is
forced to store biomass as the bound is set to be zero in 2040.

Net negative emission system
Figure 7.1 shows the total emissions and CO2 storage of the system for three different

still exist in the CCS case, they are counterbalanced with the negative emissions in the
system. The renewable case is lowered to a ”zero” because it is the only option to meet
the emission bound. However, emissions are present, these represent emissions that are
not possible to remove with none emitting technologies in the current models.

8.3
Limitations and boundaries the of study
Although this study provides a good insight into how CCS technologies can be implemented
into an energy system model and how that effects the overall system, it is important to
consider its constraints and limitations.
Firstly, the limitation regarding the costs used in the study are discussed. The materials
in terms of defining the costs of the CCS technologies are scares, and therefore the costs
have a large margin of uncertainty. The main concern relates to the capture costs, where
most of it is based information from various sources. A investigation made on the average
capture costs are covered in appendix I.7, where in table I.22 these costs are found in
the unit EUR/CO2.
Comparing the costs to the ones from the ETSAP report [27],
where the capture costs range from 30 EUR/tCO2 captured to 90 EUR/tCO2 captured
and from 13 EUR/tCO2 captured to 97 EUR/tCO2 captured for CCS capture in the
power and heat and industry respectively, the cost for new capacities follow the same
tendencies while the cost for retrofitted is most likely too large. The ETSAP report

larger. However, the study’s capture costs are up too three as large as stated by ETSAP,
which is quite high. This can be misleading; however, the cost is not a definitive factor
of whether the retrofitted is chosen in the model and therefore not a cause for concern.
When looking at the investment cost for biomass, it is relatively large compared to
the 30 to 90 EUR/tCO2 captured range in ETSAP. This mainly stems from limited

Another limitation of the study relates to the inner workings of the models.
The
models operates under optimal conditions with a perfect foresight and perfect market,
as mentioned earlier in chapter 3. When the models run, they therefore know exactly for
how long a technology is going to produce, furthermore, with the aspect of the perfect
market the models can coordinate the perfect combination of production possibilities in
the system. This is shown by the quick shift of technologies as seen in figures 7.1 and 7.2.
Therefore, the models operate in an idealised world, as this transition would in reality
happen more gradually.
In terms of the biomass usage in the model, it is important to look at how it is
considered in the model, both emission- and trade-wise. The biomass emissions are seen
as being completely neutral when used in the model. However, the annual potential
and consumption of 2050 are shown in figure I.13 in appendix I, and the potential is
taken from multiple sources, giving it a reasonable level of uncertainty. The assumptions
with regards to whether biomass is considered carbon neutral are numerous, and these
should also be applied to the potentials available each year, as many states that biomass
might not be as carbon neutral as currently stated in the model. The whole concept
of the negative emissions are bound in the assumption that biomass is carbon neutral,
and therefore an essential assumption for this study. This should thus be kept in mind,
when considering the results of the study.
Moreover, it is indicated that the biomass is easily accessible both mining- and import-wise.
This could be considered to be controlled more, to make the outcomes more realistic.
In the models, biomass mining and import is controlled only through costs, and there
is no limit to the amount that can be imported from outside the bounds of the model,
i.e., outside the Nordic regions. This is problematic as there is not an endless supply
of biomass in reality. Therefore, the model could be induced with either a bound on
the total amount capable of being imported from outside the spectrum of the model, or
the model could be introduced to a discretisation of the biomass prices. Subsequently,
it would be possible to increase the price with increased importation resulting in more
realistic results.
Many assumptions are applied to the transportation of CO2 to be stored, which differs
from the reality.

emissions would properly be lowered due to a more renewable energy system. However,
it gives an approach of putting value on the transportation technologies.
Additionally, when CCS transportation system are installed it normally works in plateau
levels, and the likelihood that it will reach a full utilisation, compared its built capacity,
from day one is unlikely. Consequently, this induces the risk of the transport system
being under used during its lifetime. This is in accordance with Kjärstad et al. and
Skagestad et al. [26, 24], and therefore suggest the idea of the transport of CO2 is
ramped-up gradually, subsequently effecting which transport technology is most interesting
to use. If the flexibility of the ships is also included, the ships could then be used at one
location during ramp-up, and then be moved to another location when the location has
its transportation at full capacity.
Lastly, there are also some limitations to the storage cost defined. The costs consider
drilling wells, monitoring, etc. However, the point of injection rates is not implemented,
as according to Ramírez et al. [23], could effect the costs considerably. This is however
not investigated in this study, and could be argued to be covered already for the volumes
transported in the transportation costs.
8.4
Becoming net negative
The aim of this study is to determine how the Nordics can become net CO2 negative by
2040. The results show that the Nordics can only become net negative if emissions are
removed from the atmosphere. This it is possible by using either the CCS technology
or other natural solutions such as forestation, which should be built at a capacity that
would be removing emissions at a higher rate than they are being emitted. However, the
use of these removal technologies are alone not enough, and action of lowering emissions
must be taken in order to reach the targets of becoming net CO2 negative. Therefore,
emissions from large emitting sectors such as the transport sector, and industrial sector,
and power and heat sector should lower their emissions considerably by switching to
non- or low-emitting technologies, e.g. by electrifying the inland transport sector.
The use of the CCS technologies should be implemented into sector on which they have
a high impact. With the current existing technologies this concerns a combination with
energy or industrial producing technologies, where the created off gases are rich in CO2.
The energy producing technologies covers CHP plants, and heat boilers, and heavy
industrial boilers and other processes found mainly in productions such as chemical,
cement, steel and iron, and aluminium production. Furthermore, using CCS in combination
==PAGE:115==
8.4 Becoming net negative
91
with fossil fuels, lowers the emissions - making them close to carbon neutral. However,
combining it with newer technologies, using biomass, could create a negative emission
flow through the assumption of carbon neutral biomass.
Due to large potentials of storing CO2 in both on- and offshore geological storage
locations, and as large portfolio where CCS can be implemented exist, there is a large
opportunity that these technologies can be implemented into the Nordics in a feasible
manner. Using emission clusters, the transportation of CO2 to these locations can be
made viable, lowering risks and costs from each of the regions considered.
Therefore, a solution of how the Nordics can become net CO2 negative by 2040 is to define
an energy system which combines the CCS case and the renewable case. The renewable
case would lower the impact of CO2 emissions, and the usage of CCS in combination
with biomass would allow the possibility of utilising the aspect of carbon payback, which
is unavoidable for reaching both the 1.5◦C degree and 2◦C degree target.
For a more practical solution, incentives for the power and heat and industry sector
is needed.
The use of CCS in large scale projects is a new concept and therefore
the effects of CCS in energy systems are uncertain. Hence, more research is needed.
However, to successfully implement and study this technology in large scale projects,
new legal frameworks concerning CCS are needed.
Moreover, these studies need to
focus on identifying stakeholders and their interest in CCS and address the concepts of
sustainability, safety and health in relations to these technologies [52].
An approach to make CCS technologies a more attractive solution, is to implement it in
terms combining CCS and CO2 utilisation. According to the Finnish technology centre
VVT [53], the approach involves using the captured CO2 in other processes, either as a
raw material or as a part in a bulk of chemicals for further production (a more detailed
description can be seen in appendix J). This could increase the use, as it is possible to
sell captured CO2 for further use and production. However, an important aspect of this
approach is that most of the emission of CO2 into the atmosphere is not avoided, only
delayed. Therefore, this is not optimal in terms of become net CO2 negative. More
investigations are needed in terms of understanding the full potential of the captured
CO2.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
The world is facing a global challenge as the effects of climate change influences the
human ability to adapt. Therefore, a record number of the world’s countries have agreed
to reduce the CO2 emissions and thereby limiting the average temperature increase to
2◦C, and preferably 1.5◦C compared to pre-industrial levels. However, as the global
carbon budget has reached a point where more drastic measures must be taken in order
to reach the targets. Therefore, the Nordic countries, Denmark, Norway and Sweden
are investigating the ambitious goal, to become CO2 negative. Thus, the main purpose
of this study is to determine how the Nordic countries can become net CO2 negative by
2040. This is determined by using the two energy system models TIMES-Nordic and
Balmorel with CCS technologies implemented. These technologies have been defined
using specified data and assumptions that are customised specifically for the regions
considered.
The result of these models shows that by implementing CCS technologies while transitioning
to more renewable and non-emitting technologies, it is the most favourable way to
achieve a net negative state, than going completely renewable. Renewable and non- or
low-emitting technologies allow for a more feasible approach of reducing CO2 emissions
while the CCS technologies, when used with fossil fuels or biomass, allows for a neutral
or negative emissions respectively in the system. Considering the total costs for a system
using CCS compared to a system without are lowered with 2.3%.
Analysis of the results of the base case, where emissions are lowered through CO2
taxes, the system shifts to less emitting and renewable technologies.
Sectors which
are influenced the most, are inland transport, industry and power and heat. It is only
when the cost of emitting becomes relatively high, that the models chooses to utilise
CCS technologies to lower the emissions and subsequently the cost.
Based on this
the first hypothesis is confirmed - that using CCS is not the most attractive solution.
This can also be seen looking at the power and heat production. There the renewable
solutions are rather chosen than CHP production with CCS technologies, which only
covers around 1.5% of the total power production. In the CCS case, where a CO2 bound
is set, emissions are reduced by the use of CCS technologies.
The second hypothesis is confirmed through both the CCS and the base case - that
industry sector is more interesting for CCS technologies than the power and heat sector.
This is based on the amount of CCS present in the power and heat and heavy industry
production.
Comparing the base to the CCS case there is an increase in the CHP
plants covering 5% in 2050 of the total system power production. In the heavy industry
production, concerning chemical, cement, steel and iron, and aluminium production,
==PAGE:118==
94
9 Conclusion
approximately 90% in 2050 of this production in the system is covered by CCS related
production. Even though the largest amount of biomass is captured in the power and
heat sector, most of the industry sector has converted. Where the share between power
and heat and industry CCS capture is 88% and 12% respectively.
The results confirm that creating a system with CCS allowing emissions from the most
expensive solutions create a less expensive system compared to a similar renewable case.
In comparison to the emission budgets stated by the IPCC, in relation to the Paris
agreement, a rapid lowering of emissions is needed. The two cases of the renewable and
CCS case state that the emissions are lowered, however, they are in total 1633 MtCO2
and 1516 MtCO2 off target in order to reach the 1.5◦C limit. Here the CCS case enables
the idea of establishing capacity for carbon payback. In relation to the CCS case, and
the carbon payback aspect, by assuming the same emissions from after 2050, a payback
already by 2080 would require more than a doubling of the current biomass capture
capacity. This would follow a extremely high impact on the energy system, and not be
sustainable. Therefore, the emissions must be reduced in line with removing them, by
lowering emissions and removing CO2 using e.g. CCS or forestation.
When the CCS is chosen, it is always done in an economically manner, choosing the
cheapest solution. Results show that this is done by choosing CHP biomass steam plants,
using oxyfuel-combustion in the power and heat sector, and implementing biomass in
the chemical industry production. Furthermore, the closest storage locations are utilised
first, transporting large amounts by pipelines, both on- and offshore. Among Nordic
countries, Sweden has the largest capture capacity covering 58%, whereas Norway and
Denmark covers the remaining, by 33% and 9 % respectively.
In consideration of the costs related to the CCS technologies, the results indicate that
the capture costs in 2050 covers approximately 91% of the total CCS costs, where the
transportation and storage costs covers 4.5% each. Therefore, through an analysis of
the capture investment costs the effects of the related the capacity installed and actual
amount captured, the impact of costs changes shows an increasing interest for lower
costs and vice verse. The CO2 bound is always kept, however, capacity installed and
captured amount complies with the relative change of costs. When capture capacity
becomes relatively cheap it increases around a 20% cost decrease. However, at a 30%
cost increase it starts towards a more renewable state. In terms of the total captured,
this increased by 6% if costs decrease by 30%, and in turn, decreases to around 3% when
costs are increased to same amount.
Considering the CCS case, the technology is forced into the system bringing the net
emissions to be negative due to the CO2 bound. Compared to the base case, a small
amount is added, however, not nearly enough to reach any of the temperature targets.
Thus, supporting the third hypothesis, that frameworks and legal legislation are needed
to push the use of CCS technologies.
Therefore, this study shows that the CCS technology can be successfully implemented
into the Nordic energy system generating negative emissions through a system transitioning
towards a renewable and low-emitting system, while subsidising the CCS technologies.

CHAPTER 10
Future work
The TIMES-Nordic model is limited in terms of the industry sector, as no specific
capture technology is added to the technologies available – not distinguishing between
post combustion and oxyfuel. Therefore, to get a clearer view, of the specific capture
technology dominating in the heavy industry. This should be done by expanding the
current model layout with the other technologies, using the correct ratios related to the
technologies. Furthermore, currently the heavy industry is represented by only having
heat boilers. However, heavy industry is much more complex, as there are very specific
technology processes, that are different for each of the industry productions. Including
this would increase the liability of implementing the CCS technologies directly to where
it is available.
This study is built on implementing CCS into energy systems, but there is another
aspect of the carbon capture - the utilisation of the CO2. The emissions are lowered in
the model to comply with bounds or taxes, but adding this could increase the incentive
for the model to implement CCS into the system, as it could induced lowered costs on
other processes, which uses CO2.
Moreover, the biomass is in the model represented slightly incorrectly. It seems that
biomass it very easy to import, and that there is no real difference between the biomass
mined and imported. Therefore, the representation of mined and imported biomass need
to be updated to get more realistic results.
Finally, it is wanted to update the costs in terms of the technologies used. It seems that
costs are slightly off in terms of biomass technologies. Therefore, it is wanted to find a
new method in terms of defining costs for use of capture technologies fitted to biomass
technologies.

D.1.1
Losses from geological storage locations
According to an investigation made by Streit et. al. [49], the losses from geological
storage locations are explained by permeable zones and permeable faults. The permeable
zones exists on the border of the storage and are made of permeable silt (Mud) and
sand lenses (Porous material that is thick in the middle and thin on the edges), which
sub-seismic fault fracture networks are created from which the CO2 can escape. The
permeable faults are faults in the permeable border in the top e.g. cracks on the surface
of the storage structure.
Even so that these cracks in the formation exists, there are some mechanisms to consider
to understand the likelihood of the CO2 escaping.
The first stated it the buoyancy forces which occurs in the storage. It can be stated that
these are very likely to exist. Water exists in the saline formations, and buoyancy forces
==PAGE:143==
D.1 Accumulated storage potentials and their capacity available
119
would be able to put the CO2 towards the cracks and lead to leakage from the storage.
However, according to Sally M. Benson who gave a presentation on Geological storage
of carbon dioxide for negative emissions [54]. Here she stated that CO2 is stored at a
depth where the CO2 is denser than water. Thus, buoyancy forces would actually keep
the CO2 stored in the geological formations. This is also proven in existing projects such
as the Sleipner project in Norway.
Another aspect is injection-related over pressures. This is likely to happen, but is very
dependent on the mechanisms installed in the system. However, the system is assumed
to be regulating to always optimising its injection into the storage. Therefore, losses
from this can be assumed to be unlikely to happen.
Over pressures due to earthquakes is the final aspect of mechanisms which could induce
losses in the storage.
The study conducted by Streit et.
al.
[49] only considers
earthquakes of a magnitude of larger than 5 on the Richter scale to have a substantial
inpact on the losses in the storage. However on self-studies made it was found that
the largest earthquake that has occurred in both the North sea and the Baltic sea over
the last 23 years has been 4.8 on the scale as stated by [55, 56]. Furthermore, it was
found using the recurrence interval approach as stated by [57], the return period for an
earthquake larger than 5 on the Ricther scale is estimated to be 40 years with an annual
probability of 2.5 %. Meaning that it is very unlikely to happen. Furthermore, it should
also be taken into account of how close the actual earthquake occurs compared to the
storage location. Therefore, it is deemed very unlikely for the storage to emit due to
earthquakes in this investigation. An explicit calculation of the return period can be
found in appendix D.
Comparing the upper statements with the investigation by Streit et. al. it can be stated
that the leakages occurring would most likely be happening as fault leakage, where it is
primarily due to buoyancy forces. They give an estimate of expected leakage to be of
6-60 tonnes/yr, giving an average of 33 tonnes/yr. Comparing this to the total storage
available (in table 4.9, which is 103 Gt) it is approximately a loss of 0.00003% per year.
Therefore, losses from storage are assumed negligible.

Knowing the average energy production of the plants, and the efficiency penalty for the
specific capture technology from ETRI, the total energy production of the plant can be
calculated:
Etot,CCS,fuel = EEPT,fuel + EEPT,fuel · ηpenal,CCS,ETRI,fuel
(F.6)
Where EEPT,fuel is the energy production of the plants using a specific fuel type.
ηpenal,CCS,ETRI,fuel is the efficiency penalty of the specific CCS technology, for the specific
fuel type, as stated in ETRI.
The energy production can then be recalculated into the emissions captured from the
plant itself, using the emission factors stated in the TIMES-Nordic model and using the
capture rate of the specific capture technology used as stated by ETRI.
CO2cap,CCS,fuel = Etot,CCS,fuelρETRI,fuel
(F.7)
ρTIMES,fuel is the capture rate of the specific CCS technology, mathing the fuel type
used to with it is installed.

by stating the following:
Ecap,CCS,fuel = EEPT,fuel · ηpenal,CCS,ETRI,fuel
CO2cap,fuel
(F.8)

Costinv,MEUR/ktCO2 = Costinv,MEUR/MW/AFA/η/Ltime/CapCO2

specific plant, and CapCO2 is the CO2 captured in the process.
As can be seen from the upper equation is that the costs are found using the effiency of
the plant. As the biomass plants in general are more expensive than the conventional
fuel plants, and afterwards divided with an efficiency, which is also in general lower, the
costs are expected to be much larger.
On the other hand, the boiler costs are very low, this is mainly due to the large efficiencies
found in them, and furthermore the ratios that defines the costs of them. These are in

APPENDIX J
CO2 utilisation
Another layer in the CCS technologies is the utilisation, where the CO2 is utilised in
other processes as a raw material. The interest is increasing more in the industrial sector
as to utilise CO2 captured from a CCS process in either the power and heat or industry
sector. There are currently still a need for a sustainability assessment analysis and life
cycle analysis in order to evaluate the actual potential of the CO2 utilisation, besides
the economic advantage and disadvantages, as stated by VVT [53].
Today CO2 is already being used in commercial context, where it is used both as it
is and also in connection with a bulk of chemicals and fuels.
An overview of where the CO2 is used in different productions can be seen in figure J.1.
This shows examples of where the CO2 is used in its pure form e.g. used to carbonate
drinks, used to adjust pH levels in water treatment plants, used for EOR etc.
Furthermore CO2 can also be used as a feed stock, where it is used chemical and
fuels production, as a solvent or used for enhanced cultivation of algae production. The
productions on which it is used in this context can be seen in figure J.2. Today the current
global usage of CO2 worldwide is 130 Mtpa, where the production of Urea stands for the
largest amount cover 60% of this consumption. Even though the CO2 is transformed
to later use, it is for most of the cases released again in the form of CO2. Therefore,
utilisation is rather a question of postponing the emission of CO2 than removing it from
the atmosphere. Furthermore, in the reuse of emitted CO2 is should be at most cases
be used in a cycle where it can be captured again assuring that it is not released where
it cannot be captured for either storing or utilisation purposes. However, in regard to
utilisation more research is needed.
