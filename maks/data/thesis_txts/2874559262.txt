Abstract
The accelerating shift toward renewable energy—driven by rising fossil fuel costs and
growing environmental concerns—has positioned wind energy as a cornerstone of sus-
tainable power generation. Among the various approaches, offshore wind farms have
attracted increased attention due to their access to stronger and more consistent wind
resources. However, optimizing their performance necessitates accurate modeling of the
complex wake interactions that occur between turbines.
While traditional numerical models offer high precision, they are computationally ex-
pensive.
Conversely, purely data-driven machine learning models often lack physical
interpretability and require large datasets. To address this gap, physics-informed ma-
chine learning (PIML) has emerged as a promising hybrid approach, combining the
predictive capabilities of artificial intelligence with the constraints of physical laws.
This thesis investigates the development and evaluation of six distinct PIML models
aimed at simulating and predicting wake interactions within wind farms. These models
are built upon two foundational frameworks: the Bastankhah–Porté-Agel (BPA) Gaus-
sian wake model and Pop’s basis tensor methodology. Specifically, two models are based
on Pop’s basis tensors, two utilize the BPA wake formulation, one incorporates a wake
decay function, and one unified model synthesizes all of these approaches. Additionally,
a purely data-driven neural network is developed for comparison.
The results demonstrate that models based solely on Pop’s basis tensors generally out-
perform the others in both accuracy and computational eﬀiciency. The final chapter also
presents several supplementary models which, although less effective, provide valuable
insights and highlight potential directions for future research.

Preface
For as long as I can remember, I have been driven by a desire to understand how things
work and to explore the mechanisms behind the phenomena around me. This curiosity
eventually led me to pursue a degree in engineering, specifically in the field of mechanical
engineering.
During my undergraduate studies, I developed a deeper appreciation for fluid dynamics
and, more broadly, energy-related phenomena. It felt as though I had found the path
I was meant to follow. In today’s world, energy is both a vital and complex concept.
Gaining insight into its mechanisms and transformations provides individuals with the
tools to drive meaningful change and shape the future.
This realization inspired me to pursue graduate studies in Energy Engineering at Po-
litecnico di Milano.
Through my coursework, I explored various methods of energy
conversion and harvesting, which sparked a strong interest in renewable energy. Given
my foundation in fluid mechanics and a long-standing fascination with fluid phenomena,
wind energy, in particular, captured my attention and became the focus of my master’s
thesis.
Furthermore, in light of the rapid advancement of science and the growing role of artificial
intelligence across many fields, I chose to integrate AI into my research. This decision
marked a new chapter in my ongoing exploration of renewable energy systems.
I would like to express my sincere gratitude to Professor Hamid Sarlak at DTU and
Professor Fabio Inzoli at Politecnico di Milano for their invaluable support and guidance
throughout this project. I am deeply thankful for their mentorship.
Lastly, I extend my heartfelt appreciation to my family for their unwavering support
and encouragement. Without their belief in me, I would not have reached this point.

Introduction
One of the most pressing challenges the world faces today is the rapidly increasing
demand for energy. According to the International Energy Agency (IEA), “Global energy
demand grew at a faster-than-average pace in 2024 as the consumption of electricity rose
around the world” (Agency 2025).
The world is currently undergoing a significant transition from fossil fuels to renewable
energy sources. As highlighted in the IEA’s World Energy Outlook 2023: “The share
of fossil fuels in primary energy demand declines from 80% over the last two decades to
73% in the STEPS by 2030, 69% in the APS, and 62% in the NZE scenario” (Agency
2023). This transition offers numerous environmental and economic benefits that have
been widely acknowledged.
There are generally two primary approaches to addressing the sharp increase in global
energy demand: (1) constructing new power plants, which requires substantial financial
investment, or (2) improving the capacity factor and eﬀiciency of existing facilities.
Currently, the average capacity factor for onshore wind turbines is approximately 23%,
while for offshore turbines it reaches around 35% (WindEurope 2025). These values
indicate significant potential for enhancing turbine performance in the future.
1.1
Motivation of Work
Among all renewable energy sources, wind energy has consistently received special at-
tention. It accounted for 19% of all electricity consumed in the EU (WindEurope 2025),
and projections indicate it could meet up to 29.6% of the EU’s electricity demand by
2030 (WindEurope 2019). The growth in electricity generation from renewable sources
between 2013 and 2023 largely reflects expansion in two key technologies: wind power
and solar power (Eurostat 2024).
Wind farms are generally categorized into two types: offshore and onshore. Currently,
onshore wind farms dominate the EU market, accounting for approximately 87% of wind-
generated electricity (WindEurope 2024). However, offshore wind farms offer several
notable advantages over their onshore counterparts. These include stronger and more
stable winds, easier installation of large turbine components (Tumse et al. 2024), higher
scalability and energy generation, faster construction times, reduced time to obtain
planning permissions (Review 2025), and lower environmental turbulence (Ma et al.
2025). These benefits have led to a steady increase in the deployment of offshore wind
farms (Tumse et al. 2024).
As mentioned earlier, the average capacity factor for offshore wind turbines is around
35%, indicating room for substantial performance improvement.
A fundamental requirement for enhancing turbine performance is the ability to model

turbine behavior accurately. This helps identify key areas for improvement and informs
effective strategies for optimization.
One critical factor affecting turbine eﬀiciency is the wake effect—a complex phenomenon
that significantly influences power production (Pathway 2023). Accurate modeling of
wake behavior and downstream velocity fields is therefore essential.
The most precise method for analyzing wake behavior involves collecting physical data
from multiple sensors positioned both upstream and downstream of the turbine. How-
ever, this approach is often impractical due to several challenges: sensor installation
complexity, diﬀiculty in identifying optimal sensor placement, limited reliability in harsh
weather, unstructured or noisy data, and high resource consumption (Krishnamurthi et
al. 2020).
As a result, numerical modeling has become a widely adopted alternative. Yet, accuracy
in such models often comes at the cost of high computational demands.
For exam-
ple, Large Eddy Simulation (LES) can resolve fine-scale wake structures but requires
dense mesh grids and small time steps, making it computationally intensive (Church-
field et al. 2012).
To manage this, many large-scale wind farm simulations rely on
Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) models, which are more eﬀicient but less ac-
curate (Vanderwende et al. 2016).
A key question, therefore, is whether it’s possible to retain the low computational cost
of RANS while improving model accuracy.
Machine learning (ML) has emerged as a promising solution for enhancing the accuracy
of RANS-based simulations (McConkey, Yee, and Lien 2022). However, conventional
ML models do not inherently adhere to physical laws, which often necessitates large
datasets to achieve reliable results.
To address this limitation, physics-informed machine learning (PIML) has been pro-
posed. PIML integrates governing physical principles into ML frameworks, improving
both prediction accuracy and physical consistency. This approach also reduces the vol-
ume of training data required.
The aim of this thesis is to develop a physics-informed, machine-learning-based frame-
work for modeling turbine wake interactions in offshore wind farms.

1.2
Background of Turbulence Modeling and Wind Turbine Wake
Interaction
Wind turbine wake behavior and turbulence modeling are crucial to optimizing wind
farm eﬀiciency and minimizing the levelized cost of energy (LCoE). Downstream wind
speed can decrease by 10–40%, and turbulence intensity increases within the wake, both
significantly impacting power generation and structural loading.
Early wake prediction models, such as the Jensen model (1983) (N. Jensen 1983), used
simplified conservation principles to estimate wake spreading. However, the inherent
complexities of atmospheric boundary layer (ABL) turbulence, terrain variations, and
rotor aerodynamics have led to the development of more advanced computational fluid
dynamics (CFD) and experimental methods. This section integrates foundational theo-
ries, modern numerical models, and emerging approaches in wake and turbulence mod-
eling, with a particular focus on floating offshore wind turbine (FOWT) interactions.
1.2.1

farms.

Numerical Methods
High-fidelity large-eddy simulations (LES) resolve large-scale turbulent structures and
model subgrid-scale (SGS) effects. When coupled with actuator line models (ALM),
LES has become a standard tool for analyzing wind turbine wakes. For example, Li
et al. (Y. Li, Yu, and Sarlak 2025) used LES-ALM in OpenFOAM to simulate twin
surging FOWTs and observed that platform motions enhanced wake recovery by 2%
under turbulent inflow conditions.
In contrast, Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) models, although computation-
ally eﬀicient, are limited in capturing transient wake dynamics such as meandering.
Experimental Validation
Wind tunnel experiments (Schottler, Bartl, and Mühle 2018) have revealed non-Gaussian
velocity increments in yawed wakes, exposing limitations in steady-state models. Field

1.2.3
Wind Turbine Wake Interactions
Fixed vs. Floating Turbines
Bottom-fixed turbines typically exhibit wake interactions characterized by velocity deficit
superposition and wake meandering. In contrast, floating offshore wind turbines (FOWTs)
introduce additional complexity due to their platform motions (e.g., pitch, surge, heave),
which induce time-varying inflow conditions.
LES studies have shown that surging FOWTs increase the apparent wind speed Vapp at
the rotor:

Phase-Synchronized Dynamics
For twin FOWTs arranged in tandem, the phase difference ∆ϕS0 of their surge motions
has a negligible impact on power production (variations less than 0.4% at 5D spacing).
However, surge-induced periodic coherent structures (SIPeCS) can enhance turbulence
kinetic energy (TKE) between turbines. These structures persist farther downstream
in laminar inflow but are dampened by ambient turbulence, limiting their downstream
influence.

Background of Machine Learning-Based Wake Models
Applications of Machine Learning in Simulation Problems
Artificial intelligence (AI) is now widely recognized as a general-purpose technology
with profound and far-reaching impacts on modern life (Vaswani et al. 2022). The term
“artificial intelligence” was oﬀicially introduced in the summer of 1956 at a landmark
conference held at Dartmouth College, organized by John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky,
Claude Shannon, and Nathaniel Rochester (Today 2023). Since then, AI has undergone
numerous technological revolutions.
Early machine learning (ML) efforts were constrained by limited computational power
and data availability. However, rapid technological advancements—including faster pro-
cessors, improved hardware, and expanded storage capacities—have enabled the process-
ing of larger datasets and the application of increasingly complex algorithms (Models
2023).

Today, AI-based approaches—such as neural networks, machine learning algorithms,
and generative models—are opening new frontiers in simulating complex physical sys-
tems (Rescale 2025).
Wind turbine wake modeling has greatly benefited from the evolution of machine learn-
ing techniques, which provide promising alternatives to classical analytical and com-
putational fluid dynamics (CFD) models.
While analytical models like Jensen’s are
computationally inexpensive, they lack the precision needed to capture the complex and
dynamic behavior of wakes, especially under varying ambient conditions (Göçmen et al.
2020). In contrast, high-fidelity CFD simulations are accurate but computationally in-
tensive, making them impractical for large wind farm applications (Göçmen et al. 2020;
Luo, L. Wang, et al. 2024a).

1.3.1
Machine Learning Approaches in Wake Modeling
Data-Driven Wake Prediction
Machine learning has demonstrated strong capabilities in predicting wake characteristics
by identifying nonlinear relationships between inflow conditions and wake parameters.
In particular, Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) have shown the ability to accurately
model wake velocity deficits and turbulence kinetic energy (TKE) distributions. These
models often achieve accuracy levels comparable to CFD simulations, while remaining
computationally eﬀicient (Göçmen et al. 2020).
Recent research indicates that ANN-based models can reduce prediction errors by up to
30% when compared to conventional analytical approaches—provided they are trained
on high-fidelity data (Göçmen et al. 2020; Bastankhah and Porté-Agel 2023).
Hybrid Physics-ML Models
A key advancement in this field has been the integration of physical modeling with
machine learning to create hybrid models. For example, combining Jensen’s wake model
with ANNs allows the model to account for nonlinear effects in wake expansion—effects
that are typically oversimplified in traditional analytical models (Bastankhah and Porté-
Agel 2023). These hybrid models offer the dual benefits of physical interpretability and
the pattern recognition capabilities of ML (X. Yang and Sotiropoulos 2019).
Reduced-Order Modeling
Reduced-order modeling (ROM) techniques, when used in conjunction with ML, offer
an eﬀicient solution to representing large-scale wake flow fields. For instance, Proper
Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) combined with neural networks enables the compact
reconstruction of wake behavior from sparse sensor data. This results in significant cost
reduction without compromising accuracy (Luo, L. Wang, et al. 2024a). Such methods
are particularly useful for real-time wake estimation in operational wind farms (Luo, L.
Wang, et al. 2024a).

Problem Statement and Research Questions
Despite the remarkable advancements and numerous benefits of artificial intelligence
(AI) models, their application in the simulation of physical phenomena continues to
present several critical challenges. One of the major limitations lies in the heavy de-
pendence of machine learning (ML) models on large volumes of high-quality training
data to achieve acceptable accuracy—particularly for complex physical systems (Lab-
oratory 2025).
Furthermore, these models are vulnerable to biases inherent in their
training datasets. If the data used is biased, the resulting predictions will likely reflect
those same biases, potentially leading to inaccurate or unintended outcomes in critical
domains such as finance, healthcare, or engineering (Lab 2023).
Another concern is the limited variability and generalization of ML outputs. As noted
by Brynjolfsson and McAfee, ML systems are grounded in statistical inference rather
than deterministic laws, which restricts their reliability in applications requiring strict
adherence to physical principles (Profolus 2023). Additionally, a vast body of prior sci-
entific knowledge remains underutilized in most current ML implementations (Raissi,
Perdikaris, and Karniadakis 2019), representing a missed opportunity to improve per-
formance, accuracy, and interpretability.
Perhaps most critically, machine learning systems often operate as ”black boxes,” pro-
viding little insight into how specific predictions are generated (Profolus 2023). This
lack of interpretability undermines user trust and poses significant barriers to scientific
validation.
To overcome these limitations, there is a growing need to integrate domain knowledge—
particularly the physical laws governing the system—into ML models. By embedding
these principles directly into the structure of learning algorithms, it becomes possible to
constrain predictions within the boundaries of physical feasibility, reduce data require-
ments, enhance model robustness, and shorten training time.

The Neural Network:
Neural networks (NNs) are a class of computational models that have demonstrated
remarkable capabilities in approximating complex nonlinear functions and extracting
intricate patterns from high-dimensional datasets (Goodfellow, Bengio, and Courville
2016). Inspired by the architecture of biological nervous systems, neural networks have
revolutionized multiple domains including computer vision, natural language processing,
speech recognition, and robotics. A typical neural network is composed of an intercon-
nected set of nodes, often referred to as neurons, which are arranged in layers. These
layers can be classified into three primary types: the input layer, one or more hidden
layers, and the output layer. The input layer serves as the entry point of data into the
network. Each neuron in this layer corresponds to a feature of the input data. Following
the input layer are the hidden layers, which are responsible for internal computations
and transformations. These layers allow the network to learn and extract increasingly
abstract representations of the input data. Finally, the output layer delivers the result
of the network’s computations—such as a classification label, regression value, or action
recommendation—depending on the task at hand. Each neuron in the network operates
by computing a weighted sum of its input values, adds a bias term, and then applies
a nonlinear transformation known as the activation function. Mathematically, this is
expressed as:

Types of Neural Networks
There are several types of neural networks, each designed for specific types of tasks and
data structures. The simplest and most foundational is the Feedforward Neural Net-
work (FNN), where data flows strictly from the input to the output without forming
any cycles (Bishop 2006). These networks are primarily used in classification, regres-
sion, and general function approximation tasks.
Despite their simplicity, FNNs can
model highly complex relationships when provided with suﬀicient depth and nonlinear-
ity. Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are specialized for processing grid-like data
structures, particularly images (LeCun, Bengio, and G. Hinton 2015). These networks
utilize convolutional layers to detect local patterns such as edges, textures, and shapes.
Key characteristics of CNNs include local connectivity (each neuron is connected to a
small region of the input), shared weights (the same filter is applied across different
regions), and pooling layers which reduce spatial dimensions while retaining important
features. The hierarchical nature of CNNs makes them ideal for visual tasks.

How Architecture Affects Performance
The architectural design of a neural network has profound implications on its perfor-
mance, generalization, and computational eﬀiciency. One critical consideration is the
balance between depth and width. Deeper networks, with more hidden layers, are ca-
pable of learning hierarchical and compositional features that mirror patterns found
in complex data (Bengio 2009). However, increasing depth may lead to vanishing or
exploding gradients, making training unstable.
On the other hand, wider networks,
with more neurons per layer, might have better memorization capacity and can learn
complex functions in fewer layers. Nevertheless, they risk overfitting and are compu-
tationally more expensive (Hanin 2019). The choice of activation function is another
vital factor. Activation functions introduce nonlinearity into the network, enabling it to
model intricate relationships beyond simple linear mappings. Common choices include
the sigmoid and tanh functions, which suffer from saturation and vanishing gradients,
and the Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU), which offers improved gradient flow and faster
convergence. More advanced alternatives like Leaky ReLU, ELU, and GELU aim to
mitigate the drawbacks of ReLU while retaining its benefits (Glorot and Bengio 2010).
The right activation function can significantly affect both the learning dynamics and
the ultimate accuracy of the model. To prevent overfitting, regularization techniques
are incorporated into the architecture. Dropout randomly disables a fraction of neu-
rons during training, forcing the network to learn more robust features (Srivastava et al.
2014). Batch normalization normalizes intermediate outputs, accelerating training and
stabilizing learning by reducing internal covariate shift (Ioffe and Szegedy 2015). Skip
connections, introduced in ResNet architectures (He et al. 2016), allow gradients to flow
more easily through deeper networks and alleviate issues related to vanishing gradients.
Finally, computational considerations also guide architectural design. The total number
of parameters directly impacts memory usage, while the number and type of operations
affect both training and inference speed. Architectures that support parallel compu-
tation can be more eﬀiciently deployed on modern hardware such as GPUs and TPUs
(Jouppi et al. 2017). Eﬀicient design thus balances model complexity, training time, and
runtime performance.

The wake model used in the present study
The wake model employed in the present study is based on the analytical framework
developed by Bastankhah and Porté-Agel (Bastankhah and Porté-Agel 2014b), which
introduced a novel Gaussian-based formulation for predicting wind turbine wake velocity
distributions. This model was conceived to overcome critical limitations inherent in tra-
ditional wake modeling approaches, such as the Jensen model (N. Jensen 1983) and the
Frandsen model (Frandsen et al. 2006). These classical models often adopt overly sim-
plistic assumptions, particularly the top-hat profile for the velocity deficit, which fails to
capture the gradual and continuous variation of velocity within the wake. Additionally,
they often inadequately incorporate conservation principles—most notably, momentum
conservation is either entirely neglected or inconsistently applied.
The authors of the new model recognized that accurate modeling of wake effects is
paramount for reliable power output estimation in wind farms, as the available wind en-
ergy scales with the cube of the wind speed, U3 (Burton et al. 2001). Inaccurate predic-
tions of the velocity field can therefore result in substantial deviations in estimated power
production. To rectify this, Bastankhah and Porté-Agel proposed a model grounded in
fundamental fluid dynamics, incorporating both mass and momentum conservation prin-
ciples. Crucially, they replaced the top-hat velocity deficit with a Gaussian distribution,
a choice supported by empirical and numerical studies (Chamorro and Porté-Agel 2010;
Y.-T. Wu and Porté-Agel 2011), which consistently report such a distribution in the
far-wake regions of wind turbines.

Power prediction capabilities
One of the most significant advantages of the new wake model lies in its improved ac-

Comparative evaluations reveal that while traditional models can suffer from power
prediction errors ranging from 15% to 30% with merely a 5◦shift in wind direction, the
Gaussian model maintains accuracy within 3% to 7%, making it more robust for wind
farm layout optimization and operation under variable wind conditions.

curvature effects (Craft, Launder, and Suga 1996). These shortcomings are especially
pronounced in flows with secondary motions, recirculation zones, and strong directional
shear, where the underlying assumptions of LEVMs break down.

Hannun, and A. Y. Ng 2013). To mitigate the risk of converging to local minima, an
ensemble of 20 networks with randomized weight initializations was trained. Each net-
work was trained until the validation loss plateaued, ensuring a robust estimation of the
optimal model parameters.
Data Sets
The training dataset comprised six canonical turbulent flows: turbulent duct flow (Pinelli
et al. 2010), channel flow (Moser, John Kim, and Mansour 1999), perpendicular and
inclined jets in crossflow (Ruiz, Oefelein, and Lacaze 2015), flow around a square cylinder
(Ray et al. 2014), and a converging-diverging channel flow (Marquillie, Ehrenstein, and
Laval 2011). Each case provided RANS-generated features and DNS/LES anisotropy
tensors for supervised learning. An additional case involving a wall-mounted cube was
used for validation, and two complex cases—a lower Reynolds number duct flow and
flow over a wavy wall—served as test cases.
Key Results
Quantitative comparisons against baseline RANS models revealed that the TBNN achieved
significantly lower root-mean-square error (RMSE) values for the Reynolds stress anisotropy.
Specifically, the TBNN reduced RMSE by 44% and 56% over LEVM in the duct and
wavy wall test cases, respectively. In a posteriori simulations, the TBNN correctly re-
produced secondary motions in ducts and flow separation in adverse pressure gradient
regions, both of which were missed by LEVM and inadequately captured by standard
MLP architectures. Cross-validation across unseen flows confirmed a consistent average
error reduction of 30%, validating the model’s generalization capability.
Conclusion
The TBNN presents a powerful data-driven alternative to conventional turbulence mod-
els by preserving key physical invariances within a deep learning framework. This ar-
chitecture not only enhances predictive accuracy for Reynolds stress anisotropy but also
leads to improved mean flow field predictions in complex geometries. Its interpretability,
grounded in tensor basis theory, offers a promising path forward for developing robust,
generalizable models for turbulent flow closure.

Physical Assumptions
In the development of the mathematical framework used throughout this study, several
fundamental physical assumptions have been adopted. These assumptions are essential
for simplifying the underlying fluid dynamics and turbulence modeling, thereby enabling
a tractable yet representative formulation of the problem at hand. Each category of as-
sumptions addresses a specific aspect of the physical or mathematical modeling strategy,
including kinematic behavior, turbulence representation, and constraints on mathemati-
cal structure and field smoothness. These are elaborated upon in the subsections below.
2.6.1

The kinematic assumptions pertain to the nature of the flow field under consideration
and govern how the fluid velocities and their spatial derivatives behave throughout the
domain. First and foremost, the flow is assumed to be incompressible. This is math-
ematically expressed by the condition that the divergence of the velocity field is zero,

characterization implies that while the flow retains its three-dimensional nature, one of
the spatial derivatives—typically in the spanwise direction—is neglected or significantly
simplified. This simplification leads to a set of approximate derivative relations among

2.6.4
Neglected Physics
To simplify the governing equations and focus on the primary fluid dynamic mechanisms,
several physical effects have been intentionally neglected. Compressibility effects are
omitted by assuming that the Mach number is much less than one (Ma ≪1), allowing
the density to be treated as constant. This assumption is valid for low-speed flows where
pressure variations are insuﬀicient to cause significant changes in density.
Body forces such as gravity or electromagnetic forces are considered negligible, and the
body force vector is therefore set to zero (b = 0). This reduces the number of external
influences acting on the fluid, which is particularly appropriate when such forces have a
minimal impact compared to inertial or viscous forces.
The fluid is treated as Newtonian, neglecting non-Newtonian behavior.
The viscous
stress tensor then satisfies the linear relation τij = 2µSij, where µ is the dynamic viscosity
and Sij is the strain rate tensor.
This assumption greatly simplifies the constitutive
modeling and is valid for most common fluids such as air and water.
Transport effects of higher-order tensors, particularly those related to turbulence, are
also ignored.
This is represented by assuming that the material derivative of tensor
quantities such as T is approximately zero:
DT
Dt ≈0.
This implies that the local temporal changes and convective transport of such tensors
are negligible relative to their production or dissipation.
Additionally, the analysis assumes that there is no wake decay in the short range. In
practical terms, this means that within a limited downstream distance from a bluff
body or an obstruction, the velocity deficit and turbulence characteristics in the wake
are presumed to remain effectively constant. This assumption simplifies the modeling
of near-wake dynamics and is particularly useful in analyses that are confined to the
immediate post-obstacle region where such decay has not yet manifested.

To ensure mathematical consistency and the validity of differential operations within the
model, certain smoothness requirements are imposed on the velocity fields. Specifically,
the velocity vector field u must be continuously differentiable throughout the domain Ω,
which is a subset of three-dimensional Euclidean space. This is denoted by the regularity
condition

Test case and numerical set up
the paper by (Y. Li, Yu, and Sarlak 2025) investigates wake interactions between two
floating offshore wind turbines (FOWTs) in tandem configuration using high-fidelity
computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulations. The study focuses on two key aspects:
different fixed-surging (F–S) configurations of upstream and downstream rotors, and
phase differences between surging motions when both rotors are surging.
The research is motivated by the need to understand wake interactions in floating wind
farms, which differ significantly from bottom-fixed installations (Kuik et al. 2016; Mi-
callef and Rezaeiha 2021). Previous studies have shown that FOWT motions significantly
influence power performance and wake aerodynamics (Johlas et al. 2021; Rezaeiha and
Micallef 2021). Recent high-fidelity simulations indicate that the surging motions can
alter wake recovery rates, power output, and vortex structures in the wake, motivating
a detailed exploration of their effects under various configurations.
Methodology:
The study employs Large Eddy Simulation (LES) with an actuator line model (ALM)

Key Findings
Fixed–Surging Configurations:
Under turbulent inflow, the presence of a surging upstream rotor enhances the power
output of the downstream turbine by approximately 1.5%. However, in laminar inflow
conditions, this enhancement is far more pronounced, reaching up to 40% for ∆D = 5D.
The power gain is attributed to faster wake recovery and increased inflow velocity at the
downstream rotor due to the upstream rotor’s periodic surging. The surging motion of
the upstream rotor sheds coherent vortical structures, known as Surge-Induced Periodic
Coherent Structures (SIPeCS), which propagate downstream and modulate the inflow
field.
Phase Differences (∆ϕS0):
Variations in the phase difference between the surging motions of the two rotors have
a minimal effect on power output, with changes generally less than 1%. The impact
diminishes with increased spacing; at ∆D = 5D, the phase-induced effects become negli-
gible. Furthermore, the strength of the SIPeCS diminishes with increased rotor spacing,
reducing their influence on downstream aerodynamics.
Turbulence Effects:
Ambient turbulence significantly reduces the aerodynamic benefits induced by surging
motions. The presence of turbulence disrupts the coherence of SIPeCS, limiting their
ability to enhance wake recovery. As a result, in turbulent conditions, the differences in
power output between fixed and surging cases are small. The turbulent kinetic energy
(TKE) profiles remain similar across fixed and surging configurations, suggesting that the
dominant mixing is due to ambient turbulence rather than rotor-induced periodicity.(Y.
Li, Yu, and Sarlak 2025)

3.1
Numerical Setup
The numerical framework implemented in this study follows the high-fidelity computa-
tional fluid dynamics (CFD) methodology proposed by (Y. Li, Yu, and Sarlak 2025).
This approach integrates Large Eddy Simulation (LES) with an Actuator Line Method
(ALM) to resolve the unsteady aerodynamic interactions that occur in the wake of
floating offshore wind turbines (FOWTs). By explicitly resolving large-scale turbulent
eddies and capturing blade-generated forces through distributed actuator line forces,
this method allows the study to quantify complex wake dynamics, particularly under
dynamic platform motion scenarios. The setup includes a rigorous and systematically
designed computational domain, detailed turbine representation, motion implementa-
tion strategies, numerical discretization schemes, boundary conditions, and advanced
data post-processing procedures.
Wind Turbine Rotor Model
The three-bladed National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL) 5MW baseline wind
turbine (Jonkman et al. 2009) is employed as the canonical rotor model throughout the
study. The model selection ensures compatibility with other widely published research
and enables cross-study validation. The key rotor parameters include a rotor diameter
of D = 126 meters, a rated wind speed V0,rated of 11.4 meters per second, and a rated tip
speed ratio λrated of 7.0. These values characterize the rotor’s aerodynamic loading and
operational envelope.
To simplify the analysis and focus purely on wake-induced aerodynamic interactions,
various structural and environmental factors are deliberately excluded from the simula-
tion. These include tower shadowing, blade tilt angles, pre-coning configurations, elastic
blade deformations, ground proximity effects, and wind shear. By neglecting these com-
plexities, the model retains axial symmetry and avoids secondary flow interactions that
could obscure the core effects of turbine motion on wake development.
The actuator line representation divides each turbine blade into 40 equidistant force
points. The mesh at the rotor location is constructed to match the actuator line resolu-
tion, ensuring each force projection point lies within a control volume of size ∆r = D/80.
A tip-loss correction is applied to prevent overprediction of blade forces near the rotor
tips, where induced velocities dominate. This correction uses the Glauert function:

Surging Motion Implementation
The study investigates both static (fixed-base) and dynamic (surging) configurations.
For the latter, the turbines undergo sinusoidal translational motion in the streamwise
(x) direction, emulating platform-induced surge due to wave excitation. The motion is
prescribed as a harmonic function:
pR(t) = AS sin(ωSt +ϕS0)+ pR0
(3.4)
where AS = 4 m is the surge amplitude, selected based on metocean data reflecting
typical displacements for semi-submersible floating platforms. The surge frequency is
ωS = 0.63 rad/s, which lies within the natural range of surge motion observed in floating
foundations under operational conditions. The term ϕS0 denotes the initial phase shift,
which is varied in some configurations to investigate the effect of phase alignment on
wake coupling. The baseline position is set at pR0.
The surge velocity, derived by differentiating the position function, is given by:
VS = ASωS cos(ωSt +ϕS0)
(3.5)
The rotational speed of the upstream rotor is fixed at Ωup = 1.27 rad/s, corresponding
to the rated condition. To maintain an effective tip speed ratio in the more disturbed
wake of the downstream turbine, its rotation speed is reduced to Ωdown = 0.84 rad/s—
approximately two-thirds that of the upstream rotor. This strategic reduction ensures
that the downstream rotor operates eﬀiciently despite reduced inflow velocities.
No
active pitch control is modeled, and all blades maintain a fixed collective pitch angle of
0◦throughout the simulation.
Computational Domain and Mesh
The simulation domain is constructed using finite-volume methods with hierarchical
meshing to capture both large-scale domain physics and fine-scale blade-induced effects.
The total length of the computational domain in the streamwise (x) direction is 10D,
with the upstream rotor fixed at the origin (x = 0). The downstream turbine is positioned
either 3D or 5D downstream, creating two different inter-turbine spacings: ∆D = 3D and
∆D = 5D.
The mesh resolution near the rotors is designed to resolve actuator line forces accurately
and is fixed at ∆= D/80 at mesh refinement Level 4. The grid is progressively coars-
ened away from the turbine region using OpenFOAM’s snappyHexMesh, allowing for
eﬀicient computational scaling while preserving fidelity in wake-sensitive regions. For
laminar inflow cases, the mesh comprises approximately 10.4 million control volumes,
while for turbulent inflow, the count increases slightly to 10.9 million to accommodate
inlet turbulence generation.

Boundary Conditions
The simulations apply physically consistent boundary conditions to mimic realistic flow
development. At the inlet, two distinct profiles are used: a uniform velocity of 11.4 m/s
for laminar cases and a synthetic turbulent inflow field generated using the Divergence-
Free Synthetic Eddy Method (DFSEM) for turbulent simulations. The turbulent inflow
corresponds to a turbulence intensity (TI) of 5.3%, which aligns with typical offshore
atmospheric conditions.
The outlet boundary uses an advective condition, which allows flow disturbances to exit
without reflection. Side boundaries enforce slip conditions to approximate an infinite
lateral extent. Pressure is treated as a symmetric field at the inlet and sidewalls and
fixed at a reference value at the outlet.

Renewable energy is growing rapidly due to high fossil fuel costs and climate concerns, with wind
energy as a leading contributor. Offshore wind farms are now a major research focus because of their
higher wind speeds and better performance. Modeling wind farm performance is essential, especially
to account for wake effects that reduce output. Traditional numerical methods are accurate but
resource-intensive, while AI-based models are faster but need lots of data and can lack physical
accuracy. Physics- informed machine learning now offers a promising solution, improving accuracy
and eﬀiciency by combining AI with physical principles.
This thesis investigates wind farm wake effects using Physics-Informed Machine Learning techniques.
