The utility company Biofos currently explores ways to optimize the anaerobic digestion process at Damhusåen WWTP, Co­
penhagen. The key drivers are the increasing interest in green energy, the need for higher treatment capacities and reducing 
sludge handling costs. The digesters AD1 and AD2 are both fed-batch continuous stirred tank reactors (CSTR) with a re­
cycling line. They receive mixed sludge (primary and secondary) as feedstock. AD1 has recently been upgraded to a contact 
process with a solid-liquid seperation unit in its recycling line to increase CH4-production and sludge stabilization. Further­
more, Biofos is considering a sludge pre-treatment using Cambi thermal hydrolysis to further increase their treatment capacity.
The aim of the study was to develop a model for AD1 and AD2 to predict the state indicators VSS, CH4-production, pH, 
TAN and total VFA using the anaerobic digestion model 1 (ADM1) implemented in the software WEST by DHI. The over­
all methodology of the study followed the Good Modelling Practice (GMP, Rieger et al., 2013). Two sampling campaigns 
(April and May/June) were performed to characterize the influent and collect performance data from the ADs. Daily influent 
grab samples of each campaign were mixed and characterized and fractionated according to Arnell et al. (2016). A Monte 
Carlo-based uncertainty (UA) and global sensitivity analysis (GSA using SRC-method) were carried out for the AD2 model 
using WEST. The calibration of the AD2 model was performed in WEST using Simplex as optimization tool and the Theils’ 
inequality coefficient (TIC) to evaluate the goodness of fit. Data from the second sampling campaign was used to validate the 
AD2 model using TIC and Janus coefficient as indicators. The calibrated parameters were transferred to the model for AD1 
and evaluated using TIC and Janus coefficient. Finally, a scenario analysis was carried out to assess the impact of organic 
loading rates (OLR) and solids retention time (SRT) on CH4-production and solids reduction. 
The results of the data collection showed that the digesters were working stable, except for potential instabilities of AD1 in 
the second sampling campaign (VFA/Alkalinity ratio of 0.8). The digesters were found to operate underloaded in regard to 
low influent rates, AD1 being affected the most. The gradually increased upconcentration of solids in AD1 resulted in signifi­
cantly increased solids levels in AD1 compared to AD2. However, the solids increase from April to May/June was observed 
in both digesters and was more likely connected to the an upconentration of the influent. High solids levels causing decreased 
mixing of the sludge, in addition to fluctuating sludge temperatures might have increased VFA concentrations in AD1. An 
increased CH4-production due to the contact process configuration could not be concluded, as too many parameters were 
altered simultaneously. 
Particulate inerts were dominating the particulate COD fraction of the influent in April with 51% compared to 27% in May/
June. In both campaigns, carbohydrates dominated the non-soluble biodegradable COD fraction with 48-71%.
The application of suggested parameters and boundaries (Batstone et al., 2002b) for the UA and GSA failed, probably due to 
limited processing power of the hard-and software applied. Thus, parameters and boundaries were narrowed based on literature 
and manual tests in WEST (Ozkan-Yucel and Gökcay, 2010; Solon et al., 2015). The UA showed a good model coverage for 
CH4-production and pH, while TAN, VSS and VFA were only partly covered. The GSA resulted in nine sensitive parameters, 
which were used in the model calibration. Low r2-values for VFA and pH suggested that the SRC-method was not ideal. Ac­
ceptable TIC and Janus coefficients during calibration and validation revealed that the models for AD2 and AD1 were 
predicting pH, VSS and CH4-production satisfactorily. VFA and TAN showed high TIC and Janus coefficients indicating 
less reliable predictions.
The scenario analysis showed that the current operating conditions for AD1 and AD2 could be improved. For AD2, 
high OLR and thus short SRT caused the CH4-production to increase. This increase was though an artefact of the high 
OLR, as a low CH4-yield suggested that the substrate was not treated sufficiently. Solids reduction was stable at ca. 48% 
with a slight decreasing trend with longer SRT. For the AD1 contact process, a high OLR of 12.9 kgVS/m3d boosted 
CH4-production considerably. CH4-production and yield were stable with SRT, except for SRTs shorter than 10 days where 
CH4-production and yield plummeted. In general, higher OLR were beneficial for AD1s’ CH4-production and high SRTs 
increased solids reduction substantially (84% at ca. 75 days). The results of Cambi thermal hydrolysis scenarios were not 
conclusive regarding an increased CH4-production and solids reduction. Only hydrolysis rates were adjusted in the model 
for the scenario, as an adaption of the influent based on BMP was discarded. An expected higher biodegradability of 
Cambi pre-treated substrate could not be confirmed by the BMP-test results. Thus, it was considered unjustified to adjust 
the influent composition based on BMP results.

Damhusåen WWTP, situated in Copenhagen, currently

exploring options to enhance biogas production and 
treatment capacity of their digesters. Therefore, a contact 
process technology is tested on one digester to increase the 
active biomass in the reactor. Another technology of interest 
is the Cambi thermal hydrolysis process (THP), which 
increases biodegradability of secondary sludge.

Anaerobic digestion 
Biomethanation is a process, where organic compounds 
like carbohydrates, proteins and lipids are degraded 
anaerobically to form biogas (a mixture of CH4 and CO2) 
(Angelidaki et al., 2011, Lu, 2007). It comprises both physico-
chemical and complex multistep biochemical processes.
Biochemical processes
Anaerobic digestion is a process occurring in the absence 
of light and an environment, where inorganic electron 
acceptors (O2, Fe2+, nitrate, sulfate) are absent or limited 
(Hattori, 2008). A complex symbiotic microbial community 
is involved in a cascade of biochemical conversions: 
hydrolysis, acidogenesis, acetogenesis and methanogenesis 
(Angelidaki et al., 2011; Feldmann, 2018; Meegoda et 
al., 2018; Figure 1). The major physiological groups of 
microorganisms involved in this multistep process are 
primary fermenting (hydrolytic-acidogenic) bacteria, 
anaerobic oxidizing (syntrophic-acetogenic) bacteria and 
methanogenic archaea (Hattori, 2008; Thauer et al., 2008).
Disintegration and hydrolysis
Complex polymeric compounds in the feedstock are 
disintegrated and broken down into soluble substrates 
during disintegration and hydrolysis. A disintegration step, 
including lysis of biological sludge and complex organic 
material, is thought to precede more sophisticated hydrolytic 
steps (Pavlostathis and Gossett, 1988). The feedstock is

Trends in wastewater 
treatment and the importance 
of anaerobic digestion
The implementation of wastewater treatment was a 
milestone in human history leading to an increased public 
health as well as a healthier environment (Lofrano and 
Brown, 2010). However, utilities are now asked to answer 
multiple global trends with implications for the water 
industry. Key global trends include (Reardon et al., 2013):
•	 Changes in population and demographics
•	 Increased urbanization
•	 Increasing living standards
•	 Climate change
•	 Resource scarcity (land, water, nutrients)
As a response to the increasing interest in green energy, 
circular economy and the increase in population, 
wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs) are currently 
undergoing an enlargement of their original scope of 
action (Arnell et al., 2016). Anaerobic digestion answers 
not only demands for minimized sludge disposal costs, but 
also provides sustainable energy, the possibility for nutrient 
recovery and production of value added chemicals as well 
as a possible increase in treatment capacity (Meegoda et 
al., 2018; Batstone and Virdis, 2014). 
CH4 produced from organic waste by anaerobic digestion 
can be converted to heat and electricity (Feldman, 2018). It 
potentially increases the energy efficiency of WWTPs and 
therefore the possibility of a positive net energy production 
(Angelidaki et al., 2011). In this way, anaerobic digestion is 
an alternative to fossil fuels and can support mitigation of 
greenhouse gas emissions (Arnell et al., 2016; Angelidaki 
et al, 2011). Nutrients like nitrogen and phosphorus 
remain in the effluent and can be applied in agriculture 
as a soil conditioner (Lu, 2007). Anaerobic digestion 
stabilizes sludge (Yoshida et al., 2105) and reduces thereby 
also pathogens including antibiotic resistance genes (Lue

Acidogenic fermentation
The main substrates for acidogenesis are amino acids and 
monosaccharides, which are produced during hydrolysis 
(Angelidaki et al., 2011). The acidogenic microorganisms 
absorb the substrates through their cell membranes and 
produce the volatile fatty acids (VFA), including acetate, 
butyrate, propionate and valerate (Feldmann, 2018). 
There are two different groups of acidogens: those that 
degrade monosaccharides and those that degrade amino 
acids. Both groups form organic acids, hydrogen and 
carbon dioxide (Batstone et al., 2002). Monosaccharides 
ferment via the Emben-Meyerhof-Parnas (EMP) or 
Entner Doudoroff (ED) pathway (Angelidaki et al., 2011). 
The fermentation of amino acids to VFA typically occurs 
in pairs through the so-called Stickland reaction (Winter 
et al., 1987), where one amino acid is oxidized while the 
other one is reduced. Single amino acid degradation is 
possible, but at a slower rate compared to the Stickland 
reaction (Kovács et al., 2013).
The acetate, CO2 and hydrogen produced during 
acidogenesis can be used in methanogenesis, while higher 
VFA (higher in terms of number of carbon) need to 
degrade further in acetogenesis.
Acetogenesis 
During acetogenesis higher VFA produced during 
acidogenesis and long chain fatty acids form lipid hydrolysis 
are made accessible to methanogenic bacteria (Fournier 
and Gogarten, 2008; Figure 1). VFA such as propionate 
and butyrate are oxidized to acetate and hydrogen, along 
with alcohols (e.g. ethanol). 
Hydrogen and acetate from VFA are formed by hydrogen-
producing acetogens (Angelidaki et al., 2011), which are 
limited by unfavourable energetics of the conversion 
process (Schink and Stams, 2006). The hydrogen-producing 
acetogens can only derive energy for growth from the 
conversion process at low hydrogen concentrations, as they 
otherwise are inhibited (Angelidaki et al., 2011). Thus, 
these acetogens are dependent on methanogenic archaea 
to remove the hydrogen they have produced (McInerney 
et al., 2008; Sousa et al., 2009; Stams and Plugge, 2009). 
This syntrophic relationship between hydrogen producing 
acetogens and hydrogen consuming methanogens is known 
as interspecies hydrogen transfer (Angelidaki et al., 2011). 
Another group of acetogens - hydrogen-utilizing 
acetogens - can produce acetate by consuming hydrogen. 
This process competes with the consumption of hydrogen 
by hydrogenotrophic methanogenic archaea (Angelidaki 
et al., 2011). 
Methanogenesis
Methanogenic microorganisms are responsible for 
the formation of CH4 from acetate (aceticlastic 
methanogenesis), hydrogen and CO2 (hydrogenotrophic 
methanogenesis) or methylated C1 compounds (Thauer 
et al., 2008). Methanogens are strictly anaerobic with 
typical habitats in marine and freshwater sediments, 
gastrointestinal tracts of ruminants and insects, anaerobic 
digesters, hot springs and flooded soils (Angelidaki et al., 
2011). They typically are neutrophiles, which indicates 
that optimum growth occurs at a pH around 7. There are 
three major pathways for CH4-formation (Conrad et al., 
2010, Deppenmeier, 2002; Liu and Whitman, 2008):
(1) Aceticlastic methanogenesis - In this process, acetate 
is cleaved to CH4 and CO2. It is the most important 
pathway for CH4-production in anaerobic environments, 
as approximately 70% of the total global CH4 output is 
generated in this way (Conrad et al., 2010). 
CH3COO- + H+ → CH4 + CO2     (Eq. 1)
(2) Hydrogenotrohpic methanogenesis - Hydrogen is used 
as an electron donor for the reduction of CO2 to CH4. The 
formation of CH4 from hydrogen and CO2 (or formate) 
accounts for approximately 30% of the total global 
CH4 output in anaerobic environments (Conrad et al., 
2010). As already mentioned earlier, there is a syntrophic 
relationship between hydrogen producing acetogenic 
bacteria and hydrogen-consuming methanogenic archaea. 
The hydrogenotrophic methanogens keep the dissolved 
hydrogen concentration at a low level, as they are in close 
contact to hydrogen producers (Angelidaki et al., 2011). 
4H2 + CO2 → CH4 + 2H2O     (Eq. 2)
(3) Methylotrophic methanogenesis - A minor contribution 
to the global CH4 output in anaerobic environments comes 
from the conversion of methylated C1 compounds (e.g. 
methanol, dimethylsulfide, methylamines) to CH4.

Physico-chemical processes
Physico-chemical processes during anaerobic digestion 
are those processes that are non-biologically facilitated 
(Batstone et al., 2002). It can be distinguished between 
three broad process types:
Liquid-liquid processes (aqueous processes) include weak acid-
base reactions as well as ion pairing (Solon et al., 2015). Acid-
base equilibrium processes are described by the dissociation 
constants (pKa) of organic acids and acid-base pairs like 
NH4

-. Regarding ion pairing – 
concentrated substrates show increased interactions between 
ions and with the solvent increase (high ionic strength).  
Liquid-gas processes are based on Henrys law, describing the 
equilibrium between the liquid and gas phase of a compound. 
Gases with a substantial effect on biological processes during 
anaerobic digestion are H2, CH4 and CO2 (Batstone et al., 
2002). Other important gases include H2S and NH3.
Liquid-solid or solids precipitation processes describe the 
complexing of cations and anions in neutral inorganic 
solid form (Batstone et al., 2002). Precipitation of solids 
is a complex process which evidently depnding on kinetics 
and thermodynamics (Batstone et al., 2002). Calcium 
carbonate, calcium phosphate, magnesium carbonate, 
metal sulfide precipitates (e.g. FeS and Fe2S3) and 
magnesium-phosphate complexes are considered the main 
solid precipitants (Batstone et al., 2002).

Process parameters
Anaerobic digestion performance is dependent on a series 
of process factors. The most significant process factors are 
outlined in the following section.
Inhibition of anaerobic digestion
Inhibition refers to the restriction of biological processes, 
which can be divided into biocidal, biostatic and end-
product inhibition (Batstone et al., 2002). 
Biocidal inhibition is a reactive toxicity, which is typically 
irreversible. The toxic compound reacts with a functional 
part of the microbial cell, which causes non-functionality. 
Toxic compounds in this category include detergents, 
cyanide, antibiotics and other xenobiotics.
Biostatic inhibition describes a nonreactive usually 
reversible toxicity, which disrupts intracellular conditions 
(redox potential, pH and total salts). Causes for biostatic 
inhibition are free acids and bases (VFA, H2S, NH3), salts as 
well as changes in pH. pH inhibition is the most significant 
factor for biostatic inhibition. A typical reactor failure is 
“acid-overload” or acidification. During acid-overload 
some stress causes the accumulation of acetate, which in 
turn causes the pH to drop followed by an inhibition of 
aceticlastic methanogens. This inhibition causes further 
accumulation of acetate and a further drop in pH, which 
inhibits hydrogenotrohpic methanogens and acetogenic 
bacteria. The only remaining process is acidogenesis, 
which causes all COD to be converted to acids instead of 
CH4 (Angelidaki and Batstone, 2011).
High loads of  protein-rich substrates are known to cause 
inhibition of  microorganisms by ammonia (Labatut and 
Gooch, 2012; Chen et al., 2008; Rajagopal et al., 2013; 
Yenigün and Demirel, 2013). Ammonia is released 
during acidogenesis, where amino acids are degraded. 
Raised concentrations of  ammonia are toxic for 
different bacteria, especially aceticlastic methanogens 
(Chen et al., 2008).
End-product inhibition is caused by the drop in 
thermodynamic yield caused by an accumulation of a 
product. This closely interacts with biostatic inhibition. 
The most common end-product inhibition is hydrogen as 
well as acetate inhibition of acetogens.
Nutrient availability
Microbial processes of any kind require macro (N, P and 
S) as well as micro nutrients (trace metals) for biomass 
synthesis (Bajpai, 2017; Angelidaki and Batstone, 2011). 
Only a small amount of COD removal can be attributed 
to biomass synthesis, which lowers the requirement for 
nutrients and trace metals in the anaerobic digestion 
process (Bajpai, 2017; Angelidaki and Batstone, 2011). 
Nitrogen and phosphorus should never be limited in a 
digester (Bajpai, 2017). Micronutrients such as nickel and 
cobalt are important for optimum process rates, but these 
compounds can also be inhibitory at high concentrations 
(Bajpai, 2017; Angelidaki and Batstone, 2011). Addition 
of iron was observed to stimulate the anaerobic digestion 
process by an increased precipitation of sulfides and 
phosphates (Angelidaki and Batstone, 2011). 
Temperature
Physico-chemical processes change with temperature, 
which can influence phase distribution, mass transfer 
rates and solubility (Angelidaki and Batstone, 2011). 
Biochemical processes are also affected by temperature 
changes. Growth rates of  microorganisms are tightly 
bound to temperature (Figure 3). In anaerobic digestion 
three temperature regimes are defined: thermophilic 
(50-55ºC), mesophilic (35-40ºC) and psychrophilic (5-
15ºC) (Bajpai, 2017). The growth rate of  methanogens 
is substantially higher at thermophilic temperatures 
(Figure 3). Pshychrophilic temperatures are only found in 
low technological or natural anaerobic digestion systems 
(Angelidaki and Batstone, 2011), while thermophilic

State indicators
Anaerobic digesters can react to changes in environmental 
conditions with reactor failure (Labatut and Gooch, 2012). 
The operating conditions combined with the feedstock 
characteristics influence the perturbation or steadiness of 
an anaerobic digester (Figure 4). Key indicators to assess 
the state of an anaerobic digestion system are discussed in 
this section.
Volatile fatty acids (VFA) are a sensitive parameter to 
monitor in anaerobic digestion. As mentioned earlier, 
VFA can have inhibitory effects and an accumulation 
can lead to reactor failure. A well-functioning digester 
has a total VFA concentration below 500 mg Ac/L, while 
concentrations above 2 g Ac/L might indicate inhibition 
and thus reduced biogas production (Labatut and Good, 
2012). Although the total VFA is the typically monitored 
indicator, individual VFA were shown to give more specific 
information for process diagnosis (Ahring et al., 1995; 
Cobb and Hill 1991). Kanokwan et al. (2010) showed that 
propionate was a highly relevant indicator for manure

Molecular hydrogen is another important parameter. 
The degradation of VFA (especially propionate) 
during acetogenesis requires low partial pressures of 
hydrogen (Labatut and Gooch, 2012), which can only 
be achieved through the syntrophic relationship between 
hydrogen producing acetogens and hydrogen consuming 
methanogens. If there is no balance, end-product 
inhibition may occur. 
pH is a key indicator to control and avoid unfavorable 
conditions such as acid-overload. Acidogens prefer a 
pH of 5.5-6.5 and methanogens 7.8-8.2. Thus, a pH in 
the range 6.8-7.4 is required for the different groups 
of  microorganisms to perform the stepwise process of 
anaerobic digestion (Bajpai, 2017). However, research 
showed that pH might not respond fast enough to 
indicate the status of  the process in well buffered systems 
(Björnsson et al., 2000; Kanokwan et al., 2010). Figure 
4, indicates that the increase in VFA is generally a better 
indicator for instability, compared to pH.
Alkalinity is typically applied as an indicator for the 
buffering capacity of  a digester (Labatut and Gooch, 
2012). A good buffering capacity enables the system to 
withstand moderate shock loads of  VFA (Labatut and 
Gooch, 2012). The buffering capacity in anaerobic 
digestion is dominated by bicarbonate and ammonia 
due to a pH level around neutral. The VFA/alkalinity 
ratio is used as a determining indicator to assess the 
stability of  anaerobic digestion. A ratio <0.4 indicates 
stable conditions, 0.4-0.8 shows that some instability may 
occur and a ratio >0.8 specifies significant instability 
(Callaghan et al., 2002). 
Total ammonia-nitrogen (TAN) is an indicator for 
inhibition by ammonia. Concentrations above 1,500 
mg/L of ammonia-N are known to cause inhibition at 
high pH levels (Labatut and Gooch, 2012).
Temperature is, as mentioned earlier, important regarding 
the types of microorganisms to be present in the reactor. 
Temperature fluctuations outside the desired range 
(mesophilic or thermophilic) can influence the biogas 
production and over longer periods even cause reactor 
failure (Labatut and Gooch, 2012).

Enhancement of biogas production
Technologies with the aim of increasing process 
performance, especially in terms of CH4 production, 
are presented in the following section. Only technologies 
related to the study are outlined.
Contact process
In order to retain active biomass in a continuous or fed-
batch CSTR, it is possible to separate and recycle solids 
from the effluent (Angelidaki et al., 2011). Solids from the 
reactor outlet can be upconcentrated and returned into 
the digester. This is often applied in anaerobic digestion 
for activated sludge. In CSTR without recycling of up-
concentrated sludge, the solids retention time (SRT) 
and hydraulic retention time (HRT) are identical. With 
an up-concentration of solids in the recycling line, the 
observed CH4-yield could increase if the recycled solids 
are biodegradable. In this way, the HRT and SRT can be 
decoupled (Saw et al., 1985). Thus, much higher SRT and 
lower HRT can be achieved, thereby allowing for higher 
organic loading rate (OLR) (Bajpai, 2017).
The contact process is a well-studied concept to increase 
reactor performance in terms of conversion of organic 
matter to biogas (Sentürk et al., 2012; Saw et al., 1985). 
In a typical contact process, a degasifier and a settling tank 
are installed in the recycling line to ensure the availability 
of active biomass in the digester (Figure 5). Newer 
technologies of the contact process apply membrane

Pretreatment
By treating sludge prior to anaerobic digestion, the 
degradability of the substrate can be increased, which can 
leads to higher energy recovery and lower digested sludge 
for disposal (Carrère et al., 2010). Additionally, increased 
degradation kinetics can lead to a decrease in sludge 
retention time (SRT), which makes it possible to reduce the 
digester volume or to increase the OLR (Carrère et al., 2010). 
Sludge from the secondary line (waste activated sludge) has 
a low biodegradability compared to primary sludge. Thus, 
[17]
pre-treatment is generally applied to secondary or mixed 
sludge instead of primary sludge (Carrère et al., 2010). 
There are four main groups of pre-treatments: (1) biological, 
(2) mechanical, (3) thermal and (4) chemical. Furthermore, 
various strategies can be combined (Carrère et al., 2010; 
Ariunbaatar et al., 2014). 
Thermal hydrolysis
The general concept of thermal hydrolysis is that the 
application of high temperature and pressure dissolves 
and disintegrates the influent. This makes the substrate 
more available for anaerobic microbes, and thus, leads to 
a higher CH4-production rates and a reduction of sludge 
for disposal (Menco, 2019).
Thermal hydrolysis is offered by different enterprises like 
CTH by Aqualogy, Exelys by Veolia and Cambi THP by 
Cambi (Cano et al., 2015). This study will focus on the 
“steam explosion” thermal hydrolysis process (THP) by 
Cambi - a Norwegian company. (Pickworth et al., 2006; 
Menco, 2019).

dewatered sludge is lead to a storage silo from where it is 
fed to a pulper. In the pulper, the sludge is pre-heated with 
recycled steam from the digester and flash tank (Figure 6). 
The pre-heated sludge is then pumped from the pulper

Modelling anaerobic digestion
Mathematical models are a simplification of real systems 
and applied to predict outcome, comprehend processes 
and communicate data to stakeholders. In an ideal world, 
anaerobic digestion models describe different aspects of 
microbial and physico-chemical processes in different 
reactor configurations and with ranging environmental 
and operational conditions (Yu et al., 2013). Due to the 
complexity of this multi-stage process, anaerobic digestion 
models are still under research. A wide variety of models 
are available.
The first anaerobic digestion models were developed 
in the 1970ies (Lyberatos and Skiadas, 1999). Early 
models had a simple substrate characterization and 
lumped processes (Tomei et al., 2009). Three models 
were selected to demonstrate the evolution of anaerobic 
digestion models (Table1). The Eastman and Ferguson 
model (1981) is based on a mixed substrate (total COD)

In the initial phase of the project, a meeting involving all the 
stakeholders was held. At this meeting the objectives of the 
project were defined, the tasks and timeframe conversed, 
and the involvement of the stakeholders discussed.  
Problem statement
The goal of this study is to predict selected state indicators 
of the anaerobic digestion reactors 1 and 2 at Damhusåen 
WWTP. Digester 2 (AD2) is the original process design of 
the reactors, while digester 1 (AD 1) is currently runsas 
a contact process. The effect of different process designs, 
including different organic loading rates, the application 
of the contact process and a pre-treatment with Cambi 
THP are examined. 
Objectives 
The following milestones were identified for this study:
•	 Gaining understanding of the anaerobic digestion 
process at Damhusåen WWTP with a focus on AD1 
and AD2. This included the analysis and evaluation of 
online sensor data, process diagrams and information 
through interviews.

Description of Damhusåen WWTP
Damhusåen 
WWTP 
is 
situated 
in 
Copenhagen 
(Denmark) and treats mainly municipal wastewater 
from approximately 350,000 inhabitants. 31.94 mio. m3 
of wastewater are treated annually and with the four 
anaerobic digesters 10,282 MWh electricity and 107 
MWh heat are produced annually (Biofos, 2017). 
The entering wastewater is first treated mechanically 
with a grid, where large items and particles are removed 
(Figure 7). Subsequently, sand and fats are separated, and 
the water is directed to the primary settlers. The fats are 
directed to anaerobic digester 3 and 4, while the sand is 
used industrially. The sludge from the primary settlers is 
collected in a concentration tank, before it continues to the 
mixing tanks. After the secondary treatment, where soluble 
organic carbon and nutrients are removed, activated sludge 
from the secondary settlers is sent for recirculation. Excess 
sludge is collected in a buffer tank from where it is directed 
to a dewatering unit and further to the mixing tanks. All 
four digesters are fed with mixed sludge from the mixing 
tanks. The mixed sludge is pumped in on-off intervals to the 
different reactors. Each reactor is served with an average of 
25 m3/h sludge for 45 minutes six times a day. This results 
in an average inflow to each reactor of 5 m3/h. All digesters 
are operated as CSTR. Digester 3 and 4 receive in addition 
to the mixed sludge also fats. The gas produced during 
digestion is transferred to a gas tank from where it either is 
applied in a gas-engine to produce electricity or to a boiler 
to produce district heating and heat up the influent to the 
digesters. The effluent sludge undergoes a final de-watering 
before it is transported to incineration.
All digesters have a volume of 1,900 m3, are operated at 
mesophilic temperatures (ideally aimed at 37ºC) and have 
a recirculation line, where biomass from the outlet of the 
digester is returned to the reactor (Figure 8, page 24). The 
two mixing tanks have a volume of 100 m3 and 400 m3 
respectively. 
The process design of digester 1 (AD1) and digester 2 
(AD2) are outlined in. These process plans are based on 
plant data (Appendix A) as well as personal communication 
with Biofos staff. AD1 and AD2 are originally identical 
in their setup. However, AD1 is currently operated as a 
contact process to explore the effect on CH4-production, 
solids reduction and daily operation. The contact process 
at AD1 was started in February 2019. Since then the up-
concentration of the sludge has gradually been increased. 
Details and data about the contact process technology 
cannot be presented in this study, as it is still under 
development and confidential.

Experimental design
The BMP-test was carried out with four substrates, a negative 
control (blank) and a positive control (Table 4). Triplicates were 
applied, which resulted in a total of 18 batches. Inoculum of 
the anaerobic digester 1 from Damhusåen WWTP was used 
in all batches after an incubation period of 8 days at 37ºC. For 
the design of the experiment, guidelines outlined in Holliger 
et al. (2016) were followed. Some important conditions for 
the experimental design were:  
•	
An inoculum substrate VS-ratio (ISR) between 2 and 4
•	
A total (substrate and inoculum) initial VS concentration 
between 20 and 60 gVS/L
•	
Pressure in the batches below 300 kPa
•	
A liquid volume of at least 100 mL and a vessel volume 
between 500 and 1000 mL
•	
A pre-incubation of the inoculum
The design of the experiment was conducted in two phases. 
In the first phase, the inoculum was pre-incubated and 
preliminary calculations were conducted to estimate ISR, VS 
concentrations, pressure due to biogas production as well as 
the required reactor vessel volume (Appendix B). One day 
prior to the experiment, the TS and VS of each substrate (or 
substrate mixture) were determined and the final calculations 
for the experimental setup could be carried out (Appendix 
A). The preparation of batches as well as the GC-sampling 
procedure is outlined in Appendix B.
Analytical methods
Biochemical methane potential test (BMP-test)
The amount of CH4 produced during anaerobic digestion 
is of major interest to estimate the process efficiency due 
to economic reasons (Angelidaki and Batstone, 2011). It 
is therefore common to carry out a biochemical CH4-
potential test (BMP) and determine the ultimate CH4-yield 
and the degradability of the substrate of interest. These are 
important parameters for design, optimization, problem 
solving and economic assessment of a biogas reactor. In this 
study, a BMP-test was applied to determine the ultimate 
CH4-potential used to calculate the fraction of biodegradable 
COD, which is a crucial parameter for substrate fractionation 
for the model influent (see Box 1). Furthermore, the BMP-
test was used to determine the hydrolysis rate and BMP of a 
substrate with and without Cambi THP, which served as an 
input for the scenario analysis.
During a BMP-test, the substrate is incubated in a serum flask 
together with the inoculum from an anaerobic digester. As the 
inoculum digests the substrate over time, the CH4 produced 
in the bottle is determined by gas chromatography (GC). The 
CH4 generated during the process is reported as BMP [CH4 
yield mL/ g volatile solids] and defined in the following way:

Total suspended solids (TSS) and volatile

The total suspended solids (TSS) describe the organic and 
inorganic matter, which is retained after a sample was 
filtered and subsequently dried at 105°C. The volatile 
suspended solids (VSS) are the suspended organic matter 
only, which is lost during the ignition process.

Total lipids
An adaption of the Bligh and Dyer (Bligh and Dyer, 1959) 
extraction was applied to determine the total lipids content 
in the substrate samples. 1 mL of sample was transferred 
to a glass vial. 3.75mL of a 2:1 mixture of chloroform 
and methanol were added prior to vortex-mix sample and

1.25 mL of chloroform were added, and the sample was 
vortex-mixed again (30 seconds). After adding 1.25 mL 
demineralized water, the samples were centrifuged at 3,000 
rpm for 10 minutes. Three phases – water, solid and lipid/
chloroform – were clearly separated (Figure 16). The lower 
phase (lipid-chloroform) was collected by inserting a glass 
pipette through the two top layers and extracting the entire 
lower phase content. The lipid-chloroform phase was 
transferred to a new glass vial, which was weighed before 
and after adding the phase. In a next step, the vial was 
heated to 40°C and nitrogen was added to the headspace in 
order to evaporate all chloroform. The content of lipid was 
determined by weighing the vials after evaporation.

Uncertainty and global             
sensitivity analysis
After the model for AD2 was created and running, an 
uncertainty analysis was performed. In a next step, a 
global sensitivity analysis (GSA) was carried out to identify 
the most crucial model parameters. Both, the uncertainty 
and sensitivity analysis were carried out using WEST.
Before the analyses were executed, the target variables 
as well as the parameters and their ranges needed to be 
determined. The target variables chosen were VSS, pH, 
TAN, Total VFA and CH4-production. The parameters 
and their variability were selected based on suggestions of 
the IWA task force (Batstone et al., 2002b). 
In order to receive a pseudo-random set of parameters 
for the uncertainty and global sensitivity analysis, Latin 
Hypercube Sampling (LHS) was selected in WEST. The 
basic principle to gain “n” parameter subsets using LHS 
can be described in three steps (Helton and Davis, 2003):
(1) The parameter range for each parameter is divided into “n” 
parts with an equal probability. The distribution is uniform 
and therefore the parts need to have equal intervals.
(2) From each of the intervals a random value is selected.
(3) The random values gained in step (2) are then paired 
randomly with the other random parameter values to 
receive the desired “n” parameter subsets. 
With each of the parameter subsets a simulation was 
carried out. In this study, 500 simulations (500 parameter 
subsets) were carried out (enough to reach convergence 
on the parameter distributions). A steady state simulation 
(200 days) was carried out before each dynamic simulation 
(0-14). In this way, the end of the steady state simulation 
built the initial conditions for the dynamic simulation.
The results of the simulations of the uncertainty analysis 
were statistically assessed for median and the 95%-interval 
of the target variables. The resulting area between the 5th 
and 95th percentile was compared with the measurement 
data. In this way it was assessed if the model and the 
default parameter set (with the given ranges) could cover 
the observations (Wágner et al., 2016).
For the GSA, the results were statistically assessed choosing 
the algorithm “endvalue” in WEST. The statistical evaluation 
produced a standardized regression coefficient (SRC), 
which quantified the distribution of each input parameter

Data collection and reconciliation
In the following section, analysis results from two sampling 
campaigns in April and May/June (Figure 10) at Damhusåen 
WWTP are presented and discussed. 
Day-to-day and averaged results of the influent as well as from 
AD1 and AD2 are included in this section. Furthermore, 
relevant results from online sensors during the two sampling 
campaigns are provided and discussed. The state of AD1 
and AD2 are compared as well as the performance of the 
contact-process briefly evaluated. Detailed analysis results, 
online data and t-tests can be found in Appendix C (and 
Appendix B for BMP-test results). 
Influent
Influent flow and organic loading rate
In theory, every digester receives mixed sludge influent 
six times daily for 0.75 hours with a flow rate of 25m3/h. 
This would result in a theoretical average influent flow 
of 113 m3/d. The online data of the flow meter to the 
digesters as well as valve opening-times to AD1 and 
AD2 were evaluated (AD1 valve: V_RTY51S$545, AD2 
valve: V_RTY53S$545; flow meter: VFRTX11S545). 
This investigation showed a daily average feed flow of 
79.71±10.33 m3/d for AD1 and 82.27±7.63 m3/d for 
AD2 during the first sampling campaign (Figure 21). 
The influent flow decreased in the second campaign to 
51.76±25.76 m3/d for AD1 and to 68.99±17.49 m3/d 
for AD2. AD1 received in general less sludge compared 
to AD2. Flows varied heavily from day to day for AD1 in 
May/June (Figure 21, Appendix C4).
Calculating the organic loading rate (OLR) based on 
the daily influent flow rate and VS content, AD1 had a 
lower loading compared to AD1 (Table 5). However, the 
difference was only significant in the period of May/June. 
In general, the loading rates were in a typical range for 
fed-batch CSTR (1.6 to 4.8 kgVS/m3 d; Rittmann and 
McCarty, 2012).
Day-to-day trends
The pH level in the influent was with an average of 
6.17±0.28 stable during both sampling campaigns. 
Seasonal differences or substantial variations based on a 
different mixture of secondary and primary sludge were 
not observed (Table 6). The day-to-day pH time series 
(Figure 22) was applied for the generation of the dynamic 
influent, which will be outlined later in the study.

first campaign might have influenced the CH4-production

more frequently and increase the mixing in AD1.

Model for AD1 contact process
In order to model the contact process, the simple model 
for AD2 was expanded (Figure 35). A dewatering unit 
(DWU), which is based on a simple mass balance, was 
applied to model the solids-liquid separation. The original 
setup of AD1 consists of a recirculation line, where only 
part of the flow is directed to the solid-liquid separation 
unit (Figure 8). As the data for the contact process was 
partly unavailable, it was chosen to combine the recycling 
and separation lines. The flow of the upconcentrated 
solids (Qconcentrate) was set to 3,900 m3/d in the DWU, 
which was the flow determined for the recirculation 
(Eq. 27). The DWU calculated the permeate flow as the 
difference between inflow to the DWU and the Qconcentrate. 
The efficiency of the DWU was determined by a PI-
controller, which received the measured VSS data of the 
reactor content as a dynamic set-point. More details about 
the DWU efficiency and the PI controller are provided in 
Appendix F1.

Parameters and boundary conditions
The set of parameters and boundaries suggested by the 
IWA taskforce on anaerobic digestion was planned to be 
applied in this study (Batstone et al., 2002b). Parameter 
variabilities of up to 300% could though not be processed 
by the hard-and software applied. Literature on tested 
parameter bounds for ADM1 was not available, which 
lead to a process of finding a feasible parameter set and 
boundaries. 
In a first attempt, the bounds were narrowed. Instead 
of 300, 100 and 35% variability, 100, 50 and 25% were 
tested. The results were not very conclusive using these 
boundaries (only 1 parameter sensitive for three out of 
five target variables and r2-values below 0.7 for all target 
variables). 
Manual trial and error testing of the boundaries showed 
that for some parameters the new boundaries were too 
narrow. Responses to model outputs (e.g. VSS) at higher 
or lower values than the narrowed boundaries. Increasing 
boundaries for certain parameters to the suggested ones 
by the IWA taskforce resulted in simulation issues again. 
Thus, literature was consulted and a manual calibration 
carried out to see which parameters were the most 
relevant for the model. The parameter set was decreased 
in number of parameters and narrowed boundaries were 
applied unless manual trial and error tests showed the 
need of increased boundaries. The resulting parameter 
set is presented in table 13. The application of a tailor-
made parameter set is considered a major limitation 
and should be revised, either in a dedicated study on 
ADM1 parameters and their boundaries or with a server 
implementation of WEST (e.g. on DTU cluster), where all 
parameters may be tested with the suggested boundaries 
of the IWA taskforce.

Uncertainty analysis
The median, 5th and 95th percentile of Monte Carlo output 
(LHS sampling) was plotted over the sampling period in April 
for the AD2 model and compared to measured data (Figure 
36). In this way it was evaluated if the model with the default 
parameters and ranges covered the observed data.
The observed CH4-production and pH were within the 
coverage of the model (Figure 36). This suggests that 
the default parameter set and the boundaries chosen 
are able to predict these target variables. TAN, VSS and 
VFA observations did not or only partly lie within the 
coverage using the parameter set and boundaries selected 
in this study (Figure 36). This is interesting as an earlier 
tested parameter set was able to capture VFA and VSS 
better, but CH4-production and TAN were covered less 
(IWA parameters without yields, stoichiometric fractions 
and pH inhibition levels, 25, 50 and 100% boundaries; 
Appendix F3 model journal). This indicates that there 
might be an input uncertainty (McKay et al., 1999), 
which confirms the earlier discussed importance of the 
choice for a suitable parameter set and boundaries. The 
model coverage for VSS and pH were rather narrow, 
which might imply that the model is not able to predict 
all variations and important instabilities like acidification. 
For a target variable as VFA, the prediction of higher 
concentrations is of interest. Low VFA concentrations are 
captured deficiently, but the coverage suggests that higher 
concentrations can be captured.
Global sensitivity analysis
The parameter set was tested for parameter sensitivity in 
regard to the different target variables. The objective of a 
GSA is to identify those parameters which should further 
be used in the calibration. Only parameters with an SRC 
above 0.1 were considered as sensitive parameters for the 
target variables (Figure 37). As mentioned earlier in the study, 
when using the SRC-method, a coefficient of determination 
(r2) above 0.7 of the linear model and the original model, is 
required (Vanrolleghem et al., 2015). This was not the case 
for the pH and the VFA concentration, but for all other target 
variables high r2-values were achieved (Figure 37). Solon et al. 
(2015) described this phenomenon and argued that the SRC 
method fails for digesters at mesophilic conditions. This might 
be due to intrinsic nonlinearities of the digester system and 
the SRC-method fails in decomposing prediction variance 
of the model (Solon et al., 2015). Other suggested methods 
like Morris screening were not available in WEST. Thus, the 
plausibility of the sensitive parameters was evaluated.

Comparable studies only assessed parameter sensitivity to 
CH4-production (Ozcan-Yucel and Gökcay, 2010) or CH4-
production, CO2-production and H2-production (Solon et 
al. 2015). In this study, sensitivity to pH, VFA, VSS, TAN 
and CH4-production was evaluated. 
As hydrolysis often is the limiting step in anaerobic 
digestion, it was assumed that hydrolysis constants (khyd) 
were sensitive. This was the case in the study of Ozcan-
Yucel and Gökcay (2010), but not in the GSA results of 
this study (Figure 37). The maximum uptake rate for 
acetate (km_ac) was found to be sensitive for VFA (Figure 
37), but not for CH4-production as in Ozcan-Yucel and 
Gökcay, 2010 and Solon et al., 2015. 
In addition to km_ac, other sensitive parameters in this study 
were the microbial decay rates for acetate (kdec_xac) and 
sugar degraders (kdec_xsu), and stoichiometric fractions for 
the yield of  particulate inerts from composites (f_XI_xc), 
acetate from sugars (f_ac_su), butyrate from sugars (f_bu_
su), hydrogen from sugars (f_h2_su), propionate from sugars 
(f_pro_su) and valerate from amino acids (f_va_aa). 
The tornado plot for pH shows a sensitivity towards 
hydrogen and fatty acid concentrations, lead by acetate, 
which is the major VFA produced during the fermentation 
step (Figure 37, B). The concentration of these VFA is 
applied in the calculation of the dynamic pH in ADM1 
(Appendix D2). An accumulation of these fatty acids can 
lead to biostatic inhibition, where the system is stepwise 
acidified. pH was also sensitive to the stoichiometric fraction 
of the yield of hydrogen from sugars. The accumulation of 
hydrogen is known as end-product inhibition, which would 
interfere in the interspecies hydrogen transfer. This could 
lead to the inhibition of hydrogen producing acetogens, 
which cannot grow at high hydrogen concentrations. The 
hydrogen producing acetogens use VFA and transform 
them to hydrogen. If this transformation is inhibited, VFA 
can accumulate and acidify the system. 
VFA concentrations in the system are dominated by the 
presence of acetate. The sensitivity for acetate related 
parameters is threfore reasonable (Figure 37, D). An 
increased decay of aceticlastic methanogens will increase 
the VFA concentration. A high maximum uptake rate of 
acetate will decrease VFA concentrations in the digester. 
A higher fraction for the yield of acetate from sugars will 
increase the VFA concentration.

Calibration and validation
In the following sections the results from the model 
calibration of the AD2 model are presented and discussed. 
The model was calibrated using measured data from the 
April sampling campaign and validated using measured 
data from May/June. The calibrated parameters were 
applied to the model for AD1 and  simulation results 
evaluated with data from both sampling periods. 
AD2 model calibration and validation
Prior to the uncertainty analysis, GSA and calibration in 
WEST, a manual calibration was attempted (Appendix 
F2) to gain an idea if, calibrated parameters for the AD2 
model can be transferred to the model for AD1. As this 
approach looked (visually) promising, the preliminary 
analyses were carried out and the calibration executed 
using Simplex and TIC as a goodness of fit parameter. 
The nine sensitive parameters identified in the GSA were 
used with the boundaries defined earlier (Table 13 and 
14). The most substantial change in parameter magnitude 
was for the decay rates and the maximum growth rate for 
acetate degraders (Table 14).

Inside the reactor, nitrogen can be produced by 
acidogenesis

microorganisms during growth and produced during 
decay. Increasing decay rates of  microorganisms will 
therefore also increase TAN concentrations, making 
kdec_xsu and kdec_xac sensitive (Figure 37, C). 
Higher fractions for the yields of  different substrates, 
will increase the concentration of  the substrate. 
This in turn, will increase the growth of  biomass 
related to the substrate. The growth of  this biomass 
consumes nitrogen, which is why the fractions for the 
yield are sensitive for TAN (Figure 37, C). During 
the disintegration step, nitrogen is produced making 
the fraction for the yield of  particulate inerts from 
composites sensitive. Thus, a lower f_XI_xc fraction 
will decrease TAN concentrations. 
For the CH4-production, the acidogenic pathway seemed 
to be important. The fraction of the yield for acetate (from 
sugars and amino acids) was sensitive (Figure 37, A). A 
high fraction might indicate a high acetate concentration, 
which provides substrate for the methanogens. Also 
sensitive were the yield fractions for the formation of 
propionate and butyrate, which form acetate and provide 
again substrate for methanogenesis, when increased. 
The hydrogenotrophic methanogenic pathway was also 
important, due to the sensitivity of the fraction of the 
yield of hydrogen. More hydrogen will increase the CH4-
production (Figure 37, A).
In Eq. 23 relevant species for VSS concentrations are 
included. Higher decay rates of methanogens and sugar 
degraders will decrease the VSS concentration (Figure 37, 
E). The presence of relevant substrates for methanogenesis 
was also sensitive, expressed by the fractions for the yield 
of propionate, acetate and hydrogen (Figure 37, E). It can 
be assumed that more substrate leads to more biomass, 
which increases VSS. During anaerobic digestion, 
particulate inerts are accumulated, which is relevant for 
the VSS concentration in the reactor as well and therefore 
sensitive (Figure 37, E).
Although there were uncertainties connected to the results 
of the GSA and the parameter set applied, the sensitive 
parameters selected seemed reasonable. Thus, it was 
decided to carry on with the determined set of sensitive 
parameters to calibrate the model for AD2.

AD1 model evaluation
Applying the calibrated parameters to the AD1 model 
gave a good fit   (TIC) and acceptable Janus coefficients for 
VSS, pH and CH4-production in both periods (Table 15). 
VFA showed acceptable Janus and TIC results in April but 
not in May/June. This is a similar trend as observed for 
AD2, except that the Janus coefficient was acceptable for 
AD2 in May/June, but not for AD1. The Janus coefficient 
for TAN was as for AD2 elevated in both periods, but 
especially for May/June. 
The comparison between calculated and simualted SRT, did 
not match completely. As mentioned earlier, the calculated 
All targed variables achieved a goodness of fit with a TIC 
below 0.3 during the calibration period (Table 15). The 
calibrated parameters were applied to the model and 
the simulated SRT compared with a time-series based 
on  manual determination using Eq. 16 (Figure 38). The 
simulated SRT matched the manually calculated SRT in 
the calibration period (Figure 38). The TIC values and the 
predictable SRT are indications for a satisfactory model 
performance during the calibration period.

coefficients for VSS, pH and CH4 predictions (Table 15). 
The simulated and calculated SRT matched satisfactorily

observed data was  below 0.3 and therefore not satisfying. The 
predictive performance of TAN could not be accepted for 
the validation period based on the TIC and Janus coefficient 
(Table 15). The exceptionally high Janus coefficient might 
show an overfitting of the parameters, which makes the 
model less robust (Rieger et al., 2013).

Another important aspect is the generation of the 
dynamic influent for the model. In this study, the influent 
composition was averaged for each period due to time-
related and financial resources. The difference in the 
fractionation due to sludge characteristics was visible for 
the average of the two sampling campaigns (Figure 31, 
Table 11). A better fit might be gained by taking the 
day-to-day variations of the influent characteristics and 
fractions into account by an actual analysis (Ozcan-Yucel 
and Gökcay, 2010). 
Despite uncertainties on a daily scale, the main interest 
when working with a full-scale model lies in detecting 
long-term trends. All in all, the models for AD1 and AD2 
showed good predictive performance for CH4-production, 
pH and VSS (including SRT). The prediction of VFA and 
TAN were not completely trustworthy. Uncertainties in the 
prediction of VFA, TAN and day to day dynamics might 
be related to influent characteristics and fractions as well 
as the mixing properties, as mentioned earlier. Also, the 
chosen parameter set, which was the base for the GSA and 
calibration might have limited the model performance.
SRT was based on the assumption that the TSS in the 
permeate is negligible, which indicates a high efficiency of 
the solid-liquid seperation. The model measures the TSS in 
the permeate and adjusts the efficiency of the dewatering 
using the measured VSS timeseries. In the April period, 
the simulated and observed SRT matched until day 8, 
where the calculated SRT stayed high while the simulated 
SRT dropped (Figure 39).   The efficiency of the model 
dewatering unit was at 0.99999 until day 8 of the sampling 
campaign. Then the efficiency dropped to 0.99000 causing 
the SRT to drop as well (only small changes in efficiency 
of the model DWU caused substantial changes in model 
output). This drop in efficiency could be captured by 
the model because of the control of the efficiency with 
measured data. The operators reported issues with the 
efficiency of the dewatering, which adds credibility to the 
model results. This suggests that the model SRT results of 
the April period are more trustworthy than the calculated 
SRT based on assumptions. In the May/June campaign 
the efficiency of the dewatering was stable at 0.99999 in 
the model output. This might explain the improved fit 
between measured and observed data (Figure 39). 
The evaluation results of AD1 regarding model fit and 
accuracy were generally in line with the results of the 
calibration and validation of the AD2 model (Table 15). 
There was one deviation in the prediction of VFA. While 
the AD2 model overestimated VFA, the model for AD1 
underestimated VFA. As for AD2 the model of AD1 
showed deficiencies in catching the day-to-day dynamics.
The overestimation of TAN in May/June for AD1 was 
similar to the the results for AD2 in this period (Figure 
38 and 39 May/June). In both cases, pH predictions were 
visibly not affected by the high TAN levels. 90 mol/m3 
(Figure 38 and 39 May/June) correspond to 1,260 mg/L, 
which is high but below inhibition levels. 
General discrepancies between day-to-day simulations 
and observations might be related to a key factor for the 
digester performance, but also the model setup: mixing. 
The relevance of a correct mixing description in the 
model for better predictions was demonstrated in a study 
from 2014 (Van Hulle et al., 2014). Also, in a well-mixed 
reactor VFA are less likely to accumulate, which might 
indicate discrepancies between the modelled CSTR and 
the real mixing behavior (Van Hulle et al., 2014).

Scenario analysis 
In the following section the results from four different 
scenarios are presented and discussed. CH4-production 
and solids were target variables that showed a satisfactory 
level of  prediction by the models for AD1 and AD2 and 
were applied in the scenario analysis. The scenarios build 
the base for a more detailed analysis of  costs and profit 
maximization related to CH4-production and solids 
reduction. This is, however, not part of  the study. The 
scenarios could give an idea about how much the SRT 
can be adjusted and how much the organic loading rate 
can be changed. VSS reduction is commonly used as a 
parameter to assess the stabilization of  sludge. In this 
study the total amount of  sludge was accounted for by 
choosing TSS reduction. All detailed results are provided 
in Appendix F6a.
Scenario A: Current process 
The original process design of  AD1 and AD2 at 
Damhusåen WWTP consists of  a fed-batch CSTR 
with recycle to enhance mixing. This setup was realized 
in the model of  AD2. As mentioned earlier, the SRT 
was assumed to be equal to the HRT in this setup. By 
adjusting the influent flow rate, the SRT can therefore be 
adapted. In order to maximize biogas production/yield 
and boost the stabilization of  sludge, different influent 
flowrates resulting in different OLRs and SRTs were 
tested (Table 16).
The shorter the SRT, the more CH4 was produced (Figure 
40). The highest CH4-production of 6,430 Nm3/d was at 
an OLR of 12.7 kgVS/m3d and an SRT of 3 days. This 
high CH4-production was an artefact of the treatment of 
high organic loading rates, as the CH4-yield indicated that 
the treatment of the substrate was not sufficient (Figure 
40). SRTs shorter than 10 days decreased the CH4-yield 
drastically (Figure 40).
The solids reduction was rather stable around 48%, with a 
slight decrease towards higher SRT. 
Scenario B: Cambi THP
Disintegration and hydrolysis are the first steps in the 
anaerobic digestion process and Cambi THP should 
increase the availability of biodegradable substrate. The 
hydrolysis rate was found to be increased by 70% when 
Cambi THP was applied (Appendix B). The hydrolysis 
rates for the current process setup of AD2 were therefore 
adjusted to be 70% higher. The same change in SRT and 
OLR as in scenario A was applied (Table 16).

By applying an OLR of  12.9 kgVS/m3d, a decrease 
of  CH4-production and yield at low SRT was detected

the CH4-yield was generally higher for AD1 (Figure 41, 
Figure 30, Table 10). 
The current contact process configuration seemed based 
on these results, not ideal. A combination of a higher OLR 
and longer SRT might boost CH4-production and solids 
reduction compared to the current process configuration.

Uncertainties, recommendations 
and  further research
Models are simplifications of real systems. Assumptions 
and estimations for the models to evaluate the full-scale 
anaerobic digesters at Damhusåen WWTP were made 
about: 
•	the exact liquid volume in the reactor
•	influent flow rates
•	effluent flow rates (Qwaste)
•	the recirculation/concentrate flow rate
•	the mixing properties of the reactor
•	reactor temperature (average instead of timeseries)
The selection of parameters and boundaries for the 
preliminary model analyses as well as the calibration of 
the AD2 model was assumed to add limitations to the 
model performance. For the GSA, the SRC-method 
was not considered as optimal, based on low r2-values. 
The model setup of AD1 was simplified in regard to a 
combined recirculation and solids-liquid separation line. 
For the hand calculations of AD1s’ SRT the TSS in 
the permeate was assumed to be negligible, which was 
confounded by model predictions. Furthermore, the 
generation of a dynamic influent was based on average 
influent characteristics of each campaign instead of day-
to-day analyses. A day-to-day analysis of the gCOD/gVS 
ratio of the influent might have increased the prediction 
performance (Ozcan-Yucel and Gökcay, 2010). There 
are always uncertainties connected to analytical analyses, 
which were obvious for the individual VFA analysis and 
some VS measurements of the influent. There might have 
been uncertainties with the BMP of the substrate before 
and after Cambi treatment, which suggests a re-evaluation 
or a different approach to model the Cambi THP related 
scenarios. 
Further investigations could include:
Cambi related scenarios
An influent characterization and fractionation of the 
substrate from Fredericia WWTP before and after Cambi 
THP could be carried out. In this way the variation in the 
influent could be included in the scenario analysis. This is 
assumed to increase th accuracy of the predictions.
Another approach could be a re-analysis based on 
literature, which saves analysis costs and time.
Econimical and environmental aspects
An economic and environmental impact evaluation 
of the scenario results exceeded the scope of this study. 
It is though strongly suggested to determine optimum 
operating conditions of the digesters  from an economical 
and environmental perspective.
Parameter set and GSA
Critical investigations on the influence of parameters 
and boundaries chosen for the uncertainty analysis, GSA 
and calibration could be carried out. Due to the limited 
availability of literature on this subject, more in-depth 
studies could be performed. Otherwise, it is recommended 
to run the uncertainty analysis and GSA again including 
all parameters and boundaries suggested in Batstone et 
al. (2002b) by e.g. installing WEST in the DTU cluster. 
It is also recommended to apply Morris screening to re-
evaluate parameter sensitivity. 
Additional scenarios
A scenario analysis evaluating the effects of further 
up-concentration of the sludge using the solids-liquid 
separation unit could be carried out.
Recommendations to the operator                        
and further research:
It is recommended to review the analysis of VFA and 
Alkalinity currently applied at Biofos. Total VFA could 
be monitored more frequently for AD1, because of 
possible instabilities. It could be evaluated if propionate 
concentrations are a better state indicator than total VFA 
as suggested by Kanokwan et al. (2010). The mixing should 
be well for AD1 with the contact process configuration. 
For future investigations on full-scale anaerobic digesters, 
it is recommended to install flowmeters and online solid 
sensors in order to determine and monitor the SRT. 
The sensors should be maintained well to decrease 
uncertainties.

increased considerably by increasing the SRT. 
Scenarios with Cambi THP were less conclusive. An 
adjustment of hydrolysis rates was carried out, but altering 
the influent could not be justified, because the BMP of 
Cambi treated substrate was not significantly different 
from untreated substrate. 
A model for the anaerobic digesters AD1 and AD2 at 
Damhusåen WWTP, Copenhagen, was created based 
on the results of online sensor data and two sampling 
campaigns. Both digesters are fed-batch CSTR with 
a recycling line with mixed sludge feedstock. AD1 is 
currently configured as a contact process. The prediction 
of the state indicators VSS, CH4-production, pH, TAN 
and Total VFA was the objective.
The collected data showed that the digesters worked at 
underloaded conditions, where AD1 received less influent 
flow than AD2. The digesters operated in general quite 
stable. An increased VFA/Alkalinity ratio for AD1 in the 
second sampling campaign indicated though possible 
unstable conditions. 
The characterization and fractionation of  the influent 
differed in the two sampling campaigns, with high 
carbohydrate content (biodegradable particulate COD as 
basis). The ultimate BMP was not significantly different.

contact process might not be optimal, as no consistent 
increase in CH4-production could be detected compared 
to AD2.

Currently available average biogas production: 2005 m
3/d

headspace determined. All batches were flushed with N2-gas to establish anaerobic conditions (Figure B2). Subsequently all 
bottles were transferred to the incubator (37°C). The batches were shaken occasionally and moved around in the incubator 
to ensure mixing and compensate for temperature variations in the incubator.  
 
 
Data collection 
The collected data as well as the data treatment is provided in digital Appendix B2. The CH4 content in the headspace of 
the bottles was monitored every 3-5 days (more frequently in the beginning). A syringe with pressure lock was inserted 
through the rubber septum of a bottle and a 0.2ml gas sample taken. The pressure lock was closed, and the sample was 
transferred to the gas chromatograph. The pressure was calculated when measurements were taken (Appendix B2). As the 
pressure did not exceed 2 atm, no gas needed to be released. When the measured CH4 production in the headspace was 
levelling off, the experiment was finished.

Appendix D: Anaerobic digestion model 1 (ADM1)
D1: WEST guide on ADM1
ADM1
About the category
The ADM1 in WEST is a non-default Category Reference of the ASM1 Instance – the other non-default Category Reference being “Siegrist”.
The ASM1 and the ADM1 were developed to describe fundamentally different biological processes; they therefore do not use the same set of state
components.
As a consequence, when an ADM1-based model (e.g. an anaerobic digester) is used in the scope of an ASM1-based whole-plant model, the ASM1
state components need to be “transformed” into the relevant ADM1 state components (and vice versa) at the interfaces.
The WEST ADM1 follows the implementation of Zaher (2005) and Nopens et al. (2009).
The COD balance is implicit in the ADM1, since all the components of the model that contribute to the COD are expressed in COD units.  However, a
carbon balance was explicitly considered only when inorganic carbon is the carbon source for, or a product of, catabolism or anabolism (i.e. uptake of
sugars, amino acids, propionate, acetate and hydrogen).
Unlike the original ADM1, the WEST-ADM1, defines a carbon content (
) and a nitrogen content (
) of all model components, in order to close the
carbon and nitrogen balances.
The stoichiometry of the inorganic carbon (

The correct balance of carbon and nitrogen is crucial for the accurate calculation of the pH and of inhibition terms and ultimately of the model kinetics:
·
 inorganic carbon is responsible for the buffering capacity through the bicarbonate concentration and a pH inhibition term is present in all uptake processes;
·
 inorganic nitrogen is the source for ammonium which is also a buffering component; of ammonia, which has a toxic effect on acetate uptake;
·
 furthermore, limitation of biomass growth due to nitrogen deficiency is also modeled.
Another reason to close the inorganic carbon and nitrogen balance is concerned with pre and post-treatment processes, i.e. in context of plant-wide
modeling.  For instance, applying the model for sludge digestion, alkalinity (bicarbonate) and ammonia are components of the conventional activated
sludge processes and models. Also, the digester effluents are high in ammonia concentrations that might call for the application of advanced
processes for ammonia removal. Therefore, an accurate estimation of inorganic carbon and nitrogen in ADM1 is also important when it is used to
evaluate these processes or when it is linked to their models.

F1: Dewatering unit and PI-controller
 
Information from the Online Help of WEST version 2017 
 
DWU efficiency  
 
The solids-liquid separation unit in the recycling line of AD 1 at Damhusåen WWTP was modelled using a dewatering 
unit termed “Efficiency Thickener”. This type of thickener applies a very simplified model of the dewatering process. It 
is based on a mass balance and the level of particulate separation is described by the efficiency of solids retention. Thus, 
the higher the efficiency expression, the larger the sludge up-concentration. It is assumed that the concentration of 
soluble material is constant during the dewatering process. The flow of the concentrate and the efficiency are input 
variables to the dewatering process. The permeate flow and the actual concentrate flow are calculated from the mass 
balance along with the dry solids content.

PI-controller 
 
A PI-controller was used to estimate the efficiency of the contact process. The VSS concentration in the digester from 
the two sampling campaigns was used as a dynamic setpoint (yS). This set-point is the continuously compared to the 
model measured output (yM). The difference between yS and yM is the error.

Based on this error, a control action on the efficiency of the dewatering unit is carried out. The control action (u) is 
proportional to the error and the integral of the error. The baseline control action (u0) as well as minimum and 
maximum control action (umin and umax) are set by the modeler. Furthermore, the factor of proportionality (Kp; 
integral gain of controller) and the integral time frame (TI) can be defined by the modeler.
