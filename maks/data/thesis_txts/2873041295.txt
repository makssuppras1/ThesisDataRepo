Abstract
Light is often cited as a source of sensory overload for adults with Autism Spectrum Condition
(ASC), but quantitative evidence from housing is limited. This thesis analyses a twelve-month
field campaign carried out in thirteen Danish dwellings. Objective measurements of illuminance,
air temperature, relative humidity, CO2 concentration and sound level were logged every five to
ten minutes. The raw data were merged and values within look-back windows of 15 min, 30
min, 1 h, 2 h and 4 h were calculated such as to mean, minimum, maximum, standard deviation
and to the cumulative duration above predefined thresholds. The objective records were merged
with the questionnaires and occupant characteristics, yielding 242 complete rows (each row is one
fully merged instance that combines objective measurements, subjective ratings, and residential
characteristic data) containing three perceptual targets: light satisfaction, light acceptability, and
light preference.
Prior to modelling, an exploratory data analysis (EDA) was carried out to summarise the distri-
butions of the objective variables and to examine the frequency and seasonality of the subjective
ratings; these insights guided feature selection and helped contextualise the modelling results.
After feature selection, class imbalance was treated with SMOTE-NC and three Random-Forest
classifiers were tuned by five-fold grid search. On the hold-out set the satisfaction model achieved
79.6 % accuracy, macro-F1 0.64 and ROC–AUC 0.80, while the acceptability and preference mod-
els reached 71.4 % and 69.4 % accuracy respectively. Exploratory analysis revealed a season-
ally inverted lux–comfort relation in which fewer than 40 lx satisfied most occupants in summer
whereas 60–80 lx were preferred in winter. Window-to-floor ratio, perceived lighting control and
view quality were the most influential predictors in permutation and SHAP importance rankings.
The results suggest a window-to-floor ratio of roughly 0.20–0.30 %, layered user control and adap-
tive brightness set-points tailored to autistic sensory thresholds. Limitations include a uniform bias
in the lux sensors, the modest sample size and the absence of spectral or glare data. Future studies
should add richer light-quality metrics, benchmark the findings against a matched control group
without ASC, and validate the guidelines through longitudinal retrofit trials.

Introduction
Physical indoor spaces exert a considerable influence on the fabric of daily life, and the quality of
these environments, specifically Indoor Environmental Quality (IEQ) plays a pivotal role in shap-
ing human health and well-being [1, 2]. IEQ, which includes factors such as thermal comfort, air
quality, acoustics, and lighting, is consistently related to occupant satisfaction, comfort, cognitive
function, and productivity [3–7]. Although objective measurements of IEQ parameters are essen-
tial, a comprehensive understanding of the impact of the built environment requires a systematic
evaluation of subjective human perception, which varies significantly due to a complex interaction
of physiological, psychological and contextual factors [4, 6].
Among the various components of IEQ, lighting is particularly influential, impacting not only our
circadian rhythms and mood, but also our cognitive performance and overall sense of well-being
[8–10]. Therefore, designing optimal lighting within the built environment requires a nuanced
understanding of occupant perception and preference that extends beyond mere energy efficiency
and basic task illumination.
1.1
Background
Autism Spectrum Condition (ASC) is a neurodevelopmental condition characterized by differ-
ences in social communication and interaction, along with the presence of repetitive behaviors
and restricted interests [11]. Affecting approximately 1 in 100 children worldwide, with increas-
ing diagnosis rates in Denmark [12], understanding and accommodating the needs of individuals
with ASC is becoming increasingly important. A core feature of ASC is atypical sensory process-
ing, which involves heightened (hypersensitivity) or reduced (hyposensitivity) responses to a wide
array of sensory stimuli, including vision [13, 14]. In particular, heightened light sensitivity is
prevalent in ASC, manifesting itself as discomfort or pain in bright light (photophobia), sensitivity
to glare, adverse reactions to flickering lights (especially fluorescent) and intolerance to specific
light spectra, such as blue light from LEDs [15].
Uncomfortable lighting can trigger sensory overload, anxiety, physical discomfort (headaches, eye
strain), distractibility, focus difficulties, and avoidance behaviors [16, 17]. Current building stan-
dards and guidelines, such as ISO 7730 for thermal comfort [18] and DS / EN 12464-1 for lighting,
are based predominantly on research carried out with neurotypical populations and largely ignore
the distinct sensory processing differences characteristic of neurodivergent individuals [15, 19].
This neurotypical bias in existing guidelines highlights the urgent need for standards that are aware
of neurodiversity [15, 19].
Moreover, although research on the built environment’s impact on individuals with ASC is grow-
ing, most studies concentrate on children or specific settings like educational or therapeutic facil-
ities [20, 21]. There is limited work specifically addressing the IEQ needs of adults with ASC
within their residential environments [19, 21, 22], despite the expectation that homes should offer
the highest levels of comfort and safety [23].
1.1.1
Introduction to Machine Learning in Subjective Lighting Assessment
The subjective human perception IEQ is intricate, influenced by various physiological, psycholog-
ical, and contextual factors. Within this complexity, machine learning (ML) has become a promis-
ing methodology. ML techniques can model non-linear links between objective IEQ variables and
subjective comfort responses [24]. Traditional assessment methods, relying on static thresholds or
occupant surveys, struggle to capture the dynamic and individualized nature of visual comfort and
can be prone to response biases, making their qualitative data difficult to quantify for predictive

purposes [25]. In contrast, ML offers enhanced predictive modeling, real-time environmental opti-
mization, and robust anomaly detection in IEQ assessment [25]. The increasing use of ML in IEQ

philosophies [26]. ML algorithms learn from diverse datasets, identify subtle patterns, and pre-

While ML integration in IEQ assessment is growing, its application to predicting subjective light-

1.1.2
Machine Learning Models for Predicting Visual Comfort and Glare in
Neurotypical Populations
To understand current capabilities in predicting subjective lighting responses, it’s instructive to
review ML applications for neurotypical populations. These studies offer insights into common
algorithms, data inputs, and achieved predictive accuracy, essential groundwork before addressing
populations with unique sensory profiles like ASC. A notable trend is the use of complex, data-
rich inputs (e.g., High Dynamic Range luminance maps) and ML models adept at handling non-
linearity, a significant step from simpler regression models or single-point illuminance metrics
[27]. The complexity of visual comfort necessitates these sophisticated tools [28].
Key studies include: Ibrahim [29] aimed to develop a user-friendly ML model to predict dis-
comfort glare in open-plan offices, addressing limitations of conventional glare metrics. Using a
RUSBoosted Tree algorithm (top performer among 825 models) and features from a Multi-Region
Luminance (MRL) method, the model utilized MRL-374 features. To train the model, 25 ML
algorithms were each paired with 33 distinct feature sets, seven MRL grids plus other single- or
bundled-metric sets—so 25×33 = 825 candidate models were evaluated by 5-fold cross-validation
to identify the best-performing glare predictor. Subjective data included five-point visual comfort
and four-point glare scales. The model achieved 83.8% accuracy (TPR 0.80, TNR 0.86), signif-
icantly outperforming 24 conventional glare indices. Contributions include the Open-plan Glare
Evaluator (OGE) tool and a framework for new glare assessment ML models, highlighting ML’s
ability to capture complex non-linear relationships. Limitations noted were glare’s subjectivity,
challenges with metrics in non-dominant daylight, and the stochastic nature of glare responses.
This work underscores the potential of ensemble ML methods and HDR imagery.
Ma and Pan [30] developed an individualized visual comfort model in China using algorithms like
Random Forest (RF), Kernel Support Vector Machine (KSVM), Classification Tree (CTree), and
Gaussian Mixed Model (GMM), leading to a BIM plug-in. Feature selection identified unsatisfied
vertical illuminance (UVI), daylight glare index (DGI), luminance ratio (LR), and shadow position
(SP) as key indicators, supplemented by personalized, temporal, environmental, and system data.
Using 3-fold cross-validation, data from six students (mean age 23.3) involved recording objective
lighting parameters and subjective comfort (3-point scale: dim, satisfactory, bright) every five
minutes. RF performed best (median AUC = 0.723, well above the 0.50 random-chance baseline).
For one participant, RF accuracy was 85.71% (dim), 85.14% (comfort), and 88.33% (bright). The
study highlighted UVI, DGI, LR, and SP as influential. Contributions include the personalized
model, BIM plug-in, and demonstrated potential for 17.9%/hr energy savings. Limitations were
the small sample size, limited algorithms, short data period, simplified subjective scale, and focus
on individual rather than group dynamics. This research emphasizes personalization and RF’s
success, noting small sample sizes as a common challenge.
Luo et al. [31] investigated influential variables and interactions in daylighting glare metrics us-
ing ML and SHAP (a tool that shows how much each input feature pushes the model’s prediction
toward a specific class as the feature’s value changes), addressing prior studies’ focus on linear im-
pacts. Employing various ML algorithms and ML-based feature selection, SHAP provided model

1.1.3
Subjective Lighting Assessment for Individuals with ASC: Current
Understanding and the Potential for Machine Learning
This section reviews existing ML studies on subjective lighting assessment, critically evaluates
their methodologies and findings, and identifies research gaps, focusing on implications for indi-
viduals with ASC.
The specific lighting needs of individuals with ASC demand dedicated attention. Atypical sen-
sory processing, a core ASC characteristic, often includes heightened light sensitivity (photopho-
bia, glare sensitivity) and adverse reactions to certain light sources/spectra [13, 15]. Understand-
ing these perceptual differences is key to developing supportive environments. For instance, re-
search by Block (2018) emphasized that optimizing visual comfort for individuals with ASC can be
achieved through a reduced-stimulus environment, specifically identifying fluorescent lighting as
a common source of distraction [32]. Similarly, Noble et al. (2018) found that “glare” and “electric
light” were considerable sources of distress for individuals on the autism spectrum [33]. Caniato
et al. (2022) further identified “light contrast (glare),” “high lighting,” and “specific light sources”
as visual stressors, noting that perception could be correlated with gender, age, and comorbidities
[34, 35].
Direct applications of predictive ML models for lighting comfort in individuals with ASC are
scarce, though studies assessing lighting preferences in ASC provide foundational knowledge,
defining critical parameters for future ML models. Nair et al. [16] explored light and color ef-
fects on autistic children’s behavior via literary assessment and surveys. Findings revealed prefer-
ences for natural daylight, dislike of dark or overly bright rooms, general preference for LED over
fluorescent lights (with brightness control if fluorescent), neutral-colored or muted/pastel lights,
overhead lighting to avoid direct visibility, and windows at/above eye level. Unsuitable lighting
was linked to agitated behavior [16]. Adding to this, Sadia (2020) analyzed design preferences
for neurodivergent populations, finding that in quiet and relaxing spaces, only a minority (32.1%)
desired colored lighting, with blue light being preferred among those who did. Notably, individu-
als with ASC showed a proportional preference for lower illumination settings compared to those
with Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) [36].
While studies like Nair et al. (2022) offer specific, actionable insights for lighting design for
autistic children and highlight lighting’s impact on well-being and behavior, its focus on children
means cautious extrapolation to adults (the focus of the present thesis), and it didn’t use ML for
prediction. Nevertheless, its findings, along with those from Block (2018), Noble et al. (2018),
Caniato et al. (2022), and Sadia (2020), are crucial for informing ML model development for ASC
by identifying key input features (e.g., light source type, intensity, glare potential, controllability,
color properties) and desirable predictive outputs (e.g., preference, comfort, avoidance of sensory
overload).
A significant gap exists: a lack of ML studies predicting subjective lighting assessments for adults
with ASC in residential settings. While general IEQ ML applications advance and some research
explores neurodiversity or uses AI for cognitive biases in autism, they don’t address the critical
need for predictive lighting comfort models tailored to ASC. Adapting existing ML techniques
(Random Forests, SVMs, ensemble methods) for ASC is considerable but presents challenges.
The pronounced inter-individual sensory variability in ASC necessitates even stronger personal-

Research Questions
This thesis aims to bridge the existing gap by investigating how objectively measured IEQ, primarily
lighting and its related settings (e.g., shading, window-to-floor ratio, glare) but also other IEQ domains
such as air temperature, CO2 concentration, relative humidity, and sound level, relate to the subjective
perception of lighting among adults with ASC in their homes. Specifically, the study will address the
following research questions:
1. How do adults with ASC subjectively perceive and evaluate the lighting conditions in their homes
in terms of satisfaction, acceptability, and preference under different settings?
2. Can a machine learning model, specifically a Random Forest algorithm, be developed and effec-

insights, along with the model’s predictive capabilities, be translated into evidence-based design
recommendations and guidelines for creating more supportive and inclusive residential environ-
ments tailored to the sensory needs of adults with ASC?

2.4.1
Data Preparation and Feature Engineering
Effective predictive modeling begins with careful data preparation and thoughtful feature engineering to
enhance the model’s ability to learn from the data.
Feature Scaling
Prior to model development, feature scaling was employed to standardize the measurements across di-
verse environmental parameters, which often possess varying units and ranges. Specifically, standard
scaling (z-score normalization) was applied to continuous variables. This method transforms each fea-
ture to have a mean of zero and a standard deviation of one, according to the formula:
z = x −µ
σ
(2.8)
where x represents the original feature value, µ is the mean of that feature across all samples, and σ
is its standard deviation. This normalization is crucial as it ensures that features with larger numerical
magnitudes do not disproportionately influence the model’s learning process, particularly for algorithms
that are sensitive to feature scales [43].
While feature scaling is not necessary for Random Forests due to their scale-invariant nature, it remains
useful for enhancing interpretability, especially when employing SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations)
to analyze feature contributions [44–46]
Feature Selection
A systematic feature selection process was implemented to address potential multicollinearity, reduce di-
mensionality, and identify the most informative predictors for the model. The initial step involved rank-
ing features using the Random Forest algorithm’s intrinsic feature importance metrics (Gini importance).
This was complemented by Recursive Feature Elimination (RFE), a wrapper method that iteratively trains
the model and removes the least important features until a desired number of features is reached, while
monitoring model performance.
To specifically mitigate multicollinearity, a correlation analysis was conducted. Features exhibiting a
high correlation (e.g., correlation coefficient > 0.85; see Appendix A.1, Figure A.1) were identified.
From each pair of highly correlated features, the one with the lower feature importance (derived from the
initial Random Forest ranking) was removed. This strategy aims to reduce redundancy and simplify the
model while retaining maximum predictive information. High multicollinearity can destabilize model
coefficients and make interpretation difficult; removing one of two highly correlated features based on
its individual predictive power is a common heuristic to improve model stability and interpretability [47]

junction with the ANOVA F-test (using f_classif for classification tasks, as the predictors are con-

ANOVA naturally quantifies how strongly each feature’s mean differs across the response classes.). The

2.4.2
Random Forest Algorithm
The Random Forest algorithm was chosen as the primary modeling technique. This choice is motivated
by its robustness to overfitting, its inherent ability to capture complex non-linear relationships between
features and the target, and its capacity to handle both categorical and continuous input variables with
minimal preprocessing. The algorithm operates by constructing an ensemble (a ”forest”) of decision trees.
Each tree in the ensemble is trained on a bootstrapped random subset of the original data (a technique
known as bagging). Furthermore, at each split point in a decision tree, only a random subset of features
is considered, which helps to decorrelate the trees and further reduce variance [50].
Key components of the Random Forest algorithm include:
• Decision Trees: Individual trees within the forest that recursively partition the data based on feature
values. Splits are typically chosen to minimize impurity, often measured by Gini impurity for
classification tasks:

• Bagging (Bootstrap Aggregating): Each decision tree is trained on a random sample of the train-
ing data drawn with replacement. This introduces diversity among the trees.
• Random Feature Subsets (Feature Randomness): At each node of a tree, when deciding on a
split, the algorithm considers only a random subset of the available features. This further increases
the diversity of the trees in the ensemble.
For this study, a classification approach was adopted rather than regression. This decision was based
on the premise that the subjective nature of the responses (e.g., satisfaction levels, preferences) is better
represented as discrete, distinct categories rather than being mapped onto a continuous numerical scale,
thereby preserving the qualitative uniqueness of each response level. A regression model would implic-
itly assume that the numerical codes assigned to successive categories (for example, ’dissatisfied’ = 0,
’neither’ = 1, ’satisfied’ = 2) are at equal intervals. This aligns with common practices when dealing with

3.4
Predictive modeling with Random Forest
To investigate the predictability of subjective lighting assessments among adults with ASC, a Random
Forest machine learning approach was adopted, consistent with the methodology outlined in Chapter 2.
This modeling framework was applied to predict three distinct target variables: light satisfaction, light
acceptability, and light preference, using a supervised learning pipeline developed in Python.
The dataset, comprising 242 instances, was partitioned into training and testing sets. To address in-
herent class imbalances in the subjective responses, the Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique
(SMOTE)3 was employed on the training data within a cross-validation procedure.
Feature selection techniques were utilized to identify relevant predictors, and hyperparameter tuning for
the Random Forest algorithm was performed using a grid search with 5-fold cross-validation.4
The input features for the models were derived from a comprehensive set of collected data, broadly
encompassing objective environmental measurements, participant-specific characteristics and sensory
profiles, and contextual information about their immediate residential settings and system configurations.
Table 3.3 gives a concise overview of the variables carried forward to the Random-Forest analysis. A full
definition of each predictor, together with the complete wording of the subjective-comfort questionnaire
and the preliminary screening questionnaire, can be found in Appendices A.6, A.8 and A.7.

4
Discussion
This chapter synthesizes the principal findings of the research, interpreting their significance within the
broader context of IEQ for adults ASC. It is structured into three main sections: an exploration of the
main findings and their interpretations, an acknowledgment of the study’s limitations and suggestions
for future research, and finally, the derivation of actionable design guidelines aimed at fostering more
supportive residential environments for adults with ASC.
4.1
Main Findings and Interpretations
The initial phase of this research involved a comprehensive assessment of the general indoor climate
conditions within the participants’ preferred residential rooms. On average, these environments were
found to be thermally neutral, aligning with Category I standards, and well-ventilated, as indicated by CO2
concentrations generally below 900 ppm (see Table 3.1 and Table 3.2 in Results). Relative humidity levels
also predominantly fell within the optimal 30-50 % range (Category I). However, not all IEQ parameters
met these high standards. Sound pressure levels were relatively high (mean ≈41.5 dB(A)), placing them
in Category III, and lighting emerged as one of the least compliant parameters, with average daylight
illuminance meeting only Category II in summer and dropping to Category III in winter. Individual
participant data for temperature, RH, and CO2 largely reflected these overall trends, with some exceptions
for CO2 in two homes (see Appendix A.2).
Concerns regarding instrumentation were noted, particularly for sound pressure and light measurements.
Sound pressure level data showed suspicious consistency around the mean for extended periods, sug-
gesting potential reliability issues with the instruments. Similarly, raw light measurements were often
lower than expected, punctuated by very high, brief spikes of illuminance being above 30 klx (which is
obviously not correct), even in rooms with low Window-to-Floor Ratios (WFR). While the retrospective
rescaling of logger illuminance using point in time measurement readings proved beneficial for many
time-series by correcting systematically low readings, it occasionally exacerbated the impact of these
spikes. The application of a Tukey fence for outlier removal helped mitigate this, but the final treated
illuminance data, while improved for relative comparisons, still exhibited some lower-than-anticipated
values even in rooms with a high WFR ratio.
The EDA underscored the complexity of subjective responses to the luminous environment. It became
evident that simple predictive theories based on isolated physical factors often fall short of explaining
the full spectrum of satisfaction, acceptability, and preferences, as illustrated by the varied illuminance
distributions associated with reported light quality issues (Figure 3.19). A critical finding was the sig-
nificant influence of specific, self-reported light quality issues, such as insufficient light, glare, annoying
lamps, or lack of control, on subjective assessments. Indeed, the absence of such reported issues almost
invariably correlated with better subjective evaluations.
Seasonal variations were apparent in how illuminance levels related to satisfaction and acceptability,
with higher satisfaction at higher illuminance in the heating season, and a tendency towards satisfaction
at lower to moderate illuminance in the cooling season (Figures 3.1, 3.2). However, the influence of
parameters like artificial light status (On/Dimmed/Off) or shading position (Up/Half-way/Down) was less
direct, often being confounded by the presence of these overriding light quality issues, which appeared
across all states of these control systems.
In contrast, view out quality (coded according to DS/EN 17037:2018 ) demonstrated a more discernible
positive impact: higher view quality often corresponded with better subjective assessments and a stronger
preference for ’no change’ in lighting conditions. Conversely, lower view quality was associated with a
greater spread of preferences, often leaning towards a desire for darker conditions (Figures 3.12, 3.11,
3.10) .

Perhaps the most striking EDA finding was the counter-intuitive relationship with WFR. Higher WFRs
were frequently associated with negative subjective assessments and a clustering of reported light quality
issues (Figures 3.13, 3.14, 3.58). While the modest sample size warrants caution, this trend was supported
by multiple participants across different WFRs and aligns with the known hypersensitivity of individuals
with ASC to light, where larger window sizes might more easily trigger sensory overload or glare. The
status of operable windows offered limited direct insight, other than the expected observation of more
open windows during the cooling season.
Temporal analysis of subjective assessments revealed that satisfaction and acceptability tended to increase
towards the evening and during winter months (Figures 3.20, 3.21), potentially indicating a preference for
conditions less influenced by direct, intense daylight, which might be more prevalent during daytime or
summer and could exacerbate issues in spaces with high WFR. Preferences for ’noChange’ were dominant
across various temporal scales (Figure 3.22), though a desire for ”brighter” conditions was noted more
in spring/summer and ”darker” in winter, the interpretation of which is complex without pinpointing
specific underlying causes like insufficient daylight penetration or problematic artificial lighting. The
distribution of response counts, higher towards typical bedtimes, also contextualizes these percentage-
based observations (Figure 3.23).
The Random Forest machine learning models demonstrated commendable predictive capabilities, achiev-
ing test set accuracies of 79.6 % for light satisfaction (Table 3.4), 71.4% for light acceptability (Table 3.5),
and 69.4% for light preference (Table 3.6). These models generalized well to the held-out test data. A
consistent challenge across all three models was the prediction of neutral or ambivalent states (the ”nei-
ther” categories), which invariably yielded lower F1-scores and AUC values. This difficulty likely stems
from their smaller sample sizes within the dataset, even after SMOTE application, and potentially from
the class binning methodology, where central neutral categories do not merge and thus benefit less from
an expanded data pool compared to categories at the extremes of the scale.
Feature importance analyses (MDI, permutation, and SHAP) consistently highlighted WFR as a highly
influential predictor, particularly for light satisfaction and acceptability. SHAP plots confirmed the EDA
finding that higher WFR values often contributed to predictions of dissatisfaction, while lower WFRs
were associated with satisfaction (e.g., Figure 3.33, 3.31). For light preference, reported issues such as
”insufficient daylight” were highly influential, pushing predictions towards ”Brighter” when present, and
”too much daylight” pushing towards ”Darker” (e.g., Figure 3.53, 3.55).
Other consistently important predictors included outdoor view characteristics, aspects of lighting control
(e.g., ”light No Control on”), perceived light levels, and even proxies for thermal and air quality conditions
and personal states like sleep quality, underscoring the deeply interconnected nature of IEQ factors. This
study’s application of ML to predict subjective lighting assessments for adults with ASC in their homes
directly addresses a recognized knowledge gap, especially when compared to existing ML studies that
focus on neurotypical populations or non-residential settings (Table 3.7). It provides a valuable baseline
and demonstrates the potential for more nuanced, data-driven approaches to understanding and designing
for the sensory needs of individuals with ASC.
4.2
Limitations
Despite the valuable insights generated, this study has several limitations that must be acknowledged,
which in turn pave the way for future research directions. A primary limitation is instrumentation bias.
As discussed, suspicious readings were observed from some sound level meters and lux loggers, charac-
terized by periods of unrealistic constancy or unexpectedly low values punctuated by sharp spikes. While
data processing steps like retrospective lux rescaling and Tukey outlier removal were implemented to mit-
igate these issues, some uncertainty regarding the absolute accuracy of these specific measurements may
remain, potentially affecting the derived predictor variables reliant on them.
The sample size represents another significant constraint. With data from thirteen dwellings resulting
in 242 matched objective-subjective data rows for the ML analysis, the statistical power, particularly

==PAGE:93==
for machine learning applications, is modest. This was especially evident in the under-representation
and consequent poorer predictive performance for minority classes, such as the ”neither” categories in
subjective assessments, despite the use of SMOTE-NC oversampling techniques. A larger sample would
be crucial for validating the current findings and for building more robust and generalizable predictive
models.
The cross-sectional nature and geographical specificity of the study also limit the generalizability of its
findings. All participants resided in Denmark, meaning that cultural lighting practices, prevailing archi-
tectural styles (including typical façade designs and WFRs), and local climate-driven behaviors might
differ from those in other regions. Consequently, the direct applicability of some findings and derived
guidelines to different cultural or climatic contexts may require further investigation.
Furthermore, the reliance on self-reported data for subjective assessments, while standard in IEQ research,
inherently carries the potential for biases related to individual interpretation of scales, recall, and current
mood. For individuals with ASC, whose sensory processing is atypical and highly variable, standardized
questionnaires might not fully capture the unique nuances of their experiences or the intensity of their
sensory responses.
These limitations inform several avenues for future work:
• Enhanced Instrumentation and Data Collection: Future studies should prioritize the use of
spectrally calibrated High Dynamic Range (HDR) cameras to capture more comprehensive data
on luminance distributions, glare potential, and correlated colour temperature. This is particularly
relevant given the heightened sensitivity to glare and specific light spectra reported in ASC.
• Expanded and Diversified Samples: Enlarging the participant sample is critical for increasing
statistical power and the robustness of ML models. Importantly, future research should aim to
include a matched neurotypical control group to allow for more direct comparisons and to better
delineate ASC-specific lighting needs from general population preferences, thereby addressing the
neurotypical bias prevalent in current standards. Expanding recruitment to diverse geographical
and cultural contexts would also enhance generalizability.
• Refined Subjective Assessment Tools: Developing or adapting subjective assessment scales to be
more attuned to the sensory experiences of individuals with ASC is warranted. This could involve
exploring alternative response formats or scales that better capture the intensity and qualitative
nature of their perceptions, potentially mitigating issues with ambiguous ”neither” responses.
• Advanced Predictive Modeling and Personalization: Exploring more sophisticated ML tech-
niques, including online reinforcement learning, could lead to the development of dynamic, person-
alized lighting control systems that adapt in real-time to individual occupant needs and preferences.
This aligns with the call for more occupant-centric building philosophies and the pronounced need
for personalization in ASC.
• Longitudinal and Intervention Studies: Conducting longitudinal studies, including post-retrofit
follow-ups after implementing design changes based on research findings, is essential to verify
whether proposed guidelines translate into sustained improvements in comfort, well-being, and
occupant satisfaction.
• Deeper Investigation of IEQ Interactions: While this study included multiple IEQ parameters,
future work could more systematically investigate the complex interactions between different sen-
sory domains (e.g., the combined effects of lighting and acoustics) as they pertain to individuals
with ASC.
Addressing these areas will contribute to a more profound and actionable understanding of how to create
truly supportive and inclusive indoor environments for adults with ASC.

4.3
Design Guidelines
The collective evidence gathered from the exploratory data analysis, subjective assessments, and machine
learning model interpretations allows for the formulation of several actionable design guidelines aimed
at creating residential lighting environments that are more supportive and accommodating for adults with
ASC. These guidelines are grounded in the empirical findings of this thesis and the recognized sensory
characteristics of autism.
1. Optimize Window-to-Floor Ratio (WFR) and Prioritize Glare Control: The findings suggest
that a WFR in the range of 0.20–0.30 may offer a better balance between adequate daylight pro-
vision and the risk of excessive brightness or glare for individuals with ASC. This is supported by
observations where higher WFRs were sometimes linked to dissatisfaction. For spaces with WFRs
exceeding this range, or where direct sunlight penetration is unavoidable, the provision of robust,
multi-layered, and easily user-operable shading systems (e.g., blackout blinds, diffusing curtains,
adjustable louvers) is paramount to mitigate light quality issues and allow occupants to modulate
daylight intake according to their needs.
2. Implement Layered, Dimmable, and User-Controllable Lighting Systems: To cater to diverse
needs and preferences, and to avoid sensory overload, a layered lighting approach is recommended,
as the empirical evidence in this study shows that, there was reported issues when the artifical light
was on, dimmed and off. This involves a combination of:
• General ambient lighting that is evenly distributed and, crucially, dimmable. LED sources
are generally preferred over fluorescent lighting, given the latter’s association with flicker
and adverse reactions in some individuals with ASC.
• Task-specific accent lighting (e.g., desk lamps, under-cabinet lights) that occupants can use
to illuminate specific activity areas without altering the overall room brightness.
• Indirect lighting options that reduce the likelihood of direct views of bright light sources,
which can be a source of discomfort.
The emphasis must be on user controllability for all lighting elements (e.g., accessible dimmers,
separate switches for different layers, intuitive interfaces for motorized blinds if used). This ad-
dresses the frequently cited issue of loss of agency over one’s environment and supports the pref-
erence for control often observed in individuals with ASC, further reinforced by the predictive
importance of features like ‘light No Control on‘ in the ML models.
3. Design for Seasonally Adaptive and Personalized Lighting Conditions: Given the observed
seasonal variations in lighting satisfaction and preferences, lighting design and control strategies
should be adaptable. Daylight-linked set-points for artificial lighting, if used, should incorporate
seasonal adjustments. More broadly, the design should facilitate a high degree of personalization,
moving beyond one-size-fits-all solutions. This acknowledges the significant inter-individual vari-
ability in sensory processing within the ASC population and the potential for ML-informed systems
to eventually tailor environments to specific occupant needs, as suggested by the development of
predictive models in this study.
4. Consider the Impact of View Quality: Where feasible within architectural constraints, maximiz-
ing access to good quality external views should be considered in room layout and window design.
This study found a positive correlation between higher view quality and subjective lighting satis-
faction.
5. Minimize Known Visual Stressors and Maximize Perceived Comfort: Design choices should
actively seek to minimize common environmental triggers that can cause distress or sensory over-
load for individuals with ASC. This includes:

• Avoiding direct visibility of lamps and bright light sources; using diffusers or shielded lumi-

• Ensuring light sources are flicker-free, with careful selection of lamps and control gear, es-

• Being mindful of light spectra; while individual preferences vary, some individuals with ASC

needs of adults with ASC, thereby enhancing their quality of daily life.

5
Conclusion
This thesis embarked on an investigation into the nuanced interplay between objectively measured IEQ
parameters, with a primary focus on lighting, and the subjective lighting assessments of adults ASC within
their residential settings. Acknowledging the prevalent neurotypical bias in current building standards
and the paucity of research centered on adults with ASC in their homes, this study aimed to elucidate these
relationships and explore the viability of ML techniques for predicting lighting satisfaction, acceptability,
and preference.
The research yielded several key findings. Overall, the participants’ homes were generally thermally
neutral and well-ventilated; however, sound pressure levels were often high, and lighting conditions fre-
quently fell short of established neurotypical comfort categories, particularly in winter. Exploratory data
analysis underscored the complexity of subjective lighting responses, revealing that they are not dic-
tated by isolated physical factors alone but are significantly shaped by self-reported light quality issues,
such as glare or lack of control. Seasonal variations influenced lighting satisfaction and acceptability,
and a counter-intuitive yet critical observation was that higher Window-to-Floor Ratios were often as-
sociated with negative subjective assessments, possibly linked to ASC sensory hypersensitivities. Con-
versely, good external view quality generally correlated with more positive assessments. Temporal pat-
terns also emerged, with satisfaction and acceptability tending to be higher in the evenings and during
winter months. Some instrumentation challenges were noted, particularly concerning sound and light
loggers, which were addressed through data processing techniques like rescaling and outlier removal.
Significantly, this study successfully developed Random Forest ML models capable of predicting light
satisfaction, acceptability, and preference with reasonable accuracies (79.6%, 71.4%, and 69.4% on test
data, respectively). A persistent challenge was the prediction of neutral (”neither”) categories, attributable
mainly to their smaller representation in the dataset. Feature importance analyses consistently highlighted
WFR, view quality, reported light quality issues (e.g., ‘lightNoControl_on‘, ‘lowLight_on‘), and even
interconnected IEQ factors as crucial predictors.
This research makes several contributions to the field. It is among the pioneering efforts to apply ML for
predicting subjective lighting assessments specifically for adults with ASC in their residential environ-
ments, directly addressing a significant knowledge gap. The findings provide crucial empirical evidence
on influential environmental predictors and lay a data-driven foundation for ASC-specific design guide-
lines, demonstrating the potential of ML to foster more personalized and supportive living spaces for
neurodivergent individuals.
However, the study is not without limitations. The modest sample size (13 participants, 242 data in-
stances) impacts statistical power and the generalizability of the findings. Instrumentation biases, though
mitigated, remain a consideration. The study’s Danish context means that cultural and geographical fac-
tors might temper the direct applicability of results elsewhere. Furthermore, reliance on self-reported
data via standardized questionnaires may not fully capture the diverse sensory experiences of individuals
with ASC.
The insights gained translate into actionable design implications. These include optimizing WFR (poten-
tially towards 0.20–0.30 %) while prioritizing robust, user-controlled glare management; implementing
layered, dimmable, and highly user-controllable lighting systems to bolster occupant agency; design-
ing for seasonally adaptive and personalized lighting conditions; and carefully considering view quality
alongside minimizing known visual stressors like direct lamp visibility and flicker.
In conclusion, this thesis represents a significant advancement in understanding and addressing the light-
ing needs of adults with ASC in their homes. It underscores the imperative for neurodiversity-aware
design and showcases the promise of ML as a tool for achieving more tailored environmental solutions.

Preliminary Information Questionnaire 
 
Project name: SENSEwellbeing 
Preliminary information 
 
INSTRUCTIONS: 
Dear participant, 
This is a preliminary form regarding a study on well-being indoors of people on the autism spectrum. This form 
is used to collect information about the study participants. 
People on the autism spectrum deserve more attention to their needs for indoor comfort and well-being. Due 
to their sensitivity to indoor environmental stimuli (i.e., hyper-sensitivity or hypo-sensitivity), they might have 
particular needs in terms of thermal, visual, indoor air quality and acoustic environments. Nevertheless, 
standards and guidelines typically address average neurotypical populations, and little is known about how the 
indoor environment affects the well-being of people on the spectrum, especially from the indoor environment 
and comfort engineering perspective. Moreover, some literature suggests that optimal indoor conditions for 
people on the spectrum might be more pleasant for neurotypical people as well. This knowledge gap has 
motivated SENSEwellbeing, in which we aim to perform an extensive study on indoor well-being of people on 
the spectrum. 
For these reasons, we kindly ask you to answer the following questions as accurately as you can.  
Please note that, as personal information will be collected, GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation, EU-
Regulation 2016/679) rules and ethical principles will always be carefully followed. All data will be stored only 
after pseudonymisation. In this framework, according to the data management procedures at DTU, sensitive 
data will be encrypted and deleted two years after the end of the project. All questionnaires will be analysed 
by the researcher and the research group, they will be protected from any other access, and sensitive data will 
always be presented to the public in an aggregated form. 
Your contribution is very important for the indoor well-being of people, both neuroatypical and neurotypical! 
Thank you.
