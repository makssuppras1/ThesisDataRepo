Abstract
A complementary method to the one prescribed by the International Standard is proposed with the aim of
providing with more detailed and comprehensive information on the eﬀects produced by the combined action of
wind and atmospheric icing on overhead transmission line systems. The research project proves the inadequacy
of a design simply based on the static combination of these loads and the exigence of extending the analysis to
the dynamic response of the system which may be activated under the contemporary occurrence of wind and ice.
The study is in particular focused on the potential development of aerodynamic instability conditions which may
yield large amplitude and low frequency cyclic motions, known as galloping. The impact of this phenomenon,
and thus its not negligibility in the design of a power line, is pointed out by comparing the increment of tension
transferred to the supporting towers, due to the oscillations, with the design static load. The analysis conducted
refers to a real case of study represented by the transmission line system of the Long Lake Hydro Project, in the
British Columbia, Canada.
The development of the galloping is investigated on the basis of a statistical approach which refers to the climatic
dataset of the site of interest and ﬁeld observations of this type of motion. A parametric study in which wind
and ice loads are diﬀerently combined is therefore conceived, so that to enhance understanding of the inﬂuence
of each variable involved on the occurrence and the importance of the related instabilities. The analysis of the
aerodynamic characteristics of the line, corresponding to the diﬀerent loads combinations proposed, is carried
out both by means of physical and numerical modelling. The instability conditions are evaluated, and compared,
by referring to Den Hartog’s and Nigol’s theories. The dynamic response of the system is restricted to the case
of vertical galloping and computed by employing the linear theory of free vibrations of a suspended cable by
Irvine. Eventually, a risk analysis for the site of interest is carried out to provide with a summary of the ﬁndings
obtained through the work conducted and a useful tool for the selection of the most suitable control methods to
be employed to limit or prevent the galloping.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The ice accretion on network equipment and structures is a worldwide phenomenon which usually takes place in
mountainous areas where temperature can drop below the freezing point or where wet snow is experienced [1].
This natural event varies in form and relevance in relation to the atmospheric climatic conditions and can
be particularly detrimental for overhead transmission line systems, aﬀecting their operations and thus causing
major problems to the society. The consequence of a long blackout can bring entire cities to their knees, as
telecommunication system, water and food distribution, money transfer and modern medical care cease, only to
cite few examples. In the recent history, the ice storm occurred in Quebec and Ontario, Canada, in January 1998
produced a massive collapse of 1300 power-line towers and 35000 distribution-line towers, leaving over 2 million
people without electricity for weeks and causing 25 deaths [1].
The vulnerability of the transmission lines to the atmospheric icing, however, does not have to be considered
as the exclusive product of an increment of a static gravity load. Of equal hazard is the combination of this
event with a wind load. When ice and snow accumulate on cylindrical shaped structures they may naturally
produce asymmetrical geometries. Potentially, these proﬁles are aerodynamically unstable if exited by a wind
load of a certain speed and angle of attack. The combined eﬀect of wind and ice, or snow, can therefore cause
conductors to oscillate in low frequency and large amplitude motions. The instability, known as galloping, yields
cyclic dynamic loads which can cause severe damages to conductors, insulators, hardware and towers [2].
1.1
Literature review
The eﬀort to understand the phenomenon of ice accretion and its eﬀects on overhead transmission line systems
when occurring in combination with a wind action began in the 20th century, and its relevance has progressively
increased with the development of the computational power until to the present day. In particular, the number
of studies have been exponentially raised in the last decade, demonstrating the topicality of the problem. The
research on this ﬁeld can mainly be divided in two broad categories which are related: physical and numerical
models aimed at reproducing the ice mass distribution on the conductor and its subsequent inﬂuence on the aero-
dynamic properties of the line, and theoretical, physical and numerical models aimed at describing the dynamic
response of the system when subjected to a combination of wind and ice loads.
In relation to the ﬁrst branch, physical simulations have been conducted by means of static wind tunnel tests.
Gjelstrup (2014) [3] performed a series of tests for several variations of input conditions in order to evaluate the
resulting ice geometry accreted on a cylindrical-shaped body, such as a conductor, and calculated the correspond-
ing aerodynamic characteristics according to diﬀerent air ﬂow conditions. The need of a climatic wind tunnel
test facility to conduct the experiment and the diﬃculties on generalizing the results, however, have redirected
the majority of the studies towards the adoption of artiﬁcial ice shapes. The focus of the investigation, therefore,
has been in this case restricted to the study of the aerodynamic characteristics of the conductor, either single or
bundled, while the ice geometry accreted has been assumed according to previous ﬁeld and experimental observa-
tions. The employment of these pre-determined, and more regular, shapes have also facilitated the reproduction
of the experimental conditions by means of numerical simulations. The two dimensional CFD models so designed
have been built with the aim of matching the results obtained through the wind tunnel tests, and thus providing
with an alternative tool when performing aerodynamic analyses. Li et. al (2012) [4] conducted a research dealing
with the eﬀect on the aerodynamic properties of a single conductor produced by a variation of the ice proﬁle
1
==PAGE:14==
type and the wind speed which the body is subjected to. The outcome was compared with numerical simulations
performed by the same authors [5]. Yan et al. (2014) [6] conducted wind tunnel tests aimed at determining
the variation of the aerodynamic characteristics of single, two-bundled and four-bundled conductor caused by a
change in eccentricity of the crescent ice shape considered. Liu et al. (2015) [7] compared wind tunnel and CFD
results for both the cases of single and two-bundled conductor exposed to a single load combination of wind and
ice. From the prove of the good agreement between physical and numerical results, the authors extended the
CFD simulations to the case of a four-bundled conductor [8].
The study of the dynamic behaviour of a overhead transmission line system induced by the combined eﬀect of
wind and atmospheric icing loads has primarily focused on the understanding of the galloping, rather than other
types of motions (e.g. aeolian vibrations), due to its dangerousness. The pioneer of the theoretical analysis of
the galloping is Den Hartog (1932) [9], who explained the phenomenon as a pure aerodynamic instability applied
to a single-degree-of-freedom system (SDOF) subjected to a vertical oscillation mechanism. An extension of this
theory was conceived by Nigol and Clarke (1974) [10] who described the galloping as an aeroelastic instability,
where the motion depends on the interaction between structural data [11]. According to the authors, the event,
named ﬂutter galloping, is driven by the coupling of vertical and torsional motion. This two-degrees-of-freedom
mechanism was further extended by Yu et al. (1993) [12] who redeﬁned the phenomenon by employing a three
translational and a torsional degree-of-freedom model.
An important contribution to the development of numerical simulations of the galloping was given by Desai at
al. (1995) [13], who conceived a ﬁnite element model where the iced conductor is discretized by a three-node
isoparametric cable element and the galloping is described by means of a mode superimposition, corresponding
to a linearization of the dynamic equation [14]. The model was updated by Liu et al. (2009) [15] by considering
the geometrical non linearity, which enabled to discover a new possible galloping mode.
As for the analysis of the aerodynamic properties of the conductor, even the numerical solutions describing the
dynamic response of the line have been compared with wind tunnel tests in order to prove their reliability. Several
research projects, therefore, have dealt with the development of aerolastic models recreating a portion of the line.
The structural proprieties of the actual system have been tuned through the adjustment of the characteristics and
the position of the springs supporting the model. The vertical, horizontal and torsional displacements induced
by galloping found by means of the physical simulation have been shown to be consistent with the numerical
results. As an example of this procedure, Yan et al. (2014) [14] numerically investigated the behaviour of a
four-bundled conductor subjected to galloping by developing a nonlinear ﬁnite element method and proved its
solidity by designing and testing an equivalent aeroelastic model. Lou et al. (2016) [16] carried out a similar
research project for the case of a six-bundled conductor.
Lastly, it is worth to mention that the two types of studies described, namely aerodynamic analysis of the iced
conductor and dynamic analysis of the power line, have been recently embedded within a unique coupled ﬂuid-
structure interaction analysis by Borna (2014) [17]. The methodology so conceived is capable to directly provide
with time series of the nonlinear dynamic response of the conductor, either single or bundled, to the unsteady
ﬂow loading predicted by the CFD model incorporated in this tool.
1.2
Case of study
The uniqueness of this research project lies in the fact that it is based on a real case of study. The work deals with
the power line designed for the Long Lake Hydro Project, located on Cascade Creek, in the proximity of Stewart,
in the British Columbia, Canada. The facility has been operating since 2013 and consists of a 10 km long 138 kV
overhead transmission line system. The line delivers energy to the BC Hydro and represents also the only source
of grid connected power supply to a local mine site [18]. The power line develops on a mountainous region having
a complex terrain with continuous altitude variations. Figure 1.2.1 and 1.2.2 illustrates the geographical area in
which the line is situated and gives a schematic representation of the line route and towers location, respectively.

The location of the overhead transmission line system exposes it to harsh climatic conditions, characterized
by strong wind gusts and large accumulations of atmospheric icing, variable in form and relevance according
to the altitude. At the same time, the remoteness of the site makes diﬃcult its access and the maintenance
work, only possible by means of helicopter. Because of these reasons, the company responsible for the project
decided to evaluate the loading conditions experienced by the line at each tower and span and design the structural
3
==PAGE:16==
system accordingly. Separate loads assessments were carried out by the WindEEE Research Institute [21] and the
Norwegian company Kjeller Vindteknikk [19]. The ﬁrst analysis was aimed at determining the wind characteristics
by means of physical and numerical modeling according to the actual topography of interest, while the second
study focused on both wind and ice loads, computed by means of a WRF model (see Section 3.1). The details
collected from these investigations, however, were simply used in order to design the system as prescribed by
the International Standard (IEC 60826:2003) [22], and thus by combining the wind and atmospheric icing loads
according to the worst static scenario having an average occurrence of one time in 50 years. No analyses, instead,
were performed in relation to a possible dynamic behavior of the system induced by these loads combination.
The research project conducted is therefore based on this gap of knowledge.
1.3
Objective and research methodology
The consideration at the base of this research project is that the dynamic behavior of the system produced
by galloping is totally neglected by the International Standard’s provisions for the design of overhead electrical
transmission lines (IEC 60826:2003) [22], which consider an equivalent radial thickness when dealing with atmo-
spheric icing accretion, hence referring to a stable proﬁle and limiting the analysis to a static condition.
The main objective of this research project is therefore to develop a complementary method - respect to the
design guideline prescribed by the International Standard - to evaluate the eﬀect of the wind load on iced con-
ductors in the hypothesis of asymmetrical ice accretion occurrence. The solution is based on the same type of
climatic dataset generally employed for the static design of the line, but it aims to describe the dynamic response
of a single span when subjected to galloping and prove the not negligibility of the corresponding resulting loads.
The development of instability conditions and their characterization, in terms of oscillations and increments of
tension transferred to the supporting system, is evaluated for a large number of cases, obtained by diﬀerently
combining wind and atmospheric icing loads. Therefore, another primary objective of the work is to enhance
understanding of the eﬀect of each of the variables involved on the aerodynamic properties of the conductor,
and thus on the related occurrence and signiﬁcance of the galloping phenomenon. Moreover, as the aerodynamic
characteristics of the conductor are evaluated by means of both physical and numerical simulations, a further
goal of this research project is to prove the consistency of the outcome of the two analyses performed for a given
loads combination.
In order to prove the relevance of the loads induced by galloping and compare them with the design static load,
it is necessary to base the work on a real case of study for which wind and atmospheric icing data are available.
The project, therefore, deals with the particular case of the Long Lake transmission line system and the analysis
is limited to the span subjected to the most critical ice load, namely the one supported by tower n◦16 and tower
n◦17.
The reference asymmetrical ice shapes to be considered in the study for the diﬀerent icing events are determined
through a literature review on the geometries which are concurrently likely to build up and prone to galloping.
Similarly, the range of wind speeds is selected. A parametric study is conceived by combining the ice and wind
loads proposed and the relevance of the diﬀerent cases obtained is evaluated by performing a frequency analysis
for each of the diﬀerent types of natural events, namely wet snow, rime ice and glaze.
The aerodynamic coeﬃcients associated to the diﬀerent loads combinations considered are computed by conduct-
ing a static force balance wind tunnel test. Additionally, a CFD model reproducing the experimental conditions
is conceived with the aim of providing with an alternative method and verifying the results achieved through the
physical simulations. The possibility of development of vertical and torsional galloping within a certain range of
angles of attack is determined by referring to Den Hartog’s [9] and Nigol’s [10] theory, respectively.
In contrast to the aerodynamic instability analysis, which is extended to the case of torsional galloping, the
dynamic analysis performed addresses the span as a single-degree-of-freedom system, only susceptible to vertical
movements; the galloping is therefore treated as a pure instability phenomenon, as explained by Den Hartog.
The eigenfrequencies and eigenvectors of the conductor of the span of interest are computed by employing the
linear theory of cable free vibrations, as described by in Irvine and Caughey in 1974 [23]. By combining the
information on the structural dynamic properties of the line with the outcome from the aerodynamic analysis,
the maximum oscillations reached by the span, as well as the tension increment transferred to the towers, are
determined for the diﬀerent loads combinations.
Eventually, with the aim of proving the not neglibility of the galloping in the design of a power line and concur-
rently providing with useful information for the development of targeted control methods, a risk analysis of the
diﬀerent combinations of wind and atmospheric icing loads is conducted.

Atmospheric Icing
The ﬁrst section of this chapter provides with an overall understanding of the diﬀerent forms of atmospheric icing
which occur in nature and their properties. In the second section, the standardized model to predict ice accretion
on overhead lines and structures as conceived by Makkonen is described. Eventually, in the third section the
phenomenon of galloping is explained and a review of the related aerodynamic and aeroelastic instability theories
is given. Furthermore, the linear theory of cable free vibration, applied in the study conducted, is introduced.
2.1
Types of ice and snow accretion
Reporting the deﬁnition given in [1], the atmospheric icing is a term used to deﬁne several physical processes
where the water in various forms in the atmosphere freezes and adheres to objects exposed to the air.
Generally, the atmospheric icing accretion can be identiﬁed according to the following classiﬁcation:
• Precipitation icing
• In-cloud icing
• Hoar frost
These three diﬀerent categories are detailed in the next subsections.
2.1.1
Precipitation icing
Precipitation icing may occur in diﬀerent forms, namely freezing rain (glaze or rime), wet snow and dry snow [1].
Freezing rain
Freezing rain takes place when falling raindrops meet a cold air layer at temperatures below zero, which cools
down the droplets, but leaves them in a liquid water phase [22]. The accretion due to freezing rain is therefore
the result of the impingement of these supercooled droplets with an object surface, on which they freeze [22]. The
accretion process is governed by the surface conditions of the object, and in particular by the heat ﬂux. When
the heat transfer from the object is larger than the latent heat of fusion released, then the freezing rate equals
the impingement rate, meaning that all particles freeze at the position where they hit the object, contributing to
the ice accretion. This type of growth is known as ’dry’ and leads to rime ice formations. On the contrary, when
the heat transfer from the surface is not suﬃcient to counterbalance the latent heat, only part of the particles
instantaneously freeze, while the remaining run-oﬀor ﬂow along the object before freezing. This second accretion
process is identiﬁed as ’wet’ and yields glaze ice formations. The two diﬀerent types of accretion are schematized
in Figure 2.1.1 and 2.1.2, respectively.

The rime ice, in turn, can be divided in two subcategories, namely soft and hard rime, which diﬀer in their
structure and density. The soft rime is characterized by a granular structure with high content of air bubbles
and a white and opaque appearance [1]. Its density may vary between 200 and 600 kg/m3. On the other hand,
the hard rime builds up in a layered structure containing a mixture of clear ice and ice incorporating bubbles.
The density for hard rime is generally larger, between 700 and 900 kg/m3.
The glaze is a clear, solid and smooth type of accretion, characterized by a density of approximately 800-900
kg/m3. Its structure is generally identiﬁed by the presence of typical pendant ice drop formations, known as
icicles, caused by the freezing of the running oﬀwater on the surface. The glaze generally does not contain air
bubbles in its structure. However, air pockets can be found when the accretion process is shaped by strong wind

Wet and dry snow
A partial melting of the snowﬂakes, consisting of a mixture of ice and water, occurs when they pass through the
0◦C isotherm in the atmosphere [22]. If the partially melted ﬂakes are intercepted by an object before reaching
the ground and melting, they will contribute to the wet snow accretion on this object. The particles are generally
sticky, and thus they easily adhere on the surfaces [1]. The density of the wet snow accretion can widely vary
according to wind speed and temperature, ranging between 100 and 800 kg/m3, even though for most of the
cases it is about 400-600 kg/m3 [22]. If the atmospheric temperature drops below 0◦C after the wet snow has
accreted on an object, the formation freezes, resulting into a hard and dense layer [1].
Dry snow accretion occurs at subfreezing temperatures, meaning that the snowﬂakes hit the object prior to
pass through the 0◦C isotherm. This type of growth appears only at small wind speeds, below 2 m/s and it is
characterized by a low density of approximately 100 kg/m3 [1].

parameters controlling their growth, as reported in [22], are summarized in Figure 2.1.5 and 2.1.6, respectively.

2.1.2
In-cloud icing
In-cloud icing can be deﬁned as the natural phenomenon by which the supercooled droplets in a cloud or fog
impact on an object and freeze on its surface [1]. This type of process is particularly frequent in mountainous
regions and may strongly aﬀect overhead transmission line systems.
Similarly to the case of freezing rain, even for in-cloud icing the growth can be distinguished in dry or wet
according to the heat ﬂux on the surface of the object involved. Once again, the ﬁrst type of accretion may
lead to either soft or hard rime, while the second one to glaze ice. The transition between these diﬀerent icing
formations is mostly function of air temperature and wind speed, as represented in Figure 2.1.7.

2.1.3
Hoar frost
The hoar frost refers to the formation of white ice crystals obtained by direct sublimation of the water vapor
in the atmosphere on the object surface. This type of accretion is generally characterized by low density (less
than 300 kg/m3). For this reason, the hoar frost is not generally included among the atmospheric icing types
which can yield loading problems. However, it has to be mentioned that this is a good collector of supercooled
droplets, and thus it may facilitate the formation of rime ice on the object in favorable atmospheric conditions
(subfreezing air temperature and light wind) [1].

particles (e.g. dry snow) the factor generally drops to values basically null. However, the sticking eﬃciency can
be large even for solid precipitations when a thin water ﬁlm surrounds the elements due to favourable conditions

The factor α3 represents the accretion eﬃciency, which can be expressed as the ratio between the particles which
contribute to the mass accretion and the totality of the sticking particles. In other words, the factor accounts
for the the ratio of droplets which freeze on the surface at the same position where they hit the object and then
stick on it. The accretion eﬃciency is equal to unity when all the sticking particles freeze instantaneously, while
it tends to decrease for a larger amount of the not-solidiﬁed particles, which are partially dragged oﬀby gravity
and wind action [28]. Therefore, according to the deﬁnitions given in Section 2.1, the condition α3 = 1 identiﬁes
a ’dry-growth’, which is characterized by no surface liquid layer and run-oﬀ, and yields rime ice formation. On
the contrary, when α3 < 1 the growth is ’wet’ and a liquid layer generates on the surface, producing glaze ice.
The computation of the time-dependent ice accretion process is iterative and requires an analysis of the surface
conditions at each time step, so that to re-evaluate the variables contributing to the deﬁnition of the icing rate.
For a detailed discussion on the calculation of each correction factor and the implementation of a numerical
model describing the process, please refer to [24].
The outcome of the study is represented by the total ice mass accreted on the object in the time window under
study. According to [24], this value can be converted into an equivalent radial thickness as the change of the ice
shape with time can be neglected and a constant cylindrical geometry can be assumed as representative of the
ice growth.

2.4
Dynamic behaviour of the transmission line system
Overhead transmission line conductors are subjected to diﬀerent types of unsteady wind-induced loading and
related motions [17]. The common feature of these mechanisms is the signiﬁcant increase of their severity when
ice or wet snow accrete on the conductor [1].
Aeolian vibrations are small amplitude and high frequency oscillations induced by the pressure ﬂuctuations
produced by the vortex shedding on the leeward side of the conductor. The atmospheric ice accretion may lead
to a reduction of the self-damping capability of the structure, and thus to an ampliﬁcation of the vibrations.
This is caused by an increase in internal tensions associated to a larger weight [1]. At the same time, the ice
accreted can modify the conductor geometry, and thus the corresponding Strouhal number. This in turn yields
a variation of the oscillation frequencies, which may be not covered by the range of value which the damper is
designed for [1].
Wake-induced oscillations are small amplitude and moderate frequency motions, generally characterized by an
elliptical orbit, and associated to the downstream conductor of a bundled conﬁguration [1]. The ice accretion
results in a reduction of the ratio of the conductors spacing to their diameter. This produces an increase in the
aerodynamic forces acting on the leeward wire, which in turn causes larger oscillations of the line [1].
The need to consider the combined eﬀect of wind and atmospheric icing becomes of primary importance when
dealing with a third type of mechanism, namely the galloping. For the majority of the cases observed, this
high amplitude and low frequency (from 0.1 to 1 Hz) motion occurs on iced-accreted conductors. In contrast
with aeolian vibrations and wake-induced oscillations, which strictly induce fatigue loads, the dynamic response
produced by the galloping can be very detrimental, causing severe structural damages.
The research project conducted is exclusively focused on the galloping due to its well known relevance in the
deﬁnition of the dynamic behavior of a transmission line system and because of its close link with the atmospheric
icing accretion phenomenon.
This section provides with a physical explanation of the galloping and reviews the diﬀerent theoretical models
conceived in order to describe this type of motion. As the project is aimed at evaluating the development of
galloping instabilities on a single conductor, the case of bundled conductor is not treated in the following pages.
2.4.1
Galloping mechanism
Reporting the deﬁnition proposed by [17], the term galloping refers to a single- or multi-degree-of-freedom form
of instability related to a bluﬀbody in crosswinds. The galloping is a self-excited motion characterized by very
large amplitudes which generally occur when snow or ice accreted on conductors alter their circular shapes,
yielding aerodynamically unstable proﬁles [1]. The resulting asymmetrical geometry modiﬁes the ﬂow pattern
and the mean pressure distribution along the proﬁle, which results into a net force towards the same direction of
the conductor displacements that magniﬁes the oscillations. The motion persists and ampliﬁes as long as the net
force has the same direction of the displacements and it is larger than the combination of restoring and damping
12
==PAGE:25==
forces [1]. In other words, the total amplitude of the galloping is limited by the balance between the energy
extracted from the wind and the one dissipated in the oscillation due to the damping eﬀect [11].
Any ice accretion which asymmetrically modiﬁes the conductor proﬁle may be potentially susceptible of galloping.
However, for the majority of the cases reported, the phenomenon has been found to be related to wet snow and
freezing rain. From here it derives the consideration that the temperature at which galloping generally develops
ranges between -5◦C and 2◦C [1]. The thickness of snow or ice formation which causes the instability is usually
limited and does not exceed two times the conductor diameter (see Subsection 3.2.1).
On the other hand,
galloping can be caused by very thin formations of the order of millimetres.
Even the range of wind velocities at which the instability can develop is limited. The lower threshold depends on
the damping system and the wind turbulence, in turn related to the terrain nature. The maximum speed is mostly
dependent on the ice shape and the corresponding set of angles of attack at which the proﬁle is unstable [1].
The detailed summary of galloping reports from Japan collected in [11] has shown how the instability has been
generally observed for a 10 minutes average wind speed not greater than 25 m/s, blowing at an angle larger than
about 45◦respect to the line.
Several theories with diﬀerent levels of comprehension have been conceived to describe the galloping mechanism.
The key factor characterizing the diﬀerent methods is the number of degrees of freedom according to which the
line is modeled. The following subsections present a review and a comparison of the schemes which are employed
in this research project, namely the theories of Den Hartog and Nigol.
2.4.2
Aerodynamic analysis
The ﬁrst and most schematic vision of galloping as an aerodynamic mechanism was elaborated by Den Hartog in
1932 [9]. According to this theory the conductor is modeled as a single-degree-of-freedom (SDOF) system simply
capable of vertical motions. The oscillation is due to the ice shape and the aerodynamic characteristics of the
resulting proﬁle, other than the structural properties of the transmission line system. A further hypothesis at the
base of the scheme is that the ﬂow is considered quasi-steady, meaning that the ﬂuid force acting on the body
is exclusively dependent on the airﬂow velocity and the angle of attack1 [1]. This assumption derives from the
consideration that the oscillation frequency of the system under galloping is low compared to the wind speed.
Therefore, the ﬂow experienced by the moving body will be equal to that one seen by the same object but in a
stationary condition, identiﬁed by a modiﬁed angle of attack and relative wind speed, expression of the conductor
motion [11]. The resulting aerodynamic forces acting on the stationary body are equivalent to those acting on
the moving body. The hypothesis of stationary conditions is widely applied when explaining the principles of
the galloping phenomenon of a single ice accreted conductor as it has been proved to be fairly representative of
the actual behavior of the system. Conversely, this simpliﬁed hypothesis is not valid when dealing with bundled
conductors due to the incapability of taking into account the supplementary dynamic eﬀects of the wake [11].

shows evident limits in capturing some common cases. As previously described, the aerodynamic instability is
strictly dependent on the generation of a negative lift region, in turn related to the ice shape. While this region
may develop during the galloping of a conductor where wet snow accreted, no negative lift is generally observed
when galloping takes place on lines shaped by glaze ice [1,10].
Furthermore, as the span is only capable of vertical motions, the conductor torsion is neglected. By comparing
the cross section size of the conductor with the span length it is clear how the line cannot be rigid in torsion [11].
Counting for the ﬁnite torsional stiﬀness of the conductor would open a new range of possible instabilities which
originate when the torsional frequency approaches the vertical one (see Subsection 2.4.3). However, there is a
debate on the fact that for single conductors the combined eﬀect of wind and ice can lower down the torsional
frequency and yield a resonance condition. According to some authors, even though the wire has a ﬁnite torsional
rigidity, the coupled ﬂutter galloping cannot occur due to a change of the ice shape position respect to the statically
unstable condition [11].
Even though the limits of an aerodynamic analysis and the need of a more comprehensive method are clear,
ﬂied and experimental observations seem to prove how the most important galloping events occurring on single
conductor are driven by pure Den Hartog instability conditions. The element does not necessarily require to be
rigid respect to rotations, as these are simply forced by vertical movements [11].

2.4.3
Aeroelastic analysis
As mentioned in the previous subsection, the Den Hartog’s theory is not capable to explain the whole range of
instabilities that a overhead conductor can experience due to the restriction imposed by having modeled the span
as a SDOF system.
The range can be therefore expanded by referring to a multiple-degrees-of-freedom (MDOF) system and coupling
at least two of the possible conductor motions. The galloping cases so obtained cannot be any longer consid-
ered as simple aerodynamic instabilities, only dependent on wind speed and aerodynamic characteristics of the
proﬁle. Instead, they are deﬁned as aeroelastic instabilities as the structural properties of the system (e.g. ratio
vertical/torsional frequencies, damping, stiﬀness) may strongly aﬀect the phenomenon [11]. It has to be observed
that in contrast with the Den Hartog’s criterion, the derivative of the lift does not necessarily need to be negative
in order to achieve an unstable condition [11].

ﬂutter galloping, or simply ﬂutter galloping. In this subsection only Nigol’s theory is described as this is the one
employed in the research project. For a more comprehensive review of the numerical analyses of galloping refer,
for example, to [17].

Chapter 3
Experimental and Numerical Analysis
The chapter aims to provide with a detailed description of the tools used, the analyses performed and the
procedure followed for the study of aerodynamic instability conditions of the overhead transmission line system
under investigation.
In Section 3.1, the method applied to determine the climatic data for the site of interest is presented. In Section
3.2, a discussion on the combinations of wind and atmospheric icing loading to be investigated according to the
likelihood of galloping development is given. Furthermore, the consistency of the cases considered respect to the
available set of wind and ice data is here proved by performing a frequency analysis. Section 3.3 reports the
details of the wind tunnel test conducted, aimed at determining the aerodynamic characteristics of the diﬀerent
loads combinations, and more generically to enhance understanding of the inﬂuence of proﬁle geometry and air
ﬂow properties on the results. In Section 3.4, the CFD model conceived as alternative means for the aerodynamic
coeﬃcients computation, and prove of the wind tunnel test reliability, is described. Section 3.5 provides with
the steps followed in the calculation of the static and dynamic loads transferred from the conductor to the
supporting towers. Lastly, Section 3.6 presents the risk analysis performed on the basis of the information about
the frequency of occurrence and the impact of the diﬀerent loads combinations investigated trough the study.
3.1
Climatic data collection
The climatic data used for the project were provided by the Norwegian company Kjeller Vindteknikk [19]. Both
wind and ice loads were calculated on the basis of the mesoscale WRF (Weather Research and Forecasting)
model, which is a numerical weather prediction and atmospheric simulation system intended to be used for both
operational forecasting and research purposes [35].
For a given set of input data - geographical and meteorological data - and lateral boundary conditions, the WRF
model is capable to solve the coupled equations related to the main physical processes (e.g. wind speed and
direction, temperature, liquid water content etc.) [19].
Two diﬀerent model scales were used to calculate the weather loads. A coarser scale with a grid 4 km x 4 km wide
and a temporal resolution of 1 hour was ﬁrst employed for the reference 20 years-observation period 1995-2015.
A second and ﬁner scale with a grid 500 m x 500 m wide, and same resolution of the previous one, was then
used to simulate the atmospheric conditions of three limited time windows during the year 2015. This latter
model (WRF500m) was aimed at correcting, scaling and rotating the wind speeds and directions obtained in the
former dataset (WRF4km) according to the eﬀect that local and regional orography produce on these physical
parameters [19]. A representation of the two models is given in Figure 3.1.1.

3.2
Parametric analysis
The investigation of aerodynamic instabilities of the transmission line system was based on the application of
diﬀerent combinations of atmospheric icing and wind loads. The preliminary study, therefore, concerned the
designing of a parametric analysis which comprehended both the types of loads involved. A variation of the
atmospheric icing load was obtained by modifying the ice proﬁle accreted on the conductor, while a variation
of the wind load was achieved by considering diﬀerent air ﬂow conditions, primarily deﬁned by the wind speed
and the angle of attack. The decision on the reference values to be adopted for the analysis was derived by a
combined examination of the available climatic dataset and the load conditions more prone to yield galloping
according to the literature. In the following subsections the loads to be considered are presented and discussed.
3.2.1
Atmospheric icing load
Several atmospheric icing loads were considered by referring to diﬀerent ice proﬁles. An ice proﬁle was identiﬁed
as the resulting geometry obtained through the combination of ice shape and its eccentricity, which is given by
the ratio of the maximum ice thickness to the diameter of the bare conductor. It has to be observed that the
term ice is here intended to denote both the cases of wet snow and in-cloud icing.
The necessity of determining reference ice proﬁles was based on the consideration that the climatic dataset
available contained information exclusively about the mass accreted under diﬀerent atmospheric icing conditions.
No details, instead, were speciﬁed on the geometry produced by the ice when accreting on the line. These data
were not relevant for the designing of the system according to the International Standard, which prescribes to
assume a constant radial ice formation. In contrast with this hypothesis, the mass accumulated on the conductor
was redistributed according to typical asymmetrical snow and ice shapes collected from the literature, and based
on ﬁeld and experimental observations of diﬀerent icing events. It can be argued that referring the analysis to
standard proﬁles not directly related to the case of study is still a speculation of the physical phenomenon of
the ice accretion. However, it has to be noted that the large amount of data available and their variability over
the time period observed make it very complicated to conceive a model capable to process this information and
reproduce the most likely ice geometries. From here the need to ﬁnd a more general solution, which can also be
replicated in future studies.
As for the shape, even the evaluation of the eccentricities to be investigated was conducted by referring to the
literature. The main objective here was to determine an upper limit value of eccentricity beyond which the
galloping is less likelihood to develop. This information was compared to the reference climatic dataset in order
to understand if the eccentricity threshold considered had a physical meaning for the case of study.
In the following pages the relevant information about the deﬁnition of shapes and eccentricities to be analyzed
is given.

3.2.1.2
Ice eccentricity
According to the 124 cases of galloping observed in Japan and reported in [11], the maximum eccentricity of a
crescent shape at which instabilities were recorded has been equal to 2. This upper threshold may be explained by
two diﬀerent considerations. Thicker formations lead to an increase in weight of one side of the proﬁle, which, in
relation to its torsional stiﬀness, starts rotating and accumulating ice on the former leeward side. The conductor
torsion may eventually yield a cylindrical shape not any longer prone to gallop [1]. At the same time it has to
be observed that the adhesion of snow and ice tends to decrease for larger deposits and the mass accreted starts
breaking up under the eﬀect of wind pressure.
The upper limit of eccentricity for the parametric analysis performed was therefore set equal to 2 in agreement
with the ﬁndings. In total ﬁve diﬀerent eccentricity values were considered. The ﬁnal proﬁles accomplished as
combination of the selected shape and eccentricities, as well as the corresponding nomenclature, are reported in

3.3.1
Experiment setup
The experiment conducted was a static force balance test. The model was vertically mounted and connected to
the balance through a transfer plate at its base, where drag, lift and torque moment were recorded. Figure 3.3.3
shows the model setup in the wind tunnel facility. The dynamic behavior of the transmission line was therefore not
investigated during the test. This simpliﬁed method is generally used when studying the aerodynamic properties
of a single ice accreted conductor and is based on the hypothesis of a quasi-steady ﬂow condition (see Subsection
2.4.2). Its convenience should be sought in the limited number of parameters involved in the analysis. Geometry
of the iced conductor and air ﬂow properties need to be correctly set, while the actual structural characteristics
of the line do not require to be matched in the physical simulation.
The test was carried out in full scale 1:1. A section of the bare conductor was reproduced by an aluminum
cylindrical tube having the actual diameter of the line studied and a length of 0.457 m (18 in). This length
was decided according to the need of limiting the bending moment transferred from the model, which behaves
as a cantilever beam, to the balance, so that to avoid its overloading. The choice of the material to be used is
33
==PAGE:46==
extensively explained in Subsection 3.3.3.
The diﬀerent ice proﬁles conceived were modeled by foam. Shapes were obtained by means of a high precision
hot wire foam cutting machine by FROG3D. This processes the input geometries data and transfers them as a
motion signal for the incandescent wire which cuts the foam. A representation of the cutting process is given
in Figure 3.3.4, while Figure 3.3.5 shows the application of the shaped foam on the bare tube by using masking
tape.
Figure 3.3.3: Model setup.
34
==PAGE:47==
Figure 3.3.4: Hot wire foam cutting machine.
Figure 3.3.5: Application of shaped foam on bare tube.
The experiment setting was designed in order to fulﬁll a main priority, namely the need of working with a
two-dimensional ﬂow, ideally representative of the condition which characterizes the whole span throughout its
length. The objective was achieved by applying two complementary strategies.
The ﬁrst action dealt with the attempt of creating a uniform ﬂow condition, with constant properties along
the height of the conductor.
The solution applied in order to achieve this state was to elevate the model -
centered on the turntable - by using a 31 cm (12 in) height metal ring. According to the wind proﬁles provided
by the company responsible for the facility, this distance from the ground should have been suﬃcient to avoid
the inﬂuence of the boundary layer for the tested exposures (see Subsection 3.3.4). In order to facilitate the
realization of the desired free stream velocity at the model height, all the roughness elements were removed from
the ﬂoor of the facility.
The second methodology applied was aimed at minimizing the interference eﬀects produced at the edges of the
model due to ﬂow separation. As described in [45], the wall disturbance extends from the ends of the cylinder
to 15 times its diameter. Beyond this distance, a region with constant base pressure originates. From here, it
derives that no corrective actions need to be undertaken in order to achieve a central region where a 2-D ﬂow
condition develops only if the aspect ratio length/diameter of the model is greater than 30. This is not valid for
the case of study, where the ratio is approximately of 21. A solution to the problem, as suggested in [45], was
found by installing plates at the two ends of the model. The employment of the plates is proved to signiﬁcantly
reduce the wall interference extension, limiting it to 3.5d from the ends [45]. Therefore this measure enables to
achieve a central region with constant pressure distribution at each section even for aspect ratios smaller than
30. The length of the two-dimensional ﬂow condition is linearly proportional to the aspect ratio, and it is equal
to 33.5 cm for the model conceived. While the bottom plate was directly connected to the cylinder and the
balance, the top plate was slightly separated from the model. The small gap so generated ensured that the
wind loads experienced by the plate were not transferred through the model to the balance, compromising the
results. For the same reason, the plate was built so that to be suﬃciently stiﬀto not oscillate and touch the
model during the tests. The dimension of the end plates was decided by considering the fact that too large or
too small elements may negatively aﬀect the results. Too large plates would be responsible of the generation of a
signiﬁcant boundary layer, while too small ones would not be capable to conﬁne the horseshoe vortex developed
at the intersection between wall and model [45]. The plates adopted were circular aluminum elements with a
diameter of 56 cm. Figure 3.3.3 shows the setting of the model as above described.

all the diﬀerent conﬁgurations, but also for each angle of attack investigated as the distribution of pressures along
the proﬁle varies. It can be therefore concluded that the aerodynamic forces obtained by wind tunnel testing and
acting on the geometric center of the bare conductor represent only a good approximation of the actual values
of drag and lift.

3.3.3
Lock-in eﬀect analysis
The choice of the material for the physical model was led by the need of avoiding lock-in phenomena, namely
resonance conditions between the main peak of the vortex shedding frequency and the fundamental natural
frequency of the model. If the vortex shedding hits the eigenfrequency of the conductor, signiﬁcant and potentially
damaging vibrations, known as vortex induced vibrations, take place. In this case, the motion of the model would
govern the behavior of the system, and thus the vortex shedding itself. The Von Karman street would not change
by further increasing the wind speed and the corresponding frequency would be locked at the resonant value.
Furthermore, the oscillations induced in this condition would produce inertia loads at the base of the model with
magnitude of the same order of drag and lift. Hence, the measurement of the aerodynamic loads recorded by the
balance would be totally compromised.
The reasons just mentioned explain the need of ensuring that for all the diﬀerent conﬁgurations tested the
eigenfrequencies keep out of the spectrum of vortex shedding frequency peaks, and above it.
The goal was
achieved by using a material like aluminum, which provides with stiﬀness and lightness at the same time. The
necessity of these structural properties may be explained by schematizing the model as a single degree of freedom
system with concentrated mass at the midpoint, as represented in Figure 3.3.14.

3.4
Fluid dynamic simulation
Fluid dynamic simulations represent an alternative method to wind tunnel testing which can be employed when
dealing with the analysis of the aerodynamic characteristics of a body. The aim of the numerical study con-
ducted was therefore to conceive a model capable to reproduce the same conditions tested in the laboratory, and
compare, and possibly validate, the resulting aerodynamic coeﬃcients with those previously determined trough
the experiment conducted. The congruence in the results would reciprocally prove the reliability of the two
methodologies adopted.
In this section, the computational ﬂuid dynamics principles considered and the procedure followed in relation to
the design of the model are widely described and discussed. Eventually, the issues faced during the study are
mentioned.
3.4.1

The ﬂow behaviour in its interaction with a body can be characterized on the basis of the Reynolds number,
Re, which is a reference dimensionless quantity representing the ratio of inertial to viscous forces, as given in
Equation 3.4.1.

The Reynolds number is generally used in ﬂuid mechanics in order to address a ﬂow as laminar or turbulent. The

and disorganized. A turbulent ﬂow is characterized by stochastic velocity ﬂuctuations in both magnitude and
direction [28].

3.4.3
Computational approach
Diﬀerent computational approaches can be used in order to numerically solve the Navier-Stokes equations. The
Direct Numerical Simulation (DNS) is theoretically capable to simulate all turbulent ﬂows by solving the full
Navier-Stokes equations [52]. The DNS resolves the whole spatial and temporal spectrum of turbulence scales [28].
However, the range of applicability of the approach is limited to small Reynolds numbers [28], and even in this
case the computational cost is prohibitive. Hence, the prediction method is not practical for industrial ﬂows and
not even available in commercial software.
An alternative and less computational demanding approach is the Large Eddy Simulation (LES), which solves
the spatially averaged Navier-Stokes equations [52]. The strategy adopted is to reduce the numerical eﬀort by
49
==PAGE:62==
modelling eddies smaller than the mesh size, while large eddies are still directly solved [52].
The simpliﬁed
approach is based on the consideration that the most of the kinetic energy is transported by the larger turbulent
scales, while smaller scales present a more dissipative nature [28]. Although this assumption reduce the overall cost
of the simulations, the resources required by the LES are still too high for the majority of practical applications.
The need to reduce the complexity of the simulations, and thus the computational demand, has conﬁgured
the Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) Equations Model as the most widely used for the analysis of
industrial ﬂows. The method deals with the solution of ensemble-averaged Navier-Stokes equations [52] and
with the modeling of all the eddies. A schematic view of how the turbulence scales are treated by the diﬀerent
approaches described is given in Figure 3.4.2.

dimension of the proﬁle studied (D=23.55 mm); the adoption of this solution could not ensure reliable results.
Hence, the necessity to apply the Low Reynolds Number strategy and resolve the viscous sublayer by designing
a ﬁne mesh.

3.4.8
Fluent setup
The simulation setup was deﬁned in ANSYS Fluent through a series of several items, as described below.
3.4.8.1
Transient simulation
The non-stationary ﬂow condition characterizing the problem and the need to accurately predict the vortex
shedding behavior, so that to properly determine the mean aerodynamic characteristics of the body, yielded the
choice of performing transient simulations. The prediction method actually adopted was therefore the URANS,
rather than the simple RANS, where the letter U stands for unsteady. The substantial diﬀerence respect to a
steady simulation consists in the introduction of a time derivative term in the governing equations, which leads
to a solution dependence on the marching time.
3.4.8.2
Turbulence model
Among the diﬀerent turbulence models available in Fluent to compute µT , the k −ω was applied in the case
of study. The model was selected for its accuracy and robustness proved in a wide range of applications where
boundary layer ﬂows with consistent pressure gradient develop [52]. The transported variable k represents the
turbulent kinetic energy and counts for the energy in the turbulence, while the variable ω deﬁnes the speciﬁc
dissipation through which the scale of the turbulence is determined. In particular, the shear stress transport
(SST) formulation of the model was applied. The advantage of employing this formulation lies in the possibility
of directly applying it for solving the viscous sublayer (see Subsection 3.4.4) without the necessity of an additional
damping function. On top of that, the k −ω SST model has the ability to turn into a k −ε model in the region
of the domain far from the walls, where a free stream condition develops. This feature overcomes the too large
sensitivity to the turbulence characteristics of the inlet, which is proper of a standard k −ω model [61].
Because of the range of Reynolds numbers which characterize the problem (see Subsection 3.4.1), the Low-Re
correction option was selected and the model constants were kept as their default values.
3.4.8.3

3.4.8.5
Method and controls
The Navier-Stokes equation were solved by using the default pressure-velocity coupling scheme, namely SIMPLE
(Semi-Implicit Method for Pressure-Linked Equations). An overview of the steps included in the algorithm can
be found in [62].
For the spatial discretization of the gradient, the Least Squares cell based (LSCB) method was chosen because
of its high accuracy and eﬃciency. The method calculates the gradient at the center of each cell by applying the
least squares approximation [63]. The solution so found is supposed to vary linearly along the distance which
separates the centers of the neighboring cells.
A second order upwind discretization was selected for pressure, momentum, turbulent kinetic energy and speciﬁc
dissipation rate, while a ﬁrst order implicit formulation was considered to be suﬃcient as transient formulation
for the nature of the problem studied.
In the solution controls section, no changes were introduced to the default under-relaxation factors as no conver-
gence issues were experienced.
3.4.8.6
Monitors
Monitors give an understanding of the solution convergence. In the case of study, they were represented by
the residuals of the ﬁve equations solving the problem, the mass ﬂow rate between inlet and outlet, and the
aerodynamic coeﬃcients.
The convergence criterion related to the residuals was set to 10−6 for all of them. The achievement of this value
for the diﬀerent contributions represented the necessary condition for the simulation to pass to the next time
step.
Regarding the mass ﬂow rate, a value constantly null for this quantity had to be considered as an indicator that
the boundary conditions were correctly speciﬁed.
In contrast with the case of steady simulations, where the convergence of the solution can be proved by the
accomplishment of constant values for the variables investigated, for transient simulations this condition is not
expected to occur due to the nature of the ﬂow. Instead, the convergence is here obtained when the ﬂuctuations
of the quantities studied, namely the aerodynamic characteristics of the proﬁle, become stable with the time.
3.4.8.7
Time step and loading time
The time step for the evaluation of the solution was determined by considering the need of balancing results
accuracy and computational demand. As the main objective was to capture the pressure ﬂuctuations on the
downwind side of the body, related to the Von Karman street, the time step was calculated by referring to the
values of the main peak vortex shedding frequencies found through the experiment. A conservative assumption
was made and a reference frequency f=100 Hz was considered as representative for all the cases of study. Hence,
the corresponding time of a cycle shedding was found to be T = 1/f = 0.01 s.
56
==PAGE:69==
The recommended minimum number of time steps in one shedding cycle in order to properly capture the vortex
behaviour is equal to 20-25 [64]. Once again, a conservative decision was taken and the number of time steps
evaluating the solution within each shedding cycle was set to 100. From here, the time step size was calculated
and inserted in Fluent, t = T/Nstep = 0.0001 s.
Besides the time step size, the maximum allowed number of iterations per time step had to be speciﬁed. The
upper threshold was selected to be 100. However, it has to be noted that if the convergence is achieved for a
number of iterations lower than the maximum allowed, the simulation passes to the next time step without using
all the available iterations. This was veriﬁed for all the conﬁgurations tested, where after an initial condition
requiring a considerable amount of iterations, the solution started converging already after 10-20 iterations.
As for the physical simulation (see Subsection 3.3.2), even for the numerical simulation the loading time was
determined by evaluating the time needed to the cumulative moving average of the coeﬃcients to reach the
convergence. In general, it was observed that a time window of 1 s was suﬃcient to achieve stable mean values
of CD, CL and CM.

3.4.11
General remarks
According to the original plan, the numerical model should have been applied to all the cases investigated
trough the wind tunnel test. Additionally, once the agreement between the two methodologies was proved, the
CFD investigation should have been extended to the study of higher wind speeds not performed during the
experiment, namely U=15 m/s and U=20 m/s. In this way, all the combinations of wind and ice eccentricity
initially considered would have been tested and the parametric analysis conceived would have been completed.
However, this objective could not be accomplished within the available time window of the project. Two diﬀerent
sources of problems may be identiﬁed as the main responsible for the only partial completion of the study.
The ﬁrst issue is related to the nature of the ﬂow dealt with; the development of vortex shedding on the downwind
side of the body made necessary the performing of transient simulations. The combination of a small time step,
required to correctly evaluate the pressure ﬂuctuations, and the need of an adequately long loading time, so
that to achieve stable mean coeﬃcient values, led to a considerable large computational demand and unfavorable
conditions for such a comprehensive multiple analysis.
The second problem, somehow related to the previous one, was represented by the lack of a suﬃciently large
computational power. The whole study was carried out by exclusively using a HP Envy Notebook, rather than a
dedicated PC. This resulted in unfeasible conditions in terms of computational time: as an average, 10 to 12 hours
of running simulations were necessary to reproduce only 1 second of physical time. Hence, considering that at
least 19 angles should have been investigated for each loads combination, approximately 10 days of uninterrupted
simulations were required for a single case.
Due to the complications above mentioned, the numerical study was limited to only one case, namely the analysis
of the proﬁle e100 subjected to wind speed of U=10 m/s in exposure 1, according to the description given in
Subsection 3.3.4.

3.5
Static and dynamic analysis of the transmission line system
The ultimate objective of the Project was to provide with a solid comparison between static and dynamic loads
transferred from the iced conductor to the related supporting tower. For this reason, both these types of loads
had to be computed. Diﬀerent approaches were applied for the two investigations, the ﬁrst one in line with the
International Standard’s provisions, while the second one referred to the linear theory of cable structures. It has
to be noted that the analysis conducted was only focused on determining the horizontal components of the loads,
as it can be considered the main responsible for fatigue issues and structural damages of the line.
In the next subsections the procedure followed for the static and dynamic analysis conducted is described.
3.5.1
Static loads
The static analysis was performed by referring to the guideline traced by the International Electrotechnical
Commission (IEC) [22] for the design of overhead transmission line systems, as reported in Section 2.3.
The resulting horizontal static load can be seen as the vectorial sum of two contributions:
• The eﬀect of the wind pressure on the iced conductor of wind span length Lw, applied at the supporting
system and acting perpendicular to the line direction
• The horizontal component of the tension transferred to the towers due to the uniform gravity load of the
iced conductor, acting parallel to the line direction
As introduced in Section 2.3, starting from the assumption that the contemporary action of extreme wind
and atmospheric icing conditions is unlikely, diﬀerent loads combinations were considered. The low probability
value of the extreme quantity corresponded to the one obtained for the reference return period of Tr=50 years,
while the high probability value of the other quantity was represented by the average of its yearly maximum
value. The extreme values of wind speed and atmospheric icing for the reference level of reliability were given in
the report provided by the company Kjeller Vindteknikk [19]. The wind load referred to a 10-minute mean value.
Regarding the ice load, two diﬀerent values were provided for the cases of in-cloud icing and wet snow accretion,
respectively. Hence, the need of treating separately these information and performing two parallel analyses.

3.5.2
Dynamic loads
The analysis of the dynamic behaviour of transmission line system under galloping was limited to the investi-
gation of vertical instability conditions as explained by the Den Hartog’s theory. This choice derives from the
consideration that vertical oscillations are the most frequent and detrimental type of motions which may develop
for a single conductor, as in the case of study. Even if a torsional component existed, this would have been just
forced by the vertical movement of the span [11].
In order to determine the contribution of horizontal tension increment produced by the galloping, ﬁrst the dynamic
properties of the line had to be investigated. The eigenfrequencies and eigenvectors of the system considered,
therefore, were computed by referring to the linear theory of cable free vibrations, as presented in Subsection
2.4.4. Theoretically, the structure has inﬁnite vibrating modes corresponding to inﬁnite natural frequencies.
The analysis performed, however, referred to the ﬁrst four ones, which are able to describe the totality of the
structural dynamic response, as it will be proven.
The eigenfrequencies and eigenvectors related to the asymmetric modes were directly computed by referring to
Equation 2.4.32 and Equation 2.4.33, respectively. In line with the assumption presented in [11], the horizontal
component H of the static tension was referred to the case of bare conductor. The value, evaluated by applying
Equation 3.5.1, was therefore constant for all the analyses of diﬀerent ice conditions tested. The consideration
at the base of this hypothesis is that the ice mass on the line is low during galloping events if compared to the
weight of the conductor itself; from here, the decision to neglect the contribution. For the same reasons, the mass
m used for the computation of eigenfrequencies and eigenvectors was assumed to be the mass per unit length of
the bare conductor.

4.1
Analysis of the ﬂow
As mentioned in Section 3.3, three diﬀerent input voltage values were applied with the aim of producing, and
subsequently comparing, diﬀerent wind speeds. The results here presented only refers to the highest voltage
used, equal to 9 V, which is also the reference one, applied to the majority of the cases run. Instead, the results
corresponding to lower input voltages, namely 5 V and 7 V, are given in Appendix A. Figure 4.1.1 and Figure
4.1.2 report the air ﬂow properties, in terms of wind speed and turbulence characteristics, for the two diﬀerent
types of exposures applied during the experiment. The values are presented in the form of average over the time
series loaded. The reference wind speed for the aerodynamic analysis is represented by the mean value computed
from the pitot tube observations, highlighted with a green dot in the plots. Nevertheless, in order to enhance
understanding of the type of wind proﬁles obtained in the physical simulation, the mean resulting velocities from
the data loaded by the cobra probes are shown as well. Regarding the turbulence characteristics, expressed by
turbulence intensity and length scale, only the information derived from the cobra probes time series are given.
As a general remark, it has to be noted that, in order to properly characterize the turbulence behavior along the
model height, wind data should have been loaded for a larger number of intermediate positions. However, the
results so accomplished were suﬃcient for the purpose of the study, namely to obtain information on the scale of
the reference quantities and determine their mean value at the mid height of the model, which were required as
an input in the numerical simulation.
By looking at the wind speed plots of both Figure 4.1.1 and Figure 4.1.2, it can be observed how the only
component of the ﬂow is the stream wise one, as expected, and no additional lateral and vertical contributions
interfere with the results. Moreover, by comparing the trend related to the cobra probes data with the pitot tube
results, it can be seen how there is a total equivalence in the mean wind velocity at the mid height between the
two measurements.
Focusing on Figure 4.1.1, it has to be noticed how a discrepancy between the resulting trends of wind speed
and turbulence intensity and the data provided by the facility manager (see Figure 3.3.20) was detected. In
contrast with the expectations, the wind velocity was found to be not completely uniform. Instead, it increases
with the model height, meaning that the attempt of placing the model above the boundary layer partially failed.
Additionally, the turbulence intensity along the stream wise direction resulted to be still signiﬁcant at the model
height, rather than being minimum. The value observed at the mid height was equal to i=5.5%. The causes
which yielded this result may be multiple. The main sources might be represented by the fact that the data

Aerodynamic analysis
For each of the loads combination tested during the experiment, the aerodynamic coeﬃcients, as well as the

4.2.1
Ice eccentricity variation
As presented in Subsection 3.3.4, all the diﬀerent ice proﬁles considered were tested at the velocity U=10 m/s
under the ﬁrst type of exposure; the eﬀect of the ice eccentricity on the aerodynamic characteristics is therefore
referred to this air ﬂow condition. Figure 4.2.1 shows the results from the comparison of the drag and lift trends
among the diﬀerent proﬁles, Figure 4.2.2 reports the vertical instability analysis according to the Den Hartog’s
criterion, while Figure 4.2.3 collects the information on the moment coeﬃcient trends and the corresponding
torsional instability analysis, as explained by Nigol.
From the comparison of CD and CL given in Figure 4.2.1, it is evident how a common trend of these aerodynamic
coeﬃcients with the angles of attack is respected by all the diﬀerent proﬁles tested. The drag coeﬃcient presents
a symmetric bell-shape distribution, with minimum values around 0◦and 180◦, and the peak situated around
AoA=90◦. This trend can be easily explained by considering that the surface of the model directly exposed
to the wind, and thus also CD, tends to progressively increase with the longitudinal axis of the proﬁle moving
towards a direction perpendicular to the ﬂow. For angles larger than 90◦, the surface exposed reduces, and so
does the drag coeﬃcient. Instead, the lift predominately follows a sinusoidal trend with minimum absolute values

e200 the resulting CL at the extreme angles diﬀer from the other cases, achieving a value of 1 and -1 at 0◦and
180◦, respectively. This discrepancy may be explained as an instrument error; the balance tends to become more
sensitive for larger proﬁles.

4.3
Analysis of vortex shedding
The behaviour of the vortex shedding with the angle of attack is here discussed. Even though this is a secondary
analysis, not strictly related to the primary objective of the Project, it is pivotal to conduct it for two main
reasons. The observation of a vortex shedding frequency peak reaction to the change of the AoA represents
the prove that lock-in eﬀects did not take place during the experiment. Additionally, the analysis of the vortex
shedding frequencies allows the computation of the related Strouhal number St, which can be compared with
the corresponding theoretical value to check the accuracy of the physical simulations performed and the related
results. For the case of study, the aim was to verify that St was constantly equal to 0.2, independently on the
changes in the parameters involved in the analysis (AoA, ice eccentricity, wind speed and type of exposure). This
is the theoretical value related to a crescent shape.
As discussed in Subsection 3.3.4, the vortex shedding analysis was limited to the observations of drag and lift for
the proﬁles e50 and e100 and involved the computation of the main peak of vortex shedding frequency and the
calculation of the Strouhal number. A similar results comparison to the one just presented for the aerodynamic
coeﬃcients was carried out. The analysis, however, is now limited to the range of angles 20◦-90◦. The upper
threshold derives from the consideration that the frequency trend is symmetrical for angles larger than 90◦. On
the other hand, the lower limit is due to the fact that for angles smaller than 20◦it was not possible to identify
the main frequency peak of the vortex shedding in the power spectral density (PSD), especially in relation to
the drag, which is subjected to lower intensity ﬂuctuations compared to the lift, as shown in the following pages.
Variations in the ranges of the results presented respect to these limits are explained by the reason just mentioned.
4.3.1
Ice eccentricity variation
The results from the comparison of the main peak of the vortex shedding frequency and St for diﬀerent ice
eccentricities tested in exposure 1 at U=10 m/s are reported in Figure 4.3.1 and 4.3.2, respectively.

of the main vortex shedding frequency peak conducted on the basis of the PSD calculation is given in Appendix
A (A.3.1- A.3.4) for the two proﬁles e50 and e100.
From the analysis of the available results it can be observed that, as expected, a decay of the main frequency
peak is experienced when increasing the angle of attack, and thus the cross-wind width of the iced conductor.
For the same reason, the frequencies corresponding to the larger proﬁle e100 lie on lower values compared to
those obtained for e50. The distance in the resulting frequency tend to enlarge at higher angles, where also the
gap between the characteristic dimension of the two proﬁles is bigger.
Figure 4.3.1 shows how the experimental results are further compared with the theoretical ones. The expected
frequency trends were determined by applying Equation 3.3.7 and specifying the wind speed as the mean value
derived from the time series loaded during the tests. A good correlation in the frequency trends is observed

Nevertheless, no such a problem is observed in the trend related to the lift. Therefore a resonance condition did
not take place, and the observation could not ﬁnd an explanation.
The consistency of the experimental results with the theory is conﬁrmed by observing the variation of the Strouhal
number with the AoA for two proﬁles, given in Figure 4.3.1. The St trend constantly lies on values close to the
theoretical number related to the crescent shape.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
This ﬁnal chapter of the Thesis is aimed at summarizing the major ﬁndings of the study and giving an outlook
of what could be the main directions towards which to orientate a further development of this work.
5.1
Summary
From the analysis of the climatic dataset describing wind and atmospheric icing loads - assumed to grow accord-
ing to a crescent shape - experienced by the line over a period of 20 years, it was found that the most favourable
conditions for the development of galloping were attributed to wet snow events. The lower rate of circumstances
in which the galloping is associated to rime ice, instead, is explained by the larger mass accreted on the conductor
during this type of natural event, which more easily exceeds the upper threshold generally detectable during the
instability phenomenon. This result is in agreement with ﬁeld observations of galloping [11].
From the investigation of the eﬀects produced by a variation of the ice proﬁle on the aerodynamic properties
of the conductor, it was deduced that the trends of drag, moment and lift coeﬃcients - given as a function of
the angle of attack - become steeper in correspondence to their peak values when increasing the ice eccentricity.
Hence, the thicker the ice accumulation, and the more likely the vertical and torsional galloping, as explained by
Den Hartog’s and Nigol’s theory respectively, to arise.
The examination of the eﬀect produced by modifying the wind speed on a given proﬁle highlighted how only the
lift trend signiﬁcantly reacts to an increase in the velocity by rising more rapidly and reaching larger maximum
values at its peaks. The inﬂuence of the wind speed variation on the CL trend is more pronounced for larger ice
eccentricity and lower turbulence intensity. Conversely, the CD trend is not subjected to relevant variations with
a change in the Re number. By combining this information in the study of the Den Hartog’s instability, it could
be concluded that vertical galloping is more likely to happen at small angles of attack for larger wind speeds.
The same conclusion is valid even at high angles of attack if referring to the case of exposure 1, while it is not
applicable to the second exposure for which no changes in CL were here observed. The result so accomplished
clearly proves that the problem studied is Re-dependent, as the impact of a wind speed variation on development
of aerodynamic instabilities may be crucial. A generalization of the outcome from the comparison of moment
coeﬃcient trends is not possible. While for exposure 2 similar conclusions to those presented for CL can be
drawn, in case of exposure 1 there is no evidence of a remarkable eﬀect of a wind speed increase on the resulting
CM trend, and thus on the corresponding torsional instability conditions.
The study of the inﬂuence of the exposure type on the aerodynamic characteristics of the conductor proved that
the peak values of CD tend to enlarge at higher turbulence intensities. Concurrently, a decrease in the trend
steepness for CL and CM is experienced at small angles of attack when increasing the turbulence intensity. The
eﬀect of the exposure variation on the trends is more evident at lower wind speeds and larger ice eccentricities.
It could be therefore concluded that an increase in the turbulence intensity of the ﬂow yields a reduction of the
likelihood of occurrence of both vertical and torsional galloping. This eﬀect was explained by considering that
larger turbulence intensity could lead to a decrease in pressure ﬂuctuations produced on the leeward side of the
body. In order to verify the soundness of the conclusion drawn, however, it might be necessary to perform a
further test by employing a third value of turbulence intensity and adopting a wind proﬁle consistent with one
of those already used.
From the observation of all the loads combinations tested, two common ranges of angles of attack within which
96
==PAGE:109==
vertical galloping may occur were identiﬁed, namely the intervals 15◦-30◦and 165◦-180◦; these limits slightly vary
according to the case considered. The ﬁrst range so speciﬁed was found to be also representative of the interval
of angles within which the torsional galloping can develop. Conversely, at high AoA, a shift of the second range
towards lower values was experienced compared to the vertical instability; the galloping as explained by Nigol is
more prone to take place in the range 160◦-170◦. If considering that during an icing event the conductor might
slightly rotate under the gravity load produced by the ice mass accretion, the likelihood that the angle of attack
falls within the ranges 15◦-30◦or 160◦-180◦- and thus that favorable aerodynamic instability conditions develop
- is remarkably high. This consideration underlines the importance of carrying out an aerodynamic analysis as
the one performed.
The application of the parametric study to the analysis of the vortex shedding behaviour, given as function of the
angle of attack, yielded the conclusion that a decay of the main frequency peak is produced by an increase in the
ice eccentricity; the discrepancy in the resulting values tend to rise at larger AoA as consequence of the increasing
diﬀerence in the cross-wind width of the body. Similarly, a reduction in the frequency peaks is observed when
decreasing the wind speed. The trends corresponding to smaller Re number becomes ﬂatter and the variations
in the results between diﬀerent ice eccentricities tend to nullify. While the frequency range modiﬁes with the
velocity, the distribution of the energy carried by the vortex at diﬀerent AoA is not altered; the energy, however,
increase in magnitude with the wind speed. The comparison of the resulting trends achieved for diﬀerent expo-
sure types highlighted how a delay in the vortex shedding behaviour with the angle of attack is experienced at
larger turbulence intensity: the Von Karman street begins at higher angles and a shift of the energy distribution
takes place. The reliability of the results accomplished was proved by the calculation of the Strouhal number
for diﬀerent conﬁgurations tested, which was found to be constantly in the range of 0.2, as suggested by the
literature for the case of crescent shape.
The comparison between the aerodynamic coeﬃcients trends and vortex shedding behaviour between the wind
tunnel test and CFD analysis was restricted to the unique loads combination which was possible to numerically
simulate within the time window of the Project. Even though further simulations and subsequent comparisons
need to be performed in order to generalize the conclusions, the results obtained through the numerical solu-
tion are largely consistent with the corresponding experimental ones. The small discrepancies between the two
solutions observed in the lift and moment coeﬃcient at their ﬁrst peak might be to some extent related to the
non-uniformity of the exposure adopted in the wind tunnel facility. For this reason the comparison of the two
methodologies should be extended to the second exposure, which was proved to produce a uniform ﬂow condition.
From the dynamic analysis performed it was possible to conclude that a variation in the ice eccentricity mainly
aﬀects the structural response at small angles of attack: the vertical motions of the line induced by galloping,
and thus the corresponding horizontal tension increment, tend here to increase with the ice mass accreted on the
conductor. Conversely, a generalized trend is not traceable at high angles, where such dependency is not clear.
As for the ice eccentricity, even the wind load eﬀects on the vertical galloping are more signiﬁcant within the
ﬁrst range at which instabilities occur and yields larger responses at higher velocities applied.
The general increase in the galloping signiﬁcance with ice eccentricity and wind speed highlighted how the choice
of having identiﬁed each of the subsets of climatic data proposed to their upper limit (e.g. all the wind speeds
within the range 5 m/s< U <10 m/s are interpreted as U=10 m/s) represented a conservative choice in relation
to the deﬁnition of the dynamic response of the system.
The risk assessment performed evidenced how the majority of the loads combinations investigated are not negli-
gible in terms of occurrence and impact on the transmission line system under study, potentially yielding severe
fatigue issues. Therefore, control methods should be employed accordingly with the aim of limiting or preventing
the galloping of the line.
5.2
Outlook
Several future studies may be conducted on the basis of this Project in relation to both the general understanding
of the physical problem dealt with and the particular case of interest.
It has been underlined how, due to the limits of available time and resources, it was not possible to investigate all
the combinations of wind and atmospheric icing loads proposed neither by means of physical or numerical simu-
lations. Therefore, the natural next step of this work would involve the extension of the study to the remaining
cases which were speciﬁed in the parametric analysis conceived but were not tested. A more comprehensive com-
parison of the results would enhance understanding of the eﬀect of each parameter involved on the aerodynamic
properties of the conductor, and thus on the development of instability conditions. Moreover, the development
97
==PAGE:110==
of the work towards this direction would provide with a better overview of the potentially most detrimental loads
combinations for the particular transmission line system examined. The same methodology designed for the
analysis of a single span could be eventually applied to the other spans of the Long Lake transmission line system
with the aim of achieving an overall picture of the risk associated to the occurrence of vertical galloping. From
here, the most suitable control method to be employed with the aim of reducing the eﬀects of this phenomenon
might be examined.
It has been discussed how the large computational demand associated to the CFD simulations restricted the
comparison of the aerodynamic characteristics of the conductor with the wind tunnel test outcome to a unique
loads combination. Therefore, an important research project to be conducted would deal with the an extension
of the numerical simulations to all the conﬁgurations already investigated by means of the physical experiment.
The comparison of the aerodynamic properties for the diﬀerent cases should reciprocally prove the reliability of
the two methods, obtained through the results match, but it could also point out some general discrepancies
between the two tools employed.
An alternative, or additional, study could also deal with the repetition of the same investigation carried out but
in a diﬀerent wind tunnel facility. This research project should aim at proving the independence of the results
achieved on the particular laboratory used for testing. In performing this analysis, the aerodynamic properties
could be loaded for further intermediate angles within the ranges in which the largest variations in the coeﬃ-
cients trends were observed. This would provide with an even more detailed and accurate description on the
development of aerodynamic instabilities conditions compared to the study conducted.
It has been mentioned that a more solid conclusion on the eﬀects produced by a variation in the turbulence
intensity on the aerodynamic characteristics of the conductor could have been achieved if further cases were
investigated. For this reason, an interesting research to be conducted could focus on this speciﬁc topic and prove
the dependence of the possible occurrence of instability conditions on the turbulence properties of the ﬂow. The
study might be repeated both for uniform and not uniform ﬂow conditions, similarly to what already done.
It has been remarked how the dynamic analysis performed represents only a ﬁrst approximation of the actual
response of the line subjected to galloping. A future development of the study, therefore, might deal with the
creation of a ﬁnite element model capable of describing the actual behaviour of the span when subjected to this
type of instability condition. The aerodynamic coeﬃcients found through the previous investigation would still
be employed for characterizing the external loads to which the system is exposed, but further information on the
structural properties of the conductor and the supporting towers would additionally be needed. The FEM could
ﬁrst be restricted to the study of the vertical galloping by schematizing the line as a single-degree-of-freedom
system. The resulting total displacements and tension increments achieved through this solution should be then
compared to the outcome of the analysis previously performed for the diﬀerent loads combinations proposed.
This would allow to obtain an understanding of the magnitude of the error associated with having based the
initial investigation on the linear theory of cable free vibrations. Lastly, the FEM could be extended to a two
and three dimensional model, so that to capture the eﬀects on the structural system produced by the coupling
of diﬀerent types of motions during the galloping. Particular attention should be given here to the consequence
of the combined vertical and torsional motions.
