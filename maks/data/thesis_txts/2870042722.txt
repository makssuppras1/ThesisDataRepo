Abstract
This study critically examines the relationship between primary energy consumption per
capita (PEPC) and societal well-being, measured through the Human Development Index
(HDI) and the Planetary-Pressure Adjusted Human Development Index (PHDI). Using a
panel Autoregressive Distributed Lag (ARDL) methodology, the analysis explores both
short-term and long-term effects across 134 countries from 1990 to 2021.
In order to test the hypothesis that this relationship varies with development levels, two
dummy-variable approaches were applied: one distinguishing developed countries from
others and another categorizing countries into the top and bottom 25% of HDI. The find-
ings reveal a statistically significant distinction between the relationship of PEPC and so-
cietal well-being in developing and developed countries. However, this difference is not
significant when planetary pressures are accounted for, highlighting the complexity in-
troduced by sustainability-adjusted indicators. The study further identifies bidirectional
causality, indicating that energy use and well-being mutually influence each other over
time. Additionally, the presence of statistically significant adjustments up to the fourth lag
highlights the dynamic and evolving nature of this relationship.
These results suggest the need to realign energy consumption and technological ad-
vancement with societal well-being, particularly considering environmental constraints.
Future research should focus on developing a reliable indicator to measure the impact of
energy consumption on eudaimonic well-being beyond hedonic metrics. Additionally, ex-
ploring the influence of technological advancements in energy efficiency and renewable
energy on well-being in different economic contexts could offer valuable insights. Fur-
ther studies should also assess how consumerism, advertising, and systemic incentives
drive unnecessary energy consumption despite limited well-being gains. Finally, inves-
tigating provisioning systems that enable high well-being at lower energy consumption
levels could provide critical lessons for sustainable development, especially for emerging
economies.

Introduction
Energy is a fundamental component of human existence—every action we take requires
its consumption. Even if we set aside the intricate energy balances that govern our own
biological functions, human activity, whether driven by necessity or pleasure, is entirely
dependent on energy. From the most basic forms of energy use, like fire, to the immense
complexity of power plants generating electricity to sustain our modern digital world—
where circuits within circuits form systems of unimaginable scale—energy is not just a
resource, it is the foundation of everything we do.
Historically, access to energy has been associated with societal progress, fueling indus-
trialization, technological advancements, and improvements in quality of life. However,
as energy consumption has risen, so too have concerns regarding its long-term implica-
tions. The environmental degradation observed over the past century has made it evident
that equating wellbeing solely with economic growth is unsustainable. As a result, the as-
sumption that increasing energy consumption indefinitely will continue to enhance human
development must be critically examined. There is growing evidence suggesting that be-
yond a certain threshold, additional energy use yields diminishing returns in wellbeing and
may even contribute to negative outcomes such as climate change, resource depletion,
and declining health indicators.
A key question emerging from recent literature is whether energy consumption is more
closely related to hedonic wellbeing—the ability to pursue short-term needs—rather than
to broader, long-term wellbeing metrics. Studies have started questioning whether soci-
eties that continue increasing their energy consumption beyond basic provisioning are ac-
tually improving their overall wellbeing or merely reinforcing consumption-driven comfort
and convenience. If this is the case, then a deeper evaluation of the relationship between
energy consumption and wellbeing is necessary, particularly in light of the environmental
and social trade-offs of high energy use.
The objective of this study is to evaluate the relationship between energy consumption and
societal wellbeing, not only to better understand its implications for developed nations but
also to provide insights for developing countries that still have the opportunity to follow
a different trajectory. If economic growth and wellbeing are not inherently coupled, then
continuously expanding energy use in the name of development may not be the optimal
path forward. This study aims to contribute to the academic discourse by reassessing this
relationship using a dynamic panel econometric approach, the Autoregressive Distributed
Lag (ARDL) methodology, on a data set of 134 countries from 1990 to 2021.
Most previous studies on this topic have relied on cross-sectional or time-series analy-
ses, providing a static view of how energy consumption correlates with wellbeing at a
given moment in time. However, wellbeing and energy consumption evolve dynamically,
with potential lagged effects and adjustments over time. The panel ARDL approach used
in this study allows for a more nuanced understanding of both short-term and long-term
interactions, differentiating immediate effects from sustained trends. This method also
accommodates country-specific heterogeneity, making it well-suited to analyzing global
energy consumption patterns while capturing the differences between developed and de-
veloping nations.
To further investigate how these relationships vary across different development levels,
this study employs two dummy-variable approaches. The first distinguishes developed

Literature Review
2.1
Why is this relationship important?
The relationship between energy consumption, natural resource use, and societal well-
being is crucial for understanding the pathways toward sustainable development. While
energy consumption, particularly through fossil fuel use, has historically driven economic
growth and improved human wellbeing, this progress has often come at the cost of envi-
ronmental degradation and the overshooting of planetary boundaries (Balsamo, Montag-
naro, and Anthony 2023; Murthy et al. 2021). Studies have shown that beyond a certain
threshold, increases in energy consumption yield diminishing returns in wellbeing (Akizu-
Gardoki et al. 2020; Pasten and Santamarina 2012; Mazur 2011; Aydemir and Soytas
2020; Martínez and Ebenhack 2008; Steinberger and Roberts 2010; Balsamo, Montag-
naro, and Anthony 2023), in part due to the environmental degradation and its impact on
human health. If technological progress and energy systems are to truly serve societal
wellbeing, a deeper understanding of this relationship is required to ensure that policies do
not merely optimize economic growth or energy efficiency. In the end, should increasing
wellbeing not be the focus of all societal progress (Kelly and Snower 2021)?
Energy consumption remains closely tied to resource extraction and material use, and
even if humanity achieves 100% renewable energy generation, this energy will still be
used for things like mineral extraction, and ultimately to produce goods which are very
rarely fully recyclable. Given these challenges, it is essential to question whether en-
ergy consumption should be allowed to grow indefinitely and whether the dominant focus
on improving energy efficiency merely normalizes unsustainable consumption patterns.
Studies such as Aydemir and Soytas 2020 have brought up this issue when referring to
the current focus in the development of renewable energies. As first formulated in Jevons
2013, Jevons’ paradox addresses the issue of increased efficiency leading to increased
consumption, as opposed to the contrary, which might be expected.
Shedding a light on the relationship between energy consumption and societal wellbeing
might be essential for society to rethink policy making - perhaps countries might reach
a point where they choose to attempt to reduce their energy consumption rather than
increasing energy production.
2.2
Defining Wellbeing
Defining societal wellbeing is a complicated topic in itself, but in top-down (TD) studies
(such as this one), it is often associated with the Human Development Index (HDI) and its
subcomponents, as an objective measure of wellbeing is necessary. In Steinberger and
Roberts 2010, it is explained why: life expectancy determines the time humans have to do
anything, and literacy and income are necessary to fully participate in modern societies.
HDI is calculated based on its components - Gross National Income (GNI), expected years
of schooling, mean years of schooling and life expectancy at birth. As such, HDI can be
said to be a good representation of the ability of an individual to pursue short-term needs
(Aydemir and Soytas 2020). This means that, according to the definition of wellbeing in
the literature (Ryan and Deci 2001), HDI is likely closely related to hedonic wellbeing -
short-term happiness, comfort and pain avoidance. But it does not seem to be directly
related to eudaimonic wellbeing, which is associated with flourishing, finding meaning in
one’s life, engagement, competence and autonomy (Noonan 2024).

as Aydemir and Soytas 2020 have stated, currently, no eudaimonic wellbeing index can

wellbeing.
2.3
Energy Consumption and Wellbeing
The relationship between energy consumption and wellbeing has been central to several
studies, with a general consensus that energy use is a key driver of societal wellbeing (as
measured by HDI), but only up to a certain point, often referred to as a saturation effect,
and several studies have attempted to characterize this turning point.
Martínez and Ebenhack 2008 examines the relationship between per capita energy con-
sumption and HDI across 120 nations in the year 2004 at various stages of development.
The authors observe that in energy-poor countries, HDI rises steeply with increased en-
ergy consumption, while the improvement is more moderate in transitioning nations, and
it eventually levels off in energy-advantaged nations. They describe this pattern as a ”sat-
uration point,” beyond which additional energy access does not significantly impact HDI.
However, they acknowledge that factors such as conflict and health crises may also affect
HDI and could complicate the apparent relationship between energy and development.
Akizu-Gardoki et al. 2020, by analysing a sample of 176 nations in 2015, questions
whether there is a wellbeing turning point, after which higher energy consumption may
actually lower wellbeing. The authors observe that oil-exporting countries tend to have
lower HDI values on average, despite high energy consumption, which suggests that
there may be a threshold beyond which further energy use does not equate to improved
wellbeing. However, the study acknowledges that this turning point has not been conclu-
sively demonstrated across various wellbeing indicators, limiting the generalizability of its
findings.
Steinberger and Roberts 2010 take a sample of 80-110 countries in the time-frame 1975-
2005 to show that high human development can be achieved at moderate energy and
carbon levels, suggesting a decoupling of energy use and wellbeing once basic needs
are met. They find diminishing returns on wellbeing from further energy consumption,
particularly in wealthier countries, highlighting the need for more energy-efficient paths to
human development. Furthermore, the authors discover that the total amount of primary
energy being consumed at this moment is more than sufficient to attain high human de-
velopment for all.
Aydemir and Soytas 2020 propose the concept of a ”fair energy use level,” defining an
energy consumption threshold beyond which additional energy use does not yield signif-
icant wellbeing improvements but contributes to environmental degradation. The study
examines the decoupling between energy consumption and quality of life indicators, em-
phasizing the need to limit total throughput rather than rely solely on efficiency improve-
ments. This aligns with previous discussions on energy sufficiency thresholds, but differs
in its approach by focusing on residential energy consumption rather than primary en-
ergy use. The study analyzes cross-sectional data at the country level, incorporating
climate-corrected residential energy consumption rather than total energy use to better
assess direct energy needs for human wellbeing. It excludes energy-rich and biomass-
dependent countries to control for resource endowments, which distinguishes it from most
other studies of this relationship, that rely on aggregate national energy consumption.
Using non-linear modeling techniques and the ”knee point” of maximum curvature, the
study estimates a threshold beyond which additional energy use has diminishing effects
on wellbeing. The results indicate that countries consuming beyond this threshold exhibit
inefficient energy use relative to wellbeing gains. Aydemir and Soytas 2020’ findings rein-

force the idea that achieving high levels of wellbeing at moderate energy levels is possible
and necessary for sustainability. The study also highlights the potential for redistributive
energy policies to enhance global wellbeing without exacerbating ecological stress, align-

energy sufficiency (Vogel et al. 2021; O’Neill et al. 2018). However, the cross-sectional
nature of the analysis limits its ability to capture dynamic trends over time, and the exclu-

a longitudinal approach, such as panel ARDL modeling, could provide deeper insights into
how fair energy use thresholds evolve across different development stages.
In an attempt to understand what is the energy level that provides sufficient need satis-
faction, analyzing data from 1971 to 2018, Jackson et al. 2022 uses nonlinear quantile
regression to explore how per capita energy use influences trends in health, happiness,
and economic indices. The findings suggest that the peak level of wellbeing metrics is
typically achieved at an energy sufficiency threshold of 75 GJ per person annually, which
they argue could suffice for most nations to reach their wellbeing potential. A key limita-
tion is that the study’s models do not establish causality, meaning that while energy levels
correlate with wellbeing, it is not certain that energy consumption is the primary driver of
these trends. Niu et al. 2013, however, attempts to find this causal link between electricity
consumption and human development. The results show that only in the long-term does
an increase in electricity consumption promote economic and social development, which
is thereafter reflected in a growth of electricity consumption. This relationship is to found
to be more significant in high income level countries.
In line with the theory that high levels of wellbeing can be achieved without dramatically
increasing energy consumption, Pasten and Santamarina 2012 spans data from 1980
to 2010 for 118 countries and evaluates quality of life improvements at various levels
of national energy consumption. The authors highlight that significant quality of life ad-
vancements do not require high levels of energy use, citing examples of countries that
have achieved notable wellbeing gains with minimal increases in consumption, such as
Mozambique and Cuba. They advocate for technological and resource-efficient practices
and suggest that consuming more than 5 kW per person (computed as the annual rate
of primary energy use divided by the country’s population) does not necessarily enhance
quality of life. However, they do not provide a detailed examination of the country-specific
factors that might influence these outcomes.
For countries that are now developing, understanding the link between energy consump-
tion and wellbeing is particularly important because it is still possible for these countries
not to follow the same paths as developed countries and achieve higher levels of wellbe-
ing at lower energy consumption.
The difference observed in the energy consumption and societal-wellbeing relationship
with respect to the level of economic growth has become clear in the literature. In Nadimi
and Tokimatsu 2018a, covering 2005 to 2013, it is investigated how energy consumption
impacts quality of life across pre-developing, developing, and developed nations using
linear regression and s-shaped curve models. The findings suggest that pre-developing
countries have distinct patterns in quality of life and energy use, while the impact of new
technologies on quality of life and electricity consumption per capita appears more pro-
nounced in developing countries than in developed ones. The study does not, however,
address specific socio-economic factors that might drive these differences between na-
tions.
In Mazur 2011, the authors analyzed how the changes in energy or electricity consumption
since 1980 up to 2006 affected wellbeing in 21 industralized nations. It was found that
increases in electricity consumption were not followed by corresponding improvements

One of the main reasons that provokes the decrease in wellbeing after a certain thresh-
old is related to the CO2 emissions and their impact on climate change and health issues.
Covering the period from 1992 to 2017 for D-8 countries, Murthy et al. 2021 employs panel
ARDL methodology to examine how CO2 emissions impact life expectancy in D-8 coun-
tries. The authors find that economic growth, population growth, and health expenditure
are positively associated with life expectancy, while CO2 emissions have a negative im-
pact, indicating that life expectancy may be significantly affected by environmental factors.
This study provides insight into the health impacts of emissions, but it does not account for
other potential determinants of life expectancy that may vary across the sample countries.
In Tran et al. 2019, the authors investigate the simultaneous effects of energy consump-
tion, human development and carbon dioxin emissions from 1990 to 2014, for 93 coun-
tries. The findings of this study indicate that increased human development leads to re-
duced carbon emissions for all countries. Furthermore, the results show no significant
causal relationship between energy consumption and human development. The main
conclusion that can be taken from this study is that increasing human development is im-
portant to guarantee lower impact of the environment. In both developing and developed
countries, implementing policies to limit emissions and energy usage are likely to have
no impact on human development. This study, did not, however, differentiate transitional
economies.
In Pham, Li, and Bui 2024, covering G7 countries from 1991-2015, a quantile panel data
estimation approach is used to analyze the heterogeneous effects of renewable and non-
renewable energy consumption on human development. The study finds that renewable
energy consumption has a significantly positive impact on human development across
all quantiles, with stronger effects at lower quantiles. Conversely, non-renewable energy
consumption negatively impacts human development, with the negative effects becom-
ing more pronounced at higher quantiles. The results suggest that transitioning toward
renewable energy sources is important for sustaining human development, particularly in
developed economies where energy efficiency gains are more readily achievable.
Whereas several TD studies focused on the relationship between energy consumption
and societal wellbeing, few focus simultaneously on a large sample of countries and a
broad time-frame, most notoriously: Steinberger and Roberts 2010, Kerner, Kalthaus,
and Wendler 2023, Jackson et al. 2022, Pasten and Santamarina 2012, Nadimi and Toki-
matsu 2018a, Tran et al. 2019 and Nadimi and Tokimatsu 2018b. In summary, while the
existing literature largely agrees that there is a saturation point beyond which additional
energy consumption has diminishing or even negative returns on wellbeing, the specifics
of this relationship remain unclear. Several studies have identified general trends, such
as the energy sufficiency threshold or turning points, and have suggested that a balanced
approach to energy use is necessary to maximize societal wellbeing without environmen-
tal costs. However, these analyses often fall short in accounting for cross-country het-
erogeneity, such as differing socio-economic conditions, climate, resource endowments,
and historical energy use patterns, which could influence the relationship between en-

2.4
Socio-Economic Factors and Provisioning Systems
The influence of socio-economic factors on the energy-wellbeing nexus is critical for un-
derstanding differences in how countries translate energy use into human development.
Vogel et al. 2021 bring this dimension into the discussion by emphasizing the role of factors
such as income equality and public service quality in mediating the relationship between
energy consumption and wellbeing. The authors attempt to find which socio-economic
conditions permit societies to fulfill human needs at low energy use. As such, the rela-
tionship between energy use and six-dimensions of human need satisfaction varies with
these factors allows the authors to identify that countries with better ”provisioning systems”
(i.e., effective social infrastructure and equitable resource distribution) achieve higher lev-
els of need satisfaction at lower energy use. This aligns with the argument that improving
wellbeing requires not just more energy, but more efficient and equitable energy use.
Furthermore, it is found that extravism (the removal of natural resources for export) and
economic growth beyond a certain level leads to lower need satisfaction and higher en-
ergy requirements, giving an indication of the configuration of provisioning factors that
countries should focus on developing further. This raises important policy implications:
improving social services and governance may allow countries to reduce their energy
use without sacrificing wellbeing. However, no country currently achieves the authors’
definition of sufficient need satisfaction and low energy use, so the provisioning system
configurations and the resulting energy consumption/wellbeing is only based on statistical
association (Vogel et al. 2021) and might change with time. This being a cross-national
study also implies that the context of each country is not well taken into account.
Building on this perspective, O’Neill et al. 2018 examine the possibility of achieving a ”good
life within planetary boundaries” and underscore that high levels of human development
can be reached without high energy consumption. They argue that countries with high life
satisfaction often display efficient use of resources, which they attribute to social policies,
infrastructure, and governance frameworks that effectively meet people’s needs. This
work further reinforces the importance of provisioning systems, noting that countries that
prioritize equity, health care, and education often require less energy to achieve wellbeing
goals compared to countries without such policies.
Azam et al. 2023 examines the dynamic relationship between green energy use, employ-
ment, fossil fuel consumption, and human development through a panel data approach
covering 1990-2017. The study finds that investments in green energy not only improve
human development but also create employment opportunities, mitigating the negative
economic impacts of fossil fuel divestment. However, the study acknowledges that the
transition toward green energy is highly dependent on governmental policies and interna-
tional cooperation, which may not be uniform across countries.
Steinberger and Roberts 2010 also discuss how socio-economic factors can affect energy
efficiency in achieving wellbeing. Their research demonstrates that high human develop-
ment can be attained at moderate energy levels, particularly in nations that emphasize
public services, social equality, and environmental sustainability. By comparing nations
with varying levels of HDI and energy consumption, they conclude that improvements in
institutional quality, education, and healthcare play a substantial role in reducing the en-
ergy required for societal wellbeing. These findings suggest that countries with strong
institutional frameworks may have the potential to decouple energy use from wellbeing,
thus achieving ”sufficiency” without excessive resource consumption.

In this study, it is also found that the same positive association does not apply to fossil
fuels, and it is theorized this is due to the policies implemented to internalize negative
externalities, such as fuel taxes. As such, it is concluded that while improving institution
quality is not necessarily the general solution, this may help in then applying the correct
policies to reduce procyclicality - as pointed out in Vogel et al. 2021, the relationships be-
tween provisioning system configurations and thus institutional quality may change over
time.
Furthermore, Pasternak 2000 and Goldemberg 2001 discuss energy thresholds for ”ac-
ceptable living standards,” which highlight the role of socio-economic configurations in
determining the energy needed for wellbeing. These studies suggest that factors such
as income levels, industrial structures, and government priorities significantly impact the
efficiency of energy use in achieving wellbeing. For example, Dias, Mattos, and Balestieri
2006 emphasizes that even within developed countries, socio-economic diversity leads to
varied energy needs and HDI outcomes, which suggests that policies should be tailored
to each nation’s unique circumstances.
The findings across these studies underscore that socio-economic factors and provision-
ing systems play a crucial role in mediating the relationship between energy use and
wellbeing. High-quality institutions, equitable resource distribution, and effective public
services appear to facilitate a more sustainable path to human development, where well-
being is optimized with lower energy input. However, gaps remain in understanding the
long-term impacts of these socio-economic configurations and how they might evolve with
changing global economic and environmental conditions. This dependency of the rela-
tionship between energy consumption and societal wellbeing on country-specific factors
exacerbates the need to perform studies that allows for the possibility of accounting for the
time-invariant differences between countries. Finally, the fact that some studies, such as
Kerner, Kalthaus, and Wendler 2023 find a positive association of economic growth and
resource use for countries with high institutional quality may indicate the nuanced defini-
tion of institutional quality. As Noonan 2024 have stated, if the governmental system itself
is not going in a direction which increases societal wellbeing, improving its quality might
require deep structural changes.
2.5
Methodology
Despite the wealth of insights from the existing literature, there is a notable gap in the dy-
namic analysis of energy consumption and wellbeing. Most studies rely on cross-sectional
or time-series data, which provides a snapshot of the relationship between energy use and
wellbeing but fails to capture how this relationship evolves over time. This is particularly
important given how technological advances, economic growth, and socio-political fac-
tors influence energy consumption and societal wellbeing dynamically. Studies such as
Aydemir and Soytas 2020, Vogel et al. 2021, Azam et al. 2023, and Pham, Li, and Bui
2024 analyze the relationship between energy and wellbeing but focus either on short
time frames, specific country groups, or do not fully account for panel dynamics across a
broad sample. Additionally, Banday and Kocoglu 2023 and Kaewnern et al. 2023 employ
panel methods but concentrate on subsets of countries rather than examining broader
global trends. These limitations highlight the need for a comprehensive panel data study

encompassing a large sample of countries over an extended period to capture the full
complexity of the energy-wellbeing nexus.
A few studies have employed more advanced econometric techniques to capture this
temporal complexity. Notably, Kerner, Kalthaus, and Wendler 2023 utilize fixed-effects

The ARDL approach is valuable, in this case, for examining the interdependencies be-

addresses limitations in cross-sectional analyses by capturing how shifts in these vari-

over time. Furthermore, time series data on energy consumption and societal-well be-
ing are often non-stationary, meaning that their statistical properties do not stay constant
over time. ARDL is robust against this characteristic, whereas the traditional estimation of
variables without accounting for this may lead to wrong inferences or spurious regression
(Ali 2016). A solution for obtaining correct results with non-stationary data is to difference
it, making it stationary. However, when this is done, information about the long-term rela-
tionships of variables is lost. ARDL allows for level values, differences, and lagged values
to be present in the model, thus overcoming this.
A major challenge in the field is the paradoxical and often inconclusive nature of empirical
findings on the energy-wellbeing relationship. Several studies, such as Sofien and Omri
2016, show conflicting results regarding whether energy consumption is a primary driver of
economic growth or whether economic growth leads to higher energy consumption. Some
studies support the ”growth hypothesis,” where energy consumption stimulates economic
performance, while others argue for the ”conservation hypothesis,” where reducing energy
use does not harm economic growth. Additionally, some findings suggest bidirectional
causality (Lau et al. 2019), reinforcing a feedback loop between energy and growth, while
others find no causal relationship at all.
These contradictions likely arise, in part, due to differences in methodologies, sample
selection, and the timeframe of analysis. Studies employing different econometric tech-
niques, such as ARDL, panel cointegration, and Granger causality, often produce varied
results due to their underlying assumptions and sensitivities to data characteristics (Sofien
and Omri 2016). Data limitations also contribute, as some studies focus on small country
samples or short time periods, leading to heterogeneous conclusions. Further, sectoral
differences (e.g., industrial vs. residential energy demand) and technological variations
in energy efficiency contribute to diverging findings (Pesch et al. 2023).
Lau et al. 2019 further elaborate on the importance of panel methods in energy-growth
studies, highlighting crucial steps such as testing for cross-sectional dependence, station-
arity, and cointegration. Their work underscores the importance of proper methodological
choices in ensuring robust and reliable conclusions in panel data studies.

societal wellbeing, significant gaps remain in understanding its dynamic and causal na-

3.1
Cross-Sectional Dependence
Cross-sectional dependence occurs when the behavior of one entity (a country, in this
case) in a panel dataset is influenced by others. It is a crucial assumption to test in
panel data models, particularly in globalized or interconnected contexts where such de-
pendencies are common. The presence of cross-sectional dependence may affect the
validity of statistical tests and model estimations, so measures need to be taken to miti-
gate this effect and the statistical tests must be chosen accordingly (A. Menegaki 2018).
In order to check for cross-sectional dependence (CD) in the dataset, Peasaran’s and
Breusch-Pagan’s tests can be applied. In both of these tests, the null hypothesis is that
cross-sectional dependence does not exist.
3.2
Unit Root Tests
To test for stationarity, unit root analysis is conducted. Stationarity ensures that the sta-
tistical properties of a time series—such as its mean and variance—are constant over
time. In the ARDL approach, stationarity testing helps to confirm the order of integration
of each variable, which must be either I(0) (stationary at level) or I(1) (stationary after first
differencing) for the bounds test to be valid (A. N. Menegaki 2019).
For robustness, first generation and second generation unit root tests should usually be
employed to guarantee that the variables are at most I(1) (A. N. Menegaki 2019). How-
ever, the first generation of tests assumes the absence of cross-sectional independence.
For most tests, the null hypothesis is that all panels contain unit roots (the variable is non-
stationary). As such, if the p-value is less than the significance level, H0 is rejected and
it is concluded that the variable is stationary.
When the null hypothesis of non-stationarity cannot be rejected at the level form of a
variable, the first difference is applied to resolve non-stationarity. If the test indicates
stationarity after first differencing, the variable is integrated of order one, I(1). If not, the
null hypothesis cannot be rejected and ARDL cannot be applied to the dataset.
3.2.1
Optimal Lag Selection
Depending on the chosen software, the optimal lag selection for each variable in the panel
data might need to be investigated before the unit root tests are ran. This is because not all
software run unit root tests and estimators with automatic lag selection. The lag selection
is commonly performed based on AIC (Akaike Information Criterion). The lag selection
must be performed for the level variables, as well as their first differences, as both will be
present in the models.
It should be noted that a separate lag selection must be performed for unit root tests and
for the number of lags to use when fitting the models. The optimal number of lags might
not be the same for both cases.
3.3
Cointegration Tests
Cointegration indicates if variables move together in the long run. In this model, if energy
consumption (X) and societal wellbeing (Y) are cointegrated, it means that even if they

To test for cointegration, a hypothesis is set to see if the long-term coefficients in the model
are statistically significant. If the test rejects the null hypothesis, it is concluded that there
is a long-term relationship between the variables.
Similarly to the panel unit root tests, where second generation tests were developed to
overcome the decreased validity of the tests in the presence of cross-sectional depen-

• The test requires no correlation between the units of the panel. Pedroni 1999 sug-

• T (the number of observations) should be significantly larger than N (the number of

3.4.1
Dummy Variable Inclusion in Long-Run Equation
To further investigate how the relationship between PEPC and societal wellbeing differs
across levels of development, two distinct modifications are introduced:
1. Single Dummy Model: A binary dummy variable is introduced to distinguish be-
tween developed countries (≥75th percentile of HDI) and non-developed countries
(< 75th percentile of HDI). This allows the model to capture differences in the energy-
wellbeing relationship between relatively more and less developed economies.
2. Dual Dummy Model: Instead of a single binary classification, two separate dummy
variables are used:
• Developed Dummy: A dummy variable that takes the value of 1 if a country
is in the top 25% of HDI values and 0 otherwise.
• Underdeveloped Dummy: A dummy variable that takes the value of 1 if a
country is in the bottom 25% of HDI values and 0 otherwise.
The objective here is to attempt to find differences in the energy consumption and so-
cietal wellbeing relationship. If the interaction terms with the dummies are shown to be
statistically significant, a difference in slope is confirmed, and the sign of that coefficient
will indicate in which direction that is.
3.4.2
Estimation Method
For the initial model, with no dummy variables, the coefficients are estimated using the
Common Correlated Effects Mean Group (CCEMG) estimator, as proposed by Pesaran
Pesaran 2006. This method is chosen for its robustness in addressing cross-sectional
dependence in panel data.
CCEMG effectively mitigates these issues by introducing cross-sectional averages of the
dependent and independent variables as additional regressors. These averages capture
the unobserved common factors driving cross-sectional dependence, ensuring consis-
tent and reliable parameter estimates. Furthermore, the Mean Group (MG) approach of
CCEMG allows for heterogeneity in coefficients across units, making it particularly suitable
for this study, where the relationship between energy consumption and societal wellbeing
may vary across regions or countries.
When including dummy variables, a random effects estimator is used instead of CCEMG
to avoid issues of perfect collinearity. The random effects model assumes that individual-
specific effects are uncorrelated with explanatory variables, making it suitable for panel
data analysis.

A key challenge in studying the relationship between societal wellbeing and energy con-

4.4
Unit Root Tests
As mentioned in Section 4.3, second order unit root tests have to be applied in this section,
to guarantee the validity of the results in the presence of cross-sectional dependence. As
such, if it is chosen to use a function from one of the available panel data packages in R,
there are two main options: to use the CIPS (Cross-sectionally augmented Im, Pesaran
and Shin (IPS)) test from the plm package, or to use the pIGF (Panel unit root test of
Chang(2002)) test from the pdR package. If CIPS is chosen, the lags must be chosen
manually, which can be problematic given the absence of a function that runs the AIC
criteria for each unit root test and displays the result. On the other hand, pIGF provides
automatic lag selection based on AIC, and it was chosen for this reason.

5
Results
This chapter presents the empirical results from the three econometric models used in the
analysis. The models examine the relationship between per capita energy consumption
(PEPC) and human wellbeing, measured through the Human Development Index (HDI)
and the Planetary Health-adjusted HDI (PHDI). The estimation results are structured as
follows: first, the baseline model is estimated without distinguishing between country de-
velopment levels; second, a model incorporating a single dummy for developed countries
is analyzed; and third, a model introducing both developed and underdeveloped country
dummies is presented.
The results are discussed in terms of both short-run and long-run effects, taking into ac-
count the optimal lag structure selected based on Akaike Information Criterion (AIC). The
same lag specification is applied consistently across all models. Diagnostic tests are con-
ducted for each model to assess the presence of heteroskedasticity and serial correlation,
with results detailed in Appendix A.1, A.2 and A.3. Newey-West robust standard errors
are computed for all cases where heteroskedacity or serial correlation is found.

6
Discussion
6.1
Comparison with Existing Literature
The findings of this study contribute to the growing body of research on the relationship
between energy consumption and societal wellbeing, confirming many existing trends
while also revealing important nuances.
6.1.1
Energy Consumption and Societal Wellbeing
The results show that the relationship between energy consumption and the Human De-
velopment Index (HDI) follows a pattern of diminishing returns, with a statistically signifi-
cant but decreasing impact of energy consumption on wellbeing in developed countries.
This aligns with previous research, such as Martínez and Ebenhack 2008; Aydemir and
Soytas 2020; Akizu-Gardoki et al. 2020; Jackson et al. 2022; Pasten and Santamarina
2012; Mazur 2011; Tran et al. 2019, which identified or theorized a saturation point where
additional energy use ceases to yield improvements in HDI.
Furthermore, the study by Akizu-Gardoki et al. 2020 questioned whether energy con-
sumption beyond a certain threshold could even reduce wellbeing. While the present
findings do not explicitly confirm a decline in wellbeing at high energy levels, the negative
interaction term for developed countries suggests that additional energy use has weaker
positive effects in high-income nations, consistent with the idea of diminishing marginal
returns.
The results of Pasten and Santamarina 2012, which suggested that a consumption level
above 5 kW per person does not necessarily enhance quality of life, resonate with the
present study’s findings, particularly for developed countries. Likewise, Mazur 2011 found
that increases in electricity consumption did not lead to corresponding improvements in
quality of life for 21 industrialized nations, a conclusion that aligns with the negative inter-
action term for developed countries in this study.
6.1.2
Differentiation Between HDI and PHDI
One of the key insights from this study is the difference in results between HDI and the
Planetary-Adjusted Human Development Index (PHDI). While energy consumption posi-
tively influences HDI in developing nations, its effect on PHDI is weaker and statistically
insignificant in developed countries. This finding supports the argument that sustainability
considerations alter the traditional energy-wellbeing relationship, as indicated by Aydemir
and Soytas 2020 and Tran et al. 2019, who emphasize the need to incorporate environ-
mental costs into development metrics.
Moreover, the work by Murthy et al. 2021 using panel ARDL models showed that CO2
emissions negatively impact life expectancy, further reinforcing the idea that high energy
consumption may bring adverse environmental and health consequences that offset its
benefits. This is in line with the present study’s results, which suggest that increasing
energy use does not necessarily equate to better wellbeing when planetary pressures
are considered. This could be due to the fact that this study did not account for different
energy generation or energy efficiency, as was done, for example in Aydemir and Soytas
2020.
Additionally, Pham, Li, and Bui 2024 found that non-renewable energy consumption neg-
atively impacts human development, particularly in wealthier nations. This aligns with

the weaker long-term association between energy and PHDI found in this study, which
suggests that sustainability-adjusted wellbeing does not benefit as much from increased
energy consumption.
6.1.3
Short-Run and Long-Run Dynamics
The short-run results indicate that energy consumption has a significant but fluctuating
effect on wellbeing, with effects persisting up to four time lags. This oscillatory pattern
reflects the dynamic nature of the energy-wellbeing relationship and goes in line with the
findings from Niu et al. 2013, who demonstrated that long-term increases in electricity con-
sumption are positively associated with social development, highlighting that only taking
the contemporaneous values into account is not sufficient to analyze this relationship.
The presence of Granger causality in both directions further supports the bidirectional rela-
tionship between energy consumption and wellbeing, as previously suggested by Kerner,
Kalthaus, and Wendler 2023. However, not all studies have reached the same conclu-
sion. For instance, Tran et al. 2019 found no significant causal relationship between
energy consumption and human development, suggesting that factors other than energy
availability may be more decisive in driving improvements in wellbeing. Similarly, Pham,
Li, and Bui 2024 found that only renewable energy consumption had a significant positive
impact on human development, whereas non-renewable energy had a negative impact,
highlighting that the nature of the energy source might influence the presence or absence
of causality.
6.1.4
Role of Socio-Economic Factors and Provisioning Systems
The findings of this study suggest that while energy consumption plays a key role in im-
proving wellbeing, its impact becomes saturated after a certain threshold, particularly in
developed countries. This aligns with the broader literature on energy sufficiency and pro-
visioning systems. As Vogel et al. 2021 demonstrated, income equality and public service
quality influence the efficiency of energy use in fulfilling human needs. Similarly, O’Neill
et al. 2018 and Steinberger and Roberts 2010 argue that countries with well-functioning
social policies and infrastructure tend to achieve higher wellbeing with lower energy con-
sumption. However, this study suggests that once hedonic wellbeing has plateaued, con-
tinuing along the same path of socioeconomic progress will not yield further wellbeing
gains.
A critical implication of this finding is that if institutions and socioeconomic structures re-
main focused on energy expansion, consumption growth, or traditional economic mea-
sures, they may fail to enhance wellbeing beyond the saturation point. This concern is
discussed in the work of Noonan 2024, who discuss how the continued pursuit of eco-
nomic growth under capitalism does not necessarily improve wellbeing and may even lead
to psychological stress, insecurity, and alienation. This reinforces the idea that beyond a
certain energy sufficiency threshold, wellbeing improvements must come from qualitative
rather than quantitative changes, shifting from hedonic wellbeing (comfort, consumption)
to eudaimonic wellbeing (purpose, meaning, and fulfillment).
The study by Isham et al. 2022 further supports this perspective by demonstrating that
materialistic values are negatively associated with sufficiency attitudes, mindfulness, and
flow experiences—all of which are linked to sustainable wellbeing. If societies continue
to prioritize materialistic and consumption-driven goals, they may inadvertently limit the
ability to transition toward eudaimonic wellbeing, which could serve as the next stage of
societal progress once hedonic wellbeing is saturated.
Additionally, while this study does not explicitly measure institutional quality or provisioning

systems, the observed diminishing returns in developed nations suggest that a shift in
policy focus is needed. As Kerner, Kalthaus, and Wendler 2023 found, institutional quality
influences resource use, meaning that governments and policymakers must consider how
to repurpose energy and economic resources toward wellbeing strategies that go beyond
material gains.
6.2
Limitations
In the presence of cross-sectional dependence, the usage of Pedroni tests is not recom-
mended, for the reasons highlighted in Section 4.5. An improvement to this study would
be to use second-generation tests such as Groen and Kleibenbergen (2003) and West-
erlund (2007), which are not available in R. The validity of the results should, however,
not be impacted by this limitation, as the presence of a stable cointegration relationship
was still found. As noted in A. N. Menegaki 2019, though, the presence of cross-sectional
dependence may contribute to endogeneity, which should be taken into account in future
panel data studies.
The removal of 53 countries from the dataset, many of which are small, developing, or
in transitional economies, introduces a potential limitation in the study. These countries
often face challenges in data collection and reporting, leading to missing or inconsistent
values for key variables such as HDI and PEPC. As a result, the findings may be less rep-
resentative of regions with unstable political conditions, lower-income levels, or unique
energy consumption patterns. Additionally, the exclusion of countries with distinct energy
infrastructures, such as those heavily reliant on off-grid or alternative energy sources,
could influence the generalizability of the results. Future research could explore methods
to estimate missing values or incorporate alternative data sources to improve represen-
tation. Case studies on excluded countries could also provide insights into whether their
omission impacts the robustness of the study’s conclusions.
This study controls for climate differences using Heating Degree Days (HDD) to account
for variations in energy consumption due to temperature-related heating needs. However,
other geographical and socio-economic factors that could influence energy consumption
and societal wellbeing, such as resource availability, infrastructure development, cultural
energy practices, and industrial composition, were not controlled for. As a result, the
analysis may not fully capture the heterogeneity of energy use efficiency across countries
with similar climate conditions but differing economic or technological structures. Future
research could incorporate additional control variables.
6.3
Future Work and Recommendations
The first key finding from this study is that, both in the long term and the short term,
there are statistically significant differences between the relationships of HDI vs. PEPC
for developing and developed countries. HDI is associated with hedonic wellbeing. As
such, the existence of a saturation for developed countries can be imagined when their
citizens begin to have a comfortable life, where they can easily fulfill their short-term needs
- further increases in energy consumption start having small impacts in wellbeing. Think
of someone who has a one-year-old phone and replaces it with a new one: more energy
consumption, very little return.
But this only allows one to comment on the relationship between energy consumption and
hedonic pleasure, not actual wellbeing, which, of course, needs to take into account the
eudaimonic dimension (Aydemir and Soytas 2020). If the objective of societal progress
is to actually increase wellbeing, there is a key issue: continuing to increase energy con-
sumption will very likely cause environmental degradation, as studies have shown (Murthy
Understanding the link between energy consumption and societal wellbeing
37
==PAGE:46==
et al. 2021; Tran et al. 2019; Aydemir and Soytas 2020). If there is a plateau in this type of
wellbeing, and prejudicial effects arise from increased energy consumption, wellbeing will
actually start decreasing (if the eudaimonic dimension does not improve enough to com-
pensate). This leads to the first recommendation of this study - for future studies to focus
on the relationship between energy consumption and eudaimonic wellbeing, as well as to
develop an indicator that allows for a good representation of the latter. If one dimension
of wellbeing is saturated, it would make sense for policy makers and scholars to focus on
understanding how it would be possible to increase wellbeing through different paths.
If energy consumption increase does no longer bring wellbeing benefits in developed
countries, why then, do humans keep consuming more and more energy per capita (Jack-
son et al. 2022)? Would it not make sense for humans to begin realizing that this path is
not the optimal one?
Capitalism has been effective at generating wealth, providing equitable access to health
(Noonan 2024), at getting people out of poverty, and improving people’s ability to pursue
their short-term needs. However, capitalism also has its short-comings in regards to the
ability of people to see their situation clearly. The system is based on accelerating growth
and profit - consumerism, advertising, and other attention-grabbing methods could be
important players in forgetting that societal progress should mean increased wellbeing.
This relationship should be investigated.
A decoupling from capitalism and societal progress could be occuring (Kelly and Snower
2021), and economy might currently be growing for the sake of growth, not for the sake of
improving people’s lives. The results of this study could, once more, display the symptoms
of this decoupling. The relationship between economic growth and societal wellbeing
should continue being explored.
Another key finding of this study is that when analyzing PHDI versus energy consump-
tion, the previously observed statistical differences between developed and developing
countries disappear. This suggests that including planetary pressures distorts this rela-
tionship, and the reasons for this phenomenon should be explored. A first step in this
direction would be to conduct a similar study while controlling for variations in energy
generation sources and energy efficiency between developed and developing nations.
Understanding how these factors influence the observed distortions in PHDI will provide
valuable insights into the broader implications of sustainable energy transitions.
Furthermore, this study demonstrates statistically significant dynamic relationships be-
tween HDI, PHDI, and energy consumption. Examining only contemporaneous values is
insufficient to capture the complexity of these interactions. Future research should pri-
oritize understanding these dynamic relationships over time, considering both short-term
and long-term effects. Expanding the use of dynamic econometric models will allow for a
more complete interpretation of how energy consumption patterns shape human devel-
opment under different economic and environmental conditions.
Finally, this study did not proceed with the intended objective of assessing the relationship
between energy consumption and inequality-adjusted HDI (IHDI). This is an important
gap, as inequality plays a crucial role in determining the true distribution of wellbeing within
societies. Future research should address this limitation by investigating how energy
consumption impacts IHDI and whether disparities in energy access contribute to broader
inequalities in human development.
It is important to question the direction of technological progress, as to guarantee that it
does not deviate from the direction of societal progress. Summarizing, future research

should aim to:
• Investigate how energy consumption influences eudaimonic wellbeing beyond he-
donic metrics and develop a reliable indicator to measure this relationship.
• Explore whether technological advancements in energy efficiency and renewable
energy impact wellbeing differently in developed versus developing countries.
• Analyze the role of consumerism, advertising, and systemic incentives in driving
unnecessary energy consumption despite limited wellbeing gains.
• Continue assessing how different provisioning systems allow for high wellbeing at
lower energy consumption levels and identify best practices from countries achiev-

• Continuing exploring the relationship between economic growth and societal well-

• Extend the panel ARDL methodology while controlling for variations in energy gen-

• Prioritize research on the dynamic aspects of the energy-wellbeing relationship rather

Conclusion
This study explored the relationship between energy consumption and societal wellbe-
ing, assessing how per capita energy use interacts with the Human Development Index
(HDI) and its planetary-adjusted version (PHDI). Using a panel ARDL methodology, the
analysis differentiated between short-term and long-term effects, incorporating country-
specific heterogeneity and testing for causality. The results reaffirmed the existence of a
saturation point beyond which increased energy consumption yields diminishing returns
in terms of human development. While energy remains a key driver of wellbeing in devel-
oping nations, its influence becomes less pronounced in highly developed economies.
The findings also highlighted notable distinctions between HDI and PHDI. Whereas HDI
showed a significant positive association with energy consumption, PHDI exhibited a
weaker relationship, suggesting that environmental pressures alter the conventional energy-
wellbeing dynamic. This points to the importance of considering sustainability in discus-
sions about energy and development. The bidirectional causality observed between en-
ergy consumption and wellbeing further supports the notion that these factors influence
each other over time, reinforcing the need for policies that balance energy access with
broader socio-economic and environmental goals.
Despite the robustness of the methodology, certain limitations must be acknowledged.
The exclusion of 53 countries due to data constraints limits the generalizability of the find-
ings, particularly for small, developing, or politically unstable nations. Additionally, while
climate differences were controlled for through Heating Degree Days (HDD), other geo-
graphical and socio-economic factors may contribute to variations in energy consumption
efficiency. These gaps suggest that future research should incorporate more comprehen-
sive control variables and explore alternative approaches for estimating missing data.
A broader implication of this study is the need to reassess the role of continuous energy
consumption growth in societal progress. If wellbeing gains plateau at higher levels of
consumption, future policies should shift from expanding energy use toward optimizing
energy distribution, efficiency, and the quality of human development. This is particularly
relevant for developing countries, which still have the opportunity to learn from developed
countries and optimize their societal progress.
Overall, this research contributes to the ongoing discourse on energy and wellbeing by
providing empirical evidence on their evolving relationship. The results underscore the
importance of understanding not only how much energy is consumed, but also how it is
used, and whether further increases align with long-term human and environmental well-
being. As societies continue to navigate the complexities of energy transitions, ensuring
that energy systems serve meaningful and sustainable progress will be essential.
