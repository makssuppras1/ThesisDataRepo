Abstract
Aim The main aim of the research project was to develop an algorithm capable of deriving pressure
gradients non-invasively using ultrasound imaging. Abnormal variations in pressure are used by
physicians to assess and monitor the cardiovascular system. Presently, intravascular pressure gra-
dients are measured by means of cathetarization. Although it is considered as the gold standard,
the main limitations associated with it are, the invasiveness of the procedure and the usage of ion-
ization radiation as guidance. To overcome such concerns, the research study presents a method
based on Navier-Stokes model for incompressible ﬂuid, to derive pressure gradients non-invasively
by means of synthetic aperture imaging data. The temporal and spatial acceleration estimates that
are fed as inputs to the Navier-Stokes equation, are derived using the double cross-correlation ap-
proach, taking an independent route from the velocity estimator.
Methods The research project evolves with three individual studies. The Study I was focused on de-
veloping a basic velocity estimator and a temporal acceleration estimator. The performance of the
estimators were evaluated using a simple simulation model formed from conventional ultrasound
imaging data. The velocity estimation scheme was further developed to derive the velocities along
the direction of ﬂow in Study II. The performance of it was tested by means of a straight vessel
phantom study using synthetic aperture imaging data. In Study III, these individual estimators are
combined, to form the main pressure estimation setup. The pressure drop between initial and end
point of a streamline is obtained by summing the individual pressure gradients along the stream-
line. Then the performance of the setup, was tested by means of a fabricated phantom that mimics
the blood ﬂow in common carotid artery.
Results Results of both simulated and measured straight vessel phantom studies indicated that the
directional ﬂow estimator performs well with with a relative standard deviation lower than 1 %
for a vessel with a ﬂow angle of 75◦and around 3.3-3.7 % for a fully transverse ﬂow with respect
to the peak velocity of the vessel. The relative mean bias was extremely low, reaching closer to
0 % for both ﬂow angles using simulated data, and 2.6 % for measured data of transverse ﬂow.
The ﬁnal common carotid phantom study showed that pressure gradients can be deduced with a
relative standard deviation of approximately 3 % with respect to the peak estimates. This is sig-
niﬁcantly lower than the results obtained using BK program. The derived estimates are further
compared with a ﬁnite element model simulation, to assess the accuracy of the proposed method.
The estimates, including temporal evolution of the pressure gradients along the chosen streamline,
median pressure drop as well as systolic pressure are in accordance with the FEM simulation.
Conclusion It can be concluded that, the proposed estimator performs with promising results, and
derives the intravascular pressure gradients with a precision higher than the existing BK program.
However, it would be beneﬁcial to conduct more experiments such as a carotid bifurcation study, a
series of in-vivo studies to observe the behavior of the estimator under complex ﬂow geometries.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
Summary: This chapter is divided into two folds. This ﬁrst fold describes about the background of the
research study. This includes the clinical importance behind intravascular pressure estimation, current
methods in practice, emphasizes on the importance of medical ultrasound and, the requirement for a
better estimator. The second part of the chapter presents an introduction to the research study. This
contains the hypothesis, aim and objectives and general outline of the thesis.
1.1
Background
1.1.1
Clinical importance of assessing pressure gradients
Blood pressure is traditionally deﬁned using three values: systolic, diastolic and mean pressure.
Systolic pressure can be deﬁned as the pressure exerted on the wall of blood vessels by blood at the
end of the systolic contraction of ventricles. Diastolic pressure is the pressure exerted on the blood
vessel walls at the end of relaxation or diastole. Mean pressure is the average rate of blood ﬂow
through systemic vessels. With the development of pressure estimation techniques, these variables
are associated with different values. When a pulsatile waveform is displayed, as in arterial line,
the maximum pressure is systolic, and minimum pressure corresponds to the diastolic. The mean
pressure is the area under the waveform-time curve divided by the time interval for one or multiple
heart beats [1].
Intravascular pressure gradients are essential to monitor the cardiovascular system. The stan-
dard monitoring of blood pressure in the perioperative setting is a necessity because of the acute
ﬂuctuations in hemodynamic status as a consequence of anesthesia, surgical stimulation, hemor-
rhage, pain etc. Abnormal intravascular pressure gradients indicate the presence of a vascular
disease. Such abnormal changes occur from abrupt alterations in the blood ﬂow. When blood
ﬂows through a stenotic vessel, a pressure drop occurs as a result of the dissipation of energy re-
sponsible for forming localized ﬂow jets, eddies and resisting the viscous drag encountered by the
blood cells [2]. High pressure gradients across stenotic heart valves could lead to disability or even
death.
1.1.2
Gold standard for monitoring pressure gradients
Presently, the gold standard for blood pressure measurements is the invasive procedure using
ﬂouroscopy as guidance. The invasive measurements are done in two ways. This involves ﬂuid-
ﬁlled catheters that send pressure waves to an external pressure sensitive transducer as shown in
Figure 1.1, or directly on to the pressure sensitive tip of a pressure wire. The catheter is connected
by a long, thin and liquid ﬁlled tubing to a transducer, consisting of a diaphragm which records
the transmitted blood pressure through the ﬂuid column. The transducers placed at the heart

1.1.3
Current non-invasive alternatives to assessing pressure gradi-
ents
Penaz technique
The technique offers the beneﬁts of continuous blood pressure monitoring. It is based on vascular
unloading. This device is consisted of a small ﬁnger cuff containing photoplethysmograph, a light
source on one side of the cuff, and an infrared receiver on the opposite side which can estimate the
blood volume of the ﬁnger using infrared light absorbance [1].
Arterial tonometry
This technique produces a waveform similar to the above technique. However, the principle of
mechanism of this technique differs. Here a tonometer sensor is placed over the radial artery to
compress it until the vessel is ﬂattened against the bone. The pressure transducer on the skin
surface would measure the arterial blood pressure through contact pressure [1].

Magnetic Resonance Imaging
Magnetic resonance imaging is a non-invasive modality used for the imaging of anatomical struc-
tures without using ionization radiation. The MRI physics are based on the changes in hydrogen
nuclei when they are exposed to radio frequency signals within a strong magnetic ﬁeld. Phase-
Contrast MRI (PC-MRI) measures the phase-shift of moving spins. This net phase-shift is propor-
tional to the ﬂow velocity, which can be used to deduce the pressure changes. MRI is popular for
high spatial resolution and good contrast for tissue differentiation [4].
Medical ultrasound
This imaging modality is capable of real-time visualization of anatomical structures without adverse
effects or harmful radiation. Ultrasound imaging is widely used for the imaging of nearly all soft
tissue structures in the body. 20 to 100 images per second can be obtained [5]. The scanning is
initiated by an ultrasound transducer with the emission of a sound wave, in which the strength

it has been recommended for hemodynamic assessment of nearly all thoracic vessels due to its wide
accessibility, the ability to be used routinely for diagnosis with the inclusion of a strong correlation
with invasive measurements in the heart.
1.1.4
The requirement for a better estimator
Although cathetarization is considered as the gold-standard for pressure assessment, it is an in-
vasive procedure and there are complications and risks associated with it. Current non-invasive
techniques also have their fair share of limitations and disadvantages. Pressure gradients esti-
mated non-invasively using ultrasound, MRI techniques are dependent on the precision of the ﬂow
velocity estimates [4]. Discrepancies in measurements can lead to catastrophic results. Inaccurate
estimates could result in the misclassiﬁcation of severity of the diseases, and the patient may end
up with a wrong treatment strategy.

2.2.1
Literary work
There was a research interest in non-invasive assessment of pressure gradients in 1970s. Fairbank
and Scully proposed a method for estimating local pressure changes using microbubbles in 1977.
The method was based on injecting gas-ﬁlled bubbles into the vascular system and measuring the
frequency shift resulted from the scattered spectrum when ultrasonic waves were applied. This
method gave rise to a range of other techniques. It was not a fully non-invasive method, and still
required the injection of microbubbles.
In 1976, ﬁrst fully non-invasive pressure estimation technique was introduced by Holen et al.
This was based on Doppler ultrasound. Peak systolic velocity was derived from the frequency
shifts received from the mitral jet. Using the velocity data, local pressure gradients were computed
from an oriﬁce equation. The method has been tested on 25 patients with mitral stenosis and 10
patients without mitral stenosis. Although the method was considered beneﬁcial due to the lack
of cathetarization, it has faced with various difﬁculties associated with the scanner technology at
that time. Some of the limitations include, poor signal-to-noise ratios, insufﬁcient temporal and
spatial resolutions. The fact that, Holen et al’s technique relied on a single velocity estimate made
it vulnerable to hemodynamic factors unrelated to constricted vessel’s effect on peak velocity, such
as abnormal cardiac output [10]. Strauss et al. [11] and Baumgartner et al. [12], [13] later tested
and compared Holen et al’s method against clinical pressure catheters. Later on, research studies
began to focus on estimating intravascular pressure gradients using a simpliﬁed version of Bernoulli
equation, where the viscous and temporal effects are omitted. [14].

3.1
Conventional velocity estimation
Commercial ultrasound scanners estimate the blood velocities by tracking the movement of blood
scatterers along the ultrasound beam. This is carried out by emitting an ultrasound pulse and
extracting the shift in position from pulse to pulse using cross-correlation.
The process initiates with a transmitter emitting a pulse, where the level of the resulting re-
ceived signal is adjusted by a TGC ampliﬁer. The purpose of the matched ﬁlter is to ensure that
high frequency noise above half the sampling frequency does not inﬂuence the band of interest
by sampling operation. However, for this particular estimator it is not mandatory, as the cross-
correlation itself, performs matched ﬁltration. The next stage is sampling of the received signals,
which is carried out as per Shannon’s sampling theorem [6]. Prior to computing velocity, stationary
echo cancellation is performed. At vessel boundaries, the received signal is constituted of both re-
ﬂections from the boundary, and the scattered signal from the blood. The reﬂection signal is often
assumed to be stationary, and constant over a number of pulse emissions [5]. Simplest stationary
echo cancellation procedure is performed by subtracting two consecutive signals [5].
res(n, i) = rs(n, i) −rs(n, i + 1)
(3.1)
This helps in removing the stationary component and leaving the signal suitable for velocity
estimation. The classical cross-correlation technique computes the correlation between two signals
when one signal is shifted in time with respect to other. The shift with maximal value of the
correlation represents the time shift between the signals. The most basic approach has a resolution
of half the sampling period. This resolution is insufﬁcient, when the sampling frequency is 100
MHz. A method with sub-sampling accuracy is required to estimate the time-shift.

Synthetic aperture techniques were originally applied for radar systems in 1950s. Later on, in the
1970s, synthetic aperture techniques were initially implemented using digital computers. More

advanced techniques were introduced in the late 1980s [28]. A SA radar system usually utilizes
a single transmitter and receiver, moving the antenna over the region of interest in an airplane or
satellite. It was in 1992, the SAI techniques were ﬁrst developed for medical applications by M.
O’Donnel and L.J. Thomas. The basic principles for acquiring synthetic aperture ultrasound images
are shown in Figure 3.1[29].
Figure 3.1: Basic principles of synthetic aperture ultrasound imaging [29].
A single element in the transducer aperture transmits a spherical wave covering the entire
image region. For each transmission, all or fraction of the elements in the aperture are sampled
as shown by the shaded boxes of Figure 3.1. This data are used for creating a low resolution
image. The procedure is repeated for a new transmitting element, yielding a new low-resolution
image. By combining a set of low-resolution images, a high-resolution image is created. Focusing is
carried out by a process called delay and sum beamforming. First, the geometric distance from the
transmitting element to the imaging point and back to the receiving element is found. The time of
ﬂight is then, computed by dividing this geometric distance from the propagation speed of sound.
Repeating the procedure for all the points in the image, will result in a low-resolution image. A
high-resolution image is created by focusing all points in each LRI and summing up all the LRIs.

The focus is both dynamic in transmit and receive. The highest possible resolution for delay-sum
beamforming is obtained everywhere in the image. The focusing can be carried out in any order
or direction.
However, there is a major drawback in this process. That is, the penetration depth. It is limited
since only one element is used to transmit a spherical wave. This can be overcome in two ways.
It is possible to utilize a lower frequency which attenuates less as it propagates through the tissue.
This is however, at the expense of the resolution. Second solution would be to use a collection of
transmitting elements for increasing the amplitude of the signal. Each element must be delayed
appropriately, in order to create a spherical wave with a larger amplitude. It is possible to do so, by
introducing a virtual source. The origin of the transmitted ultrasound can be deﬁned by means of
a virtual source. By placing a virtual source behind the transducer, a diverging spherical wave can
be emulated using multiple transmitting elements. The timing applied on each transmit element
is computed according to the time of ﬂight from the position of the virtual source to the receiving

3.2.2
Theory of directional Velocity Estimation
With conventional ultrasound imaging, only the displacement in the direction of the steered ul-
trasound beam can be obtained. This was considered a major limitation of the existing scanners,
since the blood ﬂow is often parallel to the skin and thus, perpendicular to the ultrasound beam.
Researchers have tried several approaches to overcome this issue. Some of the solutions they have
come up with were, speckle tracking, crossed beams and transverse modulation beams. A highlight-
ing breakthrough occurred when Bonnefous suggested using a number of parallel beamformers to
acquire a signal transverse to the ultrasound beam. In a later study, Jensen extended the Bonnefous’
idea of lateral beamforming to perform beamforming at several positions along the direction of the
blood ﬂow vector [30].
Figure 3.3: The transverse ultrasound beams (left) and the obtained lateral directional lines from
focusing along the ﬂow or beamforming for a parabolic velocity proﬁle (right) [30].
With the development of advanced imaging techniques such SA imaging, received signals can
be easily focused along the direction of the ﬂow as illustrated in Figure 3.3. A short sequence
of emissions of Nt= 4 to 8 is used. The high-resolution image lines are then focused along the
ﬂow direction x′. The velocity causes a displacement between the high-resolution images. If two
consecutive high resolution image lines are considered, the second one would be shifted spatially
with respect to the ﬁrst image line. The peak that is obtained after cross-correlating such two lines
corresponds to the shift between the lines. Dividing it by Nt Tpr f yields the true velocity magnitude
|⃗v|. This can be conducted in any direction, even transverse to the normal ultrasound direction of
propagation [29]. This theory is applied and discussed in detail under Study II.

4.3.6
Stationary echo cancellation of the received signals
A received signal is a combination of a ﬂow signal and a stationary signal. Often the magnitude of
the stationary signal is 10 to 100 times larger than the ﬂow signal. The stationary echo canceller
acts in removing echoes from the boundaries of the blood vessel and from surrounding tissues.
This ensures that only the ﬂow signal enters to the velocity estimator. Often echo cancelling is
performed by subtracting consecutive lines as modelled by 3.1 [6]. This acts as a moving average
high pass ﬁlter. Consequently, this results in a reduction of the amplitude of the ﬂow signal and
a distortion of the pulse spectrum that depends on velocity. Hence in this study, stationary echo
cancelling is performed by subtracting the mean of the received signals from each signal.

4.5
Discussion
Several factors have an inﬂuence on the performance of the estimator. Correct peak detection plays
a crucial role. False peak detection would lead to erroneous results. Fitting a second order poly-
nomial around the cross-correlation peak contributed towards acquiring velocity estimates which
are not heavily quantized. This resulted in an increased resolution of the ﬂow proﬁle. Another
such factor is the pulse length. A short pulse has been used to obtain a narrow cross-correlation
function. However this could also lower the sensitivity of the estimate. In addition to that, the
number of emissions averaged over time should be chosen wisely without compromising the frame
rate. The implemented estimator with Nc lines per estimate performed considerably well with a
lower standard deviation for each simulation (each fpr f ) carried out.
The double cross-correlation approach applied in the estimator provides acceleration estimates
independent of the velocity estimator. This is essentially advantageous in case if the velocity esti-
mates are affected by jitter or noise. Thus, resulting in more precise acceleration estimates which
will be extremely useful when estimating the pressure gradients. Although the study, provides re-
sults which are in accordance with the reference ﬂow proﬁles, there is still space for improvement,
in particularly removing the slight deviations seen in the estimated acceleration proﬁles. Overall,
both cross-correlation estimators (velocity and acceleration) performed well with σrel in the range
of 0.01%-1.6 % for the selected set of pulse repetition frequencies. Brel is closer to 0% for velocity
estimates whereas, it is around 0.3 %-1.5 % for the acceleration estimates. This indicates that the
estimators behave well even when fpr f is varied. The next study focuses on developing the ﬂow
estimator such that velocity estimates can be derived along the ﬂow.

5.3.3
Angle Estimator
Usually the ﬂow angle must be known before beamforming in the ﬂow direction. In the ideal
situation, the vessel is rigid and straight without sudden geometric changes within the range of the
B-mode image or atleast within the velocity estimation range. In this case, the beam-to-ﬂow angle
could be determined with satisfactory results from the B-mode image. It can also be done using
actual data. This study estimates the ﬂow angle using ﬂow emissions, since it is more appropriate
to implement the angle estimation scheme as a part of the directional ﬂow estimation algorithm.
The method used in this study was originally explored by [34].
The directional signals are obtained by focusing the received responses in points along the
ﬂow direction. A high correlation is obtained if directional signals obtained from two consecutive
responses are properly aligned along the ﬂow direction. Decorrelation between signals occurs as
a result of the spatial shift due to the movement of the scatterers. If the directional signals from
two consecutive responses are not properly aligned, correlation between the two signals would be
reduced. Angle estimation scheme employed in this study, utilizes this change in correlation to give
the correct beam-to-ﬂow angle.

5.5
Discussion
An estimator for deriving the velocity along the ﬂow, based on cross-correlation and the usage of
synthetic aperture ﬂow data was developed in this study. The applied method is based on the
estimator presented in [34]. The discrete cross-correlation estimates yield erroneous peaks when
there is less data or if noise is present in data. This could lead to angle estimates that differ widely
from the true angle. To avoid this issue, averaging of cross-correlated signals is done over 16
directional signals for both angle estimations. This helps in removing the random ﬂuctuations that
are present in noise. Moreover, to eliminate outliers, angle estimates are ﬁltered by considering
the deviation with their neighbors. This deviation (θdev) is speciﬁed as 15◦. The angle estimates
which deviate less than or equal to θdev are considered acceptable. Those which deviate outside
this range are considered outliers. The value of these outliers are replaced by the mean value of
their two nearest neighbors.
θnoutlier = (θn+1 + θn−1)

Although the ﬂow angle can be determined from the B-mode image, it has to be set manually
in the estimator. When working with curved vessels, this could provide incorrect angles. The con-
sequence of using an incorrect beam-to-ﬂow angle is avoided with the directional ﬂow estimation
scheme implemented in this study. In addition to that, study results showed that the beam-to-ﬂow
angle can be estimated with a standard deviation around 5 ◦.
When considering about the segment size, one could say a longer segment improves the esti-

with different velocities due to spatial velocity gradients. It could also contain echoes from scatter-

the search range of the global maximum is limited with respect to the maximum and minimum

Minimum and maximum sample shift are computed using 5.11, setting the velocity to minimum

The parameter study reveled that the number of directional lines in a data block has an inﬂuence
on the performance of the estimator. A relative mean bias closer to 0% can be achieved when
16 emission sets are employed for echo cancellation as well as ﬂow estimation. In addition to
that, both simulation and measurement studies showed that velocities can be estimated with a
relative standard deviation lower than 4%. Simulated 75◦and measured 105◦phantoms showed
the best relative mean bias and relative standard deviation out of all the data sets. No notable
differences exist in relative bias and relative standard deviation in between Hamming and Hanning
window functions. Both simulation and measurement showed that angle estimation by directional
beamforming can be conducted with a high precision, potentially yielding velocity estimates with
high precision.

6.3.2
Selection of a line segment and angle estimation
A ﬂow-mode image is created according to the approach stated in Chapter 5.3.1 to visualize the
blood vessel, and to select the desired points for pressure estimation. The line segment span from
a lateral depth (x) of -5 mm to 5 mm equally spaced by 1 mm, at the center of the vessel (z = 37
mm). Each point of the selected line segment is then beamformed for a set of angles that range from
0◦to 180◦with an angle sampling interval of 5◦along 5 mm long lines. Sampling of these lines
is done with an interval of ∆x of 0.02 mm, making them 250 samples long. Similar to previous
simulations, data are processed in blocks of 16 directional lines from 5 ﬂow emissions. Each block
is subjected to stationary echo cancellation, where the mean of the 16 lines are subtracted from
each line. Echo cancelled data blocks are taken as an input to the implemented angle estimator.
The angle estimator then estimates a ﬂow angle for each point in the line segment according to the
approach stated in Chapter 5.3.3.
6.3.3
Velocity Estimation
Here a low-resolution image is created for each emission and multi-directional lines are beam-
formed for every point of the line segment along their respective ﬂow angles. The beamformation
is carried out for 8 cardiac cycles. Directional line length and sampling interval are the same as in
angle estimation scheme. Each data block gives a total of 80 emissions, accounting for a duration
of 7.1 ms. During this time period, the velocity is assumed to be constant. Data blocks of each posi-
tion of the line segment, one at a time are echo cancelled and fed to the directional ﬂow estimator
described in Chapter 5.3.4. As the outputs, velocity estimate along with the set of correlated signals
(15 signals) of the particular data block at the speciﬁc point, are returned. Correlated signals are
needed for the acceleration estimation scheme.

The summary of the approach is shown in Figure 6.2. Already cross-correlated signals are
fed as an input to the acceleration estimators as indicated in the block diagram. The temporal
acceleration estimator takes two consecutive segments at the same position but at different time
instances as inputs. For the spatial acceleration estimator, the two segments are at the same time

the two segments are then cross-correlated once again. For example, signal 1 (Rx1) in segment 1
would be correlated with signal 1 (Rx1) in segment 2 and signal 2 (Rx2) in segment 1 would be
correlated with signal 2 (Rx2) in segment 2. The procedure repeats so on and so forth. Skipping
steps are employed when necessary, for instance when the cross-correlated estimates are too noisy.
The mean is computed and the peak is scanned. Sample-shift is obtained by performing parabolic
interpolation. Finally, the temporal or spatial acceleration is computed. This procedure is repeated
for all time instances and positions.

pressure gradients, the pressure drop that exists across the two ends of the streamline is obtained.

can be observed that, the individual pressure estimates overlap well with a relative mean standard
deviation of 3.05 %.
6.5
Discussion
The implemented temporal and spatial acceleration estimators are independent of the velocity
estimates. This prevents the noise in velocity estimates as a result of jitter or decorrelation effects,
from being transferred to the acceleration estimates [33]. Since the derived pressure gradients rely
strongly on the acceleration estimates, having an independent acceleration estimator potentially
leads to more reliable pressure results.
Averaging has also been performed when obtaining both velocity and acceleration estimates
as a method of noise cancellation. Prior to obtaining the velocity for a data block of a particular
position of the streamline, correlation functions are averaged. This essentially performs low-pass
ﬁltering of the estimates. It should be emphasized that, averaging will only work if the cross-
correlation functions within the block have the same position of the maximum peak [5]. This
applies to a stationary ﬂow. For an accelerated ﬂow, a gradual shift in the position of the peak
will occur due to varying velocity. To avoid de-correlation in the estimate of the cross-correlation
function, there should at most be half sampling interval shift due to acceleration [5]. Since the
averaging is carried out over a period as short as 7.1 ms, the ﬂow within that period is assumed
to be stationary. Similarly, prior to obtaining the acceleration estimates, the correlation functions
between two data blocks at the same position or between two positions of the same data block are
averaged.
This pressure estimation approach has the usual advantages of synthetic aperture imaging. Data
can be acquired continuously. Moreover, the position of both emitting and receiving sources are
known. Data for ﬂow estimation is therefore continuously available throughout the entire image.
This permits the focusing of data in any direction and since the angle estimator precisely estimates
the beam-to-ﬂow angle, the signals for ﬂow estimation can be focused along the ﬂow lines. In
addition to that, it allows averaging across estimates without compromising the peak of the ﬂow
proﬁle, ensuring more robust velocity and acceleration estimates. This makes it possible to average
over large amounts of data, which in turn eases the echo cancelling process [28]. It is not required
to consider the initialization effects when designing an echo-cancelling ﬁlter, since the ﬂow data
are continuous. This is especially advantageous for a ﬂow with low velocity.
Throughout this study, stationary echo cancellation has been performed by a simple subtraction
ﬁlter. However, when there is presence of strong tissue motion in the surrounding tissue, the echo
cancellation scheme needs to be improved. This can be done by ﬁtting a higher order polynomial to
a segment of directional lines, as illustrated in Figure 6.11 . Subsequently taking the mean value of
the segment after the ﬁt, and subtracting it from all the directional signals within the segment will
result in Figure 6.12. This was added as an optional echo cancellation technique to the proposed
setup. The technique was inspired by [38].

Project Conclusion
The aim of the dissertation was to address the question that, is it possible to estimate pressure
gradients using synthetic aperture imaging data with a standard deviation lower than the existing
BK program. The algorithm of the research study is based on Navier-Stokes equation for incom-
pressible ﬂuids. The pressure estimation algorithm mainly depends on the temporal and spatial
acceleration estimators. These estimators deviate from the traditional acceleration estimation of
computing velocity derivatives, but rather, follow the double cross-correlation approach proposed
by [33]. Thus, being independent of the velocity estimates. The dissertation includes the results
of three studies.
Study I focused on implementing a conventional velocity estimator and a temporal acceleration
estimator. The performance of these estimators were tested using a simple simulation model which
mimics accelerating blood ﬂow. The estimators performed well with a low relative mean standard
deviation closer to 1% and a low relative mean bias. However, this estimator could only estimate
velocity along the steered ultrasound beam, i.e axial velocity. Therefore, the estimator was further
developed such that velocities along the direction of blood ﬂow can be estimated.
The performance of this directional ﬂow estimation algorithm was evaluated using straight
vessel phantom data in Study II. Usage of synthetic aperture imaging data added several advantages
to the implemented algorithm. The main advantage being, ability to estimate in any direction
within the ultrasound imaging plane. Results of both simulation and measurement straight vessel
phantom studies revealed that velocities can be obtained with a relative mean standard deviation
of 0.75% −0.90% and a relative mean bias less than 0.63% for a beam-to-ﬂow angle of 75◦. The
approach also worked for fully transverse ﬂow yielding a relative mean standard deviation around
3.30% for simulated data and 3.66% for measured data. The relative mean bias was 0.08% and
2.64% for simulated and measured data respectively.
The next main step of the research study involved further improving the temporal acceleration
estimator developed in Study I and developing an estimator to derive the spatial acceleration di-
rectly from beamformed data. These individual estimators are the main elements of the pressure
estimation set-up. Navier-Stokes model for incompressible ﬂuid, takes these estimates as inputs
and delivers the pressure gradients along the streamline, as outputs. In Study III, these estimators
were combined, to investigate the intravascular pressure gradients in common carotid phantom
data. The directional ﬂow estimator performed well with a relative mean standard deviation of
3.89%. Similarly, using the acceleration and velocity estimates, the pressure gradients could be de-
rived with a relative mean standard deviation of 3.05%. The comparison of the derived estimates,
with BK program, revealed similar results for velocity estimates. There are mismatches between the
derived pressure estimates and the BK pressure estimates. The difference in employed techniques
could be a reason for this.
Overall, the proposed setup, with SAI data, manages to deduce pressure gradients with a stan-
dard deviation signiﬁcantly lower then BK program. Moreover, the accuracy test showed that the
==PAGE:94==

derived pressure estimates are in accordance with the FE model. The non-invasive nature of the
technique along with the ultrasound scanner’s ability to produce real time images offer a great ben-
eﬁt for routine pressure check ups. Moreover, this will enable a clinician to monitor the temporal
evolution of the pressure drop across a desired location of a blood vessel over a single or multi-
ple cardiac cycles. However, additional studies including in-vivo studies, are required to further
evaluate the correctness and the performance of the proposed setup.

APPENDIXB
Initial Project Plan
This chapter describes the initial project plan which includes a brief overview, containing the re-
search hypothsis, employed research methods, the learning objectives. Finally, the chapter con-
cludes with the initial and revised time plans.
B.1
Project overview
B.1.1
Hypothesis
The present study hypothesizes that, by means of synthetic aperture imaging data and using an
algorithm based on Navier-Stokes equation and directional velocity estimation along ﬂow direction,
is it possible to estimate the intravascular pressure gradients with a better accuracy than the existing
BK algorithms.
B.1.2
Research methods and aims
This research study mainly focuses on implementing an algorithm for estimating pressure gradients
using data obtained from synthetic aperture ultrasound imaging. The study is initiated with devel-
oping a ﬂow estimator for estimating velocities and accelerations using conventional ultrasound
imaging data. This estimation scheme is further developed to a directional ﬂow estimator which
can estimate the velocity along the blood ﬂow.
The study aims to:
1. Develop a simple simulation model of accelerating ﬂow.
2. Develop the cross-correlation estimator to compute velocity and acceleration.
3. Test the developed estimator on conventional ultrasound imaging data and validate using
standard deviation and bias.
4. Optimize the ﬂow estimation scheme to estimate velocity along the ﬂow and test it on syn-
thetic aperture imaging data.
5. Implement the ﬁnal part of the estimator for deriving the pressure gradients using temporal
and spatial acceleration data.
6. Apply the proposed estimator on common carotid synthetic aperture imaging phantom data
and validate using standard deviation and bias.
7. Compare the results of the proposed estimator with that of the BK program.
