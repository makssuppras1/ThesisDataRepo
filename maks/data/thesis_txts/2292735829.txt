Abstract
Carbon emissions from fossil fuels are believed to be the main factor behind global warming and
climate change. A permanent subsurface carbon dioxide storage in underground hydrates is a possi-
ble way to reduce the atmospheric carbon content. Methane hydrates can be converted into carbon
dioxide hydrates, hence methane gas can be produced simultaneously and while carbon dioxide is
injected. The experimental work performed in this project was to provide a better understanding
of the processes aﬀecting carbon dioxide injection into methane hydrate reservoirs. This was done
by saturating a sandstone core and applying pressure and temperature within hydrate formation
conditions. The methane hydrate formation was considered successful after pressure drop corre-
sponding to a gas consumption. A swapping gas, either a carbon dioxide or a ﬂue gas, was injected
in to the core, containing hydrates and the feasibility proven by kinetics and thermodynamics and
conﬁrmed by experiments. When a stable pressure occurred in the system, a gas sample from the
outlet was collected. The swapping mechanism was considered successful with a mole fraction of
methane in the data, analysed by a gas chromatograph. The swapping was further evaluated with
recovery calculations by using equation of state together with volume and pressure data. The best
obtained recovery showed 88.30% and other result showed 38.43%. The main diﬀerence between
those two experiments that might explain the diﬀerence, was that the former core was injected 3
times with methane while only one injection was made into the other. The experiments that were
successful, showed a correlation between the pressure and gas chromatograph data, and the results
obtained from the recovery calculations. For the experiments where brine was used instead for
demineralized water and ﬂue gas was used instead for pure carbon dioxide no signiﬁcant changes
were seen.
Key words: Methane gas hydrate; Carbon dioxide hydrate; Swapping; Gas hydrate production;
Carbon dioxide-storage; Flue gas mixture.

Introduction
Natural gas hydrates have gotten increased attention for the past years for two main reasons, their
potential for meeting the worlds growing energy needs and their ability to create a permanent stor-
age for carbon dioxide deep in the ocean and thereby reduce the concentration of carbon dioxide
in the atmosphere.
Former experiments (Ring, 2014) (Prag, 2014) have shown that the hydrate formation in porous
medium is possible and results have given a promising indication that swapping process has oc-
curred. This project contains continued work former performed by Martin Ring in his master
thesis ’Production of natural gas from hydrates with and without CO2 capture’ and by Christian
Vilhelm Prag in the thesis ’Study in formation of methane gas hydrates and their production with
CO2 and CO2/N2 as a swapping gas’. Experimental procedures together with their results and
observation will be used as a guideline throughout the experimental work. Christians idea on con-
necting two coreholders in series to obtain twice as much data within the same time frame as well
as his idea on using a cooling bath instead for refrigeration unit, were used with good performance.
Further improvements were done, including; ﬁve step saturation of the core and periodic methane
injection. The ﬁve step saturation was an advise from Alsu Khusainova, after she had used the
method with a good performance. This method is theoretically supposed to provide a better sat-
uration of the core. The periodic methane injection involved injecting methane after the system
had reached equilibrium pressure following the ﬁrst injection. The additional injection ensured
suﬃcient amount of methane during the experiment.
The ﬁrst experimental objective was to create in-situ conditions for a hydrate formation. A satu-
rated sandstone core acting as a porous media reservoir was exposed to a temperature and pressure
within the hydrate phase envelope. Methane gas was injected and a pressure drop indicated con-
sumption of the gas and thereby conﬁrm a hydrate formation.
The next objective was to replace the methane in the hydrates by injecting carbon dioxide or
a binary mixture of carbon dioxide and nitrogen (ﬂue gas). This process is referred to as swapping
and is carried out under isothermal conditions and a pressure just above the methane hydrate
dissociation pressure.
The last objective was to establish the feasibility of methane production. The methane production
involves a quantitative calculation of how much methane gas is consumed during hydrate formation
and how much methane is produced after swapping. The calculations were carried out by using
the SRK equation of state. The volume of methane and the swapping gas, injected into the core,
will be controlled and measured and the pressure change monitored. Equilibrium gas from the
experiment will be collected and analysed with a gas chromatograph. The data from the GC is
used to evaluate the gas composition and by knowing the composition the methane-recovery can
be estimated.

Peak oil and the future of energy
Modern life is highly dependent on fossil fuels, the demand grows every year whereas the oil indus-
try has realized that conventional oil reserves are depleting rapidly. Up to date, oil and natural gas
are the primary fuels but the world is moving from an era of cheap abundant energy, conventional
reserves, to an era of scarce hard to get energy, unconventional reserves.
Every June since 1963, the BP Statistical Review of World Energy publishes a consistent review
on the world energy markets. The newest numbers from June 2014 shows that the consumption
of all energy sources, except nuclear energy, has increased and reached an all time record in 2013.
Furthermore it shows that the global consumptions of fossil fuels increased by 2.3% in 2013, a
third consecutive below-average. Figure 1, presented by the BP Statistical Review shows how the
worlds primary energy consumption has developed from 1988 to 2013. China is once again the
winner when it comes to growth increment in consumption, followed by the United States. A lot
of diﬀerent predictions have been made regarding next decades of energy consumption, the results
show unsurprisingly a steep increase. (n/a, 2014a)

concern it is essential to ﬁnd alternatives. That is why commercial production of methane hydrates

2.2
Hydrates as a potential energy resource
Burning coal, natural gas and oil has been a known concept for more than hundred years, but the
existence of hydrates was only discovered in the 1960s, when a Soviet oil crew located a reservoir
of methane hydrates while drilling in Siberia. Since this discovery, gas hydrates have attracted
interest as a potential energy resource, but a lot of research and development is still left before the
production can be considered feasible and economical. (Gelpke and Visbeck, 2010)
Everybody can agree upon that the future of energy will not be covered with one alternative
to oil, but rather with a variety of new or existing resources. For this reason it is surely inter-
esting to investigate hydrates further. (Makogon et al., 2005) Gas hydrates are known to be the
largest untapped source of energy. Researchers predict that at least twice as much carbon is stored
in hydrates compared to all conventional oil, gas, and coal combined. Present calculations have
an abundance range between 100 and 530.000 gigatons of carbon, but the values between 1.000
and 5.000 gigatons are most likely. (Gelpke and Visbeck, 2010) But not all of the hydrates are
in formations accessible for development. For instance, the Gulf of Mexico is particularly rich in
hydrates with total estimates around 400 gigatons, but only around 130 gigatons are considered
to be producible. (Maftei, 2015) For these and other reasons, hydrates have a great possibility to
become an energy source for the majority of the 21st century.
2.2.1
Advantages of hydrate production
There are a few advantages when production of hydrates is considered, the main advantage is
that the production can be considered carbon neutral. Gas hydrate production can be performed
through swapping where carbon dioxide is injected and natural gas, such as methane, is produced.
This production method is generally called swapping. Another beneﬁt of swapping is that it does
not create destabilization of the sea ﬂoor. When carbon dioxide is injected into the hydrate struc-
ture it replaces the methane and thereby works as a cement that maintains the structural stability.
(Maftei, 2015) Swapping as a production technique will be introduced later in more detail in sec-
tion 2.7.4.
Not only does methane create high energy density per weight, it is also more environmentally
friendly than gasoline and coal. Methane is known to be the cleanest burning petroleum based fuel
where the emissions are 30 and 60 times less than for gasoline and coal respectively. (Demirbas,
2010) Until now, drilling for hydrates in the ocean has not been considered proﬁtable nor feasible,
but if resource prices rise it could lead to attractiveness to the oﬀshore industry. The hydrate
production is considered economically feasible at an oil price of about 50 to 60 US dollars per
barrel which implies that the production is barely proﬁtable with today’s oil prices. (Gelpke and
Visbeck, 2010) Gas hydrate deposits have been located in every continent meaning that the ones

2.2.2
Gas hydrates stability zones
The hydrate formation zone (HFZ) is deﬁned as the dimensions of sediments in which the temper-
ature and pressure are within the stable regime for a gas hydrate at a speciﬁc composition. When
the salinity of water is increased the thickness of the HFZ decreases as well as with increased
bottom temperature the zone decreases. (Makogon et al., 2005)
Figure 2: Hydrates stability zones for onshore Arctic and oﬀshore marine areas. (Harms et al.,
2007)
In ﬁgure 2 the dark gray area in the generic curves illustrates the regions, in the Arctic and marine
sediments, where the temperature and pressure as well as the concentration of methane gas are
within the hydrate formation and stability zone. These curves are based on pressure-temperature
phase equilibrium data and reﬂection seismic data collected. Reﬂection seismology and recovered
core samples are primarily used to estimate methane hydrate reserves. Core samples provide direct
evidence for hydrates but are often hard to approach. In the Arctic areas the hydrates can be stable
from around 200 - 1100 m depth, but the range for the marine settings is a lot smaller, only from
1200 - 1500 m. The concentration of methane is insuﬃcient for hydrate formation in the depth
from 400 - 1200 m the. The dotted line in both ﬁgures is a phase boundary, it separates the
ice-water phase to the left and hydrate gas phase to the right.

2.2.3
Global locations of natural gas hydrates
For the last ﬁve decades, natural gas hydrates have been discovered in 79 countries. Potential re-
serves are estimated up to 1.5·1016 m3 of global gas-in-place in hydrates, in 230 natural gas hydrate
deposits, found all over the world. Among them, there are 11 huge gas hydrate mining areas around
North America where methane resources are estimated to be 5.8 · 1013 m3 of. Russia is also rich of
hydrate deposits areas, these areas are mainly distributed around the Black Sea, Barents Sea and
Okhotsk Sea regions. These methane hydrate resources are estimated to be around 3.1 · 1015 m3.
In China, the conﬁrmed gas hydrate resource is mainly distributed in the South China Sea and Qil-
ian Mountain permafrost. Methane deposits in Japan and India are also massive. (Liu et al., 2012)
Gas hydrate deposits exist in two basic forms, primary and secondary.
A primary deposit is
formed and it never melts, they are often found in deep waters where the temperature is stable
throughout time. Primary deposits are formed by the gases that are dissolved in the reservoir
water in sediments that are based near the sea ﬂoor. Secondary deposits are generally located in
the Arctic regime. They are formed from free gas deposits that are located under traps in the
sedimentary layer. The temperature decrease in these layers is lower than the stable temperature
of the gas. (Makogon et al., 2005)
Figure 3: Methane hydrate occurrences around the world (Ruppel and Noserale, 2012)
Figure 3 shows where the gas hydrates can be found both oﬀshore in marine deposits and onshore
around the Arctic, and how scattered they are around the world. Around 99% of gas hydrates are
in the ocean sediments and the remaining 1% is found in the permafrost areas located onshore.
(Ruppel and Noserale, 2012) The green dots indicate the occurrences in the northern onshore per-
mafrost regions, including Alaska, Russia and the West Sibera Basin. (Ruppel, 2015) Occurrences
discovered by geophysical methods onshore are marked with red. Finally, the blue dots are occur-
rences that have been veriﬁed by direct sampling.

2.2.4
Natural gas hydrates and global warming
Gas hydrates can only be formed and are stable under very speciﬁc conditions. For that reason
it is a great concern that the subsequence of global warming, warming of the oceans, could eﬀect
the stability of hydrates. The instability of the gas hydrates means that former trapped methane
can be released into the atmosphere, this causes increased global warming and more dissociation
of gas hydrates like ﬁgure 5 illustrates. The most important regions regarding methane emission
research are in and around the Arctic due to its delicate condition. In the Arctic the methane gas
is both in the form of gas hydrates and as free gas trapped in the permafrost layers. The release
of natural gas in the Arctic as well as other places can have some serious consequences. The gas
hydrates behave as a cement, it ﬁlls the pores in the porous medium and thereby supports the
seabed. If gas hydrates start to dissociate it could cause instability of the seabed and in the worst
case scenario it could cause large parts of continental edges to fail, and submarine landslides could
cause tsunamis. (Gelpke and Visbeck, 2010)

Normally carbon dioxide is considered to be the most potent green house gas, but when methane

For theses reasons and many more it is urgent for researchers to participate in further investi-
gations of this subject and come up with solutions to extract the methane from the hydrates. One
of the methods that can be used is swapping, an exchange of carbon dioxide and methane, which
will be discussed in section 2.7.4 and is also the main subject of the experimental work in this
project.

2.3
Gas hydrates formation
This section demonstrates the conditions that need to be present in order to obtain hydrate for-
mation and the kinetics of the formation is explained.
2.3.1
Formation conditions
Methane hydrates can only form under very speciﬁc physical, chemical and geological conditions,
such as; thermodynamic regime, formation and migration of hydrocarbons, gas composition, gas
saturation degree and reservoir water salinity, structure of the porous medium, lithology, geother-
mal gradients of hydrate formation and basement rock zones and phase state of hydrate formers.
(Makogon et al., 2005)
Other important factors are that suﬃcient amount of water and organic materials (methane gas)
have to be in place. These organic materials are the remains of minute organisms or plankton that
have died in surface waters, sunk to the ocean ﬂoor and gradually transformed to sediments. The
best conditions for the formation are almost entirely located where the continental plates meet the
deep sea regions. The warmer the water, the more depth is needed for hydrates to develop. But
this is a thin line, because when certain depth is reached the temperature rises again due to the
Earth’s internal heat. (Gelpke and Visbeck, 2010)
2.3.2
Kinetics of formation
The gas hydrate formation process is often refereed to as crystallization process with nucleation,
growth, agglomeration and breakage. Nucleation usually starts at the gas-water interface where
the highest concentration of the gas is. As the gas concentration increases, the recently formed
crystals start to grow and after a while both agglomeration and breakage of the growing particle
can be detected. Many factors aﬀect the formation rate of the natural gas hydrates, these include
pressure, gas composition and temperature, also known as PVT eﬀects. Furthermore, the formation
rate is determined by heat and mass transfer as well as chemical reaction kinetics eﬀects. Cooling
is required to extract the hydrate heat of formation. Mass transport is essential to dissolve the
natural gas in the water and moreover to bring the dissolved gas molecules into contact with
growing hydrate crystals. (Demirbas, 2010)
Figure 6 shows the kinetics of the formation of hydrates. The most essential to enable the process
is that temperature and pressure are within the hydrate formation window. This P-T window can
be seen in the phase diagram in ﬁgure 8. Secondly, gas must be dissolved in the water such that
clusters are able to form immediately. There after the clusters agglomerate by sharing faces and
thereby increase disorder, ﬁnally when the size of the cluster agglomerates reaches a critical value,
the growth begins. (E.D. Sloan, 1998)

2.5
Physical and chemical properties of gas hydrates
Gas hydrates are white, ice-like solids that consist of gas, commonly methane gas, and water.
They are compounds where the gas molecules are trapped within a crystalline structure or a cavity
consisting of water molecules that are kept together by hydrogen bounds. Gas hydrates are stable
over a wide temperature and pressure range. For example methane hydrates can be stable from
70 - 350 K and 20 nPa to 2 GPa. (Gelpke and Visbeck, 2010)
One of the most exclusive properties of gas hydrates are their ability to concentrate and store
large volumes of natural gas within their water cage cavities.
For example, up to 207 m3 of
methane can be trapped in 1 m3 of water to form 1.26 m3 of solid hydrate, while without gas the
same amount of water freezes to form only 1.09 m3 of ice. Hydrates density can vary from 800 -
1200 kg/m3 depending on the gas composition, temperature and pressure. (Makogon et al., 2005)
2.5.1
The water molecule
Water has many usual properties which can mainly be explained by the structure of the molecule.
The water molecule is of interest because its structure leads to the opportunity of hydrate for-
mation, where hydrates are composed of 85% water and 15% gas. This high percentage of water
suggest that the mechanical properties of ice and hydrates are similar. Water as a liquid is the
most universal phase, followed by ice, on the surface of the earth. Water is found in a liquid phase
at standard pressure and temperature. The chemical formula is H2O, it consists of a single atom
of oxygen bonded to two hydrogen atoms by a covalent bond, which is a shared pair of electrons.
These electrons induce negative charges on the oxygen molecule and a small positive charge on
the hydrogen atoms, resulting in a net positive charge on the hydrogen atoms and a net negative
charge on the oxygen atom, which moreover results in a dipole moment. The dipole moment causes
an electrostatic attraction between the molecules, known as hydrogen bonding, which explains why
water is polar and moreover its high boiling point, surface tension and capillary force. (Carrol,
2009) (Sloan and Koh, 2008)
Water is solid at high pressure and low temperature and in gas phase at high temperature and
low pressure. Water coexist in a stable equilibrium at exactly 273.16 K temperature and a partial
vapour pressure of 607.95 Pa. In this point, it is possible to switch between the three diﬀerent
phases by making quite small change in either pressure or temperature. (Demirbas, 2010) Water
expands when it freezes, the molecules align up and rearrange themselves to a hexagonal crystal
structure. This structure is extended in three dimensions and that is the reason why it takes up
more space than liquid water. This behaviour is due to the shape of the water molecule and the
hydrogen bonds. Furthermore, ice has a lower density when compared to water, 917 kg/m3 and
1000 kg/m3 respectively, which explains why ice ﬂoats on water. (Carrol, 2009)

2.6.1
Gas hydrate structure types and cavities
The crystalline structure of gas hydrates are hydrogen bonded which mean almost perfect tetrahe-

crystalline structure contains a speciﬁc geometrical water cage with diﬀerent size cavities which
typically accommodate only one guest molecule with diameter on the rage from 0.40 - 0.90 nm.

2.7
Production methods
Two of the main challenges that are known when it comes to promoting methane hydrates as an
energy resource are recovery and production. To capture the volume of gas trapped within the
gas hydrates the hydrate bearing sediments must be made available by drilling. The methane
production can by accomplished with few techniques, these techniques are either based on moving
the system conditions outside of the phase boundary, making the hydrates unstable or by physically
substitute or swapping another molecule into the hydrates. The methods that are used to shift
the initial phase equilibrium conditions of the hydrate are thermal stimulation, where the local
temperature in the well is increased, depressurization, where the pressure is lowered in the well
and by inhibitor injection, where inhibitors such as methanol are injected. The gas exchange, or
the swapping mechanism, is when a gas is injected into the hydrates and thereby swapped with
the gas that is trapped within the crystalline structure. (Makogon et al., 2005)
2.7.1
Depressurization
The depressurization involves removing part of the free gas layer from the reservoir. This results
in a pressure decrease below the equilibrium phase values, making the hydrates unstable, causing
them to dissociate into natural gas and liquid water. This technique is favourable for reservoirs
with a depth of more than 700 meters and high permeability and is currently considered to be the
most promising method from both an economical and environmental point of view, since no input
of energy is necessary and no chemicals are needed. (Liu et al., 2012)
2.7.2
Thermal stimulation
The process involves dissociating hydrates by increasing the in situ temperature above the equilib-
rium point by injecting hot water, either normal or brine, or steam. The external heat source used
for injection can be a conductive heater, which heats the material directly with high temperature
liquid or air, or a radio frequency heater that heats the material by vibrating free water molecules.
The advantage of using this method is that the hydrate decomposition process and the gas pro-
duction can easily be controlled by adjusting the amount of heat injected.
When this process is looked at more closely it is not considered economical. The energy needed is
driven by the thermal characteristics of the hydrate-bearing sediment which makes it impossible
to extract hydrates from the permafrost area where the ambient temperature is very low and the
permafrost layers are too thick. (Liu et al., 2012) The energy depends on the heat capacity of the
hydrate, the heat capacity of the hydrate saturated layers, the speciﬁc amount of hydrate in the
layers and the degree of supercooling that caused the formation of the deposit. This means that
under some conditions the energy needed, to dissociate the hydrates, can be from 10% to even
exceed the potential produced energy. (Makogon et al., 2005)

2.7.3
Chemical inhibition injection
This method involves injection of thermodynamic inhibitors, such as brine, methanol or glycol,
into the reservoir. Theses inhibitors lower the hydrate formation temperature, which results in
dissociation of the gas hydrates. The advantage of this method is that the gas production rate
can be improved fairly quickly without much eﬀort. But this technique is both unfriendly to the
environment, since it involves injection of pollutant chemicals, as well as economically unfavourable
because these inhibitors are expensive. (Liu et al., 2012)
The formation of gas hydrates in transportation and production pipelines in the oil industry is
a known issue and can cause serious ﬂow assurance problems, possibly leading to catastrophic ef-
fects in the oil and gas industry. To prevent blockage of the pipelines the industry injects inhibiting
chemicals, similar to the once that can be used in hydrate production.
2.7.4
Swapping
The idea behind the swapping mechanism is injecting a gas, often carbon dioxide, into subsurface
reservoirs. This swapping methane gas displaces some of the gas in the hydrate crystal struc-
ture, converting simple methane hydrates into either simple carbon dioxide hydrates or double
methane-carbon dioxide hydrates. Studies have shown that this is possible in theory because car-
bon dioxide both forms hydrates at lower pressure and reacts spontaneously with methane hydrate.
The method is also non-destructive and does thereby leave the geomechanical stability of the reser-
voir undamaged since the pore space is re-occupied by the carbon dioxide. No chemicals need to
be injected using this method, which also leaves no contaminated water to be disposed. The only
chemicals used are carbon dioxide and nitrogen leaving minimum risk of environmental pollution.
(Jadhawar et al., 2006)
This technique can have its advantages both from a ﬁnancial and a climate perspective. If this
concept could become economically viable, it would be a win-win situation, because natural gas
can be produced from the hydrates on the same time as carbon dioxide is captured. (Gelpke and
Visbeck, 2010)
Similar experimental work, as performed during this project, has been made in Heriot-watt Univer-
sity where glass beads were used to act as the reservoir sediment. The glass beads were saturated
with distilled water and placed under thermodynamic conditions where both carbon dioxide and
methane hydrates were stable. The methane was injected and left to stabilize over night where
a sharp decrease in the cell pressure was discovered, indicating formation of hydrates. Injection
of carbon dioxide was completed in two to three injection cycles. Results showed that after 206
hours, carbon dioxide replaced 8.3% mole of methane. (Jadhawar et al., 2006)
Only limited experimental work has been done to try and use hydrates for large scale carbon
dioxide sequestration. A project collaboration between the US NETL, ConocoPhillips and the
Japanese in Alaska, involves injecting a blend of supercritical carbon dioxide and nitrogen into the
hydrate formation in order to produce methane gas. This was done to create the proper thermody-

3.3
Experimental procedure
Before any experiments were made, the core was measured, cleaned and ﬁnally saturated with
water. The experimental test consisted of the following three steps: methane hydrate formation,
injection of carbon dioxide in the methane hydrate-vapour system and lastly, measurement of
methane production through replacement in hydrates by carbon dioxide over gas chromatograph.
3.3.1
Sandstone core preparation
3.3.1.1
Core measurements
Length and diameter are measured three times with a caliper and a weighted average is taken
of the measurements. The weight of the sandstone is also measured. These measurements are
taken in order to calculate the volume and the pore volume of the sandstone and further more to
calculate the porosity, once the saturated weight has been taken.
3.3.1.2
Cleaning process
Prior to saturation, the core needs to be cleaned in order to remove any contamination. The core
was packed in a rubber sleeve and inserted into a core holder, where two pistons hold it in each
end. These pistons are featured with a tunnel, allowing liquid or gas injection into the core. The
core holder was connected to an Isco pump and a Eldex pump.
The Isco pump pumps water around the rubber sleeve, this was done to make sure that the
pressure around the core was higher than the injection pressure. During all cleaning procedures
the sleeve pressure was ﬁxed at a value of 50 bar. The Eldex pump was used to inject toluene and
methanol into the core. The amount injected can be controlled and was set to 1 cc per minute.
The cleaning is completed when the waste water from the system appears clean, which was usually
after 100 - 150 cc of each cleaning liquid.
The core was then taken out of the core holder and placed in an oven over night to make sure
leftovers of the cleaning liquids will evaporate. A diagram of the experimental setup as well as
photos taken of the equipment are found in section 3.2.1.
3.3.1.3
Five step water saturation
Before the saturation process can start, the core was taken out of the oven and left to reach room
temperature. A so called ﬁve step water saturation, which is a vacuum saturation, is performed.
(Lunde, 2013) This method is supposed to give substantially better saturation then the ones that
have been used in past experiments such as in Christians (Prag, 2014) and Martin (Ring, 2014).

will be injected into one end of the core and simultaneously produce methane gas from the other
end. The swapping gas was injected at an overpressure of 40 bar and the system is left uninterrupted
for 24 hours. The experimental setup and photos of the equipment are found in section 3.2.2.

Results and discussions
In this section the results and discussions regarding the experimental work during this project
are presented. Every experiment is described and any improvements are stated. Plots from the
formation/swapping part together with gas chromatograph composition indication of swapping
are shown and most importantly the results from the calculations attempts towards quantitative

5.1
Hydrate formation and swapping
For all experiments the temperature in the cooling bath was kept stable at 4◦C and the sleeve
pressure at 100 bar. The cooling bath had an 0.1◦C accuracy and the Isco pump 0.1 bar accuracy.
Any changes in the sleeve pressure and temperature in the cooling bath were so low that they could
be neglected. The temperature inside the cylinder could not be monitored, which meant that the
temperature change corresponding to pressure changes of the hydrates could not be observed.
Since the hydrate formation occurrence is not entirely based on temperature observation, this was
not a major issue. After every experiment a consideration for possible improvements were made
regarding the setup, the swapping gas and water saturation. These changes are introduced in the
beginning of each experiment.
5.1.1
Experiment 1 - Methane hydrate replaced by carbon dioxide
The ﬁrst experiment was done to check the equipment and see if hydrate formation occurred
and how long time it would take. During this experiment, one core was used and the volume
of the methane and carbon dioxide gas injected was not considered.
This meant that the gas
chromatograph results gave only an indication with mole fraction of the swapping process but no
quantitative analysis of the production could be carried out.
5.1.1.1
Methane hydrate formation and swapping
A core with 9.06 cm3 pore volume and 23.09% porosity, was saturated with demineralized water.
In this experiment the methane gas, for hydrate formation, and the carbon dioxide, for swapping,
were only injected once. The pressure development for the hydrate formation and swapping is
shown in ﬁgure 24. No induction period was recorded prior to the hydrate formation. Instead a
rapid pressure drop from 80 to 38 bar, which indicates hydrate growth, appears after around half
a day. The system had a dissociation pressure of 38 bar and thereby the driving force was 42 bar.
At day three, carbon dioxide was injected, with 40 bar overpressure, at 78 bar. The swapping
process shows a slow decrease in the pressure from day three and the system reaches equilibrium
on day six at a pressure around 65 bar. On day 8, a little cylinder was connected to the outlet
of the system and the equilibrium gas was collected and furthermore run trough a GC. When the
sample was taken the pressure went down to 15 bar and later up again to 100 bar and within one
day.

Conclusions
This project investigated the production of natural gas from hydrates, initially with a literature
review that was followed by experimental work. The most important aspect was to quantify the
methane production of the hydrates.
The ﬁrst objective of this project was to form gas hydrates in a sandstone core. This was proven
to be successful in almost all of the experiments. An induction period was only observed in one
experiment, for core B in experiment 2.
This was probably caused by a high driving force in
most of the experiments. The driving force in experiment 2 was considerably lower and thereby
an induction period with metastability occurred. Although most of the experiments did not have
an induction period, a rapid decrease in the pressure was considered to indicate gas consumption
and thereby conﬁrm a hydrate formation. During experiment 4, core A(3) was saturated using
brine water instead of the normal demineralized water to investigate the inhibiting aﬀect on the
hydrate formation. Surprisingly, no diﬀerence was noticed and the system reached equilibrium
pressure in less than a day. Experiments 3 with core D and experiment 4 with core C(2) were
injected three times with methane. The results showed a better recovery using multiple injections.
It should be noted that only one experiment was perform using brine and three for the periodic in-
jection, hence it is not accurate to conclude any aﬀects from these improvements on the production.
The second objective was to conﬁrm that a swapping mechanism had occurred. A trace of methane
was found in all of the samples in the experimental reports from the GC analyse. This suggest
that swapping mechanism had occurred to some extent in the core. If the swapping had taken
place the equilibrium gas from the outlet would have been pure carbon dioxide. In experiment 4
with core C(2) was injected with a binary mixture and core B(2) with pure carbon dioxide. The
results indicated that the binary mixture gives a better yield. This is according to theory because
nitrogen accommodates the smaller cavities of the hydrate cage during swapping.
The ﬁnal and the main objective was to establish the feasibility of methane production and to
quantify the production from the hydrates. The results for the recovery calculations are promis-
ing.
The data from experiment 2 showed that the pressure exceeded the sleeve pressure and
therefore it was unlikely that swapping had occurred. The recovery calculations implied that as
well. In experiment 3 for core D methane was injected three times and the calculations showed
a very high value of 88.30% compared for a value of less than 40% for other experiments. In
experiment 4 the calculations gave a greater yield for the core injected with the binary mixture.
These three experiments showed a correlation between the pressure and GC data, and the results
obtained from the recovery calculations.
After summarizing and analysing the experimental data it revealed how very random hydrates
are, which indicates the stochastic or random nature of hydrate formation and swapping. Too
many variation of the setup and in the process, were made and thereby it was hard to compare
and analyse the outcomes within the time-frame of the project.

6.1
Recommendations for future work
Experiments suggested that quite little amount of the methane gas formed hydrates. This could be
related to the sediment, the sandstone core. Although it has high porosity, around 22-23%, there
were still some issues remaining. Improvements regarding changing the sediment could be a good
idea, for example using either packed sand or glass beads in a cell. A packed silica sand has been
used in (Linga et al., 2009). The sand has to be packed in such a way that it cannot penetrate from
the rubber sleeve into the coreholder and pipelines, which would cause blocking. One suggestion
would be to pack it in some kind of membrane or a ﬁlter that would allow the water to enter the
core but keeping the sand packed. Heriott-Watt university in UK has used glass beads acting as
a porous media placed into a cell to occupy 50% of the total cell volume and then saturated with
distilled water. (Jadhawar et al., 2006)
Another recommendation for improvement of the experimental setup is to use a refrigerator unit for
cooling. The cooling bath, currently used, surly keeps the temperature more stable over a period
of time but it is not possible to have the whole setup submerged in the bath. The inlet injection
cylinder and the outlet equilibrium cylinder are not in the bath. This means the temperature is
not constant throughout the system.

Chromatography is the separation of a mixture of

Injecting a sample into the GC. (This takes place at the

takes place inside the column in the oven.)

Detecting what compounds were in the sample. (This is 
done in the detector.)
During this process, status messages from the Agilent 7890A 
GC are displayed, and user changes to parameter settings 
can be made through the operating panel.
