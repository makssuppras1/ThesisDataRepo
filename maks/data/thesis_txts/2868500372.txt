Abstract 
This master thesis investigates the behavior and printability of metakaolin-based geopolymers for 3D 
printing. An experimental setup was created, using image capturing to measure dimensional changes 
and deformations during layer deposition. Cameras mounted on a 3D-printer was set up to capture three 
sides of the printed strands, and pixel coordinates converted to world-coordinates were used to obtain 
relevant dimensions.  
Focusing on the material behavior of different activator to metakaolin ratios, a series of experiments 
were conducted to evaluate printability, stability, curing-effect and wet-on-semi-solidified properties, 
when printing. Printability tests identified an optimal ratio-range for printing, where lower ratios 
resulted in poor extrudability, and higher ratios resulted in excessive material flow. Stability tests 
confirmed the ability to move the print bed without significant dimensional changes of the printed 
strand, while curing tests using an oven induced material hardening, shrinkage, and outer shell 
formation. Wet-on-semi-solidified printing enhanced layer stability and consistency, particularly for 
flowable mixtures, lowering the relative plastic deformation of lower layers. Rheological tests using a 
rheometer were performed to determine the material properties of different ratios, where the results 
were fitted to the Bingham rheological model, to provide necessary variables for the computational 
fluid dynamics (CFD) material models.  
A CFD model based on the geometry and dynamics of the experiments were set up, using two material 
models (Bingham and elasto-visco-plastic), to replicate and compare to the experimental results. The 
CFD results revealed that the Bingham material model struggled to replicate the deformation seen in 
experiments due to its limitations. In contrast, the elasto-visco-plastic model more accurately captured 
material behavior, reflecting curing effects of the material. With further refinements and expanded 
experimental data, this model shows promising potential for predicting the behavior of metakaolin 
geopolymers in 3D printing applications.

1. Introduction 
This chapter introduces the research context and objectives of the study. It outlines the background and 
motivation, defines the scope of the study and explains the structure of the report to make it easier for 
the reader to navigate. 
1.1. Background and Motivation 
3D printing has developed rapidly over the past years and is today a widely used tool in industries like 
aerospace, healthcare, construction and architecture. As technology progresses, new methods and tools 
are emerging. One of these is CFD modelling, which is becoming more common in the field. CFD 
modelling allows us to simulate and understand how materials and fluids behave during printing, 
making it easier to optimize processes and solve complex challenges. 
We have both been passionate about 3D printing for many years, starting as hobbyists working with 
traditional desktop 3D printers. Our interest deepened when we took part in the course 41789 
“Introduction to 3D printing - processes and theory” at DTU in 2022. This course gave us insight into 
more advanced 3D printing methods and introduced us to industrial-grade technologies that goes 
beyond standard hobby printers. 
What excites us a lot is the potential of 3D printing to be used in unconventional ways, such as in space 
or on extraterrestrial bodies. In these settings, traditional manufacturing methods aren’t practical, but 
3D printing could provide essential solutions. CFD modelling can play a key role here, helping to test 
and refine ideas under unique conditions. Taking up this project, we hope to get a deeper understanding 
of material properties and how to use and compare CFD simulations with experimental references, to 
achieve results that can be used for later studies. 
1.2. Research Objectives and Scope of Project 
The primary objective of this project is to investigate the material properties of metakaolin and optimize 
3D printing techniques for geopolymer applications. Through a combination of experimental testing 
and CFD modelling, the project aims to understand how varying material properties and curing 
processes will influence the 3D printing process and final print stability. Metakaolin is chosen for the 
project due to its ability to maintain shape after extrusion, the low material cost and possibility of easy 
adjustability of rheology. Furthermore, using one type of geopolymer makes comparability with other 
types of geopolymer possible, for example geopolymers based on lunar regolith. By comparing 
experimental results with CFD simulations, the project seeks to validate computational models and gain 
a deeper understanding of the behavior of geopolymer materials during the printing and curing stages. 
The scope of the project is structured into four main areas: 
Materials 
- 
Characterize the material properties of metakaolin. 
- 
Investigate how varying compositions of different mixtures will affect the geopolymerization 
process and their suitability for 3D printing. 
Experiments 
- 
Conduct a series of 3D printing experiments using a range of material mixtures with different 
properties to determine their printability window. 
- 
Develop an image capturing setup to perform dimensional analysis of the printed structures, 
which will be critical for assessing print accuracy, layer stability, and overall print quality. 
- 
Incorporate curing processes in the experimental setup to observe how the material’s stability 
and print quality changes as the geopolymer cures.

1.3. Thesis Structure 
This thesis begins with a literature review, where the most important principles are introduced, and

2. Literature Review 
In this literature review chapter, existing literature is examined to explore key principles regarding 3D 
printing technologies (focus on material extrusion additive manufacturing) and processes. The key 
principle in geopolymers is also examined. That being metakaolin, geopolymerization, critical shear 
rates and printability classification. Additionally, research on CFD modeling in additive manufacturing, 
wet-on-wet printing, and image-based dimensional measurement techniques in 3D printing are 
examined. 
2.1. 3D Printing Technologies and Processes 
In the Article [1], an overview of the landscape of 3D printing technologies is explained, showcasing 
the 7 most common families of additive manufacturing. Here the fused deposition modeling (FDM) is 
introducing as the most commonly known 3D printing process. According to [2], the FDM model is 
further explained, with most people being familiar with the process of heating material (thermoplastic, 
fibre-reinforced polymers or even plastics with metal powder) through a printhead and extruding 
through a movable nozzle that applies the material on a print platform, where the layers are partially 
fused together in a layer-on-layer method. FDM (or also known as fused filament fabrication, FFF) 
printing is a printing category under the overall Material Extrusion Additive Manufacturing (MEX-AM) 
category [3].  
Robocasting (RC) is another MEX-AM technology, also known as Direct-Ink-Writing [4]. In 
robocasting, high-concentration powder pastes, also known as inks, are used, and is particularly well-
suited for creating ceramic and composites [5].  
As explained in [6], 3D printing concrete (3DPC) is an emerging technique for constructing buildings 
and is one of the types of additive manufacturing (AM), building layer by layer. 3D printable concrete 
is a “tailor-made” material that can be extruded through a nozzle and maintain its shape under the 
gravitational load of subsequent printed layers without additional support [7]. Different materials that 
has been utilized in 3DPC can be different cementitious materials and geopolymers [8]. 
2.2. Geopolymerization Principles & Metakaolin 
The principle of geopolymerization and the role of metakaolin alongside an overview of printability 
evaluation is explored in this section. 
Metakaolin 
Metakaolin is a pozzolanic material produced by the thermal treatment of kaolin, a clay mineral 
primarily composed of kaolinite. The calcination process occurs at temperatures between 450–650°C, 
leading to the dehydroxylation of kaolinite and its transformation into metakaolinite, a highly disordered 
structure. While kaolinite is crystalline, metakaolinite is amorphous, making it highly reactive. 
Metakaolin is widely used as a supplementary cementitious material in concrete technology, improving 
durability, strength, and resistance to chemical attacks [9]. The primary chemical constituents of 
metakaolin are Silicon Dioxide (SiO₂) and Aluminium Oxide (Al₂O₃).  
Geopolymerization 
Based on the description provided in ref. [8], the process of geopolymerization can be divided into two 
partially overlapping stages. These are the dissolution and the gelation/polycondensation stages. In the 
dissolution stage, the aluminosilicate (metakaolin) comes into contact with the OH− rich activation 
solution, resulting in Si-O-Si and Si-O-Al covalent bonds being broken, releasing silicon and aluminum 
ions into the solution. These free ions form reactive precursors with a high number of Si-OH and Al-
OH groups. In the gelation/polycondensation stage, ionic species condense to form Si-O-Al and Si-O-
Si bonds, creating a 3D aluminosilicate gel stabilized by counter-ions from the activation solution.

The hardening process of geopolymers begins with the rapid formation of the Al-rich gel ("Gel 1"), in 
the gelation/polycondensation stage, that coats unreacted particles and forms a microstructural 
framework, causing an initial increase in elasticity. Over time, silica from the aluminosilicate particles 
becomes available, leading to the transformation of Gel 1 into a denser, Si-rich gel ("Gel 2") with 
enhanced connectivity and incorporation of tetrahedral aluminum. This transition significantly 
increases the mechanical strength of the geopolymer and marks the beginning of its physical setting. 
The study in [10] indicates that the content of sodium hydroxide (NaOH) in the activation solution 
affects the hardened properties of metakaolin based geopolymers. Showing that having lower amounts 
than optimal will negatively affect the strength development in the pastes, while having too much 
accelerates the setting time and increases shrinkage and cracking during hardening.    
Effect of temperature on curing 
In [11] and [12], the effect of temperature on curing is investigated. The curing of metakaolin-based 
geopolymers is strongly influenced by temperature, which significantly accelerates the curing process 
by enhancing the dissolution of aluminosilicates and promoting oligomerization and polycondensation 
reactions. The corresponding increase in mobility of reactive species and the faster chemical reactions 
is typically described by the Arrhenius law [13], which relates the reaction rate to temperature, 
highlighting the thermal dependence of geopolymerization processes. Studies have shown that higher 
temperatures shorten reaction times and facilitate faster development of the three-dimensional 
aluminosilicate network, as indicated by rapid increases in the storage modulus (G′). This shift reflects 
a transition from viscous to elastic behavior, yielding a more rigid and durable material. However, 
excessive heat may affect the microstructure resulting in unfavorable material properties. 
Critical shear rates for geopolymers 
To get an overview of the rheological characteristics of cementitious materials and an understanding of 
the how the minimum critical shear rate for the metakaolin geopolymer can be determined, the article 
[14] was used. 
The article provides a comprehensive analysis of the rheological behavior of cementitious materials, 
specifically cement grouts, emphasizing the relationship between shear stress and shear rate under 
varying flow conditions. One of the focal points of the article is the minimum critical shear rate, which 
is defined as the threshold shear rate beyond which the material transitions from unsteady flow to a 
steady homogenous shear-flow regime. It also discusses the implications of fitting the shear stress 
change with shear rate to the Bingham model, depending on whether the data gathered below critical 
minimum shear rate is included. Corresponding figures from [14] is shown in figure 1.

The results found in the article can be used as a basis for comparison, because the rheology of cement

a yield stress. For experimental determination of the critical shear rate, tests were performed using a

parallel plate tests. Therefore, the findings provide a valuable reference for the rheological assessment 
of metakaolin based geopolymers.  
Classification of printability status 
For determining which ratios of activator to metakaolin could be suitable for 3D-printing and have 
properties that are relevant, a printability analysis can be used. For characterization of printability status, 
where printed material is categorized as not-extrudable, printable or too-flowable, the framework from 
[8] can be applied. 
The printed material would be characterized as not-extrudable if the material isn’t able to be extruded 
or if it has discontinuities due to excessive stiffness or surface defects such as “Shark skin effect” or 
“macro porosity”. If the material instead is printable but can’t support the gravitational load of one or 
multiple layers, the material will be characterized as too flowable. The material is characterized as 
printable if the layers are printed without surface defects and can retain its shape for multiple layers, as 
illustrated in figure 2 [8] . 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The methodology used in [8] should be easily applicable as the mortars analyzed are based on fly ash, 
which should have very similar properties to metakaolin based on its high silica (SiO₂) and alumina 
(Al₂O₃) content. 
2.3. CFD Modelling in MEX-AM 
CFD modeling has emerged as a valuable tool in advancing 3D printing technologies by enabling 
simulation and analysis of fluid flow and material behavior during the printing process. Its main 
relevance lies in its potential to optimize print quality, improve process efficiency and reduce trial-and-
error experimentation, making it especially helpful for complex geometries and expensive material. 
However, CFD modeling also comes with limitations that constrain its application, such as 
computational costs, accuracy of material property models and combining with complex real-world 
phenomena like structural evolutions. CFD-modeling in MEX-AM includes many parameters that 
influence the results, and in order to achieve comparable simulations to experimental results, it is 
essential to test a wide range of settings and parameters. Several successful CFD models have been 
developed over recent years, capable of supplementing the understanding of the MEX-AM process 
alongside experimental examples. The studies in [15], [16] & [17] explores the morphological 
characteristics of the deposited layers during extrusion using CFD modeling of concrete material. CFD 
modeling can also be used for different materials like in the research presented in [18],  [19] & [20], 
where the focus is on composite materials, specifically the fiber orientation in the deposited layers.

CFD modeling can also be used for different 3D MEX-AM processes than just 3DCP, as explained in 
[21], [22] & [23], including both FDM printing and digital light processing (DLP) additive 
manufacturing. Varying the material parameters and comparing CFD modeling is also essential in 
understanding material behavior, as shown in [24] & [25].  Comparing CFD modeling to real-world 
experimental results is essential for validating the model and material parameters, as illustrated in the 
studies [26] & [17].   
For example, the paper [27] explains the need for numerical modelling and simulation, focusing on the 
possibility of preparing successful and cost-effective printing, especially when printing at a structural 
scale. Predicting the structural behavior during printing and calculating premature failure, can be used 
to adjust the printing strategy by changing materials, revising design and adjusting print settings. By 
using simulation, the waste of material and time can be greatly reduced. One interesting possibility with 
CFD modeling in 3D printing applications is the inverse material characterization technique, which is 
a method where a comparison between the experimental and simulations results can be used to calculate 
hard to predict behavior of the printing material, as explained in the paper. 
[15] investigates the interface between a mortar and nozzle wall, modeled using free-slip boundary 
conditions and compared to unusual no-slip boundary conditions using both a generalized Newtonian 
model and an elasto-visco-plastic model. The results using free-slip boundary conditions showed the 
best results, and it was furthermore concluded that the elasto-visco-plastic modeling matched with the 
experimental results almost completely. Simulations of 3D printing concrete with the incorporation of 
reinforcement bars was compared with experimental results in [16], where the concrete was modeled 
using a yield-stress dependent elasto-visco-plastic model, and the influence of material properties in the 
model was presented, with focus on the yield stress and plastic viscosity of the material. The results 
showed that the model could predict air void formations in the structure with high accuracy, and which 
effect the material properties in the model had on the results. Limitations of CFD was also discussed, 
where it was concluded that a simulation of a full 3DCP structure was not possible due to computational 
power limits.  
As shown in [24], many CFD simulations of multiple layer printing of viscoplastic materials were 
tested, changing the material parameters (yield stress and plastic viscosity) and printing settings 
(printing speed, extrusion speed, layer height and nozzle diameter) to see the effect on the stability of 
the print. In this paper, good inspiration for printing settings and material parameters can be used as a 
basis for similar studies (CFD or experimental studies).  
Another interesting perspective of CFD modeling in the context of MEX-AM is its ability to predict 3D 
prints that would otherwise be impossible to achieve experimentally – or would be extremely 
challenging and/or very costly. The study in [28], developed CFD models to simulate 3D printing of 
structures under altered gravity conditions, which would not be feasible to replicate on earth. Here, the 
focus was to investigate the properties of regolith mortar being printed on the moon. What was found 
in this study was that unstable prints under earth gravity might be stable with less deformation under 
lower-gravity environments, and that CFD modeling can be a crucial tool in understanding off-earth 
printing.  
In summary, CFD modeling plays a critical role in advancing 3D printing technologies, particularly in 
optimizing complex processes, reducing material and time waste, and enabling innovative applications 
such as inverse material characterization and off-earth printing. While CFD modeling accuracy has been 
demonstrated through several studies, some limitations of computational costs and complexity of real-
world phenomena remain challenging. None the less, CFD modeling offers crucial insights into hard-
to-replicate scenarios, driving innovation in 3D printing.

2.4. Printing Strategies 
Printing a layer of material on top of another already printed layer can be defined differently depending 
on the state of the materials. If a layer of fresh material is printed on top of a recently printed layer, 
where the material properties are assumed to be the same as for the fresh material, the printing strategy 
is known as wet-on-wet printing as explained in [24]. If the first layer is solidified before printing of 
the second layer, it is considered wet-on-solidified (or wet-on-dry), as discussed in the research paper 
[25], but can result in reduced interlayer bonding strength. In this paper, a strategy in between is also 
explained as wet-on-semisolid (or semi-solidified), where the first layer is considered semi solidified 
as the second layer is printed on top. 
When talking about wet-on-wet printing, there is a possibility of a collapse/print deformation due to the 
bottom layer(s) not being able to withstand the hydrostatic pressure created by layers extruded on top. 
According to [29], the two most common wall structure failure types are explained for 3D concrete 
printing. One failure possibility is elastic buckling, and the other is plastic collapse. Elastic buckling is 
caused by geometric instability, where printed structure bends or buckles under the load. The structure 
over time will bend and eventually fail due to instability, where plastic buckling is about exceeding the 
material’s strength limit. In [30], a simulation of the buckling-effect has been set up, where the curing 
of the concrete (and material parameters) can be put in as an input, to see the effect. Based on the results 
provided, there are several potential factors to work on, if buckling is an issue. For example, printing 
slower to increase the strength and stiffness development of the printed material or to tweak the material 
properties, e.g. mixing cement accelerator. 
Plastic collapse is when gravity-induced stress 
reaches the yield stress and initiates the 
material’s rheological yielding as explained in 
[31]. When the load of the new layer is applied 
to a freshly printed layer (Wet-on-Wet), the 
structure will deform permanently if the first 
layer’s ability to withstand them is exceeded. In 
general, with wet-on-wet printing, the material 
isn’t cured yet and will be susceptible to the 
load-induced deformation. In figure 3 from 
[29], the two different failure types are shown. 
In the video [32] by Concre3DLab, the two 
different failure modes are shown, with at third 
case potentially showing a combination of 
elastic buckling with plastic collapse. 
In the research paper [24], several CFD simulation of wet-on-wet printing was performed with various 
material parameters. With a low yield stress of the material, the layers deform due to the hydrostatic 
pressure generated by the layers on top, resulting in an actual printed height lower than the intended 
height. Higher yield stress resulted in smaller deformations due to hydrostatic pressure, and therefore a 
better height, showing the relation between the plastic deformation and yield stress of the material. In 
the paper, a Wet-on-Solid simulation was also set up, where the previous deposited material was 
solidified before deposition of a new layer. Here no plastic collapse was seen, and the print was stable. 
As a conclusion, the yield stress should be higher than the hydrostatic pressure.

3.1.2. Image Processing for Print Dimension Analysis 
To analyze the dimensions of the printed strand, cameras can be used to find pixel coordinates in the 
frame and then be transferred to real-world coordinates, which can then be used to calculate dimensions. 
To use cameras for dimensional measurements, a general understanding of camera-sensors and the pin-
hole model is needed, which can then be used to explain how the transformation from a pixel coordinate 
(from the picture) into the world coordinates is done. 
Pinhole camera model 
One of the simplest ways to describe how a camera works is by looking at the pinhole model, which 
can be described as a camera without a lens. As seen in figure 9 [45] on the left, the pinhole model 
works by having a surface (red plane) which blocks the light from reaching the second surface (green 
plane). By making a tiny hole (known in optics as an aperture), the light rays can pass through the point 
and thereby form an inverted image of the object on the green plane. In the real world, a setup like this 
would not work, since the amount of light passing through the small hole would be very small, and that 
is why the camera obscure (“dark room” in Latin) box seen on the right side of the figure, can be used. 
In this setup, only lights from the hole can enter the dark room, making the object more visible. This 
simple setup is what was later used in the development of photographic cameras. The distance between 
the object and the hole, and the distance from the hole to the image surface is very relevant for the 
image and how much of the object is visible on the image plane. 
 
Real camera model and fish-eye effect 
With the basics of the pinhole camera model explained, the images using a real camera can be explained. 
One of the main disadvantages of using a pinhole model is that the amount of light entering through the 
small hole is so limited, that the exposure time of the camera (the time from start of image recording to 
end of image recording) needs to be very high, often resulting in a blurry image or not being possible, 
if moving objects are in the image.

3.2. CFD Modelling 
In this chapter, the basis behind CFD modelling is explained through three key sections. First, a brief 
introduction to CFD provides an overview of its principles, applications, and importance in solving 
complex fluid flow problems. This is then followed by an explanation of the governing equations of 
fluid flow and the constitutive models, looking at the mathematical laws that explains fluid behavior.  
3.2.1. Introduction to CFD 
Computational fluid dynamics is the science of using computers to predict liquid and gas flow based on 
the governing equations of mass, momentum and energy [51]. By discretizing complex fluid domains 
into smaller manageable elements, CFD transforms the governing equations of fluid dynamics into 
solvable algebraic systems. In the early 1900s, the basis governing equations for fluid flow, known as 
the Navier-strokes equations were developed, with the turning point in the 1950s where the computers 
emerged, making answers to flow problems available at a much higher speed. From here on, the ability 
to solve more complex problems in less time evolved and is still under evolution, why CFD today is a 
great tool for modern problem-solving.  
3.2.2. Governing Equations 
The behavior of fluids is governed by fundamental laws of physics included in the Navier strokes 
equations. The Navier strokes equations are partial differential equations which describe the motion of 
fluid and are considered the primary governing equations for modeling the fluid behavior and based on 
the conservation equations for mass, momentum and energy. [51]. Below, the general equations of 
motion are shown and explained, including equations from the documentation package provided by 
Flow-3D.

Newtonian fluid model and generalized Newtonian fluid model 
The Newtonian fluid model describes a class of fluid in which the stress resulting from fluid motion is 
linearly proportional with the rate of strain. For a Newtonian fluid, the relationship between the viscous 
stress tensor (τ) and the rate of strain tensor (D) is given by: 
 
𝝉= 2𝜂𝑫 
(29) 
With η being the dynamic viscosity of the fluid (constant for Newtonian fluids), and D being the rate of

(same as in eq. 9) The key characteristic of a Newtonian fluid is that the viscosity does not depend on 
the rate of strain, and that the shear stress is directly proportional to the shear rate. 
The generalized Newtonian fluid (GNF) model extends the Newtonian fluid model to describe non-
Newtonian fluids, which exhibit more complex behavior. For these fluids, the viscosity is not constant 
but is dependent on the magnitude of the rate of strain (or shear rate). The stress tensor for a generalized 
Newtonian fluid is given by [54]: 
 
𝜏=  2𝜂(𝛾̇)𝑫 
(31) 
Meaning that it is a function of the shear rate (𝛾̇) dependent apparent viscosity. The shear rate (strain 
rate magnitude) can be expressed as:

(32) 
The key characteristics of the generalized Newtonian fluid model is that it includes a shear-dependent 
viscosity, meaning that the viscosity varies as a function of the shear rate, allowing for a wide range of 
behavior such as shear-thinning or shear-thickening.

4.3.3. Experiments 
In this section, the four 3D printing experiments are explained including what the focus of each 
experiment is. This is further explained in the section where the G-code, used for each experiment, is 
presented. 
Printability study 
For the printability study, a large variety of activator to metakaolin mixtures are test-printed. Here one 
layer of material is printed, and using the cameras, the dimensions of the printed strand is measured 
initially after the printing. Furthermore, the printed strand is visually inspected for imperfections. The 
aim of this study is to find a printability window based on the various mixtures and to determine which 
mixtures can be categorized as printable or too flowable. In the next section, the methodology behind 
the printability evaluation is explained. 
Stability & Curing test 
For the stability & curing test, two areas are of interest. First is to determine if and how to move the 
printed strand to the oven, without disturbing the print and therefore possibly changing the geometry of 
the printed strand. The other area of interest is to see what effect the curing has on the printed strand 
(visually and dimensionally). Like the printability study, only one layer is printed in this test. The 
stability evaluation is explained in the next section. 
Wet-on-Semi-Solidified printing 
For the Wet-on-Semi-Solidified printing experiment, two tests of several mixtures are printed. Firstly, 
the wet-on-wet printing of 2 layers is performed, which is the reference for a following wet-on-semi-
solidified printing tests. Here the exact same procedure is performed but includes a movement of the 
first layer to the oven, for at curing at 50°C for 2 minutes, followed by a second layer print of fresh 
(wet) material, hence the name. What is interesting here is to see the effect of having the first layer 
cured for 2 minutes in the oven, and to analyze the plastic deformation of the first layer with and without 
the curing for different mixtures. 
5-Layer Wet-on-semi-Solidified printing 
This experiment follows the same procedure as for the 2-layer wet-on-semi-solidified print but includes 
5 layers. In this case, after extrusion of a layer, the print-bed is moved to the oven for 2 minutes, resulting 
in the first layer experiencing more time in the oven than the layers above. In this experiment, one 
mixture which has shown interesting results are chosen for the 5-layer test, and a second mixture, which 
is prone to failure (high amount of activator to metakaolin) is tested. In this test, the plastic deformation 
of layers 1-4 are analyzed, and what effect the curing in the oven has on the final print. Each test is also 
performed as a wet-on-wet printing, which is used for comparison.

4.3.4. Printability Evaluation & Stability Testing 
To determine the printability of various mixtures, the printability evaluation must be predefined. In 
general, if the material can be printed in multiple layers with a small (or no) dimensional deformation, 
the mixture is printable. Around the printability window, two other scenarios are possible. When a 
mixture has a high viscosity, the dimensional deformation might be small, but other factors must be 
included to determine if the mixture is printable or not. With a mixture that results in many artifacts 
when printing, or when the printer is not able to print the material due to the needed high extrusion 
pressure, the mixture is categorized as “non-extrudable”. On the other hand, if the mixture is very 
flowable, which results in high deformation, especially when printing another layer on top of an already 
printed (not hardened) layer, the mixture is categorized as “too flowable”. Based on a printability test 
performed by printing one layer, a printability window can be determined. Figure 24 shows how the 
general printability window is determined for the mixtures. 
As seen on the figure, a printable mixture might not have the desired and expected layer height, and 
here a minimum layer height will be determined based on the overall results from the different mixtures. 
The non-extrudable window is based both on the layer height of the printed strand and visual 
documentation of possible defects in the printed strand, such as macro-porosities and shark-skin effect. 
As explained, the curing of the printed strand will happen by physically moving the print-bed with the 
printed strand into an oven (for curing) for a specific time-period, where the print-bed is then moved 
back to the printer and placed at the same position, for the second layer to be printed on top. To prove 
that the movement of the printed strand is not affected by the movement to and back from the oven, the 
stability of the printed strand must be verified. Here the dimensions before and after the movement is 
measured to confirm the dimensional stability of the printed strand, as indicated in figure 25. 
The print-bed here is the acrylic plate, that can be removed from the print-setup when curing is needed. 
With very flowable mixtures, the stability criteria is that the printed strand does not deform during the 
movement, and the overall dimensions of the printed strand (which can be confirmed with the cameras) 
will stay the same before and after the movement.

4.4. CFD Modelling Setup 
This section will go through the CFD model set up in Flow-3D and will include multiple under 
categories related to the modelling process, the implementation of Bingham and elasto-visco-plastic 
rheological models, as well as modifications done to Flow-3D’s source code to make simulations with 
changing material parameters over time.  
4.4.1. Physics Models 
In the GUI of Flow-3D, several physics models are selected and activated for the simulation of the 
extrusion and deposition of material. The activated models are: 
Gravity and Non-Inertial - Where the gravitational acceleration affecting the material is entered.  
Moving and Simple Deforming Objects - Activates the general moving objects model in Flow-3D, 
which can simulate the rigid body motion and enable the prescribed motion of the nozzle to affect the 
material being printed. 
Viscosity and Turbulence - Provides options for different turbulence models and wall shear stress 
boundary conditions. Here it was chosen to use the “No turbulence (laminar)” option as the flow exiting 
the nozzle is assumed to be laminar and it should have “calculate wall shear stress” activated to enable 
no slip conditions to be applied to the nozzle walls.  
Scalars – Enables the definition of scalars that can be modified by the user to include scalar variables 
and can be used in the customization of the Flow-3D source code. The scalars are particularly useful in 
the modification of material parameters, as a transport equation is solved for each scalar if advection is 
activated, resulting in the properties defined in the scalar travelling with the material. 
When using the elasto-visco-plastic model, a physics model called “Elasto-visco-plasticity” is 
activated, in addition to the other models, which lets you apply the elasto-visco-plastic material model.

4.4.2. Geometry and Translations  
When modelling the extrusion and deposition of the geopolymer in Flow-3D, the geometry used in the 
model is kept as simple as possible to minimize computational time when simulating. The nozzle 
extruding the fluid is therefore modelled as a hollow cuboid, thereby reducing the complexity of the 
model. This simplification is assumed to be sufficient as the geometry of the deposited layer depends 
on the flow at the exit of the nozzle. It is also assumed that the flow and deformation will be symmetric 
on each side of the extruded strand. Therefore, a symmetry plane is used resulting in only half the nozzle 
and extruded substrate being modelled and simulated. 
Geometry 
To match the dimensions of the nozzle used in the experiments where the nozzle opening is 4mm thick 
and 20mm wide. The nozzle opening in the CFD model is made to be 4mm thick and wide because of 
the symmetry plane. The modelled nozzle also has a wall thickness of 2mm, matching the nozzle used 
in the experiments. The geometry can be seen in figure 27. 
In addition to the nozzle, a simple print surface which the printed layer can deposit onto and a small 
cuboid filling the top of the nozzle is also modelled. The purpose of the small cuboid at the top of the 
nozzle is to act as the mass source where the printable material flows from.

4.4.3. Model Mesh and Boundary Conditions 
The mesh for the CFD model is made as one cuboid overlapping all the modelled components with 
multiple mesh planes contained in it, to more accurately specify the position of mesh cells in relation to 
important geometry. For the model it was chosen to use a mesh cell size of 0.5mm for the Bingham 
model. This value was chosen based on multiple factors. One of the primary factors is that this cell size 
adds up to both 4mm and 10mm, filling the mouth of the nozzle without overlapping with the nozzle 
walls with even cell distribution. It also means that the thickness of the nozzle ends up being 8 cells, 
which gives a relatively good resolution when viewing the solution, while not increasing the total cell 
count too much. The mesh sizes chosen are based on the results from the mesh sensitivity test. The 
explained mesh can be seen in figure 29 and 30. 
 
Mesh planes along z-axis 
Within the mesh, there are 4 mesh planes defined along the z-
axis. These mesh planes can be seen on figure 30 and 31, where 
they are highlighted by thicker light blue lines in the mesh. The 
bottom of the mesh starts 5mm below the print surface. Even 
though this corresponds to one cell thickness, a mesh plane is 
made at the print surface to ensure that the cells don’t deviate 
from the intended position when changing mesh sizes for quick 
test simulation. Likewise, there are mesh planes corresponding 
to 1. and 2. layer heights to ensure the position of the nozzle’s 
bottom surface doesn’t change. Near the top of the mesh, an 
additional mesh plane has been placed, which was used for 
locally refining the mesh where the fluid enters for some of the 
initial simulation tests and mesh refinement. 
Mesh planes along x-axis 
Along the x-axis there has been placed a mesh plane at the 
center of the start and stop positions of the printed layers. These 
planes serve the purpose of lining up the nozzle and mesh 
consistently even with varying cell sizes. The mesh plane at the 
nozzle wall is used to align the mesh, to ensure a proper 
boundary, as this edge of the nozzle if particularly important to 
the formation of the first layer.

Boundary conditions 
At the boundaries of the mesh block, varying boundary conditions have been applied to determine how 
the fluid should act if or when it encounters each of the 6 sides of the mesh block. For the bottom of the

necessary as it is covered by the geometry for the print surface. At the mesh surface cutting through the 
middle of the nozzle “Y Max”, a symmetry boundary condition has been applied to simulate the material 
flowing connected to its mirrored counterpart. For the remaining four sides of the mesh, a continuative 
boundary condition was applied with the intention of enabling the liquid to flow through, so the 
deposited geometry will be minimally affected if it flows more than expected and therefore interacts 
with the mesh boundaries.

like in the instantaneous curing simulations.

4.4.5. Adjustment of Parameters for CFD Model 
When running the CFD model using either the Bingham or the elasto-visco-plastic model for 
simulating the deformation of the layers being printed, it is far from guaranteed that the deformation 
will match the recorded deformations from the experiments, even though good approximations of the 
material parameters are used. Because of this, multiple simulations are made to compare the results 
for varying parameters using both Bingham and elasto-visco-plastic models. 
Parameters for Bingham model simulations 
Because the deformation in the Bingham model simulations primarily depends on the viscosity 
calculation, which includes both the plastic viscosity and the yield stress for the material, tests for 
varying viscosities and yield stresses are performed. Initially simulations using material parameters 
determined from rheometer tests are made to establish a baseline comparison. Depending on the 
baseline results, changes to plastic viscosity, yield stress and their increase when curing, is made to 
better reflect the experimental data. 
Parameters for elasto-visco-plastic model simulations 
For the simulations using the elasto-visco-plastic model, there are 3 primary parameters which can be 
changed for the deformations to better match the experimental data. These are the plastic viscosity, yield 
stress and the shear modulus.  
Based on the information in [44], the plateau value of the storage modulus at low shear rates, can be 
used as the elastic shear modulus. From the storage modulus determined for fly ash based geopolymers 
in [8], high values for shear modulus in the range 1 to 8 MPa is found, though the values vary greatly 
depending on mixture ratio and curing time. As high values for the shear modulus increases the 
computational time greatly and often results in crashing, a value of 10 kPa. is instead used. Increasing 
this value further will only cause negligible changes to the simulated deformation as the shear modulus 
is high compared to the yield stress. A test to verify this is conducted, where the shear modulus is 
changed from 5 to 15 kPa. 
For the remaining parameters, tests varying the plastic viscosity, yield stress and their increase when 
curing, is performed.

5.2.1. Rheometer Test 
Using the rheometer setup (Described in section 4.2.1.), mixtures of 
1.45, 1.525, 1.6, 1.65, 1.7 and 1.8 ratios of activator to metakaolin 
are initially tested. For the 1.45 ratio mixture it could be seen that 
the resulting shear stress measured by the rheometer is much lower 
than what is expected, compared to the other ratio mixtures. This 
issue likely results from the separation of material along the outer 
edge of the parallel plates, which can be seen in figure 45. The 
separation of material during the rheometer tests causes the material 
to not contribute to the measured resistance resulting from the 
shearing motion induced by the rheometer. This is especially 
impactful as the material is separated along the outer edge, where 
shear rates are highest and corresponding shear stresses have the 
largest impact on the torque measured by the rheometer. Because of 
this issue the results from the 1.45 ratio mixture tests aren’t included 
in the presented data. The remaining mixtures showed little to no 
edge separation, and the results are therefore accepted. 
From the Rheometer tests, data for each case is gathered during two cycles, where the shear rate is first 
gradually increased then decreased. This results in a total of four curves for both shear stress and 
viscosity over shear rate. Especially for the shear stress curves it is seen that the results vary significantly 
depending on whether the shear rate is increasing or decreasing. An example of this can be seen for the

Rheometer data and print test comparison 
When relating the results from the rheometer tests to the 3D-printing experiments it is relevant whether 
the shear rate is increasing or decreasing when the data points are captured, as this can affect the material 
behavior. Using data from the decreasing shear rate curve will be ideal when simulating the deposition 
of one layer, as the shear rate will be relatively high in the nozzle and then decrease quickly after being 
deposited on the print surface. There will also be places in the system where the opposite is true. This 
is the case for the layer beneath the layer being printed, as the pressure from the layer above causes the 
shear rate to increase. 
Because the material behavior when the shear rate is decreasing seems to have a larger impact on the 
deformation of the layers and because the curves resulting from the rheometer tests with decreasing 
shear rate are more consistent, it was decided to only use data from the ramp-down curves for 
determining material parameters for the CFD model.

For obtaining the material parameters necessary for the Bingham rheological model, which is applied 
in Flow-3D, a linear function was fitted to the curves, corresponding to the shear stress over shear rate, 
for each of the cases based on the average of the two repetitions. The resulting Bingham model fit is 
illustrated in figure 50 for the 1.70-part activator to metakaolin mixture.  
For making the fit curve it was chosen to only use the data points in the range corresponding to a shear 
rate of 4 1/s to a shear rate of 13.2 1/s. The minimum value for the range was chosen as this corresponds 
to the lowest measured shear stress (local minimum), indicating that points at lower shear rates begin 
to become inconsistent. The upper limit for the range was chosen based on the shear rates determined 
in the CFD simulations. Here it was found that the strain rates in the system almost newer exceed 13 
1/s. Thereby limiting the range of data points used for fitting to the Bingham model will result in a more 
accurate approximation in the relevant range for the corresponding simulations. The coefficient of 
determination (R2) for the two curves are 0.956 and 0.938 (no-curing and curing).

5.2.3. Herschel-Bulkley Fitting Results 
An alternative model that could be used for fitting the measured points is the Herschel-Bulkley model. 
When fitting the measured data to the Hershel-Bulkley model it is found that the behavior of the material 
can be approximated more accurately both in and outside the shear rate range for the fitted 
measurements. The curves resulting from fitting to the Herschel-Bulkley model can be seen in figure 
51. 
The curve for the 1.7 ratio uncured material has the following formulation: 
 
𝜏= 𝜏0 + 𝐾⋅𝛾̇ 𝑛= 33.83 + 0.008888 ⋅𝛾̇ 2.295 
(45) 
Because the determined fit has a formulation where 𝑛= 2.295, it indicates that the material behavior 
doesn’t follow the Bingham rheological model. When 𝑛> 1 the material has shear thickening 
characteristics meaning that viscosity increases as shear stress increases in addition to the increase 
relative to strain rate. The coefficient of determination (R2) for the two curves are 0.999. 
Even though the measured data indicates shear thickening behavior according to the Herschel-Bulkley 
model, this model is not used when determining the material parameters for the CFD model. This is 
because the CFD model uses the Bingham model for simulating the behavior of the material, therefore 
material parameters found by fitting to the Bingham model will be more accurate for the simulations.

5.2.5. Rheology Experiment - Summary & Findings 
The rheological analysis of metakaolin-based geopolymers revealed critical insights into the material's 
behavior under varying shear rates and activator-to-metakaolin ratios. The rheometer tests demonstrated 
that shear stress and viscosity exhibit distinct patterns depending on whether the shear rate is increasing 
or decreasing. Consistent results from the ramp-down curves were used to determine material 
parameters for CFD modelling, ensuring accuracy in simulating the deposition process. 
The fitting of rheological data to the Bingham model provided key material parameters, including 
plastic viscosity and yield stress, for both uncured and cured samples. These parameters showed a clear 
dependency on the activator to metakaolin ratio, with shear stress and viscosity increasing upon curing 
for 2 minutes at 50°C. The study also explored the Herschel-Bulkley model, which provided a more 
accurate fit across the entire shear rate range, though it was not adopted for CFD modelling due to being 
incompatible with the chosen simulation framework. 
Density evaluation highlighted its critical role in determining hydrostatic pressure and material 
deformation during the printing process. The results showed a consistent relationship between density 
and the activator ratio, enabling the prediction of density values. 
In conclusion, the findings from this study contribute to a deeper understanding of the rheological 
properties of metakaolin geopolymers, which is essential for improving the accuracy of CFD 
simulations.

5.3.4. Stability & Curing Experiment – Summary and Findings 
The stability and curing tests conducted provide valuable insights into the effects of bed movement and 
curing on the dimensional stability of printed strands. The results demonstrate that controlled bed 
movement can be executed without causing significant deformation, even for materials classified as too 
flowable. This finding validates the feasibility of moving printed layers without compromising their 
structural integrity. 
Curing, however, introduces notable changes influencing the final geometry and properties of the 
printed strands. Short curing intervals resulted in minor deformation, with minimal impact on overall 
dimensional accuracy. In contrast, longer curing durations led to more pronounced changes, including 
shrinkage, curvature alterations, and the formation of a solidified outer layer. This shell-like structure 
not only modified the strand’s appearance but also reduced the flowability of the underlying material, a 
phenomenon attributed to rapid evaporation and surface solidification under airflow. Based on the tests 
including the curing, the fan speed for following tests is reduced from 50% to 10%, which should reduce 
the shell-formation.

5.5.1. 5-Layer Wet-on-Semi-Solidified Expt. – Summary and Findings 
The 5-layer Wet-on-Semi-Solidified experiment highlights the influence of curing time and activator-
to-metakaolin ratios on the mechanical stability and dimensional consistency of printed layers. The 
inclusion of curing steps, particularly a 2-minute curing period after extrusion of each layer, 
significantly reduced deformation in lower layers caused by subsequent layer, as seen in the 
comparison of Wet-on-Wet and Wet-on-Semi-Solidified results. The Wet-on-Wet prints experienced 
greater flow and deformation under hydrostatic pressure, especially for higher activator ratios, leading 
to layer width expansion and collapse in extreme cases. In contrast, the Wet-on-Semi-Solidified 
method produced more vertically stable prints with reduced deviations in layer width and height. 
The findings emphasize the importance of curing in achieving more stable and uniform multi-layer 
prints, highlighting its potential for improving print quality in similar applications.

5.6. Experimental Results – Summary and Findings 
The experiments performed systematically investigated the printability, rheology, stability, and curing 
dynamics of metakaolin-based geopolymers. By analyzing activator to metakaolin ratios, the 
experiments revealed critical insights into print stability, dimensional accuracy, and material 
deformation during multi-layer printing. 
Initially, printability tests were performed to determine the activator-to-metakaolin ratios suitable for 
extrusion and maintaining strand integrity. Mixture ratios of 1.525 to 1.65 were identified as the optimal 
range, balancing flowability and structural integrity, while lower ratios exhibited extrusion challenges 
and higher ratios led to excessive material flow.  
Following this, rheological analyses using a rheometer provided insights into the material’s behavior at 
varying shear rates. The results indicated a consistent dependency of shear stress and viscosity on the 
activator ratio. Data from the ramp-down curves were utilized for Bingham model fitting, which 
produced parameters that can be used for CFD simulations. The study also observed shear thickening 
behavior in some mixtures when analyzed using the Herschel-Bulkley model, although this is not to be 
implemented in the CFD models due to compatibility constraints, with the Bingham model 
implementation. 
From the stability tests, it is found that the controlled movement of printed strands demonstrated that 
even highly flowable mixtures could maintain dimensional stability when carefully handled. This 
established the feasibility of moving printed strands without significant deformation. 
Curing dynamics was explored through curing tests of varying duration. Short curing intervals caused 
minor geometric changes while maintaining dimensional accuracy. Prolonged curing led to pronounced 
alterations, including shrinkage, curvature shifts, and the formation of a solidified surface layer. 
In the wet-on-semi-solidified experiments, introducing a 2-minute curing step between layers 
significantly reduced deformation in the first printed layer when a subsequent layer were added. This 
approach enhanced layer stability and reduced plastic deformation, especially for mixtures with higher 
activator to metakaolin ratios. 
Finally, the 5-layer wet-on-semi-solidified tests showcased the combined effects of curing and material 
composition on the mechanical stability and geometric consistency of multi-layer prints. Compared to 
wet-on-wet printing, the inclusion of curing steps minimized deformation caused by hydrostatic 
pressure, resulting in more uniform layer widths.

6.3.4. Comparison with Experimental Results. 
When comparing the simulations done using the Bingham rheological model to the experimental results, 
there can be noticed several issues. The first issue is that the viscosity does not go to infinity as the shear 
rate goes to zero, but is instead capped at a high viscosity, corresponding to the minimum shear rate 
chosen in the Flow-3D GUI. This results in the deposited material slightly flowing over time, which 
isn’t the case for the experimental results. Even though the deformation over time is slow, it affects the 
accuracy of the simulations more than expected, as the dimensions of the simulated strand are relatively 
small. Though there are solutions that can make relative deformations more comparable, like the 
simulations performed for initial and two-layer tests separately to capture their dimensions at matching 
timesteps. This approach is far from optimal as the flow behavior will be affected by the second layer, 
thereby neglecting the accuracy of the model. Additionally, it necessitates the simulation of two cases 
for every usable result, effectively doubling the computational time. 
Another issue lies in the relative plastic deformation resulting from the simulations. When comparing 
the deformations measured from the experiments to the simulations, there are often larger relative 
plastic deformations, caused by the deposition of the second layer, than it was possible to replicate using 
the current Bingham model implementation. Based on the experimental results for relative plastic 
deformations it would be ideal to achieve values around 3-4% for the 1.7 ratio case (see section 6), 
which wasn’t achieved. This issue is likely partially caused by the materials slow but continues flow 
over time causing the first layer to be lower than expected thereby providing more space for the second 
layer. Another cause could be that the model doesn’t transfer pressure well enough through the material 
to cause strains large enough to affect viscosity at the edges of the bottom layer, as shown in figure 90. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
To compensate for the lack of relative plastic deformations, a solution could be to increase the flow rate 
of the material, which could artificially make the simulations more closely resemble the experimental 
results. The most ideal solution to try and gain a better comparison would be further lowering the 
minimum critical shear rate, but as this consistently resulted in simulations crashing, this solution isn’t 
viable.

6.3.5. Bingham CFD Results – Summary and Findings 
For the used generalized Newtonian Constitutive model with the Bingham rheological model, 
significant discrepancies have been found when comparing it with results from experiments. The 
primary reason for these discrepancies is that the model uses a maximum apparent viscosity which 
depends on the minimum critical shear rate used, resulting in the viscosity not going to infinity as the 
shear rate goes to zero. This causes a shift in the results over time. Additionally, the model isn’t able to 
reproduce large enough relative plastic deformations, when compared to experiments.  
These issues are likely exaggerated by the small dimensions of the simulated geometry and by the 
rheological behavior of the material used in experiments not completely adhering to the Bingham 
model. As an example, having a larger width would cause the effect of the relative plastic deformation 
due to flow over time to be reduced. Even though there are more ways to further improve the model’s 
ability to predict the deposition behavior of the metakaolin geopolymers, the model ends up being 
suboptimal for replicating the experimental data gathered for metakaolin based geopolymer printing of 
this scale.

6.4.6. Elasto-Visco-Plastic CFD Results - Summary and Findings 
Using the elasto-visco-plastic model, the previously described problem of natural flowing behavior of 
the material over time using the Bingham approach was removed, making the material stable, until the 
yield stress was exceeded. 
Changing the shear modulus doesn’t affect the results, as long as the value is relatively high compared 
to the yield stress of the material, but does affect the computational time of the simulation, and a good 
balance between result and computational time was found and used for the simulations. 
Since the input for the elasto-visco-plastic model is a constant viscosity, this value was analyzed, and a 
value of 8.4 Pa⋅s was chosen for the 1.7-part activator to metakaolin ratio, which corresponds to the 
minimum apparent viscosity. Increasing the yield stress of the material reduced the relative plastic 
deformation as expected, and as seen for the 3D printing experiments in combination with the rheology 
tests of different mixtures. 
The results showed some discrepancies when compared to the experimental results, and the yield stress 
of the material had to be multiplied by a value of 1.33 (resulting in an increase in yield stress from 32.4 
Pa to 43.1 Pa) for the 1.7-part activator to metakaolin simulation. With this modification, the simulation 
closely matched the experiments.  However, it should be noted that this value is based on the Bingham-
linear fit made in the rheology test, and fitting a different model (like shown for the Hershel Bulkley 
fit), a different (and possibly higher) yield stress would have been found for the material, reducing the 
needed modification of this value. The modification of the yield stress being necessary could also be a 
result of discrepancies between the rheological test and print tests. Some of these discrepancies are 
explained in the experimental results section 5.2 and 5.4. 
One interesting finding was that when including the curing-effect of the first layer, by changing the 
material properties after 15 seconds of material-age, the relative plastic deformation decreased to be the 
same as for the experimental results (under 1% deviation), without any modification of this factor. 
Based on the 1.8-mixture results it is found that applying the method developed for the 1.7-mixture 
did not directly provide results close enough to what is found from experiments. Further modifying 
the factor multiplied with the yield stress provided relative plastic deformations within the accepted 
range, which would indicate that additional simulations and comparison with experiments will have to 
be performed, in order to accurately predict behavior of untested ratios. Additionally, it could be seen 
that the change in relative plastic deformations isn’t as large as seen in experiments, when curing. 
This can likely be attributed to the formation of a cured shell in the experiments which the CFD 
model doesn’t account for, thereby indicating the need for modifications to the change of yield stress 
and viscosity with curing.

6.5. CFD Results – Summary and Findings 
The CFD results section evaluates the performance of CFD simulations for modeling the deposition 
behavior of geopolymer-based materials. Assessing the accuracy of the Bingham rheological model and 
the elasto-visco-plastic model, through simulation tests, mesh sensitivity analysis, and comparisons 
with experimental data. It evaluates different modelling approaches, key parameters, and their influence 
on material behavior and deformation.  
Bingham Model 
• 
The model struggles with sustained material stability over time, as the viscosity does not 
increase indefinitely at low shear rates, leading to continuous flow that deviates from 
experimental observations. 
• 
Plastic deformation is under-predicted, particularly when simulating multi-layer deposition. 
• 
Including curing effects improves material behavior but does not fully mitigate the model’s 
limitations, as the reductions in plastic deformation are minimal and fail to align with 
experimental results. 
• 
The natural flow behavior observed in simulations results in discrepancies in layer geometry, 
particularly when comparing relative deformation over time. 
 
Elasto-Visco-Plastic Model 
• 
The EVP model eliminates the continuous flow behavior seen in the Bingham model, allowing 
the material to maintain its shape until yield stress is exceeded. 
• 
Adjusting the yield stress improves material stability during deposition, with an optimized 
multiplier of 1.33 for the 1.7 mixture, yielding results that closely match experimental data. 
• 
The model successfully captures the effects of curing, demonstrating significant reductions in 
plastic deformation. 
• 
Changing material parameters significantly affects the outcome of simulations, resulting in it 
being easier to obtain results resembling the baseline obtained from experimental data.  
Based on the performed simulation tests, the Bingham model captures the basic flow characteristics but 
struggles with limitations such as over-simplified flow behavior and difficulty in replicating large 
plastic deformations. The EVP model offers better performance by having curing effects, and layer 
stability more closely resembling experimental results. 
Finally, it should be noted that the yield stress determined using the rheometer data fitted to the Bingham 
rheological model does not accurately represent the shear stress at low shear rates. This issue could 
have been mitigated slightly by using the Herschel-Bulkley model as previously mentioned, though it 
still wouldn’t capture the increase in shear stress seen for low shear rates in figure 47, which is 
completely neglected because of the chosen fitting range (4 to 13 1/s). The primary reasons for avoiding 
the stresses for shear rates under 4 1/s are that the flow is inconsistent, and that fitting the discussed 
models wouldn’t be able to represent the measured behavior. This issue was somewhat mitigated by 
artificially increasing the yield stress, but since shear rates observed in simulations primarily are lower 
than the fitted range, a greater focus should be placed on capturing the material behavior at low shear 
rates.

7. Conclusion 
Through the development of a system for accurately measuring the dimensions of 3D-prinded strands, 
using a camera setup mounted on the 3D-printer, it was possible to capture the deposition of layers and 
deformations resulting from the addition of subsequent layers, providing data from several printing 
experiments. The performed experiments were set up to gain an understanding of metakaolin based 
geopolymers and their application in MEX-AM, as well as for aiding in the development of CFD models 
capable of replicating geopolymers flow behavior in 3D-printing.  
Tests for printability, stability and curing, as well as printing wet-on-semi-solidified layers, were 
performed. From the printability tests, the printable range was defined to be between 1.525 to 1.65 ratio 
of activator to metakaolin, where lower ratios resulted in poor extrudability while higher ratios showed 
excessive flowability and compromised layer integrity, especially when depositing a subsequent layer. 
Stability and curing test demonstrated that it was possible to move the print bed without affecting the 
dimensions and image capturing of the printed layers. The introduction of curing steps in an oven 
showed that it was possible to alter the material behavior, though it resulted in layer shrinkage and the 
formation of a more hardened outer shell, which could be minimized by lowering curing time and air 
flow. The wet-on-semi-solidified printing tests demonstrated that including curing steps could increase 
print stability and consistency in multilayer prints, thereby enabling printing of otherwise too flowable 
materials. 
Using material parameters for the geopolymers with varying rations of activator to metakaolin, 
measured in rheometer tests, a comparative evaluation of Bingham and elasto-visco-plastic CFD models 
was made. The evaluation compares their ability to predict material behavior during deposition and 
when a subsequent layer is deposited on top, using the relative plastic deformations found from 
experiments as a reference. The Bingham model reflected the general flow behavior of the material but 
struggled with significant discrepancies due to the maximum apparent viscosity allowing the material 
to continuously flow over time. Additionally, the model wasn’t able to replicate the large plastic 
deformations determined from experiments. The elasto-visco-plastic model could capture the materiel 
behavior including the yield limit, keeping the material from flowing before the yield stress was 
exceeded. It was also able to more accurately replicate the deformations and effect of curing seen in 
experiments. 
By incorporating modified parameters, it was possible to adjust the elasto-visco-plastic model to reflect 
the behavior of geopolymers with varying ratios of activator to metakaolin based on the limited 
references achieved from experiments. With further developments through comparison with simulations 
for additional ratios and an expanded experimental data set with more replications, the elasto-visco-
plastic model showed a high potential for its use in predicting the material behavior within geopolymer 
3D-printing.

7.1. Project Reflection and Future Work 
Based on the knowledge gained from this project, several areas for future work and improvement can 
be identified.   
For the experiments and image capturing, several problems and improvement areas were addressed, 
where a more stable setup could be developed based on these. One recurring problem, that is difficult 
to realize before analyzing the experimental data, is the 3D printer’s nozzles attachment method. Before 
continuing with similar studies, it is important to address this issue, both for the extrusion height (nozzle 
height) and the perpendicularity between the build plate and the nozzle, while extruding. Although one 
might think that the attachment of the nozzle looks great before printing, this placement can shift as the 
material extrudes through the nozzle. One possibility here is to make a fixture for the nozzle, that is 
specifically made for the syringe, where the nozzle position is fixed either to the syringe or the print-
head, ensuring that the nozzle is always placed correctly. 
For the image capturing, although the chosen cameras worked well and were very simple to use, some 
future work or optimization could be relevant. One problem when reflecting on the image analysis was 
the focus of the camera. The cameras chosen for this problem had a manual focus, which in some ways 
worked out well, as we could manually adjust the cameras to get the area of interest in focus, but when 
we began extending the number of layers extruded, the focus point would shift, resulting in some layers 
appearing blurred. A different approach to image capturing, by either moving the camera after printing 
in steps to have different layers in focus or simply having a different camera with an adjustable focus 
(through software) would have been ideal. 
The image capturing in this project was used for conversion from 2D pixel coordinates to 2D world 
coordinates, but by introducing more cameras, it would have been possible to gather several more pixel 
coordinates, which could have been transferred to a 3D mapping, making a 3D visualization of the 
printed strand possible. This would have been even better for an accurate comparison with CFD-
modeling. 
Although the CFD-modeling showed good results with the elasto-visco-plastic model, the material 
behavior seen in the simulation could have been optimized further, by refining the rheological test 
procedure and by experimenting with additional material models, that would better suit the real-world 
behavior of the material. It could also be beneficial to run similar simulations with higher quality mesh, 
especially for the mesh used with the elasto-visco-plastic model, which was acceptable but far from 
ideal. This could slightly increase the precision of the results and only requires more time or computing 
power. Computing power in this project was limited since we couldn’t customize the solver in Flow-
3D on the HPC server, provided by DTU for the project. 
For future projects, the optimization of the image capturing and a different approach to the material 
curing would need to be investigated, which could lead to even more precise and interesting results. 
Furthermore, building on the foundation of this project, the scope could be expanded to include more 
exotic materials, such as geopolymers based on lunar regolith, which with the incorporation of 
additional test using a vacuum chamber, to quantify the atmosphere’s effect, would be ideal for 
predicting material behavior aiding in the construction of lunar bases in the future.

video = VideoReader('5Layer_1.7NoCuring.mp4'); %Video file to look at 
starttime = 60*5+45; %Start time of analyze (seconds) 
endtime = starttime; %end time of analyze (seconds) 
timestep = 30; %Timesteps in seconds (1=1 frame each second) Remember FPS = 
30! 
camera_no = 2; %Camera number (1,2 & 3) 
zoomfactor = 1.5; % times to zoom in image (1-3) 
steps=(endtime-starttime)/timestep; 
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%% 
 
% Input the time in seconds  
for i=0:steps 
desiredTime = starttime+(i*timestep); 
 
% Ensure the input time is within the video duration 
if desiredTime < 0 || desiredTime > video.Duration 
    error('The input time is out of the video duration range.'); 
end 
 
% Set the current time in the video to the desired time 
video.CurrentTime = desiredTime; 
 
% Read the frame at the specified time 
frame = readFrame(video); 
 
% Crop the frame to the top 1920x1080 region 
% Assuming frame size is 1920x3240, we crop only the top 1080 pixels 
if camera_no == 1 
    croppedFrame = frame(1:1080, 1:1920, :);  % Cropping rows 1 to 1080, 
columns 1 to 1920 
elseif camera_no == 2 
        croppedFrame = frame(1081:2160, 1:1920, :);  % Cropping rows 1081 
to 2160, columns 1 to 1920 
elseif camera_no == 3 
        croppedFrame = frame(2161:3240, 1:1920, :);  % Cropping rows 2161 
to 3240, columns 1 to 1920 
else 
    error('please chose a camera to view')
