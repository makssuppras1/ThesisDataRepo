Abstract 
 
The formation and occurrence of hydrogen sulfide (H2S) remains a challenge for seawater operated 
recirculating aquaculture systems (RAS). Presence of toxic H2S gas at low concentrations can cause 
decreased fish welfare and sudden mass mortalities of reared species, leading to large economic 
loses. Although a few methods for acute H2S removal like the use of hydrogen peroxide have been 
described, an urgent need for more effective strategies to remove H2S with even shorter response 
times and higher removal rates remain to prevent the risk of casualties caused by H2S. This study 
examines the kinetics and optimal dose of one oxidant and the removal rate of sulfide with one 
water treatment component at set parameters commonly applied to rearing water in RAS facilities; 
Ozone (O3) and a degas column. This study further evaluates the practical applicability of O3 and 
degassing as methods for removal of H2S and total dissolved sulfide (TDS) in seawater RAS. This 
study also investigates the optimal O3/ ozone produced oxidants (OPO) dosage where total residual 
oxidants (TRO) after the reaction with TDS remains at concentrations below toxic levels to fish. 
Furthermore this study investigates the effect of organic matter (OM) gathered from swirl 
separators and protein skimmers on the optimal O3 to TDS ratio (O3:TDS) for complete removal of 
H2S and TRO. The feasibility of using ozonation and OPO as a treatment was evaluated by testing a 
range of O3:TDS ratios in seawater. The effect of OM on optimal O3:TDS ratios was evaluated by 
testing three concentrations of total chemical oxygen demand (TCOD) from the two OM treatment 
components together with the optimal O3:TDS ratio in seawater deduced from the former 
experiment. In addition, TCOD measurements were sampled at the end of experiments from 
controls and ozone treatments with OM to elaborate on the interactions between O3, OM and TDS. 
Anions were measured in seawater and after treatments to evaluate systematic effects of solute 
composition. The kinetics derived from OPO oxidation rates of TDS were faster than what was 
measurable with the setup in this study ( < 1 min for complete reaction). The optimal mg/L O3:TDS 
ratio for 1 mg/L TDS was 2.08 – 2.16 : 1 measured as O3 and 6.88 – 7.15 : 1 HOBr:TDS measured as 
Br2. Further increasing the O3:TDS ratio resulted in measurable TRO concentrations and decreasing 
the O3:TDS below the optimal ratio resulted in measurable residual H2S. OM from protein skimmers 
and swirl separators in the range of 20 – 60 mg TCOD/L did not affect the optimal O3:TDS ratio. No 
systematic decrease in TCOD or Br- concentrations measured after the reaction between O3, TDS and 
OM were observed. ORP values at 1 mg TDS/L were between -90 and -170 mV and ORP values in the 
range of 2-2.2 mg OPO/L measured as O3 were in the range of 600 and 790 mV. There was clear 
observable differences between ORP values depending on O3:TDS ratios after reactions were 
complete. The pilot scale degas column at set parameters showed potential for continuous TDS 
removal at high pH (~8) with the H2S half-time of 19.5 ± 2 min at a starting concentration of 1 mg 
TDS/L in seawater. Initial pilot scale trials incorporating ozonation and the degas column showed no 
increased efficiency in TDS removal compared to the degas column alone, explained by the low 
ozone production of the ozone generator used for the experiment. In conclusion, ozonation is a very 
efficient water treatment technology for H2S removal in seawater with complete reaction between 
OPO and TDS within 1 minute. Additionally, a degas column has potential for continuous removal of 
TDS in RAS. Thus, ozonation technology is applicable in an industrial seawater RAS as a treatment 
method for acute levels of H2S toxic to reared species that is easily implemented and safe for fish if 
dozed correctly. Degassing is a very safe technology that besides CO2 removal can be implemented 
to counteract slow increases in TDS in seawater RAS.

Introduction 
Recirculating aquaculture systems (RAS) has the potential for environmentally sustainable and 
intensive fish production with reduced water use (0.1 – 1 m3/kg feed) (Martins et al., 2010).  In RAS, 
various water treatment components facilitate the control of biological, chemical, and physical key 
water quality parameters such as microbial control, water flow and velocity, oxygen (O2), ammonia 
(NH3), carbon dioxide (CO2), nitrogen gas (N2), nitrate (NO32-), dissolved and suspended organic 
matter (DOM, SOM), temperature (T), and pH (Timmons et al., 2022). Yet, high fish densities needed 
for RAS to be economically viable combined with increased system complexity and water reuse leave 
unsolved challenges and risks regarding fish welfare (Martins et al., 2010). Besides corrosion and 
serious odor problems, recent studies have shown that hydrogen sulfide (H2S) plays a critical role in 
fish welfare and can be the cause of mass mortalities in RAS due to its high toxicity towards reared 
species (Dalsgaard., 2019). High risk of H2S formation has been identified especially in RAS operated 
under saline conditions due to increased concentrations of sulfate (SO42-) in seawater (~2700 mg/L). 
In seawater RAS, the primary production of H2S is facilitated by sulfate-reducing bacteria (SRB) which 
utilize SO42- as a terminal electron acceptor under anaerobic conditions (Letelier-Gordo et al., 2020).  
H2S formation in RAS have shown to be caused by multiple groups of bacteria and prevention is 
therefore difficult due to high complexity of mechanisms still not fully understood. Besides H2S 
formation carried out by SRB in anaerobic conditions with availability of simple carbon sources, 
another group, cysteine-degrading bacteria, have been suggested to degrade sulfur-rich amino acids 
(ex. cysteine) into pyruvate, ammonia and H2S under both anaerobic and aerobic conditions 
(Nguyen-tiêt et al., 2023). Both pathways for H2S production are dependent on a carbon source as 
electron donor which can be derived from fish feces, feed waste and residues from bacterial decay 
that all contribute to the pool of organic matter (OM) present in RAS.  
H2S is acutely toxic to conventionally farmed fish at concentrations as low as 0.05 mg/L (Atlantic 
salmon post smolt) and dead zones in essential seawater RAS components such as pipes, and 
biofilters (29 g H2S/kg TCOD, 17 ‰) are identified as potential hotspots for H2S formation (Bergsted 
& Skov., 2023; Rojas-Tirado et al., 2021). Inside lumps of organic matter formed in dead zones, high 
concentrations of H2S can accumulate and burst into the system at lethal concentrations (Rojas-
Tirado et al., 2021). Hence, H2S formed in dead zones of RAS water treatment components combined 
with possible H2S formation facilitated by cysteine-degrading bacteria poses a risk to fish welfare 
even though O2 is continuously supplied in the system to satisfy fish demands (Nguyen-tiêt et al., 
2023; Rojas-Tirado et al., 2021). 
Hydrogen sulphide chemistry in seawater 
H2S is a colorless flammable gas and highly soluble in water (Henrys law solubility constant (Hscp) = 
0.001 mol/(m3Pa), temperature dependency constant (HscpT) = 2100 at 25 °C), three times more 
soluble than CO2 (Sander., 2023). Besides high solubility, H2S has a higher density than atmospheric 
air, which means it is more prone to accumulate in bottom areas with low turbulence (Abdollahi and 
Hosseini., 2014). In an aqueous solution, H2S is in a pH, dependent equilibrium with the less toxic 
aqueous phases, hydrosulfide (HS-) and sulfide (S2-). The species fractions are present in two 
equilibriums, described as a function of pH:

Salinity and temperature also affect sulfide species equilibrium, where increased salinity and 
temperature causes pKa1 to decrease. Hence H2S dissociation can be calculated when pH, salinity 
and temperature are known (Millero et al., 1988). In seawater RAS rearing Atlantic salmon post 
smolt (10 – 15 °C, ~35‰) the pKa1 would be around 6.7 - 6.6 (Millero et al., 1988). This means that 
at pH above 6.6, HS- is the dominant species and below 6.6, H2S is the dominant species. Typical pH 
in RAS ranges from 6.5 to 8.0, and although daily variations in single systems are minimized by 
increasing the buffer capacity of water with ex. addition of lime, it is not uncommon (Timmons et al., 
2022). Therefore, H2S concentrations below toxicity for fish can hide as HS- at higher pH levels and 
become toxic in case of a decrease in pH. 
 
Contingency measures for hydrogen sulfide removal 
In the pH range of an aquaculture setting, sulfide is present as H2S gas and the aqueous ionic form 
(HS-), making S2- irrelevant as it does not occur at pH values <10 (Millero et al., 1988). This provides 
several removal opportunities including degassing of H2S and oxidation of the aqueous HS- and/or 
H2S.  
In wastewater and well water treatment, H2S removal has been studied extensively and several 
technologies aimed at removing the contaminant have been developed. Technologies include 
degassing, adsorption with activated carbon and various technologies utilizing biological or chemical 
oxidation of HS- (Tzvi & Paz., 2019). For degassing of sulfide, Howe and Lawler., (1989) predicted that 
at pH 7-8 in a packed column with combined removal from degassing and oxidation with oxygen, the 
sulfide removal would range from 80 to 60% (G:L = 12) with increasing pH. The primary conclusion 
was that degassing efficiency mostly depended on pH and not as much flow rate and liquid 
distributor area. 
Degassing of sulfide as a technology for wastewater and well water treatment, is generally 
considered inefficient for reaching drinking water quality due to the high pH dependence (pH < 6 for 
95% removal) (Howe and Lawler., 1989). In a RAS setting, degassing might still be an appropriate 
complimentary method since degassers are already used for CO2 stripping.  Adsorption by NaOH/ 
KOH – coated activated carbon has the positive effect of not causing air pollution as opposed to 
degassing. The downside is handling and high costs. In an aquaculture setting with relatively high 
water-volumes and flowrates this method might not be considered practically applicable. Oxidation 
of sulfide by chemotrophic or phototrophic bacteria have shown to remove 82 – 100% total 
dissolved sulfide (TDS) at hydraulic retention times (HRT) ranging from 0.50 to 2.35 hours (Henshaw 
and Zhu., 2001). For incorporation into seawater RAS, this technology needs further studying as 
bacteria communities with the sulfide oxidizing capabilities in seawater have not been extensively 
studied. Furthermore, the method would require an extra treatment component which increases 
footprint and costs. Combined with the long acclimation time, and high retention time needed, this 
could be insufficient as a contingency method for acute H2S removal. 
 
Chemical oxidation of H2S in wastewater and well water treatment has been studied extensively for 
several oxidizing compounds (Tzvi & Paz., 2019). Amongst the most promising methods to seawater 
RAS, are oxidation of H2S with hydrogen peroxide (H2O2), ozone (O3) or combined UVC light and 
dissolved oxygen as these are all compounds and technologies already utilized in RAS (Timmons et 
al., 2022). However, due to the difference in chemistry and conditions of seawater RAS compared to

well water and wastewater facilities the reported effects, and kinetics on H2S nor practical 
approaches are directly applicable in the seawater aquaculture industry. 
 
Bergstedt et al., (2022), showed that the half-lives (t1/2) of H2S, derived from O2 oxidation were 118.5 
± 28.6 and 168.0 ± 18.7 min in air saturated seawater (pH ~ 7.4, 33 ‰, ~13.3 °C) and air saturated 
RAS water (pH ~ 7.4, 35 ‰, ~13.3 °C, TCOD ~ 26.1 mg O2/L) respectively. The faster oxidation rates in 
SW compared to RAS water were ascribed to microbial competition with H2S for oxygen due to 
higher TCOD levels in RAS water and the catalytic effect of trace metals was rejected as a causation 
due to the low differences in Fe2+ and Fe3+ concentrations between seawater and RAS water. 
Oxidation rates were best described by a first-order reaction, t1/2 were independent of initial TDS 
concentrations (1, 2 and 4 mg TDS/L) and calculated H2S fractions (20% H2S) were independent 
between seawater and RAS water. Experiments were conducted in 500 mL beakers at 100 rpm 
stirring, at an unidentified surface area for gas diffusion (O2 and H2S).  
 
It has been shown that an introduction of a H2O2 to H2S 1:1 mole ratio notably enhances the 
elimination rate and diminish the half-life (t1⁄2) of H2S in SW to approximately 29.5 min compared to 
oxidation with O2 (Bergstedt et al., 2022). Furthermore, increasing H2O2:H2S ratios to 2:1 and 4:1 has 
proven to further decrease t1⁄2. In this case t1/2 was decreased to 21.7 ± 5.2 and 17.4 ± 6.1 min, 
respectively (Bergstedt et al., 2022). Similarly, dissolving H2O2 at a 1:1 ratio in RAS water have shown 
to significantly reduce t1⁄2  (86.1 ± 10.1 min) when compared to t1/2 from oxidation with O2. Hence 
the article states that impact of organic matter from RAS on the necessary H2O2 dosage is evidenced 
as t1⁄2 is decreased in SW than RW (75% and 49% respectively) (Bergstedt et al., 2022). 
Hydraulic retention time (HRT) in RAS can vary between production systems and internal 
components (Timmins et al., 2022). HRT between 35 – 170 min have been reported for larger 
circular and octagonal rearing units operated at Norwegian smolt and post-smolt facilities 
(Summerfelt et al., 2016). This range of HRT gives some time before a potential H2S outburst with 
origin in other RAS component reaches full concentration in a rearing unit. Yet, there is a possibility 
for H2S concentrations in the inlet stream of a rearing unit to be high enough to reach toxic levels 
before all water in the tank is exchanged. In this case even a 4:1 H2O2:H2S dosage might not be 
sufficient for H2S removal, reaching below toxic levels before affecting reared fish negatively. A high 
H2O2 dosage can also result in residual H2O2 concentrations in rearing tanks which over longer 
periods can have negative effects on fish (Wood et al., 2021). In summary, a need for alternative and 
more effective H2S removal strategies applicable to SW RAS remain. 
 
Ozone 
Another oxidizing agent commonly applied in RAS is ozone (O3) (Timmons et al., 2022). Compared to 
H2O2, O3 has a high oxidation potential (+1.78 V and +2.07 V respectively). O3 is widely used as a 
supplementary water treatment technology in RAS (Crittenden et al., 2012; Timmons et al 2022). In 
RAS dissolved O3 has the capacity to disinfect water and oxidize a large range of compounds 
including organic matter (OM), nitrite, NH3 and larger aromatic compounds like geosmin 
(Summerfelt and Hochheimer., 1997; Summerfelt et al., 2009; Davidson et al., 2011; Tango and 
Gagnon 2003). 
O3 is slightly soluble in water (HscP = 0.0001 mol/(m3Pa), HscpT = 2800 at 25 °C), yet it is 10 times 
more soluble than O2 (Sander., 2023). Chemical reactions involving ozone in water are complex. Only 
part of the dissolved ozone reacts directly with solutes. The other part of ozone will decompose 
forming residual oxidants in reaction with OH- or other solutes. In freshwater, reactions between O3 
==PAGE:13==
 
13 
and OH- can form hydroxyl radicals (OH*). Radicals can be more reactive as oxidizing agents than 
ozone itself. Radicals also oxidize solutes and can alter reaction pathways, or react with O3 again, 
causing radical chain reactions, where O3 degradation is accelerated. The reaction between ozone 
and OH- makes ozone less stable at higher pH values. Besides this, dissolved ozone is most stable at 
lower temperatures since solubility decreases and reaction rates increase with increasing 
temperature (Sander., 2023).  
 
Ozone in seawater 
In seawater, ozone chemistry is greatly different than that of fresh water. This is due to much higher 
concentrations of dissolved ions and presence unknown OM in seawater. For O3 dissolved in artificial 
seawater (32‰), Jung et al., (2017) showed that bromine (HOBr ⇌ OBr- + H+, pKa = 8.8) is the main 
residual oxidant despite much higher concentrations of chloride (Cl-, 17.6 g/L) than bromide (Br- 61 
mg/L). This is because Br- and Cl- reacts with O3 (first-order reaction limited by O3) to produce 
bromine and chlorine with reaction rate constants 160 M-1 s-1 and 0.003 M-1 s-1 respectively (Reaction 
3 and 9 in Table 1). If some chlorine (HOCl) is produced due to the high concentrations of chloride, 
HOCl is converted to HOBr swiftly at a reaction rate of 2.95 x 103 (Reaction 11 in Table 1) (Hoigné et 
al., 1984).

Compared to O3, HOBr has a lower oxidation potential (1.59 V) in seawater but is much more stable

Total residual oxidants (TRO) from ozonated seawater, have shown be toxic to marine organisms at 
concentrations between 0.01 – 0.06 mg/L measured as CL2 (Scolding et al., 2012; Reiser et al., 2011). 
In brackish water TRO measured as oxidation reduction potential (ORP) has shown to cause adverse 
health effects on Atlantic salmon post smolt at concentrations down to 350 mV (Stiller et al., 2020).  
Ozonation is therefore an oxidation method which needs to be monitored and dosed correctly to 
ensure positive effects from treatment and not cause health risks to reared species. 
Ozone and sulfide 
No literature on the reaction between free dissolved bromine and the sulfide species H2S and HS- 
were found in literature search for this report. Compared to ions in seawater, the rate constant of 
ozone with HS- is very high and H2S is somewhat high (k = 3 x 109 M-1 s-1 and 3 x 104 M-1 s-1, reaction 1 
and 2 respectively Table 1). This indicates that ozonation of seawater containing sulfide, might 
preferably react with sulfide at very low concentrations since the rate constant for HS- is 
approximately 19 x 106 times higher than that of Br-. 
In fresh water, reported complete oxidation of HS-/H2S to sulfate is achieved at a mole ratio of 2.4 -
2.46 : 1 O3 : TDS (Overbeck., 1996; Mark et al., 2011). Neither kinetics nor optimal rates for the 
reaction between O3 and TDS in seawater has been reported which might significantly differ from 
the reaction in fresh water due to the complex chemistry of seawater. 
 
Ozone and organic matter 
Several studies have been conducted on the reaction between O3, single organic molecules and 
natural organic matter (NOM). The reaction rates of O3 and specific organic molecules vary 
significantly depending on the type of organic compound (Equation 15 in Table 1). The composition 
on NOM can vary greatly depending on the source and it is therefore difficult to predict ozone 
reactivity. In freshwater, ozone decomposition has been shown to differ in natural waters and 
wastewater as the instantaneous O3 demand (ozone decomposed prior to 20 sec) increases in 
wastewater (Buffle et al., 2006). Yet, evidence point towards production of residual oxidants with 
effective solute removal capacity under initial ozone decomposition (Buffle et al., 2006; Buffle et al., 
2006). Ozonation of RAS water has shown to improve water quality by oxidation of organic matter 
(Summerfelt et al., 1997). One study has shown that especially humic-like and unsaturated CHO 
group of compounds in RAS are oxidized from ozonation forming new fulvic-like and saturated 
compounds (Aguilar-Alarcón et al., 2022). No literature was found on the effect of OM on O3 
reactions with sulfide. Hence, the complex unquantified interactions of ozone with RAS OM 
maintains an interest in elucidating ozone -sulfide reactions applicable to RAS.  
 
 
Principles of degassing water 
In RAS, degassing technology is an indispensable tool used for stripping CO2 produced from 
respiration of reared species and aerobic bacteria in ex. nitrification biofilters (Timmons et al., 2022). 
One degassing method is performed in tricking filter packed columns. A packed column functions by 
introducing water at the top into a distributor, letting gravity facilitate the downwards flow of water 
through trickling media. An upwards countercurrent of clean air is pumped into the bottom of the 
degas column to create a laminar counter flow between the gas and liquid phase and increase 
turbulence which results in a thinner interface for gas diffusion across the liquid and gas film. Taking 
energy consumption into consideration, optimal CO2 removal is achieved at a gas to liquid ratio (G:L) 
==PAGE:15==
 
15 
of 5 to 8 (Hu Y et al., 2011). G:L > 8 is optimal not considering energy consumption (Hu Y et al., 
2011). 
Because H2S is more soluble than CO2 the optimal G:L ratio might differ. Furthermore, O2 oxidation 
of H2S and HS- will contribute to the total removal of sulfide when applying degas technology as a 
method for H2S removal (Howe and Lawler., 1989). One study on two commercial salmon smolt 
facilities (freshwater) in Norway have reported a H2S net concentration decrease measured after 
degassers (Fernandes et al., 2024). 
 
Objective and study questions 
 
A need for effective strategies for removal of acute H2S and its larger speciation portion HS- in an 
aquaculture setting applicable to seawater RAS yet remains. 
Based on literature from the wastewater sector and a few studies on H2S in RAS, ozonation and 
degassing are two methods already applied in the industry with potential for H2S removal. 
 
This thesis report aims elucidate ozonation and degassing as H2S removal methods by answering the 
study questions: 
 
How does ozone/OPO affect HS- in seawater? 
Hypothesis: ozone/OPO oxidizes HS- in seawater at efficiencies close to that of ozone in freshwater. 
 
What is the optimal ozone to TDS ratio for complete removal of H2S without forming residual OPO in 
seawater and what are the associated kinetics of the reaction between the two compounds? 
Hypothesis: There is an optimal ratio where the concentration of dissolved ozone/OPO reacts with 
all dissolved sulfide forming no residuals of either compound. The reaction rate is faster than earlier 
oxidation methods studied in seawater. 
 
How does seawater RAS organic matter affect the reaction between ozone and TDS and how does it 
affect the optimal ozone dose for complete H2S removal? 
Hypothesis: Ozone/OPO reacts with a range of organic molecules present in seawater RAS. The 
affinity towards oxidizing dissolved sulfide is higher than the affinity towards organic matter from 
seawater RAS. If the affinity is not higher, there is an optimal ozone dose where both OM 
concentrations and TDS concentrations are accounted for. 
 
What is the effect of a degassing column on H2S in seawater and how can degasser CO2 removal 
efficiency be correlated to TDS removal under system specific parameters? 
Hypothesis: Since H2S is a gas, it can be stripped from a solution. Furthermore, O2 oxidizes sulfide, so 
the continuous mixing of clean air and water facilitated by a degas column will increase the 
oxidation rate. 
 
Based on answers to the questions above, what are possible applications of one or both methods for 
a best practice H2S removal strategy applicable to seawater RAS? 
Hypothesis: Taking practical application suitable for seawater RAS into consideration, a removal 
strategy usable to the industry can be suggested based on laboratory and pilot scale experiments.

Materials and method 
Experiments described in this report were conducted at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) 
Aqua, National institute of Aquatic Resources in Hirtshals, Denmark. 
 
Water source characteristics 
The water source for all experiments performed were seawater from the shared inlet pipe provided 
by Nordsøen Forskerpark in the North Sea outside vestmolen, Vesthavnen, Hirtshals, Denmark. In 
laboratory experiments seawater was stored at RT in 20 L jugs for maintenance of a constant low 
range temperature profile throughout trials. In degas column experiments and pilot scale initial 
trials, seawater was tapped directly from the outlet source at DTU aqua due to practical 
complications in temperature control with large water volumes. 
 
Stock solutions and reagents 
Every morning a 0.1 M sulfide stock solution was prepared by dissolving 2.4018 – 2.4030 g 
Na2S•9H2O (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA) in 100 ml oxygen stripped ultrapure water. Ultrapure 
water was stripped from oxygen by thoroughly bubbling with nitrogen gas for minimum 20 min. 
 
pH of seawater in laboratory batch experiments was regulated with 4 M phosphoric acid or 2 M 
NaOH. 
 
Organic matter 
Two types of organic matter (OM) were collected. OM from swirl separators and OM from a protein 
skimmer, were collected from a pilot-scale seawater RAS with approx. 400 kg rainbow trout fed 1.8 
kg commercial feed daily. The system composed of 6 fish tanks, each with a volume of 2 m3. Water 
treatment components of the system included one swirl separator pr fish tank (Alpha Aqua, 
Denmark), one drum filter (mesh = 50 micron, HEX X2-1T CM Aqua Technologies, Denmark), a pump 
sump (0.9 m3), a moving bed biofilter (Ø = 1.5 m, h = 3 m, v = 5.2 m3), a degassing column modified 
for combined effect as a protein skimmer, and an oxygen cone (Flow = 30 m3/h, pressure = 4 bar, 
Alpha Aqua, Denmark) (Fig. 1). The total volume of the system was 20 m3, with a water exchange of 
150 L/h. The system had been operating for months with TAN concentrations around 0 mg N/L and 
NO2- - N and NO3- - N concentrations averaging 0.2 mg/L and 30 mg/L respectively.

(SensorTrace Logger, Unisense, Denmark). Both sensors were calibrated using an 8-point calibration 
ranging from 0 to 29.3 µM (R2 < 0.99) in seawater regulated to approximately pH 2 with 5 M HCL. 
 
Temperature was measured and logged every second using a temperature sensor (Unisense, 
Denmark) connected to the same device and PC as the two H2S micro sensors. 
 
In lab scale experiments ORP was measured using two Sander ORP electrodes connected to 
corresponding Sander Redox Controllers (Erwin Sander Elektroapparatbau GmbH, Germany). ORP 
sensors were tested with ZoBell´s solution (HACH LANGE GMBH, Düsseldorf, Germany) at 20 °C.

Düsseldorf, Germany) with build in temperature sensor connected to a handheld transmitter 
(HQ40D, HACH LANGE GMBH, Düsseldorf, Germany). The sensor was calibrated using pH 4, 7 and 11 
buffer solutions. 
 
Pilot scale trials 
For pilot scale and degas column experiments temperature, O3, CO2, redox and pH was measured 
with sensors in the pump sump.  
O3 concentrations were measured every 10 seconds using a digital O3 partial pressure sensor,

calibrated until stable signal was reached. Second, 1 ml calibration solution was dissolved in the 
ultrapure water and the sensor was calibrated again. Third, with the accompanying dosing spoon, 
one spoonful of pH conditioner was dissolved in the solution and the sensor was calibrated again.

Experimental setup general principles 
 
The overall purpose of this thesis was to evaluate H2S removal strategies and suggest practically 
applicable contingency methods for H2S removal in seawater RAS. Hence, all laboratory scale 
experiments were conducted with the purpose of answering questions that could guide towards the 
design of an acute H2S/TDS removal prototype pilot system based on technologies already used in 
industrial seawater RAS.

First experiment: The effect of O3 on HS- in seawater 
In this experiment, the aim was to investigate if OPO in seawater oxidizes the larger TDS fraction HS- 
present in the pH range relevant to seawater RAS. 
Temperature, salinity, and oxygen saturation were measured in the batch reactor. O3 and air was 
injected (bubbled) for 5 min in the O3 and control reactor respectively. pH was regulated to 9.5 with 
2 M NaOH. OPO was measured (1.25-1.35 mg/L in ozone treatments and < 0.05 mg/L in controls). 
The reactors were spiked with 375 µL 0.1 M sulfide stock to a concentration of 0.8 mg TDS/L (O3:TDS

measured 20 min after spike in controls and O3 treatments. 
 
 
Second experiment: Optimal O3:TDS ratio in seawater 
In demineralized water the mole ratio of O3:TDS for complete oxidation of H2S/HS- to SO42-  is 2.4-
2.46 (Mark et al., 2011; Overbeck, 1995). The molar mass of sulfide, and ozone is 32.1 and 48 g/mol 
respectively. Assuming an O3:TDS mole ratio of 2.4, that corresponds to the mass ratio 3.6. 
In relevance of seawater RAS, the goal is to reduce H2S concentrations below toxic levels for fish (< 
0.05 mg/l for Atlantic salmon). Therefore, the purpose of this experiment was to examine the 
optimal O3:TDS ratio for complete H2S removal in seawater and pH values relevant to seawater RAS.

Not to completely oxidize TDS to SO42-, hence not taking further oxidation of the reaction 
intermediates thiosulfate, bisulfite, and sulfite into account. 
Due to the ozonation setup specific efficiency, the TDS concentration was set to 1 mg/l in all 
experiments. O3:TDS mg/l ratios were tested in the range from 1.5 to 4.2 starting with high ratios

Procedure 
Temperature, salinity, pH and ORP (mV) was measured in seawater followed by sampling of TCOD 
and anions. The ozone and control reactors were bubbled (air for control) for 5 to 13 minutes 
depending on aimed O3:TDS ratio in the ozone reactor. After bubbling OPO were sampled and pH 
was regulated to 7.25 +- 0.06 by dissolving 40 µl 4M phosphoric acid in the reactors. pH and ORP 
was measured, H2S and temperature logging were initiated on the computer connected to Unisense 
software. Two minutes into logging 468 µl 0.1 M sulfide stock was spiked in the reactors (1 mg/l 
TDS). The OPO was measured three minutes after sampling. Ten minutes after H2S spike, a sample 
for TRO were taken and ORP was measured. At last, TCOD and anions were sampled, and TRO were 
measured. The experiment was conducted 14 times for satisfactory coverage of the chosen O3:TDS 
range.  
 
Third experiment: The effect of RAS OM on optimal ozone dose for TDS removal 
The optimal O3 : TDS ratio obtained is seawater experiments was tested against three concentrations 
of organic matter collected from a swirl separator and concentrated effluent from the protein 
skimmer of a pilot scale seawater RAS rearing Rainbow trout.

The procedure in organic matter experiments closely resembled the experiments conducted on O3 : 
TDS ratios in seawater.

were bubbled (air for control) for approximately 8 minutes reaching 2.02 +- 0.22 mg O3/l in the 
ozone reactor. After bubbling OPO were sampled and pH was regulated to 7.3 +- 0.06 by dissolving 
40 µl 4M phosphoric acid in the reactors. pH and ORP was measured, H2S and temperature logging 
were initiated on the computer connected to Unisense software. Two minutes into logging 468 µl 
0.1 M sulfide stock was mixed with OM from the swirl separator or protein skimmer and the mixture 
was spiked in the reactors (1 mg/l TDS and 20, 40 or 60 mg/l TCOD). OPO was measured three 
minutes after sampling. For TDS analysis (sulfulla), 4 ml sample was fixated in 1 ml ZnAc (10%) in 5

Nine minutes after spike, a sample for TRO was taken and pH and ORP was measured. At last, TCOD 
and anions were sampled, and TRO was measured.

Degassing vs TDS in seawater 
This study investigates the efficiency of a degas tower on the removal of TDS in seawater under a

countercurrent of clean air pumped from outside the facility. An experiment on CO2 degassing with 
the same set parameters was performed for correlation between CO2 gas transfer rate and TDS 
removal rate.

The configuration of the setup included a tub (pump sump) with the inside dimensions 92 x 112 x 70 
cm (Fig. 3). The corners of the tub were rounded, and the radius of the circle formed by the corners 
were 14 cm. Giving the total sump volume 0,71 m3. In the sump a 240 cm in height, Ø ~ 70 cm degas 
column made from 1 cm thick hard plastic was placed. The bottom of the column was perforated 
ensuring sufficient water flow and mixing between the column and the sump. The degas column had 
two opposing air intakes (Ø ~ 32 mm), approximately 40 cm up from the bottom and 4 possible 
water intakes close to the top (Fig. 3). Inside the degas column a triploid measuring 50 cm in height 
was placed. The function of the tripod was lifting trickling media above the water and air intake. 
 
 
Flowrate from the seawater supply hose was 84.4 l/min, calculated from measured average time to 
fill a 20-liter jug three times. At this flowrate the sump was filled for 3 min and 33 seconds giving a 
calculated volume of 300 L.  The sump was filled with all necessary equipment (pumps, pipes, 
sensors) and the water level was marked.

Setup for combined effect of ozonation and degassing on TDS 
This section aims to provide a comprehensive description of the initial design of a pilot scale batch 
reactor for independent and combined effects of ozonation and degassing on TDS. The setup was 
designed for transferable application in the industry and therefore based on components already 
used in conventional SW RAS. In RAS, several options are available with the purpose of dissolving 
gases in water (Timmons et al., 2022). Based on the configuration of a given RAS, any of these can be 
substituted for the method selected for dissolving ozone in this batch reactor.  
 
The principle of the setup was to apply an ozonation system with a controlled partial flow from the 
pump sump to the degas column reactor (Fig. 6). The combination of experimental factors included 
one O3 dose (232 mg O3/hour at a flow of 2.6 l gas/min), one TDS dose (1 mg TDS/l) and two levels of 
hydraulic loading going through the ozonation system (high flow = 1300 l/hour and medium flow = 
1100 l/hour). Only one replicate for each test was performed due to lack of time. The outlet of the 
ozonation system led into the degas column, and the total flow through the column was 2200 l/hour 
for all TDS removal experiments. The gas flow from the ozone generator was selected based on the 
highest O3 production rate of the S 500 ozone generator measured with a flow cell and 254 nm 
absorbance on a UV/Vis spectrophotometer (Beckman DU 530, RAMCON A/S, Denmark) and 254 nm 
transmittance on the REAL UV254 (REAL TECH Inc, Canada). 
 
 
 
 
Equipment and design parameters 
The configuration of the setup included an ozonation system coupled to the degas column batch 
reactor. Due to availability of materials and simplicity of installation, the gas transfer component of 
the ozonation system was a freshwater protein skimmer (Fresh – Skim 200, Erwin Sander, Germany). 
The skimmer composed of a reaction tube inner Ø of 19.4 cm and water column height of 134 cm 
giving a total volume of approximately 40 liters. A water inlet was connected to the top of the 
reaction tube. The water outlet was located next to the inlet, leading through a pipe with entrance 
at the bottom of the reaction tube. This provided a downward water flow through the reaction tube

when operated. A ceramic air stone was located approximately 20 cm from the bottom of the 
reaction tube connected with piping to the gas inlet, entering the skimmer slightly above water inlet

Outside the skimmer, the gas inlet fitting was connected to tubing forming an upwards inversed U 
tube connected to a stop valve. This functioned to prevent potential pressure from driving water 
into upstream components. A S 500 ozone generator and an air pump were connected upstream of 
the gas inlet with PTFE tubing. A 1 – 5 L/min air flow meter was connected between the ozone 
generator and the gas inlet tubing connected to the skimmer for air flow control. Results from 
preliminary analysis of gas flow rate and O3 concentrations (mg/L) measured on a BECKMAN DU 530 
Life Science UV/Vis spectrophotometer (RAMCON A/S, Denmark) showed that the ozone generator 
had the highest O3 production (232 mg O3/hour) at a flow rate of 2.6 L/min. Therefore, the airflow 
was set to 2.6 L gas /min when operating the system. 
Operation of the protein skimmer was modified from recommended use for protein skimming by the 
manufacturer (G/L ~ 0.75 - 1) to general recommended parameters for gas transfer (G/L < 0.25) 
(Timmons et al., 2022). This way the skimmer functioned as a diffusor aerator with a spiraling 
downward countercurrent of water for increased turbulence and contact time between the gas- and 
water film. 
  
The skimmer was elevated so that the outlet was located just above the top of the degas column. 
The water outlet led into the liquid distributer with a slight height difference so that sampling 
directly from the ozone gasser could be performed. Water was pumped into the ozone gasser with 
an Aqua Medic AC Runner 9.2 pump with a power control unit capable of regulating the power from 
1 to 8. The pump was connected to the ozone gasser inlet and accompanying ball valve with a PVC 
pipe Ø 32 x 1.8 mm (GEORG FISCHER, Denmark) and a flow meter 250 – 2000 L/h (GEORG FISCHER, 
Denmark). Giving a water lifting height of approximately 215 cm. The water flow into the ozone 
gasser could be regulated with the ball valve and pump power control unit. 
 
Experimental procedure 
Ozonation system efficiency in seawater 
To approximate the ozonation setup efficiency, a test on the increase in dissolved O3 and OPO over a 
20 min period was performed in 300 L seawater in the batch reactor. System parameters included a 
hydraulic loading of 1100 L/h through the ozone gasser and an ozone gas flow of 2.6 L/min (G/L = 
0.14). The countercurrent airflow in the degas column was turned off during this experiment to 
reduce the effect of the degasser. To completely remove the effect of the degasser, a bypass could 
have been installed between the ozone gasser and degas column leading directly into the pump 
sump. Salinity of the water source was measured before operating the system. Temperature, pH, O3 
(dissolved gas), and ORP was measured continuously in the pump sump and logged every 10 
seconds. OPO samples were taken in the ozone gasser outlet at time 0, 2, 5, 10, 15, and 20 min of 
running the system and measured 3-5 min after sampling. One OPO sample was taken from the 
pump sump at time 20 min and measured 3-5 min after sampling. To approximate the amount of 
ozone added to the reactor, transmittance was measured in the gas phase with a flow cell at the 
ozone generator outlet and in the gas outlet of the gasser. O3 gas measurements were conducted at 
2, 10 and 15 min. 
 
Pilot scale TDS removal 
In experiments conducted on the combined effect of degassing and ozonation, temperature, pH, O3, 
and ORP was measured continuously in the pump sump and logged every 10 seconds. A total 
seawater volume of 300 L (260 L in the pump sump and 40 L in the ozone gasser) was spiked to 1 mg 
TDS/L. After spiking, the mixing pump in the pump sump was run for approximately 5 minutes to 
ensure proper mixing. OPO and TDS (time 0) samples were taken from the pump sump.

Results 
 
First experiment: Effect of ozone on HS- in seawater 
To evaluate if OPO in seawater (35 ‰, 19.5 ± 0.2 °C, pH = 9.7 ± 0.2, O2 = 97,3 ± 1,2 % saturation) 
oxidizes the larger speciation fraction HS- present in an aquaculture setting, an experiment was 
performed on the reaction between OPO and TDS at pH ~ 9.5 ensuring no H2S were present in the 
solution. 
The oxidation curve of HS- can be seen in Fig. 7. 
As a result of pH > 9.5 before spiking to 1 mg TDS/L in batch reactors, no H2S was measured with 
sensors in the control and ozone treatment over the 20 min reaction period. No statistically 
significant differences were observed between TDS concentrations measured at time 1 min and 
samples measured at time 2 - 20 min after TDS spike in the ozone treatment. Indicating, that the 
reaction between OPO and HS- was completed within the first minute. Due to the fast reaction rate, 
kinetics were not measurable with the setup in this experiment. 
 
A statistically significant difference was observed between the control (389.3 ± 58.5 mg TDS/L) and 
ozone treatment (95.0 ± 32.8 mg TDS/L) already 1 min after spike (Fig. 7.). Hence indicating an 
oxidizing effect of OPO generated from ozonation of seawater on HS-. A steep increase in TDS was 
observed 1 min after TDS spike in both treatments. The increase in TDS 1 min after spike observed in 
the ozone treatment combined with measured OPO < 0.02 mg/l, 5 min after spike showed that an 
O3:TDS mg/L ratio of 1.5 was not high enough to fully remove TDS.

Second experiment: Optimal O3:TDS ratio in seawater 
The optimal O3:TDS ratio in seawater (34 ± 1 ‰, pH = 7.25 ± 0.06, RT) was approximated by spiking 1 
mg TDS/L into reactors with 1.5 – 4.2 mg O3/L. Visual inspection of raw data logged every second

reaction between dissolved O3 and TDS. For controls (air), it took approximately 1 min after TDS 
spike before a stable H2S signal was reached. In all replicates of all O3 treatments, H2S data showed 
negative values before H2S spike (Fig. 8.). Indicating some interference between ozone and the 
sensors.  
 
The time to reach stable H2S signals after TDS spike in O3 treatments varied between 1.5 to 4 min 
and no peaks followed by a decline in H2S was observed, indicating that the reaction had taken place 
before 1.5 min.

Pilot scale combined effect of ozonation and degassing on TDS preliminary results 
 
In all pilot scale ozone trials conducted in seawater (32 ‰, 13.4 ± 0.3 ◦C, pH = 8.1 ± 0.04) with and

concentration measured from the pump sump 20 min after ozonation was 0.06 mg O3/ L. The 
difference between O3 sensor and O3/oxidants measured values was ascribed to oxidants being 
present as bromine (HOBr) and not dissolved Ozone.

Discussion 
 
Effect of ozone on TDS in seawater 
 
Results from this report showed that ozone produced oxidants (OPO) in seawater (RT, 33-35‰) 
generated by diffusion of ozone gas into batch reactors had a very high capacity to oxidize H2S and 
the larger speciation fraction HS-. For complete H2S removal and no TRO, the optimal mg/L O3:TDS 
was 2.08 – 2.16 (1.39 – 1.44 mol/L ratio) measured as total Cl2 and multiplied by 0.68 as a CL2 to O3 
conversion factor. Overbeck., (1996) and Mark et al., (2011) reported complete oxidation of H2S/HS- 
to sulfate in freshwater at a O3:TDS mole ratio of 2.4 and 2.46. The values in this report are lower 
than these reported values which might be explained by several factors. One being that oxidation of 
TDS in this report only accounts for removal of sulfide species and not complete oxidation to sulfate. 
Secondly, the reaction in this report is facilitated by measured OPO from ozone bubbling and not a 
spike of known dissolved O3 concentrations from an ozone stock solution. Hence the oxidation 
reactions in this report are facilitated by different residual oxidant molecules like HOBr and might 
not be comparable (Jung et al., 2017). For comparable results, the experiment could be conducted 
with O3 stock solutions instead of ozone bubbling. After spiking known concentrations of O3 into 
seawater, TRO could be measured as CL2 and values could be compared. Since ozonation in RAS 
often is facilitated by continuous diffusion of ozone gas, this method was evaluated as more relevant 
to the aquaculture section (Kovács et al., 2023; Timmons et al., 2022). Jung et al., (2017), showed 
that the primary oxidizing agent produced from ozonation in seawater was HOBr with complete 
conversion from O3 to HOBr within 1 minute. This insinuates that experiments in this report were 
conducted on the reaction between HOBr and TDS though they are described as the reaction 
between O3 and TDS. Total chlorine can be converted to total bromine measured as Br2 with the 
factor 2.25 (Hach Lange., 2022). If OPO was converted to bromine, the optimal mg/L HOBr:TDS ratio 
for complete TDS removal and no TRO would be between 6.88 and 7.15. These concentrations also 
agree with reported values where 2 mg/L ozone injected into artificial seawater (32 ‰, 61 mg Br-/L) 
formed bromine concentrations of 6.4 measured as mg Br2/L (Jung et al., 2017). 
 
Kinetics were not measurable with the setup in this report and the reaction between OPO and TDS 
was completed within 1 minute. This shows that OPO is a very effective method for acute H2S 
removal in seawater. Because the reaction is completed at such a fast rate, the kinetics are not of 
high interest to the aquaculture sector. If kinetics still were to be studied, a continuous quench flow 
system method coupled to a flow cell in a spectrophotometer could be used to measure initial OPO 
decomposition as described in Buffle et al., (2006). 
 
Experiments on the optimal O3:TDS at different TDS concentrations were not conducted due to time 
limitations and ozone generator capacity. A suggestion for future work is to define and compare 
optimal O3:TDS and kinetics in seawater and seawater RAS water at concentrations of 2, 3 and 4 mg 
TDS/L. 
 
TAN, NO2- and trace metal might affect the optimal O3:TDS either through competition for ozone or 
catalytic effects (Spiliotopoulou et al., 2018; Yan et al., 2019; Yang and Liu., 2022). These effects 
were not studied in this report but could be of interest for future work. 
 
Effect of OM on optimal O3:TDS 
 
Experiments conducted on the effect of OM collected from a swirl separator and a protein skimmer 
in a pilot scale SW RAS rearing rainbow trout showed that OM concentrations from 20-60 mg 
TCOD/L did not affect the optimal O3:TDS ratio for complete removal of H2S. This can be explained by 
==PAGE:45==
 
45 
two things. One is that the reactivity of OPO and OM present in RAS is so low that the affinity 
towards HS- overweighs the affinity towards OM molecules at a high enough factor to oxidize HS- 
even though OM concentrations are 60 times higher (60 mg TCOD/L and 1 mg TDS/L). Another 
reason could be that the OM collected from swirl separators and protein skimmers don’t react with 
OPO or that the OM collected primarily constitute of particulate OM (POM) and that ozone reacts 
with dissolved OM (DOM). The composition of POM and DOM in collected OM was not measured in 
this report. Differentiating between the two could be done by centrifuging OM samples at 4500rpm 
for 15 min, then filtering supernatants with 0.2 µL syringe filters and measuring COD of the filtered 
sample (Rojas-Tirado et al., 2021). This would give the dissolved COD (DCOD). Particulate COD 
(PCOD) would be calculated by subtracting the measured DCOD concentration (mg/L) of the filtered 
sample from the TCOD measured in the unfiltered sample. 
Optimally the experimental setup for optimal O3:TDS should be designed for performance in RAS 
water. The problem with the setup in this report is that the solution is bubbled with ozone before 
TDS is spiked. DOM is difficult to isolate at high concentrations from a solution. Hence experiments 
on DOM would be performed directly in ex. RAS water. This means that if it was to be performed by 
ozonation like in this report, then OM that potentially compete for O3 with TDS would already have 
reacted with OPO before TDS spike.  
 
One way to circumvent this obstacle could be to dissolve known TDS concentrations in reactors with 
RAS water and then spike with dissolved O3 from a stock solution at different O3:TDS.  
 
Another method, like the one planned for pilot experiments in this report, is to perform continuous 
ozonation in RAS water spiked with known TDS concentrations in a system with a stable known 
O3/OPO production capacity. Results from experiments performed with this method could be 
applied directly in the industry as optimal O3:TDS could be correlated to the ozonation capacity of a 
given ozone diffusion system at an industrial RAS. 
 
 
Degas column effect on H2S/HS- 
 
Experiments conducted on a degas column with specific set parameters in SW (33 ± 1 ‰, 14 ± 1 ◦C, 
pH = 8 ± 0.05) spiked with 1 mg TDS/L showed that degassing have potential for continuous removal 
of TDS in seawater (t1/2 = 19.5 ± 2 min). Compared to t1/2 of approximately 118.5 min (pH ~ 7.4, 33 ‰, 
~13.3 °C) in O2 saturated seawater described by Bergstedt et al., (2022) this is a 5 - 6 times decrease 
in t1/2 caused by degassing. Results are not directly comparable between this experiment and the 
one described by Bergstedt et al., (2022) since pH and surface to volume ratio of seawater differ 
between the two experiments. For direct comparable results, a control treatment should be 
performed on the degas batch reactor by spiking TDS into the pump sump and running only the 
pump used in the setup for generating flow across sensors. An experimental control treatment was 
not conducted in this report due to time limitations. Furthermore, for results to be directly 
applicable to the industry, the degas setup could be conducted at different G/L ratios, pH, media 
types, column height and TDS concentrations. The G/L ratio were 18 in this report, which is a lot 
higher than the recommended 8 for CO2 stripping (Hu Y et al., 2011). This was to ensure maximum 
effect on TDS removal, and because piping selected for the experiment did not allow higher flow 
rates. For practical applications a G/L ratio of 8 would be better suited as a single set parameter for 
the experiment. pH in this report were around 8 which is higher than the normal pH in seawater RAS 
facilities. As a single set parameter, it is suggested that future trials are performed directly in RAS 
water or at pH around 7.2-7.4 closer to realistic seawater RAS parameters. The reason pH might 
affect degasser performance is that TDS speciation is pH dependent. Lowering pH further has been 
done to increase degasser performance in wastewater management (Howe and Lawler., 1989). This 
is not recommended in a RAS setting as lowering pH negatively affect fish respiration and increases 
==PAGE:46==
 
46 
toxicity of TDS. The reason for doing experiments in RAS water is that microbes in the solution might 
compete for oxygen and therefore also affect O2 oxidation of TDS and degasser performance 
(Bergstedt et al., 2022). 
Based on results from this report, degassing might not be suitable as a single standing acute H2S 
removal strategy. Yet it might function as a prevention method for slower increases in TDS facilitated 
by aerobic and anaerobic bacteria (Fernandes et al., 2024). 
 
Furthermore, degassing might function with the dual purpose of degassing O3 in case of an 
overdose. This was not tested in this report due to the low ozone production capacity of the ozone 
generator, which resulted in instantaneous production of nongaseous OPO (0 mg O3/L measured 20 
min after ozonation with optical sensors at 0.06 mg OPO/L in the pilot ozonation capacity pre-trial). 
 
Pilot scale preliminary trials 
In pilot scale preliminary trials including ozonation and degassing, no observable effect was shown 
compared to TDS removal of the degas column alone. For future pilot scale studies with larger 
seawater volumes, an increase in ozone generator capacity is recommended. When ozone generator 
capacity is not the limiting factor, one can conduct studies on optimal G/L ratios in the ozonation 
system and conduct experiments designed for extracting mass balance relationships. In this report 
the experiment was designed as a batch reactor, but a single flow experiment might be better for 
reflecting a realistic scenario in SW RAS, where a potential high concentration TDS outbreak could 
come from single components like fixed bed biofilters and move into the remaining RAS. 
 
Oxidation reduction potential 
ORP measurements throughout experiments in this report showed clear correlations with increased 
oxidation potential from ozonation and increased reduction potential when dissolved sulfide was 
present in the solution. Besides sulfide, other compounds in RAS like organic matter and nitrite have 
reduction potential measurable with ORP sensors (Spiliotopoulou et al., 2018). ORP is therefore not 
recommended as a stand-alone method when dosing ozone for acute H2S removal but can be used 
as a method for measuring TRO. 
 
Suggestion for H2S removal best practice 
Based on the results gathered from this report and limited experiments conducted pilot scale a best 
practice for acute H2S removal could be through ozonation at a O3:TDS mg/L ratio of 2. In practice an 
ozonation component could be placed between the outlet of a biofilter and inlet of a degas column. 
Maybe even as a side loop to the pump sump. H2S sensors could be placed upstream of the 
ozonation component and in the biofilter. An ORP sensor could be placed downstream of the 
ozonation component and in fish tanks. As soon as H2S is measured in ex. the biofilter a computer 
software automatically calculates TDS concentrations based on salinity, temperature and pH as 
described in equation (3.) and (4.). Based on TDS concentrations and the hydraulic flow rate, the 
ozonation system starts dosing at the optimal O3:TDS ratio. If ORP measurements downstream show 
values below 200 mV a feedback system slowly upregulates ozonation. If ORP measurements 
downstream show values above 300 mV, the same feedback mechanism slowly downregulates 
ozonation. The practical mechanisms of ozone dosing, and up or down regulation depends on the 
chosen method for ozonation. 
 
This best practice suggestion needs further studying as continuous ozonation trials were limited in 
this report, and the oxidation mechanisms might change as a result of continuous ozonation and 
water chemistry of RAS water.

47 
Conclusion 
 
The obtained results in this report demonstrate that: 
 
• 
Ozonation and resulting OPO is a very effective treatment for the removal of H2S/HS- and 
suitable for application in seawater, as the time required for complete reaction with TDS, is 
within 1 min. 
• 
The optimal mg/L O3:TDS ratio were approximately 2.08 – 2.16. At higher ratios, residual 
oxidants toxic to fish were measured, and at lower ratios residual H2S was measured. 
• 
Organic matter gathered from swirl separators and protein skimmers does not affect the 
optimal O3:TDS ratio within the range of 20 – 60 mg TCOD/L. 
• 
A degas column has effect as a method for continuous H2S removal even at pH around 8, 
with half-lives for 1 mg TDS/L of approximately 20 min. 
• 
ORP sensors are reliable as a supplementary method for TDS and TRO detection. 
• 
Conclusively, the proposed technology of ozonation in seawater is a potentially very 
effective method for close to instantaneous removal of H2S and HS- and requires technical 
equipment already applied in most RAS. The proposed technology of degassing is a risk-free 
effective method for steady removal of TDS working to assist in reducing the risk of reaching 
toxic H2S levels in RAS and requires technology already essential to RAS.
