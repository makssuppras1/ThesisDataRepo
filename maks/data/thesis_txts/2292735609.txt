Abstract 
The safety and efficacy of drugs are of major importance. Identification, quantification, and 
control of impurities in drugs are a significant part of drug development and regulatory 
assessment. Impurities in drugs can be formed by reactions of excipients and the active 
pharmaceutical ingredient (API). It is therefore important to choose suitable excipients for a 
given API, to ensure no potentially toxic compounds will occur during the manufacturing or 
storing of the drug product. Information about impurity formation in an excipient/API system 
can be gained through compatibility studies, typically conducted at elevated temperatures to 
stress the system. However, as different reactions are driven by different factors, these studies do 
not reveal all types of impurities that may be formed during long term storage. 
The aim of this master’s thesis was to investigate which types of impurity forming reactions 
occur in powder mixtures of polyethylene glycol (PEG) and nortriptyline. As PEG is known to 
undergo autoxidation to form formaldehyde (FH) and formic acid (FA), a high-performance 
liquid chromatography (HPLC) method was developed to determine FH and FA in PEG. As 
these two compounds can react with nortriptyline to form its N-methylated degradation product 
amitriptyline, a HPLC method for separation of nortriptyline and amitriptyline was also 
developed. 
Two compatibility studies were conducted. The first showed that compatibility studies of the 
PEG 1500/nortriptyline system should be conducted at high temperature with addition of water 
in order to observe most formation of impurities. The second compatibility study was conducted 
at 50 ºC, with addition of water, for the PEG 1500/nortriptyline system, where it was observed, 
that three types of impurities were formed, being PEG adducts, water adducts, and amine 
reaction products, and that formation of these impurities followed different types of kinetics.

2 Introduction 
The safety and efficacy of drugs are of major importance. Formation of impurities in drug 
products is therefore highly undesirable, why identification, quantification, and control of these 
are a significant part of drug development and regulatory assessment [1]. Impurities can be 
formed by reactions of excipients with the active pharmaceutical ingredient (API), causing 
degradation products of the API. These may alter the effect of the drug, making it potentially 
toxic and cause a loss of potency of the drug [2]. Therefore, it is important to choose suitable 
excipients for a given API, to ensure no potentially toxic compounds will be formed during the 
manufacturing or storing of the drug product. Genotoxic impurities are in particular dangerous, 
as these are DNA reactive, and therefore also handled according to stricter guidelines [3]. 
The International Conference on Harmonisation of Technical Requirements for Registration of 
Pharmaceuticals for Human Use (ICH) has prepared guidelines for handling impurities in drug 
substances and drug products, describing the threshold limits for reporting and identification of 
these [4] [5]. It is the responsibility of the company submitting the New Drug Application 
(NDA) to show that the drug is safe and effective. Part of this is to conduct stability studies, 
where it is ensured that no “significant change” is occurring in the drug product. These changes 
include a 5% change in assay from initial value and any degradation products exceeding its 
acceptance criterion [6]. In the guideline QA1(R2) ICH describes, how these stability studies 
should be performed. Long term studies should be of duration of minimum 12 months. In the 
drug development process, this is a long time, due to the patent life especially if new unidentified 
impurities are formed during the study. This would result in new long term studies, causing a 
massive loss of potential income. Knowledge about which types of reactions could occur in a 
given excipient/API system reduces these kinds of unwanted reactions. Therefore, more focus on 
elucidating reaction types between excipients and API at an early stage, could result in a new 
drug to reach the market as soon as possible thus maximising the profit. 
Traditionally, excipients have been used in drugs to identify brands and to mask a bitter taste. 
Moreover, they have also been used to increase tablet size, because a low dosage is impractical 
to handle by consumers [7]. Excipients are normally chosen based on the desired characteristics, 
such as functionality, consistency, regulatory acceptance, cost, and availability [8]. These 
choices are often related to the manufacturing process, where it is desired to have as easy 
==PAGE:12==
 
 
 
Page 11 of 101 
 
processes as possible, with less focus on the compatibility between excipient and API. The 
knowledge about impurity forming reactions in solid state pharmaceutical products, is generally 
sparse, because solid state reactions have complex reaction mechanisms [9]. Reactions in liquid 
state are more easily understood, because the reactants are in solution and not locked in a crystal 
form. Solid state reactions begin at one or more nucleation sites that are spread through the 
crystal. These sites are more or less randomly produced, resulting in complicated reaction rates 
of solid state reactions, which do not follow Arrhenius kinetics. This makes it difficult to predict 
the formation of impurities in a drug product [10].  
Compatibility studies, where API is mixed with excipients in binary or multicomponent 
mixtures, either as powder mixtures or pressed to a tablet or other dosage forms, can provide 
information about which impurities are formed in a drug product. Important factors in these 
studies are temperature, relative humidity (RH), and ratio of drug/excipient. The studies are 
usually conducted at elevated temperatures to stress the API/excipient system and thereby save 
time. A recommended [11] stress temperature, for solid state compatibility studies is 60 ºC and 
studies can be performed with 20% added water in tightly sealed vials for 4-8 weeks. The effect 
of moisture can be tested in studies carried out at 40 ºC in closed or open vials with a RH of 75% 
for three to six months, but these are only guidelines, which may not be useful for any given 
system. 
Samples from compatibility studies are commonly analysed by high-performance liquid 
chromatography (HPLC), which is a widely used analytical tool in drug development. It works 
by separating analytes using high pressure to force solvent through columns with fine porous 
particles, resulting in different times for the analytes [12]. This separation technique is often 
coupled with ultraviolet (UV) detection, why it can be necessary to derivatise non-UV 
compounds in order to achieve better detection [13].  
This master’s thesis will consist of compatibility studies of a binary API/excipient system. The 
excipient polyethylene glycol (PEG) is chosen, because it is known to undergo oxidative 
degradation forming small molecules such as formaldehyde (FH) and formic acid (FA) [14] [15]. 
The chosen API is nortriptyline (Scheme 1) due to its amine functionality, which is known to 
react with aldehydes and carboxylic acids. The N-methylated degradation product of the reaction 
of nortriptyline, FA, and FH is known as amitriptyline (Scheme 1) [16]. 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
 
Page 12 of 101 
 
 
Scheme 1: The structure of the model API nortriptyline and its N-methylated degradation product amitriptyline 
This study will provide a HPLC-UV method for the analysis of FA and FH derivatised by 2,4-
dinitrophenylhydrazine (DNPH). Furthermore, an UPLC-UV method will be included for the 
analysis of nortriptyline and its degradation products. The aim of this study is to elucidate, which 
types of degradation reactions occur, and which parameters drive these reactions. The study will 
reveal that different types of impurity forming reactions are dependent on different parameters.

4 Impurities in Drugs 
Impurities in drug substances and drug products are an important issue, because the safety of the 
drug product is dependent on the toxicological properties of both the active drug substance and 
the impurities it may contain [1]. An impurity is defined as something that is impure or makes 
something else impure. This refers to any material that affects the purity of the active 
pharmaceutical ingredient (API) or drug substance [17]. Consequently, impurities in drugs have 
to be monitored to ensure the quality of drugs. According to ICH, impurities are classified as 
organic impurities, inorganic impurities, and residual solvent [4]. Organic impurities can occur 
during the manufacturing process or storage of the drug substance and include starting materials, 
by-products, intermediates, degradation products, reagents, and catalysts. Inorganic impurities 
can likewise occur from the manufacturing process and include reagents, catalysts, heavy metals 
or other residual metals, inorganic salts, and other materials such as filter aids. Residual solvents 
include inorganic of organic liquids used during the synthesis of drug substances [4]. In this 
study, the formation of degradation products as impurities is the main focus. 
The threshold of impurities in drug products (Table 1) is governed by ICH and is based on the 
Maximal Daily Dose defined as the amount of drug substance administered per day [5]. Three 
types of thresholds are listed for the amount of impurity; reporting, identification, and 
qualification threshold. The reporting threshold is the level that must be reported to regulatory 
agencies to alert them that the impurity is present. The identification threshold is the level 
requiring identification of the impurity, and the qualification threshold is the level where 
toxicology studies have to be performed to ensure safety of the compound [10].

Impurities can occur both in drug products and in drug substances or excipients. This study will 
focus on impurities in excipients, and the reaction of these with drug substance to form 
degradation products. 
4.1 Excipients 
Excipients are historically known as inert materials. However, they are not completely without 
function as they play a role in the pharmaceutical formulation process to aid manufacturing, 
administration or absorption. Traditionally, excipients have been used in drugs to identify 
brands, to mask a bitter taste, and to increase tablet size, because a low dosage is impractical to 
handle by consumers [7] [18] [2].  
Excipients can interact both physically and chemically with the API. This has to do with the fact 
that the API is vulnerable to degradation due to the structural features that are designed to 
interact with receptors. Some of the common modes of degradation are hydrolysis, oxidation, 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
 
Page 17 of 101 
 
isomerization, photolysis, and polymerization. Direct drug-excipient interactions are mostly 
observed for water soluble compounds in liquid systems where the opportunity for collisions 
between the interacting groups is larger than in solid state [18] [2]. 
4.1.1 Polyethylene glycol 
This section will focus on the excipient polyethylene glycol (PEG) which is used not only in the 
pharmaceutical industry but in a wide variety of industries including the cosmetic industry, 
textile and leather industry, paper industry, ceramics, and glass industry as well as in rubber, 
petroleum, mining, and metal industries. In the pharmaceutical industry, PEG is e.g. used as 
ointment bases [19] and for a process known as pegylation, which is the conjugation of 
biomolecules with PEG. This pegylation is an established method for increasing circulating half-
life of protein pharmaceuticals [20]. PEG is also used as an active ingredient in laxatives because 
it has the ability to form hydrogen bonds with water [21]. 
 
Figure 2: Polyethylene glycol with n as the number of repeating units. 
PEG is a water soluble polymer of ethylene oxide, as shown in Figure 2. It is nontoxic and pH 
neutral making it attractive as an excipient [20]. PEGs are classified by a number representing 
the average weight of the polymers, meaning that PEG 400 has an average molecular weight of 
400 Daltons. When the molecular weight is below 600 Daltons, PEG is a clear viscous liquid, 
and when the molecular weight is above 1000 Daltons, PEG is a white waxy solid [22]. 
PEGs are known to undergo autoxidation by a radical mechanism which can lead to random 
chain scission [23] [24] and form several degradation products some of them being FA [14] and 
FH [25] [15] [26].

Generally, an autoxidation consists of three stages; initiation, propagation, and termination as 
shown in Scheme 2 [27].

During the initiation step, a free radical is formed to start a chain reaction. For a polymeric 
excipient, such as PEG, this can be due to homolytic cleavage of a weak bond. However in the 
solid state, formation of free radicals is slow to nonexistent and therefore it is also likely that the 
free radical comes from an impurity such as peroxide. Trace amounts of hydroperoxide 
impurities are found in many polymeric pharmaceutical excipients, because the formation of 
these excipients is through radical reactions [29]. 
Propagation is the step where the radical reacts with the drug or excipient to form a 
hydroperoxide. Termination then occurs by the combination of two radicals to form nonradical 
products – mostly via dimerization of peroxyl radicals. These can undergo further decomposition 
(if primary or secondary) to form alcohol, aldehyde, and ketone products [27].

4.3 Solid state reactions 
The reactions followed in this study are mainly in the solid state, as they are conducted as 
powder mixtures. Solid state reactions include a range of chemical reactions such as oxidation, 
cyclization, hydrolysis, and deamidation [9]. These reactions are more complex than liquid state 
reactions due to more factors involved. The solid state reaction can be thought of as a having 
four stages. 
The first step is loosening of molecules at the reaction site. This leads to breakage of reactant 
bonds and formation of product bonds. A solid solution of the product will be formed in the 
starting crystal at the site of reaction, but this product will separate when the concentration 
reaches a certain point. This is the last step where new crystals are formed. The reaction begins 
at one or more nucleation sites that are spread throughout the crystal. The nucleation sites can be 
produced by exposure of crystal product or can just be randomly produced. A random number of 
nucleation sites result in complicated kinetics of solid state reactions [9]. 
Mobility is a very important factor in solid state reactions. Degradation of pharmaceuticals is 
often related to molecular mobility, and a study by Ahlneck and Zografi have shown that water 
absorption enhances molecular mobility of pharmaceutical solids [9], which is why relative 
humidity is an important factor to take into consideration when performing compatibility studies.

5 High-performance liquid chromatography 
High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) is a separation technique where high pressure 
is used to force solvent (mobile phase) through columns containing fine porous particles 
(stationary phase) resulting in different times for the analytes to pass through [40] [41] [12]. 
HPLC has become one of the more important tools in the chemical analysis of mixtures, 
especially in the pharmaceutical industry where it is used at all stages of drug discovery, 
development and production [12] [42]. 
5.1 Types of chromatography 
There are basically four types of chromatography; adsorption, partition, ion-exchange and size 
exclusion. In adsorption chromatography the solute is adsorbed on the surface of the solid 
particles. So stronger a solute is adsorbed, so slower it moves through the column. Partition 
chromatography has a liquid stationary phase that is immiscible with the eluent and coated on an 
inert support. In ion-exchange chromatography, anions and cations are covalently attached to the 
stationary phase. Solute ions of the opposite charge are then attracted to the stationary phase. 
Size exclusion chromatography separates molecules by their size with a stationary phase 
containing material with precisely controlled pore size. Smaller molecules will elute last because 
they diffuse easier in the column material than larger molecules [40] [41].  
5.2 Phases in HPLC 
In HPLC, the stationary phase is most commonly silica which is highly pure, spherical, 
microporous particles which allows solvent to permeate [40].  
The mobile phase is typically a mixture of organic and water based solvents, and the way it is 
used can be either isocratic or gradient. Isocratic elution represents the simplest and most 
convenient form of liquid chromatography and works well for many samples, as shown for 
derivatised FH in Figure 3 [43]. Here, the solvent mixture is constant which is convenient but 
can be a problem if it does not provide sufficiently rapid elution of all components. In this case, 
gradient elution can be used where increasing amounts of solvent B are added to solvent A to 
create a continuous gradient as shown in Figure 4 [40] [41] [43].

6.4 Conclusion 
The method for FH is fast for derivatisation and determination of the amount of FH in a sample. 
FH reacts almost instantly and can be run on HPLC after only 60 minutes of reaction time at RT, 
which could probably be even shorter if tested. The method shows great robustness and will be 
of good use, when analysing the amount of FH in PEG samples. 
The method for FA needs more development to be used for reliable results. It was not possible to 
find literature, where FA was derivatised in other than air samples. It appears that the 
derivatisation of FA is a challenging reaction. It could be considered to develop a gas 
chromatography (GC) method for FA, which has been reported in the literature [54]. GC has 
been applied by del-Barrio et al. [54] by derivatisation with ethanol, using p-toluenesulfonic acid 
as a catalyst, to produce ethyl formate for headspace GC/MS analysis.

Results from MODDE will show, which model terms have the most influence on the reaction 
types, causing the different degradation products, the model terms being temperature, time, 
water, and load.  
Some important parameters to understand in this model fitting are R2 and Q2. R2 shows the 
goodness of fit, and should be as close to 1 as possible for the best fit. If the value is 0.5 the 
model has low significance. Q2 shows an estimate of the future prediction precision – that is how 
well the model is able to predict values – and should also be as close to 1 as possible. For a good 
model Q2 should be above 0.5, and the difference between R2 and Q2 should not exceed 20% 
[56].  
A model was computed for every impurity. Results from PEG 400 and PEG 1500 were of most 
interest as these contained most and highest concentration of impurities, which match the 
prediction from the investigation of FH and FA in PEG. 
When computing the models, non-significant model terms were removed. These terms include 
those having an uncertainty range that cross zero. For normally distributed residuals, these 
should be on a straight line. Data points exceeding ±4 standard deviations are considered 
outliers. If these outliers did not correspond to extreme samples, such as samples with load 10, 
with added water, heated at 50 ºC for three weeks, they were removed from the model. This 
method was chosen, due to the fact that extreme samples can be over- or underestimated. 
8.2.5.1 Samples containing PEG 400

impurities are shown in Figure 39. A positive coefficient is increasing formation of an impurity,

impurities, indicating that more impurities are formed at elevated temperatures. Time is also 
present for most of the impurities, and it appears that there is a correlation between temperature 
and time for most of the impurities. This indicates that studies of impurity forming reactions in 
PEG 400 systems should be conducted at high temperature over a long time period, in order to 
observe most impurities.

8.2.5.3 Samples containing PEG 3350 and 4600 
Working with the models for samples containing PEG 3350 and PEG 4600 revealed that the data 
obtained was not useful for model fitting. R2 and Q2 were too low, because almost no impurities 
were formed, suggesting that these PEG qualities would require different circumstances for 
studying impurity forming reactions. This is in agreement with the results from the determination 
of FA and FH in the four PEG qualities, where the concentration of these two impurities was 
very low in PEG 3350 and 4600. 
8.3 Conclusion 
Based on the results, further studies of impurity forming reactions should be conducted with 
PEG 1500 at high temperature with addition of water. As the impurity forming reactions of 
interest are those expected to occur in long term stability studies at RT, it is considered that the 
temperature in these stress-studies should not exceed 50 ºC, as higher temperatures could result 
in impurities that would never be formed in stability studies at RT. Thus further studies should 
be conducted at 50 ºC. As the load of excipient appears to have some impact on formation of 
impurities, both samples with a PEG load of 1 and 10 could be investigated. 
The influence of the concentration of FA and FH in PEG from autoxidation is also interesting, 
and analysis of formation of these impurities in PEG could be investigated during the next 
compatibility study.

compatibility study was focusing on impurity forming reactions in nortriptyline/PEG 1500 
powder mixtures at 50 ºC with addition of water. The aim of this study was to identify the type

assay determination of nortriptyline.

9.2.2 Water adducts – Impurity D and E 
These impurities include D and E with the retention times 12.4 minutes and 14.6 minutes. As 
these impurities have the same mass these will be discussed together. 
The fact that the masses of these two peaks are the same, suggests that these two impurities are 
isomers. The mass of compound 1 was also found in the peaks of these impurities, and by 
subtracting the mass of 1, a difference of 18 Da, corresponding to one mole of water, was found. 
These impurities could therefore be the result of water adding to a double bond in 1. In this 
situation the reaction have three different possible products for the two impurities D and E 
shown in Table 14. The two compounds 3a and 3b are diastereomers, and 3c is a constitutional 
isomer of the other two. All of the three possible structures have the hydroxyl group in the 
benzyl position. The two diastereomers should be formed in equal amounts, as they are equally 
activated by the benzyl rings. 3c is double activated, because this is the benzyl carbon of two 
benzyl rings, making this the more attractive position for reaction. At the same time 3c is formed 
from the most stable carbocation, as this is tertiary, and the other two secondary (Figure 48).

9.3 Conclusion 
The results of this compatibility study show that more types of reaction are occurring in this 
system. The reactions are not dependent on the same factors, which complicates compatibility 
studies. If the system is only tested at one set of parameters, it would probably not reveal all the 
possible impurities. It is seen that the formation of amitriptyline is highly dependent on the load 
of excipient. This is worth noting as almost no amitriptyline is formed with a PEG load of 1. The 
difference in formation of amitriptyline for the two loads is roughly the ratio of the two loads - a 
factor of 10. 
As the formation of these impurities does not exhibit the same type of kinetics, this could be a 
problem, if a long term stability study of this system should be performed. The two impurities 
with equal mass D and E show different formation profiles. As D is thought to be the 
thermodynamic product and E the kinetic, the formation of D could be very slow at RT. It was 
observed that the formation was slow in the beginning at 50 ºC, and therefore the formation of 
this could be very slow at RT. This would be a major problem in long term stability studies, as D 
would only form after a long time period, while E would be formed and then disappear again 
over time.  
As there are different dependencies of the tested parameters, this could result in non-expected 
impurities forming in a very late stage of the stability test. This would cause a massive loss of 
potential income, because new stability tests would have to be performed. This is why 
knowledge about the types of reactions is so important. If more focus is put on revealing, which 
types of reactions could occur in a given system, this might shorten the time, before the given 
product would reach the market. As time is a critical factor in drug development, due to the 
patent protection period, the shortening of time is very attractive. 
Compatibility studies, like the ones performed in this study, with different types of excipients 
and APIs, could give a better basis for choosing excipients for a given API, when developing a 
new drug product.

10 Perspective 
This study leaves several loose ends, due the short period of time, and much of the presented 
material could be investigated and optimised in further studies. The method for determining FA 
needs further development, as is was not possible to get FA to react to completion. As formerly 
mentioned, one possible angle of attack is to develop a GC method instead, which could take a 
starting point in the method presented by del Barrio et al. [54]. 
In the compatibility studies, there are several areas for improvement. The particle size of 
excipient relative to the particle size of the API should be investigated further, as this could 
easily result in difference in kinetics, because the API and excipients would mix differently 
according to both size of excipient and size of API. In this study, the particle size of API was not 
determined. Hence the kinetics discussed here, might not compare to a real system, where it is 
desirable for the excipient and API to be of same size, as this eases mixing. It would also be 
interesting investigating more systems than described here, with mixtures of multiple excipients 
and API. 
One clear area of further investigation is the identity of impurity A and B, as these were not 
revealed in this study. This would need purification of the samples, which could be done by 
HPLC coupled with a fraction collector, prior to running the samples by NMR. This was not 
performed in this study, as there was not enough time to develop a HPLC method for separation 
of these or scale up of the experiment.

12.1 Derivatisation method using EDC as coupling agent 
During the project it was learned that derivatisation of carboxylic acids is slow without a 
coupling reagent. This led to new tests based on the work by Peters et al. [52] where 2-
nitrophenylhydrazine is used to derivatise carboxylic acids with EDC as coupling agent and 
pyridine as a catalyst. In this project DNPH was used instead of NPH. 
12.1.1 Experimental 
12.1.1.1 Chemicals 
0.2 M DNPH phosphoric acid (in ethanol), EDC, pyridine, and FH IC standard were purchased 
from Sigma-Aldrich Chemie (Germany). 96% ethanol, 85% ortho-phosphoric acid and 98-100% 
FA were purchased from Merck (Germany). HiPerSolv CHROMANORM acetonitrile (ACN) 
was purchased from VWR (France). Water was obtained from an Advantage A10, Q-POD, 
MilliQ system, 18.2 mΩ. 
12.1.1.2 EDC solution 
EDC solution was prepared by dissolving 0.48 g EDC in 10 mL water. This solution was kept in 
a refrigerator. An equal amount of this solution was mixed with a 3% solution of pyridine in 
ethanol to give a working solution. This was prepared daily. 
12.1.1.3 0.04 M DNPH solution 
DNPH solution was prepared by mixing 2 mL DNPH (0.2 M) and 1 mL 2 M phosphoric acid in 
a 10 mL bottle and diluting with ACN. This was first tried with ethanol and 2 mL 2 M 
phosphoric acid, but precipitation occurred and ACN was chosen instead. 
12.1.1.4 Preparation of standards 
Standards were prepared from 98-100% FA and 1000 ppm FH IC standard. Standards of 10 ppm 
FA and FH were used and prepared in water/ACN 60:40. 
12.1.1.5 Sample preparation 
0.6 mL of DNPH solution and 1.2 mL of EDC solution were mixed. 6 mL standard was added 
and the solution was mixed for 30 seconds on a whirlmixer. The solution was transferred to 
HPLC vials with tight screw caps and heated in oven at 60 °C.

After reaction in oven the solution was mixed on the whirlmixer for 30 seconds.

12.2 Derivatisation method using sulphuric acid 
During the project, it was suggested that dissolving FA in ethanol/concentrated sulphuric acid 
90:10 with addition of DNPH would lead to derivatisation of FA, why this method was tried.  
12.2.1 Experimental 
12.2.1.1 Chemicals 
0.2 M DNPH phosphoric acid (in ethanol) and1000 ppm FH IC standard were purchased from 
Sigma-Aldrich (Germany). 98-100% FA, 96% ethanol, 95-97% sulphuric acid, and 25% 
ammonia solution were purchased from Merck (Germany). HiPerSolv CHROMANORM 
acetonitrile (ACN) was purchased from VWR (France). Water was obtained from an Advantage 
A10, Q-POD, MilliQ system, 18.2 mΩ. 
12.2.1.2 Sample preparation 
This reaction was performed by adding an equivalent amount of 10 ppm FA to 0.02 M DNPH in 
ACN. This was mixed on a whirlmixer and heated at 40 °C and °60 C respectively for four 
hours. Samples were diluted with water to 50:50 water/organic and 25% ammonia solution was 
added (to ~pH 2.5) before HPLC analysis. 
12.2.2 Results and discussion 
Samples of FA, FH, and a blank (ethanol/conc. sulphuric acid 90:10) were prepared and run on 
HPLC. The reaction did not lead to complete derivation of FA as shown in Figure 25. The area 
of the blank (black line in Figure 25) is of same size as the area for FA.
