Abstract
The pathogenesis of Parkinson’s disease is closely linked to the aggregation of Alpha-
synuclein α-Syn protein, despite the precise mechanisms of phase separation and transfor-
mation into cytotoxic amyloid structures remain incompletely understood. Additionally,
there is a growing interest in understanding the interactions between protein condensate
droplets and serum proteins, such as Human Serum Albumin (HSA). This research project
aims to propose a model, establish concepts, and present an investigative approach to ex-
ploring the behavior of α-Syn in the presence of HSA during liquid-liquid phase separation
(LLPS) at various droplet maturation stages.
The first phase of this research project involved identifying the conditions for α-Syn and
HSA LLPS and characterizing the protein droplets. Through titration experiments and
Fluorescence Recovery After Photobleaching (FRAP) analyses, optimal conditions for α-
Syn and HSA phase separation were determined. FRAP results indicated that α-Syn and
HSA droplets exhibited liquid-like properties initially. It was observed that HSA influenced
α-Syn phase separation by reducing the critical concentration, while α-Syn induced earlier
phase separation of HSA. Colocalization analysis and FRAP experiments demonstrated
that both proteins can modulate each other’s saturation concentration by enriching within
dense droplets.
In the second phase, Fluorescence Intensity Distribution Analysis (FIDA) was used to
quantify the α-Syn and HSA LLPS. Under monomeric conditions, there was no interac-
tion between α-Syn and HSA. However, when both proteins undergo LLPS, a dynamic
enrichment within droplets was observed, indicating a higher affinity for the droplet state
compared to the monomeric state.
The last phase investigated the interaction between α-Syn and HSA at different droplet
maturation stages. FRAP time-dependent analysis and FIDA fibril binding experiments
revealed that HSA decelerated α-Syn aggregation likely due to HSA’s enhanced affinity
for α-Syn aggregates. It was hypothesized that HSA slows down α-Syn aggregation by
preferentially interacting with α-Syn aggregates, preventing further aggregation inside the
droplet phase.
While these findings provide valuable insights into the interplay between α-Syn and HSA
and shed light on the interaction of human serum proteins with α-Syn droplet condensates,
further investigations are needed to fully elucidate the underlying molecular mechanisms
and explore the implications of this binding interaction in the pathogenesis of Parkinson’s
disease. Nonetheless, this study serves as a critical step toward comprehending how droplet
condensates interact with serum proteins in a broader perspective.

Introduction
3.1
Cellular LLPS
Liquid-Liquid phase separation (LLPS) is a common phenomenon observed within cellular
environments that serves as a fundamental mechanism for a cellular subcompartmental-
ization where substances can concentrate in one part of the cell resulting in different cell
reactions [1, 2]. The phenomenon of biological polymer LLPS, which consist of the sepa-
ration of homogeneous solution in two distinct liquid phases (dense and dilute phase), is
related to the membrane-less organelles (MLOs) formation which have a highly dynamic
structure. Proteins diffuse in or out and the droplets exchange compounds with their sur-
roundings or even sequester diverse foreign substances. [3, 4, 5]. MLO are also formed
through specific and weak interactions between proteins and nucleic acid which separate
from the surrounding aqueous phase by having different chemical and physical properties.
These liquid droplets, also called biomolecular condensates, have sizes between 0.1 – 3
micrometers in diameter, and examples of MLOs are P-bodies, cytosolic stress granules,
and nucleolus, among others [4].
In general, molecular systems have a tendency to go to a state of higher entropy which
consists of a more dynamic and homogeneous solutions state. When a specific condition
disturbs this system, for example, temperature, or pH, similar molecules start to have
more affinity to interact with each other than with the other molecules type. As a result,
these molecules increase the concentration by undergoing liquid-liquid phase separation
[5]. LLPS is driven by multivalent interactions between the proteins and many different
variables can induce the phase separation such as pH, temperature, salt concentration,
molecular crowder, structure, and protein sequence, among others [6]. For example, LLPS
is correlated with intrinsically disordered regions (IDR), prion-like domains, and Low Com-
plex Domains (LCD) [5]. The sequences of the IRD are mainly composed of polar and
charged amino acids such as serine, tyrosine, glutamine, or asparagine, but few aromatic
amino acids. The IRD has a low energy structure where no stable secondary and tertiary
structures are observed. [7]
The interactions between protein molecules generate mobile liquid condensates that can
transition from the disordered liquid state to a highly ordered gel-like or solid state[3, 2].
This event generates an increase in the nucleation rate that leads to the formation of amy-
loid fibril aggregates. In some cases, a protein that undergoes LLPS can induce aggregation
through fibril formation and by adopting solid-like proprieties, and after maturation these
systems can become toxic leading to neurological diseases [1, 5] such as Parkison disease
caused by the fibrillation of alpha-synuclein protein.[8, 9].

3.2
Parkison’s disease and Alpha synuclein (α-Syn)
According to the World Health Organization (WHO), Parkison’s disease is a neurologi-
cal disorder that shows a rapid global increase, affecting over 8.5 million people in 2019
[10]. The main symptoms of this neurodegenerative disorder include "dementia, anxiety,
insomnia, excessive daytime sleepiness, rapid eye movement sleep disorder, constipation,
difficulty in swallowing, and dyspepsia" [11]. Moreover, Parkinson’s disease is caused by
an unexpected aggregation of proteins inside the neurons, called Lewy Bodies, in the sub-
stantia nigra pars compacta (SNpc) region of the brain. The main composition of these
inclusions bodies is the α-Syn protein that presents a high concentration in the synaptic
termination when it is in the form of an oligomers or fibrils, it becomes cytotoxic and can
induce degeneration of dopaminergic neurons in diverse brain regions compromising di-
verse neuronal functions such as the mitochondrial and endoplasmatic reticulum activities,
synaptic functions, and protein degradation. Moreover, these Lewy Bodies easily propa-
gate through neuron cells through extracellular vesicles or as drifting proteins initiating
the spread of the disease without remission until the last stage. However, the process of
α-Syn aggregation is not fully clarified yet and many studies have been carried out in order
to better understand and contribute to future therapeutic options for Parkinson’s disease.
[9, 12, 11].
Alpha-synuclein (α-Syn) is an amyloid protein with a molecular weight of 14KDa, com-
posed of 140 unfolded amino acids that are expressed in the human chromosome by the
gene SNCA. In addition, the protein natively exists in neuronal cells composing 1% of
cytosolic protein of the nervous system, despite recent studies also identifying extracellular
α-Syn present in the gut and in the heart. According to Rabe et al. 2013, the α-Syn
intracellular concentration varies between 30 to 60 µM [13, 8, 11]. The main function of
α-Syn is not fully understood, but recent studies have shown that α-Syn is related to the
modulation of neurotransmitter dopamine release, trafficking in the endoplasmic reticulum
(ER)/Golgi apparatus, and synaptic vesicles [14, 8].
The α-Syn primary structure is composed of an N-terminal region, 2 low complexity do-
mains (LCD), an aggregation-prone non-amyloid ß component (NAC hydrophobic domain),
and a flexible C-terminal domain [3, 9, 12] as shown in Figure 1. This native unstructured
protein can undergo LLPS followed by a solid-like transition that leads to amyloid aggre-
gation. This gel-like state is composed of cytotoxic α-Syn oligomers and fibrils that impair
neurotransmission being a clinical hallmark of Parkinson’s disease. Despite many years of
research that have been conducted, it is still unclear how the early aggregates states from

familial mutations, high α-Syn concentration, temperature, pH, high PEG concentration,
high salt concentration, contact with a lipid membrane, metal ions, among others. In ad-

[3, 12]. Independent of LLPS, overall, the N-terminal of amyloid proteins starts to interact
with anionic membranes through hydrophobic and hydrogen bonds, when in contact with
cell membranes and lipid vesicles (amphiphilic environment) [8].
The α-Syn LLPS is the first step for amyloid formation.

3.3
Albumin
Human Serum Albumin (HSA) is the most abundant serum protein corresponding to 60%
of the total plasma protein present in the blood [20]. The normal concentration in blood
is around 42g/liter representing around 4.5%, but HSA can also be found in the lymphatic
system and in lower amounts in the skin. The main functions of HSA are the colloidal
osmotic pressure (around 80% is from Albumin), pH maintenance of intravascular and
interstitial fluid, and transport function by binding to several metabolites such as fatty
acids, amino acids, heavy metals, hormones, steroid acids, and other molecules. In the
human body, this thermally stable protein is produced and secreted through the liver and
also plays an important role as an inhibitor of polypeptide self-association which prevents
the formation of oligomers in several proteins. In addition, HSA is responsible for the
transportation of most drugs since it can bind to both anionic and cationic molecules
[20, 21, 22, 23]. One of the main roles of HSA is to act as chaperone protein assisting the
folding and protein assembly essential for the organismic homeostasis [24].
Human albumin is a well known unglycosylated protein that is located in human chromo-
some 4 and is composed of 585 residues with 17 disulfide bonds and a molecular weight of
65.5 kDa kilodalton. The general 3D Structure of HSA can be seen in Figure 3 [20, 21, 22].
The Crystal structure of HSA is composed of 3 different domains (represented by blue,
yellow, and red colors) with subdomains A and B while the secondary structure is com-
posed of
67% alpha-helix and 17 disulfide bonds. In contrast to α-Syn, human Albumin
structures lack IRD regions, and yet, this globular protein is commonly employed as a bi-
ological crowder, and can also undergo LLPS due the hydrophobic interactions, including
in the presence of molecular crowders such as PEG 8000 [5].

Furthermore, by default, serum proteins are responsible to add a new biological identity

3.4
Alpha synuclein and Albumin interaction
As mentioned before, Parkinson’s disease is mainly characterized by the presence of in-
tracellular Lew’s Bodies which are mainly composed of α-Syn. However, recent studies
demonstrated that neuronal cells secrete alpha-synuclein protein through unconventional
exocytosis leading to prion-like transmission contributing to Parkinson’s Disease.
This
extracellular α-Syn will be in contact with the serum proteins, more specifically with the
albumin [24]. It also has been published that the Human Serum Albumin can inhibit α-Syn
aggregation, but it is still unclear how this mechanism occurs and further investigations are
needed. One suggested hypothesis is that HSA acts as a monomer competitor by binding
to α-Syn through hydrophobic interactions between N-terminal and α-Syn NAC regions
inhibiting the interactions with extracellular membranes and in this way preventing the
α-Syn amyloid formation [24, 8, 23].
It has been observed that the catalytic cycle that generates α-Syn self-assembly, is pre-
vented by the hydrophobic interactions between charged HSA and charged α-Syn with
Page 9
==PAGE:11==
July 1, 2023
sub-µM affinity. This phenomenon generates a protective effect of HSA reducing the α-
Syn cytotoxicity. In addition, HSA has an antioxidative effect by protecting the red blood
cells from the α-Syn oligomers which becomes cytotoxic since generate high amounts of
reactive oxygen species (ROS) during fibril formation. [8, 23]. These protective effects of
HSA are also highlighted in the study [23], where 3 different inhibition mechanisms are
mentioned: HSA forms less toxic intermediates when binding to α-Syn oligomers and fib-
rils, albumin also prevents the α-Syn monomers turning into α-Syn oligomers, and finally
the interaction of α-Syn with lipid membranes is prevented by HSA. In addition, this study
also indicates that HSA binds to α-Syn monomers at least in the two known binding sites,
N and C-terminal. Furthermore, the interaction between α-Syn and HSA are also relevant
in cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), not only in the plasma [23].
It is clear the importance of understanding the molecular mechanisms underlying the inter-
actions between α-Syn and Human Serum Albumin (HSA) within a cellular environment.
However, is also crucial to establish methodologies, concepts, and model systems that en-
able the investigation and exploration of how proteins interact with solidifying condensates.
By developing and implementing such approaches, it is possible to build a deeper compre-
hension of the molecular dynamics and behaviors exhibited by proteins within solidifying
condensates, as well as their functional implications in biological systems.
4
Aim
The primary objective of this master thesis project is to establish methods and concepts
to generate different approaches to support the investigation of how protein droplet con-
densate interacts with serum proteins in a broader context. Specifically, this project aims
to explore the interactions of Alpha-synuclein (α-Syn) and Human Serum Albumin (HSA)
at different stages of LLPS droplet maturation. In addition, this project will analyze and
test if HSA can affect α-Syn droplet formation and explore how this behavior occurs. To
simplify and make the investigation feasible, the project is organized into three different
phases:
• Phase I: Liquid-Liquid Phase Separation (LLPS) and droplet characterization for
both α-Syn and HSA proteins. The main methods for this phase are a Bright field
microscope to identify the LLPS and evaluate the droplet size and Fluorescence
Recovery After Photobleaching (FRAP) analyses to determine the droplet’s aspects
through the translational dynamics of the molecules.
• Phase II: Dilute phase quantification. Once the LLPS were qualified the next step is
the quantification of the dilute phase using Flow Induced Dispersion Analysis through
FIDA instrument.

• Phase III: The last phase is to investigate how both α-Syn and HSA proteins influence
each other in the presence of protein aggregation using the FRAP analyses and plate
reader instrument.

5.1.2
Preparation of protein solutions and master stocks solutions
For the preparation of α-Syn stock solution, the purified protein solution was upper con-
centrated using 3 kDa filters (500 µL from Amicon ultra centrifugal cut-off filters) and 20
mM sodium phosphate buffer (NaH2PO4.H20, Na2HPO4.2H20). Two vials of the purified
stock were filtered and centrifuged at a speed of 16000 rcf (Relative Centrifugal Force), for
30 min, 4°C). The supernatant was decanted and the protein concentrate was diluted in
50 µM of sodium phosphate buffer (20 mM). The protein concentration was measured in
the NanoDrop equipment as described in the Methods section.
The HSA stock solution was prepared by weighting the HSA powder (Sartorius scale) and
dissolving it in the 20 mM sodium phosphate buffer. For a stock solution of 1500µM,
49,5mg of HSA was used in 500µL of sodium phosphate buffer and this master solution
was diluted with the same buffer type for different desired concentrations.
For the main stock solution, the items NaH2PO4.H20, NaCl, and PEG-8000 were pur-
chased from Sigma (USA), and the dilution was prepared using ultrapure Milli-Q water
and 0.05% sodium azide was added to each stock solution to prevent bacterial/fungal con-
tamination.
The sodium phosphate buffer was produced by mixing 1:2 monobasic 1M
(sodium dihydrogen phosphate (Sigma, USA) and milli Q) with dibasic 0.5M (disodium
hydrogen phosphate (Sigma, USA) and milli Q) phosphates master stocks. Subsequently,
a titration with 1.5M HCl or 1M NaOH was performed in order to reach the pH value

portions, and kept at -20°C.
5.1.3
Preparation of monomer solutions and LLPS solutions
The monomer α-Syn and soluble HSA solutions were prepared by simply diluting the
respective master stock solution with 20 mM sodium phosphate buffer (NaH2PO4.H20,
Na2HPO4.2H20) to achieve the desired concentration. While the LLPS solution was in-
duced by mixing the proteins with PEG 8000 20% (w/v), NaCl 250mM, and sodium
phosphate buffer (20 mM). All sample preparation was done in a 1.5 ml Eppendorf tube
at room temperature (25°C). The quantities of PEG and salt were determined after titra-
tion as stated in the results section. The phase separation resulted in first the addition of
buffer followed by the protein solution, salt solution, and finally the PEG 8000. After the
additions, the sample was mixed through vortex equipment for 10 seconds.

5.2.4
Flow-induced dispersion analysis (FIDA) and Capflex experiment
The Flow-induced dispersion analysis (FIDA) is an instrument that consists of a capillary
under the hydrodynamic flow and is used for the characterization and quantification of
biomolecular interaction. The principle behind this method consists of molecular separa-
tion based on Taylor dispersion theory where a laminar flow, which consists of a bigger
flow in the center of the capillary and a smaller one at the edge, generates a parabolic
profile of a distorted sample plug when the molecules present in the middle of the capillary
reaches the detector first. The sample is injected into the capillary through a 96-well plate,
which has temperature control, and diffuses through a thin capillary until it reaches a flu-
orescence detector that measures the molecule’s fluorescence. An indicator (generally the
labeled protein) and an analyte molecule are loaded and the fluoresce detector identifies
the change in the apparent size of the indicator in case of interaction with the analyte
molecule since it is generated a new complex with higher molecular weight. When the
indicator is bounded, a higher apparent size is detected generating a broader peak, while
in case of no interaction, the expected outcome is a narrow peak (the dye molecule has a
lower molecular weight). An apparent hydrodynamic radius (associated with the diffusion
coefficient of a molecule) is calculated based on the arrival time of the protein peak (Gaus-
sian peak tR) at the detector and the respective variance (σ). A schematic representation
of the FIDA instrument principle is shown in Figure 6.

5.2.5
Fluorescence Recovery After Photobleaching (FRAP) analyses
The fluorescence recovery after photobleaching (FRAP) is a microscope method to study
the mobility of the molecules. The method is applied in samples that receive a fluores-
cent label where initially the fluorescence intensity is measured followed by photobleaching
where a laser beam is emitted inducing an intense illumination and extension of the flu-
orophores generating in a dark area in the sample. Subsequently, the replacement of the
photobleached molecules is observed and visualized through fluorescence recovery. In case
there is a translational dynamics movements between the molecules a fluoresce recovery is
expected after the photobleaching, while in case of less or no mobility of the molecules, the
fluoresce recovery is less or absent [31]. The FRAP analyses were performed at DTU bio-
imaging core facility through an LMI-005-Leica Microsystems Confocal Microscope-SP8
Page 19
==PAGE:21==
July 1, 2023
where the samples were imaged by 63X (oil immersion) objective lens. For this analysis,
the proteins were labeled with two different dyes, which α-Syn received the ALEXA 488
labeled while the HSA was labeled with the Fluorophore rapid amine 503 (F-503) dye.
The visualization of both proteins was possible at the same time in two different channels
since the overlap was avoided by settings the two dyes with a distinct emission spectrum
range, 493-533nm wavelength for α-Syn ALEXA 488 and 582-695nm for HSA F-503. Fur-
thermore, the LLPS samples were prepared at room temperature (25°C) right before the
analyses. For the slide preparation, 2µL of the samples were deposited in the clean glass
microscope slide with a glass coverslip on the top and a drop of immersion oil for better
imaging. Also, the samples preparations followed the same order as LLPS samples before,
first the sodium phosphate buffer 20mM was added, and after, the unlabeled protein at
the specific concentration, followed by the 500nM of labeled protein, NaCl 250mM and at
least PEG 8000 20% (v/w). For FRAP analyses each droplet was identified and the gain
was adjusted to avoid saturation and generate a better image. The area of the bleaching
was selected in the software and a 488nm laser at 100% power was used to bleach the
droplet for 5 seconds. The extension of the bleaching was dependent on the droplet sizes,
for big droplets a half bleach was performed, where just a specific area of the droplet was
bleached while the smaller droplets received full FRAP in other words, the whole droplet
was photobleached. The post-bleached time was settled for 120s to get the fluorescence
recovery. Once the analyses were concluded the images and videos were analyzed in the
ImageJ (NIH, USA) software. For the data analyses, the raw data of fluorescence values
was first set to zero followed by a normalization according to the passive bleaching values.
Passive bleaching consists of a selected area, preferentially in the same droplet outside the
bleached area where the variance of fluoresce is measured over time.

Results and Discussion
The Results and Discussion are presented according to the three different phases that
compose this master thesis investigation. The first phase introduces the results of the LLPS
characterization of both Alpha-Synuclein (α-Syn) and Human Serum Albumin (HSA),
while phase 2 is composed of the respective LLPS dilute phase quantification. Finally,
the third phase states the results of how α-Syn aggregates are affected by HSA and the
opposite scenario where HSA is under LLPS condition.
6.1
Phase 1: LLPS characterization
The first investigation has the aim to verify if the α-Syn can interact with HSA in their
respective monomeric state. At high concentrations (600µM) HSA has a blocking effect in
α-Syn self-assembly by preventing the hydrophobic interaction between charged HSA and
charged α-Syn [23]. To test if α-Syn and HSA interact under monomeric conditions in lower
concentrations, a binding experiment was performed using the Flow Induced Dispersion
Analyses (FIDA) instrument [29].
This method measures the diffusion of an indicator
molecule under capillary flow and provides a Stokes radius (hydrodynamic radius). If the
indicator molecule binds to a different analyte molecule, the Stokes radius increases as
mentioned in Method section 5. Titrating the analyte molecule at different concentrations
can generate quantitative binding affinity curves.
For this experiment, 100nM HSA (labeled with F-503, excitation: 503nm, emission 660nm)
was used as an indicator, and different α-Syn concentrations were titrated as analyte. The
proteins were tested in the presence and absence of 250nm NaCl salt to investigate if charge
screening had any effect on their binding affinity. As shown in Figure 7, for both NaCl
concentrations, the Stokes radius of 100 nM HSA did not change ( 3.5 nm) substantially
even when 3 orders of magnitude excess α-Syn (250 µM) was used as an analyte, indicating
that there was a negligible affinity between HSA and α-Syn under monomeric conditions.

==PAGE:23==

where labelled HSA F-503 at 100nM received a varying concentration of α-Syn
.
Previously, as stated in the antecedent FIDA analyses7, it was established that HSA had
a poor affinity for α-Syn under monomeric conditions. However, inside phase-separated
environments, due to substantial enrichment of protein concentration, weak binders may
also alter LLPS dynamics. Therefore, the next step was to check if the presence of HSA
could modulate α-Syn phase separation. To investigate the consequence of HSA in α-Syn
LLPS, first, it was necessary to characterize in-vitro α-Syn and HSA phase separation
individually.
The presence of molecular crowds such as Polyethylene Glycol (PEG) and salt (NaCl) can
induce spontaneous liquid-liquid phase separation (LLPS) of Alpha-Synuclein protein [5, 1].
Under macromolecular crowding induced by PEG, screening of the C-terminal charges of α-
Syn allows necessary interactions between α-Syn molecules resulting in LLPS. In addition,
NaCl alters the hydration shell that prevents the transient, surface interactions [1]. A
titration was designed in which the PEG 8000 concentration was fixed at 20% (w/v) and
α-Syn and NaCl concentration were varied to investigate the ideal conditions that induce
a α-Syn LLPS as shown in Figure 8.

likely deriving from the stuck α-Syn molecules in the aggregate structures which prevents
their dynamic movement and consequently the FRAP recovery.

7
Conclusion
The role of Alpha-synuclein (α-Syn) aggregation in the pathogenesis of Parkinson’s dis-
ease has been extensively studied for the past two decades within the scientific community.
However, the exact mechanism underlying α-Syn phase separation and elusive transfor-
mation into cytotoxic amyloid structures remains incompletely understood. In addition to
this question, there is a general interest in understanding how protein condensate droplet
structures interact with serum proteins, such as Human Serum Albumin (HSA). This re-
search project aims to propose a model, establish concepts, and present an investigative
approach to how to explore this protein behavior specifically studying the influence of HSA
on the liquid-liquid phase separation (LLPS) of α-Syn at various stages of LLPS droplet
maturation. Furthermore, it is evident that the liquid-liquid phase separation process in
cellular environments needs to be tightly regulated. This is demonstrated by the fact that
even proteins with unfavorable interactions can interact and modify each other’s proper-
ties, including aggregation formation when subjected to LLPS conditions. Consequently,
it is crucial to exercise caution in future investigations aimed at directly exploring the
physiological and pathological implications of LLPS, as the outcomes can be influenced
by complex interactions between diverse proteins within condensates. For a better expla-
nation of the behavior of the protein behind the results, in the context of liquid-liquid
phase separation (LLPS), when one protein is in a state of LLPS while the other exists
as monomers, the protein undergoing LLPS forms droplets that serve as scaffolds. The
monomeric protein that selectively partitions or colocalizes within these scaffold droplets
is referred to as the client protein.
To explore and understand these mechanisms, this master thesis was divided into three
different phases. The first phase aimed to identify the condition for liquid-liquid phase sep-
aration (LLPS) of both α-Syn and HSA and characterize the respective protein droplets.
This involved performing a titration using different protein and salt concentrations, as
well as utilizing Fluorescence Recovery After Photobleaching (FRAP) experiments to as-
sess the translational dynamics of the molecules inside these assemblies and determine
droplet characteristics. Notably, it was observed that both proteins can undergo phase
separation. The ideal condition for α-Syn phase separation is observed with a protein con-
centration of 200µM in the presence of NaCl 250nm and PEG 8000 20% (w/v). Similarly,
optimal conditions for HSA phase separation were achieved with an HSA concentration
of 100µM, along with the same NaCl and PEG concentrations. Furthermore, FRAP re-
sults indicated that both α-Syn and HSA droplets exhibit a liquid-like state at time zero,
as evidenced by droplet fluorescence recovery. Conversely, non-fluorescent droplet recov-
Page 45
==PAGE:47==
July 1, 2023
ery showed that α-Syn droplets undergo a transition to a gel-like state with fewer mobile
molecules after 24 hours.
Cryogenic Scanning Electron Microscopy (CryoSEM) images
were employed to confirm the presence of hydrogel structures within aged α-Syn droplets,
consistent with existing literature [7, 3]. Furthermore, it was identified that HSA influences
α-Syn phase separation by reducing the critical concentration, while α-Syn also induces
earlier phase separation of HSA. Subsequently, another FRAP followed by a colocalization
analysis confirmed that both proteins can modulate each other’s saturation concentration
by colocalizing into dense droplets.
The next step in this project involved quantifying the extent of client protein (transit pro-
tein) partitioning within scaffold droplets using Fluorescence Intensity Distribution Analy-
sis (FIDA). This preliminary analyses using FIDA in the monomeric state of both proteins
revealed that under monomeric conditions, there is no interaction between α-Syn and HSA.
Likewise, when one protein is in the LLPS phase and the other remains in a monomeric
or soluble state, substantial partitioning within droplets does not occur. However, when
both proteins undergo LLPS, a dynamic movement of enrichment is observed, indicating
the affinity of the proteins for the droplet state is greater than for the monomeric state.
FIDA outcomes confirmed protein phase separation through the formation of spikes, while
the decrease in fluorescence baseline suggested a reduction in the dilute phase, indicating
migration of proteins to scaffold droplets.
In the third and final phase of the project, the interaction between α-Syn and HSA was
explored in different stages, including aged droplets. A FRAP time-dependent analysis and
FIDA fibril binding experiments were conducted to support this investigation. The FRAP
results demonstrated that HSA decelerates the aggregation of α-Syn at 24 and 96 hours.
The conversion of α-Syn droplets into irreversible or less reversible states is inhibited by
HSA, and this inhibitory behavior exhibits a dependence on HSA concentration.
This
observation can be attributed to the HSA’s enhanced affinity for diverse forms of α-Syn
aggregates, resulting from their increased surface area in comparison to monomeric α-Syn,
being indicative of a higher propensity for binding a greater number of HSA molecules to
these structures. The FIDA fibril binding experiment further confirmed HSA’s affinity for
α-Syn fibrils, also exhibiting a concentration-dependent behavior. Based on these findings,
we hypothesize that HSA slows down α-Syn aggregation by preferentially interacting with
α-Syn droplets and after partitioning inside the droplets and interacting with α-Syn aggre-
gate structures, slowing down further aggregation inside the droplet phase. The analyses
conducted in the third phase were crucial in providing support for the proposed hypothesis.
An additional observation was made regarding the fluorescence recovery of HSA molecules,
which exhibited a decrease in the presence of α-Syn, indicating a reduction in droplet mo-
bility. The hypothesis to explain this behavior is based on once HSA reaches the critical
Page 46
==PAGE:48==
July 1, 2023
concentration required for droplet formation, the surface of the HSA droplets serves as a
scaffold, facilitating α-Syn nucleation and subsequent aggregate formation. As a result,
the mobility of molecules within the HSA droplets becomes limited.
Overall the results of the third phase represent the success of the experimental strategy
to investigate the impact of HSA in different α-Syn droplet stages. Despite this hypoth-
esis was also built on the overall knowledge acquired through this master thesis process
and literature review, it is important to note that the inhibitory effect of HSA on α-Syn
aggregates does not exclude the possibility of other mechanisms being involved in this
interaction, particularly when both proteins are in the LLPS form. Explaining the in-
hibition mechanism of HSA in greater detail requires further investigation and analysis.
Techniques such as Dynamic Light Scattering and amyloid tracker analyses can provide a
more comprehensive understanding from kinetic and thermodynamic perspectives.
These findings are valuable and significantly enhance our comprehension of the interplay
relationship between α-Syn and HSA, thereby offering precious perspectives for the in-
teractions of human serum proteins with α-Syn droplet condensates. Furthermore, this
study lays the foundation and gives insight into the approaches and methods that can be
employed for further investigation of how protein condensate droplets interact with other
serum proteins, enabling a more comprehensive understanding of the complex profile of
interaction between them. While this investigation represents a crucial step towards the
comprehension of α-Syn partitioning into other unrelated protein LLPS droplets and con-
tributes to the deeper knowledge of the α-Syn protein behavior in complex environments.
However, further investigations are warranted to elucidate the underlying molecular mecha-
nisms and explore the potential implications of this binding interaction in the pathogenesis
of Parkinson’s neurodegenerative disorder.
