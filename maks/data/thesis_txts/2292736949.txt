Abstract
The ISO 15118 standard speciﬁes advanced communication between an
electric vehicle and charging station. In this thesis a reference implementation
of the standard ISO 15118 is developed. The goal is to evaluate the standard.
The evaluation is performed by exploring the difﬁculty of implementation, the
interoperability with existing devices, new applications made possible by the
standard as well as the required payment infrastructures and security fallacies.

Resumé
ISO 15118 standarden speciﬁcerer avanceret kommunikation mellem elbiler
og ladestandere. In denne kandidatafhandling udvikles en referenceimplemen-
tation af ISO 15118 standarden. Målet er at evaluere standarden. Evalueringen
bliver lavet ved først at undersøge hvor svær standarden er at implementere.
Derefter at undersøge hvor godt kommunikation vha. standarden virker med
eksisterende apparater. Derefter undersøges hvilke nye anvendelser standar-
den giver mulighed. Til slut evalueres den betalingsinfrastruktur standarden
vil kræve samt hvilke sikkerhedsrisici kommunikation, der følger standarden er
udsat for.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Climate, pollution and sustainability concerns have lead towards an increased pene-
tration of renewable energy sources around the globe. Many of these sources, e.g.
wind power, are characterized by providing very ﬂuctuant power output directly to
the power grid. This can be problematic as surplus power output cannot be readily
stored, and conventional power plants have limited ability to change power output to
compensate. This has led many countries, here amongst Denmark, to promote the
use of electric vehicles (EVs), as they charge directly from the power grid and can
support it by for instance absorbing excess wind power or charge slower in periods
with little wind or potentially even provide power back to the grid.
Supporting the grid, however, could prove inconvenient to the EV user. E.g. if the grid
operator wants an EV to charge at a slower pace from the Electric Vehicle Supply
Equipment (EVSE), the EV will take longer to be sufﬁciently charged for the needs
of the EV user. Thus some communication between the grid and the EV is required
in order to minimize the user inconvenience. Currently many smart EVSEs conform
to the IEC 61851 standard for communication (EVSE to EV). The communication is
performed across the control pilot-line, which has a separate pin in the charging cable
connector. IEC 61851 does, however, only allow for communicating the charging
ready state and the line current from EVSE to EV. While this does in principle allow
adaptive charging, it is not at the merits of the EV owner. The EVSE does not know
anything regarding the state of charge of the EV, and could incidentally make the EV
charge at slow pace when the EV owner needs it to be charged quickly. This could
cause the EV owner considerate inconvenience.
This, among other things, has led to a lot of work being put into the drafting of a
new standard which allows for a wider array of uses. This work has resulted in
the ISO 15118 standard. ISO 15118 speciﬁes advanced communication between
EV and EVSE. Both the EV owner and the power grid has the potential to beneﬁt
from the advent of the ISO 15118 standard. It allows the EVSE to provide the EV
with time-dependent grid tariffs (i.e. the electricity pricing) from the power market
(in Scandinavia, the Nord Pool Spot market, see ﬁgure 1). The standard then
allows the EV to respond with a time dependent charging proﬁle which outlines the
amount of charging power desired at given times. The charging proﬁle must satisfy a
time-dependent maximum charging power schedule speciﬁed by the EVSE.
As the power price is highly dependent on the energy supply, the expected output of
ﬂuctuating energy sources, here amongst wind power, is reﬂected upon the pricing.
The availability of the time-dependent tariffs allows the EV to customize a charging

use of ISO 15118 communication is a win-win situation for both EV owners and the
power grid.
However, currently there are no open source implementations of the communication
standard. This provides a large entry barrier for research institutions which want to
explore the possibilities that ISO 15118 communication allows for.

INTRODUCTION
1.2
Problem Statement
The aim of this thesis is to evaluate the standard. Part of the evaluation is to create
an open-source reference implementation including both SLAC, SDP, TCP/TLS and
application layer messaging. The following points will be evaluated:
1. Is the standard easily implementable using available software and hardware
components? (Answer gained by test bench development, and the implementation is
veriﬁed using tests deﬁned in 15118-4)
2. What is the degree of interoperability gained by conforming to 15118? (Answer
gained by evaluating various implementations and possible tests towards EV or
EVSE)
3. Of the power and energy services envisioned by academia – which will be
supported by 15118? (Compare with the services deﬁned by the Nikola project)
In addition security, payment features and potential fallacies of the standard will
explored and discussed. Finally, a list of proposed changes to the standard is
compiled based on the work with it.
1.3
Scope
In order to keep the implementation simpler, some features of the 15118 standard
are omitted. The omissions are listed below:
• SECC performance timings are not implemented, since all application layer
communication in the application layer happens almost immediately (i.e. way
under 100ms). SECC performance timers enable an SECC to respond pre-
emptively if it is not done with computations for an EVCC request within a given
amount of time.
• The ISO 15118-6 and ISO 15118-8 which describe using wireless communi-
cation instead of power line are omitted. Note that these standards have not
been ofﬁcially released yet, as they still are under development.
• This thesis focuses exclusively on the communication between EV and EVSE.
Communication with and implementation of 3rd parties such as energy providers
and mobility operators were omitted due to time constraints. Due to this, elab-
oration and implementation of Certiﬁcate Installation and Certiﬁcate Update
application layer requests are also omitted from this project.

1.4
Structure
The thesis has been divided into the following sections which each will be explained:
• Theory
The theory section the process of initiating and performing ISO 15118 compliant

The motivation for exploring and evaluating ISO 15118 communication is that ISO

1.7
Review of Important Literature
1.7.1
ISO 15118
The main literature for this project is the international standard ISO 15118. This
thesis uses ISO 15118-1-2013, ISO 15118-2-2014, ISO 15118-3 and ISO 15118-4.
ISO 15118 has been drafted in collaboration with IEC.
ISO 15118-1 outlines generally how the compliant V2G communication should work,
but does not go too much into speciﬁcs.
ISO 15118-2 explains SECC Discovery Protocol (SDP) and V2G application layer
messaging. SDP is used to for the EV and EVSE to ﬁnd each-other in an IP-based
network. The V2g application layer messaging among other things includes includes
authorization for charging, payment, charging parameters, charging scheduling and
the charging loop.
ISO 15118-3 documents the Signal Level Attenuation Characterization (SLAC) proto-
col. It assumes the use of Power Line Communication (PLC) between EV and EVSE
and allows for an EV to identify which EVSE it is connected to, so they can form an
IP-based network.
ISO 15118-4 writes up a number of tests one can use to test if an implementation is
ISO 15118 compliant.
All of the above standards are authored by ISO technical committees in cooperation
with the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC).

The Plug and the Connectors
The standardized plug types as of IEC 62196 (see ﬁgure 2 below) is comprised of

2.2
High Level Communication (HLC) Setup Process
Figure 3: The setup used for ISO 15118 high level communication
In order to engage in HLC, an ISO 15118-compliant EV has to be plugged into an
ISO 15118-compliant EVSE. An ISO 15118-compliant EV is assumed to contain a
Electric Vehicle Communication Controller (EVCC) which is in charge of high level
communication, as well as a PLC modem node. Likewise an ISO 15118-compliant
EVSE is assumed to contain a Supply Equipment Communication Controller (SECC)
and also a PLC modem node (see ﬁgure 3).
The communication setup process spans across multiple network abstraction layers
of the Open Systems Interconnection (OSI) model (see ﬁgure 4).
The OSI model network abstraction layer partitioning ranges from the physical layer
at the bottom to the application layer at the top.
The establishment of a Vehicle to Grid HLC link between EVCC and SECC is initiated
by the EVSE detecting that the charging plug is present. The EVSE then signals
a 5% duty cycle over the control pilot line. This prompts the EVCC to start SLAC
association in the Data Link Layer. Note that it is implicit that all communication is
done through the Physical Layer which is realized by the PLC link connecting EV and
EVSE. If SLAC association between EV and EVSE is successful, the SDP process
will be triggered. The SDP protocol is located in the application layer, but all of its
network operations work directly with UDP in the transport layer. A successful SDP
run will trigger the EVCC connecting to the SECC with TCP in the Transport Layer.
Dependent on the EV settings during the SDP process, this connection may or may
not have TLS (Transport Layer Security) on top. If the connection is not TLS-secured
the EVCC will not able to use the Plug and Charge (PnC) mode which is discussed

2.3
Triggering of High Level Communication (HLC)
The availability of ISO 15118-compliant HLC is determined by the EVSE sending an
initial 5% duty cycle PWM signal across the control pilot to the EV [6, p. 18]. This
signal is sent by the EVSE immediately after an EV has been plugged into it. If the
EV does not receive a 5% duty cycle PWM, it will continue charging according to IEC
61851.
2.4
The Power Line Communication
In the physical layer, the EV and EVSE PLC nodes (i.e. the modems) must be
able to create a data link with Homeplug GreenPhy Power Line Communication
across the Control Pilot Line. The Homeplug GreenPhy protocol uses high-frequency

In order to exchange IP-based communication the EV and the EVSE PLC nodes
must be in the same AV logical network (AVLN). Logical networks serves as a way to

As of the standard an EVSE PLC node must always be designated as the CCo of the
AVLN network, hence there must be one AVLN for each EVSE [8, p. 26].
2.5
SLAC
An EV PLC node can only join an AVLN, if it uses the correct NMK, however, is
unlikely to have this information on beforehand and must therefore obtain it somehow.
Figure 6: PLC Propagation Paths
A naive solution could be to broadcast the NMK using PLC and hope that the correct
EVSE responds. While this may seem reasonable, problems will arise if multiple ISO
15118-compliant EVSEs are in the vicinity. The culprit of this problem is that it is
difﬁcult keep high-frequency PLC signals strictly between a pair of directly connected
EVs and EVSEs. PLC signals are likely to ﬁnd paths to propagate to other nearby
EVSEs (see ﬁgure 6).
The ISO 15118’s solution to this problem is an association mechanism in the ethernet
layer called Signal Level Attenuation Characterization (SLAC). SLAC basically allows

Figure 7: SLAC association
• The CM_SLAC_PARAM request is broadcasted from an EV to determine the
SLAC parameters that should be used for the SLAC association. It also serves

• The CM_START_ATTEN_CHAR request is a broadcasted request from an EV

• A number of CM_MNBC_SOUNDS packets are broadcasted from the EV
following the CM_START_ATTEN_CHAR. The number of sounds transmitted

2.6
SDP
When the SLAC association is complete, the EVCC and EVCC are in the same
AVLN. At this point they will both have been assigned an IPv6 address by their
respective PLC node. Thus TCP communication is now in principle possible. In order
to initiate TCP-based connection the EVCC must obtain IPv6 address of the SECC.
The standard has devised the SECC Discovery Protocol (SDP) for this purpose. SDP
basically works by the EVCC multicasting its desired connection settings over UDP
(User Datagram Protocol). These settings consist of a byte ﬁeld indicating security,
i.e. if the TCP connection should be secured with TLS (Transport Layer Security) or
not, and a byte indicating the underlying transport protocol, where as of the current
version of the standard the only valid setting is TCP[7, p. 48].
As of the ISO 15118 standard, only the SECC is allowed to respond. The SECC
will respond to the sender with a unicast UDP message, which contains the SECC
IP-address and port and conﬁrmation of the desired settings[7, p. 49]. If the security
ﬁeld of the response is different from that of the request, the EV must discard the
response. This ensures that if a third party has tampered with the request or response
security parameters, no security degradation will happen.

the concept of X.509 certiﬁcates must be introduced. X.509 is a widely used Public
Key Infrastructure (PKI) and as a Privilege Management Infrastructure (PMI). For
instance X.509 is used for veriﬁcation of the identity of secure HTTPS websites.

2.7.1
Public Key Cryptography
In public key cryptography, each party must have a private and a public key which
are mathematically related with prime factorization. Public key cryptography relies
on the two parties exchanging public keys and using the public-private key relation
to establish a common secret. This common secret can then be used for encrypted
communication between the two parties. Even if the entire communication process is
wire-tapped, an attacker will not be able to decrypt the encrypted messages unless
he solves a very difﬁcult prime factorization problem. In X.509 the certiﬁcate ﬁle itself
contains the public key, while the private key is a separate ﬁle [2]. The X.509 key-pair
is, however, typically not used for encryption, but only for signatures used in the key
negotiation process (for the actual encryption other keys are used). Signing works
by having an entity digitally sign some hashed data with its private key [2, p. 19].
Another entity can then use the public key of the ﬁrst entity to check if the data is
correctly signed by the corresponding private key.
2.7.2
Privilege Management
The X.509 standard also contains the PMI, which basically is a trust management
system. It basically works by having chains of trust. A client will usually trust a
number of root certiﬁcates issued by various Certiﬁcate Authorities (CAs). The trust
is realized by having a trusted certiﬁcate sign a hash digest (typically generated with
SHA-256) of the end-user certiﬁcate. This trusted certiﬁcate does not have to be a
root certiﬁcate, but can instead be an intermediate certiﬁcate (which is signed by a
root certiﬁcate). The whole chain, however, does have to be valid. For that to be
fulﬁlled each link of the certiﬁcate chain has to satisfy the following requirements:
• The certiﬁcate must be signed by the next link in the chain which either must
be a derivative of a trusted root certiﬁcate or be the root certiﬁcate itself.
• The certiﬁcate must not have expired, i.e. the current timestamp must be
between the start and end timestamp.
• The certiﬁcate must not have been revoked. The revocation status can be
checked using the Online Certiﬁcate Status Protocol (OSCP).
The ability to revoke a certiﬁcate highlights the importance of using intermediate
certiﬁcates, as the impact of an intermediate certiﬁcate revocation will be much
smaller than a root certiﬁcate revocation in the event of a compromised private key.
An X.509 certiﬁcate can contain additional ﬁelds such as common name, organization
and country.

Certiﬁcates used with ISO 15118

2.8
TCP & TLS
Subsequent to the SDP process, a TCP connection from EVCC to SECC must
be established to the address and port given in the SDP response. Based on the
’Security’ ﬁeld of the SDP request, the connection will either be secured with TLS
(Transport Layer Security) or not. A non-secure connection will only have access to a
limited part of the ISO 15118 application layer.
TLS is cryptographic protocol for securing communication across a computer network
typically between a client (the EVCC) and a server (the SECC). TLS is commonly
used to encrypt HTTPS website trafﬁc. Before encrypted communication can begin,
the client must perform a handshake with the server. The TLS handshake when
using Difﬁe-Hellman key exchange (see section 2.8.1) consists of the following
components:
• Client Hello: The client sends supported TLS versions (1.2 for ISO 15118),
list of cipher suites and compression methods. In case of ISO 15118-2 it also
sends its client certiﬁcate.
14
==PAGE:31==
Christian T. Blach
2
THEORY
• Server Hello: The Server chooses TLS version, cipher suite and compres-
sion method. The server also sends its X.509 certiﬁcate and a public key,
corresponding to a private key it is in possession of.
• Client Key Exchange: The client sends a public key to the server, correspond-
ing to a private key it is in possession of.
• Client Change Cipher Spec: The client sends a ChangeCipherSpec record
which essentially is saying ’Everything I tell you from now on will be authenti-
cated and encrypted’. It contains an encrypted message and a hash digest of
this message. This is done so the server can decrypt the message and verify
the digest.
• Server Change Cipher Spec: The server sends a ChangeCipherSpec record
which essentially is saying ’Everything I tell you from now on will be authenti-
cated and encrypted’. It also contains an encrypted message and digest which
can be used for veriﬁcation.
For the handshake to be successful, the client must trust the root certiﬁcate used to
sign the server certiﬁcate and the server must trust the root certiﬁcate used to sign
the client certiﬁcate. Additionally the client and the server must have at least one
TLS version and cipher suite in common.
2.8.1
Cipher Suites
A cipher suite is a combination of authentication, encryption, message authentication
code and key exchange algorithms.
The TLS 1.2 cipher suites allowed by ISO 15118-2 are:
TLS_ECDH_ECDSA _WITH_AES_128_CBC_SHA256
TLS_ECDHE_ECDSA_WITH_AES_128_CBC_SHA256
The two cipher suites are almost identical. The only difference being that one cipher
suite is using Elliptic Curve Difﬁe-Hellman (ECDH) key exchange and the other is
using ECDH with ephemeral keys (ECDHE). Using ephemeral keys is slightly more
secure (but also slightly more computationally expensive), as a leaked private key
from a previous communication session cannot be used to decrypt future sessions.
This ability is referred to as perfect forward security.
The Difﬁe-Hellman (DH) key exchange allows keys to be publicly exchanged without
a man in the middle being able to steal the keys by using a shared secret model
(unless he has valid trusted certiﬁcate private keys). The elliptic curve variation of DH
offers much better security using an identical key length (e.g. an 3072bit DH key is
15
==PAGE:32==
Christian T. Blach
2
THEORY
equivalent to a 256bit ECDH key). Both cipher suites requires certiﬁcates with Elliptic
Curve Digital Signature Algorithm (ECDSA) support.
The TLS encryption in both cipher suites is done using the AES-128-CBC block
cipher which as of yet is not computationally feasible to break (http://research.
microsoft.com/en-us/projects/cryptanalysis/aesbc.pdf).
Finally the SHA256 hashing algorithm (with HMAC) is used to verify the data integrity
of a message, i.e. that the message has not been changed since it was transmitted.
2.9
V2G Application Layer
The actual V2G messaging between EVCC and SECC is done in the V2G application
layer on top of the TCP connection which possibly is secured with TLS. The V2G
messages must be encoded in a binary form of XML called Efﬁcient XML Interchange
(EXI)[7, p. 36]. EXI relies on the fact that both parties (EVCC and SECC) have a copy
of the messaging schema, which allows for a dramatic reduction in message size.
The messages are encapsulated using V2GTP which consists of a header specifying
protocol version and data length as well as the actual raw EXI data.
The EXI data is structured as a V2G EXI document consisting of a header (note this
is in addition to the V2GTP header) and a body. The EXI document header contains
a charging session identiﬁer. It also contain two optional ﬁelds. An XML signature
ﬁeld optionally used for signing metering receipts and an optional notiﬁcation ﬁeld
that allows for the transmittal of error messages related to EXI parsing. The body
contains the request type-speciﬁc parameters as well as a session id ﬁeld which is
used to identify the current charging session.
There are different message sets for AC-charging and DC-charging. This thesis will
focus on the AC-charging message set. Note that all requests in the application layer
are initiated from the EVCC, i.e. it acts as a client, and the SECC always acts as
server, responding to each of the requests. The AC-charging message set consists
of the following messages which each consist of a request and a response[7, p. 194].
• Supported App Protocols (Handshake)
• Session Setup
• Service Discovery
• Service Details (optional)
• Service Payment Selection

SECC must choose a supported protocol and respond with the corresponding id. If
the SECC supports none of the requested app protocols it must respond with an
error code.
2.9.2
Session Setup
Assuming ISO 15118 communication was chosen, the EVCC must perform a session
setup request. Sessions allow for the SECC to store data related to a speciﬁc EV, i.e.
metering data and voltage and power speciﬁcations.
The request body only consist of a 6 bytes EVCC identiﬁer. If the request is successful
the SECC will respond with its SECC id (in the body) as well an 8 byte session id (in
the header). The EVCC must include this session id in the header future requests[7,
p. 62]. Session setup can also be used for session resumption, if the request header
has a valid preset session id different from zero.

2.9.3
Service Discovery
Following a successful session setup, the EVCC needs to discover which charging
services and which value added services (VAS) the connected SECC offers. VAS
are optional. The EVCC has the option of pre-ﬁltering the returned VAS by choosing
a service category type. The possible categories are[7, p. 168]:
• EV charging
• Internet
• Contract or Certiﬁcate service (e.g. renewal)
• Other or Custom
The request has another optional element: A service scope ﬁeld, which allows the
EVCC to ﬁlter certain service providers.
If the request is successful and the header contains valid session id, the SECC will
return charging options, a list of services optionally ﬁltered by the EVCC’s chosen
service category and/or scope as well as a list of payment options.
In terms of charging options the SECC must specify the id of the charging service
(and optionally the name), if the charging service is free or not and a list of supported
energy transfer modes.
The deﬁned energy transfer modes are[7, p. 107]:
• AC single phase core
• AC three phase core
• DC core
• DC extended
• DC combo core
• DC unique
This thesis only deals with the AC charge modes, i.e. AC single and three phase. The
SECC also has the option of returning the supported VAS (e.g. internet or contract
or certiﬁcate services).

Service Details
The EVCC can optionally use the Service Details request to obtain further details
about a service by setting a service ID in the request. The SECC will respond
with a response code and optionally a list of service parameters relevant to the
given service. The ISO 15118-2 standard does not specify the exact use of these
parameters, however.
2.9.5
Payment Service Selection
At this point the EVCC should have knowledge of the services provided by the SECC.
The ServicePaymentSelectionReq allows the EVCC to choose a list of services
ids (e.g. id of charging service, internet, certiﬁcate) and the payment method (e.g.
contract or external) it wants to utilize [7, p. 70]. Upon completion of the request,
the SECC will respond with a response code indicating if the service and payment
selection was successful.
2.9.6
Payment Details
If contract certiﬁcate (i.e. PnC mode) was chosen the contract must be validated by
the SECC. This is done with the payment details request. The request must include
the contract certiﬁcate chain (hence no private keys are exposed) and an e-mobility
account identiﬁer (EMAID)[7, p. 72]. The SECC must then validate that each link of
the certiﬁcate chain is issued and signed by the next. When reaching the top link, i.e.
the Mobility Operator Root Certiﬁcate the SECC must verify that it is trusted.

2.9.7
Authorization
The authorization request is used to authorize the charging.
If using PnC (Plug and Charge, i.e. contract payment) the contract certiﬁcate must
have been validated to reach this request. However, as the certiﬁcate chain only
contains the public key of the contract, the SECC cannot know for sure that the EVCC
also has the private key. In order for the SECC to validate the EVCC contract private
key without exposing it, the EVCC must send a challenge response signed by the
private key [7, p. 74]. This is done by hashing the binary EXI-encoded request body
with the SHA-256 hashing algorithm and then signing it with the contract certiﬁcate
ECDSA private key[7, p. 330].
If indeed the corresponding Mobility Operator Root Certiﬁcate (in the payment details
request) was trusted and the SECC validates the signature using the public key from
the previously sent certiﬁcate, the charging session will be authorized.
If using external payment (i.e. EIM), the SECC must wait till the payment is veriﬁed.
If the payment is not veriﬁed in time, the SECC will send a response indicating that
payment is still is being processed, prompting the EVCC to send a new authorization
request. This continues until the payment is veriﬁed.

Power Delivery
The power delivery request allows the EVCC to start or stop the charging of the EV.
or to call for a renegotiation of charging parameters and schedules.
In case of the charging request, the EVCC has the option of choosing a charging
proﬁle, as well as choosing one of the maximum power schedules returned by the
SECC in the charge parameter discovery response [7, p. 79-81].
The EVCC charging proﬁle is much reminiscent of the max power schedule, consisting
of a number of entries for maximum power. The charging proﬁle must naturally be
below the limits of the chosen max power schedule. If this is the case, the standard
states that the SECC must accept the request. With the tariffs in ﬁgure 9, a valid
charging proﬁle might look like in ﬁgure 10, i.e. the requested power maxima are
always below the ones provided by the EVSE. An invalid charging proﬁle could
look like the one in 11, where the requested EV power maxima exceeds the SECC
maxima twice.
The SECC response to the power delivery request is little but a veriﬁcation of the
charging schedule. It can contain one other thing though, a possible notiﬁcation from
the SECC calling the EV to stop charging or perform a renegotiation of charging
parameters and charging schedule. These notiﬁcations can also be held by the
charging status response and the metering receipt response.
2.9.10
Charging Status Request
The charging status request basically makes up the charging loop, i.e. the EVCC
must keep doing this request while performing charging. Also, no parameters are
sent in the body of the request.
Apart from the response code, the response optionally contains the EVSE maximum
current, metering info, a ﬂag telling if a metering receipt is required and a notiﬁcation
that is non-zero, if the SECC requires renegotiation or the charging to stop[7, p. 93-
95]. Reaction upon such an notiﬁcation must be performed within an amount of time,
NotiﬁcationMaxDelay, speciﬁed by the SECC.
2.9.11
Stop Status Request
The stop session request allows the EVCC to pause or terminate the current charging
session. If terminated the PLC data-link between the EVCC and SECC must be
disconnected. If paused, the IP-based discovery protocol SDP must be run again
in order to start the continue the session, however, the PLC data-link should not be
disconnected[7, p. 89-90].

2.9.12
Message Flow
The application layer requests must follow a certain message ﬂow for AC-charging,
see ﬁgure 12. The idea is basically that ﬁrst a session must be established. Then
the EV must choose which services it wants to utilize and authorize itself towards
the EVSE. Then the EV and EVSE exchange charging parameters including current
and voltage limits and the EVSE provides a maximum power schedule. Then the
EV must request power delivery and send a charging proﬁle which must not exceed
the maximum power schedule. Then the EV must send the charging status request
repeatedly during the whole charging process. Finally upon the end of the charging,
the EV must send a power delivery request indicating that charging must be stopped
and then send a session stop request.
The EV can take a few detours along the way of the outlined typical charging process.
Renegotiation
The EVCC can choose to renegotiate its charging proﬁle during
the charging loop. This renegotiation can also be prompted by the SECC sending
a notiﬁcation during the charging loop. Such a notiﬁcation requires the EVCC to
renegotiate within a set time.
The renegotiation process is started by the EV sending a Power Delivery Request
with charging process set to renegotiation. The EV must then send another charge
parameter discovery request to receive the up-to-date maximum power schedule.
The EV must then send a power delivery request containing a revised charging proﬁle
conforming to the new maximum power schedule. Given that is successful, the EV
can continue the charging loop.

3.1.1
Power Line Modems
Insys Powerline GP is a power line modem that enables communication conforming
to the Homeplug GreenPHY power line standard over power lines. GreenPHY trades
speed for lower power usage, using 75% less power than HomePlug AV modems but
limiting speed to between 256Kbps minimum and 10Mbps maximum data transfer
rate[4].
The two Powerline GP Modems used are slightly different. The SECC-connected
Power Line GP modem is equipped with a built-in implementation of SLAC as per
ISO 15118. The EVCC-connected Powerline GP does not have built-in SLAC. Both
Power Line GPs are manufactured using the Qualcomm QCA-7000 GreenPhy chip
and complemental hardware. It is possible to access the functionality of the QCA-
7000 chip directly in the Powerline GP without built-in SLAC by communicating with
ethernet packets sent by the EVCC. This allows for a custom SLAC implementation
at the EV-side.
3.2
The Code
The ISO 15118 implementation code is completely written in C and consists of multi-
ple modules that provide the functionality required to setup ISO 15118 communication
link to get ISO 15118 communication up and running (See ﬁgure 5 for an overview of
the process). It is important to note that the code used for the EVCC and the SECC
are quite different, as the EVCC must act as a client and the SECC must act as a
server. This basically means, that the EVCC sends requests and the SECC receives
and responds to requests, giving EVCC the initiative. Some of the responses, like the
ChargingStatus response, does however have the ability to give notiﬁcations, giving
the SECC the ability to inﬂuence the message ﬂow (see section 2.9.12).
The code made for this thesis has been open-sourced and uploaded to a Github
repository with the following url: https://github.com/cblach/nikola-v2gstack.
Note that all ﬁle-paths speciﬁed in this thesis are relative to the main folder.
3.2.1
Concurrency
The code relies heavily on the concept of concurrency. Concurrency is when two
tasks can start, run, and complete in overlapping time periods. It does not necessarily
mean they will ever both be running at the same instant (as opposed to parallelism).

Blocking IO
An important concept in concurrent programming is the use of blocking
IO (input/output). All communication to external devices except for that between CPU
and memory is considered IO. This among other things include hard disk access and
network operations. Once a network connection (e.g. using TCP) has been made,
one can use the IO operations read and write to receive and transmit data. Examining
the read operation for instance, one cannot know exactly how long it will take for
data to be available. This is where blocking IO comes into the picture. Blocking IO
basically means that an IO operation will block the execution of the calling thread
until the operation is successful. Meanwhile other execution ﬂows can continue in
other threads.
In the SECC implementation the SLAC association, the SDP handler and the TLS
handler are assigned a thread each. Also a new thread is created for each accepted
TLS connection (which also handles the application layer). Most of the time all threads
will block waiting for an IO operation to return. In some cases the IO-operation must
timeout and return after a given amount of time. This is done by creating using a
lightweight ’mini-thread’ (i.e. with small stack size) and creating a timer channel. The
created thread does the actual IO operation, the timer channel signals back when
the timer expires. The original thread will then block until either the reader thread
or the timer channel responds. If kill timer channel responded, it will kill the reader
thread, otherwise the main thread will continue execution (see ﬁgure 14).

EVSE SLAC implementation
The SLAC implementation at the EVSE side is rela-
tively simple as EVSE SLAC procedures are already implemented in the Powerline
GP with SLAC. SLAC listening is invoked by sending a SLAC_LISTEN ethernet frame
destined to the MAC address of the EVSE Powerline GP unit through the connected
interface (e.g. ETH1).
This will make the Powerline GP listen for SLAC for 20 seconds. When an EV
attempts to SLAC-associate, the Powerline GP will handle the whole process. If
the SLAC association is successful, the Powerline GP will send a D_LINK_READY
message back to the EVCC. This message implies that the logical network has been
formed, which in turn allows IP communication (e.g. UDP and TCP).
EV SLAC implementation
The EV SLAC implementation is slightly more complex,
as it requires a custom implementation of the SLAC process as the Powerline GP
does not have EV side SLAC. The Powerline GP, however, does still satisfy the
hardware requirements for the EV-side SLAC, thus it can be implemented using
software only. More speciﬁcally the Powerline GP contains a Qualcomm QCA7000
chip, which allows for ethernet packets to be sent across power lines as well as the
creation of logical networks.
Qualcomm has published an code implementation of SLAC at https://github.
com/qca/open-plc-utils/ which the SLAC implementation made for this master
thesis draws inspiration from.
The EV SLAC association process consists of multiple steps which are described
in section 2.5. The ﬂow diagrams of the EV SLAC association implementation are
pictured in ﬁgure 15 and ﬁgure 16.
Initially, when no logical networks have been formed, the EVCC will broadcast
CM_SLAC_PARAM requests until it receives a response (which indicates an EVSE is

between 8 and 16.
When a CM_SLAC_PARAM is received, the EVCC will broadcast three START_ATTEN_CHAR
requests and immediately after broadcast the desired number of CM_MNBC_SOUNDS
packets to all nearby devices (i.e. to MAC address FF:FF:FF:FF:FF:FF), with 40ms

SDP Client in EVCC
The SDP client implementation in the EVCC basically works
as shown in ﬁgure 17. First the main thread creates a thread for writing SDP requests
and a thread for reading SDP responses, and then waits for a response from either
thread. The SDP reader thread does blocking reads until a valid SDP response has
been received, after which point it signals the main thread and exits. The SDP writer
thread multicasts (i.e. broadcasts) an UDP message every 250ms until 50 messages
have been sent. The UDP message contains the chosen security setting, 0x00 for
TLS, 0x01 for pure TCP. Both settings are supported.
If the thread has not been killed before 50 messages have been sent (i.e. 12.5s
have passed), the thread will signal to the main and exit. Upon receiving a response
from either thread the main thread will kill the remaining read or write thread and
proceed depending on which thread that it received the message from. A message
from the writer thread equals a 12.5s timeout where no valid SDP response have
been received. This results in the thread entering an error state. A message from
the reader thread equals a valid SDP response, which allows the EVCC to continue.

3.5
TCP/TLS Implementation
The TCP/TLS implementation required for doing V2G application layer communication
consists of the following ﬁles:
• v2gstack.h
• v2gconn.c
TLS library
The TLS is implemented using the C/C++ crypto-library ’mbed TLS’
(formerly polarSSL). There are plenty of reasons to use an external crypto-library. It
is generally not advisable to implement TLS and crypto libraries oneself, as they need
to be secure against a large variety of hacking attacks. The most commonly used
crypto library is OpenSSL. ’mbed TLS’ was chosen due to its simplicity, reputation
and the fact that it is backed by ARM.
EVCC TCP/TLS implementation
Subsequent to a successful SDP run, the EVCC
knows the IP address and the SECC TCP port corresponding to either TLS secured or
non-secure connectivity. The EVCC then connects to the SECC using the ’connect’
linux system call. If TLS secured communication was chosen, the EVCC then
proceeds to initiate the TLS handshake. This handshake will be succesful only if the
EVCC trusts the SECC certiﬁcate and vice-versa. If succesful the SECC creates a
thread with the sole purpose of reading the TCP or TLS stream. This thread basically
partitions the stream into separate SECC responses which are to be used in the
application layer. At this point the EVCC is ready to send application layer messages
(see ﬁgure 19).
SECC TCP/TLS implementation
A new thread is created for each accepted TCP
connection (done with the ’accept’ system call). This thread starts by handling the
TLS handshake. If successful the thread will handle the application layer messages
by calling the request handling function which was speciﬁed upon the start of the
TCP/TLS server (see ﬁgure 20).

V2G Application Layer Implementation
The V2G application layer can be implemented in many different ways. Hence the
implementation done in this thesis is referenced as an example implementation.
3.6.1
Dependencies
The implementation depends on the external libraries:
• OpenV2G
• mbed TLS
The OpenV2G library deﬁnes the data structures used for ISO 15118 communication
and enables encoding and decoding to and from the binary EXI-XML format.
The mbed TLS library is also required and is used for the ECDSA signatures and
signature validation required to do contract payment.
The application layer furthermore depends on the following internal ﬁles:
• map.c
• session.c
The map dependency is a simple implementation of a hash table which uses separate
chaining, i.e. when encountering hash collisions the hash entries will be stored using
linked lists. A hash table enables quick lookup of charging sessions which have
their functionality deﬁned in the session.c ﬁle. Charging sessions enable the SECC
to store data related to the speciﬁc charging sessions. In this implementation the
sessions store the following data:
• Selected services
• Payment type (i.e. contract or external)
• Power max schedule and tariffs
• Status information regarding progress, e.g. has payment been veriﬁed.
• The EVCC public certiﬁcate
The session is saved in the hash table using an 8 byte session id which is randomly
generated and has 8 bytes of entropy (i.e. it only consists of random data from
/dev/urandom). This allows the EVCC that created the session to do requests related
to the session, while an potential interloper has to guess the session id before being
able to join. With 8 bytes of entropy there are 264 ≊1.84 · 1019 possible session ids.

Plug and Charge (PnC) Contract Handling
When using the PnC mode the EVCC must identify itself with a contract certiﬁcate,
the SECC must verify the contract certiﬁcate. The EVCC sends the X.509 contract
certiﬁcate chain in the payment details request. The chain consists of the contract
certiﬁcate itself, possibly intermediate certiﬁcates (however not in the example im-
plantation) and ﬁnally the mobility operator root certiﬁcate. Each link of the chain
must have been signed by the private key of the next.

Upon reception of the request, the SECC implementation veriﬁes that the certiﬁcate
public key was generated with ECDSA parameters, and proceeds to verify the trust in
the received contract certiﬁcate chain. It also veriﬁes that the contract certiﬁcate has
not expired, been revoked or modiﬁed. If the certiﬁcate was not veriﬁed the SECC

The EVCC then proceeds to attach the challenge in the authorization request and
signs the request with the private key corresponding to the contract certiﬁcate. The
signature is then attached in the header and the request is sent.
Upon reception, the SECC veriﬁes that the signature with the contract certiﬁcate
public key. If this is successful, the SECC knows that the EVCC is the proper owner
of a valid contract certiﬁcate as it (i.e. the EVCC) is both in possession of a certiﬁcate
signed by a trusted authority and the corresponding private key. If the signature is
not veriﬁed, the authorization fails.

EIM mode is somewhat simpler to handle for the SECC, as the identiﬁcation pro-

chips). The SECC handles EIM authorization requests by responding with the ﬂag
Ongoing_WaitingForCustomerInteraction (which indicates that the customer need to
authorize the EV) until the session has been authorized. As of now, authorization of
EXI sessions have not been implemented, as it requires interaction with an external
payment or identiﬁcation device.

Difﬁculty of Implementation
Due to the many layers of implementation, including SLAC, SDP, TCP/TLS, appli-

complex and extensive and requires knowledge within low level networking and
X.509 certiﬁcates among other things.
3.8
Possible Improvements
A number of improvements could be made, but were not implemented due the time
contraints of a master thesis.
First of all, the EVSE SLAC implementation depends on using an Insys module with
SLAC at the EVSE side. This could be remedied by making a custom implementation
of the EVSE SLAC. It could be based on the Qualcomm’s open-plc-utils just as the
custom EV side SLAC was.
In terms of message sets, only the AC message set was implemented. The DC
message set is vastly different, and could thus be subject to a future project.
Additionally the External Identiﬁcation Means (EIM) could be further explored, by
interfacing with external devices like card terminals.
Also, as earlier noted the implementation does not include SECC application layer
performance timings. These basically cause the SECC to return with a message
to the EVCC, if the SECC is not able to answer a request properly within a certain
time threshold. These could for instance be used with application layer certiﬁcate
requests which by nature require requests to external entities and thus in principle
could take longer. Implementing these will require the SECC to start a timer upon
request receival and then cause the SECC to automatically respond with a message
containing ’ResponseCode = OK’ and ’EVSEProcessing=Ongoing’, if the timer runs
out before the processing is done. The SECC then needs to keep track of the thread
processing the request and only allow new requests of the type of the request which
is ongoing. When the SECC is done processing, it can respond to the ongoing or the
next request with a response and a ﬂag indicating ’EVSEProcessing=Finished’.

5.1
Standard Usability in regards to Nikola Services
As this master project is a result of the Danish Nikola project, the uses of the standard
in regards to Nikola will be discussed. The Nikola project focuses on the synergies
between EVs and the power system and deﬁnes a number of services that can help
EVs bring value to the power system, see appendix B. The usability in relation to the
relevant services will be discussed in the following sections.
5.1.1
Adaptive Charging
Nikola deﬁnes one service within adaptive charging:
Adaptive charging delays or advances charging in time based on e.g. energy cost
or renewable contents.
What does the standard offer?
The standard allows the SECC to send tariffs,
renewable contents and a maximum power schedule to the EVCC with the ChargePa-
rameters response, see section 2.9.8. The EVCC can then use this information to
generate a charging proﬁle (i.e. power as a function of time) and send it to the SECC
with the PowerDelivery request, see section 2.9.9.
The SECC then must accept the charging proﬁle if it fulﬁlls the maximum power
schedule. This functionality basically covers the adaptive charging service require-
ments.
Calculation of Charging Proﬁle for ISO 15118
In order to perform effective adap-
tive charging there are multiple things to consider:
• Departure
When does the EV owner need the car to be charged to a certain percentage?
• Pricing
How much is the EV owner willing to pay to get his car charged?
If the time of departure is available, calculating a charging proﬁle is a simple matter
of iterating through each pricing interval starting from the one with the lowest pricing,
subtracting the available energy in the interval from the required energy. Then the
54
==PAGE:71==
Christian T. Blach
5
DISCUSSION
iteration process can be stopped when the remaining required energy is less than or
equal to zero.
If the time of departure is not readily available, more advanced methods needs to be
employed. One can use stochastic optimization to generate a reasonable charging
proﬁle based on user behavior and pricing[5].
5.1.2
Frequency Control
Nikola deﬁnes a number of services within frequency regulation (see appendix B)
• Primary frequency control aims to keep the frequency in an interval around
50 Hz, and is typically provided by generators that are able to respond quickly,
such as diesel generators. With primary frequency control the frequency is not
restored to 50Hz.
• Very fast frequency control has the same goal as primary frequency regula-
tion, however, it is done with ramping times and precision that goes above and
beyond what traditional generators can provide.
• Secondary Frequency Control aims to restore the frequency to 50Hz.
• Tertiary Frequency Control aims to take over from Secondary Frequency
Control and is the slowest type of frequency control.
Primary and secondary frequency control are typically performed by large diesel
motors or fast reacting power plants. Tertiary frequency control is typically performed
by traditional power plants. Frequency control is rewarded monetarily dependent
on the time a given amount of power is at the disposal of the Transmission System
Operator (TSO) which in Denmark is Energinet˙dk.
What does the standard offer?
ISO 15118-2 does not support the bidirectional
power transfer messaging required for frequency regulation as-is. However, according
to the ISO 15118-2-2014 scope, the enhanced message set which is required will
be subject to the next revision of the standard[7, p. 2]. ISO 15118-1 does, however,
contain the F5 use-case which outlines the process for setup of bidirectional power
transfer[7, p. 2]. The process is outlined to go like this:
• The EVCC indicates that it supports V2G power transfer and indicates how
much energy is available for V2G operation and at which maximum power,
taking into account the goal of a charged vehicle.
55
==PAGE:72==
Christian T. Blach
5
DISCUSSION
• The SECC conﬁrms V2G power transfer support
• SECC sends sales tariff table information or a proposed charging schedule,
including a V2G tariff/segment.
• The EVCC can use or reject the offered V2G tariff/segment.
As the exact details of the this process are not speciﬁed in the use case, it does
raises some questions in regards to how frequency regulation should happen. From
the use-case it seems that a renegotiation of the charging and bidirectional transfer
schedule must be performed to react upon frequency changes.
The requests required for such a renegotiation are likely to take less than 50ms based
on the application layer timings in ﬁgure 23, though this depends on how fast the
EVCC reacts after being notiﬁed by the SECC. The amount of time the EVCC has,
before a renegotiation must be performed, is dependent on a NotiﬁcationMaxDelay
value sent with the notiﬁcation from the SECC. Setting this to a low value ensures
that the EVCC must respond quickly. In addition it will take around 50-100ms to
retrieve an external frequency measurement. The time it takes to ramp up or down of
charging power is equipment-dependent, however, it will likely take a few seconds.
This should be fast enough for very fast frequency regulation and thus also for the
other types of frequency regulation and restoration.
The current pricing model used for frequency regulation (see earlier in this section),
may not be viable when using ISO 15118 to communicate the required power transfer.
This is because the EV battery is not at disposal of the TSO in the same way that
primary reserves typically might be.
First of all, the electricity markets (such as Nord Pool) must be able to deal instan-
taneous changes of availability, as an EV is only available for regulation when it is
actually plugged in. When the owner takes a ride, the car will suddenly be unavail-
able for such services. Secondly, the fact that the EV uses a battery rather than
a generator imposes some limits. The EV should always have enough remaining
charge after performing regulation services, in order to avoid inconveniencing the
EV owner too much. Also the EV can not absorb power past being fully charged.
Thus the EV is only functional for regulation services while the state of charge is in a
certain range, say between 70% and 100%. Thus some information regarding the
state of charge is required. It could either be the actual state of charge or certain
amounts of energy in the battery which the TSO is allowed to respectively charge or
discharge.
All that said, the time resolution and the ﬂexibility of the electricity markets in regards
to frequency control is likely to increase in order to accommodate for ancillary services
provided by EVs or battery storage solutions like the Tesla Powerwall. In regards to
56

payment of providing these services, it is likely that the EV owner will be paid based
on the amount of power, the energy reserve and the time he’s able to provide it for.
While the details of the V2G tariff/segment is not speciﬁed in the standard, the ISO
committees should take into account that bidirectional power transfer should be able
to support ancillary services and the payment of such when designing this segment.
5.1.3
Voltage Control
Nikola deﬁnes one service voltage control:
LV Network Balancing mitigates over voltage of LV feeders.
The EV can use reactive power compensation provided by its power converter
(assuming its supported) to mitigate over voltage.
What does the standard offer
The ISO 15118-2 message scheme does not yet
support reactive power compensation, however, it is very likely to do so in the future
as ISO 15118-1 has an use case specifying reactive power control, i.e. use case F4
[6, p. 40]. The process for reactive compensation is outlined in the use case to be as
following:
• EVCC indicates reactive power compensation is possible and provides infor-
mation as to what kind of reactive power compensation can be supported.
• SECC requests reactive power compensation and requests a certain amount
of compensation.
• EVCC conﬁrms the reactive power compensation value.
This seems like a reasonable setup process for reactive power. The only questionable
thing is what the use case means with what kind of reactive power compensation
is supported. It likely means the amount of positive and negative reactive power
production supported by the EV, e.g. -10kVar to 10kVar.
The problem with voltage control using reactive power compensation for EVs in
general, is that there as of May 2015 are no economic incentives to offer it. That said,
it is almost free for the EV to provide, given that its power converter supports it.
It seems there are two ways to resolve this. Either by beginning to offer economic
incentives or by making EV reactive power compensation a legal requirement. In
Denmark the latter option was chosen in a similar situation, i.e. with solar cell
inverters. Now, EVs are still somewhat different as they typically only come as
prebuilt all-in-one solutions (i.e. including bodywork, battery pack and so on), while
solar cell systems tend to be more ﬂexible.

5.1.4
Prevention of Overload in the Low and Medium Voltage Grid
Nikola deﬁnes two services within overload prevention:
• MV-LV transformer and lines overloading mitigates the overloading of trans-
formers and cables of LV networks.
• LV Congestion due to fast charging stations manages EV fast charging to
keep within operational limits of LV network.
Without control, a too many charging EVs in an area without the sufﬁcient grid
infrastructure can cause line and transformer overloading.
What does the standard offer
The standard allows the SECC to send a maximum
power schedule in the ChargeParameterDiscovery response which the charging
proﬁle sent by the EVCC must satisfy. This allows the SECC to mitigate transformer
and line overload overload by setting appropriate values in the maximum power
schedule. Likewise it can be used to prevent LV congestion during EV fast charging.
Assuming the required information regarding bottleneck line or transformer load is
available from sensors. This service can either be controlled in a centralized or
decentralized manner. Centralized control requires a unit to keep track of the different
load measurements and manage the maximum power schedules of the relevant EVs.
Decentralized control is of more chaotic nature, and would allow each EVSE to act
as it sees ﬁt in regards to mitigating overload.
5.2
Payment Infrastructure and ISO 15118
ISO 15118 introduces two types of payment, each tied to an identiﬁcation mode (see
section 2.9.3). The standard goes into great detail regarding the actual communica-
tion, but not so much regarding the payment infrastructure.
5.2.1
External Identiﬁcation Means (EIM) Infrastructure
The EIM mode supported by ISO 15118 allows for the use of external payment
methods, e.g. card-based, digital, mobile phone-based or NFC-based payment
solutions. This allows the EVSE to tap into already existing payment infrastructures,
simplifying a transition to ISO 15118. This payment method naturally requires external
payment equipment which typically come at a substantial expensive. The technical
58
==PAGE:75==
Christian T. Blach
5
DISCUSSION
difﬁculty of this mode depends mostly on the difﬁculty of interfacing with the external
equipment. When this has been done, it only requires a single software-based switch
in the ISO 15118 implementation to be ﬂipped in order to indicate that the charging
session has been veriﬁed.
5.2.2
Plug and Charge (PnC) Infrastructure
The concept of contract certiﬁcates (for PnC) introduced for ISO 15118 relies on the
EV owner to have a contract with a mobility operator. This agreement technically
embodies in the SECCs trusting the mobility operator root certiﬁcate provided by the
EVCCs. The reason it is called a mobility operator root certiﬁcate, is that it is issued
by a mobility operator which the EV owner typically will have contract with.
Any single EVSE will typically be owned by a single mobility operator, and PnC
charging will thus only per default be enabled for the customers of this operator.
Mobility operators do however get a competitive advantage by having as wide regional
support as possible. It becomes very viable for other mobility operators to have a
mutual beneﬁcial agreement with the actual operator of the given SECC. This yields
the other mobility operators better regional coverage, while giving the SECC operator
fees from a customer he might not otherwise have had. This can be paralleled to
the telecommunication industry where a mobile phone might be in a region that its
mobile phone operator does not cover. The operator will, however, typically have
contracts with other operators which for a fee allows the mobile phone to use to their
transceivers. This is typically referred to as roaming.
This is a major feature of the ISO 15118 standard, as it allows EV owners to easily
access EVSEs independent of location and ownership, provided that mobility operator
tied to the EV has made the proper agreements. Technically this requires installation
the trusted root certiﬁcates of the collaborating mobility operators on the SECC,
as well as an infrastructure which channels the cost of the charging session to the
correct party (i.e. the mobility operator which represents the given EV). This is fairly
trivial in a technical sense and allows the EVSE operator to save a substantial amount
of money that otherwise should be used for external payment.
Another important observation is that the mobility operator does not necessarily have
to be locked to a car brand. Provided that the EVCC software supports it, association
to a mobility operator can also work like subscriptions do in the telecommunication

5.3
Attack Vectors on ISO 15118 Communication
Some security issues might arise from implementations following the current ISO
15118 standard. The two most important ones encountered during the course of
work with ISO 15118 will be discussed in the following sections.
5.3.1
Connection Hijacking during the SLAC or SDP process
What
Connection hijacking basically means that an attacker lets the other part
unknowingly connect to the attacker rather than the wanted destination.
How
During the SLAC process a interloping EVSE in vicinity can send a fake
attenuation characteristic with artiﬁcially low attenuation, prompting the EV to choose
the interloping EVSE. Now, this can be somewhat detected by the EV noticing that it
receives two valid characteristic. The attacker can counter this by interfering with the
response of the real EVSE, making the fake attenuation characteristic the only one
that the EV receives. This will result in a logical network being formed between the
EVCC and the interloping SECC.
A similar attack can also be done during the SDP process, as the network parameters
is sent unencrypted with the CM_SLAC_MATCH response. This makes it rather easy
for the attacker to join the network, and respond to the EVCC before the real SECC
has a chance to do so.
Repercussions
If TLS is not used, the the EVCC and the fake SECC will com-
municate in the application layer. As the fake SECC is not in control of the actual
connected EVSE, this will not inﬂuence charging. And since no sensitive data is sent
over the data link, this will not get further repercussions than the EV believing that
the EVSE does not work.
If TLS is enforced by the EVCC, the TLS handshake will only complete if the fake
SECC has both a valid certiﬁcate and the associated private key. This is due to the
trust chains described earlier in section 2.7 (i.e. the EVCC needs to trust the V2G
Root certiﬁcate used to sign the SECC certiﬁcate). Thus without a valid certiﬁcate,
an attacker can only obstruct the initialization of the communication.

5.3.2
Man in the Middle Attacks during Application Layer Communication
What
A man in the middle (MITM) attack is an attack where an attacker attempts to
monitor and/or modify data sent between to parties.
How it is done without TLS
Given that the network key is sent publicly using
SLAC, then a MITM can readily join the logical network established between the
EVCC and the SECC. Thus if TLS is not used a MITM easily can easily monitor
and/or modify any application layer messages that are not signed by the contract
certiﬁcate (e.g. the payment details request). For instance the MITM could modify
the charging parameters without the EVCC ever knowing.
How it is done with TLS
If TLS is used the attack becomes quite complex. TLS
downgrade attacks (i.e. downgrading to a less secure cipher suite) do not work in
this context, as both the cipher suites required by ISO 15118 (see section 2.8.1) are
quite strong.
If the MITM is in possession of both a valid OEM provisioning certiﬁcate (and key)
and a valid SECC certiﬁcate (and key) he can let both parties connect to him instead
of each-other and then relay the communication.
On the internet when a client wants to visit the TLS encrypted https://google.com,
the client knows that what domain to expect in the common name on the certiﬁcate,
i.e. google.com. Thus this certiﬁcate will only work at at the domain google.com.
Now, in in the EV charging context the EVCC does not know what certiﬁcate param-
eters to expect (i.e. it cannot know if the SECC certiﬁcate used is from an SECC
in another location). Thus if the MITM can get hold of a single valid (i.e. signed by
trusted certiﬁcates) pair of OEM certiﬁcate + key and SECC certiﬁcate + key he can
monitor and/or modify the TLS communication between all EVCCs and SECCs.
In order for the MITM to obtain these certiﬁcates and keys, he has to physically
access an SECC. Since EVSEs are largely unsupervised, this will only prove a
problem if the storage is encrypted. Even then, he will likely be able to ﬁnd the key in
the memory. As for the EVCC certiﬁcate it is even easier to obtain, as the MITM just
have to use an ISO 15118 enabled EV temporarily.
Repercussions
This is one of the more severe attacks, as it can result in any com-
munication between the EVCC and the SECC being compromised and/or modiﬁed.

Preventing the Attack
The ﬁrst step to prevent MITM attacks is to make TLS oblig-
atory in the ISO 15118 regardless of identiﬁcation mode, as TLS greatly increases
the difﬁculty of the attack.
Now, in the event that the MITM has gotten hold of a valid EVCC OEM certiﬁcate +
key and a valid SECC certiﬁcate + key, there’s very little that can be done. If someone
ﬁnds out the certiﬁcates are used for fraudulent purposes, they can be revoked,
however, that’s only a half-measure as it likely takes quite a while to detect.
A possible cause of action, in order to limit the repercussions of someone with bad
intent getting hold of these certiﬁcates and keys, is to tie SECC certiﬁcates to a
speciﬁc GPS locations. This could be done by putting the intended GPS coordinates
of the given EVSE in the common name (CN) or another ﬁeld of the SECC certiﬁcate.
This of course has to be done prior to the root certiﬁcate signing process. This would
enable the EVCC to check the GPS coordinates in the certiﬁcate and use them to
verify that the certiﬁcate is issued to an EVSE at the approximate location of the EV.
5.4
Other problems related to ISO 15118
A problem which was faced during the testing of ISO 15118 was regarding whether
the ISO 15118 communication was on the control pilot line or the power line. While
the standard only mentions power line communication on the control pilot line, then
in the inspected RWE eBoxes, the modem was found to be connected to the power
line L1 (see section 4.3.1). This is probably attributed to the change in requirements
from earlier versions of the standard, i.e. earlier both solutions were allowed. ISO
likely chose to go with Control Pilot only because it was simpler, as one does not
need a coil to safeguard the PLC equipment.

RECOMMENDATIONS TO ISO
6
Recommendations to ISO
During the work with this project, a number of changes that would beneﬁt the ISO
15118 standard have been compiled. The ISO committees are recommended to:
• Consider removing the option of sending application layer messages over non-
secure TCP to increase message security and integrity (i.e. enforce TLS), see
section 5.3.1 and 5.3.2.
• Consider adding a way for the EVCC to verify if the SECC certiﬁcate really
has been issued to the SECC in question. This could be done by adding the
intended GPS coordinates of the SECC to the SECC certiﬁcate allowing (see
section 5.3.2).
• Design the bidirectional power transfer scheme so it enables the EV to provide
frequency control services and receive payment based on the possible power
transfer, energy capacity and the amount of time it is provided (see section
5.1.2). Likewise consider adding messaging regarding the monetary reward of
reactive power compensation when used for voltage control.

CONCLUSION
7
Conclusion
In this section, the main ﬁndings of this thesis will be summarized and discussed.
A full implementation of ISO 15118 communication has been created to explore the
implementability and usability of the standard and later the implementation has been
veriﬁed using a number of tests from ISO 15118-4.
7.0.1
Difﬁculty of Implementation
Due to the many layers of implementation required the standard cannot said to be
easily implementable. These layers include SLAC in ethernet layer, SDP in UDP
layer, various application messages using EXI-encoded V2GTP on top of a TCP
connection optionally secured with TLS. Additionally, the relatively advanced signing
of messages required for PnC (Plug and Charge) mode contributes to increased
difﬁculty of implementation. This signing process also requires extensive knowledge
of X.509 certiﬁcates.
The difﬁculty of implementation is also clearly evidenced by the lack of publicized
papers. Perhaps the implementation made for this thesis, can serve to make im-
plementing ISO 15118 easier for other researchers and developers by serving as a
reference implementation.
7.0.2
Interoperability
In regards to interoperability, the difﬁculty of implementation additionally is likely to
have slowed the adoption of ISO 15118, as only few EVSE vendors and EV vendors
claim ISO 15118 support, e.g. RWE for EVSEs and Smart for EVs. Other EVs such
as Tesla and Nissan Leaf does not support ISO 15118 as of May 2015. Another
interesting observation which has been made in this project, is that not all charging
stations proclaiming ISO 15118 support actually support the full standard (see section
4.3.1). During the testing of the EV implementation, it was found that an EVSE which
was sold as ISO 15118 compliant and required hardware, did not support SLAC
association which a critical part of doing ISO 15118 communication. Likewise, the
support of ISO 15118 was tested with electric Smart Car was tested and found not
to support SLAC, despite DTU Ballerup having the impression that it did when they
obtained it.

7.0.3
Enabling of Nikola Services
Then ISO 15118 has been evaluated in regards to relevant services deﬁned in the
Nikola Research Project. ISO 15118 enables adaptive charging with the current state
of the standard. Future revisions of the standard will allow the EV to perform enable
frequency regulation and voltage regulation, however, the messaging scheme for
bidirectional power transfer and reactive power compensation which enables these
services are not deﬁned yet. If the messaging schemes are not designed with these
services in mind, it could limit the applications of the standard in this regard. Hence
why this is included. Hence this has been added to the list of ISO recommendations
in section 6. Additionally, ISO 15118 enables services that can help prevent line and
transformer overloading caused by EV charging.
7.0.4
Payment Infrastructures and Security
Finally the payment infrastructures and security of the standard has been evaluated.
Plug and charge is promising as it allows an EV to charge and handle payment with
no required interaction from the EV user. In regards to communication security, there
are a few possible ﬂaws including the possibility of nonsecure connections and the
fact that it currently is difﬁcult to check if the EVSE really can be trusted due to the
possibility of man in the middle attacks.
7.0.5
Recommendations
Based on the work with this project, a list of recommendations for future revisions of
the standard have been compiled in section 6. This list among other things includes
proposed solutions to the raised security issues.
