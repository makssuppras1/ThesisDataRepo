Abstract
This thesis is an investigation weather smoke spread occurs, when a "smoke ventilated system"
as described in the DS 428:2011 [8] is used in a 10 story building.
In the past few years the design of the building envelope has been more tight and the windows
have more layers of glass. This air tight building method continues in the requirements for the
building class 2015 and 2020 according to the Danish building regulation[18].
Fires inside air tight compartments leads to high overpressures. The expanded air will then be
forced into the ventilation system. To ensure the safety of occupants, the ventilation system
operates with a smoke ventilator on the roof. The exhaust fan will keep an underpressure in
the ducts, this remove the smoke and prevent smoke propagation between ﬁre compartments to
extract the smoke through the ventilation system to outside.
The investigation is a numerical modeling where a well documented CFD program FDS[17]
made by NIST is used. The compartments dimensions are 5 x 5 x 3 m. A ﬁre is simulated in
one of the compartments where the ventilation system are connected to each ﬂoor by a
distribution duct. For the modeling of the operation of the ventilator in the case of a ﬁre the
speciﬁc ventilator Exhausto BESB 400-200 Box ventilator was used.
33 simulations was carried out with ﬁve diﬀerent parameters; the season, bottom and top of
building, wind pressure, building design and if the windows breaks or stays intact during the
ﬁre. One simulation was carried out where the ventilator was placed at ground next to the
building as a described solution in the DS 428 [8].
The results showed that the overpressure in the room reaches values of between 1500 Pa and
2400 Pa for the enclosure ﬁre where the windows stay intake. No smoke spread occurred when
the ventilator was placed on the roof of the building.
For the design where the ventilator was placed at ground next to the building, the air ﬂows
were varying from the other experiments. The air ﬂows where close to zero for all distribution
ducts other than the ﬁre compartment. This indicates that this system is not as safe and
reliable as the design with ventilator placed at the roof of the building.
Keywords: FDS, enclosure ﬁre, pressure, smoke spread, HVAC, smoke ventilated system, air
tight building, tall building.

Introduction
4.1
Background
For the past few years the building envelopes are tighter and withstands more than it could
before. This leads to enclosure ﬁres where the pressure rise could cause smoke spread to other
compartments. The ﬁre becomes under ventilated, because the over pressure in the room
prevents any oxygen to enter the room. The ﬁre follows the steps as shown on Figure 4.1.
Figure 4.1: A ﬂow diagram showing the processes of a ﬁre.
The temperature curve for an
enclosure is shown in the middle.
Smoke spread to other compartments can be caused by many factors. All these factors are due
to temperature and pressure diﬀerence. Some of the more common ones are listed below [14]:
Hydrostatic pressure: The hydrostatic pressure is a pressure diﬀerence in the height. The
hydrostatic pressure increases as the height decreases. The hydrostatic pressure depends on the
density of the material and in witch height it is presented.
Expansion: The ﬁre energy release causes an increase of the air temperature inside the
enclosure. The Ideal gas law lead to a pressure rise that are proportional to the temperature
rise. The smoke in the enclosure moves towards lower pressure zones. These leakages occurs in
the building envelope, doors, windows and through the ventilation system.
Stack eﬀect: The stack eﬀect is caused by temperature diﬀerence between the inside and the
outside. The hydrostatic pressure is increasing more outside compared to the inside when the
height decreases. The density diﬀerences in the height outside are higher due to lower
temperature in the height. This pressure diﬀerence creates an intake air ﬂow in the bottom of
the building, and an outgoing ﬂow in the top of the building. This can lead to vertical smoke
spread in e.g. installation-, ventilation-, elevator- shafts or staircases.
Buoyancy: The buoyancy eﬀect is the term for the upwards ﬂow of hot gasses and the down
ﬂow of colder gasses. This is caused by the lower density of the hot gas.
1
==PAGE:13==
Wind eﬀect: The wind creates a pressure diﬀerence on the building envelope due to
turbulence. This creates a over pressure at the facade that get hit by the wind and a under
pressure on the other side. These pressure diﬀerence to the inside causes air ﬂows through
leakages.
When a ﬁre occurs the spread of smoke to other compartments are dangerous. This causes
lower visibility, hot radiation towards occupants and it is lethal to inhale the smoke. Smoke can
also cause additional property damage when spreading from one ﬁre section to another.
4.2
Motivation
Throughout history ventilation has been needed in the home and indoor workspaces. Before
electricity was invented, natural ventilation was used to lead in fresh air and to transport the
combustible particles from the ﬁre places out of the homes[13].
Now a days either or both mechanical or natural ventilation are used in most buildings.
Mechanical ventilation was developed for use in mines, but was ﬁrst seen in buildings in the
past century, where the electricity had become more available and the economy had grown[13].
Mechanical ventilation has introduced a problem with smoke spread when a ﬁre occurs. This is
due to the installation of the ventilation duct that can lead the smoke from one room to
another.
Smoke ventilation for suppression, control and to prevent smoke spread have an important role
in future buildings. Because of more complex building designs, taller buildings extends the
evacuation and response times. Therefore it is important that the regulations, guidelines and
instructions are according to the reality.
In Denmark this issue was ﬁrst described in the DS 428 - "Dansk Ingeniørforening’s Code of
Practice for Technical Measures for Fire Protection in Ventilation Systems for Buildings"[4].
Later on this has been updated both in 2009[7] and in 2011[8].
In the Nordic countries two types of systems are often used to ensure that there are no smoke
spread through the ventilation ducts. See Section 5.
In the past few years the design of the building envelopes has been more tight and the windows
has more layers of glass. This building method creates a higher overpressure in the room, in
case of a ﬁre. This air tight building method continues in the requirements for the building
class 2015 and 2020 according to the Danish building regulation[18]. This new problem is not
described in the DS428[8].
In the past few years the enclosure ﬁre in an air tight building has been investigated by a few
others.
Kastvig, J. [15] investigated the pressure rise in seven compartments, on the same ﬂoor, in a
low energy building, with a "Smoke ventilation system" by using FDS (Fire Dynamic
Simulator). The results showed that an overpressure of 1500 Pa could be reached in an
enclosure with an under-ventilated ﬁre.
Janardhan, R. K. [12] investigated the smoke spread in the ventilation system to other rooms
on the same ﬂoor, with closed, valved and open ventilation shafts both with experiments and
FDS simulation. It was also found for both experiments and FDS simulations that the
overpressure in the room reaches about 1500 Pa. For the experiments the window frame was
blown out from the building envelope, when the overpressure reached 1600 Pa.

4.3
Objective and Approach
The objective of the thesis is to investigate if there with a "smoke ventilated system" occurs
smoke spread to other compartments when used in a tall low-energy building.
The investigations is carried out on a 10-story building with 1 compartment on each ﬂoor. The
compartments dimensions are 5 x 5 x 3 m. A ﬁre is simulated in one of the compartments
where the ventilation system is connected to each ﬂoor by a distribution duct.
In this study the ﬁres energy release rate are found from the equation 5.24 in the EFD[14]. The
heat release rate of this ﬁre is found from the calculation of the theoretical energy output based
on the amount of supply air to the room.
For this study computer simulation using a well documented CFD code called FDS[17] made
by NIST(National Institute of Standards and Technology) is used. The FDS simulations are
chosen because of its precision and the chance to investigate a lot of scenarios.
Diﬀerent scenarios are simulated to test the system. The system are tested in a summer and a
winter situation, and where the compartment with a ﬁre is placed on the 1st and 10th ﬂoor.
Scenarios where the windows are open or break and where the windows stay intact are also be
conducted. A situation where the wind blow directly on the facade are simulated. All these
tests has to be, tested together, to be sure that no smoke spread occurs.
4.4
Scope and Limitations
The scope behind this thesis is by numerical modelling to investigate the pressure and smoke
ﬂow in a tall low-energy building that has a "smoke ventilated system".
A limitation for this project is that it was not possible to conduct experiments to compare with
the simulations.
The FDS program is not able to simulate the mouldering ﬁre that occurs in the scenarios where
the windows stays intact. This means that the simulations stops when the oxygen
concentration are around 12 %. This gives unreliable results after typical 220-300 seconds for
these scenarios, but the smoke spread in the ﬁrst 60 seconds if it occurs.
In the scenarios where a wind pressure is applied to the simulation, the wind eﬀect on the
ventilator is not taken into an account.
There are some limitation when using the FDS HVAC model. This aﬀects the results on the
smoke temperature as is passes through the duct system.

Regulations
Regulations and standards on a national or international level are important to ensure a high
quality of the ﬁre safety in buildings. This chapter describes the Danish regulation and
standards that addresses ﬁre safety in general and for smoke management.
5.1
Regulations on smoke and ﬁre management in Denmark
In Denmark the Building code BR15[18] chapter 5 from 2015 describes some general rules
about the ﬁre safety in the buildings. The Danish standard DS 428:2011[8] is a guideline that
describes the ﬁre and smoke protection of ventilation systems.
5.1.1
Danish building code 2015 Chapter 5
The Danish building code chapter 5[18] about Fire safety has some general regulation that
always has to be addressed:
• The placement of the building must not lead to ﬁre or ﬁre spread to other building nearby.
• There has to be good possibilities to evacuation of the occupants and animals and for the
ﬁre ﬁghters after evacuation.
• The Fire safety level has to be ensured for the full life time of the building.
The BR15 chapter 5 does categories the buildings into six diﬀerent user categories. These user
categories are describing the number of people, the compartments size and what the building
are used for. These user categories have inﬂuence in almost every aspect of the ﬁre safety of the
building.
The escape routes and their design is also addressed in the BR15 chapter 5. Here it states that
the evacuation has to be en a easy and safe until the people are safe to the outside or inside the
building.
The materials used in the structural construction will have to withstand a ﬁre so that the
occupants can be evacuated and the ﬁre brigade can suppress the ﬁre without the risk of
collapse.
The requirement for ﬁre safety installation is controlled by the user category. Witch
installation that are required for each user category is also described in the BR15. The BR15
states that the ﬁre safety installation has to be maintained and inspected so that they are
reliable for the full life time of the building.
The building has to be divided into multiple ﬁre compartments to prevent ﬁre and smoke
spread for the time it takes to evacuated the occupants.
The building has to be placed and designed so that the ﬁre brigade can help with the
evacuation if the occupants and suppress the ﬁre easy and quickly.
4
==PAGE:16==
5.1.2
Danish Standard 428:2011 - Code of practice for technical measure for ﬁre
protection of ventilation systems
The essential of the DS428:2011 that addresses the ﬁre protection of ventilation system, is to
prevent ﬁre and smoke spread from one ﬁre compartment to another through the ventilation
system in the event of a ﬁre.
The standard also address prevention of ﬁre and smoke spread from any penetration that are
related to the ventilation system.
The DS 428:2011 does ﬁrst describe how the components and its materials should be designed
in a way so that they do not contribute to the ﬁre. The components, materials and there design
that are described are: Air intake and outcast, the aggregate and ventilators, ﬁlters, heaters
and coolers, dampers, ducts, air valves and diﬀusers and penetration through walls.
The next chapter is about the system design and how it must be controlled. In this chapter
there are introduced two kinds of system.
The ﬁrst solution described in DS 428[8] is to shut down the ventilation system. This is done
by the use of smoke dampers in ﬁre cells and ﬁre- and smoke dampers in ﬁre sections.
The second solution described in DS 428[8] is called "smoke ventilated system" and are for
ventilation systems where ﬁre- and smoke dampers are not possible to install. The dynamic
pressure loss to the ﬁre compartments restricts the amount of smoke. The exhaust fan keeps an
under pressure in the ducts. This removes the smoke and prevents smoke spread between the
ﬁre compartments.
There are some advantages with the "smoke ventilated system" when it comes to economy,
installation and maintenance.[15].
The smoke ventilated system is designed to use a separate smoke ventilator that in the case of
a ﬁre are activated so that the smoke do not damage the ventilation aggregate. The smoke
ventilation then controls both the inlet and outlet in each room. By the exhaust ventilator
there will be created a lower pressure in the duct compared to the compartment. This leads the
smoke from the ﬁre compartment into the duct, and then through a main duct where it are
mixed with the cold air from the other rooms, and at last it are lead out of the building.

6
Numerical modelling
To solve the Navier-Stokes equations there are diﬀerent programs using diﬀerent approaches to
solve this. In this study the program FDS[17] is chosen.
The studied ﬁre follows a transient ﬁre that all the time change due to dynamic parameters.
The dynamic parameters are listed below:
• Inﬁltration airﬂow through the building envelope, depends of pressure and temperature
diﬀerences between the outside and the room.
• Airﬂow through the ventilator is depended on the pressure in the duct system.
• Buoyancy in the ducts, dependent of the air temperature in the ducts.
• Airﬂow in the ducts to the other compartment in the duct system, dependent on the
pressure and the airﬂow through the outlet in the ﬁre compartment.
• The ﬁre size, dependent on the amount of oxygen in the room.
Choosing the FDS program is based on the validation studies made by Kastvig, J.[15] and
Janardhan, R.K.[12]. Both found that the FDS is reliably when used for the predictions of
building ﬁres.
6.1
Turbulence model
The Turbulence model is important when the ﬂow and movement of the ﬂow has to be
simulated. For CFD simulation there are three known turbulence models that can be used.
The RANS (Reynolds Averaged Navier-Stokes), LES (Large Eddy Simulation) and DNS
(Direct Numerical Simulation).
The RANS is available for stationary results, but can with use of an average calculation of the
ﬂow in the grid cells be used to model transient solutions. The RANS model is not considered
applicable for this study. [3].
The DNS model can be used to model both transient and stationary problems. The DNS
model is requires a very ﬁne grid, and for this study where it is the pressures and temperature
that are the important factors, the model is not suitable in matter of computational limitation.
The LES model is the one that are used in the FDS program. This model is made for transient
problems only. The model is more simple than the DNS and uses fundamental equation and
empirical models to calculate the ﬂow in the grids[3]. For this study the LES model is the best
choice, because of the calculated pressures and temperatures in the room. This makes the LES
model reliable for this model and the computational limitations are meet.
6.2
Combustion model
In FDS the combustion model is a single step, mixing-controlled chemical reaction. With the
scope of this study the ﬁre quickly become ventilation controlled and for comparison that all
combustion is taken place inside the room. So for combustion the FDS program is applicable
for this study.
6
==PAGE:18==
6.3
Low mach numbers
In order to use the FDS the ﬂows has to be of low speed. This is due to the numerically
boundaries for the FDS program[17]. In this study the highest velocities are created in the duct
system and through the simulated inﬁltration. But these velocities do not exceed FDS
boundaries.
6.4
HVAC modeling
To model the air ﬂow inside a duct can be very computational demanding if it is made inside
the mesh domain. The air ﬂows varies over the cross section of the duct.
For this study it is not wanted to have such precise models of the airﬂows in the duct system.
Therefore a well documented module of FDS called HVAC that are validated for the use of
scenarios similar to the challenges found in this study[17]. This calculates the input/output of
the ducts applicable for this study.

FDS model setup
This section gives an overview on how the FDS model is setup for the simulations that are

The code that are loaded into FDS is shown in Appendix A.6. The presented FDS code is used

7.3.1
Initial conditions
The model is ﬁrst of all set to log the DUMP data every second. This is to lower the simulation
time, and for the scope of the study it is enough with data for every second.
The radiation from the ﬁre is disabled in the FDS model. The radiation has a major impact on
the temperature of the ﬁre and also for the pressure rise in the room. But for this study the ﬁre
is based on a theoretical calculation, where the ﬁre total amount of energy output is found from
the amount of oxygen that enters the room. The maximum heat release rate for the simulated
ﬁre is equal to energy release from both the heat and radiation. Therefore the radiation are a
part of the heat release rate and is disabled to avoid an oversized energy input to the room.

7.3.3
Inﬁltration
The Inﬁltration is model in FDS as a function where there is attached a leaking area to a wall.
Previous in the thesis the inﬁltration areas for a building envelope that are build, after the
Danish Building Regulations BR15 and BR20[18].
To have the best opportunities to control and measure the inﬁltration ﬂow a small box was
applied to one of the walls. The box is open in both ends with a wall in the middle. It works
like a 0.5 x 0.5 x 0.5 rectangular duct that has a blocking wall in the middle. On Figure 7.9 are
the inﬁltration box shown.
The inﬁltration area is applied to the blocking wall. This method makes it possible to measure
the intake and outﬂow correctly. When simulating a situation with wind, there is applied a
function called DYNAMIC_PRESSURE on the open inﬁltration box. When simulating a
winter situation a function called EXT_TMP that controlled the exterior temperature is
applied.

7.3.5
Fire
The simulated ﬁres heat release rate is depended on the air inﬁltration to the room, supplying
the ﬁre with oxygen. A standard function for the ﬁre development is used. The ﬁre is placed in
the middle of the room on the ﬂoor on an 1 x 1 m square.
For this method, ﬁrst the soot yields and the type of material that is used is speciﬁed in the
function REAC. The soot yield was set to 0.1 kg/kg and the material that was chosen was the
premade material PROPANE.
Then the Heat Release Rate Per Unit Area has to be speciﬁed together with time before the
ﬁre is fully developed. The functions are respectively called HRRPUA and TAU_Q. The
HRRPUA does change together with the situation of the simulation. The HRRPUA that is
chosen is described earlier in this chapter. The time before the ﬁre is fully developed has been
set to 60 seconds and is chosen to prevent any numerical issues cursed by rapidly increasing
pressures or air ﬂows.

8.1.1
Normal operation
To ensure the validation of the model this simulation without a ﬁre is conducted.
At Figure A.1 the room temperature is shown. The pressure is decreasing constantly. This is
because of the higher outﬂow of the exhaust ventilation system, shown on Figure A.4,
compared to the intake by the inﬁltration shown on Figure A.3.
The volume ﬂows in the ventilation system are in all the distribution duct around 0.05 m3/s.
These are the desired ﬂow rates and this implicates that the regulation of the duct lengths and
the operation curve for the fan is working correctly. Figure A.4 shows the volume ﬂow rates in
the distribution ducts.
The volume ﬂow rate for the 1st ﬂoor is decreasing and this is because of the pressure solver in
FDS tries to maintain a constant pressure in the room. This is done by increasing the
inﬁltration and decreasing the exhaust ﬂow.
The inﬁltration air ﬂow through the leaking area is following an0 exponential increasing
function. This is the same curve as the theoretical inﬁltration shown on Figure 7.3. This
indicates that the inﬁltration air ﬂow correctly depends on the pressure in the room. A.3.

Windows stay intact

The building class is in these simulation the dimension factor. This is due to the diﬀerence in

8.6
Summery of results
In this section a summary of the results s described. None of the simulations that were
conducted lead to smoke spread through the ventilation system.
The simulations were all similar to the results from the simulations of a summer scenario at the
1st ﬂoor. Only two simulation where diﬀerent, an increased overpressure for simulation at the
10th ﬂoor where the windows stay intact. A diﬀerent placement of the ventilator lead to
changes in the volume ﬂows in the distribution ducts.
It was shown that for a under-ventilated enclose ﬁre an overpressure of about 1600 Pa can be
reached. The ceiling temperatures are between 225-325 C before the ﬁre reaches steady state.
When the ﬁre is in steady state the ventilation system functions as a smoke ventilation with
supply air from the inﬁltration. The simulations stop when all the oxygen is used in the room.
For these simulation the Building class is the determining factor. This is due to the theoretical
determination of the heat release rate of the ﬁre from Equation 7.1.
For the well-ventilated ﬁre where the windows break, the room pressure was equal to the
outside pressure. This is because of the 2 m2 opening that allows an airﬂow that always
equalizes the pressure in the room. For the simulations where a wind pressure was applied to
the opening, there was a minor pressure build up. This is due to the to opposite directed ﬂows
in the top of the opening. The simulated ﬁre was determined by the Equation 7.2, and lead to
ceiling temperatures in the room between a 1000 to 1200 C. This ﬁre development can be
compared to a Flashover ﬁre as presented in the ISO 834 from EFD[14].
For the under-ventilated enclosure ﬁre located on the 10th ﬂoor, the overpressure in the room
are reaching 2400 Pa. The reason for this increased overpressure was not found in the results.
So this change in pressure at the 10th ﬂoor have to be investigated in further studies. The
results showed no smoke propagation through the ventilation system.
To test the boundaries and reliabilities of the system, two simulations were conducted at the
2nd ﬂoor, where the ventilator was placed on the roof and at ground level next to the building.
An underventilated enclosure ﬁre was simulated and the results for the ﬁre compartment were
similar the the results for the summer scenario at the 1st ﬂoor. The volume ﬂow rates in the
distribution ducts are close to zero at the ﬁrst 60 seconds of the ﬁre for the simulation where
the ventilator is placed at ground level next to the building.
In this study no smoke spread was observed. FDS takes buoyancy into an account. This will
aﬀect the eﬀectiveness of the ventilator when the hot smoke enters a vertical duct. For this
ventilation system there are 10 compartments connected. This makes the ventilator larger and
with a higher capacity than for a small system of 2-3 compartments. The ﬁre is the same so the
smoke the are pushed into the duct system is the same, therefore the impact on the small
systems ventilator are greater than for a large system.

9
Conclusion
This thesis investigates if smoke spread occurs, when a "smoke ventilated system" as described
in the DS 428:2011 [8] is used in a 10 story low-energy class building.
In this study a well documented CFD program FDS[17] made by NIST is used. The
compartments dimensions are 5 x 5 x 3 m. 33 simulations were carried out with ﬁve diﬀerent
parameters, the season, bottom and top of building, wind pressure, building design and if the
windows break or stay intact during the ﬁre. One simulation was carried out where the
ventilator was placed at ground next the to building as a described solution in the DS
428:2011[8].
The results showed that the overpressure in the room reaches values between 1500 Pa and 2400
Pa for the enclosure ﬁre where the windows stays intake. The temperatures depend on the
building class and reached values at the ceiling of between 225-325 C after 60 seconds
dependent. The pressure in the room gets controlled by the ventilation system by extracting
the air out of the room after 30-50 seconds. The inﬁltration air ﬂow through the leakage area
then functions as supply air to the smoke ventilation system. The oxygen concentration in the
room was constantly dropping until it reached 0 % an the simulation had numerically
instabilities and stopped.
Throughout the simulation, where the ventilator was placed on the roof of the building, no
smoke propagation through the HVAC system was found. The air ﬂows in the distribution duct
for these simulation were similar. The smoke was push into the duct system in the ﬁre
compartment, where the air ﬂow in the distribution duct reached ﬂows of 0.27 m3/s for the
enclosure ﬁres. For the other distribution ducts the air ﬂows were all higher than 0.02 m3/s.
Through the parameter study of the FDS model used for the simulation, the LOSS function of
the FDS HVAC module was not found usable. Instead the dynamic pressure losses were
modelled by using the length and area of the duct. The diameters of the ducts were determined
from DS 15251[6]. The Friction loss in the duct was modelled, so it is equal to the dynamic
pressure loss in that duct, by changing its length.
This indicates that the speciﬁc Exhausto BESB 400-200 Box ventilator is reliable for a smoke
ventilation system in a vertical ventilation system in a tall building. This speciﬁc ventilator is
often used in Denmark for these types of ventilation system. Therefore Exhausto BESB400-200
is a representative for an often used solution.
For the simulation where the ventilator is placed on the roof and the windows stay intact
during a ﬁre, a few other has investigate similar scenarios. The over pressure at the 1st ﬂoor of
1600 Pa was also found by both Kastvig, J.[15] and Janardhan, R.K.[12], in FDS simulations
and experiments. The temperatures found in this study are similar to both the FDS simulation
and the experiments carried out by Janardhan, R.K.[12].
The results for the design where the ventilator is placed at the ground next to the building, the
room pressure, ceiling temperature, inﬁltration air ﬂow and the oxygen concentration, were
similar to the results found at the 1st ﬂoor. The air ﬂows were varying from the other
experiments. The air ﬂows were close to zero for all distribution ducts other than the ﬁre
compartment. This indicates that this system is not as safe and reliable as the design with the
ventilator placed at the roof of the building.
For the under-ventilated enclosure ﬁre located on the 10th ﬂoor, the overpressure in the room
are reaching 2400 Pa. The reason for this increased overpressure were not found in the results.
41
==PAGE:53==
So this change in pressure at the 10th ﬂoor have to be investigated in further studies. The
results showed no smoke propagation through the ventilation system.
For this study the ventilator has worked in all simulation. DS 428 does not described the risk
of failure for these fans. A working fan is necessary to prevent smoke propagation in a system
design like a smoke ventilated system. The need for redundancy for the smoke ventilated
system has to be discussed to ensure the occupants safety in the case of a ﬁre.
