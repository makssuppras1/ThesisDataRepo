Abstract
Atomization of a liquid feed is an essential process in spray drying applications and greatly
effects the quality of the dried product. Accurate prediction of spray formation and droplet
size distribution is therefore crucial to determine operating parameters. Most studies of
pressure­swirl nozzles are of theoretical or experimental nature. Numerical simulations
are often conducted in low pressure regimes. A numerical study on the atomization and
sheet formation of the Delavan SDX pressure­swirl atomizer for high inlet pressures is
presented. The flow problem was approached both with a single phase setup and the
Volume of Fluid method. This thesis will serve as a starting point for further investigations
of this specific nozzle type.
Experimental tests indicated that the discharge behaviour of the atomizer in terms of esti­
mated inlet pressure for a desired mass flow can be simulated by a single phase approach.
Good agreement is found when compared to experimental data. A second setup utilize
the Volume of Fluid method. Inclusion of modelling cavitation was found to be essential for
accurate formation of the liquid sheet. Simulations showed that a sufficient high mesh res­
olution and a small time step size are required for accurate capturing of the liquid sheet.
Deviation of flow prediction based on the turbulence model was observed. Detached
Eddy Simulation, Reynolds­Averaged Navier­Stokes k −ω Shear Stress Transport turbu­
lence model and Reynolds Stress Transport model have been adopted and compared.
Due to strong swirling motion it is observed that Detached Eddy Simulation and Reynolds
Stress Transport model are more suitable to solve the high turbulence anisotropy. Addi­
tionally, the effect of viscosity has been studied with the single phase setup and found to
be tremendous.

1
Introduction
The transformation of liquids into sprays or small droplets in a gaseous atmosphere is
a key process in many applications such as combustion of liquid fuels in diesel or spark
engines, cooling in various systems, spray coating or spray drying as used in the food
industry. Achieved is this through atomization of the liquid, in which a liquid jet or sheet
is disintigrated by the kinetic energy of the liquid, exposure to high­velocity gas or by
mechanical energy applied externally. Spray drying is a system process which uses at­
omization of liquids or slurries to produce powders for various products, ranging from baby
food and milk powder to ceramics and pharmaceuticals.
The primary atomization for industrial use can be achieved using different type of atomiz­
ers which can be divided into three categories: Pressure atomizers, rotary atomizers and
twin­fluid atomizers. Pressure atomizers convert pressure energy into kinetic engergy.
The feed is forced through a small orifice, which creates a thin liquid film which rapidly
brakes up. Rotary atomizers using centrifugal energy. The feed is introduced to a spin­
ning disc where is gets pushed outwards into as a spray where it gets in contact with the
drying medium. Lastly, twin­fluid atomizers work similar as the pressure atomizer. How­
ever, high kinetic energy of the feed is achieved by mixing it with high pressure air, rather
than pressurising the liquid directly.
The prediction of performance of pressure­swirl nozzles and the breakup of liquid jets or
sheets into small droplets has been investigated for many decades. However, the ma­
jority of knowledge is based on theoretical or experimental investigations. Due to the
complexity of the atomization process theoretical approaches often fail to predict the per­
formance accurately, specially for the droplet size distribution, which is a key result of
atomization. Experimental investigations are often time consuming and expensive as
they require special setups to capture key values of interests. Study on viscous effects
has been conducted by Yao[1]. Tratnig investigated the drop size spectra for numerous
configuration of the Delavan SDX atomizer[2]. Experiments for spray dryers fitted with
any kind of atomizer are often conducted on existing plants. This can only be achieved
by temporary discontinuity in production, meaning growing financial losses for a company
as long as the experiments are performed. Experiments on various subjects have been
conducted.
With continuously increasing computational performance, immense efforts have been de­
voted to develop numerical models for flow prediction of spray dryers and atomizers. Many
studies have been conducted for pressure swirl atomizers as those are widely used. Lau­
rila et al. performed large­eddy simulations for laminar, transitional and fully turbulent
flow conditions in a pressure swirl atomizer with high viscosity fluids[3]. Renze et. all.
found good agreement between experimental and numerical results for the Delavan SDX
SE 90 nozzle operating at low injection pressure[4]. Even so pressure swirl nozzles have
been studied fur numerous years, numerical simulations on pressure­swirl nozzles in high
pressure conditions are rarely conducted. Furthermore, nozzle designs are very different,
such that it is difficult to transfer findings between the different studies. Accurate predic­
tions of atomization for spray drying is highly important for both the design of the spray
dryer and the determination of operating conditions to achieve desired product quality.

1.1
Objectives
The aim of this project is to investigate the capability and functionality of numerical models
in predicting the flow behaviour of pressure swirl nozzles, specifically the ”Delavan SDX”
nozzle. The main objective is to gain a deeper understanding of the atomization process
with this nozzle type and to develop a functional computational fluid dynamics (CFD) setup
for accurate performance prediction. The project goals can be broken down into several
stages, each increasing the complexity:
1. Predicting discharge performance in terms of pressure drop and mass flow rate
2. Accurate modelling of sheet formation
3. Capturing of sheet breakup into ligaments and droplets
4. Determining the particle size distribution at the end atomization
1.2
Organization of the Thesis
This thesis consist four main parts. The first part describes the theoretical background for
this thesis. Chapter 2 outlines the relevant aspects of spray drying and briefly explains
cavitation. Numerical modelling is explained in chapter 3. A preliminary experiment was
conducted before the numerical part of the project to obtain data for discharge of the
nozzle under various pressure conditions. The experimental part is described in chapter
4. Data from the experiment will be used for validation of the first target point. The other
goals will be compared to experimental data obtained by Tratnig[2]. Development process
of the numerical models are described in chapter 5 for the single phase approach and
chapter 6 for the volume of fluid setup. The results of this project are summarized and
the thesis is completed with suggestions for further research.

2.1
Spray drying
As mentioned, the focus lays on pressure atomizers, specifically pressure swirl atomizer.
Therefore, an introduction to the base process is necessary to appreciate the experimen­
tal and numerical investigations conducted in this project. In the following, an overview
of spray drying and the main steps involved is provided. Special attention is given to
spray dryers fitted with pressure­swirl atomizers. For in­depth understanding of the main
principles the reader is referred to Masters[5] or Lefebvre[6].
Spray drying is a process for transforming a feed from liquid state to a dry powder by
spraying the feed into a hot drying medium. This process is related to others such as fluid­
bed or flash drying. But unlikely those, spray drying offers a unique feature by combining
both particle formation and drying. Spray drying is a suitable technique to meet different
requirements of many industries regarding the product quality. Powders can be produced
with various particle size or moisture content irrespective of drying capacity. The process
of spray drying involves four steps: (1) atomization of the feed, (2) contact of spray with
drying medium (often air), (3) evaporation of moisture content and (4) separation and
recovery of product[5]. The four stages are presented in the following chapters. Primary
focus lays on the atomization of the liquid, as this is the key interest of the project.
2.1.1
Atomization
The corner stone of spray drying is the formation of liquid sheets through atomization.
Main purpose of atomization is to increase surface­to­mass ratio with ideally individual
droplets of equal size. This ensures uniform powder characteristics. Lapple[7] describes
the process of atomization as a sequence of steps:
1. Liquid sheet, jet or film formation by acceleration of fluid
2. Initiation of small disturbances at the liquid surface
3. Formation of short ligaments as the result of fluid pressure or shear forces
4. Collapse of ligaments into droplets through surface tension
5. Further breakup of droplets in the gaseous medium
Usually atomization is categorized into a primary stage, including the first four steps de­
fined by Lapple, and a secondary stage describing the further breakup of droplets. Pri­
mary atomization, also known as primary breakup, refers to the process of building a fine
spray of droplets. According to Stiesch[8] a combination of three mechanisms is caus­
ing the primary breakup: turbulence within the liquid, implosion of cavitation bubbles and
aerodynamic forces. Pressure drop over the nozzle leads to acceleration of the liquid
feed, hereby generating a high level of turbulence. Additionally, static pressure drop at
the nozzle exit due to a reduction in cross­sectional area can lead to local pressure drops
below vapour pressure causing cavitation to occur. Lastly, the relative velocity between
liquid feed and gas results in aerodynamic forces on the liquid surface, which causes the
development of surface disturbances.

The second stage, also known as secondary breakup, involves the collapse of droplets
produced by the primary breakup. It is primarily driven by aerodynamic forces enforced on
the droplet by the surrounding gas. Stiesch[8] has defined a number of breakup regimes
for droplets based on the Weber number. The Weber number is the ratio of dynamic
pressure forces of the gaseous medium and the surface tension of the droplet:
We = ρU2D
σ
(2.1)
Here, ρ denotes the density of the surrounding gas, U is the relative velocity between
droplet and gas, D being the initial droplet diameter and σ the droplet surface tension. The
aerodynamic forces cause deformation of the spherical droplets. Breakup occurs when
the surface tension, which restrain deformation, is exceeded. The critical Weber number
Wecrit denotes the magnitude of the Weber number when breakup occurs. Secondary
breakup stops when surface tension becomes the dominating force. This is the case either
for very small droplet diameters or for low relative velocity to the freestream region. The
resulting droplet size is depending on several factors such as relative velocity between
liquid and gas or physical properties of both gas and liquid such as density or viscosity.
Different types of atomizers were invented for supplying the energy for breakup to the
liquid feed. The commonly most used types of atomizers are the rotary atomizers and
pressure atomizers. The later will be explained in more detail, as the subject of investi­
gation of this project is a pressure­swirl atomizer.
Pressure­swirl atomizer
As the name suggest, pressure­swirl atomizer converge pressure to kinetic energy by
forcing the liquid feed through a small orifice. Additionally to that, a swirling motion is
added to the liquid such that under centrifugal force the liquid spreads out in form of a
conical sheet. Spinning motion is accomplished through a swirl chamber. Various types
of chamber designs have been developed. They mainly differ in the method of imparting
the swirling motion to the liquid. Most common approach is the use of tangential inlet ports
into the swirl chamber. Lefebvre describes the development of the conical spray pattern
as a process of several stages[6]:
1. Liquid dribbles from the orifice
2. Liquid leaves as a thin distorted pencil
3. A cone forms at the orifice, but is contracted by surface tension forces into a closed

2.1.2
Spray­Air contact
The manner in which the spray combines with the drying medium is highly important for the
performance of the drying process. The design of the drying chamber in combination with
atomizer position and air inlet have a high influence on the resulting spray­air movement,
which determine the time in the drying chamber for each droplet. Dryer designs can be
categorized into three groups: co­current, counter­current and mixed­flow dryers. In a co­
current setup, spray and drying medium flow through the chamber in the same direction.
Numerical simulation of the atomization process of a pressure swirl nozzle
5
==PAGE:17==
In counter­current flow, atomizer and air inlet are placed opposite to each other, often
with the air inlet sitting at the base. Additionally, the mixed­flow configuration combines
both flow designs. The chosen setup of atomizer and drying chamber should create a
flow pattern that avoids wall deposition. Deposition of the product is caused by spray
droplets travelling rapidly through the drying chamber. Drying time of the droplets is not
long enough and the wet particles collect on surfaces inside the chamber. Deposited
material can contaminate the final product quality through overheated particles reenter
the flow. Furthermore, inlet and outlet openings can be blocked or reduced is size and
therefore reducing the efficiency of the process or even causing a temporary shut down
of the system[5].
2.1.3
Evaporation
Evaporation of moisture from the droplets begins immediately with the contact of spray
and hot drying medium. It can be distinguish between the first period of drying at a near­
constant rate and the second period with declining rate until drying is completed. The
first period is dominated by mass transfer of vapour through the surface of the droplets.
In this period, the moisture level inside the droplets is large enough to maintain a con­
stant surface saturation wit the droplets held at wet­bulb temperature. Once the rate of
transport of moisture to the droplet surface reaches a state where surface wetness can
no longer be maintained, a fall in drying rate results. This level of moisture is also known
as critical moisture content. As drying continues, a dried layer builds on the surface of
the droplet, working now as the restraining factor of further drying. Evaporation stops
when the droplet acquires equal moisture content with the surrounding air. However, in
spray­drying operation the product is usually removed from the drying chamber before
equilibrium state is reached[5].
2.1.4
Separation of product
The last stage of the drying operation includes the separation of the dried product from the
air and removal of the product from the drying unit. Primary separation may happen at the
base of the drying chamber and is often followed by a secondary product separation. The
secondary separation requires a downstream placed equipment to extract the remaining
particles, e.g. cyclones or bag filters. Alternatively, collecting of the dried product can
solely take place in the secondary separation equipment[5].
2.2
Cavitation
Cavitation is a phenomenon of explosive growth and intense collapse of vapour bubbles
in a liquid. On a first glance, boiling and cavitation seems to be very similar, the phase
change from liquid to vapour. But unlike boiling, which is forming of vapour due to increase
of temperature at constant pressure, cavitation is a phase change from liquid to vapour
due to a pressure drop below saturation pressure at constant temperature. Cavitation has
been widely studied due to is negative effects on machinery. The rapid bubble collapse
is accompanied with very small, but intense pressure waves. Additionally, if the bubble
collapses near to a wall, re­entrant jets can form and penetrate the wall. This can lead to
wear on the surface of the wall causing cavitation corrosion. Pumps, turbines or propellers
are designed and installed in a way to avoid the appearance of cavitation. For other
applications, cavitation might be desired to increase the process performance. In case of
this study cavitation brings positive effects to the primary breakup of the liquid feed[9].

3.2
Turbulence modelling
An important aspect of CFD simulations is modelling the effect of turbulence. Turbulent
flows are characterized by unsteady flow fluctuations in forms of eddies of various length
and time scales. These eddies can appear in form from large structures down to the
Kolmogorov scales. The most straightforward approach of solving the governing equa­
tions is called direct numerical simulation (DNS). This approach solves the turbulence
down to the smallest scales. The requirements for mesh and time step size come with
enormous computational effort and therefore, is seldom used for modelling purposes. To
avoid these extensive computational costs, turbulence models have been developed to
account for turbulence scales by averaging over certain quantities and approximate the
impact of small flow fluctuations. The most common approach are the Reynolds­averaged
Navier­Stokes (RANS) turbulence models. Another possibility is the use of Large Eddy
Simulations (LES).

Volume of Fluid
Multiphase flows is the interaction of several (at least two) immiscible phases in one sys­
tem. A phase is generally considered as the state of a fluid, e.g. liquid, solid or gas, but
may cover more fluid characteristics in CFD simulations. Multiphase flow can be found in
many occasion. A typical example is air bubbles raising in a glas of water. There are two
approaches for simulating multiphase flows, the Eulerian approach and the Langrangian
approach. Both come with unique characteristics. In this project the Volume of Fluid (VOF)
method will be used, a sub model of the Eulerian approach. Multiphase flows require the
solver to capture the interface between the phases and it has to handle the exchange of
mass, momentum and heat through the interface. This adds a lot of complexity to the
system in comparison to single phase simulations. The volume of fluid method predicts
the distribution and the movement of the immiscible phases. The distribution and position
of a phase is described through the phase volume fraction defined as:

phases, the change of volume fraction is solved for one phase and the second phase is
adjusted accordingly. For three or more phases, the rate of volume fraction change is
solved for each phase separately. The volume fraction for each phase is then normalized
by the sum of all volume fractions to meet the requirement of P αi = 1.

3.5
Wall treatment
Due to the nature of pressure swirl flows and the thin sheet thickness leaving the nozzle
exit, it is of utmost importance to solve the near wall flow region very accurately. The
thin viscosity­affected layer near a wall is called the wall boundary layer. Inside this layer,
the flow velocity increases from zero to the free stream value away from the wall. The
boundary layer can be divided into laminar and turbulent layer, depending on the Reynolds
number. For high Reynolds number the boundary layer is turbulent and characterized by
unsteady swirling flows inside the boundary layer. This leads to mixing inside the boundary
layer. Figure 3.1 shows the velocity profile of a turbulent boundary layer.
Figure 3.1: Velocity profile of turbulent boundary layer
The turbulent boundary layer can be divided into two layers: the outer layer, which is domi­
nated by turbulent effects, and the inner layer, which is divided again into three sub­layers.
Each sub­layer is dominated by different flow characteristics. The viscous sublayer, which
is in contact with the wall, is dominated by viscous effects and therefore is laminar. The
log layer is equally dominated by viscous and turbulent effects. The buffer layer is a tran­
sitional layer between the viscous sublayer and the log layer.
There are two approaches to solve the flow in the turbulent boundary layer. Either us­
ing wall functions to approximate the behaviour in the boundary layer or fully resolve the
boundary layer. The second approach comes with an increase computational cost due to
the higher cell resolution needed to resolve the boundary layer. The non­dimensional wall
distance y+ indicates how well the boundary layer is discretized. It describes the distance
between the cell centroid and the nearest wall. The y+ value is defined as[14]:

Numerical model for Single phase
Primary focus of the first part of the numerical modelling has been the development of a
CFD setup that would accurately predict the discharge performance of the atomizer. Pre­
diction of discharge performance for this purpose means a correct estimation of pressure
drop over the nozzle for a set mass flow rate. A target point was to reduce the computa­
tional costs (running time of the simulation) to an acceptable level while keeping a high
accuracy. The problem is approached with a single phase simulation. This decision is
made based on the observations from the experimental investigation of the atomizer be­
haviour as they are presented in chapter 4. Validation of the numerical results includes
the comparison between numerical and experimental data. Therefore, the model should
replicate the experimental setup. All simulations have been carried out using the com­
mercial software package Star­CCM+[11]. Version 2206 and 2210 of Star­CCM+ package
have been used. The following sections will give an overview of the procedures involved
of CFD modelling.
5.1
Nozzle geometry
Before the development of the CFD model could begin, it was necessary to acquire the
dimensions of the Delavan nozzle used in the experiment. The nozzle parts come from
a third party. Therefore, technical drawings of the parts were not available. This required
measurements of each part by hand using a digital caliper. Additionally, 3D laser scanning
of the parts was performed. The laser scanning included a software to produce CAD
files of each part. Those CAD files could then be used to create the complex internal
geometries of the nozzle in the Star­CCM+ software. The Delavan SDX atomizer is a
modular pressure­swirl nozzle where numerous combination of swirl chamber and orifice

5.3
Physics
The Star­CMM+ software offers a variation of models to capture the physical phenomena
of the flow and to build up a simulation setup. The main models for the single phase
simulation of the pressure swirl nozzle are shown below.
• Three dimensional
• Turbulence: Reynolds­Averaged Navier­Stokes
• Turbulence model: k −ω SST Menter
• Segregated Flow
• 2nd­order Implicit Unsteady
• Incompressible flow
• Wall treatment: All­y+
• Adaptive time­step
A three dimensional approach was chosen due to the asymmetric design of the swirl
chamber. Atomization is a highly turbulent process. To account for turbulence the Reynolds­
Averaged Navier­Stokes equations with the k −ω SST (shear stress transport) model for
the closure problem have been adopted. Additionally, Reynolds Stress Model and De­
tached Eddy Simulation have been tested. In an early stage of the project the use of the
k−ϵ model and the V2F model was investigated. However, those models showed strange
behaviour for the flow field and therefore were neglected. Segregated flow solver with an
2nd­order integration scheme was implemented. The single phase flow is modelled as a
incompressible fluid. Because high swirling motion is expected with high velocity close
the orifice walls, the All­y+ wall treatment with a low y+ value as a target point was used.
An implicit unsteady simulation is adopted. Steady­state approach has been tested in the
early stage of the project. However, it was discovered that the atomization process can­
not be modelled with an steady­state approach. Adaptive time­stepping was implemented
targeting a Courant number of 1, such that no explicit time­step size was defined.

Spatial discretization
To solve the governing equations for the flow, the simulation domain has to be discretized
into sub­domains. Mesh settings should be chosen such that the flow is accurately re­
solved while the computational cost are kept at a reasonable level. The mesh is generated
as a polyhedral mesh with prism layers added to all walls. The prism layers allow the ac­
curate resolving of the boundary layer. Surface remesher is added to improve the quality
of the surface mesh. Certain regions of the domain, where high velocity gradients are
expected, have been refined through volumetric controls. The position of the volumetric
controls are visualized in figure 5.4. The refinements concentrate around the nozzle area
(blue) including refinements for the flow split, swirl chamber and outlet (green), the orifice
and outlet region (yellow) and a second refinement for the orifice and the core of the swirl
chamber (red). The cell count is gradually increased for each volumetric control such that
large volume changes between two cells are avoided. Surface controls are used to define
the prism layers.

The velocity field for the simulation domain is presented in appendix A.1. It shows the
velocity magnitude plotted on a vertical plane through the center of the atomizer. Majority

beneath the outlet of atomizer. This is mainly caused by the outflowing liquid from the
atomizer. Large velocity gradients can be observed in the swirl chamber and the orifice

the swirl chamber is included in appendix A.2. It shows that the tangential velocity slowly
ramps up when the liquid enters the swirl chamber. It increases with closing distance to
the center. However, the core of the swirling flow is characterized by very low velocity.
The liquid sheet leaving the orifice quickly looses the high velocity. This behaviour is not
unexpected, as the liquid in the domain has a high resistance due to its high density.

5.8.2
Effect of Viscosity
As Marshall has stated the liquid viscosity has a strong influence on the formation of
the air core in the orifice. Based on the size of the air core the cross­sectional area in the
orifice outlet changes. The effective outlet area has a strong impact on the resulting sheet
velocity and the required pressure to maintain a predefined mass flow rate. Because the
viscosity is such an important property for the spray drying process, the effect of changing
viscosity was studied by performing a series of test runs with different viscosity values.
Tested viscosity values range from µ = 3.16e −4 Pas up to µ = 0.1 Pas. Since the
test was conducted in a setup of single phase flow without cavitation, the development
of an air core cannot be shown. However, the effect of viscosity can be demonstrated
based on the axial and tangential velocity profiles of the flow. The axial and tangential
velocity profiles at the outlet of the orifice for different viscosities are presented in figures
5.17 and 5.18. The effect of increasing viscosity of the liquid is found to be significant.
High viscosity prevents the development of a swirling flow when the liquid enters the swirl
chamber. Tangential velocity decreases drastically with higher viscosity. Furthermore,
higher viscosity prevents the liquid from getting pushed to the wall of the orifice. Peaks of
tangential velocity appear closer towards the center of the orifice. This limits the formation
of a clear sheet. The axial velocity profiles indicate that for very viscous liquids the flow is
more likely to form a jet with concentration of high velocity in the core of the orifice. This

formation of vapour allowed until a steady solution is achieved. Cavitation is activated by
setting the positive bubble growth rate equal to one after a defined time. Achieved is this
through a user field function StartCavitation:
ScalingFac+ = ($Time < $StartCavitationTime) ? 0 : 1
The inclusion of the cavitation model requires the definition of a third phase, namely the
vapour phase for the liquid. Tratnig does not provide the mixing ratio for his test liq­
uid. Therefore, it is not possible to calculate the correct physical properties of the vapour
state. Since his mixtures are based on water the third phase is defined as water vapour.
Interaction between the phases has to be defined. It allows to add a number of various
sub­models. The effect of surface tension is added to the interaction of the liquid phase
and air. The cavitation model can be selected in the interaction folder of liquid and vapour
phase. No explicit model was included to the interaction of vapour and air.

6.5
Results
In this chapter the results of the multiphase approach will be presented. The simulation
setup was continuously improved. The results are presented in a way such that the reader
can follow the respective development steps. A first run was performed without modelling
cavitation. Due to negative pressure and the observed spray pattern it was considered
necessary to include cavitation. Refining of the mesh was performed to increase the
spatial resolution. With the mesh refinement the free surface of the liquid sheet was
captured better. As suggested by Andersen[19], cavitation parameters were adjusted to
improve the cavitation rate. In a last step the turbulence modelling approach was switched
to DES. As indicated by the single phase investigation DES can predict the swirling flow
more accurate. The main findings are presented in table 6.4.

Conclusion
The objective of this thesis was to developed a functional CFD model in the Star­CCM+
software that could predict the atomization process in a pressure swirl atomizer and the re­
sulting sheet formation. The goal of modelling sheet break­up into ligaments and droplets
and capturing of the final droplet size distribution could not be achieved due to time limi­
tations of the project period.
A preliminary experiment was conducted to obtain data on the discharge performance
of the Delavan SDX atomizer. The experiment included the testing of several combina­
tions of swirl chamber and orifice disk. Experimental results were in good agreement to
brochure data provided by Delavan. It was demonstrated that the discharge of the at­
omizer is independent of the medium liquid is sprayed into. Based on this observation
the decision was made to start the numerical modelling with a single phase approach.
Obtained data from the experiment were used for validation of the single phase setup.
Based on the experimental observations a computational fluid dynamics model in the
Star­CCM+ software package was developed. The CFD model is based on RANS k −ω
SST turbulence model. Preliminary runs with the single phase model proofed the need
of an unsteady approach due to the high turbulence anisotropy of the swirl flow. Conver­
gence study of mesh size and time step showed that an estimation of inlet pressure with
acceptable accuracy can be achieved with a coarse mesh resolution and larger time step
reducing the computational effort greatly.
Several test runs for different mass flow rates have been performed and results were com­
pared to the experimental data as a validation of the numerical model. Good agreement
was found between experimental and numerical results. A study on the effect of viscosity
on the flow field was conducted. Significant impact was observed. The results showed
that for increasing viscosity the inlet pressure for a required mass flow rate strongly re­
duces. However, no comment on liquid sheet or drop size could be made due to the single
phase approach.
Applying Detached Eddy Simulation and Reynolds Stress Transport model proved to in­
crease the accuracy of the simulation. Because they solve more of the turbulence and
use less modelling. However, these two models come with increased computational ef­
fort, specially the Reynolds Stress Transport model.

The single phase model was extended with the volume of fluid method and HRIC scheme
to simulate multiphase spraying. Changes to the orifice geometry were necessary for
comparison to validation data from Tratnig[2]. Investigations showed that the capturing of
the interface between liquid and air is highly dependent on the spatial resolution and the
time step size. High cell count across the liquid sheet is required to avoid disintegration
of the liquid surface. Small time step size is necessary to fulfill the requirement of the
volume of fluid model. A first approach excluded the modelling of cavitation. This test run
showed that modelling of cavitation is essential to allow the formation of an air core and
consequently the formation of the liquid sheet. The Schnerr­Sauer cavitation model was
used to invoke cavitation inside the flow regime. Observations showed that the standard
settings for seed density and seed diameter are not able to solve cavitation in a physical
manner. Suggestions for both variables by Andersen[19] were adopted and it was seen
that these improve the solving of cavitation. However, still negative pressure could be
observed.
Both k −ω SST model and Detached Eddy Simulation were tested with the volume of
fluid method. Good agreement between numerical results and measurements from Trat­
nig was achieved for inlet pressure and sheet velocity. DES was more accurate for the
estimation of inlet pressure, while k −ω SST simulated the sheet velocity more precise.
However, it has to be mentioned that the DES simulation could not be run until conver­
gence due to time limitations of the project period. The results from the DES run should
therefore be viewed with caution.

Suggestions for further research
This thesis started the development process for a numerical setup to replicate atomization
with the Delavan SDX atomizer. Therefore, further work should be conducted to improve
the numerical performance. It would be desired to optimize the setup such that the final
droplet size distribution can be accurately predicted. Potential future research can include:
• Optimize mesh refinement to reduce cell count far away from the free surface. These
cells can be spend around the free surface.
• Consider to apply adaptive mesh refinement
• Further investigation of cavitation inside the orifice. Full Rayleigh­Plesset equation
can be tested and results can be compared to the Schnerr­Sauer model
• Volume of Fluid method can be invoked with a modified HRIC scheme
• Study the influence of contact angle between liquid and atomizer surface
• Assuming a axis symmetrical swirl chamber it could be tested if the swirling flow can
be simulated with symmetry planes. This could immensely reduce the computational
costs
• Once a fully developed setup exists real life conditions such as spraying into a hot
air stream could be tested to replicate full spray drying process
