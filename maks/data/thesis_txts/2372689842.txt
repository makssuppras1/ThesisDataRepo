Abstract 
 
Increasing anthropogenic pressure on water resources is causing serious problems, especially in 
industrialised countries such as Denmark. The Water Framework Directive (WFD; 2000/60/EU) sets 
criteria and new objectives to reach good water quality status through integrated assessments on 
different compartments such as surface water and groundwater. Among the variety of chemicals 
polluting the environment, heavy metals are recognized to be a serious threat due to their toxicity and 
accumulation. The current study quantified and assessed the contamination of As, Cd, Cr, Cu, Ni, Pb 
and Zn in the shallow aquifer, hyporheic zone, stream water and streambed sediments at Rådvad site, a 
former metal manufacturing industrial area located in Denmark, investigating sources, pathways and 
final targets of contamination. Previous studies showed the presence of contact zones in the northern 
part of the area between the unconfined aquifer and the stream passing through the site, besides 
revealing a heterogeneous contamination (CAH, BTEX in the water and heavy metals in the soil). 
Stream water was sampled in 12 points, while groundwater was sampled in 4 wells close to the stream 
where the interaction was suspected. Sediments and hyporheic zone were sampled in pair, where 
upward hydraulic heads have been detected. A drain discharging in the river was also sampled. 
Sediments were divided in different layers and both heavy metal total concentration and chemical 
partitioning were analysed. Redox species and dissolved organic matter were also analysed in the water 
samples, while fraction of organic carbon was investigated in the extracted sediments. Results showed a 
high degree of heavy metal contamination in all the investigated resources. Groundwater was found 
under reduced conditions and polluted by Pb, Ni and Zn, exceeding the quality criteria by a factor of 
2,7, 4,2 and 9 respectively. No clear correlation between groundwater and hyporheic zone was detected. 
Consequently, it was not possible to deduce where the polluted groundwater was flowing. Once 
entered in the contaminated site, the stream was also found polluted by Zn and Cu, confirming the 
anthropogenic role to the environmental pollution of the area. Sediments’ total concentration analysis 
revealed high pollution of all the investigated heavy metals especially in the top sediments (0-10 cm), 
suggesting run-off contribution. However, the high concentrations cannot be explained just from the 
soil contamination found in the previous studies. Thus, past disposal of metal or 
construction/demolition waste could be another source of contamination. Chemical fractionation 
showed that Zn and Cd were the most easily available heavy metals, since a high portion of them was 
bound to acid soluble/exchangeable fraction, which can explain the high stream water Zn 
concentrations. Cr, Ni and As were mostly bounded to the residual fraction, while Cu and Pb were 
mainly associated with the oxidizable and reducible fractions respectively. Finally,  the classification of 
the compartments ordered according to the risk they pose was defined as: 1) soil contamination, 
evaluated as the major concern at Rådvad site since the risk of exposure to heavy metals is very high in 
the northern and southern part of the area, where a Naturskole and residential houses have been built 
to reclaim the area; 2) sediment contamination, due to Zn and Cd high portion in the acid 
soluble/exchangeable fraction that pose high ecological risk; 3) stream water contamination, as Zn and 
Cu were exceeding the maximum allowable concentration in many investigated points; 4) shallow 
aquifer contamination, evaluated as the least risky since the detected upward flow from the deeper 
aquifer to the shallow contaminated aquifer would probably avoid downward migration of the leachate 
to the deeper aquifer, which is used for drinking water purposes. The current method used to evaluate 
the contamination and assess the risk, highlighted the importance of studying contaminated sites at a 
broader scale. Integrated approaches could help determining the fate and distribution of pollutants, 
building classification risks to prioritize and optimize the management of contaminated sites.

1.  Introduction 
 
1.1. Background overview 
 
Increasing anthropogenic pressure on water resources is causing serious problems in many countries 
around the world, especially in industrialised countries such as Denmark (Boutrup et al. 1998; 
McKnight et al. 2012). It is therefore necessary to monitor and evaluate stream water and groundwater 
resources, in order to protect public health and the environment (Wu et al. 2016; Boutrup et al. 2009; 
McKnight et al. 2012). To address this, the Water Framework Directive (2000/60/EU) outlines criteria 
and new objectives regarding environmental pollution in order to control and improve surface water 
and groundwater quality (EA 2005). While most of the common management approaches to 
environmental pollution is considering single compartments (Moser et al. 2003), in recent years more 
attention has been given to the interactions between the compartments, aiming to better understand 
the processes behind and assess the impacts of chemicals released in rivers and lakes or leached in 
aquifers. A more integrated assessment is therefore needed to identify the nature of the sources, 
impacted receptors, pathways and possible consequences related to exposures to environmental 
contaminations. Consequently, it is important to develop complete risk assessments, understanding the 
chemical and hydrological processes at the soil-groundwater and groundwater-surface water boundaries 
(EA 2005). 
 
In Denmark, among the different chemicals released to water resources such as surface water and 
groundwater, heavy metals are recognized to be a serious threat for the environment due to their 
unique characteristics and toxicity (Charriau et al. 2011; EPA 2007; Boutrup et al. 2009). Metals can 
potentially contaminate the soil and leach into groundwater, the primary source of drinking water, or 
contaminate the rivers and lakes through run off or interaction between contaminated groundwater and 
surface water. In past decades, concentrations of heavy metals in soil, water and sediments increased 
due to their use in different agricultural and industrial applications, affecting human life under certain 
conditions (Knight et al. 1997; EPA 2007; Tchounwou et al. 2012). Ingestion, uptake or inhalation of 
heavy metals leads to accumulation in humans, animals and plants, becoming harmful when they 
exceed certain threshold concentrations (Jaishankar et al. 2014). According to the U.S. EPA and the 
International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC), some of the most used heavy metals such as 
cadmium, chromium, lead and mercury are classified as “known” or “probable” human carcinogens.  
 
Former metal manufacturing and processing industrial areas are well recognized worldwide to be a 
threat for human health and the environment due to their high number and the wide range of 
chemicals used in the different processes (Harber & Forth 2001; Kjeldsen T.H. & Christensen 1996). 
In Denmark, 413 contaminated sites in 2006 were classified as “iron and metal foundries/industries”, 
which indicates the extensiveness of the problem (Kjeldsen 2016). Among the contaminants, heavy 
metals such as Pb, Cr, Cd, Zn and Ni cause impairment to soil and water quality (Wuana & Okieimen 
2011; Kjeldsen T.H. & Christensen 1996; Harber & Forth 2001; Mulligan et al. 2001). Therefore, it is 
extremely important to build risk assessment and remediation strategies necessary to control and avoid 
negative effects related to heavy metal contamination.

1.2. Project aim 
 
The aim of this Master thesis is to investigate the existing heavy metal contamination at the Rådvad 
site, a former industrial metal manufacturing area well recognized to be a site where groundwater and 
surface water interact at the interface. Heavy metal contamination was previously detected in the soil, 
but no investigations were performed for stream water, groundwater or sediments, even though 
previous studies have found a deep interaction between these. The project will focus on the main 
potential heavy metals associated with metal production and manufacturing: arsenic, cadmium, copper, 
chromium, lead, nickel and zinc, making sure to distinguish natural and human contributions. 
 
The specific objectives include: 
• Quantify the heavy metal contamination levels in stream water, groundwater, hyporheic zone 
and sediments at the Rådvad site by field investigations and laboratory analysis; 
 
• Evaluate the primary sources and pathways contaminating the environment, focusing in 
particular on the interaction between soil, groundwater, sediments and stream water, with the 
aim of creating a conceptual model that delineates an integrated and complete picture of heavy 
metal distribution, sources, pathways and processes affecting heavy metal mobilization; 
 
• Assess the overall risk related to heavy metal contamination.

2. Heavy metal definition, sources and chemical 
processes 
 
2.1. Definition and general sources 
 
Heavy metals are usually defined as the metallic elements having an atomic weight higher than Na, or a 
density higher than 5g/m3 (Christensen & Holm, 1997; Tchounwou et al., 2012). In the past decades, 
concern has increased in conjunction with possible health risks associated with the exposure due to 
environmental contamination of heavy metals. According to the U.S. EPA and the International 
Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC), heavy metals induce adverse health effects such as neurologic 
disorders, diabetes, cardiovascular diseases and are classified as human carcinogens (Tchounwou et al. 
2012).  
 
Heavy metals are naturally occurring constituents in the Earth’s crust (EPA 2007; Tchounwou et al. 
2012); they can be essential to life, such as copper, chromium, nickel or zinc, since they play important 
roles in plants and animals because of their biochemical and physiological functions (EPA 2007; 
Tchounwou et al. 2012; Lee & Saunders 2003; Jaishankar et al. 2014). However, an excess amount 
affects human health, resulting in a variety of diseases or syndromes such as Wilson’s disease or tissue 
damages (Holm & Christensen 1997; Tchounwou et al. 2012; WHO 1996). On the other hand, some 
heavy metals such as arsenic, cadmium, lead and mercury have no established function to humans, and 
are therefore not considered essential elements, in addition to being toxic at all levels. Due to their high 
level of toxicity, these four heavy metals are of primary interest to protect public health (EPA 2007; 
Tchounwou et al. 2012). 
 
Sources of heavy metals can either be related to the natural background or due to anthropogenic 
contamination (Li et al. 2009; EPA 2007; Jaishankar et al. 2014). As a result of industrialization after 
1850, the exponential increase in the use of heavy metals for industrial, agricultural, domestic and 
technological applications increased the exposure to these pollutants, raising awareness on heavy metal 
exposure and environmental contamination. Some common sources of heavy metals are industrial 
effluents coming from refineries, nuclear power stations, wood preservation and paper processing 
plants, and pharmaceutical industries. Atmospheric deposition is another important contributor, 
resulting from the burning of fossil fuels, smelting or mining operations, subsequently resulting in 
widespread contamination. The use of agrochemicals has also resulted in high concentrations of toxic 
heavy metals in agricultural fields (Li et al. 2009; Holm & Christensen 1997; Christensen & Holm n.d.). 
On the other hand, common geogenic sources are associated with weathering, volcanic eruptions or 
soil processing (Cyraniak & Draszawka 2014; Li et al. 2009). It has to be considered that it is 
exceptional to find only one single type of metal (OECD 1995); usually, heavy metals are associated 
with other materials and a mixture of metal compounds will be present.

2.2. Chemical processes 
 
Heavy metals cannot be degraded similarly to organic compounds; therefore, other processes are 
governing their behaviour and distribution. The main processes are precipitation/dissolution, 
sorption/desorption, redox processes and complexation (Holm & Christensen 1997). Complexation 
affects both sorption and precipitation. Consequently, it is not always possible to make clear 
distinctions between these processes (Rieuwerts et al. 2015). Metals can adsorb into soil, be transported 
by surface runoff into rivers and lakes or leach from soil into groundwater, which is the primary source 
of drinking water in Denmark (EPA 2007; Mulligan et al. 2001; GEUS 2005).

Sorption is a fast process, which binds the heavy metal to the solid phase and reduces the concentration 
in solution. It is particularly relevant for divalent metal ions such as Cd, Hg, Ni, Cu, Zn and Pb because 
of their affinity with particles’ and soils’ negative charge. It is not really relevant for Cr and As. Sorption 
also depends on the type of soil (e.g. clay minerals, organic matter, carbonates) and it increases with an 
increase of pH (Holm & Christensen 1997; Christensen & Holm n.d.). Humic matter and metal (Fe, 
Mn, Al) oxides are among the most important sorbents of heavy metals, binding them and reducing 
their mobility (Violante et al. 2010). The sorption process controls the distribution when metals are not 
under saturated conditions. 
 
Redox processes are chemical reactions which involve the transfer of electrons between two species. 
Redox processes can both mobilize or immobilize heavy metals, depending on the species present and 
the environmental conditions. The mobility of some heavy metals such as As and Cr are strongly 
influenced by biogeochemical redox processes: under reduced conditions, arsenate reduces to arsenite, 
becoming very mobile and more toxic. On the other hand, toxic and mobile CrVI reduces to CrIII, 
which is not toxic and essential for animals (Violante et al. 2010; Holm & Christensen 1997). Cu can 
==PAGE:14==
 
3 
also be found in two different oxidation states: I and II. However, Cu(I) is very unstable and therefore 
not relevant under normal environmental conditions  (Holm & Christensen 1997). 
 
Precipitation is a slow process which requires high concentrations to be relevant. It occurs when the 
concentration exceeds a metal’s solubility. The general anions involved in the precipitation are 
phosphate, carbonate and sulphate. They can also precipitate in the form of metal hydroxides.  
Precipitation and sorption are difficult to distinguish, but they both affect the mobility of heavy metals 
in solution. Precipitation is usually described by the Saturation Index (SI), describing if the solution is 
undersaturated, saturated or oversaturated (Holm & Christensen 1997; Rieuwerts et al. 2015; 
Marchioretto et al. 2007). Precipitation occurs when the solution is oversaturated.  
 
Complexation affects both precipitation and sorption. The presence of ligands in solution can strongly 
bind the heavy metals, affecting mobilization and transport. Complexation can be with organic or 
inorganic ligands present in the solution. The most important inorganic ligands are sulfate (SO42-), 
sulfide (S2-), carbonates, hydroxides (-OH) and chloride (Cl-). Organic ligands include humic and fulvic 
acids, gallic acids or citric acids (Rieuwerts et al. 2015). The degree of complexation is proportional to 
the ligand concentration and the pH (Christensen & Holm 1997, Rieuwerts et al. 2015).  The 
complexation could either increase or reduce heavy metal mobilization: for example, the presence of 
dissolved organic ligands can increase the solubility as a result of complexation, even if the presence of 
sulphide under anaerobic conditions will limit the solubility. 
 
 
2.3. Processes affecting the mobilization of heavy metals 
 
Dissolution of metals in the soil have received a lot of attention in the past years (Garcés et al., 2008). 
Understanding and characterizing the processes affecting bioavailability, mobilization and toxicity of 
metals is clearly important for assessing the related risk (Violante et al. 2010; Du Laing et al. 2009; 
Rieuwerts et al. 2015). 
 
Soil and sediments can act both as sources or sinks for heavy metals. This is affected by different 
biological and chemical processes (Chapter 2.2) which depend on a great number of factors, the most 
important of which are pH, redox conditions, sorbent nature and presence of ligands such as organic 
matter, oxides and nutrients. (Desogus et al. 2013; Rieuwerts et al. 2015; Du Laing et al. 2009). These 
factors and processes determine in which chemical form heavy metals occur, which can explain the 
mobility and bioavailability of trace elements (Violante et al. 2010; Du Laing et al. 2009; Okoro 2012; 
Rieuwerts et al. 2015). 
 
Dijkstra, et al. (2004) explained how the leachable fractions strongly depend on the pH: concentrations 
in the water are high for low pH, but heavy metals in solution decrease drastically at neutral to alkaline 
pH due to sorption to organic matter and iron and manganese hydroxide minerals, before increasing 
again with strong alkaline pH, giving the typical V-shape. In general, solid organic matter strongly binds 
with Cu, Ni, Cd for a large pH range, especially when neutral. Zn and Pb sorb predominantly to 
hydroxides, at neutral-alkalinized pH. Regarding heavy metals in solution, complexation with dissolved 
organic matter is predominant for neutral-alkaline pH, while under acidic conditions (pH below 4) the 
free phase is predominant (Dijkstra et al. 2004).

Also redox processes influence the mobilization or retention of heavy metals. Oxidizing conditions 
form Fe-, Mn- and Al- (hydr)oxides which can strongly bind dissolved trace metals (Violante et al. 
2010; Du Laing et al. 2009). On the contrary, oxidation of sulphide to sulphate and degradation of 
organic matter can mobilize the heavy metals bound to them. However, under anaerobic conditions, 
sulfate is used as an electron acceptor and generates S2-. Sulfide can react with dissolved metals and 
form metal sulfide precipitates, since most of the metal sulfides have generally low solubility (Jong & 
Parry 2003). As already explained in Chapter 1, the mobilization and toxicity of some heavy metals and 
metalloids depends on their oxidation state (Violante et al. 2010; Du Laing et al. 2009; Holm & 
Christensen 1997; Mcmahon & Chapelle 2008). For example, the reductive species of Arsenic (AsIII) 
are much more toxic and mobile than Arsenite species (AsV), while the reduced form CrIII is not toxic 
compared to the toxic and more mobile species Cr(VI). 
 
The factors described above highly affect the heavy metal chemical fractionation. The chemical form of 
the heavy metals determines the mobility and (bio)availability in the environment (Baran & Tarnawski 
2015; Boughriet et al. 2007; Concas et al. 2006; Li et al. 2015; Rinklebe & Shaheen 2014; Shikazono et 
al. 2008; Zhang et al. 2017; Wang et al. 2012; Lu et al. 2003). The main chemical forms for heavy metals 
in soils and sediments are the acid soluble fraction, the exchangeable fraction, metals precipitated with 
inorganic compounds and large molecular humic substances (oxidizable fraction), metals precipitated 
and adsorbed to oxides (reducible fraction) and finally metals retained in the crystal structure of the 
minerals (residual fraction). The acid soluble and exchangable fraction contain respectively weakly 
adsorbed metals bound to the solid through weak electrostatic interaction and carbonates. These 
fractions are considered as potentially easily available, since they are affected by ion exchange and 
changes of pH. The reducible fraction is associated with Fe and Mn oxides and can be released due to a 
change from oxidized to reduced conditions, which leads to FeIII and MnIV reduction. This phase is 
considered as the main sink for heavy metals, as long as oxidized conditions prevail. The oxidizable 
fraction contains metals bound to organic matter or sulphides and might be released in oxidizing 
conditions due to degradation of organic matter or oxidation of sulfide. The metals found in this 
fraction are considered not easily available due to the stability of the humic substances and sulfide. 
Lastly, the residual part contains the immobile metal fraction retained within the crystal structure 
(Rauret et al. 1999; Ashraf et al. 2012; Okoro 2012). 
 
Many investigations on the fate of heavy metals during the interaction between groundwater and 
surface water have been carried out in the past two decades. Fuller et al. (2000) reported the uptake of 
the hyporehic zone of a Creek in Arizona recharged by a neutralized groundwater contaminated by 
mine tailings (high concentrations of Mn, Cu, Ni, Zn). Results indicated how manganese and iron 
oxides are an important sorbent phase where stream water dissolved metal concentrations decreased 
more than 30% in only 2,8 km. They also observed that the continuous formation of Mn-oxides in the 
river enhances metal uptake. Benner et al. (1995) also describes the co-precipitation and adsorption of 
trace metals to Fe and Mn-oxides, which increases at higher pH. Moser et al. (2003) and  Winde & Van 
Der Walt (2004) observed the same retardation process for chromium and uranium.

2.4. Waste from metal processing 
 
The most frequent heavy metals found at contaminated sites are Pb, Cr, As, Zn, Cd, Cu and Hg, posing 
a risk of superficial and groundwater contamination (Wuana & Okieimen 2011). Industrial areas where 
metals were manufactured are an important source of chemical pollution which derives from a wide 
range of associated manufacturing processes. Table 2 gives an overview of the common materials used 
and wastes generated depending on the type of industrial process (EPA 1995; Rajagopalan 1984). 
 
The production of metals involves different stages, including mining, mineral extraction and refining 
(Wuana & Okieimen 2011; Harber & Forth 2001). Metal manufacturing business have the purpose of 
forming or shaping metal surface in order to prepare products ready for the market. Besides the usual 
shaping processes, other manipulations are applied to create the final product: common operations are 
for example grinding, welding, painting, plating or galvanizing. Moreover, solvents, alkali and acids are 
used to clean the surfaces before the products are ready to be used in their final application. 
 
Manipulation and steel finishing operations such as casting, welding, cleaning, parts assembly, painting 
and plating produce slag, flue dusts and wastewater containing a wide range of contaminants, including 
heavy metals (EPA 1995; Guyer 1998). Strong acid wastes are produced from hot dip galvanizing, 
metal colouring, hot tinning and many others, while strong alkaline wastes are generated by using 
pickled aluminium and zinc. Electroplating produces plating wastes: in this process, acidic solutions 
containing heavy metals such as arsenic, chromium, copper, nickel, zinc, cadmium and lead are used. 
Wastewater streams may contain toxic heavy metals such as cadmium, lead, copper and zinc. Other 
potential contaminants include oil compounds, used for lubrification and quenching; cyanide solutions, 
used for cleaning and hardening; and chlorides and sulphates (EPA 1995; Harber & Forth 2001). It can 
be concluded that contamination from iron work sites is highly heterogeneous: metals, organic and 
inorganic compounds are usually the main groups of pollutants causing environmental contamination.

3. Description of Rådvad contaminated site 
 
This chapter aims to give a general knowledge regarding the history of Rådvad, the hydrogeology and 
the past investigations carried out at the site. The information described in this chapter were essential 
for the planning and execution of the field campaign described in Chapter 4. 
 
3.1. Rådvad site history 
 
Rådvad is a former industrial area located north of Copenaghen, Denmark, between Lyngby-Taarbæk 
and Rudersdal municipalities. The site has been primarly used in the past two centuries for metal 
manufacturing activities, of which the most famous was the well-known knife factory which produced 
domestic metal wares from 1879 to 1972. Rådvad hosted a large number of other factories, such as a 
bronze foundry and a metal workshop, for an unknown period. Today the area is used for various 
professions such as offices, housing, as well as a Naturskole, which offers free activities in the open air 
and close to nature for all primary school classes in the related municipalities. The southern part of the 
site is now a residential area. Rådvad is characterized by special drinking water interests (OSD), 
bordering Rådvad Dam and the Mølleåen stream (NIRAS 2012). From Rådvad Dam, two distinct 
channels flow across the site before merging at a confluence 100m downstream. The two channels will 
be henceforth termed the northern stream and the southern stream. Figure 1 shows the plan view of 
the site. A collection of pictures from the site can be found in Appendix A.

3.2. Hydrogeology 
 
The contaminated site is located on a layer of peat which has a thickness of about 5-6 meters, with 
different sand lenses all over the layer. Then, the terrain underneath is characterized by alternating 
layers of sand/gravel and glacial deposits. The bottom layer is composed of limestone, 40-45 meters 
below ground level (thickness unknown). The terrain is relatively flat (NIRAS 2012; Lemaire 2016). 
 
The upper peat/gyttja layer overlays the upper unconfined aquifer, which has an unknown thickness, 
while the deeper limestone aquifer is confined at approximately 35-40 m.b.s. From the investigations 
carried out by (NIRAS 2012), hydraulic connection is suspected between all layers: upward flow from 
the deeper aquifer to the upper aquifer was assumed, as well as from the upper aquifer to the gyttja 
layer.  
 
From the groundwater investigations carried out in 2009 the southern stream is recharged by the upper 
aquifer from the southern part of the area, while the northern stream is recharged by the northern part 
of Rådvad, near the bronze workshop (schematic overview of the site and expected groundwater flow 
in Figure 2). Between the two streams, the flow conditions in the aquifer are more complex, with no 
clear direction of the flow (NIRAS 2012; Lemaire 2016). 
 
3.3. Stream water and groundwater contamination 
 
In the past years, different investigations  have discovered both groundwater and stream water 
contamination due to chlorinated solvents and petroleum products, exceeding the Environmental 
Protection Agency standards (Lemaire 2016; NIRAS 2012; ORBICON 2016). The main source of 
contamination was identified in the northern area (Hotspot 1 in Figure 2). Another minor source was 
found close to the junction of the two streams (Hotspot 2), where two waste tanks had been buried, 
which caused pore air and water contamination. High concentrations of chlorinated compounds have 
also been detected in the stream, close to the wall dividing the northern bank and the Mølleåen stream 
shown in Figure 2. Contaminated groundwater has been infiltrating into the stream directly from the 
streambed through possible contact zones between the upper aquifer and the stream or through 
unexpected pathways such as a crack in the wall and a sewerage drain discharging directly to the stream 
(detailed photos in Figure B1 and B2 in Appendix B).

4. Investigation approach and methods 
 
The information gained from the previous studies and from the literature review helped to build a 
conceptual model of the overall system, plan the field and laboratory campaigns and finally assess the 
risk to contamination. This chapter describes: 1) the approach used to understand the processes 
affecting the remobilisation of heavy metals in Rådvad contaminated site, 2) the field investigation and 
3) the methods for laboratory analysis used to quantify the degree of pollution.  
 
It has to be highlighted that, even though soil contamination was detected pretty much throughout the 
entire site, this investigation focused especially on the northern part, close to the bridge. The reasons 
for this are that firstly, most of the contact zones found from the previous studies are located in the 
northern area, and secondly, groundwater boreholes are located close to the northern stream.

sources

It is relevant to describe and analyse the possible sources and pathways of contamination from the 
information of the previous studies and the characteristics of the site. Figure 5 shows a conceptual 
model for the northern area of Rådvad, representing cross-section AA’ in Figure 2. Three main sources 
were identified: 
 
1. Soil: from the results obtained by NIRAS, it is clear that polluted soil in Rådvad is the major 
source of contamination for the other resources (stream water, groundwater, sediments). Heavy 
metals can leach from soils and spread in the groundwater, contaminating the stream via the 
contact zones. Surface run off could also transport heavy metals to the stream. Leached heavy 
metals could pass through not clearly visible paths, such as the drain and cracks in the wall 
(Figure B1 and B2 in Appendix B). 
 
2. Natural (background) source: another important source of contamination may not be related to 
human activities at Rådvad. It is therefore very important to assess the heavy metal 
concentrations in stream water, groundwater and sediments taking into account the background 
concentrations, in order to understand and exactly characterize the anthropogenic contribution. 
 
3. Waste disposal: heavy metals could have been discharged in the stream long ago and still be 
retained in the sediment matrix. The sediments could be an important sink for heavy metals, 
but at the same time represent a significant source. Contaminants could be remobilized due to 
changing environmental conditions in the sediments and resuspension with an increase of 
stream flow velocity or upward flow from the shallow aquifer to the stream.

4.2. Possible scenarios for the interaction between soil, 
groundwater and surface water 
 
Soil analysis performed by NIRAS gave information regarding the total concentration of heavy metals 
in soil, while only the potentially soluble fractions of metals determine the real mobility and 
bioavailability of these contaminants. Therefore, chemical partitioning is more useful in the geo-
environmental studies of risk assessment (Lu et al. 2003; Wang et al. 2009). 
 
Due to the soil characteristics (gyttja/peat), organic matter is going to be the predominant reactive 
surface binding heavy metals. This is because near the knife factory, groundwater pH was found to be 
between 6,8 and 7,7 at 1-4 m depth. Assuming that in the 1st meter (where NIRAS’ soil samples were 
taken) the pH is still between this range, heavy metals will predominantly bind to organic matter. If a 
high concentration of DOM is present, this could be the predominant complex found in solution 
subsequently leaching to the groundwater. Boyle & Fuller (1987) and Antoniadis & Alloway (2002) 
found that leaching of Zn, Ni and Cd was enhanced by elevated DOC. Garcés et al. (2008) found also a 
positive relationship between DOC and dissolved Cd, Zn and Cu , while a negative relation for Pb was 
found. Moreover, competition to sorption sites between heavy metals could increase the dissolved 
concentrations.  
 
From NIRAS (2012) and Lemaire (2016) investigations, the groundwater is characterized by iron-
sulfate reducing conditions. Once heavy metals leach into the superficial aquifer, the presence of strong 
reducing conditions would limit the solubility of heavy metals. In fact, even small quantities of sulphide 
react with heavy metals forming metal sulphides which precipitate. However, if high concentrations of 
==PAGE:27==
 
16 
Fe and Mn-oxides are present, reduction and solubilisation of these oxides could release Pb, Zn, Cd, Ni 
(Du Laing et al. 2009; Violante et al. 2010). 
 
If high concentrations of heavy metals are present in the aquifer, part of it could be discharged in the 
river, passing through groundwater discharge zones. Due to neutral range pH and aerobic conditions, 
precipitation and sorption to hydroxides will govern the fate of heavy metals. However, low 
electrostatic interactions, changing of redox conditions, pH and/or competition between heavy metals 
could lead to remobilization of dissolved heavy metals in Mølleåen stream.  
 
 
4.3. Investigation at Rådvad site: field campaign 
 
In this section the field and laboratory plan will be described. The previous studies at Rådvad site 
deeply investigated a variety of dangerous contaminants such as BTEX and Chlorinated Aliphatic 
Hydrocarbons (CAH) (Lemaire 2016; NIRAS 2012; ORBICON 2016). However, heavy metal 
contamination has only been investigated in the soil, without considering other possible affected 
resources. Therefore, the current investigation at Rådvad site analysed solely heavy metal 
contamination: in particular, As, Cr, Cu, Cd, Pb, Ni and Zn were studied, focusing on:  
 
1. Investigation of As, Cd, Cu, Cr, Zn, Ni, Pb concentrations in the bed sediments. 
2. Investigation of As, Cd, Cu, Cr, Zn, Ni, Pb dissolved concentrations in the hyporheic zone. 
3. Investigation of dissolved As, Cd, Cu, Cr, Zn, Ni and Pb in Mølleåen stream. 
4. Investigation of dissolved As, Cd, Cu, Cr, Zn, Ni and Pb in the secondary (upper) aquifer.

4.4. 
Sediment and water analysis 
 
The sediment laboratory analysis was divided into three steps. Firstly, after the collection, a 
characterization was performed in order to have a first sight of the sediments’ basic properties. This 
was done through a visual characterization after the collection of the sediments and the analysis of the 
fraction of organic carbon (foc) present at each sampling point. Then, the total concentration and 
chemical partitioning of heavy metals were investigated, in order to estimate the pollution status and 
(bio)availability of trace metals. This was done since the total concentration of heavy metals provides 
an estimation of the pollution status of the streambed sediments, but cannot provide a sufficient 
indicator to assess sediment contamination since the mobility and availability of heavy metals depends 
mostly on the physico-chemical form more than the total concentration. Therefore, in the third step 
and through a sequential extraction, it is possible to evaluate in which form the metals are found, thus 
helping to detect natural processes that could immobilize or dissolve hazardous substances and support 
in assessing the risk of pollution (Rinklebe & Shaheen 2014; Baran & Tarnawski 2015; Li et al. 2015; 
Wu et al. 2016; Wang et al. 2012) 
 
4.4.1. Preparation of the sediments 
 
The sediments collected in Rådvad had to be prepared for the sediment digestion procedure. A 
complete image sequence of the steps followed in the laboratory to prepare the samples is shown in 
Figure 11. The day after the collection of the sediments, the first step was to pull out the solid part 
from the plastic tube storing the samples. Before proceeding, every sample was visually characterized in 
order to document the different layers composing the sediments. The length of the sediments extracted 
was measured using a ruler, as shown in Figure 11a. The tool in Figure 11b and c was used to pull out 
the sediments from the case, dividing the core into three sub-layers representing the surface (upper) 
sediments (0-10cm), the mid-sediments (10-20cm) and the bottom sediments (20-30cm). 
 
Once extracted, only a little part of each layer (~100g) was used for the laboratory analysis. The 
sediments of each layer were first well mixed and then dried for 3 days at 60 °C (Figure 11d-e). Then, 
==PAGE:33==
 
22 
each sample was sieved with a 2 mm grid in order to remove grain sizes bigger than 2 mm (Figure 11f-
g). Before proceeding with the metal extraction, the sediments needed to be grinded in the Vibratory 
Disc Mill (VDM) showed in Figure 11h. Every tool was thoroughly cleaned using first a vacuum 
cleaner and then an air compressor in order to prevent cross-contamination. Attention was made in 
order to avoid dust during the laboratory operations. For sample locations S5 and S6 it was necessary 
to grind the sediment by hand using the pestle shown in Figure 11i, since there wasn’t enough sample 
left due to high content of water evaporated during the drying process. As a last step, the new vials 
containing the powdered sediments were dried again for one night at 60 °C. After cooling, the 
sediments were ready for digestion.

4.4.4. Analysis of NVOC content, redox indicator species (in water) and foc 
in sediments 
 
Sediment and water samples were analysed more deeply: the content of organic carbon (foc) was 
analysed in the sediments while the redox indicator species and the non volatile organic carbon 
(NVOC) content were measured in the water samples.  
 
The fraction of organic carbon (foc) in the sediments was analysed through the LECO Induction 
Furnace CS-200. The LECO Instruction Manual (LECO 2014) was followed to obtain reliable and 
accurate results. The principle of the analysis consists on calculating the difference between measured 
total carbon and inorganic carbon (foc= TC-IC). Since only organic carbon was important for the 
analysis, the inorganic carbon (IC) was firstly removed from the sample through the addition of 
phosphoric acid, which is able to convert IC to CO2 and volatilize. After adding Sodium Persulfate as 
oxidation agent, the sample was then heated in a high-frequency induction furnace (850 °C) to convert 
the remained foc to CO2, which was detected by the Non Dispersive Infrared Sensor (NDIR). The 
peak area of the carbon dioxide was then converted to foc concentrations using a calibration curve. 
 
==PAGE:38==
 
27 
Dissolved organic matter (DOM) was estimated by measuring the NVOC content in the water samples. 
The measurement of NVOC is an indirect method to calculate the dissolved organic matter involved in 
the mobilization and complexation with pollutants. The NVOC analysis principle refers only to the 
high molecular weight compounds such humic acids, avoiding the detection of Volatile Organic 
Compounds (VOC) (e.g. benzene or toluene). The applied procedure is similar to the measurement of 
foc content for solids. Phosphoric acid was added first to the water sample, before stripping for 5 
minutes the generated Carbon dioxide using nitrogen gas. This first step eliminated the inorganic 
carbon present in the sample. Then, Sodium Persulfate was added as oxidant and the organic carbon 
converted into carbon dioxide, which was measured by an infra-red detector using the auto sampler 
ASI-V (DTU 2009).  
 
Finally, Redox indicator species were measured from the collected water samples through the ICS—
5000 Conductivity detector. The ions detected are: chloride, bromide, fluoride, nitrate, nitrite, 
phosphate and sulfate.  It was used for the analysis of chloride, nitrite-N and sulphate-S concentrations 
in water, which could help to understand the overall redox potential. The principal behind the ion-
chromatography (IC) is the detection of suppressed conductivity of water samples under isocratic 
conditions using carbonate and bicarbonate eluents system. Separation of ions is performed on an 
anion-exchange column through injection of 45 ml of 0,1M NaHCO3 and 14 ml of 0,1M Na2CO3. The 
injection is maintained constant at 1,2 ml/min, with a constant temperature of 20 degrees and a 
running time of 13 minutes per sample.

6. Discussion 
 
This paragraph focuses on describing and discussing the results, which were treated and assessed to 
obtain a complete picture of the dynamics between the compartments, with the aim of defining an 
updated conceptual model and assess the risk related to heavy metal contamination at Raadvad site. 
 
6.1.

This reflects also on the soil contamination: K2, (area above well 202, 203 and 204) exceeds the cut off 
criteria only for Zn. Table 16 shows the comparison between soil and groundwater contamination. The 
red cells indicate concentrations above the soil quality criteria or the groundwater quality guideline.

Through a comparison between soil and groundwater, it is noticed that the area K1 and the part of the 
upper aquifer beneath that is the most relevant in terms of contamination. Concentrations of sulfate 
found in well 201 were higher than in the other wells: this could indicate low concentrations of 
sulphide. Sulphide is considered as one of the major responsible of heavy metal immobilization (Holm 
& Christensen 1997), thus, low concentrations of sulphide could have contributed to the mobilization 
of heavy metals, leading to higher concentrations in W201 compared to the others.  
 
 
6.2. 
Hyporheic zone, sediments and stream water 
 
By analysing the hyporheic zone concentrations, no clear correlation could be found between the 
concentrations in groundwater and the ones in the hyporheic zone. As, Ni, Cd, Cu, Cr and Pb 
concentrations were below the stream water guideline values at every sampling point, and lower than 
the high concentrations found in well 201. Hyporheic zone results were close to the pattern found in 
the stream water, meaning that the hyporheic zone was more similar to the stream water compared to 
the polluted groundwater detected in W201. From these results, it seems that the groundwater did not 
pollute the hyporheic zone of the stream. 
 
Stream water concentrations varied depending on the sampling location. SW0 and SWBG provided the 
background concentrations in the stream water, since the values were below the guideline criteria and 
similar to the average background concentrations found in Danish streams. Once the Mølleåen enters 
the contaminated site (after the bridge), Zn and Cu water concentrations increased drastically, especially 
Zn. The concentrations of Zn were exceeding the stream water guideline value in every point 
investigated (except SW*1). This clearly shows that Rådvad’s past industrial activities are the cause of 
heavy metal contamination. It has to be highlighted that high concentrations of zinc were also found 
after the confluence, 100 m downstream the contaminated site. 
 
Different studies reported stream water quality affected by Zn contamination: high concentrations of 
Zn were found by Jiang et al. (2013) in the Xiawangang River in China, close to a smelting industrial 
base.  Mayes et al. (2010) and Gozzard et al. (2011) reported that zinc is the most frequent heavy metal 
polluting rivers and lakes in mining areas in the UK. Same results have been reported by Shikazono et 
al. (2008) close to one of the oldest zinc-lead mining areas in Japan. 
 
==PAGE:53==
 
42 
From the results obtained by the sediment total extraction, it is possible to have a first overview of the 
streambed sediment contamination. By combining the sediment total concentrations and the respective 
layer, it is clear that higher concentrations of heavy metals are found in the upper layer (0-12 cm). This 
indicates that probably surface run off was also contributing to the contamination of the sediments. 
However, the high concentrations found in S2 and S3 could be only partially explained by the run off 
or by the interaction between contaminated groundwater and stream water. Cr and Ni concentrations 
in S2 and S3 were much higher than the soil concentrations detected in the northern area (K1, K2, K3, 
K4) and in well 201. Therefore, other sources may be involved in the contamination of the sediments. 
Past dumping of metal waste or construction/demolition waste could explain the completely different 
and clearly higher concentrations found in S2 and S3. Moreover, it seems to have also a correlation 
between fraction of organic carbon and concentrations of heavy metals: close to the wall, S2 and S3 
have the highest content of organic carbon compared to S1, S4 and S5. The higher the organic carbon 
content, the higher the heavy metal concentrations (relation between sediment heavy metal 
concentrations and organic carbon content showed in Appendix H). These results confirm the 
important role of solid organic matter as a heavy metal binding phase (Dijkstra et al. 2004).  
 
6.3. 
Chemical fractionation of the sediments 
 
The chemical fractionation of heavy metals can provide an important insight of the potential 
(bio)availability of heavy metals. Zn and Cd were found to be the most mobile heavy metals, while Cr 
and As the least mobile. Results explain partially the concentrations found in the river. Stream water 
was found to be heavily contaminated primarily by Zn. The high proportion of the acid soluble and 
exchangeable fractions to which zinc is bound can explain the high mobility and concentrations of zinc 
in Mølleåen stream. Zinc association with the most easily available fraction implies that it is more 
difficult to avoid dispersion and therefore the contamination of stream water. Cd was distributed 
similarly to Zn, with high proportions in the acid soluble/exchangeable and reducible fraction. 
However, differently from Zn, the concentrations in the sediments were 3 orders of magnitude lower. 
Once dissolved in the stream, the dispersion would dilute the concentrations, resulting in very low 
concentrations also in the stream water. Ni, As and Cr were mainly associated with the residual 
fraction, indicating low mobility. This explains why the stream water is not contaminated by As, Cr and 
Ni, which is positive from a risk assessment point of view, since it implies that the high concentrations 
of Cr found in sediments S2 and S3 are not going to pollute the river as Cr is bound to the crystal 
structure of the sediments and not available. Cu is mostly present in the oxidizable fraction, bound to 
organic matter and sulphide. This implies that it can potentially be remobilized in case of re-
oxygenation of sediments. The reducible fraction, consisting of FeIII and MnIV (hydr)oxides is the 
major host of Pb. Under reducing conditions, lead could be released as a trace metal. 
 
Similar to the results of the current study, Wu et al. (2016) found in the streambed sediments of 
Dongbao River high proportions of Cd and Zn associated with the acid soluble/exchangable fraction 
while Cr mostly associated with the residual fraction. However, Ni and Pb were predominantly 
associated with acid soluble and the residual fraction respectively, which is contradictory to the current 
study. Cd and Zn high portion in the exchangeable/acid soluble fraction was also confirmed by Baran 
& Tarnawski (2015). Wang et al. (2012) and Zhang et al. (2017) also documented how cadmium is 
predominantly bound to the most easily available fraction.

stream. Run off could be another pathway of contamination, responsible of higher concentrations in 
the upper 10 cm streambed sediments compared to the deeper layers.

7. Risk Assessment 
 
The investigation carried out at Rådvad contaminated site showed high concentrations of heavy metals 
in the environment. This chapter focuses on evaluating the overall risk related to soil, groundwater, 
stream water and sediment contamination.  
 
Soil contamination poses a major risk of exposure over the entire area. Heavy metals present in soils in 
high concentrations can be directly ingested or inhaled by humans, accumulating, and finally causing 
different health problems (Wang et al. 2009; EPA 2007; Nriagu & Pacyna 1988; Tchounwou et al. 
2012). Concentrations in the soil exceeded the cut off criteria for one or more heavy metals in one or 
both investigated depths. Dividing the area in 3 parts, the assessment can be summarized as follows: 
 
• Area south of the southern stream: in 5 of the 9 sampling areas, concentrations exceeded the 
cut-off for one or several metals in one or both sampling depths. The area is primarily used for 
private gardens, which indicates the possibility of cultivating food crops for private use. An 
accumulation of heavy metals in soils of private gardens could result in uptake by food crops 
and consequently being a threat for the inhabitants. 
 
• Area north of the northern stream: In 4 of the 5 sampling areas, concentrations exceeded the 
cut-off for one or several metals in one or both sampling depths. Concentrations of lead, nickel, 
zinc were exceeding the cut off criteria by a factor of 2, 1,2 and 1,5 respectively.  There is risk of 
exposure since in this area it is present a Naturskole, which is daily attended by young children 
of primary schools. 
 
• Area between the southern and northern streams: 9 of the 12 sampling areas exceeded the cut-
off for one or several metals in one or both sampling depths. Cadmium, chromium, copper, 
lead, nickel and zinc exceeded the cut off criteria by a factor of 5, 3, 4, 8, 6, 33.  Today this area 
is mainly used for commercial purposes, therefore it seems that this area poses less risk 
compared to the other two previously described. 
 
However, it has to be taken into account that the investigation carried out by NIRAS focused only on 
heavy metal total concentrations. This information is insufficient for an adequate risk assessment 
(Baran & Tarnawski 2015; Wu et al. 2016; Thomas et al. 1994). Thus, a sequential extraction could help 
understanding the geochemical behaviour and mobility of heavy metals. 
 
Groundwater was also found contaminated by heavy metals. Zn, Ni and Pb were exceeding the 
groundwater quality criteria in well 201, while Zn exceeded the guideline value in every investigated 
well. Rådvad is located in an area characterized by special drinking water interests (NIRAS 2012). 
However, the upward flow from the deeper aquifer to the upper aquifer detected and reported by 
NIRAS (2012) would reduce the downward migration of the leachate to the deeper aquifer. Therefore, 
it seems that the heavy metal contamination in the shallow aquifer poses less risk for human health 
compared to the other contaminations. 
 
==PAGE:57==
 
46 
Stream water concentrations of Zn were exceeding the guideline value once the stream enters the 
contaminated area. Cu was also exceeding the maximum allowable concentration in SW2. Results 
showed that the environmental quality objectives cannot be reached. As a consequence, the 
contamination poses a risk to the aquatic ecological system. In terms of threat to human health, there is 
no risk of exposure due to the absence of water activities (canoeing and kayaking) in this area. 
However, high concentrations of Zn were also found after the confluence, therefore it should be 
highlighted that the downstream extent of heavy metal contamination in Mølleåen stream was not 
investigated.  
 
Streambed sediments can pose a high ecological risk due to possible remobilization if the exchangeable 
and acid soluble fraction is the major fraction that binds heavy metals. To evaluate the risk of 
remobilization, the Risk Assessment Code (RAC) was used as an index to evaluate the ecological risk 
associated to the concentrations of heavy metals in sediments (Zhang et al. 2017). RAC was calculated 
as the ratio between the heavy metal concentration in the acid soluble/exchangeable fraction and the 
sum of all fractions. Figure 23 shows the RAC values for Zn, Cd, Ni and Pb in the sediment layers of 
each investigated point. The RAC results for As, Cr and Cu are showed in Appendix I. The index was 
divided into five classes, as described in Zhang et al. (2017) or Baran & Tarnawski (2015): RAC< 1%, 
no risk; 1%<RAC<10%, low risk; 11%<RAC<30%, medium risk; 31%<RAC<50% high risk; 
RAC>50% very high risk.  
 
The RAC results showed that zinc and cadmium pose the highest ecological risk, with higher RAC 
values in the second layer (~10-20 cm) compared to the uppermost layer (~0-10 cm) for almost every 
investigated point. This is in contradiction with the previous total concentration results, which showed 
higher concentrations in the uppermost layer. However, it has to be highlighted that high 
concentrations do not mean high risk, as the ecological risk is determined by the portion of acid 
soluble/exchangeable fraction compared to the total concentration. Cr for example had the highest 
total concentration detected in Rådvad (Table 15), but mostly retained in the crystal structure of the 
sediments, which is not available and therefore not posing an ecological risk. S1 posed very high 
ecological risk for cadmium, with RAC values exceeding 50% for both layers. Nickel exhibited low to 
medium ecological risk, besides S3-2 which posed high ecological risk. No significant risk was observed 
for Pb, except for S4-1 and S5-2 where the ecological risk was found to be high. The RAC values for 
As, Cr and Cu (Appendix I) showed that the streambed sediments of Mølleåen did not pose a 
significant risk, with RAC values always below 15%.  
 
After analysing one by one the contaminated compartments, the classification of the resources ordered 
according to the risk they pose was defined as:  1) soil contamination, 2) sediment contamination,  
3) stream water contamination, 4) groundwater contamination.

8. Conclusions 
 
 
The main objectives of the present study were to understand the contamination patterns and potential 
interactions between the compartments in order to evaluate the contamination through an integrated 
risk assessment at Rådvad, a former industrial area that manufactured and processed metals in the past 
two centuries. Due to the heterogeneous contamination, different investigations have been made to 
characterize CAH and BTEX contamination in groundwater and stream water, as well as heavy metals 
in the soil. Regarding the latter, no investigations have analysed the groundwater, stream water and 
streambed sediments. Therefore, the current investigation at Rådvad site analysed As, Cr, Cu, Cd, Pb, 
Ni and Zn for these compartments. A conceptual model describing the possible interactions was firstly 
built, and afterwards the upper aquifer (flowing beneath the contaminated soil), the water of Mølleåen 
stream, the hyporheic zone and the streambed sediments were sampled. The study focused 
predominantly on the northern area of the site, as most of the contact zones between groundwater and 
Mølleåen stream found in the previous studies were located there. After collecting the samples and 
treating the data, it can be concluded that all the investigated resources are affected by heavy metal 
contamination related to the former industrial activities.  
 
The groundwater was found heavily contaminated by Zn, Ni and Pb probably due to leaching of the 
contaminants from the soil beneath the portion of aquifer investigated. This conclusion was deduced 
from the similar contamination found in the wells and in the soil above the sampling locations. The 
reducing conditions of the aquifer could have increased the mobility of heavy metals, leading to 
dissolved Zn, Ni and Pb concentrations exceeding the respective groundwater quality criteria. These 
values were compared with the typical average groundwater concentrations found in Danish aquifers, 
leading to the conclusion that the actual concentrations were much higher compared to the background 
levels of Danish aquifers. Even if Rådvad area is characterized by special water interests, the upward 
flow from the deeper aquifer to the upper aquifer detected and reported by NIRAS (2012) would 
reduce the downward migration of the leachate to the deeper aquifer, which has to be protected from 
pollution as it is used for drinking water extraction. Therefore, it seems that the heavy metal 
contamination in the shallow aquifer poses low risk for human health. 
 
The stream water quality was found to be affected by high Zn concentrations, exceeding the maximum 
allowable level in almost every investigated point. Cu concentrations were also exceeding or close to the 
corresponding guideline value in some investigated points. Two upstream points were also sampled to 
characterize the background concentrations of the stream, and it can be concluded that the main cause 
of the high heavy metal concentrations at Rådvad contaminated site are related to the past industrial 
activities. The stream water is affecting the ecological status of the stream, posing a risk to the aquatic 
ecosystem. 
 
By analyzing the hyporheic zone, which is the interface between the shallow aquifer and the stream, the 
redox conditions were similar to the groundwater (low dissolved oxygen, high dissolved Fe), but the 
heavy metal concentration pattern was comparable to the stream water conditions with high 
concentrations of Zn present in the hyporheic zone water. It can be concluded that the groundwater is 
not the primary source of heavy metals to the hyporheic zone. 
==PAGE:60==
 
49 
The sediments were extracted in the same points where the hyporheic zone was sampled. From a first 
analysis of the total heavy metal concentrations, it can be concluded that every investigated point was 
highly contaminated by one or more heavy metals. The top sediments were more contaminated than 
the bottom sediments, indicating that run-off could be another relevant contamination pathway. 
However, soil contamination alone cannot explain the extremely high streambed sediment 
concentrations of some heavy metals (especially Cr) in a specific area of the stream close to the wall, 
which indicated probable past disposal of metal or construction/demolition waste. 
 
 A deeper investigation was performed on the sediments, as the heavy metals chemical partitioning was 
studied for each sampling point to have a better insight of the potential (bio)availability. Using the Risk 
Assessment Code (RAC), it can be concluded that Zn and Cd pose high ecological risk, as they are 
mostly associated with the acid soluble/exchangeable fraction, which is considered to be the most 
easily available fraction. This could explain the high concentrations of Zn in the stream water. Cd had 
low concentrations in the stream probably because of dilution process.  Even if high concentrations of 
Cr were detected in the sediments, it does not pose an ecological risk since it was mainly bound to the 
crystal structure of the sediments, and consequently not available. The same conclusion was obtained 
from the analysis of As and Ni fractionation. Cu was predominantly bound to the oxidizable fraction 
and the high concentrations in the stream in some points could be explained by the oxidation of sulfide 
or degradation of organic matter which was binding the heavy metals to the sediments. Even if in two 
points the acid soluble/exchangeable portion of Pb was high, this heavy metal was mainly associated 
with the reducible fraction (bound to Fe and Mn oxides), and thus not particularly available under the 
current environmental conditions. 
 
The investigation carried out in the current Master Thesis helped to delineate a complete picture of the 
system characterizing Rådvad site. Finally, after the deep investigation performed in Rådvad and the 
integrated risk assessment of all the compartments involved in the heavy metal fate and distribution, a 
risk classification was possible. The classification of the resources ordered according to the risk they 
pose was defined as:  1) soil contamination, 2) sediment contamination, 3) stream water contamination 
and 4) groundwater contamination. Soil heavy metal contamination can be evaluated as the most 
relevant threat to human health at Rådvad site, since the risk of exposure to heavy metals is very high in 
the northern and southern part of the area, where a Naturskole and residential houses have been built 
to reclaim the area after the former industrial activities.  
 
From the current study, it was possible to understand the importance of studying contaminations at a 
larger scale, trying to build conceptual models that describe the overall system, as pollutants can spread 
and contaminate other compartments such as sediments, stream water or aquifers. The fate and 
transport of heavy metals are challenging topics that depend on a variety of factors and conditions such 
as kinetics, pH, soil composition and redox conditions. For this reason, indirect and integrated methods 
to determine their distribution and possible mobility need to be implemented to understand the risk 
and suggest possible remediation plans in case the contamination threats the ecological status or human 
health.

9. Future outlook and recommendations 
 
The following suggestions are provided to help improve the knowledge and the understanding of 
Rådvad’s complex system, besides giving recommendations to protect human health from heavy metal 
contamination: 
 
- 
The stream water background concentrations were deduced from the results obtained in the 
sampling points SWBG and SW0, which highlighted the responsibility of the past working 
activities of Rådvad to the current environmental contamination. However, no investigation 
was performed to understand the extent of downstream zinc contamination, which has to be 
taken into account also from a risk assessment point of view. The aim should be to get an 
overview of other possible sources of contamination (natural or anthropogenic) after the 
confluence and understand if the contamination extends up to the mouth of the stream. 
 
- 
The groundwater heavy metal concentrations found in well 201 didn’t impact the hyporheic 
zone or the stream water quality: no clear information was obtained about where the 
contaminated groundwater coming from well 201 is flowing and if other contact points are 
present in the stream. Consequently, more investigations should be performed between 
sampling points S1 and S2, since the polluted groundwater found in well 201 could interact 
with the hyporheic zone in this specific area, affecting its quality. 
 
- 
The soil investigation performed by NIRAS in 2012 focused only on the heavy metals’ total 
concentration. However, the bioavailability and mobilization of heavy metals depend on the 
chemical fraction they are bound to. Thus, it is extremely important to assess the fractionation 
of heavy metals in Rådvad soil as already done for the sediments in the current study. 
 
- 
After the risk assessment developed in the current study, the following task should be to 
develop a remediation plan to protect human health from exposure to heavy metal 
contamination. Currently, the investigated area is used for different recreational (Naturskole, 
private gardens) and working activities without any restriction or precautions. This could 
threaten the health of children regularly attending the Naturskole or the health of the people 
living in the residential houses in the area southern the south stream.  
 
- 
The results from stream water showed that zinc contamination is present also in the southern 
stream. Even if it is clear from the concentrations detected that the level of contamination is 
lower here than in the northern stream, it would be useful to investigate more deeply the 
streambed sediments of the southern river and sample the stream water at other points, as past 
dumping and water contamination cannot be excluded in other parts of the stream.
