Preface
This dissertation presents the dilemma of establishing on-shore wind turbines in a industry
were objections is rising. To fulﬁlment the ﬁnal requirements of the Master degree within
Industrial Engineering and Management at Danish Technical University concludes with
this report. The investigated topic was chosen in collaboration with Kristian Borch and
afterwards speciﬁed and adjusted by the project’s author.
Through this study a consultant role has been taken for the organisation Strategic Energy
and Planning (SEP) to analyze the current business situation, in order to further generate
beneﬁcial improvements for local development of on-shore wind turbines. The empirical
data has formed the basis for a real life case study and have been used to conduct a
comprehensive discussion of how technology co-design can be used to develop on-shore
wind turbine cases.
The gathering of data in this dissertation is chosen to be a qualitative study of participants
and further hearing statements from the case of Haved.

Abstract
Strategy in beneﬁtting from technology co-design through wind power pro-
jects: A case study of the associated dilemmas of constructing on-shore wind turbines
due to rising protests from citizens.
Abstract: On-shore wind turbines generate disorder such as; noise, shadow, and visual
disruptions, and value simultaneously when producing energy. These aspects are tied to
the surroundings of a wind turbine, or at least one would think. Unfortunately, this is
not always the case, when examine associated values. As the renewable energy sector
grows, it can become a challenge that the value is created but disappears out of the area,
of which the surrounding society doesn’t receive the beneﬁt. This dissertation uses the
theory of technology co-design to elucidate, how this can minimise some of the challenges
associated with establishment of wind turbine projects. Technology co-design encompasses
external systems such as social and technological subsystems, mainly to work out a joint
solution.
For the theory to be able to deal with the pressure of the energy sector, a
holistic approach1 is used to verify, that the theory has the necessary capacity to solve
these issues. As a result of this dissertation, recommendations for future changes within
planning and scheduling, to optimise the chances of social acceptance of on-shore wind
turbines is investigated.
Keywords: Technology co-design; Sociotechnical system theory; Energy policy; Co-
creation; on-shore wind; local growth

Chapter 1
Introduction
The implementation of technologies in peoples everyday have never been plug and play,
project developers encounter a lot of social resistance, which need to be minimised to
ensure a sustainable way of establishing on-shore wind turbines. Through the 21st cen-
tury the worlds energy consumption has been growing rapidly, resulting in a demand for
increased energy production (Lime Marques, 2017). This demand alongside with policies
and green visions are creating pressure on developing renewable and sustainable energy
sources, and innovative technologies and appropriate usage. Denmark has a vision of a
fossil-free energy production by 2050 as elaborated in Energistyrelsen (2018); however,
this vision has met several obstacles.
From the goal of zero impact, we know that Denmark are heavily in need of renewable
technologies to increase the renewable energy production capacity (Energistyrelsen, 2018).
The challenge is to produce this at the lowest price possible, where on-shore turbines are
dominating the industry when it comes to levelized cost of energy (LCOE) (Breum, 2019,
World, 2019).
Through the rising demand of renewable energy, business opportunities appears and can
be used for local value creation of which rural areas can beneﬁt (OECD, 2012).
The
Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD, 2012) introduce us to
ﬁve1 possible types of value creation through renewable energy in rural areas, which show
how a wind turbine can beneﬁt a locality.
However, a well-known issue within the literature of social acceptance and real life cases
such as Vesterhav Syd or Haved respectively, show that resistance from citizens can lead
to change in or even abandonment. Due to previous cases and disorders associated to
wind turbines, citizens tend to be more aware of how to handle future projects, to avoid
such issues, which can compromises their living conditions. For example, the surrounding
community of the district’ Haved showed great concern, when a project proposal of 48
on-shore wind turbines was proposed in their neighbourhood (Tønder municipality, 2015).

Through this dissertation a look at the diﬀerent actions associated to the district’ Haved

this ﬁeld, the data collected is more reliably and comparable, when locating wind turbines
on local grounds.
To examine the actions within the case of Haved, and what informants believe should be
investigated in on-shore wind turbine projects, this dissertation apply analytical mech-
anisms proposed by Greenhalgh et al. (2014) and Wüstenhagen et al. (2007) to see if a
minimisation of social protest can be obtained. Greenhalgh is working within healthcare,
trying to formulate diﬀerent methods of establishing collaborative processes, however his
research are still highly relevant when the aim and perspective is changed towards wind
power, even when principles stay the same. Wüstenhagen’s(2007) thoughts work with
concepts of social acceptance and what to look fore when seeking accept at diﬀerent levels
in realising renewable energy projects. Through the analysis and ﬁndings thereof, an elab-
oration toward the case of Haved has been made (Tønder municipality, 2015), to give an
understanding of how challenges have or haven’t been addressed throughout the case.
The Haved project is large and based locally within southern Jutland in between Esbjerg
and Tønder municipality. This project relates to well-know issues and concepts elabor-
ated by Devine-Wright (2005, 2009) concerning location, privacy and disorders, which in
this world can be summarized as aesthetics or NIMBYism, which is known in the wind
industry as "Not In My BackYard". This concept often use the word resistance and so-
cial acceptance. To include these concepts, resistance and social acceptance is elaborated
through Devine-Wright (2005), Wüstenhagen et al. (2007) and Davis and Andrew (2017).
Over the past years, wind turbines have grown in size, and likewise in eﬃciency. The
increased size both in term of height and quantity have led to and reinforced resistance
against on-shore wind turbines. Most municipalities are still positive for on-shore con-
struction, due to their comparably low cost to other sources available (IRENA, 2018).
However, the resistance by local people have led some municipalities to plan their energy
strategy elsewhere than on-shore wind turbines, due to disorders associated. And exactly
resistance is acknowledged to be a major challenge and a main reason for non-technical
delays (Borch et al., 2014). Even though it is rarely seen, communities do have succeed
in changing or halting on-shore wind turbine projects(Vibjerg, 2019, Tønder municipality,

1.1
Scope
The future is set to be digital, thereby demanding an enormous amount of power which
is not yet at our disposal, at least not in the shape of renewables2. The goal of the green
transition in Denmark is to be CO2 neutral, which is promised by the government with
a transition to renewables before 2050 (Energistyrelsen, 2018). To accommodate these
goals Denmark needs to produce more renewable energy in the most eﬃcient way, due
to our geographical position, the most eﬃcient renewable energy sources is wind power,
which also happen to be the cheapest way of producing renewable energy due to LCOE
calculations (IRENA, 2018).
This dissertation will investigate how to enhance social accept of on-shore wind turbines
through the theory of technology co-design (Greenhalgh et al., 2014). This study will
focus only on wind energy even though there are other energy sources which could help
produce the amount of energy needed3.
The investigation will be set in Denmark, more speciﬁcally the project in Haved and muni-
cipalities hosting data centres4 in southern jutland. Most of these areas have a favourable
location for wind turbines(Sol&Vind) as well as cheap land (Finanstilsynet, 2019). This
dissertation has gathered information from participants working at the organization of
Strategic Energy and Planning (SEP Sønderjylland) associated with southern Denmark,
and has the current challenge of making this region more energy eﬃciency and increase
their renewable-percentage5.

1.1.1
Problem statement
In the context of the green transition and associated on-shore wind turbine projects, act
of resistance have been a part of earlier power plant development, which has shown to
have a major impact on the ﬁnal outcome. To better understand where local resistance is
generated and how to promote future on-shore wind turbines, this dissertation will look
at the following problem statement;
How can we use the knowledge of technology co-design, to better understand and develop
on-shore wind turbine projects?
1.1.2
Research questions
To work out a complete solution to the problem formulation, this paper will elaborate
on a set of research questions to clarify the understanding and further generating a com-
prehensive solution with backing from each research question, which will be answered
throughout the project.
• What role does social acceptance have in wind turbine projects?
• How can co-creation be used to enhance social acceptance in renewable energy pro-
jects?
• Which opportunities are there to generate local value through renewables
1.2
Structure
This dissertation starts with an introduction[1] of the investigated ﬁeld, which sets the
basis for choices and considerations during the empirical research. Secondly, the analysis[2]
presents and frame the theoretical parts of this dissertation, with the aim of using this
later in a combined case and discussion section. Chapter [3] elaborate the considerations
of this dissertation, by looking at both strengths, weakness and opportunities, and at the
same time also describe the overall working process in a chronological order. Through
the fourth chapter [4] a combined case study and discussion is carried out. This is done
by presenting results, discussing them against each other at the analytical framework, to
last being able to present ﬁnal results and recommendations. At the end a conclusion [5]
is presented to evaluate the used tools in this dissertation, namely the tool of technology
co-design.

2.1
Social acceptance
When considering the governments target of zero impact, Denmark need to increase the
share of renewable energy. Academic engagement eg. Devine-Wright (2005), Wüstenhagen
et al. (2007), Lantz and Flowers (2009) with the topic show that social acceptance is
increasingly recognized as a binding factor of achieving and establishing wind turbine
projects (Wüstenhagen et al., 2007). To achieve the goal of a transition to renewable
energy, it may be a necessity to pursue social acceptance within associated societies,
in three diﬀerent facets; socio-politics, community acceptance and market acceptance
(Wüstenhagen et al., 2007). These factors are essential for understanding the objections
of renewables and the diﬃculties of realizing projects.
To understand how social acceptance is a binding topic within the wind industry an
explanation1 of the three facets is investigated and shown in ﬁgure 2.1.

Socio-political accept, is acceptance on the most generalized level, where both policies
and technologies can be subject to social acceptance. Even though this topic is of great
concern, it is shown statistically that key stakeholders and citizens tend to support sugges-
tions for renewable energy, even if the governmental support is fairly low (Wüstenhagen
et al., 2007, Lantz and Flowers, 2009).
The positive vibe from the population have led policy-makers to conclude that social
acceptance is of no concern (Wüstenhagen et al., 2007).
However, when it goes from
national to local and from general support of technologies and policies to concrete projects,
investments and location; the term changes and there is clearly a challenge for social accept
of project realization (Wüstenhagen et al., 2007).
Community acceptance is that part of social acceptance, that deals with local resistance
to speciﬁc wind turbine projects, especially with citizens and local politicians. This refers
to speciﬁc acceptance of local projects, which is done by local stakeholders associated,
such as citizens and local authorities (Lantz and Flowers, 2009).
This level of social acceptance is often linked to the syndrome of NIMBYism2, where
diﬀerences between acceptance and resistance to local projects, can be explained by the
statement, that citizens in general support renewables as long as it won’t eﬀect their
everyday. The approval of wind projects in a community is quite important, since this is
required before any construction can commence. However, research has shown that wind
projects and social acceptance can’t be reduced to NIMBYism, because of a way more
2"Not In My Back Yard" syndrome, which occurs when decisions is coming from a top-down perspective,
and when people are less open minded.

Market acceptance is another part of social acceptance and can be interpreted as market
adoption of technology and innovation by stakeholders. The market acceptance can to
some extent be compared with maturity, robustness and reliability of a certain technology.
These terms help utilities, developers and investors to observe the potential of a given

2.2.1
Technology co-design
The method of technology co-design originally derives from computer science and man-
agement studies, from the 50’s which were based on socio-technical system theory. Where
Greenhalgh et al. (2014) states that this theory propose that technologies are best designed
in collaboration using participatory methods, and were working to generate creative and
sustainable solutions within the public sector. The theory states that to achieve a joint
optimization (and thereby achieve the most objectives associated to a speciﬁc project)
the social and technical systems most work together as shown in ﬁgure 2.2 (Eason, 2008).
By working together some compromises must be taken to achieve a somewhat optimal
solution.
Figure 2.2: Sociotechnical systems theory (Eason, 2008)
Through the literature of social acceptance and hearings associated to the case of Haved,
we know that the most common topics open for objection is aesthetics and disorders
such as; location, noise, size, shadow, pattern of placement and quantities. Technology
co-design addresses these challenges with collaborative processes to acknowledge citizens
know-how about the area, and how they want technology to interfere with their surround-
ings (Devine-Wright, 2009). Real life projects developed with similar approach could be
"Middelgrunden" or "Østrup", where local commitment has proven to be a key factor;
where hearings were held to debate the visualization and environmental impacts, as a
part of a co-creation method. Through collaborative processes, aesthetics were changed
at Middelgrunden, such as the quantity went from 27 to 20 turbines and the design from
three rows to a single row with a curved line (Copenhagen Environment and Energy Of-
ﬁce, 2003). In the case of Østrup, citizens got the possibility to call and stop the turbines
if disorders becomes too much (Jammerbugt municipality, 2014).
The mentioned examples deﬁnitely show how technolgy co-design can help to implement
technology into citizens every day. Its all about making the best out of a given situation,
to generate a joint solution (Eason, 2008).

4.3
Worries of disorders
By looking into hearings from Haved, it is evident that the ﬁeld of aesthetics receive the
greatest attention, dealing with noise, shadow eﬀects, visual signiﬁcance and health risks
(Tønder municipality, 2015). However, through interviews of participants these topics did
not receive the same attention. It is interesting that two central players in the group of
stakeholders, can have so diﬀerently opinions at what is important.
This subject can sometimes be intangible because each individual has their own opinion,
meaning that you can never satisfy everyone (Devine-Wright, 2005). Due to a intangible
situation in the planning phase, a co-creation process could be suitable (Greenhalgh et al.,
2014). In the case of Haved, calculations of noise and shadows have showed that these
wind turbines adheres to all limit values. Citizens are not satisﬁed with calculations as
they want real life data to ensure that they won’t be bothered of these disorders (Tønder
municipality, 2015). One of the hearing statements, was to ensure that calculations were
with backing of real life cases; "It turns out that noise limits and distance from turbines to
the nearest house are all calculations and not concrete measurements."(Hearing; 8 citizens).
Similar situations arise under topics of shadow and visual disturbances, and is further
brought to how these courses can aﬀect health and living conditions. According to theory
of social acceptance, a look towards the content of community acceptance is needed, due
to a speciﬁc project proposal (section2.1). Wüstenhagen et al. (2007) argues that "trust is
a key issue in all facility siting issues and are always heavily loaded with risk components.".
Trust is an inherent need. Through empirical data, people acknowledge the importance
of being part of a project and the decision-making process, to enhance trust.
"I also
believe that the needs of the local community is there, not only to create resistance but
assistance. Land-based projects need to be developed with local people, who are going to

live with disorders associated." (Interview, Søren E.3). Søren thereby possess the same
attitude towards this challenge as citizens, bringing participants and citizens a little closer
together even though the topic of disorders is of only little interest according to interviewed
participants. Wüstenhagen et al. (2007) further states, that trust is something build over a
period of time resulting from openness, ﬂexibility and willingness to accommodate local’s
wish of being a part of the development. The term of trust is truly fragile, and years of
work towards establishing a favourable relation between stakeholders, can be shattered
immediately by one wrong decision (Wüstenhagen et al., 2007).
A part of trust is also to know when to make certain decisions, and learn to adapt to the
surrounding environments of citizens. As a project developer, an everyday task includes
transparency to minimise risk, when violating and involving citizens with many uncom-

not to just dismiss what citizens think is important just because they don’t know better,
project developers need to listen and try to understand why citizens do have complains
about these projects. This can be drown towards speciﬁc comments such as wind turbines
not being sustainable. Citizens with place attachment, will do their best to protect their
local area against unforeseen events from "outsiders", which can be summarised as place-
protective actions (Devine-Wright, 2009). Citizens and government oﬃcials are especially
concerned of the case Haved, due to size of this enormous wind farm, and that value
generated through renewable energy does not exceed the disturbances it creates. "The as-

Looking at other projects near communities, data show that there will always be some
associated challenges; however, the challenges can be minimized through collaborative
processes to meet a joint optimization between developer and concerned citizens (Eason,
2008). As the hearing goes on a statement is presented "No one should make any decision
of completing the project, before we can ensure that citizens can stay in their homes and
feel safe." (Hearing, 17 citizens), which turn the decision-making back to belief and trust
in the project developers.
To accommodate these challenges the theory of technology co-design can be of great and
creative use due to its ability to solve complex problems, such as implementing technologies

4.5.1
Recommendations
Through this dissertation and the use of technology co-design, to evaluate the case of
Haved and further to understand and develop the industry have proof eﬃcient. The proof
of eﬃciency of this concept is found within its ability of structuring and breaking down
complex projects and associated challenges. By breaking it down to smaller problems
the process become more transparent, making it more manageable especially for stake-
holder who are only engaged in some parts of a given project e.g. citizens who are mostly
worried about disorders. On basis of this analysis, it is therefore recommended to test
this approach in practise when going for on-shore wind turbine planning; due to its ver-
satile ability to accommodate both citizens, government oﬃcials and project developers.
Through this study my hypothesis is that if Haved had used more focus on a collaborat-
ive processes towards implementing technologies and humans together, the project would
have a better chance of realisation.
Moreover, it is recommended to look at when to include citizens. Through the literature
research of the ﬁeld, most cases include people late in the process. This limits the ability
of a co-design process, therefore other solutions should be investigated, to see if it would
be beneﬁcial to including citizens before usual, maybe involving citizens already in theme
and energy planning 4.1. The region of southern Denmark have lost many on-shore wind
turbines, as elaborated due to objections. It is therefore recommended for the organisation
SEP to include people already in the strategy phase, to minimise distrust and help a
collaborative process to work towards a joint optimisation.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
The energy sector is facing major changes with several challenges; the purpose of this
dissertation was to analyse some of the most frequent challenges associated to on-shore
wind turbine projects, and how important it is to understand these factors for future
projects to turn out successfully. This dissertation does not present the one and only
way to handle on-shore wind turbines, it suggests one way of approaching some of the
challenges associated to development of onshore wind capacity. On basis of the analysis,
following can be concluded.
This dissertation have been investigating on-shore wind turbines with the concept of tech-
nology co-design. This concept works with societies and technological implementing with
citizens every day.
Through this concept, I have connected the concept with theories
of social acceptance, sociotechnical theory and value creation, to suggest an optimized
solution to wind turbine planning.
As technology co-design is derived as a facet of co-creation, this approach work towards
collaborative processes to generate a joint optimisation of a wind turbine project. Optim-
isation is exactly what to look for, when trying to make something better. The approach
of technology co-design strives to incorporate all stakeholders associated to a speciﬁc case,
to get the actual picture of a current situation and whats about to happen, like in the case
of Haved where the next step where to accept or reject the project and thereby construct-
ing it. The most important aspect of technology co-design, is in fact to have involved
stakeholders to somehow achieve ideas of a democracy and thereby openness to collab-
oration to promote social acceptance and technological implementation. To enhance the
chance of social acceptance and involvement of citizens stand as key principles. Through
the process of technology co-design we move the actual decision-making process from an
oﬃce out to the reality where the challenges really are, and are thereby making sure to
avoid ivory tower statements. This approach can thereby help the industry of on-shore
wind turbines, to generate a joint optimisations, gathered through collaboration between
all aﬀected stakeholders opinions. When including all stakeholders, a holistic view of what
exactly the challenge is can be created, and thereby solved with the best possible solution,
to any given project to increase social acceptance.

The action of a collaborative process isn’t shown through the case of Haved, due to lack
of social acceptance. Lack of social acceptability will turn the responsibility back on those
who would like to implement a technological change to any society. This happened in
Haved, where citizens and government oﬃcials received a project proposal by outsiders,
who wanted to interfere with their surroundings. The case in Haved, truly showed how
important constructive communication and feedback are, if a such a big project is to
succeed. Vattenfall as project developer failed to involve citizens in the decision-making
when needed, and trust were thereby lost and was not regained. As a result, the case of
Haved was rejected and thereby abandoned.
On basis of this dissertation, it is argued that the approach of technology co-design is
a viable method of how to handle future on-shore wind turbine projects. If the green
transition is to succeed, backing from citizens and societies hosting renewable energy
plants is desired, to create a joint optimisation.
If the organisation of SEP and the politicians within want to be part of the green transition,
they should strive to incorporate new initiatives to enhance the will and desire to be a
part of the green transition and maybe involve citizens already in the process of energy
strategy or theme planning to enhance the opportunities of a collaborative process.
Throughout the work with this dissertation, it has become clear that there indeed is a
need for further research into associated topics, such as wind policies, planning practices,
citizens, compensation and implementation practices. These actions could create a unique
approach towards establishment of wind turbine projects, to avoid rejecting projects. It
is recommended to look at when to include citizens and what to include them in, in order
to involve citizens as early as possible to establish the best conditions for a co-creation
process. Therefore, it could be exciting to make further research on this topic in relation
to wind turbine projects. It could especially be interesting to see how much inﬂuence
citizens should have, before interest and recognition exceed the level of compensation and
green beneﬁts.
