Abstract
Calibrations of eye tracking devices often require the participant to complete a se-
quence of actions, following the presented instructions. This creates a personalized eye
model of the participant’s eyes, which is used to pinpoint subsequent gaze positions.
Infants are not likely to complete such calibrations, as they do not understand the
concept. Without calibrations, inaccurate readings of a participant’s eyes are bound
to ensue. Inaccurate gaze data makes it diﬃcult to discern whether a participant is
following displayed stimuli, complicating the evaluation of visual cognition.
This thesis presents the use of a default eye model, eliminating the need of a
calibration process. With an eye model no longer tailored to the individual, accuracy
of the gaze data is limited. To this end, the studies conducted herein explore methods
of minimizing these errors. Several approaches to cleaning up these types of errors
have been proposed, but do so on tractable participants and calibrated eye trackers.
This paper introduces methods of correcting accuracy errors, requiring no preceding
calibration step of the participant.
The correction algorithm, which was found to
perform the best on infants, uses an aﬃne transformation to reduce the angular o↵sets
of gaze data, improving the accuracy by 53.28% on average. Before correction, the
infant gaze data is observed to be between 2◦and 5◦of visual angle away from the
targets. The corrected gaze data demonstrates angular o↵sets between 0.5◦and 3◦of
visual angle.
Further presented in this paper is an approach to calibrating eye trackers so that
infant participants can establish a personalized eye model with much higher rates of
success. Two custom calibrations are built for this purpose: A faster 2-point calibra-
tion and a more accurate 5-point calibration. All infants were able to complete the
2-point calibration, whereas 71.43% completed the 5-point calibration. In comparison,
no infants were able to complete the native eye tracking calibration. The correction
approach resulted in accuracies comparable to those of the 2-point calibration. In en-
vironments where a calibration cannot be established, the correction approach proves
useful, yielding accurate results, allowing researchers to analyze and evaluate infant
gaze patterns.
Keywords: re-calibration, correction, eye tracking, infants, cerebral palsy,
attentiveness

Preface
This thesis is prepared under the Department of Applied Mathematics and Computer
Science at the Technical University of Denmark in the period from February 2019 to June
2019. The project is a collaboration between the authors Sebastian Nyholm and Lukas
Villumsen, and accounts for 30 ECTS points respectively - forming the basis of acquiring
an M.Sc. degree in Computer Science and Engineering.
The thesis studies the complications within eye tracking of infants, such that eye tracking
can become a useful tool in limiting the e↵ects of cerebral palsy. The accuracy of eye
tracking devices depends on a calibration, which is established by a sequence of instructions
and deliberate eye movements. Infants are not able to follow these instructions and as
a consequence, the accuracy of the eye tracking data will be erroneous.
To this end;
the thesis is about developing a correction algorithm, using a default eye model instead
of a calibration step, to transform and optimize the accuracy of eye tracking data for
infants. All ﬁgures used to describe the development and explanation of concepts, have
been derived from personal data collection if not otherwise speciﬁed.
The thesis consists of the following sections.
Sections 1 - 2 present the thesis, introducing cerebral palsy and why eye tracking on
infants are of interest in this regard. This also includes our objectives and goals and how
we plan to achieve them.
Sections 3 - 4 cover the development of the experiments. Here technical information
about the utilized equipment is described, as well as how to obtain an optimal environment
for eye tracking and what to be aware of. Likewise, details about what kind of calibrations
and exercises used, will be explained.
Sections 5 - 7 is about the collection and analysis of gaze data. The analysis demon-
strate how correction algorithms take shape, and follows an outlining of a ﬁltering- and
manipulation process of the data.
Sections 8 - 9 cover the results and revolves around all the experiments conducted
and the ﬁndings within. Results of the experiments, as well as alternative approaches,
are discussed and compared to gauge the e↵ects of the corrections, both for infants and
adults.
Sections 10 - 11 round up the thesis with concluding remarks and the potential for
further work in this area.
Contributions by This Thesis
This thesis contributes to knowledge in the following areas of research:
• In identifying visual perception patterns of young infants.
• Establishing calibration schemes which infants can complete (Section 4.3).
• Demonstrates the possibility of correcting gaze data of infants from an un-calibrated
eye tracker (Section 7).

Introduction
Cerebral Palsy (CP) is a permanent disorder caused by brain injury, limiting movements
and posture as the motor skills are reduced. CP is found in 2.1 per 1000 of all live births
(Oskoui et al., 2013) and is estimated to a↵ect 17 million people worldwide (Kerr, 1993).
As the disorder is permanent, no cure exists. However by early intervention, training the
motor skills, the limitations can be reduced.
For now, most diagnoses are often determined within the two ﬁrst years of age, but more
often to the end of that spectrum (MyChild, 2019a). Researchers at the Elsass Institute,
and others, are developing techniques for earlier diagnosis, which likewise puts a demand
on new training methods for earlier interventions.
The human visual system is one of the ﬁrst developed systems after birth (Gredeb¨ack
et al., 2010). Visual perception is therefore rapidly maturing in young infants, who re-
ceive information almost exclusively through visual cues. As CP causes reduced motor
skills (Dutton et al., 2006), which includes the oculomotor system, eye tracking is an
interesting ﬁeld of study in this regard.
Training infants using eye exercises allows for an early intervention. However, to be able
to train infants in a proper way, we need to know where they are looking, to ensure that
they beneﬁt from the exercise. Many years of continuous research in eye tracking has lead
to an accuracy of less than 1◦of visual angle. To acquire such accuracy, eye trackers need
to be calibrated, as no two eyes are identical. This raises a problem when talking about
infants, as infants are not likely to complete a calibration. Therefore, a new approach
for calibrating eye tracking equipment is needed, to provide a good accuracy for infants.
Otherwise it can become diﬃcult to determine the beneﬁt from using the eye exercises.
1.1
Problem Statement
Eye tracking of infants can be a diﬃcult task when trying to calibrate the system. Without
a calibration, the measured gaze points tend to be skewed and the accuracy low. The issue
that arises when trying to achieve a good accuracy of an infant’s gaze points, is that the
infant is not able to follow the instructions needed to calibrate the system. Therefore, the
calibration procedure cannot make a complete analysis of the eyes, and fails to construct
a personalized eye model.
The question to the problem is then; is it possible to use a default eye model, such that the
infants do not need to go through the calibration process, while retaining high accuracy
for the gaze data. Furthermore, whether is it possible to add an extra layer on top of the
default eye model, which analyzes the infants’ eyes as data become available, building up a
pseudo-calibration. I.e. an algorithm able to transform the raw gaze points into corrected
coordinates. Figure 1 illustrates the idea of this concept.
The main purpose of this study is to open up the possibility of using eye tracking to
evaluate infants’ ability to follow eye training exercises, without a lengthy calibration
process.

1.2
Neurological Complications
In order to better understand the relevance of this study, a short introduction to what CP
is and how it a↵ects people is presented.
1.2.1
What is Cerebral Palsy
CP is caused by brain injury or malformation that usually occurs under the development
of the brain, either during pregnancy, at birth, or shortly after. However, the cause is often
unknown. The disorder is often observed by noting disturbance within the perception-,
cognition-, communication-, and behaviour sensations, usually caused by epilepsy and sec-
ondary musculoskeletal problems (Rosenbaum et al., 2007). It can be diﬃcult to diagnose
a child whose symptoms are not visible. In cases with milder forms of CP, the symptoms
might ﬁrst be noticeable when the brain is fully developed, between the age of three to ﬁve
years (MyChild, 2019a). Certain risk factors show evidence of being strongly associated
with CP. Among these are premature birth, low birth weight, twins, placenta abnormali-
ties and tight nuchal cord. The research is however still limited and the inﬂuence by the
risk factors are not proven (Ramin and Gilstrap, 2000).
1.2.2
The Consequences of Cerebral Palsy
Consequences of CP can be expressed as uncontrolled or unpredictable movements and
trembling.
In severe cases, people can have trouble eating, breathing, controlling the
bladder and controlling the head and neck. Communication skills can also be a↵ected,
with impairments to speaking, hearing and vision (Cerebral Palsy Alliance, 2019). Figure 2
presents the frequency at which di↵erent consequences are observed.

1.2.3
Gross Motor Function Classiﬁcation System
People with CP are divided by the Gross Motor Function Classiﬁcation System, which
contains 5 levels. Level 1 being the mildest form of CP, while level 5 is the most severe
(Kerr, 1993).
Level 1:
A person within level 1 is able to walk without limitations.
Level 2:
A person within level 2 is able to walk with limitation. They might have trouble walking
longer distances and can’t run or jump. When learning to walk, they might need mobility
devices.
Level 3:
A person within level 3 needs a mobility device to walk. When walking inside a home,
a hand held mobility device can do, while a wheelchair is needed outside. When sitting,
they might need support.
Level 4:
A person within level 4 needs a motorized mobility devices to move around, however,
self-mobility is limited. Sitting usually requires support.
Level 5:
A person within level 5 needs assistance to move around, if they are not able to control a
motorized mobility device. Support to maintain head and neck position is required.
The Elsass Foundation, who we have worked with during this study, has to do with infants
usually of the levels 1-3.

1.2.4
How to Treat Cerebral Palsy
Despite there not existing a cure for CP, it is however possible to reduce the physical
disabilities caused by CP. Conditions of the disorder can be somewhat managed with
therapy and training using adaptive equipment as treatments. Surgical interventions and
drug treatments can also be required in some cases. Early treatment can also help lessen
the impact of the conditions, as the body is not yet fully developed. By intervening as
early as possible, the body is more susceptible to improvements (MyChild, 2019b).
1.3
Objectives and Scope
As the problem is stated, the objectives for the thesis will now be presented. Furthermore,
the plans and methods to achieve these objectives are explained and the scope is set.
The concept of a calibration is to establish a model of the eyes, such that the eye tracker
can analyze the eyes during a session and measure their gaze positions.
A default eye model can either be a standard eye model provided by the eye tracker
company, if one exist, or an eye model of another person’s eyes. Using a default eye model
is expected to reduce the immediate accuracy, but will allow the infants to perform eye
training exercises without a calibration step.
To account for the lost accuracy, we will try to establish a pseudo-calibration. As this is
not a calibration as such, but rather an algorithm, we will call this a correction algorithm.
The concept of the correction algorithm is to collect data as the exercises are running,
and train the algorithm on that data. The data collected consist of target points that
we provide to form the basis of an exercise, as well as gaze points recorded by the eye
tracker. We assume that the infant is looking at target point. For a perfect calibration,
the measured gaze points will align with the target points. However, as infants in this case
do not have a calibration, the measured gaze points will not align with the provided target
points introducing an error or inaccuracy. When enough data is collected, the algorithm
will try to minimize these errors, resulting in a function that transforms new recorded
gaze points best possible, according to the error minimization. Figure 3 illustrates this
concept, showing how a gaze point is ﬁrst measured by the eye tracker, for then to be
transformed by the correction algorithm into more accurate data.
Contrary to a calibration, which uses continuously data without interruptions, the advan-
tage of the correction algorithm is, that the data collected can consist of merged chunks.
Meaning, that the infant is allowed to lose focus for short periods during the exercises.

In parallel to the main objective, we will test whether infants are capable of follow a
custom calibration built for infants. A native eye tracking calibration step will also be
investigated. In this thesis we take a look at the eye tracking manufacturer Tobii, both in
terms of equipment and native calibration.
As this thesis revolves around eye tracking of infants, the primary target group will be
infants of 4-12 months of age. Toddlers, of 1-3 years of age, are not in focus for this thesis.
For comparison and baseline measures, eye tracking data from a control group consisting
healthy adults from the age of 20 years will be analyzed alongside the infant data.
To this end we will summarize the objectives in short:
- Test Tobii’s calibration scheme on infants, to check whether it is possible.
- Test if custom calibrations, made speciﬁcally for infants, are applicable on infants.
- Test how well correction algorithms can transform the data of a default eye model
on infants.
Another interesting ﬁeld within eye tracking of infants is to analyze di↵erent kind of
stimuli and how they a↵ect infants. This could improve the overall results, as infants
might be more attracted by stimuli of a certain size or color, increasing their awareness.
This includes size, color, shape, patterning and static vs. dynamic presentation.
1.3.1
Methods
To accomplish the objectives, we will make use of existing eye tracking technologies, see
speciﬁcations in Tools and Hardware (section 3), in order to collect gaze data of the eye.
This will be done through iterations of experiments, as we delve into the thesis. The
structure of the project will be of iterations of the following steps:
1. Research previous studies in correction approaches and infancy eye tracking.
2. Develop an environment to collect data in a consistent manner.
3. Collect test data for early development, both for calibrated equipment and by using
a default eye model.
4. Analyze gaze data collected to better understand the errors.
5. Develop correction algorithms based on the analysis.
6. Develop custom calibrations for infants based on research of infants’ behaviour.
7. Measure the accuracy of a control group using Tobii’s calibration scheme, to establish
an ideal baseline.
8. Conduct experiments on both a control group and infants using custom calibrations
and default eye models, including correction algorithms.
9. Analyze the data of both the control group and the infants and compare the accuracy
against each other, including the ideal baseline from step 7.
To compare the di↵erent approaches, i.e. the di↵erent calibrations and the default eye
model, both ﬁxation and smooth pursuit exercises will be developed to determine which
approach performs better. However to make a valid comparison, we need to take multiple
parameters into account.

==PAGE:15==
As only one approach can be active at any given time, each infant will have to participate
in multiple sessions of the same exercises. A session being a single run of all exercises with
a given approach.
Furthermore, infant’s interest in the exercises is prone to decrease over time, which means
that we expect the ﬁrst approach tested to showcase more accurate tracking.
Another issue within the same area is that, if each infant has a unique sequences of
approaches, we introduce the same pitfall: Observing the ﬁrst approach in a sequence to
be more accurate can just as likely be attributed to interest decreasing over time, and
should not be regarded as evidence and insight as to which approach is more accurate. To
this end; a mix of approaches in a sequence, with duplicates, should be employed.
A last pitfall to take into account is that an infant is able to learn and adapt to the exercise
over time, thus performing better due to learning and not necessarily the approach used.
To be able to detect this issue, one or more of the approaches should be used multiple
times on each infant.

proaches will be employed. Gao (2005) e↵ectively summarizes the Latin Squares design,
illustrating how it is used to balance di↵erent blocking approaches to experiments. This is
directly applicable for the blocking custom calibrations and default eye model approaches
of this study, as well as the exercises that follow.

likewise required more experiment sessions with infants, in order to fully determine the

2.2
Related Work
This section presents the stepping-stones of this project discovered through the literature

2.2.1
Attention-driven Stimuli
Already from birth, infants are emotionally capable of processing basic emotional re-
sponses. While triggering joyful emotions in infants are strived for by parents and re-
searchers alike, these are not guaranteed to be the ones to generate the most attentive
behaviour in infants. Carver and Vaccaro (2007) reports that more neural resources is al-
located by infants when presented with stimuli of negative emotions expressively painted
on the face of adults. For infants not to be emotionally overwhelmed, it is vital for them
to develop ways of coping with the emotions they face. As such, infants learn to regu-
late their emotions within the ﬁrst year, by turning away from the upsetting event or by
rocking themselves to create relief (Sigelman and Rider, 2009).
As to not provide the infants with an uncomfortable environment (and to not make us
an enemy of their parents), stimuli aimed at joyful emotion responses will be used in the
studies. Multiple studies have also shown that familiar faces are among the most attention
triggering stimuli presented to newborns, e.g. Reynolds (2015); Simion and Giorgio (2015).
Stimuli made from pictures of the infants’ mothers have been tested with great attentive
responses from the infant population of these studies.
A study exploring di↵erent additional attributes to the familiar face stimuli is Courage
et al. (2006), who found that dynamic high contrast stimuli had the largest e↵ect on infants
for attention. They also found that young infants were looking at the stimuli for longer
time than older infants, however with a more sporadic pattern. Whereas older infants did
look for longer periods, but for a shorter total time, indicating boredom.
2.2.2
Approaching Infancy Eye Tracking
Work related to events that a↵ect oculomotor behaviour have been scrutinized. From the
comprehensive work by Holmqvist et al. (2011) we adopt many best-practices approaches
to eye tracking studies, pointing out many general observations to be mindful of.
Di↵erent tracking techniques are appropriate for di↵erent situations. Bright pupil detec-
tion is found more accurate for Hispanics or Caucasians, whereas this technique is not
as suitable for Asians for whom dark pupil tracking shows greater accuracy (Tobii-AB,
2019). Examples of both tracking techniques can be found on ﬁgure 4. It is important to
collect as high quality data as possible, and therefore the appropriate tracking technique
should be applied. Reports by Gredeb¨ack et al. (2010) determined that tracking accuracy
of individuals with highly reﬂective retinas, such as infants, beneﬁt more from bright pupil
observations. This is an important observation to note and be aware of, concerning the
eye tracking equipment to be used in our project.

Self-correction
An interesting study is the work that has been done on algorithmic constructions for self-
adjusting calibrations. Whether such calibrations are at all needed is the essence in paper
by Takegami et al. (2002), wherein an approach to a reiterated eye model is presented;
eliminating the need for a static calibration step. The iterative process creates an eye
model, e↵ectively substituting the eye model otherwise generated by the calibration. The
algorithm proposed by Takegami et al. (2002) does however require not only an eye tracker,
but also an eye camera to capture and analyze eye images. In this study, the setup is of
simpler origin as only an eye tracker is available - and thus it is not possible to capture
eye images to construct eye models from. Instead the eye model has to be created from
only the data stream of the eye tracker.
Another eye image analysis approach to avoid the manual calibration step is by Sugano and
Bulling (2015). While also a model based approach, using saliancy models, the underlying
problem of drift correction during eye tracking sessions is investigated.
The proposed
method for counteracting gaze drift herein is data driven, but requires large quantities
thereof to be competitive with calibrated eye trackers. Sugano and Bulling (2015) applies
these concepts to longer daily-life sessions of much longer time spans (hours or even days)
than applicable in this study, consisting of short sessions spanning a couple of minutes.
The self-correcting aspects of this project will be on the basis of data analysis, rather than
model based image analysis.
Most notably relevant is the study by Vadillo et al. (2015) who try to improve poorly
calibrated eye trackers. In this study, they do not try to change the calibration itself, but

4
Setting up the Lab
Eye trackers are somewhat delicate devices and are sensitive to ambient changes.
This section delves into the aspects of eye tracking which inﬂuences the quality of the
gaze data. Common pitfalls and factors, which play a role in data quality, are covered
in Eye Tracking: A Comprehensive Guide To Methods And Measures (Holmqvist et al.,
2011). The most prominent ones in relation to experiments conducted in this project, will
be evaluated in the following.
Furthermore, this section explains how the stimuli and the exercises has been developed,
as it plays an important role towards the results. Using wrong stimuli or a badly executed
exercise can mislead the infant.
4.1
The Physical Environment
Experimental studies are to be conducted in a controlled environment, as to not attribute
corruption of data to the hardware and software used. Before the experiments start, it
is therefore important to eliminate as many external sources of errors as possible, leaving
only the imprecision and noise inherent of the equipment.
4.1.1
Light Sources
Disturbances in light during experiments can be distracting for the participants, as well as
the eye tracking equipment. Most commonly this is associated with ﬂickering of sunlight
or light from lamps. This is not only attributed to direct light but also ambient light. A
large portion of light related disturbances can be eliminated by setting up the lab where
no windows can facilitate direct sunlight and contribute to ambient light.
The eye tracking equipment used in this project, uses infrared light to highlight and track
the corneal reﬂections of the eye. This is a common method in many eye trackers and
thus light sources emitting infrared light (like halogen lamps) should be avoided, as to not
a↵ect the eye trackers.
Movements in the scene can also cause incoming light to di↵ract di↵erently, as well as
distract the participant. While experiment sessions take place, movement should therefore
be at a minimum to retain as constant an ambience as possible.
Overall, these factors contribute to risks of optical artefacts, imprecision of recordings and
data loss.
4.1.2
Vibrations
Should the eye tracker in any way be displaced during the experiments, the vibrations
exerted on the device would corrupt the entire data collection. This can happen either
from direct contact with the eye tracker or from an indirect impact to its mounting - e.g.
a monitor.
Therefore the lab in which the experiments are to be carried out, the eye tracker should
be mounted on a sturdy surface, separate from the location of the participant.
24
==PAGE:27==
4.1.3
Distractions
The foveal vision makes up the approximate span of 1.5 −2◦of the central ﬁeld of vi-
sion (Solso, 1996). With attention directed at an object, the visual acuity of the object is
regarded as 20/20. Movement events happening inside the foveal vision can most of the
time be anticipated. However registering movement in the periphery of ones vision, where
no detail is conceived, triggers the curiosity of need to know in most people. Especially
with the attention patterns of infants, distracting events in the peripheral vision could
undermine the experiment session.
When setting up the experiments, it should therefore be done in such a way, that it
minimizes the number of distracting objects in the ﬁeld of vision of the participating
subject. The monitor where the participant will be looking, and the exercises displayed,
is to be placed without moving objects or direct sunlight behind it. The controls for the
exercises will be on a laptop, out of line of sight for the participant.

Since a family home should not be subjected to the standards of a research-oriented
environment; a strict and controlled environment for the setup may not be achievable. As
such, the algorithms used to transform input data need to be robust enough, to not be
heavily inﬂuenced by skewed data from external disturbances like vibrations and light. A

To ensure that the eye tracker correctly and continuously tracks the eyes of participants

observe eye positions during the session.
4.2.1
Attentiveness of Infant Participants
With the inherent unreliable attention of infants from that of adults, accurate tracking of
infant’s eyes is ﬁrst and foremost an attentiveness problem to solve. Stimuli bear a big
part of that responsibility, being that it is what the infant sees on the screen.
Stimuli should be big enough that they are clearly visible to the participant, but without
compromising their centered focal point. The stimuli should also represent an Area of
Interest (AOI), such that the attention is not lost immediately. The visibility issue can
be solved simply by increasing the size of the stimuli, making it bigger than the two-
dimensional target point that it represents.
To center the focal point of the stimulus
it should be symmetric in shape, in order to naturally draw the gaze toward its center.
Complex asymmetric stimuli may cause the participant’s gaze to be o↵the centered target
point positions.
Another aspect of the stimulus’s contribution to attentiveness is its color. More specif-
ically; its color in contrast to the background of the exercise.
High contrast likewise
accentuate the stimuli on the screen, making it visually stand out. E.g. white stimuli on
a black background. From Reynolds (2015) we also know that familiar faces are a good
place to start when wanting to catch the attention of infants. For these reasons, we have
initially settled on a yellow smiley-face stimulus (ﬁgure 8) with a contrast ratio of 19.55:1
on a black background.

5.2
Event Latencies
The eye tracker will continuously invoke a callback function whenever a recording takes
place, every 11.111 ms at 90 Hz. When a target point is moving, the program needs to
update the current target which the following recorded gaze points should be mapped
to. Recorded data is written to ﬁle in the callback, so this also happen every 11.111 ms.
The program, however, also needs to render the stimuli to have it appear on the screen,
at the updated target position. Using PsychoPy to render stimuli, the program utilizes
hardware-accelerated OpenGL rendering where possible. This means that rendering of
stimuli with PsychoPy allows stimuli to be read into memory of the graphics card as the
pixels are rendering - the back bu↵er. When rendering is complete, the back bu↵er and the
bu↵er of the screen, the front bu↵er, are ﬂipped. Flipping of the bu↵ers is synchronized
with the monitor refresh rate in order to render without artefacts on the screen, which can
disturb the experiment. It also means that the drawing of the stimuli can only happen
at times which is a multiple of the monitor refresh rate. This allows for the program to
know the time at which the subject can see the stimuli. And the program can make sure
to only update the target point mapping after the stimuli has visibly changed position on
the screen.
For a monitor with a 60 Hz refresh rate, the stimuli is drawn on the screen in multiples
of 16.667 ms. The recording of gaze points can be at most 11.111 ms behind the stimuli
appearing on the screen. Should a target point update right after a monitor refresh, and
the eye tracker record just before the next monitor refresh, we get the upper time limit
of desynchronization between a recorded gaze point and its intended target point, as we
have to wait for the eye tracker to record once more after the next monitor refresh. I.e.
delay(ms) 2 [0, 16.667 + 11.111[ = [0, 27.7778[. A segment of the frequencies at which
events happen is visualized in ﬁgure 12.

the exercises. For the ﬁxation exercise, the problem only occurs when the target point is
changing location. Due to the desynchronization, two recordings are the upper bound of
wrong recordings during this change. However, as the change happens instantaneously, the
person looking will have a natural delay as well, causing a few gaze points to be recorded

6
Filtering
One of the most prevalent data observations in experiments is noise - and eye tracking is
no exception.
There is also the case of missing data. With an eye tracking device capturing gaze positions
many times a second, some recordings are bound to overlap with a subject blinking. Eye-
lids covering the eyes makes for invalid data, as the corneal reﬂection cannot be detected
during this time, and will result in (nan, nan) positional readings by the eye tracker.
In this section, approaches to ﬁltering the recorded gaze data will be evaluated. Before
delving into ﬁltering however, it must be clear what types of noise to expect.
6.1
Sources of Noise and Artefacts
Many of sources for noise and various optic artifacts can be attributed to the physical
environment. These aspects are covered in section 4 - Setting up the Lab.
Here we take a look at the artefacts related to the precision of the eye tracker, which can be
a challenge to separate from real eye movements - i.e. system inherent artefacts (Holmqvist
et al., 2011, p. 181-182). These erroneous readings of the eyes are more commonly found in
lower quality eye trackers, but even the most high-end equipment does not guarantee per-
fect measures. The Tobii 4C eye tracker used in this project has, as previously explained,
an expected accuracy of around 0.4◦of visual angle (rd. section 3.1). Another aspect of
artefacts in the gaze data, is the occurrence of optic artefacts from very quick jumps in
eye movement. These are not as diﬃcult to ﬁlter, as they appear as sudden spikes in the
data set, assuming correct readings otherwise, and are caused by the eye tracker failing
to track the pupil and/or corneal reﬂections. It should be noted that these instances are
di↵erent from blinks or other events classiﬁed as nan readings.
6.2
Threshold Filtering
There exist many ways to set a threshold when ﬁltering out noise. In a ﬁxation exercise,
the target point will be displayed in di↵erent places on the screen for a ﬁxed duration
at each position. In this sort of exercise, when the target point moves, the gaze point of
a subject will follow the target. However, due to a delay in a subject’s perception and
reaction time, the subject will likely look at the old target point for some milliseconds
before saccading to the new target point. This will allow multiple measurements from the
eye tracker to be recorded during the saccades. When measuring accuracy, i.e. how close a
subject’s gaze point is to the target point, these measurements will clearly be noise during
target point switches - due to the relative large distance between the previous- and new
target point.
One way to ﬁlter out this noise is to set a threshold, which ﬁlters out recorded gaze data
during the estimated span of time it takes for a subject to refocus at the new target point.
Here the threshold should equal the refocus estimation time. Using such a threshold gives
a subject enough time to react and to focus at the new target point, before the data
collection is resumed.
37
==PAGE:40==
Another way to set a threshold is to set a distance from the target point that all mea-
surements should lie within. If any gaze point is further away than the threshold, it will
be removed as part of the ﬁltering. However, thresholds should be used cautiously in this
manner as it, in certain cases, can ﬁlter out data that should have remained.
Take as an example a poorly calibrated eye tracker. Fixations could then appear to be
happening elsewhere than the subject is actually looking. The eye tracker then proceeds
to record all of the subject’s gaze points to be further away from the target point than
the threshold allows - and as such they will all be removed.
6.3
Fixation Clusters
A di↵erent approach to ﬁltering is to cluster gaze points to establish a ﬁxation. With
an eye tracker recording gaze data at a rate of 90 Hz (see section 3 for all hardware
speciﬁcations), there is a gaze point registered every 11.111 ms. A single ﬁxation typically
lasts 200 ms to 300 ms (Holmqvist et al., 2011), which requires between 18 and 27 gaze
points to be recorded around the same location to conﬁrm a ﬁxation. As a lower bound
for cluster formation, 18-27 gaze points should therefore ideally be considered.
The idea of ﬁltering using clusters is that the intermediate gaze points, recorded during
saccades from one target point to another, will not part of the bigger ﬁxation cluster
developing at the new target point. That way, non-clustered data points can be regarded
as noise and ﬁltered from the gaze data accordingly.
In connection with this project and the experiments conducted herein, the exercises can
have any number of target points. Popular clustering algorithms like k-means clustering,
which forcibly split data into k clusters, would struggle in this setting, even with a known
amount of target points. The problem comes from participants brieﬂy looking away from
the intended target. Not only should these deviating gaze points be regarded as noise, but
if enough of them are recorded to form a ﬁxation, their inﬂuence on the clustering starts
to be noticeable.
Without a predeﬁned cluster count for the data, algorithms such as mean-shift and
DBSCAN can be used, as they base the number of clusters on the processed data instead of
a dictated amount. Take note that the amount of clusters is not of signiﬁcant importance
and are simply used for ﬁltering out noise, i.e.
any non-clustered data.
The DBSCAN
algorithm is a good candidate for this project, as it determines noise points during its
execution. This is a notable advantage over the mean-shift algorithm, which does not
support noise detection inherently, and makes DBSCAN ideal for the use cases presented
here.
Despite DBSCAN being a good ﬁt, some pitfalls still exists. While no fault of the algorithm,
a scenario can arise where DBSCAN will cluster non-following points into the same cluster.
Out of the box, DBSCAN does not care about the order of how the data is being read.
However, when determining ﬁxations, the order plays a certain role. Therefore, looking
for ﬁxations, a single gaze point that is not accepted within the current cluster, should
break the ﬁxation, but using DBSCAN allows later observations to be accepted into earlier
clusters.
Take for example a subject that is looking at a certain point for a minute.
During this minute, the subject’s gaze ﬂickers and a number of non-following gaze points
are recorded to be at another point. Even though the subject did not look at the other
point, DBSCAN will record these non-following gaze points to be a cluster, since the order
38
==PAGE:41==
does not matter.
A method to circumvent this is to simply close the current cluster once the ﬁxation has
been broken, while still allowing for gaze points to ﬂicker away and back to a certain
extent. Closing a cluster can be done by iterating forward from the current cluster instead
of through all clusters.
A run of DBSCAN supporting closing of earlier clusters will be
denoted as a linear run of the DBSCAN algorithm.
6.3.1
Filtering with Moving Targets
During pursuits, where target points are constantly changing, clustering does not happen
in the literal sense of centered proximity. Instead the clustering happens in proximity
relative to previous target points. Again using DBSCAN, the clustering measures are based
on neighbor logic, which encapsulates relative proximity.

6.4.2
In-between exercise ﬁltering
Crucial to the eye tracking recording, is that the infants are in fact looking at the screen
when the exercises play. To keep them engaged and continuously looking at the screen,
the exercises would need to be played immediately after each other, without any downtime
in-between (rd. section 4.2.1). The situation is this: Depending on where the stimulus
of an exercise is located on the screen, the exercise that follows it could start with a
stimuli appearing at the other end of the screen. For any human there will be a period
of time where locating and saccading the foveal vision to the new point takes place.
Figure 25 demonstrates the situation, with the center target point of ﬁgure 25a being
the last place where a stimulus is shown for that exercise. Then follows the exercise on
ﬁgure 25b, showing how multiple gaze point are recorded in the center of this exercise,
before saccading to meet up with the already started circle exercise stimulus.
Infant humans, developing these skills, show signs of a longer such period of time. It
would not be a fair evaluation of their ability to follow the exercise, if we were to compare
gaze points of the adjustment phase between exercise stimuli, to the target point of the
new stimuli. As such, there ought to be a grace period in between the exercises. Not a
period where nothing happens, the exercises remain continuous, but a blind-eye process
running in the background. That is, ﬁlter out the gaze data recorded during the expected
latency it takes for an infant to direct its gaze to the new stimuli. This can be achieved by
applying a threshold ﬁlter at the beginning of each new exercise. For infants, the observed
latency of gaze points before catching up the new target point was around 45 recordings,
equal to half a second of data with a 90 Hz eye tracker. For the control group the latency
was slightly less, with the participant of ﬁgure 25b taking 31 records (0.34 seconds) to
meet up with the new stimulus.

7
Correction Algorithms
As raw gaze data, streamed from an eye tracker, can be inaccurate or imprecise, a trans-
formation can help minimize these factors and make the data meaningful. Even though a
good calibration can provide an accuracy of less than 1◦of visual angle, larger errors can
occur. When talking about infants, an accurate calibration is not always easy to obtain,
since an infant does not have the attention span to try many times, until a calibration
is established. That is why a default eye model in certain cases has to be enough and a
transformation can be helpful.
Usually, a default eye model still provides a certain amount of precision, however with a
low accuracy since the gaze points are recorded out of alignment with the display. The
precision can retain its quality due to the calibration often shifts the data, rather than
spreading the gaze points around. Therefore a pattern can still be visible from such eye
models. To this end, di↵erent approaches to improve the accuracy has been suggested,
which transform the raw gaze points recorded into more accurate data.
As the data is often observed shifted in one direction when applying a default eye model
(rd.
section 8.5), a linear transformation can be a good tool to adjust the recorded
gaze points. Furthermore, as each eye has its own errors, each eye should have a unique
transformation. In this thesis, the following approaches will be considered and tested:
- Linear Transformation
- Aﬃne Transformation
- Translation only
- Regular
- Composite
- Regression-based Transformation
These will be referenced as we walk through the experiments in section 8.
As an extension, an area-weighted transformation approach will be applied on both the
linear transformation and aﬃne transformations, to test whether it can improve the results.

7.3
Regression-based Transformation
Another approach, slightly di↵erent from the previous ideas, is the idea of a regression-
based error correction. That is, to use a best-ﬁtting polynomial for the gaze data and
subsequently use it as a function to predict error estimates. With some variance, which is
not guaranteed constant, the recorded gaze points will inevitably be o↵a target point by
some pixels.
Taking inspiration from a proposed spatial recalibration method by Zhang and Hornof
(2014), a data-centric approach to eye tracking data will be evaluated.
”The other category of error correction methods, data-centric methods, works
by extrapolating the pattern of the error from the data and then removing the
error based on the extracted pattern.”
(Zhang and Hornof, 2014, p. 95)

performed for each of the coordinate axes. The error pattern is to match the polynomial
function values, reﬂecting the pixel o↵set of one axis based on the gaze position on the other
axis. This way, polynomial functions can estimate the expected horizontal and vertical
errors of a gaze point, as functions of where on the screen it is positioned. An example of
such analysis can be seen on ﬁgure 28; a collection of ﬁgures showing recorded gaze data

7.3.1
Varying the Polynomial Degree
With the presented ways of transforming gaze data by means of polynomials, comes the
question of which polynomial degree to use. Granted, higher order polynomials will never
ﬁt the data worse than a lower order polynomial from a data point of view. Even if there
are no apparent higher order relation between the data points, higher order polynomials
force their turning points to ﬁt the data nonetheless. When used to model horizontal-
and vertical errors as in the regression-based transformation method, turning points of
the ﬁtted model can cause the predicted errors to be heavily inﬂuenced by extremity data
points. A very simple example of this is data consisting of two points: A least squares ﬁt
of increasing order going through these points, can see dramatic slopes at either end of
the data points. Figure 29 gives an example of polynomial ﬁts of orders 1, 2 and 3, each
with more ﬂuctuation than the previous. Modeling with higher order polynomials are at
increasing risk of forming a steep slope at extremity points, since there are no more data
points to dictate its path. This can cause errors at these extremities to be predicted far
from its true error, contributing so much to its ability to correct data, that the model
becomes worse than its lower order counterparts, which are more conservative in their
correction.
Also, correcting errors based on higher order polynomials may cause it to
overﬁt, being too trained on the original data set. When applying the polynomial error
corrections to a new data set, the traits of the higher order polynomial may not be general
enough to capture the errors of the new data.

and cubic as magenta.

gaze data and not the gaze data itself, higher order polynomials ﬁnds less value when

7.4
Area-weighted Transformation
All suggested correction algorithms form a transformation where all gaze point are mostly
shifted in a single direction. However observations have shown that in certain cases the
shift of gaze points to take the form of an exploding pattern. Meaning that in the left
side of the display the data is shifted left, while in the right side of the display the data
is shifted right. In this case an out-of-the-box transformation might not be enough, since
e.g. a translation vector cannot shift gaze points in both negative and positive directions
along the same axis.
To solve the issue regarding exploding patterns, a thought of multiple area-based trans-
formations was introduced. The idea is to separate the screen into multiple areas, where
each area has its own transformation. In this test, we will use ﬁve areas, as illustrated in
ﬁgure 32.
Figure 32: Separation of screen into ﬁve areas to perform a transformation in each of them.
Using this idea, a more optimal transformation for each area can be found. However, if
a gaze point is recorded close to the border between two areas, it can be hard to decide,
which area the gaze point belongs to. Using a strict separation as this idea suggests, the
gaze point will be corrected using the transformation that belongs to the area the gaze
point is within, which can lead to a wrong correction of that gaze point. This would be
an extensive problem for smooth pursuit exercises, since the borders are likely crossed
multiple times and the pattern would be shattered as a result. To this end another idea,
building on the same principle, was taken into account. Instead of having a strict border
that decides which transformation to use, the ﬁnal transformation for a gaze point should
be a mix of the di↵erent transformations.
Depending on the position of the recorded
gaze point, a weighted mix of each transformation should be applied, calculated using the
distance from the gaze point to each areas’ target point. The closer the recorded gaze
point is to a target point of an area, the more inﬂuence the corresponding transformation
has on the ﬁnal transformation.

8.2
Tobii’s Calibration Scheme on Infants
As with the control group, it would be easy just to use Tobii’s calibration scheme to
calibrate the eye tracker for infants. However, when it comes to infants, we theorize that
they cannot complete Tobii’s calibration scheme. Not only are the blue dots small, but
their contrast to the background is low as well (5.04:1), making them rather uninteresting
even for adults to look at. The exploding dot animation helps to keep the attention on the
stimuli, but does nothing to acquire it in the ﬁrst place. With infants not able to adhere
to the instructions of the calibration scheme, it puts all the responsibility on the stimuli
to catch their attention. As Tobii’s calibration scheme consist of a sequence of tasks that
subjects have to complete in order to achieve a calibration, the lack of perception in infants
becomes a liability.
To determine whether this is the case, we had the participants of test group 1 try and
complete such a calibration.

Results
As expected, none of the two infants were able to complete the calibration. In fact, not
even a single blue dot was made explode by the infants. Infants quickly lost interest in
actions on the screen and started to look around the room for other stimuli.
Discussion
In this experiment only a few infants were tested. It might be possible to complete Tobii’s
calibration scheme for some infants, but we found it to be a very unreliable method. This
calibration was therefore not continued in the experiments with test group 2. A more
attention-focused approach to actively calibrating the eye tracker is needed for infants.

8.5
Implications of a Default Eye Model
In the earlier experiments we successfully calibrated the eye tracker for infants using
custom calibrations, however not all infants were able to complete a 5-point calibration.
Therefore, the concept of using a default eye model is introduced. The default eye model is
an existing eye model which is to be used to skip the calibration step. Being created from a
calibration process in advance, and over eyes of an arbitrary person, the default eye model
is likely to heavily increase the average angular o↵set due to no eye being identical for any
two people. To account for this loss in accuracy, a correction algorithm will be applied,
which collects data during eye exercises and learns how to transform raw recordings into
more accurate data, reﬂecting the true gaze direction better. In place of a personalized
eye model, requiring a personal calibration, the subjects will have a personal correction
algorithm which, when applied to the raw gaze data, aim simulate the transformation
between the default eye model and a personalized one.
In this experiment we look into how the data is a↵ected by the use of a default eye model.
Furthermore, we will collect data to build a correction algorithm that can account for the

Discussion
Training the correction on the ﬁxation and circle exercises produce the most accurate
transformations of the gaze points. Notice that both the ﬁxation and the circle exercise
are symmetric across both axes on the display if origin is in the center of the display.
The triangle and the spiral exercise are not symmetric, pushing the correction algorithm
out of balance in determining the optimal transformation. Hence building an exercise to
train the correction algorithm should both be easy to complete, symmetric and cover gaze
positions in di↵erent directions.
When deployed in settings where only type of exercise is used, consider training the cor-
rection algorithm on that speciﬁc exercise type, as this yields the most accurate results.

Discussion
As we have discussed the result of individual experiments in Experiments and Results (rd.
section 8), they will not be the focus in this section.
In this thesis the focus has been on correction algorithms and lowering the angular o↵-
sets. This area of discussion is dedicated to the commonly observed aspects of accuracy
measures, and will discuss alternative ways of correcting the angular o↵sets as well as

Furthermore we will present an approach where the angular o↵sets are not of interest, and

9.1
Comparing Correction to Known Approaches
Throughout the experiments, correction data from two recent and promising post-hoc
calibration approaches have been evaluated. The linear transformation error correction
inspired by Vadillo et al. (2015), was able to achieve an average accuracy of 2.02◦for
infants in the ﬁxation exercise and 3.04◦in the triangle exercise. Also the regression-
based approach inspired by Zhang and Hornof (2014) was likewise implemented and found
to improve the average accuracy for infant gaze data to 3.02◦for the ﬁxation exercise
and to 3.26◦for the triangle exercise. In comparison, error correction by means of aﬃne
transformation was able to achieve an average angular o↵set of 1.44◦for the ﬁxation
exercise and of 2.44◦for the triangle exercise.
From the initial average angular o↵set of the ﬁxation exercise across infants of this study,
3.94◦, the aﬃne transformation error correction manages to shave o↵63.90% of the aver-
age angular o↵set. The linear transformation manages to eliminate 48.63% of the average
angular o↵set, and only 22.88% of the o↵set is disposed of by the regression-based ap-
proach. For the full table of infant results, from which these numbers are found, refer to
appendix D. The approaches found in the literature set out to correct already accurate
gaze data. Both Vadillo et al. (2015) and Zhang and Hornof (2014) apply their correction
algorithms to gaze data with an initial angular o↵set of around 1◦on average. This is of
course another starting point entirely from what is seen in this study, with initial o↵sets
many times larger.

training process. The result of this is that the transformations tries to accommodate for
this abnormal data, a↵ecting the transformation of the good parts of the data. Inclusion
of the abnormal gaze data therefore makes the overall correction of the good data less
accurate.
A simple distance threshold could be implemented to ﬁlter out the abnormal gaze data,
removing gaze points not within a certain distance of the associated target point. Accuracy
di↵ers from participant to participant, and the angular o↵sets can be quite large when
using a default eye model.
Furthermore, the accuracy can vary across the display in
both axes, making one side more accurate than the other. As a result, a simple distance
threshold ﬁlter cannot e↵ectively remove this type of data without a↵ecting the rest.
Another way to deal with the abnormal gaze data is to use outlier detection or similar. We
initiated some preliminary tests with outlier detection, based on the standard deviation σ
as seen in equation 16. Throwing away data exceeding m times the standard deviation.
|xi −X| > m · σ(X)
(16)
In the equation, x is the distance between a gaze point and it corresponding target point,
and X the set of all distances with X being their average.
The standard deviation coeﬃcient needed to remove the unwanted data without removing
good data, was very individual and it was not possible to settle on an m, usable for all
participants. If a general value for m was used, either some unwanted data remained or too
much data was removed. In the tests, the amount of data removed ranged from close to
nothing to approximately 50%, for values of m ranging from 1 to 1.5. If a ﬁltering process
can be established such that it will remove just the abnormal gaze data, the correction
algorithm is expected to perform better.
9.3
Mean of clustering
It became apparent after analyzing our collection of gaze data from di↵erent experiments,
that infant gaze points appear more scattered around target points than adult gaze points.
The scattering, compared to the control group, is observed to be of microsaccadic ampli-
tude, and could be the result of intersaccadic eye movements, capable of shifting the retinal
image over an area as large as 1 deg2 (Ko et al., 2016). Assuming that drift correction
happens uniformly around the AOI, accuracy of a ﬁxation can be improved simply by
bringing the gaze points into closer proximity of each other. In the general case, this is a
ﬁne-tuning of the precision measure (see equation 8). To achieve this adjustment to the
precision, we can utilize the already formed ﬁxation clusters (see section 6.3).
When linking a gaze point to a ﬁxation cluster during the DBSCAN run, after adding the
gaze point to the ﬁxation cluster, we can replace its coordinates with the mean coordinate
of its associated cluster. The mean coordinate of the ﬁxation cluster is naturally placed
closer to wherever in the cluster most gaze points reside, meaning that replacing gaze point
coordinates with mean coordinates puts all gaze points of a ﬁxation cluster closer to each
other - increasing the precision of that cluster, as the cluster grows. When this happens
around the target point or AOI, accuracy too will improve. Figure 55b show gaze points

not provide any more insight, as they will always be evaluated by accuracy measures. But

pattern can be forcibly ﬁt to the recorded gaze points. The equation of the gaze data
can then be compared to the target points’ equation to determine the attention of the
participant during exercises.

not dependent on either scale or position of the shapes, but only depends on the orientation
of the shapes.
Before implementing equation 24 as a metric for measuring similarities, each pattern must
be expressed as shapes in terms of their tangent vectors. This is a trivial task for the
target pattern, as it follows smooth curves and lines. But for the gaze points, not only are
they tracing a more rough pattern, they also remain ﬁxed from time to time (ﬁxations).

Which is to say that the expectations of the similarity measures are met, and correspond
to human recognition.
Then rises the question of when the human recognition stops identifying two shapes to
share the same pattern.
In other words; at what value should the evaluation of the
similarity measure report an insuﬃcient trace. This can be regarded as a personal matter,
as some humans will perceive two objects to share a certain pattern while others will not.
Note that this method can be applied to any polygonal shape, which is an attractive
feature when arbitrary exercises are concerned, instead of having to manually tailor shape
similarity to every pattern.
This method is however not without ﬂaws when considering the scenario presented in this
study, as the shapes can be incomplete. When data is missing and the gaze pattern is in-
complete, tangent vectors of the target shape at positions where no data is available for the
gaze point shape, will be compared to tangent vectors where data is present. This compar-
ison could therefore be between tangent vectors at opposite ends of the shape, contributing
negatively to the overall similarity score. When enough of these non-corresponding vec-
tor comparisons are part of the similarity measure, it will drastically a↵ect the similarity
measure between the two shapes.
One ﬁx to keep the similarity measure from including non-corresponding vectors, is to
ﬁlter target shape positions for non-matching gaze shape positions. This way, only corre-
sponding vector positions are left, representing a segment of overall shape. With matching
turning points of the patterns usually being indicative of a successful trace, if the segment
does not include turning points (the segment is perhaps linear), a penalty to the similarity
measure should be enforced to reﬂect the segmentation bias. This penalty could be based
on the segments size compared to the overall size of the shape.

Conclusion
In this thesis an eye tracking study on infants has been conducted. As expected, infants
were not able to complete a calibration based on Tobii’s calibration scheme, a manufac-
turer of eye tracking equipment. On the contrary, all tested infants were able to complete
a custom developed 2-point calibration, designed for infants. Another custom calibration
was likewise developed for infants, featuring a 5-point calibration step, with higher resul-
tant accuracies, but a completion rate of only 71.43% for the 7 infants in this study, due
to environmental inﬂuences. This did not quite satisfy our validation criterion of at least
80% completed calibrations.
For a testing environment, two exercises were developed. Respectively, a ﬁxation exer-
cise with ﬁve target points, and a smooth pursuit exercise with four intermediate target
points, forming the shape of a triangle. To set up a baseline measurement for the accu-
racies, a control group consisting of ﬁve healthy adults completed a personal calibration
based on Tobii’s calibration scheme, which resulted in an average angular o↵set on 1.09◦
of visual angle for the ﬁxation exercise and 0.98◦for the pursuit exercise. Likewise, using
the custom 2-point calibration, the average angular o↵sets were found to be 1.60◦and
1.73◦, respectively for the ﬁxation- and the pursuit exercise. The custom 5-point cali-
bration resulted in even more accurate gaze data, with an average accuracy of 1.32◦and
1.45◦, respectively for the ﬁxation- and the pursuit exercise. The bulk of the gaze points
accuracies of the 5-point calibration demonstrate even smaller angular o↵sets than those
measured with the native Tobii calibration, although with a larger spread.
For the infants, the 2-point calibration provided an average accuracy of 1.61◦and 2.15◦
of visual angle for the two exercises. The average accuracy of infants able to complete
the 5-point calibration was 0.87◦and 1.70◦, respectively for the ﬁxation- and the pursuit
exercise. These results show that infants using a 5-point calibration can obtain a better
accuracy than the control group achieved by Tobii’s native calibration.
The applicability of a default eye model has been investigated; an eye model of an arbitrary
person constructed from a native calibration process prior to any experiments. The default
eye model eliminates the need of a calibration for experiment participants, but requires
a layer of correction on top, reﬂecting the gaze direction of the participant.
For this
purpose, a variety of correction algorithms were developed. As a result, both an aﬃne
transformation and a composite aﬃne transformation were found to perform very well. For
a control group, without any correction, a default eye model was observed to provide gaze
data with angular o↵sets between 4◦and 8◦of visual angle. For infants, the uncorrected
accuracy was measured to be between 2◦and 5◦of visual angle. Correcting the gaze data
from the eye tracker with the default eye model, the angular o↵sets were lowered to be
in the range of 1◦to 3◦of visual angle for the control group, and between 0.5◦and 3◦of
visual angle for the infant test group.
On average, the control group’s gaze data was improved to 1.68◦and 2.28◦of visual angle,
respectively for the ﬁxation- and pursuit exercise. With respect to infants, the average
angular o↵set of the corrected gaze data was 1.58◦of visual angle for the ﬁxation exercise
and 2.65◦of visual angle for the pursuit exercise.
The accuracy implications of using a default eye model are comparable to that of a custom
2-point calibration. While it is found that it is one’s best interest to use a calibrated eye
tracker to achieve the highest accuracy, we show that a default eye model together with
an aﬃne transformation of the recorded gaze data can be used with little loss of accuracy.
93
==PAGE:96==
At the same time, the default eye model approach allows a participant to completely skip
the calibration step, making it an e↵ective way of conducting eye tracking experiments
with participants with small attention spans. We show that the correction algorithm can
be trained on various exercises, giving the conductor of an experiment great ﬂexibility in
exercise creation.
Additionally, pattern recognition, using precision measures and tangent vectors, was found
to deliver promising results in detecting and comparing shapes of exercises. Similarity
measures of incomplete shapes require more extensive research and testing to incorporate
the pattern recognition approach into the evaluation process of an exercise being followed.
The experiments conducted in this thesis involved infants from the age of four to seven
months. None of the infants were, at the time of testing, diagnosed with cerebral palsy.
However, infants with milder forms of CP behave as any other healthy infant does. Finding
in this study are therefore expected to be similarly applicable.

Future work
More accurate corrections are achieved when the training exercise of the correction algo-
rithm matches the test exercise. To capture the broadest amount of eye characteristics,
it would be interesting to see how well the algorithms would perform after training on
a bit of every exercise. This also ties into the possibility of a continuous recalibration
of the transformation parameters. That is, as the exercises are played, keep tweaking
the transformation to optimize the parameters to account for nuance changes to the gaze
characteristics - a live calibration.
Training on multiple exercise types will take a longer time, which is not a willingly given re-
source in infancy eye tracking. Live, continuous data collection to optimize transformation
parameters has the drawback of also being prone to collect corrupt data and potentially
regress its ability to correct gaze data. The work here lies in the incorporation of multiple
exercise types without requiring very long sessions.
We also expect that the accuracy can be improved by improving the stimuli.
As the
stimuli is the must prominent factor to attract the infant’s gaze, better stimuli will keep
the infant focused for a longer time with lesser interruptions.
Many interruptions are
ﬁltered away by DBSCAN and threshold ﬁltering (rd. section 6.4), however in certain cases
abnormal gaze data is observed (rd. section 9.2). Abnormal gaze data exists partly due
to infants losing focus for a short moment, as the stimuli do not stimulate the infant. By
improving the stimuli, abnormal gaze data can be minimized, as infants are more likely to
keep focus on the stimuli. Hence, the correction algorithms will be built on more accurate
data, minimizing the errors from abnormal gaze data.
Although, improving the stimuli are expected to improve the accuracy, due to the correc-
tion algorithm being more accurate, we do not expect that the infant are looking more
accurate towards the target point itself. An exception might be smaller stimuli, as its
covers a smaller area, leading the infant to look more accurate. However, using smaller
stimuli could also reduce the infant’s interest.
In the thesis we only touched on the possibilities of using pattern recognition to evaluate
the gaze path of an eye tracking participant. Much more analysis and methods can be
applied in this regard, especially for more intricate exercises. Precision measures may be
more indicative of the way infants are looking compared to accuracy measures.
A precursor to training eyes of infants with CP is that they can be diagnosed in such early
stages. While not a priority in this study, the possibility of using gaze data to form a
diagnostic tool for infants with CP is there. Long-term prospects are that early recordings
of infant’s eye models can help later deduce the gaze characteristics of the no-longer infants
who turned out to be diagnosed with the disability. This way, one can feed the gaze data of
all the recorded infants to a machine learning algorithm and train the algorithm to identify
the gaze characteristics of infants who later turned out to have CP. This approach could
aid future diagnoses by ﬂagging infants who show signs of gaze-based CP traits in the
machine learning model. With enough data and assuming that there is some di↵erence in
the eye models of infants with and without CP, it could become a diagnostic tool. This is
not something we had time for in the allotted time of this thesis, but it was an interesting
thought that crossed our minds during its course.
