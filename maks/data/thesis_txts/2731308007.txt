ABSTRACT 
 
 
Robots have been playing an increasingly important role in our modern world for the past 
few decades and they are likely to take on more roles in the near future. With the 
advancement of technology and as robots become more autonomous, we are already seeing 
robots taking their first steps in the world. However, when tracking or monitoring these 
robots, various techniques/systems have come into consideration. OptiTrack, industry 
leading precision motion capture and 3D tracking systems. There are also several methods to 
monitor a robot. This engineering project undertakes a comparative study of two distinct 
positioning systems employed for monitoring mobile robots. The investigation centers on 
evaluating the performance of the OptiTrack system in contrast to a monocular camera, 
specifically the Basler camera, within the experimental framework. The primary focus lies in 
quantifying and analyzing the robot tracking error generated by each technique. Toward the 
conclusion of the thesis, a succinct analysis is presented, leveraging results obtained from 
the implementation of the Extended Kalman filter on data gathered from both OptiTrack and 
the Basler camera. This research contributes valuable insights into the comparative efficacy 
of these positioning systems, offering a nuanced understanding of their respective strengths 
and limitations, with implications for advancements in mobile robot monitoring 
technologies.

1. INTRODUCTION 
The concept of robots has roots in ancient Greek mythology, where a deity associated with 
metalwork was said to have mechanical servants. However, the practical realization of 
working robots, as defined today, dates back over a century. Over the years, robots have 
found applications across diverse fields such as healthcare, agriculture, and entertainment. 
The evolution of robots has been marked by a relentless pursuit of autonomy, a trend that 
has intensified in recent decades. 
In the realm of robotics and automation engineering, mobile robots represent a distinct 
subfield that has gained prominence, particularly in commercial and industrial applications. 
Differential drive mobile robots are widely adopted due to their inherent flexibility, 
simplicity, and ease of control. The Department of Electrical Engineering at the Technical 
University of Denmark (DTU) has been actively engaged in research on mobile robots, 
accumulating significant expertise in this domain. Among the diverse array of robots 
available for autonomous research at the institute, the Small Mobile Robot (SMR) stands 
out. Developed in-house, the SMR serves as a versatile platform extensively utilized in both 
educational and research contexts. While the SMR is not tailored to a specific problem, it 
serves as a general-purpose platform that can be expanded to address a myriad of challenges. 
The robot employed in the present study is equipped with distinctive features, operating 
seamlessly with hardware and software. The control language designed specifically for these 
robots streamlines and expedites the programming process for the SMR to undertake novel 
tasks. In addition to facilitating robot control, the SMR Control Language (SMR-CL) 
encompasses flow control, mathematical functions, expressions, and logging functionalities 
based on user-defined variables or various state variables. 
Mobile robots have had marvellous breakthroughs in the fields of medical science, industrial 
sector, social science, education, space application research, agriculture, games, etc. One of 
the challenging sector for autonomous mobile robots is navigation. To make the navigation 
successful, it has to pass through perception, localisation, cognition and motion control 
phases. In the perception phase, data interpretation is done by the sensor built into the robots 
and subsequently current location is estimated by the robots from a sensor placed externally 
in the localisation phase, finally robot reach the target by a set of prepared plans through 
manipulating and making sets of algorithms in the cognition phase. The set trajectory is 
==PAGE:9==
 
 
 
- 8 - 
achieved in the motion control phase by modifying its motor outputs. In the past few years, 
localisation has received most research attention (Siegwart et al., 2011).                 
Optical Motion capture systems (OMC) are widely used in various fields, including 
biomechanics, to measure human kinematics. The operation principle of OMC systems is 
based on stereophotogrammetry. In this three-dimensional coordinate of points on an object 
on which measurements are produced in two or more photographic images. These images 
are taken from different positions. OMC consist of markers, cameras, and processing 
software. The users must consider system errors when evaluating their results to be used in 
these fields on which the system's accuracy is based and also validated on relative distance 
and displacement measurements. In recent years, the use of low-cost OMC multi-camera 
systems has gradually spread widely in biomechanics research and rehabilitation. These 
systems use retroreflective markers and triangulation to calculate marker coordinates. 
Identification of their accuracy, validity and reliability are the important criteria of their 
application. Before OMC measurements, calibration of the camera is an important activity. 
OMC software provides calibration quality characteristics to the system. The acceptance 
primarily depends upon mean reprojection, residual mean error of the markers, 3D error and 
suggested maximum length for the reprojection rays. The OMC system purely self-evaluates 
these calculations. Therefore, additional validation studies on the accuracy of motion capture 
systems are most of the time necessary to validate the research. In the present study, we 
established the 3D accuracy by using 16 camera OptiTrack of an OMC system for tracking 
the markers within measurements of the relative displacement in a trajectory mostly moved 
by a small mobile robot. After obtaining the results from the OptiTrack, we have 
implemented an Extended Kalman Filter on the result to compare and see how effectively 
the EKF model tried to reduce the sensor noise and make the results obtained from 
OptiTrack smoother. 
For the second tracking method of the mobile robot, we are using the Basler camera model 
number ACE2. It is a monocular camera. A pylon camera software (software suite provided 
by Basler AG) is necessary to view the video or image data from the camera. After receiving 
the video recorded data from the camera, the object detection of the robot is done, and data 
of the robot is extracted. The extended Kalman Filter Algorithm is also implemented here to 
smooth out the sensor noise caused by getting the robot's position. The Ace2 series cameras 
are designed to minimize latency, enabling faster response times in tracking the robot's 
movements.

Kalman filtering is an algorithm by which one can estimate some unknown variables of a 
given measurements observed over a particular time. KFs have demonstrated their 
applicability in various robotics sectors and relatively simple form, which requires small 
computational power. Like the Kalman Filter, the Extended Kalman Filter is also used for 
estimation techniques used in control systems and signal processing to estimate the state of a 
system given noisy or uncertain measurements. It is also widely used in robotics, aerospace, 
navigation, and many more fields. The Kalman Filter and the Extended Kalman Filter are 
powerful tools for state estimation, but they have limitations. For highly nonlinear systems, 
the EKF might suffer from divergence or poor performance due to linearization errors. More 
advanced techniques, like the Unscented Kalman Filter (EKF) and Particle Filter, address 
these issues for even more complex systems. In summary, the Kalman Filter is used for 
linear systems with Gaussian-distributed noise, while the Extended Kalman Filter is an 
extension that handles nonlinear systems by linearizing their equations using Jacobian 
matrices. 
Problem statement: 
In the realm of system development and technological validation, the paramount 
considerations are accuracy and precision. These qualities are pivotal in ensuring the 
flawless functionality of any system. In the context of mobile robot tracking, achieving a 
high degree of accuracy and precision is imperative to ascertain its optimal performance 
across a spectrum of applications. The overarching goal is to validate technology with 
minimal errors. 
Within the technology validation process, numerous challenges are encountered and 
systematically addressed. The current study aims to delineate and resolve the following key 
problems: 
1. Operation Accuracy of OptiTrack System under 16 Camera Setup: 
The first challenge involves assessing the operational accuracy of the OptiTrack system 
when configured with a 16-camera setup. This evaluation is crucial for understanding the 
system's precision and reliability in capturing the mobile robot's movements. 
 
2. Basler Camera Tracking with Aruco Marker on SMR: 
The second challenge revolves around investigating how the Basler camera tracks the 
position of the mobile robot when equipped with an Aruco marker. Specific attention is 
==PAGE:11==
 
 
 
- 10 - 
directed towards identifying potential errors in the system's functionality, particularly when 
Aruco markers are mounted on small mobile robots (SMR). 
3. Enhancements with Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) Implementation: 
The third challenge involves exploring the enhancements introduced by the Extended 
Kalman Filter (EKF) following its implementation in both the OptiTrack and Basler camera 
systems. This investigation aims to quantify the improvements in tracking accuracy and 
error reduction facilitated by EKF. 
4. Comparison of Robot Tracking Error between Systems: 
The final challenge seeks to determine which of the two systems, OptiTrack or Basler 
camera, exhibits superior performance in terms of robot tracking error. This comparative 
analysis is instrumental in discerning the strengths and limitations of each technology. 
Addressing these challenges systematically contributes to a comprehensive understanding of 
the intricacies associated with mobile robot tracking, thereby paving the way for 
advancements in system development and validation methodologies. 
The following project objectives should be met by the end of the project: 
1) Presentation of a literature review about indoor localization of mobile robotic platforms 
and identification of research gaps relevant to the project scope.  
2) Presentation of the state-of-the-art OptiTrack system and analysis of the working 
principles. 
3) Collection of experimental data of the localization of a mobile robot using OptiTrack, and 
discussion of findings. 
4) Selection of one or more alternative technologies for indoor localization, development of 
a hardware-software solution for the actual localization of the mobile robot and discussion 
of methods of choice and explanation on how such methods are applied to the problem at 
hand. 
5) Planning and executing relevant experiments to collect data with the secondary 
localization system(s) and data analysis. 
6) Proposal of relevant metrics and carrying out the comparative study. Discuss the 
advantages and disadvantages of the different localization system(s) in light of the chosen 
metrics.

2. REVIEW OF LITERATURE 
 
2.1. MOBILE ROBOT 
 
Autonomous Mobile Robot (AMR) is a system designed to function within an unpredictable 
and partly unfamiliar environment. This entails the robot's capacity to navigate seamlessly 
and possess the capability to steer clear of any obstacles encountered within its range of 
motion. The AMR requires minimal to no human intervention for its movement, is designed 
to adhere to a predetermined path whether in indoor or outdoor settings. The incorporation 
of various sensors on the mobile robot for intricate functions such as estimation and 
localization renders the design of the entire system quite demanding. The fundamentals of 
mobile robotics encompass locomotion, perception, and navigation (Alatise and Hancke, 
2020) 
 
2.1.1. Locomotion:  
 
The locomotion system constitutes a vital facet of mobile robot design, encompassing 
considerations beyond the robot's movement medium. Factors such as maneuverability, 
controllability, terrain conditions, efficiency, stability, among others, play a pivotal role 
(Rubio et al. 2019). Based on the intended functionality, designed robots are classified 
into stationary and mobile variants, capable of operating on land, water, or in the air. The 
demand for autonomous mobile robots is substantial owing to their adeptness in 
executing tasks that might pose challenges for humans. Various designed mobile robots 
fall into categories such as wheeled, legged, walking, or hybrid, each with distinctive 
capabilities. Locomotion methods for mobile robots predominantly involve legged, 
wheeled, and articulated body designs (Saudabayev et al., 2015). 
 
2.1.2. Perception:  
 
For an AMR, gathering information from its surroundings, detecting nearby objects, and 
assessing its relative position are of utmost significance. Perception constitutes a vital 
dimension in the study of mobile robots. If a mobile robot cannot accurately and 
effectively perceive its environment, tasks like precise object position estimation could 
become problematic (Kortenkamp, 1994).  
==PAGE:15==
 
 
 
- 14 - 
 
2.1.3. Navigation: 
 
Navigation represents a foundational challenge across robotics and other crucial 
technologies. To achieve autonomous navigation, a mobile robot must possess the 
awareness of its current location, the target destination, and the means to reach that 
destination. The central focal point in mobile robot design resides in its navigation 
capabilities. The aim is for the robot to autonomously traverse between distinct points, 
regardless of whether it's within a familiar or uncharted environment (Tang and Yuta, 
2002). 
 
The popularity of mobile robots has grown significantly due to their expanding range of 
applications across diverse domains. These include security surveillance, health monitoring 
within homes, entertainment, research, education, and more (Phala et al., 2016). 
Surveillance robots, in particular, have found their way into domestic settings for everyday 
use. They offer straightforward deployment, often connecting to home Wi-Fi networks or 
smart environments. This allows them to report and monitor ongoing activities within the 
surroundings. They have undergone further design enhancements to undertake tasks like 
house cleaning and precise object placement as required. Notably, home robots are now 
being utilized by the elderly during emergency situations. This application highlights how 
these robots contribute to technology that identifies and responds promptly to urgent events, 
promoting safety and convenience (Berns and Mehdi, 2010). Another burgeoning arena for 
mobile robots is education. Educational robotics primarily aims to develop robots that aid 
users in acquiring practical, didactic, and cognitive skills. Additionally, this method also acts 
as a catalyst for fostering curiosity in research and science by means of a wide array of 
activities designed to bolster particular realms of knowledge and skills. The integration of 
mobile robots has expanded not only within tertiary-level institutions and scientific research 
centers but also into lower education levels, including primary and secondary schools. As a 
result, people worldwide have gained a deeper understanding of mobile robots (Crnokic et 
al., 2017). 
 
2.1.4. Challenges Of Mobile Robot  
The escalating demand for varied applications has solidified the indispensability of 
autonomous mobile robots. However, despite their promise and potential, achieving peak

with, a fact that has piqued growing interest among researchers. Among the primary

and obstacle avoidance (Alatise and Hancke, 2020). 
 
2.2. OPTITRACK SYSTEM ACCURACY EVALUATION 
 
The outcome of the database search showed that previously there were different authors 
(Thewlis et al. 2013; Aurand et al. 2017; Carse et al. 2013; Hansen et al. 2014 and Furtado et 
al. 2018) have already attempted to validate the accuracy of OptiTrack systems. Out of these 
studies, three research investigations (Thewlis et al. 2013; Carse et al. 2013; and Hansen et 
al. 2014) primarily focused on utilizing the OptiTrack camera system to measure the 
established distances of firmly positioned markers and subsequently assessing the deviations 
from the theoretical distance whereas Furtado et al. 2018 focussed the comparative analysis 
of four different optitrack systems in relation to tracking accuracy, workspace volume, 
marker size and camera range. Minor relative marker movements were assessed by Aurand 
et al. (2017) in different zones within a substantial capture volume. The significant 
information obtained from the mentioned research studies comparisons is summarised in  
table 1. 
 
A comparison study was conducted by Thewlis et al. (2013) between the accuracy of a 12-
camera Vicon MX-F20 camera system and a 12-camera Flex:V100R2 OptiTrack system, 
both operating at 100 Hz. The comparison encompassed static and dynamic linear accuracy 
(using the marker distance method) as well as gait kinematics. For this comparison study, 
they concurrently recorded the coordinates of markers positioned on anatomical landmarks 
and computed joint angles. Although they came to the conclusion pointed to a comparable 
accuracy between the OptiTrack system and Vicon for gait kinematics, they discovered that 
differences exceeding 3° in the knee and hip angles between the two systems. Their findings 
also indicated that absolute percentage errors did not exceed 1% from the established 
distances (Thewlis et al. 2013). 
 
A comparative analysis conducted by Carse et al. (Carse et al. 2013) that included an 8-
camera Optitrack Flex:V100R2 system (100 Hz), an 8-camera Vicon 612 system (120 Hz), 
and a 12-camera Vicon MX system (100 Hz), utilizing the marker distance method. The 
systems were described based on the coefficients of variation, the most significant 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
 
- 16 - 
differences between the systems, and the presence of trajectory gaps using the mean vector 
magnitudes of four markers attached to a rigid cluster. During their investigation, both the 
Vicon 612 and Optitrack systems yielded similar results, yet the Vicon MX system stood out 
as the superior choice. Vicon 612 vs Vicon MX had the biggest mean vector magnitude 
discrepancies, at 2.1 percent, and Optitrack vs ViconMX, at 2.2 percent. Vicon MX had a 
coefficient of variation of 0.3 percent, Vicon 612 had a coefficient of variation of 2.5 
percent, and Optitrack had a coefficient of variation of 2.3 percent (Carse et al. 2013). 
 
The marker distance approach was tested by Hansen et al. (2014) utilizing a 10-camera 
OptiTrack 250e video system (250 Hz). This testing encompassed both static and dynamic 
conditions, comprising both the motorised rotation of a marker cluster and stationary marker 
clusters. Nine marker distances collected from stationary and rotating rigid bodies were used 
to evaluate the measurement precision. The benchmark system utilized was an 8-camera 
Vicon M2 camera system (250 Hz). Given that the raw variance in measurements between 
each system remained modest (average: 0.58 (standard deviation: 0.25) mm for the nine 
distances ranging from 64 to 500 mm), their conclusion suggested that the system proved to 
be appropriately reliable for biomechanical investigations and related applications (Hansen 
et al. 2014). The accuracy of a 42-camera OptiTrack Prime 41 system (180 Hz) have 
investigated by Aurand et al. (2017) measuring the small relative displacement of an 
individual marker. This measurement was conducted across various positions within a 
substantial measurement area. The assessment involved measuring the 3D error against a 
ThorLabs LTS300 linear motion stage, which displaced the marker with a 5 μm off-axis 
error within a 100 mm range. Their conclusion indicated that for 97 percent of the 10.4 mm 
× 6.5 mm measuring area, the most significant error in these relative movements is below 
200 μm, and it only exceeds 1 mm error at the periphery of the measurement area. 
 
According to Hansen et al. (2014), Carse et al. (2013) and Thewlis et al. (2013), there were 
no apparent conflicts of interest that would have unreasonably influenced the findings of 
either study. In contrast, the manufacturer of the OptiTrack systems provided the reference 
gear and provided technical advice for the measurements in the instance of which could have 
given the final measures a positive bias.

2.3. LOCALISATION STRATEGIES FOR MOBILE ROBOT: 
 
Localization stands as a crucial element within the realm of diverse autonomous mobile 
robots. To ensure proficient navigation and effective adaptation, a robust localization 
strategy is imperative. Depending on the available initial position data, the localization 
challenge is categorized into global positioning/localization, position tracking and the 
kidnapped robot issues (Panigrahi and Bisoy, 2022) . Regarding position tracking, the initial 
positioning of the robot is established, aiming to track the robot's location at each moment as 
it maneuvers within the surroundings. As the robot navigates, the localization algorithm 
employs its previous position to continually update its current coordinates, facilitated by 
vigilant route monitoring. Position tracking draws upon odometry and sensor data for this 
purpose. However, under significant uncertainty, the robot may struggle to establish accurate 
localization. Conversely, in position tracking, the initial assumption of robot (the best 
estimate of its initial state) conforms to a distribution. Relocation pertains to tracking a 
mobile robot even when its initial pose is unknown, leading to another class of localization 
challenges. This paves the way for a unique set of localization challenges known as global 
localization. Within this classification, the robot is devoid of any initial position knowledge. 
According to Negenborn (2003), the robot possesses the capability to ascertain its global 
position within the environment. In certain scenarios, the robot may find itself tracked to an 
arbitrary location during pose tracking an unknown site. This phenomenon falls under the 
purview of the kidnapped robot problem, where the robot is aware of being kidnapped. 
Consequently, the recovery from such situations, termed "Kidnap recovery," is imperative 
for autonomous robots. Often, the robot employs current sensor data to estimate its pose. 
Fundamentally, resolving the relocation challenge relies on finding the best alignment 
between known data and sensor-generated data. For an autonomous robot, the ability to 
simultaneously manage pose tracking and relocation is crucial. It should possess the 
capability to recognize instances of kidnapping and apply the appropriate relocation 
technique to recover its pose. A comprehensive analysis of the existing localization systems 
is outlined in the table, and a comparative evaluation of mobile robot algorithms, based on

Later, research into the Kalman filter carried out by NASA's Dynamic Analysis Branch, 
under the leadership of Dr. Schmidt, played an unquestionably pivotal role in the 
development of what is now recognized as the Extended Kalman Filter (EKF). The 
Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) is an advanced variant of the traditional Kalman filter 
designed to address estimation problems in nonlinear systems (Hao et al. 2022). The EKF 
expands the original Kalman filter's capabilities to nonlinear systems by linearizing the 
system dynamics and measurement equations around the present estimate, whereas the 
==PAGE:24==
 
 
 
- 23 - 
original Kalman filter is very effective for linear systems. Using past estimates as a starting 
point, the EKF repeatedly predicts the system's state and then refining that prediction using 
actual observations. Even in the presence of noise and uncertainty, it uses data from the 
system's dynamic model with actual observations to predict the system's true state (Urrea 
and Agramonte, 2021). Both the EKF and KF have applications in the field of robotics, 
being used for tasks like trajectory tracking, position estimation in manipulator robots, 
Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM), and object detection. The specific 
application depends on whether the model exhibits linearity or nonlinearity 
 
2.4.1. Utilization Of The Kalman Filter In The Field Of Robotics: 
 
The Apollo space programme especially marked the first use of the Kalman filter and its 
expansion in the field of guided navigation, ever since the Kalman Filter and its variations 
have been used for a variety of activities, including manufacturing, instrumentation for 
nuclear plants, demographic modelling, and all types of navigation (including air, land, and 
sea navigation). It is used to estimate the angular velocity of a motor in the low-speed range 
and when random external disturbances are present, as indicated by (Shin and Kwon, 2015). 
The benefit of this Kinematic Kalman Filter (KKF) variant is that by adding proportional 
action, the measurement may be continued right away, and integral action lowers the 
stationary status error.  
Numerous other types of robots employ the KF or its subsequent advancements, including 
localization, a pivotal factor in attaining autonomous, reliable, and resilient navigation by 
determining vehicle location and orientation at all times. In addition to object identification 
and tracking, another significant application area for this type of filter is trajectory or path 
tracking. It serves as a predictive module, computing motion vectors for objects in motion. 
As per the research conducted by Hartley et al. (2018), contact-aided RI-EKF can be 
employed to estimate the state of a legged robot. Similarly, this principle can be adapted to 
manipulator robots, given proper validation of the points. Utilizing a biped robot from the 
Cassie series for dynamic simulation, a comparison is made between the performance of the 
Q-EKF (Quaternion-Extended Kalman Filter), frequently employed in this robot category, 
and the performance of the RI-EKF (Right Invariant-Extended Kalman Filter), which 
encounters initialization challenges. 
 
Manipulator robots are progressively gaining traction in industrial operations (Ghiasi et al. 
2015). A recommendation is made to incorporate the AREKF (Adaptive Robust Extended

Kalman Filter), which combines the EKF with Lyapunov's discontinuous control theory. 
This approach is employed in managing a 2-DoF manipulator robot, specifically for 
predicting the position and velocity of individual joints. This yields enhanced performance 
in trajectory-tracking tests compared to relying solely on the EKF. 
 
Robot teams are progressively finding applications across various industries, including 
military and rescue operations. Reily et al. (2018) propose the utilization of extended 
Kalman filters to predict forthcoming skeleton trajectories. This approach results in the 
creation of a B-IPOD (Bio-Inspired Predictive Orientation Decomposition) representation, 
possessing crucial attributes for reducing the impact of noisy skeletal observation data and 
predicting ongoing behaviours. 
 
A new online technique based on DKF (Discrete Kalman Filter) is provided in a recent paper

investigated by the robot. 
 
Numerous studies have made an effort to show that this KF variant is preferable to the EKF

are utilised in a robot system, where five ultrasonic sensors and an algorithm are used to

Medicine is one other area where the EKF may be used. To tackle the estimate problem for

AUVs (Autonomous Underwater Vehicles), which are used in complicated situations and

3.1.1. Theory Behind OptiTrack: 
OptiTrack is a motion capture technology that uses specialized cameras and markers to track 
the movement of objects or people in 3D space. It is commonly used in animation, visual 
effects, sports science, robotics, and virtual reality. OptiTrack cameras are typically set up in 
a room or studio, and reflective markers are placed on the objects or people that need 
tracking. The cameras then capture the position of the markers in real-time and use advanced 
algorithms to calculate the precise location of each marker in 3D space. This data is then 
used to create a virtual representation of the object or person's movement, which can be used 
for various purposes. OptiTrack offers various camera models and software packages to suit 
different use cases, from small-scale systems for individual researchers to large-scale 
systems for professional motion capture studios. 
3.1.2. Software Used: 
The Motive software from OptiTrack is employed to process camera data, resulting in 
precise global 3D positions, marker IDs, rotational data (relative to the object's local 
reference), and hyper accurate skeletal tracking. OptiTrack's precision long-range cameras 
adeptly track passive or retro reflective markers, encompassing various forms such as 
spheres, hemispheres, and flat reflective discs. Our study employed spherical markers to 
monitor the robot's position.  
Motive+OptiTrack cameras provide the most advanced real-time tracking capabilities for 
both humans and objects in current use. In our OptiTrack setup, we utilized Motive 3.0, a 
precision solver that effectively captures the precise skeletal motion of tracked objects. This 
results in heightened confidence and nuanced performance capture, which is particularly 
beneficial for character animation. 
Motive also serves as a resilient solver, excelling in inaccurate marker labelling and skeletal 
tracking even in scenarios where numerous markers are obscured from cameras or 
temporarily unavailable. This capability ensures a higher level of dependable tracking data 
and significantly minimizes the need for manual adjustments across a diverse range of 
applications. 
In human motion tracking, a solver essentially pertains to the computational procedure for 
estimating individual bones' pose (6 degrees of freedom). This estimation is derived from the 
markers' actual positions at each measurement frame.

3.2. OVERVIEW OF SMALL MOBILE ROBOT (SMR) 
The SMR stands as a PC mounted on an aluminium framework, equipped with wheels, 
actuators, and sensors to navigate and engage with its surroundings. Its power source is a 
12V battery. Establishing connectivity with the institute's computer system occurs through a 
wireless network. Operating on the PC, the program functions in cycles of 10 ms, 
establishing the sampling time. 
Within each cycle, the PC retrieves sensor data, performs necessary computations and 
logging, and generates control signals. The SMR incorporates two busses that link the PC 
with the peripherals. While both busses utilize the PC's RS-232 interface, one incorporates a 
converter and employs the RS-485 interface to communicate with multiple devices sharing 
the same bus. A visual representation of the SMR's components is depicted in the following 
figure.

Enabling individual control of the wheels allows the SMR to execute turns. Positioned 
between the PC and each motor, a Pulse Width Modulator (PWM) and an H-bridge exist. 
Gearing interconnects the motors with the wheels, while two caster wheels are at the SMR's 
front. 
The program executed on the PC transmits a duty cycle value. Denoted as D, this 8-bit 
binary number encompasses a spectrum of values from -128 to 127. Within this range, -128 
corresponds to full-speed reverse, 0 signifies a complete stop, and 127 indicates maximum 
forward speed for the motors. 
However, to safeguard the SMR from potential harm, the output is constrained within the 
range of -38 to 38. The PWM harnesses this duty cycle value to generate a modulated signal, 
which subsequently governs the H-bridge. The H-bridge switches a 12-volt supply across 
the motors. The mean voltage imparted by the H-bridge to the motor corresponds to the 
value of D. Remarkably, the motors' time constant measures 25.6 ms, substantially 
exceeding the time constant of the PWM signal (5.56×10-5 ms). So, the resultant speed of 
the motors is as if they received the DC value equal to the average AC value. 
Each drive wheel features an encoder, producing 2000 ticks for every complete revolution. 
These encoders transmit their values via the RS-485 bus to the PC. The wheels possess a 
radius of 0.0325 meters, and the distance separating them is 0.26 meters. Utilizing the 
encoder readings alongside the wheel radius and separation, it becomes possible to derive 
the SMR's velocity, the individual wheel velocities, and the odometry data. 
Beneath the front section of the vehicle, the line sensor is positioned. This sensor gauges the 
light reflectivity of the underlying surface. Comprising eight light sensors, it spans a 13 cm 
line with even distribution. Adjacent to these sensors, along the edges, were nine red LEDs. 
These LEDs emit modulated light to mitigate background noise. Each sensor furnishes an 8-
bit value representing the received light intensity, which is conveyed to the PC via the RS-
232 serial interface. The light intensity data from these sensors can identify a line with a 
distinct reflection compared to the surface. 
The SMR has five IR (InfraRed) sensors to gauge proximity to nearby objects. These IR 
sensors offer precise measurements within approximately 5 cm to 60 cm. They establish a 
connection with the PC through the RS-485 serial interface. All sensors are affixed to the 
front of the SMR, with three oriented forward and the remaining two positioned on each 
side. Leveraging these sensors, the SMR is adept at evading obstacles and discerning basic

3.6. KALMAN FILTER 
Mobile robot localization is always a critical issue in autonomous robot systems. 
Global localization enables the robot to ﬁnd its position on a map based on the 
information acquired from the environment. Only when the robot precisely knows where 
it is, it can plan a proper movement in the next step. Kalman ﬁltering allows us to 
combine the uncertainties regarding the current state of the robot (i.e. where it is located 
and in which direction it is looking) (Kong et al., 2006) 
In this project we try to implement Extended Kalman filter on both the techniques to 
see on which of the methods it tries to smooth the noise caused by the sensors making it 
more relevant. The Kalman ﬁlter is implemented for integrating the robot movement 
detected by the encoder and the camera mounted on the ceiling in the lab where the 
experiment is conducted. Below is the description of the model on how the Extended 
Kalman filter is working. The methodology is described as follows.

A. State Space Model 
 
A state space model or state transition model is a mathematical equation that represents the 
motion of a robotic system from one-time step to the next. It describes how the current 
position (e.g. X, Y coordinate) and orientation (yaw angle γ) of the robot is affected by the 
given control inputs (velocity v). 
 
    
 
Fig 9: Robot position parameters in the global XY reference frame  
 
The yaw angle γ describes the rotation around the z-axis in the counterclockwise direction 
(from the x axis). The units for the yaw angle are typically radians.  
ω is the rotation rate (i.e. angular velocity or yaw rate) around the global z axis. Typically, 
the units of ω is radians per second.  
The velocity of the robot v is in terms of the robot reference frame (labeled xrobot and 
yrobot). 
The present study focused on differential drive robot. In Fig. 9, the robot is simply 
represented as a rectangle. Robot’s two wheels are parallel. The reference point is located 
equidistant between the two wheels to simplify the model dynamics. Differential drive 
means that each wheel has its own independent motor and can operate independently of each 
other. In a differential drive robot, the motors controlling each wheel are separate and each 
motor can spin the wheels at different speeds and directions (i.e., forward or backward). The 
robot position and orientation information were derived from the wheel odometry model.   
The relationship between the linear velocities of the left and right wheels (SL and SR) of a 
differential drive robot and its linear velocity (𝑣) and angular velocity (ω) can be described 
by the following equations: 
i) Velocity (𝑣) = (SL + SR) / 2;

left and right wheels.

proportional to the differences in the linear velocities of the left and right wheels. ‘d’ refers 
to the distance between the reference point and the wheels.

C. Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) theory 
A robot is attached with sensors that is capable of measure the robot position. But, the 
robot’s sensors aren’t 100% accurate. So, all sensor observations are processed through an 
Extended Kalman Filter theory (EKF) to smooth out noisy sensor measurements and 
calculate a better estimate of the state of the robot at each timestep t as the robot moves 
around in the world.  
The EKF is an algorithm that applies the knowledge of the physics of motion of the system 
(i.e. the state space model) to make small adjustments to (i.e. to filter) the actual sensor 
measurements (i.e. what the robot’s sensors actually observed) to reduce the amount of 
noise, and as a result, generate a better estimate of the state of a system. It calculates 
a weighted average of actual sensor measurements at the current timestep t and predicted 
sensor measurements at the current timestep t to generate a better current state estimate. 
The regular Kalman Filter is aimed to generate estimates of the state just like the Extended 
Kalman Filter. However, the Kalman Filter only works when the state space model (i.e. state 
transition function) is linear; that is, the function that governs the transition from one state to 
the next can be plotted as a line on a graph). Whereas the Extended Kalman Filter was 
developed to enable the Kalman Filter to be applied to systems that have nonlinear dynamics 
like our mobile robot. 
 
EKF Algorithm 
It has two stages, a predict phase and an update (correction) phase.  
a) Predict Step 
==PAGE:41==
 
 
 
- 40 - 
• Using the state space model of the robotics system, predict the state estimate at time 
k based on the state estimate at time k-1 and the control input applied at time k-1. 
• Predict the state covariance estimate based on the previous covariance and some 
noise. 
b) Update (Correct) Step 
• Calculate the difference between the actual sensor measurements at timestep k and 
measurement model derived predicted sensor measurements at current timestep k. 
• Calculate the measurement residual covariance. 
• Calculate the near-optimal Kalman gain. 
• Calculate an updated state estimate for time k. 
• Update the state covariance estimate for time k.

5. DISCUSSION 
 
In essence, the primary objective of undertaking this comparative study on localization 
systems for monitoring mobile robots was to discern the superior approach in tracking 
and analyzing the movements of these robots. The investigation aimed to provide a 
comprehensive understanding of which tracking method yields more accurate and 
insightful results when juxtaposed with other techniques. 
Through a meticulous examination of the OptiTrack vision system and the Basler 
monocular camera vision system, both enriched with the Extended Kalman Filter 
(EKF) algorithm, the research sought to unravel the strengths and weaknesses inherent 
in each system. The ultimate goal was to ascertain which system offers more robust and 
reliable results in monitoring the position of mobile robots equipped with markers. 
The significance of this comparative analysis lies in its potential to guide decision-
makers and researchers in selecting the most effective and efficient localization system 
based on their specific requirements. By scrutinizing the performance of these systems 
under various conditions and scenarios, the study aimed to contribute valuable insights 
into the nuanced differences that could influence the choice of a tracking method for 
monitoring mobile robots. 
The present study aimed at a comprehensive evaluation of the OptiTrack system's 
operational accuracy under a 16-camera setup, encompassing both static and dynamic 
experiments. The static experiment, involving manual movement of the robot to four 
distinct positions, demonstrated an impressive level of precision. The recorded error, 
measuring less than 1 cm, underlines the robust accuracy of the OptiTrack system in 
capturing static positions. Further, the assessment of robot tracking error, revealing a 
minimal range between 1 cm to 2 cm, substantiates the overall accuracy of the system 
in dynamic scenarios. 
In the dynamic experiment, the OptiTrack system exhibited its capability to accurately 
record the robot's trajectory as it traversed programmed paths, including a rectangle and 
a zigzag pattern. Real-time data acquisition demonstrated a high degree of precision, 
highlighting the system's effectiveness in capturing dynamic movements. Notably, our 
study distinguishes itself by employing a 16-camera setup, which, to our knowledge, 
has not been previously explored in existing literature. 
==PAGE:70==
 
 
 
69 
A comparative analysis with prior studies, conducted by Thewlis et al. (2013), Aurand 
et al. (2017), Carse et al. (2013), Hansen et al. (2014), and Furtado et al. (2018), reveals 
a unique contribution. Previous researchers focused on camera setups ranging from 8 to 
18, emphasizing marker distance measurement and deviation assessment. Our study 
diverges by concentrating on a 16-camera OptiTrack system, specifically evaluating 
accuracy under varying conditions such as light darkness and movement on uneven 
paths. This nuanced approach enables a more targeted understanding of the system's 
performance in conditions mimicking real-world scenarios. 
Comparative studies by Thewlis et al. (2013) and Carse et al. (2013) with 12 and 8 
camera systems respectively noted significant differences, trajectory gaps, and errors 
ranging from 1-2%. In contrast, our study reports a consistently low mean error, 
suggesting the system's precise operation under both light and dark conditions. This 
result implies the system's reliability in positioning and tracking small mobile robots 
across diverse environmental settings. 
Despite the absence of a direct comparison with earlier studies due to variations in 
camera numbers, our study introduces a critical component by implementing the 
Extended Kalman filter algorithm. This analysis aimed to assess the system's ability to 
mitigate sensor noise. Interestingly, the results demonstrated a minimal robot tracking 
error, reinforcing the robustness of the OptiTrack system even when subjected to noise, 
thus enhancing its suitability for real-world applications. 
This study extends the understanding of OptiTrack system performance by exploring a 
16-camera setup under diverse conditions. The demonstrated accuracy in both static 
and dynamic scenarios, coupled with the effective noise reduction through the 
Extended Kalman filter, positions the OptiTrack system as a reliable tool for precise 
robot positioning and tracking. 
The utilization of Basler camera tracking with Aruco markers on the SMR presented an 
alternative method for effective mobile robot tracking. However, it is noteworthy that 
our approach involved extracting recorded video data from the Basler camera rather 
than real-time data consideration. The subsequent calculation of robot positions using a 
Python algorithm revealed occasional discrepancies in determining the robot's position 
when tracking Aruco markers on the SMR. Notably, the robot tracking error exhibited 
greater dispersion and higher standard deviations when compared to the OptiTrack 
==PAGE:71==

system. 
 
A critical observation from our experiments is the efficacy of the Extended Kalman 
Filter (EKF) model in minimizing errors for both the OptiTrack system and the Basler 
camera. The high variability in robot tracking errors observed with the Basler camera 
tracking system was substantially mitigated by the application of the EKF model. This 
underscores the effectiveness of the EKF in enhancing the accuracy and reliability of 
position tracking systems. 
In the context of our study, the Kalman filter-based localization, particularly the 
Extended Kalman Filter, emerged as a robust solution for addressing challenges in 
position tracking when compared to other tracking methods. The implementation of a 
state space model, encompassing space and observation models, further refined the 
determination of the state position of the model robot. This was particularly evident in 
scenarios where the robot demonstrated movement with a velocity of 0.3m/s, traversing 
horizontally along the X-axis in a global reference frame. 
The adoption of the state space model in our study illustrated how changes in control 
inputs, such as velocity, impact the current position (X & Y coordinates) and 
orientation of the robot in the world. This approach aligns with a rich history of 
localization approaches utilizing EKF, with more than 20 researchers applying various 
models since 1992, as documented by Panigrahi and Bisoy (2022). These models have 
addressed diverse applications, encompassing probabilistic MCL, vision-based 
tracking, GPS-based approaches, and intelligent space systems, each with its own set of 
advantages and disadvantages. 
 
Comparatively, our study contributes to this landscape by highlighting the effectiveness 
of the EKF model in reducing noise and improving efficiency in mobile robot 
localization. While alternative approaches, such as Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO), 
have been proposed by researchers like Havangi (2019), our findings suggest that EKF 
remains a reliable choice for precise mobile robot position estimates, outperforming 
simpler methods like the Kalman Filter (KF).

Kalman Filter in addressing challenges associated with mobile robot localization,

6. CONCLUSION 
In conclusion, this thesis systematically investigated and compared two localization 
methods, namely the OptiTrack vision system and the Basler monocular camera vision 
system, both augmented with the Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) algorithm. The 
primary focus of the research was to evaluate their efficacy in providing accurate 
positioning for a small mobile robot equipped with markers. 
The OptiTrack system, employing a motion capture approach with multiple cameras, 
demonstrated exceptional precision and accuracy in tracking the movement of markers 
attached to the robot. Its capability to capture images from various angles contributed to 
its high performance. On the other hand, the Basler camera vision system, utilizing a 
single camera and Aruco markers, emerged as a cost-effective and flexible alternative 
to OptiTrack. 
The findings of this study revealed that both OptiTrack and the Basler camera vision 
system, when integrated with the EKF algorithm, can effectively localize mobile 
robots. The research also established that the accuracy of the Basler camera could be 
significantly enhanced through the implementation of the EKF algorithm. Furthermore, 
the application of EKF to the OptiTrack system contributed to minimizing errors in 
measurements. 
In conclusion, both OptiTrack and Basler camera systems prove to be viable options for 
localizing mobile robots, each with its distinct advantages. The optimal selection for a 
particular application should be guided by a careful consideration of the specific 
demands and constraints of the given scenario. This includes a thoughtful evaluation of 
factors such as required accuracy, budgetary considerations, and the flexibility needed 
in the localization process.
