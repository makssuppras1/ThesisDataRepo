Summary
Availability of forecasts for smart energy systems is becoming an increasingly im-
portant part in society, as still more energy comes from the unsteady production of
renewable energy sources. The focus of this thesis, is the energy management of a
residential smart house.
A smart house is able to automatically heat the house, buy and sell electricity
from the market, charge the electrical vehicle (EV) and harvest electricity from solar
panels on the roof. The use of weather forecasts enable the owner to make more
optimal decisions about e.g. when to buy additional electricity, due to insufficient
amounts generated from the solar panels.
In this thesis we formulate models for climate processes, that play a key part in de-
scribing the important disturbances for a residential smart house. These are the solar
radiation and the ambient air temperature. All models are formulated in continuous
time, since this is often more intuitive and requires fewer parameters. We introduce a
model for a smart house suggested by the literature. Furthermore we investigate the
use and effects of the short-term weather forecasts in model based control of the for-
mulated smart house. Lastly we explore an opportunity of simplifying the model for
a residential street of smart houses, which can greatly reduce the computational time.
The short-term weather forecast scheme was shown to significantly improve the
control solutions for the smart house compared to a naive forecasting scheme. How-
ever it was also clear, that meteorological forecasts are needed for long-term forecasts.
More work is required on this e.g. to find the point in time, when meteorological
forecasts are preferred over short-term.
The aggregation scheme for solving the computational problem involved with the
residential street did not show to work for smart houses having different physical
properties, fx if they have different sizes, isolated walls, different heat pumps etc.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
As societies transition to greater parts of sustainable technology and energy, various
challenges follow. In Denmark, an ongoing discussion is the capacity of the electric
grid, as still more and more devices become electrified, such as our cars or heating
systems/devices.
Recent technology allows our electrical cars (EVs) to charge within few minutes
with the so-called ”super chargers”. But studies from e.g. (Energi, 2019) show that
the current capacity of the electrical grid in Denmark is not able to supply all with
the required amount of electricity at the same time. This ultimately leads to insuffi-
cient delivery of electricity to the end users. To avoid this scenario requires expensive
replacements of the cables in the ground.
Another and perhaps more attractive solution to this problem, is to completely
avoid large peak amounts of electricity demand, by making the consumers use energy
more equally distributed across the day. Such solutions do not yet exist in practice.
Many people and companies have however shared their visions on so-called ”smart
houses”, such as Elon Musk. Lambert (2018) briefly describes the potential future
smart house. It consists of an efficient heating, ventilation and air-condition (HVAC)
system, an EV, stationary batteries to store surplus energy to be used when the sup-
ply is low and photovoltaic (PV) cells on the roof.
The literature proposes many models for similar smart houses. This thesis will
work with a simple state-space model based on stochastic differential equations. H.
Madsen and J. Holst (1995) proposes a simple model describing the heat dynamics
of a house. Halvgaard et al. (2012) uses this model and describes the combined sim-
ple heat pump model for a single house. Schlüter et al. (2019) uses the heat pump
model and describes the smart house model including stationary batteries and an EV.
Optimal control of such a smart house requires decent forecasts of the influencing
variables. This is in particular the electricity prices and the local weather. Two of
the most important weather elements is the solar radiation and the ambient air tem-
perature. The solar radiation influences how much electricity the PV cells generate,

1.2
Introduction to EMPC
Model predictive control (MPC) in general is well described by the literature, e.g. by
Maciejowski, 2002. Economic MPC (EMPC) is a kind of MPC that typically focuses
on minimizing some economic cost, hence the name. The overall goal of MPC is to
control a system output x(t), formulated as a model, in a certain way specified by the
user. The way the controller interacts with the system is through an input, u(t), and
is typically the only way to alter the system. However, the system is often disturbed
by some disturbances, d(t), which cannot be controlled. If these disturbances are not
taken into account and modeled properly, the controller can only to some extend,
control the system optimally.
In the case of the smart house model, the disturbance variables are the ambient air
temperature, Ta(t), and the solar radiation, IG(t). If these are not properly described,
the controller might not know how much electricity the PV cells produce tomorrow,

1.3.1
The weather disturbances
To describe the key weather disturbances require good models for the solar radiation
and ambient air temperature. However, these components are naturally correlated
and depends on multiple climate processes. A key process is the cloud cover.
The cloud cover is accumulated condensed water in the air, that blocks solar rays.
So the solar radiation is directly dependent on the amount of clouds. Furthermore
the ambient air temperature is dependent on both the long- and short wave radiation.
It is hence very key to have a sufficient cloud cover model in order to obtain a proper
weather model.
This thesis aims at proposing models for short term weather forecasts (or statis-
tical forecasts). That involves the cloud cover, the solar radiation, the net radiation
and lastly the ambient air temperature to form the disturbances ˆdk = [Ta,k, IG,k].
Multiple challenges hereof arise, one of which is the natural annual differences of the
weather behavior, which requires non-homogeneous models. We deal with this by
restricting the modeling to concern data from March.
Compared to meteorological forecasts, the short term weather forecasts perform
best up till 4-10 hours ahead in time. After this point, the meteorological forecasts

1.4
Structure of the report
This thesis has the following structure:
Chapter 2 deals with models for the cloud cover. This includes first a discrete- and
later a continuous state-space models. The latter model has the advantage of fitting
into a continuous disturbance model framework.
Chapter 3 presents models for the solar radiation based on kernel regression. Com-
pared to recent work on this, the model also proposes a thorough model for the
deviation. The chapter then introduces linear models for the net radiation and the
ambient air temperature.
Chapter 4 introduces the combined continuous state-space model for the smart house.
This includes a model for the indoor room, the heat pump and the stationary and
EV battery. Lastly the chapter describes the disturbance model framework and how
it plays into the smart house model.
Chapter 5 introduces the MPC setup and optimization problem for the smart house
model. Afterwards the statistical forecasts are used in the control of a simplified
smart house model, compared to more naive forecasts to illustrate the effect of the
forecasts more clearly. Around 7 months of data is used to compare long term behav-
ior.
Chapter 6 present a small simulation of the full smart house using statistical forecasts
compared to the naive forecasts.
Chapter 7 presents a model for a residential street of smart houses and shows how
to optimally control them. However the optimization problem quickly becomes large
and infeasible to solve computationally. This chapter compares simplified models,
using aggregation, to solve the optimization problem for control of a street of smart
houses.

CHAPTER 2
Cloud cover models
The cloud cover plays a huge role for statistical forecasts of solar radiation and am-
bient air temperature. Therefore a good model describing the cloud cover and its
dynamics are crucial for obtaining good statistical forecasts to be used for control.
This chapter introduces two kinds of models for the cloud cover. The first model bases
on previous approaches from the literature and is a discrete state-space model. The
data is discretely taken on the okta scale and a discrete state-space model is therefore
natural to employ. This model shows to be an overall sufficient model which is also
easy to interpret.
Even though the simple Markov model sufficiently describes the behavior, it is
not straight forward to use it in control-systems. The disturbance model framework
is easier to use, by calculating predictions using a Kalman filter. This chapter fits
a SDE for the cloud cover. However the design of the drift function of the SDE is
not at all trivial to formulate. However the dynamics and interpretations found in
the discrete state-space model shows special behavior of the cloud cover in each state.
This knowledge is very useful in the design process of the continuous state-space
model.
2.1
Introduction and problems
The type, height, and the amount of cover of the clouds at a given instance have a big
impact on both the solar radiation hitting a surface on the earth and on the ambient
air temperature. For example when a heavy cloud cover is present, much less direct
solar radiation gets through. This also impacts the temperature of the air compared
to a clear skies situation. In the case of clear skies, a certain fraction of the solar rays
hitting the ground is absorbed and heats up the ambient air. Therefore it is intuitive
to assume that the solar radiation and the outdoor temperature is correlated and
hence both are correlated with the cloud cover.
A good model for the cloud cover is hence necessary to obtain good models for
the weather in general. The cloud cover is measured on the okta scale. An okta
is an integer in the range from 0 to 9 where 0 is clear skies, 8 is full overcast and
9 is when the cloud cover is not observable. The latter case is e.g. in foggy conditions.

2.1.1
Hourly correlation
The cloud cover is from nature physically correlated. Clouds typically form due to
changing temperatures and humidities in the atmosphere. These changes are typi-
cally driven by larger global meteorological systems and formations. Therefore the
same kinds of clouds form at the same places at the same times.
Henrik Madsen, Spliid, and Thyregod (1985) explains that the cloud cover is gov-
erned by the generally high correlation between weather events. Figure 2.2 shows
the hourly autocorrelation function for the data over the 15 years. The correlation
over the lags look very smooth due to the large amount of data. A clear exponential
decay of the autocorrelation function indicates that Markov model is needed. Fig-
ure 2.3 shows the partial autocorrelation function for the same data. The large peak
in lag 1 confirms that a first order Markov model is sufficient in modeling almost all
correlation.

2.2
Discrete state space model
The litterature shows various times that a homogeneous first-order Markov model
sufficiently describes the behavior of the cloud cover, e.g. by (Henrik Madsen, Spliid,
and Thyregod, 1985). This means that the future cloud cover depends almost en-
tirely on the cloud cover present right now. Investigation has also shown the largest
variation in parameters in such models are on an annual scale. This work focuses
on the cloud cover in March only, therefore a fair assumption is to make the model
homogeneous. In the discrete (state) model, the states represent the oktas of the
cloud cover, i.e. 10 states in total.

okta directly above or below the given okta during the next infinitesimal time ∆t
or remain the same. A slightly modified birth and death process can describe this
process. (Pinsky and Karlin, 2011) describes a brith and death process in more detail.

The Lamperti transformation
State-dependent diffusion terms in SDEs give rise to problems in estimation and
simulation hereof. Møller and Henrik Madsen (2010) explains and outlines some of
the reason behind this
• Simulation of a SDE having state-dependent diffusion, the convergence of the
Euler-Maruyama method can be slow and can require small time steps
• State-dependent diffusion also gives rise to problems in estimation: however the
extended Kalman filter (EKF) is widely used in estimation due to its simplicity
regarding implementation

arise. One solution to this problem, is to employ higher order Kalman filters. An-
other solution is to use a transformation of the process into an equivalent process
with constant (or event unit) diffusion term. As Møller and Henrik Madsen (2010)

and cold and the sun is also present. The discrete cloud cover model was a natural
choice for a model, since the data and the state-space (the okta scale) is discrete. By
modeling the cloud cover as a Markov model with a state for each okta, the cloud

To make a controller of a smart house perform as good as possible, it is crucial to
describe and forecast the disturbances well. The solar radiation together with the am-
bient temperature are responsible for the weather disturbances for the smart house
considered in this thesis. They dictate how much electricity the PV cells generate
and how much heating is needed. What is probably imaginable, is that the outdoor
temperature and the solar radiation are inherently physically correlated variables due
to their mutual dependence on the cloud cover. In this chapter the aim is to develop
a continuous-time stochastic model for these elements to be used to better control a
smart house.
An overall picture of the models required and their interactions are illustrated in
Figure 3.1. All models are either directly or indirectly dependent on the cloud cover,
which chapter 2 develops models for. Investigations will show that a dynamic model
is required hereof. This chapter proposes a new non-parametric approach, based on
kernel regression, to model the direct and diffuse radiation respectively and then to
model the global radiation. The ambient air temperature requires a model for the net
radiation - that is all the short-wave radiation at the surface of Earth. This chapter
deals with models for March only, due to the annual variation of the climate dynamics.

3.2
The global solar radiation
The term global solar radiation covers all the short wave radiation at the surface of
the earth. That is all the radiation which influences photo-voltaic cells and transfers
large amounts of energy when absorbed into objects. It is therefore also responsible
for heating a house from inside if the rays are allowed to enter through a window or
door etc. The aim is to obtain good models to describe the global solar radiation. The
work so far on this matter ranges from simple polynomial fits of data to more complex
black-box models including neural networks, see e.g. (Sharma et al., 2016; Ehnberg
and Bollen, 2005; Yang, Jirutitijaroen, and Walsh, 2012; Shamim et al., 2015). The
work in this thesis proposes a non-parametric model based on kernel regression, which
proves to perform well. Further investigations show that the noise model also needs

4.3
Extended battery model
Earlier attempts at modeling a smart home with batteries, see (Halvgaard et al., 2012;
Schlüter et al., 2019), uses a simple integrator for battery models. This assumes that
the SOC is directly observed from the battery. However it is well known, (Bhangu
et al., 2005), that the SOC of a battery is not directly observable but depends on the
open circuit voltage (OCV), that is the electrical voltage between the terminals of the
battery when nothing is connected to the battery. This section shall also refer to this
voltage as the bulk voltage, VCb. The literature also shows multiple times that the
SOC depends non-linearly on the bulk voltage, see e.g. (Zhang et al., 2015; Bhangu
et al., 2005).
Figure 4.2 shows a sketch of the non-linear dependence between the OCV and
the SOC for a typical battery. The graph takes inspiration from Zhang et al. (2015)
and displays essentially the same graph. The graph in general needs experimental
data to be found. Even though the specific graph is battery-specific, and therefore is
individual for each battery, it is widely accepted that the graph has the same overall
shape. The graph shows some large non-linearities in the end of the support of the
SOC. Below and above 0.1 and 0.9 respectively the dependence is very non-linear.
However between 0.1 and 0.9 the dependence is close to linear. This indicates that a
linear model can be suitable for estimation of the SOC if the battery is restricted to
operate at values between 0.1 and 0.9.

Conclusion and summary
This section presented the full model for the smart house including the heat pump

Model predictive control (MPC) has been successfully adopted and applied in the
industry due to its simplicity and intuitive use, see (Maciejowski, 2002). The basic
idea of MPC is to use a model describing a given system, to be able to predict the fu-
ture behavior of this system and act accordingly. Via optimization the optimal input
can be found in order to put the system into a pre-specified state. The model intro-
duced in chapter 4 hence enables MPC to be employed for controlling the smart house.
In this chapter, the basic theory of MPC used in this thesis will be described
and reviewed. Afterwards the specific control problem concerning the smart house
is introduced and explained. The work of this thesis has greatly been focusing on
developing better models for the weather disturbances. As this chapter will show,
better forecasts will improve the performance of the MPC by allowing more optimal
solutions to the problem. This chapter uses the weather models developed in the
chapter 2 and 3 in a MPC framework to evaluate the performance. All optimization
uses the conelp method from CVXOPT.

7.4
Conclusion and summary
This chapter presented the computational difficulties of controlling a residential street,
while under the constraint of restricted rate of electricity to the street. This chapter
then presented an aggregation scheme, that takes advantage of the linearity of the
smart house model. The aggregated model corresponds to control an average of all
the smart houses. The idea is, that if the required electricity for aggregated model
for times t ∈[0, ∞[ is equal to that of the full model, then the aggregation can save
a lot of computational time and calculate the same solution.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
This thesis has developed a combined disturbance framework for use in model based
control settings to forecast statistical short term predictions. All models have been
formulated and estimated in continuous time, since this usually requires fewer param-
eters and is more intuitive.
The weather disturbance model required a cloud cover model. After a lot of model
iterations, a suitable SDE was found. Afterwards, non-parametric kernel regression
was used to estimate the global solar radiation for each cloud cover together with
models for the residuals.
The above models allowed us to construct linear models for the net radiation and
the ambient air temperature. These models, however, are taken from the literature
and are very simple - more sophisticated models are probably required and should be
looked into in future work.
The thesis afterwards showed the effects of using the statistical forecasts com-
pared to a more naive forecasting scheme in smart house control. This showed to
significantly improve the solutions.
Lastly, the thesis investigated an opportunity of simplifying the control problem
of a combined residential street by aggregation. This showed to deviate from the full
control problem by quite much at times.
The following bullets list possible improvements and future work:
• Improve on the weather models in general - especially the net radiation and the
ambient air temperature model.
• Due to the annual variation of the climate processes, the weather models have
only been fitted for March data. An obvious extension is to fit the disturbance
model for the rest of the months.
• An important work in the future, in order to apply this in real smart houses,
is to consider meteorological forecasts. Examination of when to switch from
statistical to meteorological forecasts is needed.
