2 Abstract 
Drug induced liver injury (DILI) is a frequent cause of late-stage safety related failures of small 
molecule drug candidates, while idiosyncratic DILI is the most frequent reason for withdrawal of drugs 
from the market. Early identification of drug candidates with potential for causing DILI would 
therefore be desirable. Cell based assays using immortalized hepatocytes for detection of DILI have 
low predictive value, and even primary human hepatocytes (PHH), considered a gold standard, quickly 
loose hepatic features due to dedifferentiation, when cultivated in monolayer. Animal models, 
meanwhile, may not predict human DILI due to differences in sensitivity and hepatic metabolic 
activities. Compared to the environment of cells cultivated in monolayers, hepatocytes grown in 3D 
culture as aggregates, experience an environment that closer mimic their natural environment defined 
by complex cell to cell interactions within hepatic tissues. Until recently however, 3D cell culture was 
not compatible with high-throughput assays due to variability of cellular aggregates known as 
spheroids, and due to limitations in liquid handling.  
In this project we will evaluate whether recent technological developments within 3D cell culture 
systems and high content imaging makes it feasible to establish a high throughput and high content 
assay for early prediction of hepatotoxic potential of small molecule compounds on hepatic spheroids. 
To assess feasibility, we will develop the assay that if successfully implemented will be used to 
compare the effects of known hepatotoxic compounds on hepatic cell lines grown in monolayer and 
3D culture.

3 Dansk resumé 
Lægemiddel inducerede leverskader (DILI), er en af de hyppigste sikkerhedsrelaterede årsager til sene 
afbrydelser i udviklingen af lægemiddel kandidater, mens idiosynkratisk DILI er den hyppigste årsag til 
tilbagetrækning af lægemidler fra markedet. DILI udgør derfor en økonomisk og sikkerhedsmæssig 
udfordring for den farmaceutiske industri og der er behov for nye metoder til tidlig identificering af 
lægemiddel kandidater der kan forårsage DILI. Metoder, baseret på udødeliggjorte lever celle linjer 
kan forudsige generel celle toksicitet, men har ringe evner til forudsigelse af lever specifik toksicitet 
grundet metodens manglende fysiologiske kompleksitet samt cellernes mangel på lever specifik 
metabolisme. Selv primære humane hepatocytter (PHH), der anses som bedste model for efterligning 
af lever metabolisme, mister karakteristiske lever funktioner indenfor 24 timer efter kultivering i 
enkellag på grund af de-differentiering af cellerne. Trods intakt fysiologi forudsiger dyremodeller ofte 
ikke DILI set i mennesker grundet forskelle i følsomhed og metaboliske aktiviteter. Sammenlignet med 
miljøet oplevet af celler dyrket i enkeltlag, oplever celler dyrket som aggregater i 3D-kultur et miljø 
defineret af komplekse interaktioner mellem celler, der bedre efterligner naturlig leverfysiologi. Indtil 
for nyligt var metoder baseret på 3D cellekultur dog ikke kompatibelt med højkapacitetsmetoder der 
typisk bruges i tidlige stadier af lægemiddeludviklingen, grundet variabiliteten af cellulære aggregater, 
kendt som sfæroider, samt begrænsninger relateret til væskehåndtering.  
I dette projekt vil vi evaluere om den seneste teknologiske udvikling indenfor 3D-cellekultur systemer 
og automatiseret multiparametrisk mikroskopi gør det muligt at etablere en metode med høj 
kapacitet til forudsigelse af små molekylers hepatotoksiske potentiale på lever sfæroider. For at kunne 
vurdere muligheder og barrierer relateret til etablering af en sådan metode vil vi forsøge at udvikle og 
implementere den. Ved succesfuld implementering vil vi benytte metoden til sammenligning af små 
molekyler baserede lægemiddelkandidaters hepatotoksiske potentiale mod lever celle linjer dyrket i 
enkeltlag og i 3D kultur.

4 Introduction 
4.1 
Challenges for modern medicine and safety 
Penicillin, smallpox vaccine, morphine, insulin, chlorpromazine, and aminopterin are examples of 
pharmaceuticals within different drug classes that radically improved human health during the last 
two centuries. Along with the success stories of pharmaceutical drug development are also several 
examples of drugs brought to market that caused severe adverse effects.  
To ensure that pharmaceuticals are safe and effective, medical authorities enforce strict regulations 
for the drug development process. The strict regulations, high failure rates of drug candidates and 
long development times leads to high development cost, that must be recuperated before patent 
expiration. Public healthcare systems, meanwhile, may restrict access to expensive medicines, making 
development of new drugs a challenge. To ensure the future development of new medicines, a 
reduction in the cost of drug development is required 
Cost reductions in drug development could be achieved by addressing the high late stage failure rate 
of drug candidates. While most safety issues related to general cytotoxicities are discovered during 
preclinical drug development, tissue specific toxicity such as cardiac and hepatic toxicities are difficult 
to predict and often remain undetected during early drug development, resulting in many late stage 
failures. Drug induced liver injury (DILI) is particularly difficult to predict and is one of the most 
common causes of post marketing black box warnings and market withdrawals of drugs[3]. 
Development of assays for early prediction of tissue specific toxicities is therefore required to address 
late stage failures of drug candidates.  
One explanation of the lack of predictivity of early preclinical hepatotoxicity assays is that simple cell-
based assays do not mimic liver function well. Studies in animals may meanwhile fail to predict human 
hepatotoxicity due to differences in sensitivity and metabolic capacities. Dose dependent DILI not 
predicted during preclinical studies are normally detected during human clinical trials. Dose 
independent or idiosyncratic DILI (IDILI), that depend on additional factors such as genetic 
predispositions or comorbidity, however, may not be discovered during limited clinical trials leading 
to marketing of drugs with potential for hepatotoxicity. 
4.2 
An in vitro assay for the prediction of hepatotoxicity 
To address the need for in vitro assays that better predict tissue specific toxicities, we will employ 
technical developments in 3D cell culture systems and high content imaging to evaluate the feasibility 
of establishing a high throughput assay for prediction of hepatotoxic potential of small molecule drug 
candidates. The assay should use fluorophore panels to reveal morphological and mechanistic 
features related to cell health, and unbiased multiparametric analysis of multiple features to reveal 
insight to the specific mechanisms involved in drug induced hepatotoxicity.

4.4 
Functions of the liver 
The liver contains 25% of the cardiac output of blood in a person at rest, 75% of which comes from 
the portal circulation that delivers various metabolites including nutrients, minerals, and xenobiotics 
absorbed through the digestive tract[7]. The liver has more than 500 vital functions related to 
metabolism and detoxification; energy storage and homeostasis; production and excretion of bile 
acids; hormone, protein, and metabolite biosynthesis and secretion[8]. Detoxification involves 
metabolism, conjugation and clearance of natural compounds (e.g. bilirubin and steroids) as well as 
xenobiotic compounds including drugs. The first phase of detoxification is reduction of compound 
lipophilicity through oxidation, reduction, and hydrolysis reactions catalysed by the cytochrome 
P450 monooxygenase (CYP) enzymes. of the more than 50 CYP’s expressed in humans, the most 
important with regard to drug biotransformation are CYP1A2, CYP3A4/5, CYP2D6, CYP2C9 and 
CYP2C19 that together metabolize 90% of known drugs [9, 10]. The second detoxification phase 
deactivates electrophilic compounds and increase their size and polarity by conjugation to sulphate, 
acetylate, glucuronidate or glutathione in reactions catalysed by transferases with broad specificity. 
The last detoxification phase involves further metabolism of compounds to aid their excretion by 
export pumps including multidrug resistance proteins[11]. Hepatic metabolism and clearance of 
ingested drugs reduces their bioavailability. Meanwhile, prodrugs may depend on metabolism to 
reach its active form. While the process of detoxification leads to clearance of xenobiotics, the phase 
I metabolism may result in generation of reactive metabolites that are more harmful than parent 
compounds.  
4.5 
Reactive metabolites and DILI 
Reactive metabolites in hepatocytes may destabilize cellular components through covalent binding 
leading to stress responses such as mitochondrial or endoplasmic reticulum specific unfolded protein 
responses [12]. These responses may involve intracellular signalling or stabilization of transcription 
factors that allow the cell to adapt to toxicity, but overwhelmed responses may also lead to apoptosis 
or necrosis[13]. Cellular proteins compromised by covalent binding of reactive metabolites are 
degraded by the proteasome. The resulting peptides are displayed on the cell surface by MHC class I 
molecules where they due to metabolite adducts may be recognized by T-cells and elicit an adaptive 
immune response.  
4.6 
The immune system, tolerance and IDILI 
Adaptive immune responses in the liver are rare but associated with high risk or acute liver failure and 
the described mechanism, known as the hapten hypothesis, is thought to be the primary mechanism 
related to IDILI[14]. Other mechanisms that are thought to contribute to immune mediated IDILI, 
include activation of T-cells by drug stabilized interaction with MHC I [15], and redirection of peptides 
to wrong MHC molecules leading to autoimmunity [16]. One of the reasons for the unpredictable 
nature of IDILI is that factors unrelated to the drug itself such as age, gender, cytochrome P450 and 
HLA polymorphisms together with comorbidity and combined drug interactions may be preconditions 
for IDILI to occur. Co-occurring inflammation may also lead to innate immune signalling via TOLL-like 
receptors that affect the cytokine environment in the liver in a way that facilitates IDILI by adaptive 
immune response. One of the reasons that IDILI is rare is that tolerogenic immune conditions are 
prevalent in the liver dominated by regulatory T-cell and an immune dampening cytokine 
environment[17]. The finding that microbiome derived lipopolysaccharide contributes to 
maintenance of the tolerogenic environment of the liver[18] is supported by cases of IDILI associated 
with use of antibiotics[19].

4.7 
Biomarkers for unmasking of IDILI 
Dependence on the immune system and the requirement for interference with tolerance, combined 
with the multifactorial nature of IDILI, prevent the use of simple high throughput assays for 
identification of compounds with the potential to induce this rare, but serious event. Direct effects of 
drugs on cell health that contribute to IDILI may however, be evaluated in assays of moderate 
complexity. These intrinsic effects may cause cell stress and mild toxicity within the adaptive capacity 
of the cells and therefore undetected by established toxicity endpoints. In this case, markers for the 
adaptive responses may themselves, represent more sensitive endpoints for predicting the potential 
of compounds for induction of IDILI in combination with extrinsic factors. Cellular adaptive stress 
responses that have been successfully used for detection of DILI include the antioxidant response, the 
unfolded protein response of the endoplasmic reticulum, DNA damage response, and inflammatory 
signalling response [20]. Complex multiphysiological systems and animal models with compromised 
immune tolerance, lacking mechanisms for adaptation to toxicity, may also eventually contribute to 
preclinical unmasking of IDILI.

4.9 
High content imaging for characterization of cellular phenotype 
High content screening (HCS) is a high throughput method for acquisition and analysis of images based 
on automated microscopy. HCS is compatible with multi-well plates for imaging including the 384 and 
1536 well plate formats. The method allows phenotypic characterization of cells by analysis of 
intensity, morphology and texture of multichannel microscopy images and can generate large 
unbiased multiparametric datasets. Individual correlated parameters, merged using dimensionality 
reduction algorithms, can generate sensitive derived parameters for detecting the effects of 
compounds on cells. Differences between high content imaging devices that affect their utility include 
the light source (LED or laser), confocal optics for optical sectioning, environmental control to facilitate 
live imaging, as well as software based differences such as automated routines for rare object 
localization at low magnification used for high resolution imaging. 
4.10 Cellular assay formats 
4.10.1 2D cell culture 
Cellular assays allow high throughput investigation of biological processes. Cells are typically grown in 
monolayers in multi-well plates and the simple assay format facilitates compound screening for 
potency or cytotoxicity. While the two-dimensional (2D) culture conditions used in early toxicity assays 
performed over 24-72 hours with high compound concentrations do not reflect the cellular 
environment in vivo, the assays are useful for identification of compounds with intrinsic cytotoxic 
effects. The hepatic origin of the widely used HepG2 human hepatoma cell line, indicates its suitability 
for liver toxicity studies. Due to the limited capacity of HepG2 for drug metabolism however, the 
important contribution from reactive drug metabolites to hepatotoxicity may not be identified using 
this cell line [21]. Even primary human hepatocytes, the current gold standard for cell-based 
hepatotoxicity assays, start dedifferentiation within hours of plating, with gradual loss of capacity for 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
drug metabolism upon cultivation in 2D, as maintenance of hepatic phenotype relies on a physiological 
cellular environment that cannot be replicated in 2D cell culture [22, 23].  
4.10.2 3D cell culture 
Alternative cultivation methods for preservation of hepatic phenotype include sandwich culture and 
different forms of 3D cell culture that allow cells to form aggregates, so-called spheroids that interact 
and communicate in a physiologically relevant manner. Adoption of 3D cell culture can also extend 
cultivation from days to weeks facilitating repeated dose toxicity studies. Issues that have prevented 
widespread adoption of 3D cell culture assays have been variability of spheroids, and lack of 
throughput compared to monolayer cultivation in multi-well plates. Recently, two types of 
microplates for standardized high throughput generation and cultivation of spheroids became 
available, based on hanging drop cultivation and anti-attachment coated wells. In the hanging drop 
system, spheroids are generated from cells seeded in droplets suspended in an upper compartment 
from where it is eluded to anti-attachment coated wells in the lower compartment once aggregation 
of cells have taken place. In the simpler second system, cells are seeded directly in U-shaped anti-
attachment coated wells where their aggregation into spheroids is driven by their mutual attraction 
relative to the anti-attachment coated well surface. The hanging drop system has been discontinued, 
but the simpler spheroid plates are presently available from multiple vendors in 96, 384, and since 
recently, 1536 well formats. Due to physiologically relevant interactions, PHH’s in spheroid aggregates 
retain their hepatic phenotype and metabolic capacities for several weeks. The extended cultivation 
makes PHH spheroids useful for repeat dose toxicity studies at an early stage with compound 
concentrations in a clinically relevant range. PHH's multiple donors may be combined to avoid genetic 
bias associated with PHH’s or cell lines derived from a single donor. A drawback of PHH’s is that limited 
stock availability from individual donors makes it difficult to ensure standardisation and continuity in 
screening assays, not to mention that costs of PHH's may prohibit their use in high throughput assay 
formats.  
The human hepatic cancer cell line HepaRG was reported as the first human hepatic cell line infected 
with hepatitis B virus [24] and has since been established as valuable tool for the study of hepatic 
processes including liver toxicity [25]. In contrast to HepG2 cells that keep proliferating in culture, 
HepaRG are bipotent progenitor cells that differentiate into populations of terminally differentiated 
hepatocytes and immature cholangioblasts upon reaching a threshold cell density. The HepaRG 
coculture of hepatocytes and cholangioblasts organize in pattern that resembles hepatic tissue and 
form functional bile canaliculi. HepaRG hepatocytes express hepatic markers and have drug 
metabolising capacity comparable to PHH’s. Reflecting the genotype of cell line donor, HepaRG 
hepatocytes have a relatively low CYP2D6 activity [26]. Compared to PHH’s, HepaRG cells are 
economic in use and facilitate assay standardisation by avoiding the issues of donor limited stock and 
metabolic variability associated with individual donors of PHH. The HepaRG cell line therefore 
represents a good compromise between simplicity and physiological complexity bridging the gap 
between HepG2 cells in monolayer culture and PHH’s. 
The various manifestations of DILI caused by factors intrinsic to the effect of drugs on hepatocytes 
may be predicted using relatively simple cell-based assays. Idiosyncratic DILI caused by intrinsic and 
extrinsic factors meanwhile, requires the inclusion of additional physiological complexity to reflect 
human toxicity and prevent marketing of hepatotoxic drugs. This complexity is provided by 
multiphysiological assay platforms, where physiologically distinct microtissues (e.g. hepatic and 
immune derived) interact via microfluidic perfusion channels. Such platforms, also known as organs 
on a chip or body on a chip, are aiming to eventually mimic human physiology in a miniaturized format. 
Multiphysiological systems may improve our understanding of the combination of intrinsic and 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
extrinsic risk factors for IDILI and eventually aid early identification of drug candidates with IDILI 
potential. Meanwhile, simpler 3D cell culture assays once validated, can be implemented as a more 
physiologically relevant replacement for 2D assays without loss of throughput.

5 Aims 
In this project we wanted to identify and test in vitro assays for prediction of drug induced liver injury. 
For this purpose, high content imaging was recognized as a flexible platform capable of simultaneous 
evaluation of multiple toxicity related endpoints in a single assay, while human hepatic cell lines grown 
in 3D cell culture was recognized as a relevant biological system with potential for prediction of drug 
induced liver injury. 
Our first aim in the project was to test whether available technology would allow us to develop a 
robust high throughput, high content assay for identification of hepatotoxic potential of small 
molecule compounds. 
Dependent on the success of our first aim, we would use our assay to compare differences in 
predictive potential of two human hepatic cell lines, HepG2 and HepaRG, grown in monolayer or 
cultivated in 3D.

6 Materials and methods 
High throughput 3D cell cultivation and high content imaging are the main techniques used in this 
project. Both techniques rely on automation and instrument programming that is best described 
schematically. Examples of protocols for automated pipetting as well as image recognition and 
analysis protocols are therefore included in appendix D and appendix C respectively. To simplify 
identification of materials and equipment used in this project, cells, reagents, materials, instruments 
and associated software are listed in appendix A. A condensed stepwise protocol of the overall assay 
workflow and associated liquid handling operations is included in appendix B. 
6.1 
Cell culture 
The human hepatic cancer cell lines HepG2 and HepaRG were used for assay development and 
compound toxicity evaluation during the project. Parameters for the assay development, such as 
number of cells and duration for spheroid formation were guided by manufacturer’s instructions and 
application notes. The cell lines were maintained in a humidified incubator at 37°C supplemented with 
5% CO2. 
6.1.1 HepG2 cell line 
The HepG2 cell line was cultivated in T75 culture flasks in RPMI 1640 medium supplemented with 10% 
foetal bovine serum, 1% Sodium Pyruvate, 1% glutaMax and 1% penicillin/streptomycin. Medium in 
the culture flask was changed every three days and cells were passaged to new flasks by trypsinisation 
in conjunction with cell harvests for experiments every seven days. Cells were reseeded at a density 
suitable for reaching ca. 80% confluence within a week. Cell count and viability was quantified for 
experiments using the Nucleocounter automated cytometer.  
6.1.2 HepaRG cell line 
The HepaRG cell line was supplied in cryovials and stored at -80°C. The cryoprotective medium was 
exchanged with William’s E medium supplemented with GlutaMAX and a proprietary HepaRG 
supplement after thawing, according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Viability and quantity were 
evaluated prior to dilution and plating of cells for experiments.  
6.1.3 Cell seeding and spheroid formation 
1500 HepG2 or HepaRG cells were seeded in a volume of 40µl per well for 3D cultivation in spheroid 
plates, while 5000 HepG2 or 20.000 HepaRG cell were seeded in 40µl per well for 2D cultivation using 
automated pipetting. All steps were performed using aseptic techniques and automated pipetting was 
performed within the laminar flow hood using an Integra Viaflo 16 channel pipette attached to, and 
in control of an Integra Assist or Assist Plus robotic platform. Automated cell seeding prevented 
trapped air within wells and preserved spheroids at later stages by precise and consistent positioning 
of pipette tips within wells. HepG2 spheroids were formed after 48 hours, while HepaRG spheroids 
were fully formed after 96 hours. In contrast to HepG2 spheroids, spheroids of terminally 
differentiated HepaRG cells did not grow and retained a diameter of 200-250µm, whereas HepG2 
spheroids reached a size in excess of 450µm. The smaller HepaRG spheroids were highly mobile and 
required larger distance from pipette tips to avoid spheroid loss during aspiration. The differences in 
tip immersion depths resulted in different minimum well volumes of 10µl and 20µl for HepG2 and 
HepaRG spheroids respectively. The increased minimum well volume decreased the maximum single-
step dilution factor from 8 to 4 with implications for subsequent washing steps.

6.1.4 Medium change 
Medium was changed prior to addition of test compounds to the newly formed spheroids. An Integra 
Viaflo384 automated multichannel pipette was used for aspiration, while the Integra 16-channel 
pipette on the Assist or Assist Plus was used for dispensing of media in the laminar flow bench. 
6.1.5 Compounds addition 
Compounds at 200 times their final concentrations were ordered as two-fold titrations in 96 well 
plates prepared as DMSO solutions by the LEO Pharma compound management department. By 
diluting the compounds 200 times we limited the DMSO volume to 0,5% in the assay. 
Compound titrations were pipetted from the 96 well plate into a special 384 well low dead volume 
(LDV) plate for use with a Labcyte Echo acoustic liquid dispenser. The Echo was used for ejection of 
200nl concentrated compound up into the inverted cell culture microplates with well volumes of 40µl. 
Six concentrations from each compound titration was transferred in triplicates to the destination 
plates in a custom pattern defined in the Echo Reformat transfer software as shown in figure 3, below. 
Plate handling operations during acoustic dispensing were handled by the Labcyte Access automation 
system controlled by the Labcyte Tempo software.  
When it was discovered late in the project, that Echo dispensing to inverted target plates was the 
cause of spheroid loss, an alternative protocol for compound application had to be implemented: Echo 
dispensing was used for transfer of compounds to an intermediate plate maintaining the plate layout 
depicted in figure 3. From the intermediate plate, 20µl compound at twice the final concentration, 
was transferred to the cell culture plates with the same well volume. 
After application of compounds, cells were incubated for 72 hours except for the projects last two 
experiments where they were incubated with compounds for 96 hours.

6.2 
Imaging 
6.2.1 Defining an imaging experiment 
After loading an assay plate into the PerkinElmer Operetta CLS high content imaging instrument, the 
Harmony software was used to defined experiment parameters. Parameters to be defined included 
type of objective, widefield or confocal imaging, choice of wells, well fields and optical sections for 
stack recording, as well as number of channels including exposure and power settings. 
6.2.2 Recording a measurement 
After an experiment was defined it could be executed as a measurement. Measurements included the 
opportunity to run consecutive experiments in which the first was performed at a low magnification 
in conjunction with a simultaneous image analysis for locating features of interest. Once the analysis 
of the low-resolution experiment had located X, Y positions of interest in wells, these could be 
selectively imaged in the second experiment using a high-resolution objective. This feature called 
Preciscan by PerkinElmer was of particular use for localization and imaging of spheroids in a single 
field at high resolution. The robustness of the method however relied on the ability of the feature 
localization algorithm to handle exceptions. 
6.2.3 Image analysis development 
The first steps of building an image analysis sequence was based DNA staining for recognition of nuclei 
used for cell segmentation. Features extracted and quantified in subsequent analysis steps could then 
be associated with individual cells and used for characterization of the cell population. For spheroids 
the 3D module in the PerkinElmer Harmony software was used for image analysis. With spheroids, the 
region of interest first had to be defined by features not affected by compound treatments used in 
the experiments. Noise derived from light scattering within the spheroid then had to be reduced by 
filtering to improve subsequent nuclei identification and cell segmentation. Functional, morphological 
and texture features could then be associated with individual cells within the spheroids. Alternatively, 
average values of quantified image features within the spheroid regions could be used as analysis 
parameters. 
6.2.4 Measurement evaluation and data analysis 
Once image analysis algorithms had been built, they could be applied in the evaluation module of the 
Harmony software for extraction of features from complete assay plates measurements. The features 
extracted from assay plate wells could be directly visualized as heatmaps or displayed as graphs when 
an assay layout had been attached to the measurement. The evaluation result file could be exported 
to external data analysis software. By importing the result file into Tipco’s Spotfire software with the 
High Content Profiler module from PerkinElmer, multiple measured parameters could be integrated 
to generate combined parameters with stronger correlation than the individual features.

7.1 
Planning of assay development 
To develop the assay for high content imaging of 3D cell culture, the following specific tasks were 
defined. The tasks were related to the identification of assay parameters and readouts and to the 
development and optimization of protocols as summarized below: 
1. Identification of mechanisms by which drugs have been shown to cause liver injury. 
2. Identification of drugs known to cause DILI, suitable as reference compounds for mechanisms of liver 
injury. 
3. Identification of biological system for high content screening of liver injury. 
4. Identification of complementary fluorescent dye panels for quantification of morphology and texture 
features as well as features related mechanism of toxicity. 
5. Defining the assay requirements for each of the fluorophore panels. 
6. Development and optimization of programs for automated liquid handling, required for preservation of 
spheroids in 384 well microplates. 
7. Development and optimization of assays based on requirements defined for each fluorophore panel. 
8. Development of a robust algorithm for localization of spheroids at low magnification for subsequent 
image acquisition in a single field at high magnification. 
9. Development of 2D and 3D imaging analysis algorithms for extraction and quantification of features for 
each of the fluorophore panels. 
10. Development of data analysis workflow for integration and interpretation of assay readouts from 
differently stained assay plates

7.1.2 Choice of biological system 
The choice of biological system for the assay was a compromise between simplicity for robustness and 
complexity for biological relevance to prediction of DILI in humans. A multiphysiological or a coculture 
system comprising primary human hepatocytes and cells of the immune system would be optimal for 
biological relevance. A robust implementation of a system of such complexity, however, could not 
realistically be achieved within the time available for the project. The human hepatic cell line, HepaRG, 
has been evaluated under the MIP-DILI project [33], and was chosen for the assay as a compromise 
between biological relevance and robustness. HepaRG cells overcomes the metabolic limitations of 
the widely used HepG2 cell line and differentiates into a coculture of hepatocytes and biliary epithelial 
cells upon reaching a certain cell density. By representing two of the natural cell types of the liver, the 
HepaRG cell line gives rise to a biologically complex system, while minimizing assay variables.

7.1.4 Choice of assay format and plate layout 
Literature and application notes from commercial vendors recommend three to four technical in 
spheroid assays to compensate for variation and for potential loss of spheroids. Since the assay was 
designed to support the early phase of drug development, we wanted to be able to include at least 
ten compounds plus assay controls. Since we also wanted to cover a wide range of concentrations and 
avoid using the edge-wells of plates, the use of 384 well plates was an obvious choice. The choice of 
384 well plates however also required automated liquid handling. Liquid handling also had to be 
optimized for spheroid retention while minimizing the residual well volume for efficient medium 
changes and washing steps. The resulting 384 plate layout, visualized in figure 3 and 4 in the previous 
section, has a capacity for 12 compounds including controls, in six concentrations in triplicate.  
7.2 
Liquid handling optimization 
HepG2 cells were used for the first experiments performed during this project as these cells have been 
reported to form spheroids within 48 hours and were immediately available. Initial cell seeding by 
handheld multichannel pipette resulted in inconsistent pipetting revealed by trapped air in several 
wells beneath the dispensed liquid. Consistent cell seeding and elimination of trapped air was 
achieved by attaching the multichannel pipette to the Integra Assist platform for automated pipetting. 
The program for automated cell seeding using the Integra Assist Plus is included in appendix D (1).  
Liquid handling for medium change and washing steps was optimized by aspiration of 30µl medium 
from 40µl well volumes using different tip immersion depths to find the best compromise between 
spheroid retention and minimum residual well volume. As indicated in figure 6, below, heights of 4,3

Preciscan image analysis development 
Most of the spheroids were aspired from the wells during medium change prior compound application 
for the first experiment after switching from the HepG2 to the HepaRG cell line. Contrary to spheroids 
of HepG2 cells, spheroids of differentiated HepaRG cells did not grow and were highly mobile within

spheroids was collected in 50ml centrifuge tubes and harvested by centrifugation. The harvested 
spheroids were resuspended and replated in a flat-bottom PerkinElmer CellCarrier ultra imaging 
microplate. With recent course knowledge of the proprietary Preciscan function of the PerkinElmer 
high content imaging instrument, it was attempted to localize the replated spheroids from low 
resolution images with whole well coverage, for subsequent high-resolution imaging in single fields. 
In this way it could be seen that most of the aspired spheroids were successfully recovered and located 
in the wells as seen in figure 7.

failed to recognize spheroids leading to lack of acquisition or included artefacts as spheroids leading

channel for recognition. In this way, less strict recognition criteria for both channels avoided false 
negatives while using one channel as a filter for the other avoided false positives. After further 
testing, implementation of exception handling and optimization of parameters, a robust preciscan 
algorithm was developed. The final image analysis sequence of the preciscan algorithm is shown in 
appendix C (1).

Discussion and conclusions 
The project had two major goals. The first was to demonstrate the feasibility of implementation of a 
high throughput, high content imaging assay using hepatic spheroids. The second goal was to evaluate 
the ability of the assay, once developed, to predict DILI by comparing the effect of drugs with 
hepatotoxic potential on different hepatic cell lines cultivated in monolayer or in 3D. The dependence 
of the second goal on achieving the first provided limited flexibility in the project. The specificity of 
the second goal, however, helped to focus the planning and execution of assay development. A similar 
interdependence was encountered during the development of the assay, where each consecutive step 
relied on completion of the former. Liquid handling optimization for instance was required for 
spheroid retention, that was required for the development image analysis for spheroid localization, 
which was again required for acquisition of high-resolution confocal images for 3D reconstruction of 
spheroids. High resolution spheroid images were a prerequisite for development of the image analysis 
algorithms for feature extraction and quantification, which was itself an iterative process and not 
completed for all fluorophore panels.  
A notable obstacle during the assay development was that the acoustic liquid handling instrument 
used for direct dispensing of undiluted compounds and stains to assay plates was a cause of spheroid 
loss. The instrument relied on target plate inversion, which may have relocated spheroids from the 
anti-attachment coated well bottom. The spheroids likely fell to the medium meniscus from where 
they could slide and attach to the well edge or be hit by the DMSO diluted compound or stain droplets 
ejected by the acoustic dispenser. In some of the wells from where spheroids were lost, cell debris 
was found, consistent with damage from droplet ejection, while in other wells deformed spheroids 
were found when defocusing a microscope toward the top the well. After evaluation of alternative 
methods for directs dispensing, indirect dispensing via intermediate plates was adopted. 
The technical challenges related to assay development was successfully overcome during this project 
although liquid handling for indirect stain application remained to be optimized. While efficient use of 
materials is desirable in a final assay, the method used for application of compounds can be used for 
stain application in the short term although at this method would increase the consumption of 
fluorophores. 
One of the requirements of the assay was that it could be used for prediction of DILI caused by multiple 
mechanisms. Fluorophore probes for mechanistic readouts was therefore required in the assay. To 
facilitate cell segmentation during image analysis however, fluorophores for visualization of 
morphological features also had to be included in the assay. Since only four fluorophores could be 
accommodated in a single assay due to spectrum limitations, it was decided to split the assay into 
three separate assays with complimentary fluorophore panels to obtain the required mechanistic and 
morphological readouts for DILI prediction. After division of the assay into three separate assays, it 
was decided to include only one cell line in the experiments to keep assay development manageable. 
This meant however that a comparison of the abilities of the HepG2 and HepaRG cell lines for 
prediction of DILI could not be made. 
To access the impact of cultivation method, each assay panel was tested on cells grown in monolayers 
and as spheroids. The multiple assay panels and different cultivation methods necessitated the 
development of multiple image analysis algorithms. Development of these algorithms was time 
consuming and could not be completed for all panels within the allocated time. A factor that 
contributed to the compressed timetable was that alternative liquid handling protocols had to be 
established, when the instrument used for direct application of compounds and stains to assay plates 
was found to cause spheroid loss. Likewise, when more suitable instruments for the liquid handling 
workflow became available, new pipetting programs also had to be established. 
==PAGE:29==
29 
 
The changes in the liquid handling workflow resulted in assay plates with only few lost spheroids at 
the point of imaging. The intact spheroid plates allowed final optimization of the pre-scan imaging 
algorithm for localization of spheroids. The robust pre-scan algorithm minimized the number of 
spheroids lost due to failed recognition. The successful establishment of the robust pre-scan routine 
was probably the most important accomplishment in project since it greatly facilities high content 
imaging of 3D cell culture.  
At the final stages of the project the emphasis turned to data interpretation which, due the high 
content nature or the assay, required methods for multiparametric data analysis. While the integrated 
Harmony software package handled all parts of the imaging workflow, it lacked options for 
multiparametric data analysis in its 3D module. Fortunately, during this period we were able to test 
PerkinElmer’s High Content Profiler module for the Spotfire data analysis software. By using the High 
Content Profiler module, we could perform multiparametric data analysis in which correlated 
morphological, texture and intensity readouts from image analysis were combined into derived 
features that could be used for detection of non-obvious phenotypes. With reservation for the short 
time spent and the few datasets processed, we conclude that the software is useful for unguided 
analysis of the multiparametric datasets generated by high content screening assays. The software 
would also greatly facilitate the integration of the readouts from our differently stained assay plates. 
The software could potentially be used to further improve the assay by supplementing readouts from 
high content imaging with additional features, such as albumin readings from aspired supernatants, 
that could be integrated in the multiparametric analysis. 
We have demonstrated the technical feasibility of establishing a high throughput, high content assay 
for imaging of 3D cultivated hepatocytes. We have also demonstrated that 3D image analysis 
algorithms can segment individual cells from 3D reconstructed although light scattering within 
spheroids limits the depth of imaging.  
 
9 Future perspectives 
During the project we met challenges that prevented us from fully implement the assay, that we would 
have used to compare the abilities of cells grown in monolayer and in 3D culture for the prediction of 
DILI. 
The questions left unanswered in this report would be relevant to address in future work on this 
project. It would also be relevant to investigate the impact of extended, repeat dose toxicity tests on 
the predictive power of 2D and 3D cell cultivation systems. Additional reference compounds and non-
drug positive controls should also be included in future work to facilitate final data analysis. The 
compounds required in high concentrations should in the future be dissolved in assay medium directly, 
instead of in DMSO, to avoid that the requirement for dilution of DMSO determines the maximum 
compound concentration applied in the assay as was the case for several compounds used in this 
project.

Appendix B: Stepwise assay workflow overview 
The overall assay workflow involves the following steps and associated liquid handling operations: 
1. Cell seeding 
a. Cells were resuspended according to provided instructions, quantified and tested for 
viability 
b. The suspension was diluted to 1500 live cells per spheroid or to 20000 live cells for 
monolayer culture in 40µl per well. 
c. A 16-channel electronic pipette placed in a robotic pipe holder was used for automatic 
dispensing of 40 µl cell suspension per well at maximum pipetting speed to avoid 
trapped air at the bottom of wells. 
d. Plates were placed in a humidified box in a humidified incubator at 37°C supplemented 
with 5% CO2. 
2. Incubation for spheroid formation 
a. Plates were incubated for 96 hours 
3. Change of cell culture medium and addition of compounds (with phospholipid stain) 
a. Volume in assay plate was reduced to 20µl by aspiration of 20µl using a 384-channel 
robotic pipette. 
b. Serially diluted compounds in a 96-well plate were replicated to a low dead volume 384-
well source plate for compatibility with liquid handling using acoustic droplet ejection  
c. Compounds in 384-well source plate were reformatted into an intermediate plate at 2X 
final concentration using direct well to well transfer by acoustic droplet ejection. 
d. 20µl 2X concentrated compounds were replica plated into the spheroid or monolayer 
assay plates using a 384-channel robotic pipette. 
4. Incubation with compounds 
a. Assay plates were incubated with compounds for 72 or 96 hours 
5. Prefix staining 
a. Volumes in assay plates are reduced from 40µl to 20µl using a 384-channel robotic 
pipette. 
b. Direct and indirect methods are available for stain application 
i. Direct stain dispensing: Undiluted stains are pipetted to a Labcyte low dead 
volume source plate. The Labcyte Echo acoustic dispenser is used for direct stain 
transfer from the source into the inverted assay target plates. This method has 
proven incompatible with unattached spheroids at low well volume of 20µl and 
to a lesser degree at 40µl. 
ii. Indirect stain dispensing: A source plate for plate replication is prepared  using 
one of three methods: A volume of each of three 3X concentrated stain 
combinations is prepared and distributed to a source plate in the pattern 
required for the assay using either a combination of single and multichannel 
pipettes, a peristaltic multidrop dispenser, or by preparing the staining mix 
source plate using acoustic droplet ejection dispensing from a low dead volume 
plate with undiluted stains. A 384-channel robotic pipette is used to transfer 
10µl staining solution to the assay plate. 
iii. After addition of stains, the assay plates are incubated for 1 hour at 37°C.  
iv. Assay plates are protected from light in all subsequent steps.  
6. Fixation 
a. Volumes in assay plates are reduced from 60µl to 20µl using a 384-channel robotic 
pipette outside the fume hood. 
==PAGE:35==
35 
 
b. An equal volume of 4% formaldehyde is added to the assay plate wells using the 16-
channel robotic Integra Assist inside the combined sterile and safety cabinet, resulting in 
a final formaldehyde concentration of 2% in the wells. 
c. Assay plates are incubated with formaldehyde for 1 hour at room or sealed overnight at 
4°C in the refrigerator. 
7. Neutralization and permeabilization 
a. 40µl of 200mM glycine PBS is added to the assay plates for formaldehyde dilution and 
neutralization. 
b. Volumes in assay plates are reduced from 80µl to 20µl using a 16-channel pipette on 
Integra Assist Plus robotic platform. 
c. 60µl of 200mM glycine in PBS or 200mM glycine, 0,1% saponin, 1% BSA in PBS is added 
to the assay plates for further formaldehyde dilution and neutralization. Assay plates to 
receive stains post fixation are also permeabilization with saponin at this stage. After 
this step, formaldehyde is considered diluted and neutralized to a degree that allows the 
removal of the assay plates from the safety cabinet. 
d. Volumes in assay plates are reduced from 80µl to 20µl using a 384-channel robotic 
pipette outside the safety cabinet. 
8. Postfix staining 
a. Designated assay plate wells with permeabilized cells are stained with membrane 
impermeable Alexa phalloidin and LipidTOX neutral lipids stains, using the same indirect 
method for staining described in section 5.b.ii. 
b. Plates are incubated with stains for 1 hour at room temperature. 
9. Washing 
a. Assay plates are washed three times in PBS (with 0,05% NaN3 for conservation) to 
reduce unspecific staining. Washing is performed using the 384-channel robotic pipette 
for aspiration and dispensing within the minimal residual well volume and maximum 
working volume of 20µl and 80µl respectively. 
b. 40µl are aspirated to leave well volume of 40µl for imaging. 
10. Imaging 
a. An assay plate is loaded into the high content imager. Experiment parameters including 
objective, widefield or confocal imaging, plate wells, well fields, optical stacks, channels 
with exposure and power are defined in the Harmony software from PerkinElmer.  
b. Measurements can be recorded using the defined experiment. 
c. Image analysis algorithms are built from recorded images using the analysis module in 
the Harmony software. 
d. Image analysis algorithms are used for extraction and quantification of image feature of 
completed measurements. 
11. Data analysis 
a. Harmony can used guided machine learning to identify experimental classes from 
multiple parameters. This function can be used for unbiased identification of features 
that correlate with experimental controls. 
b. PerkinElmer’s High Content Profiler module for Tipco’s Spotfire software can perform 
dimensionality reduction of multiparametric datasets and group compounds into classes 
based on unguided machine learning.
