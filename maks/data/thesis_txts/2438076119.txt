Abstract
Members of the Pseudomonas genius are well known for their excellent ability
for adaptation as well as for a large plasticity of their genomes. In combina-
tion these properties provide the perfect conditions for secondary metabolite
production. Secondary metabolites are responsible for a long list of com-
pounds with antimicrobial eﬀects. Soil, and especially the rhizosphere, rep-
resents a large, under-investigated ﬁeld for the potential discovery of novel
antimicrobial compounds and species suitable for protection of crops. In this
study we have further analysed Pseudomonas strains previously isolated from
soil in relation to their potential use as biocontrol agents (BCA). We have
developed and optimized a potato virulence assay for investigation of both
the virulence of potential biocontrol agents as well as conducted an investig-
ation of co-cultivation of a pathogen and a potential BCA. We successfully
constructed a mutation in an uncharacterized gene of the Pseudomonas prote-
gens strain 9.1 isolated at DTU in the spring 2017. This mutant surprisingly
showed a slight increase of bioactivity towards three known phytopathogens.
By whole genome sequence analysis of nine Pseudomonas isolates with the
webtool antiSMASH we show that all nine strains encode a gene cluster
potentially producing an antibiotic compound. We also show that more al-
gorithms has to be applied in order to gain a broader knowledge of the gene
clusters responsible for bioactivity.

Preface
This report is a Master’s thesis carried out during the course of ﬁve months
(February-June 2018) and is equivalent to 30 ECTS-points. The work con-
ducted has been done in the Inﬂectional Microbiology Group (IMG) at The
Technical University of Denmark under supervision of Professor MSO Lars
Jelsbak. Prior to the work described in this thesis a special project equival-
ent to 10 ECTS-points was conducted in the fall of 2017, and the following
section will give a small introduction to this previous work and results that
was used as stepping stones in this Master thesis.
2.1
Isolation and Characterization of Pseudomonads
from soil
The aim of the project conducted in the fall of 2017 was to isolate and char-
acterize Pseudomonas species from soil regarding their bioactivity towards
known plant pathogens.
Six soil samples was collected around Zealand, these samples led to a total of
61 Pseudomonas isolates. All 61 isolates were characterized based on their
ﬂuorescence (Yes/no, intensity and colour), their bioactivity and their pre-
dicted specie based on 16s rRNA sequencing.
The 16s rRNA sequencing led to identiﬁcation of 16 diﬀerent Pseudomonas
species from the 6 soil samples, the four most abundant being: P. jessenii (15
isolates), P. migulae (6 isolates), P. putida and P. frederiksberggensis (both
5 isolates). 20 isolates showed bioactivity, ten of which showed bioactivity
towards more than one pathogen. No isolate showed activity to more than
two of the plant pathogens. Ten isolates were chosen for whole genome se-
quencing (carried out in this thesis, see section 8 for results) based on their
bioactivity. Table 1 shows the selected isolates with isolate ID, species, illu-
minating properties and their bioactivity. Throughout this thesis, the isolate
ID will be used for simplicity.

2.2
The 9.1-strain
In the spring 2017 two students, May Iren Aune and Marie Højmark Fischer,
isolated ﬂorescent Pseudomonads from soil samples [1]. One of the strains
(9.1) isolated showed excellent bioactivity towards four out of four pathogens
(note that none of the strains isolated in the fall of 2017 showed bioactivity
to more than two of the investigated pathogens). Sequencing of household
genes revealed 9.1 to belong to the specie Pseudomonas protegens. Mem-
bers of this specie is well known for their ability to protect plant roots from
pathogenic attack by other microorganisms thus acting as biocontrol agents.
Whole genome sequencing of the 9.1-strain revealed several gene clusters
encoding antimicrobial compounds including pyoverdine, aryl polyene, algin-
ate, pyoluteorin, pyrrolnitrin, orfamide and DAPG. Besides these well-known
compounds an unknown, uncharacteristic gene cluster unique to this strain
was also discovered. This gene cluster encodes a potential PKS, the largest
gene of the cluster is the CLA18-06265 gene of approximately 6000 bp. One
of the aims for the current master’s project is to construct a 9.1 mutant car-
rying a large insert in the largest gene of the unknown gene cluster. This
should disrupt the function of this gene as well as the function of the whole
gene cluster. This will potentially give insights to the phenotypical traits
encoded by this cluster.

3
Introduction
Ever since the birth of agricultural practices when man started to cultivate
crops and became farmers, the fear of attack by phytopathogens has been
present. Some of the most well-known phytopathogen epidemics are those of
the great Irish famines (1739 and 1845-1849) caused by Phytophthora infest-
ans, the last epidemic led 2 million people to immigrate to America and is
accountable for the death of 750,000 people. It is estimated that 10-20% of
the world’s agricultural production is aﬀected by phytopathogens leading to
food shortages aﬀecting 800 million people [2].
3.1
Techniques for phytopathogen control
The use of agrochemicals
Modern conventional farming heavily depends on the use of chemical in-
puts such as fertilizers and pesticides to avoid phytopathogens and maintain
constant high yields. The immediate eﬀectiveness and ease of application
made synthetic pesticides and fertilizers extremely popular and their use in-
creased tremendously in the 1960’s where they became available world wide
[3]. However, growing concern regarding the consequences by the widespread
use of chemicals in agriculture forces the industry to rethink the combat of
phytopathogens [4], [2]. The growing concern of the long-term side eﬀects
of traditional synthetic pesticides and bactericides include the fear of irre-
versible damage to the soil ecosystem, contamination of water with chemical
residues, negative impact on nontarget species as well as the negative ef-
fects on the health of the workers handling the chemicals. Further more,
consumers as well as health authorities are showing greater and greater con-
cern with potential chemical residues in food products leading to a growing
market for organically grown crops.
This interest has lead to a renewed
motivation for ﬁnding alternatives to traditional control of pests and phyto-
pathogens that can improve quality, yields and reduce economic losses due
to phytopathogenic invasions, using more environmental friendly techniques
[5].
Suppressive soils
In certain areas of the planet, it seems that crops are left unaﬀected by
the invading microorganisms that every year costs billion of dollars in crop
yield losses in other places of the globe. These areas are known as naturally
suppressive soils [2], [6]. The roots of crops grown in suppressive soil are
4
==PAGE:11==
protected from diseases that, other places, are caused by soil-born pathogenic
microorganisms. The reason for this suppressiveness, has been hypothesized
to be due to the presence of antagonistic microorganisms. This hypothesis
is supported by the fact the the suppressive ability of suppressive soils are
abolished when heat treated with either pasteurization or autoclavation [6].
It however seems that these antagonistic microorganisms cannot account
for all the suppressiveness, or at least that aspects such as pH, humidity,
temperature and available nutrients play a key role in the eﬀectiveness of
antagonistic bacteria [6].
Nevertheless, the use of bacteria as agents for
control with phytopathogens holds a great potential and the topic receives
increasing interest from both farmers, consumers and legislative institutions
[7].
Induced Systemic Resistance
In suppressive soil a phenomenon known as Induced Systemic Resistance
(ISR) is elicited by members of the soil community. ISR allows plants to
withstand pathogenic attack without oﬀering total protection. Many strains
known to be beneﬁcial to the suppressiveness of soil has shown to be able to
perform ISR to their host plant. Experiments with inoculation of pathogens
show that ISR-positive plants are in an alerted state that allows them to react
faster and more strongly by inducing defense mechanisms. As no speciﬁc
ISR elicitors has been identiﬁed, it has been proposed that a combination of
siderophores, O-antigens and ﬂagella might account for the ISR eﬀect [6].
Use of Biological Control Agents for control of phytopathogens
Using microbial agents as an eﬀective alternative to chemical control of phyto-
pathogens has received immense attention lately. The soil microbiome holds
great promise for contributing to a more sustainable agriculture with species
such as Pseudomonas and Bacillus being of particular interest and subject
of research [8], [9]. Soil microbiomes are intricate and highly diverse eco-
systems, consisting of thousands of interacting microorganisms. Naturally
occuring soil microbes inﬂuence plant health by promoting plant growth and
eﬀectively suppres pathogenic microorganisms. However, the potential of the
use of natural microorganisms as eﬀective phytopathogen cotrol has been
under-exploited to date [10]. A general trait dominating the microbes in-
vestigated as potential biocontrol agents (BCA) is the ability to colonize the
plant roots, the so-called rhizosphere. The rhizosphere is an area surrounding
the plant roots where microbial interactions are maximized. The rhizosphere
is habitat for several bioactive microorganisms and it has been hypothesized
5
==PAGE:12==
that the rhizosphere has a denser population of microorganisms than other
parts of the soil. These bacteria, that both inhabits the rhizosphere and
are beneﬁcial to the plant are called plant growth promoting rhizobacteria
(PGPR) [11].
PGPRs and the rhizosphere
Plant growth promoting rhizobacteria (PGPR) are known to inﬂuence plant
growth in various direct and indirect ways. It is a heterogeneous group of bac-
teria including species from the genera of Pseudomonas, Azotobacter, Bacillus
and Streptomyces. These microorganisms can communicate through diﬀerent
mechanisms such as quorum sensing, most likely with both other bacterial
species and the host plant. The degree of root colonization is of great im-
portance in relation to plant growth and it has been shown that the presence
of ﬂagella and/or the presence of particular outer membrane polysacchar-
ides are cruicial for eﬃctive root colonization [12], [13]. The exact mode
of action by which PGPRs promote plant growth are not fully understood,
but are thought to include i) the ability to produce or change concentration
of regulators related to plant growth, such as indoleacetic acid, ii) asymbi-
otic N2-ﬁxation and iii) antagonism against phytopathogens by production
of siderophores and antibiotics [11]. Antagonism can be caused by the pro-
duction of several classes of compounds, the main ones being: i) antibiotics
such as 2,4-diaceryl phloroglucinol (DAPG) and pyoluteorin, ii) volatile com-
pounds such as hydrogen cyanide, iii) siderophores and lipopeptides.
Diﬀerent species and even strains diﬀer in their key biocontrol mechanism,
some show broad spectrum antagonism while other only aﬀect a speciﬁc
pathogen [8]. Root colonization can roughly be dividend into recognition,
adherence, invasion (for endophytes and pathogens), colonization and growth
[14]. For Pseudomonas species the early stages are facilitated by ﬂagella and
the production of biosurfactants, in combination these facilitate coordinated
swarming motility. The later stages are characterized by formation of micro-
colonies on the root surface and the establishment of mature bioﬁlm [10].
Most, if not all, of the genes responsible for the encoding bioactive com-
pounds and compounds needed for bioﬁlm formation are mapped outside
of the core genome. This includes genes encoding Non-Ribosomal Peptide
Synthetases and Polyketides (PKS), all in all denoted secondary metabolites
[9].

3.2
Secondary metabolites
Secondary metabolites are naturally occuring compounds that originate from
sideways of the central metabolism, they have no role in energy uptake or
storage but are crucial parts of the organism’s ability to ﬁght other organ-
isms [10]. Secondary metabolites include Non-Ribosomal peptide synthetase
(NRPS), Polyketide synthetase (PKS), (cyclic)lipopeptides ((C)LP) and a
hybridization there of [15], and are all encoded on the chromosome[16]. It
has been shown that conditions of restricted growth and high cell densities
favour the production of secondary metabolites, in contrary, it seems that
optimal nutritional conditions favour primary metabolism and rapid growth
[6].
NRPSs
NRPSs are large multi-enzymes that carry out assembly of amino acids to
form linear or cyclic peptides. They are composed of multiple modules, all
of which contains: an adenylation (A) domain responsible for amino acid
selection; a thiolation (T) domain responsible for thioesteriﬁcation of the
activated substrate, and ﬁnally a condensation (C) domain responsible for
the formation of a peptide bond between two amino acids [2], [17].
PKSs
PKSs are multi domain proteins producing polyketides in a mechanism sim-
ilar to the fatty acid synthase. The protein (or protein complex) join a starter
unit (usually acetyl-CoA) with extender units (usually malonyl-CoA) by a
Claisen condensation, a step called ketosynthase (KS), Keto Reductase (KR)
reduce the oxo group, forming a hydroxyester, Dehydratase (DH) leads to
conjugated esters and ﬁnally Enoyl reductase (ER) gives a reduced ester,
all step does not happen at once, but one extender unit is added at a time
and the whole compound is then reentering the cycle. This mechanism is
the backbone for all polyketides, however, polyketides are diverse group of
compounds and are thus divided into three subgroups, type I PKS, type II
PKS and type III PKS. Type I PKSs are found in bacteria and fungi, type
II PKSs are restricted to bacteria and type III PKSs are found both plants,
bacteria and fungi [18].
3.3
Fluorescent Pseudomonas
Pseudomonas is a genus of Gram-negative bacteria belonging to the class
of gammaproteobacteria. All members are rod-shaped, posses at least one
7
==PAGE:14==
polar ﬂagella, are non-spore forming and has a high C-G content of 59-
68%. The group is highly diverse and the members inhabit various ecological
niches and are known to produce a cascade of biologically active metabolites
such as antibiotics, bacteriocins, biosurfactants, volatiles and iron-scavenging
siderophores.
Their genomes are complex, encoding approximately 6000
genes but with a core genome of just 20%. This genetic plasticity reﬂect
the outstanding adaptive abilities of the members of this genus. The group
of ﬂuorescent Pseudomonas distinguish themselves from other Pseudomonas
by producing the extracellular ﬂuorescent pigment, pyoverdin.
Pyoverdin
is a siderophore that under certain conditions has inﬂuence on the growth
of competitive organisms. The most well-known member of this group is
P. aeruginosa, a well-studied opportunistic human pathogen associated with
lung infections and antibiotic resistance [2], [10], [15]. However, the group
of ﬂuorescent Pseudomonads are also known for their abundance in soil and
their ability to colonize the rhizosphere. Combined with their well-developed
machinery for secondary metabolite production, ﬂuorescent Pseudomonads
has received a great deal of interest as BCA and PGPR [10]. Pseudomonads
are excellent adapters and cope well with nutritional limitations [6].
3.3.1
Compounds related to bioactivity of Pseudomonas
DAPG
2,4-diacetylphloroglucinol (DAPG) has received immense attention because
of its ability to control root pathogens and since a wide range of Pseudomonas
species are able to produce this compound. DAPG has mainly been investig-
ated in relation to its antifungal activity, but recent studies have shown that
DAPG has both antifungal, antimicrobial as well as antinematode activity
[19]. This is a phenolic compound produced by a PKS, the biosynthetic loci
for DAPG has shown to be conserved among all DAPG producing strains,
regardless of originating continent [7], [20].
Siderophores
Siderophores are small, high-aﬃnity iron-chelating compounds excreted by
several microorganisms. Siderophores are generally a part of the primary
metabolism, as iron is an essential element. Many siderophores has, how-
ever, shown to employ more roles than just that. These includes antibiotic
activity, increasing virulence and bioﬁlm formation. Further more they are
increasingly being recognized as being toxic themselves [21].
These roles
are normally assigned to secondary metabolites, thus the line between the
primary and secondary metabolism has been blurred [6].
8
==PAGE:15==
Cyclic Lipopeptides
Cyclic lipopeptides (CLP) are produced by distinctly diﬀerent groups of bac-
teria, both Gram positive and Gram negative. The high diversity both re-
garding producer strains and chemical structure indicates that CLPs serve
diﬀerent, and potentially multiple, roles. This might explain the diﬃculty in
assigning a speciﬁc role for CLPs. A number of CLPs has been reported to
be involved with swarming motility as well as biosurfactant properties. Also,
in many cases CLP are involved with antagonistic interactions with other
organisms such as plants, fungi, bacteria and vira [22].
According to Zachow et al. [23] poaeamide, viscosin, WLIP and entolysins
are all cyclic lipopeptides encoded by a NRPS located at two diﬀerent loc-
ations in the genome. This is contrary to the cyclic lipopeptide orfamide
that is encoded by a single operon.
This could indicate a closer relation
between the viscosin and poaeamide, than to orfamide. Zachow describes
in 2015 that poaeamide is similar in bioactivity to viscosin and orfamides
and that it plays a key role in the motility of Pseudomonas poa. Further
more, they show that poaeamide is associated with root colonization, even
though a poaeamide-deﬁcient mutant showed increased cell densities on sugar
beet roots compared to the wild type strains [23]. Alsohim and colleagues
described in 2014 the important role of viscosin in relation to motility and
plant protection. Viscosin, that has been found in strains of Pseudomonas
ﬂuorescens, is a potent antimicrobial and is, like poaeamide, a biosurfactant
[24]. White-line-inducing principle (WLIP) is, as the other lipopeptides in
this group, associated with bioﬁlm formation as well as swarming motility.
Further, it has been shown to have antagonistic eﬀects against Xanthomonas
species [25].
Orfamide-type cyclic lipopeptides are biosurfactants produced by Pseudo-
monas. They are in the rhizosphere involved with biocontrol of oomycete
zoospores, fungi and have shown insecticidal activity [26].
Pseudopyronines
Pseudopyronines are known to inhibit growth of diﬀerent algae species and
bacteria, such as Bacillus subtilis, Methilin resistant Staphylococcus aureus
and vancomycin-resistant Enterococci, primarily by inhibiting the fatty-acid
synthesis II and membrane disruption. Pseudopyronines have been found
in Pseudomonas putida isolated from the rhizosphere of banana plants by
Bauer in 2015 [27]. The fatty-acid synthesis has been proposed as a target
for development of new antibiotics. Thus pseudopytonine is interesting in a
biocontrol point due to what future research will reveal of this compound[27].

4.1
Pathogens used in this study
Both Dickeya and Protobacterum are soil associated bacteria responsible for
a long list of crop diseases leading to economic losses for farmers. Especially
the blackleg and soft rot diseases in potato caused by these phytopathogens
are of major concern [5], [28]. The gammaproteobacterial genus Xanthomo-
nas is, also, a soil associated plant pathogen. Three species of Xanthomonas
is present on the list of the top ten bacteria causing the greatest economic loss
in the agricultural ﬁeld. Xanthomonas campestris is responsible for black rot
in all types of cabbage [29]. Also present on this list is Pectobacterium caroto-
vorum, Pectobacterium atrosepticum and Dickeya solani. Table 2 shows the

4.2
Disruption of potential PKS
The strategy
Two previous students isolated a strain from soil in the spring 2017 [1].
This strain, called 9.1, showed exceptional high bioactivity towards four well-
known pathogens. Whole genome sequencing revealed a gene cluster, unique
to this strain, encoding a potential polyketide synthetase (PKS). This PKS
potentially has an eﬀect on the bioactivity of the 9.1 strain. As nothing is
known about this gene cluster, our strategy is to destroy the function of the
largest gene in the cluster to investigate if this has any obvious eﬀect on the
bioactivity.
A simple, straight forward approach that has previously been shown to
work in Pseudomonas strains is chosen [30]. The strategy includes construc-
tion of a plasmid with an insert matching 500-700 bp in the gene to be
disrupted. This stretch of base pairs is ideal for homologous recombination
10
==PAGE:17==
to take place. After construction of the plasmid, it is transformed into an
E. coli strain suitable for conjugation. Conjugation is facilitated by tripar-
ental mating between the 9.1 strain, the transconjugated E. coli strain and a
helper strain. The presence of antibiotic resistance genes favours homologous
recombination between the plasmid-insert and the corresponding region in
the gene on the chromosome, leading to a 6000 bp insertion 1⁄3-1⁄2 downstream
of the promoter. We hypothesize that this insertion will disrupt the function
of the gene.

4.3
Inhibition assay
To investigate the bioactivity of the 9.1-mutants an inhibition assay was set
up, this assay was optimized in the fall of 2017 modiﬁed from Li et al. [17].
In this assay the isolates of interest are grown alone o/n in liquid LB. On day
one, the culture is spotted on an LB plate and incubated for 24h. On day
two the plate is exposed to chloroform vapors in order to kill the bacterial
colony. The plate is then left in an air ﬂow chamber, ensuring no chloroform
is left, this process also ensures no volatile compounds are left thus, only
metabolites deposited into the agar is aﬀecting the growth of the pathogen.
By inoculating the pathogen in soft agar (0.5%) and then pour on top of
both the colony and the agar, the pathogen is evenly distributed on the
plate. After 24-48h of incubation, visible zone(s) will appear due to lack
of pathogen growth. Figure 4 shows a schematic representation of the agar
plate after application of soft agar inoculated with pathogen on day 2.

We have adapted and modiﬁed our assay from des Essarts et al. [28] and

5.1.1
Virulence of isolated Pseudomonas, monocultures
Preinoculation
Organic potatoes bought in a supermarket are rinsed with water to remove
soil and dirt and then dried with tissue paper. Potatoes are then surface
sterilized by submerging in Klorin (> 5% Sodium hypochlorite, bought in
supermarkets) for 20 minutes prior to rinsing with Mili-Q water. Sterilized
potatoes are cut in slices of 1 cm and placed on tissue paper wetted with 1*
ml 0.9% NaCl in a petri dish. Wells are dug into each slice by using a p200
pipette tip, resulting in a well app. 8 mm in diameter and 4 mm deep.
*2 ml of 0.9% NaCl were tested as well, this seemed as a better solution, but
due to time limitations the experiments already done were not redone using
the new 0.9% NaCl volume.
Inoculation volume, cell density and incubation time
Overnight cultures are diluted to OD600=1 and washed twice with 0.9% NaCl.
20 µl of the suspension is spotted in the well of the potato slices. The lid is
placed on the petri dish and all dishes are placed in a closed plastic bag to
avoid evaporation. Potatoes are incubated at 25°C for 48h. All experiments
are made in replicates.

5.2
Whole genome sequencing
Puriﬁcation of genomic DNA
Selected strains were grown in liquid LB media for 24 hours at 25°C. We use
PureLinkTM Genomic DNA Mini Kit from ThermoFisher for puriﬁcation
of genomic DNA. We follow the manufacturers protocol for Gram negative
bacteria, with the following amendments:
• Lysate, step 4: Incubation on heat block for 60 minutes
• Eluting DNA, step 1: Leave tube open for 1 minute before continuing
• Eluting DNA, step 2: Elute in 60 µl sterile mili-Q water
Preparation for sequencing
To verify successful puriﬁcation, 5 µl DNA is run on a 0.8% agarosegel,
concentration and quality is checked by NanoDrop measurements. Puriﬁed
DNA is stored on ice and -20°C. 25µl undiluted DNA is delivered for library
preparation and Whole Genome Sequencing using MiSeq, Illumnia at NGS
lab, DTU Biosustain (Technical University of Denmark).
Output trimming
Paired end reads of 2·150 bp are generated. All ﬁles related to the same strain
is combined. These are trimmed, de-novo assembled and ﬁltered using CLC
Genomics Workbench (Qiagen). Trimming with the limit of 0.05, contigs
shorter than 200 bp or with coverage below 10x are removed. BLAST is
used to ﬁnd a reference strain suitable as a reference for assembly of a draft
genome.
Assembly of draft genome
The software Mauve the contig mover (the Darling Lab) is used to rearrange
the contigs. Emboss (EMBL-EBI) is then used to combine the rearranged
contig into one genome ﬁle. The online tool RAST is then used to annotate
the genome.
Analysis of whole genome sequences
Finally, the draft genomes are uploaded to the webservice antiSMASH for
prediction of secondary metabolite gene clusters. Both the standard setting
and the ”ClusterFinder” algorithm was used [33].

Potato Virulence Assay
Here we investigate if our isolated Pseudomonas strains show virulence to-
wards potato tubers, as it is crucial that a potential biocontrol strain is not
pathogenic to the plant it is supposed to protect. Also, we wanted to invest-
igate if a mix of a known pathogen and a biocontrol strain could decrease or
even abolish the virulence of the pathogen. We used a potato tuber based
assay to investigate these two things.

Mono-cultures
In this setup we test the virulence of the strains on potatoes. Table 8 shows
the amount of infection caused by each strain to the potato tubers. We see
that the two strains 1.7 and 3.4 are virulent to the potato, but we also see
that for most of the strains the results are not fully clear. For the strains
1.6, 3.4, 5.4, 5.10, 6.1, 6.7 and 6.10 the infection level has been denoted to

no obvious infection is present on the tubers, but that the tubers had brown
colouring that could not be ruled out to be due to bacterial growth, but most

colouring is a well known phenomenon in many fruits and vegetables. The
amount of brown colouring vary within diﬀerent varieties of the same type of

Adjusting the volume of 0.9% NaCl on the tissue paper
During the work with these assay set-ups we noticed that, despite wetted
tissue paper, the potato tubers were much dryer after incubation than prior
to. Another observation was the fact that the infection on the potato tubers
incubated 24 h prior to inoculation with the pathogens was less virulent than
the ones where the pathogens were inoculated on fresh potato tubers. This
observation led us to believe that the (slightly) reduced infection level of
the incubation time variance assay, was due to lack of water content in the
potato tuber and not due to microbial control. For this reason, we tested
out the ratio diﬀerence assay with a higher amount of water on the tissue
paper. We further more saw that the inoculation volume seemed to aﬀect
the spreading of the infection on the potato tuber slice, in an attempt to
avoid this phenomenon and to get more accurate comparisons, we kept the
inoculation volume the same, but changed the OD concentration of the mixes.
After 48 of incubation the tissue paper was still wet and the surfaces of
the potato tubers was less dry. The infection level on the potato tubers from
the new set up was the same as on the potato tubers inoculated at the same
30
==PAGE:37==
time as the new set up, but with the old assay set up. Based on this, we
recommend the slightly modiﬁed assay for future experiments. Pictures of
the potato tubers from the new assay is not included.

Whole Genome Sequencing
To investigate the genetic make-up of our selected isolated strains, we per-
formed whole genome sequencing. As several genes and gene clusters are
known to be responsible for bioactivity and a lot of these gene clusters
encode secondary metabolites such as non-ribosomal peptides (NRPS) and
polyketide synthetases (PKSs), we wanted to know if our strains encoded any
known gene clusters or any unknown clusters potentially responsible for their
bioactivity. For this, a 2x150bp kit was used for MiSeq sequencing on the
Illumina sequencer at DTU Biosustain. Prior to analysis, all genomes have
been trimmed, ﬁltered and assembled. In order to annotate the genomes, a
reference strain for each isolate genome was required. We chose a reference
strain based on the 16s rRNA sequence data from the Fall 2017. These ref-
erence strains were used as templates for the rearrangement of the contigs
of the isolated strains by the use of the software Mauve the contig mover
(the Darling Lab). To detect secondary metabolites, the whole genome se-
quences were analyzed using the webtool AntiSMASH. This tool is invented
for searching genome sequences for secondary metabolite biosynthetic gene
clusters of microorganisms such as bacteria and fungi. Besides standard set-
tings, this tool also has an algorithm called ”ClusterFinder”. This algorithm
is a probabilistic biosynthetic gene cluster ﬁnder that ﬁnds putative clusters
within the genome. We chose to use this algorithm as well as the standard
setting, to increase the chances of ﬁnding clusters responsible for known bio-
activity. The AntiSMASH outputs with standard setting can be viewed in
appendix E and an overview of the can be found in table 15. A table combin-
ing the output from both the standard setting and the ClusterFinder setting
can be seen in table 14. When evaluating the output of the ClusterFinder
setting, it is important to keep in mind that these results are indications of
potential gene clusters with a some-what similarity to known gene clusters
and not a validation that these gene clusters are present in the evaluated
strain. Further more, these analysis are based on draft genomes and not
actual genomes, all in all will the results from AntiSMASH be able to point
us in the right direction, but will not tell the whole story of why these strains
exhibit bioactivity.

otic, virulence, lipopeptide, other with bioactivity, other without bioactivity

Knock out of the CLA18-06265 gene in strain

We made a gene knock out in the 9.1 strain, isolated in the spring 2017.
This strain has shown to be very eﬃcient in inhibiting the growth of several
known phytopathogens, further more, when whole genome sequencing was
performed, it was discovered that this strain encodes a unique gene cluster
with no known function.

by homologous recombination by the use of the in-house designed plasmid

designed to amplify a product of 713 bp, the second, Primer#2, was designed
to form a product of 550 bp, both primers were designed to amplify a piece
of DNA located about a third to halfway through the gene. A picture of the
agarose gel loaded with PCR product can be viewed in the appendix A ﬁgure
28, so can the gel loaded with digestion mix (ﬁgure 29).

Part IV
Conclusions and Discussion
12
Conclusions and discussion
12.1
Potato virulence assay
The potato virulence assay was new to the group and further more only
described brieﬂy in the literature - often with another purpose than ours.
We have revealed this assay to be eﬃcient in detecting a strain of interest’s
ability to infect potato tubers. However, this assay is also prone to external
inﬂuences that can vary or twist the results obtained, these includes potato
tubers infected with other microorganisms, variations in water content from
potato to potato due to diﬀerences in storage time, insuﬃcient surface disin-
fection and variations in nutrient content due to diﬀerences in potato species
and variations in growth conditions.
We have build a stepping stone for future investigations of the ability of
potential biocontrol strains to inhibit plant pathogens in more natural en-
vironments. The potato virulence assays featuring co-cultivation has shown
promising results, but further investigations has to be made in order to stand-
ardize and verify the results of the assay. Especially the potential reduction
of virulence of pathogens due to lower water content after 24 hours of incuba-
tion in the time/ratio variance assay needs attention. Torres et al. found that
quorum sensing is a crucial need for virulence of Pectobacterium carotovorum
and that exposure to qourum quenching compounds abolished the bioactiv-
ity of this bacteria [32]. We know that cell density and cell communication is
also a crucial need for a bacteria to express antagonistic properties, in our co-
cultivation assays with potato tubers, the pathogens was all known to infect
potato plants, however, we don’t know if our potential biocontrol strains are
able to replicate and form bioﬁlm on a potato slice. In case their growth is
inhibited, 24 hours might not be enough ”head start” to form a solid bioﬁlm.
It could provide valuable knowledge to set up a system allowing for detection
of growth on the potato tubers. This could potentially be done by tagging
the strain under investigation with a ﬂuorescent marker like gfp. This would
allow for detection of growth by e.g. confocal microscopy. An extension to
this set up could be to tag the pathogen with another ﬂuorescent marker
thus allowing investigation of the changes in ratio between the two species
in co-cultivations during a time course.

12.2
Whole Genome Sequencing
We have successfully isolated genomic DNA from all 10 strains, whole genome
sequences was of high quality and it was possible to assemble draft genomes
for 9 out of 10 strains. The antiSMASH results provided useful insights to the
secondary metabolism of these strains. Comparison between the secondary
metabolism of the 9.1 strain and the isolates reveal that 9.1 is the only one of
these strains encoding DAPG, this can indicate that the outstanding activity
of 9.1 is associated with DAPG production.
Looking at the diﬀerent patterns of biocontrol of the isolates, we see that 1.7,
3.4, 5.4, 5.5, 6.3 and 6.7 all inhibit the growth of both Dickeya solani and
Xanthomonas campestris, 5.10 is able to inhibit only D. solani and 6.10 only
X. campestris whereas 6.1 can inhibit both D. solani and P. carotovorum
and 1.6 both D. solani and P. atrosepticum. Only 1.6 and 6.1 are able to
inhibit Pectobacterium, looking at the secondary metabolite clusters we see
no obvious similarity unique to these two isolates. 6.10 is the only strain
encoding a cluster with similarity to cichofactin. Cichofactin is a lipopeptide
and could potentially account for at least some of the bioactivity of the 6.1
strain. Interestingly, 1.7 is the only strain encoding a betalactam thus it
could be interesting to investigate this strains bioactivity towards a Gram-
positive bacteria.
In conclusion we can determine that more software and potentially larger
databases has to be queried in order to obtain a wider picture of the secondary
metabolites encoded by these isolates.
12.3
Construction of a 9.1-mutant
The fact that we have obtained ﬂuorescent colonies being able to grow on PIA
plates with tetracycline is a very strong indication that we have successfully
made a 9.1-mutant by homologous recombination. Designing two sets of PCR
primers to each amplify an 800 bp region covering the two places where the
chromosome and the plasmid DNA has been joined, will allow for two things
i) from the band length on an agarose gel it will be possible to verify that
the integration into the chromosome has happened at the predicted place, ii)
sequencing of the PCR product would reveal exactly where the integration
has taken place and what part of the plasmid has been integrated into the
chromosome. However, sequencing of the whole area would provide deeper
knowledge of the mutants, especially if we have successfully cloned all 6000
bp into the chromosome of 9.1. This is relevant knowledge as the size of the
chromosomal-insert is likely to aﬀect the eﬃciency of the gene-disruption.
Since there seems to be a small variation between the mutants, it may indic-

ate that some mutants have a semi-functionally CLA18-06265 gene left, thus
setting up inhibition assays with all of the mutants could potentially provide
more information about the success rate of the homologous recombination
as well as pin-point a direction of the function of the CLA18-06265 gene.
Also, deeper characterizations of the mutants would be beneﬁcial both un-
derstanding the role of the CLA18-06265 gene and the general understanding
of the conditions under which microorganisms exhibit biocontrol activities.
Our investigations did not reveal an obvious function for the CLA18-06265
gene, however, we have succeeded in providing an excellent stepping stone
for further analysis that hopefully can reveal more about the unknown gene
cluster that CLA18-06265 is part of. We have only tested the constructed
mutants under a very few conditions as well as only towards four pathogens,
it is however very likely that this gene is expressed under diﬀerent conditions
and/or are active against other organisms than the investigated ones. Since
Pseudomonas genomes are known for great plasticity and members of the
genus are excellent adapters.
13
Future Perspectives
Do to the time limitations of this project some experiments had to be left
out.
In case more time had been available, in-dept investigations of the
growth conditions of all strains would provide an overall basic knowledge
to be build upon. It is obvious that many of the experiments conducted
during the course of this project has to made in more replicates. Especially
the growth assays and the inhibition assay has to be replicated in order to
provide reliable results. Further more it could be interesting to set up a mod-
iﬁed version of the inhibition assay where minimum media plates are used
instead of LB-plates. This would provide information about the biocontrol
strain’s ability to inhibit growth of the pathogen when starved for nutrients.
This set up is likely to better imitate the environment of the soil and also as
secondary metabolism is favored under nutrient limitation, this setup could
potentially reveal bioactivity of strains that was previously thought to have
no bioactivity. Metagenomic sequencing of the sampling site would provide
details regarding all the other organisms present. Getting a deeper under-
standing of the soil community could potentially pin-point the right direction
for investigation of what species to test for antagonism. Further more, re-
search have shown that inter-species communication is likely to take place in
the soil, and especially in the rhizosphere. Data from a metagenomic analysis
would thus also be beneﬁcial for providing a list of speciﬁc species that might
cooperate to promote plant growth by combating phytopathogens.
