Abstract
Current robotic control strategies are mainly based on trajectory plans that adjust
the movements based on the next desired state. These control policies do not efﬁciently
perform where the dimensionality of the control problem increases or disturbances are
perturbing the system from the external environment. These issues are critical in locomo-
tion tasks and the need for different control methods arises. To make autonomous robots
able to move in a real and dynamic environment, the research is focused on biologically
inspired controller, such as neuro-controller.
This research project is based on the design and validation of a bio-inspired control ar-
chitecture for robotic locomotion. Our project proposes the interaction among different
bio-inspired motion controllers whose communication represents a simpliﬁed model of the
neural motion control in vertebrates.
The presented solution combines classical control strategies with reservoir computing and
spiking neural networks to obtain a scalable and adaptable controller by taking advantage
of the different learning properties of neural networks. In the report, the features and the
advantages of a central pattern generator (CPG) as a trajectory planner for robotic locomo-
tion are described. Further, the inclusion of an adaptive contribution of a cerebellar-like
neural network in the control loop is argued and discussed. The experiments are performed
on the Neurorobotic Platform (Neurorobotics Platform (NRP)) which offers a simulation
environment where an interface between spiking neural network and simulated or real
robots is easily achievable. The experiments run with the complete control architecture
show improvements on the locomotion performances of the robot in terms of position
error, time delay and covered distance, compared to a control strategy where just the
CPG network is taken into consideration. In conclusion, we can derive that a bio-inspired
control strategies to design new efﬁcient and adaptable robotic locomotion controller is
possible.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The purpose of this research work is to design an adaptable control architecture for
robotic locomotion, based on a simpliﬁed model of the central nervous system control of
locomotion in vertebrates.
This research question has been motivated by the Human Brain Project (HBP) (Markram
(2012)), a large ten-year scientiﬁc research project whose purpose is to build a collaborative
ICT-based scientiﬁc research infrastructure to allow researchers across Europe to collabo-
rate and advance knowledge in the ﬁelds of neuroscience, computing, and brain-related
medicine. In particular, this master project makes its contribution in the Sub-Project 10
of the HBP. This part of the ﬂagship is called Neurorobotics (Falotico et al. (2017)) and
aims to create a communication frame between virtual and real robots and spiking neural
networks, to test brain simulation on robotic bodies which can interact with either virtual
or real environments. This simulation framework is called Neurorobotic Platform (NRP)
(Falotico et al. (2017)).
The idea of using bio-inspired controller in robotics is a major issues of the last ten years,
since autonomous robots, able to learn how to perform many and different task, began to
be very common. The last technological breakthroughs are leading to the question for new
and more efﬁcient adaptive and learning algorithms to control machines.
In order to have robots behaving as close as possible to complete agents, living organisms
have been soon identiﬁed as a rich source of inspiration and as a starting point for innova-
tive artiﬁcial designs. In particular, nature is the best "designer" we have ever observed so
far and through evolution has showed a possible way to ﬁnd out optimized solutions for
speciﬁc environmental constraints. One of the best results of evolution is the parallelism
and the adaptability of the human brain and the central nervous system more in general.
This evidence leaded many research groups of engineering to collaborate with neuroscien-
tists to better understand the key principles and the details on the neural control of different
task and functions in living organisms. On the other hand, neuroscientists would like to

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
see their brain model tested on physical bodies for validating their assumptions and ﬁnding
new signiﬁcant evidences by taking advantage of powerful neural networks simulators.
Since the perceptron (Rosenblatt (1958)), considered as the ﬁrst idea conceived speciﬁcally
as a method to make machines learn, many steps forward has been made. Nowadays,
we are living in the period when the third generation of neural networks, the spiking
neural networks (SNN), are ﬁnding more and more applications. Biological inspiration has
been used in Deep Learning to build hierarchical algorithms for features extraction. The
design of the neural networks is based on the hierarchical organization of many biological
microcircuits, as the one in the retina. More generally, the neurons in SNNs have transfer
functions mimicking the voltage dynamics of biological neurons. In these networks, the
learning mechanism is implemented taking advantage of the different spike timing of a
pair of connected neurons (synaptic plasticity, Abbott and Nelson (2000)).
This work concerns the design and the implementation of a controller for robotic loco-
motion task and it takes inspiration from the neural locomotion control in vertebrate either
in the information exchange among the different modules of the architecture, in the neural
networks topologies and neuron models. The heterogeneity of the architecture design
required knowledge about the neurophysiological principles of locomotion control, the
working principles and implementation strategies for achieving learning and information
transmission in both spiking and standard neural network and the basic theory of robot
robot and tools for running experiments in virtual environment.
This report covers and describes the initial idea for this research and its motivations
(Chapter 2). All the biological assumptions which are behind the design are listed and
all the background information about the concepts and the tools used are detailed in the
chapter. A general description of the working and learning principles of the control strategy
is shown, too. A detailed description on the trajectory planner and the feedback control is
given and reasons for the different implementation choices are discussed (Chapter 3). In
the same chapter, the control architecture design and the communication is integrated with
the description of the adaptive feed-forward contribution. A more technical description of
the software organization in the simulation virtual environment is illustrated (Chapter 4)
and the experimental set-up and results are discussed (Chapter 5). Different and new ideas
for possible future works are presented (Chapter 6) and detailed (Appendix A). Eventually,
we have stressed our conclusions for this project (Chapter 7).

CHAPTER 2
Idea and Concept
Neuro-robotics is a new robotic branch that aims to create model of the neuro-biological
processes, recreate them in a digital and artiﬁcial form and exploit them to control robots.
This goal requires knowledge about:
• the neurophysiology of the process to model;
• the available technological tools for the implementation of the model;
• the relative state-of-the-art accomplishments.
Along this chapter, the addressed problem and the ideas and the theory which inspired
this work are presented. A description of the main neurophysiology principles in vertebrate
locomotion is provided since it is useful to understand the ﬁnal design of the control archi-
tecture. Further, details about spiking neural network dynamics and implementations are
listed to highlight the advantages of using the third generation of neural network compared
to the other previous developed typologies. Eventually, the reservoir computing principles
are presented as a new and efﬁcient way of implementing periodic time sequences learning
and their integration in the control architecture is justiﬁed.
2.1
Problem Statement

2.2
Neurophysiology of Locomotion
Locomotion appears spontaneous and effortless to be performed by both animals and
humans, but a complex neural system controls it. Movements are mainly controlled by
the Central Nervous System (CNS) which generates commands at a cortical and spinal
levels and integrate those commands based on different sensory feedbacks. All the muscles
activation and coordination process is unexpectedly produced without the need of conscious
control (Takakusaki (2013)).

pointed out with black dashed lines and they are devoted to transmit the sensory data from
the PNS to the CNS.

instantly react to their sensory inputs.

All along the way from the spinal cord to the muscles and back, millions of neurons

The information in the nervous system is encoded in the membrane potential of a neuron
and, more in detail, in its frequency of communication with its neighbour neurons.
Neurons communicate to each other through synapses. Synapses is the point where a
nervous impulse passes from one neuron to another. When the membrane depolarization
reaches the terminal part of the neural cell, called axon or pre-synaptic button, the synapses
happens. There exist two types of synapses:
• electrical synapses: a special channel called gap junction connect two neurons. It
is capable of passing an electric current, causing voltage changes in the pre-synaptic
cell to induce voltage changes in the post-synaptic cell. The main advantage of an
electrical synapse is the rapid transfer of signals from one cell to the next one;
• chemical synapses: the electrical activity in the pre-synaptic neuron is converted
(via the activation of voltage-gated calcium channels) into the release of a chemical
called a neurotransmitter that binds to receptors located in the plasma membrane of
the post-synaptic cell, increase the membrane potential in the post-synaptic button
in the dendrite of the close neuron (Fig.2.5). Compared to the electrical synapses,
they are more diffuse in the cortex and they can gain the received input signal.

Each synapses does not respond with the same responsiveness to input currents coming
from different neurons. Most of the neural circuits are not hard-wired, but their connection
relevance changes in response to a variety of stimuli. This process of experience-dependent
changes in synaptic connectivity is called synaptic plasticity (Ho et al. (2011)). Processes
that are modulated by experience to change synaptic connectivity are alterations in neuro-
transmitter release, trans-synaptic signaling, post-synaptic receptor dynamics and gene
expression within neurons. The main two biological mechanism which regulate synaptic
plasticity are called Long Term Potentiation (LTP) and Long Term Depression (LTD). LTP
is related to the opening of N-Methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) channels, which increase the
cellular membrane depolarization and leads to a rise in post-synaptic Ca2+ concentration.
On the other hand, LTD is due to weaker depolarization resulting in less Ca2+ entering the
post-synaptic neuron and lower intra-cellular Ca2+ concentrations.

Reservoir Computing and Learning
Among the currently used methodologies for implementing learning in neural networks,
reservoir computing came out to be particularly interesting.
Reservoir computing is based on the communication between a ﬁxed and random dynami-
cal system, called reservoir, and a simple readout mechanism. Typically an input signal is
fed into the reservoir and its dynamics map it to an higher dimension and then, while the
readout mechanism is trained to read the state of the reservoir and map it to the desired
output.
Based on the state-of-the-art (Wyffels and Schrauwen (2009)), an implementation of a
CPG model with reservoir computing is possible and it can be exploited in real robotic
locomotion, too.
Though a general method to design CPG does not exists, the article lists the main char-
acteristic to design a proper CPG. Here below, a list of the most important features for a
CPG network:
• ability to generate rhythmic patterns;
• stable pattern generation and robustness against perturbations is necessary; ﬁtting
in a hierarchical scheme: rhythmic patterns are generated on low level, high level
controllers only deal with modulation of locomotion. Thus, the models have low
dimensional inputs with which the shape of the learned signals can be controlled;
• ability to cope with multidimensional pattern generation. Apart from the patterns,
the phase relation between the generated patterns is determined.

2.7
State-of-the-art
The idea of using a bio-inspired controller for locomotion task is not a completely new
idea. During the last years, neuro-controllers have become a common solution to design
adaptable controllers.
Spinal cord model have been used to prove how a primitive neural circuit used for gen-
erating certain kind of periodic motion pattern can be extended for generating different
type of locomotion. In the case of the work from Ijspeert et al. (2007) the CPG model is
used for demonstrate the switching between swimming-like to walking-like locomotion
by just changing few parameters of the model, as the oscillation threshold for the neural
oscillators. In particular, the work proposes that the addition of oscillatory centers together
with the modiﬁcation of intrinsic and saturation frequencies in spinal oscillators could
provide a general mechanism for the generation of multiple gaits in vertebrates.
The main implication of this study for vertebrate locomotion is to show how a tetrapod
locomotion controller can be built on top of a primitive swimming circuit and explain the
mechanisms of gait transition, the switch between traveling and standing waves of body
undulations, and the coordination between body and limbs. This work extends models of
gait transitions as bifurcation phenomena by taking evolutionary modiﬁcations into account
and proposing that the addition of oscillatory centers together with the modiﬁcation of
intrinsic and saturation frequencies in spinal oscillators could provide a general mechanism
for the generation of multiple gaits in vertebrates. CPGs offer an interesting approach
to solving the problem of online trajectory generation by using the limit cycle behavior
of coupled oscillators to produce the motor commands in real time. CPG-based control
allows to reduce the dimensionality of the locomotion control problem while remaining
highly ﬂexible to continuously adjust velocity, direction, and type of gait according to the
environmental context.

A planned set of trajectories and a desired walking speed are the information provided by
the cortex, speciﬁcally in the motor cortex to the spinal cord. Based on the higher level
desired trajectories and on the desired velocity, the CPG network oscillations modulate the
periodic activation of group of motor-neurons (muscular synergies). The motor-neurons

bio-information about the muscular force goes to the spinal cord as a reﬂective information
about the actual state of the muscle. The desired trajectories and the walking speed
for the robot can be represented as a command coming from the cortex to the spinal
cord and to the cerebellum. In the spinal cord the position command is converted into
a torque command for the motor neurons to reach the speciﬁc muscles. The feedback
bio-information about the muscular force goes to the spinal cord as a feedback control
loop and to the cerebellum. As in the case of modulated locomotion, the cerebellum
provides a muscular force correction to be summed up to the neural motor signal form the
CPG. The cerebellar microcircuit takes as input the ﬁnal force command to the muscle,
the current joint position and the planned position to abstract an inverse model of the
locomotive system and minimizing the error between the plan and the desired anatomical
joint position.

3.1
Trajectory planner
As described in the previous chapter, vertebrates are able to generate rhythmic activities
creating locomotion patterns as a low level brain function. Neural networks dedicated
to this purpose are located in the spinal cord. In most of the animal this part is what the
central pattern generator (CPG) achieves together with the muscular synergies, mapped in
the spinal cord, which allow vertebrates to move, combining the activation of muscular
motor primitives.
In this work, the Central Pattern Generator (CPG) neural network, which has the role of
a trajectory planner for the robot, is constituted by a spiking neural network (SNN), a
network where the different neurons transfer functions mimic the voltage dynamics of
biological neurons. In addition, compared to conventional artiﬁcial neural networks, SNNs
yield higher computational power, due to the binary encoding of the information, and their
learning and computing mechanisms are closely resembling the ones in the brain (see
synaptic plasticity at section 2.3).
The topology of the used SNN is shown in Fig. 3.4. A similar network has been already
designed and used as a trajectory planner for a compliant quadruped robot, Tigrillo robot
(Willems et al. (2017)), at the University of Ghent and to pursue the solution for this
project, its size and parameters have been adapted to the particular case of the quadruped
Fable robot.
The motivation under the choice of this neural network is its ability to learn easily different
periodic trajectories. The great amounts of possible tuning parameters to improve the
learning performances, due either to the articulated topology of the network itself and to
the spiking neuron units, is also relevant for the choice. In the speciﬁc case of this project,
it is possible to modulate the walking speed of the robot, by modify one parameter of the
network, corresponding to the frequency of the π/2 shifted alternating signals input to the
ﬁrst layer of network.
Thus, in developing new controller architecture based on neural networks, the emulation
of the spiking dynamics of biological neurons became of strong interest.

Neuron). This model is very simple and efﬁcient from a computational point of view and it

3.1.2
Neural network learning
The design of the neural network exploits the concept of reservoir computing and
its expansion property to extract more complex rhythmic pattern, having as input to the
network simple signals (for this project, two simple alternating signals). Using reservoir
computing to design CPG is not a new idea, but the concept of using spiking neural instead
of the usual sigmoid neuronal units is one of the main innovative features of this network.
The reason for using spiking neurons in reservoir computing is to use population coding
to encode the useful information and achieve a learning mechanism. In this case, the
reservoir exploits the same principles of rate based encoding, but in this case having as a
reservoir unit a population of spiking neurons.
The connections where the learning phase is actually performed are the ones between
the reservoir and the readout layer, following the same working principle as Echo State
Networks (ESN) (Jaeger (2007)). The 4 readout neurons are connected to all reservoir
populations and the weights of these connections are learned through FORCE learning
(Sussillo and Abbott (2009)). This learning mechanism can be easily implemented using a

controller in this case is a torque value. According to what the ﬁgure shows, the tuning
of the three K gains is needed in order to obtain a controller which reacts to the error in
the desired time and with the desired accuracy to stabilize the system. The contributions
which should be summed up to give the correct control command to the plant are three:
• P: it makes its contribution based on the present state of the system by multiplying a
Kp gain to the error;
• I: it plays its role based on the accumulation of the past state of the system by
multiplying a Ki gain to the integral of the error (in the discrete time implementation,
the gain is multiplied by the sampling time and by the summation of the error over
time);
• D: it contributes to predict the next state of the system because takes into account
how the system dynamics are changing, that is if the error is increasing or decreasing.
Its action is performed by multiply a gain Kd to the derivative of the error (in the
discrete time implementation, the gain is multiplied by the difference between the
present and the past error, divided by the sampling time of the process, computed in
real time);
Thus, tuning a PID means select these 3 K gains in order to have the desired dynamic
response in the system. There exist multiple methods for tuning PIDs in an optimal way
(Panda et al. (2004)), but for the purposes of this project we experimentally tuned the
controller, looking at the response dynamics of the system. Following a rule of thumb, we
ﬁrst select the P gain, then the D and ﬁnally the I.
Once we found the desired gains magnitudes, we need to take into account the inverse
dynamics of the system. For this purpose, the controller output is multiplied by the inertia
matrix representing the robotic link that the actuated revolute joint is controlling.

In our simpliﬁed control model which mimics the biological neural motor control, this
part of the architecture has the role of abstracting the internal model of the robot and
providing a corrective effort commands based on the information about the current state
of the system (current position and desired position at each time-step) and on a teaching
signal (the motor command from the CPG). More speciﬁcally, the LWPR algorithm derives
its contribution τ LWPR, elaborating the internal model of the robot, while the cerebellar
network provide the corrective contribution τ DCN based on the teaching signal. These tho
effort commands are ﬁnally summed up and added to the CPG effort command τ CPG.
The LWPR is an algorithm for incremental nonlinear function approximation in high-
dimensional spaces with redundant and irrelevant input dimensions (Vijayakumar et al.
(2005)). This machine learning technique is computationally efﬁcient and numerically
robust thanks to its regression algorithm; it creates and combines N linear local models
which perform the regression analysis in selected directions of the input space, taking
inspiration from the partial least squares regression. The main advantages of using the
described learning algorithm are listed in the following:
• it optimizes the role of a particular group of cells in the cerebellum, called granular
cells which exploit their particular plasticity to learn the dynamic model of the body
for motor control;
• it allows rapid learning based on incremental training which perfectly ﬁt in the
speciﬁcation of our system which should be able to perform on-line learning, based
on the dynamical environmental constraints;
• its learning is extremely fast and accurate since the weights of each kernel is based
only on local information and its computational complexity is linear for each input
information.

4.1.4
Cerebellar neural network tuning
The parameter of the ULM which has been mainly tuned are listed below; ﬁrst the two
main parameters related to the creation of new receptive ﬁelds (granular cell neurons) are
explained and then, two important parameters, related to the local regression algorithm are
described.
• init D (Dk): it represents the initial distance metric which is assigned to each new
created receptive ﬁeld. Even if the distance metric is updated at each iteration, the
initial value plays an important role in the learning procedure. Small values of D
lead to bigger receptive ﬁelds and delayed converge while large values carry small
receptive ﬁelds and possibility of over-ﬁtting.
==PAGE:73==
4.1. IMPLEMENTATION

• w gen: the weight activation threshold is critical for the creation of new receptive
ﬁelds. If no local model shows an activation greater than this value, a new receptive
ﬁelds is generated.
• init α: it is the initialization value for the learning rate in the gradient descent
algorithm which is minimizing the error in the different regression of the input space.
Increasing this value can lead to a fast learning, but too large values cause instability
in the convergence of the system.
• add thresh: it operates as a threshold value to stand when a new regression direction
should be added to the algorithm. If the ratio between the mean squared error of the
current regression dimension and the same mean squared error, at the previous time
iteration, is lower than this value, thus, a new regression direction can be exploited
in the robot modeling process.

4.3.5
Controller Transfer Function
The controller tf is strictly related to the ULMs class, explained in the previous section.
This tf is the main part of the software for the control architecture, where the adaptive
contribution from the cerebellar-like neural network is computed and interfaced to the
robot.
Here, the motor effort published on the topic spinal cord is read and adjusted by taking
into account a non-precise model of the inverse dynamics of the Fable module and will
be called τ CPG. Then, for each of the 4 actively controlled joints, the sensory information
(desired position for the joint and current position of all the joints) is input to the LWPR
module of the Unit Learning Machine class which plays the role of the cerebellar mossy
ﬁbers and the τ CPG is input to the DCN module as a teaching signal to the inferior olives in
the cerebellum. Eventually, the ﬁnal control input is computed as the sum of τ CPG and the
output of the LWPR and DCN module in the ULM class and it is sent to the robotic joints
through ROS services. Also the neck joints receive a control input which in this case it is
just τ PID, because their reference is zero position. They are not subjected to any adaptive
control and their control input takes into account the inverse kinematics of the robotic
module to keep the joints in zero position, even if the dynamics model is very inaccurate
on purpose.
The last part of the transfer function is devoted to the update of the ULMs. To this purpose,
the information used before as input to the LWPR module and the total control input
contribution for the 4 adaptive joints are input to the ULMs class, carrying information
about the current state of the system.

5.3
Discussion
In the previous section, how the information is communicated through the control
system in the different stages has been explained and illustrated, through plots and ﬁgures.
In this section a statistical analysis and discussion on the data is presented.
Before, the case when a Savitzky-Golay smoothing ﬁlter is applied to the reference
trajectories is presented. Thus, the case when the reference trajectories are on-line provided
by the spiking neural network is analyzed and ﬁnally, a brief comparison between the two
cases has been introduced. All the simulations have been run under the same conditions and
for the same time interval: 3.38 minutes in the simulation environment which correspond
to 14:23 minutes of program execution. These multiple experiments have been performed
in order to have the different trials as more independent and identically distributed (iid) as
possible and to check the repetibility of the controller performances.
As expected, the cerebellar-like neural network performs better in the case of ﬁltered
reference trajectories due to the great periodicity of the signals (Fig. 5.36). On the other
hand, the distance covered by the robot in the same time windows is in both cases longer
when we integrate the ULM contribution in the control loop, but the trials where it walks
the longest distance are the ones when no S-G ﬁlter is applied (Fig. 5.37). This result is
due to the fact that the movements of the robot during the simulation are noisier without
the ﬁlter action, and the continuous swinging makes the simulated robot walking more
around in the environment even though the following of the reference trajectories from the
CPG is less accurate (Fig.5.36). In any event, this behaviour is justiﬁed by the fact that the
teaching signals for the cerebellar network are independent for each one fo the leg so that
the neural network have 4 sub-objective to optimize and not an unique global goal for the
robot, such as the maximization of the covered distance. More details about a global robot
objective for the ULM learning are treated in Chapter 6.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusions
This report contains all the documentation relative to the design and implementation of
a bio-inspired architecture for robotic locomotion task.
The proposed control strategy provides the robot with a spiking neural network in charge
of being a trajectory planner for the robot and a roughly tuned PID regulator to assure a
preliminary stability of the system. The control system performances are then improved by
an on-line learning neural network which can assure a better behaviour of the system, in
terms of position error, both in the case where the reference trajectories are directly output
from the CPG during the simulation or in the case where a smoothing ﬁlter is applied on
the desired trajectories, in advance.
The feed-forward effort contribution decreases the mean of the position error of 0.3 radiant
and its variance of 0.01 radiant compared to the case when just the effort command from
the CPG is provided to the robot. Moreover, the trend of the error is decreasing along
the simulation time and the distance covered by the robot with the cerebellar-like neural
network contribution is 9.48 m while the CPG-PID controller contributes to have the robot
walking for 1.39 m.
The modular conﬁguration of the Fable robot makes easier to test the control strategy for
different conﬁgurations of the robot and patterns of locomotion, having the cerebellar-like
neural network compensate the error, since it has previously learnt the internal model
of the system. All the experiments have been performed on the Neurorobotics Platform
(NRP), to give new users the chance to work on similar neuro-controller implementations
by having this project as a starting point. The NRP is open-source and it also allows to
run spiking neural network on neuromorphic hardware and control real robots by the ROS
communication protocol.
So far, the most reliable way to ﬁnd out the working principles of the neural locomotion
control in vertebrate is the in vivo recording of their neural activities, at different levels
(microcircuit activity or neurons membrane potential). This research direction implies

many challenges and technological and ethical limits. It is clear that, despite the issues of
performing experiments on conscious animals, this may be the only way to understand how
all areas of the locomotive system are incorporated into the goals of the animal (WHELAN

bio-inspired models to control robotic tasks, as in the case of this project, can ﬁnd proofs of

control architecture design, which takes an accurate inspiration by neuron-physiological
data-based models. Thus, the study of the behaviour and the performance of the model in
the control of real robots would be a new foundation to make assumptions and verify them
in the biological case.
