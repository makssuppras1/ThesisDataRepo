Abstract
This thesis investigate the possibilities in vision based feedback for industrial robots. To
investigate this a UR10 robot arm, a Guppy F-036C camera and a Robotiq gripper has been used in
combination to manipulate an object by vision based feedback. The performance of the UR10 is
examined, resulting in the creation of a simulation model and its corresponding feedback controller.
Forward kinematic is used to ﬁnd the location and orientation of the end effector, and thereby to
know the location of objects seen from the camera. The grippers encompassing grip is used to
release the locking mechanism, required prior to pulling out the knife of the pig. This leads to the
system implementation, where the test case is a slaughterhouse, where a knife need to be pulled
out of a pig. It is concluded that it is possible to successfully remove the knife from the pig, and
place it in the sheath, by the combinational use of robot arm, video feedback and the gripper. A
fast version of the solution is also provided.
Movej cannot be used when a continuous movement is updated along the way, this makes the
robot arm jerk when interrupted, resulting in a torque limit violation. The movement command
speedj don’t have this problem and is used instead. There is a delay of 24 ms when sending a move
command to the UR10. The acceleration and velocity are limited to within +/- 35.5 rad/s2 and
+/- 3.3 rad/s respectively. The simulation model was created with its corresponding feedback PD
controller showing that a P value of 18.62 and a D value of 1.2 sufﬁciently decreases the overshoot
without slowing the system too much. An image can be received and processed with a frame rate
of 34.89 fps by splitting the process into threads. A HSV ﬁlter in combination with morphological
operations are successfully used to remove noise from the pictures, making it possible to locate the
objects center of mass in camera frame. Finally ideas for future work are presented.

Resume
Dette projekt undersøger mulighederne i vision baseret feedback i forbindelse med indus-
trirobotter. For at undersøge dette er kombinationen af en UR10 robot arm, et Guppy F-036C
kamera og en Robotiq gripper brugt til at manipulere et objekt ved hjælp af vision baseret feedback.
Ydeevnen af UR10 er testet og brugt til at lave simuleringsmodellen og dens tilhørende feedback
kontroller. Forward kinematik bliver brugt til at ﬁnde lokation og orientering af endeeffektoren og
derved at kende lokationen af objektet set af kameraet. Gripperens omsluttende greb bliver brugt
til at udløse låsemekanismen, som skal gøres før man kan hive kniven ud af grisen. Dette leder
til system implementeringen, hvor et slagteri bliver brugt som test case, her skal en kniv trækkes
ud en gris. Det bliver konkluderet at det er muligt ved brug af henholdsvis robot arm, kamera og
gripper at trække kniven ud af grisen og placere denne i dens tilhørende holder. En hurtig version
af løsningen er også implementeret.
Movej kan ikke bruges til at kontrollere en kontinuerlig bevægelse der opdateres undervejs, i det
dette vil få robot armen til at rykke kraftigt, hvilket resulterer i en overtrædelse i momentgrænsen.
Bevægelseskommandoen speedj har derimod ikke dette problem og bliver på den baggrund brugt
i stedet. Der er en forsinkelse på 24 ms når man sender en bevægelseskommando til UR10’eren.
Accelerationen og hastigheden er begrænset til at være indenfor henholdsvis +/- 35.5 rad/s2 og
+/- 3.3 rad/s. Simulationsmodellen blev lavet med dens tilsvarende feedback PD kontroller, med
en P værdi på 18.62 og en D værdi på 1.2 der begrænser overskridelsen uden at gøre systemet
alt for langsomt. Et billede kan modtages og behandles med en billedhastighed på 34.89 billeder
per skund, dette sker ved at splitte processen op i ﬂere tråde. Et HSV ﬁlter i samarbejde med
morfologiske operationer bliver med success brugt til at fjerne støj fra billederne, hvilket gør det
muligt at lokalisere objektets tyngdepunkt i det enkelte billede. Til slut bliver ideer til fremtidigt
arbejde præsenteret.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Robots are getting more common in the industry, replacing human labour in repeatable arduous
work, where the same task is executed over and over again. But the traditional industrial robots has
their limitations, they are vulnerable to changes in the environment and have a hard time to adapt, as
they are specialised to only one task. As the robots operate blindly, they rely on ofﬂine data in order
to plan the path of their movements. It requires expensive devices to ﬁxate the objects, in order
to operate without feedback. If there are variations in the task the robot is unable to predict the
location of the object. These robots also need to be caged in, in order to separate them from humans.
This setup is both costly and time consuming. Then it is cheaper to have a more fault-tolerant setup
with a camera that can recognize the object without the need to be a speciﬁc place each time, this
can be done during video feedback. The video feedback combined with human friendly robots, that
does not require a cage, makes it much more ﬂexible, lowers the cost and is faster to implement.
The purpose of this project is to show the possibilities of automating using video feedback,
with a robot that is safe enough to neglect the need of a safety guarding, thus eventually making the
process easier and more efﬁcient.
The project is about the process of slaughtering a pig. The process of slaughtering a pig starts
with anesthetizing the pig, while being unconscious it is being hung up in the leg and a knife is
stabbed into the carotid artery, and the pig dies from blood loss. The knife is hollow and connected
to a hose, to remove the blood transferring it to a chamber, therefore the knife is left in the pig.
After some time the knife is pulled out. The problem I will investigate is to automate the last step
in the process of slaughtering a pig; pulling out the knife.

It is safe for humans to work around the robot and it does not require a safety guarding. If the robot

The robot is controlled by transmitting predeﬁned commands via the network, using the

The UR 10 can be controlled in three different levels, see ﬁgure 2.2.
1. The graphical user interface level using PolyScope Robot User Interface from Universal Robots,
it runs on a touch screen attached to the UR controller unit.
2. The script level using URScript, the robot programming language used to control the robot.
3. C-API level, this is a low level language which is good for research and for torque control.
It is not possible to use the ﬁrst level of control, with the graphical user interface, as the solution
require interaction with external video devices, unsupported by PolyScope. The low level C-API is
not desirable as it is time-consuming and overcomplicated compared to a higher level language. As
the high level language using URScript fulﬁls the need for operation, this is chosen as the level of
control, see diagram 2.2.
Connecting to the controller over TDC/IP can be done using one of the three ports: 30001,
30002 or 30003. Port 30001 and 30002 are the primary and secondary robot state interfaces and
communicate at 10 Hz, while port 30003 is the real time client interface and communicates at 125
Hz. The robot state interfaces provides information about the robot states, including: controller
temperature, main voltage, average robot power, robot current, IO current, tool current, individual
joint voltage, temperature, current load, joint positions etc. Unlike the secondary interface, the
primary interface also include status and warning messages shown on the GUI. The real time
interface receives 756 bytes including actual and target joint- position, velocity, acceleration and
current, and actual joint moments (torques), TCP- force and speed and digital inputs. The real
time interface has been chosen in this project as it has the speed needed for providing the extended
status information to the real time vision feedback system.

The commands used in this thesis are shown in table 2.1. When the robot arm is reaching for
the knife, the goal position is updated along the way using vision feedback control. Initially the
code was designed to use movej instead of speedj, but the robot arm was jerking when interrupted
causing a torque limit violation, similar to the ﬁndings in Ravn et al. (2014). Instead a speedj
command is used as it takes the joint speeds, joint acceleration and time. It is easier to update the
joint speeds, as it is a continuous movement with roughly the same direction. The joint angles
corresponding to the different positions is found using inverse kinematic.
I’m using the Kelsey Hawkins inverse kinematics for the universal robots UR10, Hawkins
(2016-02-05). There are up to eight possible solutions with the combination of shoulder right/left,
elbow up/down and wrist up/down positions. When I get the different solutions, I make a test to
see which of the possible solutions is closest to the current joint angles. As seen in equation (2.1), I
calculate the difference between the current angle position and the angle positions suggested by the
solutions. Then I summarize the differences from the six angles and compare the result to the other
possible solutions. The solution giving the lowest summarized difference is the solution chosen.
The joint angles from the solution is used in the speedj command, in order to give a continuous
movement when reaching for the knife.

For the ofﬂine navigation part, movej have been used instead of movel, as movel uses the target
position, acceleration and velocity. Even when the target position only changed a few centimetres,

fail-safe, a user have to manually operate the touch screen attached to the controller.

will be interrupted and the second command will be executed instead. To make sure that the

two commands are executed as a series of continuous movements, a delay is used between each
movement. To make sure that the duration of the delay is exactly the same as the duration of the

It is not possible to make individual accelerations for each joint, when reaching for the knife,
and it therefore end up in unwanted directions along the way, where the gripper and the camera is
not pointing towards the knife during the movement. To solve this I had to make ten way points
along the way with updated goal positions. Each time the robot arm gets to a waypoint, it receives
the updated joint- speeds and acceleration, corresponding to the direction of the next waypoint.
2.4
Performance test
To make a performance test, the latency have to be estimated. By sending a move command to
the UR10 and continuously log the received positions, it is possible to measure how many samples
the delay consists of, that is the time it takes from the move command is sent, to the robot starts
moving. By looking in the log, it is stated that it takes 4 samples from sending the command to the
robot arm starts moving, this makes a delay of 3 samples, as only the ﬁrst 3 samples had the same
unchanged value. A new sample is received every 8 ms, the delay is then roughly estimated to 24
ms.

2.5
Evaluation of model
A P-controller is designed with the value of 18.62 but makes an overshoot, due to the rate
limiter. To compensate for that a D controller is implemented in parallel with the P controller, by
simulation it is seen that using the value 1.2 for the D part will greatly reduce the overshoot in the
simulation, going from an overshoot of 27.67 % to an overshoot of only 1.05 % 2.5(a). Using a
lower D value would not sufﬁciently decrease the overshoot and a higher value would make the
system slower.
When looking at the difference between the simulations and the measured values from the
UR10 2.5(b), it is clear that without the D controller the maximum difference is 6.9 times larger
due to the overshoot. The PD controller appears to be very similar to the measured values from the
UR10 when responding to a step, compared to rise time, overshoot and settling time, as expected.
Though the simulation has a larger error band of +/- 68 · 10−4, while the UR10 has an error band
of +/- 2 · 10−4. Due to the relatively small values, these errors are of no signiﬁcance.

will interrupt the ﬁrst one, and the second command will be executed instead.

points need to be used in order to make sure that the camera is pointing towards the object at all
time.

HSV color space
HSV (Hue, Saturation and value) is often used in image processing applications because it,
unlike RGB, separates hue from brightness. This allows for more robust color representation, as a
shadow on a red surface in the RGB scale will make the surface seem different from red, in the
HSV scale the hue will remain the same, shadow or not, and only the saturation and the value

Image processing
There will often be noise in the image, some minor areas "lighting up", disturbing the bigger
picture, ﬁgure 4.9(a). The morphological operations erode and dilate is used to ﬁlter out noise from
the image. The erode method erodes into the white space, making it smaller or non-existing, this
removes the minor white areas but it also decreases the white area of interest, ﬁgure 4.9(b). To
counteract this a dilate function is used, to increase the size of the remaining white space, this ﬁlls
in the small gaps that was created at the erode function, ﬁgure 4.9(c). The erode and dilate function
used in this project, uses a erode and dilate rectangle matrix element, with the size 3 by 3 and 8 by
8 respectively this has shown good results in noise reduction.
In order to locate the object, a vector of contours is found from the binary image. The moments
method then takes the vector of contours as input and outputs the x and y center coordinates of the
largest contour deﬁned by its inner area.
Here the openCV moment method has been used, the order of moments is deﬁned by summarizing
the two indices j and i, the summarized value is the order of moments.

The ﬁrst order moment, (j,i) = (1,0) or (0,1), describes the center of mass of the object ¯x and ¯y.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
It was seen that only the movej command was suitable for continuous movements, updates
underway. A signiﬁcant time delay was discovered in the communication when sending move
commands to the UR10. The performance of UR10 was further examined revealing a acceleration
and velocity limitation of within +/- 35.5 rad/s2 and +/- 3.3 rad/s respectively. This examination
lead to a simulation model of the UR10 including a PD feedback controller. The controller reacted
very much like the real system when giving a step input.
Even when the HSV scale is more preferable than RGB, the HSV gets disturbed by the change
in sunlight during the day, this is due to the clouds making a change in light temperature, between
sunlight and electrical light, making it harder to have a stationary HSV value. It would be a much
more stable system if it was tested in a closed room with controlled lighting like a production
facility. The frame rate test showed that after splitting it up into two threads, the mean fps was
34.89 frames per second. The frame rate would be faster if it was executed on a faster computer or
was split up into even more threads.
With this thesis it has been showed that it is possible to successfully use video feedback to
manipulate objects, without knowing its precise location in the beginning.

CHAPTER 8
Future work
• When the gripper is close to the object the robot is blinded, as it cannot see the object
anymore, nether does it know if the object is still in the same position. It would be an
advantage with two cameras placed on a stand in each side of the taget area, so that it could
grab the knife even when the gripper is very close to the object. The stereo vision also makes
it possible to use triangulation in order to calculate the distance to the object, see appendix
A.2.
• The orientation can be determined by looking at the edges, if the color coded plate is ﬂat.
This is possible by measuring the pixels of the four sides, if the left side appears longer than
the right we know that the left side is closer, and the camera view is thereby to the left of
the object. By looking at the top and bottom lengths of the color code the tilt up and down
can be measured in the same way as the left and right. At the same time the distance to the
object can be determined as the size of the object is known. Then we have both orientation
and position of the object.
• It would be a advantage to have feedback from the gripper, to know if the gripper has grabbed
the object or not. This would let the gripper try to grab it once more, if it detected that it
missed the ﬁrst time.
