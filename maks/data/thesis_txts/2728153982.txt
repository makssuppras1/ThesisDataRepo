Abstract
The escalating energy demand and limited space for bottom-fixed offshore wind farm
projects require the acceleration of floating offshore wind projects. In this master thesis,
the original model for bottom-fixed offshore wind farms is extended to floating offshore
wind farms. The objective is to provide a tool that estimates a floating wind farm’s potential
production and cost based on easily accessible input parameters.
Firstly, in the production model, a calibration of the wake parameter is implemented. It is
obtained from calculating production at different locations inside a basic equally-spaced
square layout of wind turbines using the Fuga wake. This calibrated version is tested and
compared with the original implementation on a PyWake-compatible experimental setup.
This last one implies the derivation of the area of the wind farm with the wind turbine layout
and the extraction of a wind climate from the Global Wind Atlas for six real bottom-fixed
wind farms. The results include a comparison with other PyWake models and demonstrate
an improved estimation accuracy with the Fuga-calibrated version of the ”Minimalistic”
model.
Secondly, on to the global Capital Expenditure (CAPEX) and Operational Expenditure
(OPEX) analysis, a comprehensive breakdown of the different components of a floating
wind farm is conducted. The dependencies between cost and the input parameters of the
model are derived. Specifically, the reliance on turbines, transmission systems, anchors,
and installation costs on input variables are identified and supported by an extensive liter-
ature review. The floating platform cost is evaluated by multiplying the estimated platform
weight by the material cost. At the same time, a similar approach is used to estimate the
mooring line cost by multiplying the estimated length of a catenary mooring line by the
cost of the chain. Through a CAPEX breakdown, the significant influence of wind turbine
and floating platform costs on the total project cost is uncovered.
As a result, using both production and cost estimates derived from these analyses, LCoE
is calculated for a fictive case. It involves a wind farm with 100 wind turbines with a
power rated of 15MW and the available wind and bathymetry data from the North Sea.
The results indicate that a semi-submersible floating wind farm becomes a cost-effective
choice over a bottom-fixed wind farm at depths exceeding 60 - 65 m. Within the specific
case in the North Sea, the estimated LCoE ranges between 11 and 14 €cents/kWh with
a stronger dependence on wind speed compared to bathymetry.

Background and motivation for the research
Climate change has significantly impacted the Earth, leading to a pressing need for imme-
diate action to address this critical issue. At the same time, the increasing global demand
for energy has presented additional challenges that require attention. These intercon-
nected challenges necessitate a transition towards sustainable and renewable energy
sources, which has sparked a global movement towards adopting renewable energy.
The continuous rise in global temperatures is evident, with July 2022 marking the 46th
consecutive year and the 451st consecutive month where temperatures exceeded the
20th-century average. Factors like inflation, energy price fluctuations, and geopolitical
conflicts like Ukraine’s have elevated global food prices.
Moreover, electricity prices
within the European Union reached unprecedented levels in 2022. The progress of the
energy transition has encountered significant obstacles, exacerbated by the aftermath
of the COVID-19 pandemic and the repercussions of the Ukraine crisis. Based on these
facts and according to the International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA), it is of utmost
importance to take swift action, as even slight changes in global temperature can have
profound consequences for natural systems, human societies, and economies. Urgent
and transformative measures are imperative to realign the energy transition trajectory [1].
While numerous nations have ratified the Paris Agreement and submitted their Nationally
Determined Contributions (NDCs), there is a pressing need to enhance collective ambi-
tion to effectively limit the global temperature increase to 1.5◦C. Despite the impressive
milestone of investing USD 1.3 trillion in energy transition technologies in 2022, annual
investments must substantially increase to align with the desired 1.5◦C trajectory. To suc-
cessfully mitigate global warming, a cumulative investment of USD 150 trillion by 2050
is required, translating to an average annual investment of over USD 5 trillion. Con-
sequently, an additional USD 47 trillion in cumulative investments must be redirected
towards energy transition technologies and infrastructure. This signifies the urgency to
abandon reliance on fossil fuel-based technologies [2]. Refer to Figure 1.1 for a visual
representation of this information.
Offshore wind energy stands out as one of the most competitive renewable energy sources.
Governments worldwide are increasingly focusing on offshore wind because it can revo-

ligence indicate that over the next ten years (2022-2031), more than 315 GW of new

Literature review
2.1
State of the art of floating wind technology
In recent years, offshore wind installed capacity has increased significantly globally, sur-
passing 56 GW worldwide, reaching the grid connection of 21.1 GW in 2021, three times
more than in 2020 [3]. However, with the prime sites for bottom-fixed wind farms already
claimed, floating structures have become the only option for exploiting the vast majority
(80%) of offshore wind resources located in waters deeper than 60 meters [7]. Floating
offshore wind technology offers various benefits, such as increased wind speeds from
deployment in deeper waters, more spacious deployment options, reduced visual impact,
and a gentler effect on the seabed environment. Furthermore, it can benefit countries
with limited potential for bottom-fixed wind by providing socio-economic benefits, such as
job creation and a smoother energy transition. It also allows them to benefit from oil and
gas expertise in delivering massive engineering projects in deep water locations.
MW-scale floating technologies have been tested through demonstration and pilot projects
in Europe and Asia over the past decade. Currently, floating offshore wind farms operate
successfully in the UK and Portugal, with a significant pipeline of projects in different global
markets. The success of the UK’s ScotWind leasing round, where 15 GW out of 25 GW of
sites awarded leasing contracts are for floating projects, highlights the sector’s appetite
for delivering large GW-scale projects. In addition, floating offshore wind markets are
emerging in several countries, including France, Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, and the
UK, with policy-making underway to drive growth [7].
Despite these advantages and promising pipelines, the technology still needs to mature,
with only 73 MW of FOW operational as of 2020. It is estimated that up to 70 GW of FOW
could be operational by 2040 [11]. However, several barriers currently prevent FOW from
becoming commercially viable:
• Cost: One of the main challenges is the cost of floating offshore wind projects. They
require specialised equipment, installation, and maintenance, which increases the
cost compared to traditional bottom-fixed wind turbines.
• Technological challenges: The technology for floating offshore wind turbines is
still in its early stages, and there are challenges regarding reliability, efficiency, and
scalability.
• Limited operational experience: Limited operational experience with floating off-
shore wind turbines makes investors and developers hesitant to invest in the tech-
nology.
• Infrastructure: Developing the infrastructure necessary for floating offshore wind
projects can be challenging, including ports, cabling, and operations and mainte-
nance support.
• Regulatory challenges: The regulatory environment for floating offshore wind en-
ergy is still evolving, and there may be permitting, licensing, and environmental
assessment obstacles.
• Competition with traditional energy sources: Floating offshore wind energy still
faces competition from conventional energy sources such as fossil fuels, which are
well-established and widely used.

2.1.1
Overview of floating platforms
Offshore wind turbines have traditionally relied on monopile or jacket foundations, which
limit their deployment to waters less than 50 meters deep [12]. However, the most im-
portant potential markets for offshore wind energy are in deeper waters, which necessi-
tates developing and deploying floating wind technology. Floating offshore wind turbines
(FOWTs) can harness the wind’s power in deep waters where the wind resource is usu-
ally higher and more stable, providing an opportunity for significant growth in the offshore
wind industry.
FOWTs are commonly classified based on their primary stabilisation mechanism. Three
main approaches are used to ensure the static stability of a floating system: stabilisation
through the ballast, stabilisation through the waterplane (or buoyancy), and stabilisation

2.1.3
Overview of Operation and Maintenance for floating wind farms
Operation and maintenance (O&M) for floating wind farms present distinct challenges that
set it apart from the maintenance practices employed in bottom-fixed wind farms. The
unique nature of floating platforms, designed to harness wind energy in deeper offshore
waters, requires specialised considerations in O&M operations.
One crucial factor that demands careful attention is the motion of the floating platform.
Unlike bottom-fixed wind farms, where turbines are anchored to the seabed, floating wind
farms experience constant movement due to wave and current loads. The dynamic na-
ture of the platform’s motion poses significant challenges for maintenance activities, ne-
cessitating innovative approaches and engineering solutions to mitigate its effects. These
issues are worse at high heights, for example, the wind turbine’s nacelle, where any low-
frequency movement of the floating substructure is amplified. Factors such as platform
motion, wave and current loads, and mooring line tensions directly impact the O&M pro-
cess of floating wind farms. Understanding and managing these forces are essential for
ensuring wind turbines’ longevity and optimal performance [21].
Aside from motion-related challenges, accessibility poses another critical consideration
in maintaining floating wind farms. These offshore installations are typically located in
deeper waters, often far from the shore. The increased distance from the shore cre-
ates logistical complexities in accessing the wind turbines for maintenance. For example,
specialised vessels and equipment are required to transport personnel, tools, and spare
parts to the turbines. Moreover, scheduling maintenance activities must consider suitable
12

weather windows that provide safe and efficient conditions for O&M operations [21].
Furthermore, the logistics and infrastructure supporting floating wind farms are inherently
more complex than their bottom-fixed counterparts. The unique nature of floating plat-
forms demands developing specialised equipment, tools, and techniques tailored to the
challenges of floating wind farms. Maintenance bases, port facilities, and storage areas
must be strategically located to facilitate the transportation of personnel, equipment, and
supplies to and from the floating wind farm.
Given these challenges, the industry has developed various methods to address the
unique O&M requirements of floating wind farms. One such approach is the ”tow-to-
shore” (T2S) method, which involves towing the wind turbine to the shore or a sheltered
area with shallower waters. Conducting main component refurbishment, replacement
and repair in situ is challenging as motion-compensated lifting operations are required.
Alternatively, floating offshore wind turbines can be towed to the port for main component
refurbishment, replacement, and repair. Towing the components to shore or an O&M port
allows extensive maintenance activities to be carried out on the platform in a more acces-
sible location. However, while off-site maintenance methods can provide better access to
specialised equipment and skilled technicians, they are time-consuming and expensive
due to the need to tow the entire wind farm. Moreover, this also involves disconnecting
the floating offshore wind turbine from its moorings [22].
Alternatively, on-site maintenance solutions are also being explored for floating wind farms.
However, the infrastructure and expertise required for conducting maintenance in deeper
offshore sites are less advanced than in near-shore locations. Overcoming these chal-
lenges necessitates the development of specialised equipment, advanced technologies,
and training personnel to effectively carry out maintenance tasks in the unique and de-
manding environment of floating wind farms [22].
2.1.4
Evolution of the floating offshore wind industry and future trends
The floating wind sector is witnessing a surge in investments, significantly decreasing
the Levelised Cost of Energy (LCoE). As a result, it is projected that the cost of floating
wind will decrease by nearly 70%, reaching a global average of 40 €/MWh by 2050 as
seen in Figure 2.5. These cost reductions will primarily be achieved through introducing
larger turbines, establishing larger wind farms, significant technological advancements,
and developing a highly competitive supply chain [23].
In the context of wind turbines, most floating offshore wind turbines currently in use are
adapted from onshore wind turbines. However, the industry must develop wind turbines
specifically designed for floating wind applications. While turbines with gearboxes cur-
rently dominate the market but have higher maintenance costs. In contrast, gearless or
direct-drive turbines are more suitable for floating locations as they offer lower mainte-
nance costs. This is due to the direct connection between the rotor parts and the gener-
ator, eliminating the need for complex gear systems [24].
Regarding floating platforms, designers are actively exploring hybridisation to achieve op-
timal characteristics. The goal is to develop hybrid floating platforms that accommodate
various stages, including construction, assembly, load-out, and operation. There is ongo-
ing research into synthetic materials that can reduce costs regarding mooring systems.
Dedicated concepts for suspended dynamic cables and mooring are being developed and
deployed commercially to enhance cost competitiveness with shallow floating projects [7].
Furthermore, innovative anchoring methods such as automated anchor installation and
shared anchors among multiple turbines are being explored. These advancements can

3.1
Modelling of the production model
3.1.1
Production comparison between floating and bottom fixed wind
turbines
The question of whether to update the production equation for floating wind turbines com-
pared to bottom fixed wind turbines has been addressed in the literature review stage of
this thesis.
Both floating and bottom fixed wind turbines employ similar technologies. The main dif-
ference lies in the foundation design. Once operational, both types of turbines have the
same principle to capture wind energy and convert it to electricity.
However, the motion of a floating wind turbine can introduce some level of aerodynamic
disturbances, potentially affecting energy conversion efficiency. The movement of the
floater may lead to variations in the angle of attack of the turbine blades, resulting in
changes in aerodynamic forces and torque. Wang [31] showed that the incoming wind
flow is affected by the surge motion of the floating platform by adding to the incoming
wind speed a component that has a cosinus shape. This motion will dynamically affect
the wake, but the mean surge motion wind velocity is equal to zero due to the cosinus
shape of this added component, leading to the same wake losses on a yearly average
level.
Moreover, the tower inclination is higher for a floating wind turbine than a bottom fixed wind
turbine, and the wave conditions affect the hub acceleration, according to Martini [32].
The met-ocean conditions impact wind turbine production because too harsh a climate
would lead the wind turbine to exceed its operating threshold, resulting in a wind turbine
shutdown. Additional losses on yearly production can be observed due to this excess.
However, these are difficult to access because depending of the safety tolerance required
in the design stage.
Nevertheless, careful design and control systems can minimise these motion disturbances,
allowing the turbine to maintain a relatively stable and efficient operation. Floating wind
turbines employ advanced control systems that actively compensate for the motion of the
floater. These systems adjust the turbine’s pitch, yaw, and other parameters to optimise
energy production and mitigate the effects of wave-induced movements [33] [34]. The
detail of these systems and their efficiency in reducing the losses induced by the floater’s
motion haven’t been discussed in this thesis.
While operational shutdowns due to harsh wave conditions may be a unique consideration
for floating wind turbines, the overall energy production potential between floating and
bottom-fixed turbines will be assessed similarly in this master thesis. For these reasons,
the same production equations will be kept for the floating Minimalistic model.

3.1.3.1
Fuga wake model
In order to access an empirical formulation of a(NT , U, S), the wake model Fuga in PyWake
is used [36].
The Fuga model is a wake model based on a linearised Reynolds-averaged Navier-Stokes
(RANS) approach. The wake is characterised by the distribution of velocity deficits and
turbulence intensity downstream of a wind turbine. The model solves the linearised RANS
equations to determine the velocity and turbulence profiles in the wake region. It considers
the mean flow velocity, turbulence intensity, and turbulence kinetic energy. It, therefore,
accounts for the effects of turbulence and flow structures.
Calibrating the parameter a on one PyWake model such as Fuga is interesting for several
reasons. Firstly, it allows to get the wind turbine production at any position inside the
wind farm, and therefore to catch the transition between the free wind speed and the wind
speed in the region of equilibrium with the ABL. Secondly, as it is using RANS equation,
it results in a simplified CFD model that is faster to compute, which is convenient for the
tests performed in subsubsection 3.1.3.2. This will allow to calculate the production of a
wind turbine at different locations in a fictive wind farm and get the dependencies with the
three input parameters (NT , U and S).

3.2.1
Strategy for determining the CAPEX and OPEX costs
This section outlines the approach to developing a comprehensive global model for esti-
mating the Capital Expenditure (CAPEX) and Operational Expenditure (OPEX) of floating
offshore wind farms. It is important to emphasise that the fundamental concept of the cost
model remains consistent with the minimalistic model approach, which involves conduct-
ing a broad and generic study using simple cost models.
The initial step involved analysing the Minimalistic model proposed by the supervisors
and the master thesis written by Killian Cabioch. These references provided detailed
equations, found in subsubsection 4.2.2.3. The supervisors’ article titled ”A Minimalistic
Prediction Model to Determine Energy Production and Costs of Offshore Wind Farms”
[4] served as a foundational reference for understanding the cost components in offshore
wind farms. However, since this model primarily focused on bottom-fixed wind farms,
adjustments were necessary to accommodate the characteristics of floating wind turbines.
The next step involved conducting an extensive literature review to gather insights into
the costs associated with floating wind farms. Alongside the literature review, in-depth
analyses and calculations were performed to enhance understanding of various aspects
relevant to the study.
Building upon existing knowledge and incorporating specific considerations for floating
wind farms, the global model was developed to estimate both CAPEX and OPEX. This
model captures the main cost factors while maintaining simplicity and generality.
To visualise the different components constituting a floating wind farm, refer to Figure 3.7.
These components have been taken into account when estimating the CAPEX costs.
Furthermore, the analysis includes considerations for installation and other associated
expenses. Finally, the OPEX costs were also evaluated as part of the model.
Wind turbine costs - The estimation of the costs was based on references from Killian’s
Master Thesis [6], supplemented with other articles and updated information from the
latest annual reports of turbine manufacturers.
Transmission system costs - Reliable sources such as academic articles and industry
reports were consulted. This approach allowed for a precise estimation of the CAPEX
associated with each system component.

3.2.2.2
Transmission system
Several key factors influence the transmission system cost of a floating offshore wind
farm. The main drivers impacting the cost of the transmission system include:
• Distance between the wind turbines (for the array cables): The distance between
the wind turbines within the wind farm affects the length of the array cables required.
Array cables connect individual wind turbines to a central point for onward transmis-
sion of the generated electricity. As the distance between turbines increases, the
length of array cables increases, leading to higher material and installation costs.
• Distance to grid connection (for the export cable): The distance from the wind farm
to the onshore grid connection point determines the length of the export cable. The
export cable carries the electricity generated by the wind farm to the shore. Longer
distances to shore require more extensive export cable installations, resulting in
higher costs associated with cable materials, installation, and burial. For the scope
of this master thesis, the distance to the grid connection point has been simplified
as the distance to shore expressed as Y .
• Rated power of the entire wind farm (for the offshore substation): The rated power of
the wind farm, which refers to the total power capacity of all wind turbines combined,
impacts the design and capacity of the offshore substation. The offshore substation
collects and transforms the electricity from individual wind turbines before transmit-
ting it to the onshore grid. Higher-rated power wind farms require more extensive,
more powerful offshore substations, increasing infrastructure and equipment costs.
These factors interact to determine the overall cost of the transmission system in a floating
offshore wind farm. As will be seen, these factors are represented in the three significant
sources that were used to conduct the transmission system costs for this model:

3.2.2.4
Mooring lines
In order to determine the CAPEX of the mooring lines in itself, one can calculate the total
length of the lines and multiply it by the cost of the material. This process involves es-
tablishing static and dynamic analysis design criteria and ensuring the lines can handle
the defined load scenario. Considering safety margins and load scenarios, one can as-
sess the required material’s cost by determining the length of a mooring line that fulfils all
design criteria. Finally, multiplying the length of each mooring line by its corresponding
material cost yields the CAPEX for the mooring system.
As explained in section 2.1, taut/semi-taut and catenary lines could be suitable for both
spar and semi-submersible floating platforms. However, the design criteria for taut/semi-
taut mooring lines are more complex and depend on the specific environmental conditions
where the floating wind turbine will operate. The calculation of CAPEX for these lines
cannot simply rely on the cost of materials multiplied by the length of the line. It must
incorporate additional factors, such as installation complexity and resistance to break-
ing, which cannot be easily determined using the simplified input parameters of the Min-
imalistic model. Furthermore, if the cost calculation is based solely on the material cost
multiplied by the length of the line, taut/semi-taut mooring lines will always appear more
cost-effective due to their shorter length, as they form a straight line at a 45-degree angle
(assuming same mass per section of chain). As previously mentioned, the wind farms
being developed are currently predominantly in favourable conditions, which is why cate-
nary mooring lines are commonly chosen as the preferred technology. However, in the
near future, taut/semi-taut mooring lines are expected to become more prevalent as they
are better suited for harsh environmental conditions [16].
Therefore, for the purposes of this master thesis, the cost assumption for the mooring line
systems will only consider catenary lines in all cases.

3.2.3
Modelling of installation costs
Installation costs for floating wind farms differ from those of traditional fixed-bottom off-
shore wind farms. The following outlines key installation procedures for floating wind
farms and their associated costs:
Wind Turbine: The assembly of floating wind turbines is typically conducted onshore or in
a sheltered area near the port. This process includes assembling the tower, nacelle, rotor
blades, and electrical/control systems. Onshore assembly is preferred due to reduced
offshore lifting risks and installation time, as offshore locations are susceptible to delays
caused by adverse weather or sea conditions [77]. The cost of wind turbine installation is
combined with the installation cost of the floating platform.

3.2.4
Modelling of additional expenses
Additionally, it is vital to account for the costs of planning, development, and financing in
estimating total wind farm costs. These costs are typically assumed to be around 10%
of the total project costs, based on the information provided by the supervisors’ article.
While these costs could be higher, insufficient evidence supports such an increase in this
model’s development. Therefore, it was deemed appropriate to maintain the same value
for these costs.
On the other hand, there may be arguments for including decommissioning costs in the
initial estimation of a wind farm’s capital expenditure (CAPEX). However, it is generally
considered unwise to do so, particularly for projects with lifetimes of 20 or 30 years, due
to several reasons:
• Timing: Decommissioning costs are incurred at the end of the wind farm’s oper-
ational life, so including them upfront would inaccurately inflate initial investment
figures.
• Uncertainty: Estimating decommissioning costs accurately upfront is challenging
due to factors like technological advancements and regulatory changes.
• Financial considerations: Including decommissioning costs upfront would impact
the financial viability and attractiveness of the project for investors and lenders.
Instead, decommissioning costs are addressed through separate planning, financial mech-
anisms, and regulatory frameworks to ensure responsible and sustainable wind farm op-
erations throughout their lifecycle.

3.3
Modelling of Levelised Cost of Energy (LCoE) for floating
wind farms
The Levelised Cost of Energy (LCoE) is a widely used metric in the energy industry to
compare technology costs and track their economic progress over time. Its primary pur-
pose is determining the minimum selling price of energy output required to ensure project
profitability and attractiveness throughout its lifespan.
LCoE combines these factors into a single metric, unlike analysing cost and energy pro-
duction separately. While technology players and industry enablers utilise it, project in-
vestors typically prioritise metrics like internal rate of return (IRR) or net present value
(NPV), considering company-specific features such as taxes. Nonetheless, LCoE re-
mains an invaluable tool for making broad comparisons between different technologies
and projects.

However, it is crucial to acknowledge that various institutions employ diverse method-

to include. Consequently, it is essential to exercise caution when comparing LCoE esti-
mates from different sources. Thoroughly researching the methodologies used by each
institution and the specific cost elements considered is advisable. Furthermore, maintain-
ing transparency in LCoE calculations by clearly outlining the approach and assumptions
allows third parties to reconcile and compare the estimates.
This master’s thesis significantly contributes to determining the Levelised Cost of Energy
(LCoE) for wind farms, which is a crucial outcome. The LCoE analysis considers both
production and cost factors, providing valuable insights into the investment potential of

LCoE represents the average cost incurred throughout the lifetime of a project, account-
ing for the time value of money. It aims to determine a constant value (LCoE) that, when
multiplied by the energy production, accurately reflects the total project costs while con-

the interest rate on debt, respectively.

that this consultation specifically focused on bottom-fixed projects in the North Sea region.
While it can be argued that the WACC for floating projects should be higher due to in-
creased technical risks, it is also necessary to consider the potential for advancements in
floating offshore wind technology and the anticipated growth in deployment. As technol-
ogy improves and experience accumulates, floating projects’ perceived risks and associ-
ated costs are expected to decline. Consequently, the WACC for floating offshore wind
projects may converge with bottom-fixed projects over time.
In conclusion, the decision to utilize a 5% WACC in this thesis is based on the previ-
ous master’s thesis and information gathered from the Danish Energy Agency’s report.
It acknowledges the ongoing developments in the field and recognizes the potential con-
vergence of discount rates between floating and bottom-fixed offshore wind projects.
The inflation data used in this analysis has been obtained from The World Bank [43]. The
data from European countries between 2000 and 2022 has been considered to calculate
the average inflation rate. The resulting average inflation rate is 2.2%.
This inflation rate is crucial for estimating how the operational expenditure (OPEX) costs
will increase over the years of operation. Applying the 2.2% inflation rate makes it possible
to project the expected growth in OPEX costs and account for the impact of inflation on
the project’s financial analysis.

4.1
Challenges of validation and limitations
Data validation is a crucial and challenging aspect of this master’s thesis. In any scientific
endeavour, establishing the credibility of results and identifying potential errors or areas
for improvement relies on ensuring the accuracy and reliability of the model.
However, the lack of commercially operational large-scale floating wind farms poses a
significant obstacle in comparing and calibrating the data and results obtained from the
model, particularly regarding production and costs. The validation process becomes more
complex and uncertain without access to real-world reference data. Additionally, even
for the available prototypes, the scarcity of publicly available data makes it difficult to
accurately compare and validate the model’s predictions in terms of production.
Furthermore, floating wind farm technology’s complex and evolving nature further compli-
cates the validation process. The current industry landscape primarily consists of installed
prototypes, which may only partially represent future commercial projects’ optimised size
and design. Consequently, there can be notable disparities in production and cost es-
timates between these prototypes and the projected performance of future full-scale in-
stallations. Commercial projects are necessary to accurately predict scaling effects and
assess the model’s performance under realistic conditions.
Regarding the model’s limitations, it is crucial to recognise that the model utilised in this
thesis is site-specific and relies on European cost estimates. This simplified model pro-
vides detailed estimations for certain aspects of the wind farm’s design. However, it does
not account for specific layouts, area shapes, individual turbine production, individual
wake losses, or detailed costs such as array cables and mooring line costs at each turbine
position. The model overlooks the intricacies of site-specific factors by assuming a total
area and a fixed number of wind turbines. As a result, the model’s ability to encompass
the full range of environmental complexities and accurately project production and costs
is limited. Additionally, it is essential to note that the model is based on available data,
typically representing specific projects. Therefore, when extrapolating the equations to a
general model, there is a risk of making mistakes due to the omission of project-specific
characteristics.
Despite these challenges, this master’s thesis aims to provide valuable insights and con-
tribute to the existing knowledge base within the constraints of available data and model
assumptions. The following sections are going to present the outcomes of the model thus
developed.

• The wind climate used to calculate the Annual Energy Production (AEP) for the wake

using a different wind climate may yield different outcomes. For instance, if the
Global Wind Atlas overestimates the mean wind speed at the selected sites (based

applied to simplifying the wind turbine definition.
• Additional losses in wind energy production are uncertain and site-specific. It would
have been beneficial to compare the results based on gross annual energy produc-

farm data were unavailable for the thesis.
• Evaluating the actual wind farm production is challenging. It depends on the as-
sumptions made, as evident from the discrepancies between values derived from
the Danish Energy Agency and those from Energy Numbers.

Conclusion and future directions
5.1
Summary of the research findings
This thesis presented a simple tool that estimates the feasibility of floating offshore wind
farms, building upon the model developed by J.N. Sørensen and G.C. Larsen. The model
was validated based on real bottom-fixed wind farms and then extended to account for
cost considerations related to floating components, as described in recent literature.
The work focuses on two main aspects. Firstly, the thesis improves the production model
by calibrating the wake parameter, resulting in a new prediction model for offshore wind
farm production. An experimental setup using PyWake compares this model with others
across various wind farm layouts. Secondly, the thesis estimates the costs of different
components, including CAPEX and OPEX, for floating wind farms based on a literature
review and simple mechanical or mathematical laws. The analysis also computes the
Levelised Cost of Electricity (LCoE) as a key parameter for project feasibility.
Regarding the production model, the calibration of the wake parameter (a) proves to give
a complex relationship with the input factors (wind turbine interspacing, mean wind speed,
and the number of turbines). While the testing setup provides relevant insights and shows
promising results for the Minimalistic model, interpreting these results needs to consider
the choice of input parameters and the high uncertainty and site dependence of additional
losses. Although long-term reference data for floating production is lacking, operational
shutdowns due to harsh wave/wind conditions may lead to an additional loss factor for
floating production that has not been considered in this work.
In terms of cost estimation, the global CAPEX is derived by summing individual CAPEX
values for floating components. Wind turbine and transmission system costs are esti-
mated by averaging multiple references and identifying key input variables. The platform
cost is determined using scaling laws that link the turbine’s total mass and rated capacity
multiplied by the material’s mass cost. However, the platform cost estimation is subject to
uncertainty due to the wide range of possible scaling laws and the use of fictive studies or
prototypes as data sources. The mooring lines and anchors costs are derived from cate-
nary equations and literature-based line tension and cost information, respectively. The
installation and O&M costs derived from the literature showed high discrepancies due to
the different techniques still in discussion for floating wind turbines. The breakdown anal-
ysis highlighted the significant contributions of wind turbine and floating platform costs,
each accounting for over 25% of the total CAPEX.
Finally, the thesis computed the LCoE, considering the discount rate and the changing
time value of money.
The analysis revealed that floating technologies become cost-
effective after a water depth of 60-65m for a wind farm consisting of 100 wind turbines
with a capacity of 15MW each. The results also indicated a high dependence on mean
wind speed and a low dependence on water depth.
In summary, this thesis enhanced the prediction model for floating offshore wind farms,
improved cost estimation methodologies, and provided insights into the feasibility of such
projects. However, the limitations of the simple model, the prototype phase of floating
designs, and the lack of industry standardisation limit the model’s ability to capture the
complexity of cost factors associated with floating components.

5.2
Review of limitations and suggestions for future research
and improvements
This section will discuss the limitations encountered in this master thesis and suggest
future research and improvements.
Instead of delving into the specifics of each cost component, the thesis aims to provide a
comprehensive framework. However, a major limitation arises from the lack of a mature
industry database on Capital Expenditure (CAPEX) and Operational Expenditure (OPEX)
for floating wind farms. Most floating wind turbines operational in 2023 are pilot projects,
with confidential and limited accessibility to their data. Moreover, the absence of stan-
dardisation indicates that the industry will undergo changes, and prices will differ once
the supply chain is fully established. Therefore, it is crucial to validate this model using
data from existing projects once they become available.
It is essential to prioritise calibrating capital expenditure (CAPEX) estimates based on
large-size wind turbines to ensure accurate results. This approach will allow for a more
representative assessment, considering that larger turbines are anticipated to dominate
the future market.
The thesis could benefit from assigning higher coefficients to the
highest-rated power category when calibrating or regressing cost equations with rated
power. Therefore, relying solely on data from existing wind farms, primarily consisting of
smaller turbines in the studied offshore sector, may present limitations in predicting future
years accurately.
One area that warrants improvement is the cost of floating platforms. Currently, sev-
eral prototypes exist, but as the industry progresses, standardisation is likely to occur,
with one concept being selected through the industrialisation of floaters. The categorisa-
tion of floaters into semi-submersible, spar and TLP platforms is debatable, as the semi-
submersible platform category encompasses vastly different designs, leading to signifi-
cant variations in their total mass.
Further investigation into mooring lines is necessary, particularly as projects expand into
deeper waters. The current model primarily concentrates on chain catenary lines; how-
ever, alternative materials and approaches will be needed as catenary lines become
longer. To improve mooring line costs, exploring using non-constant weight per unit length
of chain could enhance catenary cost calculations. Additionally, the thesis did not cover
the criteria for selecting and designing taut and vertical tension mooring lines or the num-
ber of mooring lines highlighting potential areas for future research.
The transmission system should also study HVDC (High Voltage Direct Current). As the
distance to shore increases, particularly in floating projects, alternative solutions will be
needed for the transmission system to mitigate high voltage losses and effectively control
reactive power.
A more detailed analysis could be conducted to further enhance the understanding of the
Operational and Maintenance (O&M) costs, focusing on specific strategies like ”tow-to-
shore.” This analysis would involve assessing costs associated with leases for tug boats,
travel time, and maintenance duration. However, this aspect alone could be the subject
of an entire thesis in itself. The O&M techniques will likely evolve and improve over time.
Thus, more extensive work and validation should be undertaken once the industry reaches
a more mature stage.
Finally, enhancing the calibration of the constant ”a” would benefit from a larger sam-
ple size of wind farm cases, focusing on only comparing the Annual Energy Production

E
Net P50 AEP computed with
Copenhagen Energy’s internal tool for
the wind farm sites
This section outlines how annual energy production (AEP) was computed using Copen-
hagen Energy’s tool for six offshore sites in Denmark and Sweden. Table E.1 contains the
input parameters used. A central point was chosen at each site to derive omnidirectional
Weibull distribution parameters (λ, k) from Vortex MAP [84]. This distribution was then
combined with the wind turbine’s power curve provided by suppliers or simplified power
curves. Wake losses were calculated using the TurbOPark wake model [92] and 12 wind
direction sectors for the incoming wind. The same wind turbine positions derived from [39]
are used. The results include gross AEP (no losses), AEP with wake losses only (”w.l.”),
and AEP with all losses (Net P50). Additional losses in the system account for 3.6% for
array cable efficiency and availability, degradation losses, the balance of plant, and grid
availability. In addition, 0.1% to 0.4% is added for export cable efficiency, and 5% to 7%
is considered for turbine availability.
