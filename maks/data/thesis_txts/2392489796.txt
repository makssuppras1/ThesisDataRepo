Abstract
This thesis presents the design of a versatile Li-ion battery able to compete in an EV for
the Eco Shell-marathon. Research, design and prototype testing are the development stages
covered in the document. Aiming a design that can be used in more than one application,
a battery pack that can be split in several modules and combined is designed using electro-
chemical batteries. Along with the design, the s-BMS system of Lithium Balance is designed
to be implemented. Individual cells and one of the modules is used as test probes where
self-discharge and charge-discharge cycles are performed. In the end a nine 4s2p module
lithium ion battery was designed with independent and adaptable modules. The importance
of crucial indicators e.g. voltage in battery testing and fault diagnosing was recognized .

1
Introduction
Electrochemical batteries are a necessary for the current situation in the world. There is
loads of research in this topic and it is becoming even bigger. There are also initiatives
impulsing the development of better eﬃciency, better performance and new ways to store
energy electrochemically. Shell’s Eco Marathon is one of the biggest events worldwide that
promotes improving EV technology and the batteries inside. One aim of this thesis is to
design and test a battery that is able to be used in a car racing in the Eco Shell Marathon.
The other one is to design a battery pack that can be used in several applications of diﬀerent
needs.
1.1
Motivation of the project
The electric vehicles racing in the Eco Shell Marathon, they are all restricted by the same
rules. Batteries are no exception and the speciﬁcations are very clear. The electrical aspect
of the EV is crucial when racing with vehicles that are 100% electric. Certainly, the battery
is one of the key elements to the good performance and therefore, to the good competition
results. Unfortunately, when the race is over, the battery will most likely not be used for
other applications unless it is improved and rebuilt. For that reason, creating a versatile
battery that is able to be useful after the race has past is an important part of this project.
Versatility is a wide concept so the idea in this project is to make a battery that can be split
for applications with smaller energy needs.

2.2
Working principle
The battery cell is divided into three crucial components, the cathode, the anode and the
electrolyte. The interaction of these three elements creates a potential diﬀerence that can
be used as a voltage source. These interactions are based in REDOX (Reduction-Oxidation)
reactions. They are chemical reactions related to the concept that is called electron aﬃnity.
The electron aﬃnity is the capability of an element or material to donate electrons. In the
Figure 1, the discharge process of a battery can be seen.
The cathode is made by the material that tends to gain electrons. These are, in the chemical
reactions, called acceptors of electrons and the process of accepting a electron is called
reduction. The anode is composed by a material that tends to donate electrons. These
materials are called donnors of electrons and the process of donating the electrons is called
oxidation.
The electrolyte is a insulating component that makes this REDOX reaction
happen because of the capability of electricity ionic conduction within the material. Without
the electrolyte, it would not be possible the interaction between the anode and the cathode.
Following the scheme shown above, when the anode and the cathode are placed in the same
electrolyte, the electrolyte allows ions to move within it. Since the cathode tends to reduce
and the anode to oxidize, the REDOX reaction will happen and cation (reduces ions) and
anions (oxidized ions) will ﬂow in diﬀerent directions shown in the Figure 1.
To make sure that the batteries are within the operating ranges and for the safety aspects,
some parameters need to be monitored and analyzed. These parameter are: the State Of
Health (SOH), the State Of Charge (SOC), the temperature, the internal resistance and the
capacity. A explanation of these is made below.

2.2.1
Capacity
The total capacity of a battery cell is deﬁned as the multiplication of the time that the
battery lasts at a certain discharge current (C-rate) when discharged from its maximum
operational voltage to its minimum, also called cut-oﬀvoltage. is one of the most important
parameters in general terms. This parameter can open the doors to a lot of information
about the battery cell, specially when comparisons are made over time. It is a majorly
important parameter when it comes to estimating the ”health” of the cell and when the
battery cells are new, their full capacity will be used as a reference to compare the following
capacities later.
The full capacity of a new battery is set at a certain discharge current. The normalized sys-
tem for this discharge current is called C-rate. This system is based on the relation between
the discharge current and the amount of time to discharge. For example, the rate 1C is the
constant discharge current that makes the battery last 1 hour. For depending on the total
capacity of the batteries, the current meaning 1C rate will be diﬀerent. In the same way,
0,5C will be the discharge current that will make the battery last for 2 hours. Batteries are
often categorized by the maximum discharge current that they can handle. This is also made
using the C-rate system. Suppose there is a battery of 3Ah and the maximum discharge
current is 9A. Then, the battery will be rated at 3C.

Some of the indicators that capacity can be used to estimate e.g. the state-of-charge, state-
of-health. Measurements of the capacity can be done in several ways. Some of them are
more precise than others. It is important to mention that the full capacity of a cell in a
charge or discharge cycle depends on the demand of the injection of energy that it gets. So
capacity is an indicator that is related to the amount of time that the battery can run (either
charge or discharge) and also the current that is injected or demanded from it. The higher
the demand or the injection is, the less time the battery can operate. Below some methods
are explained. Depending on the end-use of the battery and the type of battery the methods
can vary. In the case of the application hold in this document, the most common ones would
be the following ones.
• Discharge method
This method is one of the most common ones to measure the capacity. A constant
known current is demanded from the battery and then the time until the cut-oﬀvoltage
is measured.
The known current has to be within the speciﬁcations of the battery to avoid any
issues. Then the measured time is multiplied with the current and the capacity is the
result. The taken measurements are not always constant since the performance of the
battery is conditioned by the environment it is in. Temperature and relative humidity
are two of the parameters that aﬀect the most to the performance of the battery so in
the case of an unstable environment, specially in these two aspects, the results will be
very diﬀerent.
The type of the chemistry also aﬀects to the results. Some of the technologies are
more stable when it comes to the capacity tests than others. As an example, lead
acid batteries are usually inconstant with the results in these tests, whereas other

2.3
Battery Management System (BMS)
In the following paragraphs the topic of the battery management system (BMS) will be
presented. Firstly, some deﬁnition and theoretical background will be commented and then
the diﬀerent types will come into play as well as their working mechanisms.
2.3.1
Introduction
As a deﬁnition a BMS is an electronic system responsible for the optimized performance
and the protection of the battery pack by accessing and controlling each one of the cells. It
makes sure that the batteries are not operated out of their operational limits set by the man-
ufacturers usually. Apart from that, by taking action in every single battery, it improves the
performance of the pack to its best. To meet its purpose properly, there are certain things
it has to fulﬁll.
Safety is one of the main tasks that the BMS needs to take care of. Performing out of the
speciﬁcation’s range of the batteries might deteriorate, permanently damage or even cause
failure in the cell. For that reason, the BMS takes the needed actions to make the batteries
work within the limits. In addition, external or internal events might also damage of destroy
the batteries and the BMS does, within its limits, whatever needed to protect the battery
pack from any possible damage.

The other main task of the BMS is to ensure the best performance out of the battery pack.
For this purpose, balancing the battery’s voltages in the charging process will be needed for
all the batteries to be totally charged and equally charged in a safe environment. Normally,
even with the best quality batteries, batteries are not identical and there are small diﬀer-
ences in their internal resistances and other parameters that lead to a diﬀerent charging and
discharging rates between cells in the same pack. Without the BMS, supposing a discharge
process, then the ﬁrst cell reaching the cut-oﬀvoltage sets the lower limit and on the other
hand, when charging, the ﬁrst cell reaching the maximum permitted voltage sets the upper
limit. This means that due to the fact that the rest of the cells are not used in their complete
potential, there is some potential loss that the BMS should take care of by balancing the cells.
In order to meet the objectives above, some practicalities are needed. First of all, the BMS
must be able to collect data and analyze it. The collected data is then related to the indica-
tors and parameters of the battery model and further checked with the speciﬁcations of the
application and battery pack. This gives an idea to the BMS of the status of the battery
so that it can recognize non-optimal situations and predict and avoid endangering situations.
After analyzing the situation, the next step is to take action to fulﬁll the aims. For that
purpose, it is necessary for the BMS to be able to communicate with the devices that can
aﬀect the situation e.g. chargers, relays, inverters.
The following step is to take the action and communicate them to the devices presented
before. As an example, in case that the battery pack is over charged, then the BMS should
send the order of stopping to the charger in order prevent the permanent damage in the
cells.

Dissipative balancing takes the idea of dimming the charge of the

that there is no energy transfer between the cells or the pack. These methods, specially the
one with ﬁxed shunt resistors are applicable to technologies that can handle overcharge like
nickel and lead acid batteries.

3.1.5
BMS
The ﬁnal BMS system is provided by Lithium Balance. It is a company that specializes in
BMS and chargers in the battery industry. They also provided a manual for their product
[1]. From these manual, the following information about the their functioning was extracted.
In regards to the balancing method the BMS is using, it is a dissipative method. As men-
tioned before, it is based on the excess energy dissipation of the batteries that, when charg-
ing, ﬁrst reach their maximum charging voltage or they are close. From the s-BMS product
manual provided by Lithium balance [2] the following explanation is extracted.
Cell balancing is a necessary part of improving the performance of a battery. This is due to
the fact that the maximum discharge capacity is determined by the least charged battery in
the pack. The main objective of the BMS is to get the charge of the batteries as close to
each other as possible so that the capacity is maximized.
There is a voltage interval where all the batteries aim to be. When the voltages of the
batteries are between the so called ”Cell target voltage” and ”Minimum target voltage”
then the battery balancing stops. The speciﬁc steps to achieve that are the following.
• Step 1: Starting the charge
When the charger is connected to the batteries, the SOC is usually below 100% and

so, the battery is charged with the maximum charging current.
• Step 2: Balancing charger reduction
Along the charge, due to the capacity diﬀerences of the cells, the voltage levels will
not be the same. Some will reach the 100% SOC faster. Before that level, there is a
threshold called ”Charger regulation voltage” where the charging current will be re-
duced. This reduction will only aﬀect to the cells that reached the threshold voltage
and the amount of current is calculated by the s-BMS as a function of the individual
cell voltage.
• Step 3: Start of the bleeding process
The charging with reduced current continues until one of the batteries reaches the
”Minimum target voltage”. Whenever this happens, the s-BMS will simultaneously
continue charging the cells with a voltage below ”Min. target vol.”at a reduced current
and bleed the cells above that voltage.
• Step 4: Pause the charging
Depending on the diﬀerence of voltage between the cells, two things might happen. A
possibility is that one cell exceeds the ”Cell target Voltage” while others are under the
”Min. target vol.”. In this case the charger will pause the charging and it will continue
the bleeding of the cells above the ”Cell target vol.”. The other possibility is that all
the cells will pass the ”Min. target vol.” while others do not exceed the ”Cell target
vol.”. In this case, the charging will be complete.
• Step 5: Start of the bleeding process
If the charge was paused due to a too big diﬀerence, eventually, the overcharged cells
will get under ”Cell target vol.” and the step 3 will be applied followed by the step
4 until all the cells are between the ”Minimum target voltage” and the ”Cell target
voltage”.

boards measure the voltage and the temperature and they send the information to the
BMCU. Each LMU is able to monitor 8 cell voltages and 2 temperatures maximum. The
current is measured using a shunt resistor monitored in the BMCU directly. In the Fig. 21
the recommended circuit is represented.

In the charge case, then the switch in the left (S1) will be closed and the switch in the right

go through the diode and get in the battery. The path will be closed from the negative pole
of the battery to the negative pole in the power supply.
The discharge process will be the other way around. S1 will be opened and S2 will be closed
to make sure that the battery discharges in the load only. In this case, the current will also
run clockwise and the process will be similar the the charging one. The diﬀerence here is
that the battery will take the power supply role this time whereas the load will take the
previous battery role as a load.

Test methodology
This section will show what the procedures will be for the tests performed in individual cells,
the prototype and the full pack. These tests were designed to see and understand how the
batteries perform both individually and in packs and to see what the limits of the batteries
are. Unfortunately, as the batteries used (LG IRN18650 MJ1) are protected,there is no pos-
sibility to push the limits of the batteries too far. Anyway, in order to see the performance
of the batteries, three levels were planned.
Firstly, some tests will be performed in the individual cells. These tests will be more de-
tailed below although the general idea of them are the following. One of the tests will be
self-discharging the battery and the rest of the tests will be based in charge-discharge cycles.
Secondly, a small prototype will be built in order to test the performance of small packs. It
is also a way to take a small step to the big pack. Also, it will be a way to test a battery
easier to handle if something needs to be changed or repaired.
The design of the small
prototype was discussed in the previous chapter (3) but the tests performed in it work with
the same methodology. They will all be based in charge-discharge cycles with diﬀerent setups.
Lastly, the ﬁnal prototype with the oﬃcial size will follow the same procedure as the small
prototype proportionally in the parameters. For example, if the small prototype is tested in a
partial discharge with a 2A current and 20V voltage, then the big pack will be proportionally
tested at 6A current and 80V voltage.
4.2.1
Individual cell tests
Self-discharge test
Self-discharge of the batteries is an important parameter to consider
because if a battery has a high self-discharge rate, then the overall running time of the bat-
tery can decrease due to the constant drain of energy produced by the high self-discharge
rate. This leads to less possible energy delivery ability and generally a worse performance
in the discharge. In the charging side the charge duration will increase due to the drain.
To perform this test, one cell is fully charged and separated from the group and the charge
will be measured every day for around 40 days. The voltage will be measured every day
in order to see how much the voltage drops although, according to manufacturers data, is
deﬁnitely not enough time to discharge the battery or to see a drastic change on it.
Charge-discharge cycles
Charge-discharge cycles are a necessary test in batteries to
check their overall performance in diﬀerent conditions and compare it to the manufacturer’s
data. Moreover, it is a good way to follow the development of the indicator and in case of
needing an intervention, detect the dangerous situation as soon as possible.

Without BMS

in this technologies, might imply ﬁre or explosions.

6
Conclusion & Future work
6.1
Conclusion
To sum up, the design of a versatile Li-ion battery able to compete in an EV for the Eco
Shell-marathon was made in this thesis. The versatility of the battery lies the capability of
the battery to be used in nine separated modules as well as a whole. The 4s2p modules can
be combined in several ways starting from a voltage of 14,4V and a capacity of 7Ah until
a voltage of 43,2V and a capacity of 21Ah. Hence, the application variety is broader than
the battery ﬁxed as a whole. In addition, if any of the batteries were damaged, it would be
easier to replace.
In terms of battery technologies, it was concluded that for the EV battery applications, Li-
ion batteries are, in terms of eﬃciency, the best option in the market nowadays. However,
the dangers of ﬁre and explosion in over demanding conditions must not be neglected. To
avoid any hazard, the BMS from Lithium Balance was analyzed and prepared for the imple-
mentation.
A module was mounted to test the behaviour in diﬀerent conditions. It was concluded that
for a proper design and fault diagnosis of a battery, the maximum elements of the system
should be somehow monitored. In the technical side of the tests, there are many elements
to take into account when testing.
It is crucial to consider the components of the circuit when choosing or setting the voltage
and current in the devices that will charge and discharge the battery. Monitoring the volt-
ages, temperatures, currents and internal resistance is key to fault diagnosis. Every atypical
trend in the any of the proﬁles should be analyzed. As an important note, if a battery level
indicator e.g. voltage, current presents a peculiar trend, its eﬀect in the pack level indicator
trend may be barely noticeable. Thus, it will be more diﬃcult to diagnose issues in it. For
that reason it is better to measure indicators. It was noticed that the charging process has
a bigger eﬀect in the rise of the temperature than the charge has. Therefore, in case of a
need of a stronger system for cooling, using it while charging would be a priority.

6.2
Future work
Due to some external issues, not all the aims of the project were completed. As future work,
one of the points to cover are the tests remaining to the batteries that are shown in the fol-
lowing paragraphs. The other one is the implementation of the Super capacitor to the system.
The remaining tests in the prototype are the test charge-discharge cycles with the BMS im-
plemented. The aim of these tests is to verify that there is a diﬀerence in the performance of
the battery when it comes to the running time of it after the balancing process that the BMS
provides and the diﬀerence in the charging time. However, from the theory it is known that
it will be more than the timing without any management system since there is no balancing
possibility.
Apart from that, the building of the full pack is postponed to future work. The full battery
would be tested with the same procedure than the small prototype. Subsequently,the results
of the full pack and the small pack would be compared to search for correlations.
The tests of the full pack would consist of charge-discharge cycles at 55V and 60V and the
test currents would be 9A, 15A, 30A, 45A and 60A.
6.2.1
Full pack tests
To test the full pack used for the EV, the procedure of the tests will be similar to the pro-
totype one. As mentioned in the prototype section (4.2.2), charge-discharge cycles will be
performed in the battery to see its performance under load conditions.
For this full pack the output voltages for the both power supply and the load will be 55V and
60V and the currents will be 9A, 15A, 30A, 45A and 60A. Same as the previous prototype,
charge-discharge cycles will be applied to the battery both partially and fully.
6.2.2
Super capacitors
Adding super capacitors to the system would be the next step in regards to the evolution
of the technology. These have almost linear charge-discharge cycles that could be helpful in
peak operation of the battery. Specially in the EV race, current peaks are very common and
the implementation of the super capacitors could improve the eﬃciency of the energy system.
Although in the rules of the race is not allowed yet, as a technology, it can be applied to the
everyday cars as well.

50A, 600V Ultrafast Diode
The RURG5060 is an ultrafast diode with soft recovery 
characteristics (trr < 65ns). It has low forward voltage drop 
and is of silicon nitride passivated ion-implanted epitaxial 
planar construction.
This device is intended for use as a freewheeling/clamping 
diode and rectiﬁer in a variety of switching power supplies 
and other power switching applications. Its low stored charge 
and ultrafast recovery with soft recovery characteristic 
minimizes ringing and electrical noise in many power 
switching circuits, thus reducing power loss in the switching 
transistors.

5. Caution 
Warning: Using the lithium ion rechargeable battery, mishandling of the battery may cause heat, fire and 
deterioration in performance. Be sure to observe the following. 
5.1 Cautions for Using and Handling 
 
When using the application equipped with the battery, refer to the user’s manual before usage.  
 
Please read the specific charger manual before charging. 
 
Charge time should not be longer than specified in the manual. 
 
When the cell is not charged after long exposure to the charger, discontinue charging.    
 
Battery must be charged at operating temperature range 0 ~ 45℃.  
 
Battery must be discharged at operating temperature range -20 ~ 60℃. 
 
Please check the positive(+) and negative(-) direction before packing. 
 
When a lead plate or wire is connected to the cell for packing, check out insulation not to short-circuit. 
 
Battery must be stored separately. 
 
Battery must be stored in a dry area with low temperature for long-term storage.  
 
Do not place the battery in direct sunlight or heat. 
 
Do not use the battery in high static energy environment where the protection device can be damaged. 
 
When rust or smell is detected on first use, please return the product to the seller immediately. 
 
The battery must be away from children or pets 
 
When cell life span shortens after long usage, please exchange to new cells. 
 
 
5.2 Prohibitions 
 
Do not use different charger. Do not use cigarette jacks (in cars) for charging.  
 
Do not charge with constant current more than maximum charge current. 
 
Do not disassemble or reconstruct the battery. 
 
Do not throw or cause impact. 
 
Do not pierce a hole in the battery with sharp things. (such as nail, knife, pencil, drill) 
 
Do not use with other batteries or cells.  
 
Do not solder on battery directly. 
 
Do not press the battery with overload in manufacturing process, especially ultrasonic welding. 
 
Do not use old and new cells together for packing. 
 
Do not expose the battery to high heat. (such as fire) 
 
Do not put the battery into a microwave or high pressure container. 
 
Do not use the battery reversed. 
 
Do not connect positive(+) and negative(-) with conductive materials (such as metal, wire) 
 
Do not allow the battery to be immerged in or wetted with water or sea-water.

Agilent dc Electronic Loads
Agilent dc electronic loads are ideal for the test and evaluation of
dc power sources and power components and are well-suited for
applications in areas such as manufacturing, research and devel-
opment, and incoming inspection.
The Agilent One-Box Solution
Agilent single-input loads and load mainframes are equipped with
standard GPIB interfaces. This built-in IEEE-488 interface allows
complete control of all load functions as well as readback of input
voltage, current, power, and detailed operating status. Each  stand
alone load or load module also includes programming inputs that
allow control of load current via an analog voltage. Other system
features contributing to the one-box solution concept are internal
voltage and current monitors and an internal transient generator
with programmable amplitudes, frequency, duty cycle, and slew
rate. The one-box solution saves space, cost, and time while 
making these dc electronic loads easy to integrate into automated 
test systems.
Agilent dc electronic loads are optimized to address a broad
range of dynamic loading applications. They are specifically
designed for stability in applications where fast transients are
applied to the load inputs, such as during dc power supply startup
characterization or transient response testing.  Dynamic load per-
formance can be further tailored to specific application needs with
the programmable slew rate feature.
Fully-Compatible Operation
These dc electronic loads respond to instructions from the
industry-standard SCPI command set. Moreover, the features of
these dc electronic loads are fully compatible with one another.
For example, test programs developed for 6060B 300 W single-
input electronic load or 60502B 300 W single-input load module 
are interchangeable.
The dc electronic load family is also fully compatible with the
59510A relay accessory (see page 33). The 59510A provides physi-
cal isolation of the dc electronic load from the device under test or
any other test instrument by switching power and sense leads.
Capable of switching up to 60 A and 200 Vdc, the 59510A 
can be controlled by rear-panel signals on the electronic load.
Battery Testing
The 6050A Option J10, 6051A Option J10 and 6060B Option J10
electronic loads are modified for battery testing. These products
provide tri-level pulse loading, to simulate accurate conditions on
batteries. They also feature a programmable minimum battery
voltage threshold (measured at load terminal). If the voltage of the
battery under test falls below this threshold, the load will automat-
ically turn off.

System or Manual Applications
Agilent dc electronic loads are equally suitable for manual use on
the bench. The front-panel LCD meters indicate voltage, current,
and power readings. The full-function front-panel keypad allows
easy, repeatable, and reliable control of the load when it is used
manually. Six volatile user-definable states allow you to easily
save settings for later recall. An additional user-definable power-
up state allows you to define settings that are remembered when
the unit is switched off and then recalled when it is switched 
on again.
Specifying System Performance
Because Agilent electronic loads feature an integrated GPIB 
programmer, pulse generator, current shunt, DMM, and cabling,
their performance is specified as a system. Specifications cover all
the integrated functions as one unit, which eliminates the need to 
calculate the actual performance of the automated test system
based on each component’s specification. The one-box solution
makes the integration and documentation of your test system 
fast and easy.
Single-Input Products
The 6060B and 6063B are single-input loads with standard rear-
panel inputs. They are also available with optional front-panel
inputs in addition to the rear-panel inputs. Front-panel inputs
(Option 020) make input connections to the electronic load 
convenient for bench applications. These front-panel terminals 
are capable of handling the entire current rating of the load and
can accept wire gauges up to AWG#4 (22 mm2). They require no
tools to tighten, making the connections quick and easy.
Mainframe Products
The 6050A 1,800-W and 6051A 600-W electronic load mainframes
accept the user-installable load modules for easy system config-
uration and future reconfiguration, if desired. The 6050A holds up
to six 60501B, 60502B, and 60503B load modules, or three 60504B
and 60507B load modules, allowing up to 1,800 W of total maxi-
mum power. The 6051A holds up to two 60501B, 60502B, 60503B
modules, or one 60504B or 60507B module allowing up to 600 W 
of total maximum power. One GPIB address is all you need for
complete control and readback of all load modules within a single
mainframe.
Operating Agilent Loads Below the Minimum Input
Voltage Specification
Agilent electronic loads meet all specifications when operated
above 3.0 V; however, the dc operating characteristics also extend
below this minimum-input voltage for static tests. Because of the
FET technology used in the power input circuits, these electronic
loads have a low minimum- input resistance allowing them to sink
high currents even at low voltages.

volts, is possible at correspondingly-reduced current levels,
depending on the minimum resistance of the load. These electronic
loads, therefore, can be used in many applications that previously
required zero-volt loads.

LiBAL s-BMS™
Integration Board Battery Management System
INTRODUCTION
The s-BMS is an exceptionally flexible and cost effective 
Battery Management System for automotive, industrial and 
stationary battery packs ranging from 12VDC up to 1000VDC. 
It manages rechargeable lithium batteries of any chemistry 
and from any battery supplier allowing you maximum battery 
sourcing freedom.
The system consists of a master board (BMCU) communicating 
with up to 32 monitoring boards (LMU).  Each LMU manages 
3–8 cells in series and 2 temperature sensors. The BMCU 
handles pack level measurements, data logging, application 
and charger interfaces. 
The PC Diagnostic Software provides an intuitive suite of 
system configuration tools as well as displays for monitoring 
battery and BMS performance. It allows you to set battery 
parameters such as limit voltages and temperatures, allow-
able charge and discharge rates or improve SoC estimation 
with your own battery model.
To simplify integration, CAN frames can be constructed at 
“Bit level” to broadcast any of the parameters measured and 
calculated by the s-BMS. A post processing module allows you 
to scale and manipulate values and broadcast them on the 
CAN bus with no custom development needed. This allows 
the s-BMS to work as a drop in replacement for many existing 
systems.
