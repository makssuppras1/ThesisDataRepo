ABSTRACT  
Previous studies have shown a connection between a good indoor climate and the student's 
performance and comfort. The purpose of this report was to analyze the air quality and thermal 
environment in 8 out of 11 schools in the municipality of Greve. Subsequently, two solutions 
were analyzed. 
The analysis was initiated with a visual review of each school, where the ventilation and heating 
methods were noted. 2-3 measurements of CO2 concentration and operating temperature were 
recorded at each school. The measurements were planned to be representative for the entire 
school. 
The measurements showed that more than 50 % of the schools had significantly worse indoor 
climate compared recommendations made by Danish standards, the Building Regulations and the 
Labor Inspection. The worst cases were schools without mechanical balanced ventilation. 
The second part of the analysis involved the study of two types of ventilation, planned to improve 
the indoor climate in the classrooms. The first analysis was made of a mechanical balanced system 
at Karlslunde School. The analysis showed that the air quality was significantly improved after 
the installation of mechanical ventilation. The second analysis was of two intervention studies at 
Holmeagerskolen, which aimed to improve the indoor climate by natural ventilation. The analysis 
showed that it was not possible to bring down the CO2 concentrations to an acceptable level. 
Finally, the analyzes were presented to the city’s mayor Pernille Beckmann. The mayor was aware 
about the problem, but not the importance. In response to the analysis, Mayor Pernille Beckmann 
would present the problem at a national assembly and suggest the creation of a pool for improving 
the indoor climate at Danish schools.

RESUMÉ 
Tidligere studier har påvist sammenhængen mellem et godt indeklima og elevers præstation og 
komfort. Formålet med rapporten var at analysere indeklimaet på 8 ud af 11 skoler i Greve 
kommune, med hensyn til luftkvalitet og termisk indeklima. Efterfølgende blev 2 relevante 
løsningsforslag opstillet og analyseret.  
Analysen blev indledt med en visuel gennemgang af hver skole, hvor de benyttede ventilations- 
og opvarmningsmetoder, blev noteret. Herefter blev der foretaget 2-3 målinger af CO2 
koncentration og operativ temperatur på hver skole. Målingerne var planlagt således, at hvis der 
var flere typer ventilation på skolen, blev der foretaget en måling i forbindelse med hver type.  
Målingerne viste at mere end 50 % af skolerne havde væsentlig ringere indeklima end 
anbefalingerne fra Danske standarder, Bygningsreglementet og Arbejdstilsynet. De værste 
tilfælde var skoler uden mekaniske balanceret ventilation.  
Anden del af analysen indebar analysen af 2 metoder til forbedring af indeklimaet. Den første 
analyse var af et mekanisk balanceret system på Karlslunde skole. Analysen viste at luftkvaliteten 
var væsentligt forbedret efter etableringen af mekanisk ventilation.  
Den anden analyse var af 2 interventions studier på Holmeagerskolen, der havde til formål at 
forbedre indeklimaet via naturlig ventilation. Analysen viste, at det ikke var muligt at bringe CO2 
koncentrationerne ned på et acceptabelt niveau via naturlig ventilation.  
Afslutningsvis blev analyserne forelagt Greve kommunes borgermester Pernille Beckmann. 
Borgermesteren var bevist om at der var en problemstillingen men ikke omfanget. Som reaktion 
på analysen, vil borgermester Pernille Beckmann fremlægge problematikken på en landssamling 
og foreslå oprettelsen af en pulje til forbedring af indeklimaet på de danske skoler.

1 INTRODUCTION  
Denmark is a modern society driven by knowledge, intelligence and hard work. The foundation 
for our contribution to society starts in our early years. The right learning environment must be 
ensured for the students and teachers, to support an effective acquiring of knowledge.  
For the first time in years, new statistics show that the number of pupils is increasing, and will 
increase towards 2020 (BUPL, 2016). Another fact is, that Danish students spend on average 
10.960 hours in primary school. (OECD, 2016). Only exceeded by students in Australia.  
Regardless of an increasing number of children, facts show that the number of public schools in 
Denmark are decreasing from 1.671 in 2000 to 1.289 in 2016 (www.uvm.dk, 2017). This will 
result in an increased number of students per classroom, unless new schools are built.  
A good indoor environment promotes learning. Studies show that speed and accuracy are 
increased in routine based school work by up to 15 %. (Wargocki & Wyon 2007a; Wargocki & 
Wyon 2007b; Bakó-Biró et al. 2012).  
A 15 % increment in academic achievement could mean that students and teachers need less hours 
to achieve the same goals, or that the students could achieve even more knowledge than before. 
Associated professor at The Danish Technical University(DTU) Jørn Toftum, made a report in 
2015, concluding that students in classrooms with mechanical ventilation get better results in the 
national test, compared with students in classrooms without mechanical ventilation. (Toftum et 
al. 2015) 
The purpose of this report, was to analyze the indoor environment in a number of public schools 
in a representative Danish municipality. Subsequently this report investigates relevant solutions 
and methods that could improve the learning environment for the students.  
The analysis of the current situation includes measurements of factors influencing the air quality 
and thermal environment. The measurements made were compared with measurements made all 
over Denmark in 2014 and 2016 by DTU.  
It is a well-known fact that the municipalities are under a great pressure from citizens and the 
Danish government, to allocate their resources in the best way. The Mayor of Greve, Pernille 
Beckmann says in an interview the 5th September:  
“We are behind on points! The situation on and quality of our schools are poor, but we are trying 
with all the means we have to squeeze out money from the budgets, to improve our schools”  
Two different solutions were investigated.  
The first solution, was the usage of the more expensive installation of a well-balanced mechanical 
ventilation system. The second was a low-cost solution, which only included the means already 
present at the schools, to take the municipality’s limited budgets into consideration.   
The solutions were analyzed, according to the quality of the indoor environment and the student’s 
comfort and performance.

2.1 INDOOR CLIMATE PARAMETERS  
2.1.1 
GENERAL INFORMATION 
The quality of the indoor environment (IEQ) in a classroom depends on four main parameters.  
The factors can influence the students’ health, comfort and productivity and besides that the 
energy consumption, overall economy and the quality of the building. The life time of some 
materials in the buildings is highly depending on the temperature, humidity etc. When prioritizing 
rather to improve the indoor climate or not, it is important to reminder all these parameters that 
depend on the quality of the indoor environment.  
  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The four indoor environment parameters are all influenced by both internal and external factors. 
Some of these factors could be; wind pressure which can influence the pressure in the building 
and the control of the ventilation. Particles from traffic, industry etc., is seen as one of the main 
sources of pollution. The sun will influence the temperature and light in the classroom. Noise 
from the schoolyard will influence the comfort and ability to concentrate. All these factors, and 
many more, are important to consider when designing buildings and classrooms.   
Beside external parameters, the indoor climate is also very much influenced by the occupants. 
Humans emit several different chemicals which together with the production of body heat will 
influence the indoor climate.  
The focus of this report is on air quality and thermal environment, which are both measurable and 
comparable with data from previous studies.

2.1.2 
INDOOR AIR QUALITY  
The indoor air quality (IAQ) is affected by different sources of pollutants. These 
contaminants may be a natural part of the indoor climate or be derived from man-
made products and materials. Dust, mold, human odors and emissions etc. are all a 
natural part of the indoor environment in a classroom, and will always occur in some level. 
Particles, emissions from materials etc. are also part of the indoor climate, but are all 
anthropogenic. Regardless the origin of the pollutants, a good air quality ensures that the limit 
values are not exceeded and that the students are not affected by them.  
Carbon dioxide (CO2) is a colorless gas consistent of one carbon atom and two oxygen atoms. 
CO2 is a by-product of combustion and is often released into the free when producing energy. But 
is also a product of respiration which happens in all breathing creatures. CO2 is not dangerous in 
the concentrations people are subjected to in their everyday life (Clausen et al., 2014), but high 
concentrations will influence the comfort and productivity.  
CO2 is an important indicator for the air quality in rooms where humans are the main source of 
pollution. An adult person produces 17 l CO2 per hour and it is assumed that pupils in the age of 
12-15 produces the same due to their slightly higher activity level.  (Christian Hviid, 2014). CO2 
is also a part of the outdoor air and the concentration of often around 380 parts per million (ppm). 
(Toftum and FOA 2011) 
Lars Gunnarsen from Statens Byggeinstitut says about CO2 
”CO2 is not a problem, but you can measure CO2 as an indicator for the pollution of people” 
The CO2 concentration in a classroom depends on the air change, the production from occupants 
and the room size.  The ventilation rate is a measurement of supply air in liter per second per 
person (l/s/person and will together with the CO2 concentration be used as an indicator for the air 
quality in the classrooms.

2.1.3 
THERMAL ENVIRONMENT 
The thermal environment includes all factors influencing the occupants heat balance 
(Danvak ventilationsteknik, 2000). Assuming that there is no heat accumulation, the 
students will experience the thermal environment based on, their metabolic rate, the 
clothing insulation value, the humidity, the air velocity and the operative temperature.  
The operative temperature (C) is a combination of the air temperature and the mean radiant 
temperature inside the classroom. The metabolic rate (Met) is proportional with the activity level 
and expresses the energy produced during physical activity. The met level for school work is 1.2 
Met corresponding to 58 W/m² skin (Toftum, thermal environment). The clothing insulation value 
(Clo) defines the insulation value of the worn cloth. There is an obvious connection between the 
Clo value and the season. Which is the reason why the operative temperature must variate during 
the year.  
Previous studies have shown that thermal environment have an impact on the students’ 
performance and comfort (Wargocki and Wyon, 2007). Assuming that the metabolic rate is the 
same for all the students and that they all dress properly according to the season, the comfort 
depends only on the indoor environment. It is proven that the effect of the humidity is minimal in 
the preferred temperature (Danvak ventilationsteknik, 2000) and will therefore not be investigated 
in this report.  
This leaves out the operative temperature and air velocity as the thermal impacts on the students’ 
comfort and performance. It is very difficult to control the air velocity in natural ventilated 
classrooms and therefore it will not be neglected in any other ways than asking the students if the 
feel discomfort due to draft.  
This report will investigate the operative temperatures inside the classrooms on the schools of 
Greve and estimate how the temperatures will influence the students’ performance and comfort. 
2.1.4 
CONSEQUENCES  
Pupils and teachers are spending the main time of a school day inside the 
classrooms. The classrooms set the basis for an effective learning for the students 
and the teachers ability to teach.  
A good air quality is sufficient for the occupants’ health. The pupils are especially vulnerable 
when it comes to air born pollutants, because they are still developing and breaths in more air 
compared with their size (Annesi- Maesano et al., 2013; Mendell and Heath, 2005).  
The consequences of an inadequate indoor climate in the classrooms are, health issues, respiratory 
symptoms, poor concentration and insufficient performance (Stafford et al, 2015; Csobod et al., 
2014; Turunen et al., 2014; Annesi-Maesano et al., 2013; Sundhedsstyrelsen, 2012; Toftum et al., 
2011).

Some of these symptoms are categorized as Sick building syndromes (SBS), which means that 
the symptoms are associated to the time the students spend in the school. More severe 
consequences, such as death or trouble breathing, are related to buildings with exposure to radon, 
carbon monoxide (CO) or second-hand tobacco smoke. (Sotiris Vardoulakis el Al. 2015) 
The symptoms of a poor indoor environment can differ from student to student as well as some 
students might be more sensitive to the exposure then others. In general, the effects can influence 
the students learning ability, comfort and absenteeism. 
Poor concentration and insufficient productivity are attributable to bad IAQ and a bad thermal 
environment. The students might feel distracted and unfocused when present in a poor indoor 
climate. Different studies clarify how performance correlates with the volume of fresh air supplied 
to the classroom and the operative temperature (Wargocki and Wyon, 2007).

2.2 VENTILATION CONCEPTS AND PRINCIPLES  
The main purpose of ventilating is to maintain a fresh and satisfying indoor environment for the 
occupants, by removing and/or diluting the pollution. Several ventilation systems with or without 
mechanical components and controls, can be designed to provide a satisfying indoor environment.  
The following sections will describe the main ventilation systems and control strategies found in 
Danish classrooms.   
2.2.1 
MECHANICAL VENTILATION 
Mechanical ventilation is a concept, where mechanical equipment is used to move and remove 
air from a room or area. The inlet fans are used to control the air supply and the extractor fans 
will adjust the extraction of exhaust air. Together the suppliers and extractors must secure a well-
balanced and satisfying indoor climate. The quality of the air depends, among other things, on the 
air handling unit. This unit is equipped with components to preserve a good air quality by filtering 
the supply air, heating or cooling the air to the right temperature and extracting energy from the 
warm exhaust air to preform sustainable. This unit can either be centralized or decentralized.  
The effectiveness of the ventilation can be measured as the ratio between pollution in the extracted 
air and in air in the occupation zone. The measurement must be corrected for concentrations in 
the supply air. (DS447, 2013) (Hviid, et al., 2014) (Stampe & Ludvigsen, 2015) and (Zhang, et 
al., 2016) 
Many classrooms are ventilated either by mechanical ventilation or natural ventilation. 
Mechanical ventilation covers both balanced and not balanced ventilation. Balanced mechanical 
ventilation (BMV) is commonly used in newly constructed or refurbished classrooms. BMV 
ventilation can be obtain by different concepts, described in section 2.2.1.1, 2.2.1.2 and 2.2.1.3.  
Mechanical exhaust ventilation is a non-balanced mechanical ventilation system and is often seen 
in schools. The concept will in this report be referred to as Mechanical exhaust ventilation (MEV) 
and is described in section 2.2.1.4.  
2.2.1.1 BMV - MIXED VENTILATION 
The concept of mixed ventilation is to get the same distribution of temperature and emissions in 
the entire room by diluting the polluted air with fresh air containing oxygen.  
Conventional mixed ventilation is a common type 
of ventilation, where the fresh tempered air is let 
in by supply fans placed outside the area of 
occupation. The fresh air will effectively be 
mixed locally in the occupied zone and the 
warmer and anoxic air will raise because of the 
weight and be removed by the extractors. When 
using conventional mixing one must be aware of 
the risk of short circuiting due to the levitation 
from the occupants. The heat from the occupants 
can influence the mixing between clean air and 
polluted air.

Displacement ventilation is a concept where the 
supply air is let in directly near the floor at a 
temperature below the desired room temperature. 
The air will circulate in the occupied zone and be 
heated up by the occupants and equipment 
present. When the air is heated the weight will

This system assures a constant good air quality

exhaust valve in the celling.  
The ventilation effectiveness is usually between 
1.2 and 1.4 for mixed ventilation. (DS447, 2013)

2.2.1.4 MEV - EXHAUST VENTILATION  
Exhaust ventilation is a non-balanced ventilation 
concept, where one or several mechanic outlets 
extract the warm and polluted air from the 
classroom. This creates an under pressure in the 
room which forces the fresh air in through 
openings, inlets and cracks.  
The concept can be supported by heat recovery 
units, which preheats the air before it enters the 
occupation zone.  
The pollutant air will raise because of gravity and be extracted by the fans. Because of the lack of 
control, it is difficult to ensure a good mix of supply air in the zone of occupancy.   
A disadvantage is, that during the heating season the risk of discomfort derived from draft will 
increase. It is very common in schools, to place one or several manual inlets behind radiators, 
which will work as a heat exchanger and minimize the draft. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
This solution is often seen in elderly buildings and is found in several of the schools of Greve. It 
is experienced that it is a challenge to maintain the inlet valves, due to the age and lack of use. 
And during a visual investigation of the schools, it was revealed that the main part of the inlets 
was either closed or blocked.

2.2.2 
NATURAL VENTILATION 
Natural ventilation is a ventilation concept that will use the natural air flow in a room and building. 
The air flow is mainly influenced by temperature differences, wind and pressure. There are three 
different types of natural ventilation, where the design of the room is the major difference.  
One sided natural ventilation (1), is a concept where inlet and outlet are placed in the same side 
of the room. The effectiveness of this concept is limited and depends on wind pressure, 
temperature and design. A rule of thumb is that the depth of the room maximum must be 2.5 times 
the height.  
Cross sided ventilation (2), is a concept where the openings are placed opposite of each other. 
The inlet must be placed in a façade with the main wind pressure. The outlet must be placed in 
the lee side. The draught through the room will insure an air change. A rule of thumb is that the 
depth of the room maximum must be 5 times the height.  
Ventilation by levitation (3), is a concept the warm indoor air will levitate and create a circulation 
with fresh air from inlets in the facades. The chimney effect will support the levitation and let out 
the warm indoor air above the roof.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Natural ventilation can be manually or automatically controlled. Manually controlled natural 
ventilation is often seen in schools, where it is very common that teachers or students opens 
windows or door when they feel like it. The manual concept may cause lower temperatures and 
draft during the winter and periods with lower outside temperatures 
2.2.3 
HYBRID VENTILATION  
Hybrid ventilation combines the principles from mechanical and natural ventilation. The 
mechanical ventilation is often used doing the heating season, where natural ventilation might 
cause draft and discomfort. The heat recovery insures a comfortable temperature and a low energy 
consumption. The advantages of hybrid ventilation are during the summer time, where the natural 
ventilation is used to ventilate and extract the heat and polluted air.   
Challenges to be aware of is to make sure that the windows and openings are placed correctly 
according to wind, pressure and suction. Furthermore, the geometry of the rooms and building 
must be planed so it supports the use of natural ventilation.

2.2.4 
CONTROL PRINCIPLES 
The controlling principles are used both with mechanical, natural and hybrid ventilation. The 
principles will have an influence the indoor climate and the energy consumption, among other 
things. The overall concept is to control the airflow due to the time. This can be done with 3 main 
concepts.  
2.2.4.1 CAV – CONSTANT AIR VOLUME 
When controlling the ventilation according 
to a constant air volume (CAV), a time 
schedule is made to fit a standard situation 
with a uniform pollution and heat load. 
The air supply and exhaust are constant 
from start to stop. This is suitable in rooms 
where the situation according to pollution, 
temperature, occupancy and activity is 
always the same. In rooms with divergent 
usage, this solution will result in energy 
waste and potential discomfort in situations 
with higher temperatures and levels of pollution. A disadvantage with CAV is, that energy 
consumption will be higher compared with VAV and DCV.

2.4 USER BEHAVIOR  
The user behavior in schools have changed during the years. The kids are no longer sent out 
during the breaks to play. Instead they are sitting inside playing with phones and tablets. 
Assuming that this was not the original purpose with the breaks, the design of natural ventilation 
might no longer be sufficient.  
A report from 2016 produced by Realdania in collaboration with DTU and the Alexandra Institute, 
analyzes how it was possible to affect the user behavior in terms of indoor climate and manually 
airing. The analysis was part of an extended report on Indoor climate in public schools and 
renovations over the past years. It was analyzed how a well-integrated behavior policy can be an 
alternative to mechanical ventilation.  
The new school reform introduced in 2014 resulted in longer days for pupils in grade 0th to grade 
9th. But it also meant that the teachers now have more time with the students and that exercise 
breaks were introduced. In average, the students have 45 minutes a day for exercising (Ritzau 
2013). This is a great opportunity in which it is possible to incorporate an increased focus on 
manual airing of classrooms without mechanical ventilation.  
The investigated project included 6 classes in Denmark, where in 3 different initiatives were 
introduced. The purpose with the initiatives was to bring the CO2 concentration down during the 
lectures. The initiatives were: 
1. Sending the student outside during the breaks. 
2. Introduce activities done outside the classrooms.  
3. Involve the student in airing the classrooms.  
Figure 2-16 shows that it made a different to send the students outside during the breaks. The CO2 
concentration was significantly lower after the break where the students have been outside.  
The introduction of smaller activities outside the classrooms did not show a significant decrease 
in the CO2 concentration.  
Involving the students in airing the classrooms showed good results for the first two weeks. 
Thereafter, the CO2 concentration went back to normal. That indicated that the students have been 
well-aware of airing the rooms but the focus decreased over time. (Realdania et Al. 2016)

The conclusion of the analysis was, that it was difficult to maintain the attention and it was 
difficult to make the initiatives in to habits. Neither the students nor the teachers could feel effect, 
and because of that the motivation to maintain the initiatives dropped.  
Subsequent interviews with the teachers clarify reasons why it was difficult to maintain the 
initiatives. (Real Dania et Al. 2016)  
1. The habits might be barriers. The teachers have a busy and well-structured day, where 
their main priority is to teach. The initiatives get deemphasized in an everyday driven by 
habits.  
 
2. The time is a factor. Even though the consequences of the new school reform are more 
hours, the teachers find it difficult to prioritize other activities, because of the also 
increasing work pressure. The initiatives interrupt with their workflow and is therefore 
deemphasized.  
 
3. The students must be part of it. It is difficult to maintain the students’ interest when 
they cannot feel the effect. Furthermore, they were not interested in remembering 
alterations which they found irritating. They did not feel like going outside during the 
breaks, and therefore they did not remind the teachers if they forgot.  
Together with the teachers, the authors settled on 5 parameters which are essentials to make an 
effective incorporation of indoor climate initiatives. (Real Dania et Al. 2016)  
1. Increased focus. The project must involve the entire school to ensure that the focus is 
uniform.  
 
2. Collaboration and unison. It is not just the single classroom which must be involved. A 
changed culture towards breaks, teaching etc. are essential.  
 
3. The big picture. An improved indoor climate must be part of the work environment 
improvement, which includes much more than just the indoor climate parameters.  
 
4. Simplicity. To maintain focus, the initiatives must be simple and easy to incorporate.   
 
5. Ownership and effect. It is important to involve the students, so that they can contribute 
actively. To involve the students, they must be aware of the effect, even though they 
cannot feel it or at least do not notice it.

3 GUIDELINES  
When designing new schools, the designers are dictated by the Danish building code. But there is 
no substantial rules or laws for existing buildings to insure a sufficient indoor climate for the 
students. The teachers are protected by the work environment act which sets restrictions for 
temperature and air quality. The educational environment act, supposedly does the same for 
students, but has no significant boundaries for any of the indoor climate parameters.   
Though, building owners must be aware of the rule of profound changes according to the building 
code. Profound changes cover, extensions, renovations and change of use. If these are completed, 
then the building/area must meet the terms in the current building code.  
The Danish standards recommend limits for all indoor climate parameters and includes section 
specifically designed for public schools.

3.1.1 
THE DANISH BUILDING CODE 2010 (BR10): 
BR10 was introduced in 2010 and replaced in 2016 by BR15.  
The requirements for ventilation in schools in Denmark was: 5 l/s per person, and 0.35 l/s per m² 
floor. Furthermore, it must be insured that the CO2 concentration does not exceed 0.1 pct. (Up to 
1499 ppm) in long-lasting periods.  
The ventilation must be executed by a unit that includes both exhaustion and supplement.  
The building owner defines the hours and temperature limit in building different from residential.   
3.1.2 
THE DANISH BUILDING CODE 2015 (BR15) 
BR15 was introduced in 2016 and updated in 2017.  
The requirements for ventilation in schools in Denmark was: 5 l/s per person, and 0.35 l/s per m² 
floor. Furthermore, it must be insured that the CO2 concentration does not exceed 1000 ppm in 
long-lasting periods. The ventilation must be executed by a unit that includes both exhaustion and 
supplement.  
The temperature may not exceed 27°C more than 100 hours per year and 28°C only 25 hours per 
year. 
3.1.3 
THE DANISH BUILDING CODE 2020 (BR20) 
BR20 will be introduced in 2020. As building owner, you can choose to abide the demands in 
2020, even though it is not a requirement yet.  
The requirements for ventilation in schools in Denmark are expected to be similar to BR15: 5 l/s 
per person, and 0.35 l/s per m² floor. The limit for CO2 concentration is expected to be 900 ppm 
in long-lasting periods.  
The ventilation must be executed by a unit that includes both exhaustion and supplement and a 
VAV solution is recommended.  
The temperature demand is not fully determined yet, for BR20.

At-instruction A.1.2 from January 2008 
The Danish Work Environment Service (Arbejdstilsynet) is an executive service who reacts on

4.3.4 
PERFORMANCE TEST  
The students were asked to fill out a d2-test for every scenario. The purpose with the d2-test is to 
measure the students’ ability to concentrate and pay attention. It consists of 14 rows of the 
characters d and p, the characters are all marked with one or two dashes above and/or below. (Jørn 
Toftum et Al. 2017). The students had 20 seconds per row, to mark as many of the characters d 
with two dashed.  
The d2-test are assessed by using the concentration performance method (KY). This method is an 
improvement of the TS-F method. The method assesses the total score (Y) by counting the total 
number of correctly marked d2’s (TS) and subtracting the total number of wrongly marked 
distracting characters (F2). (d2 Test, instruction and scoring Pdf) 
It is well-known that the students understating of the test will increase for each time they do it. 
To make reservations for improved performance and learning, to methods were used.  
The learning effect is largest after the first time the students do the test. And therefore, was the 
test introduced during the introduction week. The purpose was, to let the students try out the test 
and ask questions, before they were evaluated by the results. This should minimize the percentage 
of improvement.  
The second initiative introduced to minimize the effect of learning, was a randomizing of the 
scenarios. The overall purpose with the tests, was to evaluate the performance during each 
scenario. By randomizing the scenarios as illustrated in table 4-1, it was insured that the students 
would not necessarily score best during scenario 3.

4.4 EQUIPMENT  
A Vaisala GMW90R sensor connected to a HOBO UX120 logger, was used in all investigations.  
The HOBO logger was used to log the data from the sensor, and was with a cable 4-20mA 
connection. The accuracy was ± 0.001 mA and ±0.2 % of reading. (HOBO® 4-Channel Analog 
Data Logger (UX120-006M) Manual) 
The Vaisala GMW90R sensor measured CO2, [ppm], relative humidity [%] and air temperature 
[°C]. The accuracy for CO2 was ± 30 ppm of range and ± 2 % in reading, ± 2.5 % for humidity 
and ±0.5°C for temperature. (Vaisala GMW90R datasheet) 
Both sensor and logger were mounted in a safety suitcase.  
According the investigation of the current indoor climate state in the municipality of Greve one 
suitcase was placed in each classroom with different type of ventilation. The case was placed 
inside the occupancy zone close to the students and teacher. The interval of the measurements 
was set to 5 minutes.  
According the investigation of the retrofitting solution in Karlslunde skole, one suitcase was 
placed in each of the 6 classrooms. The case was placed inside the occupancy zone close to the 
students and teacher. The interval of the measurements was set to be 5 minutes.

classroom to measure the humidity, temperature and CO2 concentration. The case was placed 
inside the occupancy zone close to the students and teacher. The interval of the measurements

4.6 
PROCESSING OF DATA 
4.6.1 
GENERAL PROCESSING 
The analysis of the CO2 concentration and temperature in the schools of Greve, were planned to 
only investigate the occupied periods.  The recordings were made from 08:00 to 14:00 Monday 
to Friday. The sorting did not consider whether there were students or if they had class in other 
rooms. The used CO2 concentration and temperature, were calculated as the median value of the 
entire data.  
The measurements used for the analysis of Holmeagerskolen was sorted in relation to each class’ 
time table. This ensured that the results were based only on time with occupancy. The time tables 
are to be found in Appendix A6. The CO2 concentration and temperature during the tests, were 
calculated as the median value in the previous lection of 45 minutes.  
4.6.2 
MEDIAN VALUE VERSUS MEAN VALUE 
In general, the median value was used instead of mean value, to illustrate the representative CO2 
concentration or temperature. By using the median value, the results were corrected for any biases 
or failure points. The mean value should only be used in analyzes of normal distributed data.  
The mean value describes the average value of the data set. The median value describes the middle 
value, when the data is sorted from lowest to highest. The difference between values is that the 
mean value does not correct for biases.  
4.6.3 
BOX PLOTS  
The box plot was used to as a statistic illustration of one or several 
datasets. Whiskers indicates the outlies which is data points 
outside of the quartiles. The whiskers were not included in the 
illustration, because they were not relevant for the analysis.  
The values of the box plots are divided as seen in figure 4-17. The 
top line indicates the maximum value measured. This value was 
only used in comparisons with other measurements. The top line 
of the box indicates the upper 25 % quartile. The median indicates 
the middle value. The bottom line of the box indicates the lower 
25 % quartile. The bottom line describes the lowest value 
measured. This value was used to detect biases and when 
describing the temperatures.  
4.6.4 
CUMULATIVE FREQUENCY  
The cumulative frequency describes the percentage of measurements within a specific interval. 
It was used to determine the percentage of measurements below or above a specific level of 
temperature or CO2 concentration.  
The curves were calculated as a function of the sorted data and the normed normal distribution. 
By using the normed normal distribution, the intermediate values were iterated. This resulted in 
the smooth s-curved curves.

LINEAR REGRESSION 
Linear regression describes the connection between two coherent data points. The data points are 
plotted into a xy-diagram, and the linear regression describes the correlation.  
The correlation coefficient R2 describes the correlation between the data. If R2 = 1 then there is 
a perfect correlation between the data. If R2 = 0 then there is no correlation between the data.  
The linear regression was used to analyze the correlation between the indoor environment 
parameters and the pupils’ performance or comfort. If there was a linear correlation between the 
data points, it would be possible to e.g.  predict the performance to a given temperature.

5.1 INVESTIGATION OF THE CURRENT STATE IN GREVE 
The current indoor climate situation at the schools of Greve has been investigated, to illustrate 
how the situation according to CO2 concentration and temperature was inside the pupils’ 
classrooms. The investigation started with an assessment of the present mechanical equipment at 
each school, were ventilation, heating systems etc. were evaluated. The assessment was compared 
with the building plans and a percentage distribution of the different ventilation systems was made 
for each school.  
Two to three measurements were conducted at each school, during a week in March/April. The 
measurements were planned to cover all ventilation concepts at each school. The measurements 
were analyzed and compared to current rules and guidelines and previous studies. 
5.1.1 
VENTILATION CONCEPTS  
Figure 5-1 shows the distribution of ventilation concepts on each school. The assessment of each 
school and the room distribution plans illustrated how each school was covered according to 
ventilation concepts described in section 2.2.  
3 out of 8 investigated schools were fully equipped with balanced mechanical ventilation (BMV). 
The mechanical units were installed at different points of time during the previous 21 years. Both 
Karlslunde skole and Tune skole were refurbished recently and are ventilated by new central 
BMV units. Karlslunde is fully equiped and Tune skole Højen partly. Krogårdskolen and 
Strandskolen are entirely covered by BMV but have elderly ventilation units.  
The most common type of ventilation, was mechanical exhaust ventilation (MEV) and used at 5 
out of 8 schools, whereof Tune skole Lunden was the only one fully ventilated by MEV.  
The manually opened inlets in the MEV systems, are designed differently. The most common 
system is with an inlet behind the radiators, in which the radiators in theory heats up the fresh 
inlet air to a comfortable temperature. The investigation shows that the main part of the inlets was 
blocked and/or off.

5.2.4 
 SUMMARY RETROFITTING KARLSLUNDE SKOLE 
The analysis of Karlslunde skole was made to show the effect of introducing balanced mechanical 
ventilation and estimate the effect on the pupils’ performance.  
The median CO2 concentration was above 1000 ppm in all five investigated classrooms before 
the retrofit. After the retrofit, the median was kept below 700 during entire week. The CO2 
concentration after the installation was classified as a good indoor environment according to 
DS3033. 
The mean temperature was in the acceptable range both before and after the retrofit. The first 
investigation was made in the heating season and the second outside the heating season. This 
changes the acceptable range of temperature.  
Box plots of data measured before the installation of balanced mechanical ventilation showed that 
the lowest measured temperatures were way below recommended. This could indicate that it is 
difficult to air the rooms naturally during the heating season, without causing low operative 
temperatures in short periods of time.  
The improved CO2 concentration corresponded to an estimated increased performance of 10 % in 
routine school work. The increased temperature might cause a decrease in performance, but this 
must be expected going from winter to summer.  
All in all, the retrofitting has had a positive impact on the indoor environment in the five 
classrooms at Karlslunde skole.

5.3 HOLMEAGERSKOLEN INTERVENTION ANALYSIS 
In this section, the three interventions carried out at Holmeagerskolen are studied. The method 
used is described in section 4.3.   
The first part concerns the indoor climate inside the classrooms during the 3 investigation weeks. 
The air quality and thermal environment are analyzed and compared for each scenario, to 
conclude which scenario did best according to air quality and thermal environment.  
The second part concerns the performance tests and questionnaires filled out for each scenario. 
The results are compared and analyzed regarding the indoor environment at the time. The analysis 
was made determine which scenario did the best according to the students’ comfort and 
performance. 
The purpose with the analysis, was to determine if there is a less expensive alternative to 
mechanical ventilation, and how it will affect the student’s performance and well-being.

SUMMARY 
None of the natural ventilation interventions assured a sufficient indoor environment in any of the 
investigation weeks. The CO2 concentration was way above the recommended level of 1000 ppm 
during all the weeks. The distribution and average CO2 concentrations were not very different 
when comparing the three scenarios, which might have been the biggest impact on the insufficient 
comparisons. Scenario 1 had an average CO2 concentration of 1487 ppm, scenario 2 had an 
average CO2 concentration of 1514 ppm and scenario 3 had an average CO2 concentration of 1440 
ppm.  
The classroom based analysis, showed that scenario 3 had the lowest average CO2 concentration 
in 2 out of 3 investigated classrooms. Which indicated that the teachers were already airing the 
classrooms, and even more frequently than described for scenario 1 and 2.  
There was no obvious connection between temperature and scenario, but the analysis of 
performance indicated that the students performed better in a colder indoor environment. 6D had 
a lower average temperature and a higher average performance score compared with 6A.  
Scenario 3 was the pupils’ favorite scenario, both according to thermal environment and air 
quality. Which indicated that they probably preferred to be in charge of the airing.

6 
DISCUSSION  
The purpose of this thesis was to investigate the current situation and provide a quantitative 
illustration of the indoor environment in the schools of Greve. Furthermore, to inform about 
consequences associated with a poor indoor environment and suggest a set of different solutions.  
The solutions must first and foremost ensure a sufficient indoor environment for the students and 
teachers, but besides that, oblige to the fact that the municipalities are obligated to listen to the 
citizens’ needs when allocating their financial resources.  
6.1 THE INDOOR CLIMATE STATE AT THE SCHOOLS OF GREVE 
The investigation showed that 6 out of 8 schools had worse air quality compared the average of 
the region and that 50 % were worse than the average in Denmark. The comparison of air quality 
and ventilation concepts emphasized the theory, that the classrooms with the best air quality is 
the classrooms with balanced mechanical ventilation.  The investigation of the thermal 
environment indicated no significant temperature problems inside the classrooms. The main 
problem according to thermal environment is during the summer season, where the risk of high 
peak temperatures is more frequent. Obviously, it is a problem when the temperature drops below 
the recommendations and it forces the teachers to neglect the airing of the classrooms, but this 
problem should as well be solved with mechanical ventilation.   
About the air quality investigations and measurements, Mayor Pernille Beckmann said: 
”I know we have a problem, we are not the only municipality, but I think our problem is serious!” 
The investigations in Greve were made during an entire school week and sorted so that they only 
included measurements made in the period from 08:00 to 14:00. This means that the average 
concentration was lowered because of measurements made during the time without occupancy, 
when the students were receiving education in other rooms.  On the other hand, many of the older 
students attend class until later than 14:00, which means that the high concentrations at the end 
of the day were excluded in some cases. Furthermore, it is important to remember that only 2-3 
measurements were made at each school, without taking into account the number of students, 
their age and the airing habits.  
When comparing the results of the Mass experiment (2014) with the results of the measurements 
in Greve, it is important to notice that the measurements are made using different methods. The 
measurements of the Mass Experiment were carried out by the students, who measured the CO2 
concentration twice by using a tube and a measuring pipe. The first measurement simulated a 
winter environment, where the windows were closed during the lecture. The second measurement 
simulated a summer scenario, where the students were sent out during the breaks and the 
classroom was naturally aired. The measurements were made at the end of the lecture, when it is 
assumed that the CO2 concentrations were at their highest.

6.2 THE SOLUTIONS  
6.2.1 
BALANCED MECHANICAL VENTILATION 
The analysis of Karlslunde skole showed a convincing improvement of the air quality after the 
installation of balanced mechanical ventilation. The measurements indicated that the ventilation 
system with DCV control was able to keep the CO2 concentration below 900 ppm, which was 
even better than recommended.  
According to thermal environment, the analysis showed a large difference in the temperatures 
measured before and after the installation, where the second measurements of temperature were 
considerably higher than the first and very close to the upper recommended value. The variation 
was caused by an increased outside temperature. The first set of measurements were recorded 
from the 6th of March to the 10th of March – during the heating season. The second set was made 
from the 29th of May to the 2nd of June – outside the heating season.  
The recommended operative temperature is higher during the summer than during the winter 
(DS15251). The reason for the different recommendations is, that it is expected that the occupants 
will wear clothes with a lower insulation value during the summer and therefore desire higher 
indoor temperatures.  
It must be expected that the temperatures will exceed the recommendation during peak periods. 
The average outside temperatures will be higher during August than during the beginning of June 
(DMI) and it will not be possible to keep the inlet temperatures significantly below the outside 
temperature without the use of mechanical cooling.   
The installation and maintenance of a balanced mechanical ventilation system is clearly more 
expensive compared with manual or automatic natural ventilation (Christian Hviid et al, 2014). 
But the operation costs are lower. The mechanical ventilation will result in an increased electricity 
consumption, but a decreased heating consumption. Previous studies show that the electricity 
consumption in systems with balanced ventilation and heat recovery, often is way less than the 
savings and therefore profitable. (Christian Hviid et al, 2014. Energi effective bygninger, 2001) 
6.2.2 
NATURAL VENTILATION 
The investigation of the indoor climate interventions at Holmeagerskolen showed that none of the 
scenarios were able to reduce the CO2 concentration to an acceptable level. When analyzing the 
measured CO2 concentrations, the graphs illustrated a different distribution than expected. The 
analysis indicated that the instructions were not followed properly, and that the situation in the 
classrooms was not always as expected.  
The comparison of the theoretical concentration and the measured concentration, indicated that 
the calculated CO2 production and/or air change had been different from the reality. The 
production rate was based on 25 occupants during scenario 1 and 27 occupants during scenario 2 
with a production of 17 l CO2 per hour per occupant. The number of occupants referred to the 
number of students participating in the test and questionnaires the same day.  
A reason why the concentrations were different could be that the number of students were not the 
same at the beginning of the day compared with the time of the test. This would result in a lower 
production rate and a less steep graph. Besides this, there are arguments for using a different 
production than the 17 l CO2 per hour per occupant. DS3033 suggests a production of 18 l CO2 
per hour per occupant, for adults.

Another just as probable reason, could be that a door or window had been open during the time 
when all openings were supposed to be closed. It is plausible that the door to the hallway had 
been open, and this would result in an unintentional positive affect on the dilution. If the 
theoretical model were to be more precise, it would have required a stricter observation of the 
interventions.  
The method used was a quantitative approach, where the teachers were asked to follow the 
instructions handed out and note irregular behavior that could influence the indoor environment. 
The use of a mixed method approach, where quantitative and qualitative methods mixes (e.g. the 
ethnographic method), could have assured a more detailed analysis. The qualitative observations 
could have provided a more precise description of the user behavior and other influencing factors 
– like in the study made by the Alexandra institute. If this should have been possible, it would 
have required a spectator to be present during the entire school day. The spectator should note 
changes, number of occupants, airing habits etc. And in addition to this, be in charge of airing the 
classrooms during scenario 1 and 2. This could have prevented that the teachers’ and students’ 
fluctuating engagement would have had an impact on the results.  
The report made by Realdania and the Alexandra Institute, underlined several barriers that could 
prevent a good integration of indoor environment interventions in classrooms. One of them was, 
that if the participants cannot feel the effect of their effort, the engagement will decrease and their 
usual habits will take over (Realdania et al, 2016). The teacher of 8B wrote in the notebook:  
“The students felt no effect on their performance, as a result of being outside during the breaks.”  
This statement and the measurements of CO2 concentration during scenario 2, confirm the theory 
made by Realdania and the Alexandra Institute. If the students did not feel the effect of being 
outside during the breaks, then the effort would be useless. Which was confirmed by the CO2 
measurements, that indicated that not all the students left the classrooms during the breaks. This 
has also effected the CO2 concentration and the final results.  
Besides evaluating the measured indoor environment parameters, the investigation analyzed how 
the different scenarios affected the students comfort. The analysis showed that there was no clear 
correlation between measured indoor environment and experienced indoor environment.  
It was expected, that the students would be able to sense the changes in air quality and temperature 
and evaluate them as being better or worse. This was not the case and the main reason might have 
been that the concentration and temperature were not that different in the two scenarios. The 
students preferred scenario 3, where they were allowed to follow their usual habits and air the 
classrooms when they felt like it. This confirms the conclusions in the Realdania study, which 
stated that it is difficult to change the occupants’ usual behavior and habits and it indicates that 
the sense of freedom might have had a positive psychologic effect on the students.   
Besides evaluating the experienced indoor environment, the students’ performance was analyzed 
due to each scenario. Based on the theory from Wargocki & Wyon (2007), it was expected that 
there would be a correlation between performance and temperature and between performance and 
CO2 concentration. There were several sources of error associated with the experiments carried 
out at Holmeagerskolen. The main reason is assumed to be the small difference in air quality and 
temperature when comparing the scenarios. The effect would supposedly be bigger if the 
comparison was made on a good vs. bad indoor climate, instead or bad vs. bad. 
Another source of error was, that the method used to evaluate the performance did not take the 
students learning ability into account. It is well-known that the students’ understanding of the test 
==PAGE:84==
Indoor Environment and User Behavior in the Primary Schools of Greve                                 
• • • 
 84 
will improve for each time they participate and that the highest percentage of learning is achieved 
after the first test. This was taken into account by introducing the test during the introduction 
week, and it gave the students a chance to try the test without it being evaluated. Beside trying to 
minimize the learning by letting the students try the test before, was the scenarios randomized by 
the use of a 3x3 matrix. The randomizing had the purpose of minimizing the effect of learning by 
letting the classes experience the scenarios in different orders.  
The evaluation of the tests showed that there was a linear increment in the test scores, from week 
one to week four. Only week three stood out. 8B’s performance was in average better in week 3 
compared with week 2 and 4. The CO2 concentration was likewise lower during that week. This 
indicated that the improved air quality had a positive effect on the students’ performance. But 
additionally, it was concluded that 1 positive result out of 9 was not enough to confirm the 
hypothesis.  
The final conclusion of the analysis was, that it was not possible to either confirm or reject the 
hypothesis about a correlation between indoor environment and performance, because of the 
comprehensive sources of errors and uncertainties. The measures made to minimize the learning, 
showed a small effect on the performance in 8B, but unfortunately not enough to prove an overall 
correlation between indoor climate and performance.   
6.3 INTERVIEW WITH THE MAYOR OF GREVE  
The mayor of Greve – Pernille Beckmann (V), was informed about the findings through a short-
written summary and was then interview. The purpose was to get her view on the report and the 
results and to inform the chief executive in Greve about the situation on the schools.  
The following discussion will treat the most relevant and interesting statements made. The full 
Danish transcription of the interview is to be found in appendix A5.  
The first question to the mayor was what her immediate response to the results was. The question 
was asked to initiate the conversation, and to lure her attitude towards the report and topic.  
Pernille Beckmann answered:  
“It is interesting! Maybe it confirms some things I already knew, and it just puts them in a clearer 
perspective. It is disturbing that there is that much difference in the schools. I think my illustration 
was different.”  
The statement indicated that the mayor was aware of the problem, but not the importance. She 
seemed clearly shocked when talking about the graphs and illustrations. Subsequently she said:  
“You can sense it when you enter the schools. The air quality is bad and characterized by smelly 
feet. It makes it difficult to concentrate.”  
It was later discussed how politicians and laymen in Greve relate to the problem. And it was clear 
that the citizens’ opinions play a big part when it comes to the distribution of finances.   
“The problem is, that a renovation towards an improved indoor environment is not a visual 
improvement. It is difficult as a politician to sell an item like this. I am not going to be reelected 
by insuring improved ventilation on all the schools.”

The statements, together with the context of the interview, indicated that there is a belief that the

Subsequently she said: 
“If it is possible to prove that the result of the learning is improving and that the students at

6.4 PERSPECTIVE 
In future studies, it could be interesting and beneficial to analyze the issues from a more 
socioeconomic aspect. The positive derived effects from a good indoor environment with regard 
to grades, economic and sick leave, could be a unique argument to affect the people distributing 
the central money pools. And it would probably be easier for laymen to relate, if the pros and cons 
were converted to economy. Theoretically could an 15 % improvement in performance, save the 
government a significant amount of money.       
When evaluating the methods used in this report, could a mix methods study be interesting and 
maybe more useful especially when looking at user behavior. Questionnaires, tests and check lists 
are all quantitative methods, which gathers a wide amount of general data. This could be 
combined with qualitative studies of human behavior with reference to, airing habits, ability to 
concentrate and performance. However, one of the problems with a qualitative approach is that it 
is usually more expensive.  It would require the observations of one or several professionals with 
a curtain degree of expertise.  
Another interesting study could be, an analysis of the thermal environment outside the heating 
season, to investigate the consequences of higher temperatures in the classrooms. The outcome 
could be an analysis of performance and comfort, where the students were evaluated on familiar 
school tasks, which. This would probably minimize the impact of increased learning on the 
results.

CONCLUSION  
Based on the investigations and analyses made, it is concluded that there is a problem with the 
indoor environment in the main part of the schools in Greve. The main indoor environment 
problem was the air quality. The CO2 concentration, which is an indicator for the air quality, 
exceeded the recommended level of 1000 ppm in all the schools except one. Only two schools 
had a better air quality than the average in the region and four schools risk injunctions from the 
Labor inspectorate. 
The measurements were made during the heating season, which implies that high operative 
temperatures should not be the key problem. The investigation confirmed the hypothesis and 
showed that the main problem according to thermal environment, was the temperature drops when 
the occupants aired the classrooms naturally.  
The analysis of ventilation types showed that mechanical exhaust ventilation is the most common 
type of ventilation in the classrooms of Greve. The investigations also showed that classrooms 
with mechanical exhaust ventilation had the highest CO2 concentrations compared with 
classrooms with balanced mechanical ventilation. Through a dialogue with the teachers at 
Holmeagerskolen, it was confirmed that the manual inlets in classrooms with mechanical exhaust 
ventilation, were unusable and the windows would be opened instead.  
Based on the investigation of the two different solutions, it is concluded that balanced mechanical 
ventilation with VAV or DCV control, is the best solution. The CO2 concentration in the newly 
retrofitted classrooms was kept below 900 ppm during the entire investigation week.  
The intervention study at Holmeagerskolen showed that none of the natural ventilation initiatives 
were able to keep the CO2 concentration at an acceptable level. Furthermore, there was no 
sufficient correlation between the indoor environment and the students’ performance and comfort. 
The main reason for the missing correlation, was that the air quality was not improved to a level 
where the students could actually feel a difference. The analysis of the CO2 distributions showed 
that the handed-out descriptions were not followed sufficiently, which confirmed the essential 
initiatives stated by Realdania for making an effective incorporation of indoor climate initiatives.  
Based on the interview with the Mayor of Greve, Pernille Beckmann, it is concluded that the 
problem with the poor indoor environment in the classrooms of Greve, is well-known. The 
interview implied an inadequate awareness among politicians and citizens when it comes to the 
importance of a good indoor environment in the classrooms.  
The politicians all over Denmark are under a great pressure when it comes to the distribution of 
finances and for the students’ indoor environment to be prioritized, it is necessary to inform the 
people that cares for the children’s interests. Parents, teachers, school boards and politicians must 
be aware about the consequences of a poor indoor environment – as otherwise it will never be 
desirable for a politician to prioritize a non-visual improvement like this.

P: Ja så holder de bare en fridag. Så ja, det er da et obs punkt. For jeg synes, vi har et problem, 
vi er ikke den eneste kommune, men jeg synes vores problemer stort, det må jeg blankt erkende.  
 
C: Det er det desværre, også når man sammenligner med landsgennemsnit og kigger på vores 
nabolande, så er de generelt mere strenge i deres krav og grænseværdier.  
 
P: Der er mange politikkere, der spejler sig til Finlands skolesystem, og det er også rigtig fint, 
men så kan man sige: ”Måske er det ikke alene de pædagogiske metoder, men måske skulle man 
se lidt bredde og vurdere, hvad er det egentlig den samlede pakke skal bestå af.” – og det tænker 
jeg da, at vi skal forfølge. Der er en dagsorden her som er umiddelbart lidt nørdet og kedelig, 
men som har en kæmpe værdi – og det synes jeg da er spændende.  
 
P: Det har været spændende læsning,  
 
C: Som sagt vil jeg meget gerne sende rapporten til dig, når jeg er færdig.  
 
P: Det må du meget gerne, og med din tilladelse vil jeg gerne have lov at sende den videre rundt 
i systemet. Og sige, at der er noget her, som jeg synes vi skal kigge på. Det synes jeg kunne 
være interessant.  
 
C: Min tanke med det har var jo at bruge vores snak i diskussionen. For en ting er jo hvad den 
gode og forgyldte løsning er, men nogle gange må man også være realistisk  
 
P: Det jeg måske tror er den største udfordring her, er at man politisk ikke kan sælge sådan en 
vare. Jeg bliver jo ikke genvalgt af at gå ud og sige, at nu skal vi have fed ventilation på alle 
skolerne. Det er måske ikke helt rigtigt, for jeg tror faktisk godt at forældrene ved det i dag. 
Men det har jo også været en proces for dem, og alle er ikke så langt, og det sælger jo bare ikke 
billetter. Prøv at forestille dig et folketingsmedlem, der havde sat en pulje af til bedre 
ventilation, det lyder bedre at sige, at man havde været ude og sige, at man havde sat penge af til 
flere bad til de gamle.  
 
C: Man skal jo sælge den på produktiviteten og velvære.  
 
P: Så jeg synes egentlig at der er et dilemma i det, men fakta taler jo sit klare sprog. 
 
C: Men også når man kigger på sygefravær og komfort.  
 
P: Præcis 
Måske kunne man med fordel undersøge med sygefravær, og om det har en effekt.  
 
C: Der er jo lavet rapporter, og man kan se at det har en effekt.
