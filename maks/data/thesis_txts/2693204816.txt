Abstract
In drug development process, pre-clinical studies are essential for the drug to be approved
and reach the market. Pre-clinical testing processes often take up to 10-15 years, can be
highly costly, and include many ethical issues since animal testing is involved. However,
over the latest years, researchers have been trying to optimize in vitro testing processes to
eliminate ethical issues and reduce the cost signiﬁcantly. Though, since most platforms are
established in 2D and static conditions, they are not capable of mimicking dynamic in vivo
conditions and do not fully demonstrate the physiological micro-environment in the body.
This study focuses on developing a 3D gravity-driven perfusion platform applicable to mimic
the dynamic physiological structure of organs, in the body. The ﬂow regime will be inves-
tigated to determine how representative the system is in comparison to the actual organ.
The emphasis will mainly be on blood vessels and its barrier functions, as blood vessels
are essential for transporting blood and oxygen around the body, they interact with many
organs and play a crucial role within the body.
This thesis will include design and development for the 3D fabrication of the dynamic plat-
form. However, this is further investigated by mathematical equations for ﬂow, focusing on
the importance of shear in the body. Computational simulations were performed to gain
more knowledge of the ﬂow pattern within the system and lastly an experimental setup was
completed, to prove the mechanism of the system. The results present the accomplishment
of attaining ﬂow parameters close to the ones found in blood vessels.
Furthermore, the
limitations and beneﬁts of the system are discussed.

Introduction
In recent years in vitro testing has largely been investigated and optimized for pre-clinical
trials. The reason being, constant optimization, and improvement for clinical studies. The
need for improved method is essential, due to ﬁnancial, scientiﬁc and ethical reasons. The
ﬁnancial issues arrive from the percentage of failed drugs 92 %, which never reach the market
even after many years of development[54] .Other aspects are the expensive methods used for
testing in 3D cell culture systems. The ethical matter stems from animal testing in pre-
clinical trials. This type of in vivo testing raise many ethical questions, since the animal
used for experiments undergo suﬀering and distress. Furthermore, even this method has
shown high costs, since the animals need to be accommodated and disposed [29]. Lastly,
animal-testing also raises the question of comparison between humans and animals. Pistello
et al [40] talks about how animals, raised in sterile conditions does not fully translate into
human physiology. This can be solved by focusing on in vitro studies that can rule out these
issues, and are cost-eﬀective and time-eﬃcient. Currently, basic in vitro testing includes
two-dimensional (2D) cellular assays that come with their limitations; i.e. not being able to
mimic the physiology of human tissues. The main reason being that cells shows high sensi-
tivity to their microenvironment. The environment in which they normally live is relatively
rich in molecular signals, attained from other cells and the ECM. They are also exposed
to mechanical stimulation perfused by ﬂuid ﬂow. This dynamic environment is lacking in
2D cell culturing system[29]. Numerous in vitro methods have proved to give better phys-
iological results. Three-Dimensional (3D) systems, have shown great prospective to mimic
the physiological systems, with the production of mechanical stimuli exerted by a ﬂow and
co-culture. The motivation of this study is to create necessary ﬂuid ﬂow and shear stress to
mimic the natural physiological conditions inside the body.
4.1
Hypothesis and aim
Following hypotheses are set up:
• Gravity driven system will create a simpliﬁed, cost-eﬀective and time-saving method
for in vitro testing.
• 3D printed shear stress system can solve the above mentioned issues and reﬂect real
physiological aspects of the blood vessel barrier.
• Inserts for cell cultures can be attained via a 3D, SLA printer.
In order to investigate the hypothesis following aims have been presented:
• Design and develop inserts for culturing on a 12 - well plate
• Characterization of the eﬀect of shear stress and ﬂow rate of the gravity driven system.
• Demonstration and visualization of ﬂuid ﬂow through channels in insert.

4.2
Pharmacology
In pharmacology, a drug also called a medicine is deﬁned as a chemical compound that is
designed to produce a biological eﬀect so as to prevent, cure, treat, diagnose a disease or
to promote the well-being of the individual human-being, when administered inside a living
individual human. There are immensely large numbers and types of pharmaceutical drugs. A
few examples include: Benzodiazepines (used to cure seizures), inotropic drugs like digoxin,
berberine, angiotensin (to enhance cardiac contractibility to prevent heart attacks), opioids
(to prevent cough). These drugs can be administered into body via diﬀerent routes. Some
common modes of drug administration include: enteral (orally), parenteral (intravenously),
intramuscularly, intrathecally (in space between spinal cord). Subcutaneously (beneath the
skin), sublingually (beneath the tongue), buccally (between gums and cheek), rectally or
vaginally. Other modes of drug administration include ocular, otic, nasal or nebular[27].

In order to understand pharmacology and the mechanism of drug transport, it is necessary
to emphasize the basic physiology of the organs that are involved in absorption, transport
and metabolism of ingested drug. Absorption of drugs takes place in the small intestine, its
transport is mediated by the cardiovascular system and its metabolism occurs in the liver.
A brief description of these systems in the human body is given in the upcoming sections.

4.3.1
Cardiovascular system
Cardiovascular system also known as circulatory system consists of heart (pumping unit)
and a closed system of vessels (arteries, veins and capillaries). The name circulatory system
derives from the phenomenon that blood is pumped by the heart around a closed circuit of
these vessels. This promotes transport of nutrients, gases and signalling compounds around
the body of an individual. The circulation of blood helps to maintain the homeostasis in
the body. The main functions of the circulatory system include: Transport of blood from
heart to other tissues, exchange of nutrients and oxygen and CO2 to and from the tissues,
transport of hormones, immune cells and the coagulation molecules to diﬀerent tissues in
the body and ﬁnally helps to maintain the blood pressure and the blood ﬂow to maintain
the bodies homeostasis. The vessels associated with the circulatory system are of two types:
pulmonary vessels and the systemic vessels. The pulmonary vessels help in transporting the
blood from right ventricle of the heart to the lungs and back to the left atrium of the heart.
The systemic vessels on the other hand help in transporting the blood from the left ventricle
of the heart to the body and back to the right atrium of the heart. The blood vessels which
form the continuous passage of the blood from the heart to the rest of the body and back
to heart are of 3 types: arteries, veins and the capillaries [1].

4.3.2
Histology of blood vessel
Arteries transport blood from the heart to other parts of the body through a continuum
of large arteries to smaller arteries of size 40 to 300 µm and lastly to smaller arterioles
of diameter as small as 10 µm to as large as 40 µm. Arteries and veins are both triple
layered blood vessels. The inner layer of arteries and veins is called inner tunica intima, the
middle is tunica media and outer are named as tunica adventitia. Tunica intima is based
of squamous epithelial cells also known as endothelial cells, which are continuous with the
endocardium of the heart. The endothelium is arranged on a thin layer of the connective
tissue, called lamina propria and thin layer of elastic ﬁbers which separates tunica intima
from tunica media. The middle layer (tunica media) is composed mainly of smooth muscle
cells. These muscles play a vital role in maintaining the blood ﬂow by either vasoconstriction
or vasodilation. Vasoconstriction (due to constriction of the smooth muscle cells) decreases
the diameter of the blood vessel and therefore creates greater ﬂow and increases the blood
pressure and vice versa for vasodilation. The middle layer of arteries and veins also contains
a range of diﬀerent elastic and collagen ﬁbers. The external elastic membrane divides middle
tunica media from the external tunica adventitia, which is made up of connective tissues.
Towards the tunica media, tunica adventitia is composed of very dense connective tissue
which loosens up towards the outer part of blood vessel and merges completely with the
surrounding tissue. Tunica adventitia is relatively thin as compared to that of the tunica
media.
The amount of smooth muscles presented in the middle layer of arteries varies
considerably. Large muscular arteries have 20 to 40 layers of smooth muscles present in the
tunica media, while smaller muscular arteries have only 3 to 4 layers of it. Presence of large

Physiology of human body
Capillaries range in size from 7 to 9 µm in diameter and around 1 mm in length and are the
smallest type of the blood vessels present in the body. The walls of the capillaries is based
of squamous epithelial cells which rest on a basement membrane. A layer of loose connective
tissue surrounds the basement membrane and it with the surrounding tissues. The transport
of nutrients and oxygen from the blood in capillaries to the surrounding tissues occurs by
diﬀusion. Toxic substances and CO2 are transported in the reverse direction by diﬀusion
too. The ﬂow of the blood from these capillaries back to heart is mediated by veins which
are larger in size as compared to the capillaries. The smallest veins (venules) are 50 µm in
size and usually have lower number of smooth muscles associated with them. They have
very thin walls and structurally are very similar to the arteries. Diﬀusion of substrates is
possible to a certain extend in venules but as the veins get bigger and bigger the possibility
of diﬀusion gets minimum. Medium and large sized veins have a very thin inner layer (tunica
intima) and consists of thin layer of endothelium and loose collagen rich connective tissue
and elastic ﬁbers. The middle layer has a thin layer of circular smooth muscles, while the
outer layer is much prevalent and is rich in collagenous connective tissue. Due to reduced
blood pressure in the veins, the possibility of back ﬂow of blood is there. This backﬂow is
prevented by presence of the unidirectional valves in the veins which prevent the blood to
ﬂow in the reverse direction[14].
4.3.3
Physiology of small intestines
Small intestines are a part of gastrointestinal tract, the main function of which is to propel,
blend, digest, absorb and eliminate food, liquid and other elements. The small intestine is
divided into 3 parts: duodenum, jejunum and ileum. Jejunum marks the start of the small
intestines and connects it to the stomach while the ileum is the last part which connects
small intestines to the large intestines. To make sure that the materials ﬂow in one direction
only and any back ﬂow of intestinal chyme is prevented, a unidirectional valve (ileocecal
sphincter) is present[14].
Small intestines serve multiple functions inside the human body which include:
1) Neutralization of stomach acids by transfer of bicarbonate ions from pancreas to
duodenum[14].
2) Digestion of food by mixing bile from liver and pancreatic amylase in duodenum. Bile
causes emulsiﬁcation of lipids and pancreatic amylase helps in digestion of amylase in the
food. Degradation of the proteins in jejunum by the action of peptidases and enterokinases[14].
3) Absorption of nutrients starts at small intestines. The villi and micro villi of the small
intestines increase the area for absorption[14].
4) Peristaltic movements caused by the smooth muscles of the intestinal wall help in mixing
and propulsion of the chyme at the rate of 1 cm/min[14].
5) The secretions from liver, pancreas and enteroendocrine cells help in digestion of food.
The left over needs to be excreted and small intestines facilitate the movement of that[14].

4.4
Pharmacokinetics of orally administered drugs
Pharmacokinetics refers to the reaction of the body to the drug i.e. what the body does to
the drug. It refers to the movement of the drug into the body, its absorption, bioavailability,
distribution, metabolism and excretion from the body. Pharmacokinetics of a speciﬁc drug
depends on both the drugs chemical properties as well as the patient related factors. Around
60 % of the drugs are administered orally as liquids, tablets, capsules or chewable tablets.
[16]This route is preferred because administering a drug orally is usually very convenient,
safe and least expensive. Absorption of orally administered drugs may begin in the mouth,
however it has been reported that 90 % of the adsorption takes place in small. intestine[18]
For this they have to go through the complex environment of the stomach, which has been
reported to be a major limitation of drugs administered, orally. When the drugs reach the
small intestine, they pass through the intestinal wall and travel via the portal vein to liver,
where from they are transported via the blood stream to the target site. Most of the drugs
are chemically altered by the small intestines and the liver which decreases the net amount of
drug reaching the bloodstream. Bioavailability, which is the measure of the amount of active
drug reaching systemic circulation in comparison to administered drug dose is signiﬁcantly
aﬀected by ﬁrst pass metabolism (the metabolism of these drugs before reaching systemic
circulation) of orally administered drugs.[41]

4.5
Drug development process
The process of drug development is a ﬁve-step process. The steps include: Drug Discovery
and Development, Pre-clinical research, Clinical research, FDA review and FDA Post-Market
Safety Monitoring.
The overall process is time-consuming and expensive.
It had been
estimated that the average cost involved in the process of drug development is around 1.8-
2.6 billion USD.[38]
Figure 6: Stages in drug discovery and development; adapted from North East BioLab[4]
The process of drug discovery starts with researchers gaining new insights into the diseases
and trying diﬀerent molecular compounds to inhibit or minimize the eﬀect of a disease. This
is the primary stage and at this stage thousands of diﬀerent compounds may appear to
be potential candidates for the development of the ﬁnal drug. The rigorous testing helps
to eliminate most of the chosen drugs and thus helps to narrow down the list of candidates
for further study. After identifying a promising candidate for development, studies are con-
ducted on the ADME of the compound. The development process also includes studies on
the mechanism of action of the drug, the potential beneﬁts and side eﬀects of the drug, the
mode of drug administration, interaction studies with other drugs and the eﬀectiveness of
the drug compared to other similar drugs. [36] [4] [3]
The second step, the pre-clinical research, is a very important stage in which researchers
try to ﬁnd out the toxicity that the drug can have before actually administering the drug
into the human body. Pre-clinical trails can be conducted either in vitro or in vivo. In vitro
studies are performed in cells or tissues and assists the researchers to gather further infor-
mation on ADME of the drug by the target cells and tissues. The idea of in vitro pre-clinical
trials is to gain insight into hoe the drug will behave during in vivo studies. Currently, these
studies are conducted by culturing cells which model tissues. Better modelling of ADME

Signiﬁcance of pre-clinical trials
proﬁles of the drug during in vitro studies, helps to identify the best drug candidates for in
vivo studies, and therefore considerably reduce the overall cost of the process. Pre-clinical
trials must provide a detailed information on toxicity levels and dosing of the drug. These
studies help the researchers to review their ﬁnding and decide whether the drug should be
texted in people [3].
The third step, clinical trials, refers to studies of ways in which the drug interacts with
the human body. These studies are conducted in people. Designing the clinical trial is a
process in which the researchers try to focus on the aspects that the trail will cover. These
may include: selection criteria for the drug to participate in the process, number of people
involved in the study, time course of study, method of drug administration and dosage of
drug to people, types of assessments to be made and the data to be collected, the analysis
of the data obtained etc. Clinical trials follow a series of phases from early small-scale phase
1 to late stage, large scale phase 3 studies. In phase 1 the drug is administered to 20-100
healthy volunteers or people with disease. The behaviour of the drug is studied for period
of several months and the study focuses on the safety and dosage of the drugs. Around 30%
of the drugs are eliminated at this phase. Phase 2 clinical trials are conducted in several
hundred people having the disease. There studies last for a period of several months to may
be 2 years and the studies focus on eﬃcacy and the side eﬀects of the drug. Only 33% of
the drugs entering clinical trails pass this phase and move on to phase 3. In phase 3 studies
on drug eﬃcacy and monitoring of adverse reactions are conducted on people over a time
period of 1-4 years. The study participants include around 300 to 3000 people with medical
conditions. Around 20% of the drugs pass this trial. In ﬁnal phase i.e. phase 4 these studies
are conducted in several thousand people with disease and all the selected parameters are
monitored [3].
After successful completion of the clinical trials, if the developer has the evidence that the
drug is eﬀective against the disease and is safe for the intended use, then the company can ﬁle
the application to market the drug. The concerned authority thoroughly examines all
the data and ﬁnally makes a decision to either approve or reject the drug. The process of ap-
proval may take around 6-10 months and after approval the developer can market the drug.[3]
The last step is post market safety monitoring, in which the concerned authority mon-
itors all drug and device safety once products are available for use by the public.[3]

Classical 2D models
A basic eﬀort made in the early phase of the study was to grow intestinal cells as monolayers.
CaCo-2 cells have been widely used to study of the drug permeability in humans. Caco-2

The discovery of Lgr5 stem cells in 2007 by Clevers and collegues have brought new insight

living organisms. Another limitation of organoids which limits their use in drug testing and
permeability studies is their irregular shape in terms of their 3D spatial arrangement. Some
research groups tried to overcome this limitation by developing 2D enteroid monolayers, that
are Lgr5 derived epithelial cells. These monolayers provided support for basolateral addition
of other cell types.[18]

4.11
Microﬂuidic devices - Organ-on-a-chips
The most recent advanced technology used for in vitro clinical trials of drugs is the mi-
croﬂuidic devices also known as Organ-on-a-chips. These devices usually consists channels,
chambers/reservoirs that are perfused with culture media for the speciﬁc cell type. The
cells are seeded in such a way that upon colonization and organization they simulate the
tissue and organ level physiology. Early reports of microﬂuidic devices go back to 2008 when
Kimura et al. developed the ﬁrst pumping system used on a chip[31]. They cultured Caco-2
cells on the device for a period of 14 days. Measurements of perfusion and transport of
media were conducted which ensured the application of the micro bioreactor for applications
in drug screening and toxicity testing[31][18].
Microﬂuidic devices try to mimic the natural environment by controlling the micro envi-
ronment (cell matrix, temperature, pH and chemical gradients) around the cell[17]. Use of
microﬂuidic devices has increased biological relevance of the cell models while maintaining
the throughput of the current methods. Advances in microﬂuidics have led to strong innova-
tions in cell culturing, separation and cellular assays in systems where small volumes of ﬂuids
were required. Microﬂuidic devices have been used for culturing various types of cells (re-
quiring strict culturing environments), which includes both adherent and non-adherent cells.
Advantages of these microﬂuidic systems include: continuous supply of nutrients, removal of
waste, possibility of periodic media injections with higher automation and control [17]. Re-
duced consumption of ﬂuids and the time-eﬃciency makes these systems cost eﬀective over
longer time. These devices also make it possible to monitor other physical parameters (pH,
oxygen, osmolarity, temperature, pressure and shear stress) which are associated with the
culturing of cells[17]. Behavior of cells by mimicking both static and dynamic microenviron-

4.12
Theory of ﬂuid dynamics
Fluid ﬂow can generally be categorized into diﬀerent patterns. The ﬂuid ﬂow through a
channel may either be laminar or turbulent. Laminar ﬂow, also known as viscous ﬂow can
be further categorized into disturbed and undisturbed ﬂow[12]. Undisturbed laminar ﬂow
means that the ﬂuid ﬂows in laminations, without causing any turbulence or whirls. In this
ﬂow type the smooth streamlines would be visible as seen in ﬁgure. In disturbed laminar ﬂow
the ﬂow still follows straight streamlines but have disruption in certain areas. In areas where
the ﬂow is disturbed the ﬂow behave abnormally and reversed ﬂow can occur. Furthermore
there could be separation in ﬂow, reattachment of forward ﬂow or even recirculation[12].
As the ﬂow rate of the ﬂuid increases, so does the velocity at any given point throughout
time, which means that the ﬂow will change from laminar to turbulent ﬂow, even through
the overall ﬂow is steady. Turbulent ﬂow is categorized with swirls and disturbance. To
categorize whether the ﬂow is laminar or turbulent, Reynolds number (Re) can be calculated.
In low Re values (typically <2000) ﬂow is laminar and high Re values (typically >2000) ﬂow
is turbulent[12].

Shear stress in microﬂuidic device
Shear stress is the stress that results when a tangential force acts on a surface. In microﬂuidic
devices, it is the frictional drag force of the ﬂowing ﬂuid on the cells or tissues cultured on
the device. Shear stress acting on the cells in such devices is dependent on the viscosity of
the ﬂuid (nutrient medium in our case) and the velocity of the ﬂuid[6]. As discussed in the
above sections behaviour of cells and tissues are dependent on the tensile, compressive and
the shear stresses acting on them. Therefore, while designing the microﬂuidic device, special
attention should be paid to the stress experienced by cells due to ﬂuid ﬂow especially the
shear stress as animal cells (due to lack of cell wall) are prone to damage by shear. The
shear experienced by these cells is calculated by the equation[6]:

Criteria for inserts
i. Inserts should be able to ﬁt into a 12-well culture plate.
ii. Inserts should be printed as single, three-coupled, four-coupled or even 12-coupled.

Discussion
7.1
Design and functionality of the gravity driven system
The ﬁnal optimised design of the system gives promising results and open doors for fur-
ther development. The development of this system surely supports the hypothesis of how a
customized milliﬂuidic insert achieved with a SLA printer, along with a simpliﬁed platform
can reﬂect real physiological aspects of the body. The need for alternative in vitro testing
methods have been an interest for many researchers along the globe as mentioned in the
introduction. Mechanically stimulated cell models have showed leading interest. The under-
standing of how cell can sense mechanical force and translate into a biological response, is
becoming more widespread. This is specially important for tissue engineering strategies and
many researchers have shown promising results, like Kim et al.[30] that conﬁrmed the cul-
turing of cells in a bioreactor or Wang et. al. that created unichip-bichip for organ-on-chip
models[56]. It was the idea of perfusing cells with so called channels to achieve a system
that mimics the microenvironment of the body more accurately. The requirements for in
vitro alternatives have caused many to work in micro-scale rather than milli-scale. Firstly,
the requirements include creating a system that fulﬁlls the function of the speciﬁc organ
and thereby creating the microenvironment in vitro, leading to co-cultures in many cases.
Secondly, it is desired that system is robust and repeatable. Thirdly, the scalability of the
system, low consumer cost and ethical issues in order to justify its usage[29]. The scalability
is one reason for creating microﬂuidic devices, since there is wide usage of larger multi-well
plates and microtiter plates in the pharmaceutical industry. Even though researchers like
Wang et al and Shuler et al.[56] seems to regain promising results, this comes with its com-
plications. Manufacturing microﬂuidic devices can be diﬃcult to reproduce and often there
is diﬃculty seeding cells and even issues within the ﬂow can occur, like air bubbles and
blockages in the channel [29]. Though, with these challenges microﬂuidic devices have their
disadvantages. This gravity driven perfusion system has many components that are similar
to the ones found in micro-ﬂuidic devices but focuses on the idea of creating a system that is
easy to use, reproducible, printable with SLA printer, cost friendly, time-saving and without
compromising on micro-environment created for cells. The system is meant to work on a
12-well plate, which is therefor a great tool to use for especially: biologists, pharmacists,
chemists or others in likewise ﬁelds.

7.2
Calculation, simulation and experiment
In this report various methods have been used for the investigation of gravity-driven perfusion
system. Flow rate and pressure are two important parameters to keep in mind, when inducing
ﬂow in the channel of a milliﬂuidic device. The reason for the importance of ﬂow rate is
the perfusion of nutrients and oxygen to the cells but also shear stress level induced on
the cells[29]. Moreover, looking at pressure peaks can help predict if any cell damage is to
be expected [55]. The focus was especially on achieving a ﬂow rate and thereby a shear
stress level that is closest to the one found in blood vessels. Furthermore the ﬂow pattern
was investigated, since the behaviour of the ﬂow also would eﬀect if the system was able to
mimic real physiological aspects. The method of using equations to calculate certain ﬂow
parameters was useful, in terms of choosing insert dimensions and platform tilting angle.
By doing so, it was evident of which of dimensions showed results that were closest to the
physiological. Though, this also came with its limitations. The dimensions chosen, in the

parameter that was not taken into account was the bottom of the channel. In practical, the
channel has a opening at the bottom, in which a hydrogel bottom is placed and cells are
cultured. In the calculation, no kind of turbulence is predicted, since a smooth bottom is
assumed. Another limitation to the calculated parameters were that two parameters were

Conclusion
The main objectives of the thesis was to create a perfusion system to improve the cell
functions. This was achieved by creating a gravity driven, dynamic platform along with a
3D printed milli-ﬂuidic insert. The aims involved designing and developing inserts for the
culturing of cells in a 12-well plate, and the characterization of the eﬀect of shear stress and
ﬂow rate in the gravity driven system and lastly visualize them through ﬂuid ﬂow in the
insert.
he outcome of the results showed great succes in fullﬁlling these aims. The gravity driven
system managed to create a simpliﬁed, cost-eﬀective and time-saving method of in vitro
testing, along with the experimental setup and theoretical calculations, CFD showed results
of the expected ﬂuid behaviour within the channel.

The results from the calculation showed a prediction of the ﬂuid behaviour within the channel
of the milliﬂuidic insert, when placed on a dynamic platform. This gave promising results,
since the shear stress level still remained within the range what is found in blood vessels.
More over the ﬂow pattern was predicted and thereby simulated to actually visualize what
to expect from a dynamic perfusion platform. The simulation showed how the diﬀerent ﬂow
parameters changed in relation to the time, where the insert was tilted. The prediction of
the ﬂuid ﬂow and pattern within the insert was quite accurate to what was expected, though
the only drawback was the limitations of the all the parameters that could be added to the
simulation or calculation to make it more accurate and comparable.
Lastly the experimental setup proved to give very reliable results in terms of the system
actually working as predicted by design, calculation and simulation. The insert designs also
proved itself by creating a ﬂuid ﬂow as desired, along with gelatin membrane. However, even
though the shear stress level in this system reached what was close to the physiological levels
in blood vessels, this system can be used for organs in other parts of the body too.
Thus, many aspects of the results can still be argued, since the culturing of cells is needed
to ﬁnalize its usage in in vitro studies. The system is therefor open to improvements after
this step is performed.
