Abstract
The African power system can be characterised by a lack of access to modern energy services,
a high dependence on fossil fuel based generation and a dependence on foreign investments
mainly coming from multilateral development banks and China. As the electricity demand is
expected to undergo a rapid increase over the next decades it is of importance to enable decision-
makers to make informed long term investment choices. Using Nigeria as a case study, a long-
term investment model of their power system has been developed using linear optimisation
techniques. The model aims to ﬁnd the cost optimal solution of the power system subject
to several operational constraints.
Speciﬁcally, the model determines the optimal installed
capacities and generation levels of a number of generating units in the period from 2020 to
2050. The model develops through three scenarios where each scenario increases the model
complexity. Across all scenarios, the modelling results show that the Nigerian power sector
will maintain its high dependence on electricity generation from natural gas power plants.
Moreover, new capacity additions of utility solar PV are deployed. The massive deployment
of utility solar PV occurs in 2030 and results in a completely renewable electricity mix from
2030 to 2033.
Hereafter, the share of renewables decrease towards 2050 as the subsequent
demand is satisﬁed by a higher deployment of combined-cycle gas turbines in the system.
Additionally, a sensitivity analysis is undertaken where main ﬁndings include the eﬀect from
the cost volatility of natural gas. Future improvements to the model include combating the
inherent data uncertainty, increasing the temporal resolution and thereby allowing for the
introduction of storage technologies and ﬁnally incorporating cross-border interconnections to
analyse the electricity trade between countries.

Resumé
Det afrikanske elsystem er præget af mangel på adgang til moderne energitjenester, en stor
afhængighed af fossilbaseret elproduktion og en afhængighed af udenlandske investeringer, der
hovedsageligt kommer fra multilaterale udviklingsbanker og Kina. Da elektricitetsbehovet for-
ventes at gennemgå en voldsom stigning i løbet af de næste årtier, er det af betydning at sætte
beslutningstagere i stand til at træﬀe informerede langsigtede investeringsvalg. Ved at bruge
Nigeria som et casestudie er en langsigtet investeringsmodel for deres elsystem blevet udviklet
ved brug af lineære optimeringsteknikker. Modellen har til formål at ﬁnde elsystemets omkost-
ningsoptimale løsning givet ﬂere operationelle begrænsninger i systemet. Konkret bestemmer
modellen de optimale installerede kapaciteter og produktionssniveauer for et antal elproduc-
erende teknologier i perioden fra 2020 til 2050. Modellen udvikler sig gennem tre scenarier,
hvor hvert scenarie øger modelkompleksiteten. På tværs af alle scenarier viser modelleringsre-
sultaterne, at den nigerianske elsektor vil fastholde sin store afhængighed af elproduktion fra
naturgas. Desuden viser resultaterne nye kapacitetsudvidelser af storskala solceller. Den mas-
sive udbredelse af solcellerne sker i 2030 og resulterer i et fuldstændigt bæredygtigt elsys-
tem fra 2030 til 2033. Herefter falder andelen af vedvarende energikilder frem mod 2050, da
den efterfølgende stigning i efterspørgsel dækkes af en højere udbredelse af naturgas i sys-
temet. Derudover foretages en følsomhedsanalyse, hvor hovedresultaterne omfatter eﬀekten
fra volatiliteten på naturgaspriserne. Fremtidige forbedringer af modellen omfatter tiltag for
at minimere datausikkerheden, en øget tidsmæssig opløsning som vil muliggøre introduktion
af ellagringsteknologier og endelig en inkorporering af transmissionslinjer der vil muliggøre en
analyse af elhandelen mellem lande.

Introduction
The African power system is characterized by a lack of access to modern energy services, par-
ticularly in Sub-Saharan Africa where around 55% of the population, or 635 million people,
still lack access to electricity [1]. The power supply sector faces two important challenges in
the future which are a) to improve the electricity access rates and b) to cope with the rapidly
increasing demand for electricity. To address these challenges extensive investments are needed
in generation as well as transmission and distribution capacity [2]. However, a large share of the
existing generation on the continent is dominated by fossil fuels. Africa’s current power system
is made up of several stakeholders such as multilateral development banks, China, national
governments and other ﬁnancial institutions. These are are powerful actors with their massive
investments on energy infrastructure projects.
A better understanding of the implications their activities have on the continent’s development
pathways seeks to be investigated. Therefore, some important questions are whether developing
countries with large fossil fuel reserves could avoid investing in power polluting technologies or
if they are being constrained due to the lack of cost-competitiveness from sustainable energy
technologies. Likewise, it is of interest to analyse the degree a technological lock-in of fossil
fuels could make a green transition more costly in the future.
Focusing on the largest economy in Africa by GDP as well as the most populous country on
the African continent, Nigeria, is selected as a case study for developing a long term investment
planning model of their power system. Nigeria has a national electriﬁcation rate of 60% and an
estimated 12.5 GW of installed grid-connected generation capacity which is largely dependent
on natural gas [3]. However, the country experiences challenges in its power sector in order to
ensure the security of supply as well as cost-competitive electricity for the end-consumers [4].
Moreover, Nigeria’s electricity demand is expected to rapidly increase over the coming decades
due to economic and population growth along with more people becoming electriﬁed [2].
The long-term investment model is developed using linear optimisation techniques. It seeks to
ﬁnd the cost optimal system in terms of installed capacities and their associated generation
levels for a number of generating units subject to several operational constraints. Additionally,
multiple scenarios and sensitivity analyses are undertaken to assess the changes in the Nigerian
power system.
In general, the model shall enable decision-makers to make more informed
investment decisions on the long run. However, the long-term investment model is developed
for the Nigerian power sector but can readily be changed in terms of assumptions and data
input and thereby applied to analyse the power system of other countries.

The African Power System
1.1
Current Status of the African Power System
Nearly 80% of the present electricity generation in Africa comes from fossil fuel based tech-
nologies with non-hydro renewable energy accounting for just over 3% [5]. At the same time,
Africa’s electricity demand is set to rapidly increase to industrialise and achieve improved eco-
nomic development for the world’s fastest growing population [6]. The associated growth rate
of carbon emission are already among the highest in the world [6]. Speciﬁcally, Sub-Saharan
Africa (SSA) has abundant primary energy resources to satisfy this demand: recoverable oil
resources will be suﬃcient for the next 100 years, coal for over 400 years and gas for 600 years.
Morever, the region is further endowed with renewable energy sources [7]. Unless, new power
generating capacity shifts towards renewables, Africa is at risk of being locked into a carbon
intensive development. Interestingly, the potential risk of a lock-in is happening despite the
goal of the Paris Agreement which was signed by 196 nations in 2015 to limit global warming
to well below 2 degrees Celsius compared to pre-industrial levels [8].
However, African countries are not a signiﬁcant source of greenhouse gas emissions. For in-
stance, SSA accounted for less than 3.7% of the world’s carbon dioxide emissions in 2014, with
emissions per capita much lower than the world average and remaining stable at 0.85 metric
ton in the past 30 years [1]. Therefore, apart from South Africa which is heavily reliant on coal
power plants, most SSA countries do not have either moral or legal obligations in committing
to cut their carbon emissions under current international climate negotiations and agreement [1].
Moreover, in SSA the economic growth and living conditions are severely constrained by a
lack of power generation, transmission and distribution capacity. Over 635 million people live
without electricity in the region and the SSA power sector is especially in need of greater access
to capital funds, technologies and capacity building [7]. Furthermore, the per capita electricity
consumption of SSA actually decreased from 520 kWh to 484 kWh in the past decade [1] [7].
Even in the areas that do have an acceptable energy supply, the quality of the service is often
low and unreliable, with frequent power outages aﬀecting the performance of the industrial
and service sectors [1]. Therefore, backup generators are a major source of electricity access in
some developing regions, providing approximately 9% of the electricity consumed in SSA [9].
In western Africa, generators account for over 40% of the electricity consumed annually. This
requires considerable quantities of fossil fuel and it is estimated that 20% of the gasoline and
diesel consumed in SSA is burned for electricity generation [9].

1.2
Planned Power Generation Projects in Africa
A recent study from 2021 based on the largest dataset on historic and planned power plants
available for Africa uses a machine learning approach to predict Africa’s electricity mix based
on planned power plants and their chances of success [6]. When applying the model on the
current project pipeline it was found that the share of non-hydro renewables in electricity gen-
eration is likely to remain below 10% in 2030 despite total generation more than doubling [6].
These ﬁndings point to high fossil fuel based technological lock-in risks for Africa unless a rapid
decarbonization occurs leading to large-scale cancellation of the fossil fuel based technologies
in the current pipeline [6].
On the other hand, the International Energy Agency (IEA) data shows that 37% of total ca-
pacity additions between 2010 and 2015 in sub-Saharan Africa come from renewable energy
[7]. However, as additional capacity additions fall under public sector spending from a coun-
try’s national budget, some African countries are reaching external debt limits and may face
diﬃcult ﬁnancial constraints [7]. Overall, project ﬁnancing remains challenging and is tending
to diversify and move progressively away from public lending towards more equity ﬁnancing [7].
According to the IEA, projects in which a Chinese ﬁrm is the main contractor alone accounted
for 30% of the new capacity additions in sub-Saharan Africa between 2010 and 2015 [7]. Of
these projects, 56% are in renewable energy, with the vast majority being in hydro power, but
increasingly also in wind and solar energy [10].
1.3
Main Investors and Motivation
One of the main investor groups in the African power sector are Multilateral Development Banks
(MDB). MDBs are international ﬁnancial institutions chartered by two or more countries for
the purpose of providing ﬁnancial assistance to developing countries to encourage economic
development typically in the form of loans or grants [11]. MDBs have been ﬁnancing power
generation projects in developing countries for more than 50 years, mainly for the development
of coal, gas and large hydro power plants [12]. However, since 1999 and 2000 MDBs have began
to formulate strategies for directing their investment portfolios towards low-carbon technolo-
gies [12]. In a quantitative analysis of ten MDBs investment trends in power generation from
2006 to 2015 it was found that the share of non-hydro renewables in MDB investment has
risen steadily, from a low of 9% in 2007 up to approximately 50% in 2013 and beyond [12].
The MDBs ﬁnancing towards renewable energy technologies has increased signiﬁcantly, both
in absolute terms and relative to fossil fuel based technologies [12]. This development is in line
with the strong decrease in cost especially for solar PV and wind power [13].
Another key investor in Africa’s energy transition is China. Power projects built by Chinese
companies in SSA have signiﬁcantly contributed to the region’s power sector capacity expansion
in recent years [7]. A substantial proportion of Chinese power projects in SSA are aimed at
expanding access to electricity [7]. For instance, from 2010 to 2020, a total of 120 million people
gained access to electricity through the power grid. This has been enabled by grid development
and increasing power generation capacity, of which Chinese contractors are responsible for 30%

Overall, China’s involvement in Africa can roughly be divided into two stages. The ﬁrst stage
is characterised by China’s massive investments in the crude oil sector since the late 1990s,
partly due to their fast-growing domestic demand for energy and natural resources [1]. The
second stage starts in 1993 when China became a net oil importer and was actively seeking to
maintain energy security by diversifying oil imports from diﬀerent regions, including SSA [1].
In fact, over 28% of China’s oil imports in 2010 came from Nigeria, Sudan, and Angola [1].
Close to one out of ﬁve power plants built by Chinese companies aims to supply electricity to
a speciﬁc industry which is mostly in mining [7].
Furthermore, China’s overseas energy activities is closely linked with its domestic market and
policy changes. An increasingly strict coal phasing out policy and rapidly growing renewable
energy sectors within China are pushing Chinese fossil fuel industries to ﬁnd markets abroad

their domestic wind and solar market development or other overseas energy activities like large
hydro or coal power facilities [7].
Over 90% of Chinese-built power projects in the region are contracted by Chinese state-owned
enterprises (SOEs) and Africa is the largest overseas market for some major Chinese energy
infrastructure SOEs. Africa’s industrialisation and economic development is seen by Chinese
stakeholders as important for Chinese exports of manufactured goods in the region [7].
However, a source of unlocked ﬁnance that is far from utilised is the private sector. The pro-

energy sector but the private sector is far from active enough as they are driven by risk and
return considerations [14]. Increasing the role of the private sector stands out among the widely
advocated options for future investments, especially given the low levels of investment by the
African governments [14]. However, a high level of corruption in Africa deters private sector

1.4
Investment Strategies and Financing Schemes
Based on the quantitative analysis of ten MDBs investment trends in power generation from
2006 to 2015 there were hardly any diﬀerences in the used ﬁnancial instruments between tech-
nologies [12]. Approximately, 90% of the ﬁnancing was provided via debt while the remaining
share was distributed between equity and grants and primarily for renewable energy technolo-
gies [12].
The diﬀerences between the technology portfolios of the public- and private-sector branches of
the MDBs are much larger among the regional MDBs [12]. The public-sector branches have
a higher share of commitments allocated to fossil fuel based power generation projects with a
much smaller share assigned to non-hydro renewables. Moreover, targeted concessional ﬁnanc-
ing facilities that subsidise public-sector lending to renewable energy projects may help MDBs
to increase the share of low-carbon lending in the public-sector branches [12].
China’s main investment model is based on preferential loans and export credits provided to
project developers. In addition, direct equity based investments, commercial loans and grants
are also provided [10]. Loans are disbursed directly to the Chinese companies and then repaid
by African governments. The bank usually ﬁnances 85% of the total project cost, while the
remaining 15% is paid directly from the state budget of the African government [7]. The major-
ity of Chinese-built power projects are still ﬁnanced by sovereign loans guaranteed by African
governments. As more Chinese commercial banks and funds enter the market, the development
lending model is trying to progressively switch to more equity ﬁnancing [7].
There is a tendency for Chinese investors and contractors to supply projects on a turnkey basis
delivered as a bundled package comprising a considerable representation of Chinese investors,
engineering companies and technology suppliers [10]. The picture that emerges is thus one
of increasing Chinese market share and a dominant pattern of full package provision [10]. A
possible reason for the development of this Chinese model may be the nature of China’s funding-
support requirements, which stipulate that investors are eligible for export credits only if the
equipment used is manufactured in China [10]. Additionally, since China is the single largest
creditor in many of the SSA countries, a narrative was developed that China is using debt to
gain geopolitical leverage by trapping poor countries in unsustainable loans [1]. Furthermore,
concerns over competition, transparency and sustainability in Chinese project ﬁnancing have
been raised [7].

So far, a brief overview of the current power system in Africa, the prospects and the challenges
that lie ahead in order to ensure a stable, reliable and environmentally friendly power system of
the continent were established. A more practical approach is now undertaken through an anal-
ysis of the Nigerian power sector which is used as case study for a long-term investment model.
This shall enable decision-makers to make informed investment decisions. Assuming that the
necessary funding are secured, what could potentially be the development of Nigeria’s future
energy mix from a cost minimisation perspective subject to several operational constraints?
Are the sustainable energy technologies cost-competitive in Nigeria’s future power system or is
Nigeria potentially at risk of a technological lock-in of fossil fuels in the coming decades? In
other words, which technologies should decision-makers invest in on the long run from a cost
optimisation perspective in the case of Nigeria?
A long-term investment model of the Nigerian power system using linear programming is de-
veloped. The model aims to determine the optimal installed capacities of a number of power
generating units such as to minimise total investment and operation costs subject to several
operational constraints and the restriction that the capacity can be operated to satisfy the
electricity demand [15]. The model only considers two end-use categories corresponding to the
demand from the urban and industrial sectors and thus, the model does not account for the
rural demand. Finally, the model includes three diﬀerent scenarios where each increases the
model complexity.
2.1
The Nigerian Power Sector
The African continent has a vast endowment of fossil fuel reserves and is experiencing a sky-
rocketing population and economic growth. This trend is not expected to slow down in the
coming decades. In particular, Nigeria is not only the largest economy measured by GDP in
Africa, but it is also the most populous country on the continent with an estimated population
size of 206 million people in 2020. However, by 2050 Nigeria’s population is forecasted to reach
400 million people and thus becoming the world’s third-most populated country [16]. This is
despite the Government’s attempt to curb the rapid population growth by e.g. having oﬀered
free contraception over the past 10 years and by taking steps to ﬁnancially discourage people
who are looking to have large families [16].
Nigeria’s overall electricity access rate was 55% in 2019 in comparison to the 2019 SSA aver-

expected to grow from 25 TWh in 2015 to more than 123 TWh in 2030, thus almost a ﬁve-fold
increase in only 15 years [20].
Nigeria exempliﬁes the challenges that the rest of the region faces with a historic dependence
on oil and gas exports leaving the economy vulnerable to a green energy transition that can
lower the price or demand for fossil fuels [4]. In fact, Nigeria is Africa’s largest oil producer
and while oil only accounts for around 16% of the GDP, it accounts for approximately 75% of
the Government’s revenues and 90% of export earnings [19]. In addition, Nigeria has one of the
top-ten natural gas reserves in the world and the largest endowment in Africa. Despite their
huge gas endowment, the production remains low as Nigeria ranks number 22 on the global
production scale as exploration have been limited. However, natural gas is Nigeria’s dominant
source for generating electricity despite that the fact that less than 10% of the production in
2014 was supplied to the domestic sector for electricity generation [18]. The gas infrastructure
plays an important role in enhancing the country’s security of supply. The electricity gen-
eration has however been limited due to issues with inadequate gas infrastructure, economic
disincentives as foreign sales have oﬀered higher natural gas prices than the domestic price as
well as frequent vandalism of existing gas pipeline infrastructure by militant groups [21] [18].
This results in low gas supplies to the thermal power plants, creating gas shortages and thus
forcing the plants to operate at a lower capacity.
In fact, in 2015 thermal gas plants made up 85% of the installed capacity while the remaining
15% came hydroelectric power plants [18]. The majority of the thermal gas plants are open-
cycle gas turbines (OCGT) [18]. Nigeria has 25 on-grid power plants which are concentrated
in southern Nigeria. More speciﬁcally, the total installed capacity amounted to 12,522 MW in
2015 of which 10,592 MW came from 22 thermal gas plants and the remaining 1,930 MW came
from three hydroelectric power plants [18]. The Nigeria Power Baseline Report from 2018 aims
to provide an objective perspective on the current state of the power sector and identify the
key constraints [18]. This is partly done by analysing the performance of the Nigerian on-grid
power system between January to August 2015 [18]. According to the report, maintenance and
repair issues in 21 out of the 25 generating plants reduced the installed capacity by 5,381 MW
to a total of 7,141 MW of available capacity. Furthermore, seven of the power plants operated
at less than 10% of their installed capacity in the same period [18]. Additionally, operational
constraints such as gas, water, frequency, line among other constraints further decreased the

with limited grid coverage and high cost of oﬀ-grid generation options can lead to a suppressed

back-up generators are a signiﬁcant source of air pollution that negatively impacts the health
and environment in local areas along with noise pollution issues [9]. All of these conditions
make it challenging to estimate what the exact electricity consumption in Nigeria would be
were adequate power generation available at reasonable cost.
In essence, the Nigerian power sector is not only in need of additional generation capacity. The
system also needs to be supported upstream by increased gas availability and downstream with
additional transmission and distribution capacity [18].

2.1.1
Stakeholders in the Nigerian Power Sector
Overall, the Nigerian power system is subject to structural challenges in all their key areas from
generation, gas supply, transmission and distribution. Therefore, the Federal Government of
Nigeria (FGN) has made several initiatives in an attempt to combat the poor performance and
reliability of their power system.
Until 2007, FGN was responsible for all policy formulation, regulation, operation and invest-
ment in the power sector with regulation handled by the Federal Ministry of Power (FMP)
and operation handled by the state-owned National Electric Power Authority (NEPA) [19].
In 2007, NEPA was restructured into the Power Holding Company of Nigeria (PHCN) and in
2013, generation and distribution assets were full privatised to improve eﬃciencies and attract
investment into the sector. PHCN was unbundled into 6 state-owned generation companies
(GenCos) and 11 distribution companies (DisCos) and sold to private entities [19]. The re-
sulting distribution companies vary greatly in terms of network size, number of customers and
geographic area [18]. The FGN retained ownership of the national transmission network under
the Transmission Company of Nigeria (TCN), but in 2015 an unbundling into a public inde-
pendent system operator and a private transmission service provider began [19].
Furthermore, the Nigerian Electricity Regulatory Commission (NERC) is the power sector’s
regulator and thereby in charge of the electricity tariﬀs. However, multiple blocks in price
increases have taken place in an attempt to maintain public support of on-grid electricity [4].
NERC is also responsible for handling the necessary licenses to operate transmission based on-
grid generation, embedded generation and oﬀ-grid generation if any of them exceed an installed
capacity of 1 MW. Captive generation implies that electricity is entirely consumed by the gen-
erator itself for which reason no power purchase agreement (PPA) and thus, only a permit from
NERC is required [19]. According to NERC, licenses for on-grid power plants amounted to
19,407 MW in 2014. In comparison, oﬀ-grid licences corresponded to a production capacity of
305 MW, while embedded generation capacity represented only 49 MW [19].
It is clear that the eﬀorts in privatising the power sector have yet to result in a well-functioning
market delivering reliable and aﬀordable power which is highly challenged by weak governance
and institutions [4]. For instance, the biggest challenge in the distribution sector is the level
of distribution losses which includes technical, commercial (energy not billed for due to non-
metering), and collection losses (energy billed but not paid for by the consumers). In 2014,
approximately 46% of energy was lost through technical (12%), commercial (6%), and collec-
tion losses (28%) [18].
Overall, domestic and foreign investment are deterred by a lack of ﬁnancial viability. These
include the perceived high risk investment environment due to security issues, widespread cor-
ruption, a multiple-rate foreign exchange regime, and political instability [4]. Additionally, the
lack of appropriate regulatory frameworks for renewable energy technologies, such as PPAs for
renewable energy suppliers, discourages investment in the Nigerian power system [4]. Finally,
the Government’s inability to enforce the rule of law to ensure bill payment further increases the
lack of public trust in the DisCos which ultimately makes it harder to implement the necessary
reforms needed in the power sector [4].
Despite Nigeria’s vast fossil fuels reserves the country also has an abundance of well-distributed

2.2
Existing Models of the Nigerian Power Sector
Analysing Nigeria’s power sector presents its challenges due to the lack of a comprehensive and
commonly accepted database of power statistics for the country [19]. An analysis of some of
the existing models of the Nigerian power sector is performed in order to establish and ensure
proper data foundations and robust generic assumptions for the investment model in question.
Two diﬀerent models of the Nigerian power system are explored further in the following sections
with regards to the structure, data quality, assumptions, and model results.
2.2.1
SPLAT-W
The International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA), have developed a power sector plan-
ning model for West African countries - the System Planning Test model for Western Africa
(SPLAT-W). This model is built on a database of the West African Power Pool (WAPP) system
and consists of existing generation units, international transmission lines and a range of future
technology options [20]. The ﬁrst version of the SPLAT-W model is used in IRENA’s 2013
West African Power Pool report and takes the 2011/12 WAPP Master Plan as a starting point
to build the system database. SPLAT-W was updated in 2018 with input from national experts

==PAGE:33==

along with updates on the resource availability and the cost of renewable energy technologies
[20].
The SPLAT-W model is developed using the Model for Energy Supply Strategy Alternatives and
their General Environmental Impact (MESSAGE) modelling platform software. MESSAGE is
a dynamic bottom-up, multi-year energy system model applying linear and mixed-integer opti-
misation techniques [20]. The modelling platform was developed at the International Institute
of Applied System Analysis (IIASA) but has been further enhanced by the International Atomic
Energy Agency (IAEA) [20]. The model aims to ﬁnd the least cost objective over the mod-
elling period by ﬁnding technically feasible technology mixes while satisfying various system
requirements and user-deﬁned constraints such as emission limits. The underlying data comes
from publicly available information as well as inputs from a number of national experts [20].
The power systems of each of the continental member states of the ECOWAS are modelled in
the SPLAT-W model and interlinked by transmission lines. Speciﬁcally, these countries are:
Benin, Burkina Faso, Cote d’Ivoire, Gambia, Ghana, Guinea, Guinea-Bissau, Liberia, Mali,
Niger, Nigeria, Senegal, Sierra Leone and Togo. Cabo Verde is considered as a separate entity
in the modelling, as it is not connected to the WAPP regional grid. An important assumption
of the SPLAT-W model is the fact that the electricity trade between the countries are limited
by existing infrastructure and planned transmission projects i.e. additional extensions to the
transmission networks are not considered.
The SPLAT-W model includes three diﬀerent scenarios: a Reference Scenario, a Regional EREP
Target Scenario, and a National Targets Scenario. The Reference Scenario describes renewable
energy deployment in the absence of national or regional targets. The Regional EREP Target
Scenario describes how the Reference Scenario changes with regional renewable targets set by
the ECOWAS Renewable Energy Policy (EREP), while the National Targets Scenario imposes
national renewable targets [20].
The modelling period spans from 2015 to 2030 with yearly simulations and the monetary unit
is the 2015 USD rate along with a real discount rate of 10 %.
The electricity demand is
based on bottom-up projections and is divided into four end-use categories: heavy industry;
commercial and small industry; urban residential and rural residential which allows for a better
representation of the load curve for each of the demands.
Every year is characterised by
sector-speciﬁc load proﬁles for the various seasons and parts of the day. The SPLAT-W model
contains three seasons: presummer (January – April); summer (May – August); and post-
summer (September – December) [20]. In each season the days are characterised by ten blocks
of equal demand including a peak demand block at 8pm. The daily time slice aggregation is
displayed in Figure 2.1 [20]. Thereby, the SPLAT-W model contains a total of 30 model time
slices per year i.e. three seasons each with ten daily time blocks.

The model includes four diﬀerent types of power generation options: existing capacity; projects

cost reduction his assumed for the thermal energy technologies. The generic technologies are

• Diesel/gasoline 1 kW system to supply urban and rural demand.
• Diesel 100 kW system to supply industrial demand.
• Diesel centralised connected to upstream transmission.
• Heavy fuel oil connected to upstream transmission.
• Open-cycle gas turbine (OCGT) connected to upstream transmission.

Moreover, the maximum technical potentials for the renewable energy sources are identiﬁed for

and a more accurate representation of variable renewable generation i.e. the power system
can not only comprise of variable renewable generation due to their natural variability (unless

The main ﬁndings in the Reference scenario of the SPLAT-W include the fact that energy
mix of West Africa is heavily dominated by natural gas in terms of both the installed capacity
and the electricity generation from 2015 to 2030. This is followed by hydro and together the
installed capacity of these technologies roughly triple by 2030. Solar PV and wind account
for roughly 10% of the electricity generation by 2030 due to their cost competitiveness. Over-
all, the grid-connected electricity generation from renewables accounts for approximately 25%.
However, the Regional EREP Target scenario has a target of 31% grid-connected generation
from renewables. This is mainly achieved by substituting a share of the natural gas generation
to biomass and solar PV in Ghana and Côte d’Ivoire [20]. Finally, the National Target scenario
has the highest share of renewables in the regional generation mix of 38%. From 2015 to 2025
the majority of the new capacity additions are natural gas and hydro power, which is well in
line with the fact that natural gas and hydro power together made up 85% of the region’s
planned capacity pipeline in 2015 [20]. However, from 2025 to 2030 the non-hydro renewable
capacity accounts for 66% of the new capacity additions due to their cost competitiveness and
in order to meet the national targets.
The results for Nigeria speciﬁcally show that natural gas accounts for more than 80% of the
electricity generation in 2030 followed by hydro power and net imports from surrounding coun-
tries in both the Reference and the Regional EREP Target scenarios. However, in the National
Target scenario the share of natural gas decreases to around 65% and is substituted by solar
PV followed by a minor share of biomass in 2030 [20]. The national grid-connected renewable
electricity generation target correspond to 20% by 2020 and 31% by 2030 for Nigeria [20].
Since the SPLAT-W model is a long-term model, it has the drawback of not being designed to
assess the full impact of variable renewable energy (VRE) deployment which requires a higher
temporal resolution and improved time slice calibration. This aﬀects the economic impact of
VRE as it cannot fully capture the alignment of VRE supply and variable demand as well as
the time-linked operational constraints of power system such as ﬂexibility [20].
Finally, it can be noted that IRENA’s 2018 edition of the West African Power Pool report
makes up the majority of the data foundation for the investment model in question. This is in
terms of the existing, planned and generic technology options their associated costs and other
technology speciﬁc parameters.
2.2.2
TEMBA
The Electricity Base Model for Africa (TEMBA), is a model of the African electricity supply
that represents each continental African country’s electricity supply system and transmission
links between them [23]. TEMBA is sponsored by The World Bank and the Swedish govern-
ment agency for development cooperation (SIDA). The underlying dataset is developed by the

==PAGE:36==
Chapter 2. Investment Planning Model of the Nigerian Power Sector
Royal Institute of Technology (KTH) division of Energy Systems Analysis in Sweden and is
updated on a regular basis to ensure more precise and country-speciﬁc data across the continent
[23]. A simple dashboard is available online where it is possible to observe the optimisation
results on a country-level or for each of the ﬁve power pools in Africa from 2015 to 2040 [23].
The TEMBA dataset can be downloaded online and was last updated in 2018 [24].
TEMBA is open source and is written in the Open Source energy MOdelling SYStem (OSe-
MOSYS) which is a bottom up, multi-year energy system model applying linear optimisation
techniques used for long-term energy planning. In the past, OSeMOSYS has been used to
investigate climate resilience of power projects in Africa and thus has its functionality tested
in large models. Finally, OSeMYSOS assumes a perfect market with perfect competition and
foresight [2].
TEMBA aims to examine the potential for and relationship between electricity investments
and power trade in Africa. More speciﬁcally, TEMBA investigates to what extent the grid
interconnections aﬀect investments on power generation infrastructure through two scenarios;
the Reference Trade Scenario and Enhanced Trade Scenario. The former scenario is restricted
to existing and committed grid interconnector projects where the latter allows for grid expan-
sion. This is done in order to identify the African countries with the greatest potential of
electricity export as well as those with the largest expected demands for cost-competitive elec-
tricity. An article published in 2016 titled An Indicative Analysis of Investment Opportunities
in the African Electricity Supply Sector — Using TEMBA [2] presents the initial results of
the TEMBA model. Some of the major ﬁndings include the fact that up to 2040 an enhanced
grid network can alter Africa’s generation mix as unexploited renewable energy potential can be
traded and thereby reduce the electricity generation cost [2]. The results show that the majority
of the countries are divided into either net importers or net exporters of electricity. For instance,
South Africa and Egypt are projected to be the largest electricity consumers corresponding to
a total of 45% of the total electricity consumption on the continent by 2040. However, this
results in the fact that some countries such as Sudan then become a transit country as they
are importing electricity from Ethiopia of which the vast majority is further exported to Egypt
[2]. On the online TEMBA dashboard it can be seen that the generation mix in Nigeria from
2015 to 2040 in the Reference trade scenario is dominated by natural gas power plants, solar
thermal, hydro power and net imports. On the other hand, it can be seen that the share of
natural gas is decreasing over time due to higher net imports in the enhanced trade scenario [23].
TEMBA consists of the electricity supply system of 47 African countries which are modelled
individually and linked via transmission links. Thereby, the optimal production mix of tech-
nologies to satisfy an exogenously growing demand is determined. The modelling period spans
from 2010 to 2040 with yearly simulations where every year is represented by 4 characteristic
time periods (2 seasons with day and night distinction). In addition, the modelling period is
extended to 2050 to avoid edge-eﬀects. The demand is divided into three diﬀerent end-use
categories; industrial, urban and rural and TEMBA includes diﬀerent generic centralised and
decentralised generation options of which some are restricted to serve speciﬁc end-use demands
(e.g. rural). A learning curve from IRENA is assumed for the renewable energy technologies
resulting in lower investment costs [25]. Moreover, TEMBA also includes existing and planned
generation capacity based on input from IRENA’s 2013 West African Power Pool report. Fi-
nally, the monetary unit in TEMBA is the 2010 USD rate along with a real discount rate of 5%.

The demand projections, the existing and planned capacity as well as the generic technology
options and their associated costs among other parameters used in TEMBA are largely based
on data from IRENA [2]. In fact, the majority of the generic technology options corresponds
to the ones listed in the description of the SPLAT-W model along with hydro electric dams,
CSP with gas co-ﬁring as well as PV options with 1 hour of storage that can serve either urban
or rural demand. Therefore, the generic technology options in TEMBA correspond to the ones
used in the ﬁrst version of SPLAT-W model from 2013 [25]. Moreover, the country-speciﬁc data
for Nigeria and the diﬀerent technology options in TEMBA are well in line with the data from

Potential improvements of TEMBA include a higher temporal resolution allowing for a more
accurate estimation of the potential for electricity exchanges between countries as the demand
would be represented more accurately. However, this would be at the cost of longer computation
times. Other improvements could be: local or technological speciﬁc constraints; national and
regional emission targets etc.
2.3
Description of Modelling Scenarios
The emphasis and sole focus of the remaining of this thesis is on the development of a new
long-term investment model of the Nigerian power sector. This his done in order to create
a complementary open source framework to enable modellers and decision-makers to perform
analyses of what the future of the Nigerian power system could look like given diﬀerent assump-
tions and input data. Due to limited data availability of Nigeria’s rural demand, the model
only considers Nigeria’s urban and industrial demand. The model is developed through three
scenarios where each scenario increases the model complexity. Compared to the TEMBA and
SPAT-W models, all scenarios developed here are longer-term investment models of the power
system applying linear optimisation techniques and cover the period form 2020 to 2050. The
following sections contain more detailed scenario descriptions.
2.3.1
Base Scenario
The Base scenario only considers new capacity additions installed after 2020 in order to satisfy
the yearly demand increase. As the modelling period starts in the year 2020, it is assumed
that the demand in 2019 is fully satisﬁed by the existing on-grid capacity. The yearly increase
in demand is afterwards calculated for the entire modelling period and this speciﬁc demand
increase will then be satisﬁed by new capacity additions. Therefore, the lifetime of the exist-
ing on-grid capacity is not taken into consideration, despite the fact that most of the existing
capacities will have reached their retirement age during the modelling period (except for two
hydro dams). However, the lifetime of the new capacity additions are taken into account as
some the technologies have shorter lifetimes than the modelling period of 30 years cf. Table 2.1.
In short, the Base scenario aims to analyse which technologies will be deployed to satisfy the
yearly demand increase. This is in the absence of national renewable targets and thus, cost-
competitiveness is the key driver for the deployment of the various technologies in the power
system.

==PAGE:38==

2.3.2
Capacity Extension Scenario
The Capacity Extension scenario builds upon the Base scenario. However, this scenario is an
extension to the investment problem in the Base scenario as the existing and planned capacity
of the Nigerian on-grid power system are included. The investment problem thereby becomes
the so-called capacity extension problem [15].
Unlike the Base scenario, the demand is no longer given by the yearly increase, but instead
by the aggregated yearly demand throughout the modelling period. This is due to the fact
that the existing and planned capacity are included in the model. Hence, the model is able
to determine the generation level from both the existing and planned capacity in the optimal
solution. Moreover, it is assumed that the planned capacity is built at their respective capacity
levels and the necessary funding are secured. Additionally, the lifetimes of the existing and
planned capacity are included in the model such that they can not generate electricity after
they have reached their technical lifetime i.e. retirement age.
In other words, this scenario aims to analyse the generation levels of the existing, planned and
new capacity additions in order to satisfy the annual aggregated demand while minimising the
total production and investment costs throughout the modelling period.
2.3.3
National Target Scenario
The National Target scenario builds upon the Capacity Extension scenario as the underlying as-
sumptions are the same. However, this scenario further explores the environmental performance
of the power system. The scenario imposes a generation level from the deployed grid-connected
renewable energy units such that they in total correspond to at least 31% of the total demand
by 2030. This target is then assumed to continue until the year of 2050. Therefore, it is possible
to assess and compare whether there is a change in the deployed technology mix, generation
levels, emission levels as well as the total system cost with the Base scenario and the Capacity
Extension scenario.
2.4
Model Limitations
The long-term investment planning model developed in this study has some limitations. Firstly,
as the electricity demand is given on a yearly basis the model does not include any storage tech-
nologies (e.g. stand-alone batteries) or technologies with storage options (e.g. solar PV with
1 kW battery) and thus no constraints relating to electricity storage are implemented. The
production proﬁles of the storage options usually require a higher temporal resolution of the
demand (e.g.
hours) such that the main beneﬁt of the storage technologies are taken into
account in the model. Thereby, it will be possible for the storage technologies to e.g. charge
electricity when the demand is low and conversely, discharge when there is a high demand. If
the storage constraints were to be implemented in the model it would create a bias as it then
would take a year to charge and another year to discharge the electricity which does not align
with most storage production proﬁles (e.g. solar PV for urban areas with 1 kW battery). In
addition, the model does not consider demand side management or energy eﬃciency measures

==PAGE:39==

as the electricity demand is exogenously given.
Secondly, limited data availability and a high share of stand-alone generators in Nigeria make
it diﬃcult to perform demand assessments and thereby quantifying the rural demand [26] [9].
Furthermore, the share of minigrids in Nigeria is expected to increase in the coming years to for
instance supply rural locations. Minigrids are stand-alone power generation systems of up to
1 MW capacity that provide electricity to multiple consumers through a distribution network
where storage options might be of high importance to ensure the security of supply for house-
holds and businesses with a continuous need for electricity [26]. One can imagine other rural
locations that are not supplied by minigrids to also depend on storage in order to ensure the se-
curity of supply. Overall, due to the fact that the model does not consider storage technologies,
the rural demand is excluded such that the model only considers the demand from two end-use
categories: urban and industrial, respectively. Furthermore, the existing and planned oﬀ-grid
generation units are not assumed to contribute towards satisfying the demands as there exists
limited information about the installed capacity and associated generation levels. Therefore,
the urban and industrial demand can only be satisﬁed by new capacity additions installed after
2020 along with the existing and planned on-grid capacity.
Thirdly, the model does not consider cross-border electricity interconnections i.e. import and
export of electricity to neighbouring countries. This involves both existing and planned inter-
connections as well as the fact that the model does not determine the capacity of cross-border
interconnections that could be built which is for instance the case in the TEMBA model. Cross-
border electricity connections can help enhance Nigeria’s security of supply as well as lowering
the on-grid electricity prices.
This is due to the merit order principle that determines the
market clearing price of electricity markets where electricity ﬂows from low price areas to high
price areas as a function of the available transmission capacity.
Fourthly, the model does not include the existing or needed capacity extensions to the transmis-
sion and distribution grid. This would require a higher geo-spatial resolution as the transmission
and distribution lines would need to be strategically connected with the generating power plants
to the diﬀerent end-consumers. All of these locations, required transformer stations and the
like remain unknown in this optimisation model for which reason the cost of transmission and
distribution also are excluded from the model. However, the model can give an indication of
the magnitude of the needed capacity extension based on the yearly installed capacity and
generation levels as these are determined such that the electricity demand is satisﬁed at all
times and accounts for the transmission and distribution losses in the system.
In addition, the model does not consider any type of sector-coupling as the generated electricity
could be used to generate heat by the means of heat pumps or generate electricity to decar-
bonise the transportation sector such as electric vehicles. Another example is Power to X where
an electrolyser is used to convert electricity from renewable energy into hydrogen which can be
processed further into e.g. green gas or biofuels and thereby help reducing greenhouse gases [27].
Finally, the model does not account for the construction time of the new capacity additions.
The model therefore assumes that any technology can generate electricity in the same year as it
is chosen by the model. In reality, this would rarely be the case for units with long construction
times such as large hydro dams whereas the construction time for solar PV is low in comparison.

2.5
Model Assumptions
The underlying assumptions of the investment planning model are now described through an
analysis of the used input data as well as discussing possible biases due to limited data avail-
ability. In addition, an entire section is devoted to explore the expected model results for the
Nigerian power sector. This is due to the fact that the technology choices are determined with
the objective of minimising the total system cost subject to satisfying the electricity demand
and other operational constraints such as the reserve margin and maximum technical potentials
of renewable energy sources.
2.5.1
Overall Assumptions
In general, limited data availability of the Nigerian power sector result in modelling diﬃculties
as the model results and reliability are heavily inﬂuenced by the quality of the data input. In
order to ensure that the model is built on a reliable data foundation, a large part of the used
data is derived from the IRENA’s 2013 and 2018 edition of the West African Power Pool report
as well as from the TEMBA database and article. Thereby, a large part of the data input is
already validated by professionals and national experts as it is used in other energy system
models of the Nigerian power sector i.e. they are assumed to be representative. This should
combat any severe biases that could occur if data were collected from many diﬀerent sources
and had not been subject to any model validation. In order to combat the inherent uncertainty
in the data, sensitivity analyses are performed.
The overall assumptions across all scenarios are as follows:
• The monetary unit used throughout is the 2015 USD rate and adjustments to the reported
data in USD from other years are made using the US gross domestic product (GDP)
deﬂator from the World Bank [28].
• The model assumes a perfect market with perfect competition and foresight.
• The modelling period spans from 2020 to 2050.
• The new capacity additions installed after 2020 are modelled without a speciﬁc reference
to any unit size.
• The underlying data such as the capacity factors, availability factors and thermal eﬃcien-
cies are maintained constant throughout the analysis. In addition, the ﬁxed O&M costs
and variable costs are kept constant throughout the analysis. This is considered a reason-
able assumption as both the TEMBA and SPLAT-W models also keep these parameters
constant.
• The lifetime of the new capacity additions, existing capacity and planned capacity are
modelled such that they will stop generating electricity after they have reached their
technical lifetime i.e. retirement age. This is despite the fact that many of the plants
operate beyond their expected lifetime [20]. In addition, the model does not account for
retroﬁtting of any of the technologies.
21
==PAGE:41==

• Finally, the model does not account for the heat that is generated from the combined
heat and power plants (CHP) such as biomass and natural gas combined cycle i.e. it
is assumed that the heat is wasted. In the case of natural gas, it is later seen that the
combined cycle has a higher thermal eﬃciency and lifetime but lower ﬁxed O&M and
variable costs in comparison to the single cycle. From an economic point of view, the
latter has half the overnight investment cost but might still risk being outperformed by
the combined cycle in the long run.
2.5.2
New Generation Capacity
The investment model of the Nigerian power system includes a mix of 15 diﬀerent dispatchable
and non-dispatchable technology options which are taken from the SPLAT-W model [20]. It
is assumed that all the technologies are connected to the transmission and distribution net-
works. This is with the exception of diesel power plants (1 kW, urban) and solar PV (roof
top, urban) that are only assumed to connect to the distribution network, while the diesel
power plants (100 kW, industry) would be located on-site to where the electricity is needed
i.e. it is not connected to either the transmission or distribution network. In the Base sce-
nario the model solely has to ﬁnd the optimal technology mix among these 15 technologies
in order to satisfy the yearly demand increase. However, in the Capacity Extension and the
National Target scenario the existing and planned generation units are included as well and
thus, can become a part of the optimal generation mix in order to satisfy the aggregated de-
mand of the system. To underline the fact that the 15 technologies units physically do not
replace any existing or planned capacity they are in the latter referred to as the new capacity
additions installed after 2020 or the new capacity additions. The new capacity additions are
numbered from 1 to 15 in the model. It shall be noted that the model diﬀers between which
type of demands each of the technologies can satisfy i.e. urban, industrial or both demands.
Speciﬁcally, technology number 4 (diesel power plants (1kW, urban) and number 14 (solar PV
(roof top, urban) can only generate electricity to supply the urban demand whereas technology
number 3 (diesel power plants (100 kW, industry) can only supply the industrial demand. The
remaining technologies are assumed to be able to supply both the urban and industrial demand.
The new capacity additions are modelled as generic technology options i.e. modelled without
a speciﬁc reference to any unit size. The associated plant characteristics used in the model
are based on data from IRENA’s 2018 edition of the West African Power Pool report and are
presented in Table 2.1 [20]. These include the availability factors, lifetimes, thermal eﬃcien-
cies and Nigeria’s country-speciﬁc capacity factors for solar and wind. The capacity factor is
the ratio between what a generation unit is capable of generating at maximum output versus
its actual generation over a given period of time e.g. a year. The dispatchable technologies
are not assigned a capacity factor as they in principle can generate electricity on demand as
long as there is a suﬃcient fuel input. However, the disptachable technologies are subject to
other operational constraints such as longer time periods of maintenance as opposed to the
non-dispatchable technologies such as wind. Therefore, they are assigned an availability factor
which is the ratio between the amount of time that a unit is able to generate electricity over
a given period and the amount of the time in that period. The plant characteristics for CSP
without storage and with gas co-ﬁring are based on IRENA’s 2013 edition of the West African
Power Pool report [25]. It is further assumed that the potential electricity generation from the
CSP with gas co-ﬁring is distributed evenly between the solar radiation and gas co-ﬁring i.e.

2.5.3
Existing Generation Capacity
According to IRENA’s 2018 edition of the West African Power Pool report the total existing
on-grid installed capacity amounted to 12,202 MW in 2015 [20]. The capacity was distributed
between nine generation units consisting of six gas power plants corresponding to a total of
10,302 MW and three hydroelectric dams corresponding to the remaining 1,900 MW. Of the six
gas power plants, four of them are combined cycle while the remaining two are single cycle. The
model assumes that the existing capacity corresponds to that of 2015 and therefore includes
these nine generation unites. They are numbered from 101 to 109 in the model.
The plant characteristics of the existing capacity are presented in Table 2.3 [20]. The stated
lifespans must be taken as indicative as many plants operate beyond their reported ﬁgures. The
literature does not specify whether the plants have been subject to retroﬁtting i.e. extending the
lifetime. The Gas turbine GAS and Steam Turbine Gas corresponding to technology number
102 and 106, respectively, not only have the lowest availability factors but were also expected
to stop operation in 2007 and 2013, respectively [20]. No updated information is available as to

Fuel Availability and Costs
As the model includes thermal power plants such as diesel and natural gas, the associated fuel
availability and fuel cost projections are estimated for Nigeria. The fuel related assumptions
in the model correspond to those stated in IRENA’s 2018 edition of the West African Power
Pool report and thus used in the SPLAT-W model [20].
The report distinguishes between diﬀerent types of fuel availability for each of the fuels, thus
resulting in diﬀerent costs for the same fuel. For instance, it distinguishes between two types
of fuels for biomass; moderately priced biomass and scarce priced biomass, where Nigeria’s is
assumed to be moderately priced i.e. the lowest price of the two. Both heavy fuel oil and diesel
are assumed to be coastal resulting in lower prices as opposed to the inland prices. Finally, it is
assumed that Nigeria uses locally sourced natural gas and coal such that no import would take
place and thus resulting in lower prices. Overall, the assumptions made on the fuel availabilities
in Nigeria leads to the lowest fuel cost possibility for all the diﬀerent fuels. This is also in line
with Nigeria’s earlier mentioned vast fossil fuel endowments.
Likewise, the fuel cost projections used in the model are based on the projections from IRENA’s
West African Power Pool report, which are mainly derived from the IEA New Policies Scenario
in its 2016 World Energy Outlook (2016) [32]. As there is limited literature on fuel cost projec-
tions after the year of 2030 for West Africa and Nigeria in particular, the fuel costs are assumed
to be constant from the year of 2030 to 2050. However, as the fuel prices are critical deter-
minants of the future technology choices in an investment planning model a price sensitivity
analysis is performed.
The fuel availabilites and cost projections used in the model are presented in Table 2.7 and
depicted in Figure 2.3. It can be seen that diesel and heavy fuel oil are not only the most
expensive fuels, but they also experience the steepest price increase in the modelling period.
On the other hand, biomass is the cheapest fuel and is assumed to remain constant over the
entire modelling period. There his no assigned fuel cost for the hydro power, solar and wind
technologies. However, as the potential electricity generation from CSP with gas co-ﬁring is
assumed to come from 50% gas and 50% solar radiation, it is assigned a fuel cost equal to half
of the fuel cost of natural gas throughout the modelling period.

Reserve Margin
The model includes excess operational capacity that needs to be installed in order to ensure
power system reliability during peak demands caused by daily and seasonal variations. This is
32
==PAGE:52==
Chapter 2. Investment Planning Model of the Nigerian Power Sector
referred to as the reserve margin and is deﬁned as the diﬀerence between the operable capacity
and the peak demand for a particular year as a percentage of the peak demand [20]. Moreover,
the reserve margin aims to create a more accurate representation of the variable renewable
energy technologies from the new capacity additions such as wind and solar as their load pro-
duction proﬁles are not implemented in the model as the demand is given on a yearly basis.
However, it is clear that there already exists a large reserve margin in the Nigerian on-grid
power system as the operational capacity is less than a third of the installed capacity due to
operational constraints such as gas availability. The reserve margin is set to 10% in all the
scenarios which also aligns with value used in the SPLAT-W model [20].
In relation to the constraint enforcing the reserve margin each technology is assigned a so-called
capacity credit. Dispatchable technologies such as thermal power plants that are guaranteed to
be available to meet demand receive a capacity credit of 1, whereas the capacity credit for the
non-dispatchable technologies is treated more conservatively. It is assumed that the reservoir
levels in the hydroelectric dams will allow for electricity production on demand for which rea-
son they receive a capacity credit of 1. The solar PV plants and run-oﬀriver hydro plants are
given a 5 % capacity credit while wind capacity is not considered to contribute to the reserve
margin cf. the SPLAT-W model [20]. Concentrated solar power with gas co-ﬁring is assigned
a capacity credit of 30% which aligns with the value used in the 2013 SPLAT-W model [25].
Furthermore, the constraint also involves the peak demand which refers to the highest electricity
demand that occurs over a speciﬁed time period. The peak demand is usually characterised
as annual, daily or seasonal and has the unit of power. As the model is based on a yearly
demand, the annual peak demand is estimated on the basis of historical data from 2017 and
2018 obtained from the Nigerian National Bureau of Statistics [33]. The data corresponds to
the daily electricity generation from the on-grid connected units [33].
The data is used to
assess the peak demand ﬂuctuations from the average demand on both a monthly, quarterly
and annual basis for the entire year of 2017 and 2018. Despite high ﬂuctuations of the total
generated electricity, the annual peak demand is found to be greater than the annual average
demand with a factor of 1.2 in both 2017 and 2018. This peak demand factor of 1.2 is used
in the reserve margin constraint and assumed to remain constant throughout the modelling
period.

2.6
Mathematical Model Formulation
The following sections aim to present the associated mathematical formulation of the Nige-
rian power sector as an optimisation model by using operations research and applying linear
programming techniques. In general, energy systems can be evaluated using diﬀerent criteria
such as long-term security of supply, economic eﬃciency and environmental impact for which
reason three diﬀerent scenarios are developed. The associated linear programming formulation
for each of the scenarios are presented given the stated assumptions. The three scenarios can
be summarised as follows:
• Base Scenario: New capacity additions after 2020 to satisfy the Nigerian urban and
industrial annual demand increase from 2020 to 2050.
• Capacity Extension Scenario: New capacity additions after 2020 along with the ex-
isting and planned on-grid capacity to satisfy Nigeria’s aggregated annual urban and
industrial demand from 2020 to 2050.
• National Target Scenario: This scenario builds upon the Capacity Extension scenario.
It imposes a generation level from the deployed grid-connected renewable energy units
such that they in total correspond to at least 31% of the total demand by 2030. The
target is assumed to continue until the year of 2050.
The objective across all scenarios is to minimise the total production and investment costs over
a long-term planning horizon subject to the operational constraints that ensure production com-
plies with the capacity restrictions and satisﬁes the electricity demand, respectively. Thereby,
it is possible to determine the optimal installed capacities and corresponding generation levels
of the new capacity additions in all the scenarios as well as the generation levels of the existing
and planned capacities in the Capacity Extension scenario and the National Target scenario.
It should be clear that cost-competitiveness is the main key driver for the deployment of the
various technologies on the long run while satisfying the operational and demand constraints.
2.6.1
Base Scenario
The Base scenario only consists of new capacity additions installed after 2020 to satisfy the
yearly demand increase. The full mathematical formulation of the Base scenario is stated in

Results
The mathematical model for each of the three scenarios is built using the General Algebraic
Modelling System, abbreviated GAMS. The optimisation results obtained from GAMS are then
exported to the MATrix LABoratory, abbreviated MATLAB, for further analysis and graphic
representations. The following sections aim to summarise the main ﬁndings of each scenario
and a comparison of the results is performed at the end of the ﬁnal section.
3.1
Base Scenario
Overall, the optimal solution for the Base scenario results in a high dependence on combined
cycle natural gas (CCGT) plants as well as utility solar PV of which the latter are deployed
on a massive scale in 2030. In fact, the total installed capacity in 2050 corresponds to 126
GW of which CCGT and utility solar PV in the modelling period amounts to 44.6 GW and
48.2 GW, respectively. Overall, the results are well in line with the ﬁndings and expectations
with regards to the LCOE results from Table 2.12 in Section 2.5.11 (Levelised Cost of Elec-
tricity). Furthermore, the total system cost from 2020 to 2050 i.e. the value of the objective
function, amounts to 448 billion USD (2015) where the majority of the costs are coming from
the investments and fuel related costs. The corresponding emissions amount to a total of 1.28

3.2.1
National Target Scenario
The National Target scenario results in the exact same total system cost i.e. objective value
function as well as the same total CO2 emissions from the Capacity Extension scenario. Ad-
ditionally, all key parameters between the two scenarios are compared and lead to identical
results. Therefore, the National Target scenario collapses into the Capacity Extension scenario
as the constraint separating the two scenarios from one another are satisﬁed throughout the
modelling period. Speciﬁcally, this concerns the constraint relating to the generation level from
the deployed grid-connected renewable energy units such that they in total correspond to at
least 31% of the total demand in 2030 to 2050. In short, the ﬁgures and values for the National
Target scenario correspond to those belonging to the Capacity Extension scenario.

in the National Target scenario and diﬀers the two scenarios from one another. Over the entire
modelling period the average share of renewables in the Nigerian electricity mix correspond to
68.6% and 55.4% for the Base scenario and the Capacity Extension scenario, respectively. Ad-
ditionally, Table 3.1 shows the share of renewables in the technology mix i.e. installed capacity,
in 2020, 2030, 2040 and 2050. Similar conclusions to the share of renewables in the electricity
mix can be drawn.
In summary, the modelling results show that the Nigerian power sector will maintain its high
dependence on natural gas across all scenarios. In addition, the results show that the estimated
technical potential for hydro dams are fully exploited throughout the modelling period. Across
all scenarios, new capacity additions in the form of solar PVs are deployed and make up the
largest share of the installed capacity in 2050. However, due to its associated capacity factor
of 19.2% it does not make up the majority of the generated electricity in 2050 which is highly
dominated by the CCGT plants. These ﬁndings are well in line with the expectations given
the calculated LCOE for the new capacity additions and the planned capacity in Table 2.12 in
Section 2.5.11 (Levelised Cost of Electricity). Moreover, the model favours building new CCGT
plants in comparison to utilising the existing and planned OCGT and CCGT plants due to
their higher operational costs. As a result of the massive deployment of solar PVs in 2030 the
Nigerian electricity is exclusively characterised by renewable generation in the subsequent years
until additional CCGT is deployed to account for the further demand increase towards 2050.

Sensitivity Analysis
A sensitivity analysis is undertaken on the basis of the National Target scenario as it is the
most advanced scenario that can describe the future Nigerian power system. Furthermore, it is
of interest to investigate how the results change given diﬀerent price and emission sensitivities
such that the target of at least 31% of the annual generation must come from grid-connected
renewables in 2030 to 2050 is still satisﬁed.
The sensitivity analysis consists of varying the following parameters in four separate simulations.
• Natural gas prices are among the most volatile commodity prices and are aﬀected several
factors such as seasonality, supply shocks and demand shocks [36]. As the modelling
results show a high penetration of natural gas its associated fuel cost is changed in two
separate model runs corresponding to +50% and -50%, respectively in order to reﬂect the
price volatility.
• As the results also show a high penetration of solar PV the learning curve for the overnight
investment cost of utility solar PV (technology number 13) and urban rooftop solar PV
(technology number 14) is assumed to occur over a period of 15 years instead of 10 years
i.e. from 2020 to 2035 instead of 2020 to 2030. This is done to reﬂect delays in realising
the expected cost reductions of solar PV [13].
• In order to quantify the cost of a potential lock-in of fossil fuels the existing and planned
capacities are assumed to operate at maximum operational capacity during their entire
lifetime. Currently, the model can determine their generation levels in order to ﬁnd the
minimal the total cost of the system. Therefore, the associated generation constraints for
the existing and planned capacity cf. Equation (2.26) and Equation (2.27) are changed
to strictly equal instead of less or equal.
When the fuel cost for natural gas is increased by 50% it results in a great substitution of the
deployed CCGT plants in the National Target scenario with coal power plants. In fact, the
installed capacity for coal power plants amount to 30.75 GW in 2050 accompanied by only 14.5
GW of new CCGTs capacity additions. Furthermore, the optimal solution introduces run-oﬀ-
river hydro from 2021 with a continuous electricity generation until 2050. As the cost of natural
gas increases it does not come as a surprise that the existing and planned natural gas plants are
not utilised to their fullest extent. Furthermore, utility solar PV remains in the system and is
deployed at a massive scale in 2030 resulting in an completely emission free system from 2030 to
2035. In comparison to the National Target scenario, the total system cost increases with 7%,
while the total CO2 emissions increase with 56% cf. Figure 3.20. The corresponding change in
the share of renewables in the electricity mix and technology mix can be seen in Figure 3.21
and Figure 3.22, respectively. The share of renewables in the electricity mix increase with 17%
and 15% points in 2040 and 2050, respectively cf. Figure 3.21 .

Discussion
In general, the developed long-term investment model can serve as a complementary open
source framework to enable modellers and decision-makers to perform analyses of what the
future of the Nigerian power system could look like given diﬀerent assumptions and input data.
Model extensions can fairly easily be implemented to enhance the scope of the model to com-
bat the current model limitations. Likewise, the input data and underlying data assumptions
can be changed and thereby the model can be applied to analyse long-term investment strate-
gies of the future power system in other countries. On a broader context the models of the
individual countries can then be coupled in order to analyse the power system for larger regions.
Overall, the modelling results are well in line with the ﬁndings for Nigeria in the SPLAT-W
and TEMBA models. All models show a high continued inﬂuence from natural gas where it
accounts for the majority of the electricity generation. Similarly, solar PV is deployed in both
the SPLAT-W and TEMBA models and hydro dams are installed to their maximum possible
extent. Again, these ﬁndings align well with the modelling results of the developed investment
model of the Nigerian power sector. This can be explained as the developed model is highly
inﬂuenced by input data and assumptions made in both the SPLAT-W and TEMBA models.
However, there are also some important diﬀerences to note between the models. For instance,
as the SPLAT-W and TEMBA model include interconnections with the neighbouring coun-
tries the share of natural gas is expected to decrease due to a higher net import of electricity.
Furthermore, the modelling horizon of the developed model spans to 2050 in comparison to
the SPLAT-W and TEMBA models corresponding to 2030 and 2040, respectively. As a conse-
quence the uncertainty of the results therefore increase.
The developed model assumes a perfect market with perfect competition and foresight for which
reason the results should only be considered as an indicative future pathway for the Nigerian
power sector. High uncertainty about Nigeria’s future power system exist related to quanti-
fying the rapidly increasing demand as well as operational challenges for the existing ﬂeet of
generation units along with transmission and distribution constraints. Additionally, as it is a
cost minimisation model a key driver is the cost-competitiveness of the diﬀerent technologies
subject to operational constraints. Therefore, the uncertainties related to associated costs and
plant characteristics for the technologies must be kept in mind. Moreover, across all scenarios
a massive deployment of solar PV takes place in 2030 and the model does not include any limit
in terms of what is technically feasible to commission in a year. In other words, the model
does not consider the feasibility of actually deploying the suggested installed capacities in the
modelling horizon which in reality are subject to diﬀerent construction timelines. However,
solar PVs are fairly easy to install and can be realised in a short timeline as opposed to a hydro
dam.
65
==PAGE:85==

Additionally, the validity of the modelling results are also subject to the overall model limita-
tions. These include the lack of cross-border interconnections which can decrease the system
cost, emissions and installed capacity as it would allow Nigeria to engage in electricity trade
with neighbouring countries. This limitation constrains the model as the import of electricity is
found to be of high importance in both the SPLAT-W and TEMBA models over the long run.
However, to combat the limitation it requires to include the power systems of the neighbouring
countries into the model. Furthermore, the developed model does not consider the existing
or needed capacity extensions to the transmission and distribution networks. Based on the
deployment of the grid-connected new capacity additions the model can give an indication on
the magnitude of the needed capacity extensions in the transmission and distribution grid. A
total of 149 GW of new capacity additions are installed in 2050 in the National Target scenario
of which 145 GW are grid-connected. In 2015, Nigeria’s transmission grid only corresponded
to 5.3 GW which is already 42% lower than the installed capacity in 2015. The pathway sug-
gested by the developed model therefore relies on an enormous extension of the transmission
and distribution networks towards 2050.
Moreover, as the model does not include storage technologies in combination with the variable
renewable technologies, the optimal solution is restricted aﬀecting the overall system cost, de-
ployed capacity, generation levels and total system emissions. For instance, utility solar PV is
deployed on a massive scale across all the scenarios. If the model included an associated storage
option to solar PV its corresponding installed capacity and generation level could change as
the ﬂexibility in terms of the electricity dispatch increase. However, adding the storage option
to solar PV could change its cost-competitiveness versus the other technologies in the system
as the costs would increase due to the added storage option. It would then be of interest to
analyse the trade-oﬀbetween the increase in cost versus its added ﬂexibility of dispatch in the
system. For instance, adding a battery of 2 kW to the urban rooftop solar PVs only increases
the expected LCOE for West Africa with 6 USD/MWh to a total of 132 USD/MWh in 2030 [20].
On the other hand, the expected LCOE of CCGT in West Africa amounts to 81 USD/MWh in
2030 [20].
To fully analyse this trade-oﬀanother model limitation must be accommodated namely, the
electricity demand which is given on a yearly basis. Increasing the temporal resolution of the
model would allow for the introduction of storage technologies as well as a replacement of the
capacity factors for the renewable technologies with a load production proﬁle to represent their
natural variability. However, it must come as a trade-oﬀas including the hourly demand in each
year from 2020 to 2050 will heavily increase the computation time of the model. A reasonable
assumption could be to divide each modelling year in to e.g. seasons to reﬂect the seasonality
of some of the renewables. Further, the seasons can be divided in to time blocks representing
the hours of the day e.g. morning, midday, evening and nighttime such that the corresponding
load production proﬁles come to justice.
Based on the same reasoning an increase in the temporal resolution would allow for the incor-
poration of the rural demand in the model to reﬂect Nigeria’s entire power system. Speciﬁcally,
the model can also favour a number of renewable technologies as well as storage options to
supply the rural demand while ensuring the security of supply given the natural variability of
renewable sources. This is of particular importance in rural areas that are not connected to a
66
==PAGE:86==

distribution network.
Finally, as the modelling results show a low utilisation of the existing and planned natural gas
plants the reality will most likely turn out diﬀerently. The sole reason for the model to disfavour
these natural gas plants is due to their higher costs and lower availability factors in comparison
to deploying new capacity additions. Nigeria already faces diﬃculties in attracting domestic
and foreign investments in their power sector despite the Government’s recent privatisation [4].
Therefore, it seems unlikely for them to become stranded assets meaning that the performed
sensitivity that forces the existing and planned capacities to operate at their maximum oper-
ational capacity load throughout their lifetime might be the best representation of the future
Nigerian power sector. However, this implies a technological lock-in of the existing and planned
capacities which amounts to a diﬀerence of additional 91 billion USD (2015) in comparison to
the National Target scenario over the entire modelling period.
The developed long-term investment model can be subject to further model improvements not
already covered in the previous sections. Most importantly, there exists a great deal of uncer-
tainty related to the input data and the future demand and price projections towards 2050.
This can be accommodated by engaging in conversations with the diﬀerent stakeholders to
ensure the best data foundation for the model and regular updates to the model. Secondly,
carbon capture and storage technologies could be included to analyse the realisation of more
aggressive emission targets cf. the Paris Agreement. Carbon capture and storage was excluded
from the developed model as they are still an early stage of development and thus, not deemed
cost-competitive in developing countries where ﬁnancial resources are scarce. However, this pic-
ture could change drastically towards 2050. Thirdly, the model could include subsidy schemes
or emission taxes in order to promote and direct investments towards renewables in the Nige-
rian power sector. In addition, the model can also include diﬀerent ﬁnancing schemes for the
technologies. Finally, the investment model could be changed into a stochastic optimisation
model. This will provide insights on the sensitivity of some of the most uncertain parameters
such as the demand along with the investments and fuel costs e.g. through box uncertainty.
In summary, based on the modelling results from the developed long-term investment model of
the Nigerian power sector along with the results from SPLAT-W and TEMBA models Nigeria
is expected to maintain its high share and inﬂuenced from natural gas. The Nigerian power
sector is already severely challenged from an operational, ﬁnancial and political perspective
and one can imagine that these will can be ampliﬁed if the country decides to follow advanced
economies in decarbonising their energy systems [4]. Based on the modelling results, additional
ﬁnancial support as well as regulatory frameworks are needed in order to redirect investment
towards renewables.

Conclusion
Africa faces great challenges in terms of their power sector and especially in SSA where around
55% of the population to not have access to the electricity [1]. Furthermore, nearly 80% of the
electricity generation comes from fossil fuels and the region has the highest growth rate of car-
bon emissions in the world [5] [6]. Nonetheless, SSA accounted for less than 3.7% of the world’s
CO2 emissions in 2014 [1]. However, as Africa’s electricity demand is set to rapidly increase
there is a risk of a technological lock-in of fossil fuel based generation unless new capacity addi-
tions shift towards renewables. Due to a lack of ﬁnancial resources in many African countries,
the continent is heavily relying on foreign investments from mainly MDBs and China. It is
of importance that decision-makers and are given the proper tools and information to make
informed investment decisions to respond to the rapid demand increase in Africa’s power system.
In particular, the future electricity demand in Nigeria shows that it is expected to almost grow
ﬁve-fold from 2015 to 2030 [20]. Currently, the Nigerian power sector is dominated by natural
gas which constituted 85% of the total installed capacity in 2015 with the remaining coming
from hydroelectric dams. However, only 25% of the installed capacity was distributed to end-
consumers in the ﬁrst half of 2015 [18]. The sector is severely under-performing and is in the
need of additional generation capacity, upstream support with increased gas availability along
with downstream support with expansions in the transmission and distribution networks [18].
Despite the Government’s recent privatisation of the sector, it still experiences high distribution
losses which further discourage domestic and foreign investments [4]. Overall, Nigeria serves
as an interesting case study as their power sector will undergo a huge transformation in the
coming decades with a potential risk of a technological lock-in of fossil fuels if the historical
pattern of natural gas continues.
Therefore, a long-term investment model of the Nigerian power system has been developed
using linear optimisation techniques. The model aims to ﬁnd the cost optimal solution subject
to several operational constraints to enable decision-makers to make more informed investment
choices on the long run. Speciﬁcally, the model determines the optimal installed capacities and
generation levels of a number of generating units in the period from 2020 to 2050. The model
considers the electricity demand from the urban and industrial sectors and develops through
three scenarios where each scenario increases the model complexity. The Base scenario only
considers new capacity additions to supply the yearly demand increase whereas the existing and
planned grid-connected capacities are incorporated in the Capacity Extension scenario and the
National Target scenario to supply the aggregated yearly demand. The latter scenario imposes
Nigeria’s own target of 31% generation from on-grid renewable sources by 2030.
Across all scenarios, the modelling results show that the Nigerian power sector will maintain
its high dependence on electricity generation from natural gas power plants. Moreover, new

Further Work
The following directions are proposed for future research which is beyond the scope of this
thesis. The ﬁrst step would be to accommodate the data uncertainties as well as the model
limitations by increasing the temporal resolution and introducing the cross-border interconnec-
tions as described in Section 4 (Discussion).
Furthermore, there is no guarantee that the results from the long-term investment planning
model would actually be able to match the demand and supply in real-time [15]. This is espe-
cially important in the presence of a high penetration of intermittent renewable technologies.
The main purpose of performing an investment planning model is for long term planning such
that the installed capacities of the diﬀerent energy technologies can be determined.
Therefore, it would be interesting to use the results from the developed investment planning
model in an economic dispatch problem or a unit commitment problem which deals with short
term planning. More precisely, this means that the temporal resolution of the model increases
as the the modeller e.g. would simulate every hour in a given time period. This would enable
to investigate the issues with the intermittent technologies as well as the importance of energy
storage in the Nigerian power system. The economic dispatch problem assumes that all gen-
eration units in the power system are online and ready to produce energy if needed. Thereby,
the modeller would get the most cost eﬀective operation of the power system. However, the
unit commitment problem is an extension to the economic dispatch as it takes operational con-
straints of all the technologies into account such as ramping up and ramping down times. It is
therefore possible for the units to change status (online vs. oﬄine) [15]. Thereby, it does not
assume that all technologies are online at all times and will give the most realistic cost eﬀective
operation as it includes start-up costs and ﬁxed costs of being online (e.g. a lower part load
eﬃciency) [15]. Note that the unit commitment problem is thus an integer program whereas
the two other can be modelled as linear programs. In short, the economic dispatch and unit
commitment problems would be able to assess the importance of energy storage and system
ﬂexibility.
Finally, a multi-objective optimisation could be undertaken to analyse trade-oﬀs between diﬀer-
ent objectives such as economic costs, job creation and distribution as well as pollution control.
These insights could be investigated by e.g. coupling the energy system models with economic
impact models [4].
