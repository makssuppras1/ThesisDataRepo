Abstract
English
Dissolution Dynamic Nuclear Polarization (dDNP) is a hyperpolarization technique which signiﬁcantly
enhances the signal of Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR). This thesis seeks to explore the quantitativeness
of dDNP NMR data and whether a statistical model based on this data can distinguish between different
cancerous cell strains.
For in vivo experiments cell cultures where grown and incubated with 13C-enriched glucose and extracted
metabolites were hyperpolarized and NMR spectra were obtained. Integral values were measured in mNova
and Principal Components-Discrimination Function Analysis (PC-DFA) on the data were performed in
python. With this a classiﬁcation model of ﬁve cell lines were made and evaluated.
Dansk

NMR data er kvantitativt og om en statistisk model baseret på det kan adskille forskelige kræftcellelinjer.
I in vivo eksperimenter blev cellekulturer avlet og inkuberet med 13C beriget glukose og udtrukne
metaboliter fra disse blev hyperpolariseret og NMR spektra blev frembragt. I mNova måltes integralværdier
og Principal Components-Discrimination Function Analysis (PC-DFA) på data blev udført i python. Herfra
blev en klassiﬁkations model af fem cellelinjer evalueret.

processing involves selecting an algorithm for multivariable statistical analysis, implementing this in the
Python platform, and evaluating data.

CHAPTER 1
Theory
This chapter will go through a brief introduction to NMR, the hyperpolarization technique
dDNP and the reading of obtained data from NMR, followed by a view on prostate cancer from
a metabolic perspective and the relevance of NMR here and ending with a describtion of the
statistical methods to be used on the data.
1.1
Nuclear magnetic resonance
Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) was discovered in 1945 and NMR spectroscopy has since become an
important and versatile tool in metabolomics for identifying and quantifying known metabolites in complex
mixtures. [Fan and Lane (2016)]
NMR exploits the spin of the atom’s positive charged nucleus and measures the change in energy when
the spin "ﬂips" between energy states. When an atom with spin 1/2 is placed in a strong magnetic ﬁeld, only
two energy states will be possible: A low energy state, where the spin is aligned along the magnetic ﬁeld,
and a high energy state where the spin is oriented in exactly the opposite direction. The difference between
the two states will be
∆E = γ¯hB0
where ¯h is the reduced Planck constant (6.58 × 10−16 electronvolt seconds ), γ is the gyromagnetic ratio - a
constant for a particular type of nucleus, and B0 is the strength of the magnetic ﬁeld. When a radiofrequency
pulse with an energy equal to ∆E is applied to the system, ít will resonate between the two energy states.
After the pulse is turned off a free induction decay (FID) can be meassured as the induced voltage, as the
system returns to equilibrium. From this relaxation to the equilibrium comes T1 the spin-lattice relaxation
time, the time lenght for 63% of the nuclei to have returned to the equilibrium. T1 depends on the nucleus’
environment, so different nuclei in the same molecule can have different relaxation time and the time can
vary with the composition of the sample. Similarly T2, the spin-spin relaxation time, describes how fast the
absorbed energy gets redistributed among nuclei within the system. T2 can be related to the line width of the
signal. [James (1998)]
Not all nuclei have a spin. The most abundant isotope of carbon - 12C - for instance have no spin, and
thus cannot be detected with NMR. But its other stable isotope, 13C have spin, I = 1/2, and is very useful for
biological NMR studies. The only trouble is that natural carbon only consist of 1.1% 13C and synthetically
isotope labelled molecules are expensive. [Eicher et al. (2012)]

For C-13, as this is the focus of this thesis, γ = 6.7·107 radians per Tesla per second. So as the radiofrequency
pulse is applied, only about 16 out of two million nuclei can be detected in the FID, the others will simply
cancel each other out, as they jump from the higher to the lower state and vice versa. [James (1998)]
This is the reason NMR is considered a low sensitivity tool. If natural abundance carbon were used it
would be even worse, as only a fraction of the nuclei would produce signal. The brute-force solutions are to
use enriched carbon sources, with one or more C nuclei in the molecule replaced with C-13; to use a stronger
magnet; and to lower the temperature in the NMR chamber signiﬁcantly. The latter is of course not possible
if one wish to be able to do real time NMR experiments, where cells have to be able to live and metabolize
substrates in the chamber.
1.2
Hyperpolarization
A range of different methods have been developed to increase the signal-to-noise ratio to way above that
of room-temperature NMR, these methods are collectively known as hyperpolarization. [Lee et al. (2014)]
Of those, dynamic nuclear polarization (DNP) is the one that enables the study of the broadest range of
metabolic substrates. [Dumez et al. (2015)] Hyperpolarization has been proven enhance the signal-to-noise
ratio of NMR as much as 44.000 times. [Ardenkjær-Larsen et al. (2003)]
In the DNP polarizer, the compound is mixed with an electron paramagnetic agent, placed in a strong
magnetic ﬁeld at temperatures as low as possible and irradiated with microwaves, by which the spin
polarization from the free electrons is transferred to the nuclei in the sample. [Nelson et al. (2013)] As seen
in ﬁgure 1.1 electron spin is naturally much more polarized at low temperatures and in a strong ﬁeld, and
can therefore successfully build up to be transferred. This means that the same electron can transfer spin to
multiple nuclei, as its spin will quickly regain polarization, which is good as a high concentration of free
electrons in the sample will shorten the T1 of the nuclei due to its paramagnetic effects. The low temperatures
required for the high spin polarization is achieved with liquid helium. The more homogeneously the free
electrons are distributed in the sample, the more effective the spin transfer will be. Therefore a glass forming
solution is added, to prevent crystallization. [Ardenkjær-Larsen et al. (2003)]
The process for dissolution DNP (dDNP) is the same until the transfer of sample from the polarizer to
the NMR magnet. Here the dDNP sample is rapidly dissolved by use of a hot buffer liquid, so it will be in
liquid form when the NMR is performed. [Jähnig et al. (2016)]

As mentioned in 1.1 T1 is dependent of the environment of the nucleus. This provides a problem for
the quantitativeness of dDNP NMR: If we get a larger signal from one metabolite compared to another,
is it because there is a larger amount of the ﬁrst metabolite in the sample, or simply because the second
has lost its polarization in the time it took to transfer the sample from the polarizer to the magnet? This
possible limitation is explored in the article in Appendix B. Here it is found that the measured signals are
reproducible and recovery coefﬁcients are determined, which can be used to calculate amounts of produced
metabolites.
1.2.2
Hyperpolerization of metabolites
Traditionally dDNP experiments have been performed by hyperpolarizing a substrate (like lactate or
pyruvate) and then injecting it into an living organism were the metabolism is then monitored with NMR.
[Dumez et al. (2015)] This limits the possible time scope of the experiment as the relaxation time T1 is on
the scale of one minute. [Karlsson et al. (2012)] The actual metabolic process that can be measured has
to happen in what little time is left after the substrate is transported from the polarizer to the subject, the
injection, and - if relevant - the internal transportation to the organ of metabolism. Therefore metabolism
over longer time cannot be quantiﬁed or compared with this method.
A way around this challenge is to hyperpolarize the metabolites after they have been removed from the
organism. This has made it possible for this project to look at metabolites from cells that have incubated with
labelled glucose for 30 minutes. The down sides with this method however are that it is no longer possible to
look at real time build up of metabolites; the metabolism can only be viewed in discrete time steps; and in
the transfer between the polarizer and the NMR magnet, where the magnetic ﬁeld is unknown and shifting in
space, the T1 of the metabolites can change differently, affecting the quantiﬁcation of the ﬁnal signal. A
proposed solution to the latter challenge is a magnetic tunnel for transfer between the two machines. [Jähnig
et al. (2016)]

1.3.1
Spin-spin coupling
If a nucleus interacts closely with other nuclei with spin, it can result in spin-spin coupling and splitting
of the peaks in the spectrum. [James (1998)] This is why we do not see seven neat peaks for the glucose in
1.3, but instead a lot of signal jumbled together in the 60 to 105ppm range of the spectrum.
The number of multiplets the peak will split into is given by:
2nI + 1
Where n is the number of other spin-nuclei bonded and I is the spin number. [James (1998)] So if in fully
labelled pyruvate for instance, one carbon is bound to two oxygen and one other carbon nucleus, so it will
have 2 × 1 × 1
2 + 1 = 2 multiplets, the next carbon is bound to one oxygen and two carbon nuclei giving
2 × 2 × 1
2 + 1 = 3 multiplets, and the last carbon is bound to one carbon and three protons and will have
2 × 4 × 1
2 + 1 = 5 multiplets.
The multiplets are symmetrical, with intensity given by binomials so a doublet is split 1:1, a triplet 1:2:1
and a quartet 1:3:3:1. [James (1998)]
In reality, many peaks will not show the splitting exactly how the multiplets are theoretically predicted;
interactions with atoms not immediately bond to the nucleus and different lengths of the bonds will also
affect how the peak appears.
Splitting can make the spectrum harder to read (as with the glucose in 1.3), but it can also be a useful
tool to identify compounds as it gives additional structural information to the chemical shift.
1.4
Prostate cancer
1.1 million new cases of prostate cancer (PCa) arises each year the world over. More common in
developed countries, it is still one of the most frequent forms of cancer in men world wide - second only
to lung cancer. [Torre et al. (2015)] Even though the frequency of PCa diagnoses are rising in the western
world, the mortality is falling. [Center et al. (2012)] This trend is contributed in part to better treatments, but
also to PSA testing - a method for potentially detecting PCa in its earliest stages.[ Torre et al. (2015)] But
the usefulness of PSA is highly debated. Estimates of overdiagnosis range from 23% to 42%, leading to
unnecessary treatment, which can be both expensive and harmful. [Draisma et al. (2009)]
Prostate cancer is more common in elderly people, and the disease is normally slow to develop, a person
could have prostate cancer for years without it being their cause of death. On the other hand if the cancer
is aggressive it can suddenly and rapidly spread from the prostate to the rest of the body. [Draisma et al.
(2009)] Because of this, the need for more sensitive diagnostic tools, should be apparent. A tool that could
distinguish between aggressive cancer, where treament is needed, and the more benign version, where the
cancer is unlikely to develop or spread in the patient’s life time.
This situation makes PCa cells an interesting subject for metabolic NMR studies; all types of cancerous
cells undergo metabolic reprogramming, and prostate cancer cells has been shown to have unique metabolic
features, different from other types of cancer. [Zadra et al. (2013)] Normal prostate cells accumulate zinc
to produce large amounts of citrate, which they secrete as part of prostate ﬂuid. Though it is known that
malignant tumour cells loses this function, the intermediate metabolic steps, both in the normal process and
in the development of malignancy is not fully understood. The production of citrate in normal prostate cells,

1.5
Statistical analysis
In order to determine if metabolite data obtained by dDNP NMR can be used to distinguish between
healthy and cancerous prostate cells, and between PCa of varying malignancy, a statistic analysis must
be applied to the obtained data. Principal components-discriminant function analysis (PC-DFA) as a
technique used on metabolomics data is well described, it handles noisy data well and compared to the more
predominantly used partial least squares-discriminant analysis (PLS-DA) it has stronger predictive power.
[Gromski et al. (2015)] This makes it a good candidate for evaluating the strength of the obtained data.
PC-DFA is implemented as a principal component analysis followed by a linear discriminant analysis.
1.5.1
PCA
Principal component analysis (PCA) is an unsupervised learning method, meaning that it does not
consider the classiﬁcation or labels of the points in the data. The goal of PCA is twofold; to reveal hidden
structures in the data and to eliminate redundant dimensions. This is accomplished by projecting the data
from its original basis onto a new one, made from a linear combination of the old. This new basis is made
so its dimensions are orthonormal and that each one captures as much variance as possible. Thus the ﬁrst
dimension -or component - of the new basis are chosen along the line of most variance in the data, the second
perpendicular on the ﬁrst and again along the line of most of the remaining variance and so on. The result
will be a basis containing as many dimensions as the original but ranked in descending order of captured
variance. A cut-off can then be chosen at some sum of variance like 90 or 95% for instance - thus a data set
with a huge number of dimensions with some dependency in between can be explained by a smaller number
of independent dimensions. [Shlens (2014)]
The strength of PCA is its simplicity and its user independence; there are no parameters to be guessed at
and no prior knowledge of the relative importance of each measured variable is necessary. The simplicity
also brings the main weakness of PCA; as the new basis consist only of linear combination of the original
components, it cannot detect more complex underlying structures of the data. [Shlens (2014)]

1.5.2
PC-DFA
Principal components-discriminant function analysis (PC-DFA) is an analysis method where linear
discriminant analysis (LDA) is applied to the PCA output for the data. LDA is a supervised learning method,
where classes in the data are speciﬁed and a linear separation is ﬁtted to maximize the difference (distance)
between the classes and minimize the distance of the data points inside each class. LDA is very sensitive to
collinearity between the dimension and prone to over-ﬁtting if it is present, therefore it works well together
with PCA, as there is no collinearity between orthonormal components. [Gromski et al. (2015)]
The strength of PC-DFA is its simplicity and robustness. It provides a tool for determining if classes can
be separated on the grounds of the data, and a model for classifying new data. [Gromski et al. (2015)]

2.1.2
Growth conditions
The cells were grown from frozen stock and grown in RPMI-1640 medium with added FBS and
penicillin+streptomycin antibiotic. They were incubated at 37◦Celsius with 5% CO2 content, and grown to
at least 80% conﬂuence before harvesting.
At harvest the cells were washed with DPBS and removed from the surface of their container with
trypsin. They were then spun down at 100 g for 3 minutes and re-suspended in a 40mM phosphate buffer
with pH 7.34 at a concentration of 20 million cells per ml.
2.2
Cellular extracts
Eppendorf tubes with 500 µl cells in phosphate buffer (a quantity of 10 million cells) are placed on at
shaking thermostat at 37◦Celsius. To each sample 100µl 120mM fully labelled 13C,D7-glucose is added.
The cells incubate with the glucose for 0, 1, 3, 10 or 30 minutes, and then 400µl 2.2M PCA - that have
been kept on ice - is added, to kill the cells and stop all metabolic processes. The samples are now kept
on ice for at least 10 minutes before being centrifuged for 10 minutes at 10.000 rpm and 4◦Celsius. The
supernatant is tranferede to new tubes and 60µl universal indicator is added for pH neutralizing with KOH
and possibly HCL. Again the samples are placed on ice for at least 10 minutes before being centrifuged at
same conditions. The supernatant is transferred to new tubes and these are freeze dried.
2.3
DNP and NMR
The freeze dried sample was re-suspended in 150µl polarization medium (1227mg glycerol, 944mg
Milli-Q-water, 70mg OX063 (a free radical trityl), and 28.8mg Gadoteridol (a gadolinium contrast agent))
and 5µl 50mM HP001 was added for reference. Too insure homogeneity the sample was vortexed, dipped in
a ultrasound bath, vortexed again and spun down, before it was transferred to the sample cup.
DNP were performed at 3.35 T and 1.4K in a HyperSense Polarizer for 90 minutes. After this time the
sample was dissolved in 5ml phosphate buffer with 40mM EDTA (Ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid calcium
disodium salt) and transfered to the Varian spectrometer where NMR spectra were recorded at 9.4 T and
with a 70 degree pulse angle.
2.4
Technical samples
In addition to the cellular metabolite extracts, technical samples were made for the article (included in
Appendix B) for evaluating and optimizing the dDNP hyperpolarization.
For these samples ﬁve test compounds, all 13C labelled, were mixed with Milli-Q-water: 27 mM
pyruvate, 34mM lactate, 54mM alanine, and 50 mM HP001. 5 µl of each of these stock solutions were mixed
together with 100 µl polarization medium (made as described in 2.3), 25 µl Milli-Q-water and 11.58 mg

2.5
mNova
All raw spectral data was processed with the software mNova [Mestrelab Research S.L. (2015)]. Here
each FID was automatically Fourier transformed. Manually a 5Hz exponential apodization function was
added, the phase corrected, and the baseline was corrected with Whittaker smoother. The reference peak was
calibrated to a chemical shift of 23.4 ppm.
After this preprocessing, integrals of all peaks placed lower than 50 ppm and higher than 110 ppm (ie.
out side the area where the chemical shifts from glucose lie [MDL - The Magnetic Resonance Metabolomics
Database (2005)]), where measured manually, and the value from the reference peak at 23.4 (HP001) was
standardized to 1.
2.6
Statistical analysis
All data processing after mNova analysis was done in Jupyter notebook [Project Jupyter (2016)] notebook
using Python [Python Software Foundation (2015)] with scikit-learn [Pedregosa et al. (2011)]. The code
written for this is in Appendix C.
Standardized integral values were imported, and peak positions were rounded to the nearest whole
number. PCA and LDA were performed, LDA both without validation and with leave-one-out (LOO)
cross-validation.
The analysis was run of four different subsets of the data:
• The full data set
• Full data set with integrals values from peaks at 125 and 161ppm omitted
• Samples with incubation time > 3 minutes with integrals values from peaks at 125 and 161ppm omitted
• Samples with 30 minutes incubation with integrals values from peaks at 125 and 161ppm omitted
The reason that 125 and 161 ppm peaks were omitted from runs, is that they correspond to the carbon of
carbon dioxide and bicarbonate, [MDL - The Magnetic Resonance Metabolomics Database (2005)] and are
likely artefacts from the pH neutralizing of the samples. The Henderson-Hasselbalch equation [White et al.
(1968)] describes the relationship between carbon dioxide, bicarbonate and pH:

CHAPTER 4
Discussion
This chapter goes through some challenges and limitations on the results of this thesis, evaluates
on the performance of the model and what it tells about the cell line, and discusses the future of
NMR in prostate cancer diagnosis.
4.1
Measuring the integrals
It can not be overstated how important the correct reading of the spectra obtained by the NMR is. The
values found by integrating over the peaks are the quantitative data that the whole evaluation of dDNP as a
method relies on. A concern during this project has been that if the person measuring the integrals has prior
knowledge of what the spectrum at hand "should" show, then these biases could effect the measurements.
Even though the scientist is well meaning and does her best to report the peaks as they actually are, it can be
difﬁcult to distinguish between signal and noise for the low peaks. If she knows that the last spectrum from
that cell line had a peak present at a certain shift, it will inﬂuence how she see the next spectrum. A carefully
placed integral can give a 0.01 or 0.02 value (compared to the reference) from noise with no signal present.
Another challenge is distinguishing between single peaks and coupled peaks (described in section 1.3.1),
with a low signal-to-noise ratio the peaks will not be perfectly symmetrical.
To circumvent these problems, the spectra has been read and integrals measured as mechanically as
humanly possible. With signals just above noise level the apodization has been changed between 5 Hz and 0
Hz, to check if there still is a visible signal without the exponential function and only then place an integral.
A suggestion for future research could be to look into the possibilities of automatizing the measurement
of integral values, to limit the human tendency of pattern recognition.
4.2
dDNP on metabolites
From the results of dDNP NMR experiments on the technical samples (decribed in 2.4 results found in
Appendix B) it can be concluded that dDNP NMR on metabolites works as highly robust and quantitative
method. It is seen that measured data are reproducible and consistent.
If one wished to measure actual quantities of the analysed compounds they would have to be recalculated
with respect to the difference in signal loss due to differing in T1 between the metabolites, but as long as
comparison is made only between the same metabolite in different cell lines, and not between differing
metabolites, real quantities does not have to be known. As long as external factors, such as transfer time
or the pH value in the sample are the same, the integral values for a chemical shift (standardized to the
reference) in one sample is comparable to the same shift in another sample.

With this established, the concern raised in 4.1 can be addressed. Looking at chemical shift, peak
splitting, and correlation between peaks together, gives a strong basis for knowing what metabolite gave rise
to the peak. In 3.6 it was determined that peaks with chemical shifts of 27, 171, and 206 ppm had a strong
positive correlation. In ﬁgure 4.1 a spectrum with these three peaks highlighted are shown.
With these peaks’ correlation, together with their chemical shift placement, and the doublet shape of the
171 ppm peak and the triplet shape of the 206 ppm, there is know strong grounds to say that these peaks are
made from pyruvate.
Similarly with the other two sets of correlated peaks: 20 ppm and 183 ppm peaks ﬁts with lactate; and
150 ppm and 172 ppm ﬁts with phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP), where the third peaks are lost in the area of the
spectrum with the glucose peaks.
With less strong correlations, comparing correlations between shifts for a single cell line with the
correlation of the shifts between cell lines, could help to make clear whether the correlations come from the
peaks being from the same metabolite or from different metabolites being produced at the same rate by a
cell line.
4.3
Evaluation on the classiﬁcation
As reported in section 3.5 the PC-DFA was able to classify the cell lines with a success rate ranging
from 54.8% for the whole dataset without the peaks at 125 and 161 ppm to 76.9% for only data from cells
incubated in 30 minutes, also without 125 and 161 ppm peaks, with LOO cross-validation. The importance
of validating the results, cannot be stressed to much, as PC-DFA by construction is prone to over-ﬁtting.
[Gromski et al. (2015)] LOO was chosen as a parameter for the cross-validation as the dataset was too small
to do 5-fold cross-validation; 3-fold could have been performed on the ﬁrst 3 data subsets, but when looking
at only 30 minutes incubation time samples, there are only two samples from LN-CaP and PNT, so even that
would not be possible.
It is curious that excluding peaks at 125 ppm and 161 ppm would bring down the classiﬁcation success,
as explained in section 2.6 these shifts are suspected to come form carbon dioxide and bicarbonate, which
should not correlated with speciﬁc cell lines, but be products of the pH-neutralizing process after the cells
have been removed from the metabolites. As such the presence of these compounds should only introduce

more noise to the data and thus make classiﬁcation harder. A possible explanation could be that the difference
lies in the hands of the researcher working with the cells; the samples for the different cell lines have been
prepared by different researchers, and the process of pH-neutralizing involves adding KOH to the sample
until the indicator changes colour. If too much KOH is added, HCL will be added and so on. If the more
times the researcher misses neutral the more of the artefacts arise, this could give a systematic difference if
one researcher is less careful than another.
Looking at ﬁgure 3.5 and ﬁgure 3.7 it is clear that the model has a much harder time separating samples
incubated 10 minutes - and for 3 minutes it is nearly impossible - compared to those incubated for 30 minutes.
As also described in section 3.2.1 the metabolic proﬁle is much clearer after 30 minutes. If more samples
had been incubated for 30 minutes or perhaps even longer maybe the classiﬁcation could have been stronger
or at least much better validated. As it stands the possibility of over-ﬁtting must be a concern when only 13
data points for 5 different classes exist in the subset of data with long incubation time.
Even though more data deﬁnitely is need, the results are promising: When looking at samples incubated
for 30 minutes, the PC-DFA makes three misclassiﬁcation errors. Of those, one is between DU 145 and PC-3
and it can be argued that this is no misclassiﬁcation as both these cell lines are aggressive and metastatic
cancer cells. That the model groups these two cell lines together afﬁrms that there are signiﬁcant metabolic
changes between cells in differing states of malignancy.
The last two misclassiﬁcations are between PNT and MCF7 samples. As explained in section 1.4, healthy
prostate cells show a deviation from the glycolysis compared to other cell types, and this makes them more
like cancer cells with respect to metabolites. Concerning further use of the model and its possibility for
distinguishing between states of malignancy, it is not a problem if it cannot identify other types of cancer
cells except for PCa.
An interesting observation is that the healthy cell line are placed in between the some-what aggressive
and the very aggresive cell lines. More experiments with more different cell lines could maybe determine if
aggressive PCa in some metabolic way "reverts" as it spreads, or if LN-CaP is an outlier in this regard.
4.4
dDNP NMR for diagnosing prostate cancer
Even though the sample size for this thesis have been small, and the results seen may be boosted by
over-ﬁtting, dDNP NMR on metabolites does show possibility for becoming a diagnostic tool. dDNP NMR
has been shown to produce reliable results with a sensitivity where we can distinguish between differences in
cell lines. Prostate cancer cells do show a metabolic change, both as they become cancerous, and a difference
between malignant and less aggressive states of cancer.
As dDNP NMR is non-invasive the patient procedure would only amount to either, inject the patient
with labelled glucose and then draw ﬂuid containing metabolites from them after an incubation time had
past; or sampling tissue from patient to be cultured in the lab the same way as with cells in this study.
If the classiﬁcation results found here holds up when more data on even more cell lines are used, then it
could become a important tool for determining when surgical intervention or medical treatment is necessary
for a person when prostate cancer.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
dDNP enhanced NMR was found to be a reproducible and quantitative method for measuring certain
cellular metabolites.
PC-DFA was chosen as a statistical platform for differentiating between ﬁve different cell lines, because
of this analysis’ ability to handle noisy data.
Scripts were set up in python to import and set up mNova data, implement PCA and LDA and make
correlation matrices of measured peaks.
With cell lines grouped together after aggressiveness PC-DFA classiﬁcation had a 64.5% succesrate for
all data and 84.6% if only samples incubated for 30 minutes were included. More experiments with long
incubation time should be made to check if results are solid or over-ﬁtting has occurred. Cells incubated for
only 3 minutes showed little to nof metabolic differentiation.
dDNP NMR shows promise as a method for diagnosing prostate cancer and determining if the cancer is
aggressive; prostate cancer cell lines in different stages of metastasis shows different metabolic proﬁles and
dDNP NMR is able to detect this.

"""arg:
ranges: Column from Dataframe with start- and endvalue of
peaks seperated by ' .. '
returns:
middle: Array with average values, rounded to whole number
"""
middle = np.array([])

middle = np.append(middle,round((float(lst[0]) + float(lst[1]))/2))
return middle

In [3]: def corsub(dat, cell, t):
"""arg:
dat: Dataframe with integral values, refs in column "23" and celltype in column "typ
cell: name of cell type (f.eks "pc3"), or 0 for all
t = int. only samples with time values higher than t is included
return:
none
"""
#takes subset if cell type specified
if cell:
dat = dat[dat.type == cell]
dat = dat[dat.time.values.astype(float) > t]
#Cuts last two columns and ref column
dat = dat[dat.columns[0:-2]]
dat = dat.astype(float)
dat = dat.drop("23.0", 1)
#makes correlation matrix and removes lines with only NAN values
correlation = dat.corr()
correlation = correlation.dropna(1, 'all')
correlation = correlation.dropna(0, 'all')
sns.heatmap(correlation)
sns.set_palette("RdBu_r")
