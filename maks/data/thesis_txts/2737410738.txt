Abstract
This thesis presents a feasibility study of a MIMO RF frontôÙend designed for ship geoloôÙ
cation systems from space. The primary objective is to evaluate the potential of using
multipleôÙinput multipleôÙoutput (MIMO) technology to enhance the accuracy and reliability
of maritime surveillance systems deployed on satellites.
The research begins with the construction and testing of an Electronic Support Measures
(ESM) antenna, validating its performance through measurements. Building on this founôÙ
dation, the study explores various approaches for processing data from multiple antennas,
comparing existing methods and their applicability to the MIMO receiver design.
A highôÙlevel design of the MIMO RF frontôÙend is developed and simulated using Advanced
Design System (ADS) software. Challenges encountered during the simulation process,
such as software limitations and hardware constraints, are discussed in detail.
Key findings include the identification of desirable output characteristics, such as an output
voltage as a function of the phase difference of the received signals and an estimation
of the system parameter k, crucial for predicting Angle of Arrival (AoA) accuracy as a
function of Signal to Noise Ratio (SNR). The study also highlights the limitations of the
current design and proposes several areas for future research and development.

1.1
Background
Maritime monitoring is critically important for military operations and national security.
This importance is particularly pronounced in Denmark due to the strategic significance
of monitoring oceanic activities around Greenland. Greenland, an autonomous territory
within the Kingdom of Denmark, holds a pivotal position for safeguarding territorial integrity
and addressing security challenges in the Arctic region. In the current global landscape
marked by numerous regional conflicts, maintaining vigilant maritime surveillance has beôÙ
come essential.
Various tracking methods are employed to ensure effective maritime monitoring, including
radar systems, satellite technology, and the Automatic Identification System (AIS). AIS
is a widely used tool for ship tracking and collision avoidance, and it is mandatory for
a broad range of vessels under International Maritime Organization (IMO) regulations.
According to the International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea (SOLAS) Chapter
V, Regulation 19, [1], AIS is required for:
ãÂ All ships of 300 gross tonnage and upwards engaged on international voyages.
ãÂ Cargo ships of 500 gross tonnage and upwards not engaged on international voyôÙ
ages.
ãÂ All passenger ships, irrespective of size.
While smaller vessels, such as small commercial ships and cruising yachts, are not manôÙ
dated to carry AIS, many opt to install it for safety reasons.
AIS provides crucial data for monitoring and managing ship traffic, thereby enhancing
maritime domain awareness and enabling a proactive response to potential threats. Ships
equipped with AIS continuously broadcast their location, speed, and other vital informaôÙ
tion, making it easy to track their movements. However, problems arise when ships deôÙ
liberately turn off their AIS. This can be done to evade detection for various reasons,
including engaging in illegal activities or avoiding surveillance.

Microstrip Antennas
Microstrip antennas offer several advantages, including their compact size, lightweight
design, cost effectiveness, and ease of installation. However, they also come with certain
disadvantages, such as lower efficiency, limited polarization purity, and narrow bandwidth.
These attributes make microstrip antennas well suited for satellite applications, as they
can be conveniently mounted on the spacecraft structure without the need for complex
deployment or moving parts.

2.2.1
Radar Waveform
Typically, radar uses pulse waveform. The waveform selected influences the radarãs resôÙ
olution and detection capabilities. A radar pulse typically consists of a short burst of elecôÙ
tromagnetic energy, followed by a listening period during which the radar system receives
the echo signals. Figure 2.3 shows an example of radar pulse waveform.
ãÂ Pulse Duration and Bandwidth: The duration of the radar pulse, also known as
pulse width, determines the radarãs range resolution. A shorter pulse width results in
higher resolution, allowing the radar to distinguish between closely spaced objects.
The bandwidth of the radar pulse is inversely related to the pulse width, with wider
bandwidths providing better resolution.
ãÂ Pulse Repetition Frequency (PRF): The PRF is the rate at which radar pulses are
transmitted. It affects the maximum unambiguous range and the Doppler resolution
of the radar. A higher PRF allows for better velocity measurements but can limit the
maximum range.
ãÂ Modulation: Radar pulses can be modulated to improve performance. Common
modulation techniques include linear frequency modulation (chirp) and phase modôÙ
ulation, which enhance the radarãs ability to detect and resolve targets in the presôÙ
ence of noise.

2.2.4
Detection of Radar Signals in Noise
Detecting radar signals in the presence of noise is a fundamental challenge in radar sysôÙ
tem design. Noise can originate from various sources, including thermal noise, atmoôÙ
spheric noise, and electronic noise within the radar system itself. The effectiveness of a
radar system in detecting signals in a noisy environment is often quantified by its SignalôÙ
toôÙNoise Ratio (SNR) and its ability to implement advanced detection algorithms, such as
the matched filter.
Advanced detection algorithms, such as matched filtering and constant false alarm rate
(CFAR) processing, are employed to enhance the detection of radar signals in noise.
These algorithms improve the radarãs ability to distinguish between actual targets and
noise or clutter.
A matched filter is an optimal linear filter used to maximize the SNR of the received signal
in the presence of noise. It is designed to correlate the received signal with a template
or replica of the transmitted signal, thus maximizing the output when the received signal
matches the template. The matched filter is particularly effective in radar applications due
to its ability to enhance the detectability of known signal waveform. By correlating the
received signal with the expected signal shape, the matched filter effectively rejects noise
that does not match the template.
Although implementing a matched filter would be the optimal solution for rejecting noise
and detecting signals with very low SNR levels, it is not feasible in this case. Our satellite
is designed to receive radar signals from various ships, each equipped with radars from
different vendors that use different pulse waveforms. Consequently, it is impossible to
implement a single matched filter. One potential future improvement could be the develôÙ
opment of a pulse waveform detector capable of generating matched filters digitally in
real time. Artificial intelligence could play a significant role in this endeavor by identifying
patterns of known radar pulse waveforms, thereby enhancing the systemãs ability to adapt
to and process diverse radar signals effectively.

2.4
Mixers
The MIMO receiver design in this project incorporates a double balanced mixer. This
section provides an overview of the theory behind this component.
Mixers are nonlinear devices that combine two input signals to produce new frequencies
at the sum and difference of the original frequencies. This process is essential in many
RF applications, particularly in receivers where frequency conversion is necessary. [10]
The fundamental operation of a mixer involves multiplying two signals: one is usually
a modulated signal, and the other is a stable local oscillator (LO) output. The purpose
of this process is to shift the carrier frequency of the modulated signal while retaining the
informationôÙcarrying envelope intact. This frequency shifting is crucial for downôÙconverting
highôÙfrequency signals to lower intermediate frequencies (IF) or baseband for further proôÙ
cessing.
Mixers can be used in various stages of a receiver:
ãÂ RF Front End: To downôÙconvert the received highôÙfrequency RF signals to a lower
IF, making it easier to filter and process.
ãÂ UpôÙConversion: In some transmitter designs, mixers upôÙconvert the baseband sigôÙ
nals to the desired transmission frequency.
2.4.1
Intermodulation Products
Intermodulation products are undesired frequency components generated when two or
more signals are mixed in a nonlinear device. When a mixer multiplies two input signals,
not only are the sum and difference frequencies produced, but also additional frequencies
known as intermodulation products. These products result from the nonlinearities in the
mixing process and can interfere with the desired signals.
For example, if the input signals to a mixer are at frequencies f1 and f2, the output will

2.4.2
Balanced and Unbalanced Mixers
Mixers can be categorized into balanced and unbalanced types based on their ability to
suppress certain frequency components.
An unbalanced mixer allows all input frequencies (RF, LO, and their harmonics) to appear
at the output. This type of mixer is the simplest form but suffers from several disadvanôÙ
tages, such as poor isolation between ports and the presence of strong undesired signals.
Balanced mixers are designed to suppress certain unwanted frequency components, proôÙ
viding better performance in terms of isolation and spurious signal suppression. There are
two main types of balanced mixers:
ãÂ SingleôÙBalanced Mixers: These mixers suppress either the LO or the RF signal at
the output, but not both. They provide better isolation than unbalanced mixers and
reduce the amplitude of some intermodulation products.
ãÂ DoubleôÙBalanced Mixers: These mixers suppress both the LO and RF signals at the
output, providing superior isolation and reducing the number of unwanted intermodôÙ
ulation products. DoubleôÙbalanced mixers, such as the Gilbert Cell mixer, are widely
used in highôÙperformance RF applications due to their excellent performance.

4.2
Satellite Orbit and Doppler Effect
The design of the MIMO RX RF frontôÙend must consider the satelliteãs orbit, as it influôÙ
ences both the satelliteãs speed and altitude. These factors affect the received signal
level because altitude impacts path attenuation in the link budget. Additionally, the orbit
determines the duration of each pass, which for Low Earth Orbit satellites typically ranges
from 10 to 15 minutes. Circular orbits describe a circular illumination area covered by the
satellite, as illustrated in Figure 4.1, which shows the ground track of the International
Space Station (ISS). The yellow circle around the ISS represents the illuminated area,
which is the region on the ground from which the satellite is visible with an elevation angle
of a few degrees above the horizon, typically a minimum of 5 to 10 degrees.
Moreover, due to the relative motion between the satellite and the ship, the Doppler effect
causes a frequency shift in the received signals. This shift must be accounted for to ensure
accurate phase measurements. Orbit parameters such as altitude and velocity determine
the magnitude of the Doppler shift. However, since the Doppler effect can be corrected
and affects the radar signal receive by multiple antennas in the same way, it will not be
taken into account in the systemãs design.
The Doppler effect information can be used to calculate the location of the source of the
RF signal, even when receiving only one signal from one antenna. When the satellite is
approaching the signal source, the received frequency exhibits a positive Doppler shift.
When moving away, the Doppler contribution to the frequency is negative. The slope of the
frequency shift curve helps estimate the signal sourceãs location relative to the satelliteãs
trajectory.

4.4
Analog vs. Digital Signal Processing
Angle of Arrival estimation can be conducted in both the analog domain, keeping the
signal in its original frequency and processing it using analog circuits or in digital domains,
converting the signal using an Analog to digital converter (ADC) and processing it digitally.
ãÂ Analog Domain: In the analog approach, phase differences between signals are
directly measured using analog circuits such as phase detectors and mixers. This
method excels in realôÙtime processing with low latency, making it suitable for apôÙ
plications requiring immediate response. However, it requires the use of precise
and stable analog components, which may increase the complexity and cost of the
system.
ãÂ Digital Domain: In the digital approach, received signals are first digitized, and
phase differences are then calculated using digital signal processing techniques.
This method provides greater flexibility and allows for the implementation of comôÙ
plex algorithms that can enhance the accuracy and robustness of AoA estimation.
However, digitization introduces latency, and highôÙspeed processors are required
to handle the computational load, especially as the number of antennas increases.
Additionally, in satellite applications, it is crucial to conserve power. The increased
processing power required in the digital domain translates into higher power conôÙ
sumption on the satellite.

4.7
Phase Detection Techniques
Some receivers, such as Phase Modulation (PM) receivers, use phase detectors. A comôÙ
mon implementation of a phase detector is through the use of a mixer. One effective type
of mixer for phase detection is the double balanced mixer. Our goal is to measure the
phase difference between signals received by two antennas, and the double balanced
mixer is wellôÙsuited for this purpose. The double balanced mixer operates by mixing two
input signals to produce an output in DC as a function of the phase difference of the
input signals. In the context of AoA estimation, the mixer compares the phases of sigôÙ
nals received from different antennas. This section provides a detailed explanation of its
functionality, based on the principles outlined in [16].
The double balanced mixer is an essential component in many RF systems due to its
ability to accurately determine phase differences between two signals. The basic operôÙ
ation involves applying two identical frequency, constant amplitude signals to the mixer,
resulting in a DC output proportional to the phase difference between the signals.
The circuit includes the following signals and components:
ãÂ Two input signals, LO and RF: These signals are readings from each of the two
antennas.
ãÂ One output signal, IF: the output is the phase difference between the two inputs as
a DC voltage.
ãÂ Four Diodes, D1ôÙD4: The mixer core consists of four diodes arranged in a ring
configuration.
ãÂ Two Three port transformers or balun (unbalanced to balanced transformer): the
purposes of the transformers in the double balanced mixer are:
ã Impedance matching: the transformers help matching the impedance of input
and output ports to maximize power transfer and minimize signal reflection.
ã Balancing: maintaining the balance of the mixer circuit, the two input signals
should be applied symmetrically to the diode ring.
ã Isolation: providing isolation between the input signals and the output, in case
some reflections take place in the circuit.
According to Kurtzãs explanation in [16], the double balanced mixer works as follows:
The voltage at the secondary winding of the LO transformer causes currents to flow
through two pairs of diodes, depending on the polarity: through D1 and D2 when negôÙ
ative, or through D3 and D4 when positive. The voltages at nodes B and C will alternate
at virtual ground depending on the polarity, at a rate equal to the LO signal frequency.
The output voltage at the IF port is determined by the voltage at the RF transformerãs
secondary winding and which node (B or C) is at ground potential. The output at the IF
port includes the sum and difference of the input signal frequencies. When the RF and LO
signals have identical frequencies, their difference is zero Hz (DC), which is the desired
