Summary
Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFASs) are anthropogenic chemicals widely used in industrial prac-
tices and consumer products. Their widespread use in combination with their persistence to degradation
have caused PFASs to be ubiquitous in the environment, and due to their toxicological properties, ex-
posure of PFAS to the human body can cause various health problems. One of the exposure pathways
of PFAS to humans is through dietary intake and, according to the European Food Safety Authority
(EFSA), ”Fruits and fruit products” have been found to be one of the main food categories contributing
to the total PFAS exposure. This assessment is only based on a limited number of analyses, and further
data would have to be collected to close the knowledge gaps. Moreover, there are around 5,000-10,000
different PFASs on the global market; however, only a small number of these compounds have been
quantitatively analysed. To determine a reliable estimate of PFAS exposure, it is necessary to have data
on at least the primary consumed fruits in Denmark. Thus, the scope of this project included the analysis
of fluorotelomer compounds and native PFAS replacements (fluoroether compounds) in apples, bananas,
grapes, pears, and strawberries. The fluorotelomer compounds included fluorotelomer sulfonates (FTSs),
fluorotelomer carboxylic acids (FTCAs), and fluorotelomer unsaturated carboxylic acids (FTUCAs).
For this purpose, a method based on the Quick, Easy, Cheap, Effective, Rugged, and Safe method
(QuEChERS) and liquid chromatography coupled with tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) was
used for determination of fluorotelomers and native PFAS compounds. The method was optimised with
the aim of obtaining the linearity, trueness, and precision required by the European Union Laboratory
for Halogenated Persistent Organic Pollutants in Feed and Food (EURL POPs) to obtain the LOQs for
the studied compounds at relevant levels. The method was validated for 12 compounds and LOQs were
between 0.005 and 0.5 ng g–1 wet weight fruit. It was observed that short fluorotelomer compounds
were difficult to quantify in the fruit samples. Furthermore, it was observed that FTUCAs were easier to
detect than FTCAs and that the LOQs were lower for FTUCAs than for FTCAs. The composition of the
fruits might have had an effect on the efficiency of the method. Here, it was observed that the two main
components that might have affected the extraction and clean-up was the water and the carbohydrate
content.
The optimised method was then used to analyse 60 fruits (20 apples, 10 bananas, 10 grapes, 10 pears,
and 10 strawberries) from the Danish market. In 23 cases, PFAS was detected in non-quantifiable levels.
None of the compounds were detected in quantifiable amounts.
6:2 FTUCA was the most detected
compound. As a result of the analysis, it was assessed that the exposure of PFAS through fluorotelomer
and native PFAS replacements in fruits, available on the Danish market, are low. After this study, there
are several steps to be taken; one of them is to extend the scope to other fruits, to be able to do a more
comprehensive estimate of the PFAS exposure through the consumption of fruits. Another is to further
optimise the method to obtain even lower LOQs.

1
Introduction
1.1
What is PFAS?
Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFASs) are chemical compounds that contain at least one fully
fluorinated methyl or methylene group.1 PFASs are widely used in industrial practices and several con-
sumer products, including surfactants, surface protection products, additives, lubricants, and pesticides.2
The definition of PFAS from the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD)
is: PFASs are defined as fluorinated substances that contain at least one fully fluorinated methyl or
methylene carbon atom (without any H/Cl/Br/I atom attached to it), i.e., with a few noted
exceptions, any chemical with at least a perfluorinated methyl group (-CF3)or a perfluorinated methylene
group (-CF2-) is a PFAS.3
This is a new and revised definition from 2018 that streamlines the understanding of the term ”PFAS”.
The revision provides a coherent and consistent definition for PFAS from a chemist’s point of view and
makes it easier to distinguish between PFASs and non-PFASs.1
1.1.1
Structure of PFAS
In general, PFASs consist of a hydrophilic end group (X) and a chain of partially or fluorinated carbons
(R) that are typically 4 to 16 carbon atoms long (see Figure 1). The fluorination, which classifies the
compound as a PFAS, occurs on the carbon atoms. If the carbon chain is fully fluorinated, which is not
required for the molecule in order to be classified as ”PFAS”, the molecule is also called a perfluoroalkyl
substance.4
The carbonfluorine chain is hydrophobic while the attached functional group is hydrophilic, which means
that PFASs are amphiphilic because they consist of both a hydrophobic part and a hydrophilic end. The
hydrophilic end typically consists of groups such as sulfonic acid, carboxylic acid, or hydroxyl.4,5 The
general structure of PFAS is depicted in Figure 1.
Figure 1: General structure of PFAS, where R = C chain, X = hydrophilic end group, Y = F or C.
The chemical and physical properties of PFASs are the reason they have been so highly utilised. In
addition to being amphiphilic, the PFASs are also lipophobic which is caused by the several C-F bonds.
C-F bonds are highly polarised, which is caused by the high electronegativity and low polarisability
of fluorine, thus creating significant dipoles between C and F. This creates an extremely strong bond.
The low polarisability of fluorine also leads to weak intermolecular interactions, such as van der Waals
interactions and hydrogen bonding because the nucleus in fluorine has stronger interactions with its own
electrons. As the average volumes of CF2 and CF3 are larger than CH2 and CH3, the carbon-fluorine
chain is also much more rigid than its hydrocarbon equivalent. The rigidity of the carbon-fluorine bonds
makes PFASs more chemically inert because they are less able to fit into the structure network of for
example water. The high degree of C-F bonds enhances both the thermal and the chemical stability,
making them very resistant to degradation and useful in the industry at high temperatures or pressures
or in corrosive environments.5,6,7

PFAS can sometimes be classified as long-chain (>C7) or short-chain (C4-C7) PFAS depending on the
length of the carbon-fluorine chain. The length of the carbon-fluorine chain affects the properties of the
compound. It has been seen that PFASs with longer fluorinated chains have more chemical and thermal
stability because of the more extensive C-F chemistry.8 Long-chain PFAS that have been well studied
and widely present in the environment are referred to as legacy PFAS.9
1.2
Classification of PFAS
In 2011, Buck et al. made a fundamental distinction in PFASs by dividing the substances into two
primary categories: non-polymers and polymers. This is because polymers have physical, chemical, and
biological properties that are very different from those of substances with low molecular weight. The
definition of a polymer here is a ”substance consisting of molecules characterised by the sequence of one
or more types of monomer unit.”10
1.2.1
Polymer PFAS
For the polymer category, the polymers are synthesised by incorporating one or more PFAS monomers
into a chain. They have the potential to degrade, which can lead to the release of monomer PFASs into
the environment. The subcategories for polymeric PFAS are fluoropolymers, perfluoropolyethers, and
side-chain fluorined polymers.
Furthermore, in 2021, Buck et al. provided a comparison of the categorisation of PFAS in the 2018
OECD/UNEP report with the original 2011 categorisation. The comparison resulted in the use of the
original grouping and categorisation of PFAS by Buck et al. from 2011.11
1.2.2
Non-polymer PFAS
For the non-polymer category there are two subcategories: perfluoroalkyl and polyfluoroalkyl substances.
Perfluoroalkyl substances
Compounds in the category of perfluoroalkyl substances include com-
pounds for which all the hydrogens on all carbons have been replaced with fluorine, except the carbons
associated with the functional group. Within the non-polymer group of perfluoroalkyl substances, per-
fluoroalkyl acids (PFAAs) play an important role. PFAAs include perfluoroalkyl carboxylic, sulfonic,
sulfinic, phosphonic, and phosphinic acids. PFAAs are important because they are or have been highly
used and because they are highly persistent compounds that have been emitted directly to the environ-
ment or formed indirectly from environmental degradation or metabolism of precursor substances. Due
to their low dissociation constant (pK a), PFAAs often dissociate more or less to their anionic form in
most environments. Their ability to be in anionic form means that they are highly soluble in water and
have very low vapour pressure. These physiochemical properties suggest that the compounds can easily
spread through water and have low volatility.10
Perfluoroalkyl carboxylics, also called perfluorocarboxylic acids (PFCAs), is a major group of PFASs
(and PFAAs) that are highly discussed in the literature.12 The general chemical formula for PFCAs
is CnF2n+1COOH and they have been used mainly as surfactants. Furthermore, PFCAs are terminal
degradation products of several other precursor PFASs.
The most well-known PFCA is the legacy
perfluorooctanoic acid (PFOA), which is well-known because it has been used for decades as an essential
processing aid in the manufacturing of polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE), commonly known as Teflon.10
Another major group of PFAAs is the group of perfluoroalkyl sulfonic acids or perfluoroalkane sulfonic
acid (PFSAs), which has the chemical formula CnF2n+1SO3H. Perfluorooctane sulfonic acid (PFOS) is
another legacy PFAS, which has also been used extensively and mainly as a surfactant, just like PFOA.

It has been detected in both biota and humans. Although PFOS is not as detected as PFOA, it has been
shown to be 10 times more toxic than PFOA, which is why the compound is receiving so much attention

Polyfluoroalkyl substances
Remaining in the non-polymer category, the other subcategory of polyflu-
oroalkyl substances contains a greater amount of different PFASs and consists of compounds for which at
least one, but not all, carbon is fully fluorinated. These include perfluoroalkane sulfonamido derivatives,
fluorotelomer substances, and other miscellaneous compounds like semifluorinated n-alkanes and alkenes.
Within this category are the perfluoroalkyl and polyfluoroalkyl ether carboxylic acids. The salts of per-
fluoroalkyl and polyfluoroalkyl ether carboxylic acids have been widely cited in patents as alternative
processing aids in fluoropolymer production, and they are more environmentally and/or toxicologically
acceptable than the usage of PFOS.10

1.2.3
Fluorotelomers
There are around 5,000-10,000 different PFASs on the global market; however, only less than thirty
compounds have been quantitatively analysed.13 For this reason, the scope for studying PFAS can be
extended to fluorotelomers.
Fluorotelomer-based products describe a group of raw material building blocks, surfactants, and poly-
meric products.10 Fluorotelomer-based polymers and phosphate mono- and di-esters have in recent years
been used as fluorinated alternatives to long-chain PFCAs and PFSAs and their derivatives in surface
coating. They have also been used as fluorinated alternatives for surface coating applications to long-
chain PFCAs, long-chain PFSAs, and derivatives thereof.14
The degradation of fluorotelomer-based products can be a potential source of PFAAs in the environment
since fluorotelomers are precursors to PFAAs. For example, PFCAs can be formed by degradation of

certain fluorotelomer compounds.10 One of the places where fluorotelomer sulfonates (FTSs) have been
detected is in soil samples near an industrial wastewater treatment plant.14 FTSs comprise an even
number of fluorinated carbons, typically ranging from 2 to 12, which are attached to two non-fluorinated
carbons. The general chemical formula for FTS is CnF2n+1CH2CH2SO3H. FTSs have been produced
as a replacement for PFOS; however, they have been around for decades. Like PFAAs, FTS compounds
also have low pKa values (estimated to 0.36-1.32), which means that they also dissociate at most pH
levels.15
Fluorotelomer alcohols (FTOHs) and sulfonates are formed by telomerization with fluorotelomer iodides
as the intermediates.16 Fluorotelomer carboxylic acid (FTCA) and fluorotelomer unsaturated carboxylic
acid (FTUCA) can be formed from fluorotelomer alcohol and fluorotelomer sulfonate. FTOHs are key
compounds in the formation of n:2 fluorotelomer acrylates and n:2 fluorotelomer methacrylates, which
are used as surface coating for textiles, leather, and paper substrates. FTOH is biodegraded by aerobic
oxidation to form the corresponding n:2 fluorotelomer aldehyde (n:2 FTAL). The aldehyde is a highly
reactive species and therefore relatively short-lived as it will be rapidly oxidised to form the corresponding
n:2 FTCA. FTCA has the general chemical formula CnF2n+1CH2COOH, where the acid is separated
from the carbonfluorine chain by a methylene group. The FTCA can then undergo dehydrohalogenation
to form FTUCA, which has the general formula Cn –1F2n –1CF––CHCOOH. Dehydrohalogenation of
the n:2 FTOH can also yield FTUCA through the intermediate compounds unsaturated fluorotelomer
alcohol and unsaturated fluorotelomer aldehyde.10
1.2.4
Native PFAS replacements
Legacy PFASs (e.g. PFOS and PFOA) have previously been produced globally since the 1950’s; however,
most developed nations have now phased out the production of the compounds, which means replacement
PFASs have emerged. Common replacements are fluoroalkylether compounds, also called ether-PFAS,
which contain at least one alkylether linkage in the fluorinated carbon chain and replace long-chain
PFAAs.17 One of the benefits of ether-PFAS is the potential reduced bioaccumulation by introducing one
or several alkylether linkages.18 Some of the ether-PFASs are perfluoroethercarboxylic acids (PFECAs)
and polyfluoroethersulfonic acids (PFESAs). Among these groups are hexafluoropropylene oxide dimer
acid (HFPO-DA), dodecafluoro-3H-4,8-dioxanonanoate (ADONA or DONA), and F-53B (9Cl-PF3ONS
and 11Cl-PF3OUdS), which are all popular replacement compounds for legacy PFAS.19
HFPO-DA is a PFECA compound.
It is a short-chain PFAS alternative and has been specifically
engineered to be a replacement for PFOA. It is used for the production of fluoropolymers.14,19 DONA is
another PFECA compound consisting of two alkylether linkages in the carbonfluorine chain, which has
been used as a replacement for PFOA.19
The industrial chemical known as F-53B is a trade name that is typically referred to its major compound
9Cl-PF3ONS. However, it can also be used to refer to both 9Cl-PF3ONS and its minor 8:2 homologue,
11Cl-PF3OUdS. The 9Cl-PF3ONS compound consists of 8 fluorinated carbon atoms and the 11Cl-
PF3OUdS compound consists of 10 fluorinated carbon atoms.
Each compound includes a Cl-atom,
that has replaced an F-atom on the terminal carbon, an alkylether linkage, and a sulfonate group. Both
compounds are PFESA compounds, and they have been adopted as alternatives to PFOS. The potassium
salts of the compounds have been produced for decades and have been used in both decorative and hard
metal plating used in China.17,19

1.3
Removal of PFAS
The two most common methods that have proven effective in removing PFAS from water are the use
of granular activated carbons (GAC) and ion exchange (IX) resins. However, these adsorbents exhibit
limited removal efficiency against short-chain PFAS. Because GAC have a nonpolar surface, they are
more effective in removing long-chain PFAS than short-chain PFAS. Furthermore, the surface of GAC
often contains groups with negative charges, which can cause electronic repulsion for anionic PFASs,
which most acids do most times. This makes it difficult to use GAC to remove short-chain PFAS. Even
IX resins, which are based on electrostatic interactions, have proven difficulties when removing short-
chain PFAS. Some solutions have been found, where the hydrophobic and electrostatic interactions have
been combined. However, this solution was more costly and less effective, as not all resin regeneration
methods are capable of desorbing all of the PFAS from the resin surface. It has also been shown that
short-chain PFASs are more difficult to remove from the resin surface than long-chain PFAS. Lastly,
the disposal and recycling of the used IX resin should be thoroughly considered to prevent PFAS from
being released back to the atmosphere. The difficulties in removing PFAS from various materials is a
contributing factor to the toxicity of PFAS.8
1.4
Toxicity
1.4.1
Toxic effects of PFAS
Even though the high stability of PFASs is one of the reasons that PFAS is so highly utilised, their
high stability also results in high resistance to degradation which has given them the nickname ”forever
chemicals”. As PFASs are not metabolised in the body, they exhibit a wide range of toxic effects and
are mainly associated with elevated risks of cancer,20 immune responses,21 developmental problems,22
and reproductive effects.23 Due to these adverse effects, PFAS can be classified as a potential endocrine
disruptor, as the compounds have the ability to disrupt the endocrine system, which is responsible for
creating and releasing hormones to regulate body biological processes.2
Among the different types of PFASs, PFCAs and PFSAs are some of the most persistent to degrada-
tion.4 PFOA and PFOS are two of the most detected PFASs in aquatic systems and drinking water
worldwide.6 Although they have been phased out globally, they still persist in the environment. Their
high widespread utilisation has also resulted in their widespread occurrence in the environment. The

Another reason why PFAAs are still present in the environment is the indirect release by environmental

The persistence depends partially on the chain length of the PFAS compound. It has been seen that the
volatility of PFAS usually decreases as the chain length decreases because the short-chain PFAS tends
to have a lower vapour pressure. This also means that the aqueous solubility and mobility increase when
the chain length decreases, if the functional group is the same. If the functional group is charged, the
aqueous solubility will increase, which will reduce hydrophobic interactions. The short-chained water-
soluble PFAS compounds will therefore be able to travel longer distances in aqueous environments. This
makes it more difficult to control the spread of the short-chain PFAS compared to the long-chain PFAS,
and this is also why short-chain PFAS are predominant over long-chain PFAS.6,8

1.4.2
EU regulation for PFAS in food
Since food can be a form of exposure pathway for PFAS, the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA)
established in September 2020 a new safety threshold for PFAS in food. The tolerable weekly intake
(TWI) is set as the sum of four PFAS compounds: PFOS, PFOA, perfluorononanoic acid (PFNA)
and perfluorohexane sulfonic acid (PFHxS) and is set to 4.4 ng kg–1 body weight per week. The risk
assessment is limited to these four compounds as a result of a health risk assessment.4 As of now,
eggs, fish meats, bivalve molluscs, and various other meats are the only foods in which the amount of
PFOS, PFOA, PFNA, and PFHxS have been regulated by the EU. This can be found in ”Commission
Regulation (EU) 2023/915 of 25 April 2023 on maximum levels for certain contaminants in food and
repealing Regulation (EC) No 1881/2006”.
To provide scientific and technical advice to European authorities and National Reference Laboratories
(NRLs), the European Union Laboratory for Halogenated Persistent Organic Pollutants in Feed and
Food (EURL POPs) exists. The EURL POPs is an important part of the development of analytical
parameters and conditions to reach low limits of quantification for halogenated POPs, such as PFAS.
They also address knowledge gaps and provide guidance for analytical issues related to the analysis of
PFAS.

As part of sample preparation, extraction and clean-up steps are crucial, as we want to extract the PFAS
analytes from the sample matrix and isolate the analytes from other interfering ions. Therefore, choosing
the appropriate extraction and clean-up method is one of the most important steps in determining the
content of PFAS.27 Quantification of PFAS in complex matrices is still a challenge, and it is necessary
to develop and validate sensitive and reliable analytical methods for a large group of PFASs.14
Two well-known methods for extraction and clean-up are the solid phase extraction and the QuEChERS
method.
1.5.1
Solid phase extraction
Solid phase extraction (SPE) is an extraction method that is commonly applied to extract PFAS from
various sample matrices, hereunder food matrices. It is a well-established method and is based on the
partition of the analyte between a solid phase and a liquid phase. Usually, the solid phase is the sorbent,
which is held in a column, and the liquid phase is the sample matrix or solvent with the analytes. The
analytes are then eluted from the solid phase with an appropriate mobile phase.28 In SPE, the first
step includes conditioning the cartridge with a solvent that activates the sorbent by passing the solvent
through it. The solvent typically has the same properties as the solvent in which the sample has been
diluted, in order to maximise retention of analytes to the sorbent. The sample is then loaded onto the
sorbent, where the analytes are retained. Once the analytes are retained on the column, a washing step
will remove the interfering compounds without removing the analytes of interest. The washing can be
done in multiple steps, in which the polarity of the solvent can be either gradually increased or decreased.
After the washing step, the analytes can be eluted and collected for further clean-up or detection. For
PFASs, the solvent will typically start polar to wash out all the polar impurities, while the PFASs are
retained on the non-polar stationary phase. The polarity of the solvent is then decreased, which will
result in PFASs eluting once the PFASs bind more strongly to the solvent rather than the sorbent.29,30
One of the most commonly used cartridges for PFAS analysis is the Oasis® weak anion exchange (WAX)
cartridge, which is a mixed mode sorbent that combines hydrophobic and polar moieties. The Oasis®
WAX cartridge consists of copolymers of 1,3-divinylbenzene and N-vinyl-pyrrolidone, modified with a
weak anion exchange moiety that has one secondary amine and one tertiary amine. The mixed-mode
sorbent is optimal as most PFASs usually exhibit an acidic character, which gets deprotonated and can
therefore bind to the positively charged amine moiety, whilst the hydrophobic C-F bonds will interact
with the benzene moieties. To elute the PFAS analytes, a solvent with a higher pH value will be used,
so the sorbent will lose its positive charge. In this way, the analytes will be detached from the sorbent
and eluted with the solvent.30
SPE is generally widely applied in PFAS analysis, and it is a powerful method as it provides separation,
concentration, decontamination, and clean-up. It is especially a vital procedure in food analysis. The
SPE procedure was originally developed to serve as an alternative extraction method to liquid-liquid
extraction (LLE). Some of the main advantages of SPE are that it has high recovery levels and is
adaptable to chromatographic analysis. Compared with liquid-liquid extraction, it reduces the analysis
time by easy automation, decreases the number of steps in the procedure, and decreases the amount of
organic solvent. Furthermore, SPE is highly selective for specific analytes, which allows a high cleaning
efficiency.28
A disadvantage of SPE is that samples with high contents of foreign particles in water can cause PFAS
to bind to those foreign particles. The PFAS will then be lost with those foreign particles, causing low
analyte recoveries. Therefore, to prevent low analyte recoveries, these kinds of samples must be filtered
before SPE. Furthermore, there is a risk that the cartridge might clog when PFAS is removed from
heavily contaminated samples. Lastly, since the SPE system is a relatively open system, it is more likely

to be contaminated by surrounding particles.12
1.5.2
QuEChERS
The QuEChERS method is short for the Quick, Easy, Cheap, Effective, Rugged, and Safe method.
It was originally introduced to analyse pesticide residues in fruit and vegetables; however it has since
been shown to be used to analyse other compounds such as PFASs.31 The method is used in sample
preparation and has been known for its many advantages, such as being easy and effective. It involves
two overall steps: i) an extraction step, which involves liquid-liquid partitioning with acetonitrile (ACN)
and ii) a purifying step, which is done by dispersive solid-phase extraction (dSPE) using sorbents and
salts for a further clean-up. Combining QuEChERS with detection by either LC-MS/MS or GC-MS/MS
makes for a cost-effective overall analysis.32,33
When the extraction step in QuEChERS is started, the following aspects must be considered: the choice
of extraction solvent and sample/solvent ratio, the amount of the sample, influence of sample pH on
recoveries and the type and amount of salts used for induction of phase separation.33
The extraction in the QuEChERS method is based on salting-out assisted liquid-liquid extraction
(SALLE), which is an improved form of LLE. Solvents that are miscible under normal temperature
and pressure, such as water and acetonitrile, can be separated into a water-rich and an ACN-rich phase
with SALLE by the addition of salts. Salts can change the surface tension of the solvents and therefore
cause phase separation.34
The recovery rate has been shown to depend on the type and amounts of salt used in the salting-out
step.
The salts used most are anhydrous MgSO4 and NaCl to induce separation of the ACN-water
phase. Anhydrous MgSO4 allows the best salting-out of undesired compounds in ACN, the binding of
large amounts of water, and the co-extraction of undesired polar compounds from the sample matrix.
Furthermore, NaCl increases the selectivity of the extraction because the addition of salt increases the
ionic strength of the aqueous phase, thus ensuring the phase separation is more complete between water
and ACN. The addition of salts will also increase the distribution of the target analytes in the organic
phase, which will improve the extraction efficiency.32,33,35
For most extractions, ACN is used as an extraction solvent, as it is a versatile solvent. It allows for the
extraction of a broad range of analytes, yet it is still selective as it only co-extracts a small amount of
matrix components. Furthermore, ACN is also compatible with detection methods such as LC-MS/MS.
ACN is a commonly used solvent for QuEChERS, since it has a suitable miscibility with water, which
allows the partially organic phase to penetrate the aqueous phase and also permits phase separation
by adding water. Suitable miscibility with water is useful for fruit samples as they usually have a high
percentage of water.36
Compared to traditional SPE, QuEChERS usually does not require any additional cleaning steps, making
it low cost and more effective for complex samples. Furthermore, QuEChERS is suitable for use for
simultaneous extraction and purification of multiple target substances, as it has high recovery rates for a
variety of analytes, including PFAS. However, since SPE is more selective to specific analytes, it also has
better clean-up efficiency compared to that of QuEChERS because QuEChERS is not as selective. This
results in higher recovery rates and a significant reduction in matrix effects for SPE. Although SPE is
powerful for clean-up, it is also a slow method and has low cost-effectiveness, which is why QuEChERS
have been employed in routine analysis, primarily because of its speed and simplicity. For these reasons,
the QuEChERS method is good for extraction, whereas SPE is powerful for clean-up.31,35,37

1.5.3
dSPE and comparison with traditional SPE
As a part of the QuEChERS method, dSPE is used for clean-up. Some of the same principles apply as
for SPE, as both involve partitioning of analytes between a solid (the sorbent) and a liquid (the solvent).
However, instead of passing the analyte solution through a cartridge with the solid-phase material, the
adsorbent is introduced directly to the analyte solution via dispersion. By adding the adsorbent directly
to the analyte solution, loss of analytes due to washing steps is avoided.12
Due to the many advantages the method possesses, the attention to dSPE has increased significantly in
recent years. There are huge advantages in using dSPE instead of traditional SPE, one of which is that
dSPE does not use a large mass of adsorbent and uses a low volume of solvent, which is a benefit as it
is more sustainable. Furthermore, advantages include high analyte recoveries and the fact that dSPE
allows maximum interaction between the sorbent material and the target analyte due to the exploiting of
the maximum adsorbent surface. As dSPE does not require a column, clogging of the column will not be
a problem, which is a huge advantage for dSPE compared to traditional SPE. Moreover, the advantages
include short equilibrium times, no need for a conditioning state, high extraction recoveries, and easy
operation. dSPE is used both in extraction, isolation, and clean-up of compounds in complex matrices,
as in PFAS analysis.12,38 The difference between traditional SPE and dSPE is shown in Figure 6.
Figure 6: Difference between SPE and dSPE.39
Some of the challenges that are presented when using dSPE include the difficulty in achieving detection
levels similar to those of SPE. The clean-up with dSPE has in general been shown to be less effective.
The residual salts used for the clean-up might dissolve in the mass spectrometry source, causing an
increase in dead volume and peak tailing. Furthermore, the dSPE methodology is highly dependent
on the water content of the sample, which makes the technique less robust for varying environments,
especially for fruits, since they can have varying water content.12 A commonly used sorbent for dSPE in
QuEChERS extractions is primary secondary amine (PSA), which is a weak anion exchange. The sorbent
is a silica-based sorbent that contains primary and secondary amines. However, it has been shown that
PSA cannot be used for the analysis of all PFASs in fruits because some of the PFAS compounds contain
carboxylic and sulfonic acids. Since primary and secondary amines are fairly strong bases (pKa ∼10.1
and 10.9), they will create strong ionic bonds to PFAS compounds, which are often deprotonated due
to their acidic nature. This will make it difficult to recover the PFAS compounds from the sorbent and
would require harsh conditions to obtain good recovery results. Furthermore, the sorbent also retains
other compounds in addition to PFASs, which reduces the specificity and can result in a poor clean-up
efficiency.40 Specifically, PSA was shown to not be suitable for use in QuEChERS for strawberries.40
For food matrices such as fish and milk samples, dSPE was used with C18 sorbents, ENVI-carbTM
along with the QuEChERS method. This method proved higher recoveries (70%-120%) compared to the
conventional SPE method.41 Since QuEChERS and clean-up with dSPE is an easy, cheap, and quick
method, it is optimal as a routine PFAS analysis method to screen for target PFAS compounds. SPE

1.6
LC-MS/MS
1.6.1
General system
After extraction of PFAS from the samples, the analytes can be detected and quantified with the use of
liquid chromatography coupled with tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS).30 The LC-MS/MS system
is depicted in Figure 7.
Figure 7: Depiction of liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry.42
The EURL POPs recommends that high performance liquid chromatography is coupled to mass spec-
trometry for the analysis of PFCA and PFSA.43 Coupling liquid chromatography with tandem mass
spectrometry is a widely used method of analysis that is sensitive and selective for PFASs. It is also
versatile because the ion source can be altered to fit the analytes of interest.41 Furthermore, the use
of LC-MS/MS allows for accurate quantitative measurements with low detection levels at the parts per
trillion (ppt) level. The separations are fast and efficient to use in the separation and analysis of com-
plex mixtures.44,45 LC-MS/MS can also be used to determine the limit of detection (LOD) and the
limit of quantification (LOQ). The LOD is the smallest quantity of analyte that is significantly different
from the blank. When the signal is three times greater than the noise, the signal can be qualified as
”detectable”.46 The LOQ is the smallest analyte concentration that can be quantified with reasonable
statistical certainty. That is, for PFAS, the lowest concentration of analyte that can be validated accord-
ing to the analytical method and criteria set by the EURL POPs.43 The use of LC-MS/MS allows for the
LOD and LOQ to be determined at even lower concentrations. One of the disadvantages of using LC-
MS/MS is that the ionisation of the analyte is sensitive to matrix components, which were not properly
removed during sample preparation. Those components can be lipids, inorganic salts, or proteins, which
we are not interested in analysing.30 Another disadvantage is the low sensitivity of compounds that are
irreversibly adsorbed by the stationary phase and thus cannot be detected with the use of LC-MS/MS.44
1.6.2
Liquid chromatography
Liquid chromatography has become a highly essential tool for modern analytical laboratories. Liquid
chromatography is a more versatile tool than gas chromatography because it can be used for analysis
of both volatile and non-volatile compounds. In gas chromatography, only volatile compounds can be
analysed.
In liquid chromatography, separation occurs between a mobile phase (the solvent) and a
stationary phase (the column). The system is composed of reservoirs for mobile phases, pump, sample
injector, column, and a detector or an MS interference.47
The elution
The most common HPLC separation technique is reversed-phase chromatography, which
is based on the partitioning principle. With partition chromatography, the retention time of the analyte
Page 11 of 54
==PAGE:17==
Sanni Gao
January 30, 2025
is dependent on the extent to which the analytes move from the mobile phase to the stationary phase
and then again from the stationary phase into the mobile phase. In reversed-phase chromatography, the
stationary phase is non-polar and the mobile phase is polar, which will cause the polar compounds to
elute first. The less polar compounds can be eluted by decreasing the polarity of the solvent.44,46
The column is part of the HPLC system, where the extract meets the stationary phase. The components
of the extract are separated by physical and chemical interactions between the components and the
packing particles.44 The column is filled with the stationary phase, which is usually highly pure, spherical,
microporous particles of silica. The majority of the surface area of the stationary phase is within the
pores of the silica, where the components of the extract can interact with the functional groups of the
stationary phase. The column that is the most used for separation of PFAS and is recommended for
PFAS analysis by the EURL POPs is the octadecylsilyl (C18) column.30,41 It consists of the octadecyl
substituent (ODS) functionalised to a silica gel and is highly non-polar due to the long carbon chains.46
Previously, it has been observed that compounds with longer chains are more strongly retained on the
C18 column, as they make hydrophobic interactions with the ODS functionality, which increases retention
times.15 Furthermore, as most PFASs are acidic and have negative charges in most environments, the
acidic group (e.g. carboxylate or sulfonate) can interact with the -OH groups on the silica gel by hydrogen
bonding, which will strengthen the retention of the PFAS compounds on the C18 column as an addition to
the hydrophobic interactions. However, the column might cause problems with ultrashort chain PFASs,
as these compounds are very small and usually very polar, which will cause them to not be retained
sufficiently on the column.48
The mobile phase often consists of a mixture of an aqueous solvent, often a buffer, and an organic solvent,
such as methanol, acetonitrile, or ethanol. The choice of organic solvent can depend on several factors,
such as the properties of the column, the shape and size of the peaks, and the viscosity. Viscosity is one of
the factors that should be considered. A solvent with a high viscosity can cause improper mixing with the
aqueous solvent and a high back pressure in the instrument, which is problematic because it can damage
both the mechanical parts of the instrument and the packing particles of the columns. Furthermore, it
can affect the flow rates and retention times, which can lead to poor performance, ultimately resulting
in a deficient separation of analytes. Therefore, it is desirable to use solvent with a low viscosity, such
as ACN.44 The aqueous solvent is often a buffer, which is advantageous because of the buffer’s ability
to resist pH changes even if a small amount of acid or base is added to it. Buffers containing inorganic
ions, such as potassium and sodium acetate, should be avoided in LC-MS/MS as they can cause several
problems in the analysis by causing ion suppression or can form MS adducts with sodium and potassium,
which can contaminate the ion source. Therefore, it is a good idea to substitute buffers that are more
compatible with MS, such as ammonium acetate, ammonium formate, or ammonium bicarbonate.49
In the separation and analysis of PFAS, it is common to use a binary gradient elution with a constant flow
rate.43 With a binary gradient elution, two solvents (organic and aqueous) are used as the mobile phase,
and the composition changes over time as a function of the gradient. This provides a more sufficient
rapid elution of all the components.46 In reversed-phase chromatography, the composition of the mobile
phase starts with a majority of polar aqueous solvent, and the polarity of the mobile phase is decreased
by adding more organic solvent to the mobile phase.30 By changing the mobile phase and increasing the
amount of organic protic solvent, such as MeOH, the hydroxyl group on MeOH will interact strongly
with the silica, which will break the PFASs’ hydrogen bonding with the silica gel and weaken the overall
retention of the PFASs to the column. Furthermore, the mobile phase will also become less polar, which
will strengthen the mobile phase and cause the PFASs to elute with the mobile phase.
Delay column
In PFAS analysis, it is a good idea to place a delay column just before the injection
valve. According to the guidance document for the analysis of PFAS in food by the EURL POPs, a

delay column is recommended because the tubing in the system often consists of polytetrafluoroethylene
(PTFE), which is a fluoropolymer and can contaminate the extracts. The delay column will delay the

1.6.3
Mass spectrometry
Mass spectrometry is often used to provide both quantitative and qualitative information after chro-
matographic separation of ions. This is also the case for the detection and monitoring of PFAS in food
matrices.41,48 The sample is injected into the mass spectrometer by the sample inlet. When the mass
spectrometer is coupled to a chromatography system, an interface is necessary, as the LC and MS in-
struments are otherwise not compatible due to pressure differences. The sample will then be eluted from
the LC system to the MS system, where it will be led into the ion source and transformed into gas-phase
ions. The ions will then be accelerated by an electromagnetic field leading to the mass analyser, which
will separate the ions based on the mass-to-charge (m/z) ratio. Lastly, the ions will be counted by a
detector and the signal will be recorded and processed by the MS data system.50
Ionisation method
After injection of the sample into the MS system, the sample is ionised. The
electrospray ionisation (ESI) mode is the most common ionisation method for PFAS analysis.41 Since
most PFASs have an acidic character and are deprotonated in most environments, the negative ionisation
mode is often chosen.30 ESI is well suited for moderately polar molecules and is also good for analysing
PFAS, since PFAS often has a polar functionality. ESI is considered a soft ionisation technique, as the
sample is subjected to a relatively small amount of energy, which causes little fragmentation to occur.
This means that fragments with high mass, such as the molecular ion, are commonly observed, as opposed
to hard ionisation techniques, where fragments with low mass are more common.49
The interface of negative ESI can be seen in Figure 8. In ESI, the dissolved extract is pumped through
a narrow steel capillary with a strong electric field (3-5 kV), causing it to be nebulised at the tip of the
capillary and creating an aerosol of charged droplets. The droplets are then subjected to heat and dry
nitrogen to cause evaporation, resulting in single charged droplets, which can then be accelerated by an
electric field and transferred into the part of the mass spectrometer with high vacuum: the mass analyser
and detector.49
Mass analyser and scan mode
The mass analyser used to analyse most PFAS compounds is a
triple quadrupole (QqQ), also called tandem mass spectrometry (abbreviated MS/MS).30 A quadrupole
is constructed of a set of four parallel, cylindrical or hyperbolical metal rods. The quadrupoles are applied
with both a constant voltage and a varying radio-frequency voltage, allowing transmission only for ions
with a particular m/z. By varying the voltages, the paths of the different ions are either stabilised or
destabilised. The ions with the destabilised paths collide with the rods and are lost before they can
reach the detector. The ions with the stabilised paths travel down in between the rods towards the
detector. This makes it possible to scan across a range. The quadrupole can also be set up to monitor
one specific m/z value, then monitor another m/z value, and so on.
This is done by stepping the
voltages and is useful when detecting specific target analytes.46,49 A triple quadrupole is constructed of
two quadrupoles separated by a collision cell. In the collision cell, the ions collide with an inert gas, such
as Ar or N2, at ∼10−5 bar, causing the ions to undergo fragmentation.46,49 The triple quadrupole can
operate in different modes of acquisition, in which the multiple reaction monitoring (MRM) mode, also

1.6.4
Fragmentation patterns
To find out which product ions are formed in the collision cell, it is important to understand how the
fragmentation occurs. The fragmentation pattern of the precursor ion to form the product ion is based
on the dissociation energy (BDE) between each atom. The α-cleavage, also called radical-site-initiated
fragmentation, is one of the most common one-bond cleavages. α-cleavage occurs when the bond between
the functional group and the α-position is cleaved.50
For carboxylic acids, α-cleavage can occur on either side of the C––O, resulting in COOH or OH loss,
respectively. For ether compounds, α-cleavage and loss of alkoxy radicals are some of the principal modes
of fragmentation.50
For PFAS compounds, the BDE is often high for the terminal –CF3 group. For fluorotelomers specific,
the bond between the –CF2 and –CH2 group(s) has a high BDE, which will make it unlikely to be
cleaved here. For long-chain PFASs, the BDE is often lower in the middle of the chain of C-F groups.
For the rest of the bonds, it is difficult to say much about a trend between the bonds. In ether compounds,
the BDE is typically lower for bonds between an oxygen atom and the adjacent carbonfluorine group
than for the rest of the bonds in the compound. A CF2 group directly attached to a carboxylic acid

also typically has a lower BDE than the rest of the molecule and the BDE between CF2 and COOH
typically increases as the chain of the C-F groups increases. However, for a sulfonate group, the CF2
group directly attached to a sulfonate group has not shown to have a lower BDE.51,52
1.6.5
Use of internal standard
Internal standard (IS) is useful for accurate quantitative analysis and is used to provide accurate results
and eliminate differences between each initial sample. It can be used to ensure that error generation
and sample loss are minimised between each step during sample preparation. In order to validate the
complete analytical procedure, the IS should be added in a constant amount to all samples prior to
the extraction.33,41 For quantification with LC-MS/MS, it is preferred to use ISs that are structural
analogues and stable isotopically labelled, because they have the same structure and chemical properties
as their native target analytes. ISs with the same chemical properties as their native analyte will result in
similar chemical behaviour during the analysis, but will ultimately be separated in the MS part because
of their differing masses. The ISs for PFAS compounds typically have the same structure as the analyte,
where two or more carbon atoms are replaced with their 13C isotope. In addition to ensuring error
generation and minimising sample loss, IS can compensate for matrix effects.35 It is worth mentioning
that, although the use of IS may compensate for matrix effects, the approach may not overcome the loss
of sensitivity due to matrix effects. Furthermore, ISs are costly and might not always be commercially
available for all compounds.35,53
1.6.6
Matrix effects
When detecting specific ions, matrix effects might occur and LC-MS/MS often suffers from matrix effects.
Matrix effects are caused by interference from background ions or matrix compounds that co-elute with
the target analytes. Co-eluting matrix compounds can alter the ionisation efficiency of target analytes
and influence the signal intensity because of competition for available charges and access to the droplet
surface for gas-phase emission during the electrospray process.53 The background ions can affect the
analyte signal and cause an artificial decrease in the signal, known as ion suppression, or an artificial
increase in the signal, known as ion enhancement. Therefore, it is important to analyse the effect of
the matrix on the PFAS analytes along with the recovery efficiency. The typical matrix effect must
be between 80% and 120 %. If the matrix effect is below 80%, it is considered ion suppression and if
it is above 120%, it is considered ion enhancement.31 In addition to IS, the standard addition method
is also available to compensate for matrix effects for complex matrices. This method includes spiking
each sample with known amounts of analyte to an unknown sample. Standard addition with multiple
solutions of constant volume can be used to monitor PFAS levels in unknown fruit samples. With this
method, multiple solutions containing equal amounts of fruit are spiked with varying concentrations of
the PFAS analytes in constant volumes. The result is ideally a linear relation between the signal and
the analyte added to the standard, which allows for determination of the unknown concentration by
extrapolation of the zero analyte signal.53

Objectives of the thesis
Background for the study
The Danish Veterinary and Food Administration (Fødevarestyrelsen)
recommends that Danes eat 600 g of fruits and vegetables per day, where at least half of them should
be vegetables.55 According to EFSA, ”Fruits and fruit products” have been found to be one of the main
food categories that contribute to the total PFAS exposure for all population groups. They expect that
for European citizens 13% of the PFAS exposure through food comes from fruits.56 However, this is an
assessment based on a limited number of analyses, taking into account that it is a very diverse food
group. From the data, it is not clear, for example, which fruits are primarily contaminated with PFAS.
Therefore, it is necessary to study the PFAS levels in various fruits to close the knowledge gaps.57
In collaboration with the Danish Veterinary and Food Administration (DVFA), it is determined which
fruits to study. First, it is important to analyse the fruits that are the most popular in Denmark. This
was found in a report made by the DTU National Food Institute called ”The dietary habits in Denmark
2011-2013”.58 Here, apples, pears, bananas, muskmelons, oranges, mandarins, and grapes are at the
top of the list. Muskmelons, oranges, and mandarins are also a part of the top of the list, however,
we have excluded those fruits from the study, since they have a non-edible peel. Yet, bananas are still
included in the study, since pesticides are highly utilised on banana plantations, which can lead to PFAS
contamination.59 Furthermore, fresh strawberries are also included in the study, as a recent study from
the UK discovered PFAS pesticides in 95% of their strawberries (see Section 4.4.1).60
For these reasons, this study will include the analysis of PFAS compounds in apples, pears, bananas,
grapes, and strawberries.
Aim of the study
Based on the limited number of analyses and knowledge gaps of PFASs in fruits,
the following aims for thesis were formulated: 1) Development and validation of a analytical method for
detecting fluorotelomer and native PFAS replacement compounds in fruits at low detection limits, 2)
Obtain LOQs for fluorotelomers and native PFAS replacements in fruits, 3) Application of the method
to assess the presence of PFAS in commonly consumed fruits in Denmark.
In order to fulfil the aims of the thesis, the MRM method is established by the MRM method builder
and then the method is tested in connection with the pre-experiments for validation. Furthermore, the
compounds are extracted by the QuEChERS method, and the analysis is performed with LC-MS/MS
in MRM mode. The results from the analysis data can help us validate the method and determine
the LOQs. After method validation, the analytical method is used to analyse 60 fruits (20 apples, 10
bananas, 10 grapes, 10 pears, and 10 strawberries) collected by the DFVA to detect and quantify the
occurrence of PFAS. This can give an indication of the extent to which these 14 compounds are present
in some of the most consumed fruits in Denmark and how they contribute to the total PFAS exposure.
Hypothesis
The working hypothesis for the thesis is: “Significant levels of PFAS compounds, including
fluorotelomer compounds and native PFAS replacements, are present in commonly consumed fruits in
Denmark, contributing notably to the overall PFAS exposure in Denmark.”

3.4.4
Validation
The method was validated following the criteria from EURL POPs guidance document to demonstrate
the linearity, trueness, precision, and limit of quantification.43
As the calibration curve should contain at least five concentration points, quantification was done from
six level standard calibration curves (weighted 1/x, forced through zero) with the concentrations 0, 0.05,
0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 2.0 and 5.0 ng mL–1 in 75% MeOH, In the assessment of linearity of the calibration curve,
the least-squared regression method was used, where the measure of linearity was the square of the corre-
lation coefficient R2. The requirement for the correlation coefficient was R2 ≥0.95. For each extraction,
calibration standards were run to create the calibration curve.
The trueness and precision were determined based on a recovery study conducted on five fruit matrices.
The validation was carried out on four replicates at five spiking levels for apples and strawberries: 0.001,
0.005, 0.01, 0.1 and 0.5 ng g–1 wet weight fruit (w.w.), which served as pre-experiments. For bananas,
grapes, and pears, the validation was carried out on four replicates at four spiking levels: 0.005, 0.01,
0.1 and 0.5 ng g–1 w.w.
The trueness is another term for the average apparent recovery (or spike recovery) and is defined by
the proportion of analyte that is added to the samples in the spiking experiments, which is extracted,
detected, and quantified. The spike recovery was calculated as the ratio between the detected analyte
minus the detected analyte in the blank (if there is a signal) and the added concentration. It can also
be expressed as % recovery, which can be seen in Eq. 1, where C is the observed concentration of the
samples (spiked samples), the blank (blank) and the initially added concentration (added), respectively.

Lack of second transition
In general, the lack of a second transition can be explained by the variation
of transitions for each instrument. Furthermore, it can be explained by the molecule’s ability to fragment.
For molecules that are persistent and for which the BDEs are high, it would require more energy in the
collision cell to fragment them. It would therefore be necessary to alter the method, which could be by
choosing another MRM transition for the compounds that did not exhibit a second transition. PFAS
compounds with only one specific MS/MS transition should be verified using a second chromatographic
separation method or another MS method. LC with a different column or eluent could be a second
chromatographic separation method, while another MS method could be a high resolution MS method,
like Orbitrap.43
4.1.2
Ion ratio
The ion ratio for the analysis samples should be within ± 30% of the average of the calibration standards
from the same sequence. The ion ratio is measured as the area of the qualifier peak divided by the area
of the quantifier peak.
In general, the peak ratios were within the acceptable range. However, it was observed that the ion ratios
for low level calibrants were sometimes not within the ± 30% range, which could be caused by the noise
and the increased sensitivity of the lower concentrations. This was also observed for samples with low
spiking levels, where it was more difficult to identify the qualifier peak due to noise, which ultimately
resulted in ion ratios that were not within the ± 30% range.
Furthermore, for 4:2 FTS and the corresponding ISs, the ion ratios were also not within the acceptable
range. Here, the peaks for the calibrants fit the Gaussian shape and were easy to integrate. However, the
sample peaks were very broad and some of the peaks were difficult to integrate, resulting in inconsistent
ion ratios that were often outside the acceptable range.

4.2
Procedure for extraction and analysis of PFAS compounds
The structural similarities between legacy and alternative PFAS compounds allow alternative compounds,
for the most part, to be determined with the same analytical techniques as are used for legacy PFAS.19
However, since the procedure can be influenced by the functional group, a validation study must be per-
formed to confirm that the method could be extended to fluorotelomers and native PFAS replacements.
In this study, the procedure was used to analyse PFAS levels at 0.001 ng g–1. Being on the pico scale,
which is a very low level, the requirements for sample preparation and analysis are higher. This means
that small changes to the method could alter the results a lot, which could mean that the method is not
that robust. As an example, for one of the runs, no results were observed, where one of the explanations
could be the change in the pH of the buffer. The pH of the buffer was below 8, when it was supposed to
be 8-8.5. This could affect the charge of the compounds, which in turn would affect their behaviour in
the LC-MS/MS.
4.2.1
Calibrants
Since quantification is based on the calibration curve, it is important that the calibrants have clear peaks
to integrate and provide the right values. For most parts, the calibrants had clear peaks that were easy
to integrate, resulting in concentrations close to the true value and calibration curves that could be used
for quantification. However, for low concentration calibrants, at 0.05 and 0.1 ng mL–1, for FTSs and
FTCAs, it was difficult to distinguish the peaks from the noise, resulting in the calibrants not always
being close to their true values. Therefore, the results obtained for the lower spiking levels (0.001-0.01
ng g–1) might not be as reliable, since the quantification of these levels relies on the low concentration
calibrants. In the future, this could cause problems with the reproducibility of the results.
4.2.2
Importance of IS
It is necessary to consider the importance of the use of IS, since no ISs were available for the native PFAS
replacements (9Cl-PF3ONS, 11Cl-PF3OUdS, HFPO-DA and NaDONA) and 10:2 FTS. HFPO-DA and
10:2 FTS do have analogous labelled ISs; however, they were not obtained for this project.
In previous studies, analytes that lacked an analogous labelled IS were quantified with the use of the IS
with the closest retention time. For 9Cl-PF3ONS and 11Cl-PF3OUdS, the IS for PFOS, 13C4-PFOS,
were used to quantify the compounds.66,71 For ADONA, the isotopically labelled analogue for PFHpA,
13C4-PFHpA, was used as the IS.71 These insights can be useful for further development of the method
if the ISs are still not developed or available for the native PFAS replacements in the near future.
For 10:2 FTS, the data in this project were processed with two approaches; without IS and with M2-8:2
FTS (the 8:2 FTS IS) as the IS. This was done to find out whether it was best to use an IS that has
slightly different chemical properties or to not use an IS at all. From the validation studies, it is observed
that the use of IS is important, which can be seen, for example, in the pear samples. The LC-MS/MS
system was not entirely stable for the run with the pears, resulting in fluctuating peak areas. For the
samples with IS, the ISs could compensate for the fluctuating data and still provide a calibration curve
where no calibration points were omitted. However, for the native PFAS replacements, it was difficult to
obtain reliable results, since some of the calibration points had to be omitted to fit the linear calibration
curve. In Figure 21a, the calibration curve for HFPO-DA in pears can be seen as an example of an
unstable system, while in Figure 21b, it can be observed how the calibration curve for 8:2 FTUCA for
the same run looked after correction with IS.
This showed that it is important to use an IS for each analyte, which is why the IS for 8:2 FTS was
used as the IS for 10:2 FTS. From the retention times (Figure 20), it can be observed that the retention

4.2.3
Scaling of the procedure
As part of the development, the QuEChERS method was altered to fit the fruit samples better; however,
not all aspects of the procedure were altered, which is something to consider when working with the
project in the future. With the alteration, the amount of sample went from being originally 2.00 g to
5.00 g in this project to fit the recommended 0.001 ng g–1 detection limit.43 However, the amount of salts
and dSPE powders used was not increased, which could have resulted in a less effective clean-up. As an
example, with 2.00 g of fruit samples, 2.00 g MgSO4 would have been enough to absorb all the water.
However, with 5.00 g of fruit samples, the amount of MgSO4 used was not enough to absorb and remove
all the water from the samples, which could be a problem, since it made it more difficult to isolate all the
supernatant from salts and powders during pipetting. With bananas, it was much easier to pipette the
supernatant from the salts and powders, which could be a result of the lower water content in bananas
compared to the other fruits. However, since there is a large amount of carbohydrates in bananas, the
amount of dSPE powders might not have been enough for an efficient clean-up, which could have affected
the results.
4.2.4
Analytical methods used in previous studies
Previously, different methods have been used to extract and clean-up PFAS compounds in various fruit
matrices. Although the QuEChERS method has emerged as the most widely applied extraction and
clean-up technique for the isolation of PFAS from fruits, other methods have also been developed.72
In a study by Roberts et al. (2022), it was shown that although the QuEChERS method is useful, it does
not adequately remove all interferences in strawberry samples and requires further sample treatment for
long-chain PFAS samples.31 This is only an example for strawberries, but it can be an indication that
the QuEChERS method might not adequate for both extraction and clean-up at 0.001 ng g–1 levels, and
that an additional clean-up step can be added.
Page 31 of 54
==PAGE:37==
Sanni Gao
January 30, 2025
In a study by Genualdi et al. (2021), a WAX clean-up step was added instead of altering the dSPE
components, which was ultimately validated for more complicated food matrices and used to successfully
analyse 91 different types of foods in a total diet study.40
Ericson et al. (2008) used liquid extraction, SPE, and additional clean-up with EnviCarb to study PFCA
and PFSA levels in pooled fruit samples containing pears, apples, bananas, and oranges, resulting in
LODs from 0.001 to 0.65 ng g–1.73
A study by van der Vegt et al. (2022) compared the use of dSPE and SPE to analyse PFAS levels in
apples. The results were that the dSPE was an easy method, which allowed for analysis of both ionic and
non-ionic PFASs. However, the LOQ was generally higher in the samples when using dSPE, which could
be caused by a lower matrix-to-column ratio and background signals. With the use of SPE-WAX, analysis
of specific types of PFASs was not achievable; however, the clean-up were more powerful compared to
dSPE allowing lower LOQs for some PFASs. This showed that the specificity for SPE-WAX was higher,
as it had a more powerful clean-up, but also failed to isolate some of the PFAS compounds. dSPE
showed to be more versatile, as it could analyse more compounds at the same; however, the specificity
and selectivity were much lower.74
In a study by Meng et al. (2022), an extraction and clean-up method was developed to quantify 45 PFAS
compounds in fruits and vegetables, hereunder FTSs, FTCAs, FTUCAs, and the fluoroether compounds
studied in this project. Instead of QuEChERS, an extraction with basic MeOH followed by SPE clean-
up with Oasis WAX SPE cartridge was performed. This procedure was successful for almost all the
compounds in grapes and might be an alternative to the QuEChERS method used in this study.70
Previously, there have been challenges associated with monitoring HFPO-DA and other PFECAs, one
of them being their poor LC-MS/MS sensitivity. The poor sensitivity appeared to be partially related
to in-source fragmentation during ESI.19 A study by Brase and Spink in 2020, showed that altering
the ESI probe position could improve the LC-MS/MS sensitivity for several PFECA compounds.24 This
improvement is outside the scope of the project; however, it shows that alternative compounds can be
incorporated into existing PFAS analysis methods by making small adjustments to the method.
4.3
Method validation
The method used for the analysis of fluorotelomers and native PFAS replacements should be validated
to prove that the developed and optimised method could be extended to cover the studied compounds
and to prove the method’s accuracy and sensitivity.46 The different parameters that must be fulfilled
were linearity, trueness and precision, in order to determine the LOQs.43 The results for apples and
strawberries from the pre-experiments were also included in the results for method validation.
4.3.1
Linearity
Linearity is measured to make sure that the signal response is proportional to the quantity of the analyte.
Ideally, the response, and therefore the calibration curve, should be linear.
All of the compounds, apart from 6:2 FTS, showed linearity by having R2 ≥0.95. For 6:2 FTS specifically,
the calibrants with concentrations 2 and 5 ng mL–1 did not integrate to the amount of that it should,
which was why those points were omitted from the calibration curve.
Unfortunately, since there was an inconsistency between the procedure and the method in MS worksta-
tion, the calibration standard 2.0 ng mL–1 had a concentration of 2.5 ng mL–1 instead of 2.0 ng mL–1,
although it was set to 2.0 ng mL–1 in the system. Since changing the method would require all the
data to be processed again and the time was limited, the method was not changed, and therefore the
calibration curve had a small error. This proves the importance of 1/x weighting, which was used to
apply more weight to the calibration points with the lower concentrations and less weight to the high

4.3.4
Trends for compounds across fruits
Across the different fruit matrices, it was possible to observe some trends in the resulting LOQs. In
general, compounds with similar chemical structure were observed to show approximately the same be-
haviour. With a few exceptions, the LOQs were similar for all the fruits for the FTCA and FTUCA
compounds, respectively. Only the FTS compounds exhibited very different behaviours within the dif-
ferent FTS compounds. This could be partly due to the fact that 4:2 FTS was the ultimately shortest
compound in the study, which could have affected its behaviour. Furthermore, a contamination of 6:2
FTS was observed in the samples, which could originate from the laboratory or the instrument. Despite
the fact that the native PFAS replacements are all ether compounds, their chemical structure still vary,
which could explain their different behaviour and LOQs. For the Cl-PFESA compounds, which have
very similar chemical structures, their behaviour was almost identical and, as a result, their LOQs were
also almost identical. In the following sections, some of the observed trends for certain compounds or
groups of compounds will be discussed.
4:2 FTS
For 4:2 FTS, it was observed that the analyte peak co-eluted with the another peak in the
samples. For high levels of apples, pears, and strawberries, the analyte peaks were larger than the co-
eluting peaks and it could therefore be quantified. However, the IS sample peaks were very broad and,
for pears and strawberries, the two co-eluting peaks fused into one big peak, which meant that it was
difficult to distinguish the two peaks and integrate them separately resulting in misleading peak areas.
An example of this can be seen in Figure 23a. For the IS sample peaks for apples, the two co-eluting
peaks had two peak tops that were separated enough that the peaks were easily distinguishable, which
is why an LOQ could be reported for apples. For bananas and grapes, the ISs showed clear and narrow
peaks. However, here the sample peaks co-eluted with another peak, which was so tall that the actual
analyte peak could not be chosen for integration, as the signal-to-noise ratio would then be too small.
This meant that for banana and grape samples at low spiking concentrations, it was not possible to
quantify the compounds. An example of this can be seen for a banana sample in Figure 23b.
A way to solve the issue with co-eluting peaks could be to replace the low resolution method MS/MS
triple quadrupole method with a high resolution MS method.

6:2 FTS
For calibration curves, the amount of 6:2 FTS for the two high calibration levels (2.0 and 5.0

intense peaks for this particular calibration level caused problems for the integration of the 6:2 FTS
compound. For the calibrant with the concentration 5.0 ng mL–1, the response of the analyte was not

Furthermore, the reagent blanks showed tall
peaks that contributed with more than 30% of the levels in the analysis samples, which could be due to
contamination of 6:2 FTS from the sample or the system.
FTCA and FTUCA
The results for trueness, precision, and LOQs indicate that FTUCA could be
easier observed than FTCA, especially in low concentration samples. In general, FTUCA compounds
exhibited lower LOQs than FTCAs, since most LOQs for FTUCAs were 0.005 ng g–1, while for FTCA
most LOQs were 0.1 ng g–1. This means that the double bond in FTUCA must have a significance in
either fragmentation or detection of the compounds, since the two compounds form the same product
ion in the quantifier peak. It might have been easier for FTUCA to form the ion, since it breaks and
forms one bond less than FTCA to form the triple bonded fragment. It could also be due to the double
bond in FTUCA, which makes FTUCA more stable than FTCA before fragmentation. This results in a
higher intensity for FTUCA, which makes it easier to detect the ions in the chromatogram. Furthermore,
for FTCAs and their ISs, it was observed that the ion ratios fluctuated a lot, which meant that a lot of
both calibrants and samples could not be passed within the ion ratios. For example, for 8:2 FTCA in
strawberries, the ion ratios for the calibrants were in the range 120-180%, while the samples were in the
range 50-110%.
Ether compounds
In general, ether compounds are volatile in nature, due to their weak intermolec-
ular forces. Since all the native PFAS replacements are fluoroether compounds, they are more volatile
than the fluorotelomer compounds. This could have affected the quantification of the compounds, as
they could have evaporated from the rest of the sample during sample preparation or from the filter
vials, where we stored our samples. However, the lack of ISs for the replacement compounds might have
also affected the quantification of the compounds. In general, the results for the ether compounds were
good and very satisfactory. However, for some matrices, even better results could possibly have been
gained by not storing the samples in filter vials and by the use of ISs.
Chain length
For the FTS compounds, it is apparent that the chain length had an impact on the
detection of the ions. 8:2 and 10:2 FTS were found to be easier to detect than 4:2 and 6:2 FTS, resulting
in lower LOQs for the long-chain FTSs. For the FTCAs and FTUCAs, there was no clear trend based
on the chain length. For FTCAs in apples, the LOQs decreased with increasing chain length; however,
this could also be a coincidence, since apple is the only matrix for FTCAs that has a higher LOQ than
the rest of the fruits.

4.4.1
Other reports on PFAS in fruits
Previously, other studies have been conducted to quantify PFAS levels in various fruit matrices, which
can be compared to the results in this study.
In a study by Cornelis et al. (2012), occurrence of PFOS and PFOA was observed in the range <0.017-0.7
and 0.037-1.6 ng g–1 fresh fruit, respectively, in apples and strawberries. The apples and strawberries
were pooled into a ”fruit” category, so the PFAS levels were not provided for each fruit type.75
In a study by Ericson et al. (2008), 11 PFAS compounds (PFCA or PFSA) were analysed in pooled fruit
samples containing pears, apples, bananas, and oranges. The results showed that all compounds were
under the LOD for the fruit samples.73
In another study, the level of PFAS was studied in strawberries bought from different UK supermarkets
and shops. The study monitored 12 PFAS compounds within the PFCA and PFSA categories, where
only perfluorobutaonic acid (PFBA) was detected in the quantity 0.20 ng g–1 fresh fruit.31
In a study from Kause et al.(2024), six types of fruits was studied for PFAS. The studied fruits were
apples, strawberries, white grapes, plums, pears and red berries and were bought in Dutch supermarkets
with varying origins.
LOQs were determined for the fruits as one category and were not separated
for each fruit matrix. 50 fruits were analysed and for 21 of those fruits, the presence of PFAS could
be confirmed. No specific fruits were standing out, and there was a lower occurence of PFASs, where
mainly perfluoroundecanoic acid (PFUnDA) and PFOA were found, all at concentrations below 0.006
ng g–1 w.w.76
Sznajder-Katarzy´nska et al. conducted a study in 2018, where 10 PFAS compounds were studied in
bananas, apples, and strawberries.
The fruits were all purchased from Polish markets with varying
origins. All of the studied PFASs were PFCAs and PFSAs, where 7 of the 10 studied compounds were
not detected in the fruits. The detected PFAS compounds in the fruits were PFBA, PFOA and PFOS.
None of the PFAS compounds could be studied in the strawberry samples. For bananas, only PFBA
Page 39 of 54
==PAGE:45==

January 30, 2025
could be detected and in the range 5.330 to 13.450 ng g–1 w.w., which is somewhat a high concentration.
For apples, PFBA and PFOA were detected but in the range 0.049 to 0.450 ng g–1 wet weight, which is
significantly lower than the PFAS levels in bananas.77
In a more recent study by the Pesticide Action Network UK, 10 different PFAS pesticides were present in
various fruits and vegetables. They found PFAS pesticides in strawberries, grapes, and apples, where the
strawberries were the biggest concern, as 95% of the 120 samples contained PFAS pesticides. For grapes
and apples, the percentage of samples containing PFAS pesticides were 61% and 2%, respectively.60
During the literature search, the fluorotelomers and native PFAS replacement analysed in this study
have previously only been found in crops grown near fluorochemical production plants (FCPPs) (within
15 km). In a study conducted by Pancras et al. (2024) in the Netherlands, mainly PFOA and HFPO-DA
were detected in apples, grapes, pears, and strawberries grown near a FCPP. The highest level of PFAS
was detected in the crops grown nearest the FCPP. For crops grown 1-14 km away from the FCPP, the
PFAS compounds were detected at pico-levels and HFPO-DA was generally detected at higher levels
than PFOA. HFPO-DA was detected an average of 0.002 ng g–1 w.w. for apples, 0.014 ng g–1 w.w. for
grapes, 0.004 ng g–1 w.w. for pears and 0.008 ng g–1 w.w. for strawberries.67 The highest average of
detected HFPO-DA was found in grapes, which was also the one fruit where HFPO-DA was detected in
this study. In another study conducted by Laster et al. (2024) in Belgium, 4:2, 6:2 and 8:2 FTS, and
11Cl-PF3OUdS amongst other PFAS compounds were analysed in homegrown crops placed close to a
FCPP. The fruits were pooled into large fruits (where apples and pears were included) and small fruits
(where grapes and strawberries were included). 4:2 FTS was detected at an average of 0.384 ng g–1 w.w.
for large fruits and at 0.325 ng g–1 w.w. for small fruits. 6:2 FTS was detected at an average of 0.108
ng g–1 w.w. for large fruits and at 0.457 ng g–1 w.w. for small fruits. The mean level of 8:2 FTS and
11Cl-PF3OUdS were both <LOQ.78 The amount of detected 4:2 and 6:2 FTS was fairly high, which
differs from the result presented in this study, since none of the FTS compounds were detected in any of
the fruits. This could be a sign, that the fruits in this study were not grown near a FCPP.

one of the reasons why PFASs might not be observed in large quantities and there are no current EU
regulations for PFAS in fruits. On the other hand, eggs, fish meat and various other meats generally
have a higher protein content, which means the risk of PFAS contamination is higher, as the PFASs can
bind to those proteins.
4.4.3
Exposure assessment
Although fruits and fruit products are one of the main food categories contributing to the total exposure
to PFAS, there are no EU regulations on those products.4 With an exposure assessment, it is possible
to estimate the Danes’ exposure to PFAS through fruits.
First, the TWI for the four PFAS legacy
compounds was found for the average Dane. Since the TWI has been established for the four legacy
compounds, it was not accurate for fluorotelomers and native PFAS compounds, as the toxicity varies
with the structure of the compound.
However, as there are no TWI established for fluorotelomers
or emerging PFAS compounds, this was the best way to estimate how the exposure fits the tolerable
amount of PFAS. The average body weight of a woman is 71.4 kg, which means that the TWI is 314.2

4.4.4
TF and EOF results
Samples were sent to the University of Graz in Austria, where the TF and EOF content was determined
for the 60 fruit samples. For TF analysis, the 60 fruits were freeze dried and sent separately, while for
the EOF analysis, the samples from the same matrix were pooled into 5.00 g samples. The samples
were extracted and cleaned up by QuEChERS and the samples were sent for EOF analysis. For the TF
analysis, the method LOD (MLD) was found to be 240 ng F g–1. Only 12 samples (4 apples, 6 grapes
and 2 bananas) of the 60 samples showed TF values above MLD, where the concentrations ranged from
817 ng F g–1 (grape) to 2950 ng F g–1 (apple). For apples, bananas, pears and strawberries, the median
concentrations were <MLD for TF.
For the EOF analysis, MLD was found to be 10 ng F g–1. Three of the five extracts showed EOF
levels above MLD, which were strawberry (40 ng F g–1), apple (18 ng F g–1) and pear (69 ng F
g–1). These were also the three fruit matrices that were observed by LC-MS/MS to obtain the highest
concentration of PFAS in the exposure assessment. Apart from PFASs, the EOF results can also include
other organofluorine compounds, such as pesticides. The high concentrations of EOF and TF might be
due to the high MLD for the method compared to the targeted LC-MS/MS analysis.
Only apples showed both EOF and TF concentrations above MLD, where the EOF concentration ac-
counted for 2.6% of the median TF. These results are consistent with the LC-MS/MS analysis results,
where it was observed from the exposure assessment (Table 8), that apples had the highest amount
of detected PFAS. The grape and banana samples did not show EOF concentrations, suggesting that
the samples did not contain organofluorine or that the organofluorine had been removed from the ex-
tract during sample preparation. The high TF content suggests a high amount of inorganic fluoride
or non-extractable organic fluoride, and further investigations are necessary to identify these fluoride
compounds.
5
Conclusions
Based on the results of the study, all three aims for the thesis was fulfilled.
1) The existing method, used for legacy PFAS compounds, was extended to analyse most fluorotelomers
and native PFAS replacements (fluoroether compounds) in fruits. The method was successfully validated
for 12 compounds with most trueness and precision results within the acceptable range and good linearity
with R2 ≥0.95.
2) The obtained LOQs was between 0.005 and 0.1 ng g–1 for FTSs, 0.01-0.1 ng g–1 for FTCAs with one
exception, 0.005-0.01 ng g–1 for FTUCAs and 0.005-0.5 ng g–1 for native PFAS replacements. Most of
the time, the LOQs were similar for the same compound (like 6:2 FTUCA) across the fruit matrices.
For the same compound group (like FTCAs), the LOQs were also similar. However, across compound
groups (e.g. comparing FTCAs with FTUCAs), the LOQs were not similar. These observations mean
that the method can to a large extent be extrapolated across fruits, but cannot be extrapolated across
PFAS compounds. Due to the broad peaks for 4:2 FTS and contamination and non-linearity for 6:2
FTS, more work of the method would be required in order to extent further the scope of the method.
3) In the analysis of the 60 fruits, PFAS compounds was found in few samples, but only at non-quantifiable
levels.
Across all fruit matrices, FTUCAs was the most frequently detected compound group, and
individually, 6:2 FTUCA was the most frequently detected compound. As a result of the analysis of
the 60 fruits and the exposure assessment, the levels of fluorotelomer and native PFAS replacements in

fruits, available on the Danish market, are low. Thus, the hypothesis is rejected and the risk of PFAS
exposure through consumption of fruits is low for Danes.
6
Future perspectives
In the continuation of this project, the analytical method could be improved to achieve lower reporting
limits. With lower reporting limits, the detection will be more comprehensive and the exposure assess-
ment of PFAS compounds will be more accurate. First, this can be done by altering the amounts of
salts and dSPE sorbents to the amount of sample. Specifically, the amount of MgSO4 can be increased
to absorb the large amounts of water in fruits. It might be relevant to tailor the method to each fruit
matrix, since the varying composition of fruits can also require different amounts of sorbents to be the
most efficient. Following the QuEChERS method, an additional SPE-cleaning step can be added to
the procedure. A study by Genualdi et al. (2021) showed that to increase method sensitivity for more
complicated matrices, an additional SPE WAX clean-up step could be required.40
Furthermore, the study can also be extended to fruits outside of the scope of this project. For example,
the method can be extended to fruits with a non-edible peel, such as orange, muskmelons, and man-
darins. The method should be validated for each new fruit matrix, and the analysis of PFAS should be
based on at least 10 samples to obtain a representative estimate of the PFAS content in that fruit type.
