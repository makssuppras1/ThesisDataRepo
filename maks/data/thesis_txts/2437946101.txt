Abstract
Optical ﬁber communication is limited by dispersive and nonlinear propagation eﬀects. Because of the
global dependence on optical ﬁbers to provide the backbone of the global data networks it is sought to
approach the capacity of the optical ﬁber: the upper limit of information transfer. To do this, numerous
nonlinearity mitigation schemes have been devised and this project explores the possibilities in the
pulses used to modulate the baseband signals. The M-shaped pulses have previously been shown to
have favorable behavior in nonlinear channels and the these results are replicated. These pulses are
also successfully combined with probabilistic constellation shaping, demonstrating the suitability of
combining these techniques. Two new pulses are also proposed which seek to alter the decay of the
established pulses for modulation. A generalization of pulse shaping is explored in multipulse shaping
is also explored, where it is possible to alter the pulse shape depending on the symbol amplitude.

Resumé (Dansk)
Optisk ﬁber kommunikation er begrænset af dispersive og ikke-lineære eﬀekter under signal propager-
ing. Den globale afhængighed af optiske ﬁbre, til at bære den største mængde data på de global
netværk, gør det nødvendigt at efterstræbe kapaciteten af den optiske ﬁber: den ypperste øvre grænse
for data overførsel. I bestræbelsen på dette er ﬂere ikke-lineæritetskompensering blevet udtænkt og
dette projekt udforsker mulighederne i de pulse former some bruges til modulering af baseband
signalet. M-pulserne har i tidligere forskning vist fordelagtige egenskaber i forhold til ikke-lineære
kanaler og disse resultater er eftervist i dette projekt. Ydereligere bliver disse pulser nu kombineret
med sandsynlighedsfordelte konstellationsmønstre og det er derved demonstreret at disse metoder kan
kombineres. To nye pulser bliver også fremstillet; de baserer sig på justeringer af deres tidsmæssige
forfald. En generalisering af pulseformning bliver også udforsket, kaldet ﬂerpulsformning. I denne
metode bliver forskellige pulse tildelt symboler alt efter deres amplitude.

Chapter 2
Introduction
Society has been transformed with the ubiquitous access to fast and reliable network access and in
particular the Internet and global network at its backbone. The growth of the Internet traﬃc is at an
ever increasing pace, with a recent prediction stating that traﬃc will have an annual growth of 40 %,
which compounds to a three fold increase from 2016 to 2021[12]. Furthermore, compared to 2005 the
traﬃc in 2021 will be 127 times larger. Optical communication has enabled this current infrastructure
of modern society and advances in this ﬁeld has carried the growth of capacity on the Internet[30].
But it is becoming an increasingly burdensome challenge to tackle the optical ﬁber[14]. In Figure 2.1a,
the record achievable data rates for optical communication systems are plotted against time and there
is a trend towards a leveling oﬀin the data rate increases. This trend forecasts a looming “capacity
crunch”[30], which will reveal the upper limit at which data can be transmitted through optical ﬁbers.
The primary challenges of optical ﬁbers are dispersion and nonlinear eﬀects[15]. The conclusion
is that increasing distances will increase noise, but signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) can not be easily
increased by using a greater signal power, since this induces nonlinear interference. Likewise, at
short distance the signal power can not be increased indeﬁnitely because of the nonlinear eﬀects. As
shown in Figure 2.1b, the spectral eﬃciency, a measure of expected bit-rate given a used bandwidth, is
plotted against transmission distances. Even though there are orders of magnitude in diﬀerence in the
transmission distance, the spectral eﬃciency is not signiﬁcantly improved.

2.1
Overall structure of paper
In Chapter 3, the theory and background of communication systems with quadrature-amplitude
modulation and pulse shaping will be described. The optical ﬁber channel and its properties will also
be elaborated upon, especially in regards to the eﬀects of dispersion and the nonlinear eﬀects in optical
ﬁbers. Some nonlinearity compensation techniques will be explained, but the focus will mostly dwell
on pulse shaping for nonlinear channels – of which there have been several results in the recent years.
In Chapter 4, two new pulse shapes are proposed, which are based upon a well established pulse shape:
the raised cosine. The concept of multipulse shaping is also applied to the problems facing optical
ﬁber transmission and a scheme is proposed for combating nonlinearities. Finally, the simulation
parameters are deﬁned.
In Chapter 5, the results of the simulations are presented. A wide range of pulse parameters are
simulated across several launch powers and ﬁlter conﬁgurations. Based upon previously observed
results on the channel’s dispersion compensation schemes such simulations are also performed. A
comparison of the spectral eﬃciency for the various pulse shapes is made in order to pin down the
trade-oﬀs associated with the proposed and prior pulses. Finally, a simple combination of constellation
shaping and pulse shaping is made. All of this compares with a raised cosine baseline.
A discussion of the results and the approach taken is made in Chapter 6. The project is concluded in
Chapter 7.
2.2
Project description and statement
The achievable information rate through optical ﬁbers is limited by the non-linearities present in
high-energy signals. Techniques such as constellation shaping have sought to combat the non-linearities
by adapting the symbol constellations. This projects seeks to investigate how achievable information
rate can be improved through pulse shaping optimized for certain channel models and especially how
this can work in harmony with constellation shaping.
The questions which this projects seeks to answer are:
1. To what extend can quadrature-amplitude shaped signals improve symbol detection performance
in channels resembling those of optical ﬁbers?
2. How can families of pulse shapes be made, which allow for optimization of the achievable
information rate?
1. To what degree does these adaptations hinder or support that ability to improve the
achievable information rate?
The original problem statement included the following question
How usable are the noise characteristics of constellation shaped signals in optical ﬁbers
for designing pulse shapes?
It has been excluded from this reports considerations.

Chapter 3
Theory and background
In this chapter the theory behind communication systems and optical propagation will be laid out
and explained. The initial focus will be on the general overview of a communication system and its
components mostly basing it upon modern optical communication systems. This explanation will
also deal with modulated waveforms which will lead to the considerations regarding pulse shaping
in communication systems. To quantify communication, the concept of mutual information will be
introduced along other information theoretical concepts. Following this, the optical ﬁber is described
along with the theory behind the propagation of signals in optical ﬁbers. The two major eﬀects of ﬁber
impairment, dispersion and nonlinearities, will be explored leading up to the nonlinear Schrödinger
equation. Finally, the prior work in pulse shaping for nonlinear ﬁbers will be explored and discussed.

Digital communication systems are distinguished by transmitting values from a discrete alphabet,
in contrast to analog communication where the transmitted values are from a continuous range.
The advantages of digital signals are numerous, [17] lists cheap, reliable and miniaturized hardware
and standardized and generalized interfaces across coding, channel and network layer. Typical
communication system models are schematized as Figure 3.1 where a, potentially continuous, source is
encoded to digital data and then further encoded for channel speciﬁc needs before transmission[33]. At
reception the channel decoder recovers the digital data and a source decoder reconstructs the original
source signal – within some degree of error. This project deals with the match between channel
encoder, decoder and the channel itself and will thus not be concerned with any source coder details.
Before the channel coding can be elaborated upon it is ﬁrst necessary to say a few words about the
channel and what it entails. There is no doubt that the channel includes the medium through which the
communication takes place, but when, for example, an engineer is designing an optical network they
have several choices in terms of ampliﬁcation schemes, dispersion compensation and span lengths.

The general components of an optical transmission system will be brieﬂy outlined in the following;
later in this chapter, a subset of high-relevance to this project will be further discussed, but for now the
concepts will be mostly general. These paragraphs draw heavily from [17] for general communication
systems design and [1] for optical communication speciﬁc components. Starting on the transmitter side,
a binary signal is given as input. The input signal will be typically compressed to remove redundancy
and it can be assumed to be a uniformly distributed binary signal. This signal is passed through an
encoder which applies forward error correction (FEC) coding and maps binary digits to symbols for
transmission. The symbols are then passed through a pulse ﬁlter, yielding the baseband signal.
The baseband signal is in the electrical/digital domain and will now pass into the optical domain. This
transition depends upon the used modulation format. A widespread modulation format is Intensity
modulated/direct detection (IM/DD), which only modulates the intensity of the laser driver, hence
the name. A diﬀerent approach involves modulating the laser phase and amplitude, which gives
rise to the numerous high-order modulation formats such as QAM. The laser driver sets the center
or carrier wavelength and a electro-optic phase modulator applies the baseband signal to the laser.
Multiple modulated lasers, at diﬀerent wavelengths, can propagate along the same optical ﬁber in a
wavelength-division multiplexing (WDM) system.
During propagation the optical signal is ampliﬁed to compensate for ﬁber loss. Lumped and continuous
ampliﬁcation are both possible entirely in the optical domain. A standard “lumped” ampliﬁcation
scheme is using an erbium-doped ﬁber ampliﬁer (EDFA). Along with ampliﬁcation it is possible to
include other optical signal processing components in the ﬁber link. Fibers exhibit dispersion which
can be compensated for using special optical components. Optical networks will also include add-drop
multiplexers, band-pass ﬁlters and other components needed in routing.
At the receiving end the optical signal is converted to electrical signals through a demodulator. The
electrical signal is possibly ﬁltered and then sampled using a analog-to-digital converter with an
appropriate sampling rate. The recovery of the data signal is highly dependent on digital signal
processing, which enables compensation for linear eﬀects such as chromatic dispersion, ﬁltering
for channel eﬀects and pulse shapes, channel equalization, phase noise detection and nonlinearity
compensation. With the recovered or at least estimated symbols the error correction can be applied
and the output binary signal is delivered.

3.1.1
Quadrature Amplitude Modulation
On-oﬀkeying is widespread in optical communication, where it is typically called IM/DD. Thus
information is transmitted by either emitting a pulse of light or not propagating a pulse of light[1]. As
with other communication channels, it is also possible to modulate a carrier wave in order to transmit
information, among these is QAM. For the purposes of this thesis we will only discuss QAM signals.
QAM signals are characterized by symbols belonging to a set, S, called the constellation. The symbols
are complex valued, inﬂuencing both the amplitude of the carrier and the phase of the carrier. The
signal will then have the following waveform[17]
y(t) = re{u(t)} cos(2π fct) −im{u(t)} sin(2π fct)
where u(t) is the complex-valued, data carrying baseband waveform and fc is the carrier frequency –
in an optical system this would correspond to the driver laser’s wavelength. At the moment, the exact
waveform of u(t) will be ignored and instead it will be said that u(nT + d) ∈S for n ∈Z where T is
the symbol interval and d is a delay. That is, the waveform will at certain, periodic, intervals attain
values from the constellation set.
3.2
Pulse shapes for communication systems
The considerations for the rest baseband waveform is subject to pulse shaping. Given a discrete time
signal consisting of input symbol, d(n) ∈S, the baseband waveform is obtained by convolving it with
a pulse shape, g(t),
u(t) = d(n) ∗g(t)
The pulse shape, g(t), will determine how the transition between symbols occur and typical discussions
of pulse shaping dwell on issues of inter-symbol interference (ISI), spectral eﬃciency or orthogonal-
ity[19]. In Figure 3.3, a pulse shaped baseband signal is plotted in the complex domain; the symbols
are marked with red dots. The black curve traces how the amplitude and phase will tween from symbol
to symbol and from this it should be clear that the choice of pulse shape has a great inﬂuence on the
properties of the baseband signal. The three plots denotes diﬀerent parameters of the same pulse and it
is clear that they pass through the constellation symbols; it is less visible that they indeed do so at
regular intervals.
A set of requirements for pulses is outlined in [17]:
1. The pulse, g(t), must be 0 for t < −τ for some τ
2. The frequency spectrum of the pulse must be appropriately bandlimited
3. The retrieval of the symbols from the pulse modulated symbols should be simple and reliable
The ﬁrst and second requirements seem rather contradictory, as a signal can not be simultaneously
time and frequency limited[17]. However, these requirements can be slacked, by considering that the
out-of-band interferences must be small enough to be negligible or, alternatively, that g(t) rapidly
approaches 0.
The third requirement seems reasonable enough, but its vagueness hints at its inherent complexity.

3.2.3
Matched ﬁlters
Matched ﬁlters split the processing involved in pulse shaping between the transmitter and the receiver.
Given a pulse g(t) with Fourier transform G( f ) it is possible to factor it into two identical transfer
functions[17]
G( f ) = |P( f )|2 = P∗( f )P( f )
which through the properties of the Fourier transform yields that
g(t) = p∗(−t) ∗p(t)
The matched ﬁlter is then, simply, a ﬁlter, but the remarkable property is that the transfer function P( f )
will not in general hold the Nyquist ISI criterion. The transmitted pulses will have interference, but
after reception through the matched ﬁlter, the inter-symbol interference will, ideally, cancel out[17].
This assumes linear channel, whereas for a nonlinear channel, such as the optical ﬁber, the channel
will induce an intensity dependent phase shift (see Section 3.4).
From the point of view of detection and estimation theory, a matched ﬁlter is equivalent to a replica-
correlator detector, which maximizes SNR of the output of the ﬁlter. To clarify, the SNR is increased
for the test statistic/symbol compared to the SNR per sample[24]. The detectors are here concerned
with pulses which are subject to additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN), which can not be assumed
when overlapping pulses are used, as they are in the discussed communication systems.

3.2.5
Multipulse systems
Multipulse systems refer to, generally nonlinear, pulse modulation systems where diﬀerent pulse
shapes are transmitted for each symbol. Instead of simply ﬁltering, as in the case of pulse shaping for
pulse-amplitude modulation (PAM) and QAM, multipulse shaping can use completely diﬀerent pulses
and the shaping is thus a nonlinear process[36].
Multipulse shaping has been applied to optical channels[36]. In the paper it is mentioned that
terrestrial and indoor wireless communication are too time-dispersive to allow for the scheme known
as orthogonal multipulse modulation (OMM). The main issue is that time-dispersion dilutes away the
orthogonality of pulse set.
A set of orthogonal pulses are proposed in [19] speciﬁcally for optical channels, again using OMM.
Since the pulses are mutually orthogonal, a matched ﬁlter will only produce a signiﬁcant test statistic if
the associated pulse is present. In this manner multiple bit streams can be simultaneously transmitted.
The scheme does not discuss nonlinearity tolerances but focuses solely on the simplicity of the
implementation compared to PAM.

The close relationship between channel capacity and mutual information indicates that the maximum
achievable information rate (AIR) is the mutual information[5].
3.3.1
Spectral eﬃciency
From the Shannon–Hartley theorem[34][17]
C = B log2(1 + S/N),
where B is bandwidth, S/N is the signal-to-noise (power) ratio, we immediately note that the capacity
is directly proportional to the channel bandwidth for a continuous-time channel with Gaussian noise.
The dynamics will undoubtedly be diﬀerent for an optical ﬁber exhibiting nonlinear eﬀects, but it does
display the need for not considering purely information rate but instead spectral eﬃciency (SE)[17].
Spectral eﬃciency (SE) is deﬁned as information rate over bandwidth with the unit bit s−1 Hz−1[17]
and leaving awkward units aside, it does give the insight that splurging on spectral broadening might
lower power into the linear region, but this comes at a cost of occupying a wider wavelength band. The
chosen pulse and the bandwidth of the baseband signal are related[17] and hence SE plays are key role
in bandlimited communication systems.

3.3.2
Bit error rate and reach
It is appropriate to brieﬂy mention bit error rate and its relation to communication systems. As the
name indicates it is concerned with the rate or frequency at which individual bit errors occur and
it can operate both with and without FEC. Calculating bit error rates will for the simplest systems
involve assigning a mapping between constellation symbols and bits and then estimating the symbol
distributions as above[17]. For systems with FEC this is however more involved[41]:
• Burst errors inﬂuence FEC diﬀerently than uniformly random errors
• Is the FEC hard decision or soft decision?
• Design parameters naturally inﬂuence the design of FEC decoders
Thus bit error rate (BER) is not directly corresponding to mutual information, but FEC decoders have
thresholds at which error correction can be arbitrarily good[29] (cited in [15]). A clear advantage of
BER is the picture of end-to-end performance of an entire system include details such as burst errors.
Another possible measure of system performance is reach which is deﬁned as the maximum possible
distance where the FEC decoder can produce an arbitrarily low error-rates[15]; with a threshold
around 3.8 · 10−3[11][29] (cited in [15]). Thus, ﬁber distance can be increased up until this threshold
is triggered and two systems can then be compared in how far they can transmit information.

3.4.1
Dispersion and dispersion management
Dispersion, or more speciﬁcally, chromatic dispersion is a measure of how much a propagating pulse
is spread in a ﬁber[1, ch. 2]. A geometrical-optics description leads to a comparison with multipath

dispersion because of wavelength dependent dispersion. Since the carrier laser is modulated with
the baseband signal it occupies a broader spectrum and these diﬀerent wavelength components will
propagate diﬀerently. The dispersion is in this case caused both by material properties in ﬁbers and of
the inherit behavior of waveguides[1, ch. 2].

optical ﬁbers perfectly circular since manufacturing imperfections, surface roughness and any other
bending or twisting cause ﬁbers to obtain an elliptical proﬁle. Fiber propagation equations, such as the
nonlinear Schrödinger equation (NLSE), assume a circular geometry, but the imperfections lead to

3.4.4
Nonlinearity mitigation
Two nonlinearity compensation techniques will be brieﬂy discussed. In Section 3.5 “Pulse shaping for
nonlinear ﬁber channels”, the application of pulse shapes for nonlinearity mitigation will be elaborated
upon.
Digital back-propagation
Digital back-propagation (DBP) uses knowledge about the channel model to almost fully reverse the
eﬀects from propagation. Albeit the optical ﬁber is a nonlinear channel, it is invertible, meaning that a
given output can be traced back to a certain input. Digital back-propagation (DBP) essentially works
backwards from the output by negating all parameters and simulating the ﬁber in reverse[20][9].
The feasibility of this approach is tied to the split-step Fourier method (SSFM), which is described
in Section 3.4.5. In short, the SSFM provides a substantial speed-up by using fast algorithms for
calculating Fourier transforms. However, there are two caveats with DBP. First, it can only reverse
the determinsistc aspects of the ﬁber channel. Stochastic processes such as PMD, ampliﬁer noise and
laser phase noise which inﬂuence the ﬁber are, by their nature, impossible to predict and will thus also
manifest as noise in the back-propagated input signal.
Second, the complexity of the algorithm is tied directly to the ﬁber distance. For most simulations
this will result in a doubling of simulation time, but for online systems DBP is prohibitively complex.
Several methods for reducing complexity by ﬁltering WDM channels has been proposed[23].

3.5
Pulse shaping for nonlinear ﬁber channels
Nonlinear propagation of pulses has been widely studied for ﬁber optics, as an example an entire
chapter being devoted to it in Agrawal’s Nonlinear Fiber Optics[2, ch. 2]. This work lays the foundation
for understanding the interplay between propagation and dispersion in the nonlinear media. However,
the pulses are time-domain pulses modulating the light intensity, whereas the pulses of interest for
high-order QAM modulate the amplitude and phase of a carrier.
In [8] and [28], return-to-zero (RZ) pulses were found to yield a signiﬁcant advantage in nonlinearity
tolerance compared to non-return-to-zero (NRZ) pulses. In [8], the setup also compared how RZ
and NRZ pulses behaved together with digital back-propagation, where RZ pulses with no DBP
outperformed NRZ with DBP. This case involved inter-channel eﬀects, which in relation to the earlier
cited claim that “[DBP] eliminates all signal-signal intrachannel nonlinearities”[15].
The disadvantage of RZ pulses is their broad bandwidth: twice that of NRZ pulses. It does however
give credibility to the assumption that pulse shaping is a viable method of increasing achievable
information rate.
In [10], a family of pulses is proposed which maximize the energy content in the high frequencies of
the baseband signal. The reasoning is based upon results from [7], where it was observed that pulses
are more nonlinearity tolerant in dispersion post-compensated systems when there is a greater amount
of energy in the high frequencies of the frequency-domain representation of the pulse.

4.2
Multipulse / nonlinear pulse shaping
Multipulse shaping refers to the technique of applying multiple pulses to a signal. As mentioned earlier,
this technique has seen it use in channelization. A type of multipulse shaping is now proposed, which
applies diﬀerent pulses depending on the amplitude of the ﬁltered constellation symbol. It is thus a
nonlinear ﬁlter.
It is well established that symbols of a larger amplitude in the constellation will incur larger nonlinear
phase rotation because of the resulting signal will be more energetic – this is the principle of SPM.
Because of the optical channel’s properties, transmission at a lower launch power does not exhibit
nonlinear eﬀects to the same degree as transmission at higher launch power. By selectively applying
diﬀerent pulses depending on the amplitude of the symbol it should be possible apply the right pulse
for the right channel characteristics.

The multipulse shaping is schematically shown in Figure 4.4. The upsampled symbols arrive from the
constellation mapper which maps bits to symbols – whatever symbol mapping scheme is used. The
symbols’ amplitude are then compared with a set of threshold passed through a multiplexer/switch,
which routes the symbols towards the appropriate ﬁlter. The assigned ﬁlter does the actual pulse
shaping which is a simply a conventional FIR ﬁlter.
The ﬁlters operate independently and are free to implement whichever ﬁlter the system designer
chooses, except for the constraint that the FIR ﬁlter must have the same “delay” until the peak as any
of the other ﬁlters. The principle is illustrated in Figure 4.5. If the ﬁlters implement pulses which
decay faster or slower then they delay until the peak must align to keep keep the signal inter-symbol
interference free. The simplest method of handling this requirement is to generate pulse covering an
equal duration of symbols.
Finally, the shaped signals are summed up and sent on further in the transmitter.

4.3
Frequency- vs. time-domain synthesis of pulses
The raised cosine and the M-pulse are both deﬁned by a frequency spectrum function. To obtain the
time-domain form of the pulse it would be possible to derive a closed-form expression and use this for
pulse synthesis. As an example this is known for the raised cosine pulse to be as in Equation 3.1 from
Section 3.2.4. In [39], a closed form expression of the M-pulse is also given. The introduction of the
matched ﬁlter, will however complicate this since a closed-form expression of the root versions are
generally quite complicated. For the root M-pulse an expression is listed in [37].
For the purposes of this project a few diﬀerent approaches are taken in the generation of time-domain
pulses
• Root raised cosine (RRC) and M-pulses have a closed form expression
• Root M-pulse (RMP) are generated in the frequency-domain and then converted to the time-
domain using the inverse discrete Fourier transform
• EWP and TWP use a raised-cosine prototype (using Equation 3.1) and then apply their respective
windowing
4.4
Limitations of optimization versus brute force
It was considered to implement an optimization scheme, but the experiments proved unfruitful. Albeit
a locally convex assumption is reasonable for the power vs. mutual information (MI), it is probably too
coarse for any other parameter when dealing with a nonlinear channel – there is no reason to believe
that there could not be multiple local minima/maxima. One could then ask if diﬀerent algorithms
could be applied, such as Bayesian methods, but a diﬀerent perspective will be discussed here: the
need for an optimization scheme.
The parameter space is as follows: power, channel spacing, 1 to 3 parameters, pulse type and ﬁlter type.
In addition to this are various channel parameters such as: distance, dispersion compensation, etc. At

most seven parameters, if the channel is considered ﬁxed and of these two are potentially unbounded.
Constraining power and channel spacing to 5 options each and likewise for the parameters it yields an
upper bound of 5 · 5 · 53 · 3 · 2 = 18750 conﬁgurations per channel.
However, simply by considering that only one of the pulses has 3 parameters it is seen that the number
of conﬁgurations reduces to 3750. With a cluster computing system, such as DTU’s HPC it is trivial to
simultaneously run hundreds of parallel simulations and whilst the total CPU time is possibly 3750 h,

4.4.1
Limitations for multipulse systems
Whilst parallelization is simple for a modest parameter space it quickly becomes untractable. The
multipulses are essentially described by at least 3 times the number for a pulse parameters, and with
the above calculation, this yields a parameter space of more than 2 million conﬁgurations for some
pulses! Applying an optimization routine to multipulse systems seem to be the most sensible approach,
however a suitable approach to determining the steps or candidates is needed. The multipulse setup
will instead be selected based upon the best performing pulses on conventional pulse shaping schemes.
4.5
Overview of simulation setup
In this section, the simulation setup used in this project will be described. The input, binary data
are generated using a pseudo-random number generator with a ﬁxed seed for reproducible results.
A turbo-coded FEC is applied to the binary data. The binary data are transmitted in blocks of
20 972 bit and 2 blocks per polarization; both polarizations were used. Constellation/symbol mapping
is conﬁgurable: uniform 16-QAM for most simulations and the constellation shaped simulation uses
64-QAM with at most 6 bit of entropy. Symbols are shaped using the chosen pulse shaper with
oversampling set to 64 smpl and each pulse spanning 256 sym. The symbol rate is set to 16 GBd.
Initial synchronization is obtained using a Zadoﬀ–Chu sequence which is prepended to each block. To
aid in keeping synchronization, quadrature-phase shift-keying (QPSK) pilot symbols are interleaved
with the transmission. The number of pilot symbols is set to 10 %.
The optical signal’s propagation is simulated using the SSFM as described in Section 3.4.5 with a
ﬁxed step size of 2 km, except where otherwise noted. The optical ﬁber link is organized in spans of
80 km interleaved with EDFA, which compensate for propagation losses.
The simulated ﬁber is standard single-mode ﬁber (SSMF) with parameters:
• Span length: 80 km
• Dispersion: 17 ps nm−1 km−1
• Dispersion slope: 0
• Attenuation: 0.2 dB km−1
• Nonlinear coeﬃcient: 1.3 W m−1
Idealized components are assumed with no impairments such as phase noise, skew or amplitude
imbalance.
The received signal is ﬁltered using either a Butterworth low-pass ﬁlter or a matched ﬁlter corresponding
to the selected pulse shape, resampled to 2 smpl sym−1 and chromatic dispersion compensation is
applied. The polarizations are identiﬁed and the synchronization symbols are used to align with the
block structure. A decision directed equalizer equalizes each block in turn and the received, estimated
symbols can be used to estimate the MI.

is characterized by a residual dispersion percentage or ratio – unfortunately this parameter is diﬃcult
to compare across systems. In [38], the residual dispersion is generated from only some of the spans
and seems to be combined with the EDFA.
In Section 5.5, “Pulse shaping in channels with residual dispersion” DCF will be inserted between
spans. A ratio is used to control how much DCF is inserted, this ratio is called residual dispersion
ratio per span (RDRPS) and may sometimes be expressed as a percentage. At 100 % there is maximal
residual and thus no inline dispersion compensation and in the opposite case, with 0 % RDRPS there
is full, inline dispersion compensation.

5.5
Pulse shaping in channels with residual dispersion
Since M-pulses are designed to disperse faster it is thus relevant to consider how they behave in systems
with diﬀerent dispersion management schemes. Drawing inspiration from [38] a setup with DCFs will
be considered. DCFs will be inserted after each span. To allow for some residual dispersion, they can
potentially be reduced in length denoted by a RDRPS ranging from 0 (fully dispersion compensated) to
1 (no dispersion compensation at all). A total distance of 1680 km is evaluated, since the impairments
are quite pronounced. A low-pass ﬁlter is used at the receiver.
In Figure 5.14, the best performing conﬁgurations are seen for four possible residual dispersion ratios.
When there is full dispersion compensation, the M-pulses perform about as well as the RRC pulse.
Every pulse performs better with 50 % residual dispersion. This could be a sign that nonlinearities are
greatly increased by the frequent dispersion compensation and thus increased PTAP.
Between 50 % residual dispersion and 75 % the RRC curves signiﬁcantly drops in performance. Even
when there is no dispersion compensation, the M-pulse is still able to carry information, where the
RRC pulse is unable to be detected.

Spectral eﬃciency
Since the pulse shaping schemes were seen to improve the mutual information per symbol it is relevant
to investigate if these improvements have a negative impact upon the spectral eﬃciency. Since roll-oﬀ
for RRC pulses and α for M-pulses are both directly corresponding to the spectral width, the change
in these parameters will be compared with changes in channel spacing to two neighboring channels.
In the following several contour plots will be shown, they compare how channel spacing and pulse
parameter inﬂuence the estimated MI or SE. In all plots it holds that brighter is better.
The low-pass ﬁltered signals are compared on the opposite of this page, the results for the signals
received with the matched ﬁlter are eﬀectively identical but the contours are included in the Appendix A.
The ﬁrst rows corresponds to the RRC, RMP and M-pulse respectively, with MI contours on the left
and SE on the right.
A general trend is immediately noticed between the M-pulses and the RRC pulse: RRC is far less
sensitive to inﬂuence from neighboring channels – even with a large roll-oﬀfactor! This seen by the
low inclination of the roll-oﬀvs. channel spacing edge. In contrast, the M-pulses both require a much
wider separation between channels to guard from interference.
It is worth noting that maximum information rate and spectral eﬃciency lie in opposite corners of the
parameter space.
Finally, it is interesting to note that the 1.7 contour for spectral eﬃciency extends much further into
the high channel separation numbers for the RMP and M-pulse. That is, the even though the two
nonlinearity tolerant pulses occupy a wider spectral area, their advantage of nonlinearity tolerance is
concretely visible.

Chapter 6
Discussion
The discussion of the results will be presented in this chapter.
6.1
Advantage of pulse shaping
The observed gain from pulse shaping were minimal – at least in the case of the dispersion post-
compensated channel. The ﬁndings are, however, consistent with those from [39] so it is possible that
the presentation or analysis simply fails to extract the real advantage. For example, in [39], reach is
used as a measure of performance and here an improvement of a 35 % increase is observed, from
6600 km to not quite 9000 km. In both this project and the reference article, a similar increase in
optimal launch power is observed: +0.5 dB.
In the channel simulations where inline dispersion compensation was applied, the results are appreciably
diﬀerent – in several cases the block detector failed to detect the RRC-shaped signals, whereas the
M-pulse shaped signals managed, albeit with a heavily reduced AIR. In the case of full inline dispersion
compensation, a RDRPS of 0 %, the nonlinear eﬀects would be expected to be the largest since the
pulse peaks are maximal and not temporally dispersed.
The choice of constellation order is also worth commenting on. The author is not familiar with any
application these of pulse shapes for modulation orders higher than 16-QAM. Higher modulation
order would be associated with a need for greater launch power in order to improve symbol detection
performance. Applying a pulse shape which allows for increased launch power, but with minimal cost
in terms of nonlinear interference, seems to be a clear advantage. This could possibly counter the
spectral ineﬃciencies associated with the broad spectrum of the M-pulse.
The above is also why pulse shaping and constellation shaping are a good match. Both techniques seek
to allow for an increased launch power. This area should be explored further.
Irregardless of the magnitude of the performance improvement, pulse shaping – not matter the shape of
the pulse – carries an inherit advantage which makes it an almost given to consider for inclusion. It is
practically free, since it operates using components which are already present in any QAM transmitter
and receiver: the pulse shaper.
6.2
Favoring large bandwidths
For all simulated pulses the highest MI was obtained when the roll-oﬀfactor was at its maximum,
corresponding to the largest possible pulse bandwidth. Looking at Figure 6.1, it is clear that raised
cosine pulses associated with narrower bandwidths will also have larger between-symbol amplitudes,
45
==PAGE:48==
simply because their pulse decay is slower. The broader distribution of frequency components
associated with larger roll-oﬀfactors will in turn yield a shorter-duration pulse.
This is a somewhat similar result to that obtained in [18], where it was too found that a low roll-oﬀleads
to a great amount of nonlinear interference. The simulation setup used ﬁbers with inline dispersion
management using DCF. Their ﬁndings reveal that high roll-oﬀ, that is, a large bandwidth, is also
subject to generating nonlinear interference, which they attribute to the high peak power. Again there
is a good correspondence between the results from this project and the results from their paper, as the
only raised cosine pulse which was successfully received was with a low roll-oﬀfactor.
Their conclusion is the roll-oﬀfactor is optimal around 0.4 and 0.6, but the author of this project will
instead claim that the optimal choice is very dependent on the rest of the system and especially the
dispersion compensation method.

6.3
Multipulse shaping
Multipulse shaping proved to not provide any beneﬁt compared to shaping all symbols with the same
pulse shape. A probable explanation is that the equalizer on the receiver was not able to properly
equalize when multiple pulses are used to modulate the symbols. Since the equalizer is a linear ﬁlter
it will only be able to invert the linear eﬀects of the channel – the proposed multipulse shaping
introduces a further nonlinearity to the channel by making the pulse parameters dependent on the
symbol amplitude.
It is perhaps surprising that the eﬀects are not as signiﬁcantly worse – after all the channel is eﬀectively
behaving in a more nonlinear fashion from the point-of-view of the receiver. This is consistent with the
observation that multipulse shaping where all pulses are diﬀerent is performing consistently worse.
A matched ﬁlter would probably tackle this problem, but this leads back to the problem of how to
design matched ﬁlters for a nonlinear ﬁlter. A possible approach would be to allow the receiving
matched ﬁlter have full knowledge about transmitted symbols – this would, in some form, allow
for applying the correct ﬁlter to the correct samples. This approach would be unsuitable for a real
communication system, but it would allow for setting an upper bound on the possible capacity
associated with multipulse shaping.

6.4
Channel spacing as parameter
In the simulations three channels were simulated, but only the center channel was used for calculation
MI. To estimate the bandwidth requirements of the signals, the channel spacing was adjusted and
correlated with changes in generalized mutual information (GMI).
This approach has the ﬂaw that the central channel is inﬂuenced by two neighboring channels and that
this does not give a good indication of what kind of optical, electrical or digital ﬁltering would be
suitable for processing the channel. At any rate it gives an indication of cross-channel interference,
which is important too, but the whether these considerations give a clear picture of the spectral
eﬃciency obtained can be questioned.
It beckons the question: what is truly important when considering AIR? If it is only considering a
single channel, then disabling the neighbors would greatly increase AIR. If it is a system overview,
then the neighboring channels should be included.
6.5
Narrow range of parameters for TWP and EWP
The poor performance of the TWP and EWP pulses could be attributed to a very narrow range of
values simulated.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
The challenges faced with optical communication are no small matter. Part of it arises from the
channel’s inherit properties of nonlinearities, imperfections etc. The other part is associated with
the sophisticated and broad range of techniques, which despite the despite aforementioned channel
properties succeed in delivering the capacity necessary to enable the modern, interconnected society.
“Despite” is perhaps the wrong word to use, since the most novel results arise exactly because of the
optical ﬁber channel’s properties. Among these is pulse shaping, which this project explored. For
conventional quadrature-amplitude modulation signals a slight increase in the achievable information
rate was observed and the simulated results point towards a nonlinearity tolerant behavior. Partly, this
was observed with root raised cosine pulses, which are well-known in communication systems, where
an increased roll-oﬀ, and thus increased spectral width, resulted in better achievable information rate.
These results are consistent with prior work.
Among pulse families which are speciﬁcally designed for nonlinearity tolerance are the M-pulses
from [39]. Results regarding M-pulses were replicated, with the most signiﬁcant improved, over root
raised cosine pulses, in channels with partial inline dispersion compensation. Apart from replication of
results of the M-pulses, a set of pulses based upon windowed root raised cosine pulses were proposed.
Unfortunately, the simulated parameters were too narrowly selected and no conclusion can be made at
this point. In the case of all the discussed pulses, they are parameterized and thus form families.
The challenges in dimensionality were seen with regards to challenges in optimizing the pulse
parameters. In the end, parallelization of many simulation parameters proved to be the fastest and most
complete approach.
Irregardless of the current state of the results, this project explored a rather unexplored “dimension” in
the ﬁeld optical communication and will hopefully sow seeds for future work and results.
7.1
Future work
Evolutionary algorithms (EA) are population-based optimization algorithms with the unique advantage
that they do not need a model of the underlying cost function (ﬁtness landscape in EA terminology). A
particular advantage is natural parallelism of EAs, where a whole population of candidates can be
simulated in parallel. They have recently been used for geometric constellation shaping [13].
In [36] a method of equalization multipulse modulated signals is investigated. Multipulse systems are
naturally diﬃcult to equalize since the received symbols – and hence the pulses – are unknown. The
described technique uses formulations similar to MIMO ﬁlters to obtain linear feedback and decision
feedback ﬁlters.
49
==PAGE:52==
The EWP and TWP should be properly explored across a more appropriate span of parameters. A root
version should also be created.
Further explorations of the combination of constellation shaping and pulse shaping should be conducted.
Since pulse shaping increases nonlinearity tolerance it would allow for altering the distribution of the
probabilistic constellation shaping.

Appendix B
Self-evaluation
All projects involve challenges and obstacles which, expected or not, must be overcome and dealt
with. For me the biggest challenge was the tremendous amounts of material surrounding optical
communication, which I had to accustom myself to and learn to a degree where I could reason about
how everything plays together. The signal processing aspects and the general communication system
aspects were at least familiar, but jumping head ﬁrst into a quite new ﬁeld did not leave enough time to
digest and properly internalize the new knowledge. Personally, I fear this has hindered my ability to
contribute much of my own working, since a lot of time had to spent on getting details right.
The inclusion of constellation shaping was a mistake. Not because it was interesting – it was in
fact made me curious about optical communication – but because it broadened the scope too much.
Ultimately it ended up being a tacked on and kinda-half-and-not-really-anything thing, time which
could undoubtedly have been spent on more fruitful or interesting directions. An adage goes, “under
promise; over deliver”. The project would not have suﬀered, from excluding constellation shaping
from the beginning.
Despite the not-so-good parts, I personally feel that this project has been a personal success in terms of
learning about advanced, modern research into the optical ﬁber and its use in optical communication.
Looking back, I feel with conﬁdence that I’m capable of tackling new challenges in any of the ﬁelds of
communication theory – I’m deﬁnitely not all-knowing, but I am deﬁnitely able to approach the yet
unknown.
