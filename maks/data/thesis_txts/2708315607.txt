Abstract
Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM) is an essential and constantly evolv-
ing part of providing robust autonomous navigation, from robot vacuum cleaners to
autonomous cars. This thesis will address the method of landmark-based graph-SLAM,
mainly the back-end, where landmarks in the environment are used for navigating and
providing information about the robot’s location in the world.
The development of this algorithm is to be done through simulation with the help of
Geographic Information System (GIS) data and tries to answer whether a bearing-only
approach is a probable method of landmark-based SLAM in an urban environment for
Capra Robotics.
An assessment and considerations regarding the development of simulating the front-
end are discussed along with its limitations. The graph-SLAM system has been built
from scratch to allow for modifications throughout the project. Additionally, implement-
ing the bearing-only method requires robust techniques for initializing landmarks that
are tested and evaluated.
The results suggest that both the depth-based and bearing-only SLAM algorithms show
great promise in providing proper localization and mapping. In addition, bearing-only
shows great resilience towards outliers; however, in general, bearing-only graph-SLAM
seems to require higher quality data as it is working with minimal information, which
might be hard to guarantee in a real-world setting.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Robot: A machine controlled by a computer that is used to perform jobs
automatically
The Cambridge Dictionary presents a non-rigid definition of what makes a robot and
opens new possibilities for the minds of engineers to explore. In the beginning, industrial
robots such as the ”Unimate” were used for trivial tasks in well-defined environments
serving as a replacement for human labor, relieving hard repetitive work, and creating
a paradigm shift in the industry.
Now, Autonomous robots come in all shapes and
sizes, from vacuuming floors to exploring extraterrestrial planets, and are still of ever-
increasing importance in the rapidly growing industry 4.0 and can provide access to
growth in areas where it seemed impossible before [1].
A tremendous amount of research is put into developing the field of robotics and
autonomous navigation to provide proper robust systems to cope with everything that
the robot can encounter in terms of errors, malfunctions, and inaccurate measurements.
Urban mobile robots are a subset of these autonomous robots intended to navigate
an urban environment. This will usually entail considering the surrounding world of
people, streets, sidewalks, and other interesting landmarks in the area that could be
used for information.
1.1
Motivation
If mobile robots could freely navigate all modern terrain, e.g., urban and rural, it would
open up an abundance of worldwide applications. For example, one of the main problems
of interest at Capra Robotics is using an Urban Mobile Robot (UMR) as part of a
smart-city project regarding picking up cigarette butts in highly populated urban areas.
Cigarettes are the most littered waste product in the world. Up to 75% end up in nature
or on the streets, and much of it ends up as microplastic in the oceans and eventually
in our food chain [2], and Capra wants to change this.
1.2
Project description
The main objectives of this thesis are to investigate, develop and provide a landmark-
based graph-SLAM algorithm for urban mobile robots in collaboration with Capra
Robotics. Furthermore, pose-landmark graph optimization methods aim to minimize

1.3 Previous work
3
SLAM through time
The last three decades have seen an astonishing amount of progress to the method of
SLAM by its community, starting from establishing the problem of SLAM to enabling
large-scale real-world applications that are now slowly transitioning to the industry.
This section mainly focuses on back-end SLAM and will summarize scientific articles
that have helped develop SLAM to become what it is today.
The standard SLAM approaches that we know of today were formulated back in the late
80’s [4] with a focus on solving spatial relations in robotics; the next important work
followed in the 90’s by [5, 6]. The articles gave rise to modern approaches that improved
both eﬀiciency and robustness when dealing with the underlying optimization problem.
The authors of [7, 8, 9] present the basis of SLAM as a nonlinear least-squares problem
where the goal is to find a minimal error graph-node configuration.
The method of SLAM is unique because of its ability to include various sensor mod-
els, e.g., Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU), wheel encoders, GNSS, camera, and Light
Detection and Ranging (LiDAR) sensors. Another unique property of SLAM is its abil-
ity to take in new measurements over time as new information is gathered, as seen in
this article from 2017 [10].
Overview of linear solvers and frameworks
The minimization problem is usually solved using iterative linearization techniques such
as the Gauss-Newton method, gradient descent, or Levenberg-Marquardt (LM) method,
where it is discussed in [11] that the LM method is the more effective optimization al-
gorithm but may fail if the initial guess is far from a minimum. Fast linear solvers are
essential in modern SLAM solvers, and computational eﬀiciency is achieved by exploit-
ing linearity to construct sparse matrix structures [9, 12, 13, 14]. Konolige et al. [15]
describe how to construct linear matrices eﬀiciently in 2D pose graphs. However, the in-
creased environment scale in recent years affects the computational speed to the degree
that it is diﬀicult to meet in a practical application. The authors of [16] suggest decom-
position of pose graph’s sparsity to increase the speed of graph optimization calculations.
Current popular open-source SLAM libraries (e.g., iSAM [12], g2o [13], SLAM++ [14],
GTSAM [17], ORBSLAM [18], Ceres [19], SESYNC [20]) are able to solve systems with
many thousands of variables in a matter of seconds and are used in both research and
industry.
Most libraries provide the user with examples from real-world SLAM problems. Hands-
on tutorials such as [21] give the user a great introduction to the world of SLAM. A
comparison of graph optimization approaches [22, 23] can guide choosing between dif-
ferent libraries.

Robust least-squares methods
In graph-based SLAM, achieving a robust least-squares will allow the underlying SLAM
algorithm to perform well even when data association is not perfect. To combat the pos-
sibly catastrophic outcome of erroneous loop closures, [24] uses a method that optimizes
the map while determining correct loop closures within a single Bayesian framework in
the presence of non-Gaussian noise.
Approximately at the same time, the method of Dynamic Covariance Scaling (DCS)
was proposed in [25]. DCS is an effective approach to optimization under the presence
of outliers that uses a down-weighing function on the information matrix to reduce the
confidence in outlier measurements.
Many procedures and tools are available to help provide a robust estimation for the
SLAM system; however, these often need to be chosen well after data collection has been
done, as the choice of a kernel for these robust functions needs to align with the outlier
distribution. Barron [26] proposes a generality of these robust kernels by implementing
an alpha parameter that can change the shape of the loss functions to fit the outlier
distribution.
Building upon the adaptive robust kernel approach by Barron, Chebrolu et al. [27]
uses an Expectation-Maximization(EM)-based optimization approach where they iter-
ate between an estimation and maximization step to obtain the best performing alpha
parameter for each step, allowing for a dynamic alpha value in contrast to the static one
described by Barron.
Bearing-only SLAM
Due to the much-limited nature of using only landmark bearings in SLAM, the majority
of previous work has been in the field of Extended Kalman Filter (EKF)-SLAM.
In Deans [28], a method is put forth which uses odometry and monocular vision for
building maps and localizing. The technique relies on bundle adjustment, deterministic
sampling and recursive filtering to solve the problem of bearing-only SLAM.
Bailey presents a solution to the feature initialization in bearing-only SLAM through
constrained initialization [29]. In the article, he uses EKF-SLAM and triangulation meth-
ods for delayed feature estimation, while making sure to only use the sensor information
when landmarks measurements are well-conditioned.
SLAM using GIS/OSM data
Improving robust navigation in an outdoor urban environment might be achieved by
fusing GIS data from OSM into the SLAM algorithm.
In Floros et al. [30], they propose a localization framework utilizing OSM data to
improve visual odometry during longer distances navigated.
Similar use of the OSM data is used in [31], where Vysotska et al. uses laser-based
navigation is to match the extracted GIS maps. Using buildings as landmarks while

Concept
The innovative patented wheel frame shown in figure 2.2 and 2.3 is the core framework

This design allows the 4-wheeled robot to maneuver easily in environments that would

Some critical features for the framework are that the robot can drive up slopes with a
50 % increase and climb curbs of 15 cm due to their All-Wheel-Drive (AWD) design.
Practically any terrain is available for navigation.
Differential and Ackermann methods for steering in 4-wheeled robots are the most fre-
quently used. Differential drive is based on having separately motorized wheels placed
on either side of the chassis, often with a castor wheel at the front for stability. With
this setup, the robot can change direction based on the input provided to each wheel
by varying wheel rotation rates. In essence, if the same speed is applied to both wheels,
it will drive straight forward, and if the speed of the right wheel is increased, a left
turn is engaged vice versa. Ackermann steering instead provides turning maneuvers by
turning the front wheels while keeping the back wheels fixed. This form of steering
was originally proposed to avoid tire slippage when driving in a curve, and the basis of
Ackermann can still be seen in modern cars, even though the concepts used today are
much more complex. The movement capabilities at Capra can be seen as the best combi-
nation between differential and Ackermann steering, providing both agility and stability.
Capra has put much emphasis on providing an actual outdoor mobile robot. The frame-
work has obtained the IP65, which proves that the robot is entirely dust-proof, as well
as protected against water jets projected from a 6.3 mm nozzle at every angle [33]. In
addition to this, the battery gives the Capra platform a driving range of more than 100
km on a single charge. They use self-made firmware for a multitude of internal sensors,
such as the wheel encoders for odometry. Therefore, limited information in terms of
datasheets is not available. A ZED-F9P positioning chip from ublox [34] is implemented
for their GNSS module to deliver ultra-low-power positioning in an ultra-compact form

2.2.1
Odometry
The acquired odometry data was based on a combination of wheel odometry and IMU
data to generate a belief system from proprioceptive sensors about where the robot is
at any time. There are several different ways to do self-localization by odometry, e.g.,
wheel encoders, radar, IMU, visual methods, and by the use of laser as proposed in [37].
This section will solely focus on wheel odometry.
Wheel odometry is a simple way to do self-localization relying only on internal sensors
and has been used for a long time in the robotics community. The wheel odometry
methods use wheel encoders mounted on the robot to count the wheel rotations. The
number of revolutions is then used as part of a dynamic model to compute the relative
driven distance related to the starting position.
Depending on the wheel kinematics of the robot, Wheel Odometry (WO) can suffer
from several limitations. First off, WO can only be applied to ground vehicles. Secondly,
wheel slippage and drift may misrepresent the distance traveled as the counted wheel
revolutions do not match the actual distance traveled. A systematic bias in WO might
also result in a sizeable accumulative positioning error. Uneven terrain, muddy dirt, and
slippery surfaces are all contributors to errors in WO.
Adding an IMU to the odometry can help increase the reliability of pose estimations.
Using the IMU to determine the orientation is significantly more accurate than using
only the ratio between the rotation of each wheel.
A drawback is the angular drift
over time caused by the gyroscope inside the sensor. This is a widely known issue, and
articles like [38] discuss potential solutions.
2.2.2
GNSS
The GNSS is a constellation of satellites able to determine geo-spatial positioning of a
receiving antenna [39]. It is given as longitude, latitude, and altitude, which is a three-
dimensional metric called the Geographic Coordinate System (GCS) and is the most
common spatial reference system. Various GNSS’s have been launched, including Euro-
pean Union’s Galileo, the USA’s NAVSTAR Global Positioning System (GPS), Russia’s
GLONASS, and China’s BeiDou System [40]. They work by measuring the delay of
transmitted signals along a line of sight by radio from the satellite.
How well GNSS performs is assessed through four criteria; accuracy, integrity, continu-
ity, and availability. The average accuracy of a GPS-enabled smartphone is typically
within 4.9-meter radius under open sky [41], and high-end equipment can reach centime-
ter accuracy. However, the performance will worsen in urban areas. This is a significant

limitation of GNSS, as urban areas are the cause of signal blockage and signal reflections
(multipathing), leading to incorrect data.
The next generation of GNSS real-time kinematic positioning system, like Testbed in
Aarhus for Precision Positioning and Autonomous Systems (TAPAS) [42] can be used for
high accuracy positioning purposes even in urban areas such as street canyons. TAPAS
has 11 reference stations located around Aarhus city in Denmark with a distance between

The accuracy can be as high as 1 cm-level in real-time without suffering from signal

The goal is to do self-localization by

3.1
Graph-SLAM backbone
The central teachings to gain from this chapter is what makes the basics of graph-based
SLAM an eﬀicient way of solving the problem that autonomously navigating robots
are facing when having to both localize and acquire a mapping of the environment
simultaneously.
This task is usually solved by using proprioceptive sensors to estimate the change
in position over time, such as WO, where the integration of an IMU often handles the
heading of the robot. These internal sensors are commonly combined with exteroceptive
sensors, e.g,. LiDAR, for range-based laser measurements or cameras that can provide
visual feedback for bearing-only and depth estimation amongst other information. The
sensor data is collected and handled by the front-end and then sent to the back-end
to be optimized, as shown in figure 3.1. The terms front-end and back-end concerning
SLAM describe graph construction and graph optimization.
Theoretically, if both internal and external sensors being utilized by the robot platform
were flawless, the collected sensor measurements would allow us to build a perfect map
and navigate through it with ease, knowing where the robot is at all times. However, in
real life, the situation is more complex. Sensors are noisy and often come with a bias.
Wheel encoders can introduce drift in addition to having non-perfect measurements.
On top of this, the data association coming from the front-end feature extraction is
rarely ideal, resulting in data outliers and false-positive loop closures. Acquiring these
erroneous measurements such as the ones described is precisely the reason that the state-
==PAGE:30==
16
3 Methodology of SLAM optimization

of-the-art algorithms behind graph-based SLAM need to be eﬀicient and robust.
The most popular approaches to SLAM are extended Kalman filters (EKF), extended in-
formation filters (EIF), particle filtering, and least-square error minimization approaches.
This project will focus on the least-squares minimization approach used in graph-based
SLAM and its features.
Least-squares approaches are great for state estimation problems being solved in graph-
SLAM, where the problem can be treated as an optimization problem. In general, least-
squares is used when an over-determined system, having more equations than unknowns,
is obtained to compute the solution. However, there is always noise in the system that
needs to be optimized, and minor errors will be present; therefore, the perfect solution
will rarely be acquired. However, LS will minimize the sum of squared errors in the
system to obtain the most likely set of parameters given the obtained observations.
The building block behind the SLAM implementation for this project derives from the
theoretical knowledge through the work done in Tutorial on graph-based SLAM[21],
providing a great introduction to the inner workings and mathematical theory behind
what makes graph-based SLAM a crucial component in any such autonomous navigation
system.
To grasp the concepts of graph-SLAM, many of the initial underlying assumptions
in the first part of this chapter on how the constraints are built between non-successive
poses are done through laser-based range detection and ICP scan-matching. This con-
cept is then built upon further with a description on how this can be achieved with
a camera and by implementing landmark observations from the environment into the
graph.
The method used to describe the graph-SLAM problem is to imagine a graph whose
nodes correspond to the robot poses at different points in time in the environment,
labeled with their respective positions. The graph’s edges then represent spatial con-
straints between the robot poses, obtained through pre-processed sensor information.

3.1.1
Landmark-based SLAM
Most of the basis for the graph-SLAM problem so far has been built around using
laser range detection methods such as LiDAR, acting as the front-end part of the graph-
SLAM method and extracting the data. This method provides a graph where only robot
pose nodes are saved, using the information gathered by the laser to update the poses
without actually using the information of the surroundings in the graph. Instead, the
graph-SLAM problem can be described by adding landmarks in the robot’s vicinity to
the graph as it travels through its local environment.
Throughout the last decade, an immense amount of progress has been made in object
detection, with the popularity of machine-learning methods. This progress has made
using a camera as the primary exteroceptive sensor much more feasible as an option for
navigating autonomously.
Not only are most of the cameras useful for object detection much cheaper than a
state-of-the-art LiDAR, but the amount of data needed to be gathered and stored is
minor in comparison. Moreover, in the 3D case, lasers will provide data in a large point
cloud to present a measurement of the environment at each pose of the robot, whereas
cameras can provide a much less dense representation of visual features. Therefore the
use of cameras is becoming more and more prevalent as an alternative solution to laser-
based SLAM.
In landmark-based SLAM, parts of the environment will now be saved in the system,
often explicitly chosen for their traits in the area needed to navigate.
This change to the graph-SLAM method means that the nodes and edges in the graph
are also updated to hold information regarding landmarks, making the environment part

3.2
Loop closure
Throughout the chapter, the notion of loop closure has been mentioned a few times,
and it is one of the ingenuities of the SLAM method. Loop closing is the technique
autonomous robots use to recognize the return to a previously visited region. Using the
information gathered by the sensors, the robot can provide a better estimate of where
the robot is placed in the map.
Working with a practical system where noise and spurious measurements are present,
without correction, the certainty of the robot position and the positional error would
grow unbounded. Hence, it is understandable why such a method as loop closure is
crucial for the success of the SLAM system.
Throughout the route that the robot will autonomously navigate, the constraints that
arise between poses or possible identifiers such as landmarks in the world have been kept
track of. Along with the information they carry, these constraints can now be used to
correct the pose estimate of the current robot pose. Essentially, these constraints be-
tween poses and landmarks can be imagined as malleable linkages that can be tightened
or loosened to provide the best fit, as illustrated in figure 3.8. Having these links saved
throughout the route allows for the graph to not only correct the current robot pose
when a loop closure is observed, but the entire graph.

3.3
Homogeneous coordinates
Calculating transformation between poses, used for defining the error of an edge or the
impact of a loop closure constraint, are done for every single edge between nodes in the
graph. Being able to define the entire difference between two different robot pose nodes,
both the translational and rotational error are important to gain the full picture. How-
ever, in the euclidean space that the robot navigates in, the geometry of this coordinate
projection is not trivial.
Homogeneous coordinates (HC) solves this non-trivial issue and allows the transforma-
tion of translation and rotation in a very elegant way.
In HC, a single matrix can
represent aﬀine and projective transformations.
The use of HC is attractive since the translation and rotations are described through

CHAPTER 4
Simulating the Front-End
This section describes the implementation of the simulator serving as the front-end
of the graph-SLAM algorithm. A simulator is a controlled environment where it can
recreate specific scenarios for analysis. The simulator should create a realistic dataset
that considers the different sensors that the robots at Capra use.
4.1
Simulator overview
The problem of graph-SLAM is a chicken-or-egg problem as a map is needed for local-
ization, and pose estimations are required to build a map. In other words, a graph from
the front-end needs to be built before any back-end optimization can be carried out.
A simulator is convenient as it can change the uncertainties of the robot sensors with-
out making any hardware changes, e.g., how the back-end handles systematic odometry
noise or false landmark detections.
The idea behind the simulator is to generate a SLAM dataset that takes up the least
possible memory and yet contains all the necessary information to build a graph without
dearth of information. Additionally, using a file format that is easy to read dramatically
reduces the diﬀiculty of debugging.
The front-end simulator should build a graph containing nodes and edges derived from
sensor measurements.
It should also represent the real world simplified, i.e., sensor
uncertainties are presented by models whose characteristics mimic the natural world’s
complex behavior. Furthermore, GIS data will be used to build a predefined map of
the environment to obtain an initial belief system of where landmarks and obstructions
reside. However, the extracted information from OSM comes with uncertainties and
does not represent a complete, accurate version of the world. Therefore, the simulator
is not a tool for loading a map directly for navigation by a Capra robot but primarily
for generating data with real-world GIS landmarks.

4.2
GIS extraction
There are many variables to consider when utilizing GIS data for robot navigation and
localization. OpenStreetMap [3] is a collaborative project to create a free editable geo-
graphic database of the world, meaning that everybody can apply to become an editor
for OSM. Hence, one must be aware of the resulting consequences of a free medium
open for the public to edit. If external databases are used to recreate the natural world
through simulation, some considerations have to be made:
• What information layers can be used?
• How is the data stored?
• What features are stored that can be used as landmarks?
• How often do OSM update their data?

Anyone with an OpenStreetMap account can edit the data whenever they want. Being
able to edit privately without supervision can significantly impact the reliability of the
data and the accuracy of landmark coordinates. However, it is free to use, and it might
be worth using even though it has some disadvantages.
The update frequency of data for a service like OSM that is based on people volun-

• Most of the changes in OpenStreetMap are manually updated by humans. Urban

• No one is in charge of scheduling data collection, which means there is no way of

• The quality of data depends on the available equipment. E.g., GNSS coordinates

Modeling in a 2D plane instead of 3D space omits many attributes in this representation.

these issues but are challenging to interpret physically. That being said, working in an
urban environment, the terrain is often flat and can be assumed to be a 2D plane.

visible from the robots’ Point Of View (POV)), accessibility (how much of the feature
is accessible to the robot), size, shape, and appearance is not taken into account.

keeping the angle in the interval (−π, π].

Data convention
Many of the simulator features use the term constraint to describe a relationship between
two types of data. The front-end simulator should create constraints with the necessary
information for the SLAM algorithm to do graph optimization. A constraint consists of
different elements based on the type of data creating it, following a specific format.
This work tries to follow the data convention that was standardized for the g2o
framework. However, the graph optimizer has been built from the ground up; it will
differ from the existing format due to the position information. This work utilizes three
disparate constraint types, pose-pose, pose-landmark, and bearing-only.
The imitation of the g2o-file format for the 2D dataset contain two ”sections”. The
description below originates from ”Format for 2D datasets” [53] and only considers pose-
pose constraints:
1. Vertices: Description of the vertices of the pose graph. Each pose correspond to
a line following the format: ”ID x y th” where ”ID” is a unique number assigned
to the associated pose, and ”x y th” are the initial pose guess obtained from the
odometry sensors. ”x”, and ”y” are the initial guess node’s planar position, while
”th” is the initial guess for the node’s orientation in radians.
2. Edges: Description of the edges of the pose graph. In g2o, each line follows the
format: ”IDfrom IDto dx dy dth Ω11 Ω12 Ω13 Ω22 Ω23 Ω33”, and describes an edge
going from a node to another with index ”IDfrom” to node with index ”IDto”. An
edge describes a measurement of the pose of node ”IDto” in the reference frame of
node ”IDfrom”. The elements ”dx dy dth” are the relative planar pose. The last 6
elements describe the uncertainty of the relative pose measurement and together
they form the symmetric 3 × 3 Information matrix:

4.5.3
Outliers
The notion of outliers and erroneous measurements coming from the front-end in a real-
world SLAM system was mentioned earlier. A big part of solving the problem of SLAM
requires the system to be robust against outliers. Hence, the simulator needs a way to
create outliers that occur realistically.
The method for outlier generation is heavily based on the articles [25], [54]. They de-
scribe how they created simulated and real datasets and corrupted them with simulated
outliers. The simulated outliers are sorted into different approaches, namely ”random”,
”local”, ”random grouped”, and ”local grouped” outliers. The authors of [25] write: ”we
believe that randomly grouped outliers are the most realistic form of outliers as such
constraints are similar to systematic errors generated due to perceptual aliasing by the
front-end.” Therefore, it was decided to implement randomly grouped outliers as a way
to corrupt the datasets provided by the simulator. Random outliers randomly connect
two sampled nodes in the proximity of each other. For grouped outliers, 10 clusters of
mutually consistent outliers are created. This kind of outlier generation is created for
pose-pose loop closures and pose-landmark loop closures.
The simulator has a boolean variable capable of activating outlier generation. The user
then dictates the wanted amount of outliers a cluster should contain. Figure 4.8 shows
an example of outlier simulation of type ”random grouped” of a WO route and for pose-
landmark. 4.8a, pose-landmark, and 4.8b, pose-pose, have true constraints marked as
purple while outliers are represented by the transparent grey lines.
Figure 4.9 visualizes a high-level flow chart comparison to grasp the difference be-
tween an actual front-end and the simulator. A real-life front-end requires various sensors
to be fused to give an output for the back-end. However, a simulator can do the same
with much less effort allowing for more time to understand back-end graph-SLAM.

Graph-SLAM Implementation
Having a fully functioning front-end through the simulated environment described in
the previous chapter, the construction component of graph-SLAM is finalized, and the
optimization segment can be implemented.
Initially, this chapter presents a brief explanation of some of the considerations and
assumptions before implementing the algorithm. Afterward, an overview of the archi-

5.1
Considerations and assumptions
As with many such processes like developing a project for a master thesis, it is a pro-
gressive one. The aim and the questions sought to be answered are not necessarily the
same at the beginning and the end of the project. The method of smoothing was chosen
early on as the approach to the graph-SLAM problem.
Smoothing is an off-line method known as full batch estimation, where an a posteriori
approach is used. Batch estimation considers the entire suite of data after it has been
gathered to compute the best solution. In many instances throughout the last couple of
decades, this approach has not been feasible as it demands large quantities of data, but
as the capacity and computing power have grown, so has the use of batch estimation.
Even if this method is not an on-line method, it is still praised as a great way to
benchmark autonomous navigation. In contrast to the on-line methods, batch estima-
tion can be seen as a golden standard approach as it computes the best solution given
the entire dataset, providing a complete picture.
Taking the problem into account of trying to validate the use of mobile urban robots
combined with GIS data, due to inadequate GNSS signal, it was apparent that landmark-
based graph-SLAM was the approach as well as the ambition from Capra Robotics.
Scouring the internet for the resources available and gathering the information needed,
it was decided to move away from open-source solvers such as the likes of g2o and Ceres.
As getting a proper grasp of the mechanics driving graph-SLAM was essential, the deci-
sion was made to build the system up from scratch.
==PAGE:64==
50
5 Graph-SLAM Implementation
To acquire the understanding of the underlying theory, the simulator and the SLAM
algorithm were built based on starting with laser-range based methods. This is where
most of the theory lies, which was mirrored in chapter 3.
Another reason for starting here is to do proper benchmarking on acknowledged
datasets giving a better idea if the algorithm has been executed correctly. As expressed,
a lot of the early progress in the field was through laser range-based methods, and there-
fore many of the datasets have been developed or collected for that purpose.
As the project moved forward, an emphasis was put on the need to validate the eﬀiciency
of a graph-SLAM algorithm only through using bearing angles to landmarks coming from
a monocular camera. Bearing-only graph-SLAM is not a field where much research has
been done and was previously reserved for probabilistic methods such as EKF-SLAM.
It is also important to note that using bearing is what can be seen as the minimum-
information version of graph-SLAM. Having less information to optimize the robot route
comes with issues of proper initialization and robustness measures, which will be de-
scribed in this chapter.
The reason for going with this method is the feasibility of
applying an affordable way of sensing and navigating to make an autonomous robot
such as the one provided by Capra Robotics accessible to a broader range of people
and companies. Monocular cameras are cheaper than LiDAR, and with the evolution of
object detection, the change to camera is becoming more and more attractive.
Another vital consideration is whether or not there is enough knowledge of the robot’s
whereabouts when the navigation commences. In many real-world cases, there is not
always an awareness of the starting position due to, e.g., lack of initialization or loss
of signal throughout the route navigated, requiring the development of methods that
can estimate the most probable pose in an area. However, it is assumed that there is
often an awareness of where the robot starts for this project, having full data association
between robot and starting position. Therefore, the need for probabilistic methods of
determining the starting position is not essential nor implemented.

5.2.1
Landmark Initialization techniques
Most solutions to the SLAM problem utilize laser and bearing range sensors to perceive
the environment and are therefore relatively easy to incorporate as it allows for immedi-
ate and precise landmark initialization coming at a cost. This is not possible when using
bearing-only information because the distance to the landmark is not directly obtained.
Furthermore, a proper estimate of a landmark position requires multiple measurements
taken from different poses in time; hence the landmark initialization is delayed. This
can be a problem for real-time systems, but this work only concerns the method of batch
estimation and, therefore, will not consider the initialization delay.
Landmark-based SLAM is heavily reliant on computing a good estimate of the landmark
position. This is seen in many different SLAM approaches regarding EKF-SLAM, but
it also extends to bearing-only graph-SLAM. Initialization techniques for bearing-only
==PAGE:68==
54

SLAM are essential as they may help the optimization algorithm avoid diverging, going
into a local minimum, or at least reducing the number of iterations before reaching an
optimal graph configuration. It is also equally important to realize when combined in-
formation from multiple measurements are ill-conditioned.
The assumption has been made that the problem of data association has been solved in
order to proceed with landmark initialization. However, without known data association,
the problem of landmark initialization would lead to a completely different type of SLAM
implementation extending beyond this work’s scope.
Admittedly, to collaborate with Capra Robotics to prove the concept of bearing-only
SLAM, one has to know how to achieve data association. If data association is not
possible in the front-end, the back-end could be altered to build the map ad hoc without
general correspondence of landmarks, as proposed by Thrun et al. in [55].
Several articles are dedicated to finding the best initialization technique [29], [56], [57],
but the following sections will only focus on three ways of initializing landmarks through

5.2.2
Unqualified initialization
The simplest way to initialize the graph with landmark positions would be to define
a landmark guess by placing a point at some distance along the line formed by the
bearing measurement towards a landmark. Thus, it is assumed that the graph-SLAM
algorithm can converge to an optimal graph configuration. The advantage of using such
a method is its simplicity and avoiding any computational heavy landmark initialization
algorithm.
Theoretically, the SLAM algorithm should be able to handle unqualified
landmark initialization as it would only require more iterations for the optimization to
converge. However, this did not prove to be an eﬀicient way of initializing the landmark
as it is diﬀicult for the graph to align landmarks if the measurements were too noisy.

Diverging landmarks
The last realization that arose when testing the implementation of the bearing-only
algorithm was the divergence of landmarks. When navigating a particular route in the
simulator, the amount of measurements from the robot poses to a specific landmark are
not necessarily ideal. Perhaps only measurements on one side of the robot are acquired
and not abundantly. This would after a certain amount of iterations sometimes cause the
landmark to move further and further away, theoretically moving towards infinity. The
reason for this was found to be due to the nature of the bearing-only error, where if an
optimized configuration of a landmark pose could not be found, the solver would try to
minimize the angular error. By moving the landmark towards infinity, the error between
the robot-landmark angle and the bearing measurement would, in turn, be minimized.
An example of this is seen in figure. 5.6.

The current fix is to check whether a landmark is further away than a certain euclidean
distance from the connected robot pose.

5.4
Robustness
After deriving the individual error functions, and non-zero blocks of the corresponding
Jacobian, the system is essentially ready to be built up, and all the information needed
to construct H and ξ is known.
However, one of the failings that come with graph-SLAM is that the nonlinear least-
squares minimization problem is due to its nature susceptible to the influence of outliers,
erroneous measurements, or through a bad initial guess of the state. Least-squares min-
imizes the squared error sum, assuming a Gaussian distribution, and this distribution
is a pretty good approximation for the sensor noise. However, in the context of the
mentioned outliers coming from, e.g., an imperfect front-end, it will create a disparity
in the data association, and the use of the Gaussian is no longer justified.
This fault in data association can be explained due to the squared error since in the
least-squares optimization, every edge is treated with equal importance. Imagining a
noisy outlier in the dataset, the further away it is from the expected distribution, the
more significant its impact, mainly as it is then squared. Therefore, even a few distant
outliers can easily mess up the system model parameters, leading to faulty optimization.
Ambiguous data association in the realm of landmark-based SLAM could come from
near-identical landmarks, for example, two trees far away from each other being reg-
istered as the same, or while driving and the GNSS module receives a wrong signal
through multipathing.

To combat these potential data association errors, measures are taken to provide a more
robust least-square optimization. When examining the state-of-the-art in section 1.3
there are plenty of novel methods that has been developed over the last decade, and a
few of the major ones were detailed.
After a thorough review, the decision was made to continue with implementing DCS [25].
DCS introduces a method of down-weighing outliers through dynamic weighting of the
information matrix Ωij without compromising on convergence speed of the optimization.
The choice of robustness measure was based on the balance between implementation
time and how robust the results were. Reviewing the research article and knowing the
credibility of the people behind it while also seeing significant improvement with the
addition of DCS it seemed a logical choice. Another aspect is that the implementation
of DCS is not very cumbersome, and after getting an understanding of the theory, it can
easily be implemented.

5.6
Solving and iterating
As the system has been built, the optimization of the graph can now commence. For
building a more adaptable and robust optimization algorithm, the iteration method cho-
sen is Levenberg-Marquardt (LM). It is essentially a combination of the Gauss-Newton
algorithm and steepest descent by adding a dynamic damping factor λ. For the ap-
plication of this the expression for H need to change to allow the implementation of
λ.
Hλ = H + λI
(5.36)
Where I is the identity matrix, with the dimensionality of H.
Another reason for adding this damping factor is to avoid issues when building H due
to an under-determined system. As a result of the nature of landmark-based SLAM and
the inherent need to acquire more than one landmark observation to do localization and
association, significant in bearing-only SLAM explained in section 5.2.1, the risk of an
under-determined system is prevalent.
The implication of needing more than one observation of a landmark is that there
might be a situation where the rank of H is less than full rank as H is always less or
equal to the sum of its component constraints.
Briefly discussed in chapter 3, least-squares optimization is used when an over-
determined system is present, and full rank is needed to solve this system. The addition
==PAGE:84==
70
5 Graph-SLAM Implementation
of the damping coeﬀicient λ essentially takes care of not running into this issue by always
securing full rank through the identity matrix.
Having this implementation does not ensure that the entirety of the graph is well-
conditioned as λI does not provide any valuable information to the optimizer in terms
of moving towards the optimal graph configuration.
Looking back at chapter 3, the concept of sparse structure was discussed. It is clear
when examining the H matrix in figure 5.9 that much of this matrix only contains zero
elements that do not carry any necessary information. However, these zero elements still
take up much memory, especially as the graph becomes larger, and the computation time
is much improved when this unnecessary information is discarded. Luckily the scipy
sparse library [58] has an easy implementation through the csr_matrix(compressed
sparse row) function that returns a sparse H matrix containing only the essential infor-
mation. Using the same H from figure 5.9, the associated data memory used from a
simple memory check is:
H = 278.78Kb
Hsparse = 18Kb
(5.37)
As the size of the graph grows, and the H matrix becomes more sparse, the use of
the csr_matrix functionality becomes more and more important when having to iterate
through and building the matrix multiple times.
This updated sparse H matrix is what is used to solve the system alongside ξ to provide
an updated and hopefully optimized graph.
For this purpose the implementation uses the sparse solver spsolve from scipy that
solves the sparse linear system Hx = ξ and returns the updated change in the graph
dx.
dx = spsolve(−H, ξ)
(5.38)
Iterating through the slam algorithm to provide an optimal solution, this change in the
graph dx is what is added to the current configuration of nodes to move towards this
optimal solution. The procedure of the iteration process explained in this section occurs
in the slam_iterate module.
The graph containing the nodes and edges is loaded, and the global error of the current
graph iteration is calculated and appended to a list that will contain the error value
of each iteration. The initial use of this error is a metric to whether the algorithm is
moving towards an optimized solution. However, this error is also used to keep track
of the Levenberg-Marquardt damping implementation from eq. 5.6. When the error
decreases from the current iteration to the next the damping factor is decreased as well,
but if the error increases between iterations, so does the damping factor, and the previ-
ous version of the graph configuration is recovered. This iteration process will continue
either for the specified number of iterations or when the absolute change in the graph

CHAPTER 7
Discussion
A discussion of the thesis results based on simulation and SLAM implementation is
given in this chapter. The first section comments on the simulator’s limitations and
performance as a front-end for SLAM. Moreover, an evaluation of the graph-SLAM
algorithm based on the results from the previous chapter.
7.1
Simulator
Developing the simulator to act as a front-end was not the intended goal of this thesis;
however, it became a significant part of the workload. Capra has a robot simulator, but
it is deterministic and does not implement object detection for landmark observations.
In addition, no simulators are openly available for landmark-based SLAM; therefore, a
simulator was built for data collection.
The initial GIS data collection from OpenStreetMap as a simulated version of Aarhus
worked well for buildings, streets, and landmarks. There was easy access to data, and
with the help of QGIS to extract the data, the maps came out with a realistic scale. It
was essential to get a proper setting for simulating the robot as there was no access to
an actual robot, and there was a need to test it in an urban environment. However,
because of the limited time of the project, the complexity of the simulator was kept
minimal.
Using the mouse clicker and Catmull-Rom splines to generate robot paths created a
realistic ground truth trajectory for comparing the odometry and the optimized route.
However, this method comes with some limitations.
Firstly, it is almost impossible
to recreate the same control points as mouse-clicking is not pixel accurate, but manu-
ally defining poses in a list would be tedious. Secondly, the varying spacing between
mouse-click control points results in different distances between robot poses and thus
inconsistent interpolation that impacts the odometry noise.
Finally, adding noise to each point in a highly dense spline curve from the interpo-
lation will create an unrealistic and discontinuous odometry path. Hence low density
would not underestimate the intended noise level. An example of this is given in Ap-
pendix B figure B.5. When generating the spline, the intention was to aim for one meter
between each pose so that it was easier to anticipate the general drift of odometry versus
the ground truth. Table 6.2 show that it was achieved to some degree when comparing
nodes with the length of the route.
==PAGE:112==
98
7 Discussion
Generating different loop closures, pose-pose, pose-landmark, and pose-bearing per-
formed as intended.
It was decided not to further test pose-pose loop closure simu-
lations as it was mainly used for development while learning, since the most commonly
known SLAM algorithms are built around this concept, and secondly there were some
discrepancies in the method of pose-pose loop closing and how ICP scan matching works.
To this point, there is a disparity in the variance added to the bearing and how it, in
turn, shows up in the system H matrix. This is because the variance for the other param-
eters is generally calculated in meters, whereas the heading and bearing are in radians.
Therefore, taking the inverse of the variance of a measurement done in radians will often
return a large value, meaning that there is high confidence in the bearing information,
which is much higher than landmarks measured with depth information. Moreover, a
bearing uncertainty of 2 degrees could seem small, but as the distance between robot
and landmark increases, so does the sine of the angle error.
Benchmark datasets were a good way of evaluating the algorithm’s performance. There-
fore, datasets of similar constraint types could be compared with the performance when
using simulated data. However, as no graph-SLAM bearing-only benchmark datasets
are available, it was not possible to cross-reference performance.
It was discussed in chapter 2 that the concept of data association when observing a
unique landmark is a diﬀicult task, and therefore it was important that synthetic outlier
generation was implemented to the simulator to test the SLAM algorithm robustness.
7.2
SLAM
Developing a landmark-based graph-SLAM algorithm practically bottom-up comes with
its considerations and thoughts regarding limitations along the way. As mentioned, it
was decided to build the system to handle both pose-pose constraints and pose-landmarks
constraints. This was both a method for learning SLAM theory and a measure of know-
ing if everything was moving in the right direction.
In section 6 and appendix B, it is shown that the algorithm is able to reach an optimal
configuration of the benchmark datasets that visually compares to some of the open-
source algorithms. It was not possible to acquire the ground truth for error comparison
of the benchmark datasets. Optimizing using depth estimation for landmarks reduces
the absolute error by using only a few iterations; e.g., figure 6.7 shows only two iterations
needed before reaching convergence. Figure 6.6 show pose errors less than 5 meters of
error in x and y, but the algorithm struggles when correcting the orientation having an
error of up to 0.5 radians.
==PAGE:113==
7.2 SLAM
99
It was chosen to implement Dynamic Covariance Scaling as a way of down-weighing
outliers, as this was easy and effective.
However, looking at the research and some
of the articles mentioned in the literature review in section 1.3, more complex meth-
ods of discarding outliers might have performed better. It would be interesting to see
how the algorithm could perform bearing-only optimization with different initialization
techniques of the graph nodes and edges, such as the one proposed by the authors of [62].
Unfortunately, there was no possibility of testing the algorithm on actual data collected
by a Capra robot; however, it would be interesting in the future to see how it would be
performing and how the real-world data would differ from the simulated data.
The implemented landmark initialization techniques regarding bearing-only had inter-
esting configuration changes during the different iterations when watching how the least-
squares method of finding the landmark and the SLAM algorithm placed the landmark
close to the ground truth. This form of SLAM comes with further delay in initializa-
tion compared to pose-pose or depth measurement, which has to be considered if used
on-line. The robustness of bearing-only SLAM depends on good bearing measurements
and ensuring that diverging landmarks do not affect the graph. However, completely
removing landmarks is not an eﬀicient robustness measure. Further development on this
issue should be considered.
The use of the Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm proved essential for the landmark-based
implementation. There were times when an under-determined system was obtained for
a dataset and would not be solvable by the algorithm without the damping coeﬀicient λ.
There are problems with the bearing-only algorithm regarding performance compared
to landmark optimization with depth and pose-pose measurement.
There are overwhelmingly many parameters to consider when implementing bearing-
only graph-SLAM to come to a single best parameter configuration for all routes. It
would be interesting to see with further development and with real datasets if this dis-
crepancy is because of the bearing-only algorithm or because the simulated data is too
simple.
Another issue that was only considered late in the thesis process can be seen in the FOV
analysis tables in the result section is the different trajectory of the noisy route when
generating a new dataset. When generating the noisy route from the saved ground truth
route, e.g., route one, the ground truth is being saved, the user changes the FOV, and
a new noisy route is generated. As the noise is random from a Gaussian distribution,
even if the ground truth route is the same, the noisy routes are different afterward.
If time allowed, it would have been wise to either do multiple noisy route generations
with the same FOV and average the pre and post-error calculation or save the noisy
route instead of the ground truth route to ensure each noisy route had the same trajec-
tory.

7 Discussion
Commenting on the Capra Robotics application of driving around an urban environment,
most likely on sidewalks, there is still development to be done and tests to be made to
provide better insight into whether this is feasible or not. Even if the algorithm moves
the error much closer to the ground truth, there are still issues. Having more than
a couple of meters disparity between the ground truth and the optimized odometry
route is not good enough to navigate an average sidewalk with a width of 1-3 meters
[63]. Improvement towards better error minimization could be made with an on-line
application of the algorithm alongside further robustness implementations and methods
for detecting the surroundings.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion and future work
This chapter presents the conclusion on the entirety of this thesis, especially regard-
ing the problem formulation and the result section. Furthermore, some considerations
and suggestions are given on possible implementations that could further develop the
simulator and landmark-based graph-SLAM algorithm to a higher level of performance.
8.1
Conclusion
This thesis’s primary objective has been to provide a landmark-based SLAM solution
for an urban mobile robot developed by Capra Robotics. As the scope of the project
narrowed, this solution was further developed towards a bearing-only SLAM algorithm.
The implementation of the algorithm was implemented and showed great promise in
providing a minimal-information solution with further development. Building on this,
different types of landmark initialization techniques were implemented and tested and
showed positive results in providing proper estimates of the landmarks configuration in
the environment.
Different SLAM and loop closure designs were developed. First, pose-pose loop clos-
ing for benchmark dataset testing, then a landmark-based depth measurement solution,
and lastly, bearing-only SLAM being the minimal information representation of the en-
vironment.
Unfortunately, no proper data collection campaign was achieved alongside Capra and
is yet to be tested and determine whether the developed solution will provide robust
SLAM in a real-world setting.
A basic two-dimensional simulator was implemented to counter the issues of not
working with an actual robot. However, using the word ”basic” is only related to the
intricacies of an actual robot, and many complex methods were applied in the simulator.
Using GIS data from OpenStreetMap was a great addition and worked well in providing
an a priori map of the environment and the landmarks present. The simulator also pro-
vided a clear understanding of the possible test scenarios for the actual robot and how
the environment might look in terms of attainable landmarks to be used for navigation.
Lastly, to give some evaluation and conclude on the actual performance of the bearing-
only version of the SLAM algorithm.
As discussed in chapter 7, there is still much
work to be done to optimize further the solution provided in the thesis. The overall
algorithm does a satisfying job of optimizing when the gathered quality of data obtained

8 Conclusion and future work
is not ill-conditioned; however, the errors have to be minimized further as a measure
of keeping on a sidewalk. Furthermore, the problem of landmark divergence during the
optimization process results from an insuﬀicient amount of data to a specific landmark.
Therefore, it is not enough to only have well-conditioned data. However, it is necessary
to have multiple observations of a specific landmark at different angles to get an accurate
landmark position. Testing on an actual robot and through an on-line application where
the loop-closures can be done via landmarks as soon as they are perceived would be
interesting.
8.2
Future work
Bearing-only graph-based SLAM is a relatively unexplored territory in the realm of
SLAM. This entails plenty of things that are come across concerning graph-SLAM and
in terms of probabilistic SLAM and other subjects within autonomous navigation that
could improve the performance. Therefore, for the future development of bearing-only
graph-SLAM this section provides some ideas and thoughts on the subject.
Robustness measures are being improved constantly, and techniques such as the one
explained in [27] could perhaps provide further robustness measures, especially in envi-
ronments where humans and other non-static objects are present.
Regarding the use of GIS data and open-source databases, furthering how these
pose-landmark constraints can be used navigating could provide a lot of promise. As
was shown in section results, both depth and bearing-only are excellent at obtaining
a close to ground truth configuration of the landmarks. Having this landmark config-
uration could allow for robust data association. The relation between each landmark
could help identify each specific landmark and make the front-end instance recognition
more robust. On top of this, the implementation of a method that will allow adding
unknown landmarks to the GIS map and removing landmarks that do not exist in the
environment was not fulfilled in this thesis, but could be a significant addition.
Making extensive use of the environment could be an interesting implementation. For
example, knowing the robot is navigating in an urban environment, buildings, windows,
and other structures could be used for navigating alongside the GIS landmark data, as
the robot would most likely have a building on at least one side at practically all times.
Lastly, further development on the front-end simulator to get it closer to mimicking
an actual Capra robot would provide easier access to delivering a better bearing-only
SLAM algorithm.
