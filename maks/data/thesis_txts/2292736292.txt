Abstract
In 2008 the Greenlandic inhabitants voted to become became more self-governing.
As a part of the increased independence the block grant that Greenland receives
from Denmark was frozen. This means that Greenland’s GDP decreases a little bit
each year due to inﬂation. Therefore there is a pressure on Greenland to be self
sufﬁcient, but the island suffer infrastructural problems: no roads between towns,
large distances and few access gateways to the country. One of the most impor-
tant access gateways is Greenland’s International Airport in Kangerlussuaq. The
airport has a road connection to a harbor, which means that Kangerlussuaq is a
connection point between the greenlandic ship lines and international air trafﬁc.
The problem is that the water depth outside the current harbor in Kangerlussuaq
is too shallow for large ships. This means that cruise and cargo ships must anchor
far out in the fjord, and send containers and tourists by small vessels, which is not
cost efﬁcient. As a direct consequence, it takes up to 4 days to unload a container
ship, compared to 6 hours if the ship were docked at the harbor.
A solution to this problem is to make a new harbor. A new location is found
10 km further out in the fjord, with sufﬁcient depth. It is believed that the location
should not have any problems due to sedimentation in 100 years. The challenge is
the remoteness of the location, and a seabed consisting of weak material. Measure-
ments indicates that the bedrock is at close proximity and inclined, which makes
it challenging to ﬁnd a suitable and durable construction.
By investigating the environmental actions at the location, it was seen that the
new location is sheltered from both wind and currents. The currents at the location
were in the order of 0.12 m/s at most, while the wind returned a design wave heigh
of 3.1 m. To meet the challenging geotechnical conditions and with the base in
the governing environmental actions, a sheet pile retaining wall was found to the
most cost efﬁcient for for a new harbor. From the preliminary design calculations
it was found that the design moment at the sheet pile wall was in the order of 1150
kNm, which means that the solution is highly plausible for the site. The solution
were also customized for the location, by optimizing the use of on-site materials
to avoid under water work.

Introduction
Greenland is the largest island in the world, and the arctic climate makes it one of the
most unique. In 2008 the island regained some of its power and became a more self-
governing Danish territory. Greenland still receives a block grant from Denmark, but it
is frozen. A consequence of this is that due to inﬂation the grant will become less and
less worth. This means that the island must increase its self sufﬁciency and the ability
to support itself (Mcsmidt, 2008). The unique arctic climate makes Greenland an in-
teresting destination for tourists, but there have been a decrease in the tourism after the
ﬁnancial crisis. Luckily, ﬁsh export, which is the main income for Greenland, have had
a steady increase over the same time period (Vahl and Kleemann, 2014). Further more
there are indications valuable metal deposits in Greenland as well (Mcsmidt, 2008). If
Greenland wants to reclaim the tourism and to be able to handle import and export and
mining in remote locations, the infrastructure should be facilitated properly. Unfortu-
nately that is easier said than done, as the towns and settlements are not connected by
roads, but by air and sea trafﬁc. The main gateway to Greenland is the international
airport in Kangerlussuaq, a small settlement of 500 inhabitants at the innermost part of
the 180 km long Kangerlussuaq Fjord. The inlet of the fjord is located at the west coast
of Greenland, where the fjord reaches into the country, towards the inland ice with a
North East (NE) direction. The fjord and the settlement can be seen in Figure 1.1,
Kangerlussuaq is located within the municipality of Qeqqata, and the municipality’s
administration is located in Sisimiut.

1.1
Geotechnical conditions
One of the main challenges regarding the harbor’s placement is the conditions of the ma-
terial outside the pynt. Core samples from nearby locations collected in 2013 and tested
by Orlander (2014) showed ﬁne grained marine sediments which consisted mainly of
cohesive sediments with low shear strength Orlander (2014). Stenstad and Eppeland
(2014) took grab samples in 2014 outside Hancock Pynt of the top sediment layer on
the seabed. The sampled material showed a negative consistency index (Stenstad and
Eppeland, 2014), which means that the natural content of water is bigger than the liquid
limit of the sediments. This indicates that the material is unsuitable as a supporting
material. In september 2014 sediment core samples where taken just outside Hancock
Pynt, which indicated a material with low bearing capacity, with sediment depths vary-
ing from 20 cm to 7 meters (Jeppesen, 2015). It should be noted that the results from
september 2014 are notes of the boring log, and the ﬁnal analysis and laboratory results
of the investigation have not yet been published. Nevertheless, all tests indicates that the
material outside the harbor is unsuitable for foundation support. The bedrock consists
mainly of gneiss along with dark magmatic rocks (Henriksen, 2008), and bathymetry
measurements taken outside the harbor indicates that the bed slope is steep outside
the harbor (Wengel-Hansen, 2014). The fjord is located in a climate of arctic tundra
(Covey, 2002) with a mean annual temperature of −5.7C (DMI, 1999), the fjord is
therefore covered with ice for most parts of the year (Hansen, 2014).
1.2
Thesis aim
At the current moment no thorough analysis on the governing environmental actions
at the location have been conducted, which is a natural step in the direction of a new
3
==PAGE:16==
harbor. Thus the aim for this thesis is to map the key environmental actions at the chosen
location. Based on this a preliminary harbor design will be made. Hansen (2014) states
in his report that the design of the harbor most likely will be a sheet pile wall, ﬁxed in
a concrete trench. This is common practice when making quay walls in Greenland, and
have been done with success in Sisimiut (Aarsleff, 2013). As a part of this thesis it will
be investigated whether the preconceived concept is the most optimal for the location.
The preliminary design will initially be customized to conditions at the chosen loca-
tion, but the possibility to adapt the design elsewhere will also be of importance, as
the increased focus on exploiting the nature resources in Greenland and increasing the
country’s tourist activity will require harbors at similar remote locations, such as this.
Thus in the preliminary design, the harbors adaptability, the construction time and the
possibility to utilize on-site materials will be weighted highly. This means that the
structure must be ﬂexible regarding water depth, and it must possible to construct it
directly on a steep seabed consisting of materials with low bearing capacity, where the
bedrock might be at close proximity. When choosing a structural design an analysis
of the most cost efﬁcient design will be conducted, where the design parameters from
Tsinker (1997) are used as a base for the ﬁnal decision. The parameters are:
• Geological conditions
• Water depth
• Cost and availability of construction materials
• Environmental constrains

1.3
Environmental actions and selection of calculation method
To be able to investigate the consequences of the environmental actions, they must be
identiﬁed. In the following the key environmental actions will be presented, along with
their inﬂuence on the harbor structure.
• Tidal water - The change in tidal water can result in large pressure differences
on a structure. The change of water level can also cause problems for a harbor
structure when the water depth is an issue. The tidal water can also return currents
which can increase the wave height if the current is strong and is opposing the
wave direction. Currents will also induce mooring forces, and can therefore be
dominating when choosing the direction of the berth line.
• Wind - The wind is the main parameter for wave generation and induces mooring
forces. The wind can therefore be dominating when choosing the direction of the
berth line. In hard winds the operation of a harbor shuts down.
• Waves - The waves are governing for the ships motions. The design wave is
often governing for determining the crest height of the harbor structure, due to
the limiting amount of allowable overtopping of water.
• Ice - The ice can be a dominating factor in the arctic region. The thermal expan-
sion can create large pressure forces, while large ice sheets can return large lift
forces when under tidal conditions. Floating ice can also create large horizontal
forces on the structure due to impact.
The most precise way to get a full overview of the environmental actions described
is to perform accurate measurements over several years, covering a large area, but this
method is very expensive and not time efﬁcient. Another and more cost efﬁcient method
is to map the environment through numerical modeling, predictions, theoretical calcu-
lations and statistical analysis. Thus the latter is chosen in this thesis.
The tidal water investigations will be done by preforming a time harmonic analysis to
identify the tidal changes in the fjord, where the tidal currents will be located by using
the numerical modeling software MIKE 21 - Hydrodynamic Flow Model (HD) by DHI
(DHI, 2014 a.). With the model it is possible to simulate the currents speed and direction
for an area, due to an incoming ﬂux of water. The model also retrieves the surface
elevation for the whole area of interest. The wind is actually measured at a nearby
location over a period of 40 years, thus simulations and predictions are not needed, but
a statistical analysis will be preformed to retrieve the characteristic wind speed for a
design event. The wave climate on the other hand will be identiﬁed by the numerical
modeling software MIKE 21 - Spectral Waves Flow Model (SW) by DHI DHI (2014
b.). To retrieve the effect of the harbor on the wave the numerical modeling software
MIKE 21 - Boussinesq Wave Model (BW) by DHI (DHI, 2014 c.) is used to get the
near shore actions of the waves. The ice climate will be found by investigating climate
normals in combination with theoretical ice development formulations. All calculations
could have been done by theoretical formulations, for example by following guidelines
such as the Shore Protection Manual for predicting the wave climate for given winds
(SPM, 1984). The advantage of simulation software is that they can reproduce an area,

generalized. The disadvantage of using a software is that the calculation procedures

Core samples
I september 2014 a total of 12 core samples of the sea bed outside Hancock Pynt were

The tidal water measurements have 2 main purposes in this thesis. The ﬁrst is calibrat-
ing the HD ﬂow model, and the second is to ﬁnd the tidal constituents for harmonic
predictions of the tidal water investigations.
The tidal water was measured by a Levelogger, which is placed in the water with some
distance from the surface, measuring the water pressure in meters. Since the distance
from the levelogger to the surface is constant, it measures the pressure difference as the
tidal wave passes. The levelogger measures the atmospheric pressure as well as the wa-
ter pressure, thus the atmospheric pressure must be subtracted from the measurements,
as it will vary with time as well. The atmospheric pressure was measured with a level-
ogger on land over the same time period as the leveloggers in the fjord, and is assumed
to be constant over the whole area. The measurements of the atmospheric pressure is
therefore subtracted from all of the data sets.
The absolute distance down to the Levellogger is not know, but it is only the relative
water level difference that is of interest. Thus a zero value have been sat for each mea-
surement. This chosen to be 0, which means that the mean of each sample is subtracted
of the whole sample. In this way the tidal water level time series ﬂuctuates about 0 and
the relative water level measurements can be compared. The locations with the corre-
sponding water levels is presented in Figure 2.3. From Figure 2.3 it can be seen that
there is a varying amount of scatter for the different locations. This scatter is caused
by the wind induced wave motion (M. H. Nielsen, pers.com. 2015). It is seen that the
at the location «boundary» and «Camp Lloys» the scatter is minimal, which indicates
good tidal measurements. Therefore these two points will also be used as the Boundary
and calibration of the numerical MIKE 21 by DHI models.

2.5.1
Calculations of signiﬁcant wave height and peak period
The wave environment is found with the MIKE 21 Spectral Wave Model (SW). The
SW model does not produce the surface elevation as a time series, but it simulates
14
==PAGE:27==
the signiﬁcant wave heights (Hm0), with corresponding peak periods (Tp). Therefore
the measured time series must be transformed to a signiﬁcant wave height and a peak
period. The signiﬁcant wave heigh is the mean of the highest third of the waves for a
given time period. The peak period is found by using the frequency spectrum. Irregular
waves, such as the time series measured, can be describes by the energy is distribution
of the wavelets. This means that the measured heave time series can be plotted as an
energy - frequency plot, or a variance spectrum (Liu and Frigaard, 2001). The peak
frequency ( fp) is the frequency with the highest energy, which returns the peak period
through the relationship Tp = 1/ fp. The signiﬁcant wave heigh can be found through the
frequency spectrum by taking the square of the total area under the energy density curve,
and multiplying it by 4. This means that the signiﬁcant wave heigh and peak period are
just two statistical parameters which can be used to describe a time series of irregular
waves Liu and Frigaard (2001). The calculation of Hm0 and Tp from the measured time
series were preformed in the frequency domain by a discrete signal analysis according
to Liu and Frigaard (2001). For the calculations a sea state of 3 hours can be assumed
(Saulnier et al., 2011). This means that the measured time series from 03.09.2013 to
02.10.2013 is the is divided into time series with 3 hours intervals, where each interval
represents one sea state. This means that each sea state returns a variance spectrum with
corresponding signiﬁcant wave height and peak period. The calculation procedure with
more detailed descriptions and corresponding Matlab script is enclosed in Appendix D
on page 87, where the given time series is transformed into variance spectrum by a Fast
Fourier Transformation (FFT) using built in functions in Matlab.
For a time series analysis the signiﬁcant wave height can also be found trough the time
domain as well, but since the SW-model use a variance spectrum for wave calculations,
the frequency domain were chosen for comparison. The signiﬁcant wave heigh in the
time domain is found by taking the mean of the highest third of each sea state (Liu and
Frigaard, 2001), and comparing it with the results from the frequency domain.

3.1
Ice environment - Method
There is limited statistical information about the ice and the ice movement in the area,
but measurements of the ice thickness have been found to be over 1 meter thick (Morten
Holtegaard Nielsen pers. com). Due to the uncertainties of the ice thickness, Stefan’s
law for ice growth is used, which estimates the ice growth based on the number of
frozen days per year (Høyland, 2015). Since the fjord is more or less ice free each year,
it is assumed that only ﬁrst year ice that will interact with the structure, and that icebergs
are unexisting. The number of frozen days per year is estimated by taking the monthly
average temperature multiplied with the number of days in each respective month. The
average temperature for Kangerlussuaq is found at DMI (1999).

3.2.2
Tidal current calculations - Hydrodynamic Flow Model (HD model)
The MIKE 21 HD model by DHI (2014 a.) is used for getting the tidal current climate

3.2.2.2
Calibration of the hydrodynamic Flow Model (HD model)
To calibrate
the model the surface elevation time series measured by Morten Holtegaard Nielsen
at the location named «Open Boundary» in Figure 3.2 was used as input data, where
the output data were compared and calibrated with the measured surface elevation time
series at the point named «Camp Lloyd». To calibrate a HD model, a change of the
the bed friction and/or change of the Horizontal Eddy Viscosity are usual adjusting
parameters (DHI, 2014 a.). The bed friction can in a HD model either be described as
the manning number (M) or the chezy number (C), where the manning number for this
case have been chosen. The lower the manning number, the higher the friction (DHI,
2014 a.). From Figure 3.3 it can be seen that test runs with a high manning number of
45 m1/3/s, which indicates small bed friction, returns spikes and a scatter in the surface
elevation. Whereas a low manning number, resulted in less scatter. When the manning
number is as small as 15 m1/3/s, the surface elevation have a harmonic shape, which is
to be believed to be the correct, as the surface elevation is primarily inﬂuenced by the
tidal water. It should be noted that the surface elevation time series in Figure 3.3 is over
an arbitrary time period, and is only shown for the purpose of illustration.

3.3
Wind environment - method
The wind will have a large effect on wave generation and mooring of the ships. In this
thesis its the design situations which is of interest regarding the wind-wave interaction.
The design wave is assumed to be a result of the design wind. Therefore a design
wind speed is found through statistical analysis of the maximum wind speeds. The
generation of wind waves is a factor of 2 parameters. The wind speed and the fetch
length, which is the distance that the wind blows over water is important (Liu and
Frigaard, 2001). Therefore an analysis of the fetch length was preformed to ﬁnd the
directions which had the longest fetch lengths. Even though most winds come from NE-
directions, the harbor is sheltered from this direction. To ﬁnd the critical fetch length
direction, a visual analysis was preformed by using the map. By analyzing the fetch
lengths two sectors were categorized as critical; south west (SW) and south east (SE).
The directions are graphically shown in Figure 3.5. The data was sorted in sectors spans
which covered a wider area than the span of the fjord, due to the uncertainties of the
local wind directions. This way the sectors cover a greater area, which means that there
is a greater chance for high wind speeds. Figure 3.5 also shows the analysis for the mean
fetch length. The mean fetch length is used in the theoretical design wave calculations
which is used to for comparison with the results from the design wave simulations.
The mean fetch length is found by using 9 radials with 3o intervals, making the range
become 227o ±4·3o for SW direction and 121o ±4·3o for SE direction in accordance
to Liu and Frigaard (2001).

The maximum wind speeds for the statistical analysis for each sector were selected by
two different methods. One which ﬁnds the maximum wind speeds from a peak over
threshold (POT) method, and one which selects the annual maximum. For the peak
over threshold method, the criteria of homogeneity is adhered by assuming that a storm
lasts for 3 days (Liu and Frigaard, 2001), thus setting this as a limit distance between
25
==PAGE:38==
the maximum values. The threshold values were set to 12.3 m/s for SE and 13.2 m/s
for SW. In this way the POT method returned the same amount of data as the annual
maximum wind, which is 41. This is representative for a maximum distribution since
the requirement is that the statistical method should not be preformed if the amount of
data is smaller than 10, and it should be bigger than 20 Palutikof et al. (1999).
The design wind is used to ﬁnd the design wave. To ﬁnd the design wave for the design
for wave generation, it is assumed that the wind is constant over a long enough time
period for sea to be fully arisen. This means that the wind’s energy input is equal to
the energy loss (Liu and Frigaard, 2001). Since the wind measurements are given in 10
minutes mean wind speeds, they must be transformed into the necessary time for the
sea state equilibrium to be fulﬁlled. To do this linear interpolation of the measured data
was preformed on the measured wind data. When investigation of the wind data was
preformed, it was seen that the wind speeds were varying rapidly, which can create un-
certainties in the linear interpolation. In addition, the wind recordings were made over a
interval of three hours until 1998, which increases the inaccuracy of the linear interpo-
lation. Therefore the 10 minutes wind speeds were transformed into mean equilibrium
wind speed by using a logarithmic transformation in accordance to USACE (2008) as
well.
To ﬁnd the design wind speeds from the critical sectors a characteristic return period
of 50 years is chosen, which is equivalent with a yearly probability of exceedance of
0.02, which is used according to Eurocode 1: General actions - Wind actions (CEN,
2005). For statistical prediction of the of the design wind speed a 2-parametric Gumble
distribution i used in accordance to Palutikof et al. (1999), where the design wind speed
is found using a reduced variate method. From each critical direction the following
statistical design calculations were made:
• Maximum annual wind speed transformed to mean equilibrium wind speed in
accordance to USACE (2008)
• Maximum annual wind speed transformed to mean equilibrium wind speed by
linear interpolation
• Maximum wind speed from POT transformed to mean equilibrium wind speed in
accordance to USACE (2008)
• Maximum wind speed from POT transformed to mean equilibrium wind speed
by linear interpolation
The statistical Gumbel calculations were preformed for all sectors with a fetch length
longer than 16 km. The design wind speeds from each sector was found by evaluating
the results from the Gumble distributions using the goodness of ﬁt and the maximum
design wind speeds.

Wave environment - method
3.4.1
Undisturbed wave calculation - Spectral Waves - Flow Model (SW)
To ﬁnd the design wave actions the MIKE 21 Spectral Waves - Flow Model (SW) by
DHI was used. The model simulates the «growth, decay and transformation of wind-
generated waves» (DHI, 2014 b.), and can simulate waves over a large area, which
means that local conditions can be detected. The model have been proven to give a good
representation of both costal and offshore regions (DHI, 2014 b.). The main purpose of
the Spectral Wave model is to ﬁnd the design incident signiﬁcant wave height (Hm0−i)
with corresponding incident peak period (Tp−i) through simulation. The incident wave
is the undisturbed wave without near shore effects.
To make sure that the model creates a reliable result, calibrated against the measured
heave described in Section 2.5. This is done by using a wind speed and wind direction
time series for the same time period as the measured heave as input data. Along with the
wind data, the current speed and direction from the HD model for the same time period.
The output is then compared with the signiﬁcant wave period and peak period, calcu-
lated from the measured heave time series. In Figure 3.6 the location of the Waverider
can be seen, along with the mesh used in the runs. It is seen that the mesh is more dense
around the headland and close to the Waverider, where the resolution is 100 m with a
maximum area of 8000 m2. For the SW model there are no open boundaries, because it
is the wind that affects the wave creation, but the surface elevation will ﬂuctuate due to
the changes in the water level output from the HD model. The land is set automatically
to 10 m, which is the default setting in MIKE.

3.4.1.3
Calibration Process
To calibrate the SW model, the settings were adjusted
in accordance to (DHI, 2014 b.). The parameters were adjusted within the recommend
values to see if it was possible to recreate the measured signiﬁcant wave heights, by
using the measured wind. In total 19 different settings were tried out. The one which
returned the most accurate result will be described. The time formulation was set to be
Quasi-stationary, since the model is assumed to have fetch limited wave growth. It was
assumed that the currents are too small at the point of interest to create wave blocking,
thus for the current conditions the default blocking factor of 0.1 was used. A quadruplet-
and triad-wave interaction, with a transfer value of 0.25 were included. For the wind
forcing the air-sea interaction was set to a coupled formulation, with Charnock param-
eter of 0.01, as it was found to ﬁt the model the best, even though this formulation can
cause overestimation of the signiﬁcant wave height for spatial space smaller than about
10-100 km (DHI, 2014 b.). It was found to deviate less than the uncoupled formulation
for the peaks of interest. To be able to include the smallest time periods, the number of
frequencies was set to 35, with a minimum frequency of 0.08. This means that the SW
model is able to simulate periods in the range of T=0.44-12.5 seconds. For the wave
generation, a standard JONSWAP spectrum was chosen.
The environmental input data in the calibration of the model were wind, current and
surface elevation. The wind was set to vary in time, but constant in the domain, which
means that time series was assumed to be constant over the whole model area. The
wind data used as input is the time series is seen in Figure 3.7 and described in 2.4.
The current speed, current direction and surface elevation was set to varying in time
and varying in domain. The input data was found by using the time series in Figure 3.8
as input in the HD model, which returned an area ﬁle for the SW model area. The time
series used was predicted by t_predic.m (Pawlowicz, 2014).

rents and surface elevation from the highest tidal wave, with constant design wind as
input. The simulation must last for minimum time until the sea is at equilibrium in
accordance to Liu and Frigaard (2001). Since the current also plays a small part in the
incident wave analysis, the simulation period is taken for a full tidal period of 12 hours.
The simulation must be preformed from all critical wind directions. The deign incident
signiﬁcant wave height is found taking output from a line series perpendicular to the
critical direction. To ﬁnd the maximum values, 5 points of output data is taken along
the line, where the maximum signiﬁcant wave height of the 5 points on the line is used in
further calculations. The design values are compared with fetch limited theory from Liu
and Frigaard (2001) for result validation. In the theoretical calculations the signiﬁcant
wave heigh and the peak period uses the average of «9 radials from the point of interest
at 3-degree intervals» as fetch length (Liu and Frigaard, 2001), which is previously
explained in Section 4.3.
3.4.2
Near shore wave action - Design wave - Boussinesq Wave model
The Boussinesq Wave (BW) is used to simulate the near shore effect on incident waves.
In this case the purpose is to ﬁnd the harbor’s effect on the incoming waves, where the
result is used for determining the crest height of the quay wall and wave forces on the
quay wall. The model is based on numerical solutions of time domain formulation of
the Boussinesq formulation. The formulations include both nonlinearity and frequency
dispersion. This means that it is not longer a monochromatic wave situation, thus calcu-
lating wave-trains traveling from deep to shallow water (DHI, 2014 c.). The BW model
calculates the combined effect of various factors that occur for a wave train, such as
diffraction, refraction, partial and full reﬂection and transmission and shoaling (DHI,
2014 c.).

3.4.5
Boussinesq modeling process
The input data in the BW model is a time series of a line of water ﬂux. The time series
used, is a random generated ﬂux time series, generated by the MIKE toolbox «random
Wave Generation». The toolbox was used to generate a standard JONSWAP spectrum,

wave height and peak wave period from the SW model.
The results from the Boussinesq wave model will be compared with results from linear
wave theory from Svendsen and Jonsson (1982), by using the design signiﬁcant wave
height from the SW model. For the calculations from the linear wave theory, it is as-
sumed that the bottom contours are parallel, and that the waves are propagating towards
the shore with perpendicular orthogonals to the quay wall. For the interaction between
the wall and the wave it is assumed that full reﬂection occurs. It is also assumed that
no energy is dissipated due to bed friction (Svendsen and Jonsson, 1982). The duration
of the simulations in the BW model are done over a period of time long enough for the
reﬂection pattern to be fully developed.

3.5

3.5.1
Tidal current effects and tidal model uncertainties
For the tidal water level calculations there is unfortunately no information regarding the
current measurements, which makes the surface elevation the only calibration parame-
ter. The prediction of the tidal surface elevation shows a maximum deviation of 0.56 m,
while the the calibration results from the hydrodynamic ﬂow model shows a deviation
of 0.22 m for the surface elevation at the calibration point Camp Lloyd, when using the
measured input at the boundary. This means that the there can potentially be a total
deviation which is larger than both values. The deviation from the calibration results
do have a scattered variation, which indicates that the deviation can be a result of local
winds, rather than a parameter that could not be simulated. The tidal prediction using
the program t_tide.m and t_predic.m (Pawlowicz, 2014) do have a sinusoidal variation
which indicates that the prediction does not take into account the tidal effects. Another
weakness of the prediction is that it uses the same time series for calculations of the
constitutes, and then uses those constitutes to predict the same time series. Here either
a longer time series or another time series measured at another time, could have given
more accurate results. Another observation is that there are some patterns in the devia-
tion which show effects larger than one cycle of 24 hours. This can be an indication of
conditions, such as winds or swells, which the tidal prediction cannot predict.
The possible high deviation could have been alarming, but the most important observa-
tion is that both the prediction and simulation of the tidal conditions have small phase
shift, meaning that the accuracy in the time of the crest and the trough of the is good.
This is important since the model is used for the wave current interaction, and the timing
of time current is an important parameter. It was also seen that the tidal waves did not
return higher currents than 0.16 m/s outside the headland. Therefore it was concluded
that this way of modeling the currents were sufﬁcient.
This was conﬁrmed for the run for the design wave in the SW model, where it is seen
that the currents have a minimum effect on the signiﬁcant wave height in the SW model.
It was also found in the calculations that the tidal currents did not return problems for the
mooring of the ships. It can be concluded that the tidal water differences will not have
any practical inﬂuence on either the structure, or the waves regarding wave blocking. It
will, on the other hand, inﬂuence the structure with respect to lifting forces due to ice.

3.5.3
Wind and heave correlations
The model for investigation consist of many uncertainties, where the biggest one is
the wind. It is seen in the calibrations of the SW model that the calibration is far from
perfect. When considering the wind data which is used as an input, it is seen that there is
little correlation between the the measured heave and wind. One way to solve this could
be to adjust the wind data to ﬁt the surface elevation better, and then trying to reproduce
this data in the SW model. This method was disregarded because of high uncertainty,
and it was concluded that it would probably do more harm than good. Instead, the
analysis was preformed as it is now, where ﬁtting patterns were analyzed, rather than
absolute results. In this way the model’s strengths and weaknesses are found. And
the end result showed that the model seemed to pick up the patterns for the extreme
wind directions, where the model was able to reproduce the peaks. The model also
under estimated some of the winds from north, even though the SW model does not
take into account the heights over 10 meters on land, and the location of the Waverider
is much more sheltered in real life than what in modeled. It was seen in the result
that at the ﬁrst peak the the wind was blowing about 145o, where the measured Hm0−M
returned a response of about 0.8 times the normalized wind speed. For the second
peak, the wind was blowing from about 245o which returns a decrease in the relative
difference between the normalized wind and the measured Hm0−M down to 0.6 times
the normalized wind speed. For the simulated signiﬁcant wave height Hm0−SW, the

response was 0.7 at the ﬁrst peak and 0.8 at the second. The reason for the scatter in
the response can be a direct consequence of the SW models formulation of land since
the point of interest is much more sheltered at the second peak than the ﬁrst. Thus
when the SW model simulates the waves using a constant land boundary of 10 m, it can
create problems for narrow fjords such as this. This can also be seen throughout the
calibration, that the simulated values are in general higher than the measured values.
On the other hand, the wind was also quite slow during estimation periods. Therefore it
is difﬁcult to draw a concrete conclusion out of the models weaknesses. These results
could indicate that the model is not able to recreate winds from north, or that the model
is not able to predict the surface elevation caused by slow winds. The previously dis-
cussed correlation factor between land and water wind inﬂuences slow winds more than
high speed winds, which is a factor to take into account. The reason for the large devi-
ation could also be a result of local wind speeds or wind directions, due to the location
of the measure station compared to the location of the harbor. If this is the case, it is
logical that the wind have a better ﬁt with the heave, when the direction is parallel with
the fjord. Since if the wind is not parallel with the, there is a bigger chance of obstacles,
which can return local wind directions.
Ideally the heave measurements should have been made over a longer time period,
where it were stronger winds with longer constant direction. This would have made
it easier to evaluate the models strengths and weaknesses better. Nevertheless, in the
calibration process it was seen that event though the parameters of the model were
changed, it did only return small variations of the simulated signiﬁcant wave height.
This indicates that it could not compensate for the difference between the wind speed
and measured heave. This it is a clear indication that the biggest uncertainty parameter
is the wind and measured surface elevation correlation. Therefore it was concluded that
the SW model was good enough for the design purpose.

3.5.5
Uncertainties in the calculation for the disturbed waves
For the Boussinesq wave model, the wave breaking criteria is the limiting value for
the minimum waver depth, where it is believed that wave breaking would not have
inﬂuenced the signiﬁcant wave height by any means. On the contrary, wave breaking
results in lowering of the wave height, which could result in a lower structure. This
could become a problem for the design forces. The reason for this is that the steepness
of the slope outside the quay wall and the wave height will break as plunging waves at
the quay wall. If plunging waves breaks at a vertical wall, they can leave a hammering
shock due to entrapped air inside the wave. This force can exceed the regular wave
force caused by the pressure distribution along the wall (PIANC, 1992). Therefore it
was important to look deeper into the waves from the Boussinesq wave model to see if
there were potentials for increased wave forces. When analyzing the results from the
Boussinesq wave model it was seen that the waves outside the quay wall fulﬁlled the
empirical shallow water breaking criteria, which means that wave breaking do not occur
outside at the quay wall, without the adjusted bathymetry as well. This means that the
assumption regarding non-wave breaking for the Boussinesq wave model is a veriﬁed
assumption, and using the shallow water breaking criteria for the minimum water depth
around the harbor will not inﬂuence the results regarding the design wave height, and
the design forces at the wall.

5
Harbor design considerations
5.1
Selection structure type
There are several considerations that have to be taken into account when choosing a
constructing a harbor in a remote location. This will make transportation of materi-
als and equipment expensive, which makes usage of on-site materials favorable. The
cold long winters make the fjord freeze with a ice thickness of over 1 meter (Morten
Holtegaard Nielsen pers. com), which can cause substantial problems and large forces
on the structure, in combination with large tidal differences. At the same time both
Stenstad and Eppeland (2014), Orlander (2014) and Jeppesen (2015) indicated in their
investigations of the sea bed in the area, that the offshore sediments at the location had
low bearing capacity and is not suitable to support foundations. The construction must
therefore be able to resist these challenges. The analysis of the cost efﬁciency were
done by using the 4 main design parameters from Tsinker (1997). In this thesis the
three main types of harbor structure have been evaluated, regarding cost efﬁciency. The
three types are: precast gravity walls, a sheet pile retaining wall and a piled solution
(Tsinker, 1997).
5.1.1
Geological conditions
If the bed rock is present at close proximity, it favors a gravity-type structure (Tsinker,
1997). For example a gravity based quay-wall composed of prefabricated L-shaped.
The drawback for this type of construction is that the installation requires big cranes,
both offshore and onshore. Another disadvantage is that according to Tsinker (1997)
counterforts must be used as stiffeners when the height exceeds 7 meters. This is of
course a problem as it is expensive to transport heavy elements, also as municipality
would like to be able to increase the harbor in the future, new elements must be casted.
Also, when investigating the area, it was seen that the inclination of the the seabed is
steep. This may therefore require additionally under water work, as a L-shaped wall
requires a relatively ﬂat surface, such as a plateau to stand upon.
5.1.2
Water depth
The water depth is a large concern at the location, as the berth line must be extended
from the headland to get 10 m water depth at lowest astronomical tide (LAT). This
means that there must be a gateway out to the berth line. For a piled solution, this can
require many piles, with a concrete deck, but here ice can freeze within the structure,
which can cause large problems. This favors both the pre-casted concrete solution and
the sheet pile wall solution.
5.1.3
Cost and availability of construction materials
The on-site offshore materials lack of the support do not favor the sheet pile or the piled
either, since both solutions are dependent on support from pile driving. This means that
the support must be taken from the bedrock. This can be done by using explosives to
make a trench for the sheet pile, or holes for the piles, then casting underwater concrete
52
==PAGE:65==
to ﬁx the structure to the bedrock. The problem with these solutions is that this requires
under water work, which can be expensive and time consuming. On the other hand,
the solution of an concrete trench is well know for the Greenlandic contractors, and
have been proved to be a solid and valid harbor solution in other Greenlandic harbors
(Hansen, 2014). A sheet pile solution can also gain resistance from the backﬁll. If using
this type of method, means that all sediments must be removed before using rockﬁll
behind the quay wall, as the material at the site will most likely not be able to carry
resist the gravity of the rockﬁll.
5.1.4
Environmental constrains
From the analysis of the environmental actions it was seen that the waves are relatively
small. Usually, a piled solution is better than a quay wall if the harbor is unprotected are
wave actions are large, which is not the case in at this location. When considering the
climatic factor, the soil retaining structure or a concrete wall can withstand ice forces
much better than piles. If freezing of ice between the piles occurs, the tidal differences
will increase the forces.
5.2
Structure type conclusion
The remote location make repairs expensive, due to logistical challenges. Thus the a
durable low maintenance solution must be chosen. The piled solution can have severe
problems due to ice, while a pre-casted concrete wall will require expensive transporta-
tion, stiffeners, and a ﬂat plateau. Thus with the base in the Greenlandic experience, the
possibility to construct on uneven terrain, the ability to withstanding the design impact
forces due to the passive soil, and the possibility to use the on-site materials as backﬁll-
ing (Tsinker, 1997) (Stenstad and Eppeland, 2014), a sheet pile retaining wall anchored
in the bedrock is concluded to be the most cost efﬁcient solution for the harbor location.

Structural design and force calculation - method
6.1
Crest height, structure length and berth line orientation
To determine the structures crest height, an overtopping limit is set regarding the amount
of allowable water that harbor can handle during a design signiﬁcant wave. It is assumed
that there are no pedestrians, buildings or vehicles when the design wave occurs. The
limit for the discharge therefore set to be 100 l/s per meter, as it will not result in dam-
ages on an asphalt structure(PIANC, 2003). The calculations for overtopping follows
the procedure from Pullen et al. (2007), where the design signiﬁcant wave from the BW
model is used. The length of the structure will try to be kept at a minimum, since a
short berth line structure is wanted to reduce costs of the construction (Hansen, 2014).
Therefore a structure design to take the berthing impact from a ship other than the quay
wall, is assumed. In this case breasting dolphins are assumed when considering the
berth structure. The length of the berth line, including breasting dolphins, is found in
accordance to Thoresen (2010). The procedure is for berth structures for oil tankers,
but will be adapted for this scenario. The plans for the harbor is to expand gradually, so
that eventually larger cruise ships can use the harbor (Hansen, 2014). Thus the largest
cruise ship will be used as the dimensioning factor of the length of the berth line, which
is 0.25-0.4 times the length of the largest ship. For the length of the quay wall, it is
assumed that there should be enough space for 2 40-feet containers next to each other,
plus operational space. The orientation of the berth line and the quay wall on the head-
land will be found by analyzing the current, wind direction and distance out to the 10
m limit at lowest tide. There is no accurate data available, regarding the thickness of
the soil layer at the sea bed, the berth line will therefore be set at the distance from the
shore line where the water depth at LAT is 10 m, by using the data which is avalible at
the moment. The placement and orientation of the berth line is chosen by analyzing the
wind, current and wave directions around the headland, as these are important factors
for the orientation of the berth line(Tsinker, 1997).
6.2
Sheet pile anchoring method
The sheet pile’s reaction forces is determined by analyzing two different anchoring so-
lutions of the sheet pile to the bed rock. One which ﬁxed in to the bed rock by blasted
trench ﬁlled with concrete, and one which is anchored by dowels to the bed rock. The
ﬁxed anchoring solution was used at a similar harbor project in the Greenlandic city
Sisimiut (Aarsleff, 2013). It is also the preconceived solution for location (Hansen,
2014). The dowel connection is a simpler solutions, where several dowels are casted
into the bedrock, and connected to the sheet pile. Thus the latter will be used as a
comparison with the concrete trench connection to investigate if it is the optimal so-
lution for the location. The two solutions can be seen in Figure 6.1. The difference
between the two solutions is that the one ﬁxed to the bottom can obtain moment, while
the one which is anchored with dowels is considered to be joined at the bottom, only
transferring horizontal forces. The stability of the ﬁxed retaining wall is found through
procedures from Tsinker (1997), while the dowel anchored solution is found through
by following the guidelines in the geotechnical handbook developed by The Norwegian
Public Roads Administration (Vegdirektoratet, 2010). The principal of the calculation
method for the concrete trench solution can be seen Figure 6.1.

all activity will be stopped, and a vertical evenly distributed load of 6 kN/m2 is assumed.
This refers to for example the weight of a reach stacker. The total mooring force is ex-
pected to be divided on 4 bollards for M/S Naja Arctica and 3 for MV Artania, due to
assumed number of tight mooring line, relative to the ship length (Tsinker, 1997). It is
expected that the bollard force will be evenly distributed along the berth structure, and
that mooring force will return a bollard pull of 100 percent in the horizontal direction,
and 87 percent in the vertical direction (Thoresen, 2010).
6.3.3
Berthing loading
For docking, it is assumed that the largest and heaviest ship will also return the design
driving forces on the structure. Since this is a preliminary design, the design berthing
energy is found through the empirical method from Thoresen (2010). For the design
calculation, abnormal impacts are taken into account. The berthing energy is absorbed
by the fender and transferred to the structure. Thus all fenders at the structure should
be capable to withstand the design berthing (Thoresen, 2010). The fender type is found
by using fender tables in Thoresen (2010), where the fender force is found for a given
berthing energy.
6.3.4
Ice loads
The ice will load the structure as a uniform dead load top of the quay and act as a ﬂoating
live-load which will encounter the quay wall. The highest strains when a ice sheet
encounters a vertical wall is when crushing occurs (Høyland, 2015). The calculation for
the maximum pressure a ice sheet can preform a quay wall is calculated in accordance
to ISO19906 (2010), where it is assumed that the point of impact is in half of the ice
sheets height. The can also freeze at the quay wall which can create a lift force. This
lifting force is found by using ﬁgures in Thoresen (2010). It should be noted that the
tidal difference and ice thickness did not ﬁt the ﬁgure, thus a conservative interpolation
was preformed.
6.3.5
Wave forces
The wave forces working on the quay wall is calculated by the procedure of Goda’s for-
mulation for vertical wall in accordance to PIANC (2003). It is assumed that the sheet
pile is outside the surf zone. This means that the practical relationship for the maximum
design wave height of Hd = 1.8·Hs can be used, with a corresponding signiﬁcant peak
period (Ts) of Ts = 1.1·Tm. Tm is the mean period (PIANC, 2003).

7.1.3
Breakwater on the side
A breakwater will dissipate the energy of the wave. It was seen that a breakwater
reduced the signiﬁcant wave height with over 2/3. Without an energy dissipation mech-
anism the crest height of the harbor would have been increased with 0.6 m, due to the
increased signiﬁcant wave height. This would have resulted in about 60 m2 of addi-
tional sheet pile. The concept of replacing the some of the concrete trench with on-site
soil material can be seen in Figure 7.1, where it is seen that it is lower than the quay
wall. This is because the breakwater only needed a crest height 0.5 m over HAT.
The breakwater solution was found to be the most cost efﬁcient, since it will have a
double effect. First of all it will have the effect of being a energy dissipation mechanism
on the side of the harbor structure. Another advantage with this type of harbor design is
that it will support the sheet pile. Since the crest height of the breakwater is 0.5 meters
over HAT, it means that the sheet pile will be embedded in over 14 m of materials.
This means that that it can reduce the structure with sheet pile anchoring along both
sides of the structure, which means a minimum of under water work, which makes
the construction process easier. This way it is possible to utilize the on-site material,
rather than using explosives and concrete. A non embedded solution would have had
horizontal forces which would have required tie rods. If the pile is embedded in soil,
this will not be necessary. Therefore it is recommended that the breakwater solution
should be considered when constructing the harbor. When looking at the Figure 7.1 it
can be seen that the breakwater is lower on the east side of the structure. This is because
the harbor is signiﬁcantly more sheltered from wind in this direction, which means that
the energy dampening effect is not needed, thus the height of the soil is for stabilization
of the sheet pile only.
7.1.4
Location of berth line
The location of the berth line is chosen because it is the shortest distance to where it is
10 m at LAT. Because of the small currents in the area, this becomes the dominating
factor along with the sheltered side for strong winds. By choosing this location, the
mooring forces becomes very small, even with a large cruise ship. At this location it is
also possible to use the land for mooring. This means that it can potentially reduce the
need for mooring dolphins, and in this way reduce the total cost of the project.
An important aspect for the location is the water depth. The core sample notes from
Assiaq, indicated that less soil depth where the harbor is proposed to be (Jeppesen,
2015). This means that another location around the headland could be better suited
regarding the depth to bedrock, if there is a thicker soil layer elsewhere. Therefore it is

advised that a new evaluation is done when accurate measurements and full evaluation
of the core samples are available. Nevertheless, with the basis in the material available
it is concluded that the location where the harbor, is proposed is the most cost efﬁcient
regarding material usage and design mooring force.

faults can be located in a controlled environment. The knowledge can then be utilized if
the exploitation of natural resources should require a harbor solution which is portable
and mobile, and can efﬁciently be operational.

Conclusion
Greenland needs to increase its self sufﬁciency, as the island is dependent on block
grants from Denmark, which due to inﬂation will decrease. As one of the least dense
populated countries in the world, it is important to be efﬁcient. A step towards a more
efﬁcient Greenland is a better infrastructure. At the current moment, the connection
between the international airport in Kangerlussuaq and the sea trafﬁc of Greenland is
inefﬁcient, as the harbor has a depth of 2 meters, making large ships anchor out in the
fjord. The solution of this is to make a new harbor 10 km further out in the fjord at
Hancock Pynt. Here the water depth is sufﬁcient for larger ships to dock.
The aim of this thesis was to get an overview of the key environmental actions at Han-
cock Pynt and to investigate if the location is suitable for a harbor. And if so, a pre-
liminary design should be made and compared with the preconceived design concept
by Hansen (2014). The ﬁnal design were to be optimized for the location, regarding
cost and ﬂexibility. Another aspect of the thesis is to see if it is possible to create a de-
sign which is adaptable to other locations in Greenland, as the island might need smart
harbor solutions for possible expansions of exploiting natural resources.
The location seems highly suitable for a harbor structure considering the environmental
actions. The wind analysis concluded that at Hancock Pynt the wind climate is moder-
ate, as the location is sheltered regarding fetch from most directions. Especially from
north east, which is why the berth line was chosen to be at an angle of about 50o to
north. It was also found that the currents were not severe, and that the wind gener-
ated waves returned short period waves of small magnitude. It was found that the most
severe wind resulted in design signiﬁcant wave heights of up to 3.1 m, where it was
seen that the chosen design could handle without problems. The chosen structure was
found to be the sheet pile wall solution, ﬁxed in a concrete trench, as it handled the
difﬁcult conditions on-site, the materials where of light weight, and it could handle the
ﬂexibility regarding varying depths to bedrock. This means that the investigation of
the environmental actions favors the preconceived design by Hansen (2014). Neverthe-
less, the solution could be further improved regarding the cost efﬁciency. The achilles
heel of the solution is the expenses of under water work, thus the focus was to reduce
the amount of concrete trenching. Thus it was concluded that the concrete trench only
should be used at the harbor front. The rest of the harbor structure should be a stone ﬁll-
ing, where the sheet pile is rammed into rockﬁll. The stone ﬁlling will have an energy
dissipating effect on the waves from the sides, such as a breakwater. This means that
the stone ﬁlling will in addition to reduce the amount of under water work, also reduce
the crest height with over 0.6 m.
The structure still require signiﬁcant amounts of stone materials. The solution can also
be expensive due to the amount of under water work. A suggestion is therefore to use
a ﬂoating deck barge as a temporary harbor, which at a later stage can be expanded to
a sheet pile solution. This way the the harbor will be accessible for container ships at
a earlier stage, where the harbor can expand at a later stage for the largest cruise ships.
If it chosen to let the harbor gradually expand, the cost and proﬁts of the harbor can
evaluate along the way, which means that the beneﬁts can be thoroughly investigated.
By choosing the procedure of starting with a ﬂoating deck barge as a harbor and gradu-
74
==PAGE:87==
ally expand to a sheet pile solution, it seems like a logical concept, as the projects risks
and the time and costs until the harbor is operational can be reduced. If the solution was
applied as a ﬁrst step in the process of a new harbor at Honcock Pynt, and it was suc-
cessful, it could be a great stepping stone for other harbor projects in remote locations
in Greenland, which encounter the same problems as the harbor at Hancock Pynt.

Further work
There are still many things that must be done regarding the project. The most impor-
tant part is to evaluate the sediments outside Hancock Pynt. This can be crucial when
considering the placement of the berth line and harbor structure. It it was found that
the sediments actually had a capacity that was greater than expected, it could also re-
duce the need for rockﬁll. Nevertheless, the most important factor is the depth down to
bedrock, as this is limiting for the investigations of how far out the harbor must be.
It is also important that a thorough investigations of the mooring of the ships is con-
ducted, to see if there is actually possible to use the headland as mooring points. The
mooring will also require strong points (mooring dolphins), where a ﬁnal design should
be made when the investigations of the geotechnical conditions i done. This emphasizes
the importance of ﬁnding the strength and depth of the sediments outside the pynt.
For the Greenland’s economy the barge solution should be given a detailed investiga-
tion, as it can be a step towards a more self sufﬁcient Greenland. If the harbor is as
mobile and ﬂexible regarding conditions, it can be a great way getting mining projects
in remote location operational faster. This fact that the harbor itself is a ﬂoating de-
vice deck makes it possible to transport the equipment for the harbor installation on the
barge. So for the barge solution, a thorough investigation of an easy assembly process
should be prioritized. An important part with the investigations of the barge solution
is to investigate if the simple connection between the structure and the supportive piles
can be made without needing to move it in the winter time, due to freezing of ice. It
is also important that the motion of the deck barge is checked thoroughly, as this may
cause problems.
