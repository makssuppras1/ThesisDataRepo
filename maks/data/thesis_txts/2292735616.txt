Abstract 
Many organizations are showing an increasing interest in adopting and using visuals as 
a way to share a vision, to engage their employees, to communicate plans and to keep 
everybody on the same track. Current research looks into the specific elements that 
constitute this method attractive through the use of a case study at the Municipality of 
Gentofte. A series of interviews was conducted in order to reveal the effect of visuals in 
a specific culture change process. The input is counter-analysed to existing theories in 
the literature and alternatives means of communication. The findings validated the 
effect of the visuals and the preference of the employees against alternatives. Focus is 
given separately on each of the two different concepts of communication and change 
separately and also on the combination of them. A conceptual framework is developed 
that can be used by a team or an organisation as a road map, illustrating the different 
steps of the process, from the creation of a visual to the communication towards the 
organisational change.

1.1 Broad introduction to thesis topic 
How many times have you heard the phrase “I don’t believe it if I don’t see it?” 
Nowadays, large organisations, companies and corporations are starting to use visuals 
as a way to share a vision, to engage their employees in their strategies or changes that 
they want to implement, to provoke constructive dialogues, to communicate plans and 
to keep everybody on the same track. Complex processes, as strategies or 
organizational changes, are sometimes difficult to communicate or to make sense to 
everybody; visuals can be used as a tool to improve the effectiveness of the 
communication. On the other hand, the ease to understand a visual can sometimes 
lead to wrong way of understanding or to lead the thought to a completely different 
path or goal from the expected. 
The purpose of the thesis is to reflect the impact of the visuals within an organisation in 
terms of common understanding, sense making, effective conversation, dialogues and 
also to suggest improvements in the way of using them.

This project will be based on a case from the municipality of Gentofte (north 
Copenhagen), which is in the middle of implementing a significant change of culture, 
and they are using a specific visualisation to communicate the vision and the strategy. 
Around May 2014, the social department of municipality of Gentofte in Denmark had to 
implement a major organizational change. This social department consists of 1500 
people and the main focus is to help and improve the life of the elder people by 
providing services that makes their everyday life easier. Until that point the 
municipality would send people at the houses of elder people two times per week in 
order to help them with the cleaning of the house, grocery shopping, personal 
treatment or whatever assistance an old person need. 
Until that moment the personal participation of the person wasn’t needed and the 
helper would do all the work. The change that the municipality wanted to implement 
was about the active participation of the elder person with the helper so the person can 
live with more freedom and be more independent after a while. This change was called 
“Træn dig fri” which means training for freedom and had to be communicated through 
all the municipality, from the project leaders, to the project managers, to the helpers 
educators and to the helpers that going on the field. The visualisation is shown in the

1.2 Research problem. What is the problem?  
The aim and the mission of the thesis are to analyse how to enhance communication 
through visualizations, and reflect the impact of them. Many organisations are facing 
difficulties to communicate a new strategy or change to their employees and it takes a 
lot of time to align all their employees. 
There are challenges to overcome when it comes to communicate a change or to 
actually implement a change. The misalignment of the employees, the different 
cultures or launguages can lead to waste of time, conflicts and also to face difficulties 
during the decision-making process. The visuals can be tools support verbal 
communication to be more efficient, transmit the right messages, provoke 
conversations and dialogue and bring everybody in the same track. This thesis will 
analyse these results to see if actually the visuals can have an impact in communication 
and can make an organization’s change more efficient.  
The objectives are to investigate (monitoring): 1) what is different, what is the impact of 
using visuals than just verbal word, 2) what challenges can overcome, 3) what are the 
new challenges, (discovery) 4) what are the results and 5) what can be improved. 
Research questions: 
The main research questions that will be investigated in this thesis are the following: 
1.  What is the impact of visualisations in the communication of an organizational 
change? 
2. How can the visualisations of the vision of change influence the organization change?

2.1.1 What is a Visualisation?  – Pros and Cons  
A visual can be considered anything that is related to seeing or sights. Anything in the 
world that can be seen through eyes is called a visual element. Visualization is an ability 
to interact with the environment by processing the information transferred through a 
visual light. This ability is called eyesight. We visualize through the visual system, which 
is an organ that captures visual light and transfers it to information signal to the brain 
from the retina. In our life, visualization is the most powerful way of communication 
(Kenneth Louis Smith, 2005).  
Visuals can be also used as a platform for alternative opinions and perspectives, inspire 
thinking, challenge stakeholders with different interests, trigger a change, motivate or 
convey passion (Bell, 2012). 
Pauwels defines a visual as physical representation of a referent (the object represented 
by the visual) (Pauwels, 2006). This referent can be data, concepts, ideas, objects, 
people or projects that are presented in a two-dimensional plane.  
The translation of the perceived reality to a visual can add value to it, as it makes people 
think that is a true representation of its referent, by displaying data in a concrete and 
precise way, even though the data are not so accurate. Meyer stated that visuals 
transport “specific ideas behind a of seemingly objective representation” (Meyer et al, 
2013).  
On the contrary, when the translation is distant from the perceived reality a visual can 
have a negative impact on people and can be considered just as manifestation. Weick 
points out that humans live in two worlds - the world of events and things (the territory) 
and the world of words about events and things (the map). The process of translating 
the territory to a map is necessary but inherently inaccurate; cause the world changes 
continuously and no two events are the same. (Weick, 1990)  
The cause of a visualization disadvantage can be the interpretation of the designer or 
the user (Tufte, 1986) 
Hence, it is important to keep in mind that this translation is a choice and can be biased. 
This is evident in the case of the photos that a photographer captures. By using 
different cameras, lenses or lighting, photos can easily distort reality. The 
photographer also decides what is inside the frame of the photo and what is left out, it 
becomes therefore at best a piece of reality, but not ‘the reality’ (Geraldi, 2014). 
Additionally, visuals “are never innocent or neutral reflections of reality… they offer not 
a mirror of the world but an interpretation of it.” (Midalia, 1999), and hence, as we see 
visuals, we see through the eyes of other people (Geraldi, 2014).  
==PAGE:14==
7 
Bertin argues that visuals can represent many dimensions although they do so in a two 
dimensional scheme, the plane, which can be for example a paper, a wall or a computer 
screen. On this plane, visuals can use at least three sensory variables like variations of 
marks (e.g. colors and shapes) and the two dimensions of the plane (Bertin, 2010).  
In addition, the verbal language that uses only the sound and the time as written 
language uses letters to represent the sounds and the punctuation to represent time. 
Also, a visual can transmit the three dimensions instantaneously, while verbal language 
reveals the meaning with a sequence and in a linear way. These two characteristics, 
non-linearity and immediacy, can be considered as important competences for the 
visuals (Bertin, 2010).  
By drawing on the concept of Bertin, Fichhoff has been able to show that non-linearity 
make visuals a tool to present complex information They have the ability to explore 
complex associations between entities. These can be tasks, arguments, and people. A 
visual, i.e. a mind map or a roadmap can provide the whole process, rich overview of 
arguments, and their relationships more effectively. The creation of a visual 
representation of thought can improve clarity of thought and reveal unclear or hidden 
assumptions, incomplete analyses, missing information, and therefore improve 
problem solving. (Fichhoff, 2006) 
Additionally Few and Henderson went a step further and found out that immediacy 
helps people energize their short term memory and keep the information in their minds 
in two different ways.  
Firstly visuals compress big amount of information like for example, a set of numbers or 
a line graph are stored as a piece of information, although the line graph may include 
more information (Few, 2006).  
Secondly, a visual could be an extension of the short-term memory, as it is processed 
quickly, so we can regain access to information of a visual in seconds. This can be useful 
in solving complex problems or presenting complex processes as a visual can ‘hold’ 
information while we examine other aspects of the problem or the process (Henderson, 
1999).  
Finally, as we communicate through visuals, we are not only providing what is explicitly 
displayed in the visual, but also constructing an image or a vision for the future of 
organizations and ourselves (Foss, 2004).  
2.1.2 The visual as a story-telling tool  
The abilities of the visuals that were explained above can make them effective tools to 
communicate a story. Story telling is used by leaders to inspire the organization, set a 
==PAGE:15==
8 
vision, teach important lessons, define culture and values, and explain who you are and 
what you believe (Smith, 2012) 
 “Stories make information easier to remember and more believable. They are a 
powerful means of communicating values, ideas, and norms. Stories usually are more 
powerful than statistics. Each listener sees himself/herself in a story and unconsciously 
relates it to his/her own experience, asking questions such as “When did something like 
that happen to me?” We remember what happened to us because our own personal 
experience is more memorable than an event that happened to someone else.” 
(Morgan & Dennehy, 2007) 
Osterwalder points out the basic benefits of storytelling in three points (Osterwalder, 
2010): 
1) Engaging People: People are motivated more by stories than by logic. Story 
telling helps and engages listeners into the new or unknown by creating the logic 
of a model or a vision into a compelling narrative.  
2) Clarification: Telling a story that illustrates how e.x a business model solves a customer 
problem is a clear way to introduce listeners to the idea. Stories give you the “buy-in” 
needed to subsequently explain a model, a strategy, a change or a vision in detail.  
3) Make the New Tangible: Explaining something completely new and untested, is like 
explaining a painting with words alone. But telling a story  for example of how a change 
can happen or how a business model creates value is like applying bright colors to canvas. 
It makes things tangible. (Osterwalder, 2010) 
2.1.3 The visuals as a sense-making tool 
In the literature there are many definitions “sense-making”. Among those, two of 
them will be mentioned here, as they fit more with the scope of the thesis. Weick, the 
“father” of “Sense-making” defines it as a tool that “unfolds as a sequence in which 
people concerned with identity in the social context of other actors engage ongoing 
circumstances from which they extract cues and make plausible sense retrospectively, 
while enacting more or less order into those ongoing circumstances.” (Weick et al, 
2005). 
In the field of change management a common definition is that “Sense-making has to 
do with the way managers understand, interpret, and create sense for themselves based 
on the information surrounding the strategic change.” (Rouleau, 2005:1415). From a 
sociological perspective “Sense-making refers to processes of meaning construction 
whereby people interpret events and issues that are somehow surprising, complex, or 
confusing to them.” (Cornelissen, 2012:118)  
==PAGE:16==
9 
The abilities of the visuals to convey meaning (Whyte et al, 2008), to create shared 
understanding (Kaplan, 2011), to display different perspectives (Smelcer & Carmel, 
1997) and at the end to represent the bigger picture make them very useful for people 
to understand and give sense to what is communicated through them.  
2.1.4 The different kinds of visuals 
Visuals can be considered as tools that can be created and modified according to needs 
that we have to fulfil and different ways of use. According to the content, the purpose, 
the process, the method, the situation and the people which are being targeting, visuals 
can be modified to cover the different needs and expectations (Eppler & Burkhard, 
2007)  
According to Eppler and Burkhard, there are six categories of visuals related to different 
knowledge transfer: 
1. Structured text and tables: organizing knowledge with grids. 
2. Heuristic sketches: the ad hoc depiction and creation of insight. 
3. Conceptual diagrams: the elicitation of implicit knowledge through templates. 
4. Visual metaphors: relating domains to improve knowledge transfer. 
5. Knowledge maps: charting knowledge application and navigating expertise. 
6. Interactive visualizations and animations: exploring complexity. 
During an organizational change is expected to use the two categories of conceptual 
diagrams and visuals metaphors, as they can show the path of the change with the 
different milestones and goals and their main purpose is to enhance brainstorming and 
to prompt collaborative work within employees. Therefore, this thesis focuses on the 
use of those two types of visuals, evaluating their role and impact in organisational 
change. 
2.1.5 The visuals and their different nature (fluid and frozen objects) 
According to the use and the purpose of a visual it can have a different nature. When 
the purpose of a visual is to provoke interaction, to make people work together and be 
an object of co-creation, its nature can be considered “fluid”. That means that the 
purpose is to change, to be modified and to involve people in order to fit in the goals of 
its creations. The “fluid” visuals are more appealing between people with the same 
power or in the same hierarchy so everybody can modify it, take ownership and feel 
more engaged (Whyte, 2007). 
==PAGE:17==
10 
A visual that cannot be changed and its purpose is to communicate a plan or a strategy 
is called “frozen”. This type of visual cannot be modified or evolve and most of the 
times it is used as a tool of communication between people of different hierarchy and 
power in the sense of guiding, share a vision and establish specific steps or goals. 
(Whyte, YYYY). 
The different nature of the visuals will be analysed in this case study to understand how 
the management team used the visuals. Although, during a change the “fluid” nature is 
more appealing to define the vision and the goals and to engage the management 
team but when it comes to communicate the visual to other employees, the nature of a 
visual can become “frozen”.

2.2.1 What is organisational change? 
The most common definition of organizational change comes from Bamford and 
Forrester who defines it as, the step one organisation is facing that has never been 
greater (Bamford & Forrester, 2003). Another definition is coming from Kotter, who 
argues that all types of organizations will be at some point affected by change, as 
change is generated by a combination of external and internal forces (Kotter, 1996). 
External forces tend to embrace globalization, market competition, new technologies, 
large political happenings, governmental regulations, and the overall expectations of 
society and customers. On the other hand, internal forces tend to include increased 
workplace diversity, administration structures, strategic directions, implementation of 
new technology, and market opportunities (French & Bell, 1999).  
According to Furnham (2005), organisations change the interaction of various factors 
like centralization of decision-making, degree of formalization and professionalization, 
organizational hierarchy, complexity, organizational age, and size, combined with 
personal factors as employees’ age, education, training, values, beliefs and positional 
rank. 
Steps in organisational change 
Although there are many different models that describe how to make a change in an 
organization successful. John Kotter (who teaches Leadership at Harvard Business 
==PAGE:18==
11 
School) has made it his business to study both success and failure in change initiatives 
in business. "The most general lesson to be learned from the more successful cases is 
that the change process goes through a series of phases that, in total, usually require a 
considerable length of time. Skipping steps creates only the illusion of speed and never 
produces satisfactory results" and "making critical mistakes in any of the phases can 
have a devastating impact, slowing momentum and negating hard-won gains". Kotter 
summarizes the eight phases as represented in the following figure. 
 
 
Figure 3. Steps to achieve a successful organizational change (Kotter, YYYY)

2.2.2 Organisation culture and its different levels 
Organizational culture is the behaviour of humans within an organization and the 
meaning that people attach to those behaviours. According to Needle (2004), 
organizational culture represents the collective values, beliefs and principles of 
organizational members and is a product of such factors as history, product, market, 
technology, and strategy, type of employees, management style, and national culture. 
Culture includes the organization's vision, values, norms, systems, symbols, language, 
assumptions, beliefs, and habits. Ravasi and Schultz (2006) wrote that organizational 
culture is a set of shared assumptions that guide what happens in organizations by 
defining appropriate behaviour for various situations. It is also the pattern of such 
collective behaviours and assumptions that are taught to new organizational members 
as a way of perceiving and, even, thinking and feeling. Thus, organizational culture 
affects the way people and groups interact with each other, with clients, and with 
stakeholders. In addition, organizational culture may affect how much employees 
identify with an organization. 
Schein (1992), Deal and Kennedy (2000), and Kotter (1992) advanced the idea that 
organizations often have very differing cultures, as well as subcultures. Although a 
company may have its "own unique culture", in larger organizations there are 
sometimes co-existing or conflicting subcultures because each subculture is linked to a 
different management team. 
Using Kennedy and Deal's simple definition of culture ('the way things are done around 
here'), Rosauer further defined culture as an'emergence'-an extremely complex and 
often immeasurable state, resulting the combination of relatively few ingredients. 
From an organizations point, Rosauer argues the ingredients are 'employee (the people 
who get things done), the work (the things that actually get done), and the customer 
(the consumer of the provision). 
Schein's model of organizational culture, originated in the 1980s, identifies three 
distinct levels in organizational cultures (Schein, 2004): 
1. Artefacts and behaviours 
2. Espoused values 
3. Assumptions 
==PAGE:20==
13 
The three levels refer to the degree to which the different cultural phenomena are 
visible to the observer, as depicted in the following figure. 
 
Figure 4. Schein´s model of organizational culture (Schein, 2004) 
 
Artefacts include any tangible, overt or verbally identifiable elements in an 
organization. Architecture, furniture, dress code, office jokes, etc. all exemplify 
organizational artefacts. Artefacts are the visible elements in a culture and 
people not part of the culture can recognize them (Schein, 2004). 
 
Espoused values are the organization's stated values and rules of behaviour. It is 
how the members represent the organization both to themselves and to others. 
This is often expressed in official philosophies and public statements of identity. 
It can sometimes often be a projection for the future, of what the members 
hope to become. Examples of this would be employee professionalism, or a 
"family first" mantra. Trouble may arise if espoused values by leaders are not in 
line with the deeper tacit assumptions of the culture (Schein, 2004). 
 
Shared Basic Assumptions are the deeply embedded, taken-for-granted 
behaviours, which are usually unconscious, but constitute the essence of 
culture. These assumptions are typically so well integrated in the office dynamic 
that they are hard to recognize from inside (Schein, 2004). 
2.2.3 What are the challenges in organisational change? 
Organisational change is a very challenging process for many managers around the 
world from different dimensions that combines planning, communication, employee’s 
resistance and difficulties from agreement and alignment of the management level 
stuff. During a change, there is a possibility to restructure the organisation or rearrange 
the mix of skills or to create new systems (Gaunt, 2007). 
==PAGE:21==
14 
2.2.2.1 Communication 
An effective communication of the vision of the change, and what will be different after 
it in the organization, will usually result in better outcomes. It is general accepted that 
where communication of the change’s vision is poor and stakeholders do not 
understand and accept the need for change, these stakeholders are less likely to adopt 
the change, and change is therefore less likely to be successful (Dolphin, 2005; Quirke, 
1995; Kotter, 1996). The importance of communicating the vision is seen as a crucial 
part of gaining the stakeholders’ involvement into change. Hence, it is important that 
communications of the vision are well designed, and the responsible ones are capable 
to do this. (Nadler, 1981) 
2.2.2.2 Lack of Consensus 
A lack of alignment of everyone with the change can lead to barriers during the change. 
The decision to implement changes should come from the top level of the organization 
and all management level staff needs to be aligned and able to deal with the changes 
elsewise conflicts within between the management team or with the staff may occur. It 
is challenging to have everyone on board from the beginning although, by showing 
managers the vision, the steps for implementing the change and how the change will 
affect the organization, it helps get them on board if they initially have reservations. 
(Frost, 2007) 
2.2.2.3 Resistance 
As afore mentioned, organisational changes often requires employees to modify their 
way of doing things, which can lead to resistance. Resistance of the employees most of 
the times is caused for two reasons Uncertainty and Job insecurity. The main reasons 
for opposition against any change are that employees become comfortable and safe 
with the way the business is run, they know their expectations and their role within the 
company, and they do not want to relearn their jobs or change the way they carry out 
tasks.  
2.2.2.4 Uncertainty 
Uncertainty during change processes is typically about the aim, process and expected 
outcomes of the change and implications for the individual employees (Buono and 
Bowditch, 1993). Knowledge is not only a pre-requisite to the ability of influencing the 
outcomes (Terry and Jimmieson, 1999), but knowledge about the motives for change 
will also help reducing uncertainty and creating readiness for change. In that sense 
effective change communication can be viewed as a mean to proper manage 
uncertainty (DiFonzo and Bordia, 1998) 
==PAGE:22==
15 
2.2.2.5Job insecurity  
Job insecurity has three components (van Vuuren, et al., 1991), first of all, it is a 
subjective experience or perception. Different employees might perceive the same 
situation differently. Job insecurity also implies uncertainty about the future, for the 
person it is uncertain whether he/she will be able to continue to work, or whether he or 
she will be made redundant. Finally, doubts about the continuation of the job as such, 
are central to job insecurity, these aspects of specific aspects of the job (changes in 
income or position within the company) are commonly not seen as part of the concept 
of job insecurity (De Witte, 1999). 
Thus, a significant aspect of trying to manage the change process is internal 
communication with employees, who play a key role in any organization (Daly, Teague 
& Kitchen, 2003).  
Once a change is going to be implemented, a good communication of it, with clear 
guidelines, and the establishment of small goals with measurable timeframes and 
outcomes, can reduce employees’ resistance, their fear and increase their hopes and 
motivation in the process (Gaunt, 2007).

Organizational communication can be defined as communication within an 
organization, between different groups and individuals at different levels and with 
different specializations, that is designed to organize day-to-day activities It is very 
complex concept, as it includes a variety of different aspects including symbols such as 
logos or artefacts, and structures such as policies and procedures. Additionally, it can 
include discrete messages, interactions, relationships, narratives and meetings. 
(Dolphin, 2005). 
Communication through visual support is described as the “transmission of ideas and 
information in forms that can be read or looked upon. Visual communication in part or 
whole relies on vision and is mainly presented or expressed with two-dimensional 
images. It includes: signs, typography, drawing, graphic design, illustration, colour and 
electronic resources. It also explores the idea that a visual message, supplementary to 
==PAGE:23==

text, has a greater power to inform, educate, or persuade a person or audience.” 
(Kenneth Louis Smith, 2005). 
2.3.2 Challenges of communication in organisational change 
There can be no doubt that communication is a very important part of the change 
process. The importance of communication throughout a change has been 
demonstrated and agreed by practitioners (Lewis, 1999). Lewis argues that empirical 
studies show the inextricable link between the communication process and the 
successful implementation of an organization change (Lewis, 1999:44). The way the 
changed vision and the new focus are communicated plays an important role in the 
consequent steps and may result to misalignment within the organization. 
One purpose of communication during organisational change can be to prevent 
resistance to change, or at least try to reduce this. When resistance to change levels is 
low within an organisation, one could argue that the effectiveness of the change-effort 
will be higher. Since an organisation’s functioning depends on the actions of its 
members, the organization can change only when members’ behavior changes 
(Goodman and Dean, 1982; Tannenbaum, 1971). Poor communication of the change 
can lead to resistance of the employees and most of the times is caused because of 
uncertainty and job insecurity which was analysed previously on in the challenges 
during an organizational change (Elving, 2005). 
Ford and Leppitt, suggest that a failure to create a shared understanding amongst 
participants in the change may occur if the vision is not communicated effectively, 
which can lead to a failure to create a statement of the reasons for change (Ford, 1995) 
(Leppitt, 2006). Communication is vital to achieve a common understanding of the 
issues and potential problems that organizations have to face in order to overcome 
possible barriers (Bennebroek Gravenhorst et al., 1999). This can, in turn, create 
resistance amongst the participants in the change (Ford, 1995) (Leppitt, 2006).  
Effective communication with people of different cultures is especially challenging. 
Cultures provide people with ways of thinking, ways of seeing, hearing, and 
interpreting the world. Socio-cultural factors including differences in demographics, 
language, age, education can affect the business ethics and the way the organization 
operates (Hayes, 2007). Thus, the same words can mean different things to people from 
different cultures, even when they talk the "same" language. When the languages are 
different, and translation has to be used to communicate, the potential for 
misunderstandings increases (Ting-Toomey, 2003). Pfeffer argues that special focus 
should be given on scanning the ‘cultural fit’ and attitudes rather than skills (Pfeffer, 
1998). In this context, Hayes (2007) claims that change managers shape their approach 
==PAGE:24==
17 
and develop communication strategies to face cultural dynamics within the 
organization. For this reason it is vital to understand existing cultural differences 
(organizational, international, or functional) because how people act and perceive 
reality highly depend on cultural values (Englund et al., 2003).  
During organization change, timing in communication plays an important role. 
Information may/should be given about the change within the organization before, 
during and after the change (Elving, 2005). This can avoid overloading and confusion, as 
there are limits to the amount of information that can be received, coded, and 
effectively handled each time by one individual (Kotter, 2006). 
Another factor that could affect communication during the change process is trust 
between management and employees. Commitment and trust are very close related to 
organizational culture and organizational environment. Trust can result to more 
positive attitudes, higher motivation, more cooperation among members and 
enhanced performance (Dirks and Ferrin, 2001).

3.1. Introduction to case study and description of method and 
design 
There are multiple definitions and understandings of the case study. According to 
Bromley (1990), it is a “systematic inquiry into an event or a set of related events which 
aims to describe and explain the phenomenon of interest” (p. 302). 
Eisenhardt defines case study as “a research strategy, which focuses on understanding 
the dynamics present within single settings” (Eisenhardt, 1989). 
The unit of analysis can vary from an individual to a corporation. Data come largely 
from documentation, archival records, interviews, direct observations, participant 
observation and physical artefacts (Yin, 1994). Case study research gives an 
understanding of a complex issue or object and can extend experience or add strength 
to what is already known through previous research. 
Case studies emphasize detailed contextual analysis of a limited number of events or 
conditions and their relationships.  
==PAGE:26==
19 
Researchers have used the case study research method for many years across a variety 
of disciplines. Social scientists, in particular, have made wide use of this qualitative 
research method to examine contemporary real-life situations and provide the basis for 
the application of ideas and extension of methods.  
Researcher Robert K. Yin defines the case study research method as an “empirical 
inquiry that investigates a contemporary phenomenon within its real-life context; when 
the boundaries between phenomenon and context are not clearly evident; and in which 
multiple sources of evidence are used”(Yin, 1984, p. 23). 
This thesis was based on the case study of Gentofte municipality and the instrument of 
data collection was interviews with people that working there and interviews with an 
external consultant.  
The design of the method is based on the two papers: “Building theories from case 
studies” from Eisenhardt and “Analyzing Qualitative Data” from Ellen Taylor-Powell 
Marcus Renner. (Eisenhardt, 1989) (Taylor-Powell & Renner, 2003). 
The first paper was used as a guide through the whole process of the thesis as a guide 
of inducting theory using case studies-from specifying the research questions to 
reaching closure. The table with the specific steps described in the paper can be found 
at the appendix of the thesis. 
The second paper was used to analyze the data from the interviews to reach to 
conclusions. The steps that were followed can be seen bellow (Taylor-Powell & Renner, 
2003). 
The analysis process of the data:  
Step 1. Get to know your data. 
Step 2. Focus the analysis.   
Step 3. Categorize information.   
Step 4. Identify patterns and connections within and between categories.  
Step 5. Interpretation – Bringing it all together  
The use of these steps can be seen at the chapter 4.

Inductive Approach with Interviews 
Inductive approach starts with the observations and theories are formulated towards 
the end of the research and as a result of observations (Goddard and Melville, 2004). 
Inductive research “involves the search for pattern from observation and the 
development of explanations – theories – for those patterns through series of 
hypotheses” (Bernard, 2011). In other words, no theories would apply in inductive 
==PAGE:29==
22 
studies at the beginning of the research and the researcher is free in terms of altering 
the direction for the study after the research process had commenced.  
Neuman (2003) argues that inductive research begins with detailed observations of the 
world, which moves towards more abstract generalisations and ideas.  
In other words, when following inductive approach, beginning with a topic, a researcher 
tends to develop empirical generalisations and identify preliminary relationships as he 
progresses through his research. 
 In this case, the first interviews with the municipality were more general to understand 
what is happening and to have some insights about the change that is take place in the 
municipality, and then following interviews were becoming more specific about the 
communication of the change and the use of the visuals.   
Inductive approach “essentially reverses the process found in deductive research” 
(Lancaster, 2005). Specifically, no hypotheses can be found at the initial stages of the 
study and the researcher is not sure about the type and nature of the findings until it is 
completed. 
It is noted, “inductive reasoning is often referred too as a ‘bottom-up’ approach to 
knowing, in which the researcher uses observations to build an abstraction or to 
describe a picture of the phenomenon that is being studied” (Lodico et al, 2010, p.10). 
In other words, in inductive studies no known theories or patterns need to be tested 
during the research process. 
3.4 Criticism of inductive method 
The problem of induction is the philosophical question of whether inductive reasoning 
leads to knowledge understood in the classic philosophical sense since it focuses on the 
lack of justification for either (Vickers, 2011): 
1. Generalizing about the properties of a class of objects based on some number of 
observations of particular instances of that class (for example, the inference that 
"all swans we have seen are white, and therefore all swans are white", before the 
discovery of black swans) or 
2. Presupposing that a sequence of events in the future will occur as it always has in 
the past (for example, that the laws of physics will hold as they have always been 
observed to hold). Hume called this the principle of uniformity of nature (Hume, 
2007). 
==PAGE:30==
23 
The criticism of the inductive way of research is taken under consideration in this 
project and in order to reach to the conclusions different people with different positions 
in the organizations have been interviewed. This helps to listen different voices and 
opinions about the impact and the communication of the visualizations of the 
organizational change. 
Therefore, the interviews aimed to people at the strategic level that wanted to 
implement the change, to project managers that wanted to communicate the change, 
to employees that were presented the visuals and also to external consultants that 
helped to create the visuals. In this way, the researcher could combine the different 
perspectives, and come closer to what really happened.

The next step involves the narrative analysis, which consists on the story behind the

The narrative of this section will be divided in three major chapters: 1) the creation of

4.3.1 Prologue 
Around May 2014, the social department of the municipality of Gentofte in Denmark 
had to implement a major organizational change. This social department consists of 
1,500 people and their focus is to help and improve the life of the elder people by 
providing services that makes their everyday life easier. Until that time, the 
municipality would send people to the houses of elderlies two times per week, in order 
to help them with the housework, grocery shopping, their personal care or whatever 
assistance they could need. 
With that way of working, the personal participation of the elderly was not needed and 
the helper would do all the work. The change that the municipality wanted to 
implement required the active participation of the elder person with the helper, so the 
person can live with more freedom and be more independent after a while. This change 
was called “Træn dig fri”, which means training for freedom and had to be 
communicated through all the municipality, from the project leaders, to the project 
managers, to the helpers educators and to the helpers that go on the “field”.

5.1 Introduction: Building the theory 
Communication & visuals: How can they make you change culture?  
The findings from the research illustrate how a visual poster impacts the 
communication of a culture change and also the actual implementation of the change. 
The findings are based in the research conducted at the municipality of Gentofte. Focus 
is initially given separately on each of the two different concepts of communication and 
change separately and in continuation on the combination of them. Although the main 
research focus is on communication and change, following the interviews and the data 
analysis, some key points and data related with the creation of the visual came out. This 
information regarding the creation of the visual is going to support communication and 
change. This is in line with a basic belief of the researcher that effective communication 
will occur with the right context of the poster and with the right way of creating it.

5.2 Creation of the visual: Context & Process of creation  
Regarding the context of the poster, the findings conclude that it is important that the 
poster contains the right information and includes messages that trigger the change. 
This information, which is described in Model 1 as “messages” can include what, is 
important for the people to understand in order for the change to happen. Some 
examples can be the things that will be performed differently, like everyday tasks or 
activities, some specific milestones or goals, legal issues, challenges that the change 
can bring or any kind of information that is needed to be shared. Also it should consider 
how people will see the poster in order to be able to identify themselves in it and to be 
clear for them what they have to do and make feel part of the change.  
An organizational change includes many different steps and these have to be clear 
during the creation of a visual poster. The people who see the poster should be able to 
follow the steps so they have to be defined very clearly and with the right order. A road 
or a path with a start, a route and an end that shows the vision can be ways of 
demonstrating the change as a metaphor that is very easy to go through. The clear 
structure and steps of the visual can make it easier to make sense and “convey 
meaning”(Whyte et al, 2008).  
The corporate identity of a company according to Pat Matson Knapp is the overall 
image of an organization in the minds of diverse publics; such as customers, investors 
and employees, and big part of the identity are the colors and the logo. By using the 
colors of an organization and also the logo in the visual, employees can identify 
themselves in it and feel more engaged to the change. (Pat Matson Knapp, 2005) 
The different pictures and drawings that were used in order to describe the change 
define the context of a visual. It came out of this research that simple pictures have to 
be used so people are familiar with and are easy to understand. Also figures that 
demonstrate people and more specific figures that show people performing the tasks 
required for the change, is a way to make the employees relate themselves with these 
figures and to better understand what they have to do.  
Apart from the context of a visual, it was observed that also the process of its creation 
could play a significant role on the impact of the change. Therefore in the beginning a 
visual should be a tool for collaboration and co-creation. The nature of it should be 
“fluid” (Whyte, 2007) so people can work together to decide about the context and the 
way that the change should be presented through it. By interacting, co-creating and 
spending time drawing, thinking, discussing and exposing ideas about it, people will be 
engaged, they will feel part of the change and having more ownership of the poster.

On the contrary, during the creation process resistance of people to create it can be

A critical point at the creation step is that the messages that communicated by a visual

5.3 Communication: The impact of a visual in communication 
==PAGE:50==

1. Starting conversations 
A visual poster is catching the attention of the people very fast as something new and 
different, and they are willing to have a conversation or to start asking questions about 
it. This finding adds value to the research of Wattenberg and Viegas (2008) who argue 
that a visual “it gets people talking”. It can be used as a way to provoke and to promote 
storytelling as you can communicate a story by following the pictures and the steps in 
an easy and interactive way. This can correlate with the fact that visuals use “at least 
three sensory variables like variations of marks (e.g. colors and shapes) and the two 
dimensions of the plane” (Bertin, 2010). 
People can remember what to say easier as they see the pictures and the roadmap and 
can base what they say to the visual (immediacy as core advantage of visuals (Bertin, 
2010)). The discussions and dialogues that are coming up during a presentation of a 
visual, seem to make the messages that are communicated very clear and 
understandable for the people, as the narrative is supported by pictures that the 
employees can see their self in, relate and feel part of the change or the vision and also 
to understand in which part of the process they are.  
2. “Sense-making” tool 
The visual can be used as a way for “sense-making” of the project, that can be very 
beneficial as the people know and understand what you are explaining and be more 
aligned, have a common basis and share the bigger picture of a vision or a change that 
is about to be implemented.  
At the municipality of Gentofte, an example that proves the previous arguments is that 
many people, who had to go in the field and help elder people, they weren’t familiar 
with concepts like “rehabilitation”. The visualizations helped them understand the 
concepts and what they specifically have to do in their work, as the poster was 
describing the everyday tasks. 
This finding draws on the definition of Weick of sense making that “unfolds as a 
sequence in which people engage ongoing circumstances from which they extract cues 
and make plausible sense” (Weick et al, 2005). 
3. Easier remembering 
Another finding that is relevant with the communication of the poster is the impact that 
it has in the memory of the people. As it seems, a visual is easier to remember than a 
text or a spoken word. This is based on the fact that the human brain can easier recall 
an image than a text. Having a tool like a poster that people can see every day and can 
refer to, or have a discussion on it, it’s a way to help them retain some critical 
==PAGE:51==
44 
information on their minds and also stay aligned with the vision of the change. 
Remembering can be more efficient when the poster is used as a reference point and 
people see all or parts of it in a repetitive way. This finding draw on the work of Few & 
Henderson who argue that visuals extend short term memory as they “compress 
information” (Few, 2006) “ can be processed in seconds” an can ‘hold’ information while 
we examine other aspects of the process” (Henderson, 1999).  
4. Visual language 
Important finding can be considered also the creation of a visual language that people 
develop after using visuals. A visual language can help the communication by means of 
time, so one can explain something to the team faster and more easy but also can 
reduce the language and culture differences. 
Although, here it has to be mentioned that the choice of the pictures that represents 
the visual language has to be done carefully, cause different cultures can give different 
meanings to picture and that can cause conflicts or misunderstandings.  
Criticism at the part of the communication part of a visuals or points to pay attention is 
that if I the person who present it doesn’t have communication skills and the visual is 
very complex, people may not  understand it, which can lead to negative results and the 
visual may not “work”.

observations. The framework represents the results of the communication of a visual as

transparent. This leads to understanding, which can be enhanced by creating a visuals 
language and by use a well-structured visual as a sense-making tool. The conversations, 
the better understanding and the use of visual as reference point can make people 
remember easier the vision of the change that is communicated.

5.4 Culture Change: The impact of a visual in a change 
1. Enhanced Communication & Understanding 
Organizational culture change is a complex process that can contain many different 
aspects and themes from the organization's vision, values, norms, systems, symbols, 
language, assumptions, beliefs, habits or way of working. (Schein, 1992) 
The findings from the research showed that by putting these aspects on a visual poster, 
with the use of drawings and simple pictures that appeal to our visual instincts, people 
are more willing to start discussing about it, they can understand better, remember it 
easier. Thus, it provides a simple way to communicate a direction for the change that is 
about to be implemented.  
The use of a visual poster can make the communication of a change faster as it gives to 
a team the opportunity to create a visual language that can be used to point out, to 
explain or to refer to something quicker. An example that proves this is the 
management team of the municipality of Gentofte which after using the poster for 
some times in their meetings, they developed a common visual language that helped 
them communicate in a more efficient way as both project managers argues.  
The visual can be used as a tool to create and define the direction of the change among 
the management team but also to show and to communicate that direction to all the 
employees in an organisation. Another example in the municipality of Gentofte shows 
how they used the poster when they had to communicate the change to the 
employees. So, apart from presenting it to the people, they used the visual as an 
==PAGE:53==
46 
“artefact” (Schein, 2004) that everybody can see, go through and say the story that it 
was represented. It was hanging in every office in the municipality so the people can 
actually see it every day and discuss about it. By using the visual as an “artefact” 
(Schein, 2004) it appears that the communication can be enhanced because the visual 
catches the attention and makes people discuss and interact which can lead to a better 
understanding of the change.  
2. Alignment of the people  
Gaunt claims that the alignment of people, even within the management team, is a key 
challenge when it comes to communication of organizational change (Gaunt, 2007).   
This challenge according to the findings of this thesis seems that can be overcome by 
using visualizations that describe an organizational change. This is happening when 
people are discussing and using a visual as it makes them see the same picture and 
share the same vision. When people can see themselves in the process of change and 
they are able to understand in which specific part of it they fit, this can lead them to feel 
part of the change, more involved and give them responsibility for specific parts of the 
change. With a visual and a good communication of it, people start having a common 
ground to discuss, can see the bigger picture, from where do they start and where are 
they going, understand the essence of the change, make the connections for the 
different steps of the change and at the end be more aligned with each other.  
3. People resistance 
According to Buono and van Vuuren, the two main reasons that cause the resistance of 
employees during an organizational change are uncertainty and job insecurity. The 
things that are actually changing and affect the way that employees are working can be 
presented and visualized in a poster. 
This can make the employees more relaxed with less frustration about the change 
because they can understand the intentions and the visions of the management. The 
employees can be familiar with the tasks that they have to perform at the moment that 
the change will start being implemented, they can identify themselves in the visual and 
also how things will be done differently and is very helpful when the visual describes the 
employees’ everyday life during work. When this occurs the employees are not 
possessed by fear of losing their job and also they don’t feel uncertain about the 
change, so they are more willing to express less resistance for change.  This requires 
that the poster describes the new tasks of the employees as was the case with the 
poster at the municipality of Gentofte, and that they can relate themselves with the 
drawings. When resistance to change levels is low within an organisation, “one could 
==PAGE:54==
47 
argue that the effectiveness of the change-effort will be higher” (Goodman and Dean, 
1982). 
 
4. Behavior changing 
Another finding that could show the effect of a visual to the implementation of a 
change is that a visual can influence the behavior of an employee towards a specific 
task that s/he has to carry out or a belief or an idea which is connected with the change. 
An employee by seeing specific actions that has to perform, visualized in a way that 
s/he can understand and personally relate with the drawing on the visual, it’s more easy 
to actually to start doing things different or at least taking steps towards that direction. 
An example, from the case study at the municipality of Gentofte was a specific part of 
the poster that was representing the different services that the people have to offer to 
the elderly people found it very helpful, as they were able to go to the field and 
implement the different tasks that were shown at the poster.  The importance of this 
finding draws on the work of Tannenbaum who argues that ”since an organisation’s 
functioning depends on the actions of its members, the organisation can change only 
when members’ behaviour changes” (Tannenbaum, 1971). 
5. Faster change (efficient)  
The final point of this research concludes to the outcome of all the previous points as a 
cumulative effect of the inductive approach. This means that when the visual appears 
that can have an effect in all the previous points, one can safely assume that the whole 
change will be affected and occur in a more effective way. 
Common prescriptions for effectively managing change include encouraging 
participation from as many employees as possible, addressing their concerns in the 
change program, or ensuring that leaders act as role models for the changes 
(Heracleous, 2002).  
Therefore, when the people have a visual tool to communicate, to discuss and to better 
understand the different aspects and challenges of the change, they can be more 
aligned, have the feeling that are all together in the same page and share the same 
vision. They can also see the bigger picture of the change and be less suspicious of the 
management intentions. 
Also their fear for uncertainty and job insecurity can be reduced as they can visualize 
specifically what they have to do from the next day.  
==PAGE:55==
48 
People by sharing the bigger picture of the change and have less uncertainties and job 
insecurity are more willing to start taking steps towards the change, start doing the 
things differently as have to do.

5.5 The impact of the visual as a whole process  
Although the findings are divided in three categories it seems that all of them are 
connected together with a specific sequence that can be shown to the following 
framework (Mode 4) with is called “3C Method of visuals in change”. That means for a 
good communication of a change, a visual has to contain the right elements and 
messages that reach the goals and the vision of the change and it has to be created in 
specific ways to provoke engagement and ownership. Also for an efficient change, a 
good communication is needed so people can understand and remember what they 
have to do. So with the appropriate creation and communication of a visual people can 
be aligned, have less resistance, change their behaviour and start taking steps towards 
the change.

6.1. Brief overview – The opinion of the researcher 
The research on the case study of Gentofte municipality brought up many insights 
about the benefits and the drawbacks of the visuals and this is what will be discussed in 
this part.  
The power of the visuals is laying on the fact that they stimulate the curiosity to 
understand what they display, trigger our visual instincts and can be tools to 
communicate and motivate or inspire people do things differently.  
It is proven that people can work better by collaborating and by sharing their ideas and 
visuals provide a tool to do that. One can understand better the whole process, the 
bigger picture and the different perspectives when these are displayed on a paper. 
Visuals provoke conversations, questions, and dialogues but also can bring on the 
surface conflicts as what is represented on them is challenging to satisfy everyone.  
==PAGE:61==
54 
During an organisational change, a visual can be a helpful tool to communicate, tell the 
story and to make people discuss. It can give the direction of the change, align the 
people, reduce their resistance, motivate people and make them feel part of the 
change. A culture change is something that is related with our ideas and the way that 
people see the world, therefore it takes time until it actually happens. Visuals can make 
people taking steps towards the change and of course is not the sole factor to make the 
change come. It needs a lot of efforts from people to be done to make a culture change 
successfully implemented.  
During this thesis there were many positive comments and reactions about the impact 
of the visuals but there were also some negative ones that will be discussed in this part. 
Firstly, the hand drawing visuals that were examined in this project can be considered 
as childish, as something not formal or prestigious and people can feel a bit distant 
from them. Some people would say “These are for kids” or “it cannot represent my 
work”. It is challenging to change these ideas of these people and they can be very 
negative to use them. Although maybe this could be true, it is not the results of how 
good drawing is represented on the visual that counts at the end. One of the values of 
the visuals is that it can bring people to work together to exchange ideas and 
collaborate to achieve something bigger. 
Another important point about the negative impact that visuals can have, regards the 
authenticity of their context. When the story that is represented is not true, and doesn’t 
rely on what is really happening then people can have negative reactions. This is 
something that can happen accidentally also, without the intention to mislead. That’s 
why the people that are creating the visuals should be sure that they are able to display 
the story correctly in order to reach the people and make them feel part of the story.  
The advantage of the visuals to display a lot of information simultaneously can also 
become a disadvantage. When a visual is very complex, people cannot understand it 
and cannot relate themselves with it. Also they can feel stressed from the overload of 
information and might keep a defensive position about what is going to happen.  
A visual by itself cannot change many things if the people are not using it with in the 
right way. The way that one tells the stories that are displayed, the way that is used in 
everyday tasks like for example how many times people see it during work, or if it 
represents the current state of change are factors that can have a significant role in the 
impact of the visual. A visual that is out-dated, that shows things that are already done 
can be considered as “finished” and even though people see it, doesn’t make any 
difference. During a culture change a visual has to evolve with the people and the 
==PAGE:62==
55 
organisation. In Gentofte municipality the visual has changed two times and now the 
third version is preparing.  
The discussion about the drawbacks and the benefits of the visuals can be continued 
forever. Although the real value of them is coming out when they are used to bring 
people to discuss, to collaborate, to work and do stuff together and that’s the point 
when things start changing and becoming better.  
6.2. Limitations of the thesis and bias  
The basic limitation of the thesis was the time and the timing. The best case scenario 
would be to start observing the change from the time that started, one and a half year 
ago, until the time that will be implemented completely, which is not known. The time 
period of the thesis is six months so that was not possible.  
Another limitation is that the findings were based on a single case study. By having 
different case studies the researcher can have more data and have more different 
experiences and opinions to use and analyse. 
During the investigation many people couldn’t give specific examples of their 
experience of using the visual because they were using long time ago so they couldn’t 
remember. That was an obstacle for the research to go deeper into more details.  
The inductive approached that is used here about the impact of the visuals brings up 
the question if visuals can have an impact in all kinds of organisations. Although visuals 
can help, changes that involve technical, very complex or legal problems can be very 
difficult to be represented. So maybe there are organisations that can embrace visuals 
easier or harder according the subject.  
The most important bias from the research can be that all actors involved in this project 
were using visuals in advance so they had a more positive approach about their impact.  
6.3. Contribution to “theory” 
The contribution to the theory of this project is the approach of combining the three 
different aspects of creating a visual, communicating it, and the impact of this in 
change. According to the knowledge of the researcher, although there is a lot of 
literature for these three themes separate, there is not something that combines them 
and presents a framework as the “3C Method of visuals in change” which represents the 
creation of a visual, the communication and the change as one whole process. 
==PAGE:63==
56 
6.4. Suggestions for future research. 
Suggestions for future research will be to have more case studies with many different 
organisations and see how the model“3C Method of visuals in change” can be 
implemented and used. Also a researcher if has more data and examples can go deeper 
in each different “block” of the model and bring to the surface more aspects. 
Also, the researcher should take part on the different parts of the process in the 
creation and in the communication of it (e.g. meetings of poster presentation) 
The researcher should be able to compare what is the difference with and without 
visuals.  
Another suggestion would be to have more social & anthropological focus to 
understand better the reactions of people. 
6.5. Suggestions for the municipality 
The poster that communicates the culture change in the municipality of Gentofte 
represents the things and the services that had to be done differently from the people 
that going to the houses to help the elderly people. Although the vision is to help the 
elderly to be independent, the elderly are not informed about these so they are 
confused when they are asked to do stuff together with the helpers.  
An idea would be to include these elderly people to the change and to make a 
campaign to inform them about the benefits of rehabilitation. This is when the whole 
change will take place. 
Another suggestion about the people that are going to the field is to provide them with 
more detailed brochures about the new services and to have them with them. In this 
way they would remember what they have to do better and check every month what 
they did better or worst thus improve their services.  
For the internal communication of the municipality, the electronic form a visuals with 
different templates can be established, although the use of paper should be 
encouraged as many people like it and feel more comfortable to use.

something similar

developed framework “3C Method of visuals in change”. In chapter 5.5.1 it is explained

encounter some challenges, which can be properly tackled if identified beforehand, 
such as resistance of people to draw, difficulties to simplify big amount of information, 
and to distil the essence of the change and visualise it.

CONCLUSIONS 
The purpose of the current study was to determine how visualizations could influence 
the communication of a culture change and affect the actual implementation of the 
change and the change itself. 
The inductive method approach with interviews, which was used to gather data and 
relevant insights, provided the opportunity to hear opinions from people from different 
positions, in different hierarchy levels and with different roles in the changing process. 
Following this methodology helped to listen the story from different angles and enrich 
the data collected. 
The major limitation of this study is that the researcher could not be present when the 
communication of the change to the employees was taking place. He could not 
experience by himself how people were actually using the visuals and which is different 
with and without them. He could only “live” the change through the experiences and 
the perceptions of the interviewees. 
The most notorious finding from this study is the aligning of all the employees towards 
the same vision. When people have a visual tool to communicate a story, to discuss and 
understand better the different aspects and the challenges of a change; they have the 
feeling that are all together on the road to the same destination and share the same 
vision. They can identify in the visuals what will happen from the day the change will 
start taking place, they can see what they have to do differently and relate themselves 
with some parts of the visual. By sharing the bigger picture of the change, people have 
less uncertainty and less job insecurity, which otherwise they would face, and are more 
willing to start taking steps towards the change, start doing things differently, and that 
is the point when a change becomes efficient. However, when visuals are not 
containing the right information or are not the result of collaborative efforts or are not 
used correctly can have negative impacts. The real value and the impact from visuals is 
coming out when they are used to bring people to discuss, to collaborate, to work and 
do stuff together and that is the moment when things start changing and becoming 
better.
