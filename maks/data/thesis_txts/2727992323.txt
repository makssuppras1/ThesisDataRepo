Abstract 
 
The growing demand for succinic acid in the global market has prompted the need for more efficient 
bioproduction methods to compete with petroleum-based production. Modeling approaches provide 
a promising solution to optimize and refine bioproduction processes. Conventional Kinetic models 
require extensive data and make various assumptions, while Genome-scale metabolic models (GSMs) 
even though they provide big data regarding metabolism, genes, enzymes and intracellular fluxes lack 
the ability to describe dynamic environments since they assume steady state. 
In response to these challenges, a hybrid model is proposed, integrating a kinetic model with Genome 
Scale Model, to accurately portray the dynamic behavior of fermentation processes. The model 
focuses on Actinobacillus Succinogenes as a robust microbial host, capable of metabolizing various 
sugars such as glucose, sucrose, maltose and fixing CO2, making it an ideal candidate for high succinic 
acid production. 
The GSM is a constraint-based model that includes toolboxes such as Flux Balance Analysis (FBA) and 
is able to provide information regarding uptake fluxes and product fluxes. The hybrid model includes 
31 variables and 26 processes representing growth, decay, mass transfer, uptake of CO2, and acid-base 
dissociation. The processes serve as boundaries for the uptake rates of sugars and CO2 in the GSM, 
which determines consumption and production rates using FBA. This innovative interaction of the two 
models generates a model capable of describing accurately the evolution of productivity Succinic acid 
and the consumption of the sugars. Furthermore, CO2 fixation profile and dynamic yields can be 
provided. The portrayal of the sugar preference and the capturing of shifts in the metabolism are two 
noteworthy advantages. 
Parameter estimation is challenging due to high computational demand, but a manual tuning strategy 
was employed, resulting in a well-tuned model capable of describing the dynamics of fermentation 
In conclusion, the hybrid model presents a novel and effective approach to optimize succinic acid 
production and offers valuable insights into the metabolic behavior of Actinobacillus Succinogenes 
during fermentation. The hybrid model demonstrates the potential to advance bioproduction methods 
and contribute to sustainable and cost-effective succinic acid production. 
 
 
 
Keywords: Hybrid model, Biosuccinic acid, CO2 fixation,  A. Succinogenes, Genome-Scale Model, Flux 
Balance Analysis, Sugar Preference Portrayal, Dynamic Yields

1. Introduction 
1.1. 
Succinic Acid Production 
 
Succinic acid (SA) is used widely in creating a range of economically valuable products, such as 
synthetic resins, biodegradable polymers, cosmetics, food additives, and pharmaceutical ingredients. 
From 2017 to 2024, its demand is anticipated to nearly double, from 50,276 tonnes to 97,099 tonnes, 
at an estimated cost of around 3 USD per kg. (Thanahiranya et al., 2023) 
Historically, SA was produced from petroleum by hydrogenating maleic anhydride. However, more 
recently, certain bacteria strains like Actinobacillus Succinogenes, Basfia succiniproducens, and 
Mannheimia succiniciproducens have been used to create SA through fermentation.Click or tap here 
to enter text.. Even though more than half of the SA market still uses petroleum-based production as 
of 2021, there is a growing trend towards bio-based methods, driven by inconsistent petroleum prices 
and environmental implications.  
 
Figure 1 .A range of valuable compounds produced by SA 
 
 
Actinobacillus Succinogenes is a particularly effective SA producer, able to create the compound from 
a variety of materials, including simple glucose, glucose-heavy substances like wheat hydrolysate, and 
agricultural byproducts such as corn stalks and straw. Current industrial methods of producing bio-
based SA are heavily reliant on hexose sugars from glucose or sucrose. However, A. succinogenes can 
utilize both hexose and pentose sugars (D-xylose and L-arabinose), making it ideal for fermentation 
==PAGE:10==
 
 
10 
 
processes that use pentose-rich mixtures, such as those derived from hardwood or agricultural waste. 
(Li et al., 2011) 
High oil prices initially drove the adoption of bio-SA, resulting in the establishment of commercial bio-
SA plants between 2012 and 2015. Mancini et al., (2022).But, many of these plants have since closed 
due to multiple factors. While a catalytic approach to making bio-SA from biomass is promising and 
offers some benefits over microbial fermentation, it's not yet widely adopted. To boost the 
competitiveness of bio-SA from microbial fermentation, production costs need to be cut, ideally via 
sustainable methods. Issues that need addressing include intensive pretreatments, low yields and 
productivity, and costly recovery and purification steps. 
Small-scale studies have begun to tackle these issues. They've explored various pretreatment methods 
for different biomass sources and investigated fermentation optimization and the use of specific 
microorganisms to boost yield and productivity. Several technologies have also been explored for 
purifying the final product. 
Despite progress in genetically modifying microbial hosts, the overall process of bio-SA production, 
including separation, and upstream and downstream operations, remains relatively unchanged. 
Consequently, this field has yet to make a significant impact on the market.(K.-K. Cheng et al., 2013) 
In conclusion, succinic acid's wide-ranging commercial potential is clear. Although petroleum-based 
methods are currently predominant, the interest in bio-based technologies is increasing due to 
petroleum price volatility and environmental concerns. The bacterium A. succinogenes offers 
promising results for natural SA production, and ongoing efforts aim to overcome the obstacles of bio-
SA production. By lowering production expenses and using sustainable practices, bio-SA may 
eventually supplant petroleum-based SA. However, additional research and technological 
developments are needed to overcome existing challenges and to position bio-based SA as a strong 
contender in the market. (Mancini et al., 2022) 
 
1.2. 
Actinobacillus Succinogenes 
 
Actinobacillus Succinogenes is a gram-negative facultative anaerobic bacterium found in bovine 
rumen. A. Succinogenes has a versatile metabolic capacity, capable of metabolizing a broad range of 
sugars and other carbon sources. These include glucose, xylose, arabinose, mannose, galactose, 
fructose, sucrose, lactose, cellobiose, mannitol, maltose, and glycerol. Intriguingly, carbon sources that 
are more reduced than glucose, such as sorbitol, glycerol, and mannitol, contribute to higher succinic 
acid yields. Conversely, C5 sugars like xylose and arabinose typically result in lower succinate yields. 
Pateraki et al., (2016) 
 
The metabolic journey of glucose in A. Succinogenes is intricate. Glucose is primarily catabolized to 
phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP) via the glycolytic pathway. NADPH, a crucial element in the metabolic 
process, is derived from NADH via the action of transhydrogenase or the concurrent activity of NADH-
oxidizing malate dehydrogenase and NADP-reducing malic enzyme. Moreover, NADH is generated 
through the activity of pyruvate dehydrogenase and formate dehydrogenase.(K. K. Cheng et al., 2012) 
==PAGE:11==
 
 
11 
 
Contrary to some microbes, the glyoxylate and Entner-Doudoroff pathways are absent in the 
fermentative metabolism of A. succinogenes. The main routes to succinic acid conversion involve a 
cascade of reactions from phosphoenolpyruvate to oxaloacetate, malate, fumarate, and ultimately 
succinate. 
There are three primary pathways for SA biosynthesis: the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle operating in 
an oxidative direction, the glyoxylate shunt, and the reductive TCA pathway. Under aerobic conditions, 
SA production can be achieved either through the oxidative TCA cycle or the glyoxylate shunt. In 
contrast, under anaerobic conditions, the reductive branch of the TCA cycle can be deployed. However, 
this is frequently restricted by the availability of NADH or redox balance issues. 
Interestingly, A. succinogenes produces SA anaerobically through the reductive branch of the TCA 
cycle, with fumarate acting as the final electron acceptor. The metabolic flux at the PEP node governs 
the amount of flux channeled towards the C4 (Succinic acid) and C3 pathways, regulating the balance 
of fermentative products.(Pateraki et al., 2016) 
 
 
Figure 2. Pathways that lead to the production of Succinic, Acetic and Formic acid and Ethanol. (Pereira et al., 2018) 
The metabolic flux, a measure of the rate at which the intracellular carbon flows through a metabolic 
pathway, is directed towards succinate production mainly at phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP), oxaloacetate 
(OAA), malate, and pyruvate nodes. Key enzymes implicated in this pathway, as revealed by metabolic 
flux analysis, include PEP carboxykinase, malate dehydrogenase, fumarase, and fumarate reductase 
(fig. 2). PEP carboxykinase catalyzes the addition of CO2 and more specifically bicarbonate (HCO3-) to 
PEP (3-carbon compound) to produce oxaloacetate (4-carbon compound) and inorganic phosphorus 
which is used for the regeneration of ATP.  If the PEP carboxykinase rate is limited due to availability of 
CO2 then carbon flux is in C3 pathway at the acetyl-CoA node is directed not only to acetate but also 
to ethanol to balance redox. 
 
==PAGE:12==
 
 
12 
 
The theoretical limit of glucose to succinic acid conversion yield is projected to be 1.12 g/g. However, 
this is often unachievable due to the simultaneous formation of biomass and by-products, which are 
integral for the regeneration of NAD(P)H and ATP. (Pateraki et al., 2016) 
 
 
Figure 3. A. succinogenes can utilize a plethora of sugars. The colors showcase the different transportation system used by 
the bacterium.(Pateraki et al., 2016) 
In fig. 3, it can be easily observed that different transportation systems are utilized for the transport of 
the different sugars in the cell and that defines also the energy consumption needed and the substrate 
preference. 
In a study from (Vigato et al., 2022), A. succinogenes exhibited its ability to grow in the presence of 
various sugars as substrate, such as glucose, sucrose, and maltose, showcasing its broad substrate 
preference. Interestingly, it was observed that A. succinogenes primarily consumed glucose and 
sucrose, leaving maltose as the main remaining sugar after 24 hours of fermentation. The bacteria 
began consuming maltose only after the complete consumption of glucose and sucrose. This 
preference is attributed to the intracellular transport mechanisms. Both glucose and sucrose are 
imported with the phosphotransferase transport system, which allows their transport into the 
cytoplasm to be directly linked with their phosphorylation into Glucose-6-phosphate and Fructose-6-
phosphate. Sucrose, however, requires an additional step - the breaking of the α-1,β-2-glycosidic 
linkage - before its consumption, thereby increasing the energy demand for the reaction. Maltose, on 
the other hand, is characterized by an ATP-dependent transport system, further increasing energy 
consumption by the bacteria.

1.3. 
The importance of models 
 
The production of succinic acid (SA) through biotechnological processes has gained significant 
attention due to its relevance in the context of biorefineries and global sustainability policies. To enable 
successful industrialization of SA production, accurate empirical models (kinetic/ODE models)  play a 
vital role in understanding and optimizing the fermentation process. These models offer a 
comprehensive overview of the evolution of compounds involved in fermentation, aiding in the 
development and scale-up of the process. They provide insights into the temporal behavior of the 
system, making them essential from an engineering standpoint. Proper selection and design of 
reactors can be facilitated by these models, leading to improved performance and productivity. 
Furthermore, these models go beyond process design and are instrumental in control system 
development, as well as scale-up and simulation of industrial processes. By utilizing these models, 
valuable time and resources can be saved through optimization and techno-economic studies, which 
provide a deeper understanding of the feasibility and economic viability of the process. Kinetic models, 
play a crucial role in studying microbial growth, product formation, and their interactions with the 
environment. By employing simplified reaction networks and differential equations, these models 
describe the rates of biochemical reactions and facilitate the prediction of compound production rates, 
conversion degrees, and yields. 
Conducting fermentation processes at an industrial scale involves considering various factors such as 
mass transfer among phases, reaction kinetics, and cellular stress. Isolated studies of these individual 
phenomena and variables on a laboratory scale are necessary before integrating them into a 
comprehensive model. Properly coupling these phenomena is essential to accurately describe the 
overall process behavior. In addition, factors like inhibitory compounds in raw materials, pH levels, 
regulators, osmolarity, and substrate inhibitions significantly influence the kinetic behavior of SA 
production. Understanding and considering these factors ensure optimal process performance and 
desired outcomes. 
In conclusion, the use of empirical models in the biotechnological production of SA is pivotal for 
process optimization, scale-up, and industrial implementation. These models enable a deeper 
understanding of the fermentation process and provide valuable insights into reactor design, control 
system development, and the economic feasibility of SA production.(Escanciano et al., 2022)

1.4. 
Metabolic Models 
Computational models of metabolism such as Genome-Scale Metabolic models are critical research 
tools in the fields of biotechnology and medicine, helping to enhance the understanding of metabolic 
systems. GSM models are based on network reconstruction. A reconstructed network is a list of all the 
biochemical reactions that take place in the microorganism and depicts the association of each 
reaction with metabolites, proteins, genes and transcripts. An example of a stoichiometric matrix 
representing a metabolic network is depicted in figure 4, where rows correspond to metabolites and 
columns represent the reactions. 
 
Figure 4. Stoichiometric matrix of GSMM illustrating the association between metabolites and reactions (Becker et al., 2007) 
 
The information for these models is gathered from a variety of studies such as genomics, 
metabolomics, genetics, and physiology studies. It is used to create a comprehensive database that 
describes the metabolic network of a cell. These reconstructed networks are generated and made 
publicly available.(Becker et al., 2007) 
 
Many different toolboxes can be found in these models, including Flux Balance Analysis, which is a 
constraint-based optimization method. In this method, optimization is performed to determine the 
combination of metabolic fluxes that maintain the objective function. In most cases, the objective 
function is set as the growth rate of the cells, assuming that cells strive to maximize their growth. 
Constraints can be applied to limit the range of possible network states that can support the same 
growth. These constraints can include considerations such as thermodynamics, maximal uptake rates, 
mass conservation, and more. 
 
 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
15 
 
 
 
Figure 5. Process flow for developing a GSMM(Becker et al., 2007) 
 
Delving deeper into a more mathematic perspective, a stoichiometric matrix S with size m*n, 
represents the metabolic reactions, where, m (rows) corresponds to the compounds and n (columns) 
to the reactions. The number that will be inputted in the position Sm,n represents the stoichiometric 
coefficient with which the m metabolite in the list, participate in the n reaction. It can be negative, 
positive or zero for metabolites that are consumed, produced or not participating, respectively. The 
fluxes of the reactions are represented by vector v with a length n. In steady state the mass balance 
system equals to 0 which is described by the following equation: 
𝑆∗𝑣= 0  (1) 
Normally, a metabolic network consists of more reactions that metabolites (n>m). Therefore, there are 
more variables than equations thus, more possible solutions to one state as mentioned above. 
FBA is a tool that aims to maximize or minimize and objective function, Z, defined as follows: 
𝑍= 𝑐⊤𝑣  (2) 
 
Where c is a vector of the weights that represents how much each reaction affects the objective 
function. If the reaction does not have contribution zero will be at the entry that describes it in the 
vector. 
FBA computations take place with linear programming. In MATLAB, the models are formulated as 
structures with several fields. For instance, 'rxns' comprises a directory of all the reactions, 'mets' is a 
list of all the metabolite names, and 'S' refers to the stoichiometric matrix. The 'optimizeCbModel' 
function is deployed to carry out Flux Balance Analysis (FBA). To adjust the boundaries on reactions, 
one can utilize the 'changeRxnBounds' function and for adding new constrains ‘addCOBRAConstraints’ 
is used.Orth, Thiele, & Palsson, (2010) 
This approach allows for rapid iteration and provides information about the fluxes in the system. 
However, it assumes that the system is in a steady-state and does not account for all dynamic factors. 
Despite their respective strengths, each modeling approach (kinetic models vs. GSM models) has its 
limitations. Kinetic models require extensive data for parameterization and can be complex to manage, 
while constraint-based metabolic models lack the ability to simulate detailed time trajectories out of 
steady state.

2. Scope of work 
 
The global market is experiencing an escalating demand for succinic acid, yet the bioproduction of this 
compound fails to compete with petroleum-based production due to the decreasing prices of 
petroleum. To meet the need for succinic acid, the focus is shifting towards refining bioproduction 
methods. In this context, Actinobacillus Succinogenes emerges as a robust platform. The versatility of 
this organism lies in its ability to metabolize an array of sugars, making it a prime candidate for high 
succinic acid production 
In the current project, a dynamic metabolic model for succinic acid fermentation by A. Succinogenes 
is developed. This model can describe both the fermentation variables' dynamics and the fluxes in the 
intracellular catabolic pathways leading to all fermentation products. To construct this dynamic 
mechanistic model encapsulating key aspects of A. Succinogenes metabolism, classic dynamic 
modeling tools are integrated with a Genome-Scale Metabolic (GSM) model specific to this strain, as 
presented by (Pereira et al., 2018) 
This integration of kinetic and GSM models will include constraints to accurately portray the sugar 
consumption preference when the strain is cultivated on mixed substrates such glucose, sucrose and 
maltose. The model is expected to describe the evolution of succinic acid productivity, along with other 
byproducts, the CO2 fixation rate, and other phenotypic changes arising from altered operating 
conditions. 
Apart from facilitating experimental design and process optimization, the model aims to offer a deeper 
understanding of the metabolic aspects that drive changes in succinic acid productivity in A. 
Succinogenes. We are currently implementing the computational aspects of this work using MATLAB, 
integrating classical Ordinary Differential Equation models with GSM models and Flux Balance Analysis 
via the COBRA Toolbox.

3.2. Medium composition and microbial cultures 
 
The microorganism Actinobacillus Succinogenes 130Z (DSM 22257) was investigated. The stock culture 
of it was stored in glycerol at − 80 ◦C. A reactivation bottle was prepared by adding 1 ml of BHI stock 
solution to 4 ml of water and then adding around 1.5 ml from the glycerol stock, 
After reactivating the cultures from frozen stocks, the precultures medium were carefully prepared 
with stock solutions following these concentrations: Glucose/Sucrose/Maltose/Mixed (20.0 g/L), yeast 
extract (YE) (5.0 g/L), NaHCO3 (20.0 g/L), NaH2PO4 x 2H2O (14.4 g/L), K2HPO4 x 3H2O (30.45 g/L), 
MgCl2 (0.2 g/L), CaCl2 (0.2 g/L), and NaCl (1.0 g/L). 
Ensuring an environment devoid of oxygen, the medium was flushed with N2 and then sterilized. This 
prepared medium was set to incubate at a steady temperature of 37.5 oC under continuous agitation 
at 100 rpm, providing an optimal condition for bacterial growth. 
In contrast, for the fermentation process in the bottles, the formulation of the medium was slightly 
different. It was composed of the following constituents in given proportions: Yeast extract (YE) (10.0 
g/L), K2HPO4 (3.0 g/L), MgCl2 (0.2 g/L), CaCl2 (0.2 g/L), and NaCl (1.0 g/L). The concentration of sugars 
depended on the nature of the experiment, and it was either Glucose/ Sucrose/ Maltose/Mixed 20 g/L 
or 10 g/L. 
To ensure sterility of unwanted organisms, the prepared culture medium was subjected to high-
pressure sterilization at 121 ◦C for a duration of 20 minutes. This methodical and strategic preparation 
of both preculture and fermentation media is designed to create the most favorable conditions for 
efficient fermentation. 
3.3. Experimental setup  
 
For the calibration of the model with parameter estimation 8 datasets were acquired by experimental 
work whereas 3 of them were obtained from (Vigato et al., 2022). The 8 of the experiments were 
conducted with 10 g/L and 20 g/L of sugars (2 only glucose, 2 only sucrose, 2 only maltose and 2 with 
all three sugars). Each experimental condition was conducted in triplicates for the determination of 
the standard deviation. 
 
The experiments were conducted in 330 ml flasks with final liquid volume of 100 ml. The flasks are 
filled with 80 ml of the inorganic medium (water, salts, buffer solution and NaHCO3), sealed with plastic 
rubber stoppers and flushed with nitrogen for securing anaerobic conditions. After autoclaving 5 ml 
sterile yeast is added along with the sterile sugars and water to achieve the concentrations of sugars 
described above. The composition of fermentation broth is demonstrated in the table below. The 
experiment started with the inoculation of 10 ml from the preculture and the setup looks like the one 
in the following figure. Sampling took place every 1.5-2 hours for optical density measurements and 
quantification of sugars and products.

3.4. Analytical methods 
 
The composition of sugar was determined using the Dionex ICS-6000 DC instrument. A dilution of 
1:1000 was necessary since the range of detection of the sugars is 1-30 mg/l. Carbopac Pa20 column 
was used with a mixed eluent consisting of NaOH at concentrations ranging from 10 to 200 mM. The 
ICS eluent flow rate was set at 0.4 mL/min, and the temperature was maintained at 30°C for a total 
analysis time of 35 minutes.  
Fermentation products were collected and analyzed using the Shimadzu Nexera XR HPLC system with 
an Aminex® HPX-87H Ion Exclusion Column for organic acid analysis. The eluent for this analysis 
consisted of 12 mM H2SO4. Micro-Guard Cation H+ Refill Cartridges from Bio-Rad were used as a pre-
column. The HPLC analysis was conducted at a temperature of 64°C for 25 minutes, with a stable eluent 
flow rate of 0.6 mL/min. A dilution around 1:2 with an addition of 40 µ𝑙 sulphuric acid 10% acid was 
performed, for the preparation of the HPLC samples.  
Bacterial growth was monitored by measuring OD600 using the Shimadzu UV-1280 UV-Vis 
spectrophotometer.  
 
 
 
 
 
3.5. Model Description 
 
For the construction of the model, we integrate a Genome Scale Metabolic Model (GSMM) of A. 
Succinogenes into a kinetic model. This integration is based on the fact that the GSMM can accurately 
determine steady-state yields using the complex stoichiometry present in a metabolic network. 
However, during fermentation, the system is never in a steady state as the consumption rates of sugars 
and CO2 and the production rates of succinic acid and byproducts fluctuate due to varying conditions. 
To address this, ordinary differential equations describing the processes are implemented alongside 
the metabolic model. These equations are solved for each time iteration, providing rates that serve as 
constraints for the GSM. As a result, steady-state yields are calculated for each timepoint, making them 
dynamic yields.

3.5.2. GSMM, FBA and Constraints 
 
The equations presented above are ordinary differential equations commonly used in conventional 
kinetic models. However, the equations for describing the kinetics of product and byproduct formation 
are not included in these equations. Instead, the rates of production are calculated using Flux Balance 
Analysis (FBA) with the help of a Genome-scale metabolic model. 
 
As described in the metabolic model section, FBA provides fluxes in the metabolism that optimize the 
objective function, typically the growth rate, given certain constraints. The constraints used in FBA are 
the uptake rates of glucose, sucrose, maltose, and CO2, which are determined using Monod equations. 
These uptake rates are set as the upper bound of the respective fluxes,. The upper bound of the uptake 
fluxes is set to zero, so the FBA solver is free to choose the optimal uptake rates of each substrate 
between 0 and the uptake rate given by the monod model. The units for the fluxes are mmol/(g CDW 
h), so the rates need to be appropriately converted before inputting them into the Genome-scale 
metabolic model.

4.2. 
Parameter Estimation 
 
In the pursuit of enhancing the accuracy of the dynamic metabolic model for A. succinogenes, 
parameter estimation was conducted to optimize crucial model parameters, including μmax of the 
sugars, Bio_sc, uptk_max and nuptk_lb. These parameters were selected due to the importance they 
have on shaping the dynamics of the fermentation. An attempt was made to employ a Genetic 
Algorithm (GA) approach for iteratively refining these parameters over 15 generations. However, due 
to computational constraints, the completion of the GA optimization within a feasible timeframe was 
not achieved. 
Despite the challenge, a manual tuning strategy was adopted, prioritizing the calibration of biomass-
related parameters initially. Promising results were obtained through this approach, as improvements 
were observed in the model's representation of biomass formation. Subsequently, parameters related 
to substrate consumption and product formation were manually tuned, providing valuable insights 
into the utilization of different substrates by the bacterium and the production of succinic acid. While 
the manual tuning showed improvements, the model's accuracy was not perfect, indicating that more 
work is needed. The GA approach, though challenging in terms of computational time, holds potential 
for further optimization. The need for a balanced approach, leveraging both manual and automated 
techniques, is acknowledged to achieve a more refined and predictive model. 
A dedication to model refinement and calibration forms a robust foundation for gaining comprehensive 
insights into A. succinogenes' metabolic behavior. With continued efforts and a balanced optimization 
strategy, the development of a highly reliable model capable of accurately representing the 
bacterium's dynamic metabolic dynamics under diverse conditions is aimed for.

4.3.1. Biomass, SA and byproducts  
The biomass serves as a key indicator of the bacterium's physiological state and its overall 
metabolic activity. When biomass is well-tuned, it provides valuable insights into the allocation 
of resources towards growth and product formation. Consequently, this positively impacts the 
predictions of substrate preferences, product yields, and the interplay between various 
metabolic pathways. The model presented, predicts biomass growth as shown in the figure 11 
below with high accuracy even when manually tuned, which further validates its capability to 
capture the intricacies of A. Succinogenes' metabolic behavior. 
 
Figure 11. Biomass predictions compared to experimental data in 1.6 atm pressure 
 
 
Regarding the product formation, the graph in figure 12 exhibits the dynamic evolution of product 
concentrations during the fermentation process by A. Succinogenes. Each compound is represented by 
a different colored line, with blue indicating succinic acid, yellow for formic acid for acetic acid, red, 
and magenta for ethanol. The x-axis represents time, while the y-axis indicates the concentration of 
the compounds in g/L.  
As we observe the graph, we can see that succinic acid is produced in higher amounts compared to 
the other byproducts, owing to the fact that the metabolic pathways are directed towards the C4 
==PAGE:35==

pathway with the addition of CO2. This metabolic shift towards the C4 pathway enables the bacteria 
to produce larger quantities of succinic acid. 
 
 
Figure 12. Predictions of the product concentration without implementing the nuptk_lb constraint 
The concentrations of all compounds increase sharply at the onset of fermentation due to the 
exponential growth phase. As time progresses, the growth rate slows down, leading to a lower slope 
in the concentration curves. This signifies the transition to the stationary phase, where the bacteria 
continue to produce the compounds but at a slower rate, eventually reaching a plateau phase. 
However, the model's predictive capability for other byproducts, such as acetic acid, formic acid, and 
ethanol, is not as accurate. This discrepancy is evident from the difference between the continuous 
model predictions and the discrete experimental data for these compounds. 
 
The main reason for this discrepancy lies in the default uptake rate boundaries of certain nutrients, 
which are set very high. By leaving them unchanged, the uptake of these nutrients is likely to increase 
significantly, resulting in higher growth rates and lower acetic acid production. To address this issue, 
we introduced the constraint "nuptk_lb" as described in the GSMM, FBA and Constraints section. This 
constraint limits the maximum uptake of specific nutrients, including L-Cysteine, L-methionine, L-
Glutamate, and Pyridoxal phosphate. With the implementation of this constraint, the product 
distribution is more effectively regulated, and the model provides predictions that are closer to the 
experimental data as shown in the figure 13.

predict the production of acetic acid, formic acid, and ethanol, leading to a more accurate 
representation of the fermentation process by Actinobacillus. Once more the predictions are very close 
to reality even though parameter estimation had not been completed. These findings highlight the 
importance of carefully tuning the model parameters and constraints to achieve better agreement 
between model simulations and experimental observations.

around 4 to 4.5 hours, and then start to decrease. This trend can be attributed to the fact that the 
bacterial biomass stops its exponential growth and enters the stationary phase.

early stages of fermentation, while ethanol shows zero production. However, after approximately one 
hour, a notable shift occurs in the metabolic pathways. Acetate production rate declines, while ethanol 
production rate starts to increase. This phenomenon indicates a shift in the acetyl-CoA node, where 
the carbon flux is not solely directed towards acetate, but also towards ethanol production. This shift 
happens again from the other direction after 5 hours where the flux in the node mentioned above is 
directed towards acetate thus production rate of ethanol reaches zero

5. Conclusions and Future perspective 
 
A new concept of model is introduced, where a hybrid model is created by integrating a Genome-scale 
Metabolic Model (GSM) into a kinetic model with ordinary differential equations (ODEs). Significant 
advancements in understanding A. Succinogenes' metabolism are provided by this hybrid approach, 
offering valuable insights into the dynamics of fermentation processes and enabling the monitoring of 
metabolic pathway shifts. Notably, its ability to incorporate relevant constraints resulted in accurately 
portraying the preference of the bacterium for different sugars.  
Furthermore, the evolution of succinic acid productivity and CO2 fixation rates at various time points 
can be captured by our model. The information provided by the model proves valuable in gaining a 
deeper understanding of the fermentation process. However, it is acknowledged that the 
computational demand for calibration and parameter estimation remains a bottleneck, requiring 
additional improvements and resources. 
Nonetheless, the inclusion of the reconstructed metabolic network from the GSM in the hybrid model 
is beneficial. Its predictive capabilities are demonstrated even when manually tuned, allowing 
bypassing extensive parameterization. To further enhance the model's performance, future 
perspectives involve obtaining consistent experimental data for precise parameter estimation and 
allowing more time for thorough tuning. 
Moving forward, significant contributions can be made by the hybrid model to experimental design 
processes and the optimization of biosuccinic acid production while simultaneously reducing CO2 
levels. The model can contribute to sustainable solutions for CO2 reduction by A.Succinogenes such as 
biogas upgrade, while promises prospects for the advancement of eco-friendly and more competitive 
bioprocesses.
