Abstract
AmietвАЩs model for the prediction of wind turbine noise is validated and improved in the project.
A particular attention is given to the trailing edge modelling but the model accounts also for the
inпђВow noise. The model for trailing edge noise is improved considering three diпђАerent formulations
for the wall pressure spectra. Rozenberg, Roger and MoreauвАЩs model, GoodyвАЩs model and Catlett,
Anderson, Forset and StewartвАЩs model. Model implementation on the full scale turbine provides
good values of the total noise emitted from the turbine. The trailing edge noise model on the
full scale provides signiпђБcant contribution to the total noise at high frequencies and results to be
well predicted with Rozenberg et al. wall pressure spectra model. The other two cases result to
be not satisfactory. Rozeberg et al. wall pressure spectra results to be a good improvement and
provide very good values for the wall pressure spectra emitted at the trailing edge of the blade
and the modelled results match very well with the surface microphoneвАЩs measurements at middle
frequencies even if underestimation is given at low and high frequencies.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Wind turbines produce energy extracting it from the incoming пђВow. While the turbine operates,
it emits noise due to the interaction between the structure of the turbine and the atmospheric
turbulence, as well as the turbulence generating around the structure itself. Rotating mechanical
parts and cooling components, as well as the loading variations on the structure contribute to
the generation of noise from wind turbines. As wind energy has grown and the installation of
wind farms has increased, noise emission became a signiпђБcant problem for the acceptance of wind
turbines and farms among people. Noise emissions are also regulated by legislation that varies
from country to country, and prediction of noise became of interest for the manufacturers and
part of the design process.

In order to better describe the objective of the project, it is considered to provide, in this sec-
tion some basic knowledge about wind turbine noise deпђБnitions, generating mechanisms of wind
turbine noise and an overview on prediction methods for wind turbine noise.

1.1.2
Wind turbine noise mechanisms
Noise emitted from wind turbines is of two kinds: mechanical and aerodynamic noise.
Mechanical noise is tonal and is originated from the relative motion of mechanical parts and the
dynamic response between them. The main sources are the gearbox, the generator, the cooling

In particular the gear box is responsible for the main part of the mechanical noise generated
by a wind turbine. Its noise is due to transmission errors of the pair of gear meshes, vibration
of the components and to their loadings. Transmission errors are due to imperfections in the
gear pitch, the form of the meshing teeth and distortion due to tooth loading. The noise gener-

transmission path the mechanical noise can be classiпђБed in air-borne noise and structure-born
noise. In the пђБrst case the noise is directly propagated from the component source into the air.
For the structure borne noise, noise is пђБrst transmitted along the structure and then it is radiated

A reduction of the mechanical noise can be achieved with nacelle insulation and vibration isolation

Aerodynamic noise is induced by the пђВow and is due to the interaction of the пђВow turbulence
with the structure. It is mainly radiated from the blade and it is due to turbulence interaction
with the blade surface. Aerodynamic noise can be of three kinds. Some of the noise is due to the
variation of the loading on the blade surface. This can be due to the rotation of the blade and it is
associated with a low frequency, for example the blade passing frequency through velocity deпђБcit
generating behind the tower. The inпђВow noise is instead due to the turbulence in the atmosphere
and its interaction with the blade surface with it while the incoming пђВow hits the blade. This
is mainly radiated from the leading edge, as this is the part that mainly encounters the пђВow

Turbulence
Turbulence characterizes most пђВows and it is likely to generate in several пђВow conпђБgurations
around aerodynamic surfaces, as a wind turbine blade is. Turbulence is characterized by a mo-
tion with irregular пђВuctuations, as mixing or eddying motion, which is superimposed to the main
пђВow [2]. The пђВuctuating motion of the superimposed part is complex in its details but results in
important consequences for the пђВow course and for the equilibrium of the forces. In turbulent
пђВows, the viscosity is increased and at high Reynolds numbers, there is a continuous transport of
energy from the main пђВow into large eddies, and from this to smaller ones and so on, being then
dissipated to the smallest eddies and then to heat [2]. Close investigation of turbulent пђВow reveals
that in turbulence, velocity and pressure, at a пђБxed point in space, do not remain constant with
time but пђВuctuate irregularly at high frequencies [2]. The boundary layer developing around an
aerodynamic surface transits from laminar to turbulent at a certain distance from the leading
edge and depending on the Reynolds number. The high pressure пђВuctuations are responsible for
the generation and radiation of noise when they interacts with surfaces, in particular with sharp
edges.
In the atmospheric boundary layer, momentum is exchanged by viscous to turbulent shear
stresses. This results in the developing and decay of turbulent eddies which manifest themselves
in gusts varying in time and space. The driving mechanisms of turbulence are aerodynamic and
thermal. The aerodynamic mechanism is generated by the interaction of the пђВow with the surface
and the thermal is generated by the buoyancy of the air caused by the local heating of the sun.
The atmospheric boundary layer contains eddies of all sizes, the dominant ones at a certain height
h are found to have scales of the same order h.

1.1.3
Overview on noise prediction models
Noise prediction codes are of fundamental importance in the design process of a wind turbine, in
order to satisfy the need of minimized noise impact [1]. A classiпђБcation of noise prediction codes
made by Lowson divides the prediction codes in three classes according to the complexity of the
models applied, and to how detailed the description of the parameters are.
Class I models estimates the overall sound pressure level using simple algebraic functions and
considering the basic wind turbine parameters. Of this kind are some rules of thumb that estimate
sound power level based on the power of the wind turbine, or the diameter of the turbine, or
the diameter and the tip velocity. A class I model for the estimation of the sound pressure level
suggested by Hagg, Brochure and De Wolf [1] considers some constants and parameters like the
tip velocity, the ratio between rotor and blade area, the axial force coeпђГcient, the ratio between
the diameter and the radius and the diameter itself. Class I code should be used only for rough
estimations of noise emitted but can not be considered enough for good prediction and for design

Class II models are developed accounting for the diпђАerent noise generation mechanisms, are semi-

Conclusion and further work
AmietвАЩs model is used to predict the noise from the NordTank 500 kW wind turbine at far
пђБeld. The model implementation results to predict very well the noise emission. Throughout the
whole spectra in fact, modelled results match with the measurements. At low frequencies, around
20 вИТ30 Hz the model provides an underestimation of the measures of 6 вИТ7 dB. This is can be
due to the lack in the prediction of the atmospheric turbulence and the inпђВow noise. Despite
this, the correction of the results with the background measurements provides an improvement in
the prediction at low frequencies of circa 5 db. This suggests also an inпђВuence of the background
noise in the mismatch between results and measurements at low frequencies.
The prediction of the total trailing edge noise using the formulation of the wall pressure spectra
from Rozenberg, Roger and Moreau shows a signiпђБcant contribution to the total noise at middle
and high frequencies and the results are satisfactory. As expected the trailing edge noise provides
low values at low frequencies where most of the noise is due to inпђВow noise.
AmietвАЩs model using GoodyвАЩs formulation for the wall pressure spectra underestimates the values
of the trailing edge noise providing results far from the measurements also at high frequencies.
Moreover it results in values lower than the formulation with RozenbergвАЩs model between 10 and
15 dB throughout the whole spectra. It can be then concluded that applying the adverse pressure
gradient model developed by Rozenberg at al. is a good improvement if AmietвАЩs model for the
trailing edge noise prediction for wind turbine applications
Trailing edge noise results for each section singularly show low values at far пђБeld, as expected for
the contribution of a single source point at far distance. The results from the suction side are
higher that the one generated from the pressure side showing a major contribution to noise from
suction side. The overall evaluation of the trailing edge noise results at far пђБeld, considering the
complete implementation including pressure and suction side noise is compared with the approx-
imations. Considering a double suction side contribution overestimates the correct results for low
and middle frequencies while underestimates them for high frequencies. Therefore the complete
approach results to be highly preferable.
The Doppler correction is well implemented and its frequency range validation is studied as well.
The correction provides an improvement of only circa 2 dB along the whole spectra as expected
from Schlinken and AmietвАЩs theory.
The study of the diпђАerent formulations of the wall pressure spectra concludes with very satisfac-
tory results from the formulation of the wall pressure spectra formulated by Rozenberg, Roger

and Moreau and implemented. Comparing the results provided by their formulation of the model,
the resulting wall pressure spectra matche very well with the surface microphones measurements
especially at middle frequencies. It provides better results than both GoodyвАЩs zero pressure gra-
dient model and CatlettвАЩs adverse pressure gradient model. The last one provides unexpected
results for wall pressure spectra at pressure side and showing an incapability of the model to

of the parameters used by Catlett can be suggested.
The inпђВow noise deserves a more detailed investigation and validation towards cases from lit-
erature. The importance of the atmospheric turbulence intensity in the inпђВow noise has to be
considered and the thickness correction described by Roger and Moreau and applied to real at-
mospheric turbulence conditions show no signiпђБcant eпђАects as the turbulence length scale is much
higher that the reference value. Developing a correction and the study of appropriate reference
values for real cases are suggested in order to have a detailed model for the inпђВow noise.
In order to have a comprehensive tool for the prediction of the noise from wind turbines, the
model can be extended including models for the prediction of the other mechanisms that gener-
ate noise from a wind turbine. In particular a good choice would be to include the tip noise in
the model and the separation noise.
Separation/stall noise in particular still requires better understandings of its physical mechanism.
The study of the dynamic stall with measurements of the noise generated in this condition can
be performed in wind tunnels on airfoils and blades.
Even if RozenbergвАЩs model provides good results, and allow to improve AmietвАЩs model especially
at trailing edge, literature studies still show that the model underestimates the measurements
especially for airfoil applications. Therefore, an implementation of AmietвАЩs model with studies of
the wall pressure spectra by computational methods, as Reynolds averaged Navier-Stokes simu-
lations can be investigated.
The prediction model can then be extended from a single turbine to a wind farm case and the
noise coming from wakes can be studied. Moreover extension of the model with propagation
methods can be performed.
It must to be remarked that, in order to have a more precise prediction model, a better dis-
cretization of the blade is strongly recommended. Furthermore, a future validation of the model
on diпђАerent turbines and for diпђАerent wind speed conпђБgurations is suggested.

DTU Wind Energy is a department of the Technical University of Denmark with a unique integration of research, education, innovation and 
public/private sector consulting in the field of wind energy. Our activities develop new opportunities and technology for the global and Danish 
exploitation of wind energy. Research focuses on key technical-scientific fields, which are central for the development, innovation and use of wind 
energy and provides the basis for advanced education at the education. 
 
We have more than 240 staff members of which approximately 60 are PhD students. Research is conducted within nine research programmes 
organized into three main topics: Wind energy systems, Wind turbine technology and Basics for wind energy.
