Abstract
Leading edge erosion is a limiting factor designing and operating large modern wind
turbine blades. Due to leading edge erosion, the tip speed of a wind turbine blade can
not exceed 80−90 m/s. Material certiﬁcation of protecting systems for the leading
edge is typically performed in a Rain Erosion Tester, where rotating small models of
the blade are running through falling water droplets. In order to further understand
the leading edge erosion damage mechanism, and simplify the test setup, a so-called
Single Point Impact Fatigue Tester (SPIFT) has been developed where small rubber
balls under controlled condition are hitting small material samples. Thereby, the
fatigue damage mechanism can be studied in great details. In order to correlate
the observed damage mechanism with governing material parameters, ﬁnite element
modelling (FEM) is needed for the impact events. In the current work, A 2D axisym-
metric model of a rubber ball impacting onto two case studies, steel and polyurethane
(PU), at strain rates comparable to rain droplet impact on a wind turbine blade was
developed in Abaqus commercial code. The model was validated by observing a good
correlation between the results by comparing the deformations and rebound speed
of the ball in the model to experiments. Besides, the non-linear elastic behaviour of
materials and their dependency on deformation rate were eﬀectively achieved. It was
observed that, a signiﬁcant amount of impact energy is dissipated in viscous losses in
the ball. As such 97% energy loss from the rubber ball impacting the PU substrate
in speed about 170 m/s. Furthermore, the stress distribution in the PU coating was
studied. It was identiﬁed that the maximum principal stress and strain components
are happened in the material in a radius of 1.5 mm and a depth of 0.5 mm from
the center of the substrate. Finally, the dissipated energy due to the deformation of
the substrate after the impact was compared with the surface temperature captured
from experiment. It was indicated that there is a good match between experiment
and FEM model.

Introduction
1.1
Wind energy
Wind energy is known as one of the best renewable sources and alternative for the
limited fossil fuels resources in the world.
The increase in population, economic
growth and energy consumption are threatening our future environmental sustain-
ability. In response, there has been a signiﬁcant increase in the number of wind
power installations in recent years. A three bladed horizontal axis wind turbine is a
common design and most eﬃcient for wind power generation [1]. The kinetic energy
in the wind transforms to mechanical energy by rotating the shaft which connects
the blades to the generator and ﬁnally converts to electricity.
The blades consist of a main spar, which is mainly responsible for carrying loads
and two shells that are adhesively bonded together and to the main spar that form an
airfoil shape. The schematic shape of the airfoil components are shown in Figure 1.1.
In the main spar (load carrying box), the load carrying ﬂanges (spar caps) towards the
shells consisted of mainly unidirectional laminates which are responsible for ﬂapwise
loads coming from wind pressure. The main spar walls (shear webs) towards the
leading edge and trailing edges are made of sandwich panels with balsa wood core

edgewise loads coming from blade gravity and twist loads. The shell materials are

1.2
Leading Edge Erosion (LEE)
In order to generate more power and eﬃciency, reduce number of expensive towers,
foundations and the need for power collection cables, the size of wind turbine blades
are getting larger. Larger blades allow the turbine to produce more power, since the
power output of the wind turbine is directly related to the swept area1 of its blades
[3]. However the large wind turbines at normal operating condition experience high
wind velocity in range of 90−110 m/s at the blade-tip, which is exposed to frequent
impact of rain and hailstone on the leading edge and subjected to a constant fatigue
loading. This can cause erosion on the leading edge as shown in Figure 1.2 .

The so-called leading edge erosion phenomenon is one of the most important issues
in large modern wind turbine blades. It could cause aerodynamic performance loss
due to the roughness on blade surface and results in substancial increase the drag
and decrease the lift coeﬃcient which, decreases the power production and additional
repair and maintenance costs [5]. Therefore, there has been a great emphasis on
developing protective solutions to increase the lifetime of wind turbines like gelcoates,
ﬂexible gelcoatings, leading edge tapes, erosion shields and integrated erosion shields
[6].
1.3
Rain erosion test (RET) methods
Rain erosion tests are performed to evaluate the erosion resistance of materials and
recognize their erosion behaviour.
One of the most common approaches for rain
erosion testing is the Whirling arm test where rotating small models of the blade
are running through falling water droplets simulating rainfall. Figure 1.3 shows an
example of the Whirling Arm Rain Erosion test. The high cost is however one of
the downsides for this test as well as the limitation in mass and geometry of the
sample due to high level of vibrations and also limitation in rotational speed as this
facility exhibits a high centrifugal force on the specimen when reaching the average
rotational speed of about 150 m/s [7].

Another approach is the rotating disk erosion test where the rotating disc as a
result of the balance can reach the speed up to 500 m/s [9]. The disc is placed in a
vacuum chamber. Therefore, unlike the whirling arm approach, the droplet distortion
due to the turbulent circulation of air is to be minimized. The temperature rise will
also get minimized as a result of the friction between the rotating disc and air. The
number of droplets impacting the sample at each revolution can be quantiﬁed exactly
in this approach since a nozzle is used with the rotating disc rigs [10].
According to Bartolomé and Teuwen [11], rotating devices mainly are not capable of
reproducing the movement of the turbine blade during rainfall impacts. In principal,
the impact velocity and impact angle depend on the position of the blade during the
raindrop impact [12]. Therefore, the simulation of the impact layout in the wind
turbine blade still remains unrealistic for all water droplet erosion testing devices.
To study the rain erosion more realistically, it is important to model the mate-
rial properties correctly. For wind turbine blades an epoxy in-mold gel coat and
polyurethane (PU) in-mold gel coat are applied in the current market. It has very

1.4
Single Point Impact Fatigue Tester (SPIFT)
In order to identify the initial damage on the leading edge of a wind turbine blade,
a so-called Single Point Impact Fatigue Tester (SPIFT) has been newly developed
where small rubber balls under controlled condition are hitting the coated laminate
[14]. The schematic setup is shown in Figure 1.5.
14
==PAGE:17==

Introduction
Figure 1.5: Single point impact fatigue test setup [14]
The initial damage on the specimen surface is observed by a high speed digital
camera during the test. The high quality image is one of the advantages of this
approach compared to the whirling arm test, where the camera is performed outside
of the test setup since it is diﬃcult to make the high resolution capture of the rotating
target. In addition, the elastic stress wave energy traveling through a material after
the impacts can be detected by an acoustic signal. Thereby, the fatigue damage
progression mechanism can be studied in great details. Other justiﬁcation of the
SPIFT is the capability of controlling the impact rate and temperature of the surface.
Weigel [15] observed that, at high strain and strain rate, the polyurethane elastomer
material loses its viscosity leading to an unnatural heating of the specimen and
resulting in changes in mechanical properties, which makes the cyclical fatigue testing
impossible. This is also an advantage for the SPIFT when the impact fatigue process
develops in Finite Element Model (FEM). Thereby, it is possible to calculate the
stresses and strains in FEM and compare with observed damage from the SPIFT
and predict the generalised criteria for the fatigue life of the material. Finally, the
low cost for having this test setup and the samples enables more accessibility of this
technology for the manufacturer [16].
In this study, the impact of a rubber ball onto two case studies, steel and PU coat-
ings, is developed using ﬁnite element modeling in Abaqus commercial code. The
hyperelastic and viscoelastic properties of the material model are extracted from ex-

2.1
Hyperelasticity
Hyperelastic materials [17] such as elastomers, polymers, rubber and biological mat-
ter (arteries, muscles, skin, etc.)
exhibit highly nonlinear elastic response when
subjected to very large strains. Figure 2.1, shows the stress-strain curve for elastic
materials. In zone (I), the material behave as linear elastic while, hyperelastic ma-
terials have also a non-linear elastic region represented in zone (II). The material
returns back to the initial state with no internal energy dissipation, and undergo
large shape changes when unloading. Such behaviour is typical for rubbers and other
polymers as well as foams/sponges. In this study, it is assumed that the material is
initially isotropic, elastic and nearly incompressible, see Figure 2.2.
In order to deﬁne a hyperelastic material, a strain energy density function is re-
quired to describe the high deformability, recoverability when unloading and non-
linear stress-strain behaviour of this kind of material. In general, a deformation is a
motion due to the change in shape which is identiﬁed as the equation of motion in
Equation 2.1.
F = R · U = V · R
(2.1)

2.2
Viscoelasticity
Viscoelasticity means that the material’s elastic stress-strain relationship depends
on the strain rate (how fast material is deformed) and time.
They also exhibit

both elastic and viscous characteristics (they have both solid and ﬂuid properties),
hence the name. These materials have some special properties. Unlike regular elastic
materials, they dissipate energy during the deformation due to the fractional sliding
of the long molecules across one another. This energy dissipation turns into heat.
The generated heat raise the temperature and in case of cyclic loading (so called
hysteresis) it can cause fatigue failure. The dissipated energy on the stress-strain
relation is represented by the area between loading and unloading curve as shown in
Figure 2.3.
Another special property is viscoelastic creep. It means that the material experi-
ence a time dependent increase in strain while the stress is held constant. They also
relieve stress over time for a constant strain which is known as stress relaxation. A
typical curve of these two phenomena is shown in Figure 2.4.

Summery
In summery, the 2D axisymmetric model was validated by comparing the results
from the deformation and rebound speed of the ball and dissipated energy in the PU
substrate with the observations from the SPIFT experiment. The indication of the
damage from the SPIFT test can also be compared with the dissipated energy from
the model. For this particular PU substrate the damage from the SPIFT was not
achieved. However, the damage of some other type of PU [14] was indicated in the
same range as in the current FE model with high level of dissipated energy. By using
this FE model together with SPIFT experiment we are able to study the fatigue life
in leading edge erosion of wind turbine blade.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
In conclusion, in this work a 2D axisymmetric model of a rubber ball impacting a
coating substrate was developed in Abaqus. The model was validated by observing
very good correlation in results comparing with experiments. A good understanding
of nonlinear elastic and linear viscoelastic behaviour in rubber material was obtained
by observing the non linear relation of incoming speed and rebound speed and cor-
responding energy loss in the rubber ball. It should be mentioned that, if we do not
include the viscoelastic part into the material model, we will get the rebound speed
equal to the incoming speed corresponding zero energy loss. About 97 % energy loss
at high impact rates were able to capture. This means that a signiﬁcant amount of
the kinetic energy is dissipated in the ball due to viscous loss. Moreover, diﬀerent
paths through the critical point of the substrate were obtained by which, the locations
where diﬀerent stress and strain components have their maximum values were be able
to captured. It can be stated that the critical points are gathered in a volume with
the shape of a disc with radius of 1.5 mm and the thickness of 0.5 mm. Furthermore,
the dissipated energy from the PU substrate due to its deformation was calculated.
It was observed that, there is a strong correlation between the dissipated energy in
the FE model and the surface temperature coming from the generated heat in the
substrate in experiment, which can be related to the damage of material. Therefore,
the fatigue damage mechanism in leading edge erosion of wind turbine blade can be

The current model is applicable to study diﬀerent parameters for diﬀerent types
of materials for coating substrate. the same test can be implemented at reduced
temperature by cooling the SPIFT test setup to zero degree, getting the master curve
at the same reference temperature for the model and include this experiment. The
model can be used not only for leading edge erosion but also in any other applications
related to impact of surface at high strain rates.
The non linear viscoelastic model can be studied as the future work. Then, addi-
