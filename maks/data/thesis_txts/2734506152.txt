Abstract
Aviation is responsible for 5% of global anthropogenic climate impact, of which air-
craft condensation trails (contrails) contributes significantly. Contrails differs from
CO2 and other non-CO2 emissions with their exceptional short life times of up to
60 hours observed, and its’ high dependence on meteorologic conditions. Thus, it is
potentially very valuable to develop Characterisation Factors (CF) for contrails. There-
fore, State-of-the-Art contrail modelling and characterisation compliant with the Life
Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA)-framework, is identified through two comprehensive
literature reviews. To date, the most nuanced CF is regionalised and estimated for
several time horizons. In this study, the CFs for contrails are differentiated based
on altitude in accordance with the LCIA Global Warming Potential over a 100 year
time horizon (GWP100). Five altitude levels of 50 hPa ranging from 150-400 hPa
(level 1 to 5) is utilised, with level 1 at (150-200 hPa). Furthermore the energy forcing
(EF) has been quantified for both mean cruise altitude (EFmean) and the five alti-
tude levels. To perform the analysis, the Contrail Cirrus Prediction Tool (CoCiP)
is employed with European Centre for European Centre for Medium-Range Weather
Forecasts (ECMWF) reanalysis data, and 100 global flights from the 1st of January 2020.
GWP100 (150-400 hPa) is estimated to 10 [kg CO2eq/flown km] (with good confidence),
which is within a likely range, estimated through a case study. GWP100 (level 1-3)
ranged from 7.5-14.3 [kg CO2eq/flown km], with peak forcing per km at level 2 (200-250
hPa) (with medium confidence). No climate impact is found at level 5 (350-400 hPa),
whereas level 4 had a cooling effect. Level 4 and 5 are estimated on small data sets
of 8 and 1 flight trips respectively, which entails inherent significant uncertainties.
EFmean is estimated to 1.9 [105 J/person meter (pm)], and falls within the low end
of existing ranges. The case study employing EFmean, however, overestimated global
aviation climate impact from contrails by a factor 101-112%. It could not be determined
conclusively, if the overshoot is due to underestimated seats per aircraft alone, or if
it also stems from the conversion of EF [J/pm] to annual mean net radiative forcing
[W/m2]. Altitude differentiated EFs followed the same trends as altitude differentiated
GWP100, but with low confidence for EF (level 1-3).
The methodology for GWP100, including altitude differentiated GWP100s, is validated
with good-high confidence. The methodology used to estimate EFmean and EF (level
1-5) is validated with medium-good confidence. The confidence level of the individual
findings should be increased in future findings by employing more flight data, including
seasonal variations and preferably spanning over several years to mitigate influence
from temperature anomalies. The complexity of assessing climate impacts for contrails
compared to other greenhouse gases, due to their regional, seasonal, and altitude-
dependent nature, underscores the necessity of a robust metric that is agreed upon across
the aviation sector. It is recommended to investigate possible metrics, better suited for
practical implementation in route-optimisation and decision making, exemplified as
based on flown kilometres within specified Ice Supersaturated Regions (ISSR) or per
contrail length.

Introduction
1.1
Climate Change Impact of Aviation and Contrails
Climate change is one of today’s increasing challenges. According to the planetary
boundaries (PB) framework, anthropogenic activities overshoot the annual planetary
budget. Consequently, the Earth is currently in the zone of uncertainty with a continu-
ously increasing risk of reaching climate tipping points (Richardson et al., 2023). In
order to stay within the climate goal of 1.5oC global averaged warming, it is critical to ad-
vance state-of-the-art climate change assessments, including those of non-CO2 emissions
(Steffen et al., 2018). Aviation is estimated to contribute with 1.6-2.5% of anthropogenic
global Effective Radiative Forcing (ERF) due to CO2 emissions alone. However, when
including non-CO2 emissions, it increases to 3.5-5% of global ERF (D. S. Lee et al., 2021,
Breakthrough Energy, 2023b). Aviation may even warm the climate about three times
faster, than first estimated by CO2 emissions alone (D. S. Lee et al., 2021). Non-CO2 cov-
ers emissions contributing to the greenhouse gas effect, such as nitrogen oxides, sulphate
aerosols, soot, water vapour and Condensation trails (typically referred to as contrails,
see Figure 1). The biggest aviation non-CO2 climate impact is contributed by contrails
(D. S. Lee et al., 2021), making contrails a significant non-CO2 emission (Kärcher, 2018).

1.2
Physics and Life Cycle of Contrails
IPPC, 1999 estimates that cirrus cover about 30% of the Earth, while contrail cirrus are
predicted to cover 0.5-0.8% by 2050 (Penner et al., 1999). Contrail formation appears
mainly in the upper troposphere for Subsonic Aviation (flying speed below the speed
of sound) (Penner et al., 1999). In this section, it will be discovered why contrails form
(Section 1.2.1), how they evolve (Section 1.2.2) and lastly why they have a climate
impact (Section 1.2.3).

The Schmidt-Appelman Criterion

Contrails can form when the Schmidt-
Appelman Criterion (SAC) is fulfilled.
SAC consists of two separate theories,
which was first combined into one crite-
rion by U. O. Schumann, 1996. SAC de-
scribes the relation between Water Vapour
Partial Pressure and Ambient Tempera-
ture for respectively water vapour and
ice. If the ambient temperature is lower
than the ambient threshold temperature
for saturation of water, then clouds can
form, as the air will be Supersaturated
with water (Figure 2) (U. O. Schumann,
1996). Contrails will mainly form at alti-
tudes of 7.4-14 km for Subsonic Aviation
(U. O. Schumann, 1996, U. Schumann et
al., 2017, Kärcher, 2018, Penner et al.,
1999), however, studies have found con-
trails at 18.7 km altitude, typically caused
by supersonic aviation (U. Schumann et
al., 2017, Penner et al., 1999). Average
global peak conditions for formation of
contrails have been found at 9 km (Rädel
and Shine, 2008).
If the atmosphere is ice-Subsaturated, the contrails can only persist up to a few minutes,
in contrast to several hours for ice-supersaturated conditions (Relative Humidity over
ice (RHice)> 100%) (Rädel and Shine, 2008). A cold ice-supersaturated region (ISSR)
can be defined by RHice >80% and T < −40oC, which create likely conditions for
contrail formations (Rädel and Shine, 2008). When contrails form in areas without
ice-supersaturation, they wont persist nor lead to any significant change in radiative
forcing (RF) (Kärcher, 2018, Table 2). The Schmidt-Appleman criterion predicts that
contrails are more likely to persist at cold ambient temperatures, and high relative
humidity levels (U. O. Schumann, 1996). SAC is illustrated by Figure 2, where two
aircrafts are placed along the international standardised temperature profile (average
temperature at a certain altitude), to exemplify when contrails are most likely to form.
The aircraft to the left would always lead to contrail formation, the aircraft to the right
would never lead to contrail formation. And inbetween the dashed lines, contrails may
form, depending on the relative humidity (Figure 2, U. O. Schumann, 1996). SAC also
explains the increased occurrence of contrails at high latitudes for low altitudes, while
it near the equatorial line is increased for high altitudes (Teoh et al., 2023). Clouds
form around nuclei stemming from engine exhaust, among other things. If no nuclei is
present, the atmosphere can be ice-supersaturated with no cloud formation present
(Kärcher, 2018).

1.2.2
Phases of Contrails
Contrails are split into varying phases within literature depending on the context (Lim
et al., 2016, Paoli and Shariff, 2016). In this study, the phases are described according
to Paoli and Shariff, 2016, with the three following phases:
• Formation
• Vortexting
• Diffusion and dilution
Formation happens in the first seconds of particle emissions from the jet. In ice-
supersaturated areas the particles function as nuclei and catalysts for condensation
and deposition of water vapour into ice-crystals (Lim et al., 2016). These contrails
are characterised by the linear-shaped condensation trails. After about 3 minutes the
vortexing phase begins (Lim et al., 2016), at which the air-parcels and ice-particles
may be pushed into lower atmospheric layers (about 100 metres). When pushed
down, the Partial Pressure will increase, decreasing the size of the air parcel. For this
to happen the ambient temperature increases, which may lead to Ice-Subsaturated
conditions (Kärcher, 2018). Similarly, if RHice >120% the ice particles, may grow
heavy enough to fall to lower altitudes and Sublimate (Teoh et al., 2023). Contrails
is the general term, typically referring to condensation trails with their linear shape
(Table 2), which disappears within 10 minutes after formation. Due to their short
life time, their climate impact is deemed negligible (Kärcher, 2018, U. Schumann, 2005).

If the atmosphere is ice-supersaturated, conditions are satisfied for long lived contrails.
Contrails with a life time of up to 10 hours, are defined as persistent contrails, and
estimated to have a small climate impact globally (Kärcher, 2018). When persistent
contrails are formed, they may lead to further cloud condensation, because clouds
form around nuclei. When they persist long enough to reach the length of 10-100
km (with a lifetime above 10 hours), they are classified as contrail cirrus. The large
surface area and long life time, contributes to a global large climate impact (Kärcher,
2018). Contrail cirrus, also referred to as Aviation Induced Cirrus (AICi), has been
observed with lifetimes up to 18 hours (Minnis et al., 1998, Haywood et al., 2009), and
in rare cases days (up to 60 hours) (Breakthrough Energy, 2023b, U. Schumann et al.,
2017). Contrail Cirrus can be difficult to distinguish from natural frost clouds (cirrus)
(Fuglestvedt et al., 2010), because contrails begin to loose their linear shape, typically
after the first five hours (Burkhardt and Kärcher, 2011), due to diffusion and dilution
(Kärcher, 2018). Contrails, persistent contrails and contrail cirrus, are all covered by
the term Aircraft Induced Clouds (AICl) (Kärcher, 2018). All terms and definitions
are presented in Table 2.
1.2.3
Radiative Forcing of Contrails
The Earth’s energy balance is influenced by contrails (presented in Figure 3) reflecting
incoming short-wave radiation from the sun during the day leading to a cooling effect.
The cooling, however, is counteracted by contrails taking up outgoing long-wave
radiation emitted from the Earth both during the day and night leading to an overall
net warming (Teoh et al., 2023, Breakthrough Energy, 2023b).
Figure 3: Contrails’ influence on the energy budget. Red arrows symbolise outgoing long-wave
radiation. Yellow arrows indicate incoming short-wave radiations. Outgoing long-wave radiations
are present both during day and night phase, however, for simplicity it is only presented in the
night emission budget (left), and not during day radiation budget (right). Intensity of radiation is
symbolised by the intensity of the lines, with full line = full intensity, and finest dotted lines = lowest
intensity. The effect is shown for contrails below, without and above existing cloud formation.
The change in radiative forcing from contrails, is further dependent on existing cloud
cover. The warming and cooling is amplified when contrails form above and below
existing clouds, respectively (Breakthrough Energy, 2023b). Per flown km, the highest
changes in radiative forcing from contrails is found at a 10 km altitude, even though
the optimal conditions for contrail formation (per flown km) is at 9 km (Rädel and

1.3
Life Cycle Impact Assessment and Climate Forcings from
Condensation Trails
The only flight related climate forcing currently regulated by the European Union
Emission Trading System is CO2-emissions (D. Lee et al., 2020. Non-CO2 emissions
are excluded (Cox and Althaus, 2019), as their relation to atmospheric mechanics
require deeper understanding (Penner et al., 1999), this includes the formation of
contrail and contrails influence on cirrus cover (Kärcher, 2018, D. S. Lee et al., 2021).
The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) provides characterisation
factors used in Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA) to estimate the climate change
impact of a product or service (Jolliet et al., 2016). At status quo there is no common
consensus on which methodological emission weighting factors (EWF) should be used
when estimating non-CO2 forcings (Cox and Althaus, 2019). Two examples of EWFs
could be using radiative forcing [W/m2] directly versus Global Warming Potential
(GWP) [kg CO2eq/ kg emission emitted] (Cox and Althaus, 2019). Both measure
the change in radiative forcing, however, GWP does it specifically to a predetermined
time horizon (often 100 years), and is therefore often the preferred metric (Cox and
Althaus, 2019). This is also the standardised method to estimate climate forcing within
LCIA (Cox and Althaus, 2019). Climate metrics will be discussed further in Section 2.2.
Cox and Althaus, 2019 estimates EWF of AICl to 0.84 [kg CO2eq/ kg aviation CO2
emitted], with an uncertainty range of 0.3-2.3. The climate impact of persistent con-
trails is measured during the aircraft cruise phase, which is the time after stabilising
in the air and before descent. Some studies define cruise phase (> 950 m altitude) in
contrast to landing and take-off phase, which is below 950 m (Cox et al., 2018). Cox
and Althaus, 2019 results apply fuel consumption as the estimator of the climate change
impact, where consumption rate per flown km is assumed constant. However, the
climate impact of contrails differentiates from CO2 and other greenhouse gases, as the
resulting change in radiative forcing is very dependent on the atmospheric conditions
(Dischl et al., 2022). Fuel consumption does not consider the non-linear response to
atmospheric regional and temporal changes (Kärcher, 2018), leading to significant un-
certainties of how much contrails and AICl actually changes the radiative forcing when
GWP of contrails are measured as [kg CO2eq/ kg CO2 emitted] (Cox and Althaus, 2019).
For long term mitigation of aviation climate change impact, CO2 emissions is more
important than mitigating non-CO2 emission (Kärcher, 2018). However, the short life
time of AICl, allows quicker mitigation. To ensure proper mitigation, the knowledge gap
must be bridged, of how AICl interact with the atmospheric mechanics (Kärcher, 2018).
It is estimated that about 80% of radiative forcing (RF) from global contrails, can be
mitigated through additional costs of +0.1% for flight trajectory contrail optimisation
(Breakthrough Energy, 2023b).

1.4
Objective of Study
The aim of this study is to develop altitude-differentiated characterisation factors (CFs)
for contrails produced by subsonic aviation. These CFs are intended for application
in LCIA to evaluate climate impacts in kg CO2eq per km flown. The appropriate
methodology is discovered through two literature reviews, documenting state-of-the-art
contrail modelling and climate impact assessments (Section 2.2 and 2.3).
The results will go through sensitivity analysis (Section 4.2), uncertainties will be
disclosed (Section 4.3), and a case study will be conducted to compare this study’s
findings with existing literature and validate the findings (Section 4.4). In the case
study global climate impact from AICl will be estimated, based on a GWP for mean
flight altitude. Furthermore, it will be calculated for altitude differentiated GWPs, to
identify the potential influence globally. Finally the implications of the findings will
be disclosed, particular emphasis on the appropriate utilisation of aviation contrail
GWPs within the LCIA framework, and outlining potential future research directions.
Data is collected from several literature studies and datasets, which will be reviewed
(U. Schumann, 2012, Copernicus Climate Change Service, 2024).

Two main methodologies to characterise the climate impact from contrails are identified

(For details see Appendix’s A.4 A.2), which is a significant difference (Lund et al., 2017
Tasca et al., 2021). This exemplifies the importance of differentiated CFs.

decreased the mean square error to 0.24 from 1.18., and even further down to 0.19
when taking mean latitude into account, for flights routes of 1000-7000 km (Dahlmann
et al., 2023). No literature included LCIA climate change CFs with temporal or vertical
differentiations.

2.2.3
Limitations and Opportunities for Development
The two main limitations of the LCIA of contrail cirrus, is that:
1) Contrails differentiates from regular greenhouse gas emissions, as the climate impact
is regional dependent, due to atmospheric properties. However vertical differentia-
tion is not accounted for yet, which is otherwise recommended (Lund et al., 2017).
Studies exist that proves the vertical influence, however, not in alignment with the
LCIA-framework (Rädel and Shine, 2008). Currently no studies have covered more
than one differentiation at once within the same set of CFs, which is a limitation for
estimating the climate impact of contrails (Table 4).
2) While there exist several accepted metrics for measuring climate change impacts,
no single metric or time horizon is universally optimal for all applications (D. S. Lee
et al., 2021).
Atmospheric conditions
The climate impact of contrails differentiates from CO2 and other greenhouse gases, as
the radiative forcing is very dependent on the atmospheric conditions (Dischl et al.,
2022). Contrails are not formed equally often, if at all, in all regions and altitudes.
In 2011 a study shows that altitudes below 500hPa in northern Europe (53oN) the
ambient temperature is too high for contrails to be formed (Gierens and Dilger, 2013).
At 350 hPa most of Europe have atmospheric conditions which satisfy for contrail
formation. At 400 hPa it is only Northern Europe (further north than 53oN) where
conditions are satisfied. Thus regional differences alone is not the sole deciding factor for
contrail formation, as the altitude and ambient temperature is important too (Gierens
and Dilger, 2013). Current contrail cirrus GWPs should not be applied for aviation
deviating from standard cruise routes, altitudes, nor if the climate changes, or if tech-
nologies advances changing the propulsive efficiency (Lund et al., 2017). The vertical
sensitivity of aviation has yet to be estimated within the LCIA framework, i.e. altitude
differentiated GWPs. Based on this literature review, addressing this knowledge gap
is recommended to accommodate climate impact assessments of aviation deviating
from standard cruise altitudes. Preferably as spatial and altitude differentiated CF
accommodating to the LCIA framework.
Metrics
Within LCIA the current accepted metric is using the global warming potential (GWP)
(Fuglestvedt et al., 2010), which arrives from the Kyoto protocol. However, it is
discussed within the scientific community if it is a sufficient metric, as GWP does not
address any direct climate change parameter such as temperature change. Furthermore,
GWP is not able to address regional and temporal independent short-lived emissions
(Fuglestvedt et al., 2010). Another metric used in four of the eight identified LCIA
studies is Global Temperature Change Potential (GTP) (Fuglestvedt et al., 2010 Lund
et al., 2017, D. S. Lee et al., 2021, Tasca et al., 2021). GTP has the advantage that it
identifies the physical change in temperature, which illustrates the relative importance
differently. However, GTP also entails larger uncertainty than GWP, because it is an
end point metric in contrast to GWP, which describes the effect and is more difficult
to correctly asses. GWP is a better representation of the cause (quantification of

greenhouse gasses) and not the effect (Fuglestvedt et al., 2010).
Other metrics similar to GTP exists, such as average temperature response (ATR),
which expresses the near-surface average temperature change over a given time-frame
within an atmosphere-ocean system (Dahlmann et al., 2023), or Absolute Regional
Temperature Change potential (ARTP) which entails four latitude bands (90 −28oS,
28−28oN, 28−60oN and 60−90oN), allowing for regionalised differentiation of climate
sensitivities to pulse emissions. The sensitivities are based on coupled atmosphere-
ocean climate model simulations (Lund et al., 2017). In accordance with Fuglestvedt
et al., 2010 Lund et al., 2017, D. S. Lee et al., 2021, and Tasca et al., 2021 the most
appropriate method appears to include GWP as well as GTP, as GWP is commonly
used within LCIA, however, GTP may be more appropriate to reflect the impact of
short-lived non-CO2 emissions from aviation, such as contrail cirrus.

The output is then used to calculate GWPs and energy forcing per person kilometre.

The default time step for integration is set to 30 minute (Shapiro et al., 2023) and

3.2
Data Input
3.2.1
Flight Data
Flight data was provided in a CoCip tutorial (03 CoCip-Output, 2024), consisting of a
total of 100 flights, with cumulative flight routes covering a distance of 536,016 km.
CoCiP generates a waypoint when atmospheric conditions facilitates production of
contrails, thus modelling contrails along the entire trajectory. For each waypoint and
associated contrail, CoCiP provided estimates for contrail length [m], width [m], and
lifetime [hh:min:sec]. The data covered a global scope, with a mix of continental and
intercontinental flights. All continents, except Antarctica, were represented. Notable
destinations included the United States, Canada, Hong Kong, Singapore, Australia,
Germany, Czech Republic, Malaysia, United Arab Emirates, India, Taiwan, Mexico,
Peru, among others. All flight data were collected for the 1st of January, 2020 within an
18-hour time frame. Flight durations varied from 1.33 to 14.83 hours. Given the tutorial
nature of the dataset, no preprocessing of the flight data was required. Flight data
included specifications such as flight ID, air craft type, wing span [m], air pressure [Pa],
altitude [feet] and [m], pressure level [hPa], longitude, latitude, duration, fuel type, fuel
distance [kg fuel/m], fuel calorific value [J/kg fuel] and flight distance [m]. Furthermore,
emission data for each flight is provided with emission indexes [kg emission/kg fuel] for
CO2, H2O, OC, SO2, sulphates, hydrogen content [%].

3.4.1
Regional Differentiation
CoCiP can estimate both global and regional changes in RF (U. Schumann, 2012, Teoh
et al., 2023), as long as sufficient data is provided. For latitudes > 80o and latitudes
<-80o, data must be treated differently, calculated in Cartesian Coordinates, due to
polar singularities (U. Schumann, 2012). Similarly, data should be treated cautiously at
longitudes of ±180o, for flights crossing the date line. This is automatically accounted
for in CoCiP (U. Schumann, 2012).
Short term emissions will have different impacts, depending on the regional and tempo-
ral differences, because of their short life time (P. Forster et al., 2007). Characterisation
factors with regional differentiation have already been developed (Lund et al., 2017,
Dahlmann et al., 2016), with a ranging of 0.4-1.0 CO2eq/kg aviation CO2 (CO2eq/kg
CO2 emitted from the aircrafts) over East Asia (EAS) and South America and Africa
(SAF), respectively (Lund et al., 2017). The biggest annual mean contrail net RF,
however, is observed over Europe (876 mW/m2) and the US (414 mW/m2). Two regions
with smallest influence in mean RF from contrails are East Asia (64 mW/m2) and
China (62 mW/m2) (Teoh et al., 2023). The low values estimated over Asia is among
other things due to lower flight altitudes and atmospheric dynamics of a global air cell
(named the Hadley cell) decreasing the amount of high ISSR areas (Teoh et al., 2023).
Furthermore, regional differences are also found for similar changes in cruise altitude.
The radiative forcing from contrail cirrus increases with cruise altitude in southern
part of North America and the tropics, but decreases with altitude in the mid latitudes
(Matthes et al., 2021).
Consequently, altitude differentiated characterisation factors should be regionalised too,
as globally averaged altitude differentiation may be insufficient. Regional RF values
can be estimated similarly to global RF, but the energy forcing should be averaged over
the studied area and not global surface area (Teoh et al., 2023). Due to the limited
access to flight data, a combined set of altitude differentiated regionalised GWPs was
not conducted. In this study average GWP100 and EF will be estimated as global
averages
3.4.2
Temporal Differentiation
Temporal differentiated GWPs has not yet been estimated (Section 2.2), however
the temporal variations have been quantified for annual mean net RF (Stuber and
Forster, 2007). Temporal differentiations can be split up to seasonal and day/night
differentiations, both following diurnal cycles.
Day and Night Variations:
63.4% of all flights are estimated to fly during local day time (36.6% local night time),
however, the impact contribution shows the opposite trend, with 60% of contrails
global annual mean forcing stemming from contrails persisting during local night time
(18:00-6:00) (Stuber and Forster, 2007). Consequently, if not accounted for when
utilising contrail cirrus GWPs, diurnal variations may lead to overestimation of global
mean contrail RF impacts by 10-30% (Marquart et al., 2003, Stuber and Forster, 2007).
The imbalance is caused by lack of incoming SW-radiation as illustrated in Figure 3
21 of 68
==PAGE:30==
Master Thesis
February 18, 2024
(Section 1.2) (Marquart et al., 2003, Stuber and Forster, 2007), where the reflection of
incoming SW-radiation off-sets about 62-67% of the additional accumulated forcing,
depending on overcast or clear sky conditions (Stuber and Forster, 2007). To account
for this variation, the data used to estimate the CFs should match the time a day,
intended of the study. In this study, it was not succeeded to split the data into subsets
of day and night, as the flights operating during night hours could not be reliably
identified. Therefore the CFs are estimated, as an average of both night and day
climate impacts, as both phases are represented within the utilised flight data.
Seasonal Variations:
The global peak contrail season is in the Northern Hemispheric (NH) Winter, in op-
position to peak flight season (Summer, NH) (Breakthrough Energy, 2023b, Stuber
and Forster, 2007), as optimal atmospheric conditions are the determining factor and
not travel activity. The magnitude of temporal differentiation is not uniform across
different regions (Yin et al., 2018, Stuber and Forster, 2007). However, optimal atmo-
spheric conditions for contrail cirrus formation is typically during winter with lower
temperatures. Whereas, the annual smallest global impact from contrails is in August,
which is NH summer time (Stuber and Forster, 2007). The Equator experiences less
regional differentiation than other regions, partly due to a more stable climate, but
also due to the contrast upward motion driven by the Hadley Cell. (Yin et al., 2018).
Consequently, the altitude with optimal conditions for contrail cirrus formation will be
dependent on both season and region (Yin et al., 2018).
Ideally the characterisation factors should be temporal differentiated for each region,
through seasonal specific data, and averaged over the length of the season (Further
described in Section 3.5.3). In CoCiP, the time stamp of the flight data has to have the
same time stamp as the meteorological data. Due to the limited access to flight data,
seasonal differentiation was not possible. Data for the 1st of January was available,
therefore this study is representative of global aviation during January. The findings
may be representative of winter forcings too, but with greater uncertainty.
3.5
Estimating Characterisation Factors for Aviation
The metric chosen for characterisation of contrails’ climate impact is global warming
potential (GWP), in accordance with IPCC 1990 and the Kyoto Protocol (P. Forster
et al., 2007). GWP represents the warming potential of a given climate forcer compared
to a reference gas over a time horizon (P. Forster et al., 2007). The chosen reference
gas is CO2 in accordance with protocol (P. Forster et al., 2007), and the chosen time
horizon is 100 years (GWP100), as GWP100 is the standardised method for estimating
climate change impacts withing the IPCC framework (Penner et al., 1999). The chosen
metric for GWP100 is CO2eq/km flight distance, estimated in three steps: 1) finding
the energy forcing of contrails per flown kilometre (Section 3.5.1), 2) find the Absolute
Global Warming Potential (AGWP) of CO2 (Section 3.5.2), and 3) estimating the
global warming potential of contrail cirrus (Section 3.5.3). The values used in this
section are CoCiP model output, unless other is specified.

4.2.2
Flight Distance
A sensitivity analysis has been conducted on the determined flight distance of each
altitude level (Appendix B, 3.2) . Modifying the flight distance by ±10% yields an
opposite proportional increase and decrease in GWP100 per flown distance, respectively
(Table 10), making it a sensitive parameter. This outcome is to be expected, considering
that GWP100 in this study is inherently expressed ’per flown km’.
The flight distance estimated (Table 8), includes the entire routes, and not only the
cruise altitudes. About 75% of fuel burn, happens above 7 km altitude (Wilkerson
et al., 2010). The fuel flow, is higher during the climb than cruise phase (Chati and
Balakrishnan, 2014), therefore less than 25% of the flight distance, should happen
during climb and descent below 7 km. This should be enough to explain the difference
between GWP100main result and GWP100Lee. It is recommended for future studies to
only include flight distance above 7 km, which is where contrails mainly form (Section
1.2.1), to increase the precision of contrail cirrus’ GWP used within LCIA.

Choice of reference year for annual mean net RF
The ratio of ASK to departure, which otherwise were assumed constant, changes with
the reference year used to estimate EFmean (Equation 3), as exemplified by four years:
222 seat/departure (2021), 224 seat/departure (2020), 275 seat/departure (2019), 268
seats/departure (2018) (Airlines for America, 2023). In 2020 and 2021 many flights
were cancelled due to Covid-19, resulting in decreased aviation activity (U. Schumann
et al., 2021). This may explain the ASK anomalies 2020 and 2021. The sensitivity to a
change in baseline year is presented in Table 11, indicating that EFmean would change
with about [-19:1]%, depending on reference year. EFmean is within past findings (
Teoh and Stettler, 2022) independent of the four exemplified reference years, and does
therefore not invalidate the results. However, EFmean is very sensitive to the amount of
seats per departure. If the flight data, does not cover all annual flight, and the aircraft
fleet distribution is unknown, it is recommended to use an averaged reference years.
2020 and 2021 should be included in the average, depending on whether the leisure
industry still is affected by COVID-19. If they are not, it is recommended to exclude
COVID-19 years from the averaged reference.

4.3
Uncertainty
As discovered in Section 2.3.3, Table 7, there are many model parameters, which could
potential lead to uncertainty modelling results. In this section the most significant
uncertainties will be discussed, along with what influence they impose for the validity
of the results. Seasonal uncertainties will be discovered (Section 4.3.1), followed by
regional uncertainties (Section 4.3.2). Data sample size and quality will be discussed
in three steps (Section 4.3.3), and finally uncertainties related to CoCiP settings with
respect to chosen time step, and other out of scope uncertainties related to modelling
contrail cirrus (Section 4.3.4).
4.3.1
Seasonal
About half of contrails’ annual average climate forcing in 2019-2021, were emitted,
over 110-121 days (Teoh et al., 2023). Hence, there is a great uncertainty related to
this study’s methodology, as it relies on data for one day only (the 1st of January
2020). Seasonal variations should be less significant for global than regional coverage.
However, because of higher flight density in the Northern Hemisphere than the Southern
Hemisphere (Southern Hemisphere) (Teoh et al., 2023,Gössling and Humpe, 2020), it
is not recommended to be dismissed. January 2020 was a record warm month, with
+0.77oC compared to mean January temperatures from 1981-2010 Copernicus, 2020.
This is significant compared to annual global average temperatures varies from about
12.5-16.5oC (Copernicus, 2023). When comparing a map of contrail cirrus annual
mean net RF for 2019 (Teoh et al., 2023) with surface air temperature anomalies
from 2020 (Copernicus, 2020), it is evident that there is an overlap of where the high-
est temperature anomalies were measured and where the highest impact from cirrus
contrails were estimated. About 80% of all aviation RPK is found over Asia-Pacific,
Europe, and North America (32.5% , 22.7% , and 25.6% respectively for 2018 data)
(Gössling and Humpe, 2020) Over Europe the average temperature were significantly
high +3.1oC compared to mean January temperatures from 1981-2010. An average
cooling were found over North America, while Asia Pacific was mainly warmer than
reference years (Copernicus, 2020). These factors may explain why GPWmain result is
lower than GWP100Lee, even though the study is performed on a winter day, which

4.3.3
Data
Sample Size of Flight Data
The flight data sample size is a fraction (10−4 −10−6) of other existing studies of about
2-100 million unique flights (Teoh et al., 2022 and Teoh et al., 2023) (Appendix B,
Section 4.1). Considering all persistent contrails stems from 20% of all flights, of which
10% (2% in total) are responsible for 80% of the energy forcing from contrails Teoh et al.,
2023, the small sample size contributes significantly to uncertainty, because GWP100
may vary by several percentage points per any flight producing high-impact-contrails
added or subtracted from the dataset.
CFs for altitude level 5 and 4 entail significant uncertainties, given they are based on
only one and eight flights, respectively. Consequently, additional modelling with a
larger flight sample is recommended before drawing conclusions regarding the climate
impact from contrails at the altitude range of 300-400 hPa. Especially because current
non-CO2 forcings are associated with great uncertainties, about eight times higher than
for CO2 emissions, and associated with low confidence levels .
The remaining results from Table 8 aligning well with existing findings (D. S. Lee et al.,
2021, Rädel and Shine, 2008), despite small flight sample size. The data is sourced from
CoCiP tutorials, which have increased the representativity of the flight data, compared
to a random sample of 100 flights. This can be determined, since all 100 flights lead to
persistent contrails, which otherwise would be statistically unlikely according to Teoh
et al., 2023’s findings. As the order of magnitude is correct, the data uncertainty is
not enough to dismiss the methodology of the findings, however, the results should be
used with caution, and the sample size on it’s own leads to high uncertainty in the
estimated CFs.
Meteorologic Data Quality
For this study ERA5-reanalysis data is used. Other studies using the CoCiP model,
however, typically uses ERA5 resolution of high resolution also known as Atmospheric
Model high resolution 10-day forecast (HERAS) (Teoh et al., 2022), together with
10-ensemble reanalysis of ECMWF data. Both data set are based on atmospheric data
from ECMWF, and share many similarities. A crucial difference is that HRES has a
vertical resolution of 1000 feet (about 300 metres), where ERA5 only has a vertical
resolution of 4000 feet (about 1200 meetres) (Breakthrough Energy, 2023b). Thus it is
not guaranteed that altitude differentiated atmospheric conditions of ERA5-reanalysis
data aligns with the altitude bands, chosen for this study. This is an uncertainty, which
should be accounted for in future studies of altitude differentiated contrail climate
impact, to improve the quality of the results. Furthermore ERA5 has been found to
overpredict the average lifetime of contrails, compared to HERAS. In the North Atlantic
it was overestimated by 7.7%. This leads to uncertainty in impact from contrails that
persist into the night, and vice versa. As a result ERA5 attributes 80% of total energy
forcings to a smaller percentage of flights compared to HERAS (Teoh et al., 2022).
This is another source of overestimated results, increasing the uncertainty of this study.

Data Descriptions
When converting altitude units between metres and hPa, the data is organised uni-
formly. This implies that only one of the parameters was originally provided, as
pressure levels vary with altitude due to high and low-pressure areas (Swiss federal
authorities, 2024). The specific method for gathering flight data is not specified, and
both values exist within the flight dataset prior to any data treatment. Flights may
have been ascribed incorrect altitude bands due to averaged conversions from metres
to hPa. This introduces uncertainty concerning altitude-differentiated results, but not
averaged GWP100s. The absence of detailed data descriptions hampers uncertainty
evaluations, and can not be determined definitively. The lack of flight descriptions
contains a general uncertainty, as other similar unknown uncertainties may exists as well.
4.3.4
CoCiP
Time Step
CoCiP is a relative simple model, made to ensure relative short computation times
(U. Schumann, 2012). The tutorial default time step of 5 minutes were used. The
uncertainty was investigated by rerunning the main results, where the CoCiP inte-
gers over time step equals to 1 minute and 10 minutes, respectively (Appendix A).
GWP100main result and GWP100test decreased with 0.2% (Appendix B.4.2), indepen-
dently of increasing or decreasing time step to 10 and 1 minute, respectively. Compared
to other parameter uncertainties this appears insignificant (Table 7). This aligns with
existing theory, that contrails persisting less than 10 minutes are associated with little
to no impact Kärcher, 2018).
Other Known Uncertainties, Out of Scope
This study adopted the methodology from Teoh et al., 2023, who utilised CoCiP to
model global climate impact and GWP. They found a high uncertainty related to the
chosen value of nvPM soot number emission index (nvPM EIn) , with a factor 5.5
(Appendix B.4.3) when changed from nvPM EIn= 1014 per kg fuel to nvPM EIn = 1015
per kg fuel Teoh et al., 2023 . nvPM EIn is set to the default value of 1015 per kg fuel,
and does not change with altitude, besides from the fuel consumption provided in the
flight data (Breakthrough Energy, 2023b). Impact from air pollutants are in general
related to a low confidence level (D. S. Lee et al., 2021). Therefore it is recommended
for future studies of altitude differentiated contrail impacts, to investigate if nvPM EIn
requires further altitude dependence, besides indirect variations due to changed fuel
consumption. Because nvPM EIn is a sensitive parameter for contrail formation, this
is also a recommended approach to reduce climate impact from aviation, by reducing
nvPM EIn (D. Lee et al., 2020).

4.5
Overall Confidence of Methodology and Findings
In this section, a synthesis of sensitivity and uncertainty, is presented to evaluate the
validity and the robustness of methodologies and findings of GWP100s and EFmean.
The methodologies are evaluated as if ideal data was available. Whereas the CFs are
estimated, based on the confidence-level of each parameter. The confidence levels are:
• High: With no or insignificant uncertainties.
• Good: With some anomalies, resulting in minor uncertainties
• Medium: The input or methodology, is uncertain, but the overall results still
align existing literature.
• Low: The data or methodology should be greatly improved, and results are of a
different order of magnitude than existing literature.
• Poor: The data or methodology are too uncertain, to identify where incoherence
is stemming from.
The small data samples of level 4 and 5 results, would be classified as poor sample size,
and they are automatically attributed no confidence. No further confidence evaluation
is performed on these levels. Table 15 is only filled out for the remaining three altitude
levels (1, 2, and 3). All confidence levels can be found in the bottom of Table 15. The
methodologies of EF and GWP, have been attributed two confidence levels each. The
low ratings, are the confidence level of the altitude differentiated results (EF (level 1 -3)
= medium, and GWP (level 1 -3) = good). The high ratings, are the confidence level
of the non-altitude differentiated results (EF = good, and GWP = high) (Table 15).

4.6
Implications
4.6.1
Main Findings and Application of Characterisation Factors
GWP100main result and GWP100test have been conducted on the entire flight routes,
from take-off to landing, with mean cruise altitude of 170-310 hPa, and meteorological
data included for 550-150 hPa (Figure 6). Theoretically, the GWP100s are repre-
sentative for the entire distance travelled (climb, cruise and descent), thus including
altitudes 1014-150 hPa. However, for practical applications they are recommended
to employ on aircraft fleets with mean flight altitude of 150-400 hPa, as no contrail
formation is detected outside these altitude bands. The order of magnitude has been
validated with good-confidence, about 25% below existing scientific findings (Section
4.4.1, Fuglestvedt et al., 2010). Altitude differentiated GWPs is estimated for the first
time. The findings align with existing theory of peak energy forcing (Section 1.2.3,
Rädel and Shine, 2008, U. O. Schumann, 1996). However, they are estimated based
on very small data samples, and solely on meteorological data from January. Due
to the significance of seasonal variations, elaborated in Section 4.3.1, the GWP100s
are only representative of January, and to a smaller degree representative of winter
emissions. For altitude levels 4 and 5, the data sample is too small to be conclusive (no
confidence). Level 1, 2, and 3 are still too small to be robust, however as they align
with existing literature, they are estimated with low confidence, and the method used
is medium-good confidence.
80% of contrail cirrus climate impact can be mitigated, by rerouting mere 2% of all
flights (Teoh et al., 2023), leading to fuel cost increases of about 0.1% (Breakthrough
Energy, 2023a. However, due to contrails’ strong dependency on daily meteorological

conditions, this mitigation can not be achieved through traditional LCIA methodology
of estimating flight routes climate impact, based on average regional, temporal and
altitude characterisations. Instead contrail mitigation requires meteorological forecasts
and modelling, to estimate where and which high ISSR areas to be avoided (Roosen-
brand et al., 2023). This may explain the limited amount of GWP estimated for
contrail cirrus, compared to the extensive exploration of global mean net radiative
forcing (RF) attributable to contrails over the past few decades (Section 2.2). It should
be further investigated, what and if an ideal GWP metric for contrail cirrus climate
impacts is possible. A more appropriate GWP metric may be based on flown km
within in specified ISSR areas, to accommodate practical use, withing aviation route
planning, ideally conducted on meteorological forecasts. Teoh et al., 2023 presents
regional differentiated EF as [J/contrail length], a similar unit may be ideal such as a
GWP [CO2eq/contrail length]. These characterisations may be more functional within
LCIA, as they can be employed for aviation in regions with frequent occurrence of high
ISSR areas, which are investigated by Roosenbrand et al., 2023.
Another challenge related to non-CO2 forcings, is that not all is covered by the Kyoto
Protocol (D. S. Lee et al., 2021), thus officially they should not be included in LCIA
evaluations. However, excluding non-CO2 emissions completely from LCIA climate
estimations, would lead to a significantly underestimated climate impact of aviation
(as described in Section 1.1, and Section 13). Future studies should therefore have a
focus on deepening the understanding of non-CO2-emission climate impacts for not
only aviation, but from all types of transportation, to ensure a coherent framework,
similar to that of greenhouse gases included in the Kyoto protocol.
4.6.2
Recommendations for Future Research
The greatest uncertainties related to estimating CFs of contrail cirrus, which should be
corrected for in the future is:
• Flight data sample size, should be greater, and include global seasonal represen-
tation, with complementary meteorological data.
• Weather data from HERA5 is preferred choice compared to ERA5, as the use of
ERA5 may have lead to uncertainty of contrail lifetime of about 8%.
• Altitude differentiated GWP should be regional differentiated too.
• The aircraft fleet composition assumption, leads to underestimated number of
seats per departure. And therefore the EFmean is overestimated. The ASK
should therefore be based on actual fleet compositions, and not annual average
for smaller flight samples.
• Estimate regionalised altitude differentiated GWP for contrail cirrus based on
Teoh et al., 2023, with similar unit [CO2eq/contrail length].
Furthermore, it can be argued that it is an overall problem and uncertainty within the
LCIA framework, that impacts such as contrail cirrus from aviation is not included.
Contrail Cirrus alone potentially is resulting in more than 1% of global anthropogenic
climate change impact. If one single emission type from one singular activity alone, can

Conclusion
Through two extensive literature reviews the methodology is chosen for assessing
contrails’ Global Warming Potential (GWPs) over a 100-year time horizon (GWP100),
and altitude-differentiated GWP100s. Both methodologies have been validated with
high and good confidence, respectively. The GWP100 of mean cruise altitude serve as
a valuable tool to underscore global aviation climate impact from contrails, and the
imperative need for prompt mitigation strategies. Altitude-differentiated GWP100s
provides additional nuanced insights to the complexity of estimating contrail cirrus’
climate impacts.
GWP100s for flights within the altitude range of 150-400 hPa are estimated to 8.79-10
kg CO2eq per flown kilometre ([kg CO2eq/flown km]) (with good confidence). The case
study revealed that these are within likely ranges, compared to existing literature. The
mean contrail cirrus radiative forcing (EFmean) is estimated to 1.92×105 [joule/person
metre] (with good confidence), falling within the lower range of values reported in
previous studies. The methodologies employed to estimated EF forcings is validated
with medium-good confidence. Additionally, altitude differentiated characterisation
factors is calculated for five altitude levels, and presented accordingly: Altitude level:
GWP100 (with medium confidence), EF (with low confidence).
1. Level 1 (150-200 hPa): 7.71 [kg CO2eq/flown km], 1.51 ·105 [J/pm]
2. Level 2 (200-250 hPa): 14.27 [kg CO2eq/flown km], 2.80 ·105 [J/pm]
3. Level 3 (250-300 hPa): 7.48 [kg CO2eq/flown km], 1.47 ·105 [J/pm]
The characterisations of altitude levels 4 (300-350 hPa) and 5 (350-400 hPa) were
based on an insufficient sample size consisting of eight and one flight trips, respectively,
leading to no confidence in results. The case study is conducted in two parts: The
first part, employing GWP100main result, estimated that contrail cirrus contributed to a
global impact of 296-337 Tg CO2eq, which is approximately 1% of global anthropogenic
CO2 emissions, aligning well with existing literature. The second part, employing
EFmean, overestimated global contrail cirrus climate impacts, primarily due to flawed
assumptions regarding flight distribution, leading to underestimated seat capacities and
overestimation of EFmean. To increase robustness and address inherent uncertainties
arising from limited data, future studies should employ datasets that covers seasonal,
diurnal, and global variations. Ideally, these datasets should cover multiple years to
mitigate uncertainties associated with temperature anomalies.
In conclusion, the field of contrail modelling is experiencing rapid growth; however,
significant knowledge gaps persist. Addressing these gaps is crucial for enhancing the
Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA) framework concerning aviation climate impact,
particularly with regard to contrails. Key areas requiring further investigation include:
1) Formulating a comprehensive set of characterisation factors for LCIA that concur-
rently incorporate altitude and regional differentiations. 2) Conducting additional
investigations to assert a sufficient metric applicable to individual flight routes.

Recommendations
Considering the uncertainties and sensitivities observed in the study, the following
recommendations are proposed for future studies:
• In the future, an optimal strategy should entail development of a comprehen-
sive array of LCIA characterisation factors that incorporates regional, altitude,
seasonal, daily, and aircraft-specific differentiations.
• Employ bigger flight data quantity for GWP and EF estimations of contrail
cirrus.
• Employ meteorological data of higher quality, such as HERA5.
• Ensure low uncertainty within distance flown and seat capacity, this could be
done through differentiations based on short-, medium-, and long-haul flights.
• Further research into which metric is best suited to facilitate inclusion of contrail
cirrus into the LCIA framework, with a minimal amount of inherent uncertainties.
• Future studies should focus on deepening the understanding of non-CO2 emissions’
climate impact for all transportation modes to ensure a coherent framework,
supplementary to the Kyoto Protocol for greenhouse gas estimations.
