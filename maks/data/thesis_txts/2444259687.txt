Abstract
In recent years deep learning models, that are often perceived as black-box methods,
have started to explore the spectrum between accuracy and interpretability. To ex-
plain the predictions of convolutional neural networks, different explanation methods
enable the networks to learn to perform object localization by visualizing the predicted
class score on any image, highlighting the discriminative object elements identified by
the convolutional neural network, as a heatmap. This thesis provides an evaluation
of four such explanation methods. The four examined explanation methods are the
class activation mappings Grad-CAM and Modified Grad-CAM, and the pixel-space
gradient methods Sensitivity and Guided Backpropagation.
By applying distortions to images, it is examined how robust convolutional neural
networks and the considered explanation methods are to images of lower quality.
Furthermore, the explanation method heatmaps are compared to heatmaps gener-
ated from human gaze data, obtained from eye-tracking experiments, to examine the
interpretability of the explanation methods.
The results show that when comparing explanation method heatmaps for original and
distorted images, by using the Euclidean distance as a similarity measure, the class ac-
tivation mapping methods significantly outperform the pixel-space gradient methods.
However, when considering the Pearson correlation coefficient, Guided Backpropaga-
tion explanations show to be most robust to images of lower quality. Furthermore,
when analysing the explanation method interpretability, Guided Backpropagation
heatmaps yield the overall best similarity with human gaze heatmaps in terms of
both Euclidean distance and Pearson correlation coefficient.
Finally, this thesis demonstrates that applying Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram
Equalization to explanation method and gaze heatmaps improves similarities between
explanation method and gaze heatmaps, which may advance the interpretability of
heatmap explanations.

Resumé
I løbet af de senere år er deep learning modeller, som ofte er opfattet som black-box
metoder, begyndt at udforske spektrummet mellem præcision og fortolkelighed. For
at forklare forudsigelserne af et convolutional neuralt netværk kan forskellige forklar-
ingsmetoder gøre netværket i stand til at udføre objektlokalisering ved at visualisere
den forudsagte klasse for ethvert billede og fremhæve de diskriminative objektele-
menter, det neurale netværk identificerer, som et heatmap. Denne afhandling giver
en evaluering af fire forklaringsmethoder. De fire forklaringsmetoder, der bliver eval-
ueret, er class activation mapping metoderne Grad-CAM og Modified Grad-CAM,
samt pixel-space gradient metoderne Sensitivity og Guided Backpropagation.
Ved at tilføre billederne støj bliver det undersøgt hvor robuste convolutional neurale
netværk og de undersøgte forklaringsmetoder er overfor billeder af lavere kvalitet.
For at undersøge fortolkeligheden af forklaringsmetodernes heatmaps, vil de deru-
dover blive sammenlignet med heatmaps genereret fra eye-tracking data, indsamlet i
et eksperiment med forsøgspersoner.
To lighedsmål er blevet brugt til at evaluere forklaringsmetodernes robusthed og deres
lighed med eye-tracking data fra mennesker. Resultaterne viser, at når der ses på den
Euklidiske distance, så er class activation mapping metoderne mere robuste over for
støj i billeder end pixel-space gradient metoderne. Når Pearsons korrelationskoeffi-
cient bliver brugt som lighedsmål, ses det derimod at Guided Backpropagation er
mest robust overfor støj i billeder. Når fortolkeligheden analyseres ses det, at Guided
Backpropagation heatmaps overordnet set er mest ens med heatmaps genereret på
baggrund eye-tracking data fra mennesker. Guided Backpropagation er mest ens både
når der ses på den Euklidiske distance og Pearsons korrelationskoefficient.
Sidst, men ikke mindst demonstrerer denne afhandling at Contrast Limited Adap-
tive Histogram Equalization kan benyttes til at forbedre lighederne mellem forklar-
ingsmetodernes heatmaps og heatmaps genereret på baggrund af eye-tracking data
for mennesker, hvilket muligvis kan øge fortolkeligheden af heatmap forklaringerne.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Advanced machine learning and deep learning models have recently allowed for ex-
traordinary performance in different computer vision tasks such as object detection
[21], speech recognition [12] and image classification [30]. As the training methodolo-
gies for a Deep Neural Network (DNN) have improved, along with increasing compu-
tational efficiency [40], DNNs are able achieve record performance. Recently intro-
duced DNNs that have shown state-of-the-art performance in several tasks contain
more than 200 layers [24]. Consequently, deep learning models have started to ex-
plore the spectrum between accuracy and interpretability [43].
While the performance of these models in many cases is superior to simpler ones, the
simpler models are more well-defined and understandable. This lack of transparency
and decomposability into recognizable components is a drawback of the more complex
models. For instance in deep learning, models are perceived as black-box methods.
They pursue an approximation of a function, which transforms a given input into a
desired output, without any knowledge of the function itself [9].
It is reasonable to assume that if users do not trust the predictions of a model suffi-
ciently, they will not use it. To build trust in these advanced models, such that they
can be integrated into everyday lives, it is important to understand how and why
their decisions are made. Hence, the importance of model explanations and inter-
pretability is increasing. This is especially true for tasks that involve risk, including
but not limited to automated driving systems and medical decision support.
Generally speaking, interpretability and explanation of machine learning models are
beneficial at three different levels [43]. First, when the models are not ready to be
implemented, model explanations can aid model development by identifying when
and why the models fail. Secondly, when models are reliably deployable, the aim
of model explanations and interpretability is to establish user confidence. Lastly, in
cases where machine learning models are significantly stronger than humans, the goal
of model explanations is in aiding humans understand the models and learn how to
make better decisions [43].

1.1
Related Work
In their work from 2016, Riberio et al. [39] argued that trust is crucial for effective
human interaction with machine learning systems, and that explaining individual pre-
dictions is important in assessing trust. They proposed Local Interpretable Model-
agnostic Explanations (LIME), a method that is able to explain the predictions of any
machine learning model by learning a simpler interpretable model locally around the
prediction such as sparse linear models. Furthermore, they conducted experiments
which illustrated that explanations are useful for several models in trust-related tasks
in text and image domains for both expert and non-expert users.
To explain the predictions of a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN), Zhou et al.
[61] proposed the Class Activation Mapping (CAM) technique using global average
pooling. This method enables classification-trained CNNs to learn to perform object
localization by visualizing the predicted class score on any image, highlighting the dis-
criminative object elements identified by the CNN as a heatmap. The CAM method
requires a CNN structure without fully-connected layers, as this enables identification
of important image regions by projecting back the weights of the output layer to the
convolutional feature maps. This limitation is a drawback in practice, since removing
fully-connected layers and replacing them with global average pooling layers leads
to a drop in classification accuracy [61]. To overcome this constraint, Selvaraju et
al. [43] introduced Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping (Grad-CAM), which
uses the gradients of a target class flowing into the final convolutional layer to pro-
duce a localization map highlighting the important parts of an image for prediction
of that specific class. As such, Grad-CAM is a generalization of CAM and is applica-
ble to a broader range of CNN families, while simultaneously avoiding the accuracy
vs. interpretability trade-off associated with the CAM method [43]. Building on the
Grad-CAM method, Chattopadhyay et al. [9] recently proposed Gradient-weighted
Class Activation Mapping++ (Grad-CAM++), which uses a weighted combination
of the positive partial derivatives of the last convolutional layer feature maps with
respect to a specific class score to generate better visual explanations. While pro-
viding better visual explanations than Grad-CAM, Grad-CAM++ also outperforms
other state-of-the-art models with regards to detecting occurences of multiple objects
in a single image [9]. A brief overview of the three methods, CAM, Grad-CAM and
Grad-CAM++ is depicted in Figure 1.1. The fundamental assumption of all three
CAM methods is that the Class Score (Y c) for Class (c) can be computed as a lin-
ear combination of its global average pooled Last Convolutional Layer Feature Maps

1.2
Objectives
The aim of this thesis is to provide an evaluation of four existing explanation methods
for CNNs operating on image data. The explanation methods, that will be consid-
ered, are Grad-CAM [43], Sensitivity [46], Guided Backpropagation [49] and Modified
Grad-CAM [43] [49]. In real world settings, where such explanation methods could
be applied, images are not always of great quality. Thus, by applying distortions to
images, it will be examined how robust convolutional neural networks and the con-
sidered explanation methods are to images of lower quality.
It is reasonable to assume, that if the way convolutional networks perceive images,
which can be investigated and visualized using these explanation methods, is similar
to how humans perceive images, then the explanations are interpretable by humans.
Thus, to provide an understanding of the interpretability of the explanation methods,
eye-tracking experiments with human test subjects will be conducted. The explana-
tions produced by the four utilized explanation methods will then be be compared
to the eye-tracking data. In this manner, this thesis will also provide insight on and
compare how image distortions affect state-of-the-art explanation methods and the
visual perception of humans.
Hence, this thesis considers two overall objectives. The first objective is to examine
and evaluate the robustness of explanation methods for deep convolutional networks.
The second objective is to evaluate the interpretability of these explanation methods
by comparing them to human gaze data.

2.2
Convolutional Neural Networks
Networks widely used within object recognition and image classification are convolu-
tional neural networks as they are invariant to certain transformations of data [6, p.
267]. In the case of classifying an object in an image, each input image constitutes a
set of pixels and their respective RGB-values [16]. The desired output is a posterior
distribution over the amount of classes that are invariant under translations, scalings
and rotations [6, p. 267]. A factor to consider when modelling image data is the key
property of images, which is that nearby pixels are often more correlated than more
distant pixels. Several modern approaches to computer vision exploit this property
by extracting local features that are only dependant on a subregion of the image. In-
formations from these features can then be merged in later stages of data processing
to detect higher-order features that yield information about the entire image [6].
CNNs incorporate these notions through local receptive fields, weight sharing and
subsampling. Part of the structure of a simple convolutional network is depicted in
Figure 2.2.
In convolutional layers, units are organized into planes called feature
Figure 2.2: Illustration of parts of a simple convolutional neural network showing
an input layer followed by a convolutional layer followed by a layer of subsampling
units. The convolutional layer illustrates an example of a feature map [6, p. 268].
maps. Each unit in a feature map takes input from only a small subregion of the
image and all of the units in a single feature map are constrained to share the same
weight w. An example could be a feature map consisting of 100 units in a (10 × 10)
grid, with each unit taking inputs from a (5 × 5) pixel patch of the input image. The
whole feature map then has 25 adjustable weight parameters and one adjustable bias
==PAGE:26==
10
2 Theory
parameter [6, p. 268]. This means, that if the units are thought of as feature detectors,
then all of the units in a feature map detect the same patterns in different parts of an
image. Because of the weight sharing, the evaluation of the activations of the units
is equivalent to a convolution of the image pixel itensities with a kernel composed of
the weight parameters [6]. If the input image is shifted, the activations of the feature
map will be shifted by the same amount, but will otherwise be unchanged. This pro-
vides the basis for the invariance of the network outputs to translations, scalings and
transformations of the input image. More often than not, each convolutional layer
will consists of multiple feature maps with their own associated weights and biases,
as this allows for detection of multiple features which is essential when building a
strong model [6, p. 269].
Outputs of a convolutional layer typically form the inputs to a subsampling layer.
For each feature map in the convolutional layer, the subsampling layer has a plane of
units, where each unit takes input from a small receptive field in the corresponding
feature map of the convolutional layer [6]. For instance, each subsampling unit in a
subsampling layer could take inputs from a (2×2) region in the corresponding feature
map and compute the average of those inputs, multiply the result with an adaptive
weight, add the corresponding bias and then transform the result using a nonlinear
activation function [6].
In practical CNN architecture there are often several pairs of convolutional and sub-
sampling layers. The final layer of the network is typically a fully-connected layer
with a softmax output nonlinearity, when the task is multiclass classification. The
entire network is trained by error minimization using backpropagation to evaluate
the gradient of the loss function [25]. Due to the local receptive fields, the number
of weights in the network is smaller than a regular, fully-connected neural network

2.3.1.3
Overlapping pooling
In CNNs, pooling layers summarize the outputs of neighbouring neurons in the same
kernel map. A pooling layer can be thought of as a grid of pooling units spaced s
pixels apart, each summarizing a neighborhood of size (t×t) centered at the location
of the pooling unit [30]. Usually, the neighborhoods summarized by adjacent pooling
do not overlap (t = s), but throughout the VGG16 network the authors set s < t
(s = 2, t = 3) thereby using overlapping pooling which yielded stronger network
performance in terms of top-1 and top-5 errors [30].
2.3.1.4
Network architecture
As illustrated by Figure 2.3 the VGG16 network contains eight layers with weights.
The first five layers of the network are convolutional, while the last three layers are
fully-connected. The output of the last fully-connected layer is fed to a 1000-way
softmax that produces a distribution over the 1000 classes [30]. As the network has
been trained on two GPUs, certain convolutional layer kernels are only connected
to specific kernel maps in previous layers that reside on the same GPU [30]. The
neurons in the fully-connected layers are connected to every neuron in the previous
layer. The response-normalization introduced in Section 2.3.1.2 follow the first and
second convolutional layers and the max-pooling of the kind described in Section
2.3.1.3 follow the response-normalization as well as the fifth convolutional layer. The
ReLU nonlinearity is applied to the output of all the hidden layers.
The input to the VGG16 network is RGB-images of size (224 × 224 × 3). The first
convolutional layer filters the input image with 96 kernels of size (11 × 11 × 3) and a
stride of four pixels. The output of the first convolutional layer is response-normalized
and max-pooled before given as input to the second convolutional layer, which filters
it with 256 kernels of size (5 × 5 × 48). The following three convolutional layers are
connected to each other without any normalization or pooling layers. The third convo-

2.8
Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization
Explanation methods generated from the previously mentioned methods, may not be
optimized for visualization purposes and comparisons. A way to improve the contrast
for heatmap visualizations, is histogram equalization proposed by Shannon et al. [44].
The contrast can be enhanced by using the cumulative histogram of the heatmap as
a gray-level transform [62]. However, histogram equalization may cause an increase
in intensity of background pixels, resulting in mainly improving visibility of the noise
in the image.
The human eye adapt to local context of images, thus it is ideal to take local contrast
into account. Pizer et al. [37] proposed Adaptive Histogram Equalization (AHE) as
a way to filter out irrelevant pixel values. This method divides the image into a grid
of contextual regions, in which the contrast is computed. The size of the contextual
regions is found experimentally – usually regions with size (8 × 8) produce good re-
sults [62, p. 475]. Histogram equalization is computed in each of these contextual
regions – ignoring region boundaries by utilizing bilinear interpolation. However, in
each of the regions, noise in still an issue. to reduce this contrast, Pizer et al. [37]
furthermore proposed Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization (CLAHE).

2.11
Eye Movements
This thesis investigates similarities between human gaze and the explanation methods
outlined ealier in this chapter. These similarities are analysed to provide measure of
the explanation method interpretability. This section outlines a brief description of
the human eye and how to capture eye movements.
2.11.1
The human eye
The visual process of the human eye begins when light is converted to electrical signals
passed on to the visual cortex at the rear of the brain [4]. The center of the field of
view contains an area called the fovea. When looking, the center of what is projected
inside the fovea is called a gaze point. When a person is looking at a screen, the place
where the person looks is called the pixel coordinates.
2.11.2
Eye fixations
The human eye tends to fixate at some pair of pixel coordinates for a short period
of time, typically 200-300 milliseconds, but may vary between ten milliseconds and
several seconds. This is called Eye Fixations.
2.11.3
Eye-tracking
The process of capturing eye movements is called eye-tracking. Eye-tracking works
by using an eye-tracker, which utilizes a set of infrared Light-Emitting Diodes (LEDs)
and an infrared camera. The camera captures glints of the LEDs in the eye and the
pupil [4, p. 7]. By calibrating the eye-tracker to a certain person, and keeping the
infrared LEDs and the screen containing the object of interest steady, the position of
the pupil relative to the glints can be projected into gaze coordinates.
For more information about eye-tracking, the reader is referred to [4], [5] and [17] as
a more thorough description of the human eye is not the scope of this thesis.

3.2
Finetuning the VGG16 Network
The VGG16 network is finetuned to the dataset described in Section 3.1 to evaluate
the classification accuracy for regular and distorted images. It should be noted, that
original VGG16 network is still utilized to generate the aforementioned explanation
method heatmaps. The network contains a total nine layers including the input and
output layer as depicted in Figure 2.3. To finetune the network, the final softmax
layer, with the 1000 output classes, is omitted from the network, while keeping the
first eight layers.
These eight layers are frozen, such that they are not retrained
in the finetuned network. A new final dense softmax layer is added at the end of
the network, representing the output of the network for the 23 classes depicted in
Table 3.1. Finally, the network is retrained utilizing the Adam optimizer [28], with a
learning rate of 0.001 during a total of 20 epochs. The dataset is randomly split into
a training set consisting of 460 images, a validation set consisting of 115 images and
a test set consisting of 115 images.
The finetuned VGG16 network is implemented in Python using Keras [19].

3.5.2
Testing the effect of the subject to monitor distance
Since the human field of view is larger than just the gaze points of the human’s two
eyes [10], it is important to make sure that the subjects have to move their eyes
around the image to discover its important regions. If the entire image is captured
by the test subject’s visual field, the subject could potentially look at the center of
the image while still perceiving the entire image.
To address this issue, a pilot experiment is conducted for three test subjects. Initially,
for all three of the subjects the monitor is placed at the edge of the table (opposite
of the test subject), and a calibration of the subject’s eyes is performed. After the
initial calibration, the monitor is moved 10 centimeters closer to the test subject, and
the calibration is performed again.
For two out of the three test subjects, the Tobii Eye Tracker fails to calibrate when
the monitor and the eye tracker is moved closer to the subject. From this, it can be
concluded that it is not possible to move the setup closer to the subject, as it will likely
not be possible to gather data for all test subjects for both of the monitor distances.
Instead, to incorporate and challenge the human visual field, a pilot experiment is

Finetuned VGG16 performance
The performance of the finetuned VGG16 network for various levels of distortion

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
This chapter will outline, interpret and analyse the findings and conclusions that can
be made based on the results. The first objective in this thesis is to determine the
robustness of the utilized explanation methods to low quality images. The second
objective is to analyse the interpretability of the explanation methods by comparing
their heatmaps to human gaze heatmaps. First, the spreads of the explanation meth-
ods, computed for images with different kinds of applied distortions, are examined.
Secondly, the robustness of the explanation methods in relation to added distor-
tions is discussed, and the explanation method heatmaps are compared to the gaze
heatmaps for images with different distortions. Hereafter, it is evaluated whether
the application of CLAHE and histogram matching leads to improved results and
more significant conclusions. Lastly, the different measures and methods used are
evaluated and discussed.
5.1
Finetuned VGG16 Performance
The classification accuracies depicted in Figure 4.3 have been computed to examine
how robust the finetuned VGG16 network is, when the quality of images are reduced
by applying Gaussian blur and Gaussian noise. Due to the results obtained by the
authors of [15], the hypothesis is that the network is more susceptible to Gaussian
blur than to Gaussian noise.
From Figure 4.3, it is apparent that when applying a matching amount of Gaussian
blur and Gaussian noise to the images, as defined in Section 3.6.1, the finetuned
VGG16 network achieves a higher classification accuracy for the images with added
Gaussian noise. For instance, when applying Gaussian blur using kernel size (51×51)
and σB = 25, the network achieves a test accuracy of 40.9%, while it achieves a test
accuracy of 73.0% on the images with a matching amount of added Gaussian noise.
For the distorted images used in the eye-tracking experiment, the network’s perfor-
mance decreases rapidly. For Blur Settings 1 and 2, the finetuned VGG16 network
achieves test accuracies of 39.1% and 11.3%, respectively. For Noise Settings 1 and 2,
the finetuned VGG16 network achieves test accuracies 6.1% and 5.2%, respectively.
Especially the last two classification rates are only slighty better than a random guess,
which would obtain an accuracy of 1/23 = 4.3%. It should be noted that in terms
of the finetuned VGG16 performance, the classification accuracies for the distortion

that both human gaze and explanation method heatmaps are affected when one of
the four distortions are applied to the images.

5.5
Evaluation of Similarity Measures
Grad-CAM and Modified Grad-CAM are class activation mappings, while Guided
Backpropagation and Sensitivity are pixel-space gradient methods. The class activa-
tion mappings capture coarse class-discriminative regions, while pixel-space gradient
methods highlight the most discriminative pixels in the images. Thus, if the CAM
methods capture somewhat the same regions for image of distortion types, it will
result in a low Euclidean distance. On the other hand if one pixel-space gradient
method heatmap highlights an important pixel for the original image, and the corre-
sponding heatmap for a distorted image highlights a neighbouring pixel, there will
be a large Euclidean distance between these two pixel pairs. Hence, the Euclidean
distance may not be the best measure to evaluate heatmap similarities.

5.6.1
Increasing the classification accuracy for distorted images
A way to diminish the effect that incorrect classifications have on the produced
heatmaps is to increase the classification accuracy of the utilized network. The clas-
sification accuracy of the VGG16 network for the distorted images could possibly be
increased by finetuning the network to images with similar distortions applied. Fine-
tuning the VGG16 network to distorted images used could possibly lead to a decrease
in classification accuracy for the original images [15], thereby introducing a new com-
plication. An imageniable way around these issues would be to introduce a network
trained on both regular images and highly distorted images. However, the VGG16
network have been used in this thesis as it exhibits the best performance in terms of
classification accuracy for various distortion types according to [15]. Additionally, it
has not been the scope of this thesis to optimize CNNs for distorted images, which
would take a substantial amount of time [30, p. 2]. For these reason, a different
network has not been implemented.
5.7
Alternative Explanation Methods
The four explanation methods utilized in this thesis – Guided Backpropagation, Grad-
CAM, Modified Grad-CAM and Sensitivity – have been chosen because they are well-
known state-of-the-art methods. Additionally, they are either easily implementable or
already available through existing Python libraries. These explanations are however
only a subset of explanation methods that are available for CNNs and other machine
learning models. Examples of other available explanation methods are LIME [39] and
Grad-CAM++ [9]. As briefly mentioned in the introduction, LIME is an explanation
technique that explains the predictions of any classifier, by learning an interpretable
model locally around the prediction. LIME has nevertheless not been tested in this
thesis, as it is not specifically developed for CNN models.
Grad-CAM++ is a generalization of Grad-CAM, proposed in 2018 by Chattopadhay
et al. [9] which provides stronger visual explanations of CNN model predictions and
better object recognition, especially in terms of multiple objects in a single image
[9]. For these reasons especially, the Grad-CAM++ method would have been ideal
to apply in this thesis instead of Grad-CAM. However, Grad-CAM was conveniently
available through the keras-vis Python library and was therefore used. The Grad-
CAM++ method is shortly outlined in Section 5.7.1.

5.8
Future Work
When looking at what can be done going forward to improve the similarities, an obvi-
ous thing to consider is the utilized VGG16 network. The VGG16 network that has
been used to generate the explanation method heatmaps is trained to classify images
into one of the 1000 classes displayed in the listing in Appendix A. Ideally, the net-
work generating the explanation method heatmaps should be the finetuned VGG16
network. Using the finetuned VGG16 network would likely lead to a higher classi-
fication accuracy for each image, thereby leading to heatmaps better capturing the
true class. It should however be noted, that the finetuned VGG16 network performs
very poorly on the images distorted using Blur Setting 2 and Noise Settings 1 and
2, as depicted in Figure 4.3. Additionally, the true class labels in the used dataset
could also be utilized to generate the explanation method heatmaps. By doing this,
only the robustness of the explanation methods would be examined, as the network
classification would be neglected.
In the presented results, explanation method heatmaps generated from gaze data are
used as an interpretability benchmark, that the explanation method heatmaps are
compared to. Alternatively, the benchmark presented by Mohseni et al. [32] could
be used. The human explanations in this benchmark are from user annotations of
images, that are accumulated to create a weighted explanation heatmap over the im-
age. Furthermore, their work demonstrates how a human-grounded evaluation could
be used as a measure to qualify machine-learning explanations.
As illustrated in Chapter 4, the implementation of CLAHE and histogram matching
improved the results in terms of similarities when comparing the explanation method
heatmaps generated original and distorted images. A larger improvement in similarity
measures could possibly be obtained by combining CLAHE and histogram matching.
That is, by first applying CLAHE to explanation method heatmaps, and afterwards
using histogram matching to combine them, an additional improvement in similarities
may be obtained.
It is reasonable to assume, that the attention of the test subjects varies during the
three seconds they are shown the images. To further analyse the gaze data, one ap-
proach could be to split the data for each image shown to a subject into time intervals,
to evaluate how the attention of the test subject varies. Another way to partition the
gaze data could be by splitting the total dataset of 115 images shown to subjects into
intervals, such that it can be investigated whether the quality of the gathered data
datadecreases. A hypothesis here could be that subjects will get tired after a certain
number of images thus exploring subsequent images less.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
The results presented in this thesis show that image quality has a huge impact on
whether a CNN is able to correctly classify images containining objects belonging to
a single class. Furthermore, it can be concluded that the VGG16 network is more
sensitive to images with applied Gaussian blur than to images with added Gaussian
noise.
It can be concluded, that applying distortions to images affects the spread in explana-
tion method heatmaps. However, the spreads in heatmaps generated by the different

Modified Grad-CAM show a significant increase of the mean spreads in heatmaps
