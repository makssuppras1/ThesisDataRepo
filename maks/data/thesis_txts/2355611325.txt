Abstract
Water-based metasurfaces (MTSs) are designed and investigated both numerically
and experimentally in the microwave range. The MTSs are composed of a Rohacell
51 HF medium with water inclusions, where spherical, cylindrical and ’rod-like’ ones
are investigated. A particular physical size of the ’rod-like’ water inclusion, makes
the MTS function as an on/off transmission switch by a 90◦mechanical rotation.
Another size successfully makes it an absorber. The MTSs are characterized by their
transmissive and reflective properties as well as their polarizabilties.
The MTSs with cylindrical and ’rod-like’ inclusions have been manufactured and
measured in waveguide (L-band) and free-space (below 1 GHz) setups. In the wave-
guide setup, the transmissive and reflective properties are measured with high accu-
racy. The waveguide environment introduces aperiodicity in the MTSs disturbing the
resonances in their inclusions. The free-space setup demonstrates a quick and easy
method for measuring the transmissive properties of the MTSs.
Additionally, a configuration of stacked MTSs is studied numerically showing sig-
nificant focusing/guiding effects, which can be switched on/off by 90◦rotation of the
configuration. Methods quantifying the focusing and switching effects are developed.
The abundance, low cost and environmental-friendly nature of water make the
water-based MTSs potential candidates for extremely cheap microwave components
as well as for educational and scientific purposes.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Metamaterials (MTMs) have attracted great and broad attention the past years in the
electromagnetics community. The exploration of materials exhibiting unnatural behaviour
has led to the possibility of achieving superlenses [1], cloaking [2], subwavelength cavities [3]
etc. The two dimensional equivalents to MTMs are metasurfaces (MTSs), which introduce
abrupt changes to the wavefront of electromagnetic waves. Most MTSs are composed of an
assembly of subwavelength scatterers with induced magnetic and electric dipoles. Generally,
fabrication of MTSs is much simpler than MTMs making the MTSs superior for large-scale
applications.
In optics, conventional components alter light over several wavelengths, while MTSs
mould the optical wavefront in less than one wavelength [4]. Currently, MTSs consisting of
all-dielectric media are heavily investigated due to their low losses compared to those in met-
als in the visible [5]. Metallic (plasmonic) MTSs utilize light-matter interactions by the light
coupling to plasmons, while all-dielectric MTSs employ Mie resonances in high-permittivity
subwavelength resonators.
Some investigated all-dielectric MTSs have exhibited tunable
properties by the alteration of the temperature, polarization (by rotating the MTS), apply-
ing strains etc. [6, 7]. Many MTSs designed for optical applications have been experimentally
demonstrated in the microwave range due to limitation in fabrication facilities and/or cost
[4]. These demonstrations function as proof of concepts and the idea is to adapt the MTSs
to optics.
In the microwave regime, many materials exhibit relative permittivities of several hun-
dreds. One that has not attracted much attention, namely water, has been overlooked due
to its losses and impurities. However, at low frequencies (below 1 GHz) the losses in wa-
ter are low and its real permittivitity is high (approximately 80). Water-based MTSs has
recently been investigated numerically and experimentally demonstrating numerous tunabil-
ties [8, 9, 10]. The abundance, low cost and ecological advantages of water makes the MTSs
suitable for quick and cheap prototyping as well as educational and scientific purposes.
In this study, the exploration of water-based MTSs exhibiting tunabilities is continued.
The investigated MTSs consist of a Rohacell 51 HF medium with inclusions of deionized
water. The MTSs are investigated numerically in COMSOL Multiphysics and experimentally
in a waveguide (L-band) and free-space (below 1 GHz) setup. A MTS manufactured for the
free-space setup is shown in Figure 1.1. The electromagnetic response of the shown MTS
depend on the polarization of the incident wave such that its response can be controlled by
mechanical rotation of it. The investigated MTSs are characterized by their transmissive and
reflective properties and magnetic and electric polarizabilities. Additional, a configuration
composed of stacked MTSs exhibiting focusing/guiding effects is studied.
The report is divided into seven chapters. In Chapter 2, a brief introduction of MTMs
and MTSs are given, where some recent studies of tunable all-dielectric MTSs are presented.
Chapter 3 introduces the tools for characterizing the MTSs and the model of water permit-
tivity. Chapter 4 and Chapter 5 contain the numerical results of the investigated MTSs.
The results for the stacked MTSs are included in Chapter 5. The measurements of water
permittivity, material parameters of Rohacell 51 HF and the manufactured MTSs are pre-

CHAPTER 2
Artificial material structures:
metamaterials and metasurfaces
We give a quick introduction of metamaterials (MTMs) and their 2-D equivalent meta-
surfaces (MTSs). We present basic configurations of them and explain how they can be
characterized. Furthermore, we go through a few studies on all-dielectric MTS designed for
optics. Finally, we present recent studies on water-based MTSs.
2.1
Metamaterials
MTMs are synthetic materials not found in nature. They are compositions of natural ma-
terials, which result in new materials with properties different from the natural ones alone.
The applications of MTMs are diverse and include filters [11], sensors [12], superconductors
[13], waveguides, antennas [3] etc. With MTMs, properties as e.g. negative refraction, near
zero refraction, etc. can be achieved.
Normally, electromagnetic materials are classified by their real values of permittivity (ϵ′)
and permeability (µ′), which can be illustrated by a two axis coordinate system as shown in
Figure 2.1 [3]. The imaginary parts (ϵ′′ and µ′′) are associated with losses in the materials.
The signs of ϵ′ and µ′ determine where materials are placed in Figure 2.1. The 1st quadrant
of Figure 2.1 holds the DPS (double positive) materials, which are the most common ones

found in nature (e.g. dielectrics). A DPS material has both a positive permittivity and
permeability, and its refractive index is positive. The 2nd quadrant holds the ENG (epsilon-
negative) materials. Many metals in the visible spectrum exhibit these properties. An ENG
medium has negative permittivity and positive permeability, and the first concept of a MTM
with these properties, was presented by Pendry et al. in [14]. The 3rd quadrant holds the
DNG (double-negative) materials, which are not yet discovered in nature, but have only been
achieved artificially. A DNG medium has both negative permittivity and permeability and
therefore has a negative refractive index. The 4th quadrant holds the MNG (mu-negative)
materials, and some gyrotropic materials exhibit these properties in certain frequency ranges.
The first MTMs with these properties were conducted by Pendry et al. in [15]. Two more
possible types of materials are MNZ (mu-near-zero) and ENZ (epsilon-near-zero) materials.
The MNZ materials are when the materials are near their magnetic plasma frequency, while
ENZ materials are when the materials are near their electric plasma frequency. Materials
with low permittivity can be used for e.g.
induced transparency and cloaking purposes
[16, 17].
Generally, most natural materials are dispersive and their properties vary with frequency.
This applies as well on MTM, which wanted effects are often in a very narrow frequency
band. A material can be a DPS at one frequency and an ENG at another as with some
metals in the visible.
A sketch of a MTM is shown in Figure 2.2 and consists of a three dimensional periodic
unit cell (meta-atom) composed of a two media: host and element. For this MTM, the
unit cell is periodic in three dimensions, while its 2-D equivalent (MTS) is two dimensional
periodic material.
If the unit cell is only periodic in one dimension, it is a 1-D MTM.
The cells of the MTS has to be subwavelength for the material to be homogenized and
the effective permittivity, ϵeff, and permeability, µeff, to be determined accurately [18]. The
electromagnetic properties of the MTM are due to the resonances in the unit cell rather than
the periodic effects as e.g. in electromagnetic band gap materials and frequency selective
surfaces.

A MTM can be isotropic or anisotropic. Isotropic MTMs are more simple, but some-
times it can be difficult to achieve the wanted properties of a MTM without introducing
anisotropy. For example cloaking an object using the coordinate transformation technique
seems impossible with isotropic MTM, since the concept is to bend the waves around the
object [2]. However, there are other cloaking techniques that can utilize isotropic MTMs.
2.2
Metasurfaces
The idea of MTSs is to achieve thin planar arrays of subwavelength resonators that produce
abrupt changes in the amplitude, phase and/or polarization of an incident wave [4]. Basically,
MTSs can be divided into two types: metafilms and metascreens [19]. A metafilm is shown
Figure 2.3(a) and consists of a host medium in which scatterers are. A metascreen is a sheet
containing apertures as shown in Figure 2.3(b). The MTSs shown in Figure 2.3 both consist
of a rectangular lattice of identical and periodic unit cells containing two media. The unit
cell of the metafilm has a host medium in which a scatter of another medium is. We refer
to the scatterer as the element. The MTSs investigated in this report are all metascreens.
Generally, scientists characterize MTSs by their transmissive and reflective properties
given by the transmittance and reflectance, respectively. Furthermore, they can be described
by the magnetic and electric polarizabilities (αm and αe, respectively) of a single unit cell.
The polarizabilties describe the ability of the unit cell to form magnetic and electric dipoles.
To calculate the polarizabilities, the unit cell of the MTS has to be periodic and identical as
we will explain in Chapter 3.
For most MTSs, a plane wave incidence in free-space is investigated.
They all have
thicknesses which have to be much smaller than the wavelength of their surroundings (free-
space: λ0) of the incident wave. If this requirement is not complied, the MTSs cannot be
considered two dimensional structures. Many MTSs have square lattices with periods that
are comparable to the wavelength. For example in [20], a MTS with a cut-wire pair unit cell
is investigated. The period is 512 nm and a magnetic dipole resonance at λ0 ≈1000 nm is
reported. Here, the period is approximately λ0/2. However, the thickness is approximately
λ0/10 making it electrically thin. Similarly, the absorber unit cell presented in [21] has a
period of λ0 and thickness of λ0/20 at maximal absorption. It seems that scientists are more

2.2.1
All-dielectric metasurfaces in nanophotonics
Many electromagnetic MTMs are designed for optical applications due to the lack of materi-
als in the visible and near-infrared. Here, metals do not behave as perfect electric conductors
(PECs), but more as plasmas with losses. Still, some metals are used due to their negative
permittivity as in e.g. in nanoparticles [22]. In nanoparticles localized surface plasmons are
excited on the interface of negative and positive media by an incident field resulting in a
strong luminescence. However, the losses in the metal diminish the resonance. The losses
can be circumvented using gain like e.g. quantum dots, but these are rare and expensive
[23].
Recently, MTMs composed of all-dielectrics has gained increasing attention in nanopho-
tonics. They have been shown to have electromagnetic responses in all four quadrants in
Figure 2.1 [5]. All-dielectric MTMs employ materials with high permittivity, such as silicon,
germanium and tellurium.
They are used as the medium of the elements shown in Fig-
ure 2.3(a). One can tailor the magnetic and electric resonances in an element by choosing a
proper design of it.

2.3
Summary
We gave a brief introduction of MTMs and MTSs. We explained how to characterize them,
which is by the effective material parameters for the MTMs.
For the MTSs, the trans-
mittance, reflectance and magnetic and electric polarizabitlities (if possible) is used. We
presented few recent studies on the tunable all-dielectric MTS for nano- and for microwave
photonics. The ones for microwave photonics was water-based and exhibited hydrophobic,
thermal and/or mechanical tunabilities. In Chapters 4 and 5, we will investigate the me-
chanical tunability of water-based MTSs.

CHAPTER 3
Theoretical background
We show how to calculate the electric and magnetic polarizabilities for a single unit cell in
a MTS consisting of an arrangement of identical unit cells. To do this, we need to know
the lattice constant, the transmission and reflection coefficients and the interaction constant.
The latter can be calculated analytically, which we will show how to do in the beginning of

3.1
Metasurface characterization tool
The polarizabilities are very useful to characterize the magnetic and electric responses of
a unit cell in a MTS. A MTS consists of an arrangement of unit cells, which do not need
to be identical. However, when they are identical and periodic, the polarizabilities can be
calculated for a single unit cell.
Generally, a MTS can be considered as an arrangement of electric and magnetic dipoles
located in free-space as shown in Figure 3.1(a), which are excited by the field of a plane
wave at normal incidence. The electric and magnetic dipole moments excited in a single
unit cell will depend on the incident field and the field coming from the dipole moments in
the other unit cells. Simply, we can just add the fields coming from each unit cell, however,
for large number of unit cells, this method becomes computationally heavy. Instead, we
simplify the configuration by replacing (most of) the dipoles with a current sheet as shown
in Figure 3.1(b). Thus, we just need to calculate a constant that accounts for the interaction
between a single unit cell and the rest. We call this constant the interaction constant.
We mainly focus on the electric dipole response in the MTS during the derivation of the
interaction constant and polarizabilities, since the magnetic dipole response can be obtained
by using the duality theorem. The derivations have been done following Tretyakov in [25]
with some clarifications appended.

4.1
Design of the metasurfaces
The unit cell for a general MTS with a rectangular lattice is shown in Figure 4.1. It consists
of two parts: the element and the host. A cartesian coordinate system (x, y, z) is introduced
such that the origin is at the center of the unit cell. The unit cell is quadratic in the xy-
plane with side length a and has thickness d. The angle ϕ describes the rotation angle of
the surface in the xy-plane. In this chapter, we investigate a plane wave at normal incidence
and with the fields Ei and Hi.
Due to the state of water at room temperature, we need to design unit cells which can
maintain the liquid within them. An easy method to manufacture the unit cells is to simply
mill the elements in the host medium, fill them with water and then close them with strongly
adhesive tape. In this report, we use Rohacell 51 HF as the host medium. Since Rohacell
is made of closed unit cells, the water should not be absorbed by the material. Some of the
water will evaporate through the tape over time meaning the MTSs must be measured the
same day they are filled with water. However, the unit cells can easily be refilled and sealed
again at another time.
The milling method has some design limitations like e.g., the unit cells cannot have inner
corners in the designs. This can be circumvented by cutting the corners to make smooth
transitions. Furthermore, it is difficult to fill the elements completely with water, since there
will always be a small curvature on the surface of it. It can be difficult not the push some
of the water out the unit cells when placing the tape, which will make it cumbersome to get
the tape to stay on the surface. A way to flatten out most of the curvature of the water, is
the overfill the unit cells exploiting the surface tension of the water, which prevents it from
flowing out. Then by extracting some of it with a syringe, an almost completely flat water
surface can be attained.

COMSOL model
The COMSOL model is shown in Figure 4.2 and consists of a rectangular cuboid of free-

periodic boundary condition, which, assigned on a surface, will make the model periodic
over that surface [27]. The periodic surfaces are marked in Figure 4.2 by the red arrows.
Applying this on four of the six surfaces, an infinite periodic surface structure is simulated.
The MTSs were designed and simulated in COMSOL Multiphysics in which the 3D
Electromagnetic Waves physics with the Frequency Domain study was used. The model of
water from Section 3.2 was implemented into the (COMSOL) model as well as the material
parameters of Rohacell determined in Chapter 6.
An easy way to obtain the reflection and transmission coefficients in COMSOL is to use
ports, where a set of two (Port 1 and Port 2) was defined in the model as shown in Figure 4.2.
The excitation was set in the port settings by defining the mode of the y-component of the
electric field to be e−jk0z, where k0 is the wavenumber in free-space.
This corresponds
to a plane wave with normal incidence on the MTS. In a two port system, COMSOL has
defined the S-parameters, where S11 and S21 corresponds to the reflection and transmission
coefficients, respectively.
One should notice that COMSOL does not correct the phase
differences between the transmitted and reflected waves at the ports. It means that the
reflected and transmitted waves are subjected to different phases due to their direction of
propagations. This phase difference depends on the length, dm, in the longitudinal direction
(^z) of the model and the wavelength. The phase can easily be corrected by multiplying S11
with ejk0dm. Of course, this is only necessary if the complex reflection coefficient is used,
while the reflectance will not change due to the fact that |ejk0dm|2 = 1.

4.2.1
Mesh settings and convergence
The mesh in the model does not need to be fine everywhere for the results to converge. In
media with no curved geometries or corners, the mesh can be quite coarse.
Curvatures,
edges and corners need a fine mesh marking the smooth and sharp transitions, which will
make the results converge with less mesh elements compared to a fully refined model. The
number of mesh elements is directly related to the amount of memory needed, so a less
powerful computer can be used if fewer mesh elements can be employed.
Due to the Floquet periodic boundary conditions, the surfaces parallel to each other
need to have identical mesh grid. This is due to the fact that these surfaces are joined by
the periodic condition. If this is not complied, it will show up in the field plots as unusual
field propagations and discontinuities at the periodic interfaces. The ports do not need to
have an identical mesh grid due to the way the S-parameters are calculated [28, p. 76].
The simulations in COMSOL are numerical analyses, and therefore we need to consult
the convergence of the results. This is done by defining a parameter which increases (or
decreases) the fineness of the mesh in the whole model.
COMSOL uses the number of
Degrees of Freedom (DoF) to describe the size of the system of differential equations that it
needs to solve to obtain the results. This quantity is directly related the fineness of the mesh
and the amount of memory needed to compute the results [29]. We want to find out when
the result is more or less stationary as the mesh is changed. This analysis has been done
for all models in the report, while only the results for the spherical element are included to
shorten the report.
We have investigated the convergence of the results for the spherical element.
The
transmittance as a function of the number of million DoF is shown in Figure 4.3 at the
two resonance frequencies found in the investigated spectrum of the MTS, which we will
review in Section 4.4. Clearly, the results converge to a specific value of the transmittance
as the number of DoF is increased. The transmittance only varies at its third decimal as
the number of DoF is heavily increased. This is more than accurate enough for this work,
where we want to compare simulated results with the experimental ones, where it is hard to
measure such small deviations.
A significant source of error in the model, is the distance between the MTS and the ports,
which can be observed in Figure 4.3(b). Here, the transmittance is shown as a function of
the distance between the ports (in wavelengths) at the resonance frequency f = 0.77 GHz.

Water slab
We investigate the transmissive and reflective properties of a water slab of thickness ds that
is illuminated by a linearly polarized plane wave. We are interested in what is obtainable
with such simple surfaces, which tunability is limited to variation of temperature and its
thickness. The transmission and reflection coefficients can easily be calculated analytically
by the equations given in Appendix A.1. The transmittance and reflectance are shown as
functions of frequencies in Figure 4.4 for a water slab of values of ds. We observe that the
transmittance is mostly low for all slabs, except when the slab becomes electrically thin
or nλw/2 thick with n being a real integer and λw the wavelength in the water. As the
transmittance increases, the reflectance decreases, while we observe that some of the power
is absorbed in the slab since the absorption A ̸= 0.
The high transmittance at every nλs/2 thicknesses can be explained by the boundary
conditions and is known as the half-wave window. In this configuration, we have two bound-
aries formed by an air-water and a water-air media. Due to these two interfaces, the field
inside the water medium are a sum of a transmitted and multiple reflected waves. Therefore
the reflected wave at the air-water interface depends on the thickness of water slab. When
it approaches nλw/2, the magnitude of the reflected wave decreases, while the magnitude of
the transmitted wave increases.

magnetic dipole resonance when rcyl/λ0 ≈0.038 giving rcyl/λcyl ≈0.38 for a TMy polarized
incident plane wave.
In our case, at f = 1.15 GHz, rcyl/λcyl ≈0.37, which is close to
that obtained in [30]. So, unlike the electric dipole resonance, the magnetic one is mainly
affected by the geometry of the individual cylinder and not the alignment of the cylinders
in the present configuration.
In Figure 4.9(a), we observe that at f = 1.15 GHz, the transmittance and reflectance

have a small peak and dip, respectively. The absorbance at this frequency is approximately
0.5, meaning that half of the power is dissipated in the MTS. The high absorbance is due
to the combination of electric and magnetic dipole resonances, which changes the effective
material parameters such that they are equal [21]. When this occurs, the input impedance
of the surface is the same as free-space resulting in zero reflection. A single infinite dielectric
cylinder cannot exhibit a magnetic and an electric dipole at the same frequency, while a
single infinite magnetodielectric cylinder can by setting the permittivity and permeability
properly [30]. However, in our results we observe that an arrangement of infinite dielectric
cylinders can also have this feature. One can shift the electric dipole resonance frequency
by changing the displacement of the cylinders without notably affecting the magnetic dipole
resonance. This results in simultaneous electric and magnetic resonances making the MTS
an absorber if the magnitudes of the dipole resonances are correct. This, we will examine
with the absorber elements discussed in Chapter 5.
4.6
Summary
We investigated simple MTSs by examining their transmissive and reflective properties. We
gave an explanation to the appearances of the properties by examining the polarizabilities
and the local fields of a single unit cell.
We studied characteristics the MTS consisting
of spherical elements.
This MTS has already been studied in [8], and we obtained the
same results validating the COMSOL model used in this report.
The spherical element
exhibited a low transmittance and an increase in the reflectance when the spheres exhibited
a magnetic dipole resonance. We showed that the dipole resonances in the spheres where
mainly dominated by the geometry of the spheres and not the array effects. This was due
to the large separation of the spheres resulting in low coupling of them.
We found that the water slab generally exhibited low transmittance and high reflectance
except for d = nλw/2. The MTS with cylindrical elements had similar transmissive and re-
flective properties in some parts of the spectrum as the water slab except at the frequencies
where dipole resonances in the cylindrical element were present. The magnetic and electric
dipole resonances in the cylindrical element had almost the same resonance frequency, which
resulted in a high absorbance. We argued that the electric dipole resonance was heavily af-
fected by the small displacement of the cylinders, since the resonance frequency was far from
that expected from that of a single cylinder. Oppositely, the magnetic dipole resonance fre-
quency was located closely to that of a single cylinder. We concluded that the displacement
of the cylinders can be a way to tailor the dipole resonances (especially the electric one) so
that they e.g. have the magnetic and electric dipoles at the same resonance frequency.
The polarization sensitivity was investigated for the cylindrical element only, which ex-
hibited completely different transmissive and reflective properties when the MTS was rotated
90◦. This tunability is not present in the spherical element and the water slab due to their
geometric configurations.
In general, these results and conclusions helped us to design a unit cell that is easy to
tailor for the different requirements. The unit cells with cylindrical elements was very polar-
ization sensitive. However, it mainly reflected the incident power in most of the spectrum,
which can be a problem in some contexts. Since the spherical element was not polarization
sensitive, we are more interested in cylindrical designs.

Significance of the lattice constant
One geometric parameter that we have not yet discussed fully is the lattice constant, a.
How does the displacement of the elements influence the transmittance and reflectance?
This is illustrated in Figure 5.4(a), where the transmittance and reflectance are shown as
functions of a at f = 0.8 and 0.76 GHz. Clearly, the graph of the transmittance for f = 0.8
GHz has an optimum close to a = 50 mm, where the transmittance has a minimum. We
notice that by increasing a minimizes the interaction between the elements shown by the
decreasing reflectance and the increasing transmittance. However, these observations are
for a fixed frequency.
By increasing the displacement of the elements, the minimum in
the transmittance is shifted towards the magnetic dipole resonance frequency, since there
will be less coupling between the elements. This is shown by the graph for f = 0.76 GHz,
where the dipole resonance in the single element is strongest, revealing a more constant
transmittance as a is increased. Furthermore, we observe that the transmittance increases
as a is decreased confirming the disturbance of the resonance caused by the interaction of the
elements. However, increasing a will make the MTS more narrow-banded, since the coupling
will be diminished and only the dipoles will minimize the transmittance. This is what we
observed with the spherical element in Chapter 4, where the shortest distance between the
elements was λ0/24.6. For the present element, the shortest distance is λ0/156.3, which is
more than 6 times smaller than the spherical element.

Waveguiding metasurface configuration
We present the simulation results for an extension of the MTS in the third dimension by
stacking multiple MTSs. Several MTSs are simply placed periodically in the z-direction
resulting in couplings of the MTSs. We call the configuration a waveguiding metasurface
(WG-MTS). Some of the results in this section are included in a conference paper submitted
to European Microwave Week 2017 and in an abstract accepted by Progress In Electromag-
netics Research Symposium. The paper and abstract are included in Appendix D.
Usually, a waveguide is used to transmit a signal from one point to another by preserving
most of the power in the signal. The idea is to show that the WG-MTS will focus the power
in one direction when it is in its transmitting state (ϕ = 90◦) resulting in an increase in
received power compared to what is received without the WG-MTS. To show this feature, we
cannot use the previous COMSOL model due to the plane wave excitation, which is already
travelling in the direction of the focusing. Instead we use a point source which radiates in
the outward radial direction. We insert the WG-MTS in front of the source, thus exhibiting
the focusing effect of the WG-MTS.
The type of source forces us to implement a finite WG-MTS, so instead of having bound-
aries making the configuration periodic, we need layers that attenuate the fields simulating
an infinite space. These are called Perfect Matched Layers (PMLs) and the best geometry
in COMSOL that does not introduce corners is a sphere. The COMSOL model is found in

geometric parameters are defined together with the location of the source marked by the

5.3
Summary
We presented a MTS with a versatile unit cell that exhibited multiple properties by varying
the geometrical size of its element. One size of the element resulted in a MTS very sensitive
to changes in the polarization of the incident wave. It transmits most of the power at one
polarization and reflects it at another. Another size led to a MTS with high absorbance
(88 %). Both MTSs were designed for f = 0.8 GHz, and their main results were given in
Table 5.5 along with their geometric parameters. The numerous parameters made it easier
to tailor the resonances and the transmissive and reflective properties of the MTS compared
to the more simple MTSs studied in Chapter 4.
We showed that the MTS with the reflector I-elements could be used for waveguiding
purposes by stacking multiple of them.
The configuration focused the radiation in the
direction of the alignment. We successfully presented a way to quantify the focusing by
calculating a transmittance equivalent to a two port system. Having several MTSs improved
the efficiency of the focusing effect making the configuration more directive. Furthermore,
we showed that it had adopted the polarization sensitivity from the MTSs.
Thus, the
transmission could be turned on and off by mechanically rotating the MTSs functioning like
a switch.

CHAPTER 6
Measurements
We present the experimental results and the methods for obtaining them. This includes the
measurements of deionized water, the host medium and the manufactured MTSs. The water
permittivity was determined by using a dielectric probe, while the material parameters of
the host medium were obtained using a waveguide setup. The transmissive and reflective
properties of the MTSs have been determined by using two different methods: the waveguide
setup and a free-space setup. The free-space setup consists of two antennas directed towards
each other with the MTS in between them. The first method used L-band frequencies, while
a large frequency band was available with the second, but was only used for measuring in
the UHF-band. The different advantages and disadvantages of the methods will be discussed
later in the chapter.
We measured the MTSs with the cylindrical and reflector I-elements in the waveguide
setup. In the free-space setup, we only measured a MTS with reflector I-elements.
The fabrication of the MTSs was done by the workshop located in building 348 at DTU.
The MTSs were designed such that they could easily be fabricated by simply cutting, milling
and/or drilling the elements in the solid host medium. The elements could then be filled
with water and closed off using some strong adhesive tape.
Of course, these MTS were
not long-lasting, since the water would dissolve the adhesive on the tape over time creating
paths where the water could escape. Furthermore, we discovered that some of the water in
the MTS evaporated until the tension in the tape prevented further evaporation. With that
said, it was never the intention to manufacture long-lasting MTSs, since we only needed to
measure in a limited time, which took less than one hour. The MTSs could easily keep the
water for a day, and even after several weeks all the MTSs still contained more than 90 %
of the water.
The results are distributed by their measurement method, so that the dielectric probe
measurements are presented in Section 6.1, the waveguide measurements in Section 6.2 and
the free-space measurements in Section 6.3.
6.1
Water permittivity
The water permittivity was measured using a simple setup to be sure that the water model
found in Chapter 3 corresponds to the water in the MTS. The setup consisted of a vector
network analyzer (VNA) connected to a dielectric probe. The VNA used in the experiment
was a Hewlett Packard 8753C (30 kHz to 6 GHz) connected to a Hewlett Packard 85047A
S-Parameter Test Set and the dielectric probe was an Agilent 85070E Dielectric Probe Kit.
The VNA was calibrated through a GPIB connection by the use of the software Keysight
(Agilent) N1500A Material Measurement Suite installed on a computer. It directly provided
the complex permittivity.
The dielectric probe is just an open-ended transmission line

field, the reflected signal changes as well, and can be described by the reflection coefficient
which can be related to the permittivity. The calibration procedure was done by a three
measurement setups consisting of measurement of air, shorting block and deionized water

that it is the electric current loops. This results in a circulation of the electric field that

will have y- and z-components inside the elements, and therefore tangential electric fields to

The measurement setup is shown in Figure 6.14. It shows two wide band horn antennas:
Satimo SH 400 and ETS-Lindgren QRH 615 both capable of measuring from 0.4 to 6 GHz.
An absorber wall with a hole where the MTS can be fitted into was placed in between
the antennas. The antennas were directed to each other and the distance between their
apertures was 1.1 m. They were both connected to an HP 8753D VNA. The MTS was placed
such that it was equally distanced to the apertures of the antennas. In this measurement
configuration, we did not attempt to calibrate the VNA because an anechoic space was not
possible to create in the room. Instead, we simply compared the upper and lower bounds
of what was possible to measure with the setup.
We say that having nothing placed in
the hole corresponds to full transmission (thru), while inserting a metal plate corresponds
to zero transmission (reflect). The larger the separation between the bounds is (dynamic
range), the greater shifts in magnitude we can detect in our system. If the dynamic range
is only 2 dB, then we must be able to detect very small variations in the magnitude of the
transmission. It is very unlikely that we would do so in the highly reflective room, which is
why the absorber wall has been placed as shown in Figure 6.14. It enhances the performance
of the measurement system by reducing the radiation, which would pass by the MTS. Still,

6.4
Summary
We presented all the measurements performed during the project in this chapter. It included
the measurements of the permittivity of deionized water, the material parameters of Rohacell
and measurements of the MTSs using two different measurement setups. The water was
measured using a dielectric probe, which turned out to be an unfulfilling way to measure the
permittivity. This was due to the calibration method of the system, where deionized water
was used during the calibration.
The material parameters of Rohacell were determined using a waveguide setup. The
transmissive and reflective properties of MTSs with cylindrical and reflector I-elements were
measured in the same setup. Due to the fixed geometry of the waveguide, two MTSs of
each design were needed to measure the polarization sensitivity of them. Furthermore, a
new COMSOL model simulating the conditions in the waveguide was made to compare the
measurements and the simulations. The simulation results and the measurements exhibited
a great agreement with each other. Finally, we compared the results from the two COMSOL
models with the same MTS, which were comparable, but not the same.
The transmissive properties of a MTS of I-elements of 6 × 6 unit cells were measured
in an free-space measurement setup, which turned out to be accurate, but not very precise.
The system was not calibrated due to limitations in facilities, and therefore we could not
measure the reflectance. However, the simulation results and the measurements of the trans-
mittance exhibited a fine agreement showing the transmissive properties and the polarization
sensitivity of the MTS.
The two measurement setups both had cons and pros and which one of them that is the
best setup depends on the MTS design. However, calibrating the free-space setup such that
both the transmission and reflection coefficient can be measured and improving the precision
of the measurements will make it superior to the waveguide setup.

CHAPTER 7
Summary and Conclusions
We studied water-based metasurfaces (MTSs) with properties tunable by mechanical rota-
tion. The MTSs were designed for microwave frequencies below 2 GHz and consisted of unit
cells with elements of deionized water maintained by the host, Rohacell 51 HF. The MTSs
were investigated numerically in COMSOL Multiphysics and experimentally in a waveguide
and free-space setup.
In Chapter 2, we provided a brief introduction of metamaterials (MTMs) and MTSs, and
reviewed recent studies on all-dielectric and water-based MTSs. Here, the MTSs exhibited
mechanical, temperature and deformation tunabilities.
We presented the tools for characterizing the MTSs in Chapter 3. The transmittance
and reflectance describe the transmissive and reflective properties of a MTS. The magnetic
and electric polarizabilities exhibit the ability of the unit cell to form magnetic and electric
dipoles. The polarizabilities can be calculated if the unit cell is periodic. Furthermore, we
presented a model for water permittivity showing its frequency and temperature dependency.
We found that water-based MTSs have to be designed at as low frequencies as possible to
avoid the losses (imaginary permittivity) of water and maintaining a large real permittivity.
In Chapter 4, we provided the numerical results for a water slab and simple MTSs with
spherical and cylindrical elements.
The magnetic and electric resonances in the spheres
resulted in a low transmission. On the contrary, we showed that the low transmission for
the cylinders was not due to resonances within them. Due to their geometry, the cylinders
were far more polarization sensitive than the spheres.
Chapter 5 contained the results for a more advanced (but easy to manufacture) element,
refereed to as the I-element.
One size of the I-element was highly polarization sensitive
such that the transmission could be switched on/off by rotating the MTS 90◦. Another size
exhibited low transmission and reflection due to the combination of electric and magnetic
dipole resonances resulting in a large absorbance.
The focusing/guiding effect of stacking MTSs was also investigated in Chapter 5. The
MTSs were identical and consisted of the MTS investigated previously in the chapter. We
presented methods for quantifying the focusing effect of the configuration showing a great
enhancement of the transferred energy. Furthermore, the transmission through the MTSs
could be switched on/off by a 90◦rotation of them.
Finally, we presented the measurements in Chapter 6. It included the measurements
of the MTSs with cylindrical and I-elements in a waveguide setup operating at L-band
frequencies.
The size of the MTSs and the period of the unit cell were limited to the
size of the waveguide. Furthermore, the waveguide introduced asymmetric periodicity in
two directions affecting the resonances in the elements and therefore the transmissive and
reflective properties. The simulation results of the MTSs in the waveguide environment are
in (very) close agreement with the measurements.
We provided the measurements of a MTS with I-elements in a free-space setup operating
at UHF. The setup was not calibrated due to limitation in the test facilities. However, we
were still able to measure the transmittance, which are in good agreement with the one of the
simulation. We could not measure the reflectance due to reflections occurring everywhere
==PAGE:78==

in the system, which also affected the transmittance. The measurement accuracy can be
improved by calibrating the setup, but this requires anechoic surroundings suppressing the
reflections in the room.
Additionally, the study work led to two conference publications, which are included in
Appendix D.
The investigated MTSs cannot compete with conventional microwave devices. However,
substituting the Rohacell medium with a plastic container, the MTSs will be extremely
cheap reconfigurable microwave components. They can also be used for educational and
scientific purposes, e.g. for demonstrations, proof of concepts and prototyping.
We suggest further investigation of the stacked MTSs in order to improve their focusing
effects. Furthermore, designing more complex MTSs/unit cells can lead to ones with more
exotic properties. In the future, the quality of the free-space measurements can be improved
by enabling a calibration of the setup.

Abstract— All-dielectric metamaterials are the growing trend in 
optics and electromagnetics. They require materials with high 
permittivity, for example silicon in photonics. Aiming the 
microwaves range we present here water as a unique substance 
for employing in metamaterials design. Dependence of water 
parameters on temperature and easy reconfiguring of the shape 
of unit elements make water metamaterials promising direction. 
We report here on focusing and guiding effects in arrays of 
water-filled elements, which can be tuned either by heating or 
mechanical means, that is deformation and rotation.     
I. INTRODUCTION 
All-dielectric metamaterials (MMs) are an attractive 
alternative to the resonant metal-based ones due to 
significantly reduced material losses [1]. Typically, such MMs 
are composed of high-permittivity inclusions in a low 
dielectric matrix. Combination of structure-induced individual 
and collective responses with high electric permittivity ε of 
the inclusions works for the extraordinary metamaterials 
properties. On the top of the desired properties for 
metamaterials potential reconfigurability and dynamical 
tuning are the greatest challenges so far. 
Recently it has been proposed to base MMs on water [2,3]. 
Water has a remarkable potential as an inexpensive, abundant 
and bio-friendly material with high permittivity in the 
microwave range. In addition it is liquid and thus always takes 
the shape of a reservoir or a container. Therefore, tuning of 
the properties exhibited by an array of water-filled reservoirs 
can be done thermally, mechanically and even by gravitation 
[2]. First characterization of mechanical tuning of a  
metasurface (two-dimensional array) with units partially filled 
with water was reported in [4]. Here we elaborate on water-
based metamaterials and show their performance, when 
elements are arranged not in a two-dimensional (2D) 
transverse array, or in a single metasurface (MS), but are 
stacked in the direction of propagation. 
The suggested water-based metamaterials can find 
applications not only as cheap and ecological microwave 
devices, but also as prototypes of optical and terahertz 
metamaterials and educational labs equipment.

prominent property of water is its dielectric permittivity 
temperature dependence, which is well described by the 
Debye formula [5]

VI. CONCLUSIONS 
We have discussed tuning properties of a stack of 
metasurfaces and one-dimensional periodic arrays formed by 
water-filled containers. The advantage of both configurations 
is the broad field of possible tuning options. In particular, we 
consider three basic regimes of tuning: by temperature, by 
deformation or by orientation. All cases show excellent 
behavior in terms of switching the transmission properties 
from “On” to “Off” states. Thus, we believe that water-filled 
periodic structures can be a solution for extremely cheap 
reconfigurable microwave components. 
We admit that water based metamaterials cannot compete 
with other switchable or tunable microwave devices in terms 
of speed. Indeed, field-effect transistor based switchers can 
operate with the switching speed of several tens GHz, while 
water metamaterials are limited by slow thermal or water 
redistribution processes and can hardly be faster than 1 Hz. 
However, the main advantage of water is its extremely low 
cost, abundance and ecological compatibility. The suggested 
metamaterials can be created literally from trash (plastic 
bottles, rubber balloons, plastic bags etc.) Moreover, the 
sources of the microwave radiation are also inexpensive. This 
provides not only a way of quick and cheap prototyping of

All-dielectric realizations of metamaterials (MTMs) and metasurfaces (MTSs) have attracted notable attention 
due their small material losses [1]. They rely on properly shaped high-permittivity inclusions situated in a low 
dielectric matrix. In the optical/near-infrared ranges, the relative permittivity of the available materials typically 
does not go beyond 50. At lower frequencies (e.g., radio/microwave ranges) more attractive materials can be 
found with much higher permittivities [2]. One interesting example is that of water; although its losses might be 
a problem at certain frequencies, its abundance and price (as compared to e.g., barium strontium titanate [2]), 
and environmental-friendly nature makes it a useful candidate for the consideration in artificial material design.   
 
In this work we present numerical/experimental results for several MTSs based on very simple “rod-like” water 
inclusions at radio/microwave frequencies. The unit cells are situated in a dielectric Rohacell 51 HF matrix, 
which allows for a very accurate experimentation of the prototypes thereby providing very clear results. 
Mechanically tunable MTSs have been realized and measured in both the waveguide (L-band) and free-space 
environments (below 1 GHz). Through a 90o rotation of the inclusions (figures to the left), high/low reflection 
(R) and low/high transmission (T) could be obtained (figures in the middle). The details of the numerical and the 
experimental results will be discussed in the presentation. Moreover, attention will be devoted to configurations 
resulting from a proper juxtaposition of several of the designed MTS and their ability to effectively guide the 
waves emitted by e.g., a near-by point dipole antenna; see the figure to the right which shows a very directive 
power flow density for a 7-layer MTS illuminated by a point dipole from its left side.
