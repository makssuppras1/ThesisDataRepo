Abstract
The archipelagos of Dodecanese and Cyclades are facing saltwater intrusions in the aquifers
threatening the quality of the available water. This, together with the seasonality in the water
demand along the year is the cause of water shortages in many islands. The solution often
adopted is the transportation of drinkable water from the mainland or a bigger island nearby,
which leads to a very high water price.
As an alternative, seawater desalination is becoming more and more popular. However, the
energy requirement for desalination is usually covered with diesel generators or other fossil-
fueled power plants. The natural resources in the Aegean Sea can provide an alternative to
the energy supply for desalination plants. Solar and wind-powered desalination have already
been tested and installed for different plant capacities all over the world. In addition, new
methodologies are being investigated to provide this energy. This is the case of wave and waste
energy.
Waste management is another concern in the islands due to the limited available storage space.
It is known that Municipal Solid Waste (MSW) can be burnt to generate electricity. Moreover,
implementing heat recovery could lead to economic savings in these kind of plants while pro-
viding a second source of energy. The study of this resource is interesting as water demand, the
amount of MSW and population are directly related.
This work analyzes the water and energy system of Patmos. First, a desalination plant is di-
mensioned in order to cover the water demand with a perspective of 20 years. For that, it is
necessary to predict the population seasonality along the years. Then, different combinations
of solar, wind and Waste-to-Energy plants are proposed to create a sustainable energy system
based on the renewable potential of the island.
The main output of the work is the cost of electricity generation and water production cost,
which allows quantifying the savings of this system compared to the actual one. It is proved that
the systems proposed generate beneﬁts for the government and the population. Water acquires
a competitive cost. Independence from fossil fuels and water importation is achieved.
Funding from the European Union (EU) can be obtained. The investment cost of the Renewable
Energy Technologies (RET) or the desalination plant can be covered up to 50%. An analysis is
carried out to show that the biggest effort should be done in order to ﬁnance the RET, as their
cost most greatly affects the water and electricity generation price.
Finally, the emissions of the different savings are presented in order to help decision makers to
choose the system regarding their environmental impact.

Introduction
1.1
Context
The Earth is mainly composed of water. From the 71% of water covering the planet, only a
small proportion (3%) is sweet water. 69.7% of the sweet water is frozen in the poles and 30%
is underground water, which means that the water found in surface lakes and rivers constitute
only a 0.3% of the sweet water [1]. Moreover, water is unequally distributed and it might seem
contradictory but many islands suffer from water shortage problems.
There are two groups of islands in the Greek territory that are classiﬁed in the category of
arid terrain, where the climate is dry Mediterranean. These archipelagos are Dodecanese and
Cyclades, located in the Aegean Sea, in the Eastern part of Greece. The aquifers are suffering
from salty water intrusion threatening the quality of water reserves [5]. In summer, this problem
it is even more severe, as the population increases up to 5 times, representing the 50% of the
annual needs of water.
The most used solution for this problem is to transport water from the mainland or a bigger
island nearby, but its associated cost is quite high (between 5 and 8 e/m3 as an average [2]).
Nowadays, a total of 20 islands depend on water transportation to supply their needs. This
solution is supported by the subsidies given by the Ministry of the Aegean. Another popular
option is to install desalination plants in order to obtain potable water. The objective of de-
salination is to minimize the number of particles present in the water. These plants can use
seawater or brackish water as an input. Different technologies can be used, mainly classiﬁed
into thermal processes and membrane methods. For the thermal processes, heat is needed, while
for the membrane processes electrical energy is required to ionize or pressurize the water. In
the Aegean Islands, the second method is the most extended. In general terms, and due to the
already installed desalination plants in the Aegean Islands experience, it is possible to claim
that the cost of this solution is lower than water transportation.
However, large amounts of energy are required for desalination. Usually, energy is provided
by diesel generators or another kind of environmentally harmful fuels. With the fragile envi-
ronmental situation that now exists on the planet, the installation of desalination plants would
contribute to the emission of large amounts of greenhouse gasses. The total amount of emis-
sions due to water desalination all around the world is higher than the emissions of a country like
Sweden [3]. An alternative to fossil fuels has to be provided, as there is a need for independence
of external conditions to provide water in a sustainable way and fuel price ﬂuctuations.
On the other hand, most of the islands of the Aegean Sea have isolated electricity systems rely-
ing on fossil fuels. With the yearly increase in energy demand and the unequal distribution of
the population in the islands due to vacations, the generators could not provide enough energy.
In addition, the installation of desalination plants increases the electricity requirements for the
island. Increasing the electricity demand would mean the increase in GHG emissions. Taking
into account that Greece should still reduce its emissions related to the energy sector in order to
comply with Kyoto Protocol and the EU compromise, the share of renewable generation in the
electricity grid has to be increased.

INTRODUCTION
1.2
Renewable energies as a solution
All around the world is possible to ﬁnd renewable resources from which is possible to obtain
energy. The sun is our biggest provider of energy: without sun people could not live on the Earth
as it maintains the planet’s temperature. But the sun also provides light and many environmental
effects such as the wind, which is a consequence of temperature gradients.
The most common process in desalination all around the world and in the Aegean islands is
Reverse Osmosis (RO). The technological advances in this ﬁeld during the last decade led to a
signiﬁcant reduction of its energy requirements. Moreover, the comparison of energy demanded
to desalinate water is lower than the requirements for a thermal desalination plant. However,
the efﬁciency and energy required depending on, among other factors, the quality of the input
water.
Regarding the economical aspect, fuel prices are increasing every year and the tendency seems
to continue [6]. Meanwhile, renewable energies are becoming cheaper as their use is more
spread and the technology is more mature. Depending on the size of the desalination plant
different Renewable Energy Sources (RES) should be analyzed to decide the most suitable and
cheapest option for the given location and its conditions.
The natural resources in the Aegean islands are quite abundant and so, the use of renewable
energies to run desalination plants should be studied for each speciﬁc island. Previous studies
claim the feasibility of these installations in locations such as the Middle East and North Africa
(the area known as MENA), China or the USA.
The ﬁrst desalination technique used was run by solar energy. Sailors would evaporate seawater
in their boat and collect this vapor in order to condense it. The result is water free of salt
and other particles that, when ingested in excess, can damage the organs. Later in time, with
the popularity of fossil fuels, the generation of energy for desalination plants by using diesel
engines became more frequent. This is noticeable mainly in the MENA countries, where most
of the world desalination plants are located. Nevertheless, due to the increasing climate change
concern, renewable energies have become more and more popular. It is the job of the current
generation to promote renewable resources as the primary input for energy generation.
Nowadays, around the 75% of the water consumed in some Greek islands, such as Patmos [5],
is shipped and the increasing need of water due to tourism is making the situation worse. More-
over, even if the natural resources are considerable they are not exploited. Those, apart from
generating energy for the desalination plant, could be used to cover the electricity demand of the
whole island. This demand is going to be increased by the implementation of the desalination
plant, and the isolated electric system of the island has to cover that increase without the help of
other generators in islands nearby. By designing renewable power plants, the diesel generators
available on the island would be only used for peak demand and sudden support. Getting the
most sustainable solution for the energy generation of the island is one of the objectives of this
study.
Much research has been done in this ﬁeld. [2] [7] study the desalination possibilities in the
Aegean islands. Others focus into coupling the desalination plants with renewable energies [8]
[29] [44]. However, the lack of installation of Waste-to-Energy (WtE) plants in Greece offers a
new possibility of sustainable energy generation. It has been decided to add this type of plant

1.3
Renewable energies situation in Greece
Greece’s dependence on fossil fuels is higher than the average of the continent, with a share of
54%. Many islands still rely on inefﬁcient and expensive diesel generators to supply energy.
Diesel and also natural gas is imported at a high price. Greece imports around 61% of the
primary energy required. From domestic energy resources, only 22% corresponds to renewable
energies [20].
As many other European countries, 20% of the gross ﬁnal energy consumption has to be pro-
duced by renewable energies by 2020. This target can be achieved by improving the efﬁciency
of the existing systems and increasing the penetration of RES into the grid, heat supply and in
the transport sector.
Nowadays, many reforms in the energy market, grid infrastructure, and support system are un-
dergoing a transition to facilitate the integration of renewables in the market and to ensure the
accomplishment of the European Union regulations [20]. In 2008, there was a very tight lim-
itation to RES integration in the grid, accounting with a maximum of 18% of the capacity or
30% of the peak demand [5]. However, it was demonstrated that if only technological limita-
tions were taken into account, up to 73% of the generation could be provided by green sources.
Nowadays, this limitation is obsolete. In fact, one of the objectives is to introduce large-scale
wind projects.
From the 41.6 TWh of electricity generation of the interconnected grid, RES accounted for
almost 30% of the installed capacity. However, in the non-interconnected islands, RES contri-
bution was not higher than 22% during 2016. Due to the adjustment of the FIT carried out in

1.4
Current energy policies in Greece
The Greek energy policy has three main objectives [21]:
1. To manage energy resources in a safe, smooth, reliable and uninterrupted way, and to
provide affordable and secure supply of energy to all users
2. To secure energy stocks and protect consumers
3. To lead a sustainable development of the energy sector from the production to its use
while protecting the environment
Those objectives are expected to be achieved by accomplishing some rules. Among them, it is
possible to ﬁnd: to provide access to a wide variety of energy sources, subsidize RES plants,
improve the efﬁciency of houses and industries, or establish targets for emissions reduction
[21].
In order to help the introduction of RES in the system, a Feed-in Tariff (FIT) was provided for a
period of 20 or 25 years, depending on the type and size of the plant. The FIT was technology-
speciﬁc and selected according to the size of the RES installation (smaller plants would beneﬁt
from higher FIT). Law 3468/2006 and Law 3851/2010 regulated this system until the end of
2015 [20].
Due to the increasing share of RES in the energy mix, a new support scheme was introduced, the
Feed-in Premium (FIP). This was expected to facilitate the integration of RES in the electricity
market. From 2016, the electricity generated by renewable resources has to be sold in the
national electricity market and the FIP is granted. The Law 4414/2016 also provides balancing
responsibilities to these plants. The objective of changing the scheme is to encourage RES
generators to adapt their production to price signals and so, the demand [20]. Some small PV
plants keep the FIT but with lower prices, and PV projects larger than 500kW are included in
the tender’s category.
RES are ﬁnanced through a special charge introduced to electricity suppliers which depends on
their share in the market (Law 4414/2016). This charge shall be levied based on avoided average
cost of electricity from the market in case that any RES was available. The rationale of the
introduction of this charge is that renewable energy decreases the wholesale market electricity
price, which results in cheaper electricity for suppliers than in a fossil fuels’ system.

2.1
Desalination
To desalinate water means to separate salt and other particles from the water in order to make
it drinkable. It is a natural process present in the Earth in the called water cycle. The heat of
the sun evaporates water from rivers, lakes, seas and oceans. This vapor is condensed forming
clouds. Finally, water returns to the surface in liquid state as rain, snow or hail, which is already
sweet water. During this process, the particles present in the water remain in solid state and do
not follow all the process.
2.1.1
History
It is possible to ﬁnd the ﬁrst seawater desalination application when sailors were in need of
water. In Figure 3, it is possible to see the representation of a sailor boiling water in a vessel
with a sponge on top. The collected water in the sponge was found to be fresh water.
Figure 3: Sailors producing fresh water from seawater distillation [28]
However, it was not until 1872 that the ﬁrst big desalination plant was created by humans. It was
built in Chile and was called Las Salinas. The objective was to recreate the natural water cycle
by using renewable energies. Wind energy would be used to lift the water from a waterhole and
solar energy to evaporate the water by installing a glass over the open containers of salt water.
Some pipes would collect the steam and lead the clean water to a new container [22].

In the 1950s, desalination plants started to be built all around the world: from the USA to
Australia. Most of them were thermal processes using solar energy. One decade later, four
desalination plants were installed in Greek islands in order to provide fresh water to their in-
habitants [23].
In 2013 was accounted that Saudi Arabia and the United Arab Emirates (UAE) had the largest
commissioned seawater desalination capacity [24]. Some countries as Kuwait and Qatar rely
100% on desalination plants in order to provide fresh water for domestic and industrial supplies.
In the MENA countries can be found more than 38% of the world’s desalination capacity [25].
It is not only because of the increase in the demand and the decrease of water reserves but also
due to the richness in cheap fuel in the area [26].
2.1.2
Technologies
There are two big groups of desalination processes: thermal processes and membrane processes.
Their differences lie in the input energy required and the number of phases in which the water
can be found. Thermal, or phase-change processes, involve distillation of the saline solution.
Membrane processes work with electricity and there is no phase change.
Figure 4: Classiﬁcation of the different technologies used for desalination
It is possible to claim that the efﬁciency of the membrane methods makes them more popular
nowadays, and a total of 65% of the desalination plants installed use Reverse Osmosis (RO).

These plants pump the water through a semipermeable membrane system strong enough so
the fresh water can cross it, while the remaining salts are collected [27]. The temperature of
the intake water and its salinity determines the lifetime of the membrane. The only electricity
needed is the required to pump water in the intake section. It is possible to obtain between 500
and 1500 liters of fresh water per 1 m2 of membrane [28]. To extend the lifetime of the system,

The most common pre-treatments are nano-ﬁltration (NF) and ultra-ﬁltration (UF).

2.2
Renewable powered desalination plants
The total amount of emissions due to water desalination all around the world is higher than the
emissions of a country like Sweden [3]. As water requirements are increasing with time, a more
sustainable way of producing water should be found. Because of that, renewable powered de-
salination plants are becoming more popular, specially in places without fossil fuel reserves and
abundant natural resources. Moreover, many countries are compromising in reducing their CO2
emissions to the atmosphere. Summarizing, renewable powered desalination plants look a good
option to the increasing water demand without damaging the environment. Some examples of
RES desalination plants around the world can be found in [25] and [32].
The three energies that are going to be explored in this project are wind, waste incineration, and
solar energy.
2.2.1
Wind powered desalination plant
In [71], a wind powered desalination plant is designed for variable load in the United Arab Emi-
rates (UAE). It is shown that the water production follows the same pattern as the wind speed.
However, when the wind cannot provide enough energy to produce the base water requirements,
energy is imported from the grid. The number of operating wind turbines and RO units are de-
cided according to the water demand trends. It was decided not to maximize wind penetration,
but to design the wind farm to cover the average demand on the basis of the minimum deviation.
From a technical point of view, it is concluded that over sizing the desalination plant by 50%
was the most attractive solution because it was more energy-efﬁcient due to higher utilization
of wind energy to cover its energy requirements and then, a higher grade of grid independence
can be achieved. In this case, 78-82% of wind penetration is obtained.
This study concludes that the LCOW obtained is within previously reported values and compet-

Study area
The case study of this report is based on the Greek island of Patmos which, even if it is not

However, during summer its population rises up to three times due to tourism. It is classiﬁed as

Figure 11: Patmos island air view [97]
Patmos water problems are present for more than a decade. With the exploitation of wells, water
reserves started to ﬁlter seawater threatening water quality and the droughts occurring, it was

government started shipping water to the island and what it should have been a temporary
solution has been done for more than 10 years already. This means that the price of water can
reach a price higher than 10 e/m3 during the summer season.
Up to the date, Patmos does not have a wastewater treatment plant. Because of that, all the
waste water produced in the island is thrown into the sea. Due to the problems that can stem this
practice (health problems, endangers marine fauna due to eutrophication, a threat to tourism),

obtained to start the construction of the wastewater treatment plant, which is expected to be
working in the second semester of the current year.

3.11
Emissions analysis
In order to address the environmental effects of the different scenarios, the CO2 emissions are
calculated. Only emissions produced during the energy transformation are taken into account,
excluding the ones from commissioning, decommissioning and transportation of fuel and ma-
terials. As it is known, solar and wind energy do not use any chemical transformation and so,
no CO2 is emitted when producing energy. Diesel combustion , however, produces a signiﬁcant
amount of this gas. It can be considered that diesel produces 0.55 tons of CO2/MWh [61].
MSW is another source of GHG emissions. Waste-to-Energy plants are considered to be a
renewable source of energy as the fuel used would be transformed anyways due to natural
processes. However, a part of the content in the waste is composed of fossil derived elements
which are burned producing CO2. This is the reason why only that small share of carbon is
considered in the emission analysis of the plant. According to [62], around one third of the CO2
emitted is due to carbon from fossil origin, although it depends on the MSW composition. For
the current project, 1MWh produced by MSW is assumed to produce 0.33 tonCO2 [81]. If the
same amount of waste was just landﬁlled, the amount of CH4 produced would contain higher
global warming potential [19]. In a time horizon of 20 years, CH4 has 56 times more global
warming potential than CO2, and it needs other 80 years to reduce this potential to half [80].
One of the international measures adopted to limit theCO2 emissions released to the atmosphere
is the called Emission Allowances. One allowance represents 1 ton of CO2. The rationale to
introduce these allowances is to control how much CO2 is emitted by each country. The amount
of allowances decreases each year by around 2% [59]. Public and private entities can trade these
allowances giving rise to a new market.

3.13
Assumptions and limitations
The installation of a desalination plant is the main input. It is assumed that the desalination
plant should cover at least 95% of the water demand in order to achieve independence from
external sources. When the coverage is obtained for the 20th year of the project, it is assumed
that it is to be constant for all the previous years.
The diesel generators will remain in Patmos along the lifetime of the project. As the total power
that they can provide is limited, due to an increase of the demand, Renewable Energy Technolo-
gies (RET) are needed to be installed in the system to provide enough energy. Different RET
conﬁgurations are analyzed and compared. Moreover, the objective of reducing dependence of
fossil fuel leads to the decision of not installing more generators of this kind.
Regarding the electricity demand, it is assumed that every year the energy consumption grows
with the same rate. However, this value could change every year causing deviations from the
calculations done.
The wind data used was recorded almost ten years ago. More than 1-year data would represent
more exactly wind trends and average values. However, trends could also be quantiﬁed by
means of recent wind data of nearby islands. Access to these data was not provided. Due to the
irregular terrain and elevation variation of the island, the measurements are adviced to be taken
in the location of the wind turbines.
The price for the solar panels and wind turbines have been obtained directly from the manu-
facturer’s price. This means that in case of selecting another machinery, the price will vary. In

5
Conclusions and further work
During the realization of the project, different objectives were achieved:
• Modeling of a desalination plant which can cover a signiﬁcant percentage of the water
demand
• Propose different energy systems depending on the share of renewables integration in the
grid
• Present the sensitivity of the scenarios to the different economic parameters
• Calculation of the costs of electricity generation
• Calculation of water production cost and the savings from the actual importation system
• Point out the importance of making efforts in ﬁnding ﬁnance for the project
• Quantifying the emissions of the different energy scenarios and their cost
• For one speciﬁc scenario, compare the total cost of water supply with another desalination
plant size
A tool has been developed to optimize the performance of the desalination plant and the storage
tank. The water balance can be calculated from the model in case the conditions change, or
the size of the desalination units is decided to be different. The distribution and number of
the desalination units can be changed to better ﬁt the demand. It is recommended to continue
working with this model to ﬁnd more accurate results. For that, the market of reverse osmosis
units should be studied carefully but also the distribution of the working units with time. For
example, ﬁtting the demand and supply on a one or two weeks basis instead of monthly.
The installation of the desalination plant provides an economic incentive in comparison to the
actual water importation by ship. Thus, the government is expected to be encouraged to install
it.
The results of the different scenarios are expected to be useful for the decision makers when
selecting the share of renewables in the system. In general, the higher the RES share in the grid,
the lower cost of water and electricity. This means lower costs for the government but also for
the population. Moreover, the climate impact is lower, reducing the amount of CO2 emissions
to the atmosphere and the CH4 emitted due to landﬁll storage.
The investment cost has been proved to have a greater impact on the energy price than the
operational and maintenance costs. The wind energy has been proved to be the cheapest RET
while the WtE is the most expensive one. However, the hybrid system designed can cover at
any moment the base demand. WtE and solar energy are higher in summer, while wind energy
produces its maximum during the winter period. Cheaper wind turbines and solar panels should
be chosen in order to reduce the cost of electricity. For that, it is required a deeper market
research in Greece regarding suppliers and transportation costs to the islands.

CONCLUSIONS AND FURTHER WORK
Higher efforts should be done in getting ﬁnance for the RET than for the desalination plant
price, as the percentage of savings in the system is higher. This happens because the investment
is higher and it affects not only to the electricity production cost but also to the cost of water.
The seasonality of the demand leads to an over-sized system for the low season. In the water
system, the different units can work alternatively to carry out the O&M work and cleaning in
the stopped ones. This is to ensure that they are available when all of them are required. In the
case of RO units, the cleaning is a very important factor regarding the lifetime and efﬁciency of
the plant.
It has been proven the environmental beneﬁts due to the change on both, the water and energy
systems of Patmos. On the one hand, the local production of water leads to high savings in
emissions due to the reduction of water importation. On the other hand, the integration of RES
in the grid shows to have a positive impact because of the reduction of diesel consumed.
Two different desalination plant sizes have been compared for the same energy scenario. It has
been proven that when the price of water obtained to cover the total water demand is higher
than 3.1472 e/m3, the plant with a higher capacity should be installed.
One of the factors that keep the LCOE still high in respect of the mainland is the lack of an
energy storage system. By introducing batteries in the system the renewable production of
electricity could be maximized and the diesel generators could be used fewer hours of the year.
In order to get more accurate results, it is proposed to carry out an optimization of the model for
the energy balance, which can provide the best share of each technology. The limitations due
to terrain characteristics should be introduced and the economic parameters should be studied
accurately.
Other suggestions for further work on this topic are presented:
• The Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) could be carried out. By doing so, the
placement of the power plants can be chosen to minimize the impact on population, ﬂora,
and fauna of the area. The location of the desalination plant and power plants could
be proposed. For that, a suitable area for each plant is needed. The location has to be
strategically chosen to minimize losses due to the distribution system distances.
• The generators are seen to be old, and the objective would be to reduce their use. The
use of the sludge from the wastewater treatment plant and other local resources could be
studied to produce biofuels. Then, the generators should be adapted to this new fuel. This
way, a higher energy independence level could be achieved by Patmos.
• A deeper study of the water distribution system is proposed in order to reduce the losses
of the system and optimize the water of the island.
• The economic study from the private sector point of view could be done in order to look
for investors. For that, some economic parameter should be taken into accounts, such as
the FIP, interest rate and electricity selling price.
