Department of Electrical Engineering

Evaluating and comparing coverage and capacity of Low Power Wide Area
Network technologies
by Alessandro Scarlatti
IoT is a fast growing market. In the context of large scale applications, IoT has shown
a clear need for long-range and low-power type of communication in order to support a
high number of smart devices spread over a wide area. Low Power Wide Area Networks
(LPWAN) oﬀer a low-cost solution to this need. The present study analyzes two LP-
WAN technologies chosen for their interesting technical diﬀerences, namely NB-IoT and
LoRa, though with a stronger emphasis on the latter, which is extensively investigated. A
mathematical model is presented for estimating the oﬀered LoRaWAN network capacity
in the case of an area covered by a single gateway. In addition, the methods used for
evaluating network performance include a simulation framework speciﬁcally developed for
the scope of this study, and a real network setup composed of several LoRaWAN-enabled
modules. By combining results obtained from simulations and from practical tests, it was
found that LoRa can beneﬁt from the use of three transmission bandwidths, with results
that show an increase in capacity of up to 60% when a maximum of 2000 devices within
a cell are considered. The ability of LoRaWAN to support concurrent transmission was
also evaluated, indicating how the combination of 3 frequency channels and 6 orthogonal
spreading factors is beneﬁcial for reducing packet loss in high traﬃc conditions. In ad-
dition, it was demonstrated that the network capacity can be enhanced at zero costs by
optimizing the spreading factor distribution within the network. Lastly, the impact of the
packet’s payload size is considered. The obtained results show that, if too large, it can
degrade the performance of the network by a factor proportional to the increase in ToA
of the packet. It follows that it is a parameter that must be taken into account when
designing large scale IoT applications.

1 Statement of Purpose
The objective of this study is to evaluate network performance in the context of Low
Power Wide Area Networks (LPWANs) for IoT applications. For this purpose, two LP-
WAN technologies are evaluated, namely, NB-IoT and LoRa, chosen for their increasing
popularity. However, LoRa can be considered to be the technology at the center of the
investigation carried out in this study. Furthermore, as part of the main objective, this
study aims at providing the reader with a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the LoRa
technology, which can serve as a tool for understanding its strengths and weaknesses.
1.1
Thesis layout
The structure of this work is as follows: Chapter 2 brieﬂy introduces the topic of the
IoT and its main features and capabilities.
Chapter 3 presents a general overview of
LPWA networks, in terms of their distinctive characteristics, such as low power, long
range, and low cost, among other technical aspects of these types of networks. Chapter 4
focuses on two speciﬁc LPWAN technologies, namely, NB-IoT and LoRa, for comparison
purposes. Both technologies are extensively discussed, however, with a stronger emphasis
on LoRa. Chapter 5 presents a mathematical model for the evaluation of the capacity
in a LoRaWAN network, discussing the advantages deriving from the use of orthogonal
transmissions. Chapter 6 describes the experimental setup used for the tests, from the
modules, to the backend monitoring system. Chapter 7 is a validation of the simulation
framework developed for this work by comparison with results taken from the literature.
Chapter 8 presents the results obtained from the experiments that were conducted, along
with a discussion for interpreting and describe the signiﬁcance of the ﬁndings. Chapter
9 is a conclusion to the project, aiming at summarizing the obtained results as well as
suggesting topics for further research.

2 The Internet of Things
The term Internet of Things (IoT), ﬁrst coined in 1999 by British technology pioneer
Kevin Ashton [7], deﬁnes a network composed of a number of everyday physical objects,
such as sensors, appliances and actuators, that are enabled to send, receive and process
data. These heterogeneous objects are, in fact, embedded with network connectivity and
computing capability, and are uniquely identiﬁed by their IP address. Moreover, they can
communicate and exchange information between each other autonomously and take action
without the need of human intervention.
In the last decade, the number of these objects connected to the internet grew at an
unprecedented rate, as reported by the latest release of the Cisco Visual Networking
IndexTM (VNI) [13]: researchers at the ﬁrm predict that the global IP traﬃc will grow to
278 EB per month by 2021, a threefold increase over 2016. According to the same forecasts,
IP traﬃc is expected to rise at a Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) of 24 percent
from 2016 to 2021, as it can be seen from Figure 2.1. Furthermore, given this exponential
growth of ”things” and smart devices connected to the internet afore mentioned, M2M
communication, which accounts for the exchange of any machine generated traﬃc, is also
expected to grow at a rate of 49 percent by 2021.
Figure 2.1: Cisco VNI IP traﬃc growth forecasts
The trend that is shown by these forecasts can be explained both by the very large number
of applications that can be developed using smart objects and by the tendency of the
industry to make use of IoT technologies, as they are becoming more and more accessible
and have the potential to improve the quality of our lives. Many are the domains where

IoT is already in use and many more are likely to be deployed in the near future. Examples
of these can be found among four major categories [9]:
• Transportation and logistics: sensors are used for traﬃc control, assisted driving
(for improving navigation and safety), and supply chain real-time monitoring (i.e.
purchasing of material, product tracking).
• Healthcare: position tracking is used to determine the location of objects as well
as of patients, in order to make the workﬂow in hospitals better. Identiﬁcation of
patients is another application, and it is used for avoiding incidents that could be
harmful to them (such as wrong treatments). Moreover, wearable technology can be
used to monitor various parameters of a patient’s health condition.
• Smart homes/Oﬃces environment: room heating and room lighting can be au-
tomated and can be adapted to the time of day, and energy savings can be achieved,
for instance, by automatically switching oﬀappliances.
• Personal and social applications: RFID tags attached to personal objects are
used for tracking them and avoid losses, and can be applied for avoiding theft as
well.
2.1
IoT Elements and Requirements
In order to gain a better insight of what IoT really is and what are the services that it can
oﬀer, it is important to understand what the building blocks of this technology really are,
and what elements, and capabilities, are needed for enabling its functionalities [3]. These
elements are the following:
• Identiﬁcation: this aspect is vital for identify each object within the network.
Every device must have its own unique address and identity.
• Sensing: it can consist of a set of sensors and actuators with the purpose of collecting
and transmitting data. They can communicate to users on one end and to gateways
on the other.
• Communication: this element of the IoT is representative of all the communication

3 Low Power Wide Area Networks
In the context of IoT, a new type of wireless communication network, called LPWAN,
is emerging, with the purpose of transmitting data over long distances at a low bit rate,
with consequent savings on power consumption. This type of communication network
diﬀers quite substantially from legacy wireless technologies, for they evolved based on the
needs of human users, which required support for bandwidth-heavy applications, such as
download of large ﬁles, or streaming of video, for instance.
For clariﬁcation, LPWAN is not a standard. It is a term that is used to encompass proto-
cols and implementations that share the same two characteristics, as the name suggests:
Low Power and Wide Area. Devices and smart objects operate on batteries that can last
ten years or more, hence the low power. The other aspect is the wide area: the operating
range of these networks is in the order of kilometers, depending on the area of deployment
(i.e. rural, urban).
These particular types of networks, given their very distinctive features, are the ideal can-
didate for enabling M2M and IoT applications that were previously prohibitive due to
cost, such as smart grids, agriculture, smart metering, among others. In fact, with the
deployment of LPWANs not only is it possible to obtain long battery life for devices and
long range communication, but also enhanced coverage and power eﬃciency. However, all
of these advantages are obtained at the expenses of low data rates. It is, therefore, clear
how not all IoT use cases can be solved with the deployment of this fairly new technology.
LPWANs are considered for applications where high latency is not a major issue, nor is
low data rate. Furthermore, low cost remains one of the main drivers for the adoption
of this type of network, for the number of devices connected to the internet is already
massive and predicted to increase.
Figure 3.1 is very interesting, as it show visually where each type of network that could be
used for IoT applications is located, in terms of data rate, range, and power consumption.
As one can see, the overlap between the various types of networks is very little if not zero,
showing how the use of one over another is very much application-dependent. Cellular
networks such as GSM and LTE for instance, oﬀer wide-area coverage. However, when
it comes to battery consumption the diﬀerence with LPWANs is evident. Bluetooth and
ZigBee are examples of technologies that oﬀer low-power connectivity and good battery
life. Though, they do not satisfy the long range communication requirement.

3.1
LPWANs Distinctive Features
This Section will introduce in detail which are the characteristics of LPWANs that set
them apart from other IoT-type networks. Speciﬁcally, it will be investigated what are
the design goals for LPWA technologies as well as the techniques that allow them to
achieve and bring such improvements to M2M-type communication.
As mentioned before, the main objective of LPWANs is to oﬀer low-power connectivity
over a long range, at a low cost.
3.1.1
Low Power
Low power is obtained mainly thanks to two aspects. Firstly, the network topology used
by LPWANs is a star topology, as opposed to mesh topology networks, as depicted in
Figure 3.2. In a star network, end-devices are connected directly to the access points,
allowing devices to save on battery, as they do not need to receive and repeat the signals
from neighbouring devices. Furthermore, another advantage of this conﬁguration is that
star topology networks are less inclined to experience congestion.
Secondly, the duty cycle, deﬁned as the maximum ratio of time on the air per hour, is a
scheduling mechanism responsible for determining when a speciﬁc device can occupy the
channel and is, therefore, scheduled to transmit or receive. When a device is not on-air
it is possible to turn oﬀspeciﬁc hardware components (i.e. transceivers) and increase its
battery lifetime.
There is a third aspect worth mentioning that plays a role in keeping LPWAN operations
at a low power: complex tasks are carried out by base stations or by the back-end system
rather than the devices, which allows to keep the design of the end-device as simple as
possible. LoRaWAN, which is the LPWA technology that is chosen as focus of this work,
is a great example of the oﬄoading of complex tasks from end-devices. In this case, in
fact, the back-end system is able to adapt certain communication parameters, such as
==PAGE:27==
3.1. LPWANs Distinctive Features

modulation parameters and data rate for maintaining an optimal uplink and downlink
connection. More about this will be presented in Section 4.2.
Figure 3.2: Diﬀerent network topologies: Star Network versus Mesh network
3.1.2
Long Range
Long range is obtained thanks to the use of the Sub-1GHz band (for most LPWA tech-
nologies, although not all), and special modulation schemes. These two aspects combined
together aim at producing around +20dB gain over legacy cellular systems, which allows
end-devices to connect to base stations at longer distances (from a few to tens of kilome-
ters), as reported by [32].
The use of the Sub-1GHz band (typically 433 or 868 MHz in Europe, and 915 MHz in
the U.S.) allows for a reliable and robust communication, as it is less congested than the
2.4 GHz band, used by most wireless technologies. Furthermore, signals transmitted at
low frequencies experience less attenuation, as well as less multipath fading, which oc-
curs when the signal arrives at the receiver via various paths (and not only via the direct
path), as a result of reﬂections and obstacles, causing interference and therefore fading.
However, being that diﬀerent continents use diﬀerent bands and being that the U.S. band
is available in only a third of the world, the process of standardizing LPWAN bands is
nearly impossible [23].
Special modulation techniques make possible for receivers to decode signals which have
been severely attenuated. This is achieved by putting more energy in each transmitted
bit or symbol. Two types of modulation techniques are used by diﬀerent LPWA technolo-
gies, namely, narrow-band and spread spectrum, and will both be investigated in sections
3.5 and 4.2.5, respectively. Furthermore, it should be mentioned that, apart from the
adopted modulation technique, LPWAN receivers have a higher sensitivity compared to
non-LPWAN receivers. In fact, they can detect signals as low as -130dBm, as opposed to
non-LPWAN receivers, which have a sensibility between -90 to -110 dBm [22].

3.1.3
Low Cost
As previously discussed, this aspect of LPWANs is the reason of its commercial success.
The way these technologies achieve such low costs (hardware-wise below 5$, and per unit
connectivity at 1$ [32]) and reduce CAPEX and OPEX for both operators and end-users, is
by using star-type connectivity, by reducing the device’s hardware complexity, by adopting
a very simple network infrastructure, as one base station is able to serve thousands of
devices randomly distributed over a wide area, and, lastly, by using license-free or owned
licensed bands, in order to avoid licensing costs.
3.1.4
Scalability
Another design characteristic to consider when deploying LPWANs is the scalability. This
aspect is fundamental for supporting the exponential increase in number of devices that all
networks are experiencing now-a-days. Though, as LPWANs are designed for supporting
a larger number of devices than any other network, this aspect is of particular interest.
There are diﬀerent techniques that are used to deal with this issue. Among them, the
use of multi-channel and multi-antenna communication (managed by the back-end system
and base stations) is vital for transmitting in parallel to and from the devices. Moreover,
the deployment of more base stations in areas where there is a large quantity of devices,
similarly to what is done with cellular networks, improves the capacity, although some
considerations must be made for avoiding interference.

ISO-OSI Model
Given the quite evident fragmentation of the market caused by the increasing number of
IoT and LPWAN technologies that are oﬀered, it is clear how a communication standard
for applications must be agreed on. The OSI model is a conceptual framework that de-
ﬁnes how data communication should take place between two or more systems. To do so,
the functions of the communication system are standardized into seven diﬀerent groups,
deﬁned as layers. This model is used by the majority of vendors, and developers, and it
is intended to facilitate interoperability among applications. It was created under recom-
mendations from the ISO, and the current standard published in 1996 [39].
Figure 3.4 presents the seven layers of the model. These are, from top to bottom: Applica-
tion Layer, Presentation Layer, Session Layer, Transport Layer, Network Layer, Data-Link
Layer, Physical Layer. Each one has its own unique function and each one provides ser-
vices to the layer above and it is served by the layer below. Data transfer is started at the
top layer, the application layer. It is then passed down, within the same system, to the
physical layer, which then passes it on to the receiving network. When data is received
from the physical layer, it then follows the opposite path, moving up to the application
layer. Furthermore, for the communication to be successful, both systems should run the
same protocol stack.
Being the scope of this work to evaluate and compare speciﬁc LPWAN technologies, the
presentation of this model is quite relevant, as it helps the reader to visually understand
which layer is addressed when discussing capacity and coverage of such networks. Two
are the layers that will be addressed in this work, namely the physical layer, and the data
link layer. The physical layer is responsible for the transmission and reception of raw
bits over the medium, and it provides a standardized interface for the physical medium in
terms of, for instance, mechanical/electrical speciﬁcations. Furthermore, it speciﬁes the
transmission technique to be used, which can largely aﬀect the eﬃciency of the layer. In

The Link Budget
One key parameter that is used for comparison between LPWAN technologies is the link
budget, which allows to evaluate the communication range of the overall system. In fact,
this parameter accounts for all the gains and losses from the transmitter to the receiver,
through the medium, which in this case, being a wireless network, is free space.
It is expressed as:
PRX[dBm] = PTX[dBm] + GTX+RX[dB] −LSY S[dB]
(3.1)
where the terms account for:
–PRX, the received power

3.4. The Link Budget

–PTX, the transmitted power
–GTX+RX, the sum of sender and receiver antenna gains
–LSY S, the sum of all the losses (sender and receiver losses, free space losses, multipath...)
When discussing range, a large link budget is desired. Though, as long as the estimated
received power is large enough for the speciﬁc receiver sensitivity, data can be sent. But
what exactly inﬂuences this parameter?
As seen from the equation, three are the quantities that impact the link budget, namely,
power (sender,receiver), gains (antennas) and losses(medium, multipath...).
Power is
closely related to another quantity: the receiver sensitivity. By deﬁnition, the receiver
sensitivity is the minimum magnitude of the input signal required to achieve the mini-
mum error rate given a speciﬁc signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) [22]. As already mentioned in
Section 3.1.2, LPWAN receivers have, by design, a higher sensitivity than non-LPWAN
receivers. Though, one must make sure that the power of the received signal is above the
receiver threshold, for its correct detection and demodulation.
The other quantity aﬀecting the link budget is the gain. More speciﬁcally, it is the antenna
gain relative to both the transmitter and the receiver, and it is dependent on the speciﬁc
antenna hardware. The antenna gain at the receiver can help reduce or avoid interference
from other directions. At the transmitter, the gain should help focus the available trans-
mitted power in the required direction. The reason why the gain of the antennas must
be included in the equation of the link budget is due to the fact that the power from the
antenna does not spread out equally in all directions.
The biggest issue of the link budget though is to cope with losses. Path losses is the
main contributor when assessing losses, as the travelling electromagnetic waves can be
obstructed by obstacles (i.e. buildings,...) along the way. Path losses account also for
free space losses, which are losses simply caused by the distance that the EM waves travel
before being received. In fact, any transmitted wave is subject to deterioration due to loss
of power as the distance from the transmitter increases. Moreover, losses can be caused
by multipath, which is the phenomenon for which propagating waves get absorbed, scat-
tered, refracted, reﬂected, or diﬀracted by various types of obstructions, losing, therefore,
intensity. Figure 3.5 is a representation of all the factors that are accounted for in the
calculation of the link budget, and their contribution to it. Clearly, path losses deeply
aﬀect this parameter. Though, as mentioned earlier, LPWANs, by decreasing the bit rate,
are able to gain in communication range [12].

LPWAN Modulation Techniques
The term modulation deﬁnes a process where information is added to a periodic waveform,
usually referred to as carrier signal or carrier wave, in order to be transmitted over the
communication channel to the receiver. The modulating signal containing the informa-
tion (i.e. data, speech) is imposed onto the carrier signal, resulting in a variation of its
periodic properties. In fact, information can be added to the carrier by changing either its
amplitude, its frequency, or its phase. Common modulation methods include amplitude
modulation (AM), frequency modulation (FM), and phase modulation (PM).
Bit rate and modulation rate are two important parameters in wireless communication
theory. The bit rate, or data rate, is deﬁned as the number of bits that are processed in
a time unit, and is measured in bits per second. The modulation rate, also called symbol
rate or baud rate, is the number of symbols that are transmitted per time unit, and it is
measured in symbol per second. These quantities are related as shown in Equation 3.2.
SymbolRate =

BitsperSymbol
(3.2)
The symbol rate is also related to the signal bandwidth needed for the communication
channel.
By adopting more complex modulation techniques, which allow for a higher
number of states, it is possible to increase the bits per symbol. It follows that at an equal
bit rate the symbol rate decreases, and the same amount of data is sent over a narrower
bandwidth [2].
This is the case of LPWANs, as they are deployed for applications in
which a high bit rate is not a requirement. Moreover, with a decreased symbol rate, it is
possible to put more energy in each transmitted symbol and obtain a longer communication
range. As a consequence, also the receiver’s sensitivity is increased, as more energy per
symbol means that LPWAN receivers can decode signals that are severely attenuated. To
summarize, narrower transmission bandwidth, increased receiver’s sensitivity, and longer
communication range are all aspects of LPWANs that are obtained at the expenses of a

3.5.1
Ultra Narrow-Band (UNB)
UNB is a modulation method that provides high receiver’s sensitivity, and thus an increase
of the transmission range and of the link budget, by making use of narrow RF channels
(usually less than 1kHz wide)[38]. In fact, by using narrow spectrum channels, most of the
noise is ﬁltered out by narrow receive ﬁlters, thus resulting in low in-band receive noise. In
this type of modulation, it is used a separate narrow-band channel for every transmission,
allowing multiple transmissions to occur simultaneously by using access methods such as
FDMA. The capacity of UNB based systems increases as the channel bandwidth reduces.
However, this has a drawback in terms of hardware. In fact, radio receiver ﬁlters make
use of crystal oscillators because of their stability in terms of a precise center frequency
and steep band-pass characteristics. Due to the aging of the component or changes in
temperatures and humidity for instance, these oscillators can experience an eﬀect called
frequency drift, which introduces an oﬀset in the oscillator from its nominal frequency. If
this oﬀset increases excessively, a transmitted signal could unintentionally be ﬁltered out
by the receiver. To cope with the issue, it is possible to use high-stability crystal ﬁlters,
though at the expenses of higher cost and maintenance [26].

QPSK
As reported by [37], the highest modulation scheme used for NB-IoT is the QPSK, which
stands for Quadrature Phase-Shift Keying. It is a type of PSK modulation, in which data
is transferred by changing the phase of the carrier wave. The simplest type of PSK mod-
ulation is the BPSK (Bi-Phase Shift Keying), which uses two phase states, thus resulting
in a symbol rate equal to 1 bit/symbol. QPSK, on the other hand, is a more advanced
type of phase modulation. It oﬀers a better eﬃciency in terms of used spectrum, as it
uses four diﬀerent phase states, which allow for the transmission of double the informa-
tion over the same bandwidth, when compared to the BPSK [29]. In fact, the use of four
phase states conveys 2 bits/symbol. Figure 3.7 shows the constellation relative to both
BPSK and QPSK. As it can be noticed, in a BPSK modulation, being that the signal has
only two possible phase states, it can represent either a 0 or a 1, thus the 1 bit/symbol
limitation. On the other hand, when looking at the constellation of a QPSK modulation,
each one of the four states is deﬁned by a distinct pair of bits, allowing the transmission
of 2 bits/symbol.
It is now clear why NB-IoT adopts a QPSK modulation over, for instance, a BPSK.
Though, what is the reason behind the choice of a phase modulation technique over a
frequency or amplitude modulation technique in the ﬁrst place?
In short, when com-
pared to phase modulations, amplitude modulations have the disadvantage of being more
susceptible to noise. As for frequency modulations, they require a larger bandwidth.

4 LPWA Network Technologies
In the previous sections the general characteristics of LPWA networks were discussed
qualitatively. The purpose of this section is to give the reader an in-depth overview of
two of these network technologies, namely, NB-IoT and LoRa, which were chosen for
their increasing popularity and their interesting technical diﬀerences in terms of network
architecture, MAC protocol, and physical features [37].
4.1
NB-IoT
NB-IoT, as mentioned in Section 3.2, is the LPWA standard introduced by 3GPP in their
Release 13, with the purpose of providing a solution for wide-are coverage for the IoT.
According to [1], the standardization of this technology was completed in mid 2016, after a
fairly short process lasted 9 months, and integrated into LTE standards. 3GPP recognized
the importance of adding support for IoT features and, with this release, aimed at better
serving IoT use cases, such as the extension of cellular networks coverage, reduction in the
complexity of UE, and the increase of their battery lifetime, among others. But how well
does it integrate with legacy GSM and LTE technologies?
There are three deployment modes speciﬁed by 3GPP, namely, standalone deployment,
LTE guard-band deployment and LTE in-band deployment [8]:
• Standalone Deployment: refers to when operators deploy NB-IoT by making use
of idle network resources, which can derive from the operator’s spectrum fragments
or from other radio access technologies’ unused spectrum. For instance, in standalone
operation, a NB-IoT cell can be deployed by replacing a 200kHz GSM carrier. This
also allows the allocation of a guard-band of 10kHz on both sides of the spectrum.
• LTE guard-band deployment: As the name suggests, this deployment mode
makes use of the already existing LTE spectrum resources by allocating one of the
physical resources blocks (of at least 180MHz) in the guard-band. The reason for
the 180MHz bandwidth requirement comes from the NB-IoT speciﬁcations, which
require, as already mentioned, a minimum of 180MHz for both downlink and uplink
communication, respectively [40].
• LTE in-band deployment: In-band deployment, similarly to the guard-band de-
ployment, makes use of the LTE resource blocks, only this time, in-band.
It is
important to specify that, for this particular deployment mode, there is no limita-
tion within the LTE carrier on which PRBs to use for uplink and non-anchor carriers.
However, for the downlink anchor carrier, the values are set.

The LTE design is reused quite extensively for NB-IoT, which is also the explanation for
the short time that was required to standardize this technology. It uses, in fact, the same
licensed frequency bands that are used in LTE, as opposed to non-licensed ones used, for
instance, in LoRa. However, LTE features, such as handover, measurements for monitoring
channel quality, carrier aggregation, and dual connectivity were removed in order to save

The similarities with LTE can be found in many aspects of the air interface, as shown
from the physical layer’s uplink and downlink transmission schemes of this IoT technology
outlined in the next two sections.
4.1.1
Downlink Transmission Scheme
The downlink transmission scheme is based on OFDMA, and, like LTE, uses a 15kHz sub-
carrier spacing. As mentioned before, the full carrier bandwidth required for NB-IoT is
180kHz, resulting in OFDM symbols consisting of 12 sub-carriers, equivalent to one LTE
PRB. Seven of these OFDMA symbols make up one slot, which has a duration of 0.5ms,
and it is characterized by the resource grid seen below, in Figure 4.1:

Two slots form one sub-frame of a duration of 1ms (2x0.5ms), and ten sub-frames create
one frame, whose duration is 10ms. Furthermore, by using the same numerology as LTE,
it is possible to maintain the orthogonality between the NB-IoT PRB and LTE PRBs,
and therefore facilitating the coexistence performance in the downlink.

4.1.2
Uplink Transmission Scheme
The uplink transmission scheme is based on SC-FDMA and can support both single-
tone and multi-tone transmissions. The latter uses the same sub-carrier spacing and slot
duration as LTE. It is an optional capability with the purpose of providing higher data
rates in conditions of normal coverage. Single-tone transmission, on the other hand, can
support two diﬀerent numerologies, namely, 15kHz and 3.75kHz. The slot duration for
the 3.75kHz single-tone numerology can easily be derived and it is equal to 2ms. However,
the best coexistence performance is still obtained when using the same numerology as
LTE, and that is relative to the 15kHz sub-carrier spacing. The total system bandwidth
required for the uplink is equal to that of the downlink, and that is 180khz.
4.1.3
Network Architecture
When looking at the network architecture, the analogies with LTE are evident. The two
components that make up the overall architecture are, as for LTE, the access network and
the core network.
The architecture of the access network remains unchanged from that of LTE. In fact,
NB-IoT uses the same access network standard of LTE, which is the Evolved Universal
Terrestrial Radio Access Network (E-UTRAN) [5]. It is made up of a single node, the
evolved NodeB (eNB), that interfaces directly with the UEs for reducing latency derived
from radio interface operations. Even though there is no handover speciﬁed for NB-IoT,
eNBs are still connected to one another via the very same X2 interface for enabling a fast
resume of the UE, after it has gone to idle state [33].
However, two diﬀerent types of optimization for the cellular internet of things (CIoT)
were introduced in the core network, referred to as Evolved Packet Core (EPC), in order
to support IoT applications, namely, the User Plane CIoT EPC optimization and the
Control Plane CIoT EPC optimization. The user plane CIoT EPC optimization allows
both IP and non-IP data to be transmitted over radio bearers via the SGW and the PGW
to the application server. On the Control Plane CIoT EPC optimization, uplink data is
transferred from the UE to the MME through the evolved base station, or eNodeB. At
this point there are two paths that are deﬁned: IP data will be transferred to the serving
gateway (SGW), then to the Packet Data Network Gateway (PGW), and ﬁnally forwarded
to the application server. Non-IP data, on the other hand, is transferred from the MME to
the newly deﬁned node SCEF, to the application server. The SCEF is a node speciﬁcally
designed for delivering machine-type data. In addition, it provides an abstract interface
for network services[33]. Figure 4.2 illustrates the network structure just described. What
is interesting to notice is that, with the introduction of the SCEF, there are multiple paths
deﬁne for handling user data. The paths colored in blue show the user plane CIoT EPC
optimization, which transfers data as conventional LTE. The paths in red, on the other
hand, are relative to the Control Plane CIoT EPC optimization. In summary, it is possible
to notice how the E-UTRAN architecture as well as the core network can be extensively
reused for NB-IoT, facilitating its deployment.

4.1.4
Evaluating NB-IoT in Terms of IoT factors
This section will present some considerations regarding the performance achieved by NB-
IoT in terms of IoT requirements. The purpose is to give a brief overview of this technology
taking into consideration factors such as battery life, quality of service, deployment mode,
network coverage, and cost [37][27]:
• Battery life: NB-IoT can provide a battery life up to +10 years, under the as-
sumption that devices transmit 200 bytes per day on average. However, due to the
fact that it works on a cellular network, devices are required to perform regular
synchronization activities, which, as a consequence, will shorten the battery life. In
addition, its OFDM/FDMA access method increases the energy consumption, as it
requires more peak current. Overall, NB-IoT has a worse performance in terms of
battery life when compared, for instance, with LoRa technology.
• Quality of service: NB-IoT, by using licensed bands and LTE-based synchronous
protocol, oﬀers a high quality of service. However, it is achieved at the expenses of
a higher cost.
• Deployment mode: the deployment of NB-IoT is restricted to areas in which LTE
is supported, as it is deployed by upgrading existing cellular networks. The area in
which is deployed should have 4G coverage, thus it is not ideal for rural or suburban
areas.
• Network coverage: NB-IoT has a shorter range and coverage capabilities com-
pared to other LPWAN technologies.
In fact, its range is approximately 10km,
where other technologies have a range of roughly 20km (LoRa), and the coverage is
limited to regions in which are present 4G/LTE base stations.
• Cost: The major costs of this technology derive from the use of a licensed spectrum,
which is priced at over 500 million dollars per MHz. In addition, the network upgrade

4.2
LoRa and LoRaWAN
The second LPWAN technology that will be investigated is LoRa, which is also the main
subject of study for this work.
In the recent years, LoRa is gaining popularity among LPWAN technologies for IoT appli-
cations, as it can, similarly to NB-IoT, oﬀer all of the advantages typical of such networks,
which are wide area coverage, low power operations, secure data transmission, as well as
communications that are multipath and Doppler resistance, among others. The LPWAN
solution that LoRa oﬀers includes two main components, namely LoRa, and LoRaWAN.
Often, these terms are used interchangeably in the literature, however they deﬁne two
very diﬀerent aspects of this technology:
LoRa, which is short for ”Long Range”, deﬁnes the physical communication layer (see
Figure 3.4, Section 3.3) that uses the proprietary spread spectrum modulation technique
developed by Semtech. This modulation is a derivative of the Chirp Spread Spectrum
(CSS), and it integrates Forward Error Correction (FEC) [11].
LoRaWAN, on the other hand, refers to the most popular MAC layer used for LoRa, and
developed by the LoRa Alliance as an open standard [35]. The Alliance, founded in 2015
by industry leaders in Barcelona at the Mobile World Congress, is an open, non-for-proﬁt
organization promoting the standardization and interoperability of LPWANs, with the
ultimate purpose of unifying the fragmented wireless market and subsequently improve
the ROI, as described in their mission statement [6]. Among the LoRa Alliance members
are found multi-nationals telecommunication companies, equipment manufacturers, and
system integrators, creating a strong organization covering all aspect of the IoT ecosystem.
4.2.1
The 863-870MHz ISM Band
LoRaWAN operates in unlicensed frequency bands, avoiding therefore licensing costs and
making this technology particularly suitable for low-cost applications. It utilizes the In-
dustrial, Scientiﬁc and Medical (ISM) radio bands, which, depending on the region of
deployment, have diﬀerent speciﬁcations, in terms of spectrum allocations and regulatory
requirements. For instance, LoRaWAN uses the 902-928MHz band in North America, the
779-787MHz band in China, and the 863-870MHz band in Europe [24]. Being this work
centered on the evaluation of LoRa in an European context, the focus of this Section will
be on the EU 863-870MHz band and its regulations, for a better understanding of the
LoRa limitations.
The EU ISM spectrum use is deﬁned by the ETSI standard. The 863-870MHz ISM band,
or in short the 868MHz ISM band, is a fraction of the license-free spectrum allocated for
Short Range Devices (SRD), and it is used in LPWA networks, such as LoRa. The use of
this band is subject to regulations on bandwidth, Eﬀective Radiated Power (ERP), and
on the spectrum access method, which sets a limit on the time for which a device can
transmit per hour. Regarding the latter, the ETSI regulation allows a choice between two
options: a duty cycle limitation, or a Listen Before Talk (LBT) transmission management
[21]. Currently, LoRa relies on a duty cycle limitation of 1%, meaning that a device can
transmit a maximum of 36sec/hour, calculated using Equation 4.1. This type of limitation,

This section will present the architecture of a LoRa network. Typically, its conﬁguration

end-devices, LoRa Gateways, and LoRa Network Servers. These are interconnected as

signal (i.e. the SNR), and forwards it directly to the LoRa Server over a backhaul interface

This architecture is a clear example of how LPWANs networks, by design, aim at reducing

mentioned in Section 3.1.1. LoRa end-devices, in fact, unlike end-devices of traditional
cellular networks, are not associated with a speciﬁc gateway. Packets are usually received

As already mentioned, however, network servers have the ability of ﬁltering these dupli-
cates. Furthermore, there is another complex operation that this conﬁguration allows to
avoid: if a device can send and be picked up by multiple gateways, it follows that no
handover is needed.
4.2.3
Device Classes
LoRaWAN deﬁnes three diﬀerent classes of end-devices, namely, Class A (default), Class B
(optional), and Class C (optional). These classes diﬀer for their downlink communication
latency, allowing for a trade-oﬀwith the power consumption and, ultimately, the battery
life.
• Class A: Class A devices have the lowest power consumption. They support bi-
directional communication with the gateway, by using two short downlink receive
windows (RX1 and RX2) that open after every uplink transmission. RX1 uses the
same data rate and frequency channel as the transmission slot, by default. For RX2,
on the other hand, these two parameters can be conﬁgured and negotiated between
gateway and end-device. Furthermore, if a frame is received during RX1, for in-
stance, RX2 will not open [30]. These are battery powered devices, characterized by
a high latency. They are used for applications that only require downlink communi-
cation from the server shortly after the end-device has sent an uplink transmission.
In fact, downlink communications from the server at any other time would have
to wait for the next scheduled device’s uplink [10]. Class A is an ALOHA-based
protocol.
• Class B: Class B devices oﬀer the same functionalities of Class A, though adding
receive windows at scheduled times.
In order for the server to know when the
device is listening, and for the device to open the RX windows at the scheduled
time, a beacon, sent by the gateway to the device, is required. These devices are
characterized by low latency.
• Class C: Class C devices, similarly to Class A and B, oﬀer bi-directional commu-
nication. However, devices of this class further reduce latency by maximizing the
receive slots. In fact, their receive window is nearly continuously open, except when
transmitting. These devices are used mainly for application with suﬃcient power
available, as they are characterized by a high power consumption.

4.2.4
Transmission Parameters
LoRa oﬀers ﬁve transmission parameters that can be arbitrarily conﬁgured for obtaining
speciﬁc resilience to noise, energy consumption, and transmission range. These parameters
are, namely, Transmission Power (TP), Carrier Frequency (CF), Spreading Factor (SF),
Bandwidth (BW) and Coding Rate (CR) [11], and are hereby introduced:
• Transmission Power: LoRa radio allows TP values that range between [-4,20]
dBm.
However, due to hardware limitation, the lower limit shifts up to 2dBm.
Moreover, the power can be adjusted in steps of 1dB.
• Carrier Frequency: It is the center frequency, and it can be chosen between
137MHz to 1020MHz, in steps of 61Hz. However, there might be limitations on the
frequency range, depending on which LoRa chip is used.
• Spreading Factor: it is deﬁned as the ratio between the symbol rate and the chip
rate. It is a parameter that it must be chosen carefully as it impacts the receiver’s
sensitivity, the SNR, and the airtime.
The value of the spreading factor can be
selected between 7 and 12. A higher SF increases the sensitivity of the receiver and
the SNR. However, it also increases the time on air of the device, for each increment
of the SF doubles the duration of the transmission. This has obvious consequences
on the energy consumption as well.
• Bandwidth: Data is transmitted at a chip rate equal to the bandwidth. It follows
that a larger bandwidth produces a higher data rate, and therefore shorter time on
air. However, this is at the expenses of the introduction of additional noise in the
channel, which has the eﬀect of lowering the receiver’s sensitivity. The bandwidth
==PAGE:46==
30

of a LoRa network can be selected between 7.8 kHz and 500 kHz. However, typical
values that are used are 125kHz, 250kHz, and 500kHz.
• Coding Rate: The coding rate is actually the FEC rate used by LoRa. It can be
set to the following values: 4/5, 4/6, 4/7 or 4/8, and it oﬀers protection against
interference.
Again, a trade-oﬀmust be made, as a higher value, which means
stronger protection to interference, also increases the time on air.

the reader to understand the contribution of each to the transmission and robustness of
the modulated signal.
4.2.5
LoRa PHY Modulation Scheme
As mentioned in Section 4.2, LoRa modulation is a derivative of the CSS. This type of
modulation was developed ﬁrstly for radar applications, used for military and secure com-
munication purposes [34]. However, in the last decade, because of its unique properties,
such as low transmission power requirements and intrinsic robustness to channel degrada-
tion, deriving from multipath and Doppler eﬀect, it has been adopted for a various number
of data communication applications.
In order to encode symbols, this modulation makes use of chirp signals, which are charac-
terized by a constant amplitude and by a frequency that increases or decreases overtime,
deﬁning up-chirps and down-chirps, respectively (Figure 4.5 and 4.6). It is a modulation
based on spread spectrum, meaning that the signal is spread over the whole channel band-
width, and typically received below the noise ﬂoor. Contrary to what could be assumed,
the apparent less eﬃcient use of the spectrum is compensated by the use of orthogonal
transmission, as it will be later described in Section 5.2. This discussion will begin by
introducing the basic quantities of this modulation, and following, an example of a real
LoRa modulated signal will be given, and its properties analyzed. It is important to re-
member that the LoRa modulation is a proprietary technology, and, as such, it is not fully
open. However, by using the documentation available, it is possible to understand quite
extensively its working principles, for the sake of a thorough and precise description.

In the previous section, the format of a LoRa packet and all of its components were

is extracted from it.

Looking at the ﬁgure, the ﬁrst thing that can be noticed is how the signal is spread over
the whole channel bandwidth. This proves the use, by LoRa, of a Spread Spectrum Mod-

a series of 8 up-chirps, which, by recalling the structure of the packet presented earlier,

preamble symbols there are 2.25 down-chirps. These make up the sync word that precedes
the incoming data. After the sync word the up-chirps begin again, but this time they are
quite fragmented. These are actually the encoded symbols that make up the payload, and
thus contain information. Though, how can the information actually be extracted and
decoded?

Similarly to the analysis presented for NB-IoT in Section 4.1.4, this section will present a
brief description of the performance achieved by LoRa technology when evaluated in terms
of IoT requirements. The discussion will take into consideration the following factors:
battery life, quality of service, deployment mode, network coverage, and cost [37][27]:
• Battery life: LoRaWAN can guarantee a very long battery life, usually of +10
years.
This is achieved thanks to the fact that LoRaWAN is an ALOHA-based
protocols. Devices can remain in sleep mode as long as the application wishes, and
wake up only when there is data to be sent. As opposed to NB-IoT, devices in a
LoRaWAN network do not perform any synchronization activity, thus saving on the
overall battery consumption.
• Quality of service: thanks to the CSS modulation adopted by LoRa, factors
impacting the QoS (such as, for instance, multipath fading and Doppler eﬀect) are
mitigated. However, the ALOHA-based access method does not oﬀer the same QoS
as time slotted and synchronous communication protocols, such as that used for
NB-IoT, resulting in an overall lower quality of service.
• Deployment mode: LoRaWAN is an open standard and, as such, it can be de-
ployed by anyone. Network operators, companies, or single individuals can easily
set up their own network and infrastructure. Major LoRaWAN networks that are
currently being deployed in Europe can be found in France, in Belgium, and in
Germany.
• Network coverage: The coverage of a single gateway can reach up to 5 km in
urban areas, and up to 20km in rural areas. In addition, the coverage can extend to
areas notoriously hard to reach, such as basements and areas below street level.
• Cost: when evaluating cost, the advantages of the LoRa technology are evident.
Operating in a license-free spectrum, there is no cost related to the use of the band.
The cost per device is in the order of 5-10$. In addition, the cost per gateway is
relatively low: as reported by [37], the cost per gateway can be between 100-1000$.
4.6
Summary
NB-IoT technology is a standard for LPWAN introduced by 3GPP. Many are the sim-
ilarities between this technology and LTE systems, as it can be seen from its downlink
and uplink transmission schemes. Two interesting improvements were introduced in the
core network, which enable and facilitate the integration of this technology into the LTE
network architecture.
LoRa technology is characterized by a simple network architecture. Three device classes
are deﬁned, which diﬀer for how they handle downlink communications, allowing for a
trade-oﬀbetween power consumption and latency. The proprietary modulation scheme
typical of LoRa is based on CSS and, because of its robustness against interference, is ideal

characterizes such network. The obtained results are relative to a network conﬁguration in

5.1
Overview of the main features of MAC protocols
Generally, MAC layers can be grouped into two categories, according to the adopted
medium access strategy [4]. These categories are, namely, contention-based protocols, and
time-slotted protocols. There is a third category that is composed by hybrid protocols,
which combine the strategies adopted by the two categories afore mentioned. However,
for the purpose of this discussion, hybrid protocols are out of the scope.
In contention-based protocols, end-devices do not require synchronization before transmit-
ting data and can therefore access the medium at anytime. Examples of such protocols
are CSMA and ALOHA. Time-slotted protocols, on the other hand, partition the time in
slots. Every end-device is subsequently assigned to a particular slot in which it can trans-
mit. These protocols require a tight synchronization among the devices in the network.
==PAGE:58==
42
Chapter 5. LoRaWAN Capacity Evaluation
In conditions of low/medium traﬃc loads, contention-based protocols perform better in
terms of latency. In addition, the joining procedure is faster, compared to that of time-
slotted protocols, as there is no need of re-conﬁguring the slots whenever an end-device
joins the network. However, in high traﬃc load conditions, the advantages brought by
the scheduling process are evident: in fact, contention-based protocols will experience a
higher packet collision rate, due to the absence of synchronization among the end-nodes.
In addition to the access method strategy just described, there is another main feature
of MAC layers that inﬂuences the network capacity, and that is whether the protocol
uses a single-channel communication or a multi-channel communication. As the name
suggests, in the case of single-channel communications, the end-nodes do not use diﬀerent
frequency channels, they share the same. This can lead to possible congestion in case
of high traﬃc loads. Multi-channel communication, on the other hand, increases robust-
ness against congestion and interference, by allowing transmissions on multiple parallel
frequency channels. Furthermore, it increases network capacity, as multiple transmission
can occur simultaneously.
5.2
Collision Behaviour
LoRaWAN is an ALOHA-based protocol, and as such, categorized as contention-based.
Devices access the medium randomly and transmit asynchronously in time (without check-
ing for channel occupancy). However, to improve the throughput, LoRaWAN makes use
of multiple channels for transmission. In fact, it speciﬁes three channels (868.10 MHz,
868.30 MHz, and 868.50MHz) in which end-devices can transmit in, and gateways receive
on. Multi-channel transmission, as previously mentioned, is one eﬀective way of improv-
ing network capacity and thus reduce packet collision: packets transmitted and received
on diﬀerent channels do not interfere with each other, under any condition. For further
reducing the probability of collision, LoRa makes use of six spreading factors (SF7-SF12)
to create orthogonal signals that can be simultaneously transmitted and received on the
same channel without interference (see Section 8.4).
Then, under which conditions is
packet collision experienced in LoRaWAN networks? For answering this question, it is
necessary to look into the time domain. For the investigation, it will ﬁst be presented the
probability of collision calculated for a simpliﬁed scenario. The model will then be ex-
panded to quantify the advantages brought by the use of multiple channels and spreading
factors. The maximum throughput of a LoRaWAN network will be obtained, in terms
of both the maximum number of devices per coverage area that maximize the number of
sent packets per day, and the maximum number of supported device per coverage area,
under the assumption that only one packet per device is sent per day.

Summary
LoRaWAN is a contention-based protocol, whose method to access the medium can be
compared to the well known ALOHA protocol. However, LoRaWAN takes advantage of
a multi-channel transmission and of orthogonal spreading factors in order to increase the
capacity and throughput of the network. In fact, by the combination of 3 frequency chan-
nels and 6 orthogonal spreading factors, 18 non-interfering channels are obtained, which
allow for an increase of the network throughput, compared to that of a Pure ALOHA, of
a factor equal to the number of non-interfering channels, under the assumption of perfect
synchronization among transmissions. This eﬀect can be seen theoretically in terms of
throughput. However, it is hardly achieved in real networks, as devices do not make use
of mechanisms for synchronizing packet transmissions.

UART Serial Communication
Data is exchanged between the host controller and the modules through the Universal
Asynchronous Receiver-Transmitter (UART) serial port. As the name speciﬁes, it is an
asynchronous serial interface, meaning that it does not use an external clock signal to
==PAGE:71==
6.1. The Hardware Components
55
keep both sides in sync (as opposed to the SPI interface, for instance), resulting in less
wires needed for the communication. Furthermore, it is a highly conﬁgurable port, as it
allows to conﬁgure the baud rate (data speed over the serial line), the amount of data
in each packet, the parity bit (for error checking), and the stop bit (for synchronization
purposes). However, it is crucial that when two devices are connected through the UART
port, they use the same parameters, as failing to do so leads to data loss, just as two people
speaking two diﬀerent languages. The conﬁguration used in this work is the following:
baudrate=19200bps, data size=8 bits, parity=none, stop bits=1, and it was conﬁgured
through Minicom.
Let’s now look at the wiring conﬁguration of the port. It is a port that requires only
three wires/serial pins, namely, the receiver (RX), the transmitter (TX), and the ground
(GND), as depicted in Figure 6.3. For a successful communication, it is important that
the TX of one device is connected to the RX of the other. Similarly, the RX of the very
same device should be connected to the TX of the other. In addition, a common ground
should be created by connecting together the two GND pins.
Figure 6.3: Wiring of the UART interface
For the scope of the experiments carried out in this work, however, it is necessary to
connect the host controller to not only one other device, but to a carrier board, and thus
8 devices. The RPi must be able to communicate both with each single module and with
all of them at once. How is that achieved? As shown in Figure 6.4, each module on the
board has both the TX and the RX wires. The 8 RX wires are connected in series on a
breadboard, together with the TX wire coming from the RPi. This allows data transfer
from the host to all the modules, simultaneously. As for the 8 TX wires, only one is
used and it is connected directly to the RX pin of the RPi. This means that the host
can only receive data from one module at a time.
However, because of the design of
the experiments, downlink traﬃc (from the gateway to the modules, to the host) can be
neglected, as it is not investigated. On the other hand, uplink traﬃc (from the host to
the modules, to the gateway) is fully supported. Lastly, by connecting/disconnecting the
RX pins to/from the breadboard, it is possible to decide the number of active nodes, as
every connected RX pin means one connected device.

6.3
Device Registration
For participating in a LoRaWAN network, every module needs to be activated.
The
activation of an end-device can be done in two ways, namely, the Over The Air Activa-
tion (OTAA), and the Activation By Personalization (APB). After the completion of the
activation process, the device stores the following information:
• DevAddr: a 32-bit unique identiﬁer (within the network). It is shared between the
end-device, and the network and application servers
• AppEUI: is a global application ID that allows the unique identiﬁcation of the
application provider
• NwkSKey: is a 128-bit encryption key. It is shared between the end-device and
the network server, as seen in Figure 6.5. It is used for providing message integrity
and for security purposes
• AppSKey: is a 128-bit encryption key. Shared between the end-device and the
application server, it provides security for the app payload, and used to encrypt and
decrypt the payload ﬁeld
Let’s now look at the two diﬀerent ways of activating a device:
• In an OTA activation, the end device sends a join request to the application server.
The join request contains three IDs, namely, the DevEUI (a global ID that uniquely
identiﬁes the speciﬁc end-device), the AppEUI, and the AppKey (assigned by the
owner of the application and used to derive the NwkSKey and the AppSKey). After
having received a Join Request, the application server sends back to the device a Join
Accept, with which the end-device can derive the two session keys needed (NwkSKey,
AppSKey). For this type of activation, every time that a device loses the session
context information, it has to go through a new join procedure.
==PAGE:74==
58
Chapter 6. Experimental Setup
• In the APB type of activation, a device is linked directly with a speciﬁc network,
thus a Join Request is not necessary. Both the NwkSKey and the AppSKey are
directly stored into the device as well as the DevAddr.
In general terms, it should be mentioned that the ABP is less secure compared to the
OTAA, because of the fact that the encryption keys are stored directly in the device, and
could easily be stolen. However, in this work, as there was no risk of losing the LoRa
modules, every device was activated through APB, as it has the advantage of simplifying
the procedure for joining the network.

7 The Simulation Framework
For the purpose of this study, it is made use of a simulation framework developed using
the software Matlab, with the intention of replicating the behaviour of a real LoRaWAN
network. The model is used for comparison with real results obtained from the actual tests
as well as for examining scalability and capacity of large-scale LoRa networks (meaning
with a high number of active nodes). In fact, evaluating the capacity of such networks
would be prohibitive in practice due to deployment costs.
7.1
Simulation Capabilities
The framework is able to simulate an arbitrary number of active devices, transmitting an
N number of packets each, over a period of time that can be adjusted according to the
scenario that one would like to simulate. For the simulations presented in this study, the
number of packets sent per device was always set equal to 1, thus allowing to neglect the
duty cycle limitation, and the time frame was equal to 1 minute, chosen to be compliant
with the running time of the practical tests. The packets transmitted by each simulated
device are characterized by three parameters, namely, payload size, spreading factor, and
bandwidth. The combination of these three quantities determine the ToA of each trans-
mission. In addition, one additional parameter that can be conﬁgured is the number of
frequency channels used for transmission. Throughout the experiments presented in this
study, the number of speciﬁed channels is always to be considered equal to 3, unless stated
otherwise. To simulate a real network, where devices are not synchronized when trans-
mitting, the transmission starting time of each device was randomized. As described in
Chapter 5, packet collision in a LoRaWAN network is experienced when packets trans-
mitted simultaneously use the same SF and the same channel, which is also how collision
is modeled in the simulation framework. Given each transmission, it is then possible to
compute the packet collision and evaluate the network performance in terms of the PRR
(see Section 8.1), with respect to the number of devices.
7.2
Simulation Limitations
As every model presents its own limitations, this model does too. In fact, now that it was
described what could be simulated, it is equally important to understand what features
could not be simulated, which include the following: every simulation assumed a scenario
in which only one gateway was present, thus each time a collision occurred both packets
were lost, eliminating the probability that either of the collided packets could have been
picked up by another gateway.
==PAGE:78==
62
Chapter 7. The Simulation Framework
Secondly, the framework does not model communication range nor the distance to the
gateway, thus it does not take into account propagation losses and it works under the
assumption that every packet can be correctly received (unless it experiences a collision),
regardless of the receiver sensitivity.
Lastly, ADR was not modeled, as it is a mechanism for optimizing energy consumption in
the network based on a history of SNR measurements performed by the gateway.
7.3
Validation of the Model
To ensure that the results obtained from the simulations are of practical relevance, the
processes within the scripts are designed and validated in accordance with the oﬃcial Lo-
RaWAN speciﬁcations released by the LoRa Alliance [25]. For instance, it is respected the
orthogonality between transmissions characterized by diﬀerent bandwidths, SF values, and
on diﬀerent channels. Furthermore, the ToA is calculated accordingly with the equations
deﬁned in the speciﬁcations, and presented in Section 4.3 of this work. To further convince
the reader of the validity of the simulation framework, it will be presented a comparison
with the results obtained by Haxhibeqiri et al. in [19]. In their work, the authors, using
real measurements based on transmission interference in a LoRaWAN network, developed
a simulation model (for assessing scalability) that is closely linked to the one developed
for the present study in terms of what the simulation can evaluate. The authors presented
the results of the percentage relative to the total number of packet lost due to collision
and due to a reception with wrong payload CRC, as a function of the number of devices.
In addition, they also computed the total throughput, again, as a function of the number
of devices. As the simulation used in this study does not model throughput nor collision
due to a reception with wrong payload CRC, the comparison will only be in terms of the
results obtained for the packet lost due to collision. In fact, it is a quantity that is closely
related to the metric of interest of this work, which is the packet reception rate. Two
scenarios are compared, and are deﬁned as follows:

8 Tests and Results
In this Chapter it will be investigated, by means of computer simulations and practical
experiments, the performance of LoRa technology in terms of network capacity. Four tests
will be presented: the ﬁrst test will quantify the advantages that could derive by the use of
three transmission bandwidths. The second test will present three network conﬁgurations
that give an idea of the best and worst performance that can be achieved by a LoRaWAN
network. The third test will expand on the second test by presenting two additional, and
more realistic, network conﬁgurations. Test 4 evaluates the impact that diﬀerent payload
sizes have on the overall packet reception.
8.1
Evaluation Metric
For evaluating the LoRaWAN network performance, it is deﬁned the following metric,
namely, the Packet Reception Rate (PRR). It is expressed as the ratio between the number
of received messages over the total number of messages sent, as shown in the following
equation:
PRR = Received packets
Sent packets
(8.1)
This metric is a value ranging between [0,1], where 1 is obtained in the ideal case in which
all packets sent are received correctly. On the other hand, the closer the value is to 0 the
worse the performance is.
8.2
Hardware Testbed Limitations
For being able to design, predict and make sense of the results obtained from the tests
that are conducted, it is crucial to be aware of the limitation that the setup of the testbed
introduces. There are four types of limitations that aﬀect the modules, which can aﬀect
either the results or the number of tests that can be conducted.
The ﬁrst limitation derives from the fact that LoRaWAN is an evolving protocol and, as
such, some of its features are not yet fully supported. This limitation concerns the CR,
which for the ARM module used in this work is ﬁxed and equal to 4/5. Values that will
be supported in future LoRaWAN evolution are 4/6, 4/7, and 4/8.
The second limitation regards the physical setup of the modules. In fact, by being mounted
on carrier boards, and thus designed to only transmit simultaneously, it is possible to incur
in periodic collisions, which can lower the overall network performance.
The third limitation is about the conﬁguration of the modules. In fact, for every module,
the ADR function could not be activated, regardless the numerous attempts of conﬁguring
==PAGE:82==
66
Chapter 8. Tests and Results
it from the command mode of the module itself.
The fourth limitation concerns the transmission bandwidths, and it is due to regional
band regulations. Currently, as reported by [24], LoRa supports 6 diﬀerent data rates
(DR0 to DR5), corresponding to the number of spreading factors available.
However,
for all of the available DR the transmission bandwidth is always 125kHz.
LoRaWAN
forecasts the addition of two extra bandwidths, namely, 250kHz and 500kHz, which will
contribute to enhance the capacity of the network. Nonetheless, for the scope of evaluating
LoRaWAN in the most comprehensive way possible, a simulation is created to investigate
the improvements that the use of three bandwidths for transmission could bring on the
capacity, and it is presented in the next section.

8.6
Test 4: Impact of the payload on the PRR
The last test that is presented examines the impact that the packet’s payload size has on
the achieved PRR. For this experiment, the modules conﬁguration is the same as that of
Setup 2 described in the previous section. The decision of re-using the same conﬁguration
derives from the fact that Setup 2 can simulate the maximum number of devices possible
with the hardware setup that was available (see Section 6.4.1). In fact, the reader should
recall that Setup 2 uses all of the 10 available modules, allowing the simulation of a total
of 150 devices (as each module is programmed to send 15 packets within 1 minute).
Three diﬀerent payload sizes were tested, namely, a 1 byte payload, a 20 byte payload,
and a 51 byte payload. The reason behind the choice of not increasing the payload size
to more than 51 bytes derives from the fact that not all spreading factors can support
payload sizes larger than 51 bytes, as it was shown in Table 4.1. Hence, for the purpose
of testing a network in which all spreading factor values can be used, a 51 byte payload
limitation is imposed.
For conﬁguring the payload size of the packets, it was used a python script. The 10 devices,
as previously mentioned, were programmed to send 15 packets each within 1 minute. For
the purpose of this experiment, the test was performed three times: the ﬁrst time every
device sent packets with a payload of 1 byte, the second time the payload was changed to
20 bytes, and the third time to 51 bytes. By monitoring the traﬃc at the backend, it was
possible to calculate the number of packets correctly received over the total sent, which
was done for all three payloads individually.
The experimental results, in terms of maximum achieved PRR, are presented in Table 8.7.
As one can see, the PRR does not have a large variation as the payload size increases.
However, it is proven that, as a general trend, the PRR decreases when larger payload
sizes are used. This can be explained by the fact that a larger payload increases the ToA
of the transmission, as described by Equation 4.11. As a consequence, due to the longer
ToA of the packets, it can be expected that a higher number of collision will occur among
the devices, thus reducing the PRR.

8.7
Discussion
Throughout this chapter, various features of LoRa technology were investigated and eval-
uated, both empirically and by means of Matlab simulations. The following considerations
can be made from the tests that were conducted:
• Test 1 demonstrated how the use of three transmission bandwidths could be ex-
tremely beneﬁcial for LoRaWAN in terms of network capacity, showing that it can
lead to a signiﬁcant increase in PRR, as the number of devices within the same
network increases. For instance, it was shown that when 2000 devices are taken
==PAGE:97==
8.8. Summary
81
into account, the PRR achieved from using three bandwidths is approximately 60%
higher than that of when one bandwidth is used. In addition, the number of sup-
ported devices, given a minimum PRR requirement, can improve signiﬁcantly as
well.
• Test 2 validated the theoretical assumption of the orthogonality between the spread-
ing factors, by empirically showing that simultaneous transmissions that are char-
acterized by diﬀerent SF values can be correctly received and decoded 100% of the
times. Two additional scenarios simulating the worst network conditions were pre-
sented, and it was demonstrated how LoRaWAN can make use of the three parallel
frequency channels to support the simultaneous transmission of packets that use the
same SF, and thus reducing packet losses even in the worst conditions.
• Test 3 showed how the spreading factors distribution within a network have an
impact on the network performance in terms of packet reception rate. It was found
that every network can be characterized by an optimal spreading factor distribution,
which can contribute to enhance its capacity.
• Test 4 analyzed the eﬀect of transmissions characterized by diﬀerent payload sizes,
and ultimately showed the impact that an increase in the ToA has on the packet
reception rate. It was demonstrated that packets with a larger payload size are more
likely to experience collisions, thus decreasing the overall network performance. This
shows that, apart from the techniques for enhancing the network capacity (such as
orthogonal SF and multi-channel transmission), the packet size should be taken into
account as well when evaluating the scalability of such networks.
8.8
Summary
Four tests were successfully conducted, by using a combination between simulations and
practical experiments. Many interesting features of LoRaWAN were observed and dis-
cussed, such as the advantages of using three transmission bandwidths, the orthogonality
between transmissions achieved by making use of the non-interfering channels described
in Chapter 5, the impact on capacity of the spreading factor distribution within a single
cell and, lastly, the degradation of performance due to the payload size.

9 Conclusion
IoT is a fast growing market. Its ﬁelds of application in people’s day-to-day life are nu-
merous, as evidenced by the forecasts regarding the signiﬁcant increase in M2M and IP
traﬃc. By embedding devices with computation capabilities and connectivity it is possible
to enable communications among smart objects, allowing the monitoring and collection
of data for both private and industrial use in ﬁelds ranging from domestic and work envi-
ronments, to transportation and logistics, and to e-health, among others.
In the context of large scale applications, IoT has shown a clear need for long-range and
low-power type of communication in order to support a high number of smart devices
spread over a wide area. Low Power Wide Area Networks (LPWAN) oﬀer a low-cost so-
lution to this need. However, due to a lack of standards, many LPWAN solutions have
been proposed, causing a fragmentation in the market. All proposed LPWAN solutions
aim at oﬀering low power and long range connectivity, though every solution presents
diﬀerences from one another. For this work, two LPWAN technologies were analyzed for
their interesting technical diﬀerences, namely, NB-IoT and LoRa. The technical feature
of NB-IoT were brieﬂy investigated, and it was given an overview of its performance in
terms of IoT factors. LoRa, on the other hand, was explored extensively, by presenting
a thorough theoretical background covering all aspects of this technology, together with
a mathematical model for estimating the oﬀered network capacity in the case of an area
covered by a single gateway.
For the purpose of further investigating and testing a LoRa network, it was designed and
built a real network setup composed of LoRaWAN-enabled modules, carrier boards, and
a RPi. The communication between the components was done via the UART interface,
through which was possible to fully control the modules and their transmissions to the
gateway. In addition, a simulation framework was developed for comparison with the ex-
perimental results, and for examining scalability of such networks.
Four tests were successfully conducted, by combining results obtained from the simula-
tions as well as from the practical tests. Among the many interesting features of the LoRa
technology that were observed, it was found that the use of three bandwidths for trans-
mission could lead to a signiﬁcant increase in the overall network capacity, allowing for a
higher number of devices to be served by a single gateway. For instance, the PRR can in-
crease of approximately 60% when up to 2000 devices are within the same cell. The ability
of LoRa to support concurrent transmission was also evaluated, demonstrating how the
combination of 3 frequency channels and 6 orthogonal spreading factors is beneﬁcial for
reducing packet loss, thus increasing the PRR and, ultimately, the network performance
in high traﬃc conditions. Lastly, two more aspects of this technology were investigated,
namely, the spreading factor distribution within the network and the payload size. Both
==PAGE:100==
84
Chapter 9. Conclusion
aspects were proven to have an impact on network capacity: by optimizing the SF dis-
tribution within a single cell, it is possible to expand the number of supported devices at
zero cost. As for the payload size, it is found that as the size increases, it is more likely
to incur in packet collisions. It follows that the size of the payload can severely degrade
the performance of a LoRaWAN network and it must be considered when designing large
scale IoT applications.
9.1
Future Works
For the purpose of further testing LoRa, it would ﬁrst be beneﬁcial to introduce some
improvements in the physical setup used for this study, which would allow for a higher
number of experiments to be conducted. For instance, it would be interesting to add the
possibility of controlling the transmissions from each device singularly, thus avoiding the
risk of incurring in permanent conﬂicts between simultaneous transmissions. In addition,
it would allow to run tests for a higher number of network scenarios and, by neglecting
the duty cycle limitation, it would allow for a better simulation of an arbitrary number of
devices within the network. Secondly, it would be interesting to evaluate LoRa in terms
of communication range, thus introducing the possibility for the devices to roam within
the coverage area.
By implementing these two hardware improvements, a few topics related to this study
could be subject of investigation for future research. For instance, it could be further
investigated the eﬀect on capacity of diﬀerent spreading factor distributions within the
network, as it is a capacity improvement that comes at no cost, and thus advantageous
for LoRa network operators. In addition, the optimal SF distribution could be evaluated
in comparison with the ADR mechanism used in LoRa networks, which was not evaluated
in this study.
Lastly, the theoretical analysis of the capacity of a LoRa network could be expanded to
the case in which an area is covered by multiple gateways.
