Abstract
The quality and quantity of available groundwater in some areas are deteriorating. In
2018, pesticides or metabolites were detected in 40.8 % of the Danish groundwater ab-
straction wells. The most detected metabolite or pesticide in 2018, N,N-dimethylsulfamide
(DMS), was detected in 29.8 % of the analyzed abstraction wells. Problems arise in wa-
ter treatment, as DMS cannot be suﬃciently removed by sand ﬁltration, biodegradation,
activated carbon ﬁltration, and nanoﬁltration. Oxidation of DMS into N-nitrosodimethyl-
amine (NDMA) is hypothesized as a possible removal technique, as several studies have
shown biodegradation of NDMA.
The purpose of this study is to investigate the removal potential of NDMA in waterworks
ﬁltration and assess whether the possible removal is caused by biodegradation. The main
objectives are: (1) to investigate whether NDMA is removed in ﬁlter material from two
Danish waterworks Skovmølleværket and Sjælsø Plant II, (2) assess whether there is
evidence that the potentially observed NDMA removal is due to biodegradation.
The removal potential within 24 h of the produced NDMA, was examined in batch ex-
periments with material from the waterworks Skovmølleværket and Sjælsø Plant II. An
ozone concentration of 2 mg/L was found to be suﬃcient to oxidize 70 % - 100 % of 3.3 -
4.8 µg/L DMS in drinking water, with an average molar yield of 47 %. Batches contain-
ing autoclaved or ZnCl2 added ﬁlter material was used for abiotic controls. The batches
were evaluated to be successfully abiotic, based on the decreased oxygen and methane
consumption.
Between 41 % and >86 % NDMA removal was observed after 24 h contact time, in all of
the experiments. The NDMA removal showed two phases, a fast occurring removal fol-
lowed by a more slow removal. The fast occurring removal happened within the ﬁrst two
hours, and shows similar removal in the pre-ﬁlter, after ﬁlter and the batches containing
autoclaved or ZnCl2 added batches. This indicates that the removal within the ﬁrst two
hours, was caused by physical or chemical processes. The adsorption distribution coeﬃ-
cient (Kd) was calculated to be around the same as Kd values obtained from literature.
This supports the suggestion that the removal within the ﬁrst two hours was caused
by sorption. The NDMA concentration in the samples from batches containing biologi-
cal pre-ﬁlter material after 24 h showed further NDMA removal. The pre-ﬁlter likewise,
showed further oxygen consumption. After 2 h no further removal was observed in the au-
toclaved or ZnCl2 added batches. This indicates that the removal mechanism responsible
for the removal between 2 h and 24 h in the batches containing pre-ﬁlter was biodegra-
dation. The waterworks sand ﬁlter was estimated to remove 22 - 244 ng/L NDMA. This
suggests that the the highest measured DMS concentration in wells used for production
( 120 ng/L) in some situations can be removed in the ﬁlter upon ozonation.
Based on the results of this study, NDMA can be partially removed in waterworks sand
ﬁlter material. The removal mechanism responsible for the removal in a realistic time
span for a waterworks ﬁlter, was suggested to be sorption. Before implementing this
solution, it is necessary to investigate the possible NDMA sorption and examine how fast
breakthrough would occur, before deciding if oxidizing DMS to the carcinogenic NDMA
is the best possible removal technique.

Preface
This master thesis was conducted to investigate the removal potential of NDMA in
two Danish waterworks, Skovmølleværket and Sjælsø Plant II. Furthermore, to examine
whether the possible NDMA removal was caused by biodegradation. This was investi-
gated in order to assess if ozonation followed by ﬁltration could be a suitable removal
mechanism for the groundwater contaminant DMS.
This master thesis was produced at the Technical University of Denmark, Department
of Environmental Engineering fulﬁlling the requirements necessary to obtain a masters
degree in environmental engineering. The writing and experimental work included in this
project was performed during the period from August 24th 2020 to January 24th 2021.
The workload for this study accounts for 30 ECTS credits.
The project is produced by Emilie Kisbye Bovin marster student in Environmental
Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark.

Introduction
Clean and safe drinking water is often taken for granted in western societies. In Denmark,
groundwater is a precious resource as 99% of the water supply is drawn from groundwater
(Bækgaard, 1998).
This precious resource is in some areas becoming scarcer as the
quantity and quality is deteriorating, as a result of anthropogenic activities (Benner
et al., 2013).
The groundwater in Denmark is being monitored in order to identify
possible threats to the drinking water quality. In 2018, pesticides or metabolites were
detected in 40.8 % of the groundwater abstraction wells (Thorling et al., 2019).
From July 2018 it has been mandatory to include the degradation product N,N-dimethyl-
sulfamide (DMS) when analyzing the groundwater for pesticides and metabolites in Den-
mark. In 2018, only 62 % of the groundwater abstraction wells were tested for DMS
(Thorling et al., 2019). The fraction of abstraction wells with pesticide detections, are
therefore expected to increase in the coming years. DMS is the most often found pesticide
metabolite when analyzing the groundwater from production wells (GEUS, 2019). It was
detected in 29.8 % of the analyzed production wells and exceeding the guideline value
of 0.1 µg/L for a single pesticide (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2017), in 7.7 % of the
abtraction wells (Thorling et al., 2019).
The waterworks Skovmølleværket near Svendborg measured concentrations of 3 - 5 µg/L
of DMS in a well not used for production (Kaarsholm, 2019). In other abstraction wells in
that area, concentrations of 0.01 - 0.11 µg/L were measured. To avoid the contamination
spreading, it was recommended to use the closed well as a remediation well (Kaarsholm,
2019).
Drinking water treatment with sorption and biological degradation during sand ﬁltration,
activated carbon ﬁltration, river bank ﬁltration and nanoﬁltration cannot remove DMS
from the raw water (Schmidt and Brauch, 2008; Zoumpouli et al., 2019). This empha-
sizes the importance of more research of possible DMS removal techniques applicable for
waterworks. Therefore, the reaction of DMS with ozone forming N-nitrosodimethylamine
(NDMA), in the presence of bromide, can be important for water treatment. NDMA is
genotoxic carcinogenic, thus the presence of NDMA in drinking water should be kept
as low as possible (Schmidt and Brauch, 2008). Sorption of NDMA has been found to
be insuﬃcient for removal, but NDMA has been found to be biodegradable. (Patterson
et al., 2012; Gunnison et al., 2000; Kaplan and Kaplan, 1985).
Schmidt and Brauch (2008) investigated the removal of DMS and NDMA in waterworks,
and found that the removal eﬃciency varied, depending on the raw water type and the
treatment steps. The largest removal was observed in a surface water treatment plant
which used ozonation, coagulation and a sand ﬁlter. Zoumpouli et al. (2019) likewise
showed removal of DMS by ozonation, forming NDMA, followed by ﬁltration through
anthracite and two columns containing sand ﬁlter from a waterworks. Both Zoumpouli
et al. (2019) and Schmidt and Brauch (2008) assumed that the removal they observed in
the ﬁlters were through biodegradation, based on the the ﬁndings of Sharp et al. (2005),
that NDMA is biodegradable.
It has not yet been proven that all waterworks are capable of removing NDMA, whether
a possible removal is caused by biodegradation nor if the possible removal is suﬃcient for

Background
2.1
N,N-dimethylsulfamid (DMS)
The most recent identiﬁed problematic pesticide or degradation product concerning ground-
water is N,N-dimethylsulfamide (DMS). Abstraction wells are analyzed for the pesticides
and metabolites in the Drinking Water Directive. From the 1st of July 2018 DMS was
included in these analysis (Thorling et al., 2019).
DMS is the most frequently detected pesticide metabolite. It was detected in 30.7 % of the
analyzed abstraction wells and exceeded the guideline of 0.1 µg/L in 8.2 % (GEUS, 2019).
An acceptable daily intake (ADI) value was tentatively set at 0.02 mg/(kg · d) (Ministry
of Environment and Food of Denmark, 2018). Assuming an adult of 60 kg drinking 2 L/d,
a health-based drinking water guideline value is set at 60 µg/L (Ministry of Environment
and Food of Denmark, 2018). This is considerably higher than the highest measured
concentration of 5.6 µg/L in a well that is not in use (GEUS, 2019).
DMS is a metabolite from the fungicides Tolylﬂuanid and Dichloﬂuanid which were used
in the fruit and berry industry in Denmark, from 1973-2007 and from 1966-1974, re-
spectively (GEUS, 2019). Moreover, dichloﬂuanid was used as a wood preservative from
1974-1999. In the application period, from 1966 to 2007, around 172 tonnes of Tolylﬂu-
anid and 37 tonnes of Dichloﬂuanid were sold (Schliemann-Haug, 2019). Additionally,
both Tolylﬂuanid and Dichloﬂuanid have been applied in paint (GEUS, 2019).
2.1.1
Environmental fate of DMS
DMS is a polar compound, and due to its high pKa value at 10.6 (European Parli-
ment, 2009), it will be found in protonated form in the treatment steps at a traditional
waterworks, where the pH is approximately neutral. DMS is soluble in water with a
solubility of 140 g/L, and based on low vapor pressure and Henry’s law constant (KH =
1.6 × 10−12 atm · m3/mol), volatilization is not considered a signiﬁcant transport process
for DMS removal (European Parliment, 2009). DMS has very hydrophilic properties,

2.2
Danish drinking water treatment
According to The council of the european union (1998) all member states shall take the
necessary measures to ensure the produced water is wholesome and clean if intended
for human consumption. Danish drinking water is primarily based on groundwater, and
requires less and more simple treatment compared to other water sources, such as surface
water.
In Denmark, 2600 centralized waterworks produce and supply 97 % of the danish popu-
lation with drinking water, and the last 3 % is supplied by around 50,000 decentralized
waterworks or abstraction wells each supplying less than 10 houses (Miljøstyrelsen, 2020).
The water treatment in Denmark traditionally consists of two treatment processes: aer-
ation followed by ﬁltration.
2.2.1
Aeration
Aeration is responsible for the liquid-gas mass transfer, is used to oxygenate the incom-
ing water, and to strip out unwanted volatile compounds such as methane (CH4), carbon
dioxide (CO2), and volatile organic compounds (VOC). The incoming groundwater con-
tains very little dissolved oxygen. As the water is aerated, meaning oxygen is transferred
from the gas into the liquid (Arvin, 2002b), unpleasant taste and odors are removed
(Gray, 1994). As CO2 is stripped from the water, the pH increases, as a result the water
is less corrosive. Moreover, the transfer of oxygen into the water is important as it is used

to oxidize soluble metals, forming insoluble hydroxides which enables a physical removal
by ﬁltration (Gray, 1994). Likewise, oxygen is necessary in the water for downstream
biological processes in the ﬁlter, such as nitriﬁcation.
Based on the low Henry’s law constant for DMS, volatilization is not considered a signif-
icant removal process for DMS. This means that DMS is not removed from the water by
aeration. In this study, oxygen is required in for the possible biodegradation of NDMA,
the aeration is therefore an important treatment in order to reach high initial oxygen
concentrations, before the ﬁltration.
2.2.2
Filtration
After the aeration step the water is typically treated using a biological rapid sand ﬁlter
where physical, chemical and biological mechanisms works to treat the water.
If the
oxygen is depleted in the ﬁlter, anaerobic processes start, which can lead to production
of bad taste and smell. Proper function of biological processes in the ﬁlter requires oxygen
concentrations above 2 mg/L (Arvin, 2002a).
The main purpose of the ﬁlter is to remove (Arvin, 2002a):
• Particles such as clay, silt, bacteria, viruses, protozoa, and Fe(OH)3.
• Soluble compounds such as CH4, H2S, Fe, Mn, NH4, Assimilable Organic Carbon
(AOC) and other chemicals e.g. hydrocarbons.
The water gets treated as it ﬂows in at the top of the ﬁlter and runs down through the
ﬁlter material (Arvin, 2002a). Typically, the ﬁlter material is sand, as it is the cheapest
option, but other alternatives such as activated carbon, anthracite, granite and polymer
materials can also be used Arvin (2002a).
Physically the ﬁlter removes particles and insoluble metals as they are transported with
the water through the ﬁlter. This separation occurs as the particles collide with the
ﬁlter material (Arvin, 2002a). As the ﬁlter treats the raw water it is colonized by native
microbial populations that attach to the surface of the grains and grow from the nutrients
in the water (Benner et al., 2013). In the top layers of the ﬁlter methane and hydrogen
sulﬁde are oxidized followed by iron, ammonium and, last, manganese (Arvin, 2002a).
Precipitation of hydroxides along with accumulation of biomass and particles can clog
the pore spaces in the ﬁlter. This can aﬀect hydraulic retention time and thereby the
treatment eﬃciency. When the head loss through the ﬁlter becomes too high, the ﬁlter is
cleaned. The cleaning is done by backwashing the ﬁlter, where the direction of the ﬂow
is reversed, and higher velocities are used. Backwashing the ﬁlters also removes biomass
which can aﬀect the removal rate right after the backwash (Arvin, 2002a).
Some waterworks have both a pre-ﬁlter and an after ﬁlter. The pre-ﬁlter is located prior
to the after ﬁlter in the treatment steps. It can therefore be assumed that more iron
is precipitated in the pre-ﬁlter compared to the after ﬁlter. This will result in larger
grain size in the pre-ﬁlter compared to the after ﬁlter. Likewise will the water running
into the pre-ﬁlter contain more organic material and higher assimilable organic carbon
concentration compared to that ﬂowing into the after ﬁlter. It is therefore assumed that
the pre-ﬁlter will contain more bacteria than the after ﬁlter.

Traditionally, waterworks were not designed to remove pollutants such as pesticides and
therefore often if contamination is found in a production well, the well is closed for the
waterworks to comply with the guidelines.
Activated carbon ﬁltration is a solution to remove some pesticides, and it is widely used
in countries where surface water is the water source. Activated carbon (AC) has a huge
speciﬁc surface area, and because of its porous structure with pore diameters from 0.5 nm
to more than 50 nm most contaminants can be trapped in the pores (Arvin et al., 2002).
A contaminant adsorbs best to AC if it has high aﬃnity for adsorption in lipid material,
whereas water soluble compounds adsorb poorly to AC (Arvin et al., 2002). Small polar
molecules will have low removal potential in AC ﬁlters. An example of such a compound
is DMS. DMS has low aﬃnity to adsorb to carbon. This is expressed in the low octanol-
water partition coeﬃcient (Kow).
Treatment of DMS using AC therefore has limited
eﬃciency (Schliemann-Haug, 2019).
2.3
Possible treatment techniques of DMS
Schmidt and Brauch (2008) found that riverbank ﬁltration, ﬂocculation, oxidation or dis-
infection using hydrogen peroxide (H2O2), potassium permanganate (KMnO4), chlorine
dioxide (ClO2) and when exposed to ultraviolet irradiation did not remove DMS. Fast
breakthrough was observed using AC ﬁltration and nanoﬁltration likewise showed insuf-
ﬁcient removal eﬃciency (Schmidt and Brauch, 2008). However, oxidation using ozone
fully transformed (>99 %) DMS. This reaction of DMS and ozone could therefore be
important in the development of treatment methods for waterworks to comply with the
guideline value for pesticides and metabolites of 0.1 µg/L.
2.3.1
Ozonation
The use of ozone in water treatment can have several purposes, but is most often used
for disinfection and oxidation. Ozone can eliminate bacteria at 1 mg/L within 10 min.
Treatment with ozone can remove unpleasant colors, taste, odours and micropollutants
(Hammes et al., 2006; Gray, 1994).
When the water is oxidized, the complex natural organic matter (NOM) is broken down.
This oxidative breakdown of NOM forms smaller, easily biodegradable oxygen containing
organic compounds, which comprises a considerable fraction of the assimilable organic
carbon (AOC) (Hammes et al., 2006). AOC is a small fraction (0.1 - 9 %) of the total
organic carbon (TOC), but it is the fraction of the dissolved organic carbon (DOC)
which is most readily consumed by the microorganisms (Hammes et al., 2006). AOC is
an important quality parameter for biological stable water, because high AOC can result
in growth of microbiological cells.
The transformation products of DMS and ozone are of particular interest due to the
elevated DMS concentrations in the source waters along with the diﬃculties in removing
DMS. At circumneutral pH, DMS reacts slowly with ozone. In bromide containing waters,
such as groundwater, bromide works as a catalyst, resulting in transformation of DMS
into the carcinogenic N-Nitrosodimethylamine (NDMA) (Von Gunten et al., 2010).

material.
Implementing ozonation of the groundwater at waterworks are assumed to
result in growth of the microorganisms present in the sand ﬁlters, due to higher AOC
concentrations in the incoming water.
2.3.2
Biodegradation
Traditional water treatment methods have little to no eﬀect on micropollutants in the
water. The most eﬀective methods are often unsustainable and expensive (Benner et al.,
2013). Biological removal by the already present microbial consortia or through bioaug-
mentation could serve as a more sustainable and less expensive solution for micropollutant
removal.
Currently, two strategies are known for microorganisms to transform or mineralize sub-
stances including micropollutants (Benner et al., 2013). These strategies are metabolic
and co-metabolic degradation. Metabolic degradation is a growth linked process where
the degradation often results in mineralization of the substance (Benner et al., 2013). If
a micropollutant is biodegraded through metabolic reactions, the microorganisms have
Page 6 of 53
==PAGE:14==
DTU Environment
Emilie Bovin s153150
January 24, 2021
specialized to use the micropollutant as growth substrate. The survival of these bacteria
requires relatively high concentrations of the pollutant (Benner et al., 2013).
In co-metabolic degradation, the micropollutant is degraded together with a growth sub-
strate, without being the source of carbon or energy. The microorganism grows from
degradation of the primary substrate, and is not beneﬁted by the degradation of the
co-metabolically degraded micropollutant (Benner et al., 2013). The co-metabolic degra-
dation is executed by organisms that contain an enzyme that co-metabolically can trans-
form the micropollutant. In this way can the micropollutants are converted into other
compounds that can be utilized by other microorganisms as a growth substrate (Benner
et al., 2013).
The organisms expressing this co-metabolic degradation must have enzymes that ex-
hibit broad substrate speciﬁcity, and in that way can catalyze oxidation reactions of the
growth substrate but also of the micropollutant (Benner et al., 2013). Both ammonia
and methane oxidizing bacteria have been found to catalyze co-metabolic degradation of
some organic trace contaminants, though ammonia and methane monooxygenase enzymes
(Benner et al., 2013; Hedegaard and Albrechtsen, 2014). In many areas, the groundwater
contains methane. The present methane might stimulate growth of methanotrophs which,
together with e.g. ammonium oxidizing bacteria are frequently found in sand ﬁlters at
waterworks (Hedegaard et al., 2020b).
The advantage of co-metabolic degradation is that it allows for degradation of pollutants
at lower concentrations than what would be necessary for metabolic degradation. Several
studies have observed NDMA biodegradation by undeﬁned microbial consortia (Gunnison
et al., 2000; Kaplan and Kaplan, 1985; Patterson et al., 2012).
This degradation of
NDMA is expected to be co-metabolic, since no microorganisms have been identiﬁed using
nitrosamines as sole carbon source (Sgroi et al., 2018). Based on the low concentrations of
NDMA observed in the environment, and low concentrations encountered from oxidation
of relatively low DMS concentrations in the source water, a possible degradation of NDMA
is suggested to be co-metabolic degradation (Sharp et al., 2005).
If the possible removal of NDMA is a result of co-metabolic degradation, increased AOC
concentrations could result in increased co-metabolic biodegradation of NDMA. This
means that the ﬁlters after some adaptation to the higher AOC concentrations possibly
could remove more NDMA.
2.4
What is NDMA?
Nitrosamines are compounds with the characteristic structural element N-NO. The most
intensively studied and structurally simplest nitrosamine is N-nitrosodimethylamine (NDMA).
NDMA is a genotoxic carcinogen and has a lifetime cancer risk of 10−6 with a drinking
water concentration of 0.7 ng/L (Sgroi et al., 2018; Schmidt and Brauch, 2008). For pre-

possible value. The World Health Organization (WHO) set a guideline in drinking wa-
ter of 100 ng/L (WHO, 2008). For lifelong oral exposure via drinking water, the federal
environmental agency in Germany recommends a precautionary value of 10 ng/L and in
the United States, the Oﬃce of Environmental Health Hazard Assessment (OEHHA) set

3
State of the art
Currently, the most commonly applied technology for removing NDMA from water, is
using ultraviolet radiation (Sgroi et al., 2018; Mitch et al., 2003). In order to reach a
log order reduction in NDMA concentration under typical conditions in drinking water
treatment systems using UV treatment, approximately 1000 mJ/cm3 are required. This is
around 10 times higher than what is required for virus removal (Mitch et al., 2003). This
means that UV treatment works to remove NDMA, but is an energy expensive solution.
Bioremediation is potentially a more cost eﬀective solution for NDMA removal. Min-
eralization of NDMA by undeﬁned consortia have been identiﬁed at laboratory scale
(Gunnison et al., 2000; Kaplan and Kaplan, 1985; Patterson et al., 2012).
At Rocky Mountain Arsenal, NDMA was found to disappear from the groundwater
aquifer. Gunnison et al. (2000) investigated the removal of NDMA in soil samples and
granular activated carbon (GAC) from the installed treatment system at that site. At
laboratory scales the sorption kinetics to the GAC and the soil was found to be insignif-
icant, with a low Kd of 0.5 to 1.2 L/kg. The NDMA degradation was examined using
14-C labeled NDMA. Measuring the mineralization by following the 14CO2 evolution.
The investigation showed that the native consortia had the capability of mineralizing
NDMA, destroying 40 % to 70 % of the added 50 µg/kg to 500 µg/kg NDMA within 30
days (Gunnison et al., 2000). Patterson et al. (2012) and Gunnison et al. (2000) found
both anaerobic and aerobic mineralization of NDMA with half-life raging between 12 -
260days. Lowest half-lives were observed in the nano-gram per liter concentrations, in
aerobic conditions (Patterson et al., 2012). Diﬀerences in observed half-lives suggest that
the half-life is site speciﬁc.
Zoumpouli et al. (2019) investigated DMS and NDMA removal potential in a small scale
ozonation and bioﬁltration setup. The water was ﬁltered over three columns, the ﬁrst
containing anthrazite, and the following, containing sand from a drinking water treatment
plant that was adapted to ozonated water. DMS was found to be at least 99 % transformed
with an ozone concentration of around 3 mg/L and contact time of 30 min. This resulted
in a molar yield of approximately 50 % (Zoumpouli et al., 2019). Only traces of NDMA
could be detected after the water had run through the ﬁrst two columns. After all three
columns, and a total hydraulic retention time of approximately 5 h the concentration of
NDMA was less than 2.22 ng/L (Zoumpouli et al., 2019).
The DMS transformation and further NDMA removal was investigated at three diﬀer-
ent waterworks by Schmidt and Brauch (2008). An ozone concentration of 2 mg/L was
found to have fully oxidized 1.14 µg/L DMS in groundwater. Schmidt and Brauch (2008)
found the largest percent wise NDMA removal treating surface water by addition of co-
agulant (iron(III)chloride) and ﬁltration, through a not freshly backwashed rapid sand
ﬁlter. However, the removal eﬃciencies varied considerably with 16-89 % NDMA removal
(Schmidt and Brauch, 2008).
Since no microorganisms have been found utilizing nitrosamines as their sole carbon
source, and the observed environmental NDMA concentrations have been low, ( which
are still assessed to be high with respect to potential human health eﬀects), a possible
degradation is suggested to be caused by co-metabolic degradation, rather than metabolic

4.1
Overview of the performed experiments
In order to investigate if ozonation followed by ﬁltration could be a vial treatment option
for the removal of DMS in water treatment, four experiments were conducted evaluat-
ing the transformation of DMS into NDMA, and subsequently the removal potential of
NDMA in waterworks ﬁlter material.
• Oxidation of DMS: The possible removal of DMS and conversion into NDMA
upon ozonation was examined. This was investigated by spiking drinking water
from the waterworks Skovmølleværket and Sjælsø Plant II with DMS and ozone.
• Removal potential: Filter sand and eﬄuent drinking water from the waterworks
Skovmølleværket and Sjælsø Plant II was used to evaluate whether NDMA could be
removed, when in contact with waterworks ﬁlter material. The removal potential
was investigated in laboratory batch experiments, where a batch was prepared
for each sample. The batches were set up by adding ﬁlter material and NDMA
containing water, produced in the above mentioned experiment.
• Abiotic controls: Autoclavation or addition of 5.5 mL ZnCl2 (98.0 - 100.5 %,
Sigma-Aldrich) was investigated as abiotic controls. The success of the techniques
used for preparing the abiotic batches were evaluated based on the oxygen con-
sumption in the batches. NDMA removal potential in abiotic controls were ana-
lyzed alongside with biological batches. This was done in order to characterize a
possible NDMA removal as biodegradation.
• Eﬀects of methane addition: To further investigate the removal mechanism, an
experiment was conducted investigating whether spiking the batches with methane
would stimulate methanotrophs, and increase the NDMA removal. This stimulation

Before measuring the ozone concentration in the samples, an indigo zero sample is pre-

4.8.3
Solid-phase extraction
Before the samples could be analyzed using LC - MS/MS they were prepared using solid-
phase extraction (SPE).
Solid phase extraction (SPE) is a technique to extract a compound from the liquid solution
it is dissolved in. It is a way of preparing the sample for analysis as it can be used to
concentrate and purify the sample. In this project Bakerbond carbon columns are used.
These are speciﬁcally designed for SPE of polar compounds. The columns are prepared
by adding 2 × 5 mL acetonitril and 2 × 5 mL milli-Q water and letting it go though the
column without suction. 100 mL of sample was added to the column and suction was
turned on to drive the ﬂow through the columns. After all of the samples had gone
through the columns the suction was increased and the columns were dried for 1.5 h. The
Page 19 of 53
==PAGE:27==
DTU Environment
Emilie Bovin s153150
January 24, 2021
glass chamber were emptied and the vials to collect the samples in were put inside and
the samples were eluted. This was done by adding 2 × 5 mL acetonitril and 2 × 5 mL
acetone. Both of these are polar solvents which can be mixed with water and they are
thereby used to extract DMS and NDMA from the column. When all of this is collected
in the glass vials, the eluent is evaporated to a volume of 0.75 mL using N2 while heated
to 40° C. This concentrate was transferred to a smaller vial along with 0.25 mL milli-Q
water used to rinse the bigger vial and make sure all the concentrate was transferred to
the smaller vial. Each sample was in this way concentrated to a total volume of 1 mL.
4.8.4
LC-MS/MS analysis
Next the samples were analyzed using liquid chromotography tandem mass spectrometry
(LC-MS/MS). This step was performed by the technician. The chromatographic separa-
tion was achieved on a 2.1 x 100 mm, 3.5 micron reversed phase column (Zorbax Eclipse
Plus C18 RR, Agilent Technologies) using 5 mM ammonium formate as aqueous phase
and 10:90 water:methanol with 5 mM ammonium formate as mobile phase. Detection
was achieved in Multiple Reaction Monitoring.

in the batches containing old ﬁlter material (see Figure 10).

that oxygen concentration was not an ideal indicator parameter for biological activity.

6
Discussion of removal mechanisms
In the previous Skovmølleværket experiment along with experiment Skovmølleværket 1
and 2, the evolution of the NDMA concentration shows two distinct phases. An initial
steep slope followed by a constant or lower removal rate after the ﬁrst two hours. The
decreasing slope of the NDMA in the after ﬁlter between 1 h and 2 h in experiment
Skovmølleværket 2, and in both the batches containing pre- and after ﬁlter material
in experiment Skovmølleværket 1 indicates, that a fast occurring removal mechanism is
responsible for the removal within the ﬁrst two hours and that the removal between 2 h
and 24 h is caused by another process.
6.1
Sorption
The NDMA concentration was slightly lower in the batches containing autoclaved ﬁl-
ter material, compared to the batches containing biological ﬁlter material after 20 min
contact time in the previous Skovmølleværket experiment and after 1 h in experiment
Skovmølleværket 2. This indicates that the autoclavation enhanced the mechanism re-
sponsible for the fast removal. When the ﬁlter material is autoclaved, it is heated. This
heating of the sand might have resulted in small cracks in the sand grains and thereby
increased the speciﬁc surface area, or have broken down some present organic material,
thereby increasing the available sorption sites for NDMA. In experiment Sjælsø, samples
from the batches containing autoclaved ﬁlter material could not be analyzed. The batches
to which zinc chloride was added did not show an increased fast removal compared to the
biological batches. Again, this suggests that the heating process in the autoclavation, is
the reason for the slightly enhanced removal.
The NDMA removal in the autoclaved or zinc chloride added batches were the same as
in the biological batches, after around 2 h in both experiment Skovmølleværket 1 and 2
and in experiment Sjælsø. This indicates that the mechanism responsible for the fast

by plating 0.25 % nickel onto the granular iron, resulting in a NDMA half life of 2-4 min

et al. (2000) was 5-28 mg/L, this is considerably higher than the investigated initial 3.3-

et al. (2000) shows that the presence of iron can be an important parameter for NDMA
removal, which supports the consideration of increased removal in ﬁlter material with
higher iron concentrations.

except in the previous Skovmølleværket experiment, where the after ﬁlter like the pre-
ﬁlter material, continued to remove NDMA after 2 h. This could indicate that the slow
removal mechanism was biological, which ﬁts with the observations, that the pre-ﬁlter
consumed more oxygen than the after ﬁlter material (see Figure 11, 12 and 13). Based
on the reduced oxygen consumption, the autoclaved and zinc chloride added batches
were assessed to be successfully abiotic, as explained in Section 5.1.2. Since no removal
was observed after 2 h in the autoclaved and zinc chloride added batches in experiment
Skovmølleværket 2 and experiment Sjælsø, the removal in the biological batches after 2 h
is suggested to be caused by biodegradation.
The pre-ﬁlter material is expected to contain more organic material compared to the after
ﬁlter, since the water reaches the pre-ﬁlter before the after ﬁlter at the waterworks. The
increased concentrations of organic material in the pre-ﬁlter would stimulate the possible
co-metabolic biodegradation in the pre-ﬁlter.
The slow removal, suggested to be co-metabolic biodegradation have a considerably lower
removal rate of 1.3 %/h and 2.8 %/h (see Table 8). This corresponds to a mass removal
rate of 1.4 ng/L - 4.5 ng/L (see Table 8). With the short retention time in the water-
works ﬁlter, co-metabolic biodegradation is not suggested to be the a suitable removal
mechanism.
Schmidt and Brauch (2008) and Zoumpouli et al. (2019) tested removal at waterworks
and in column experiments with ﬁlter material from waterworks sand ﬁlters. However, as
in this study, they did not use mineralization measurements, but the diﬀerence between
the initial and eﬄuent NDMA concentration.
Zoumpouli et al. (2019) observed >99 % NDMA removal after passing 0.59 µg/L NDMA
through an anthracite column followed by two sand ﬁlter columns, with a hydraulic
retention time of approximately 5 h. This corresponds to a removal of >20 %/h. The
observed removal by Zoumpouli et al. (2019) was assessed to be caused by biodegradation.
In this study, it was suggested that the removal is caused by biodegradation, showing a
removal rate of 1.3 %/h - 2.8 %/h (see Table 8). Zoumpouli et al. (2019) had their column
system running for longer periods, and sampled after 7 d, 24 d, and 97 d. The sample after
7 d, only shows traces of NDMA after ﬁltration by the ﬁrst column, containing anthracite.
The removal by the ﬁrst column was lower after 24 d and 97 d. This could indicate that
the removal by the ﬁrst column after 7 d is caused by sorption, and that the material had
been ﬁlled, resulting in lower removal rates in the later samples.
Schmidt and Brauch (2008) found NDMA removal between 29 % and 86 % of the initial
12 ng/L - 14 ng/L NDMA after treatment at the waterworks. The residence time used by
Schmidt and Brauch (2008) in the diﬀerent waterworks were not presented. The removal
found by Schmidt and Brauch (2008) was similar to the total removal found after 24 h in
this study, including both removal by sorption and biodegradation.
This removal observed by Schmidt and Brauch (2008) and Zoumpouli et al. (2019) could
be caused by a well developed microbial community, as stated in Zoumpouli et al. (2019)
or increased by other processes occurring in the ﬁlter simultaneously removing NDMA.

(see Table 3). With an average molar conversion of around 50 % (see Table 6) this would
result in a NDMA concentration of 35.8 ng/L. Depending on the conditions in the ﬁlter,
a removal of 35.8 ng/L is possible in the pre-ﬁlter from the waterworks Skovmølleværket.
This concentration is not expected to be removed in the pre-ﬁlter at Sjælsø Plant II.
Since the fast removal was suggested to be due to sorption, the removal potential of the
ﬁlter will decrease as the ﬁlter gets ﬁlled, and can not continue to adsorp NDMA. With
longer running times, the NDMA removal might therefore be governed by the possible
removal between 2 h and 24 h. The estimated removal in a waterworks ﬁlter with the slow
removal rate was between 6.1 ng/L and 19 ng/L (see Table 9). A NDMA concentration
of 35.8 ng/L would not be suﬃciently removed by the possible removal with the slow
removal rate. DMS is not found in all abtraction wells. Since the water from the diﬀerent
wells is mixed, the ﬁnal DMS concentration entering the waterworks might be less than
the 0.12 µg/L resulting in an even lower NDMA initial concentration. Depending on the
concentration in the incoming water the bacteria or iron present in the ﬁlter might be
able to remove suﬃcient levels of NDMA.
The highest measured DMS concentration in Skovmølleværket was found in a well not
currently being used for production, with a concentration of 4.1 µg/L (see Table 3). If this
DMS was ozonated and transformed into NDMA without dilution with water from other
wells, the concentration would be 1.22 µg/L. With the removal potentials found in this
study, this high concentration would not be fully removed in the investigates waterworks
without dilution.
However, as sorption is not fully successful in removing NDMA, one could argue whether
oxidizing DMS into NDMA is the best option, when removing DMS from drinking water.

Conclusion
This project investigated the NDMA removal potential in ﬁlter material from two Danish
waterworks, Skovmølleværket and Sjælsø Plant II. Furthermore, the removal mechanism
responsible for the possible NDMA removal was assessed by investigating a connection
between oxygen consumption and NDMA removal, as well as evaluating methods for
preparing abiotic controls.
These investigations contributed to the following conclusion:
• DMS in concentrations 3.3-4.8 µg/L was 70 % - 100 % oxidized in drinking water
from the waterworks Skovmølleværket and Sjælsø Plant II upon ozone concentra-
tions of 2 mg/L, with an average NDMA transformation yield of 47 %.
• 41 % - >86 % of the initial NDMA concentration was removed after 24 h contact
time with pre- and after ﬁlter material, with the largest removal caused by the
pre-ﬁlter material.
• The NDMA removal shows two phases. The initial removal mechanism with high
removal rate, followed by a more slow removal rate.
1. Similar removal was found within 2 h contact time in the autoclaved or zinc
chloride added ﬁlter material as in the biological ﬁlter material. The initial
removal mechanism is therefore suggested to be sorption.
2. Autoclavation of the ﬁlter material or zinc chloride addition inhibited the
oxygen and methane consumption along with the NDMA removal after 2 h
contact time. Oxygen and methane consumption, continued along with NDMA
removal, in the biological ﬁlter material. Phase two of the NDMA removal was
suggested to be caused by biodegradation.
Conducting these experiments clariﬁed that NDMA was removed with varying eﬃciency,
when in contact with waterworks ﬁlter material.
Within the relevant time frame for
water treatment, most of the NDMA was removed by sorption. However, sorption is not
a sustainable removal technique for NDMA. Before implementing ozonation followed by
ﬁltration through rapid sand ﬁlter as a treatment technique for DMS to comply with the
guideline of 0.1 µg/L, further studies are necessary.

Recommendations for further studies
The knowledge gained in this study can be used as a foundation for further research.
The methods used for investigating the NDMA removal in waterworks ﬁlter could be
optimized by:
• Conducting the experiments in larger batches or columns in order to reduce the
uncertainties connected with a batch for each sample.
• Verifying the mineralization by using 14-C labeled NDMA and following the evolu-
tion of 14-C labeled CO2.
• Investigating the NDMA removal potential by AC ﬁlters.
• Investigating whether NDMA sorps to precipitated iron, organic material or the
sand grains.
• Examining a possible connection between the NDMA removal and the iron concen-
tration along with the oxidation states of the present iron.
In order to conﬁrm the suggested removal mechanisms and potentials suggested in this
study, further research could include investigating the removal potential in continuously
running column experiments. This would show how the bacteria in the ﬁlter material
would adapt to the ozonated water or if the continuously incoming iron is suﬃcient to
remove NDMA. Furthermore, examine whether the biodegradation would increase after
a lag phase, if a fast breakthrough would be observed, or if the bacteria in the ﬁlter
would degrade the adsorbed NDMA, resulting in continuously removal through sorption
followed by biodegradation.
Investigating the removal potentials in diﬀerent waterworks ﬁlters could aid in the under-
standing of key parameters for the removal and the feasibility of this removal technique.
Ultimately, further studies are necessary before deciding whether this treatment process
could be eﬀectively implemented at waterworks.
