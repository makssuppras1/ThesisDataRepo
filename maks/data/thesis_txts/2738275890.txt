Abstract
This master’s thesis focuses on improving the autonomous navigation of de-icing trucks
used in airports. The main objective is to provide these trucks with the ability to effectively
detect and avoid static and dynamic obstacles. To achieve this, the project will work by
processing LiDAR point clouds, which will enable real-time obstacle detection. Simultane-
ous Localization and Mapping (SLAM) and Kalman Filter (KF) technologies will be applied
for object tracking and localization. Tests will be performed in a simulated environment,
with Gazebo and Rviz, to validate the accuracy and reliability of this implementation. For
the anti-collision system, the concept of ”safety bubbles” will be used to avoid collisions.
Overall, this master’s thesis aims to create an autonomous navigation and collision avoid-
ance system for de-icing trucks that will contribute to improve the safety and efficiency of
airport operations.

Introduction
The motivation behind this master’s thesis is the desire to better understand trajectory
planning and obstacle avoidance, as well as to strengthen skills in the use of ROS soft-
ware. This project aims to improve the efficiency, safety and reliability of airport operations
by reducing human error and speeding up the de-icing procedure. It is in line with the avi-
ation industry’s objectives of improving safety and optimizing the workflow. Collaboration
with Vestergaard brought insight and direction to the task, showing how this project com-
bines academic exploration with practical application.
Currently, the process of de-icing at airports involves manual operation of de-icing trucks.
These trucks are used to spray aircraft with de-icing fluid so that ice can be removed and
prevent its formation during the flight. Manual operation is required both for driving the
trucks to and from the aircraft and for controlling the cranes that apply the de-icing fluid.
This process requires a lot of time and effort, with chance for mistakes from humans that
can impact safety and effectiveness of airport operations.
One part of this de-icing process is the transfer of the trucks from the garage to the air-
craft. At present, operators do so by driving these vehicles through complicated airport
surroundings. The airport environment has many static obstacles, like buildings, and dy-
namic obstacles, such as other vehicles and personnel, that can difficult this task. This
manual driving increases the likelihood of errors and delays, and this has an impact on
the overall efficiency of the de-icing process.
This thesis builds on the work of Lars and Andreas, who wrote “LiDAR-based navigation
of de-icing trucks in airport environments” [1]. Their study is part of Vestergaard’s large
project on fully automatic aircraft deicing. Nowadays, manual operation is necessary both
for transport and for the use of cranes on de-icing trucks. With the improvements intro-
duced by Lars and Andreas, these trucks can now move autonomously while avoiding
contact with static objects.
The project, which is expected to last five months, aims to create advanced technology
that will greatly improve the self-navigation capabilities of de-icing trucks, enabling them
to handle both static and moving obstacles more effectively. The project aims to provide

Background
The research context of the project clarifies why the topic of the master thesis is valuable
from an academic and practical perspective. It focuses on enhancing airport de-icing
processes’ safety and effectiveness via automation solutions.  Courses like Advanced
Autonomous Robots, Unmanned Autonomous Systems and Perception for Autonomous
Systems in the Autonomous Systems Master’s at DTU have given applicable knowledge
and technical abilities needed for this project theme.  
 The growth in autonomous robotic applications has demonstrated that dynamic SLAM
techniques are crucial for autonomous navigation in changing environments, providing
real-time mapping and localization. Clustering methods have been recognized as an
essential part of precise detection and classification, particularly in areas where deep
learning-based analysis can provide better accuracy for analysis and classification. In
addition, advances in motion planning techniques for autonomous driving have been ob-
served, with an emphasis on enhancing safety along with operational efficacy.  This ad-
vancement in technology helps the growth of superior autonomous vehicle systems and
provides the basis for this project’s objective of improving airport de-icing operations.  
The Master thesis, titled “LiDAR-based navigation of de-icing trucks in airport environ-
ments”, written by Lars Arnold Dethlefsen and Andreas Fabian Emiliussen Nøbbe is a
starting point for this project.  This master thesis explains the design and building process
of a LiDAR-based anticollision system that can work with de-icing trucks operating in air-
ports.  The solution created here is an algorithm that has the ability to recognize static
things and stop when there is a collision risk. The already existing systems that were
used from this project are the clustering of objects from point clouds using the DBSCAN
algorithm and the creation of bounding boxes around each part of the truck, and its pre-
dictions from the ackerman model. This work attempts to enhance parts of that solution
and tackle new problems regarding localization, mapping and obstacle evasion.
Vestergaard Company has an interest in improving the efficiency, safety, and reliability
of airport operations, especially by making de-icing trucks autonomous. The progress
achieved in this project directly aligns with these objectives. By developing an object
recognition system and applying a logic to avoid obstacles that help de-icing trucks navi-
gate through airport environments, this project enhances the autonomous capabilities of
de-icing trucks. These improvements lead to several benefits for Vestergaard Company
regarding:
• Safety: The requirement for human operators is reduced, therefore decreasing the
possibility of human mistakes and improving safety in general during airport opera-
tions.
• Efficiency: Autonomous de-icing trucks can travel at faster speeds, which helps in
finishing the task of ice removal more quickly.
• Operational Reliability: By combining strong object detection and tracking systems,
the de-icing trucks can move through complicated airport settings with dependable
navigation even in changing and unpredictable situations.
• Cost Savings: Automation minimizes the requirement for human labor and extends
the lifespan of equipment by improving their operations with greater precision and
control, which results in cost savings for Vestergaard.

1.3
Methods
The project’s methods are selected to create a strong real-time detection, tracking and
navigation system for dealing with the unique complexities of autonomous de-icing oper-
ations in airport settings.
The problem of collision avoidance, which begins with the data given by LiDAR sensors,
is intended to be tackled by improving reliability and precision of point-cloud data. This
means integrating information from both LiDAR and SLAM systems into a complete un-
derstanding about what surrounds the vehicle. By putting together the spatial data that
SLAM gives and combining it with point-cloud data from LiDAR, the system can under-
stand better and follow objects in its environment. This blending cuts down trust on par-
ticular sensor information and improves the system’s power to take wise decisions at the
moment. This is very important for moving in airport settings with safety and efficiency.
To perform real-time localization, Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM) [2] is
employed. SLAM allows a robot or self-driving vehicle to create a map of an environment
while it figures out where it currently is on that map. This method requires using sensor
information for comprehending the surroundings, as well as employing algorithms to mea-
sure its location within this environment; therefore, both the map and vehicle’s position
are constantly updated during this process. This continuous updating can help navigate
and avoid obstacles in environments that change and where there is no knowledge about
the area.
For object tracking, Kalman Filter (KF) [3] is used. It is a type of mathematical tool that can
do estimation and prediction for the state of systems under uncertainties. This method
has been applied in different applications which need precise state estimation like target
tracking, navigation and control systems. The Kalman Filter functions in line with estima-
tion rules. It uses a mix of sensor measurements and forecasts from the system model to
improve guesses about the state of systems over time, going through repetitive processes.
This ongoing improvement helps the Kalman Filter make estimates that are resistant to
mistakes in measurement and uncertainty about system movements.
The use of ”safety bubbles” implies that the vehicle’s path can be changed according to
obstacles it detects. Safety bubbles are made around moving things caught in the LiDAR
detection area. If the truck’s bounding box and objects’ bubbles overlap, adjustments are
made to the path of the truck. The size of these safety bubbles varies with the speed of
objects.

1.4
Limitations
One important limitation in this project is the reliance on a existing SLAM (Simultaneous
Localization and Mapping) implementation. For autonomous navigation, SLAM allows the
vehicle to create a map of its surroundings while also keeping track of where it is present
on that map. An SLAM algorithm was not developed for this project; rather, an already
existing one from Vestergaard Company was used and adapted to make it work for the
project requirements. The main reason was to concentrate efforts on other central parts
of the project, like identifying objects and designing the anti-collision system.

2.1
Core technologies for autonomous navigation
To solve problems in dynamic airport zones, autonomous systems need a set of tech-
nologies for robust perception, filtering and real-time trajectory adjustments. This liter-
ature review will discuss the main technological components that make successful au-
tonomous driving possible under demanding navigation conditions within such complex
environments:
• LiDAR-based perception and computational efficiency: Among the sensors, LiDAR
(Light Detection and Ranging) is one of the most important, as it creates detailed
3D point clouds (Li et al., 2021) [5]. But, due to the high data density of LiDAR,
a problem arises in computation. This underlines the importance of efficient pro-
cessing methods and the potential advantages that can be derived from combining
sensors. Some studies are exploring preprocessing, clustering, and fusion methods
to improve object detection in complicated environments (Madake et al., 2022) [6].
• SLAM mapping and localization: In constantly changing environments, the de-icing
truck needs simultaneous localization and mapping (SLAM). This helps it create
maps of unknown areas while updating its own position. Active SLAM methods,
which rely on deep learning to predict, as shown in (Placed et al., 2023) [7], are
crucial for anticipating unseen obstacles and planning routes in advance.
• Filtering in noisy environments: normal filtering methods, such as extended Kalman
filters, may not work well when there is a lot of noise or many changes occur (Saman
and Lotfy, 2016) [8]. This highlights the importance of exploring strong alternatives
or custom filtering settings for the airport situation.
• Safety bubbles in collision avoidance: To maintain safety, especially in navigation,
collision avoidance systems are very important. The idea of safety bubbles is bor-
rowed from the concept of protective zones around things or vehicles and can be an
important part of collision avoidance strategies. Research such as that by Sandu
and Susnea (2022) [9], shows LiDAR-based soft collision avoidance using safety
bubbles.
2.1.1
LiDAR-based Perception and Computational Efficiency
Perception, as a fundamental component of any obstacle avoidance system, is depen-
dent upon accurate and real-time inputs. LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging), which
produces detailed 3D point clouds (Balasubramaniam & Pasricha, 2022; Li et al., 2021),
offers robust sensory capabilities for self-driving cars [4] [5]. Its power to see the shape
and distance of objects makes it very important in the airport’s complicated setting. How-
ever, effectively using LiDAR for the de-icing truck requires addressing several key points:
• Computational Efficiency: The density of LiDAR data is a computational problem, es-
pecially in real-time outdoor settings that may have sensor noise. Methods that can
process LiDAR point clouds quickly are preferred for finding and following objects
. Papers such as Madake et al. (2022) [6] offer insights about the preprocessing
methods, as well as clustering approaches like DBSCAN that are specifically used
in vehicle detection. Research such as Li et al. (2021) [5] shows new methods of
fusion with multiple lidar that might improve obstacle detection even more.
• Real-time Adjustments: Approaches like Sandu and Susnea (2022) [9], which talks
about the concept of a dynamic safety bubble, are needed for situations that need
to be changed instantly. This method constantly checks the nearby surroundings
and keeps updating the spatial limits of safety bubbles. It helps self-driving cars

recognize possible crash dangers and steer clear from them, improving general
safety and handling in complicated traffic situations.
• Shape of Obstacle: The shape of an obstacle is crucial in deciding the suitable
avoidance actions. As per Ros, (2018) [10], circular models that have been used
traditionally may not work well for longer objects. This shows the importance of
having specific detection techniques in a airport setting.
• LiDAR Disadvantages: Although it is a strong tool, there are some limitations of
LiDAR that must be recognized. Sometimes, its performance can be harmed by
bad weather like fog or heavy rain. Also, occlusions caused by other big vehicles or
structures might hinder it from accurately detecting objects. Sensor fusion could be
the answer to these issues as stated in the work of Balasubramaniam and Pasricha
(2022) [4] who emphasize on combining LiDAR with other sensors such as cameras
and radar for better detection precision and strength. Moreover, figuring out the
intention and possible paths of dynamic agents such as quick walking pedestrians
or other self-governing vehicles is still being researched.
2.1.2
SLAM for Mapping and Localization
Active SLAM systems rely on making predictions about future states and anticipating un-
seen areas for successful navigation. Placed et al. (2023) [7], emphasizes the increasing
utilization of deep learning in improving these forecasting aspects. Approaches based
on deep learning show notable progress in scene completion and occupancy anticipation,
going beyond the capacity of conventional sensor models.
The survey brings out many open research inquiries in Active SLAM area, like state pre-
diction and handling dynamic environments. It predicts the future of Active SLAM by
accepting exploration with multi-robot systems, advanced environment representations,
and the development of practical real-world applications.
The de-icing truck could apply Active SLAM for mapping parts of airport that were not
charted before, like construction zones temporarily set up or new staging areas. Predictive
path planning in these non-mapped sections is important for safe and effective movement.
The activity in airports is very changing, and this makes a special difficulty for Active
SLAM systems. Especially, it’s hard for systems that use object tracking with traditional
filtering methods. Vehicles and workers often move around, possibly having irregular
paths or sudden changes in their speed. This might bring non-linearities and noise which
can make filters like the Kalman Filter perform less effectively. Furthermore, things could
appear and vanish from the truck’s sight, making it difficult for the filter to uphold precise
state evaluations.
2.1.3
Filtering in Noisy Environments
While Extended Kalman Filters (EKFs) are commonly applied for tracking objects in au-
tonomous systems, their effectiveness in noisy and dynamic settings like airports must
be cautiously examined. This is shown by Saman and Lotfy (2016) [8], who demonstrate
the possible limitations of EKFs while noting how their accuracy might deteriorate under
fast changing conditions (Balasubramaniam & Pasricha, 2022) [4]. Their thoughts imply
that noise and high dynamics could affect EKF performance, highlighting the requirement
to either explore noise-tolerant variations of the EKF or look into other filtering as well as
estimation methods specifically designed for airport environment challenges. The airport
environment introduces different types of noise that can impact EKF performance.

2.1.4
Safety Bubbles in Collision Avoidance
The idea of safety bubbles, as explained in Sandu and Susnea (2022) [9], talks about
protective zones around a car or other mobile objects to improve accident prevention and
safety actions.
A safety bubble functions by setting a predefined protective area around the vehicle or any
other moving thing. This zone is formed from sensor data coming from LiDAR, radar, cam-
eras or ultrasonic sensors to detect if there are things present and how they are moving
within the area near the car.
Once these objects are identified, the safety bubble flexibly changes its dimension and
form. This is influenced by different aspects, such as how fast or in which way an item
moves. The quicker an object moves towards one side, the more its related safety bub-
ble’s limit stretches in that direction.
The paper’s explanation is that, when safety bubbles touch each other and show possible
collision danger, it sends signals to the upper control layers. These alerts can activate
measures for avoiding collisions, such as warning the driver, using automated brakes, or
starting evasive actions.
Fundamentally, safety bubbles function through ongoing awareness of the nearby envi-
ronment and early detection of possible collisions by observing how objects move or come
close to each other. By setting up these protective zones and quickly reacting to potential
dangers, safety bubble systems help improve general safety in transportation situations.
Safety bubbles show a promising method to prevent collisions in transportation systems.
They use sensor technologies and prediction formulas to build safety areas around ve-
hicles, reducing the possibility of accidents. Although current studies show that safety
bubbles are good at lessening crashes, improved technology and work between different
fields will help with the issues left and make this approach more successful.

3.1.2
DBSCAN
The DBSCAN algorithm was chosen to cluster the point cloud. It puts every point into
one of three groups: core points, border (edge) points, and noise points. Clusters include
core and edge points but not noise ones, which are considered as outliers and discarded.
DBSCAN has an advantage over many other algorithms because it does not need to
know the exact number of expected clusters beforehand. This is important for truck safety
systems since objects in front of the truck vary all the time.
DBSCAN works with two parameters: Epsilon (ϵ), which is the radius around a data point,
and Min_Samples that represents minimum number of data points required to form a dense
cluster. For each data point, if it has more than Min_Samples neighbors within its radius
then it is classified as ”core” point. If a point lies within the radius of core point but does
not fulfill this count condition, then such points are called ”edge” points. A noise point is
not within the range of any other points.
The algorithm starts with an arbitrary point and keeps repeating until no more core or edge
points can be added to the cluster. It then chooses another random point from the leftover
unvisited points and goes on.

The main difficulty was creating a strong anti-collision system that could identify and steer

technologies to make sure it functions correctly. The following subsections explain in

5.1.1
Problems identified
During the development of the autonomous de-icing system, several critical challenges
were identified that needed to be addressed to ensure the efficiency and reliability of the
system. Understanding these issues was essential to develop appropriate solutions and
guide the design process. The main issues identified include the following:
• Object tracking
One significant problem was the issue with object tracking. The system found it
challenging to keep track of clustered point clouds after every LiDAR scan when
they moved out from field view, due to intermittent visibility of objects within sensor’s
range. This led to low consistency and reliability in tracking.
• Point cloud processing of dynamic objects
The processing of the point cloud data presented a challenge due to the filtering
of the dynamic objects before integrating them into the SLAM. The purpose behind
this filtering was stopping noise from filling up the map. With each scan, it creates
a new map and if an item changes its place, then its old position will still stay in the
map. So, as a result, dynamic objects were not included in the processing of it to
keep the map clear and precise.
• Absolute position of clustered points
Clustered points were being referenced with respect to the truck, so it was not possi-
ble to determine if they belonged to a static or dynamic object. If the truck moved, all
the points would move with respect to it. If there is a static object, its points should
be in the same place; however this distinction was not possible due to how these
clusters were being referenced against the truck reference frame.
• Dynamic object handling
The anti-collision system faced a major challenge with dynamic objects, because
they gave less reaction and correction time than static ones. The unpredictability of
the motion required constant vigilance and flexible responses from the system. This
made it more complicated to maintain a safe area of operation, necessitating deci-
sions to be made in small time periods to ensure collision avoidance in dynamically
changing scenarios.
• Collisions processing
Calculating the distance to objects that have been detected is a complex task, as it
can require high computational power. This intensive task in the computer caused

delay for the system’s response and limited its ability for real-time collision avoid-
ance. It is important to look into other ways of processing collisions so that the
system can maintain efficient functioning.
• Reaching the goal
When avoiding a potential obstacle, it is very important to return to the initial route
and reach the target smoothly. This procedure involves the autonomous truck ac-
curately reassessing its trajectory to match the predetermined path. The system
must effectively re-route the path while constantly watching for new obstacles or
disturbances in the environment.
These challenges indicate the problems involved in establishing a robust and effective
system. Once these problems have been recognized, the basis is set for the next part,
which consists of finding solutions and methods to address these difficulties and guide
the design process appropriately.
5.1.2
Design considerations
This subsection outlines the systematic approach taken to tackle the challenges identified
during the development of the autonomous de-icing system. Each problem was carefully
analyzed, leading to the formulation of specific strategies and methodologies to overcome
them. By addressing these challenges head-on, the aim was to design effective solutions
that ensure the system’s reliability, safety, and performance in real-world airport environ-
ments.
• Object tracking
To solve the problem of object tracking, SLAM (simultaneous localization and map-
ping) was used. Tracking elements had major problems because sometimes objects
could be seen by the sensors but then not reappear, making constant tracking diffi-
cult. SLAM helped to create a constant map of the surroundings, while monitoring
where the vehicle was located on this map. In this way, SLAM made the system
able to better track objects, even if they temporarily disappeared from the LiDAR
view. This method helped to re-identify the objects when they came back into view,
increasing the consistency and reliability of the tracking.
• Point cloud processing of dynamic objects
The difficulty with point cloud processing for dynamic objects was tackled by using
two point cloud datasets together. The first dataset, which came from SLAM, gave
details about static objects and helped to create a solid map of the environment. The
second set of data was taken from the LiDAR; it included both static and moving
objects showing real-time changes in surroundings. Dynamic objects were a big
difficulty because their motion brought in noise and errors to the map. If not managed
correctly, this noise and errors can make the system work slower.
• Absolute position of clustered points

was necessary to apply a system of reference transformation. In this approach, the
SLAM system was used to provide a transformation matrix linking the truck’s local

was crucial for translating the relative positions of the clustered points, detected by
the truck’s sensors, into absolute positions, facilitating the tracking of these points
through multiple LiDAR scans. Through this method, it became possible to distin-
guish static objects from dynamic ones by analyzing the consistency of their posi-
tions over time. Static objects would have low displacements between the cluster

SLAM
SLAM, which stands for Simultaneous Localization and Mapping, is a crucial aspect in
robotics and autonomous systems. It facilitates these machines to travel through unknown
areas while creating maps at the same time. This method doesn’t need help from GPS
or already existing maps.
SLAM is a complex process that combines data from various sources to create consistent
maps of an environment while simultaneously determining the location within it. Usually,
SLAM tasks involve two main steps: localization and mapping. Localization finds out
where exactly a robot or vehicle is situated in its surroundings, which is very important for
precise movement and choices made by the system. Mapping builds up a representation
of the environment as the robot moves through it.
SLAM systems have great reliance on different sensors to collect important information
about the surrounding environment and movements of robots or vehicles. In this project,

persistent tracking of detected obstacles. Without SLAM, the truck might detect an object
in one frame but lose track of it in next frames due to occlusion. SLAM algorithms, like

Kalman Filter
The Kalman Filter (KF) is a great method for state estimation in systems that have linear dy-
namics and measurements, as well as follow Gaussian distributions. It uses the concepts
of recursive Bayesian filtering. This includes combining predictions from a linear dynamic
model with random measurements to calculate best estimates about the system’s state
during its operation.
In the simulation, the Kalman filter plays a special role. It helps to predict where objects
moving with constant velocity will be at future positions. By comparing these estimates
with the predicted position of the truck, potential collisions can be detected before they
occur. This ability to detect potential collisions enables the system to act in advance to
avoid accidents and thus increases safety and efficiency in dynamic environments.
In order to obtain predictions using the Kalman filter, a state vector and state transition
matrix are needed.

position and velocity in a 2D space.

6.3.2
System limitations
This section identifies areas where the system’s performance did not meet expectations
or where specific conditions posed significant difficulties during the test scenarios. By
detailing these limitations, the aim is to provide a transparent evaluation of the system’s
current capabilities and outline factors that may affect its effectiveness in real-world appli-
cations. Understanding these limitations is important to guide future improvements and
ensure the development of a robust and reliable anti-collision system.
The performance of the anti-collision system is affected under certain conditions:
• Looking at table 6.3, it is clear that the system performs poorly in case 6, with a
success rate of 21%. In this case, two cars approach simultaneously from one side.
If the two cars are close together, once the truck stops waiting for the first car to
pass, it may detect that the second car is ahead and not to the side. As a result,
the system may not apply the appropriate corrective action, which can lead to a
collision.
• Another critical situation occurs when the truck encounters a dynamic obstacle while
turning. Case 7, with a success rate of 62%, illustrates this problem. In this case,
the truck encounters a car at an intersection while turning left. Initially, the obstacle
is perceived as being located in front, but as the truck continues to turn, the obstacle
is eventually perceived as being located to the side. In this transition between the
two main cases, the system response is not well defined, which increases the risk
of collision.
• In addition, a limitation not observed in the cases analyzed but worth mentioning is
the behavior of the system in environments with numerous elements. Safety bub-

6.4
Future works
This part talks about the suggestions for future research and development that come
from what is discovered in this project. Making use of the strengths and limitations of the
current system, it proposes improvements and alternative methods to tackle problems as
they exist now. By discussing these upcoming works, the aim is to guide progress towards
more advanced, trustworthy and effective collision avoidance systems in autonomous de-
icing trucks along with alike applications.
6.4.1
Implement Velocity Obstacle Approach
An interesting subject to investigate is the velocity obstacle method. This technique pre-
dicts probable accidents by taking into account the velocities of the vehicle and those
from elements around it in its surroundings. By computing relative speed between truck
and obstacles, this system can identify potential collisions and change vehicle’s route to
prevent them. This method could enhance the system’s adaptability to more complicated
and shifting environments, particularly in scenarios with numerous dynamic objects.
6.4.2
Develop a Decision Tree for Control Actions
An additional way to enhance this solution is by introducing a decision tree to optimize con-
trol actions. The use of a decision tree could help in systematically weigh different factors
like nearness to obstacles, truck velocity and available routes, to decide what control ac-
tion must be taken at every moment. This approach would allow for more context-aware
decision making, possibly improving how quickly and safely the system responds. With
the help of machine learning methods, the decision tree can be trained and refined over
time, improving its performance with continued use.
6.4.3
Integrate Extended Kalman Filter for objects with variable speed
One more place to make advancements is the use of Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) for
dealing with objects that have changing speeds. The EKF, unlike the basic Kalman Filter
which supposes a steady velocity, can model non-linear dynamics. This makes it appro-
priate for predicting positions and speeds of things which accelerate or slow down as well
as change direction. By incorporating this improvement collision avoidance system can
alter truck’s path in advance according to precise forecasts. It will enhance safety and
guidance within intricate surroundings.
6.4.4
Simulate Weather Conditions
A very important part for the future is to include weather conditions like fog, rain and snow
in the simulation. These can have a big impact on how well sensors work and also vehicle
movements; this poses more difficulties for avoiding collisions. By creating simulations
of various weather situations, it becomes possible to test how strong and dependable the
system remains in tough conditions. This improvement would give helpful data about the
system’s reaction to different weather situations, aiding in the creation of approaches for
maintaining its functionality and safety under all types of weather.
6.4.5
Sensor expansion and integration
In the future, more sensors could possibly be added and combined. The anti-collision sys-
tem might include GPS, inertial measurement units (IMU) and advanced cameras. This
would supply it with better data from a variety of sources. The integration of these sen-
sors would allow the system to have a more complete understanding of its environment,
improving the ability to make informed decisions. In addition, exploring sensor fusion tech-
niques to combine data from multiple sources could improve the accuracy and reliability
of the system’s perception capabilities.
