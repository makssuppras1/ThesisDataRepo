Abstract
Denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs) are deep generative models that generate high qual-
ity images achieving state-of-the-art Fréchet inception distance (FID) and inception scores (IS) on
benchmark datasets. However, sampling from the models is extremely time consuming compared to
VAE, GAN and Flow-based models, making it computationally challenging to gain the full potential of
these models on large datasets. This thesis presents an investigation of the balance between sampling
time and image quality by analysing the effect of input dimensionality and model size. Based on these
insights, this work introduces downsampled denoising diffusion probabilistic models (dDDPMs), which
adds light-weight downsampling and upsampling networks around the standard unconditional DDPM
implementation. The dDDPMs achieve between 29 and 266 times faster sampling on 256×256 datasets
compared to standard DDPM, while providing FID scores of 37.8 on CIFAR-10, 7.9 on CelebA and 20.7
on CelebAMask-HQ. These results show that it is possible to build memory and computational eﬀicient
diffusion models while preserving most of the visual quality compared to full-sized DDPMs, providing
a promising direction for deep generative models in constrained environments. The code for the entire
project has been open sourced to a public GitHub repository1.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Over the past years, generative models have shown to be capable of generating increasingly more realistic
images (figure 1.1) with the state-of-the-art being the generative adversarial networks (GANs), until
around two years ago where a new class of generative models outperformed GANs on image generation
with the denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs) (Dhariwal et al. 2021). This new class of
models are typically easier and more stable to train than GANs, while also providing a higher quality
of generated samples and was initially inspired by non-equilibrium statistical physics (Sohl-Dickstein
et al. 2015). However, the DDPMs are very slow to sample from and different efforts have been made
to reduce this sampling time while preserving the quality of samples as best as possible. A vision of
increasing the capabilities of generative models is that in the process, the computers understanding of
the world and what it is made up of gets better and better (Karpathy et al. 2020).
Figure 1.1: Increasing sampling quality of state-of-the-art generative models from years 2014 to 2017,
2020 and 2021. Top: Generated by GANs (Goodfellow et al. 2014; Radford et al. 2015; M. Liu et al.
2016; Karras et al. 2017; Brundage et al. 2018). Bottom: Generated by diffusion models (Ho, Jain,
et al. 2020; Vahdat et al. 2021)
Two methods that increases sampling speed by reducing the amount of computational resources needed
by the DDPM has been proposed very recently.
The first of these methods is the latent diffusion
model (LDM), which uses a powerful pretrained autoencoder (AE) to downsample data to a latent
space in which the DDPM operates, where after the decoder of the AE can upsample the output of
latent space DDPM to original image space (Rombach et al. 2021). The second method, subspace
diffusion models (Jing et al. 2022), instead use a DDPM in the full-dimensional space for low noise
levels, while they downsample to a latent space using principal component analysis (PCA) instead of

an encoder network, which they use to denoise the time scales with large noise levels. Similarly, the
DiffuseVAE (Pandey et al. 2022) uses pretrained variational autoencoders (VAEs), but instead employs a
conditional parameterization of the DDPM using the VAE which improves upon the sampling eﬀiciency
of the unconditional diffusion model. Another approach has been to reduce the number of scales used to
produce samples at test time, which was experimented with in the improved DDPM paper (Nichol and
Dhariwal 2021) and similarly the denoising diffusion implicit models (DDIM) uses much fewer scales
at sampling time by generalizing the Markov diffusion process in the DDPM, providing a x10 to x50
sampling speed-up compared to the original DDPM (J. Song et al. 2020).
The goal of this thesis is to investigate the new state-of-the-art generative models introduced as the
denoising diffusion probabilistic models, reproducing results on classical benchmark datasets and invent
a light weight network that can be built around the DDPMs in order to reduce sampling time and
memory requirements during training and sampling, and allow for the use of reduced size models
operating on downsampled representations of the original data, encoded by the downsampling network,
while variables generated by the DDPM can be decoded by the upsampling network to reside in the
original data space. The main contributions of this thesis is that the proposed method,
• Heavily reduces sampling time, with a reduction in the size of the input data to the DDPM
• Introduces a simple and light encoder-decoder architecture, that can be trained easily and jointly
with the standard DDPM architecture, that consists of ResNet blocks resembling a U-Net like
architecture, without the use of skip-connections.
• Shows that you do not need big pretrained VAE encoders and decoders to downsample and
upsample the training data to use DDPMs in a downsampled space.
• Introduces a balance between sampling speed and quality, by investigating what drives speedup
in regards to input data dimensionality and the size of DDPMs.

CHAPTER 2
Background
2.1
Convolutional Neural Networks
Convolutions are a very common operation in signal processing and image analysis. This section will
explain how to perform convolutions and pooling operations for neural networks on two-dimensional
images, which can both be black and white (a single color channel) or color images (three color channels).
Additionally, it will briefly explain the residual neural network blocks and the U-Net architecture.
2.1.1
Convolution and Pooling Layers
A convolution is done between some input, x, and a kernel f (also called a filter), resulting in a
feature map y.
In general, a convolution is defined by the size of the kernel ks, the stride of the
convolution s, meaning how much the middle of the kernel moves for each output to the feature map,
and finally whether the input is padded or not. Additionally, in computer vision and machine learning,
the convolutions also employ the idea of having a certain number of channels Cin of the input and Cout
of the feature map. In general for two-dimensional images x of shape (Cin × H × W) a single pixel in
the feature map is computed by (2.1), when the kernel does not compute more than a single output
channel.

2.1.3
U-Net
The U-Net model is a convolutional network originally proposed for biomedical image segmentation
(Ronneberger et al. 2015). The original implementation consists of three main parts. Firstly, the con-
tracting path uses a number of 3-by-3 convolutions each followed by a rectified linear unit (ReLU),
where a 2-by-2 max pooling layer with stride 2 is employed at the end acting layer to perform down-
sampling. In the layer following a downsample the number of channels double, and in order to conserve
the pixel mapping it is important that each pooling layer is applied to evenly sized height and width
dimensions. Secondly, between the contracting and expanding path, there is a middle layer consisting
of a couple of 3-by-3 convolutions. Thirdly, the expanding path is like a reverse of the contracting path,
but instead it uses a 2-by-2 up-convolution at the beginning of each layer to perform upsampling. At
the end of the expanding path a single 1-by-1 convolution is used to map the feature channels into the
desired number of channels for each pixel, where these channels correspond to the different classes for
the image segmentation problem. It should be noted that the convolutions in the original U-Net do not
use padding, but since it has an exact mapping between the output segmentation map and the input,
it can easily be extrapolated to cover all pixels of the input image. A slightly modified depiction of
this architecture is shown on Figure 2.6. This modified version is more similar to the way the U-Net
architecture is used for generative modelling e.g. with the PixelCNN++ (Salimans, Karpathy, et al.
2017).

2.2
Generative Modelling
Generative modelling is a branch of unsupervised machine learning, where neural networks are trained
to approximate complicated, high-dimensional probability distributions X ∈Rn using a large number
of training data x ∼X, also called observations. Each observation is said to consist of multiple features,
where a feature for e.g. the image generation problem is typically defined to be each individual pixel.
The goal is then to create and train a generator gθ, with parameters θ, that can map samples from
another distribution z ∼Z ∈Rq to the original image space Rn, which features follow the same general
rule set as the original observations from X (Ruthotto et al. 2021; Foster 2019).
gθ :
Rq →Rn
(2.2)
When the generator (2.2) is trained, it allows for generating samples from the complicated space X
using a simpler space Z by sampling z ∼Z, and computing ˆx ≈gθ(z), where Z usually is a univariate
Gaussian distribution. Typically, z is referred to as a latent variable, while Z is referred to as the latent
space. For most models, the dimensionality of the latent space is much smaller than the original space
q << n. In terms of distributions, we can think of each data point in the dataset, x, to be sampled
from some true distribution of the data p(x). Then the goal of the generative model is to describe a
distribution pθ(x) that is as close to the true data distribution p(x) as possible, pθ(x) ≈p(x). The
way that the model generates the distribution is by mapping samples from a unit Gaussian distribution
z ∼N(0, I) using a neural network of parameters θ. An illustration of how a trained model maps the
latent to generate samples that tries to match the true data distribution is shown on Figure 2.7.

2.3.3
Model architecture for the Reverse Process
In order to model the reverse process, an U-Net architecture is used to predict the noise. This archi-
tecture is adapted, such that it is similar to an unmasked PixelCNN++ using group normalization
instead of weight normalization and with multi-head attention. Additionally, in order to share param-
eters across time, the model incorporates the diffusion time t as a sinusoidal positional embedding in
the residual blocks of the U-Net, such that it is somewhat aware of how noisy the current input is
(Ronneberger et al. 2015; Salimans, Karpathy, et al. 2017; Ho, Jain, et al. 2020; Nichol and Dhariwal
2021).
Figure 2.12: Illustration of the residual network block architecture used in the U-Net in the DDPM
reverse process.
For models trained on images of size 32 × 32, the U-Net in the standard DDPM implementation with
some adaptions from the improved DDPM paper (Ho, Jain, et al. 2020; Nichol and Dhariwal 2021) uses
four layers, such that the middle layer is 4×4, for an example see Figure 2.6. However, instead of using
simple convolutions in each layer, the architecture used for the DDPM uses two residual network blocks
(Figure 2.12) in each layer along with a linear attention module and a downsampling or upsampling
block at the end, depending on whether we are in the contracting or expanding path.

Diffusion Model Variants
The diffusion probabilistic models were initially introduced in (Sohl-Dickstein et al. 2015), but didn’t
initially spike an interest in the generative modelling community, however with the introduction of the
denoising diffusion probabilistic models in (Ho, Jain, et al. 2020), the publications regarding diffusion
models accelerated very quickly. Some methods improve the sample quality of the DDPM by using
different training techniques, altering the diffusion process or changing the reverse process architecture

2.5
Performance Metrics
One problem with generative models are that they are diﬀicult to evaluate, especially considering that
the quality of the output is largely subjective. However, some metrics have been developed over the
years that tries to evaluate the quality of the generator in an objective manner. The most commonly
used metrics are the precision, recall, inception score (IS) and Fréchet inception distance (FID), which
are explained in the following subsections. This project will use the OpenAI guided diffusion GitHub
repository5 in order to compute these metrics, which uses the InceptionV3 model (Szegedy et al. 2015)
to compute IS, FID and sFID, and a manifold estimator to compute precision and recall.
2.5.1
Inception Score
The inception score is a metric proposed for evaluating GANs, and was found to correlate well with
human evaluation (Salimans, Goodfellow, et al. 2016). The score is computed in an automated way
using a pre-trained InceptionV3 model to evaluate samples from a generative model. This is done by
computing the conditional label distribution p(y|x) for each generated sample with the InceptionV3
model, and then the inception score captures two things. Firstly, the image quality is captured by
evaluating the entropy of p(y|x), where low entropy indicates that an image has meaningful objects.
Secondly, the image diversity is captured by evaluating the entropy of the marginal
∫
p(y|x = G(z)),
which is expected to be high for a model capable of generating a wide range of different objects. The
IS is then given by (2.36), where the exponential is used to make the resulting values easier to compare
(Salimans, Goodfellow, et al. 2016).
IS = exp (ExDKL(p(y|x) || p(y)))
(2.36)
The marginal probability is calculated as the average of the conditional probabilities for the images in
the group (p(y)), and the KL divergence is calculated from (2.37).
DKL(p(y|x) || p(y)) = p(y|x) · (log(p(y|x)) log(p(y)))
(2.37)
Since we have a finite number of images, the expectation can be calculated as the sum of (2.37) for
all images, x, in the batch. The size of the batch used to compute the inception score is assumed to
be rather large, at around 50k samples (Salimans, Goodfellow, et al. 2016). A higher inception score
means better quality samples, where the lowest value is 1 and the highest value is 1000, which is the
number of classes for the InceptionV3 model trained on LSVRC6.
2.5.2
Fréchet Inception Distance
The Fréchet inception distance was proposed for evaluating GANs as an improvement over the inception
score, providing a more consistent measure of similarity of the generated images to the real images, also
called the reference images. The FID assumes that the coding units follow a multidimensional Gaussian,
then the difference of the generated and real images are measured by the Fréchet distance between their
respective Gaussian distributions (mean and covariance). The FID is then calculated by (2.38), which
is the Fréchet distance between the Gaussians for the reference images, (µ, C), and generated images
(µw, Cw) (Heusel et al. 2017).
FID = ||µ −µw||2

2.5.3
Precision and Recall
The two metrics, precision and recall, were initially made for classification models. The metrics are
somewhat similar, capturing two different aspects of the truth of the predictions made by a classification
model. Precision tries to capture the proportion of positive identifications that were correct (2.39), while
recall instead tries to capture the proportion of actual positives that were identified correctly (2.40).
precision =
TP
TP + FP
(2.39)
recall =
TP
TP + FN
(2.40)
Where true positives (TP) are the predictions of the positive class when the class is actually positive,
false positives (FP) are the predictions of the positive class when the class is actually negative and
false negatives (FN) are the prediction of the negative class when the class is actually positive. Thus a
perfect model has a precision and recall score of 1.
2.5.3.1
Precision and Recall for Generative Models
Since generative models do not perform predictions of any sorts, the way we define and compute the
precision and recall metrics are a bit different. If we denote the distribution of the real images with Pr
and the distribution of generated images with Pg, we can define the metrics with respect to these two
distributions (Figure 2.18). Then, the precision metric measures how many of the generated images
that are realistic, while the recall metric measures how much of the distribution of the training data
is covered by the generated distribution. The motivation for using the precision and recall metrics
in addition to Fréchet inception distance, is that it is impossible to infer anything about whether the
FID value indicates that the model can generate realistic images (high precision), or if it is capable
of generating large amount of variation between images to match the training data (high recall), or
whether we are somewhere in between.
Figure 2.18: Illustration of the precision and recall metrics in terms of the distributions of the real
and generated images, Pr and Pg respectively. The distribution of the real images are shown in blue,
while the distribution of the generated images are shown in red. The dots then indicate samples from
these distributions (Kynkäänniemi et al. 2019).
In order to compute the metrics, a number of samples are drawn from the distribution of the training
data, Pr, and from the generated distribution, Pg. The number of samples drawn from each distribution
somewhat affect the value of the metric and is thus chosen to be equal to the number of samples used
for the FID and IS for generality. The samples are then embedded into high-dimensional feature vectors
ϕr and ϕg and then a corresponding manifold is estimated for both of these high-dimensional vectors
over all samples. The original paper uses a pretrained VGG-16 model, however the computations done
with the Inception-V3 model leads to similar results (Kynkäänniemi et al. 2019), thus the activations
computed when computing the IS and FID will also be used to compute the precision and recall metrics.

3.2.2
Learning the Downsampling and Upsampling Networks
The idea behind learning the downsampling and upsampling networks are then two-fold. Firstly, the
idea behind learning a good downsampling network is that it will hopefully encode the original data
x0 into a downsampled space such that it improves the training of the DDPM employed in said space,
compared to simply performing an average pool to create a downsampled space. Secondly, learning a
good upsampling network will hopefully allow the dDDPM to sample images, and upsample them to
the original image space, that then look like they come from a DDPM trained in the original image
space. In order to train these networks, this thesis will propose two different training architectures
denoted as dDDPM-t and dDDPM-AE, both introducing a reconstruction loss term that is responsible
for updating the weights of the downsampling and upsampling networks using back propagation. This
reconstruction loss term, Lrecon, is computed in the original image space as the L2 term between the
input image x0 and some reconstructed image ˆx0.

image for the AE architecture will not be passed through the DDPM in downsampled space itself,
but is simply computed directly by upsampling the downsampled space input, z = downsample(x0),
resulting in a reconstruction ˆx0 = upsample(z0) that only optimizes the downsampling and upsampling
networks, without affecting the DDPM in the downsampled space (illustrated on Figure 3.7).

The third idea was to use the likelihood, p(x0|x1) in input space in order to train the downsampling and

task, thus we discarded this idea early in the process since the other ideas provided immediate results

Since the DDPM operates in a space where the data is scaled to [−1; 1], it might be interesting to
investigate how the activation functions operate around this interval (Table 3.1 and Figure 3.8), when
designing the downsampling and upsampling architectures.
Figure 3.8: Plots of several activation functions in two different ranges
It is clear that something like the rectified linear unit (ReLU) would probably perform poorly, since
half of the values will be cut off by the activation function, leaving us with only a [0; 1] interval for
the values of the data, while the activation function employed in the U-Net in the DDPM is the Mish
activation, which provides a broader range of values. Another notable activation function is the tanh
activation, which simply forces the data to be between [−1, 1], which might be useful in order to match
the likelihoods and assumptions made in Section 2.3.

Joint Sampling of dDDPM and DDPM
An idea to improve the quality of samples, while still heavily reducing the sampling time, is to at test
time combine a dDDPM and a DDPM trained on the same dataset. The idea is then to denoise the first
t = [T, ..., T1] steps in downsampled space using the dDDPM, then upsample the zT1 to input space
xT1, and input that to the DDPM, which can denoise the final few steps t = [T2, ..., 0] (Algorithm 5).
The timesteps T1 and T2 does not have to be T2 = T1 −1, but T2 can take any value up to T, depending
on how much noise is expected after upsampling zT1 to xT1.

CHAPTER 4
Experiments
In order to be able to easier compare with previous DDPM implementations, and inherit the advance-
ments made in improving sampling quality with respect to optimizing hyper-parameters, we match the
depth of the DDPM to T = 1000 and use the simple loss objective, Lsimple, to optimize sampling quality
(Ho, Jain, et al. 2020). The same argument goes for the schedule of the forward process variances, βt,
which is chosen to be a linear schedule from β1 = 10−4 to βT = 0.02.
For the reverse process modelling, the U-Net architecture and hyperparameters proposed in (Ho,
Jain, et al. 2020) is also inherited, which is a U-Net based on a Wide ResNet using four layers with
two convolutional residual network blocks in each layer, using both dropout of 10% and group normal-
ization in each residual block (Figure 2.12). The initial layer operates on 128 feature channels, while
the following three layers operates on 256 feature channels resulting in a model with approximately
22M parameters (Table 4.5), which is however 33% less than in the original of 35.7M. As in the DDPM
paper, an exponential moving average (EMA) was used on the model parameters with a decay factor
of 0.995, which was a good value in the early experiments.
For the downsampling and upsampling networks, all the dDDPMs use three bottleneck ResNet
blocks (Figure 2.5) in each layer, as shown for the dDDPM x1 (Figure 3.5), which adds around 150k
parameters per layer. A tanh output activation is added at the end of each of both the downsampling
and upsampling networks, as studied in section 4.4.3. Additionally, the number of input feature chan-
nels used in each bottleneck ResNet block is 64 and the number of feature channels of the encoded
downsampled variable is set to 8. Finally, the AE training architecture is used unless stated otherwise.
In regards to training, the Adam1 optimizer with standard parameters is used with a learning rate
of 2 · 10−4, which seemed stable to train with for all the datasets, and random flipping the training
data is only studied in the ablation in section 4.4.4. Since the GPUs have limited resources in terms
of memory, the batch size used for the large images in CelebAMask-HQ 256 × 256 was set to 32, while
the batch size used for datasets with smaller images, MNIST, CIFAR-10 and CelebA, was set to 128
following the literature. Additionally, the number of training steps are set to 800k which resulted in a
training time for CelebA-HQ of around five days, and around two to three days for CIFAR-10, using a
single NVIDIA A100 GPU2.
Note that even though all 256 × 256 face-image models were trained on CelebAMask-HQ, they
should be able to be directly comparable to models trained on CelebA-HQ as the datasets are near
identical (Table E.1 in Appendix E). In the tables in the following sections, the values reported for the
models with references are taken directly from their respective papers, DDPMs without any references
are trained and evaluated with my own implementation of the standard DDPM from (Ho, Jain, et al.
2020), while other models are evaluated using the checkpoints mentioned in Table 4.1.

4.4.3
Output Activation for Downsampling and Upsampling Networks
After evaluating multiple different hyper parameters and training routines, it was clear that the varia-
tional lower bound changed drastically across different setups, a metric that should indicate how well
the DDPM is performing, thus stabilizing this across different training routines and hyper parameters
should lead to better performing models overall. This inspired the idea of using an output activation
for the downsampling network to force the input to the DDPM in downsampled space to be within the
expected values, z0 ∈[−1, 1], which is achieved by using the hyperbolic tangent.
This is done by training a dDDPM-AE x3 on the CelebAMask-HQ, such that the downsampled vari-
able has dimensions 8 × 32 × 32, along with two alterations. For the first alteration the tanh activation
function is just applied to the output of the downsampling network, while for the second alteration the
tanh activation function is also applied to the output of the upsampling network. As seen on Table 4.8,
using these activation functions both stabilizes the VLB and the loss of the DDPM Lsimple, while also
improving the quality of samples in terms of FID and sFID.

4.4.6
Joint Sampling from dDDPM and DDPM
An attempt to increase the quality of samples, while retaining a large speedup in sampling time, has
been made by performing joint sampling of a trained dDDPM-AE x3 and the pretrained DDPM-P2.
This is done following Algorithm 5, where we first sample [T, T1] from the dDDPM and store those
samples, xT1, where after joint sampling is performed by taking these samples and use the scales [T2, 0]
from the DDPM, where T = 1000, T1 = 11 and different T2’s are evaluated as shown on Figure 4.9.
Similarily, joint sampling is performed using all scales of the dDDPM (T1 = 0), and the same varying
number of scales from the DDPM, shown on Figure 4.10. Note that when sampling from the DDPM-P2,
only every 4th scale is used, following the script on their GitHub.
From these images, we can see that sampling jointly from a dDDPM and a DDPM increases the
quality, where the best samples require around 140 seconds when T1 = 11, while they require only
around 50 or 100 seconds when T1 = 0. Thus, spending an additional second in the dDDPM, from 27
to 28 seconds, allows for somewhat fast joint sampling that might help remove artifacts from the images
generated by the dDDPMs, even when they were not trained jointly.

4.5
Discussion and Future Work
Before discussing anything else, it is important to note the performance of the baseline DDPM imple-
mentation used throughout all experiments. A choice was made to use the simplest possible baseline
in terms of hyper parameters, and not adjust it to the dataset or to the size of the downsampled space.
Additionally, as mentioned in section 2.3.3 the baseline implementation used might have a different
implementation of the U-Net than e.g. the one from OpenAI. These choices were made in order to;
firstly not spend the most part of the time on optimizing the DDPM, since there are plenty of litera-
ture on that; secondly provide some kind of lower bound on the performance such that the results of
the dDDPM hopefully only improves. Comparing the image quality generated by the DDPM used in
this thesis to the original results provided by (Ho, Jain, et al. 2020), it is seen that on CIFAR-10 the
performance is much worse (16.38 versus 3.17 FID, Table 4.2).
Inspecting the samples generated by different dDDPMs trained on CelebAMask-HQ (figures 4.3, 4.4
and 4.5), shows the downsampled space samples generated by the dDDPM on the left side, while the
right hand side is the output of the upsampling network when that specific sample is input, resulting
in 256 × 256 color images from 16 × 16, 32 × 32 and 64 × 64 downsampled space samples respectively.
These figures are meant to visually demonstrate the difference in dimensions of what is produced by the
DDPM and the upsampled results. This showcases that lightweight upsampling networks are in fact
capable of upsampling from very small downsampled spaces to a larger input space.
The baseline DDPM performance indicates that simply fine tuning the baseline DDPM used in the
dDDPM might lead to a large increase in performance for all implementations and datasets, and this
would be improved even further by using one of the improved versions of the DDPM. Using this DDPM
in a dDDPM with a single layer in its downsampling-upsampling network results in approximately a
doubling in the FID score (Tables 4.2 and 4.3), while adding multiple layers seem to again double the
FID for each layer added. However, on CelebAMask-HQ using a dDDPM x2 shows an increase in
performance over the DiffuseVAE (Table 4.4), which was not seen on the other datasets.
On the other hand, the sampling time of a batch on the dDDPMs compared to the DDPMs is far
faster, from around 2.8-3.7k seconds to 97-14 seconds on 256 × 256 datasets, and from 106 seconds to
28-17 seconds on 64 × 64 datasets (Table 4.5), which is also illustrated for 256 × 256 datasets on Figure
4.6. On the table it is also seen that it seems like the sampling time is directly correlated to the size of
the downsampled variable, and independent of the dimensions of the input data, thus at the extreme
case it is possible to sample 256 × 256 images from a dDDPM x4 in the same time as 16 × 16 images
from a standard DDPM. This is also shown on the speedup table (Table 4.6), where the DDPM trained
on 64 × 64 and the dDDPM x2 (256 →64) has approximately the same speedups as each other. On
this table it is seen that on 64×64 datasets we achieve speedups of around x4 to x6, while on 256×256
datasets the dDDPM achieves speedups of x29 to x266.
Thus, it is central to find a good mix between quality, in terms of FID, and sampling time. This
is done by investigating the FID scores and sampling times on models trained on CelebAMask-HQ,
where a hypothetical state-of-the-art model is used for comparison, consisting of the FID score from the
LSGM (Vahdat et al. 2021) and the best computed sampling time of a pretrained DDPM on 256 × 256
(Figure 4.11 and Table 4.11).

Apart from the FID and sFID, we can also measure the generative capabilities with the precision and
recall metrics. For the standard DDPM these seem to be reasonable, however as we introduce more
and more downsampling steps, it seems that the recall metric is drastically lowered, losing more for
each downsampling step performed than precision (Tables 4.2, 4.3 and 4.4). As mentioned earlier, there
are multiple reasons why the recall metric might be lower, including not capturing all the different
variations in coloring of faces and backgrounds. Another possible explanation is that the downsampling
or upsampling network produce some artifacts that are hard to spot, but are captured by these com-
putations. In order to improve the recall metric, mainly two things are suggested. Firstly, it would
be suggested to improve the expressiveness of the downsampling-upsampling network by either adding
more ResNet blocks in each layer, or improving its architecture with e.g. adding group normalization
inside the ResNet blocks. Secondly, the expressibility of the downsampled variable can be improved, by
either outputting more feature channels from the downsampling network, or by adding Fourier features
as suggested in (Tancik et al. 2020).
Another way that has been shown to improve performance in terms of image quality is by employing
multiple DDPMs at different data dimensionalities as partly investigated by jointly sampling in the
ablation study in section 4.4.6, where it was seen using trained dDDPMs and DDPMs could improve

the quality of the samples from the dDDPM, while still achieving speedups. In this study, it seems that
the best mix of using two pretrained models is sampling all steps from the dDDPM, and use the first
10 to 50 steps of the DDPM, which takes around 20 to 100 seconds. Using the last 11 steps from the
dDDPM greatly improved the image quality overall, while only costing an additional second in sampling
time. When not using the full dDDPM around 75 steps of the DDPM is needed, while still provided
worse quality images, and that takes around 150 seconds, thus spending another second in the dDDPM
saves somewhere between 130 to 50 seconds spent using steps of the DDPM.
On top of that, the sampling quality might be able to be improved without affecting the sampling
speed of the dDDPM by taking advantage of the fact that increasing the number of feature channels in
the deeper layers of the U-Net in the DDPM has low to zero impact on the sampling speed, as shown
on Table 4.10. Similarly, it is seen that the time spend in the upsampling network is so small, thus
increasing the expressibility of the downsampling-upsampling network should also come at low to zero
cost of sampling speed.
Finally, it is important to note that many other efforts have been made to improve the sampling
speed of the DDPM itself. Something that should be able to fit in the dDDPM architecture by simply
swapping out the DDPM being used in the downsampled space. One of these worth mentioning is the
DDIM, which reported to improve the sampling speed by x10 to x50. Assuming that this speedup and
the speedup provided by the dDDPM is the same when the DDIM is employed in the dDDPM, would
result in speedupts between x50 and x13000, meaning that a batch of 256 × 256 color images could
potentially be generated in approximately 0.3 seconds on a GPU A100. However, the combined decreases
in quality might render these images unable to be used in other applications, unless improvements are
made to the downsampling and upsampling networks and the DDIM architecture itself.
When all of this is said, the current work has some additional limitations, since it is only setup
and tested for image datasets and restricted to performing generative modelling tasks. Moreover, the
dimensions of the downsampled space is restricted to the specific requirements of the U-Net in the
DDPM, which means that this cannot be smaller than 16 × 16.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
This thesis has presented an approach to strike a balance between sampling time and quality of gener-
ated images, by employing a baseline DDPM implementation in a downsampled space. The baseline
DDPM achieves FID scores of 16.38 on CIFAR-10 and 4.02 on CelebA. Downsampled denoising diffusion
probabilistic models (dDDPMs) train DDPMs in a downsampled space by introducing a very lightweight
downsampling and upsampling network surrounding the DDPM. The DDPMs are then trained on down-
sampled inputs, where samples output by the DDPM is upsampled to the original input space. Using
this architecture enables between 29 and 266 times faster sampling on 256 × 256 color image datasets,
between 4 and 8 times faster sampling on 64 × 64 color image datasets, and 2 times faster sampling on
32 × 32 color image datasets. The best image quality achieved in terms of FID are then 37.8, 7.9 and
20.7 on CIFAR-10 (32×32), CelebA (64×64) and CelebAMask-HQ (256×256) respectively. Moreover,
the dDDPM can be used jointly with other denoising models providing increases in the quality of gen-
erated images while still retaining a large speedup on 256 × 256 color image datasets. Furthermore, it
has been shown empirically that sampling speed is primarily affected by the data dimensionality, thus
there seems to be a lot of improvements in terms of FID able to be developed in future works by using
a heavier downsampling and upsampling networks, and take advantage of the progressions made by
other works in the DDPM employed in the downsampled space.
==PAGE:59==
CHAPTER 6
Societal Impact
This work on introducing the dDDPMs and investigating the impact of data dimensionality and model
size in regards to sampling speed takes on a similar scope as other works on deep generative models.
This thesis tries to investigate how to strike a balance between sampling speed and sampling quality,
with introducing a light-weight addition to the standard DDPM, and may be able to improve impacts
made by other works regarding deep generative models.
By introducing a light weight downsampling-upsampling architecture that operates on a light DDPM
as well, this work enables training and sampling from DDPMs at much lower cost in regards to both
memory consumption and wall clock time. This means that it should both be possible to use these
models with cheaper GPUs and running them for a shorter time, which in turn either saves in electricity
consumption or enables generating more samples and training for longer. Additionally, this might help
make state-of-the-art generative modelling more accessible across all industries and investment levels,
such that you do not need a large GPU farm to perform state-of-the-art generative modelling.
On the other hand, there are unfortunately multiple malicious uses of generative models, especially
ones on face imagery and audio data, which the DDPMs are great at. With these types of models it is
possible to generate fake images and videos of high profile political or celebrity status individuals and
creating ”fake news” at much more ease than manually doing so. There is however methods to find the
subtle flaws that are present in images generated by generative models (Wang et al. 2019), however we
might reach a point in which this ceases to be the case.
