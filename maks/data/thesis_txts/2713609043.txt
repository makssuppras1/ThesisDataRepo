Abstract
Fire safety design of social entertainment spaces where alcohol is consumed does not
usually consider the intoxication of the occupants as the data on the subject is limited.
Recent research in the field of evacuation has shown that regardless of the alcohol
concentration in the blood, the perception of alcohol intoxication and the influence of the
surroundings may lead to the development of drunken behaviour and temporary
cognitive impairment.
The current study investigated whether there is a development of drunken behaviour
from placebo alcohol consumption and tested four different warning methods to
determine the most efficient way to shorten the affected individuals’ pre-evacuation
times. By performing four different evacuation scenarios, the aim was to examine the
effectiveness of the increase of lighting and the cessation of the background music as
an additional warning method combined with a siren or a voice message alarm on the
pre-evacuation times.
The experiment was performed with 144 participants in four
experimental runs that took place in Lyngby, in a university hall used for events and
exams. During the experiment, the participants first performed different tests to measure
their cognitive function. Afterwards, an evacuation was initiated with the start of a siren
or voice message alarm.
The participants were not told beforehand that evacuation
would be part of the experiment.
Data was collected by video recording the
pre-evacuation stages and the participants’ behaviour before and during the evacuation.
Data on the tests and participants’ self-estimation was collected through questionnaires.
The study’s findings showed no indication of drunken behaviour development affecting the
participants’ pre-evacuation times. In addition, the change in the atmosphere regarding
the lights and music at the start of the alarms was not identified to influence the pre-
evacuation times. Instead, other parameters such as language barrier, people’s general
attitude towards evacuation alarms and the reliability of the technical equipment had a
more significant impact.

Introduction
There have been several disastrous fires in licensed premises where alcohol and other
substances are consumed, causing many lives to perish.
Alcohol is considered a
depressant since it slows down the function of the central nervous system; therefore,
normal brain functions are delayed. People during alcohol intoxication are classified as
temporally cognitively impaired [1]. The cognitively impaired population belongs to the
vulnerable groups, and their limited capability to adapt to emergency conditions makes
them more susceptible to the adverse effects of a fire [2].
An increasing number of
scientific studies over the past years have focused on the evacuation characteristics of
disabled groups [3–7]. However, only a few studies have dealt with the evacuation of
people with cognitive impairments [7, 8]. The research is even more narrow regarding
the evacuation of individuals who are under the influence of alcohol [9, 10].
The human behaviour affected by alcohol consumption is not entirely dependent on
alcohol itself as a chemical component (ethanol). A person’s expectations regarding the
alcohol effect are based on previous drinking experiences [11].
Additionally, these
expectations are found to be enhanced in terms of group rather than individual alcohol
consumption [12]. Moreover, research has shown that a placebo alcohol can induce the
same physical and mental effects as the actual alcohol [13].
However, only one
small-scale study [14] examined drunken behaviour during an evacuation regardless of
alcohol consumption. The study investigated the influence the surroundings have on the
development of drunken behaviour and the findings were that the participants showed
reduced performance and cooperation after the consumption of placebo alcohol.
When
designing
or
reconstructing
buildings
and
infrastructure,
the
occupants’
characteristics and the building’s usage category are essential and must be taken into
account to ensure that the security requirements are met for all occupants in the context
of fire safety. The prescriptive design approach for fire safety uses codes from national
and international standards in which buildings are categorised based on the type of
occupancy.
In the Danish Building Regulations, BR18 [15], there are many user
categories for non-disabled occupants.
However, only one user category for people
unable to bring themselves to safety refers to people with disabilities. The non-disabled
occupants that have been temporary impaired by alcohol intoxication are not described
in any of these categories. Rather, engineers should consider that people who consume
little or no alcohol in social entertainment spaces would be temporarily impaired and
react similarly to those who drink more alcohol due to the social group effect [13].
To evaluate the fire risk in high occupancy and complex establishments such as most of
the entertainment venues that fall out of the traditional design, the performance-based
approach allows the use of computational models for the development of evacuation
scenarios as part of an engineering design solution. These scenarios ensure that no
occupant will be exposed to critical conditions during the evacuation. In this process, the
available safe egress time and the time required to evacuate people to a safe place are
determined [16].
These models use data from studies on people’s movements and
reactions. However, since the data on the evacuation behaviour of people impacted by
alcohol and the group effect is lacking or is insufficient, fire risk assessments are carried
out with data on young able-bodied people uninfluenced by these factors. Thus the wide
variation of impairments of the evacuees are often ignored [2, 14, 17].

Possible premises with people influenced by the alcohol effects are nightclubs and
places such as warehouses that facilitate one-time events, sports stadiums, and many
types of ships such as ferries, floating bars or cruise ships.
The latter has become
increasingly popular in the last decade, and it is known that one of the main occupations
of the passengers on cruise ships is drinking [18].
Although these premises were
designed in compliance with fire safety regulations, the cognitive impairment due to
alcohol influence is not considered in the design, rendering these spaces riskier than
bars and nightclubs [14]. However, concerning fire safety, the engineers should hold
high regard that the more significant part of the population in these venues is to some
extent impaired by inebriation or the deception of inebriation. This leads to a demand for
new representative data.

2000, where 309 people lost their lives in a disco housed on the fourth floor of a multi-use
industrial building [20] and the Kiss Nightclub fire in Brazil in 2013, with 245 deceased and
more than 200 injured [21]. The two most recent deadly fire events occurred at the Ghost

The overcrowded venues, limited access to exits, multiple code violations, lack of
required permits, inadequate fire suppression systems, and poor building maintenance
were some risk factors identified in the literature review as common features of several
deadly nightclub fires [55].
From the investigations of these fire incidents, many
improvements have been made in regulations concerning safety.
However, from the
historical analysis, it was observed that there was only a small reference to the
intoxication of victims, and whether and to what extent alcohol and its group effect could
have affected the evacuation process was not discussed.
In recent years, there seems to be more interest in studying the effect of alcohol on the
evacuation process, and there has been research [10, 56] about the speed, density and
travel time during an evacuation involving drunk people. Nonetheless, the temporary
impaired population’s pre-evacuation time is a poorly understood component of the
evacuation timeline by the engineers [16]. Human behaviour, the most critical factor of
the pre-evacuation time, is not homogeneous, and it is very hard to predict the
psychological parameters. For a long time, there was the assumption that the occupants
would immediately respond after receiving the first emergency cues,
providing
inaccurate results in calculating egress time [57].
Most studies show that the cognitive function is affected by the environment and social
circumstances. Previous study [11] has shown that people who were given placebos
while expecting to consume alcohol had lower performance than those who received no
beverages.
Doychinov and Dederichs [14] found indications that drunken behaviour
caused by placebo alcohol and party environment has an altering effect on the course of
action of the crowd and of an individual, which indicates that it is very likely that the
reactions of others will influence the decisions or responses of the individual. In another
experiment [13], without the participants knowing which drinks included alcohol or not,
those who drunk in groups showed higher levels of inebriation than those who drank
individually. A multicultural research [58] have brought findings showing that people’s
perception of drinking alcohol affects their actions and their mood much more than what
they actually drink.
The same research found that the effect of alcohol on individual
behaviour is mainly the result of cultural and social factors rather than the toxicity that
ethanol has on the brain. However, the sizeable cross-cultural divergence in how people
behave
during
alcohol
intoxication
has
not
been
included
in
the
evacuation
analyses [59].
Other studies [9, 60, 61] which focused on the reaction and decision time during an
evacuation, addressed that more research is required regarding whether the intoxication
level affects the evacuation capability of individuals. Those studies also lacked a control
group to compare the results. A study by Madsen and Hansen [10] regarding alcohol
and how its affects the walking speed, density, reaction and balance concluded that
under the influence of alcohol, the reaction time and vision are negatively affected.
However, the study did not examine the pre-evacuation time. Regarding the cognitive
consequences, the same study observed that after the drinking, the participants started
to disregard instructions, and their noise level increased [10].
Concerning the warning methods, several previous studies, as cited in Purser [62], have

found that spoken message alarms provide shorter and more reliable pre-evacuation
times compared to siren alarms [9, 62]. This warning method yields more favourable
results in ”awake and unfamiliar” evacuation scenarios such as nightclubs. However,

found insufficient as the voice message disappeared in the background noise of the club

Overall, the literature provides a wide range of information concerning the phenomenon
of placebo alcohol and group effect on cognitive function and human behaviour.
However, there is a lack of data in the existing research regarding the correlation of
placebo-influenced behaviour with the evacuation process.

1.2
Objective
The current project aims to study the side of the evacuation of individuals from a mixed
population with the deception of inebriation and to introduce new data that considers the
affected reaction parameters.
It is also intended to increase knowledge on the
evacuation
behaviour
and
reaction
modes
of
people
with
temporary
cognitive
impairments induced by placebo alcohol and group effect. To do that, it is investigated
whether there is a development of drunken behaviour based on the environment the
people are in, regardless of alcohol intoxication. The hypothesis of this study is that the
pre-evacuation time of people with the deception of intoxication is shortened with the
increase of the lighting and the cessation of the music as warning measure together with
a pre-recorded voice alarm or a siren alarm.
The process of the pre-evacuation time is subdivided into steps and analysed with
quantitative and qualitative measures, comprising human behaviour and the social and
physical interaction between the participants with a focus on the pre-evacuation time.
Hopefully, this project’s findings will serve to design improved evacuation scenarios
used in safety engineering simulation software, aiming at more accurate and realistic
results for the benefit of human life.
The project comprises a large-scale experiment involving participants subjected to
placebo alcohol. The experiment was repeated four times with four different warning
method scenarios.

2
Theory
This chapter presents all the basic theoretical concepts of the evacuation process.
However, in the current project, only the pre-evacuation phase is studied.
Thus, the
components of the evacuation timeline and the key factors that influence the
quantification of egress will be treated with a focus on pre-evacuation time and human
behaviour in fire.
The detection, warning and travel times are briefly described for a
complete understanding of the factors affecting the evacuation timeline. Additionally, a
reference is made to the regulations of fire safety design.
2.1
Fire safety design approach
The approaches used for designing buildings concerning fire safety are the prescriptive
and performance-based codes. The prescriptive fire design is based on national and
international standards that comprise a methodical list of requirements that a building
needs to follow. For Denmark, the construction must meet the fire safety requirements of
Chapter 5 - Fire in Building Regulations [15]. When the design complies with the codes
and is approved by the authorities, the building is considered safe for people in the event
of a fire.
However, the prescriptive codes present some limits in building design. In many cases,
due to the usage or complexity of the building, the prescriptive codes are inadequate for
designing a safe construction and the performance-based approach is used instead [63].
Performance-based design works with objectives that need to be met by analysing the
building with acceptable engineering tools. By adapting fire safety to the building, the
structures do not have to comply with constricting sets of rules. Nevertheless, the design
with the performance-based approach proposed by the engineers should prove to be as
safe as it would be if it were built with the codes. The leading principle when designing
with performance-based codes is that people must be able to reach safety before critical
conditions occur [63].
2.2
Acceptance criteria
For the evaluation of occupant safety as a part of the performance-based design,
“during the time the escape routes are to be used to clear the building, there must not be
temperatures, concentrations of smoke, thermal radiation or other conditions that will
seriously obstruct evacuation” [63]. The calculable amount of time that the occupants
need to make it to safety after the fire ignition is denoted as RSET, Required Safety
Egress Time, or in some literature referred to as the evacuation time. The calculable
amount of time available between the fire ignition and the development of critical,
untenable conditions is denoted as ASET, Available Safety Egress Time, or in some
literature referred to as the time to critical conditions [63, 64].

sufficient margin of safety:

2.3
Detection and warning time
The time between ignition and detection of a fire is called detection time. Fire detection
may be achieved by an automatic system or a person. Warning time is the time from
the fire detection to when an alarm or an individual notifies the occupants of the fire. Fire
detection and warning can take between a few seconds and a few hours, depending on the
type of the detection, the nature of the event, technologies and human resources available
[64]. In the event that the automatic system fails, there may be critical or destructive
consequences.
Detection time is often set equal to the warning time when an automatic fire alarm
system (AFA) is installed as active fire protection [63].
However, if fire or smoke is
discovered by occupants who have to activate the alarm manually, or for instance, if the
incident requires confirmation by an authorised person to allow the warning, there could
be significant delays between these two times.
In many cases, the detection and warning time is determined based on the manufacturer’s
guidance [64]. In addition, there have been fire incidents with fatalities where the smoke
detectors were operational but were not in the area where the fire started, resulting in
a significant delay in the detection time [17]. Therefore, human resources, as well as
research and incident data, must be critically used when designing or choosing evacuation
scenarios.
2.4
Pre-evacuation time
One of the critical components of engineering analysis is the determination of the pre-
evacuation time. The pre-evacuation time can be defined as the time elapsed from when
a person perceives something unusual is happening to when this person makes the first
deliberate evacuation movement [66]. It is often the most extensive evacuation phase and
is always critical. As mentioned, the pre-evacuation time is the main interest in this study,
and its two focus components are the recognition time and the response time, illustrated
in Figure 2.1.
The recognition time (trec) is the interval between the time the warning or fire cues are
perceived and the time the person understands that this warning indicates an
emergency and starts to respond.
During this phase, especially if the cues are
ambiguous such as fire alarm bells, occupants can be ignoring the situation by
perceiving the warning as false [66], or looking for information through investigation of
their surroundings or discussion with others [17]. In addition, research has shown that in
the case of fire, most people will not respond to danger signals before they see others
taking action [59].
The response time (tres) or else reaction and decision time is the interval between
recognition time and the time at which the first movement is made to commence
evacuation.
In this phase, the pre-egress human behaviour may cause significant
delays, as people carry out a range of activities and may or may not involve movement
such as investigating, firefighting, calling for help, alerting other people, searching for
friends/family, retrieving personal items and so on before they look for the exit [64, 67]. It
could also involve re-engaging activities undertaken prior to the alarm [9].

2.5
Travel time
The travel time ttrav is determined based on three key characteristics: the travel speed,
queuing/flow conditions and population density. The travel time is the time from when the
first movement is made towards a safety egress route until the last evacuee is brought
to safety. The travel time can be affected by the walking distance, level of travel, the
occupants’ characteristics and the complexity of the building.
The travel speed is decreased when walking on stairs compared to the level travel, and it
depends on the geometrical properties of the stair. The maximum travel speed is achieved
when the population density is less than 0.54 persons/m2 on level and stair travel. As
the density increases, the speed decreases linearly; thus, the travel-time increases [69].
When people density exceeds 3.5 persons/m2, it is so high that there is congestion with
little or no movement.
Besides the population density, the travel speed also depends on age, impairments and
mobility, as well as the type of formed social groups. Moreover, travel times have been
shown to increase when people have a temporary or permanent disability [70]. In addition,
research has shown that the walking speed of people exposed to fire conditions walk
slower than the derived speeds from experiments conducted in normal environments [59].
The queue/flow conditions affecting the total movement part of the evacuation are related
to the passage of people through narrow openings, which may lead to delays.

2.6.1
Human behaviour in fire
Figure 2.2 shows the process that would optimally be followed during the response time in
a fire emergency, resulting in three conditions concerning the interpretation of the severity
of the situation [57]. Decision-making is a multifaceted process because the decisions
made by individuals are dependent on physical and social environment factors. On the
one hand, this implies that an individual will react differently in different environments. On
the other hand, in a shared environment, various reactions will be seen performed by the
different people in the crowd [12].
Figure 2.2: The protective action decision model from SFPE Guide to Human Behavior in
Fire by Society of Fire Protection Engineers [57].
The first scientific research on human behaviour in fire emergencies was done over
seventy years ago [71].
The realisation and interpretation of the signals during the
pre-evacuation time have been recognised as the most crucial factors in the course of
the evacuation process [66, 71–73].
Although it is known that there is a delay in
pre-evacuation time in fires and that this delay has resulted in many casualties, it is the
most minor documented component [67].
People often delay responding to signs because they wait for others to take action first.
This behaviour depends partly on the stress they feel as they are experiencing an
unknown situation, causing cognitive impairment that affects the reaction time [59]. That
behaviour lies in the informative social influence effect. When this effect is observed, the
individuals look to others to understand the situation and follow their actions. It is also
very often to identify the herd phenomenon when the informative social effect is
observed. In addition, when people are waiting for others to respond first may result
from normative social influence.
When the norm influences individuals, they tend to
ignore given signals as they believe they will appear naive to respond first, so they
behave as if they perceived the warning as false [57].
The signs’ interpretation is usually made faster when someone is alone during an
emergency [57]. However, people tend to operate in groups rather than alone in a fire
incident. Also, suppose there is affiliation in a social group. In that case, the individuals
tend to stay together for as long as the situation allows, and their movement is at the
Pre-evacuation characteristics of people with temporary alcohol-related impairment
14
==PAGE:24==
pace of the slowest person in the group [57, 59]. In addition, it is common for people
who escaped the fire to re-enter the building to search for their family members [71, 74].
Verifying emergency cues is a characteristic of human behaviour. In many cases, the
severity of the fire is usually underestimated, leading to a significant increase in danger.
In many fire incidents, people have tried to fight the fire on their own and significantly
delayed their escape. Another important observation through investigation of actual fire
disasters is that panic behaviour does not occur during evacuation, contrary to what is
widely believed [59].
One other cause of delay in the pre-evacuation time is the
unwillingness of people to abort the activity in which they were engaged before the
emergency [57].
For example, during an unannounced evacuation in a club, it was
observed that some of the patrons stayed with the intention to finish their drinks before
evacuating [9]. In addition, people in charge or working in the space where the incident
occurs tend to take a leading role and help others evacuate.
For instance, in a
restaurant, the waiter will feel obliged to help the patrons before he or she seeks
refuge [57].
A similar trait seen during the pre-evacuation time is that the individuals perceive the
known to them exit to be the closest to evacuate from due to familiarity. Generally, it is
observed that it is unusual for people to leave from another exit than the one they entered
from during evacuation [57].
Human behaviour in fire is one of the most critical and complex parameters in any fire
safety engineering design. The engineers must consult guides and thoroughly research
the human behaviour data from studies related to their working cases in order to
responsibly account for the human life.
2.6.2
Evacuation alarm reliability
Alarms such as sirens and bells do not guarantee successful interpretation of an
emergency’s severity.
People tend to think that alarms do not indicate a serious
situation and may interpret the signal as a non-threatening or false warning [57].
In
contrast, it has been shown that giving detailed information about the threatening
situation convinces the occupants more efficiently that there is danger [9, 62].
Alarms using verbal instructions must give the information with simple terminology and
be concise.
In addition, the language in which the instructions are given contributes
significantly to whether the signal instructions are understood or not. Also, factors such
as the person’s cultural background, cognitive impairment, stress or being a non-native
speaker of the language in which information is given can hinder the understanding of
the risk [57].
The reliability of the alarm can also be lost if there are often false alarms. Moreover, if the
frequency of evacuation drills is too high, the occupants tend to perceive the danger signal
as unreal and ignore it. This loss of trust has adverse consequences as the response time
is significantly prolonged. In cases where the signal is perceived as non-threatening,
people tend to mislead others into believing the same [57].

4.2
Pre-evacuation time
After all tests had been finished, the alarm was started, and the evacuation process
began.
The pre-evacuation time and behaviour of the participants were determined
through analysis of the videos. The observed behaviour of the participants was also
correlated with the answers they gave during and after the experiment.
The pre-evacuation time is the time from the alarm initiation until the first deliberate move
to evacuate. In the analysis, this time is divided into two components, recognition time
and response time. The response time is further divided into the time to seek information
and the time it takes the participants to prepare after they decide to respond to the signal.
The terminology is further described in Section 2.4.
The identification of the time components that result in the pre-evacuation from the video
analysis is carried out based on the observed types of behaviour as follows:
Pre-evacuation characteristics of people with temporary alcohol-related impairment
31
==PAGE:41==
• Recognition: time from the alarm start until the participant has a noticeable reaction
to it. That is perceived as an indication of recognition that something unusual is
happening, and the participant starts to seek information by investigating the room
and observing how others react.
• Seek information (investigation): time from the end of the recognition time until the
person starts collecting belongings. That indicates that the decision to evacuate has
been made.
• Preparation: time from the end of the investigation until the person makes the first
deliberate move towards the exit.
• Pre-evacuation time: The sum of the recognition, investigation and preparation
times.
The time data in this section are illustrated using line graphs that analytically show the
participants’ distribution as a function of time. The same data are also presented in box
plots distributed per warning method. This method of presenting the data in summary
statistics was chosen to distinguish the patterns between the warning methods.
The
lowest, highest, mean and median values derived from the analysis and depicted in
these plots can also be viewed in Table C.1, Appendix C.1.

time from the first action that shows the intention to leave until the first towards egress
move is made. During the preparation time, various actions were observed. The most
common was the collection of personal items, getting dressed, waiting on others or moving
to instruct others to evacuate, continuing to inspect the room while getting ready and using
phones. In some cases, some participants returned to their seats after they had already
initiated the evacuation. However, this is not considered in the preparing time, since the
first inherent move to leave was set as the end of the preparation time.

The communication between the participants and the instructors or between participants
themselves is another parameter that can lead to errors. Information on how to complete
the test was given verbally and in writing to reduce possible errors. Nevertheless, some
omissions by the investigator, the difference in the native language, as well as the focus
of each participant during the time the instructions were given, can lead to possible
errors. Moreover, the experiment’s integrity may be affected if, before all experiments
are completed, information is leaked to the participants of the subsequent experimental
runs by participants who already participated in the former runs.
This limitation was
restricted by asking the participants for their discretion multiple times during the
experiments.
The experiment setup is also a potential source of error. The evacuation part itself was
not communicated to the participants, neither before nor after their registration in the
experiment. However, they were informed that they are participating in research in the
context of safety.
Such information could link the fire alarm to the experiment.
Additionally, one of the questions in the questionnaires asked whether the participants’
trust in the alarm was affected by the fact that they were participating in the experiment.
The analysis of the video material and questionnaire responses showed that this fact
made them seriously doubt the authenticity of the alarm. The location of the experiment
being the university campus, where fire drills are often held, also contributed.
The research aimed to identify cognitive impairment caused by the placebo effect during
an evacuation. However, in the case of alcohol resemblance, there is great difficulty in
finding a good placebo, as the participants recognise the taste and the effect of alcoholic
beverages. The participants’ familiarity with actual alcohol is a limitation of the study,
as some of the participants who realised that the drinks were non-alcoholic did not try
to hide it. The information potentially travelled to others that had no such suspicions.
The limitation from the unfulfilled expectation of inebriation was tried to be eliminated by
reducing the number of drinks in the subsequent runs after observations made in the first
run.
The errors regarding the test methods chosen for detecting the possible cognitive
impairment from the influence of placebo alcohol may result from three factors. Initially,
the tasks were performed by the participants without supervision of the investigators.

==PAGE:58==
This method of performing the tests was chosen so that the absence of all experimental
personnel at the time of the alarm would not appear suspicious. Also, the number of
assistants was not enough to control all the applications and the results of the exercises.
Second, when the reaction test performed with each participant’s phone device, an error
was detected which made the results of this test unreliable. It was observed that the
results differ significantly when the test is done in standard screen focus compared to
zoomed screen focus. The reaction time using the standard focus was sometimes twice
as long as on the zoomed focus. This error can be eliminated if all participants use one
single type of device. However, that would significantly prolong the experimental process.
Third, it has been found that with low levels of alcohol consumption, the reaction time can
be relatively unaffected if attention is focused on an objective or only on a task [85]. That
can be assumed for the placebo alcohol and social effect as well. The reaction tests that
the participants were subjected to did however not present any complexity. One way to
avoid this limitation is to choose tests that require more complex attention that needs to
be divided between task objectives [86]. Also, a limitation can be the training effect as
each participant performs the tasks twice. In the walk-on-line test, it is observed that most
participants performed the test faster the second time. However, the training effect was
not investigated.
The equipment can be connected to another set of possible errors.
There are two
limiting issues concerning the notification equipment used for the evacuation scenarios.
Initially, the notification that included change in the atmosphere was done with
approximately only an 80-lux difference. A larger difference would be a more indicative
and alerting cue of the emergency, making the patrons of a nightclub mobilise faster.
However, installing additional equipment for that experiment scale was impossible.
Nonetheless, the dimming of the built-in lights resulted in a noticeable change.
The
second notification issue was detected in reproducing the spoken message from a
phone to a megaphone. The sound was tested before the experiments and found to be
appropriate.
However, some feedback from the participants indicated that with the
crowd murmur and the music in the background, the message was not very clear. This
limitation is eliminated by purchasing a professional alarming system.
Regarding the recording equipment, the data size from the decibel amplitude recording
was miscalculated, resulting in data loss from one experimental run due to insufficient
memory card capacity. Also, in the recordings with the sound-meter the music is not
separated from the participants’ noise level, which affects the overall noise level to some
extent.
Another issue was that two of the main cameras failed to operate in some
experimental runs.
However, that did not cause any significant problems, as the
experiment area was recorded with more cameras than was necessary.
The geometry of the experiment was not designed to replicate the environment of a
nightclub. The study’s data can be used for consideration by the engineers and as a
basis for further research.
For their use in the design of spaces where alcohol
consumption occurs, more development is needed.

Conclusion
Based on this study’s findings, the two main conclusions drawn are that there was no
indication of drunken behaviour development and that the change of the atmosphere by
increasing the lights and stopping the music at the initiation of the alarm did not have a
significant effect on the pre-evacuation time of the participants.
With regards to the placebo effect, it was concluded that there was no development of
drunken behaviour due to the consumption of placebo alcohol. This is based on the results
of the reaction and balance tests, the observation of the participants’ general behaviour
throughout the experiment and the evaluation of the pre-evacuation times together with
the number of drinks consumed and the participants’ self-estimation of alcohol impact.
Of the reaction and balance tests, only the walk-on-line test results were shown to have
statistically significant differences between the groups of participants. However, none of
the reaction and balance tests showed a reduction in the participants’ performance. On
the contrary, all the reaction times in each test generally appeared to improve after the
consumption of the placebo alcohol due to the adaptation to the tests. The stoplight
reaction and up-and-go test results were similar to those of previous experiments for
alcohol and placebo alcohol with a significantly smaller number of participants, which
also indicated improved performance and no significant differences. These methods of
testing the cognitive function with motor responses appear impractical for up to a certain
level of intoxication.
A similar conclusion was reached when the pre-evacuation times were evaluated
depending on the number of drinks consumed and the participants’ self-estimation of
alcohol impact on their reaction and decision. The number of drinks consumed and the
different self-estimation levels showed no statistically significant differences. Moreover,
although the participants’ noise level increased after the consumption of drinks, this
alone is not enough to indicate the development of drunken behaviour.
Rather, the
overall participants’ behaviour during and after the drinking period was consistent. As
the experiment came close to its end and before the evacuation, the participants’ energy
and noise levels decreased.
The original hypothesis that the increase of luminosity and the cessation of music at the
initiation of the alarm would lead to shorter pre-evacuation time was not verified.
In
general, those changes in the atmosphere were not proved decisive factors on the
pre-evacuation time. The experimental runs with the combined warning methods were
characterised by, on average, longer pre-evacuation times than the experimental runs
using only alarms, contrary to what was expected.
Although, on the one hand, the
change in the atmosphere contributed to slightly shorter recognition times, on the other
hand, recognition times, in general, were significantly shorter compared to all the
pre-evacuation times. However, it was seen that during an unannounced evacuation in
a nightclub, there were indications that the turning on of the lights may have positively
affected the pre-evacuation time[9].
Whether the increase in the lighting and the stopping of music contributed more
efficiently to the total pre-evacuation time cannot be ascertained, as it was concluded
that other factors had affected the pre-evacuation times more significantly. To assess
which factors prevailed over the effectiveness of the change in the atmosphere, the
study also examined the pre-evacuation times between the alarm types. Regarding the

two evacuations where the siren alarm was used, a small difference was found between
the average pre-evacuation times. The scenario where the siren was combined with the
change in the atmosphere resulted in a longer average pre-evacuation time. Between
the two evacuations where voice alarms were used, there was a considerably longer
pre-evacuation time for the scenario where the alarm combined with a change in the
atmosphere.
These differences may indicate that the change in the atmosphere may have contributed
negatively to the total pre-evacuation time.
For instance, that change may have
confused the participants about the situation; however, there was no way for this
indication to be assessed.
On the one hand, it is most likely that the difference in
average pre-evacuation times between the two scenarios which used the siren alarms
was a result of the normative behaviour of the participants and not due to the change in
the atmosphere. On the other hand, the significant difference in average pre-evacuation
times between the two scenarios with voice alarms was identified because of the
language barrier. Most of the participants were non-native, thus unable to understand
the voice message since the instructions were given first in Danish and then in English,
resulting in a great delay.
Regardless of the atmosphere change, it was also concluded that, on average, the
pre-evacuation times were similar between all the scenarios, besides the one with the
language problem. Additionally, it was found that the voice message alarm was more
efficient for the investigation time, and the siren alarm was more efficient for the
recognition and preparation times. Since the investigation times were significantly longer
than the recognition and preparation times together, it appeared that when everyone
understood the instructions, the spoken message alarm showed more consistent
pre-evacuation time results than the siren alarm, which agrees with the theory.
Overall, the experiment results did not verify the initial hypothesis concerning the efficiency
of the change atmosphere on the pre-evacuation times. Additionally, other unforeseen
parameters, such as the language barrier, general attitude towards evacuation alarms and
technical equipment issues, had a more significant impact on the pre-evacuation times.
Therefore, it could not be assessed whether the atmosphere change decreased the pre-
evacuation times and whether the siren or voice message alarm was more effective to be
combined with that atmosphere change.

Recommendations
The present study’s findings form a basis for further research on the contribution of the
change in the atmosphere to the already established fire alarm systems in relation to the
pre-evacuation time. Furthermore, the results suggest the need for further investigation
into the development of drunken behaviour during the evacuation process using placebo
alcohol or alcohol.
Although the drunken behaviour and the placebo effect were not identified in current
study, further research with improved methods is required to support the findings.
Regarding placebo alcohol, finding a decent substituent is difficult as the taste easily
betrays the absence of ethanol. The non-alcoholic beer given to the participants in the
current experiments contributed to the reduction of the placebo effect to a certain
degree. Conversely, when the aroma was used as a substitute for the liquor in other
experiments [13], it was shown to be a satisfying placebo alcohol option. In addition, the
placebo given must be in such a quantity so that not much intoxication is expected. As
seen in the current experiments, while some participants had not perceived the placebo
from the taste, they began to suspect it from the absence of the expected intoxication
effect based on their past experiences.
Concerning the reaction and balance test methods, it was seen from the current and
previous studies that they are impractical for evaluating the cognitive function affected
by placebo alcohol and moderate quantities of alcohol.
Although the number of
participants was significantly increased compared to previous experiments using these
tests, there was no statistical validation of the results. Research has shown that when
there is a relatively low blood alcohol concentration, motor reactions do not show an
impact on cognitive function.
However, when tests with attention requirements are
performed instead, the impaired performance is identified [86]. Therefore, research with
more suitable tests on cognitive impairment from alcohol or the social effect associated
with evacuation is needed.
Further research is needed to determine whether this study’s results show similarities
to an experiment in which alcohol was consumed instead of a placebo. Generally, in a
nightclub, a segment of the patrons would be expected to be sober or affected by the social
effect and not be intoxicated by actual alcohol. Therefore, it would be more representative
if there was a combination of placebo alcohol and alcohol in future experiments.
Additionally, this study’s results for the pre-evacuation times concerning warning methods
lead to a need for further investigation to evaluate their effectiveness. As the human factor
was one of the aspects significantly affecting the results of this research, and since it was
difficult to interpret precisely how the pre-evacuation time was affected by the human
factor and the warning method, further research is necessary to draw more apparent
conclusions. It is suggested to test each notification system multiple times using the same
setup to be able to limit or assess with more reliability the influence of the human factor.
In addition, the experiments should be performed with a more pronounced change in the
atmosphere as part of the warning method.
Finally, further studies concerning unannounced evacuation experiments are suggested
to be carried out in premises unfamiliar to the participants. The current experiment was
performed mainly with students, and the familiarity of some participants with the
evacuation drills taking place at the university significantly influenced their reaction to

the event. It would be even more efficient if the evacuation studies concerning alcohol
were performed in actual places where there is alcohol consumption.

Dear Ethical Committee of Hovedstaden Region,
Within a framework of a Master Thesis of the Department of Civil Engineering of the Technical
University of Denmark, a study is conducted concerning evacuation characteristics of people with
temporary impairment induced by alcohol consumption. The Thesis is part of the project Safe
Evacuation at Sea funded by RISE (Research Institutes of Sweden).
Studies have shown that evacuation characteristics such as reaction and decision time are
negatively affected by alcohol consumption. Moreover, it has been found that people develop
drunken behaviour when they are in an entertaining environment even if they have not consumed
alcohol. However, the studies were carried out with non-diverse groups of people in terms of age,
impairments and culture.
The project aims to study the side of the evacuation of intoxicated with alcohol individuals coming
from a mixed population. The questions raised and aimed to be answered are if the alcohol
impacted behaviour and drunken behaviour of expecting to receive alcohol can be changed in a
diversified population and if reaction and decision time can be reduced when strong lights and
piezoelectric acoustic alarms are used as warnings.
The project by RISE consists of two parts. In the first part, a placebo of alcohol will be consumed
by the volunteers while in the second part, regular alcoholic beverages are included. The
volunteers of the experiment will be subjected to alcohol and placebo beverages consumption and
they will perform a number of exercises under the expected influence.
We hereby apply for permission to conduct the experiments of the project at the premises of the
Technical University of Denmark during the year 2022. We kindly ask for discretion regarding the
placebo content.
The experiment follows the Ethical Codex for evacuation experiments involving human beings
conducted by DTU BYG (attached file).

Information on participation in a research experiment 
Experiment title: Reaction and movement characteristics of people with temporary 
alcohol-related impairment 
 
Would you like to participate in an experiment in the form of an evening game in which we examine 
how people who consumed alcohol in a party atmosphere react while performing different tasks? 
Responsible for the experiment is Associate Professor Anne S. Dederichs and Artur Storm from RISE 
and the master student Poulcheria Serafeim from the Technical University of Denmark. 
 
The experiment will be conducted in one of DTU’s buildings. 
 
Before you decide whether to participate or not in the trial, you must fully understand what the trial is 
about and how we implement it. Therefore, we would ask you to read this participant information 
carefully. If you have an alcohol addiction, we ask you not to participate.  
 
You will be invited to an interview/meeting prior to the experiment in which information about 
participants is asked and where you can ask questions about the trial. 
 
If you decide to participate in the experiment, we will ask you to sign a consent form. Remember that it 
is completely voluntary to participate in the experiment, but the consent form must be signed before the 
experiment. You will be able to withdraw your participation at any time without giving any reason. 
 
Purpose of the experiment  
The aim of the experiment is to get a better knowledge of the reaction and movement characteristics of 
an alcohol-impaired population in the context of safety.  
 
Method 
The experiment takes between 3 and 4,5 hours. Before starting the experiment, each participant will 
receive information about the experiment and a consent form, which they must sign in order to 
participate. Those who agree to participate will inform about their weight to calculate the alcohol content 
in their blood (BAC). Additionally, the number of beverages each participant has consumed will be 
documented.   
 
Why participate? 
By participating in the trial, you will help to create new and better knowledge in the area of the building 
safety of spaces where alcohol-related impaired people gather. Also, it can be to your benefit to see how 
your reactions are affected in the context of safety when you are under the influence of alcohol. 
Furthermore, your skills as a participant may improve in a matter of reaction time and decision and it 
can make you more aware of the negative aspects of alcohol consumption. Gathering the information 
about people intoxicated with alcohol it becomes possible to take account of this population group when 
future buildings will be designed for with respect to safety. 
 


wants to consume.

After the end of the exercises and the completion of the questionnaires there will be a free pizza 
and snacks for everyone.

meeting point to DTU and the other way around.

Side effects, risk, complications and drawbacks 
The experiment involves drinking several alcoholic beverages corresponding to a weekend night out 
with your friends (for example 3 beers). The known side effects are those related to alcohol consumption 
and a BAC level of 0.05. Some of these effects are a feeling of well-being, lowered inhibitions, and 
relaxation. Judgment is slightly impaired, minor impairment of reasoning and memory and less cautious. 
Your behaviour can become exaggerated, and emotions (ex. happiness or sadness) are felt more 
intensely. 
 
There might be other risks associated with the experiment which we do not know yet. Therefore, we ask 
you to report if you experience problems with your health. 
  
Anonymity and data management 
The experiment will be filmed. The videos will be used in the analysis of the results. Videos, consent 
forms and questionnaires will only be handled by the people who in one way or another participate in 
the analysis of the results. No personal data will be reported in connection with the presentation of the 
study results. Still images from the video material may appear in the presentation of the results, but will 
be presented in such a way that you as an individual cannot be identified. In the same way, individual 
survey responses can be reproduced in the form of quotations, but never in such a way that it can be 
linked back to you personally. 
 
Rules of conduct 
You must be completely sober before the start of the experiment. You may not be under the influence 
of drugs. You may not film or take pictures during the experiment. Participants can be excluded from 
the experiment, at any time, if their behaviour is considered inappropriate towards the other participants.  
 
Access to test results 
When the experiment is completed and the data is analysed you will receive information about the 
results. Experimental results may be published in relevant scientific journals. If you require information 
about this, please state it on the consent form.
