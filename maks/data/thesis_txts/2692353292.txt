Summary 
Corynebacterium glutamicum (C. glutamicum) as a glutamate-producing bacterium, has been 
applied to industrial glutamate production for decades and some mutants could produce over 
hundreds of grams of glutamate per liter medium. So far, most of the main carbon and energy 
source for the production of glutamate is glucose. 
In the dairy industry, one kilogram of milk produces as much as half a kilogram of whey waste 
after making cheese and other products. After recrystallization, there is still a large amount of 
waste liquid that needs to be discharged. This waste liquid is called delactosed whey permeate 
(DWP) or mother liquor. Its main composition is lactose and citrate as well as a small number 
of amino acids and minerals. At present, scientists have researched the application of DWP, 
and it is now mainly used for livestock feeding and fertilizer. In this experiment, we plan to use 
citrate in DWP instead of glucose for glutamate fermentation by C. glutamicum, which can not 
only reduce production costs but also turn waste into taste. 
C. glutamicum wild-type ATCC13032 was used throughout the experiment. The experiment 
first achieved an ideal output by optimizing cell growth and catalytic conditions based on 
CGXII minimal medium. For this strain, the secretion of glutamate in intact cells is very little 
(0.01 g/L), so it is necessary to improve cell permeability. Ampicillin, as an antibiotic, can 
effectively increase cell permeability by inhibiting cell wall synthesis during cell division. The 
experiment determined that the addition of 0.2~1.5 μg/mL ampicillin at 5.5 h in the cell growth 
phase could enhance glutamate secretion to about 0.5 g/L. Citrate cannot pass the cell 
membrane to be metabolized by intact cells. However, the presence of glucose allows the 
uptake of citrate, which is because that the citrate transport system needs to be activated by 
glucose as an energy source. Afterward, it is proved that an increase in temperature (30 to 37 ℃) 
is beneficial for the effect of ampicillin and the extracellular glutamate was increased up to 
0.77 g/L. To be able to completely replace glucose with citrate, it is found out that Ca2+ can 
bind to citrate and activate the citrate transport system and cells could grow well with citrate 
as the only carbon source. Then, the optimal concentration of citrate and Ca2+ has been figured 
out that with 2% citrate and 5mM Ca2+ in CGXII minimal medium cells could reach the highest

OD600 and citrate metabolism rate, and with 6% citrate in buffer cells could produce the most 
glutamate (4.41 g/L) (citrate in the mother liquor is about 6%). By crushing cells, it is noticed 
that there is some glutamate still not secreted out of the cell. Furthermore, detergents such as 
cationic CTAB, nonionic Triton X-100, and Tween series are compared on the effect of cell 
permeability and among them, cells treated with CTAB have the highest value of glutamate 
secretion. 
To utilize mother liquor in glutamate production, it is verified firstly that cells cannot grow on 
the mother liquor. Therefore, the medium mixed with 30% mother liquor and 70% CGXII is 
used for cell growth and 100% mother liquor for cell catalysis. However, due to the high 
osmotic pressure in the mother liquor, the glutamate output of cells in the mother liquor is (3.54 
g/L) less than that in CGXII and buffer.

Chapter 1. Introduction 
1.1 Introduction of C. glutamicum 
1.1.1 Discovery and utilization of C. glutamicum 
C. glutamicum is a Gram-positive, rod-shaped, facultatively anaerobic, nonpathogenic soil 
bacterium (Kinoshita et al., 1957), which plays an important role in the amino acid fermentation 
industry in the world for the past four decades, such as the flavor enhancer glutamate and the 
food additive lysine. Kinoshita et al. (1957) first described it as a glutamate-producing 
bacterium and it was called Micrococcus glutamicus previously. Glutamate can be used as a 
flavor enhancer for food. With the improvement of living standards, people's demand for 
glutamate has been increasing. Motivated by this, Japan has planned to screen and isolate a soil 
microorganism, which was identified as C. glutamicum latterly (Jacob Roam, n.d.), which 
could accumulate glutamate during the growth in the medium. In the process of culturing C. 
glutamicum, it was found that by adding a small amount of biotin, the bacteria secreted a large 
amount of glutamate. This discovery provides the feasibility for the industrial production of 
glutamate. 
Through the successful operation of some key regulation of C. glutamicum, such as the 
inhibition of key points that allow other amino acids, i.e., serine, arginine, threonine, or 
ornithine to accumulate, the potential of C. glutamicum application in biotechnology rapidly 
increased. In addition, in the past few decades, in order to develop high-yield strains of C. 
glutamicum, scientists have adopted different strategies, which include traditional mutation and 
screening (Ma et al., 2017). With the development of recombinant DNA technology, the 
targeted genetic engineering of C. glutamicum became possible, and it was later successfully 
applied to strain research (Adrio & Demain, 2010). Today, C. glutamicum has been widely 
used in the industrial production of glutamate. 
Recently, researchers are focused on obtaining higher yields and productivity, better pressure 
tolerance, or a wider range of culture media. In addition, current research is also committed to 
creating some other products based on biological production. New products include other 
amino acids, organic acids, or biofuels that cannot be produced by bacteria.

1.1.2 Metabolism of C. glutamicum 
1.1.2.1 Nutritional requirements and assimilation pathways 
C. glutamicum is able to use many organic compounds as a single or combined carbon source. 
For example, glucose, sucrose, fructose, or mannose can be used by the phosphotransferase 
system (PTS) (Lindner et al., 2011). In the utilization of glucose, an alternative uptake system 
has been identified, which includes intracellular phosphorylation of glucose by glucokinase 
(Ikeda et al., 2011). Besides, C. glutamicum can grow on many kinds of organic acids, for 
example, citrate, lactate, acetate, gluconate, or propionate (Ikeda, 2012). In the growth of C. 
glutamicum and the overproduction of amino acids, cell growth requires a nitrogen source and 
sulfur as the main elements in addition to a carbon source (Biosystems Engineering I: Creating 
Superior Biocatalysts - Google Books, n.d.). Appropriate nitrogen sources may be ammonium 
or other organic compounds, i.e., urea or amino acids. Two alternative systems can absorb and 
utilize ammonia. When the content of ammonium is high, the assimilation of ammonia is 
mainly catalyzed by glutamate dehydrogenase (GDH); However, at low ammonia levels, 
systems containing glutamine synthetase and glutamate dehydrogenase can work but require 
higher energy consumption (Meier-Wagner et al., 2001). Inorganic sulfate is the most common 
one among suitable sulfur sources, but its absorption requires a large amount of redox power 
to reduce it to biocompatible sulfides (Eggeling & Bott, 2005b; Koch et al., 2005). Other sulfur 
sources that can be utilized by C. glutamicum are sulfonate, sulfonate esters, or cysteine (Koch 
et al., 2005). In industrial applications, the most used raw materials are molasses in Asia, and 
starch in the US and Europe, which are used with inorganic salts (Zahoor et al., 2012). 
Therefore, the main nutrients are glucose, sucrose and fructose (carbon source), ammonia 
(nitrogen), and sulfurate (sulfur source) (Wittmann, 2010). 
1.1.2.2 Carbon metabolism 
The metabolic network of C. glutamicum is composed of glycolysis, the pentose phosphate 
pathway (PPP), the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle, anaplerotic reactions, and gluconeogenesis 
(Papagianni, 2012; Wittmann, 2010). C. glutamicum contains pyruvate carboxylase and PEP 
carboxylase for the replenishment of the TCA cycle (Jitrapakdee et al., 2008). PEP carboxylase 
and malic enzyme are responsible for catalyzing the decarboxylation reaction from the TCA

cycle to glycolysis (Christian Riedel et al., 2001). It is generally believed that PEP synthase 
and oxaloacetate decarboxylase are another two gluconeogenic enzymes (Jitrapakdee et al., 
2008). Different reactions are related to the supply of NADPH (Wittmann, 2010). Apart from 
this, anabolic pathways have also been clarified. Through precise analysis of cell components, 
detailed information about the requirements of the anabolic precursor is obtained. 
Approximately 16.4 mM of NADPH per gram biomass is required for anabolism (Wittmann, 
2010). 
1.1.2.3 Product biosynthesis pathway 
Glutamate and other aromatic amino acids are most important in industrial production recently. 
Their synthesis is very close to central carbon metabolism. It is apparent that the demanding 
precursor metabolites, cofactors, and energy must be provided in certain amounts by the central 
pathway, which competes with the needs of cell growth (Wittmann, 2010). The amino acids 
synthesized from the aspartic acid family face a special problem: it has a long pathway and 
requires a highly connected network. This network contains intermediates and reactions used 
by different biosynthetic pathways at the same time. Therefore, to ensure the balance of 
synthesis of all metabolites required to meet the needs of cells, a variety of regulatory steps are 
required (Wittmann, 2010). By knocking out the central repressor McbR, almost all genes in 
methionine biosynthesis were overexpressed, but the results showed that C. glutamicum did 
not overproduce this amino acid, indicating that the synthesis of methionine has a restrictive 
mechanism (Sanchez & Demain, 2008; Wittmann, 2010). In addition to extensive 
transcriptional controls, feedback inhibition mechanisms have also been discovered recently. 
According to the description of the biosynthetic pathway of aromatic amino acids, a very 
complex pathway diagram is obtained, in which the inhibitory effects of feedback regulation 
and negative transcription control on enzymes are also included (Wittmann, 2010). 
1.1.3 Genome of C. glutamicum wild-type strain ATCC13032 
The determination of the entire genome sequence of C. glutamicum ATCC13032 was 
completed by determining 116 overlapping genome clones (Gopinath & Nampoothiri, 2014). 
Among them, 95 clones came from the ordered SuperCosI Cosmid library, which only included

chromosome (BAC) library (Eggeling & Bott, 2005a). Then, the assembled genome sequence 
is compared with all known nucleotide sequences of C. glutamicum stored in the database. For 
the differences found in the comparison process that may be caused by mutations during 
cloning, the PCR product using chromosomal DNA as a template is used to resequence, to 
establish a high-quality C. glutamicum chromosomal nucleotide sequence. 
The general characteristics of the genome sequence of C. glutamicum ATCC13032 are shown 
in Table 1. The chromosome carrying the genome of C. glutamicum is circular, containing 
3,282,708 base pairs (3.282708Mb) (Bathe et al., 1996). The GC content of the genome is 
53.8%, which is similar to that of E. coli and belongs to high GC content in classification 
(Kalinowski et al., 2003). 3002 protein-coding genes were discovered after automatic and 
manual annotations (Kalinowski et al., 2003). By comparing the homology with known 
proteins, the functions of 2489 proteins have been determined (Kalinowski et al., 2003). These 
analytical studies confirmed the taxonomy of C. glutamicum, showing the extensive metabolic 
diversity of soil bacteria. 
Using GC skew analysis, it was found that the DNA replication of C. glutamicum ATCC13032 
is bidirectional, and there are also several regions in the genome where the G+C content is 
much different from the median value (Kalinowski et al., 2003; Tauch et al., 2002). There are 
two large areas with low G+C content. The first one is about 25kb in size, including 20 coding 
regions (cg0415-cg0443) and the G+C content is 41-49% (Kalinowski et al., 2003). The genes 
in this region are related to certain aspects of murein formation and lipopolysaccharide 
synthesis (Kalinowski et al., 2003). The second G+C low-content region is much larger than 
the first, which is over 200kb, including about 180 coding regions, and mostly has no obvious 
similarities with known bacterial genes (Kalinowski et al., 2003). In contrast to these low G+C 
content regions, there is also a 14kb high G+C content region (cg3280-cg3295) in the genome, 
which is 66% (Kalinowski et al., 2003). The nucleotide sequence of the segment is over 95% 
similar to that of the C. diphtheriae genome, which could be due to the horizontal gene transfer 
from C. diphtheriae to C. glutamicum.

1.2 Introduction of glutamate 
1.2.1 Glutamate and its applications 
 
Glutamate is one of the 20 common α-amino acids that constitute proteins and are closely 
related to our lives. It naturally occurs in organisms such as animals (brains), plants (tomatoes, 
mushrooms, cereal proteins), and forestry organisms, which occupy an important position in 
many important chemical reactions, such as the protein metabolism in organisms.  
Glutamate is optically active and can be divided into three types: D type, L type, and DL type 
(Young & Ajami, 2000), and C. glutamicum is an L-glutamate-producing bacterium. D-
glutamate is the enantiomers of L-glutamate. L- and D-amino acids may have significantly 
different identical properties (melting point, color, solubility) and can behave differently 
(Grishin et al., 2020). In the previous studies, L-amino acids were found to be the major 
==PAGE:13==

constituents of living organisms, and D-enantiomers were thought to be unnatural and 
unfunctional (Bastings et al., 2019). However, D-amino acids were latterly found in the human 
body and they may originate from several sources, such as microbial synthesis, food intake the 
racemization of L-configurations(Ariyoshi et al., 2017; Bastings et al., 2019).  
As amino acids are recently becoming much closer to human life, there are plenty of products 
derived from amino acids, and the types of products are becoming more and more abundant, 
gradually infiltrating various fields of social economy from food, medicine, and chemical 
industries. As the bioengineering technology industry gradually becomes one of the main 
industries in the world in the 21st century, the demand for various amino acids is rapidly 
increasing. Since the discovery of amino acids, Japan has been actively involved in the 
production of amino acids, with a world market share of 35% and annual sales accounting for 
about 50% (Amino Acid Production Status and Market Prospect_Hebei Huayang Group, n.d.). 
The rapid expansion of the Japanese amino acid industry is not only due to its earlier 
involvement in the field, but also mainly because the major amino acid manufacturers in Japan 
(such as Ajinomoto) have a longer history of exploring amino acid technology. The excellent 
fermentation technique makes it highly competitive in the market in terms of output and 
product quality. Moreover, Japan was the first to use genetic engineering breeding to construct 
more dominant strains with excellent production capacity, which is also an important reason 
for its rapid development. 
1.2.1.1 Application in the food industry 
Glutamate is the first discovered and used amino acid. So far, it is the most widely used and 
the largest produced in the world. Glutamate is the precursor of monosodium glutamate (MSG). 
It not only has the function of enhancing the flavor of food, but also has a fresh-keeping effect 
on animal food. The most important thing is that this flavor additive is safe to eat and has a 
wide range of applications. EFSA set the acceptable daily intake (ADI) for the group of 
glutamate and its salts (calcium glutamate, sodium glutamate, potassium glutamate, 
ammonium glutamate, and magnesium glutamate) is 30 mg/(kg bw)/day in total (EFSA 
Reviews Safety of Glutamates Added to Food | European Food Safety Authority, n.d.).

The history of glutamate production is about a hundred years up to now. In 1907, Dr. Kikunae 
Ikeda, in the Tokyo Imperial University, discovered the MSG by extracting the flavor-
enhancing compounds for the seaweed kombu. He was known as the father of MSG as he found 
out the bases for the glutamate production industry (Kimber L. Rundlett, 1994). Dr. Ikeda then 
successfully extracted glutamate crystals from dried kelp and discovered that it has a unique 
taste, which is different from acid, sweet, bitter, and salty, so he named this taste "umami" 
(Tracy, n.d.). 
Initially, MSG was produced by hydrolyzing defatted soybeans or wheat protein gluten, which 
is a byproduct of soy sauce production (Wittmann & Liao, 2017). After World War II, the 
requirement of MSG increased significantly in Europe and America, and the market for 
glutamate expanded as well. However, the production method at that time was completely 
inadequate and the storage of raw materials was also a problem (Wittmann & Liao, 2017). 
Therefore, researchers started to investigate the biological method to produce glutamate, that 
is, using certain kinds of microorganisms to produce glutamate. 
1.2.1.2 Application in the pharmaceutical industry 
Glutamate can also be used in medicine. Although it is not an essential amino acid for the 
human body, it can participate in the body's metabolism as carbon and nitrogen nutrition and 
has high nutritional value. Glutamate can prevent and treat hepatic coma and it is an auxiliary 
medicine for patients with liver disease (Limón et al., 2021). Glutamate can also improve and 
maintain brain functions and can be used to treat concussions, nerve damage, epilepsy, and 
other diseases, and it also has a certain positive therapeutic effect on children with mental 
retardation (Guerriero et al., 2015). Glutamate has been made into a variety of medicines and 
used in clinical treatment, such as sodium glutamate injection, calcium glutamate injection, and 
so on (Kazmi et al., 2017). 
1.2.1.3 Application in the daily cosmetics industry 
Application in the daily cosmetics industry is also one of the important applications of 
glutamate. Glutamate is used as a hair growth agent, which can be absorbed by the scalp to 
prevent hair loss and regenerate hair, expand blood vessels and enhance blood circulation (Jara

1.2.1.4 Application in downstream products 
In the application of downstream product development, in addition to sodium glutamate, 
glutamate can also generate N-acyl sodium glutamate series products, which are used in the 
production of surfactants and soap to alleviate skin irritation (Anuradha Mishra et al., 2017). 
In addition, glutamate can be used in the production of polyglutamate. It is mainly used in food 
packaging and disposable tableware because it can be rapidly degraded in nature and does not 
pollute the environment (Ashiuchi, 2013). Glutamate can also be used in the production of 
artificial leather and high-grade artificial fibers that require good heat preservation and air 
permeability. 
1.2.2 Metabolic pathways of glutamate biosynthesis 
From the uptake of sugar to the secretion of glutamate, the main biosynthetic pathways of 
glutamate that cells undergo include glycolysis (also called EMP), pentose phosphate pathway 
(PPP, also called HMP), anaplerotic pathway, TCA cycle, glyoxylate cycle, and ammonia 
assimilation (D’Este et al., 2018; Kinoshita et al., 1957). Its anabolic pathway is shown in 
Figure 1.  
==PAGE:16==

Figure 1: Metabolic pathways of glutamate biosynthesis 
Glycolysis is a metabolic pathway that converts glucose into pyruvate. In most organisms, 
glycolysis occurs in the liquid part of the cell, the cytoplasm. The most common type of 
glycolysis is the EMP pathway. One glucose can be converted into 2 molecules pyruvate, 2 
ATP, and 2 NADH through the EMP pathway (Kinoshita et al., 1957). 
Glucose undergoes glycolysis to generate pyruvate, and pyruvate is oxidized and 
decarboxylated to generate acetyl CoA, which enters the TCA cycle to form α-ketoglutarate (or 
2-oxoglutarate), which is catalyzed by glutamate dehydrogenase (GHD) to generate glutamate 
(Kinoshita et al., 1957; Yang et al., 2014a), and then glutamate is secreted to the outside of the 
cell through transporter-mediated transmembrane to achieve glutamate accumulation. 
Biotin is a key factor in the fermentation of C. glutamicum to produce glutamate. Biotin is a 
coenzyme of acetyl-CoA carboxylase as the α-subunit of acetyl-CoA carboxylase is 
biotinylated, which participates in the fatty acid synthesis process of cells (Cao et al., 2014a). 
C. glutamicum is a biotin auxotrophic strain (Schneider et al., 2012).

1.2.3 Metabolic regulation mechanism of glutamate 
1.2.3.1 Carbon metabolism 
The regulation of carbon metabolism is mainly controlled by the energy level in the cell. When 
the biosynthesis or other energy-requiring reactions in the organism are strengthened, ATP in 
the cell is decomposed to generate ADP and AMP, and the energy level is reduced (Hardie, 
2011). Low energy activates certain enzymes that catalyze the decomposition of carbon, 
releases the inhibition of these enzymes by ATP, and inhibits enzymes related to glycogen 
synthesis, such as glycogen synthase, fructose-1,6-bisphosphate, etc. (Cooper, 2000b), thereby 
accelerating glycolysis enables the TCA cycle to generate energy, and ATP through 
phosphorylation (Chaudhry & Varacallo, 2018; Melkonian & Schury, 2019). A high level of 
intracellular energy will cause ATP to increase, inhibition of glycogen degradation and the key 
circulating enzymes for glycolysis and TCA cycle, such as glycogen phosphorylase, 
phosphofructokinase, citrate synthase, and isocitrate synthase (Chourpiliadis & Mohiuddin, 
2020; Molnar & Gair, 2015). On the contrary, enzymes required for carbohydrate synthesis are 
activated, such as glycogen synthase and fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase (Chourpiliadis & 
Mohiuddin, 2020), which then inhibit the decomposition of carbon and accelerate glycogen 
synthesis. 
The effect of biotin on carbon metabolism mainly affects the speed of degradation, rather than 
the ratio of EMP to HMP pathway (Max et al., 2010). Under conditions of sufficient biotin, 
although the subsequent oxidation activity of pyruvate has also been improved, due to the 
significant increase in the rate of carbon degradation, the balance between the rate of 
degradation and the rate of oxidation of pyruvate has been broken (Gray et al., 2014; Tong, 
2013), and pyruvate tends to generate lactate, instead of converting to acetyl-CoA and 
accelerating TCA cycle and glutamate production.  
1.2.3.2 Intracellular redox balance 
C. glutamicum can be used to produce a variety of amino acids, many of which are not redox 
neutral relative to the substrate glucose. When the reduction level of the product is higher than 
that of the substrate, additional reducing power needs to be provided. On the other hand, the 
==PAGE:18==
DTU – Food Technology – Master Thesis 
11 
 
production of substances with lower reducing power than that of the substrate will force the 
production of compounds with higher reducing power to compensate for the additional 
reducing power generated when the substrate is oxidized. In the process of glutamate 
fermentation, the reducing power of glutamate is lower than that of glucose, which contains an 
aldehyde group and could be further oxidized to carboxylic group (Reducing Sugar - Wikipedia, 
n.d.). Therefore, in addition to mass balance constraints, redox constraints will also play an 
important role in adjusting glutamate fermentation, which greatly affects the yield of glutamate. 
The medium that balances the degree of reduction between substrate and product in catabolism 
is usually NADH (Eggeling & Bott, 2005b). In the rapid NADH synthesis phase, such as the 
exponential growth phase, acetyl-CoA will mainly be oxidized from carbohydrates into ATP 
and CO2. The metabolism of coenzymes in the synthesis of glutamate by C. glutamicum is

release the inhibition of the TCA cycle. In C. glutamicum, NADH is mainly consumed by 
oxidation of the respiratory chain, producing by-product lactate is the other way to consuming 
NADH. The respiratory chain requires a large amount of oxygen, so dissolved oxygen has an 
important influence on the consumption rate of NADH, which is an important control 
parameter for glutamate fermentation. 
1.2.3.3 Anaplerotic reactions 
For the production of glutamate by C. glutamicum, the supplementation of oxaloacetate is very 
important for the continuation of the TCA cycle (Guo et al., 2013). In addition to the α-
ketoglutarate node, another important node in the glutamate synthesis pathway is the PEP-
pyruvate-oxaloacetate node. Glutamate is synthesized using α-ketoglutarate as a substrate. 
During the over synthesis of glutamate, most of the carbon atoms from glucose flow to the 
production of glutamate, and the enzyme activity of α-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase (ODHC) 
is very low at the time, causing oxaloacetate to be replenished through the TCA cycle. 
Therefore, an anaplerotic pathway is necessary in order to supplement oxaloacetate during the 
glutamate synthesis stage. 
C. glutamicum has two replenishment pathways, one is a pathway catalyzed by pyruvate 
carboxylase, which converts pyruvate into oxaloacetate, and the other one is catalyzed by 
phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP) carboxylase, which converts PEP to oxaloacetate. Petra G. Peters-
Wendisch et al. (2001) found that pyruvate carboxylase played a very important role when 
using C. glutamicum to produce amino acids. Shirai et al. (2007) discovered that the 
replenishment pathway plays an important role in the synthesis of glutamate induced by Tween-
40 through accurate 13C metabolic flow analysis experiments. Latterly, a detailed enzymatic 
analysis determined that the enzyme activities of pyruvate carboxylase and pyruvate 
dehydrogenase have changed during the glutamate synthesis stage, so that the supply of acetyl-
CoA and oxaloacetate reaches a certain balance and makes the glutamate highly productive 
==PAGE:20==
DTU – Food Technology – Master Thesis 
13 
 
(Hasegawa et al., 2008). That is, the generation from pyruvate to acetyl-CoA by pyruvate 
dehydrogenase decreases under the glutamate overproducing condition. On the contrary, the 
reaction from pyruvate to oxaloacetate increases under glutamate overproducing conditions.  
1.2.3.4 TCA cycle 
Along the oxidation pathway from citrate to α-ketoglutarate, glutamate-producing bacteria 
have two NADP+-specific dehydrogenases, namely isocitrate dehydrogenase and glutamate 
dehydrogenase (Harper et al., 2010; Plaitakis et al., 2017). The reaction of isocitrate 
dehydrogenation and decarboxylation to α-ketoglutarate catalyzed by isocitrate dehydrogenase 
and the reductive amination of α-ketoglutarate to glutamate catalyzed by glutamate 
dehydrogenase is a pair of redox conjugation reactions (Berg et al., 2002a), which means the 
amount of α-ketoglutarate and isocitrate in the cell need to be balanced. When α-ketoglutarate 
is excessive, it will feedback inhibition of isocitrate dehydrogenase and stop the synthesis of 
α-ketoglutarate and therefore reduce the production of glutamate. 
Glutamate-producing bacteria need oxidized NADP+ for isocitrate oxidation. The resulting 
reduced NADPH is regenerated into NADP+ due to the reductive amination of α-ketoglutarate. 
Since the glutamate dehydrogenase of glutamate-producing bacteria is much stronger than 
other microorganisms, the oxidation intermediate of citrate obtained from the TCA cycle no 
longer oxidizes down but accumulates in the form of glutamate (Plaitakis et al., 2017; Yang et 
al., 2014b). Glutamate has feedback inhibition on glutamate dehydrogenase. If the ammonium 
ion is further supplied in excess, the fermentation broth will be acidic and the pH will be 
between 5.5 and 6.5, and glutamate will further generate glutamine (Yang et al., 2014b). 
1.2.3.5 Glyoxylate cycle 
The glyoxylate cycle is a change in the TCA cycle, which is an anabolic pathway occurs in 
fungi, bacteria, and plants. Animals are lacking in two essential enzymes, malate synthase and 
isocitrate lyase for the glyoxylate cycle (Kevin Ahern et al., 2021). In fungi and bacteria, the 
glyoxylate cycle is able to let cells use two carbons compounds (i.e., acetate) with the absence 
of simple sugars (i.e., glucose) to realize the carbon needs (Berg et al., 2002b). The difference 
between the glyoxylate cycle and the TCA cycle is that in the glyoxylate cycle, isocitrate lyase 
==PAGE:21==
DTU – Food Technology – Master Thesis 
14 
 
converts isocitrate to succinate and glyoxylate, instead of α-ketoglutarate, which bypasses the 
decarboxylation step in the TCA cycle (Berg et al., 2002b; Kevin Ahern et al., 2021). Malate 
produced by glyoxylate and acetyl-CoA is oxidized to oxaloacetate and then PEP, which is the 
substrate in gluconeogenesis. The succinate produced in the first step can enter the TCA cycle 
and eventually form oxaloacetate as well. Therefore, the purpose of the glyoxylate cycle is to 
produce glucose from fatty acids (Kevin Ahern et al., 2021). 
 
Figure 3: TCA cycle and glyoxylate cycle (dashed arrows) ((Cheah et al., 2014) 
The key enzyme of the glyoxylate cycle, isocitrate lyase, is inhibited by glucose and succinate 
and induced by acetate (Wendisch et al., 1997; Zu Bentrup et al., 1999). When glucose is used 
as raw material to ferment to produce glutamate, by controlling the sub-appropriate amount of 
biotin, the activity of isocitrate lyase is almost invisible, which is because that the oxidation 
capacity of pyruvate decreases and the rate of acetate formation slows down (Cao et al., 2014b; 
Tong, 2013), so that the formation of isocitrate lyase induced by acetate is little.  
1.2.4 Optimization of glutamate production 
Nakamura et al. (2007) compared the mutants that can constantly secret glutamate without any 
treatment and found out that all mutants had a mutation in the NCgl1221 gene (also called

MscSG). The disruption of this gene caused an increase of intracellular glutamate even though 
under the biotin deficient condition, and the application of NCgl1221 on wild-type strain led 
to an increase of glutamate secretion, which indicates that NCgl1221 can encode a glutamate 
exporter. It is then found out by Becker et al. (2013) that NCgl1221 has an additional C-terminal 
domain compared with the homologous protein MscCG in wild-type ATCC13032 and other 
MscS-type proteins, which is 247 amino acid long and carries a transmembrane segment, thus 
plays an important role in the regulation of MscCG. In the study of Wang et al. (2018), a novel 
exporter named MscCG2 was identified in strain C. glutamicum Z188, and it exists in many 
but not all strains. Both exporter genes are located in the cytoplasmic membrane (Nakamura et 
al., 2007; Wang et al., 2018). In addition, several other methods can enhance the secretion of 
glutamate of C. glutamicum wild-type strains. 
1.2.4.1 chemical control method 
The chemical method is mainly to control the chemical components in the fermentation 
medium to hinder the normal biosynthesis of the cell membrane or cell wall so that the 
glutamate-producing bacteria are in an abnormal physiological state and the intracellular 
glutamate can be secreted out of the cell. 
a) Control the synthesis of cell membrane 
The bacterial cell membrane mainly consists of lipids and proteins, in addition to a small 
number of glycoproteins and glycolipids, and nucleic acids (Cooper, 2000a). The lipids are 
mainly polar lipids (glycerophospholipids), which are composed of fatty acids, phosphoric acid, 
glycerol, and nitrogenous bases. By controlling the synthesis of phospholipids, an incomplete 
cell membrane with insufficient phospholipid synthesis will be formed.  
Biotin as the coenzyme of acetyl-CoA carboxylase is essential for the initial reaction of fatty 
acid biosynthesis, which catalyzes the synthesis of malonyl-CoA from acetyl-CoA and then 
synthesizes phospholipids from fatty acids, ultimately forming a complete cell membrane 
(Cooper, 2000a; A. C. Smith & Cronan, 2012). Malonyl-CoA even though is not the only 
reactant for fatty acid synthase, but significant for fatty acids enter the mitochondria (Clarke & 
==PAGE:23==
DTU – Food Technology – Master Thesis 
16 
 
Nakamura, 2004). In addition, biotin limitation may also reduce the activity of 2-oxoglutarate 
dehydrogenase complex (ODHC), so that the 2-Oxoglutarate would mainly be converted to 
glutamate (Eiichir KIMURA et al., 1999). Inhibition of the synthesis of fatty acids results in 
insufficient phospholipid content and incomplete cell membrane structure, which cause the 
increase of cell membrane tension, and the exporters (MscCG and mscCG2) are activated with 
suction to release glutamate (Nakayama, 2020).  
In addition, surfactants such as Tween-40 and Tween-60 can also affect the synthesis of cell 
membranes. Eiichir KIMURA et al. (1999) by studying a C. glutamicum Tween-40 sensitive 
mutant found out that the addition of Tween-40 (Polyoxyethylene sorbitan monopalmitate) 
could decrease the expression of the dtsR gene (later renamed AccD1), which is involved in 
the synthesis of malonyl-CoA from acetyl-CoA, therefore inhibit the biosynthesis of fatty acid 
like biotin limitation. 
b) Inhibit the synthesis of cell wall 
Antibiotics such as penicillins can inhibit the synthesis of the cell wall of glutamate-producing 
bacteria by binding peptidoglycan transpeptidase. Due to the similar structure of penicillin and 
the D-Ala L-Lys D-Glu L-Ala end structure of peptidoglycan unique to Gram-positive bacteria, 
penicillin can replace the substrate used to synthesize peptidoglycan and bind to the active 
center of the enzyme, so that the alanine at the end of the pentapeptide cannot be removed by 
the transpeptidase, and the glycine cannot be connected to the alanine in front of it (Frère et al., 
1984; Yocum et al., 1979). Therefore, the network of the cell wall is interrupted and 
peptidoglycan synthesis cannot be completed, resulting in the formation of an incomplete cell 
wall. At the same time, the incomplete cell wall will leave the cell membrane in an unprotected 
state, and the osmotic pressure difference between the inside and outside of the membrane will 
cause physical damage to the cell membrane and activate the MscCG mechanosensitive 
channels (Nakayama, 2020), thus increase the permeability of glutamate to the outside of the 
cell.

Penicillin should be added at an appropriate time during the reproduction process, and the 
bacteria can be proliferated to a certain extent after addition. It is generally considered to be 
added at an early stage when the cells start to enter the logarithmic growth phase. The time and 
concentration of penicillin addition vary with the strains and growth conditions. After the 
addition of penicillin, and during the time when the bacteria divide again, the newly 
proliferated cells do not have sufficient cell wall synthesis, and the morphology of the bacteria 
changes drastically. 
1.2.4.2 Physical control method 
Glutamate fermentation is a typical metabolic control fermentation. Environmental conditions 
of fermentation, such as medium composition, pH, temperature, dissolved oxygen level, etc., 
all affect the production of glutamate to a certain degree (Xiao et al., 2006). 
Lapujade et al (1999) evaluated the effect of temperature on glutamate secretion of C. 
glutamicum 2262 and found out that a sixfold increase was observed by increasing the 
temperature from 33℃ to 40℃. Shi et al. (2020) by comparing a temperature-sensitive mutant 
strain C. glutamicum TCCC11822 and the wild-type strain ATCC13032, determined that seven 
relevant genes that were missing or mutated in TCCC11822 contributing to the synthesis of 
cell membrane/wall were beneficial for the glutamate secretion. In addition, only temperature

increases could also accelerate the glutamate secretion of ATCC13032 (Shi et al., 2020). 
However, the optimal time for temperature conversion varies with strains, inoculum amount, 
medium composition, and fermentation conditions, and after the temperature change, moderate 
residual growth must be carried out to complete the transition from glutamate non-
accumulating cells to glutamate accumulating ones. 
1.3 Delactosed whey permeate (DWP) 
Delactosed whey permeate (DWP), also known as mother liquor (liquid remaining after the 
recovery of lactose crystals), is a by-product in whey protein concentrates and isolates 
production, with lactose being removed from the permeate of whey or skim milk, which is the 
major source of DWP (Oliveira et al., 2019). The main composition of DWP is about 450 
mmol/L lactose and 340 mmol/L citrate. However, its composition varies greatly and is affected 
by the source selection, manufacturing operations used in its production, and crystallization 
during storage in the fridge (Durham, 2009; Levin et al., 2016). 
DWP is regarded as an economic burden for many products, in addition to the high organic 
content in it which may cause environmental problems, it is seen as a by-product without value. 
DWP is mainly applied in animal feed and fertilizer (Durham, 2009). It has recently been 
estimated that for every kilogram of milk used in cheese production, DWP production is 0.5 
kilograms (Liang et al., 2009). In addition, drying DWP is not easy because of the high 
viscosity and hygroscopicity mediated by lactose (Durham, 2009). To limit DWP production, 
an increase in lactose recovery through recrystallization is considered effective. Apart from its 
significant lactose content, the mineral content of DWP on a total solids basis is about 50% 
higher than that of whey (Gajendragadkar & Gogate, 2016). Therefore, DWP is valuable as a 
source of minerals as well. Besides, the large amounts of calcium, magnesium, and potassium 
in DWP can be used in the following applications: (a) enhance the nutrient content of foods; 
(b) serve as a substitute of salt (S. T. Smith et al., 2016); (c) add in baking combined with whey 
protein phospholipid concentrate as a functional ingredient in the manufacture of confectionery 
products (Levin et al., 2016). 
At present, the lactose permeate is not widely used in the food industry yet but on soil or treated 
==PAGE:26==
DTU – Food Technology – Master Thesis 
19 
 
by sewage treatment facilities (Mehra et al., 2014). Thus, the dairy industry needs to pay for 
the disposal cost of DWP, which is estimated to be 1.2 cents per liter for fertilizer and 6 cents 
per liter for land disposal (Friend et al., 2004). Except for the high cost of waste disposal, 
current environmental regulations restrict the disposal of DWP due to its high mineral content. 
Therefore, scientists have explored other opportunities to use DWP. For example, in animal 
feed supplements, DWP is used instead of molasses as a nutritionally valuable binder (Friend 
et al., 2004), directly used in animal nutrition licks. In addition, DWP can be combined with 
urea or other protein sources to be converted into dry feed supplements similar to the molasses-
urea feeding system (Friend et al., 2004). DWP combined with cottonseed meal as a protein 
supplement has been evaluated as a dry feed that can improve the feed intake and live weight 
gain of cattle consuming low-quality grain straw. Today, some countries are producing liquid 
DWP as a feed supplement for dairy cows, such as Australia and New Zealand (Friend et al., 
2004). In addition to animal feed, DWP is also used as fertilizer by metering it into land 
irrigation systems (Durham, 2009). Moreover, DWP is now been studied by scientists to 
ferment aroma substances such as acetoin by microorganisms (Liu et al., 2020) and predictably 
to be applied into industrial production.  
1.4 Project objectives 
Since the middle of the last century, scientists have begun to study the use of C. glutamicum to 
produce various amino acids, and have achieved industrialized production. However, according 
to the literature, it is noticed that to increase the production of glutamate, most of the energy 
materials used for cell growth are glucose and other broths, which makes the cost of glutamate 
production high. After entering the 21st century, with the concept of environmental protection 
and sustainable development, the recycling of production waste in various industries has 
become an urgent problem to be solved. Therefore, this project puts forward an innovative idea, 
which is to use the waste from the dairy industry to produce glutamate. 
A large amount of waste liquid is generated during the production of dairy products, and after 
recycling, the final waste liquid (also called mother liquor) is used in this project. It is known 
that C. glutamicum requires a carbon source to ensure growth. According to the metabolic 
==PAGE:27==

pathway of C. glutamicum, citrate, as one of the intermediate substances in the TCA cycle, is 
only three steps away from the synthesis of glutamate, so replacing glucose with citrate to 
produce glutamate might also save a certain amount of time. Therefore, the ultimate goal of the 
project is to use the citrate in mother liquor to complete the whole process from cell growth to 
catalysis of glutamate production, so as to achieve the sustainable development of the dairy 
industry.

Chapter 2. Optimization of glutamate secretion 
2.1 Introduction 
This chapter will mainly discuss the effect of different cell growth and catalysis conditions on 
glutamate secretion. C. glutamicum is a glutamate-producing bacterium and has been applied 
to glutamate industrial production. Therefore, a wild-type strain ATCC13032 will be used in 
this experiment. Wild-type C. glutamicum even though can produce glutamate itself, a large 
percentage of glutamate is still intracellular. In the study of (Birnbaum & Demain, 1969), 
penicillin was used as a trigger for glutamate secretion and an impressive output was achieved 
at that time. Thus, the first step is to replicate the experiment by using ATCC13032 and figure 
out the principle and key points of the procedures. Based on this, several attempts to further 
improve the secretion will be put forward. This part of the experiment will be implemented 
through the following two parts: (a) improve cell permeability, (b) improve the utilization of 
citrate.   
As introduced before, cell permeability could be increased by biotin limitation, the addition of 
detergents or antibiotics, temperature increase, etc. Due to the biotin limitation that has been 
studied a lot whether, in wild-type or mutant strains, the effect of detergents, antibiotics, 
temperature adjustment, and other methods for cell permeability increase will be discussed. 
It is known that the main components in mother liquor are lactose and citrate. However, C. 
glutamicum is unable to utilize the dairy-based components, such as lactose and galactose, 
which is because it lacks the genes involved in lactose metabolism (β-galactosidase and lactose 
permease) (Barrett et al., 2004). Therefore, citrate is considered to be the main carbon and 
energy source that C. glutamicum could utilize. To make full use of citrate in mother liquor, a 
medium with simple and adjustable composition will be used to determine the utilization rate 
of citrate in the medium through quantitative experiments. Based on this, adjustment of the 
concentration and other conditions of mother liquor will be figured out, resulting to achieve 
full utilization of the mother liquor and high glutamate production.

2.4 Summary 
This chapter mainly discussed methods to increase the production of extracellular glutamate. 
Through the replication experiment, only 0.26 g/L glutamate was secreted, which is about 1/10 
of the result from that article. Therefore, further optimization methods were carried out. 
Experiments have found that ampicillin concentration plays an important role in cell 
permeability and glutamate secretion. 0.2~1.5 μg/mL ampicillin addition in the growth phase 
at 5.5 h led to an increase of extracellular glutamate in contrast to the replication experiment. 
Besides, changes in growth conditions, such as adding citrate to the medium in the presence of 
glucose to induce the citrate transport system, increasing the culture temperature to improve 
the effect of ampicillin, etc., can effectively enhance the secretion of glutamate. Furthermore, 
Ca2+ was found effective to transport citrate into cells to be used, which allowed cells to grow 
on CGXII medium with citrate as the only carbon source. 2% citrate plus 5 mM Ca2+ for 
cultivation and 6% citrate for catalysis are considered to be optimal for cells growing on CGXII. 
As for the effect of detergents on the improvement of cell permeability, CTAB as a cationic 
detergent was the most effective one compared to other nonionic detergents.

Chapter 3. Turn waste to taste - application of DWP 
3.1 Introduction 
In chapter 2, it has been figured out that C. glutamicum wild-type ATCC13032 growing with 
2% citrate and 5 mM Ca2+, and catalyzed with 6% citrate could reach the highest citrate 
metabolism and glutamate secretion. However, according to the different compositions of 
mother liquor and CGXII mediums, it needs to be verified again to make sure cells could grow 
in the mother liquor.   
3.2 Materials and methods 
3.2.1 Bacteria strains and media 
C. glutamicum wild-type ATCC13032 was used in this chapter. Cells were prepared the same 
way described in chapter 2.  
The mixture of mother liquor, CGXII minimal medium, or distilled water was for growth 
medium. pH was adjusted to 7 for all conditions. Ampicillin was added in both growth and 
catalysis phases to increase cell permeability. (NH4)2SO4 was added in catalysis media. 30 g/L 
CaCO3 was added to the catalysis medium to adjust pH. 
3.2.2 Cell growth  
According to the result from chapter 2, 2% citrate corresponds to 30% mother liquor. Therefore, 
the mixture of 30% mother liquor & 70% distilled water, and 30% mother liquor & 70% CGXII 
minimal medium were compared to see if cells could grow only on mother liquor.  
3.2.3 Cell catalysis 
Cells were catalyzed in 100% mother liquor.  (NH4)2SO4 was added to the catalysis medium. 
30 g/L CaCO3 was added to the catalysis medium to adjust the pH. 0.2~1.5 μg/mL ampicillin 
was added in cell growth medium at 5.5 h and 4 μg/mL ampicillin was added at the beginning 
of the catalysis phase. Cells were crushed before testing glutamate.

Chapter 4. Conclusion and outlook 
The mother liquor is proved useful to glutamate production. However, its instability and high 
osmotic pressure make it difficult to be applied to industrial production. But the mix of different 
batches of DWP and quantitatively determination of the content, suitable filtration, and 
addition of other nutrient compounds may be feasible. A large number of repeated experiments 
are necessary to prove its effects on the production of glutamate by different strains.  
In addition, the reason why penicillin could affect cell permeability on incomplete resting cells 
needs to be found out. The combination of different methods to increase cell permeability could 
be attempted, i.e., use penicillin and Tween-40/CTAB for cell growth and catalysis phase 
respectively. 
Low enzyme activity of ODHC could promote glutamate synthesis (A. Y et al., 2007; K. Y et 
al., 1997). ODHC consists of three subunits E1, E2, and E3, which are coded by odhA, aceF, 
lpd, respectively. However, the knockout of the gene will cause the TCA cycle to be disrupted, 
thereby affecting cell growth. The weakening of gene expression may increase the 
accumulation of glutamate. Besides, overexpression of the gene icd related to isocitrate 
dehydrogenase may cause the accumulation of 2-oxoglutarate and thus increase glutamate 
production. The interaction mechanism between different factors needs to be further clarified.
