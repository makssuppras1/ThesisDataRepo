Abstract
This thesis combined high spatio-temporal resolution acoustic data with high spatio-
temporal resolution environmental data and successfully developed a method using
freeware for automatic identiﬁcation and characterisation of sprat Sprattus sprat-
tus balticus schools in the Bornholm Basin. These schools were then analysed for
ecological drivers and a Habitat Volume Map was developed revealing the major
constraints. Consequently, the potential existence of an ephemeral predator refuge
is highlighted and current understanding of temporo-spatial dynamics are reinforced
and further elucidated. Future distributions and dynamics of sprat schools are dis-
cussed in the context of climate change and expected changes in North Sea inﬂow
frequencies. Finally, the valuable role of these ﬁndings, in addition to acoustics in
general, are discussed in relation to Ecosystem-based Fisheries Management in the
Baltic Sea.

1
Introduction
1.1
Research Problem
Small pelagic ﬁsh such as sprat (Sprattus sprattus), herring (Clupea harengus), an-
chovy (Engraulis encrasicolus) and sardine (Sardina pilchardus) are important com-
ponents of many commercial ﬁsheries worldwide. The diﬀerent species are often
found aggregated in large schools, which is generally considered a behavioural trait
critical for predator avoidance, reduced energy-consumption and even improved for-
aging and mating success (Fr´eon and Dagorn, 2000).
It is therefore of great importance to understand the dynamics and character-
istics of school formation and its drivers given that spatial distribution dynamics
can modify species catchability to a large extent and thus aﬀect both the ﬁshing
industry and resource management (Arregu´ın-S´anchez, 1996). Furthermore, spatial
distribution of ﬁsh is a complex result of density-dependent population dynamics,
trophic interactions and environmental conditions (Ogut and Reno, 2004). A better
understanding of the cause-and-eﬀect of this spatial distribution would therefore
allow researchers to make inferences about the ecosystem based on distribution and
vice versa.
An important technology for ocean exploration and analyses of ﬁsh distribution
is underwater acoustics. Fisheries acoustic data are often available both from com-
mercial and scientiﬁc vessels and large datasets have been compiled by diﬀerent or-
ganizations. Following installation, acoustic data acquisition is largely autonomous,
requiring only set-up, calibration, data-storage management and occasional main-
tenance of the electronic components. For this reason, vessels equipped with such
equipment may record continuously throughout the entirety of the cruise, even if it
is known before hand that much of the data is not required for the immediate task
at hand.
This results in an enormous catalogue of acoustic data across the globe. Although
expert manual analysis is regularly applied to many of these data, a much larger
fraction is left unexplored. Application of automatic data analysis methods therefore
presents an eﬀective and much needed approach.
Despite the signiﬁcant technological and theoretical advancements in hydro-
acoustics over the past century, automatic species identiﬁcation remains one of the
foremost challenges, despite several attempts with varying success (see examples:
Haralabous and Georgakarakos (1996),Rose and Leggett (1988) & Scalabrin et al.
(1996)).
9
==PAGE:11==
The most common identiﬁcation method involves trawling in close temporal
and spatial proximity to where the acoustic data were collected. Ideally, the trawl
should catch the acoustic targets from the echogram, however temporal lags due to
operation logistics, movement of ﬁsh into and out of the area and non-coordinated
movement of the ship and ﬁshing gear due to currents and waves can mean that
the sampling does not match.
Furthermore, even if the trawling was known to
sweep the same area shortly after the acoustic sampling, gear selectivity and evasive
behaviours of ﬁsh to the nets may result in a biased catch.
One other method is visual interpretation of the echograms, requiring expert
knowledge gained from previous analysis of school formation patterns on echograms.
This approach is time consuming, subjective and encounters major diﬃculty when
faced with mixed species schools.
In the Baltic, the dominant species of ﬁsh are sprat, herring, cod (Gadus morhua),
and ﬂounder (Platichtys ﬂesus), however only sprat and herring regularly form
pelagic schools (Nilsson et al., 2003). Consequently, the problematic task of species
identiﬁcation from acoustic data in this study area is greatly simpliﬁed, as pelagic
schools can be assumed to be either herring or sprat with the species-resolution
being further guided by analysis of catch composition of trawls.
Despite the importance of hydro-acoustics in ﬁsheries management, the price of
commercial software may preclude its use by institutions under ﬁnancial pressure,
or even independent researchers. The emergence of freeware for hydro-acoustic pro-
cessing such as Echopype, MatEcho and Movies3D presents an alternative. While
the scope and power of these freeware may not match that of the commercial coun-
terparts, they may nonetheless be adapted into the workﬂow of a project to serve
the desired purpose.
1.2
Aim and Scope
Through use of the Matecho freeware, this thesis will attempt to characterise the
school formation of sprat in the Baltic sea in terms of their spatial, positional and
acoustic energetic properties. These metrics will be subsequently paired with envi-
ronmental and bathymetric data and explored for the existence of potential drivers.
Available acoustic data from a 2015 cruise is analysed and an automated pro-
cess for school detection and characterisation is developed in Matlab following data
processing in Matecho. Environmental data from CTD stations acquired between
transects, in addition to satellite datasets, are incorporated to further characterise
the schools and attempt to understand the drivers of the formation. Finally, the
implications for ﬁsheries, both current and future, will also be discussed.
1.3
Outline of the Thesis
This thesis will begin with a history and applied theory of Fisheries Acoustics in
chapter 2. Next, chapter 3 will introduce the Baltic Sea and the ﬁshing industry
operating within. The ecology of the main species of interest, sprat, will be explored,
in addition to a brief consideration of schooling behaviour. Chapter 4 will provide
information on the ﬁrst stage of methods, including data collection and process-
ing while chapter 5 will outline the methods used for the school identiﬁcation and

characterisation. The results are presented in chapter 6, before a ﬁnal discussion
and conclusion in chapter 7. A total of 3 annexes are included, dealing with data

Background to Acoustic Theory
Hydro-acoustics has been identiﬁed as one of the most valuable technologies in
a marine scientists toolkit for achieving the aims of the Ecosystem Approach for
Fisheries Management (EAFM) (Koslow, 2009).
The basic concept of active hydro-acoustics involves sending short, directed
acoustic pulses (termed pings) from a transducer into the water.
Some of the
acoustic energy is backscattered towards the transducer by objects with an acoustic
impedance diﬀerent from water (eg. plankton, ﬁsh, rocks or shipwrecks). Given
knowledge of the sound speed in the sampled water body, the distance of a target
can then be deduced from a measurement of the time delay between the ping and the
return of the scattered pulse (echo). Several pings of varying acoustic frequencies
can be produced at the same time allowing researchers to further distinguish and
characterise the source of the echo.
There are several reasons why hydro-acoustics has earned its role as a primary
tool in the exploration and management of our oceans, amongst more conventional
biological sampling tools. To begin with, optical tools are ineﬀective as ultravi-
olet, visible and infrared radiation are rapidly scattered or absorbed by particles,
dissolved organic matter or the water itself, meaning that light waves travel much
short distances in water than in air (Brown et al., 1989). Likewise, radio waves are
not suited to a saltwater medium due to the high conductivity (Pozzebon, 2015).
Sound waves, however, travel at approximately 1500 m/s underwater (more than 4
times faster than in air), and although transmission losses occur (see chapter 2.3),
they can travel very long distances (Brown et al., 1989). This allows a very large
area to be sampled in a short period of time.
The second reason is that acoustic sampling, when performed appropriately, is
remote, non-invasive, and non-extractive. In other words, minimal harm is caused
to the sampled community and habitat while observation bias is modest.
Thirdly, not only is the sampling range and eﬃciency vastly superior than con-
ventional means (for instance, trawling is slow and only captures one layer at a
time, which is then integrated across the depth layer and trawl time into one catch),
hydro-acoustics provides much higher spatial and temporal resolution.
Finally, once set-up and calibration have been completed, acoustic sampling
requires little attention and is relatively cheap, with data being logged automatically
around the clock.
Hydro-acoustics therefore is well suited to play an important role in many re-
search tasks, such as localisation and quantiﬁcation of aquatic species (including
12
==PAGE:14==
commercial species and layers of zooplankton) in addition to benthic habitat map-
ping (both depth and characterisation) and in some cases, even oceanographic tasks
such as pycnocline depth determination (Schaber et al., 2009).
Unsurprisingly, hydro-acoustics is widely applied in daily management of ﬁsheries
across the world, most commonly through biomass assessments using echointegration
and target strength measurement of isolated ﬁsh (Simmonds and MacLennan, 2005).
2.1
History
Underwater sound has been exploited for millions of years in the animal king-
dom, perhaps most impressively by marine mammals such as the humpback whale
(Megaptera novaeangliae) which uses songs to transmit information to con-speciﬁcs
thousands of kilometer away using the SOFAR channel (Chen, 2017). Dolphins have
ﬁne-tuned their acoustic tool kit for predation, adapting their “clicks” to localise and
characterise their prey and have even been reported to eavesdrop on other dolphins
to steal prey (Xitco and Roitblat, 1996). Even acoustic eﬀects such as cavitation
are exploited by the snapping shrimp, who rapidly snap their claws producing a
bubble which subsequently collapses, causing either shock or damage to the victim
(Versluis et al., 2000).
Humans have been somewhat slower to harness this knowledge, with Aristotle
musing in 384–322 BC whether ﬁsh could hear upon discovering that sound traveled
underwater. Perhaps the ﬁrst documented application by human civilisation was
Leonardo da Vinci, who in 1490 wrote:
”If you cause your ship to stop and place the head of a long tube in the
water and place the outer extremity to your ear, you will hear ships at
a great distance from you.”
Nonetheless, it was not until 1826 that the earliest measurement of sound speed
in water was performed.
Soon after, the development of hydro-acoustics began
to speed up at an unprecedented rate, motivated by the desire to avoid repeats
of the Titanic disaster, which collided with an iceberg in 1912 resulting in 1500
deaths. Further impetus for developing hydro-acoustics stemmed from the advent
of submarines during World War I (1914–1918), when over 4800 merchant ships were
sank by submarines.
By the early 1930’s several publications appeared, reporting the use of active
sonar technology in ﬁsheries research, notably for the detection of ﬁsh, and the
discovery of the deep scattering layer caused by zooplankton. The ﬁst visual rep-
resentation of underwater sound energy, now better known as an echogram, was
produced around this time (Sund, 1935).
Acoustic surveys gradually became more sophisticated and gained wider appli-
cation throughout the 40’s, however these ﬁndings were predominately qualitative,
though simple echo-counting allowed a limited quantitative approach, provided the
targets were shallow and well dispersed.
The computer science revolution of the late 60’s spread its inﬂuence into nearly
every aspect of technology, allowing researchers to analyse the echos with more so-
phisticated signal processing techniques. Undoubtedly, one of the greatest advance-
ments in this regard was echointegationn (see chapter2.7), which is now successfully
used to measure ﬁsh abundance in many survey cruises.

==PAGE:15==
These theoretical advancements were closely matched by technological advance-
ments such as the move from analog to digital electronics, and the improvement of a
simple single beam echosounder, leading to the dual-beam and ultimately the split-
beam echosounder, today considered the most widely used and eﬀective echosounder
in ﬁsheries science.
2.2
Speed of Sound in Water
In 1826, Collandon sat in a small row boat on a lake in Geneva with his ear to an
underwater trumpet and his eyes scanning the horizon. On a separate row boat
16km away, sat Collandon’s colleague, Sturm. By means of an ingenious mechanical
setup involving an underwater bell, pulleys, a candle and a pan of gunpowder, Sturm
simultaneously struck the bell underwater and produced a ﬂash above the surface.
The instant Collandon saw the ﬂash, he began counting the seconds until eventually,
he heard the underwater bell signal. From this delay (∼11 seconds) they calculated
the speed of sound in fresh water at 8 oC to be 1435 ms−1, admittedly not a bad
estimate compared to modern estimates (1438 ms−1).
The speed of sound (c) in water depends on the water temperature, salinity
and depth and can be accurately predicted by empirical equations. Although the
international standard is based on equations developed by Del Grosso (1974) and
Chen & Millero (1977), a simpler nine-term equation developed my Mackenzie (1981)
for c (in ms−1) is appropriate (and therefore preferable) for ﬁsheries applications:
c = 1448.96 + 4.591T −0.05304T 2 + 2.374 × 10−4T 3 + 1.34(S −35)+
0.0163z + 1.675 × 10−7z2 −0.01025T(S −35) −7.139 × 10−13Tz3
(2.1)
where T is the temperature (oC), S is the salinity (PPT), and z is the depth (m).
MacKenzie’s equation is valid for temperatures between -2 and 30 oC, salinities
between 25 and 40 PPT and depths up to 8000 m (see appendix A for justiﬁcation
of Baltic-speciﬁc values in this equation). If input values fall within the speciﬁed
ranges, the associated standard error of the predicted sound speed is 0.07 ms−1. In
cases where the depth is less than 1000 m, the ﬁnal term of equation 2.1 (involving
Tz3) is negligible and may be ignored.
2.3
Absorption in Water
As acoustic waves propagate through the water, acoustic energy is converted into
heat energy through a process known as absorption. Accordingly, the pressure of the
wave decreases exponentially away from the source. In both fresh and salt water,
viscous losses happen due to friction, and this loss is proportional to the frequency
of the wave squared.
In saltwater, certain compounds undergo a process of molecular relaxation which
results in additional losses of acoustic energy. When these compounds experience
pressure from the propagated wave, they begin a reduction reaction to form ions.
However, this pressure-induced change takes some time and if the pressure is reduced
before the reduction takes place, no acoustic energy is lost through absorption.
Consequently, given that higher frequencies cause the pressure to oscillate more

occur (MacLennan and Simmonds, 1992). The two main compounds that inﬂuence

Target Strength
An important activity in quantitative ﬁsheries acoustics involves determining the
backscattering cross section area (σbs) of the ﬁsh targets, or the the related quantity
in the logarithmic domain, known as the Target Strength (TS).
The back-scattering cross-section provides a more intuitive understanding and
can be deﬁned as shown in equation 2.2 (MacLennan and Simmonds, 1992), where

σbs = R2Ib/Ii

TS = 10log10(σbs)

be observed in the linear domain across the range of potential targets of interest. For
instance, a large adult cod could produce a σbs 2 orders of magnitude greater than
a small anchovy, (0.001 vs 0.00001) however these values are more easily compared
and expressed as decibels (-30 dB vs -50 dB ). In this way, if a target has a TS 3
dB greater (less negative) than another, then its backscattered energy is twice as
much, and likewise, if it has a TS 6 dB greater, then it backscatters 4 times as much
energy.
2.5
Split Beam Echosounder
Although a single transducer is capable of measuring the distance to a target, it
has two major limitations. Firstly, the direction of the target (i.e. the angle from a
perpendicular line to the transducer face in 2 directions) is not resolved. The second
limitation, stemming from the ﬁrst, involves compensation for the beam pattern.
In reality, the transducer face is an array of piezo-ceramic elements that result in
interference leading to a beam pattern with varying amplitude as a function of the
angle (ﬁg 2.3). Depending where the target is situated in this space (at a constant
range), the energy received from the same source will vary, causing the return echo
to likewise be a function of the location within the beam. Without knowledge of
both the direction and distance, the returning echo can not be corrected for the
beam pattern, and therefore is not a reliable indicator of the TS.
To solve this issue, the split-beam echosounder was invented, which to this day
remains a gold-standard technology in ﬁsheries acoustics.

3.1
The Baltic Sea
Since its development from the freshwater Ancylus Lake into the marine Littorina
Sea about 8000 years ago and later into today’s brackish Baltic Sea, the salinity
has been a keystone environmental factor for the whole ecosystem including the ﬁsh
fauna (Murray et al., 2019). The modern Baltic Sea (ICES subarea 27.3 excluding
27.3a) is described as a shallow, semi-enclosed, brackish sea (ICES, 2019). It has
a distinctive and rather uniform vertical proﬁle with a permanent stratiﬁcation of
brackish waters (7-8 PSU) lying above a more saline layer (∼15-20 PSU) (Conley
et al., 2009).
This relatively fresh surface layer is due to vast freshwater runoﬀ
from the surrounding 9 countries (plus 5 further countries which contribute to the
catchment). Consequently, this stratiﬁcation prevents oxygen ventilation through
vertical mixing, causing the ecosystem to rely on episodic north sea inﬂows of oxygen
rich water. Bottom water in the Baltic Sea is therefore characteristically oxygen
depleted (mostly due to microbial respiration), though it exhibits sporadic high
oxygen saturation (MacKenzie et al., 2007).
Interestingly, these episodic injections of high-saline, oxygen-rich waters also
serve to reinforce the permanent stratiﬁcation (Conley et al., 2009). MacKenzie
et al. (2007) have reported that these inﬂows are becoming increasingly less frequent.
In fact, in recent times, many basins, including the Gotland Deep, Landsort Deep
and Gdansk Deep, exhibit permanent bottom hypoxia and in some years, these low
oxygen layers expand upwards into the water column to the extent that they merge,
forming one large dead zone (Murray et al., 2019; Conley et al., 2009).
Given the role of temperature on metabolic activity, both the magnitude and
impact of this hypoxia is likely to expand due to an expected increase in marine
heatwaves worldwide (Oliver et al., 2018).
These peculiar hydrographic features mean that ﬁsh diversity in the Baltic Sea
is quite peculiar, albeit limited, with marine species such as sprat and cod being
found in the south western areas, while brackish and even freshwater species are
more common in the northeastern areas, including pike (Esox lucius) and perch
(Perca ﬂuviatilis).

3.4
Schooling Behaviour
Schooling is a ubiquitous behaviour in the marine environment, exhibited by more
than 80% of all ﬁsh species during at least one stage of their development (Fr´eon
and Dagorn, 2000). In addition to seasonal migrations between sites, sprat also
exhibit diel-vertical migrations (DVM) during which they remain at deeper depths
within schools in the daytime before dis-aggregating and ascending at dusk to feed
on zooplankton (Cardinale et al., 2003). The purpose of this daytime behaviour
is shown to be linked with the level of light, allowing them to reduce the risk of
predation from their main visual predators, cod (Nilsson et al., 2003).
Although grouping together increases the likelihood of foraging success, it simul-
taneously increase intra-speciﬁc competition, thereby counterbalancing the initial
evolutionary advantage.
Furthermore, as highlighted by cases of salmon lice in
intensive aquaculture, high density levels of ﬁsh increases the occurrence and trans-
25
==PAGE:27==
mission of infectious diseases and parasites, a scenario which has been proven in ﬁsh
schools by Ogut and Reno (2004).
Such a behaviour must therefore provide some other signiﬁcant advantage, either
at the individual or population level to explain the evolutionary selective pressure.
Although reductions in hydrodynamic drag is likely to aid in long-distance migra-
tions, it is unlikely to be the key driver for Baltic sprat. Instead, the behaviour
provides the pelagic group a means of protection from predators, an eﬀect which
can be explained by 3 main mechanisms.
First, in terms of the individual ﬁsh, they gain safety in numbers, thereby re-
ducing the chances that they speciﬁcally are captured. Secondly, the group gains an
improved visual ﬁeld for spotting a potential predator due to the greater number of
eyes searching the water column within close proximity. In addition, Rieucau et al.
(2014) reports that dense aggregations are able to transmit information via compres-
sional waves at a speed greater than one order of magnitude larger than dispersed
individuals, thereby allowing the group to be alerted to a predator much faster. Fi-
nally, the shimmering display caused by light reﬂection from the individuals scales,
in addition to the fast movement of overlapping individuals, can disorientate and
overwhelm the visual-based hunting capacity of many predators (Romenskyy et al.,
2020).
An understanding of the formation of schools, and the trophic eﬀects that emerge
from their formation and distribution is therefore important, both for improved
ﬁsheries management and as an indicative tool for mapping and understanding the
eﬀects of changing ecosystems (e.g. climate change).

4.1.4
Target Strength of Baltic Sprat
Target strength (TS) is a measurement of the returning echo intensity relative to
the original signal (MacLennan and Simmonds, 1992). It is reported that 90–95%
of the reﬂected sound is a result of the swimbladder (Foote, 1980) and consequently,
any changes to the size and shape of this buoyancy-controlling organ will aﬀect the
TS. A species-speciﬁc quantitative relationship exists between a ﬁsh’s length and its
TS. Previously, ICES used the TS derived from measurements in the North Sea for
assessing the Baltic Sea stock. However, Didrikas and Hansson (2004) derived the
following formula based on in-situ Baltic Sea measurements of sprat:
TS = 20 × log10(Length) −67.8
(4.1)
This relationship, which is ﬁxed at a slope coeﬃcient of 20 (as is standard practice
in ﬁsheries acoustics), results in approximately a two-fold diﬀerence in stock biomass
estimates. Such a large diﬀerence is due largely to the salinity diﬀerences between
the two seas. In the relatively fresh Baltic Sea, the sprat experience a decreased
positive buoyancy. In addition, Baltic Sea sprat possess less fat in general than
their North Sea con-speciﬁcs, a trait which further decreases their positive buoyancy.
Consequently, in order to maintain neutral-buoyancy, these factors are compensated
for by a slightly larger swimbladder.

4.1.6
Vertical Proﬁles
The temperature-salinity plots for 4 representative CTD stations are shown on the
right panes of ﬁg. 4.7. These plots are useful for identifying the distinct components,
or rather the water masses that make up the entire water column. Beginning at the
bottom left, cold, relatively fresh and fully oxygen saturated water is observed, in-
dicative of the surface layer. Immediately under the surface, the water temperature
decreases slightly, while other variables remain stable. This is likely due to atmo-
spheric temperatures heating the surface slightly (solar radiation), while the rest of
the layer remains buﬀered. Given the stability of this layer, it is called the Mixed
Layer as no signiﬁcant gradient is observed along these three main variables.
Next, the water column displays a long diagonal trajectory towards the top right
of the graph, indicative of the halocline. It is important to note that during this
transition, oxygen saturation begins to drop. Oxygen continues to drop, reaching
31

approximately 50% at the top right of the T-S plot, co-coinciding with maximum
temperatures and intermediate salinities. This could indicate a previous cold, salty
and oxygen rich inﬂow that has since become oxygen depleted and partially mixed
with the Baltic surface layer, reducing its salinity and increasing its temperature.
This medium density water mass sits on top of a colder, oxygen rich layer whose
salinity is also greater. This layer may indicate a more recent inﬂow event. If this is
the case, the ﬁnal, most stagnant bottom layer could originate from the same inﬂow,
given the constant gradient of salinity and temperature, however with a considerable
percentage of the oxygen saturation being depleted due to aerobic respiration over
time.
Such a vertical proﬁle is to be expected, given that researchers at the Leibniz
Institute for Baltic Sea Research Warnemunde (IOW) recorded an inﬂow between
December 13th to 26th 2014. This inﬂow, with a total volume of 198 km3 of highly
oxygen saturated salt-water, was the 3rd largest recorded since records began 60
years ago. The researchers estimate that this oxygen-rich inﬂow added approxi-
mately 4 gigatons of salt into the Baltic Sea and will replenish much of the dead
zones present in the Bornholm and Gotland basin.

Halocline Depth Determination
Halocline proﬁles were extracted from CTD and satellite data before being smoothed
using the smooth function in R (method = ”3RS3R”). Next, using a moving window
of 5, the maximum gradient was determined and the corresponding depth recorded
as the halocline. Visual inspection conﬁrmed the validity of this approach. The
results of this are indicated in ﬁg. 4.7 as the horizontal dashed line in the middle
panes. A total of 187 haloclines were computed in this way resulting in a mean
halocline depth of 45.45 m (min=26; max=50; sd=4.02).

4.2.4
Noise Reduction Filters
Acoustic measurements are subject to considerable noise, a term that covers ”un-
wanted contributions to the signal from mechanical, electrical, or biological sources”
according to Simmonds and MacLennan (2005). If the noise is not excluded, echograms
will be positively biased towards an increasing number of targets and, in the case of
echointegration, will result in an overestimation of the biomass.
Impulsive nose is an anomalous high value that only persists for one ping. One
possible cause is an unsynchronized echosounder (Ryan et al., 2015).
Transient noise and Background noise persist for several pings and even hours
(respectively), with possible causes beng low frequency signals from crashing waves,
passing boats, propellers and machinery (Ryan et al., 2015).
Certain mechanical and practical solutions can be implemented prior to data col-
lection in order to minimise noise (eg. quiet hulls and electrical shielding). Nonethe-
less, in cases were the noise is already present in the data, several post-processing
tools are available.
These cleaning algorithms, based on De Robertis and Higginbottom (2007), were
implemented using the recommended default values suited to the acoustic data.

5
School Identiﬁcation and
Characterisation
In this chapter, I outline the methods used to identify and characterise potential
sprat schools. From here on, the acoustic data used is the echo-integration output
as detailed at the end of chapter four. Additionally, Matecho is no longer used in
this process. Instead, the analysis outlined in this section takes place entirely in
Matlab.
To avoid confusion with the echogram, I will refer to the image output of the
echointegration as the echo-integram (EIG).
5.1
Choosing a frequency
Despite the presence of 4 available ping frequencies, only 38 kHz was used for the
purposes of identiﬁcation and characterisation of schools. This decision was based on
visual examination of the EIG, whereby schools were manually located and compared
across the 4 frequencies.
In all cases, 38 kHz provided the best resolution and
distinguishable borders. Additionally, this choice of frequency is used in a well cited
paper where the EchoView school identiﬁcation algorithm (SHAPES) is outlined
(Coetzee, 2000) and in a further related paper Saraux et al. (2014).
5.2
Filtering the EIG
5.2.1
Sv Threshold
Given the low ﬁsh biodiversity present in the Baltic Pelagic Ecosystem in addition
to analysis of the catch compositions, it was clear that most pelagic acoustic marks
could be assumed to originate from either sprat, herring or cod. Nonetheless, to
further limit the risk of including non-target species, a minimum Sv threshold of
-50dB was applied to the EIG. This also served to reduce the amount of data input
into the algorithms, thereby improving the computational eﬃciency. This choice of
threshold was informed by a visual inspection of various thresholds on the EIG, with
the aim of tightly encompassing only apparent schools.

==PAGE:39==
5.2.2
Surface buﬀer
All acoustic data located within 15 m depth of the echosounder were excluded due
to possible origin from artiﬁcial turbulent eﬀects caused by ship operation and bad
weather. This value was based on visual examination of the EIG.
5.2.3
Seabed buﬀer
As outlined in (MacLennan et al., 2004), even a small inclusion of the seabed in echo-
integration can greatly positively bias the biomass estimate. Accordingly, all data
located within 2 m of the seabed (corrected) were excluded. Although in theory 1 m
would be adequate to remove this risk, 2 m was chosen as a precautionary approach.
5.3
The Clustering Algorithm: DBSCAN
Density-based spatial clustering of applications with noise (DBSCAN) is a density
based non-parametric clustering algorithm. DBSCAN is an intuitive and easy to use
unsupervised learning method which was awarded the test of time award in 2014.
Other popular clustering algorithms, such as K-Means and Expectation Maximi-
sation using Gaussian Mixture Models, require a priori knowledge of the number
of clusters. In this study, however, no assumption is made about the number of
potential schools in a given transect. Accordingly, DBSCAN requires only two pa-
rameters in order to label all points as either core points, border points, or noise.
These parameters are ϵ, which relates to the search radius, and MinimumPoints,
the minimum number of points that a cluster must contain.
A point is labelled a core point if it contains at least MinimumPoints within
its neighbourhood (ϵ). A core point and all points within its neighbourhood are
classiﬁed as one cluster.
Next, all points within a core points neighbourhood are considered, and if they
too contain at least MinimumPoints, then the cluster is extended to include all
new points within this neighbourhood.
If, however, a point does not possess the MinimumPoints, but contains at least
one core point in its radius, then it is classiﬁed as a border point. The result of this
algorithm is illustrated in ﬁg. 5.1, where the addition of the green point panel B
changes the cluster labels.
If a point contains neither the MinimumPoints or a core point within its neigh-
bourhood, then it is classiﬁed as noise.
In this way, the algorithm can identify
clusters with irregular shapes, such as is expected to be the case with schools.
The original DBSCAN function available in Matlab necessitates a spherical con-
straint on the epsilon parameter. Data were therefore normalised to values beween
0-1 across all 3 dimensions (Depth, Horizontal Distance and Volume Back-scattering
(Sv)).
Next, a method for automatic optimal ϵ estimation (available in Matlab) was
employed using k-nearest-neighbor for a range of k-values (i.e. k nearest neighbours).
For a given k, the algorithm computes the distance of the k-nearest points for all
points, before ranking these distances and storing them in a vector. Next, these
distances are plotted against the ranked positional index of the vector, and the
process is repeated for the remaining values of k. The resulting graph can then be

5.4
Applying DBSCAN
Due to data including several non-contiguous temporal leaps (from 1700 hours on one
day to 0600 hours the following day for instance) the EIG was broken into sections
that corresponded to the original transects. Although this improves the robustness
of school detection, edge eﬀects still occur, for instance at the beginning or end of
a transect or just before or after a block of removed pings. In such a scenario, only
a fraction of a school may be identiﬁed in a transect, thereby negatively biasing
estimates of certain features (e.g. size and total Sv) while misrepresenting spatial
descriptors (e.g. centroid location and circularity). No satisfactory solution to this
issue could be found, however, given the large number of identiﬁed schools and the
low risk of a school being aﬀected by an edge eﬀect, the impact of the problem was
deemed negligible.
Following the application of DBSCAN on the ﬁltered EIG, all points where then
labelled as either Noise (-1), or a unique cluster number to which they belonged
beginning from 1. These clusters of high density regions represent candidate schools
(N=4827).
5.5
School Characterisation
A preliminary polygon was deﬁned for each cluster using the convhull() function
in Matlab. This algorithm deﬁnes the smallest possible polygon using a subset of
points from the original set, with the limitation that all internal vertices are less
than 180 degrees (i.e. convex).
5.5.1
Extracting Shape Metrics
Due to the echo-integration returning cells of 2 m by 1 m, the preliminary polygon
needed to be corrected to account for this scale. This was considered crucial, as
the polygons were irregular, and therefore certain measurements could not just be
doubled.
The corrected polygon was then used to compute the area, perimeter, eccen-
tricity, circularity, max width, max height, maximum feret distance and max feret
orthogonal of each candidate school. Additionally, positional and bathymetric in-
formation was recorded such as depth of cluster, bottom depth at cluster and GPS
co-ordinates.

surface).
5.5.2
Extracting Echo Statistics
In order to characterise the identiﬁed schools in terms of their acoustic properties,
no correction was required to the original polygon. Instead, all values from the EIG
lying in or on the original polygon where used to compute maximum/minimum SV,
mean/median Sv and the total Sv in each candidate school.
5.5.3
Additions to the Characterisation Table
The ﬁnal characterisation table included all previously mentioned shape and acous-

7
Discussion And Conclusion
This study represents the ﬁrst analysis of Baltic Sprat school formation and dynam-
ics using an un-supervised clustering algorithm. The 4582 schools of Baltic sprat,
identiﬁed using data processing, a clustering algorithm and post-hoc ﬁltering, pro-
vided some interesting results. In this chapter, we will consider the ﬁndings under
the light of temporal and spatial dynamics, ecological constraints, and implications
for ﬁsheries management. We will also look at limitations of the current study and
future improvements.
7.1
School Dynamics in Space & Time
A strong temporo-spatial pattern was observed, with schools located furthest below
the computed halocline around midday. Conversely, the earliest and latest recorded
schools in this analysis (0600 and 1700 hours respectively, approximating dawn and
dusk at this latitude and time of year) both showed schools closer to the halocline.
This behavioral patterns matches the ”dawn ascent” and ”dusk descent” DVM com-
monly reported for marine species (Hays, 2003).
This pattern can be explained largely by the ecology of the sprat. Although
herring exhibit a very similar ecological niche with sprat, herring are reported to
shift to ﬁlter-feeding at high zoo-planktonic prey densities in addition to feeding on
benthic invertebrates (Bernreuther et al., 2013). On the other-hand, sprat are con-
sidered fully visual predators, preying primarily on Temora longicornis and Podon
spp. (Andersen et al., 2017). At this time of the year, the zooplankton biomass is
actively exhibiting DVM, as opposed to overwintering behaviour at greater depths,
and therefore sprat have an optimal crepuscular feeding window at dusk and dawn
when illumination is suﬃcient to permit visual predation (Andersen et al., 2017).
Sprats principle predator, cod, is also a visual predator, and therefore also expe-
riences a similar crepuscular feeding window. Cod stomach content analysis where
they overlap spatially and temporally with sprat show that no feeding is observed
during daylight hours (Nilsson et al., 2003) due to a combination of the low light at
these depths and the emerging predator-deterrence eﬀects of school formations.
The formation of schools is also evident in the temporal barplot (ﬁg. 6.4(a)),
showing an increasing number of schools detected in the morning, mirrored by a
decrease in the evening.
This suggests that sprat gradually form schools as the
light levels increase, while gradually dis-aggregating in the evening, a ﬁnding that is
backed up by previous research showing that sprat schools follow light levels, while
53
==PAGE:55==
their aggregation and dissolution can be approximately modeled by passive diﬀusion
(Nilsson et al., 2003).
Although changes in light intensity due to trophic interactions are considered
the main proximate driver for DVM, bio-energetic eﬃciency has also been suggested
to play a role (Wurtsbaugh and Neverman, 1988). By migrating into colder waters
above the halocline (see ﬁg. 4.1), sprat can reduce their metabolic costs. Further-
more, being a physostome ﬁsh, the sprat experience increased negative buoyancy
at greater depths as the swimbladder compresses (Solberg and Kaartvedt, 2014).
Faced with negative buoyancy, a ﬁsh can remain within a given depth range by by
tilted swimming (Strand et al., 2005), “rise and glide” swimming (as documented by
Solberg and Kaartvedt (2017)), or by increased swimming speeds (Dempster et al.,
2009). All of these compensatory behaviours are costly in terms of bio-energetics.
Consequently, by relocating to shallower depths, their negative buoyancy eﬀect
is reduced. However, these shallower depths in the Baltic are also considerably less
saline, thus the ultimate eﬀect on buoyancy from these two mechanisms is unclear.
Interestingly, ﬁgure 6.4(a) shows a local minimum of number of detected schools
at around midday (1100 hours), a time when sprat would be expected to be at
the deepest depths and most likely to be part of a school. This local minimum
could suggest the presence of larger schools and therefore the same biomass could be
accounted for by less total schools. This idea is backed up by a temporal examination
of the Nearest Neighbour distances, which shows a possible, though statistically
insigniﬁcant, maximum value at this hour, hinting at the possibility that smaller
schools aggregate to form larger schools. Nonetheless, if this theory were true, a
similar maximum for Total Sv, or equivalently, Sprat Abundance, should be observed
and this was not the case.
One explanation is that sprat schools migrate out of the basin during midday,
however no such migration is believed to occur at this temporal scale (Hoziosky et al.,
1989). Alternatively, it is possible that a number of sprat schools at this time of day
experienced increased predation, forcing them to push their vertical game to extreme
limits, for instance to within 2 meters of the seabed. In such a case, these schools
would be under-sampled and most likely negatively biased in nearly all metrics due
to the sea-bed exclusion zone necessitated by the process of echointegration. This
pattern of distinct schooling depths matches previous observations, where sprat
between 0600 and 0800 hours dwelled in a layer of marine snow (likely due to copepod
foraging) before moving to deeper layers (likely to minimise predation) (Neuenfeldt,
Personal Communication).
A further explanation, though highly un-intuitive, could be that some sprat
disaggregate at this hour, despite the high risk predation.
It is also important
to recognise that this pattern may also be simply an artefact of under-sampling.
Speciﬁcally, during the data collection, the ship was mobile and therefore the spatial
sampling was somewhat arbitrary with respect to the time of day. It is therefore
possible that areas of higher school density were under-sampled during these hours
throughout the course of the 10 days of acoustic data collection used in this study.
To summarise, the spatio-temporal pattern of daily school formation and vertical
migration highlighted in this study are likely driven by predator avoidance, foraging
success, reduction of metabolic costs and bio-energetic optimisation.

7.2
Habitat Volume Constraints
The Habitat Volume Map (ﬁg. 6.6) reveals a general habitat map for sprat in the
Baltic in which schools are constrained between the halocline (blue polygon) and an
increasing distance from the seabed beyond a certain depth (red polygon).
This depth limit could possibly be explained by a low oxygen saturation as it is
true that at these depths, the oxygen saturation is at a local minimum, and often
even a global minimum with respect to the rest of the water column. Nonetheless,
the bottom oxygen percentage saturation values never drop below 50%, translating
to minimum raw oxygen values over 5 mg l-1. This is 10 times the minimum oxygen
tolerance limit reported by Kaartvedt et al. (2009) who found that overwintering
sprat remained at layers with ∼7% saturation (0.5 ml l-1) at 7 oC. Consequently,
a lower oxygen limit does not seem to explain this habitat constraint, at least not
directly.
There is debate between the mechanisms of varying minimum oxygen tolerance
between species. For instance, Robb and Abrahams (2003) suggested that a ﬁshes
size was inversely related to its tolerance due to a greater relative gill area and larger
haemoglobin molecules in relation to its overall volume. On the other hand,Nilsson
and ¨Ostlund-Nilsson (2008) argued that the link between oxygen tolerance and scal-
ing of physiological functions was insigniﬁcant, suggesting instead that natural se-
lection was the driver behind hypoxia tolerance via extremophilic adaptations. In
either case, in-situ acoustic observations of Baltic cod in the Bornholm basin sug-
gests that cod can only remain at oxygen saturations below 20% for a limited time
(approximately 12 hours) Neuenfeldt et al. (2009). Therefore, it can be assumed
that, given the oxygen proﬁles coincident with this study, cod are not avoiding the
bottom depths. This assumption is backed up by a previous study in the Bornholm
basin which showed cod to present at all depths under the halocline under a simi-
lar oxygen regime (Schaber et al., 2009). Nonetheless, it is likely that under more
hypoxic conditions, the sprat could exploit these deeper layers as a predator-refuge.
The cause behind this depth from the seabed constraint on sprat school distribu-
tion therefore remains. It is unlikely that the higher bottom salinities would deter
the presence of sprat schools, as the maximum values are still far below the salinities
encountered by sprat schools in the nearby Skaggerak. Like salinity, temperature
also exhibits a maximum value at the bottom within the study area. It is therefore
possible that sprat schools avoid these depths in order to minimise metabolic costs.
Furthermore, as previously stated, by going deeper sprat will be required to spend
more energy in maintaining their vertical position.
Given that light intensity is already suﬃciently low to minimise cod predation,
it is possible that the beneﬁts of going deeper do not outweigh the bio-energetic and
metabolic costs of migrating further. A greater schooling depth would also increase
the negative buoyancy, forcing the schools to expend more energy performing ”rise
and glide” behaviour, as reported by Kaartvedt et al. (2009).
It is important to note that this bottom-zone is not a constant distance from the
seabed, but rather that it increases with depth beyond ∼70 m with an apparent
rate of 0.3 vertical meters per additional meter of sea-ﬂoor depth. It is therefore
possible that this threshold depth (∼70 m) coincides with a depth where sprat be-
come negatively buoyant. Assuming that they aim to stay oﬀthe seabed, perhaps
due to sharp bathymetric features, hostile invertebrates or leaking WWII ammuni-
55
==PAGE:57==
tion, a rise and glide behaviour might be required beyond 70 m. In such a case,
this depth-dependent behaviour might therefore cause a change in the probability
density of observing a school at these depths.
Although 8 schools were located in this bottom-zone, it is possible that these
schools were in fact herring feeding on zoo-benthos.
7.3
Implications for the Baltic Fisheries
This study has highlighted some important ecological interactions that depend on
local to regional environmental conditions. Notably, the variation in habitat volume
of sprat as a function of oxygen proﬁles and more importantly, the dynamic overlap
with cod.
The habitat volume map that resulted from this study constrains most sprat
schools between the halocline and some distance from the bottom which seems to
increase from approximately 70 m, hinting at an oxygen-driven predator-refuge as
the cause. This study only used data from a time when oxygen levels were high due
to a recent inﬂow event, and therefore the predator-refuge did not exist. Nonethe-
less, its potential usage during more typical Baltic conditions is revealed. Such a
mechanism has important implications for stock assessment which in turn informs
ﬁsheries management with the aim of attaining GES with respect to descriptors 3
and 4 of the MSFD.
Hypoxia is a growing phenomenon due to both changes in currents and anthro-
pogenic stressors such as eutrophication (Bendtsen and Hansen, 2013). Despite the
hopeful predictions that the Baltic Basins will reach GES with respect to nutrient
loading before 2200 (Murray et al., 2019), the frequency of inﬂows which ventilate
the Baltic sea have decreased in recent decades, however recent research reveals
no signiﬁcant long-term pattern, contradicting the hypothesis that climate change
caused a decrease in inﬂow frequency (Mohrholz, 2018). Accordingly, the future
oxygen regime of the Baltic sea remains unclear.
Although the emergence of a predator-refuge may exist due to diﬀerences in
minimal oxygen tolerance between sprat and cod, a similar oxygen driven predator
refuge may emerge for sprat prey (e.g. copepods) at even more hypoxic conditions.
Not only would severe hypoxia prevent sprat from feeding, but it would also ver-
tically compress its habitat leading to a possible increase in spatial overlap with
cod. Thus, the diﬀerent oxygen regimes can cause signiﬁcant changes in trophic
interactions.
Despite the Bornholm basin being relatively shallow (∼100 m) a sharp gradient
is observed in the vertical proﬁle, causing spatial habitat conditions to vary rapidly
at a ﬁne scale resolution.
A solid understanding of how the the dynamics and
magnitude of these trophic interactions aﬀect natural mortality is therefore critical
to the sustainable exploitation of the sprat ﬁshery through the Baltic MAP. It may
be feasible to tune the Natural Mortality used in stock assessment models based
on either qualitative descriptions of current oxygen regimes (1: High O2 = Higher
Mortality; 2: Low 02 = Lower Mortality; 3: Severe Hypoxia = Higher Mortality),
or perhaps even through a non-linear regression model.
Although this study did not reveal a temperature constraint on sprat school dis-
tribution, the Baltic sea represents one of the northernmost edges of its geographical
range (Haslob et al., 2012). Nonetheless, the Bornhom basin is part of the south-
56
==PAGE:58==
ern Baltic Sea and therefore temperature constraints may be more clearly evident
in sprat habitat further north. As a thermophilic species, sprat have been shown
to avoid temperatures below 5 oC (??). Consequently, if sea surface temperatures
decrease in future winter regimes, sprat may experience a new temperature con-
straint on their habitat volume. If this constraint surpasses the current halocline
constraint then the sprat will be limited to an increasingly narrow volume between
the cold surface and the hypoxic depths. As a consequence, spatial overlap with
prey and predators would change, in turn aﬀecting natural mortality, growth rates
and behaviour.
Such ecological knowledge combined with a predictive capacity could lead to
objectively deﬁned ﬁshery closures with explicit temporal and spatial resolution at
a ﬁne scale.
Furthermore, this oxygen driven temporo-spatial overlap between sprat and cod
could be expoited to decrease the bycatch of cod in the sprat ﬁshery, for example
through trawling within the predator refuge where cod are largely excluded from.
With regards to the cod ﬁshery, it could also be expected that at times when
the predator refuge is available, secondary production by cod would decrease due to
decreased prey availability. This too could be useful to increase the accuracy of the
cod stock assessment. Furthermore, the distribution of sprat in the water column
as a function of oxygen may also aﬀect the level of predation on cod eggs, thereby
aﬀecting cod recruitment dynamics. This magnitude and depth of this overlap will
also be aﬀected by changing salinity proﬁles which are concomitant with the reduced
inﬂow frequency.
Recalling the curious temporal pattern, in which total school biomass seemingly
decreased at midday, there is another possible explanation hugely relevant to stock
acoustic stock assessment. Orlowski (2005) showed that TS measurements of ﬁsh
could change by up to 400% throughout the course of the day. This instability in
TS was caused by changes in the orientation of individual ﬁsh. For instance, during
dawn descent, the ﬁsh, and consequently their swimbladders, are tilted thereby
modifying the backcattering cross-section. This eﬀect could partly explain why the
number of schools decreased, while no increase in school metrics was observed to
account for the lost biomass. Consideration of TS variability is therefore important
for accurate estimates of biomass based on acoustic surveys, and may require taking
into account time of day in addition to environmental conditions.
As sprat are physostomes, they regularly exhibit gulping behaviour at the surface
(Solberg and Kaartvedt, 2014). It is possible that this behaviour is restricted by
the presence of predators, in addition to being driven by salinity, temperature and
schooling depth. Accuracy of echointegration-based stock assessment could therefore
be improved with consideration of these drivers, all of which aﬀect the size of the
swimbladder.
7.4
Current Limitations
A number of related limitations arose through the use of Matecho. Both the speed of
sound and the absorption in water could not be set manually, but are instead derived
variables based on user-provided environmental parameters, namely temperature,
salinity and depth (in addition to the frequency for absorption).
In the case of
the speed of sound, the Mackenzie formula was implemented, while the Francois
57
==PAGE:59==
Garrison equation provided the absorption values. In both cases, no alternative
equation is provided. Unfortunately these equations are only accurate for a certain
range of salinities (25 PSU < S < 40 and 30 PSU < S < 35 PSU respectively), while
the salinities encountered in the Bornholm basin are low, ranging from ∼8 to ∼22
PSU. Annex A provides an evaluation of this discrepancy.
A further limitation of Matecho with respect to the peculiarities of the study area
is the inability to provide either sound speed or absorption as a vector for the water
column. Instead, one representative value is computed and applied to the entire
water column during digital processing of the acoustic data. Given the wide range
of salinities and temperatures, these two measurements exhibit high variability over
short vertical scales compared to a typical marine proﬁle. Such deviations from
the known true values will cause the range of some objects to be miscalculated, in
addition to the reported Sv being distorted.
A bias for identiﬁcation of schools at increasing depth is probable in this study
due to the expanding acoustic beam as it travels away from the transducer. Although
the process of echointegration can account for this when integrating wide layers, in
this study the aim was to ﬁrst identify schools based at a ﬁne spatial resolution.
Given a beam angle of 7 o, the diameter of the eﬀective beam would be 3.7 m at a
range of 30m, while the diameter would be 11.1 m at a range of 90m. Consequently,
the probability of insoniﬁcation of entire or partial schools located further away from
the central axis would increase with depth, leading to possible bias.
The results of this study highlight how sprat use their habitat, however it is
important to note that these results only consider the behaviour and ecology of
sprat in schools. Fine scale DVM dynamics and use of habitat at the individual
level is not analysed, despite the persistent existence of apparent individual sprat
outside of schools during the regular schooling hours.
The presence of herring in this analysis could be problematic, potentially provid-
ing false information on sprat schooling behaviour. Nonetheless, the low percentage
of herring in catches compared to sprat, in addition to their similar biology and
ecology, limits the impact of these wrongly classiﬁed data.
7.5
Future Improvements and Research Gaps
The time-frame of this study was limited due to available resources during the
CoViD-19 pandemic, principally the required use of an aging laptop with no cooling
fan. The computational load for the acoustic data processing and echointegration
in Matecho are considerably demanding, leading to several hardware failures and
data-corruption. Consequently, only data from one year were analysed. Ideally,
this study could be repeated using all available data from multiple years, thereby
improving the reliability of the results, while temporal dynamics could be further
elucidated by including data from all hours of the day.
Nonetheless, the ﬁndings of this study suggest the existence and exploitation
of a predator refuge for sprat in oxygen-poor layers.
This hypothesis could be
tested by using acoustic data from other years to develop Habitat Volume Maps of
sprat for both well ventilated and hypoxic regimes. These could then be compared
retrospectively with the modelled SSB and natural mortality from stock assessment
models for that year.
The choice of ϵ values in this study were eﬀective at successfully identifying and

encompassing schools of sprat at the resolution provided by the echointegration.
Nonetheless, the decision was based on manual examination and trial-and-error. An
automatic optimal ϵ estimator would therefore be more objective and perhaps more
eﬀective. Two methods are currently popular for estimating epsilon, however their
application is limited to cases were epsilon is one-dimensional (i.e. the same search
radius is applied across all dimensions). This gap could therefore be explored in
future research, permitting a multi-dimensional epsilon estimator to be developed.
7.6
Conclusion
Acoustic surveys are a critical tool, both presently and in the future, for ﬁsheries
management where principle aims include sustainable harvest at proﬁtable levels
such as MSY, reduced by-catch and minimal damage to habitats. However, de-
spite its role in traditional single species stock assessments by means of providing
abundance estimates, there is mounting pressure towards the adoption of Ecosystem-
based Fishery Management where the broader eﬀects of ﬁsheries on the ecosystem
are assessed in a holistic manner (Pikitch et al., 2004). This task requires in-depth
knowledge of predation, competition and foraging dynamics of the exploited species,
in addition to a solid understanding of habitat use. Furthermore, it is clear that
these relationships and patterns are not static in time, instead being driven by
environmental dynamics which exhibit short-term cyclical patterns on a daily and
annual basis such as DVM and seasons, medium-term cyclical patterns such as NAO
and El Nino, and long-term changes due to anthropogenic stressors including cli-
mate change whose impact is not yet fully understood.
Successful management
must therefore incorporate environmental data into the models. This is especially
true for the Baltic Sea, where the rate of inﬂows and run-oﬀdictates the salinity
and oxygen levels (MacKenzie et al., 2007). Even disregarding temporal changes,
ecosystem interactions in the Baltic sea exhibit changes on very ﬁne spatial scales
driven by rapidly changing ambient hydrography.
Accordingly, the results of this study highlight the eﬀectiveness of acoustics as a
tool for non-intrusive in-situ analysis of sprat school distribution at a ﬁne spatial and
temporal scale, while the coupling with CTD, satellite and biological data permitted
an exploration of their ecology and interactions with the ecosystem. Particularly,
this thesis has provided a deeper insight into sprat schooling behaviour and habitat
use, in addition to predator avoidance strategies and response to hydrographical
features such as salinity and hypoxia. Additionally, by identifying and characterising
schools from acoustic data with respect to temporal dynamics (e.g. DVM), more
accurate abundance estimates can be computed by taking into account the ﬁsh’s
current behaviour, such as vertical migration which modiﬁes the TS due to changes
in tilt.
This study has successfully improved our knowledge of spatio-temporal explicit
descriptions of medium-scale ﬁsh distributions and their structuring function in
species interactions, speciﬁcally sprat-zooplankton and cod-sprat. These ﬁndings
are directly informative to the current sprat ﬁshery in the Baltic (e.g. highlighting
areas where cod-bycatch could be minimised; Behavioural modulation of TS; quan-
titative relationship between environmental regimes and Natural Mortality), and
could furthermore inform a similar study on other important commercial species.

Appendix A
Acoustic Data Pre-Processing
A.1
Environmental Parameters
The Baltic sea, being mediterranean, receives signiﬁcant freshwater run-oﬀ, and is
only connected to the open ocean through a narrow strait. It is through this strait
that episodic inﬂows of warm, salty and oxygen rich water occur.
Consequently, for much of the water column, the salinity falls out of the validated
range for both the Mac Kenzie Equation for speed of sound in water (1981) and the
Francois-Garrison equation for sound absorption in water (1982). Unfortunately,
Matecho forces the use of these equations during acoustic data processing despite
the existence of accurate alternative formulas at these environmental states, namely
Medwin (1975) and Ainslie & Mc Colm (1998) for sound speed and absorption
respectively. Accordingly, ﬁg. A.1 reports the diﬀerences in magnitude and direction
between these formulas across a range of salinities for 3 discrete temperatures. The
range of temperature and salinities chosen for this comparison are representative of
the Baltic Sea at this time of year and are therefore appropriate for the purpose.
With respect to the speed of sound, there is very little diﬀerence between the
2 methods over the full range of salinities and temperatures. In all cases, the Mac
Kenzie method underestimates the speed. A maximum diﬀerence of 0.240 m s−1
occurs at a salinity of 20 PSU at 3 oC, while the mean diﬀerence is 0.197 (sd =
0.0346). Converting this to discrepancies in terms of the studies acoustic accuracy,
this maximum diﬀerence in speed, at the maximum depth range of this study area
( 100m), would result in a diﬀerence in range estimate of 1.7 cm (based on time
delay of 0.14 seconds and speed of 1440 m s−1). For the purposes of this study, this
level of discrepancy is considered acceptable.
With respect to the absorption, there is also very little diﬀerence between the 2
methods over the full range of salinities and temperatures (frequency was maintained
at 38kHz). In all cases, the Francois-Garrison method underestimates the actual
absorption.
A maximum diﬀerence of 0.1370 dB m−1 occurs at a salinity of 20
PSU at 9 oC, while the mean diﬀerence is 0.068 (sd = 0.0419). Converting this to
discrepancies in terms of the studies acoustic accuracy, this maximum diﬀerence in
absorption, at the maximum depth range of this study area ( 100m), would result in
a diﬀerence in TS of 0.027dB. For the purposes of this study, this level of discrepancy
is considered acceptable.

Unlike commercial software, freeware, such as Matecho, is openly available to share
and modify. In some cases, given the open-access nature, online communities can
oﬀer vast resources of unoﬃcial support and even bug ﬁnding and ﬁxing. Nonethe-

provide long term stability and robust execution.

the Fishing Industry and Research. Matecho is much more recent, released in 2018
(c.f. 1995 for EchoView). Its robustness and accuracy is therefore not guaranteed.
