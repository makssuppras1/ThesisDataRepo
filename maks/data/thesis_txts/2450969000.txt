Abstract
This thesis serves as a basic introduction to Lorentzian Finsler Geometry and Finsler
spacetimes.
We introduce some basic notions and results of Finsler geometry and their exten-
sion to Lorentz Finsler geometry. In particular we define the conic pseudo Minkowski
norms which allow us to define the Lorentz-Minkowski norm analogously to notions
in classical Lorentzian geometry.
In the scope of Lorentz-Minkowski norms we define Lorentz-Finsler metrics, both
as a natural extension of Finsler metrics and in a way which allows for the inclusion
of a broader range of Finsler spacetimes. We further define cone and causality no-
tions associated with a Finsler spacetimes. Lastly we define the length structure for
a general Finsler spacetimes and derive the equations that govern the geodesics in
a variational setting. Moreover we extend the notion of product type spacetimes by
introducing warped Finsler products and the warped product geodesics.
As first result we show that the lightlike geodesics of a standard static Finsler
spacetimes minimizes arrival time and as such that Fermat’s principle holds for such
spacetimes. Furthermore we have shown how to characterize the lightlike geodesics
for the Friedmann-Robertson-Walker Finsler spacetimes and some more general space-
times. We further show how these results can be used, on the basis of a generalized
Fermat’ principle for Finsler spacetimes due to V. Perlick[26], to solve, in particular,
the time-dependent Zermelo navigation problem.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
The aim of this thesis is fourfold, firstly we want to define the underlying mathematics
of Finsler geometry which form the basis of our study of Lorentz-Finsler geometry. As
such we want to define the basic concepts of Finsler geometry such as the Minkowski
norm, the Finsler metric and some of their basic properties. As a natural extension
we introduce the notion of geodesics in a Finsler setting, we do this without introduc-
ing a connection in the sense that we define geodesics through the method of the first
variation formula (A equivalent notion of geodesics emerges through the introduction
of a connection e.g. the Chern connection).
Secondly, we aim to extend these notions into the realm of Lorentz-Finsler geome-
try, and more importantly we will highlight the fundamental differences this extension
imposes. This entails the extension of the Minkowski norm to the Lorentz-Minkowski
norm, which is a Conic pseudo-Minkowski norm (also to be defined) with an asso-
ciated fundamental tensor of Lorentzian index. We will in this context develop the
basic properties of the Conic pseudo-Minkowski norm and Lorentz-Minkowski norm,
in particular we point out how these differ from the classical Minkowski norm.
Thirdly we develop the notion of cones and cones structures of vector spaces
and manifolds respectively. In this setting we extend the classical notions of causality
from Lorentzian geometry to equivalent notions in the realm of cone structures. From
this more general notion of causality we introduce, among other, the notion of cone
geodesics. With the two previously mentioned constructions, the Lorentz-Minkowski
norm and cone structures, we define the Lorentz-Finsler metric in accordance with
M. A. Javaloys and M. Sánches in [11] and in turn Finsler spacetimes. In the realm
of cone structures and Finsler metrics the following theorem emerges;
Theorem 1.1. Let C be a cone structure and γ : I ∈M a curve. Then the following
properties are equivalent:
(i) γ is a cone geodesic of C.
(ii) γ is a lightlike pregeodesic for one (and then for all) Lorentz-Finsler metric L
with cone C.

CHAPTER2
Preliminary Mathematical concepts
In this Chapter we will introduce some of the basic notions and results from Finsler
geometry as well as their extensions which allows for the definition of Lorentz-Finsler
geometry. Most of the results given in this chapter will not play a pivotal role in
the main result of this thesis, however they are included to showcase that some basic
results from Lorentz geometry carries over to Lorentz-Finsler geometry and that we,
in some aspects, can view Lorentz-Finsler geometry as a generalization of Lorentz
geometry in the same way that Finsler geometry generalizes Riemannian geometry.
2.1
The Minkowski Norm
We start by defining the very foundation of Finsler geometry, namely the Minkowski
norm, which is a extension of the classical understanding of a norm. We do so by,
firstly, introducing the classical notion of a Minkowski norm, however such a norm
turns out to be too restrictive in the scope of Lorentz-Finsler geometry and as such
we will weaken the notion some what.

As usual we denote by gv the fundamental tensor in the direction of y and call
the pair (V, F) a Minkowski space.
In our pursuit of the development of Lorentz-Finsler geometry we need to slightly
weaken the notion of a Minkowski norm. The two natural consideration are as follows;
the positive definiteness of gv can be weakened to simply nondegenerate and the
domain of F could be restricted to a convex conic subset. Clearly when we weaken
the notion of gv being positive definite we, in turn, also move away from the notion of
the unit sphere or indicatrix being convex. As a consequence F may no longer satisfy
the triangle inequality, and thus fail to be a norm in the classical sense. However this
extension is natural in the setting of Lorentzian geometry where we do not expect
the indicatrix to be convex, indeed we would expect it to be concave. Furthermore
the restriction of the norm to a conic subset becomes a natural one in the scope of
Lorentzian geometry, i.e. when constricting F to a conic subset we ensure that e.g.
F > 0 in this region. Clearly in classical Lorentzian geometry we do not expect that
F > 0 on the entire space, in fact it is one of the defining features of a Lorentzian
norm that it is not. This weakening of the Minkowski norm leads to the definition of
a Conic pseudo-Minkowski norm.

Fermat’s Principle
Will will here take a step away from generality and towards a special class of Finsler
spacetimes that will help us derive and exemplify Fermat’s principle. Classically Fer-
mat’s principle can be formulated as follows:
”Among all lightlike curves connecting some event p with some timelike curve γ,
the geodesics are characterised by stationary arrival time”[26].
Here arrival times refers to an arbitrary parametrization of the timelike curve.
Usually in this context the underlying spacetimes are assumed to be stationary, i.e.
that the spacetime admits a timelike killing vector field. However it has been shown
that Fermat’s principle holds for non-static spacetimes first introduced by I. Kovner
[14] and later proved by V. Perlick [27].
4.1
Standard Static Finsler Spacetimes
We will here show how Fermat’s principle can be carried over to Finsler spacetimes by,
firstly, considering a class of static Finsler spacetimes called knowns as Standard Static
Finsler spacetimes, here based on the work of Caponio et al. [7, 6], see also [17] for a
somewhat different approach to standard static Finsler spacetimes (here just called
static). In the work of Caponio et al. [7] they set out to derive the Finslarian version of
the classical standard static spacetime, and extend this notion to standard stationary
spacetimes in [6]. In both references a hint to Fermat’s principle is included and in
fact it has been shown that Fermat’s principle holds for general Finsler spacetimes,
in the following sense
Theorem 4.1 (Theorem 4.2 [26]). A curve γ ∈Cq,α is an a arbitrarily parametrized
geodesic if and only if if it minimizes arrival time i.e.
that the first variation of
arrival time δτ(γ) = 0 for every allowed variation of γ in Cq,α.

Zermelo navigation problem
In this section we will show how we can use the notion of Finsler spacetimes inorder to
solve the Zermelo navigation problem. Namely that the upshot of Fermat’ principle
by the approach of V. Perlick in [26], i.e. Theorem 4.1, is that we can use is as a
way to solve both the time-independent Zermelo navigation problem as well as the
time-dependent Zermelo navigation problem.
Recall here that the Zermelo navigation problem aims to find the paths of shortest
travel time of objects whose maximum speed at each point depends on the point and
the direction of its velocities. We can think of it in the following sense; we consider the
trajectories of a ship sailing in an Riemannian landscape (M, h) under the influence
of some external force W, e.g. wind or current. Here h is a Riemannian metric and
W is a vector field on M. We further have the constrain that the strength of the wind
cannot exceed the power of the ships engine, i.e. |W| < |u| = 1. Zermelo’s navigation
can by exemplified through the scope of wildfire spread as described in [19] and [20]
respectively.
Thus the goal is to find curves that minimizes travel time while moving at max-
imum speed.
The way we solve this is to translate the problem in to a Finsler
spacetime setting. Thus moving at maximum speed then corresponds to only consid-
ering lightlike curves in the spacetime, furthermore given that we want these curves
to minimize travel time Fermat’s principle tells us that we ought to be looking for
lightlike geodesics.
In the case of the time independent Zermelo navigation problem it has been shown
that there are a correspondence between the classical stationary spacetimes M = R×S
and Randers metrics on S, i.e spacetimes endowed with the Lorentzian metric

as the pregeodesics of the so called Fermat metric, which is a Randers metric.

6.1
Constructing Spacetimes
When constructing a Lorentz-Finsler spacetime as defined in Definition 3.2 there is
a choice of cone, i.e. one have to determine a cone C and when such a cone is found
one have to determine weather it should constitute the future-pointing cone or the
past-pointing cone. When such a choice have been made, automatically then, the
past- (or indeed the future) pointing cone is defined as C−= −C+ where C+ denotes
the future-pointing cone. It is clear that we are forced to choose the past-pointing
cone as the negative of the future-pointing cone if we want to have nice properties, for
instance consider a curve γ, then we say that γ is timelike if g ˙γ(˙γ, ˙γ) = Lγ(˙γ) < 0, but
in order to transverse the curve in both directions we need for Lγ(˙γ) = αLγ(−˙γ) and
thus C−= −C+. However when defining these two cones, there are another choice,
namely the choice of which metric should govern these cones. In most of the work
done in Finsler spacetimes, from this point of view, the authors only concern them
self with the future-pointing cones, this restriction will suffice for a vast number of
aspects in Lorentz-Finsler geometry, see [1, 10, 11, 12, 23] .
However other authors does indeed consider both the past and future pointing
lightcone, see [26, 9]. However these approaches build on the definition of a Finsler
spacetime first introduces by Been. In Beems definition of a Finsler spacetimes (or
indefinite Finsler metrics) he considers not only the past and future pointing light-
cone, but indeed the entire space i.e. causal vectors as well as spacelike vectors, see
[4]. In Beems definitions, one issue is that, in general the the causal cones could be
picked from the connected component of C = {v ∈TM \{0}|L(v) < 0} in such a way

that the past and future pointing cones are unrelated1, and as such, Beem requires,
in some cases, that L(v) = L(−v).
The very defining property of Finsler geometry is the innate property of non-
reversibility. In the literature regarding Lorentz-Finsler spacetimes this property is
often only inherited to spacelike slices. However it is indeed the authors belief that
this property is justifiably extended to timelike directions as well2 which could lead
to some interesting phenomenons.

ways in which we could introduce a Finslarian structure in timelike directions3.
From classical Lorentzian geometry it is known that the cone structure determines
the metric of the underlying spacetime up to some conformal factor [13, Section 2.2].
A similar characterization can be formulated in scope of Lorentz-Finsler spacetimes.

Gravitational waves in FRW-Finsler Spacetimes
Given that most of the results obtained in our development of warped products and

classical results, it seems reasonable that further results would follow classical results
analogously and as such some further extensions FRW-Finsler spacetimes seems war-
ranted.
One obvious result related to FRW spacetimes that spring to mind is the develop-
ment and effects of gravitational waves. Thus a natural extension would be to develop
the notion of gravitational waves waves in a FRW-Finsler spacetimes and determine
how these deform the ambient space. This could be done by determining how nearby
geodesics behave under the influence of gravitational waves through the examination
of the Jacobi equations analogously to the approach taking in [18], i.e. through a first
order perturbation.
Clearly when considering our definition of FRW-Finsler spacetimes and the ap-
proach taken in [18] we surely lack the second order behaviour needed to deal with
gravitational waves, e.g. curvature ect. In particular we need a way of describing the
Jacobi equation in a Finsler setting and for this we would need the Riemann tenser
which in turn rely on the Chern connection[8, Chapter 2]. Thus to proceed we would
