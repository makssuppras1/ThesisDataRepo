Abstract 
The Lublin Lower Palaeozoic shale is analysed in terms of reservoir properties. Samples from 
two depth levels of 2691.33 m and 2697.45 m are used for experimental testing. The Helium 
Porosimetry, Nuclear Magnetic Resonance, Mercury Intrusion, Inductively Coupled Plasma 
– Optical Emission Spectrometry, consolidation at Constant Rate of Strain and X-ray 
Diffraction tests are done to characterize the shale. Measured data are compared with 
modelling approaches developed in this thesis or in the appropriate literature. An analysis of 
the mineralogical composition shows a large quartz content, equal to 48%, medium to high 
clay content - 36% and non-clay minerals of 16%. The porosity is measured and modelled to 
be around 3-5% in the entire depth interval. The water saturation decreases to around 50% in 
the Lower Ludlow and Wenlock formations. The shale permeability is low and does not 
exceed 100 nD. The formation shows good fracturing properties with static Young’s 
Modulus of 18.52 GPa and a Poisson’s ratio of 0.23. The brittleness index is around 60% 
while closure stress decreases at the depth of more brittle rocks. The Lublin shale shows 
moderate reservoir properties because of its low porosity, low permeability, low thermal 
maturity of the organic matter and complex geology. On the other hand, the positive factors 
are TOC (2.12%), brittleness, pore sizes and occurrence of natural fractures. 
Key words: Lublin shale, shale gas, permeability, elasticity, reservoir properties

1. Introduction 
Possibilities of unconventional shale gas exploration and production in Poland have recently 
been a subject of scientific, engineering and economic analyses. Polish shales have drawn the 
special attention of researchers, companies and politicians domestically and have become an 
interesting investment opportunity for world-wide international companies. Due to 
economic and political stability of the country, large gas demand on the Polish market, fully-
developed gas pipelines, relatively high local gas prices and clear and tolerable tax system, 
Polish shale gas play has become one of the most attractive European exploration fields [1].  
There have been reports estimating technically recoverable onshore shale gas resources in 
Poland. The Polish Geological Institute reports 8-22 Tcf, the United States Geological Survey 
estimation is 1.345 Tcf [2] while the United States Energy Information Administration gives 
an estimate of 187 Tcf [3]. This is a very wide span, however, all reports agree on the 
presence of a large amount of shale gas in Poland. Moreover, they indicate the same regions 
as the most perspective: the Baltic Basin, the Podlasie Basin and the Lublin Basin. 
The Lublin Basin has been carefully analysed by the Polish company Orlen Upstream which 
possesses several exploration licenses in this region. The potential shale gas reservoir is 
located in the Palaeozoic Silurian lower Ludlow, Wenlock and upper Llandlovery interval. 
Its reservoir properties have been partially described in the reports [2,4,5,6], however, they 
are rather estimated and there is a lack of a comprehensive study gathering and describing 
all important data for potential fracturing and production works, such as: mineralogy, 
porosity, gas saturation, pore sizes, total organic content, organic matter maturity, 
permeability, elasticity and brittleness. 
Mr. Wiesław Prugar, the CEO of Orlen Upstream, in one of his articles [7] points that 
geomechanical properties and concentration of the organic carbon in shales must be well 
understood before a development phase is achieved. He makes a statement that There is an 
obvious need for both new high-quality subsurface data and integrated multidisciplinary conceptual 
models for predicting the occurrence of resource-quality shales within this continuous though 
remarkably heterogeneous reservoir.   
This thesis provides such a research and presents petrophysical models based on the logs 
and interdisciplinary laboratory experiments performed on the plugs from the Wenlock core. 
Experimental procedures and data processing are carefully explained and results are 
compared with data published in reports. The models for the Lublin shale usually consider 
accuracy of estimation marking different cases regarding reservoir properties. Very often, 
more than only one approach are pursued and discrepancies are commented.

2. Geological setting 
The Lower Palaeozoic shale samples considered in this thesis come from the Lower Silurian 
– Upper Ordovician formation in the Lublin sedimentary basin. This region has recently 
been widely investigated as a potential hydrocarbon shale reservoir [3,5,7]. The samples 
were cut from a core at measured depth: 2691.33 and 2697.45 meters (2690.44 m, 2696.54 m 
true vertical depth, respectively). 
The Lublin Basin is located on a large syncline oriented NW-SE which spreads from 
Denmark to Lviv in Western Ukraine (Fig. 1). The region emerges in the South-East 
direction. The Lublin Basin is limited by the East European Craton (EEC) on the North-East 
and by the Trans-European Suture Zone (TESZ) on the South-West. It mainly consists of 
Palaeozoic forms with a large thickness. The Northeastern part of the basin is flattened and 
stable whereas the Southwestern part is steep and tectonic fluctuations are more frequent. 
The region is characterized by numerous dislocations and angular unconformities [5,9]. An 
example of complexity of the Palaeozoic structure in the basin can be the fact that 
Carboniferous forms can overlay different Devonian levels. 
 
Figure 1. Location of Lower Palaeozoic sedimentary basins in Poland (from Poprawa 2010 [57]). 
Shale sediments were deposited in the basins developed in Early Palaeozoic on the Western 
slope of EEC. The basins were divided into the Baltic Basin, Podlasie subsidence and the 
Lublin Basin due to tectonic and erosive processes [5]. Clastic marine deposition occurred in 
==PAGE:20==
4 
 
the Neoproterozoic. Another shallow deposition took place in Cambrian and sandstones 
from that period performed as source rocks for Lower Palaeozoic rocks in the Western EEC 
basins. The period from the Late-Ordovician to the Late-Silurian was the time when the 
subsidence of sedimentary basins developed due to flexural bending of EEC. This bending 
was a result of the Caledonian collision with Avalonia and Baltica which also caused an 
abundant deposition of detrital material [7]. The Lublin region experienced a continuous 
transition from Silurian to Early-Devonian sedimentation. The reason for this is that the 
erosion of Lower-Palaeozoic forms took place only in the areas where Cambrian uplift 
caused a landslip of the Upper-Palaeozoic overburden. The Middle-Ordovician 
sedimentation of carbonate and minor clastic material was followed by a diachronous 
reversion to silty-mudstone deposition. Silurian mudrocks originate from the Caledonian 
foredeep infill as a result of aforementioned sloped collision [10]. 
The lithology of the Palaeozoic formations at the location where the well has been drilled are 
as following: 
- 
Cambrian formations are fine to middle grained sandstones with a thinner bed of 
siltstones and claystones 
- 
Ordovician sediments are irregular and consist of two calcareous levels separated by 
tens meter claystone layer. The Ordovician spreads from Tremadocian to Ashgill.  
- 
Silurian is represented by Llandlovery, Wenlock, Ludlow and, the thickest, Pridoli 
claystones and siltstones with some interbeddings of marls and sandstones. Its total 
thickness is around 1300 meters. Silurian succession formations are rich in graptolites 
[11].  
- 
The Lower Devonian is a regressive sequence, the Middle part is transgressive and 
the Upper is regressive again. Clastic rocks mainly sandstones, claystones and 
siltstones are present. Frasnian and Lower Famennian parts are calcareous. 
- 
Carboniferous is clastic and the thickness is around 700 meters although it varies 
within the Lublin Basin. There are two types of clayey and silty sediments. The first 
type is black, dark-grey mudstone. The second type is grey and very bright fine-
grained sandstone rich in vegetable detritus. 
- 
There are no Permian or Triassic sediments in the area of the borehole.

3.1 Consolidation at Constant Rate of Strain 
Consolidation at Constant Rate of Strain (CRS) is an oedometer test in which the vertical 
compression strain is controlled.. This method gives very similar results to the typical 
incremental loading test but it takes considerably less time [12]. Moreover, it is also possible 
to determine hydraulic permeability of a tested sample. The test is not difficult to understand 
and perform, however, there are some difficulties with selecting right starting and ongoing 
parameters [13].

An oedometer diameter was measured to 37.62 mm. There is a difference in the size of the 
sample and mantle diameters. This gap is crucial for permeability measurements which 
assumes that water flows only through the sample. If the sample is smaller and there is a 
gap, there will be a leakage around the sample which highly overestimate permeability. In 
order to limit this effect three methods were tried: 
- 
application of additional grease to the mantle wall to fill the gap between the sample 
and the mantle 
 
-

-

In the first test, only first two methods were tried because the last might have led to a large 
inaccuracy of the results as aluminum can take over the vertical load underestimating static 
compressibility. As these two methods did not eventually succeed the last method was tried 
for a bigger sample B. However, it was not possible to squeeze the sample with an additional 
stripe into the oedometer mantle, therefore, it was believed that horizontal deformation 
would be sufficient at high stresses. The analysis of the result is presented in chapter 
Petrophysical Description. 
Permeability measurement requires full saturation of a sample. Initial state of saturation was 
not known, therefore, NMR measurement was done on two samples. It showed that there 
was some amount of water in the samples, however, it could not be quantified as porosity 
was not known at that time. The salt content was not known either. Cleaning of the samples 
of such a big size is very difficult, time consuming and, in the end, might be inefficient. 
Therefore, it was decided to saturate the samples at their initial state without cleaning. 
Saturation of the shale sample could not be done in a desiccator at vacuum conditions 
because if there was no confining stress, there would be a risk of sample failure due to 
swelling clay. Therefore, the sample was intended to be saturated during the triaxial test 
when the vertical and confining stresses were applied to reduce that risk. The sequence of 
operations of pushing the water through the sample when opening the bottom valve and 
detaching the top inletting tubing opening the outlet release from the hydraulic system (a 
vacuum pump was later replaced by an open water tank) was done to saturate the sample. In 
the tests, previously de-aired water was used. In order to check if the entire experimental set-
up works and is properly sealed a trial test was run on a sandstone sample. Having checked 
this the actual tests were run.

material. However, it is important to highlight that these suggestions concerned soils which 
are usually characterized by larger porosity and higher permeability than such tight 
materials as the Lublin Wenlock shale. The effective stresses higher than the 
preconsolidation stress would be hardly obtainable using the available equipment.

3.10 Total Organic Carbon content 
Total organic carbon content (TOC) is measured on a crushed, cleaned and dried sample. 
The powder is treated with hydrochloric acid (HCl) to decompose all inorganic carbon. It is 
important to note that HCl does not react with siderite [22]. However, from XRD analysis it 
is known that there is no siderite in the Lublin shale and, therefore, the result should not be 
overestimated. Afterwards, the sample was dried in an oven at 60℃ over night. Then, 
catalysts were added and the sample was burned. Flue gas was analysed and the result gave 
a percentage of organic carbon in a defined mass of the material. This procedure was 
repeated 4 times and the average was taken.

4. Petrophysical description 
 
This chapter provides a description of petrophysical properties of the Lower Palaeozoic 
Silurian shale in the Lublin sedimentary basin. A very thorough analysis is needed as there 
are several features and requirements which must be fulfilled in order to develop a proper 
and economically feasible exploration process. Kiernowski and Dyrka (2013) [4] indicated the 
most important factors which are essential for gas accumulation in shale. Using 
petrophysical logs and laboratory measurements the following characteristics are evolved in 
this chapter:

Resistivity log measures formation electrical resistivity. There were two tools used in 
borehole logging at the location considered in this thesis: Induction log and Microlog. 
Induction log can measure resistivity even up to 5.5 meters depth [23]. Brine is a good 
electrical conductor, therefore, low readings are found for brine saturated porous formations. 
However, resistivity increases with gas saturation, fluid overpressure and organic matter 
maturation. There are no characteristic limits for resistivity in shales – a range 0.5-1000 Ωm is 
given in the literature [6]. Sonic log shows the slowness of acoustic waves in the rock. 
Slowness is lower in denser, solid formations but is higher for porous or clayey mudstones. It 
is very sensitive to even small changes in texture, quartz and organic matter content. 
Logs describe four Silurian epochs: Pridoli, Ludlow, Wenlock and Llandlovery. Shale is 
present in the entire Lower Silurian interval and is represented by: 
- 
relatively low slowness 
- 
micro and deep resistivity close to each other which shows rather impermeable 
formations 
- 
large positive separation of bulk density and neutron porosity logs 
- 
high bulk density 
- 
high neutron porosity which indicates large water content; however, low slowness 
indicates that this high reading is probably due to bound or clay water. 
 
Unstable deep resistivity and frequent variations in the sonic log show the complexity of the 
Lublin Basin lithology which is rich in fractures and joints. The heterogeneity of the 
formation is seen at 2230-2250 m, 2280-2300 m, 2350-2370 m, 2400-2410 m, 2540-2560 m. 
These intervals are marls or dolomite-calcite mudstones which has been found characteristic 
for Silurian in the Lublin Basin [24]. The separation of resistivity logs can indicate invasion of 
the drilling Water Based Mud into the formation, however, high bulk density indicates 
rather tight, hardly permeable formation.  
There are general trends apparent throughout these epochs. Slowness and bulk density 
decrease while resistivity increases with depth. It can indicate developed compaction and 
possibly higher organic matter content in lower parts of the Silurian rock. Lower Ludlow and 
Wenlock are beyond the predominant trends and show lower slowness and higher resistivity 
comparing to the Pridoli interval. Slowness drops from 75 µs/ft to 67 µs/ft and deep 
resistivity increases from 11 Ωm to 100 Ωm.  Bulk density further decreases to 2.60 g/cm3. It 
all can be a trace of kerogen occurrence [6] or at least more organic matter in 
Ludlow/Wenlock which would support information reported by the Polish Geological 
Institute [25]. Neutron porosity also decreases significantly which can be identified as more 
silt or quartz mineralogy, less clayey composition and gas saturation. Thereby, interval of 
2610 m to 2750 m can be perceived as a potential shale gas reservoir. The trends seem to be 
reversed in Wenlock and the gas zone ends in Llandlovery. This section is not homogenous

4.2 Depth, thickness & pore pressure 
Depth of potentially productive shales is important as deep deposits are exposed to 
mechanical and chemical compaction [4]. Mechanical compaction can create natural fractures 
which can ease the flow of hydrocarbons during a production process. Chemical compaction 
occurs at high overburden stress and high formation temperature. It is a result of the clay 
diagenesis process in which smectite and kaolinite can transform into illite (swelling clay 
becomes less swelling). The temperature in which clay becomes unstable and starts to 
transform is reported to be above 71℃ [27, 28]. In general, swelling clay is not desirable in 
the field exploration as it plugs pore throats, reduces formation permeability and porosity 
[29]. It can also generate issues during drilling. However, the process depends on many 
factors and the temperature above 71℃ can be perceived as only one of advantageous for this 
process to happen. More detailed analysis and information would be needed to describe the 
process for this particular region.

mineralogy. However, there are some estimation provided by Chew in AAPG bulletin [35].

4.4 Porosity 
Porosity is important as it gives the fraction of a non-solid space in the formation. This space 
is occupied by brine, oil or gas. Therefore, high porosity indicates higher porous fluid 
content. The larger porosity the more positive reservoir assessment. Porosity of shales is 
usually low, often in the range of 3-10% [33]. Yang and Aplin (2014) [38] analyzed properties 
of 30 natural mudstones and found their porosity to be, generally, in the range of 5-13% with 
some exceptions when this range was exceeded. Effective porosity in the Lublin shale is 
reported to 4.7% [7]. The Lublin shale is investigated, here, using available logs and core 
samples. Porosity logs are derived from the combination of resistivity/bulk density logs and 
a sonic log. Core porosity was measured using Helium Porosimetry – Mercury Immersion, 
Nuclear Magnetic Resonance and Mercury Injection methods. This section is divided into 
experimental and modelling part.

4.4.1.2 Nuclear Magnetic Resonance 
NMR method for porosity estimation has been widely used in the industry. Its examples can 
be found in the literature [20,16,43,44]. The measurement is based on full water saturation 
and is done on, usually, large core cuttings. However, this method is likely to overestimate 
porosity due to the microcracks present in the sample [16]. HPMI method, in this sense, 
seems to be more accurate as it is performed on a crushed material.  
The procedure is to compare T2 relaxation time maximum amplitude of a saturated sample 
with pure water T2 response as a function of tested volume. In the result, the volume of pure 
water present in the sample can be found. Knowing bulk volume and assuming full 
saturation, porosity is determined.

4.4.2 Modelling approach 
Porosity evaluation in the entire Lower Palaeozoic in the Lublin Basin is needed to estimate 
potential gas amount in place. Porosity can be modelled using a combination of bulk density 
with resistivity logs and a sonic log. HPMI core porosity is believed to give the best 
approximation of a real value. This result is marked in the log plots to show the validity of 
created models.

4.5 Water saturation 
Kiernowski and Dyrka (2013) [4] state that one of essential conditions for continuous 
accumulation of gas in shale is water saturation below 45%. Szot and Gołąbek (2012) [50] 
compares the Barnett shale reservoir properties to data from the Pomeranian Polish Shale. 
Water saturation is 25% and 5.5%, respectively. Water saturation log  obtained from 
equations 38-41 is shown in Fig. 14. Two cases are considered and saturation derived from 
the core analysis in Table 7 is also illustrated in the chart. According to the modelled 
saturation log case 1 is more probable. Indeed, it was not possible to fit two cases to two core 
data by modification of Archie’s parameters. An increasing trend of gas saturation in 
Wenlock is common for two cases. If the model was calibrated to fit case 2 data water 
saturation over the entire logged interval would show values more than 1. Therefore, case 1 
fit seems to be more reasonable solution. On the other hand, it is also possible that saturation 
drops locally to the value which could have been measured in the lab in case 2 as seen in the 
figure. Nevertheless, it is important to remark that these logs are modelled using sensible, 
however uncertain, assumptions. The references used for creating the saturation log are 
representative primarily for the lower Ludlow - Wenlock interval.

4.6 Pore sizes 
Size of a pore throat is an important feature of a potential shale gas reservoir. Classification 
suggested by Rouquerol (1994) [51] gives the following scale (x-diamater): 
- 
micropores, x < 2 nm 
- 
mesopores, 2 nm < x < 50 nm 
- 
macropores,  50 nm < x 
Shale gas is present in place in two manners. It can be stored as free gas in pores and 
fractures as in conventional reservoirs but it can also condense and adsorb on the surface of 
organic matter and clay. Therefore, mesopores and micropores are preferred in shale gas 
production because they give a larger surface area for adsorption of methane [33] comparing 
to macropores.

4.7 Total Organic Carbon content 
Organic matter are those organic compounds which directly or indirectly come from cells 
and tissues of living organisms. Organic matter content is presented as a weight percentage 
of the total organic carbon in the rock (TOC). It is assumed that TOC of more than 1% can be 
an incentive for  hydrocarbon content analysis [57]. Cassa and Peters (1994) [58] suggested 
the following petroleum potential assessment based on TOC: 
- 
0-0.5% - poor potential 
- 
0.5-1% - fair potential 
- 
1-2% - good potential 
- 
2-4% - very good potential 
- 
> 4% - excellent potential 
 In general, high values of TOC are a good indicator of a potential shale gas field because 
organic matter content influences the amount of adsorbed gas capacity. Jacobi (2008) states 
that this controls the total gas content in the formation [39]. TOC in the Lublin shale is 
reported to 1-1.7% [3], 0.5-4.5% [57],  up to 3.5% [61], 1-3% [5]. 
Pyrolysis laboratory analysis gave 2.12% of TOC at 2691.33 meters. This locates the Lublin 
shale in a good petroleum potential according to Cassa and Peters’ scale. More data are 
necessary in order to create a TOC log from a bulk density log. TOC density is reported to be

4.8 Organic matter maturity 
Organic matter maturity is an important feature as it can verify if shale is hydrocarbon-
prone. In general, high organic maturity is desired for shale gas reservoirs. When a shale 
layer is rich in mature organic matter the following changes may happen (Labani and Rezaee 
(2015) [33]): 
- 
Saturated fluid changes from brine to gas 
- 
Heavier hydrocarbon components turn into lighter components to end up as dry gas 
- 
Increase of porosity in the organic matter due to thermal transformation 
- 
Increase in pore pressure because of clay diagenesis and hydrocarbon generation 
The Lublin shale organic maturity is estimated based on vitrinite reflectance. The vitrinite 
reflectance is a percentage of light reflected from the vitrinite maceral at high magnification 
when immersed in oil [64]. In the Polish Ordovician-Silurian Basin a gas window was found 
for 1.1-3.5% Ro [4]. EIA Report 2011 [3] estimates thermal maturity as 1.5% for this region. 
Poprawa (2010) [5] reports that thermal maturity changes from East to West in the Lublin 
Basin from 0.6-0.7% Ro to 1.5-2.0%. He published a map illustrating thermal maturity in 
reflectance scale for Llandovery sediments at the slope of the East European Craton (Fig. 20).

The organic matter is, thereby, immature if this approach is considered. 
4.9 Permeability 
Permeability is a key issue in evaluation of a shale gas field. It has one of the dominant 
factors that influences gas flow in the production step. The larger permeability of the rock 
the better, especially, in shales, for which permeability is unlikely in the range of tens of 
microdarcies [38]. The Lublin shale  of Ludlow and Wenlock is estimated up to 240 nD [7]. 
This was measured using pressure decay method which is performed on a crushed sample. 
Therefore, true permeability of the formation, which includes natural fractures permeability, 
should be higher. A strong interest was put in this thesis to evaluate shale permeability both 
experimentally and by modelling. However, as mentioned in the literature, permeability in 
shales is very difficult to measure [8,33]. Experimental approaches did not succeed and 
mathematical modelling is also applied to the laboratory results. This subsection describes 
permeability assessment and show the results using different approaches. 
4.9.1 Experimental permeability 
Permeability was measured during consolidation test at constant rate of strain (CRS) on two 
samples. Ideally, the samples should seal the oedometer space so no leakage around the 
sample occurs. It was not possible with initial shape of the samples, therefore, some methods 
were tried out in order to make necessary conditions for such permeability measurement 
(chapter: Description of experiments). None of the methods eventually worked. Samples 
were tested up to 40 MPa (sample A) and 45 MPa (sample B) of the vertical stress to 
eventually seal the gap. It was not succeeded and the lowest permeability obtained in a CRS 
test was 11.95 µD (A) and 8.83 µD (B). After each loading and unloading step permeability 
was measured by flushing the sample from the bottom and recording the flow rate of the 
water pushed through the oedometer to use in a standard Darcy’s equation. In this way, the 
lowest obtained permeability was: 3 µD (A) and 1.07 µD (B). Tables 10 and 11 show the 
measured values of permeability.  
The real permeability must, then, be lower than the measured values which, nonetheless, 
confirms the nanodarcy order of magnitude.

In order to find the estimation of the true permeability the following procedure was 
developed:

of the arrival time of a wave. 
2. Calculation of the corresponding horizontal strain as a function of applied vertical 
stress. 
3. Determination which horizontal strain is needed to fill the casing. 
4. Finding corresponding vertical stress needed to obtain such a strain. 
5. Finding correlation between vertical stress and permeability. 
6. Using the correlation function to find permeability at the required stress. 
This procedure is strictly mathematical and assumes that relation between the properties is a 
constant function.

4.10 Elasticity and Brittleness 
Brittleness is a characteristic which determines: 
- 
if the rock is likely to be naturally fractured 
- 
an extent of a network of fractures created in the result of hydraulic fracturing 
- 
parameters in the hydraulic fracturing process (pump rate, fracture dimensions). 
Ductile shales (opposite to brittle) tend to close created fractures. However, in some sense, 
they are also desired. If they overlay more brittle formation they work as a good seal which 
traps gas and does not let it migrate [71]. In the Barnett shale, there is a strong correlation 
between quartz content and higher brittleness. More ductile intervals are characterized by 
high clay, calcite or TOC content [72]. Brittleness is expressed as percentage and values 
above 40% indicate a prospective rock for hydraulic fracturing. There is not much research 
published concerning brittleness in the Lublin shale. Dohnalik et al. (2015) [73] found 
brittleness of the Ordovician and Silurian rocks in Poland testing 9 core samples. The 
brittleness, generally, ranged from 40% to 70% with one exception of 22%. His approach 
considered quartz mass content. 
Brittleness is assessed based on mineralogy or elasticity. Two methods are, hereby, discussed 
for the Lublin shale.

5.3 General comments to experiments 
The experiments and their analyses have been limited by technical abilities of apparatus. As 
mentioned in the main part, mercury could not be intruded to micropores because the 
injection pressure generated by the pump could not exceed 225 MPa. Therefore, the pore size 
distribution is representative only down to around 3 nm od pore radius. Using a Soxhlet 
extraction for cleaning of shales is not recommended since there might be difficulties in 
cleaning of small pores [83]. However, other methods (flow-through, centrifuge flushing) 
were considered destructive for clay or for shale in general and, therefore, Soxhlet extraction 
was used. But, to make the process efficient, small samples were cleaned for 2-3 weeks. 
Another issue was a tilting of a metal beam holding two LVDT’s recording the strain. It was 
a feature of the device which was present during all the tests (also for a trial sandstone run). 
However, taking an average of the two readings was assumed to minimize this effect.

Poland. It has been shown that reservoir properties can be classified as moderate to good.

The shale gas reservoir is located down deep in the Silurian interval and eventual investment 
decisions should be supported by careful economic analysis as making a horizontal drill on 
such a depth might be expensive. Moreover, local environmental policy has not been issued 
in this thesis. It should also be included in the final assessment of the field. 
Another objective was to provide a research and conceptual models which could fill an 
apparent gap in the literature regarding the Lublin shale permeability and elasticity. 
Different methods and approaches were developed and explained. In general, low 
permeability, in the range of less than 100 nD,  was found. Low Poisson’s ratio of 0.23 and 
static Young’s modulus of 18.52 GPa were measured to evaluate the brittleness of the rock. 
Elastic moduli, brittleness, permeability and porosity logs were created which can give an 
overview of behavior of the Palaeozoic shale in the Lublin Basin. However, the results and 
analyses are provided based on experimental measurements done on material from specific 
depth. As this reservoir is described in the literature, and it has also been shown in this 
thesis, as very heterogeneous more laboratory data, preferably from other boreholes, are 
needed in order to derive general optimal conclusions applicable for the entire Palaeozoic 
shale in the Lublin sedimentary basin.

clay modulus has been brought forward and investigated in terms of its influence on rock 
elastic properties. Usually, in this case and in the case of pore water salinity two extreme 
cases were considered in order to present a reliable spread of probable real values.
