ABSTRACT 
In the present study, a thorough investigation of L-asparaginases from Aspergillus oryzae has been 
conducted. This enzyme plays a key role in the reduction of acrylamide, a potential carcinogenic 
chemical, from processed foods and in the treatment of childhood Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia 
(ALL). Its use as a chemotherapeutic agent is well established, with three commercial enzymes in the 
market, all from bacterial sources. However, due to the nature of these enzymes, immunogenic 
effects and allergic reactions are recurrent in patients. It has been highly suggested that other sources 
have to be investigated, mainly higher eukaryotes, such as fungi. 
This study has screened the potential asparaginase genes found in A. oryzae, resulting in the selection 
of a final candidate, which has been purified and characterized. In an attempt to optimize fungal cell 
factories to achieve higher titers of production a systems approach based on gene deletions has been 
conducted.  
The potential use of the selected enzyme as a chemotherapeutic agent has been investigated through 
the in vitro treatment of an established cell line model of ALL in addition to cell line models for 
Burkitt’s Lymphoma and Acute T cell leukemia. It has been found to be highly efficient against ALL, 
with more than a 10-fold reduction in the necessary dose of treatment. The results found highlight 
the potential use of this enzyme as a new alternative to the currently in use bacterial enzymes. 
Further research in animal models needs to be conducted, to elucidate if the immunological and 
allergic reactions are indeed reduced. If successful, it can lead to improvements in cancer treatment.

INTRODUCTION 
Filamentous fungi as production hosts 
Filamentous fungi, as cell factories for the production of biotechnological products, have been widely 
used for many years. Numerous companies, such as AB Enzymes, Chr. Hansen, Novozymes and more, 
utilize these organisms for the manufacturing of a wide array of products including enzymes, organic 
acids and secondary metabolites. These products are either produced naturally by the organism, or 
by heterologous expression due to the increasing advances in genetic engineering on these 
organisms (Meyer et al., 2016). 
In the food industry, the Aspergillus genus is of significant importance for the production of enzymes 
(e.g. amylases for the production of glucose syrup) and organic acids (e.g. citric acid used in soft 
drinks, dairy products and many more) (Copetti, 2019). This same genus is also involved in other 
industries, such as the textile industry (e.g. catalases used in bleaching of fabric) (Singh, 2016) and 
the detergent industry, where enzymes are added in detergent formulations to aid in the removal of 
fat (i.e. lipases) and blood (i.e. proteases) stains (Saisubramanian et al., 2006). 
Bioactive compounds, belonging to fungal secondary metabolism, had a major role in current 
medicine, since the discovery of penicillin in 1928. Since then, more than 9000 compounds have been 
identified, including antibiotics, anticancer (e.g. taxol) and immunosuppressant drugs (e.g.  
cyclosporins) and hypocholesterolemic agents (e.g. statins). Further research of these compounds 
has the potential for the discovery of new drugs (Sanchez & Demain, 2017). 
Filamentous fungi present benefits when compared to classical microorganisms, such as yeast and 
bacteria, due to their higher secretion capabilities and their metabolic versatility. Nevertheless, major 
limitations in their usage are still found. These are caused by a shortage of high-throughput 
technologies for cloning, expression and screening of mutants. The lack of these tools, both 
technological and biological, prolongs the discovery and development of new products and 
methodologies. Industrial optimization of strains for production has been based on classical 
mutagenesis and posterior selection of higher producers. Even though this results in highly 
productive strains, it can also cause strains with reduced fitness, through the accumulation of 
undesirable mutations. In addition, there is no control in the secondary metabolism, which can lead 
to the production of mycotoxins. A more targeted approach is therefore preferred, mainly by the use 
of CRISPR/Cas9 (Meyer et al., 2016). 
With this technology, the difficulty of engineering the genomes of fungi has been greatly reduced, 
and it has additionally an extremely high efficiency. Multiplexing genome editing accelerates the 
process allowing for more than one modification at a time and the technology easily allows for gene 
disruptions/deletions, gene insertions, the introduction of point mutations and more. However, the 
process is still time-consuming for the creation of mutants and their subsequent purification (Si et 
al., 2015).  
The optimized strains used in industry have superior secretion capabilities. However, this strains fall 
under commercial confidentially and are not disclosed to public domain, limiting the knowledge. High 
production of homologous and heterologous targets is often limited. In addition, the optimization of 
a cell factory through genetic engineering for a specific target does not necessarily correspond to a 
better production of another target. The major bottleneck is the lack of understanding of the 
different secretory pathways found in these organisms. A deeper understanding of intracellular 
transport and exocytosis is needed.

Protein production and the secretion pathway 
Several approaches have been investigated in order to rationally engineer filamentous fungi to obtain 
cell factories that are efficient in the secretion of homologous and heterologous proteins. Possible 
targets to be modified range from the transcription of the DNA sequence up to the final steps of 
exocytosis. 
An overview of the complete pathway can be seen in Figure 1. Protein production starts with the 
transcription of the gene to its corresponding mRNA. The translation is made by ribosomes that can 
co or post-translationally transport the protein to the Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER). In the co-
translational transport, through the action of the signal peptide recognition particle (SRP) the nascent 
polypeptide is directly synthetized into the ER lumen. Proteins that contain a classical signal peptide 
follow this pathway, whereas proteins that do not are synthetized in the cytosol. They are kept 
unfolded though their interaction with chaperones, and the complex is able to be directed to the ER 
through the interaction of a membrane receptor complex.  
Once the polypeptide is localized in the ER lumen, it is folded and modified, with the assistance of 
several chaperones and foldases. It is here where correctly folded polypeptides undergo 
glycosylation, one of the most relevant post-translational modifications. It can affect the localization, 
the stability and the secretion of the final protein. Properly assembled proteins that will be secreted 
are then transported to the exterior of the cell. Through the transport of vesicles to the Golgi 
apparatus, the proteins are directed to secretory vesicles. These vesicles arrive to the apical plasma 
membrane through vesicle clusters in the Spitzenkörper and their content will finally be secreted 
(Wang et al., 2020).  
 
Figure 1. Classic protein secretion pathway in filamentous fungi. A schematic overview from protein biosynthesis until 
secretion can be observed. Only mature proteins are successfully secreted, whereas misfolded proteins are degraded via 
the possible action of different pathways. Extracted from (Wang et al., 2020).  
 
The pathway described consists the classical protein secretion (CPS) pathway. In order to follow this 
pathway, proteins must contain a signal peptide (SP) in the N-terminal, which is used for the 
==PAGE:9==
8 
 
translocation of the protein into the ER lumen and is cleaved afterwards. Through the use of 
bioinformatic algorithms, such as SignalP (H. Nielsen et al., 2019), prediction of SPs can be performed 
for any given protein. Proteins that are not properly folded can either activate the unfolded protein 
response (UPR) or the ER-associated protein degradation (ERAD). In the first pathway, the synthesis 
of chaperones and folding enzymes is upregulated in order to try to fold the polypeptide correctly, 
whereas in the latter, the pathway is responsible for the degradation of misfolded proteins. 
Even though it is known that proteins without SPs can be secreted, the molecular mechanisms of the 
unconventional protein secretion (UPS) are mostly unknown. It is known that some of these proteins 
are secreted through vesicles but is not clear how these vesicles pass through the cell wall. Multiple 
pathways could exist and it was found that external stimuli could modify the composition of these 
extracellular vesicles adding more complexity to the UPS (Miura & Ueda, 2018). Nevertheless, 
rational engineering can be performed in the well-studied steps of the CPS, where numerous 
possibilities are found.  
Optimization of expression and secretion 
  
The first level that can be targeted is gene expression itself. For homologous proteins, several key 
concepts can be exploited. For example, promoter swapping for a strong one can overcome 
limitations caused by natural regulation of the native gene. For heterologous targets, a very 
standardized procedure is codon optimization of the DNA sequence to the host, as it relives stress in 
translation (Su et al., 2012). For either type of target, further optimizations can be performed. 
As described, proteins containing a SP will enter the CPS. Therefore, the sequence coding for a SP can 
be introduced at the beginning of the gene or, for proteins that naturally possess one, its native SP 
can be switched for a more efficient one. A well-studied efficient SP is the glucoamylase (glaA) signal 
peptide from A. niger. It was proven to increase secretion nearly nine-fold in an engineered α-
galactosidase (Y. Xu et al., 2018).  
Both UPR and ERAD are involved in the correct folding and degradation of proteins and they can be 
modified to improve the secretion of a specific target. The deletion of key genes involved in the ERAD 
pathway can limit protein degradation and regulation of UPR related genes can aid in properly folding 
an overexpressed target. However, it has been consistently found that the manipulation of a gene 
involved in these pathways may produce vary contrary effects, even in closely related organisms. An 
example would be how in A. awamori the overexpression of bip1 (ER luminal chaperone that is 
involved in the transport of synthetized proteins into the ER lumen) promoted protein secretion, 
whereas the opposite effect was found in A. niger (Lombraña et al., 2004) (Conesa et al., 2002). It 
was hypothesized that such contrary effects were caused by a malfunction of BiP, since even though 
its main function is aid in protein translocation and folding, it can also promote the activation of the 
ERAD pathway. Other examples of contrary effects can be found, confirming that the improvements 
made through the regulation of these pathways are host specific. 
The folded proteins are transported through a series of vesicles, and therefore, genetic tuning of the 
interaction of these vesicles to one another can help increase protein secretion. Deletions, 
overexpression and point mutations in several cargo transporters have been found to have a positive 
result (Hoang et al., 2015) (Boppidi et al., 2018). Besides being secreted, some proteins might be 
transported to vacuoles for their degradation. In A. oryzae it was found that disrupting this 
localization by deleting Aovps10, a vacuolar sorting receptor, increased by up to 3-fold the secretion 
of heterologous enzymes (Yoon et al., 2010).  
==PAGE:10==
9 
 
Studies also showed that secreted protein stability can be increased by the construction of a 
protease-deficient strain (Kamaruddin et al., 2017). On a more macroscopic level, secretion can also 
be improved through the targeted modification of mycelium morphology, since secretion mostly 
occurs in the hyphal tips. More branching in the tips results in an increase in vesicle transport and 
subsequently, an increase in secretion (Fiedler et al., 2018). Lastly, the manipulation of Sterol 
regulatory element-binding proteins (SREBP), which are responsible to maintain sterol homeostasis, 
therefore essential in the maintenance of cell membrane structure and fluidity, has been found to 
be associated with protein secretion. Several studies have found that deletions in genes involved in 
this pathway help improve protein secretion (Qin et al., 2017; Reilly et al., 2015).  
 
Asparaginases 
 
Characteristics, applications and current market 
L-Asparaginase (E.C. 3.5.1.1) is an enzyme that catalyzes the hydrolysis of L-asparagine into L-
aspartate and ammonia (Figure 2). The enzyme is widely found in almost all living organisms, from 
plants to animals and microorganisms.  
Two isozymes (enzymes differing in amino acid sequence that catalyze the same biochemical 
reaction) are found in model organisms such as Escherichia coli and Saccharomyces cerevisiae. The 
isoform I is a cytoplasmic enzyme, which presents low affinity for L-asparagine, and type II is a 
secreted enzyme with considerably higher affinity for its substrate. The KM for E. coli type I and II are 
3.5 mM and 0.115 mM respectively, showing a 30-fold difference for the affinity towards L-
asparagine. In E. coli, both types have been found in homotetrameric forms, and type I has been 
found to have cooperative activation, being allosterically activated by asparagine (Yun et al., 2007). 
Its main uses are in the food industry and in anticancer therapy.

Food industry 
Approximately be twenty years ago it was confirmed that a wide array of heated food products 
contained acrylamide, a potential carcinogen and neurotoxic agent. Afterwards, it was discovered 
that it was through the Maillard reaction that this compound was formed. It is a chemical reaction 
between amino acids and reducing sugars that gives the desired organoleptic characteristics to baked 
foods such as bread and potato fries. However, at temperatures above 100°C and when the amino 
acid involved is asparagine, the byproduct of this reaction is acrylamide (Mottram et al., 2002). Since 
the Maillard reaction is desirable due to the sensory attributes that it gives to the processed food, a 
specific solution needed to be found. The pretreatment of raw foods with asparaginase lowers the 
==PAGE:11==
10 
 
concentration of this amino acid and has been successfully adapted as the mode of action to reduce 
acrylamide.  
Currently in the market, two enzymes can be found for acrylamide mitigation, PreventASeTM from 
DSM (Heerlen, The Netherlands) and Acrylaway® from Novozymes A/S (Bagsvaerd, Denmark). Both 
of them come from the Aspergillus genus, the first one being an enzyme from A. niger and the second 
one from A. oryzae and each one is produced in their native host respectively. They show some 
differences regarding optimal pH and temperature, which allows for optimization for each food. For 
example, the pretreatment of potato chips at 170°C for 5 minutes with 10000 U/L of Acrylaway® has 
been proven to reduce acrylamide formation up to 90%. With their use approved in the food industry 
in several countries, with the US government giving the GRAS (Generally Recognized As Safe) status, 
and receiving a favorable evaluation as a food additive by the World Health Organization (WHO) 
Expert Committee, their use will only increase and might help reduce the acrylamide problem (F. Xu 
et al., 2016).  
 
Cancer treatment 
The first use of a L-asparaginase in cancer treatment dates back to 1963, when it was discovered that 
it was through the action of this enzyme found in guinea pig serum that the growth of lymphoma 
cells in vivo was reduced (Broome, 1963). The posterior approval by the Food and Drug 
Administration (FDA) in 1978 of the first E. coli L-asparaginase to treat Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia 
(ALL) has increased the complete remission rate of only 20% of patients to up 80-90% of patients 
treated with this enzyme (Costa-Silva et al., 2020).  
Currently in the market, four versions of the enzyme are available. Two are derived from E. coli (a 
native and PEGylated version) and two from Erwinia chrysanthemi, with the second being a 
recombinant version of the enzyme, approved by the FDA in June 2021 (Effer et al., 2020).  
The mode of action resides in the hydrolysis of free asparagine found in the bloodstream. Neoplastic 
cells, with high prevalence in childhood ALL, present a deficiency to synthetize this amino acid, as 
their expression of asparagine synthetase (ASNS) gene is either very low or inexistent. Therefore, 
through the elimination of the circulating amino acid, ALL cells enter apoptosis caused by starvation, 
leading to cell death (Figure 3) (van den Berg, 2011). This is accomplished with high efficiency, since 
the treatment of patients using only the enzyme can cause full remission in up to 60% of the cases. 
Nevertheless, the treatment is accompanied with other chemotherapeutic drugs (e.g. doxorubicin, 
methotrexate and cytarabine), increasing to remission to 90% of cases (Aslanian et al., 2001b).

of free circulating L-asparagine induces cell death in cancer cells, due to their inherent deregulation of de novo L-asparagine 
synthesis (ASNS). Created with Biorender and adapted from (van den Berg, 2011)  
However, side effects derived from the treatment are common, as observed with most 
chemotherapeutic agents. These include mostly immunogenic effects and allergic reactions, but 
several adverse reactions are also found, including anaphylactic shock, pancreatitis, coagulation 
abnormalities, nausea, musculoskeletal pain, fatigue and others. In hypersensitivity, antibodies 
against the enzyme are generated by the patient, which inactivates the treatment. Patients that 
develop hypersensitivity to a specific enzyme are normally switched to another approved one, with 
the PEGylated version possessing a longer half-life in blood and diminished immunological responses 
but still causing, after some months, immunogenic responses (Costa-Silva et al., 2020).  
In addition, it has been found that the interruption of treatment with the enzyme can increase the 
probability of cancer relapse (Kato et al. 2018). Subsequently, research in novel proteoforms or 
sources for the enzyme has been in the rise, with the last five years having over 1000 manuscripts 
involved in L-asparaginase. In the search of new candidates, parameters to be taken into account 
include activity determination, stability and anticancer effects in vitro and in vivo. The substrate 
affinity is a key parameter (Michaelis-Menten constant KM) due to enzymes with low affinity for 
asparagine has been proven to reduce efficiency of treatment both in vitro and in vivo. It has also 
been suggested that glycosylation of the enzyme could increase protein stability in the blood serum 
but possible reduction of affinity has to be assessed, as well as possible immune responses. 
Nevertheless, it was found that glycosylation of E. chrysanthemi asparaginase resulted in less 
antibody recognition. The search of novel enzymes from eukaryotic organisms has been suggested 
as it can possibly overcome all these obstacles (Effer et al., 2020).

EXPERIMENTAL THEORY 
Selection of strategies to improve production  
 
Based on the multiple approaches that can be performed to improve protein production and 
secretion in filamentous fungi, two main strategies were undertaken. 
Through an extensive literature research, genes whose knockout improved production were 
selected. This improvement could be a higher intracellular concentration, or preferably, a significant 
improvement in protein secretion. The selection included research performed in different organisms. 
Through the search in FungiDB, homologs for each gene were found for A. oryzae. As 
aforementioned, the effect of a deletion can be highly host specific, therefore the selection of genes 
from studies in other organisms (within the fungal kingdom) would give more insight on their effect 
in A. oryzae.  
Table 1 shows an overview of the final candidates. It can be seen how the corresponding proteins are 
involved in different pathways, with a wide array of functions. Non-characterized proteins whose 
putative functions were predicted to be involved in the abovementioned pathways were also 
selected.  
To further investigate the effects of knocking out of these genes, it was decided to perform multiple 
deletions. The genes were grouped by function or pathway involvement, in a total of six groups. This 
combinatorial approach can result in an increased positive result, due to possible synergism in the 
gene deletions. 
 
Additionally, in order to specifically aid in the secretion of the homologous enzymes produced, it was 
decided to assess the effect of the glaA signal peptide from A. niger. As abovementioned, it has been 
previously found to increase up to nine-fold the secretion of a specific target. Each of the selected 
enzymes were cloned with the addition of this signal peptide in the N-terminal of the protein and 
also without alterations in their native sequences in this terminal. Some of the selected targets 
presented a predicted signal peptide, therefore a comparison of their native signal peptide against 
the glaA could be performed. 
Additionally, as an attempt to ease the purification of the produced enzymes, a polyhistidine tag was 
introduced in the C-terminal of the proteins. This would allow the purification through affinity with 
a Nickel IMAC (Immobilized Metal Affinity Column). For the posterior removal of this tag, a TEV 
protease binding site was added, between the endings of the enzymes and the tag. 
To perform the genetic engineering necessary to accomplish all of the above, CRISPR-Cas9 was 
utilized, coupled with USER cloning for vector construction.

CRISPR-Cas9 
 
CRISPR gene editing is a genetic engineering technique that allows for the targeted modification of 
the genome of living organisms. CRISPR stands for clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic 
repeats and is a family of DNA sequences found in bacteria and archaea. Coupled with the Cas protein 
(CRISPR-associated protein) it consists the immune defense mechanism of such organisms. CRISPR 
sequences consist of DNA fragments from bacteriophages that have previously infected the 
organism. They are used to recognize DNA from new infecting bacteriophages and with the help of 
Cas, introduce breaks at specific locations, making the transcription of necessary genes for the 
infection impossible, therefore protecting the organism and being a form of acquired immunity 
(Barrangou, 2015). 
Cas is a nuclease capable of creating specific DNA double stranded breaks (DBS), guided by the CRISPR 
sequence. The original system from Streptococcus pyogenes has been modified by re-engineering the 
Cas9 protein found in this organism (Barrangou, 2015b).The modified system uses a single-guide RNA 
(sgRNA) consisting of the crRNA fused to trans-activating CRISPR RNA (tracrRNA) (Figure 4). The 
crRNA sequence in the sgRNA guides Cas9 to a complementary sequence in the targeted DNA, where 
the break is then introduced. Therefore, the optimized system only employs two components, the 
sgRNA and the Cas9 endonuclease. By the modification of the crRNA, the introduction of specific 
DSBs can be accomplished and efficiently used to modify a given genome (Jinek et al., 2012).   
For the DSB to be made, the only requirement is that the crRNA sequence targets a sequence which 
immediately downstream contains a Protospacer Adjacent Motif (PAM). This motif varies between 
Cas proteins (Sander & Joung, 2014), being NGG for the used S. pyogenes Cas9. 
 
Fungal optimization 
The CRISPR-Cas9 technique has been successfully adapted in higher eukaryotes, including fungi, 
where genetic engineering is especially troublesome. The increased difficulty comes from their 
genomes being larger and presenting a higher degree of complexity (Cooper, 2000). In filamentous 
fungi, the deletion of a crucial gene involved in the Non-Homologous End-Joining pathway (NHEJ), 
ku70, renders the organisms incapable to repair DSBs by this pathway. Therefore, the main usage of 
the Homologous Recombination (HR) pathway to take place and repair the break is favored. Through 
the addition of specific repair templates with regions of homology up- and downstream of the break, 
the repair can be mended through HR and allowing the introduction of point mutations, insertions 
or deletions (Figure 4) (Löbs et al., 2017).  
For the creation of a selectable marker for transformation, the pyrG gene is also disrupted. This gene 
encodes for Orotidine 5’-phosphate decarboxylase protein, required for de novo synthesis of uridine 
monophosphate (UMP). By doing so, the strain becomes auxotrophic for uracil and uridine, which 
can be utilized for efficient transformation adding the gene in the introduced plasmid and growing 
the strain in media without the corresponding supplements (Nødvig et al., 2015).

For the application of this technique in fungi, modifications of the Cas9 endonuclease had to be 
performed. To ensure the protein localized inside the nucleus, the sequence of  SV40-NLS (Simian 
virus 40 Nuclear Localization Signal) was added in the C-terminal and the gene was codon optimized 
for A. niger production (Nødvig et al., 2018). The modified gene is cloned into an AMA1 vector that 
will also contain one or more sgRNAs targeting the desired DSBs. AMA1 is a sequence from A. 
nidulans that presents the ability to promote extrachromosomal plasmid replication (Aleksenko & 
Clutterbuck, 1996). The cas9 is cloned under the regulation of Transcriptional Elongation Factor (TEF) 
promoter and terminator, allowing a strong constitutive transcription once inside the transformed 
fungus. The plasmid used as backbone is pAC330 (Figure 7A). The individual or multiple sgRNAs are 
placed under the control of A. fumigatus U3 promoter and terminator. In order for the sgRNA to be 
accessible to Cas9, they are cloned between glycine tRNAs which present RNase P and RNase Z 
flanking cutting sites (Figure 5). As seen, more than one sgRNA can be assembled, creating a 
polycystronic transcript, allowing for multiplex CRISPR gene editing.

After the introduction of a DSB, the break is repaired through HR utilizing a repair template given to 
the cell in the co-transformation step with the plasmid. It is based on the nature of this repair 
template that either a point mutation, an insertion or a deletion will occur. If a gene deletion event 
is desired, the crRNA is designed to target a location in the specific gene or its proximity, to introduce 
the DSB. The repair template must contain regions of homology with sequences up- and downstream 
the targeted gene. One option is using a 90mer, which consists of two 45-base pair sequences 
homologous to sequences up- and downstream to the gene. By doing so, the DSB is repaired through 
HR with this 90mer and the targeted gene is eliminated from the genome (Figure 6A) (Nødvig et al., 
2018). Nonetheless, longer sequences of homology can also be used for the repair of the DSB.  
For further validation of the gene deletion, two checking primers are designed. These primers anneal 
further up- and downstream of the homology regions used in the 90mer. If amplification with these 
primers results in a PCR product with a length corresponding to the distance between the primers to 
the used homology regions the deletion is confirmed. Otherwise, the PCR product will have a length 
corresponding to their distance in the unmodified genome, including the size of the gene.  
Recently, for gene insertions in Aspergillus, a fungal multi-species optimized platform has been 
constructed, named DIVERSIFY (Jarczynska et al., 2021). It is based on a Common Synthetic Gene 
Integration site (COSI), which in the A. oryzae strain used in this project is located at Insertion site 1 
(IS1). This site contains two homology regions, A and B, and the E. coli reporter gene, uidA, controlled 
with the gpdA promoter and trpC terminator from A. nidulans. This reporter gene encodes for ꞵ-
glucuronidase (GUS). When strains containing this gene are cultivated in media containing X-Gluc (5-
bromo-4-chloro-3-indolyl-beta-D-glucuronic acid), this compound is degraded resulting in blue color 
formation. For the introduction of a DSB in an optimized location of this reporter gene, plasmid 
pDIV073 is used, which is based in pAC330 and contains a well studied and efficient crRNA. The strain 
is also transformed with a Gene-Expression Cassette (GEC) that will be used as the repair template. 
The GEC has been previously engineered to contain the specific gene of interest (GOI) and is located 
between two NotI cutting sites, allowing for the linearization of this fragment before transformation. 
HR will occur, the GOI will replace the reporter gene, making the mutant strain incapable of degrading

USER Cloning and primer design 
 
USER cloning was selected for plasmid assembly. This DNA engineering method was chosen since it 
allows the assembly of multiple PCR products in a seamless and directional way with a high efficiency 
even in complex assemblies with multiple fragments. The selected plasmids used in the project are 
constructed with USER compatible cassettes.  
The method is based on obtaining fragments with shared 6 to 10 bases of homology, flanked at the 
3’ ends by a single deoxyuracil (dU). This is performed through primer design. The fragments are then 
amplified with a compatible polymerase that accepts primers with uracil. The resulting PCR products 
are named BioBlocks, which are fragments containing compatible overhangs for posterior cloning. 
These BioBlocks are treated with USER (Uracil-Specific Excision Reagent) Enzyme (New England 
Biolabs), which removes both dU’s leaving compatible ends allowing directional cloning (Vaisvila & 
Bitinaite, 2013).   
Two different type of plasmids are to be created: plasmids containing sgRNAs for CRISPR-Cas9 
mediated gene knock out and plasmids containing genes of interest (GOI) to be inserted. Figure 7A 
shows the two plasmids used as backbones, with PAC330 used for gene knock out plasmids and 
pDIV068 for gene insertion plasmids. Both plasmids contain a USER cassette for the introduction of 
the BioBlocks. This cassette (Figure 7B) contains a PacI restriction site for plasmid linearization and 
two BbvCI sites, which can be digested using Nt.BbvCI (which only cuts the top strand) creating USER 
compatible overhangs.

The modifications introduced to the target genes are performed through primer design. These 
modifications were to add the glaA signal peptide and a polyhistidine-tag. For the signal peptide

For the sgRNA plasmids, two different approaches were taken. One is based on placing the USER 
cassette on the end of the first tRNA while the second one is based in placing the USER cassette on 
top of the crRNA to be assembled (Figure 9). The amplification of the BioBlocks is performed using 
pFC902 as template, a plasmid similar to pAC330 that has the tRNA-sgRNA construct built with a 
crRNA targeting the yA gene in A. nidulans.  
Each method has its strengths and weaknesses. Regardless of the method, a common issue is found. 
Since the flanking tRNAs used are the same (A. nidulans glycine tRNA), the primer that anneals to it 
can anneal at two different positions creating two PCR products. The short one is the one to be used, 
since the long one would incorporate the template sgRNA into the constructed plasmid. The desired 
fragment has to be gel purified before usage.  
The first method (Figure 9A), allows for a universal BioBlock with the promoter and the first tRNA. 
For the creation of a crRNA only one primer is needed. This primer has as an overhang with the 
complementary USER tail and the whole crRNA sequence and starts annealing at the beginning of 
the tracrRNA. This approach is considered efficient when plasmids containing only one sgRNA are to 
be assembled. However, if multiple BioBlocks following the same design are to be assembled, this 
method does not allow for directional cloning of the BioBlocks and presents a very low efficiency.  
An alternative method (Figure 9B), is locating the USER cassette on the crRNA. Two specific primers 
for each crRNA have to be designed. By the amplification of the reverse primer with the promoter-
annealing primer and the forward primer with the terminator-annealing primer, two BioBlocks are 
amplified that will have the assembly USER region creating the crRNA. By having the USER cassettes 
in this specific location, multiple BioBlocks containing different crRNA can be directionally assembled 
in the same backbone in a single step. To do so, the second crRNA reverse primer is paired with the 
first crRNA forward primer and the second crRNA forward primer is paired with the terminator-
annealing primer. These amplified BioBlocks present only one unique directional assembly possible 
(Figure 9C). Although this method requires the design of two primers for each crRNA and is limited 
to crRNAs that contain the requirements for creating the USER cassette (having a T and an A 
separated by 6-10 base pairs) it is highly efficient.

Media and solutions 
Minimal media (MM) / Asparagine media (AM): Liquid or solid for the cultivation of fungi. For 1 L: 50 
mL (20% w/v) D-glucose [1.80M], 50 mL 20x Nitrate salts, 1 mL 1000x Trace elements and 1 mL 1% 
Thiamine [13.3mM] and MiliQ water to a final volume of 1 L. For solid media, 21 g bacto agar is added.

media, 5 g/L of L-asparagine are added and D-glucose is reduced in half.  
For solid Minimal Media containing X-Gluc, the chemical was added to a final concentration of 
0.115mM. For solid MM and AM containing Phenol red indicator, this was added at a final 
concentration of 0.05 g/L (0.005% w/v). 
Complex AM: Liquid media based on AM, which contains richer and more complex carbon sources. 
Composition equal to AM with the addition of 3.5 g/L soluble starch and 5 g/L CMC (Carboxymethyl 
cellulose) 
Transformation media (TM): Osmotic stabilized solid media to use after fungal protoplasts are 
transformed. For 1 L: 342.3 g sucrose [1M], 50 mL 20x Nitrate salts, 1 mL 1000x Trace elements, 1 mL 
1% Thiamine [13.3mM], 21 g bacto agar and MQ water to a final volume of 1 L.

Agarose gel electrophoresis 
For the visualization of DNA, be it PCR products or plasmids, gel electrophoresis was used. The 
samples were loaded in an agarose gel containing 1000X SYBR-Safe and run in EMBI Tec RunOne 
Electrophoresis Cell at 100V for 20 minutes as the standard. This technique allows the migration of 
DNA through the gel according to size by applying an electric current to the negatively charged DNA 
toward the anode. For DNA fragments under 1 kb, 2% agarose was used to increase the resolution, 
whereas for samples above 1 kb, 1% agarose was used. 
 
Gel purification and DNA quantification 
Different PCR products of different size can then be separated through gel electrophoresis and each 
fragment can be separately purified. Gel purification was performed by placing the gel in a 
transilluminator for the visualization of the bands and cutting the desired band using a scalpel 
cleaned with 96% (v/v) ethanol. The band was then dissolved and purified using the GFX Gel Band 
Purification Kit (Cytiva), following the recommended protocol. For any DNA quantification, be it PCR 
products, purified plasmid or gel bands or gDNA, Nanodrop was used. The instrument was blanked 
with the same solution where the sample to be analyzed was dissolved. Each sample was measured, 
obtaining the concentration value (ng/µl) as well as purity ratios. 
  
E. coli transformation and plasmid purification 
The transformation was performed by mixing 50 µl of DH5α E. coli competent cells with 10 µl of pre-
chilled USER reaction in sterile 1.5 mL Eppendorf tubes, mixing the solution gently and placing it on 
ice. Up to 10 µl of USER reaction can be added for up to 50 µl of cells. After a 30 minutes incubation 
on ice a heat shock at 42°C for 30 seconds was performed followed by a quick transfer to ice for 2 
more minutes. The reaction was then plated in LB supplemented with ampicillin and let to grow over 
night at 37°C. Positive and negative controls should always be included, the first one being the 
transformation of circular plasmid containing the ampR gene and the second one being the addition 
of only MiliQ water to the cells. Colonies were then screened through colony PCR and positive clones 
were inoculated in 5 mL of LB with ampicillin (5 µl) for propagation. For plasmid purification, a 
miniprep kit (Qiagen, Denmark) was used, following the recommended protocol from the supplier.   
 
Sequencing 
For final validation of assembled plasmids, Eurofins Mix2Seq (Eurofins Genomics) kit was used which 
is based on Sanger sequencing methodology. 15 µl of the plasmid, between 50 and 100 ng/µl was 
mixed with 2 µl of a primer [10 µM] annealing at a desired position in the plasmid. A read of up to 
800 base pairs from the annealing position will be sequenced on average. If the region to be 
sequenced was larger, more reactions using other primers were to be made.

Fungal protoplastation and transformation  
Protoplasts (competent cell wall degraded A. oryzae) and the subsequent transformation were 
carried out adapted from (M. L. Nielsen et al., 2006). Firstly, A. oryzae was plated in Minimal Media 
plates and let to grow at 30°C for 5 days. The spores from one plate were then collected with 5 mL 
of YPD, with the required supplements, and transferred to a shake flask containing 95 mL of the same 
media. It was incubated over night at 30°C and 150 rpm agitation. The mycelia was collected with a 
sterile Mira cloth placed in a funnel and washed with APB to remove all media. The clean mycelia 
was transferred to a 50 mL Falcon tube, where it should occupy a volume between 15 to 30 mL. It 
was then resuspended with 10 to 25 mL of filter-sterile Glucanex solution. The final volume in the 
tube should not exceed 40 mL. The mix was homogenized by inversion and agitated horizontally at 
30°C and 150 rpm for 2 to 3 hours. It was in this step were the fungal cell wall was digested. 
Incubations longer than 3 hours might result in degradation of the protoplasts. The digested mycelia 
was filtered with a sterile Mira cloth placed in a funnel, collecting the flow through since the 
protoplasts were in the filtered solution, separated from cell debris. APB was added to a final volume 
of 40 mL. A 5 mL overlay of ATB 2-fold diluted in MiliQ water was placed carefully on top and it was 
centrifuged at 3000g for 12 minutes, with acceleration 9 and deceleration 4. If the protoplastation 
was performed correctly, an opaque halo should be found in the interface of the overlay, which 
contained the concentrated protoplasts. This halo was then transferred to a new 50 mL Falcon tube 
and APB was added to a final volume of 40 mL. It was centrifuged again at 3000g for 12 minutes, with 
acceleration and deceleration 9. A pellet should be found containing all the protoplasts. They were 
resuspended in 1 mL of ATB, 0.2 mL were aliquoted in 1.5 mL Eppendorf tubes and stored in a -80°C 
freezer. If the color of the resuspended protoplasts was more yellow than grey, meant that the 
concentration was especially high. In that case, they could be resuspended in more than 1 mL. Before 
freezing, the protoplasts were examined under the microscope to verify that the protoplastation had 
been successful. 
 
The transformation was performed by mixing 50 µl of protoplasts and the DNA to be introduced 
followed by the addition of 150 µl of PCT. It was mixed by inversion and incubated at room 
temperature for 10 minutes. Afterwards, 250 µl of ATB were added, it was mixed by pipetting up and 
down and the mixture was plated in TM without supplements. The volume of the DNA should be 
below 25% of the total volume of the protoplasts-DNA mixture, in order to avoid osmotic stress in 
the protoplasts. If not possible, more protoplasts should be added. For circular plasmids around 750 
ng were used, for 90mers used as repair templates they were resuspended in 200 µl and from 2 to 5 
µl were used, and for GECs used as repair templates 10 µl of linearized vector were used. Positive 
and negative controls were always carried out. The positive was made transforming with a self-
replicating plasmid containing an AMA1 sequence and the pyrG gene so the strain could survive 
without the supplements. The negative was made by adding only water.

Genomic DNA extraction and strain purification 
For an efficient gDNA extraction, the FastDNA SPIN Kit for Soil DNA extraction kit (MP Biomedicals, 
USA) was used. Using a sterile toothpick, mycelia from a plate was scraped and mixed with 500 µl of 
Breacking buffer + LiAC and 200 µl of 2⌀ (mm) glass beads. The tubes were placed in a Fast-prep 
machine at speed 4 for 40 seconds and spun down in a microcentrifuge. Afterwards, 150 µl of the 
supernatant were transferred into a clean 1.5 mL Eppendorf tube and 15 µl of 5 M NaCl and 400 µl 
of ice-cold 96% ethanol were added. The mixture was vortexed and spun down for 3 minutes at 
10,000g, followed by the removal of the supernatant and dried at 50°C. The pellet was resuspended 
in 200 µl of MiliQ water, vortexed and spun down. For a cleaner solution, 150 µl of the supernatant 
could be transferred into a clean 1.5 mL Eppendorf tube.  
 
In order to purify a strain after a transformation, a single spore had to be isolated. This is performed 
due to the ability of filamentous fungi of nuclear transfer. Heterokaryons, multinucleate cells that 
contain genetically different nuclei can hinder fungal transformation, since one of the nucleus might 
have been genetically modify whereas the other is unmodified (Gehrmann et al., 2018). The isolation 
of a single spores overcomes this issue due to only containing one nucleus. A spore suspension was 
prepared collecting with MiliQ water spores from a plate. Using a YPD plate with 50 mL of solidified 
media, 20 µl of the spore suspension was placed at an extreme and the plate was tilted so the drop 
fell in a straight line across the plate. Using an optical microscope (Olympus, Nikon Eclipse 50i) 
containing a fiber optic needle, the plate was placed open upside down and the spores were focused 
at 100X magnification. The needle was used to pick individual spores and move them across the plate 
far from the rest of the spores. At least four spores were separated per plate. After 2 days, the 
isolated spores were transferred to fresh MM plates. It was important to transfer the spores before 
the mycelia came in contact with other mycelia, since nuclear transfer could occur and the strain 
would not be pure.  
 
Fungal cultivations  
Solid cultivations were carried out in the appropriate aforementioned media. Re-plating of strains 
was performed in MM plates, performing 3-point inoculations using sterile pipette tips. For 
cultivations in phenol red and X-Gluc plates, the inoculation was normalized in order to obtain 
comparable results. Per each strain, 2 µl of a 106 spores/mL solution were plated. Plates were 
incubated at 30°C. 
 
To investigate asparaginase production before liquid cultivations a qualitative assay based on pH 
change was performed, based on (Gulati et al., 1997). Minimal media and Asparaginase media 
containing phenol red were used. This pH indicator changes from yellow in acidic media to 
red/fuchsia in more basic media. Since the degradation on L-asparagine by the enzyme generates 
ammonia, the media turns more basic. Therefore, a color shift towards red in the form of a halo 
around the colony would be expected. 
 
Liquid cultivations were performed in shake flasks or petri dishes, with 100 mL or 20 mL of liquid 
media respectively. The inoculation was normalized, to 106 spores/mL. Independently of the method 
used the strains were cultivated at 30°C, with shake flasks being agitated at 150 rpm. Samples of the 
supernatant for further analysis were collected every 24 hours for 3 days.  
 
When relevant, at the end of the cultivations, the cultures were filtered through a Mira cloth 
(Millipore, Germany) and the flow through was collected. The flow through was further filtered using 
a 0.45⌀ filter. From 10 to 50 mL of this clean flow through were concentrated to a final volume 
between 0.25 and 3 mL using 15 mL Centrifugal Filter Units with a cut-off of 10 kDa (Millipore, 
Denmark). The concentrators were centrifuged at 5000g for 30 minutes, followed by two wash steps 
with MiliQ water. When smaller amounts of liquid needed to be concentrated, 0.5 mL Centrifugal

Activity assays and biochemical characterization 
Enzymatic activity was determined from the amount of ammonia liberated by the hydrolysis of L-
asparagine or L-glutamine, quantified through the Nesslerization method (Imada et al., 1973). The 
reaction mixture containing 40 μl of MiliQ water, 40 μl of 40 mM L-asparagine or 40 mM L-glutamine 
and 40 μl of 50 mM Tris-HCl pH 7,5 was pre-incubated at 37ºC for 5 minutes, followed by the addition 
of 40 μl of the sample to be analyzed, mixed and incubated for 1 hour at the same temperature. The 
reaction was stopped by the addition of 40 μl of 1.5 M trichloroacetic acid (TCA). To quantify the 
ammonia generated, 10 μl of the previous reaction was mixed with 75 μl of MiliQ and 30 μl of 
Nessler’s reagent (0.09 mol/L solution of potassium tetraiodomercurate(II) (K2[HgI4]) in 2.5 mol/L 
potassium hydroxide (KOH)). This reagent changes color from a pale to a deep yellow in the presence 
of ammonia. The absorbance was measured at 450 nm and the ammonia was quantified using a 
standard curve prepared with known concentrations of ammonium sulfate (Appendix C, Figure 34A). 
Blanks were prepared pre-incubating the sample at 98ºC for 5 minutes and then treated in the same 
manner as the samples. For each sample a blank was prepared, in order to subtract the possible 
absorbance given by ammonia already present in the sample. One unit (U) of enzymatic activity was 
defined as the amount of enzyme required to release one μmol of ammonia per minute. 
To elucidate the optimum pH of the enzyme under study, the activity was measured in different 
buffers: Sodium citrate buffer 50 mM (pH 2-6), Phosphate buffer 50 mM (pH 6-8), Tris-HCl buffer 50 
mM (pH 7-9) and Borax-NaOH buffer 50 mM (pH 9-11). Once the optimum pH was found, it was used 
to determine the optimum temperature of the enzyme incubating in a range from 10 ºC to 80 ºC. 
With these two parameters found, Vmax and KM were calculated by incubating the enzyme in different 
substrate concentrations in a range from 0 to 40 mM, for both L-asparagine and L-glutamine.   
 
 
Protein quantification and purification  
For the assessment of the protein concentration, Bradford Reagent for 0.1-1.4 mg/mL protein Kit 
(Merck, Denmark) (Bradford, 1976) was used. 5 µl of sample were mixed with 250 µl of Bradford 
reagent. After a 15 minutes incubation, the absorbance was measured at 595 nm and the 
concentration was quantified trough a standard curve of different known concentrations of BSA 
(Bovine Serum Albumin). The recommended protocol allows for a quantification range from 0.1 to 
1.4 mg/mL, which, for the majority samples of this project was too high. Different ratios of Bradford 
to sample were tested and it was found that a 1:1 ratio allowed for lineal quantification of 
concentrations from 0.01 to 0.07 mg/mL (Appendix C, Figure 34B and C). Therefore, 50 µl of sample 
were mixed with 50 µl of Bradford reagent, allowing for a precise protein concentration 
determination. 
To isolate the proteins containing polyhistidine tags, His SpinTrap kit (GE Healthcare, UK) was used. 
Histidines can coordinate the nickel found in the Ni-IMAC column, therefore proteins containing a 
histidine tag, will bound the column through affinity interactions, allowing the specific separation 
and purification of proteins with affinity for the column from the rest (Bornhorst & Falke, 2000). 
Firstly, the nickel column was mixed by inversion to resuspend the storage solution. The cap was 
loosen, the bottom closure was removed and it was placed in a 2 mL Eppendorf tube and centrifuged. 
All centrifugations were performed at 70g for 30 seconds in a microcentrifuge and the column was 
placed in a clean 2 mL Eppendorf tube after each step. The column was equilibrated adding 600 µl of 
Binding buffer (20 mM sodium phosphate, 500 mM NaCl, 20 mM imidazole, pH 7.4) and centrifuged. 
Up to 600 µl of sample were loaded and centrifuged, and several sample applications could be

performed as long as the column capacity (750 µg) was not exceeded. The column was washed 
adding 600 µl of Binding buffer and the protein was finally eluted in two steps, adding and 
centrifuging each time 200 µl of Elution buffer (Binding buffer with a 500 mM concentration of 
imidazole). The first elution would contain the majority of the protein of interest. Due to high values 
of remaining activity being found in the sample flow through the protocol was optimized. The 
concentration of imidazole in the Binding buffer was lowered to 5 mM and the sample flow through 
was loaded two more times in the column, before the washing step.  
SDS-PAGE and protein de-glycosylation  
To visualize the secretome profiles of the strains cultivated, concentrated supernatants were 
separated by 12% SDS-PAGE (Sodium Dodecyl Sulphate–Polyacrylamide Gel Electrophoresis). This 
technique allows the separation of proteins by molecular weight. Samples were quantified using 
Bradford and if possible, the amount of protein loaded was standardized to 10 µg. When the 
concentration was not high enough, the maximum volume of sample was loaded (38 μl). The samples 
were mixed with 12 μl of Loading Dye (with 200mM DTT) and incubated 5 minutes at 99ºC before 
loading. As marker, 10 μl of Prestained SDS-PAGE Standards broad range were used in the first lane 
of all gels. The gels were run in 1x Tris/Glycine/SDS Buffer at 80 V for 20 minutes followed by 100 V 
for 60 minutes. The proteins were stained with Bio-Safe Coomassie G-250 Stain for 1 hour, followed 
by 1-hour destaining with warm water (all materials were purchased from Bio-Rad, Denmark).  
Endoglycosidase H (EndoH, NEB) was used for the de-glycosylation of purified proteins. This enzyme 
cleaves after the first N-acetylglucosamine, which is bonded to an asparagine. The reaction was 
carried out following the recommended protocol. It allowed the removal of the rest of the 
oligosaccharides, and was used to obtain proteins whose molecular weight was not altered by the 
presence of glycans.  
 
Mammalian cell line cultivations and antiproliferative assay 
RAMOS (RA 1) (ATCC CRL-1596), MOLT-4 (ATCC CRL-1582) and Jurkat Clone E6-1 (ATCC TIB-152) were 
selected to perform the studies, obtained from DSMZ (German Collection of Microorganisms and Cell 
Cultures). They are cell lines established from for Burkitt’s Lymphoma, Acute Lymphoblastic 
Leukemia (ALL) and Acute T cell leukemia diseases respectively. Cells were cultivated in optimal 
culture conditions described by the supplier. Briefly, RAMOS cells were maintained in RPMI 1640 
media (Merck, Germany) with 20% FBS (Fetal Bovine Serum) and 1% Penicillin-Streptomycin and kept 
between 0.5 – 1.5 x 106 cells/mL culture. MOLT-4 cells were maintained in RPMI 1640 media with 
20% FBS and 1% Penicillin-Streptomycin and kept between 0.5 – 1.3 x 106 cells/mL culture. Jurkat 
cells were maintained in RPMI 1640 media with 10% FBS and 1% Penicillin-Streptomycin and kept 
between 0.5 – 1.5 x 106 cells/mL culture. All cultures were kept at 37°C with 5% CO2. Primary T-cells 
were obtained from an anonymous donor, supplied through a blood bank. Cells were cultures in 
ImmunoCult-XF T-cell Expansion Media (STEMCELL Technologies) supplemented with IL-2 and kept 
between 0.2-0.5 x 106 cells/mL culture. Cell concentrations were measured in a NucleoCounter® NC-
200™ automated cell counter, which measures cell concentration and viability.  
For cell passage, cells were measured and the total amount of alive cells was calculated. The culture 
was centrifuged at 300g for 5 minutes and the supernatant was carefully aspirated. Fresh media was 
added, in order to dilute the amount of alive cells to their subculturing concentration. If the volume 
needed for this dilution was higher than preferred, a lower amount of original culture could be 
centrifuged, reducing the amount of alive cells in the pellet and therefore the volume needed to 
resuspend them was lower.

For the treatment with L-asparaginase, the three cell lines and the primary T-cells were subcultured 
at their optimal concentration, preparing a final culture of at least 6 mL for each of them. Five 
different conditions were tested: three different concentrations of enzyme, a control with only buffer 
and a control without any treatment. Since each condition was done in triplicates, a total of 15 
independent experiments per cell type and time course were carried out. In two different sterile 96-
well plates for cell culturing, 200 µl of culture were placed in 15 wells/cell type. Enzyme solutions 
were prepared in order to add the same volume independent of the dosage. This volume for each 
dosage was aliquoted and the same amount of just Tris-HCl buffer was added in the negative controls. 
Nothing was added to the untreated control. The two plates were placed in the incubator, at the 
abovementioned growth conditions and incubated for the desired amount time, 24 or 48 hours. 
Flow cytometric assessments were carried out on a CytoFLEX S instrument (Beckmen Coulter) using 
a 96-well plate format. It was used to measure cell viability in a high-throughput manner. It is a 
technique that allows to measure physical and chemical characteristics of a population of cells. In 
this case, only cell viability was assessed. After the incubation period, cells were harvested by 
centrifugation (300g for 5 minutes), the media was aspirated and the cell pellet was washed with 150 
µl of Phosphate-buffered saline (PBS, STEMCELL Technologies), followed by another centrifugation 
and supernatant removal. Cells were stained with 7-Aminoactinomycin D (7AAD, 1 mg/mL, 
ThermoFisher) diluted 1:1000 in PBS, by adding 150 µl of staining solution and resuspending the 
pellets. One well of the untreated control was resuspended in regular PBS, as the unstained control. 
7AAD is a fluorescent chemical compound that intercalates double-stranded DNA with high affinity. 
It is broadly used for cell viability staining since it is not capable of entering cells with intact 
membranes. However, dead cells present compromised membranes and 7AAD can enter easily. With 
the cells stained, the 96-well plate was placed in the flow cytometer. The instrument analyzed one 
sample at a time, introducing the solution in a laminar flow which allowed for a single cell to pass in 
front of the laser at a time. For each cell, three parameters were studied, Forward-scattered light 
(FSC), Side-scattered light (SSC) and the fluorescence signal of 7AAD (546 nm absorption and 647 nm 
emission). 
FSC is a direct measurement of the size of the cell since its intensity is proportional to the diameter 
of the cell. SSC measures the morphological complexity/granularity of the cell since more complex 
cells deviate more light in different directions. The measurement quantifies the amount of light 
scattered at a 90° angle relative to the laser. For the 7AAD fluorescence, the cell is excited with a 543 
nm helium-neon laser and the emission is measured. 
The data collected from the flow cytometer was analyzed using the FlowJo software package. A series 
of gating analysis were performed to differentiate the subpopulations found in the samples. By 
plotting FSC versus SSC, cells could be discriminated from cell debris. Single cells were gated by 
plotting the area versus the height of the FSC signal. Finally, to gate the population of dead cells, the 
unstained control from MOLT-4 was selected, setting the gate at a 104 units of fluorescence intensity. 
Cells with fluorescence values above this intensity were considered dead.

Bioinformatic screening of asparaginases in A. oryzae 
Firstly, the selection of asparaginases in A. oryzae needed to be conducted. A search in UniProt 
(Bateman et al., 2021) was performed, filtering the entries to the specific enzyme and organism. It 
was found that none of the entries was characterized. Therefore, the putative asparaginases found 
were selected. Since the database contains independent genomes from three A. oryzae strains, the 
first step was to remove identical proteins, which resulted in six unique putative proteins.  
These candidates were compared with the entries found in FungiDB (Basenko et al., 2018). In this 
database, seven unique uncharacterized genes with predicted asparaginase activity were found in A. 
oryzae. When directly compared to the predicted proteins it was found that each protein matched 
one of the unique genes. However, one gene did not have a match. Further investigation was made 
and it was found that even though the gene had indeed predicted asparaginase activity, its main 
prediction was to belong in the Protein kinase C family. Consequently, this gene was removed and a 
total of six genes were selected for their research. In Table 9 an overview is shown. The putative 
proteins were ordered based on their predicted amino acid length and named Asp1 to Asp6. An 
investigation on possible native signal peptides was performed utilizing SignalP and a prediction of 
possible glycosylations was performed with NetNGlyc (Gupta & Brunak, 2002).

as well as perform multiple deletions with the goal of achieving a strain that did not possess any 
asparaginase activity. In order to do so, specific crRNAs for each gene were selected and primers 
were designed. The methodology followed in this case was the first one described in USER cloning, 
with a universal BioBlock. The amplification of the different BioBlocks can be seen in Figure 10A and 
the colony PCR of the plasmids containing only one sgRNA can be seen in Figure 10B.

transformed into ORY7, using the appropriate 90mer as a repair template in order to knock out the 
genes. Mutants growing in the transformation plate were isolated and purified. Shown in Figure 10C 
are the amplifications of the six genes in the final six mutant strains from their gDNA, ∆asp1 to ∆asp6. 
The six genes were amplified in the six strains and it can be seen how, in each strain, the 
corresponding gene is knocked out (highlighted with a black circle).

Investigation of possible nitrogen repression in expression 
The results found showed that at least two other of the enzymes under study besides Asp5 (Asp1 and 
Asp2) possessed asparaginase activity, but was also found that the individual deletion of Asp5 caused 
a phenotype that did not possess any type of remaining asparaginase activity. It was hypothesized 
that Asp5 was under positive gene regulation by the presence of asparagine, since in the reference 
strain ORY7 activity was found only when grown in AM. If Asp1 and Asp2 were under negative control 
of Asp5, they would be produced in the ∆asp5 strain therefore it was hypothesized that they were 
under another type of control, more probably a negative nitrogen control in which if a preferable 
source of nitrogen was present the expression of these genes would be repressed.  
To investigate this, ORY7 and ∆asp5 were grown in normal MM and AM liquid cultures as well as MM 
and AM lacking the nitrogen source of these media, NaNO3. This would force the strains to metabolize 
asparagine as it would be the only nitrogen source in AM. Figure 19A and C, show the secreted 
protein profile and the detected ammonia in the supernatant respectively, which paralleling the 
previous results, when the strains were able to utilize asparagine, the protein concentrations were 
higher and ammonia was detected, specifically in ORY7 grown in AM and AM without NaNO3 and 
∆asp5 in AM without NaNO3.  
However, activity in the supernatant was only found in ORY7 both in AM and in AM without NaNO3 
(Figure 19B). Shown in Figure 19C are the macroscopic morphology of the strains in the four media. 
It could be seen how in MM without nitrogen sources neither of the strains could grow, just some 
dispersed mycelia was found. It can also be seen how ∆asp5 could survive when asparagine was the 
only nitrogen source, proving that it could utilize metabolize it. However, when another preferred 
nitrogen source was present, it inhibits this utilization.

Purification and biochemical characterization of asparaginase 2 
Asp2 was selected as the principal target to perform the studies to improve secretion, using it as a 
reference of production in the different strains to be constructed. It was also selected to perform the 
antiproliferative studies. That was due to the found increased and more reproducible production of 
Asp2 compared to Asp5, since its expression was found in both MM and AM with either its native or 
with glaA signal peptide, and that it was found in the supernatant as opposed to Asp1. Asp2_glaA_SP 
was ultimately selected as the producer platform because of the higher amount of activity compared 
to Asp2_OG. Before it was subsequently used, a characterization was undertaken.  
Firstly, the purification was optimized. In order to achieve the maximum binding of the protein to the 
nickel column, the concentration of imidazole in the binding buffer was lowered from 20 mM to 5 
mM and the sample loading was repeated two more times, re-loading the flow through. This showed 
a reduced residual activity in the sample flow through fraction, even though some was still present 
(data not shown). To investigate if the band at 50 kDa was indeed the protein of interest, a de-
glycosylation of the purified enzyme with EndoH was performed. By doing so, the molecular weight 
of the protein should correspond to its theoretical weight. Figure 20A, shows an SDS-PAGE analysis 
of the secreted protein profile of Asp2_glaA_SP, the elution fraction and the de-glycosylation 
reaction, all in triplicates (T1, T2 and T3). The concentrated protein and the elution fractions showed 
the same results as the previous gel. In the de-glycosylation lanes, EndoH enzyme (MW: 29 kDa) could 
be observed, located right above the 25 kDa standard. However, due to the de-glycosylation step, 
the quantity of protein loaded in the gel was lowered causing it to not be detectable. 
To compensate this, the concentrated sample of the supernatant was further up-concentrated by a 
factor of five. This sample was then treated in the same manner as the previous one for the 
purification and de-glycosylation. An SDS-PAGE analysis with the same distribution as the previous 
one was performed (Figure 20). Due to a considerably higher amount of protein loaded in the nickel 
column, it could be seen how the purified fraction in this case contained more bands than before. It 
was suggested that due to the overloading of the column, proteins without affinity for the nickel 
remained inside the column since they were blocked from being washed out. Nevertheless, the most 
prominent band was still the band located at 50 kDa. The de-glycosylated lanes showed how that 
band reduced its weight, being now located at ≈ 45 kDa. This weight was not close to the expected 
39 kDa. However, clearly seen in triplicate 3 another band was present. This band, with a weight of ≈ 
40 kDa, was also present in the other two triplicates, with a lesser intensity. In the purified fraction 
of triplicate 3 it was also seen, with a weight of ≈ 45 kDa, suggesting it was the same protein 
undergoing de-glycosylation therefore losing 5 kDa. It could be suggested that Asp2 corresponded to 
this band and that the protein of 50 kDa was also co-eluting in the purification due to either being 
the more abundant protein and not being completely washed out or by possessing affinity to the 
column.  
 
==PAGE:48==
47 
 
 
Figure 20. SDS-PAGE of concentrated, purified and de-glycosylated samples from Asp2. A and B present the same layout, with 
an optimization in B to aid in band visualization. 
Since the purified fraction did show activity in both cases, it was assumed that the protein was being 
purified even though maybe it was not cleaned completely from the rest of the proteins. With this 
semi-pure enzyme, a characterization was performed. The enzyme was incubated in four different 
buffers in a pH range from 3 to 11 in order to determine its optimal (Figure 21A). As expected, at the 
ends of the range the activity was minimal, with an active range between 6 and 9. The two pHs 
assayed that showed highest activity were Phosphate buffer pH 7.5 and Tris-HCl buffer pH 9. The 
later was then chosen for determining the optimal temperature. The enzyme was incubated at 
temperatures ranging from 10°C to 80°C with 10°C intervals, and including 37.5°C (Figure 21B). 
Activity was found even at 10°C, with a peak in the 30-40°C range and a drastic decrease from 50°C 
on.  
To assess if the enzyme followed a Michaelis-Menten kinetic and calculate the corresponding 
parameters, the enzyme was incubated in a range of substrate concentrations, from 0 to 40 mM of 
either L-asparagine or L-glutamine (Figure 21C and D). It can be seen how the enzyme did have 
catalytic activity for both substrates, following Michaelis-Menten kinetics. However, the affinity of 
the enzyme towards asparagine was 10 times higher than for glutamine, with KM of 0.44 and 4.05 
mM respectively. The Vmax found in for both substrates was similar, at ≈ 0.0012 U/mL. For the 
calculation of the parameters, the data was converted and a Lineweaver-Burk plot was made (Figure 
21E and F), having the inverted concentration of substrate in the x-axis and the inverted velocity in 
the y-axis. A lineal regression can be made, obtaining an equation of the form y = mx + n, where Vmax 
is equivalent to 1÷n and KM is equal to m·Vmax.

DISCUSSION 
 
Studying the asparaginases in A. oryzae 
Through the search of potential asparaginases in A. oryzae, six unique genes were found, and it was 
discovered that none of them were characterized (Table 9). An analysis was performed, to investigate 
similarities between the selected putative proteins and the characterized type I and II enzymes from 
E. coli and S. cerevisiae. Two of the enzymes presented high similarities, with Asp2 having a superior 
identity with both type II enzymes and Asp5 with type I (Table 10). Considering that Asp2 presented 
a predicted peptide while Asp 5 did not, it can be hypothesized that they are the homologs for type 
II and I, corresponding to the extracellular and the intracellular isoforms, respectively.  
To investigate the selected candidates, individual gene knockouts were successfully performed. The 
cultivation of these mutant strains showed how the single deletion of asp5 resulted in a complete 
loss of asparaginase activity. This was observed in solid AM phenol red plates, were this strain 
presented a complete loss of halo formation whereas all the rest and the reference strain, ORY7, did 
produce a halo (Figure 11B). This method was previously validated through the cultivation of ORY7 
in both MM and AM phenol red plates (Figure 11A). Even though some background noise will be 
present due to change in pH by other causes, inferred from the color change in the MM phenol red 
plate, it is clear when there is halo formation caused by the degradation of asparagine.  
In liquid cultivation, consistent results were found. All strains except ∆asp5 showed activity in the 
supernatant at 48 hours. Regarding concentration of secreted protein detected the supernatant, all 
strains presented similar values when cultivated in MM, between 5 to 10 mg/L after 72 hours. When 
cultivated in AM, all strains except ∆asp5 showed an approximate 3-fold increase in protein secretion 
(Figure 12). It is hypothesized that these strains, being able to hydrolyze L-asparagine, have an extra 
source of carbon and nitrogen allowing for better growth and more production. It can be seen how 
for the native production of asparaginase/s, induction by asparagine is needed, since ORY7 does not 
present any activity in MM. Consequently, the first objective was accomplished, obtaining a strain 
that did not show asparaginase production.  
For the six selected genes, amplification and cloning into pDIV068 of the native sequence with a TEV

of the screened colonies had the plasmid with the gene and the glaA signal peptide cloned. Since the

Determination of enzyme activities 
As a first screening, all strains were grown in phenol red MM and AM plates (Figure 15A). As expected 
all plates showed the expected behavior for the controls, with ORY7 having halo formation and ∆asp5 
not. Based on the signal peptide prediction, Asp1 is a predicted intracellular enzyme and it was found 
that while the Asp1_OG strain did have halo formation, Asp1_glaA_SP did not. This could suggest 
that when the enzyme is exported outside the cell because of the signal peptide it is not stable 
enough or it does not have the necessary conditions to be active. Asp2 enzyme, predicted to be 
secreted, presented a halo formation both in Asp2_OG and Asp2_glaA_SP strains, with the halo 
appearing faster when expressed with glaA signal peptide. This result could suggest that in both cases 
the enzyme is exported outside the cell, and that the glaA signal peptide is more efficient for 
secretion than its natural one.  
The reintroduction of Asp5, which was the gene knocked out in the strain used as the platform, 
∆asp5, shows how, even though it is under the control of a different promoter, makes the strain 
recover the ability to break down asparagine. It does not have a predicted signal peptide, inferring 
intracellular activity. However, Asp5_glaA_SP also shows activity, even though at a slower pace. It 
can be suggested that the enzyme is stable enough to act outside the cell. All remaining enzymes, 3, 
4 (both native form and with glaA signal peptide) and 6, did not show halo formation. The EAR plot 
(Figure 15B) shows two main tendencies. ORY7, Asp1_OG, Asp5_OG are the first strains to degrade 
substrate and present halo, with similar ratios all throughout the experiment. The remaining three, 
Asp5_OG, Asp2_glaA_SP and Asp2_OG start later, with this respective order. It is these three strains 
the ones suggested to have a secreted enzyme acting outside the cell, therefore the delay could be 
caused by an extra time required for the enzymes to be secreted. For better analysis the strains were 
cultivated in liquid media. 
Liquid cultivations were performed from here on in Petri dishes, due to their reproducibility when 
compared to standard shake flasks cultivation (Figure 16). The two reference strains, ORY7 and 
∆asp5, and the 10 asparaginase strains were cultivated, measuring secreted protein (Figure 17A), 
activity (Figure 17B) and ammonia in the supernatant (Figure 17C). The concentration of protein in 
the supernatant show two distinct behaviors. Strains able to degrade the asparagine in AM show at 
least twice as much production as the ones that cannot. The ability to degrade ammonia is validated 
by the presence of ammonia detected in the supernatant, in strains ORY7, Asp1_OG, Asp2_OG, 
Asp2_glaA_SP, Asp5_OG and Asp5_glaA_SP. This result is consistent with the pattern found in the 
phenol red plates since they are exactly the same strains that showed halo formation. Activity in the 
supernatant was not found for Asp1_OG validating the hypothesis that it is an intracellular enzyme. 
Asp2 is found in the supernatant of both strains and in both media. The quantity of ammonia 
converted in the activity assay itself is lower for the MM supernatants (data not shown), which 
suggests that the quantity of enzyme found is lower in this media. However, since U/mg takes into 
account the quantity of protein present in the sample as well it can be seen how Asp2_glaA_SP 
produces more of the inserted enzyme in respect of the total protein produced than Asp2_OG. Since 
the promoter is the same, this can be explained by a better secretion of the enzyme due to glaA 
signal peptide being more efficient that the native one of the protein. The U/mg of Asp2_glaA_SP in

quantity of protein detected.  
Asp5_OG shows activity in the supernatant, even though the enzyme did not have a predicted signal 
peptide, and Asp5_glaA_SP has a minor detected activity. Nevertheless, in both strains, ammonia 
was detected in the supernatant indicating that the enzymes are active. Consequently, it is

To investigate the profile of secreted proteins from selected strains, an SDS-PAGE analysis was 
performed (Figure 18). It can be seen how all the profiles are indistinguishable from the references,

mentioned, Asp1_glaA_SP was loaded instead of Asp1_OG, since in the latter the enzyme was 
predicted to be intracellular. Even though it is hypothesized that the enzyme in Asp1_glaA_SP is

to the references, and the elution fraction lanes only show one band, at around 50 kDa. An activity 
assay was performed for all the purification fractions. It was found that not all the targeted protein 
was bound to the column due to remaining activity in the sample flow through. Nevertheless, protein 
was efficiently bound to the column since activity was found in the elution fraction and no residual 
activity was found in the intermediate wash fraction (data not shown). 
The sample flow through lane from Asp2_glaA_SP has the same profile as the raw concentrated 
protein profile, indicating that the majority of proteins do not bind to the column. The fact that only 
one band is found in the elution fraction and that activity is found in this fraction indicates two 
possibilities. A first hypothesis would be that the observed band corresponds to Asp2, which has 
bound the column with high activity and it has been separated from the rest. Due to a major band at 
the same height being found in all other strains, an alternative possibility is that this protein is co-
eluting in the elution fraction with Asp2, and that Asp2 does not have a high enough concentration 
to present a detectable band. If the production of Asp2 is comparable to Asp1 and Asp5 (since they 
all are under the regulation of the same promoter), the second hypothesis would be more likely, 
since for the last two the concentration was not high enough to be detected in the gel. A further 
analysis through mass spectrometry should be carried out.

Investigation of possible nitrogen repression in expression 
An initial investigation to try to elucidate why the single deletion of asp5 caused the result observed 
was performed. The enzymes selected are not characterized, their functions are only based on 
bioinformatics predictions. Therefore, an explanation could be that Asp5 was the only real 
asparaginase while the rest did not possess any catalytic activity. However, the introduction of the 
genes under controlled regulation showed that at least two, Asp1 and Asp2, did also possess catalytic 
activity. Further research was carried out and it was found that in S. cerevisiae, type II asparaginase 
was under Nitrogen Catabolite Repression. That meant that if a preferred source of nitrogen was 
present, such as ammonium sulfate, asparaginase biosynthesis was completely repressed (Dunlop et 
al., 1980).  
An experiment was carried out with ORY7 and ∆asp5 where the nitrogen source, NaNO3, from MM 
and AM was removed. Therefore, the strains would not be viable in MM due to the lack of nitrogen 
and the strains would only be viable in AM if they were able to catabolize the asparagine. Results 
showed that, as expected, neither of the strains was able to grow in MM without nitrogen. In this 
media, protein concentration in the supernatant (Figure 19A) was undetectable and it can be seen 
how only some dispersed mycelia was present on the cultures (Figure 19D). Growth in regular MM 
and AM showed the same behavior as the previous cultivation, with ORY7 having more protein 
secreted in AM and activity found in the supernatant (Figure 19B) and ∆asp5 showing similar behavior 
in both media. Interestingly, when ∆asp5 was grown in AM without NaNO3, ammonia was detected 
in the supernatant and the final concentration of protein significantly increased (Figure 19D). The 
fact that the strain in this media is able to catabolize asparagine coupled to no activity being found 
in the supernatant suggests that the enzyme being produced under these conditions was Asp1, the 
intracellular enzyme. Consequently, this gene might be under nitrogen catabolite repression. More 
research needs to be done, such as transcriptomic analysis, to fully validate this hypothesis and also 
to elucidate the regulation of Asp2.  
 
Purification and biochemical characterization of asparaginase 2 
With the selection of Asp2 as the principal enzyme, due to its reproducibility and easy quantification, 
its characterization was performed. Firstly, an attempt to optimize the purification was carried out, 
by lowering the concentration of imidazole in the binding buffer from 20 mM to 5 mM, as 
recommended by the manufacturer to increase protein binding. The sample flow through was re-
loaded into the column two more times in order to increase protein binding as well. These 
modifications showed a reduced residual activity in the sample flow through. The enzyme has six 
predicted glycosylation sites, which add an unknown mass to the protein. A single glycosylation can 
add up to 2.5 kDa, and if the theoretical mass is recalculated, removing the signal peptide and adding 
the TEV sequence and polyhistidine tag, the weight of the enzyme is 39.5 kDa. Adding the mass of 
the possible glycosylations, the expected mass ranges from 39.5 kDa to 54.5 kDa. Three independent 
cultures of Asp2_glaA_SP in AM were cultivated and concentrated and the enzyme was purified and 
de-glycosylated. The de-glycosylation should remove the unknown weight of the glycans and only 
the mass corresponding to the amino acids would be expected. Two SDS-PAGE gels were analyzed 
(Figure 20). In the first one (Left), a single band appears in the purified fractions, at the height of the 
most prominent band in the concentrated samples. In the de-glycosylation lanes, EndoH can be seen, 
with a weight of 29 kDa while no other bands are present. It is hypothesized that the dilution of the 
purified sample for the de-glycosylation reaction lowered the concentration of the protein resulting 
in it not being detectable in the gel. The process was repeated up-concentrating the samples before 
the purification step by a factor of 5 and performing a new SDS-PAGE (Right). An increased number 
==PAGE:68==

of bands is found in both the purified and de-glycosylated lanes. It is hypothesized that the 
overloading of the columns could have caused a blockage, making it more difficult for proteins 
without affinity for the nickel to be washed out. The most prominent band was still the same band 
found in the previous gel, but the de-glycosylation showed that its weight was only reduced to ≈ 45 
kDa. Another band, at ≈ 40 kDa, is clearly seen in triplicate 3, and present in the others as well with 
reduced intensity. This weight is closer to the predicted weight, suggesting that the band for Asp2 
might be this one. A final validation through mass spectrometry of the bands of interest should be 
carried out.  
With activity being found in the purified fractions, this semi-pure protein was used for its

parameters were calculated through a Lineweaver-Burk plot and it was found that even though the

Engineering the secretory pathway 
All plasmids for gene knock out, including the 15 individual gene deletion plasmids and the 6 plasmids 
for multiplexing, were successfully assembled. They were utilized in the transformation of 
Asp2_glaA_SP protoplasts, resulting in nine individual genes deleted, with these strains being purified 
and validated (Figure 23D). For the remaining six genes, other crRNAs should be selected, since the 
efficiency of a given crRNA can only be assessed experimentally. These same six crRNAs did not have 
good efficiency in the multiplexing transformations, resulting in no more than one gene being 
knocked out.   
A first screening of the effects caused by the deletions was performed growing the strains in MM 
plates (Figure 24A) and in phenol red MM and MM plates (Figure 24B). In regular MM plates, it can 
be seen how all the strains present a similar phenotype, except ∆rbd2 and ∆tul1, which showed a 
higher sporulation phenotype. Due to the strains possibly presenting an altered metabolism or 
secretion compared to the references, the phenol red study was not no longer directly related to 
asparaginase metabolism. This could be indicated since the strains ∆amyR, ∆rbd2 and ∆tul1 
presented a halo in MM plates and because the overall background noise of the strains in MM was 
higher than with the previous strains. Nevertheless, strains ∆prtT, ∆trxA, ∆vip36 and ∆vps10 did show 
clear halo formation in the AM plate and all the strains had a halo after 72 hours incubation.  
For a better assessment, they were grown in liquid AM, measuring their protein secretion (Figure 
25A), and their asparaginase activity (Figure 25B). It can be seen how none of the strains presented 
an increase in protein secretion, and three of the strains, ∆amyR, ∆rbd2 and ∆tul-1 presented a 
reduced protein secretion. The last two coincide with the two strains that had a higher sporulation 
phenotype. amyR is a transcription factor that regulates amylase production, therefore the decrease 
of secreted protein is hypothesized to be due to a lower production of amylases. Activity was found 
in all the supernatants, but a significant increase in specific activity was not found either. 
To analyze if indeed the secretion was altered, an SDS-PAGE analysis was performed with 
concentrated supernatant with one triplicate from each strain (Figure 26). It can be seen how all the 
profiles, except ∆amyR, are indistinguishable from the references. As aforementioned, amyR is a 
transcription factor that regulates amylase production and it can be seen how this deletion has 
indeed changed expression of specific genes. The three triplicates from this strain were analyzed to 
validate that the result was reproducible (Figure 26, Right). The most prominent band in all the 
strains, located at 50 kDa, was no longer present in this mutant. This suggests that the band 
corresponded to an amylase. Nevertheless, a band is still found at this height, with more intensity in 
triplicate 1, which could correspond to Asp2. A further study through mass spectrometry should be 
carried out as well as the purification of Asp2 with the Ni-IMAC column using supernatant from 
∆amyR to investigate through another SDS-PAGE if a detectable band for Asp2 was found and its 
correspondent weight. 
However, an increase in protein secretion was expected in these mutants. It was hypothesized that 
no effect in secretion was found because the mutants were supposed increase protein secretion by 
relieving the stress in the secretory pathway and since the media used was a poor, the pathway was 
not stressed. To test this, a richer media was utilized, CAM.  
Regarding protein secretion (Figure 27A), it can be seen how all strains present more secretion in this 
media than in AM, with the average increasing from 60 to 100 mg/L. However, compared to the 
controls, only two strains presented increased secretion, ∆vip36 and ∆vps10. Of the fifteen genes 
selected, these are two out of the three that were selected from studies performed in A. oryzae, with 
the third not have been successfully deleted. Less secretion was found in the same strains as when 
==PAGE:70==
69 
 
grown in AM, being ∆amyR, ∆rbd2 and ∆tul1. Due to this media containing complex carbon sources, 
it can be explained how ∆amyR present a lower secreted protein concentration, since it cannot 
produce as many amylases as the other strains.  
Specific activity (Figure 27B) was not increased in any strain when compared to Asp2_glaA_SP. It can 
be seen how specific activity is higher at 48 hours than in 72 hours. Since the promoter used, gpdA 
from A. nidulans, has a constitutive expression it is hypothesized that the production of Asp2 at the 
beginning of the cultivation is considerably high compared to the rest of proteins. However, once the 
strains have started a more exponential growth, the machinery is employed for increasing production 
of other positively regulated proteins, making the ratio of Asp2 to the rest lower and therefore a 
reduction in specific activity is found.  
The four genes with a more pronounced impact, either positive or negative were vip36 and vps10, 
with an increase in protein secretion in CAM, and rbd2 and tul1, with a decrease in both assessed 
media. From the 9 successfully deleted genes, vip36 and vps10 are the only ones selected from a 
studies performed in A. oryzae whereas rbd2 and tul1 are the only ones selected from studies in N. 
crassa. The remaining five were selected from A. nidulans and A. niger. It is known that the result of 
specific gene engineering can be host-specific as well as target-specific (Wang et al., 2020). As N. 
crassa is phylogenetically further from A. oryzae it is not surprising that the effects found can be so 
distant. The studies performed in vip36 and vps10 found specific increase in heterologous secretion, 
while in the present study the studied target was homologous. The mutations have showed potential 
for their application in protein producer strains. Further research need to be performed to fully 
characterize the effects caused by these deletions, to assess if improved secretion of homologous 
can be accomplished and to investigate if their combination presents a synergic effect. 
It is not clear if only the change of media had indeed stressed the secretory pathway. A further study 
to validate this should be carried out, for example studying the expression of bipA, a known gene 
activated under stress conditions (Guillemette et al., 2007), in this media compared to regular AM 
and MM. However, stress is not always a direct consequence of high production. The machinery 
might not be stressed when homologous proteins are overexpressed, since the host organism is 
prepared for their production. In this study, where only homologous production has been 
investigated, further research increasing gene copy number and/or the use of a stronger than gpdA 
(e.g. TEF) could give insight as effective strategies to directly increase homologous protein 
production.  
 
Antiproliferative assay 
Asp2 was used, at various concentrations, to assess its potential chemotherapeutic use. The selected 
cell lines were assessed before treatment (Figure 28) and after incubation times of 24 and 48 hours 
(Figure 29). It has been proved that MOLT-4 can be effectively treated with Asp2, with cell survivals 
of 49% and 11% after 24 and 48 hour incubation, respectively. It has also been proven that primary 
T cells, normal, non-cancerous cells, have not been affected by the enzyme. However, Jurkat and 
RAMOS cells did not show sensibility to the treatment (Figure 30). Studies performed in the same cell 
lines with similar methodology as the one applied in this study were researched for a direct 
comparison. An overview is shown in Table 12. It can be seen that the dosages of treatment from the 
selected articles are at least ten times higher than the highest dosage used in this study. A limitation 
was encountered with the purification of Asp2 with the current methodology used. An optimization 
of the purification at a larger scale should be performed, in order to obtain a more pure enzyme at a 
higher concentration. From the literature research, it can be confirmed that MOLT-4 is the most Asp2

CONCLUSIONS and FUTURE PRESPECTIVES 
The investigation of the six putative asparaginases from A. oryzae has been performed. It has been 
found that in the studied conditions studied, the deletion of the gene assigned in this study as asp5 
resulted in a strain with no detectable asparaginase activity. This strain was used to produce 
individually all the putative asparaginases. It has been found that three of them, Asp1, Asp2 and Asp5 
present catalytic L-asparaginase activity and that Asp1 is localized in the cytoplasm, whereas Asp2 
and Asp5 are naturally secreted. First investigation on their native expression has found that asp5 is 
under positive induction by asparagine, asp1 might be under nitrogen repression, and further 
investigation to elucidate the regulation of asp2 has to be performed. 
It was observed that Asp2 with the heterologous glaA signal peptide from A. niger was more 
efficiently secreted than with its native signal peptide. The constructed strain, Asp2_glaA_SP, has 
been used for the production of this enzyme, under the control of gpdA promoter and trpC 
terminator. 
A characterization of Asp2 has been performed and it has been determined to have an optimal pH of 
9 in 50 mM Tris-HCl buffer and optimal temperature of 30°C. The enzyme has been purified through 
a Ni-IMAC column, thanks to the successful incorporation of a synthetic polyhistidine in its C-
terminal. However, an optimization of the purification needs to be performed, in order to obtain 
higher concentrations of more pure enzyme. 
Through individual gene deletions, an attempt has been made to increase protein secretion in A. 
oryzae. Two of the nine genes under study, vip36 and vps10, have been proven to result in strains 
with higher protein secretion. However, secretion of the targeted enzyme has not been found to be 
increased in these strains. Further research needs to be performed in order to increase the copy 
number of the enzyme under study, to investigate the potential application of these gene deletions 
in cell factories. 
Antiproliferative in vitro studies have been performed with Asp2 in three model cell lines for different 
types of blood cancers. It has been found to be highly efficient against MOLT-4, a model for ALL 
(Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia), with more than a 10-fold reduction in the necessary dose of 
treatment when compared to the published research on commercial enzymes. A direct comparison 
with the commercial enzymes needs to be performed to validate this result, which would indicate 
the potential to move forward to animal models.
