Abstract 
 Phase plates positioned prior to the sample in a transmission electron microscope can 
manipulate the beam to generate a user-defined irradiation with predetermined amplitude and 
phase distribution. Therefore, a phase plate can be employed to correct the spherical aberration 
of the beam, a prevalent issue in electron microscopy. Phase plates are traditionally fabricated 
using UV-lithography or E-beam lithography in cleanroom environments. However, these 
methods often suffer from limitations such as multiple fabrication steps or prolonged processing 
times depending on the lithography technique that has been employed. To surpass these 
limitations, and achieve rapid fabrication of complex micro-featured structures, that can present 
strong mechanical, high resolution and electrical conductivity we propose the fabrication of 
three-dimensional phase plate structures by Two-Photon Polymerization, followed by 
pyrolysis, a post thermal treatment process that can convert the polymeric fabricated structures 
into carbon microdevices. We present the influence of printing parameters (laser power, scan 
speed) and contour lines on the structural fidelity and dimensions of the structures, which can 
be attributed to voxel change. First, structures fabricated with a high exposure dose appear to 
be a few microns thicker than the designed value of 1 μm per inner ring, while those with a 
lower exposure dose tend to fall short of this target. The exact values depend on whether contour 
lines were employed. Second, the addition of contour lines results in thicker and taller 
fabricated structures compared to the designed values with an increase of approximately 
0.117μm±0.029μm in thickness and 0.154μm±0.015μm in height, respectively. After TPP and 
pyrolysis, the shrinkage percentage of the microdevices was investigating illustrating a lateral 
shrinkage of 31.4%±3.1% and 27.1%±2.1% depending on the contour line configuration. 
Optimized structures with well-defined print quality and adhesion performance fabricated on 
top of SixNy ultra-thin membranes to evaluate their beam-shaping capabilities. Finally, a novel 
free-standing printing approach using Dip-in lithography was implemented but also a 
combination between Dip-in mode and Oil-immersion, paving the way for future optimized 
construction of phase plates. Free standing fabrication can also contribute to different 
applications.

Chapter I: Introduction  
1.1 Motivation 
  
In Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM), samples such as thin polymer films or biological 
samples can produce images with low contrast and resolution. The scientific community came 
up with several methods and inventions to address this problem over the years. Ultimately, 
phase plates were used to address the spherical aberration of the profile of the TEM beam. 
Electron-optical components called phase plates are positioned in the beam path to change the 
electron wave's phase. Their main purpose is to reduce the amount of irradiation damage 
required to detect weak- phase objects by increasing contrast during imaging [1],[2]. 
Electrostatic phase plate fabrication is often carried out in cleanroom environments. The main 
contenders are lithography methods like UV-lithography and E-beam lithography. However, 
disadvantages like complex instrumentation, mainly 2D structures fabrication or lengthy 
processing times are typically mentioned [3]. Based on that and the numerous fabrications 
processes the samples go through in the cleanroom, we propose two-photon polymerization as 
an alternative to create three-dimensional phase plates. This ensures a rapid fabrication process, 
high-resolution and complex polymeric structure. Afterwards, a post thermal treatment process, 
more specific pyrolysis, will convert the polymeric structure into carbon microstructure with 
improved mechanical and electrical properties in respect of the initial polymer structure 
[4],[5],[6].

Chapter II: Theoretical background 
 
2.1 Additive Manufacturing 
  
 Additive Manufacturing (AM) is a process where the operator can fabricate parts by binding 
or solidifying materials in a liquid or powder form layer by layer using a 3D CAD design. 
Additive manufacturing is often referred to as 3D printing. AM is especially useful in situations 
requiring low production volumes, high design complexity, and frequent design modifications 
achieved to overcome the limitations of the previous traditional manufacturing methods. 
Automotive and aerospace industries as well as medical industries and research units nowadays 
are highly using this technology to manufacture their products [7]. 
 Although AM faces several challenges, including limited accuracy, long processing time, 
restrictions on part size, and high costs due to complex lithography instruments and high 
vacuum requirements, it remains a widely studied technology. Over the last two decades, a 
novel fabrication technique has been commanding the attention of both the research and 
industrial communities. This method is called two-Photon Polymerization (TPP) which offers 
higher resolution and faster prototyping making it an auspicious solution for advanced 
micro/nanofabrication [3].  
 
2.2 Two photon polymerization 
 Two-Photon Polymerization (TPP) is a 3D printing technology which allows the fabrication of 
readily assembled structures with nanoscale precision (or hundreds of nanometers resolution). 
It appears to be a remarkable and fast evolutionary technique since it is based on nonlinear 
photon absorption by photopolymers. Through TPP, the beam of an ultra-fast laser is tightly 
focused inside the volume of a transparent photosensitive material giving rise to absorb two or 
more photons and polymerize locally. TPP and the polymerization process can be initiated by 
non-linear absorption only within the focal volume pixel (voxel). By point -by-point scanning 
the voxel in space, following a path derived from a computer designed 3D model, one can thus 
create arbitrary 3D structures [8],[9],[10]. Another important point of two-photon 
polymerization is that it has plenty of applications in fields such as metamaterials, tissue 
engineering, photocatalysis, photonic crystals, micro-fluids, micro-optics, biomedical implants, 
and 3D models [11],[12],[13],[14],[15]. 
 In the following figure (2.2.1) a typical Direct Laser Writing experimental procedure is 
presented. Initially a femtosecond laser beam gets tightly focused into the volume of the 
photosensitive material that is placed on top of a cover glass. Then, by moving the laser beam 
to the sample through the stages in the three dimensions (X, Y, Z), the fabrication of the desired 
polymeric structure begins. After the end of the process, the substrate where our material is 
placed to be rinsed in specific solvent which cannot destroy the fabricated structure and during 
the rinse the non-polymerized material gets removed from the glass and the 3D polymeric 
structure remains only for further studies [16].

2.3 Two photon absorption 
  Two-photon polymerization results from two-photon absorption. In general, two major 
categories of two-photon absorption exist: sequential absorption and simultaneous absorption. 
As concern the sequential state the one photon excited in a real intermediate energy state and 
by this point the second photon gets absorbed. The presence of the intermediate energy state 
implies that the material absorbs at this specific wavelength; it will therefore be a surface effect 
and will follow the Beer–Lambert law [17]. The principles of two-photon polymerization are 
based on the simultaneous absorption which was originally predicted by Göppert-Mayer in 
1931 in her doctoral dissertation. TPP is defined as ‘an absorption event caused by the collective 
action of two or more photons, all of which must be present simultaneously to impart enough 
energy to drive a transition’. In this case there is not actual a real intermediate energy but instead 
there is a virtual intermediate state and two-photon absorption happens only if another photon 
arrives within the virtual state lifetime. The lifetime’s order of magnitude can be predicted from 
Heisenberg’s uncertainty principle. Considering that the state’s energy level should be around 
0.1 eV, its estimated lifetime is of the order of few femtoseconds. Due to virtual state’s short 
lifetime the cross-section of a simultaneous two photon absorption is small. For the 
accomplishment of this procedure, it is required to use a tightly focused femtosecond laser 
beam. The electron transition in this case is caused by two photons of energy hv/2 rather than 
one of energy hv. Figure 2.3.1 represents the single photon absorption and the two-photon 
absorption [16],[13].

Light intensity is the product of (number of photons x photon energy)/ (time x area). In a laser 
beam, the absorption rate within a transverse cross-section depends on the product of intensity 
(photons per unit time per area) and the number of molecules in that cross-section (proportional 
to area). Consequently, in one-photon absorption (OPA), the absorption rate remains 
independent of area. Since the number of molecules excited by single-photon absorption is 
uniform across any transverse plane of a focused laser beam, there is no localized excitation at 
the focal point. In contrast, two-photon polymerization (TPP) is a nonlinear optical process 
where the polymerization rate is proportional to the square of the photon density at each point 
in the photoresist. As a result, polymerization occurs almost exclusively within the high-
intensity focal region, enabling sub-diffraction resolution. The absorption rate within this 
focused region (voxel) follows a Gaussian-like distribution, scaling inversely with beam width 
and allowing precise spatial control of polymerization [18]. Figure 2.3.2 presents the 
fluorescence from a photopolymer solution, caused by single-photon excitation from a UV lamp 
and by two-photon excitation from femtosecond laser is presented as well as the integration of 
the square intensity of the laser beam when it is tightly focused on the volume element. The 
long wavelengths used for two-photon microscopy are less damaging and penetrate more 
deeply into the volume of the material. In addition, the requirement for near simultaneous 
absorption of two photons means that excitation is only achieved near the focal plane where the 
laser light is most concentrated [19].

of small unsaturated molecules in the liquid state to solid macromolecules through 
polymerization reactions. The monomers can be solidified in two main ways after being excited 
by light: polymerization and crosslinking. The chain reaction from which macromolecules are 
generated is an important aspect of polymerization, however cross linking is mainly concerned 
with the production of crosslinking with chemical bonds [20],[21].In general, polymerization 
is realized through chain reactions where M is a monomer unit and Mn is the macromolecule 
containing n-monomer units. The general idea of the polymerization is illustrated in the

To enhance photopolymerization efficiency, low-molecular-weight photosensitive molecules, 
known as photoinitiators, can be incorporated into the material. When the laser is tightly 
focused within the material, the photoinitiator absorbs two photons, generating radicals that 
initiate polymerization. The generated radicals react with monomers or oligomers, forming 
radical monomers that propagate the polymerization process. This chain reaction continues

reaction mechanism, where the addition of a monomer molecule to an active chain end

 2.5 Photo-materials 
 
 Over the years several materials have been employed globally by various research groups and 
companies for Two-Photon Polymerization (TPP). Some of these materials are specifically 
tailored for this purpose. While there may be variations, the fundamental elements of these 
materials remain consistent [23]: 
• 
a blend of monomers and oligomers that cross-link to enable the creation of 
intricate 3D structures, needed to be transparent at the two- photon absorption 
wavelength (λ/2) 
• 
a photo initiator, a molecule designed to simultaneously absorb two photons from 
incident light, triggering the formation of active species and eventually promoting 
polymerization. 
Photoinitiators are compounds that absorb light and trigger polymerization through excitation 
and decomposition, generating initiating species such as radicals, cations, or anions. Radical 
photoinitiators are widely available, while cationic photoinitiators, primarily chemical salts, 
including iodine salts and iron complexes, are less common. Benzyl groups (phenyl-CO-) are 
key components in almost all radical photoinitiators [20],[24]. A photoinitiator should exhibit 
several properties, from which the most important are:  
• 
high absorption at the exposure wavelength and high molar extinction coefficient 
• 
high quantum yield of formation of initiating species 
• 
high reactivity of the radical towards the monomer

2.7 Pyrolysis 
 The combination of direct laser writing and pyrolysis had shown well defined glassy carbon 
micro/nanostructures with functional properties. The main effect of pyrolysis is typically the 
decomposition of the initial compound with formation of smaller molecules and elimination of 
others. It is a new pathway to fabricate inorganic 3D structures that are made from materials 
that possess useful electrical, magnetic, optical, and chemical properties due to a tailored final 
material composition. Also, pyrolysis can improve the resolution of 3D structures as during 
thermal treatment the crosslinked resist is partly decomposed, and volatile species are removed. 
Apart from the reduction in size, the resist of 3D structures is made more robust and improving 
the mechanical stability of the structures [26],[27],[28].The aim is to decompose the functional 
organic groups in order to benefit from the final material. It is expected that during pyrolysis of 
polymer resins, removal of the organic part of the structures is achieved. In general terms, the 
C-O and O-O bonds are weak and as a result, they are broken first upon heating giving rise to 
the 3D functional structures consisted of a pure inorganic glassy-ceramic material. More 
specifically, during pyrolysis the polymer material undergoes a series of chemical changes, 
including dehydrogenation, hydrogen transfer, condensation, cyclization and isomerization 
[28],[29]. 
The first step is called the pre-carbonization phase and takes place at temperatures below 
300°C. Solvent molecules and residual monomers are removed from the polymer layer. 
Between 300°C and 500°C, halogens and other heteroatoms are removed, leading to the 
formation of a network of conjugated carbon systems. This is referred to as carbonization phase 
I. Around 450°C, dehydrogenation begins. At higher temperatures, up to 1200°C, in the 
carbonization phase II, most remaining hydrogen, nitrogen and oxygen atoms are removed. 
This causes the carbon network to become interconnected. Above 1200°C, in the annealing 
phase, remaining sulfur atoms and other impurities are removed [29]. Based on our pyrolysis 
procedure figure 2.7.1 illustrates the different steps during the post thermal treatment and a 
schematic conversion from polymer into carbon microstructure based on the model’s thesis 
design.

2.8 Phase plates-Beam Propagation 
 
 Phase plates are electron-optical elements designed to shape the electron beam by modulating 
its phase, enabling complex, user-defined amplitude and phase distributions. Their primary 
function is to enhance contrast when imaging weak phase objects while minimizing irradiation 
damage. Examples of irradiation damage are charging effects, physical destruction of the 
sample or reduced resolution. Spherical aberration primarily falsifies the phase information in 
the scattered wave and thereby produces artifacts and hampers a direct interpretation of the 
recorded image. A Cs-corrector can strongly reduce the impact of geometrical aberrations 
leading to improved point resolution with directly interpretable and well-localized spatial 
information. By tuning defocus and residual spherical aberration, the contrast transfer for phase 
objects can be optimized. Since correction of geometrical aberrations almost eliminates 
delocalization, the lateral incoherence of the electron source does not cause significant loss of 
information anymore when working close to Gaussian focus, which is the preferred mode of 
operation for an aberration corrected TEM. The major feature of a phase plate corrector is that 
the point resolution becomes equal to the information limit [30]. Since the optical index of 
refraction is proportional to electric and magnetic potentials, the active region of a phase plate 
behaves as a localized electromagnetic field. For effective operation, the phase plate must be 
positioned in the electron beam path, typically between the lens and the sample. Its active area 
should match the beam’s dimensions at the selected focal plane.  
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
As the beam travels through the electron microscope (EM) column, its size varies from microns 
at the condenser aperture to nanometers at the objective lens focal plane. Size and material play 
a crucial role in the fabrication of the phase plates. To ensure transparency to electrons, phase 
plates are designed with electrodes arranged around an opening or placed on an ultrathin 
support (below 50nm size) [31],[32],[33]. Typical material been employed for thin film phase 
plates is carbon. Some of its exceptional properties include high mechanical strength, chemical 
inertness, ease of preparation and transfer, excellent electrical conductivity, an amorphous 
structure, and low electron scattering [34]. Additionally, phase plates can be coated with a low-
stress (SixNy) since silicon nitride is an electrical conductor with low electron scattering and 
high mechanical stability [35]. Figure 2.8.1 depicts a schematic illustration of a transmission 
electron microscope equipped with a phase plate.

2.9 Printing Parameters 
 
 Lase power (LP) refers to the average optical power of the femtosecond laser used to induce 
polymerization in a photoresist. It determines the exposure dose and consequently, the 
polymerization rate. Scan speed refers to the rate at which the laser beam moves during the 
fabrication process. A faster scan speed means the laser moves more quickly, covering more 
area each time, while a slower scan speed allows finer detail but takes longer to fabricate. 
Slicing distance (SD) is a key parameter that defines the spacing between consecutive layers 
along the z-axis, thereby determining the thickness of the voxel building unit. A smaller SD 
results in higher resolution, requiring more voxels to be polymerized. This enables the 
fabrication of intricate and highly detailed structures but also increases printing time. Hatching 
distance (HD), on the other hand, refers to the spacing between parallel lines along the x-axis, 
which form each voxel or layer of the structure. HD plays a crucial role in generating internal 
infill by directing the laser along predetermined trajectories, thereby enhancing the stability and 
support of printed objects [36]. Contour lines are the outer boundary or shape of each layer of 
the fabricated object. In 2PP fabrication, contour lines define the overall shape and dimensions 
of the object.

2.10 Characterization techniques  
 
Optical microscope 
 An optical microscope is a scientific instrument that uses visible light and a system of lenses 
to magnify small objects, allowing detailed observation of their structure. It is relatively 
inexpensive, convenient, and user-friendly offering a quick and non-destructive approach to 
visualizing surface features, though their resolution is limited by the wavelength of light. The 
resolution of an optical microscope is limited by the diffraction of light, with the highest 
achievable resolution determined by Ernst Abbe’s equation. (See chapter 2.6)  
 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
Under ideal conditions, a traditional optical microscope using visible light can resolve details 
as small as 200 nanometers (nm). Another important aspect of optical microscope is the depth 
of focus. The depth of focus, which defines the range within which an object remains in sharp 
focus, is inversely related to the numerical aperture (NA) of the lens. Higher NA lenses provide 
better resolution but reduce focus, while lower NA lenses increase the focus but lowering the 
achievable resolution [37],[38]. 
Scanning Electron Microscope 
 Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) is a technology that uses a beam of electrons to scan a 
sample and create images of it. The electrons interact with the various atoms present on the 
sample surface, producing a variety of signals that give information about the shape and 
composition of the sample surface. (Resolution 1nm-20nm) [39]. In SEM, image formation 
relies on signals generated from interactions between the electron beam and the sample, 
categorized as elastic and inelastic. Elastic scattering involves the deflection of electrons with 
minimal energy loss, producing backscattered electrons (BSE), which are useful for imaging. 
Inelastic scattering occurs when incident electrons transfer energy to the sample, leading to the 
generation of secondary electrons (SE) with energies below 50 eV, which are also used for 
imaging. Other signals can be transmitted through SEM such as characteristic x-rays that are 
widely used for microanalytical technique and Auger electrons which are produced following 
the ionization of an atom by the incident electron beam and the falling back of an outer shell 
electron to fill an inner shell vacancy providing chemical information about the sample [40].For 
this thesis project the second electrons detector was utilized. Figure 2.10.1 indicates the regions 
from which different signals are detected.

Confocal Laser Scanning Microscopy  
Confocal Laser Scanning Microscopy is an advanced imaging tool in microscopy, providing 
high-resolution, high-contrast images by scanning specimens point-by-point and layer-by-layer 
with a focused laser. The key advantages of confocal laser include enhanced image resolution, 
contrast, and the capacity for 3D imaging, which are achieved through focused laser scanning 
and the selective pinhole system. The ability to focus precisely on specific depths without out-
of-focus interference is particularly useful in fields like cellular and molecular biology, 
neuroscience, and materials science, where structural analysis at microscopic levels is crucial. 
When combined with fluorescent dyes, confocal microscopy can target specific molecules or 
cell components, thereby making it invaluable for biomedical research and applications that 
require detailed visualization of subcellular structures [41]. Figure 2.10.2 presents a schematic

Chapter III: Materials and Methods 
3.1 Materials 
 For this thesis project, the first photoresist material was an organic negative tone photopolymer 
known under the brand name IP-Dip and containing 60–80% of 2-(hydroxymethyl)-2-[[(1-
oxoallyl) oxy] methyl]-1,3-propanediyl diacrylate (also known as pentaerythritol Tri acrylate). 
IP-Dip is a specially formulated photoresist developed for Nanoscribe’s innovative Dip in Laser 
Lithography (DiLL) technology. It functions as both an immersion medium and a 
photosensitive material by immersing the microscope objective directly into the liquid 
photoresist. With its refractive index precisely matched to the focusing optics, IP-Dip ensures 
optimal focus and delivers the highest resolution for DiLL applications [42]. A second resin 
was used during the project called HTY5 yellow provided by BMF (Boston Micro Fabrication). 
HTY5 resin is a high-performance engineering material with high strength, rigidity, and heat 
resistance, able to withstand temperatures up to 114oC. HTY5 enables high resolution features, 
making it suitable for a broad range of engineering and medical applications including those 
which require autoclave sterilization. Usually, this BMF resins are employed Projection Micro 
Stereolithography (PµSL) technology. This technology enables the production of 
microstructures with resolutions ranging from 2µm to 50µm with typical application been 
microfluidics and lab-on-chip devices [43]. For the remaining part of the thesis the two resins 
will be called IP-Dip and BMF.

3.4 Pyrolysis set up 
 The pyrolysis experiments were conducted at the Phoenix Lab using a quartz furnace. The 
temperature first increased from 23°C to 375°C at 3°C /min and kept constant for 1 h. Then the 
temperatures are ramped from 375°C to 420°C at 1°C /min and kept constant for 1 h. The last 
ramping consists of heating to 900°C at 3°C /min and dwelling at 900°C for 1 h. Finally, the 
temperature decreased to room temperature at 5 °C/min [46]. A schematic illustration of the 
process flow is presented in the theoretical part of the thesis (See chapter 2.8 and figure 2.8.1). 
Following the completion of the pyrolysis, the same characterization workflow been employed 
for the fabricated polymer structures repeatedly to assess the adhesion of the pyrolyzed 
microstructures to the substrate and to evaluate their shrinkage percentage. To calculate the 
shrinkage percentage of each structure the inner ring width was calculated and evaluated using 
a Zeiss Supra 40 VP SEM. For both experiments conducting either with IP-Dip or BMF the 
pyrolysis recipe was kept the same. 
3.5 Characterization and data acquisition 
 Throughout this thesis project, the morphological properties of the fabricated phase plates were 
thoroughly characterized. Initial inspections for misprints, substrate detachments, and general 
morphology were conducted using a Keyence digital optical microscope (VHX-X1 series).

mm was used. The secondary electron detector was used to enhance surface detail imaging and 
topographical contrast. Morphology analysis for all samples in all experiments was conducted 
using the Zeiss Supra 40 VP SEM using the same conditions.

4.1 Workflow  
Figure 4.1.1 represents the experimental workflow followed in this thesis for the fabrication 
and characterization of conductive on-chip microstructures using two-photon polymerization 
(TPP) and pyrolysis. The process begins with 3D design, where structures are digitally modeled 
with specific geometric parameters. The structures are then fabricated using TPP lithography, 
where factors such as laser power, scan speed, and resin properties will be tested to observe any

microscopy is used for an initial inspection of the structures, although its resolution is limited

adhesion, resolution, and structural quality. After further fine-tuning of the fabrication 
parameters, TPP printing on top of SixNy ultra-thin membranes will be utilized following the

4.2 IP-Dip fabrication on Si/(SixNy) 
 
 TPP fabrication parameters play a crucial role in the structural properties, print quality, and 
shrinkage behavior of fabricated structures after their conversion to carbon microstructures. 
These structural parameters are directly linked to the provided exposure dose. The TPP 
exposure dose and print quality primarily influenced on three key process parameters: laser 
average power (LP), scan speed (SS), and writing density [5]. The latter is quantified by the 
hatching (HD) and slicing (SD) distances between neighboring voxel lines. The exposure dose 
formula can be described as [36]:

 where I is the intensity adjusted by varying the laser power

 Exposure dose can determine the voxel size and distribution during two-photon 
polymerization. Variations in laser power and scan speed lead to significant changes in voxel 
morphology, directly impacting the structural fidelity and dimensions of the printed 
microstructures. Higher laser power increases the exposure dose, resulting in larger voxels due 
to more extensive polymerization. Conversely, higher scan speeds reduce the exposure dose, 
leading to smaller, more tightly confined voxels. Beforehand, we need to consider certain key 
process parameters; Laser power, scan speed, slicing distance, hatching distance and contour 
lines (See chapter 2.10) 
 Based on that, a case study was conducted using Si/SixNy substrates, IP-Dip resin and a 
combination of laser parameters with the scope of finding a proper combination that can offer 
high print quality and high shrinkage behavior. A smaller scale investigation leaded to an 
optimizing set of parameters after resin particles and burned spots were observed in structures 
fabricated with 45% laser power and misprints in structures produced with 30% laser power 
while higher scan speed faced limitations on the structures been incomplete or the rings were 
attached to each other based on the sensitivity of the structure itself. (See figure AP1). Then, an 
optimized study with new set of parameters was undertaken, by varying the laser power from 
34% to 40% with 2% step, scan speed of 4000μm/s to 7000μm/s with 1000μm/s step 
respectively and contour lines as 0 (no contour line) and 1. The total number of combinations 
are presented in the appendix section (See table AP1). Slicing and hatching distance were kept 
constant as 0.2μm and the printing orientation after each layer was 90 degrees. The total 
fabrication time of the sweep job with contour lines use was 10:32 minutes while without 
contour lines it was 8:52 minutes. Figure 4.2.1 illustrates the contour line sweep job after 
printing using an optical microscope plus a zoomed in individual structure. Both sweep jobs 
are presented in the appendix section. (See figure AP2). Each structure has been designed with 
a certain size of 60μm x 100μm x 1μm. After TPP print, the structural properties such as inner 
ring width and height were analyzed as described in subchapter 3.5. Figure 4.2.2 presents two

that can alter the beam profile of a TEM laser beam. To achieve that, a novel approach was 
utilized. 49 holes with a size of approximately 56.5μm x 56.5μm were created on silicon wafer 
substrates. Figure 4.7.1 illustrates the holes present on the substrates from top view and side

In this phase, a challenge arises of how a structure can be fabricated inside the walls of the holes 
but also on the top of it. To address this issue, a new CAD design was fabricated. (See chapter 
3.3). Firstly, slicing and hatching distance were kept the same as the previous experiments as 
0.2μm as well as the addition of contour lines. Secondly, the new design is bulkier and taller 
than the previous phase plate designs. Based on that, laser power slightly decreased to 38% and 
the scan speed was increased to 10000μm/s to reduce the exposure dose. As the goal now is to 
print not only to the surface of the substrate but inside as well, a new strategy was implemented 
to surpass any limitation and print successfully. The laser beam was aligned perfectly to the 
middle of the hole and the interface was found manually. The dip-in mode principle is to print

20μm below. This will allow the laser to start writing from inside the hole until it reaches the

the second one, it was decided to split the structure into two parts and print with two separate 
printing procedures. By this was, it is possible to accomplish to eliminate the first tackle by 
optimizing the parameters at the same time since the inner blocks didn’t face any burn spots.

Chapter V: Conclusion 
5.1 Concluding remarks 
 
The research conducted for the purpose of this master's project aimed at fabricating conductive 
on-chip structures as phase plates suitable for TEM beam shaping. To achieve this, a two-step 
fabrication process was carried out. First, two-photon polymerization (TPP) lithography was 
employed to fabricate high-quality polymer structures. Then, a post-treatment process, 
specifically pyrolysis, was used to convert the polymer structures into carbon microstructures, 
improving their electrical properties. Two different materials were tested to determine which 
one is more suitable for the application. The exposure dose during fabrication was examined to 
assess its impact on the structure's size, print quality, and adhesion behavior. After examining 
several sets of parameters, including laser power, scan speed, and contour lines, it was 
concluded that higher laser power results in a higher exposure dose, which in turn leads to larger 
structures (in height and width). IP-Dip resin, optimized for TPP, has a well-characterized 
response to femtosecond laser exposure, providing greater stability with varying laser 
parameters and higher polymerization efficiency compared to BMF resin. BMF resin has lower 
polymerization efficiency, meaning that even at higher exposure doses, the material does not 
solidify as effectively as IP-Dip. After examining the shrinkage percentage of both resins, it 
was found that IP-Dip has a higher shrinkage, 7% higher compared to BMF, which is influenced 
by the TPP fabrication parameters and their differences in composition. 
 A new substrate design was introduced, featuring a SixNy ultra-thin membrane with a thickness 
of approximately 36 nm. This design was chosen to absorb as little energy as possible when the 
phase plates are employed between the TEM and the sample. Using the optimized set of 
parameters and IP-Dip resin from the first case studies, 3D phase plate structures were 
fabricated, with a printing time of 22 seconds per membrane. In one substrate, 25 objects were 
successfully fabricated in less than one hour, including movements and manual alignment for 
each membrane (25 in total). After observing their pyrolytic activity, the breaking point of the 
photopolymerization process was tested, demonstrating that thinner structures can be fabricated 
down to a 0.4μm distance between their inner rings. S. Achenbach etc. [51] by optimizing the 
UV-Lithography process achieved a minimum feature size of isolated structures of 1.5μm 
approximately three times thicker than ours fabricated minimum feature size. P. Sahoo etc. [52] 
using E-beam lithography managed to produce lines with 0.27μm thick and a space between 
them 0.17μm but with higher processing time compared to TPP lithography. 
In the final part of the experiments, a different substrate was introduced. A Si wafer with 49 
holes, each measuring 56.5μm x 56.5μm. A new design was implemented to achieve 3D 
fabrication on top of the substrate while also printing in parallel inside the hole. Well-
performing and stable structures were fabricated using TPP for the first time with the 
Nanoscribe Photonic Professional GT+ in Dip-in mode. By lowering the Z-position after 
interface finding, printing could successfully start from the desired height inside the hole and 
finish a few micrometers above the Si surface, depending on the starting point. Lastly, the 
inability to know exactly if the fabricated structures were fully attached to the sidewalls of the 
holes all the way down plus overexposure problems in the Si substrate made us to implement a 
new strategy.  
 
==PAGE:50==
50 
 
 The design was split into two parts: one part that touched the surface of the structure was 
printed using Dip-in mode with optimized parameters with less exposure dose, and the second 
part, with the Z-axis inverted (oil immersion), started printing from the surface and moved 
down to the depth of the hole (reverse movement as initially attempted). However, interface 
finding proved challenging, and despite efforts to optimize the process, issues persisted. 
Because of time, no further exploration of this printing method was pursued within the scope 
of this project, but it may be noteworthy for future investigations. 
5.1 Implications for future research  
 
 Considering the two novel approaches for fabricating three-dimensional microstructures on 
ultra-thin membranes and within micro-scale holes, several potential implementations can be 
proposed. The successful demonstration of free-standing printing for the first time opens new 
possibilities for technological applications. However, to fully exploit this advancement, the 
issue of burn spots on the hooks must be addressed by optimizing the printing parameters. Si 
substrates are higher refractive index, and they are more sensitive to light back reflections. This 
can be achieved by reducing the laser power (and thus the exposure dose), or by splitting the 
design into parts and using a combination of Dip-in mode and oil immersion, as previously 
illustrated. Further clarification from Nanoscribe GmbH regarding the interface values for each 
printing process is necessary. As demonstrated, switching printing modes mid-process and 
splitting the design into two parts led to interface misalignment, highlighting the need for 
improved alignment strategies. 
 Additionally, a refined design with an optimized set of parameters could enable fabrication 
using only Dip-in mode, thereby reducing complexity and minimizing potential errors. Figure 
4.7.2 illustrates that the printed blocks do not perfectly adhere to the sidewalls due to surface 
roughness, which can be mitigated through improved substrate preparation. For further 
investigation, the fabricated structures can undergo pyrolysis to analyze shrinkage ratios, 
incorporating these findings into future designs. As shown in Figure 4.7.6, the inclusion of 
external rods around the ring may help compensate for dimensional changes during pyrolysis. 
This could facilitate testing of phase plate objects under TEM, allowing for beam shape 
modifications. For fabricating ultra-thin membranes, a future step would be to extend the rods 
of the inner rings and test the phase plate objects under TEM, enabling beam shape 
modifications.

Fabrication Protocols 
 
Apr1: Ultra-thin membranes TPP guidelines 
 
• 
Prior print follows pre-wash of the substrates. (10 min acetone, 10 min acetone ,5 min 
IPA,1 min DYI and dry for 5min) 
• 
For Dip-in mode clean the objective with IPA and whip it with cleanroom wipe and 
lens cleaning paper tissue 
• 
After interface finding, there is a change of wrong automatically interface (If its over 
11.5μm) Then load a quick job file and let it print to find the interface properly. 
• 
Track membranes (5x5 array) of approx. 71.5μm x 71.5μm size 
• 
Move a bit further and then perform manual alignment. (See below) 
• 
Find interface manually in the edge between Si and Si3N4 membrane. 
• 
Apply 5μm step and move from one edge to another both horizontal and vertical and 
find the center of the membrane 
• 
Load job and start job 
• 
After job complete, and one array is fully print move up and print text description of 
the printed row. 
• 
Follow the instructions about closing the printer and sample aqua solution to resolve 
the resin 
Manual alignment 
• 
On the NanoWrite window in the GWL mini script, type in "manualcontrol" and press 
Submit. 
• 
On the new window that pops up, set the Laser power (%) to 5 and click on "Shutter 
closed" to open the laser shutter 
• 
On the ZEN software, select "Draw annotation scale bar" and remove crosshair 
• 
Drag one of the edges of the scale bar and align it to the bright spot. 
• 
Drag the value of the scale bar in the outer part of the image by zoom out 
• 
On the Manual Control window, close the laser shutter and close the Manual Control 
window 
If there’s a tilt correction that needs to be applied, then: 
• 
On the NanoWrite window, make sure the "Invert Z-Axis" button is turned on (light 
green)" 
• 
Click on the Camera tab 
• 
On the Marker tab:  
• 
set M0 design x,y as (0,0); move crosshair to the left corner of a feature and click 
"Get Position" to record the real x,y coordinates 
• 
Set M1 design x,y as (1,0); move crosshair to the right corner of the same feature and 
click "Get Position" to record the real x,y coordinates 
• 
Make sure Apply to: All is selected 
• 
Click on Transformation button to activate the new coordinate system

Apr2: Holes freestanding fabrication 
 
• 
Prior print measure with the optical microscope exact dimension of the holes 
• 
For Dip-in mode clean the objective with IPA and whip it with cleanroom wipe and 
lens cleaning paper tissue 
• 
After interface finding, there is a change of wrong automatically interface (If it’s over 
11.5μm) Then load a quick job file and let it print to find the interface properly. 
• 
Track holes  
• 
Move a bit further and then perform manual alignment. (See below) 
• 
Load print here job file and print on top of the hole 
• 
Find interface manually based on the radius of the hole 
• 
Apply 5μm step and move from one edge to another both horizontal and vertical and 
find the center of the hole 
• 
Depending on the size of the structure, lower down the stage using the rotor from 
microscopes monitor and see the values of the nanowrite window. (NOT from 
microscope monitor)  
• 
Always leave some μm to be printed in the surface (If total structure is 20μm lower 
the stage 18μm approx.) 
• 
Load job and start job 
• 
After the job is finished, the surface as well as the print here text should appear. (Note 
that it can be variation, and the interface can change so the job needs to be repeated) 
• 
Follow the instructions about closing the printer and sample aqua solution to resolve 
the resin 
• 
For the two-split design with Dip-in mode and oil-immersion, First find interface print 
normally to the substrate then after printing press invert axis find interface manually, 
load job and then move 2μm down 
 
The manual alignment procedure is the same as for the ultra-thin membranes.
