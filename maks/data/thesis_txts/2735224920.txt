Abstract
i
Abstract
The study introduces an innovative Raman spectroscopy (RS) system enhanced by an
autosampler designed for high-throughput whole blood analysis. By integrating an au-
tosampler with a rotation mechanism, homogeneity and eﬀiciency was achieved, which
increased sample credibility. The approach demonstrated reliability in distinguishing
between sample concentrations with high accuracy, as evidenced by standard curves for
ethanol and isopropanol (IPA). Additionally, the system’s software automation facili-
tated rapid and smooth sample processing, outperforming traditional manual methods
in both speed and sample size. The research emphasizes the potential of automation
in RS systems, suggesting a shift in that direction. Future investigations should focus
on solving limitations such as mixing issues and blood clotting, with the aim of further
enhancing system performance and applicability in clinical diagnostics.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In medical diagnostics, whole blood analysis techniques must be accurate, fast, and
reliable. Traditional methods for determining blood types, like ABO typing, can be
time-consuming and resource intensive. This has led to a search for new solutions that
can improve how blood analysis is done. One promising approach is RS, which can
quickly and accurately identify different components in blood, including ABO blood
types, without needing complicated preparation steps.
In recent years, high-throughput RS has emerged as a promising biomedical research
and diagnostics technique, with numerous studies exploring its potential across various
domains. While highlighting its advantages and limitations, these studies provide valu-
able insights into the capabilities of RS. One such study by [1] focuses on the label-free
identification of biochemical footprints of key mutations in acute myeloid leukemia. It
demonstrates RS’s potential in identifying biochemical changes, emphasizing its capabil-
ity for disease-specific analysis. However, the study focuses more on disease identification
than broader applications like blood typing, which are central to the thesis. The second
study [2] presents an innovative approach by combining RS with fluorescence for en-
hanced analysis in transcriptomic. Transcriptomics is the study of an organism’s entire
set of RNA transcripts, which are the molecules that carry instructions from DNA to
produce proteins. This offers a pathway for multi-modal analysis of blood samples. The
third study [3] expand the application of RS to urine, demonstrating its versatility for
liquid biopsy applications. This demonstration highlights the broader diagnostic poten-
tial of spectroscopic techniques. The fourth study [4] highlights using RS combined with
machine learning for predictive diagnostics in endometriosis patients. This aligns well
with the thesis’s emphasis on automation and advanced analysis.
The project aims to develop an autosampler which can be controlled via a Python
system that combines RS with machine learning to automate ABO blood typing. Using
automation and a sequence algorithm, the project hopes to make the process faster and
more accurate than manual methods using an autosampler.
Hypothesis
Integrating an autosampler into the high-throughput whole-blood analysis process is an-
ticipated to expedite the procedure and bolster accuracy and precision when contrasted
with conventional manual sample preparation methods. Regarding the hypothesis, the

2.1
Introduction
This chapter will cover key topics necessary for understanding autosampler development.
Firstly, it will introduce RS by delving into the interaction between light and matter,
covering absorption and scattering phenomena. Subsequently, it delves into the various
scattering types, emphasizing Rayleigh and Raman scattering processes. Following this,
the sections on electric dipole moment and polarizability elucidate their significance in
RS. The study also discusses selection rules and the classification of molecules as either
Raman active or Raman inactive. Finally, it focuses on aspects such as blood typing,
the evolution of automation technology in high-throughput blood analysis, and blood
mixing.
2.2
Raman spectroscopy
The physical world centers around the interactions between light and matter.
This
encompasses a diverse range of phenomena, from the everyday experience of seeing
objects illuminated by sunlight to the abstract realms of quantum mechanics. One of the
powerful techniques that exploit these interactions is RS, a method that provides detailed
information about molecular vibrations and crystal structures [5]. Light, which is made
up of photons, exhibits both wave-like and particle-like properties. When light interacts
with matter, several processes can occur. The photons can be absorbed, scattered or
pass through a material.
2.2.1
Absorption and scattering
Absorption and scattering are fundamental processes that occur when light interacts
with matter. Absorption occurs when a photon’s energy aligns precisely with the energy
gap between electron energy levels within an atom. In such a scenario, the electron
absorbs the photon’s energy and transitions to a higher energy state. This transition

4

can lead to the excitation of the atom.
Subsequently, the absorbed energy may be
re-emitted as a new photon, a phenomenon known as emission [5].
Scattering, occurs when photons deviate from their original path after interacting
with matter. There are two main types of scattering, elastic and inelastic scattering.
Elastic scattering, also known as Rayleigh scattering, involves a change in the direction
of the photon without any change in energy. Inelastic scattering, particularly interesting
in RS, involves a change in the photon’s direction and energy. This type of scattering
results from the interaction between photons and the vibrational modes of molecules [5].
Vibrational modes refer to the distinct ways in which molecules oscillate, influencing
their overall structure and behavior.
2.2.2
Types of scattering
Scattering refers to the phenomenon where particles or molecules alter the direction of
light propagation. Various types of scattering are elucidated in Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: The diagram illustrates the molecular energy states and the processes of
Raman scattering. At the bottom are the molecule’s ground vibrational states. Above
them, dashed lines represent virtual states. The vertical arrows show transitions due to
photon interactions. In Rayleigh scattering, photons are elastically scattered, leaving the
molecule’s energy unchanged. Stokes Raman scattering occurs when photons transfer
energy to the molecule, exciting it to a higher vibrational state. Anti-Stokes Raman
scattering happens when an excited molecule transfers energy to the photons, dropping
to a lower vibrational state. Both Raman processes shift the photon energy, providing
insights into the molecule’s vibrational structure [6].

2.2.2.1
Rayleigh Scattering
Rayleigh scattering, a phenomenon categorized under elastic scattering, occurs when
light interacts with particles that are considerably smaller in size compared to the wave-
length of the incident light. This causes the light to scatter in various directions. The
intensity of Rayleigh scattering is inversely proportional to the fourth power of the
wavelength of light, expressed as
I ∝1
λ4.
(2.1)
Here I represents the intensity of the scattered light, which is the amount of light
scattered in various directions, λ symbolizes the wavelength of the incident light. This
means that as the wavelength of light decreases, the scattering becomes more pronounced.
Thus, shorter wavelength light undergoes more significant scattering than longer wave-
length light [5].
2.2.2.2
Raman scattering
Raman scattering is an inelastic light scattering phenomenon where incident and scat-
tered photons have different energies due to energy exchange with molecules. In Stokes
scattering, the scattered photon loses energy to the molecule, shifting to a lower energy
state. Conversely, anti-Stokes scattering involves the molecule transferring energy to
the photon, resulting in a scattered photon with higher energy. The energy shift cor-
responds to the energy of a molecular vibrational mode, allowing Raman scattering to
reveal molecular identities and vibrational states. Stokes scattering is more common,
as molecules are more likely to be in the ground state, making Stokes signals stronger.
Anti-Stokes scattering is less frequent and occurs when molecules are excited, leading
to weaker signals. Both types of scattering are critical tools for analyzing a material’s
molecular composition and vibrational characteristics [5, 7].

2.2.3
Electric Dipole Moment
Light interacting with a molecule can experience a frequency shift due to the molecule’s
vibrations. This phenomenon is known as Raman scattering. It occurs because the
molecule’s non-uniform charge distribution creates an electric dipole moment that inter-
acts with the light. As the light interacts, it can either gain or lose energy, resulting in
a change in its frequency. The concept of the electric dipole moment, µ, is fundamen-
tal to understanding RS. The electric dipole moment indicates the distance between
positive and negative charges within a molecule, essentially measuring its imbalance in
its electronic charge distribution. The electric dipole moment is significant because it
determines whether a molecule can interact with incident light in a way that produces
a Raman scattering effect [5].
The electric dipole moment is a vector quantity defined as the product of the mag-
nitude of the charge and the distance between the charges, expressed as
µ = qd.
(2.5)
The electric charge is denoted by q and d is the separation vector between the charges.
Non-uniform molecule charge distribution can produce a non-zero electric dipole mo-
ment, contributing to Raman scattering. In this process, incident photons interact with
molecules, inducing changes in vibrational states. The intensity of Raman scattering
is proportional to the square of the electric dipole moment |µ|2. Molecules with a sig-
nificant electric dipole moment are more likely to exhibit strong Raman scattering and
thereby produce strong Raman spectra [5].
2.2.4
Polarizability
Polarizability is a property measuring the susceptibility of an atom or molecule to form
temporary electric dipoles, which is induced by an external electric field. It is expressed
as the ratio of the induced dipole moment µ to the applied electric field E [5]
α = µ

In RS, the polarizability is a crucial factor influencing shifts in wavenumbers
observed in spectra. A high polarizability results in larger wavenumber shifts, thereby
impacting the strength of the Raman signals [9].

2.2.5
Selection rules
Selection rules in quantum mechanics are constraints that dictate which transitions are
allowed between the energy levels of a system. They are derived from the principles of
angular momentum conservation and parity. For an in-depth explanation, confer [10]. In
RS, selection rules are a fundamental principle that governs which vibrational transitions
are allowed or forbidden in the Raman scattering process. It helps to determine which
vibrational modes will be observable in the Raman spectrum of a molecule.
The primary selection rule for a Raman transition is that the molecular polarizability
must change during the molecular vibration. This can be expressed as
∆α ̸= 0.
(2.7)
The molecular polarizability, α, indicates the degree of resistance of an electron cloud
within an atom to be altered by an electric field.
An atom with its electron cloud
positioned farther from the nucleus exhibits a greater polarizibility compared to an
atom where the electron cloud is closer to the nucleus [10].
Another principle that governs which vibrational transitions are allowed to occur
during a Raman scattering event is the Mutual Exclusion Rule. It provides guidelines
for determining which modes will be observed in a Raman spectrum of a molecule. To
gain a comprehensive understanding, it is recommended to consult [11].
2.2.5.1
Raman Active and Raman Inactive
In RS, a vibration is Raman active if it changes the polarizability of the molecule, which
indicates how easily the molecule’s electron cloud can be distorted. Conditions for a
vibration to be Raman active include [9, 10]:
• Change in Polarizability: For a vibration to be Raman active, it must alter the
molecule’s polarizability causing a temporary distortion of its electron cloud.
• Symmetry: For a vibration to be Raman active, it must exhibit symmetry, mean-
ing that the atoms within the molecule move in a coordinated manner, preserving
the molecule’s overall symmetry throughout the vibration.
• Non-Degeneracy: The vibration must be non-degenerate, meaning each vibra-
tion has its own unique energy level and does not overlap with other vibrations.
To sum up, the selection rules in RS dictates that only vibrational modes that result in
a change in molecular polarizability are Raman active. This helps to interpret Raman
spectra and getting information about the vibrational properties of a molecule.

Blood typing
The ABO blood typing system, pivotal in transfusion medicine and forensic science,
traces its origins to Karl Landsteiner’s groundbreaking discovery of blood group anti-
gens in 1900. Initially, the determination of blood types was a manual and error-prone
process.
However, the advent of technological advancements has revolutionized this
field. Automated testing and molecular typing methods have significantly enhanced the
accuracy and reliability of blood typing. Future automation and integration with bioin-
formatics systems promise to maintain blood safety and enrich our understanding of
human biology [12]. Blood typing is the classification of blood based on the presence or
absence of specific antigens on the surface of red blood cells (RBC), primarily ABO and
Rh factors. The ABO system categorizes blood into four main types, A, B, AB, and O,
based on the presence of A and/or B antigens. The Rh system further classifies blood
as positive or negative, depending on the presence of the RhD antigen. Sample homoge-
nization ensures uniformity in blood samples, preventing the separation of components
which could affect test results [13]. Blood clotting and dehomogenization during blood
sampling refers to separating blood components and forming clots, impacting sample
quality and analysis. If the reader is interested in learning more about blood theory
confer [14, 15].
The Hamilton Microlab STAR Autosampler is a laboratory automated machine
that offers eﬀiciency and precision for scientific applications. Developed by Hamilton
Robotics, this instrument is crucial for high-throughput lab work, liquid handling and
sample processing [16]. Automated blood typing offers several advantages over manual
methods. Firstly, it significantly enhances eﬀiciency by processing samples at a faster
rate, thereby improving laboratory throughput. This increased speed allows for quicker
turnaround times in blood typing processes. Additionally, automated systems provide
higher levels of accuracy and precision compared to manual techniques. By reducing
the risk of errors associated with manual sample handling and analysis, automated
blood typing ensures more reliable results. However, despite these advantages, there
are certain drawbacks to automated blood typing systems. One significant challenge is
the high initial cost associated with acquiring and implementing automated equipment.
Moreover, the technical complexity of automated blood typing systems presents another
challenge. Operating and maintaining these systems require specialized training and ex-
pertise, which may not be available in all laboratory settings. Additionally, automated
systems are reliant on electricity and software to function properly. This dependency
makes them vulnerable to power outages and software glitches, potentially disrupting
operations and delaying critical blood typing processes. High-throughput in biomedical
sciences means processing much information or samples quickly and eﬀiciently. It is
like having a fast and automated system that can handle a large number of tests in a
short amount of time. This helps researchers to gather data and conduct experiments
more quickly and effectively [17]. The Hamilton machine is used as inspiration for the
developed autosampler.

The asymmetric penalized least squares (arPLS) method [20] stands out as a so-

This study focuses on developing a computational model capable of accurately clas-

fundamental in medical fields. To achieve this, a binary classifier is employed, a type of
machine learning algorithm designed to distinguish between two classes. To refine the
model’s ability to make these distinctions, it has been fine-tuned using specific hyperpa-

process of the model. For the classifier, the focus is on two parameters C and γ. The C

visible line that separates blood types O and A based on specific features observed in the

many training examples as possible correctly. However, setting C too high might cause
the model to overfit, meaning it becomes too tailored to the training data and may not
perform well on new, unseen data. On the other hand, a lower C value allows for a more
flexible decision boundary, which can tolerate some misclassifications to achieve a more
general understanding of the difference between blood types O and A. The γ parameter
plays a critical role when the classifier uses certain mathematical functions like RBF
kernels to transform the data into a more suitable form for making classifications. The
higher the γ, the closer other examples must be to be affected.

Implementing RS for blood typing introduces an advanced method that exploits RS’s

Previous studies utilizing manual blood typing reported an impressive average AUC

Although these scores are commendable, they do not reach the benchmark established
by manual methods. Possible reasons for this discrepancy will be discussed, along with
an exploration of potential improvements.
5.1.3
Model performance and stability
Two models, BWL 1 and BWL 2, were developed with distinct hyperparameters. BWL
1, characterized by C=1000.0 and gamma=10.0, exhibited a mean AUC of 0.84 ± 0.10,
indicating sensitivity to training data but potentially limited generalizability. Conversely,

What is interesting is that the arPLS method does not need any prior information about
what is in the sample, no identification of peaks, and no specific assumptions about the
distribution of background noise. This makes it simple to use with a variety of spectra.
5.2
Limitations
This section highlights the study’s limitations, addressing factors that may impact the
interpretation and generalization of findings. Recognizing these constraints is crucial for
understanding the study’s scope and implications.
5.2.1
Mixing issues
The decision to deviate from the recommended liquid volume range of 100-300 µL per
well to a larger volume of 400 µL in the experimental setup was primarily motivated by
the practical need for suﬀicient blood volume during measurements. While this adjust-
ment addressed concerns about potential blood loss, it introduced possible challenges in
mixing eﬀiciency, particularly affecting experimental outcomes, notably in the context
of blood clotting. The deviation may have contributed to uneven mixing, possibly caus-
ing sedimentation of red blood cells in some wells, leading to heterogeneous conditions.
The success of the first 96-well plate indicated that the adjustment might not have imme-
diately affected the experimental process. However, the failure of the second well plate
after 26 samples highlighted a critical issue blood clotting that occurred during the night,
obstructing the capillary and leading to inaccurate measurements. The increased liquid
volume is proposed as a potential explanation for the clotting phenomenon, suggesting
that uneven mixing in some wells might have left red blood cells sedimented.
This outcome underscores the delicate balance between ensuring an adequate sample
size and maintaining optimal mixing conditions. Further investigations into refining the
mixing mechanisms, perhaps revisiting the recommended volume range, and considering
additional measures to prevent blood clotting may be warranted for the optimization of
future experimental setups.

5.2.2
Tubing sizes and pump speed
In Figure 3.3 the three connectors indicated a change of tubing sizes in the fluidic circuit.
The first connector was between the needle and the capillary, where an rpm of 25 to 45
posed no issues in drawing the blood through the capillary that has an inner diameter
of 250 µm. An issue arose for the second connection, the capillary and the tube inlet
of the peristaltic pump.
The blood slowed down due to the change in tubing sizes.
The peristaltic pump’s tube diameter is 880 um [26], and because it is larger than the
capillary’s inner diameter, the flow rate would need to increase to compensate for the
change in tubing size. Since the peristaltic pump was running continuously with the
same rpm speed, this could not be possible, however. Thus, the first flowing issue is
seen. The third connector had similar problems, here the tube outlet of the peristaltic
pump is connected to another tube leading to the waste container. The waste container
tube is not identical to the size of the peristaltic pump, so the blood also moved slower
here. This could be fixed if the peristaltic pump speed varies or if similar tubing sizes
are replaced with the current ones.
Variations in tubing sizes and connectors pose challenges to maintaining consistent
blood flow rates, potentially leading to differences in experimental outcomes, particu-
larly concerning clotting phenomena. Implementing sensors or timing adjustments to
modulate pump speed in response to tubing changes could somewhat mitigate this is-
sue. However, the inherent limitations of maintaining a constant pump speed in the
face of varying volume pressures highlight the need for innovative solutions. Exploring
alternative pump technologies or refining experimental setups to minimize tubing varia-
tions and their impact on blood flow dynamics could offer promising avenues for future
research. Systematically evaluating the effects of tubing size changes on clotting phenom-
ena and implementing strategies to standardize experimental conditions can enhance the
reliability and reproducibility of the experimental results.
5.2.3
Camera inconsistencies
In the previous procedure, manipulating camera settings for spectroscopic analysis pre-
dominantly relied on the singular use of a central wavelength within the desired spectrum.
This methodology exhibited considerable utility when measurements were conducted
manually, as adjustments to the central wavelength were also executed by hand. Mod-
ifying the central wavelength is crucial to guarantee that the Raman spectra span the
requisite interval for accurate peak identification. In response to this necessity, advance-
ments in automation have facilitated the sequential alteration of central wavelengths,
commencing with a spectrum centered at 830 nm, subsequently shifting to 880 nm. This
process is systematically repeated for each measurement conducted. Initial validation,
conducted via the analysis of the first well plate using the generated Raman spectra,
confirmed the eﬀicacy of this automated approach in maintaining the desired spectral
coverage. The performance metrics indicated high reliability and accuracy, underscor-
ing the benefits of automating central wavelength adjustments. However, discrepancies

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion and Outlook
This thesis has introduced an enhanced RS system, augmented by an autosampler, de-
signed for high-throughput analysis of whole blood samples. A significant innovation of
this work is the development of an autosampler capable of managing sample procedures
while ensuring sample homogeneity. This was achieved through the integration of a
rotation mechanism that facilitates thorough mixing, thereby maintaining sample uni-
formity and minimizing contamination. The effectiveness of this approach is evidenced
by the standard curves for ethanol and IPA, which demonstrate the autosampler’s re-
liable performance with average detection accuracies of 83.5% and 97.9%, respectively.
The accuracy of the system was further validated by a blind concentration test, where
it successfully identified the correct concentrations in 10 out of 12 samples.
A notable advancement is the software integration within the autosampler, which
automates the sequence of sampling, cleaning, and rotating procedures for a 96-well plate.
This enhancement significantly improves the eﬀiciency of the RS system compared to
manual procedures, achieving a throughput of 122 samples out of 192 in approximately
two days a substantial improvement in both speed and volume. The AUC scores further
illustrated the system’s performance with an accuracy of 0.848 and 0.869, respectively,
for BWL 1 and BWL 2. These results indicate that the automated system is capable of
accurately classifying samples.
Looking ahead, several areas for further investigation and improvement have been
identified. One challenge encountered was mixing issues with the second blood plate,
where only 26 out of 96 samples were successfully processed. Blood clotting, potentially
exacerbated by variances in tubing sizes within the fluidic system and pump speeds, was
identified as a significant issue. A proposed solution includes standardizing tubing sizes
to match those used by the peristaltic pump and implementing automatic pump speed
adjustments based on the fluid’s position within the tubing. Additionally, integrating an
external circuit controlled by an Arduino could facilitate precise control over pump speed
and direction through serial communication. Another area for enhancement involves
incorporating sensors to detect the presence of blood in a well before performing Raman
spectroscopy. This would prevent the generation of unnecessary spectra, optimizing data
processing and machine learning efforts. Further research could also address unexpected
camera responses that resulted in unusable data for certain samples, as well as improving
tubing configurations to prevent blood coagulation.
Lastly, while the autosampler prototype constructed from PLA proved effective, fu-
ture iterations could benefit from fabrication in aluminum. This material choice would

application in the field.
