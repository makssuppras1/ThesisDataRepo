Abstract
Flood modelling is the discipline of predicting how and where water will flow across a
landscape, from drainage systems to storm surges. The ability to perform this fast and
accurately is in great demand worldwide for flood risk management and only increasing
as climate change disrupt global weather patterns. The industry standard for this mod­
elling is the MIKE software suite, and has been for decades. For overland flood modelling,
the topic of this thesis, MIKE solves a modified version of 2D shallow water equations,
iteratively updating the water level in each grid cell based on the values in the previous
time step. These equations provide reliably accurate results however, requires significant
compute and hence may be perceived as somewhat slow to solve in a practical setting.
In the design of flood warning systems, simulation time is critical when determining which
regions will become flooded. To address the performance aspects a surrogate modelling
strategy has the potential to deliver significant acceleration of runtime in the online set­
ting. This thesis investigates training a neural network with a U­net style architecture on
simulation data generated a MIKE model, with the goal of being able to accurately sim­
ulate flooding in situations not seen during training. Training of the model is slow and
depends on using a numerical model to generate training data, however, once trained the
surrogate simulation demonstrates speedups as high as 250 times faster than MIKE, with
only minor deterioration in simulation quality, estimated via a mass balance and a mean
square error loss.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
With the rise of climate change, extreme weather events are becoming more common­
place, and in turn more severe. One of the most impactful weather events that affect many
people is flooding. It can be caused by excessive rainfall or tidal surges, but whatever the
cause it can wreak havoc on populated areas. To manage these events properly, it is
vital that the time and intensity of them can be accurately predicted such that infrastruc­
ture damage and loss of human life can be mitigated as much as possible. While several
models currently exist, MIKE being one such method that is a central focus of this thesis,
their strengths lie in physical accuracy, and not in speed. If a model could be developed
with similar accuracy, but substantially faster simulation time, it could provide much value.
1.2
literature review
The field of flood modelling is rife with approaches, the most canonical being solving
systems of differential equations. This is the primary approach used by the MIKE (MIKE
2021) software suite, which is used in this thesis to generate the training and testing data.
For this thesis, the relevant equations are the shallow water equations(SWE), which are
explained in section 2.1. There are other commercial solutions such as OpenFlows Flood
(OpenFlows FLOOD 2022), but this family of solutions all suffer the same problem: Long
simulation times. This is where surrogate modelling, the goal of this thesis, is relevant.
Surrogate modelling is the process of creating a model, the goal of which is not to emulate
a physical quantity directly, but instead, to emulate another model. The traditional tradeoff
of this is to increase simulation times, often by many orders of magnitude, but at the cost
of accuracy. This is unsurprising, as surrogate models will always be an approximation of
an approximation.
One approach to flood modelling is to predict which areas in a given simulation region
will become flooded, and by how much, ignoring the path that rain or other water sources
have taken to flood the area. The paper U­FLOOD (Löwe et al. 2021) does precisely
this. It takes an image segmentation approach, where the training data given are flood
maps, showing precisely which areas will be flooded after a given amount of rainfall.
This is done by the use of a convolutional U­net model, which will be explained in more
detail in section 2.2.5. A similar approach was taken by the CA￿ffé(B. Jamali et al. 2019)
paper. They achieve the same goal with a different method, namely the use of cellular
automata. This approach also proved promising, but a strong constraint is that each pixel
in a grid only receives information from its direct neighbours. An obvious advantage to
these approaches of flood segmentation is the lack of a need for simulation of the water
flows, which adds significant computational overhead. However, doing this also puts a
layer of abstraction between the predictions and the underlying physics of the flooding.
While the results of these papers are impressive, this could in theory pose problems.
Several advances have been made in the realm of solving systems differential equa­
tions(Karniadakis et al. 2021), in particular for fluid modelling as well, (Mahmoudabad­
bozchelou, Em. Karniadakis, and S. Jamali 2022) using neural networks, specifically
physics informed neural networks(PINN). These networks have been used to learn the
complete solution to specific PDEs, given a fixed set of boundary conditions. An obvious
limitation of these methods is that the boundary conditions and PDE parameters are fixed,
requiring retraining to adapt to new constraints.

There have been steps made to ease this limitation, namely DeepONets(S. Wang, H.
Wang, and Perdikaris 2021). These networks can learn a family of solutions, such that
retraining is not required when constants are altered, or even when boundary conditions
are altered.
Generalized residue networks(gResNets) have also shown promise here, used as pre­

an equal resolution to the baseline hydrodynamic models.(Palmitessa et al. 2022) Inspired
by these results, a natural extension to the problem domain would be a fast simulation of
not only drainage networks but flooding over a 2­D domain, such as a landscape.

• Can we accurately and quickly simulate 2­D flooding using neural networks?
• Is the U­net architecture an appropriate one for this problem?
• Can the chosen model generalize to simulations beyond the training dataset?
• Can the surrogate model reduce simulation times?

Background
A flood simulation is in essence a fluid simulation, but typically on a much coarser scale.
Fluid simulations are often used for research or computer graphics, but the detail present
in these is less relevant in the case of flooding. Here, it is more interesting to see how
large amounts of fluid will move through a landscape, often a city or area near a city.
This section will describe some established methods of simulating fluids and flooding,
from the direct calculation of the governing equations to the surrogate modelling of said
equations. As this thesis uses machine learning, the theory behind this is explained, from
simpler neural networks up to the architecture used.

2.2
Surrogate Modelling
While solving these equations directly provides good results, they are slow to solve in
general. There have been many approaches developed to indirectly solve these equa­
tions, by way of surrogate modelling. This approach takes the solved equations, for a
given simulation, as input and attempts to recreate them. Below is an incomplete list of
some previous approaches.

2.2.2
Neural Networks
Neural networks have become an incredibly powerful tool in the modern world, useful in a
vast array of tasks. These range from image recognition to outlier detection, to 3­D puzzle
solving and much more. Part of the power of these networks is in the many different ways
they can be built, and how these architectures can be leveraged for a given problem. To
understand how they work, it is best to begin with the simplest architecture.
Perceptron
The most basic neural network is the perceptron, shown in figure 2.2. It is built up out
of a range of inputs, i, which are multiplied together with respective weights, w. These
are then summed together, and run through an activation function, f. The result of this
function is the output o. To allow this model to fit itself to some data, the weights need
to be iteratively updated in some fashion, until convergence is reached. For this simple
model, there are many methods, but here backpropagation will be explained, as it is a
much more general method, also relevant to more complex network architectures.

The loss function will of course not be known in its totality or this problem would be trivial,
but for a given parameter value we can calculate the loss value, and the loss function
derivative at the same point. The derivative tells us in which direction we must nudge the
parameter to decrease the value of the loss, and this can be done iteratively until a local
minimum is reached. At this point, the model can no longer be improved, and a more
desirable global minimum may exist that we will not find. There are ways to move away
from local minima, but for the purpose of this thesis, the way a neural network learns, in
general, should be sufficient.

comes when more complexity is added. If we compartmentalize a perceptron into a single

Each node in figure 2.4 is a perceptron that takes as input the output of all nodes in the
previous layer. If we used a linear activation, such as f(x) = x, this would not make
much of a difference. However, using nonlinear activation functions allows the output of

Other than the activation functions, the main hyperparameters to tune in a neural network
are the number of nodes in each layer, and how many layers there are in total. More
nodes and more layers will generally lead to a model that is more capable of fitting any data
thrown at it. However, it will also be more prone to overfitting, as the number of optimizable
parameters increases. It is not uncommon for a network to have more parameters than
data points, which is not ideal if generalizability is the goal.

PINNs or Physics informed neural networks, are a way to incorporate known physical in­
formation about a system into the learning function of a neural network. (Mahmoudabad­
bozchelou, Em. Karniadakis, and S. Jamali 2022) If we take a simple system, perhaps a
simple differential equation:

(2.3)
In the context of a neural network, our input here will be x, and we wish to predict f(x).
The naive approach is to simply use many pairs of data points, go through the forward
pass and backward propagation loop, and hope that the network learns the underlying
function. While this may work for an example as simple as this, there is a very cheap way
to increase the power of the model.
A neural network is by default differentiable, as backpropagation could not function oth­
erwise. Thus, if we incorporate the derivative of the network into its loss function, we

2.2.4
Convolutional Neural networks
Convolutional neural networks(CNN) have seen tremendous success in nearly all fields
involving 2 or 3­dimensional grid data, with uses such as image segmentation(), classifi­
cation(), super­resolution() and more besides. The core idea behind a CNN is to apply a
series of convolutional filters to some data, and aggregate these filters’ results to produce
a desired output. An example of applying a filter, also called a kernel, is shown in figure
2.6. In the figure, it is done on a single pixel, but imagining this extended to every pixel in
the grid will produce a new grid. If the filter is constructed such that certain features are
extracted, the new grid will contain this information.
8

==PAGE:15==
Figure 2.6: Applying a convolutional filter to a single pixel in a grid
This has been done in the image processing context for many years(Gu et al. 2018)e and
many hand­constructed kernels exist to perform specific tasks. The novelty in a CNN is
that these kernels are not built by hand, but learned during training of the network. A
typical neural network would have weights attached to each pixel in an image, but a CNN
instead uses information about surrounding pixels. Increasing the size of the kernel will
increase the range of this influence, and as we will see shortly the range can be extended
in another way. The main advantage of using a CNN is that much fewer parameters need
to be trained. Since the kernel sizes are fixed and can be used on images of arbitrary
size, a large neural network is not necessary.

A model architecture that has seen some success in flooded area segmentation is the
U­flood(Löwe et al. 2021) model, which is inspired by the original U­net(Ronneberger,
Fischer, and Brox 2015). It is a fully convolutional neural network(FCNN), meaning there
are no dense layers. There are two main advantages to this architecture: There are
typically fewer parameters, as they are cordoned into the kernels of each convolutional
layer, meaning the number of parameters scales with Kernel size multiplied by the number
of filters. A typical dense layer will instead scale with the number of neurons in it and the
preceding layer, which can quickly become very large. The other advantage is that it
is image size agnostic. A model with a dense layer must be constructed such that the
output of the dense layer corresponds with the number of pixels in the input image, so
it can be reshaped properly. Instead, an FCNN can by default produce an output with
the same dimensions as the input, regardless of input. The only constraint being that the
input image must be divisible by two to the power of the number of downsampling steps.
The main difference between this model and the goal of this thesis is the output of the
model. Where U­flood produces segmentation maps, the hope is the same architecture
can instead predict the following time­step in a fluid simulation.

Data
3.1
MIKE
The data used to train the upcoming models was generated using the MIKE software
package. MIKE is a very powerful simulation software, with support for urban drainage
systems, overland flooding, evaporation, catchments and many other elements relevant
to hydrology. In this thesis, the overland solver was used to generate flood data in many
different areas, beginning with some simplified custom topologies.
The simulator takes as input a landscape topology, the location of a source node for
fluid input, and a simulation time. For all these simulations, a simulation time of 1000
timesteps was generated, but due to computational constraints, only the first 500 were
used for model training. The length of the timesteps was 1 second.

3.4
Different simulation areas
The total area available for simulation in the lerbjergstien data is a 403x483 grid of pixels,
where each gridpoint is 1.6m2. Since the fluid can only propagate so far in a given time,
the relevant area to train on is typically much smaller. Because of this, during training,
64x64 subsets of the simulations are taken, due to computational constraints. Larger
subsets could be used during inference, but increasing the size by even small amounts
will lead to much larger RAM requirements.
The data can be augmented by selecting several relevant sub­areas of the same simula­
tion, shown in figure 3.4. This may cause overfitting to specific simulation scenarios, but
should lower the effect of bias on individual grid pixels. The overfitting can be avoided
partially by restricting training and validation to separate sub­areas, avoiding dependence
on location in the simulation.

4
Methods
Figure 4.1: A visual overview of the tasks employed
Figure 4.1 shows the high­level overview of how the model is trained, beginning with the
generation of the training data. The main idea of this model is to iteratively simulate the
flooding of a landscape, done by initializing with some state. This state, starting from
timestep 0, would be a landscape with no flooding, and a selected source node. Given
this information, the model will predict timestep 1. This will in turn be fed back into the
model, to predict timestep 2 etc. until a final timestep is reached.
4.1
Surrogate architecture
The chosen model to pursue was the U­net model used in the U­flood paper. It has been
altered to predict time­stepping for a given interval, as opposed to predicting a flood map,
but the model architecture is overall the same, shown in figure 4.2. This architecture runs
several convolutional layers, in this thesis 6 as a baseline, on the input. It then runs the
outputs of these convolutions through a 2x2 max pool layer, which halves the image size;
in this case from 64x64 to 32x32. These are then also run through twice as many convolu­
tional layers. This process is repeated 4 times, leading to 4 image halvings, in each case
also doubling the number of convolutions. After this, the outputs are upsampled, running
the process in reverse. Skip connections, the horizontal arrows in figure 4.2, are included
in each dimension size, to reduce information loss. This architecture allows convolutions
on several different scales on the input, and its outputs are the same size as the inputs.
The input to the model is a 64x64x4 tensor, where the 64x64 corresponds to the dimen­
sions of the 2d region in which the simulation is performed. The 3 corresponds to the
features used for input, which are ht, the height of the fluid in each cell for each timestep,
and then Qx,t, Qy,t, which are the fluxes in the x and y directions. Flux meaning the net
flow of fluid from one cell to another. Positive Qx,t in a grid cell would imply fluid flowing
from that cell to its neighbour on the right. The final input is the topology of the region or
the height map of the landscape. The Outputs of the model are then ht+1, Qx,t+1, Qy,t+1.
Topology does not need to be in the model outputs, as it is a constant value. When com­
puting the t+2, the output for t+1 is concatenated with the topology and run through the
model again.
The boundary series, or input source node, is included in the model before running the
input through the model. The ht layer of the input is isolated, and fluid input is added to
the relevant grid cell. The input is then fed through the model and the process is repeated
on the output before being fed back into the model, isolating the source node and adding
the fluid to it. For this thesis, only one source node was included in each simulation, but

Palmitessa et al. used a feed­forward neural network, which proved promising. For 2D
flood modelling however, it has some serious drawbacks. Regular feedforward networks
will typically contain a linear layer, which will require the 2D inputs to be flattened into a
1D tensor. This will fix the number of weights in the linear layer, constricting the input
dimensions to those used in training. In contrast, the U­net architecture contains only
convolutional layers. This means that the model is input size agnostic, and surrogate
simulations are not restricted to 64x64 dimensions used in training. The one caveat here
is related to the successive halving of image size in the U­net architecture. In this case,
we do four halvings, which means that the input must be divisible by 24. This is the only
constraint on the input dimension.
The loss function used for training is simply a mean squared error(MSE), over the simula­
tion window. That is, it is a pixel­wise difference between the surrogate model prediction
and the true simulation values.
4.2
Generation of data
In total, simulations were created on 6 total topologies; The four shown in figure 3.2,
lerbjergstien, and a flat landscape, as explained in Section 3. The amount of data was
augmented as shown in figure 3.4. This was then split into batches of intervals of 5­20
timesteps, and given as input to the model. These intervals are referred to as the train­
ing window, and it is the number of timesteps the surrogate model predicts before being
evaluated. The final model will be able to simulate any amount of timesteps forward, but
setting the window to some arbitrarily high number is not ideal for training. A large window
will tend to increase training times significantly, or simply make training impossible. In au­
toregressive time series forecasting, a sliding window approach is often used to mitigate
this(Hota, Handa, and Shrivas n.d.). The size of the window is investigated as a model
hyperparameter. With a window of 5, the input will be run through the model 5 times, and
an output of size 64x64x3x5 will be created. This is then compared to the true simulation.

6
Discussion
The experiments that were performed for this thesis have lead to promising results overall,
showing the promise of this approach. In the most complex scenario, the model was able
to generalize by simulating a scenario that was at no point included in the training. On top
of this, the inference was performed several orders of magnitude faster than the MIKE
simulations used for training. The loss values acheived were all relatively low, for the
better results in the range of 10−5. There are not many other papers using MSE loss
in this exact way, so it is difficult to compare results. However, the fluid heights in the
simulations are on the order of 10−2 ­ 10−1. This is is an error in the low percentages,
and so is promising.
The model is of course limited. Currently, it was trained on only one source node at a
time, and no experiments were performed with the source ”node” being spread over a
region. If one required a flood model that accounts for flooding due to rainfall, this model
in it’s current state would not be of use. As seen in the figures, the results are also less
precise than the baseline simulations; meaning the exact water levels in a cell are not
necessarily accurate. If the goal is to simply have an idea of which areas will be most
susceptible to damage then this model could function as a replacement. However if more
specificity is required it would not be a suitable stand­in. There were several different
topologies included in training, but not every conceivable type. There are almost certainly
some types of landscapes that were missing in the training loop that the model would
most likely struggle with. If we imagine the scenario of a dam break, where the topology
changes at some point in the simulation, this model would most likely fail. In future work,
this could be put to the test, and perhaps be included in the training set.
The way the model is designed forces specific nodes to be source nodes. To simulate
flooding due to weather events, it would make sense to instead have a distribution of water
inflow over an area, perhaps even chosen for each pixel probabilistically. This would more
accurately simulate the real world, and let the model be overall more useful.
It would also be prudent to include some more physics in the loss function. Some exper­
iments were performed in this thesis with mass balance, but further physical constraint
could theoretically be included, such as conservation of momentum. Net flow is gener­
ated by MIKE, and could with high likelihood be interpreted as a form of momentum that
could improve model performance when accounted for.
This thesis has also only concerned itself with the U­net architecture. DeepONets were
mentioned before, and they could theoretically be adapted to this problem. If they were
trained on the shallow water equations with the different parameters as inputs, it might
be possible to acheive very high fidelity results. If the domain was restricted to surrogate
modelling of MIKE, GAN’s might also show some promise. Using different constraint
networks would allow implicit training of mass and momentum conservation.

7
Conclusion
In this thesis, a surrogate model using the U­net architecture was developed and trained
to simulate overland flooding. The model was trained on simulations created using MIKE.
Several different topologies were used, from a simple flat landscape to a maze with sharp
edges, to a real area in Denmark called Lerbjergstien. The model was able to produce
simulations on the training data, and beyond that to generalize to topologies not seen
during training. A variety of different combinations of training and testing data were used
to ascertain this.
Simulation times on the more complex topologies had a speedup of 250x, showing that
the surrogate model has tangible performance benefits. Using mass balance and MSE
metrics, it is shown that the model was able to replicate the true simulation to a relatively
high degree of accuracy. To this effect, the U­net architecture proved to be a valid one,
and the neural network surrogate model approach in general is viable.
The most important conclusion of this work was the ability of the surrogate model to gen­
eralize beyond the training. Retraining the model on each new landscape where it might
be needed is impractical, and would remove many of the benefits of faster simulation
time. The lack of a need for retraining means all that is needed is a predicted rainfall/flood
source location and a topology.
Further work is needed to increase the functionality of the model. Increasing the number
of source nodes to a distributed area to effectively simulate flooding due to rain would be
a practical extension. Improving the performance of the model where mass balance is
included in training would also be a natural direction to explore. Finally, testing the effect
of different inflow boundary series would be valuable.
