Abstract
The current MSc thesis project is going to address the effect of the complex soil-
structure interaction on the dynamic performance of a cylindrical monopile subjected
to seismic-hazard embedded in a homogeneous soil layer on top of rigid bedrock. An
analytical model consisting on a Winkler-beam-like pile in contact with a set of ideal
springs reproducing the flexibility of the soil layer is adopted to develop the kinematic
response of the soil-pile system subjected to horizontal dynamic loading. The second
phenomenon produced by the pile-soil interaction is the inertial effect idealized by
a set of dynamic stiffnesses, also called impedance functions, which account for the
swaying, rocking and cross swaying-rocking modes of vibration. Finite linear solu-
tions to the boundary value problem are performed to evaluate the inertial response
of the pile.
A set of earthquake strong ground motions, recorded during past seismic events, will
be used to represent the seismic actions. The kinematic effect of a pile embedded
in a damped homogeneous soil layer modifies the free field response, creating what
is referred to as foundation input motion. Influence of the kinematic response fac-
tors at the pile-head on the input ground motions will be investigated in order to
evaluate the effectiveness of the waves amplification due to the presence of the pile.
Finally, ground response analysis of the same past seismic events is carried out to
scrutinize the amplification of events recorded in the earth outcrop in less stiff soil
strata, namely site responses. The scenarios explored within the thesis compose the
typical circumstances where the offshore geotechnical engineering takes place with a
perspective point of view to the offshore wind turbine farms, targeted to the areas
of the globe characterized by increased seismicity besides the boost of wind energy
market industry (i.e. Taiwan, China, India, Korea and Latin America).

1 Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Wind energy market is increasing more and more and it already prevails in the so-
called “green energy production”. China, India, Korea and Latin American countries
constitute areas of large expansion for new wind turbines farms mainly installed in
offshore environment, where an exposures’ set consisting of wind, waves and currents
along with earthquake excitations may increase the vulnerability of wind turbines
(Katsanos (2017)).
With these premises, from a structural and offshore engineer-
ing perspective, it is of great importance to safeguard the reliability and structural
integrity of wind turbines during their foreseen lifetime.
The latter triggered the
motivation for the current study.
In the last 40 years within the field of soil dynamics, accurate research on monopiles
excited by earthquake motions was investigated through the approach of inertial
interaction. The lack of a kinematic interaction in the soil-pile system seismic re-
sponse is still affecting the research along with industry design aspects. Standars and
regulations for earthquake-proof structures and infrastructures (ie. NEHRP, 1997;
Eurocode 8, 2003) contain impoverished informations and scarcely detailed design
solutions for wind turbines and offshore infrastructures. Besides, North of Europe is
not notorious for being statistically incessantly subjected to earthquake events, thus
seismic proofing in the past years did not represent the first most important challenge
for the offshore engineering and the wind farms developing in the European fault.
Due to progress and development of Third-World Countries, the wind energy market
is increasing more and more, expanding worldwide into areas with increased seis-
micity. Seismic loads, combined together with soil-structure interaction may affect
adversely the performance of monopiled wind turbines. This crucial aspect is what
mainly ignited the fire of the author in developing this work.

1.2 Current state-of-the-art

1.2
Current state-of-the-art
1.2.1
Inertial Interaction of Seismic Response of Piles
Horizontal vibration of piles occurs when the superstructures are exposed to dynamic
forces, such as those produced by wind or earthquakes. The response of a pile to
external horizontal excitation, and the associated stiffness and damping, define the
interaction problem between pile and soil. Due to the fact that huge horizontal forces
or accelerations are commonly originated by the presence of a considerable mass sup-
ported by the pile, the inertial forces created by these mass are affecting the pile-soil
interaction and thus this phenomenon can be referred to as Inertial Interaction.
A variety of theoretical methods have been developed for the dynamic analysis of
piles under lateral loads. Two important families of methods use finite and boundary
elements which treat the soil as a continuum and are used extensively in research.
These methods require a heavy computational effort especially in three dimensions.
Discrete models have the advantage of being able to incorporate non-linearity and
non-homogeneity of the soil as well as loss of bond between the pile and the soil. No-
vak (1974) used a linear elastic model in which only horizontally propagating waves
were considered with no strain considered in the vertical direction. Hence, this model
can be viewed as the plane strain case. This approach yields closed-form expressions
for all vibration modes and is suitable for high frequency response at which the elastic
waves propagate predominantly in the horizontal direction.
Simplified solutions have also been developed, most of them based on the Beam-on
Winkler-Foundation (BWF) model in which the soil reaction to lateral pile motion is
represented by independent soil springs and dashpots. For static loads, these springs
are calibrated using field load tests leading to semi-empirical non-linear ”py” curves.
For dynamic loads, theoretically calculated soil springs and dashpots have been pro-
posed based on two-dimensional like the one of Novak (1974) and three-dimensional
wave propagation theory developed by Novak and Nogami, (1977) and Nogami and
Novak, (1977). Furthermore, spring values have been obtained by matching results
from Winkler and from rigorous solutions, not treated herein.
1.2.2
Kinematic Interaction of Seismic Response of Piles
The passage of vertically propagating Seismic shear waves through the soil medium
causes deformations in the soil mass. The presence of a deep foundation such as a
pile or a pile group would change the diffraction and propagation of the waves result-
ing in a modified motion which in modern times is called Foundation Input Motion
(FIM). Viceversa, the foundation is dynamically excited by the S-waves, in terms of
a spatially-variable displacement field imposed by the surrounding soil which gives
rise to a dynamic interplay known as ”kinematic interaction” (Anoyatis, 2013). This
==PAGE:18==

effect coexists with the so-called Inertial Interaction, which consists on the motion
transmitted to the pile through the pile connection with its superstructure, described
by the basics structural dynamics (transfer of inertial forces). In the realm of the
kinematic interaction the superstructure is considered massless, thus the problem
can be assessed without taking into account the presence of a wind turbine on top
of the pile, which is instead counted in the inertial interaction. To be noticed that
Kinematic interaction is affecting the Inertial effects since the modified input motion
(aforementioned FIM) is the result of the former. A large number of analytical stud-
ies have demonstrated the importance of kinematic effects on piles.
Thanks to post-earthquake investigations, it was proved the vulnerability of pile foun-
dations even in non-liquefied soils to seismic motions, by revealing damage at the pile
head (i.e. where the inertial interaction is dominating) and/or depths where inertial
forces are negligible and the kinematic effects are controlling the motion. Even the
seismic regulations started to take the situation into account and to adopt design
choices in order to account for kinematic effects on deep foundations, however, as
previously anticipated, nowadays these don’t constitute yet a solid background to
help the design of offshore infrastructures in numerous hazardous conditions.
A well-studied approach is also the one from G. Gazetas (1984), in which a numerical
study is presented of the dynamic response of end-bearing piles embedded in a number
of idealized soil deposits and subjected to vertically propagating harmonic S-waves.
Results, for both ’kinematic’ and ’inertial’ interaction, are offered in the form graphs
and formulae covering a wide range of excitation frequencies and of crucial material
and geometric parameters.
Practical aspects of the evaluation of the influence of
piles on the effective seismic excitation of a structure were discussed. Outcomes from
Gazetas (1984) are compared and taken up in the present thesis.
1.2.3
Ground Response Analysis
In regime of elastic bedrock, traveling downward-stress waves induced by horizontal
dynamic harmonic forces (such as the seismic motion) that reach the soil-rock bound-
ary will be reflected only partially, differently than what happens if the rock stratum
is rigid: it acts as a fixed-end boundary and the waves and any downward-traveling
waves in the soil will be completely reflected back towards the ground surface. Part
of their energy will be transmitted through the boundary to continue traveling down-
ward through the rock. If the rock extends to great depth (large enough that waves
reflected from any deeper material boundary do not return to the soil-rock bound-
ary soon enough, or with sufficient amplitude, to influence the response of the soil
deposit), the elastic energy of these waves will be effectively removed from the soil
layer. This is a form of radiation damping, and it causes the free surface motion to
amplitudes to be smaller than those for the case of rigid bedrock (Kramer (1996)).
The opposite might occur in such a way that the outcrop motion might be amplified

phenomena describe the so-called Ground Response Analysis.
1.3
Methodology and Outline of the thesis
The project is going to address the effect of the complex soil-structure interaction
on the dynamic performance of a cylindrical monopile subjected to seismic-hazard
embedded in a homogeneous soil layer on top of rigid bedrock.
The soil-structure interaction phase can be split into two sub-effects: inertial effect
and kinematic effect, both included in the analysis. The first one will produce the
impedance functions of soil-pile system: a set of spring and dashpot coefficients ac-
counting for the flexibility of the soil. The second one will modify the input strong
ground motion by the addition of two kinematic interaction factors (translational and
rotational) taking into consideration the presence of the pile in the dynamic response
of the soil with respect to the free field motion. For the inertial interaction effect,
a Matlab framework resolves the boundary value problem consisting on a 4-variable
differential equation of a pile embedded in a homogeneous layer of soil on top of a
rigid bedrock. The soil profile is a linear viscoelastic layer with hysteretic material
damping; the pile is vertical, uniform, linearly elastic and with circular cross section
(refer to Chapter 2 for further theoretical background).
The second step is the study of the kinematic effect of end-bearing piles embed-
ded in homogeneous soil layers overlying rigid bedrock subjected to seismic waves.
The analytical solutions disposed by Anoyatis (2013) for two important factors called
kinematic interaction factors will be investigated. The two factors account for the
translational and the rotational degrees of the kinematic response of the pile-sile sys-
tem. The complex formulations in harmonic functions are implemented in Matlab and
will be studied for several pile geometries and soil conditions. Thereafter, the strong
ground motions of numerous past seismic events will be modified by the translational
kinematic interaction factor thanks to a fast Fourier Transform (FFT) algorithm per-
formed in Matlab. Once the foundation input motions will be produced they are
going to be compared with the original recorded accelerograms.
Ground Response Analysis will be performed on recorded seismic events in rigid
outcrop, in order to obtain the site responses in free field conditions of soil layers
characterized by smaller soil strength. The analysis is again carried out in a numer-
ical framework stationed in Matlab and evaluation of amplification effects will be
made in relation to the soil stiffness. Validation of the numerical solution proposed
by Kramer (1996) is going to be assessed with real records of the same past seismic
events in soil strata characterized by the same averaged stiffnesses.

Winkler Model for kinematically loaded piles under

The problem of inertial pile-soil interaction in horizontal mode is faced in this work
by the means of the continuum solution theoretically investigated in Anoyatis (2013),
which relies on the more rigorous works from Nogami and Novak (1977) and Novak
and Nogami (1977). The proposed model used in this thesis follows closely the pi-
oneering work of Novak and Nogami. First, the resistance of the soil layer to the
pile motion is obtained in closed-form solution and, then, is employed directly in the
solution of the pile-soil interaction problem. The main assumptions adopted in the
analysis are: (i) the pile is perfectly elastic and vertical, its cross-section is circular
and there is perfect contact at the pile-soil interface, (ii) the soil is a linearly vis-
coelastic, homogeneous and isotropic layer overlying a rigid bedrock, (iii) no normal
or shear stresses on the free surface of the soil and no displacements occur at bedrock
level (the solution is found for hinged and clamped pile base), (iv) vertical displace-
ments associated with horizontal vibration are negligibly small, (v) under dynamic
conditions the motion is considered harmonic.

2.2.1
Problem Definition and Assumptions
The problem states: a single vertical cylindrical pile of length L, diameter d, mass
density ρp, and Young’s Modulus Ep is embedded into a homogeneous soil layer of
height H (assumed to coincide with the pile Length) on top of a rigid bedrock. The
soil layer is a linear elastic material with Poisson’s ratio νs, mass density ρs, and a
material damping ξs which is frequency independent. The shear waves are in the
form of a co-sinusoidal harmonic function:
ug(t) = ug0eiωt
(2.35)
Being ug0 the strong ground motion amplitude. The harmonic function is imposed
at rock level. The internal forces acting in an infinitesimal cylindrical volume of the
pile are shown in 2.3, and the positive notation of the problem is shown therein.
Figure 2.3: Internal forces acting in an infinitesimal cylindric volume
of the pile and positive notation.
The problem at hand is governed by seven dimensional parameters (L, d, Ep,
Es, ω, ρp, ρs) but with the purpose of a parametric study it is convenient to use
dimensionless ratios and the problem ends up to be a six governing values problem

describe the dynamic interaction and only one for the static problem.

Conclusions
The problem of inertial pile-soil interaction in horizontal mode is faced in this work by
the means of the continuum solution theoretically investigated in Novak and Nogami

soil layers, for clamped tip and free pile-head conditions. Analytical solutions for pile
response were provided in closed form. The main conclusions of the study may be
summarized as follows.
The two analytical solutions build-up the necessary framework background for the
analysis of the phenomenon of Soil-Structure-Interaction of a pile embedded in a ho-
mogeneous layer of damped soil subjected to horizontal dynamic harmonic waves.
In the realm of Ground Response Analysis investigated in Chapter 6 of the present

Transfer Function of damped soil stratum on top of elastic rock describes the rate
of amplification of the propagation of harmonic s-waves at the interface between the

3.1.4.3
Comparison between stiffer and softer soils
This section is intended to show the difference of the behaviour of the dynamic stiff-
nesses between weaker and stiffer soils. It can be seen that the real stiffnesses can
diminish to zero and attain negative values when the piles are stiff (short) and the
soil is weak. The smaller, sharp minima in the real stiffness, as previously mentioned,
appear at the first resonance with the soil layer. It can be observed that the variation
of real stiffness with frequency can be very dramatic for stiff piles and very weak soils.
Conversely, they are remarkably modest for slender piles and stiff soil. However, in
some cases even an increase in real stiffness with frequency can be observed.
The input values for this section are seen in table 3.9. Higher values of the soil
shear wave velocity Vs (and thus the soil stiffness) make the soil-pile system more
flexible (lower stiffness contrast).
Figures 3.13 and 3.14 are to be compared to figures 3.15 and 3.16) which represent
the cases previously analysed with a set of weaker soils. It is clear how, as previously
mentioned, for increasing frequencies, softer soils lead to a drop of the real part of the
swaying dynamic stiffness to 0 or, to negative values. Instead, stiffer soils generally
hold the real swaying dynamic stiffness of the pile-soil system to 1 or above 1. It is
always intended as mentioned in the premises that when the author refers to values
like 1 of the dynamic stiffness, this is always normalized with the static stiffness. If
the value of Kdynamic becomes 1 after a certain number of drops (minima), this means
that for higher frequency, the dynamic stiffness equals the static stiffness (Kstatic)
which makes it possible to deduce that the stiffness of the dynamic system is heading
to behave as the stiffness of the static system (although it would be wrong to call this
as ”static condition”).

Softer soils and thus more rigid pile solutions show increasing dynamic stiffnesses
with increasing frequency (see figures 3.13-3.16) and increasing material damping.
For higher values than 1 of the swaying or rocking or coupled stiffnesses, the dynamic
value of the stiffness is higher than the static one. The physical meaning of this is
intended to be explained subsequently. Assuming an excitation (of harmonic type)
released at a high frequency, this will match the soil-pile system at the corresponding
frequency. If the pile is stiff (or the soil is weak), the response of the system will be
rigid, meaning that the lateral force (or moment) needed to translate or rotate the
entire pile-soil system, has to be considerably high. Notably, in the behaviour of the
solutions of figures 3.13-3.16 the dynamic force needed to move or rotate the system
at high frequencies, is greater than the equivalent static force needed to obtain the
same movement in static conditions. It is also a way to say that the system becomes
more rigid (stiff). In terms of practical applications, this phenomenon is positive.
It is generally seen that high frequencies excitations don’t generate problems as the
system becomes stiffer, despite the case of swaying stiffness for low strength soils. In
this latter quoted case, the dynamic horizontal force needed to make the pile shift, is
incredibly smaller than the equivalent static force in static conditions (around 20%
- see figure 3.15), which makes the system more flexible (amplification effect of the
harmonic waves).

3.2
Parametric Study - Hollow Core Steel Monopiles
In the current section the same parametric study previously presented is repeated,
but for a more specific case describing a steel hollow core monopile, which is the most
commonly used foundation solution for an offshore wind turbine. In this case, the
stiffness of the pile is the one of the steel held constant to Esteel = 210GPa, the height
is 35m with a diameter of 10m, thus the slenderness ratio for this case is lower (≈3.5).
The soil stiffness in this case is higher, with a starting value of 200m/s. Remarkably,
this second parametric study is closer to realistic solutions in the offshore industry.
The results obtained are mainly in line with the previous trends, although for some
cases they present some differences which are going to be addressed. Note: for the
derivation of the equivalent stiffness of the homogeneous cross section of the hollow
core pile, please refer to Section 4.5 of Chapter 4.
3.2.1
Constant Stiffness Ratio
3.2.1.1
Variation of L
With regards to the variation of the pile length the results are perfectly on line with
what discussed in section 3.1.3.1. The second peak of resonance (2nd soil eigenfre-
quency) only appears for the most flexible pile. The first peak of resonance (1st soil
eigenfrequency) of stiffer piles (blue lines of figures 3.17, 3.18) is more marked in the
swaying term (drop to 60%!); instead, it is unexpectedly less marked in the other
rocking and coupled terms of stiffness.

3.4 Discussion of results
65
3.4
Discussion of results
The chapter went through a parametric study of the dynamic stiffnesses - also called
impedance functions - of a pile embedded into a homogeneous layer of soil on top of
rigid bedrock, subjected to seismic waves. The dynamic stiffnesses Ksu, Ksc, Kmu
and Kmc account respectively for the swaying, rocking, crossed swaying-rocking stiff-
nesses of the soil-pile system.
Two main studies were conducted: first, a general case describing a slender pile (with
slenderness ratio equal to L/d = 20) with a stiffness likely to represent the stiffness
of a concrete pile (Ep = 20 GPa), in a notably soft soil layer (Vs = 50 m/s). One
by one, the dimensional parameters were changed in order to scrutinize the varia-
tion on the dynamic stiffnesses corresponding with an increase (or a decrease) in the
two main ratios governing the solution: the slenderness ratio and the stiffness con-
trast. Secondly, the same study was repeated, but for a specific case describing a
steel hollow core monopile, which is one of the most commonly utilized foundation
solution for offshore wind turbines. In this case, the stiffness of the pile is the one
of the steel kept constant to Esteel = 210 GPa, the height is 35m with a diameter
of 10m, thus the slenderness ratio for this case is lower (≈3.5). The soil stiffness
in this case is higher, with a starting value of 200 m/s. Remarkably, this second
parametric study is closer to realistic solutions in the offshore industry than the first
one, which is meant to be more theoretical, describing a solution which is on purpose
intended to be more dramatic for the practical applications (slender pile in soft soils).
The results from the numerical analyses, carried out in a Matlab framework where the
solutions of the complex-valued boundary problem from Novak and Nogami (1977)
were implemented, are thoroughly discussed hereafter.
The main trend to gain a foothold throughout the analyses was that more rigid solu-
tions (either with shorter and stiffer pile, or softer soil) present a sharp minimum in
all stiffnesses, in correspondence to the first natural frequency of the soil, and then,
at higher modes, work as ”isolators” of mode shapes as the rigid behaviour doesn’t
allow so and further peaks (at higher frequencies) disappear. Instead, flexible solu-
tions show several minima in correspondance with the resonances of the soil. This
is explained by the fact that a slender pile does not modify the length of the waves
propagating from the earthquake, so it doesn’t produce a ”diffraction” effect, as a
rigid pile would do and is able to amplify the motion even at higher modes. Sharper
minima occur at the resonant frequencies of the soil in flexible solutions.
Another trend which outcame from the results is that generally, the variation of
real stiffness with frequency can be very dramatic for stiff piles and very weak soils.
Conversely, they are remarkably modest for slender piles and stiff soil. However, in
some cases even an increase in real stiffness with frequency can be observed. These
results can be seen in figures 3.13 and 3.14 in comparison with figures 3.15 and 3.16.
Noticeably for the swaying real stiffness, all the 5 solutions (with a soil shear velocity
ranging between 9m/s and 94 m/s), decrease dramatically for increasing frequency
==PAGE:74==
66

(figure 3.15). This occurs as swell for the real rocking and the real coupled terms,
with the only difference that only the most rigid pile (blue curve) stays somewhat
constant. Stiffer soils make the system behave more flexibly, as it’s seen in figures
3.13 and 3.14 showing several marked minima corresponding to the soil frequencies.
However, the variation of those is not especially brutal. In fact, the curve starts at
1 and the biggest drop is the first minimum, which is 0.9 for the stiffer soil and it
is 0.95 for the softer soil, which is noticeably not a huge discrepancy from the static
value. Instead, the Ksu curves of figure 3.15 diminish to approx. 20% of the static
value at high frequency.
Focus of the present work is made on the seismic hazard thus the first range of
lower frequencies is the relevant. Mainly all the graphs contained in the chapter are
plotted until the value of f = 5 Hz. In this shorter range of frequency, approximately
the first or the second minima of Kdynamic are worth noting and they play an impor-
tant role in the seismic inertial response occurring simoultaneaously with the 1st soil
eigenfrequency. In general, it is valid to say for the stiffer piles, the first resonance
is more marked (bigger drop). For the slender piles, the first peak is less dominant
(more modest variation), but present relevant drops even at higher frequencies.
Regarding the study of realistic solutions, the same conclusion can be inferred for
both the 5 MW and the 10 MW pile prototypes. As both configurations define stiff
(short) piles, the rocking stiffnes is not subjected to relevant decreases, even in cor-
respondence of the first eigenfrequency of the soil (almost infinitesimal difference is
observed in the weakest soil both in real and imaginary parts). More significant dif-
ferences among the curves are observed in the coupled terms: whilst the pile in softest
soil keeps having a very small drop (10%), the piles in stiffer soil behave more flexibly
and have a non-negligible component in the coupled degree of freedom.
Finally, the swaying term of the stiffness presents a relevant decrease both for the
piles in stiff soils and for the pile in soft soil. However, the stiff pile solution remark-
ably differs from the flexible ones, as it perceives a decrease to 75% of the static value
whereas for stiffer soils it would drop down to almost 60%. It is seen that in general
the translational term of the real stiffness is more dangerous for stiff piles especially
in stiff soils.
The rocking term shows an infinitesimal variation, the drops of the
coupled terms are modest; the swaying stiffness is in general the most affected. It
can be seen that the real stiffnesses can diminish to zero and attain negative values
when the piles are stiff (short) and the soil is weak.
All damping terms present increasing terms along with frequency. Upsurges of damp-
ing correspond to minima of real stiffnesses.
The following trend affirms always
throughout the results: rigid solutions present always the double increment at orders
of frequency f = 5Hz. The 3 modes of damping don’t differ heavily, with the charac-
teristic that the rocking term always shows a negligible variation (or increase) as it
happens for the real part. Rotational motion in stiff monopiles is a governing mode

3.5
Conclusions
The dynamic stiffness and damping of the soil-pile system depend on a number of
dimensionless parameters such as the slenderness ratio, the wave velocity ratio, di-
mensionless frequency, Poisson’s ratio, the internal damping ratio and the mass ratio.
In this chapter a study on the slenderness ratio, the wave velocity ratio and/or stiff-
ness contrast is carried out for the problem of a pile embedded into a homogeneous
layer of soil on top of rigid bedrock, subjected to seismic waves.
It resulted from the analyses regarding solid piles that, in correspondence with the
first natural frequency of the soil, a marked drop of stiffness is obtained, which is
more pronounced for stiffer piles (more rigid solutions). Stiff piles govern the first
minimum of stiffness. Conversely, more flexible solutions (obtained by either more
slender piles or stiffer soils) show a larger amount of drops of stiffness in correspon-
dence with the higher modes of the natural frequencies of the soil, whereas in the
rigid solutions the soil-pile system does not respond to higher vibration modes.
With regards to the second study, the case of the hollow core steel pile is treated,
where a stiffer soil stratum is considered and a stiffer pile is prearranged. For this case,
similar trends occur with the difference that stiffer solutions (wider pile diameter or
softer soils) present a minor variation of stiffness at the first eigenfrequency, whereas
in the flexible solutions bigger drops of stiffness are observed.
The variation of real stiffness with frequency can be very dramatic for stiff piles
and very weak soils. Conversely, they are remarkably modest for slender piles and
stiff soil. However, in some cases even an increase in real stiffness with frequency can
be observed.
Very little damping is generated below the first resonance with the soil layer, because
no progressive wave is generated in such frequency region in the absence of material
damping and, consequently, no geometric damping occurs. Above the first resonance,
the total damping and, consequently, no geometric damping with little contribution
from material damping. In the absence of material damping, sharp minima in the
imaginary part of stiffness are associated with the sharp minima in the real stiffness.
The ”markedness” of these minima decrease with increasing order of resonances and
increasing stiffness of the pile. With stiff (short) piles, only the first resonance of the
layer is usually marked.
For practical applications, it is seen that stiff piles in soft soils might represent a dras-
tic solution as the stiffness can reduce to the 80% of the static stiffness. It is thus rec-
ommended that in a scenario presenting a stratum of soft soil layers (ρs = 1.8ton/m3
and Vs < 100 m/s) design solutions of modestly flexible piles are evaluated. The pos-
sible risk encountered with stiff (or short) piles is that at the lowest frequencies the
pile rotation might govern the dynamic response leading to a resonance phenomenon.

4.5 Parametric Study - Hollow Core Steel monopile

4.5
Parametric Study - Hollow Core Steel monopile
The parametric study carried out for a general pile (with starting values which are
listed in table 4.1), as in Chapter 3 is also performed for a steel monopile with a fixed
young modulus (Ep = 210GPa) in order to assess the case of seismic response of a
single monopile embedded in a soil layer which would represent the foundation of an
off-shore wind turbine.
This allowed the estimation and appreciation of the kinematic interaction factors
for a monopile, which are then used in chapter 5 multiplying accelerograms retrieved
from past seismic events in order to obtain a modified ground motion given by the
presence of the monopile. This is also a brief summary highlighting the final scope of
this thesis work.
It has to be premised that, the geometry of the monopile corresponds to the one
of a hollow pipe, generally with constant diameter from the pile head to the pile
tip. The thickness is very low compared to the diameter of the pile (≈1/100) since
it doesn’t increase much the moment of inertia of the cross-section, and it would
increase the weight of the steel tube as well as its cost. Important for the case of the
monopile is the equivalent stiffness used throughout the calculations. It is obtained
as a weighted-average of the bending stiffness of the steel hollow-core pile and the
bending stiffness of the ”ideal” soil pile which is created in the hollow space of the
steel tube. This ”pile” of soil material contributes as well to the bending stiffness of
the whole deep foundation, as well as its mass is influencing the propagation of the
seismic wave. The equivalent bending stiffness of the monopile is expressed by the
following:
Eeq = EpIp + EsIs
Ip + Is
(4.22)
Where the terms Es and Is respectively refer to the soil strength, the moment of
inertia of the cross section of the ”soil pile”. The same applies to Ep and Ip which
are instead referred to the steel hollow pile.
4.5.1
Starting case - ”Reference case”
The starting case for the seismic assessment of a steel monopile was conceived as
being as much realistic as possible in order to provide general guidelines to the real
design in practice. For the pile geometry, it has been made reference to the case of

4.7
Discussion of results
Throughout the chapter, two parametric studies on the kinematic interaction factors
of cylindrical piles embedded in homogeneous soil layers subjected to seismic waves
have been conducted. In the first, a general case is assessed describing a slender pile
(with slenderness ratio equal to L/d = 20) with a stiffness likely to represent the
stiffness of a concrete pile (Ep = 20 GPa), in a relatively soft soil layer (Vs = 50 m/s).
In the second one, the same study was repeated for the specific case of a steel hollow
core monopile, which is the most common foundation solution for an offshore wind
turbine. In this case, the stiffness equal to Esteel = 210 GPa, the height is 35m with
a diameter of 10m, thus the slenderness ratio for this case is lower (≈3.5). The soil
stiffness in this case is higher, with a starting value of Vs = 200 m/s. Remarkably,
this second parametric study is closer to realistic design solutions (in the offshore
industry) than the first one, which is meant to be more theoretical.
In light of the adopted analytical model suggested by Anoyatis (2013), the two kine-
matic interaction factors accounting for the translational and rotational degrees of
freedom of the kinematic response, namely Iu and Iθ, were studied. The two numerical
functions present an harmonic behaviour, both attaining maxima in correspondance
to the first eigenfrequency of the soil. Subsequently, they drop to lower values un-
til reaching minima and thenceforth, follow a harmonic behaviour with peaks which
never reach values above the first maximum, which is actually the most detrimental
peak for practical applications. In the range relevant for an earthquake assessment,
the most ”alarming” performance of the functions, is the first peak where the highest
values of Iu and Iθ are attained at the resonance of the main frequency of the soil.
Ordinarily, it is seen that rigid solutions (stiff pile or soft soil) keep the maximum
of Iu to a relatively low value (increase of 20 - 30 % of the free field). In contrast,
flexible solutions (slender pile or rigid soil) yield to a higher maximum of Iu which
ranges between 40 - 50 % in correspondence with the first soil natural frequency.
The behaviour of Iθ is generally opposite, as it accounts for the rotational degree of
freedom of the soil-pile system, which dominates the rigid solution where the short
or stiff piles behaves rigidly with respect to the soil layer. In some cases, it is noticed
that at the first eigenfrequency of the soil, the translational factor keeps constant for
low shear wave velocity and thus stiff pile (See figure 4.30). Remarkably, at the first
eigenfrequency of the soil, decrease of kinematic interaction factor is obtained when
very low values of L are associated with low values of shear wave velocities (figure
4.32). The rotational factor, in such cases, embraces high peaks (see figure 4.30); how-
ever in some cases, when ”absurd” values of shear wave velocities are implemented,
Iθ notably registers very low decrement (2 % of the free field value at the second -
refer to figure 4.33) which makes it an outlandish result showing the limitations of
the solution at extreme boundaries.

the more at-hand and simpler analytical formulation provided by Gazetas (1984) can

a homogeneous soil layer. The benefits of Anoyatis formulae for Iu and Iθ are found

the kinematic interaction factors, although the entire range of stiffnes ratios is not
covered as it’s demonstrated in section 4.2 and previously quoted.

4 Kinematic Interaction of Seismic Response of Piles
4.8
Conclusions
A beam-on-Dynamic-Winkler-Foundation model was employed to investigate on the
behaviour of kinematically stressed piles of finite length embedded in homogeneous
soil layers, for clamped tip and free pile-head conditions. Analytical solutions for pile
response were provided in closed form. The main conclusions of the study may be
summarized as follows.
The kinematic response of slender piles attain higher peaks in the translational de-
gree of freedom and lower peaks in the rotational one. Stiff piles are governed by the
rotational degree of freedom exposing higher peaks of Iθ and, in some cases, notably
constant or even descending behaviour of Iu. This result is associated with soft soils
or high stiffness of the pile material.
Ordinarily, it is seen that rigid solutions (stiff pile or soft soil) keep the maximum
of Iu to a relatively low value (increase of 20 - 30 % of the free field). In contrast,
flexible solutions (slender pile or rigid soil) yield to a higher maximum of Iu which
ranges between 40 - 50 % in correspondence with the first soil natural frequency.
The behaviour of Iθ is generally opposite, as it accounts for the rotational degree of
freedom of the soil-pile system, which dominates the rigid solution.
The dimensionless frequency term λL is the governing parameter of the solution
for different piles for the same pile-soil stiffness ratio. This yields the conclusion that
long piles exhibit the same response regardless of actual length.
A mathematical
demonstration within the chapter proved that the same is not applicable to equal
piles for different soil-pile stiffness ratios, due to non-convergence of the harmonic
solution for specific frequency. Hence, a range of realistic values (within extrem lower
and upper bound values) is derived for practical applications, associated with kine-
matic solutions determined a priori.
Successive to a comparison with previous analytical formulations found in literature,
it is conveyed that Gazetas’ (2013) formulations for the translational kinematic in-
teraction factor is more reliable, being explicitely formulated in an ”at-hand” simple
formulation (see equation 4.20) depending on both stiffness contrast Ep/Es and slen-
derness ratio L/d besides the first natural frequency of the soil and the inherent
frequency of the harmonic seismic wave.

5.3
Foundation input motions
On the basis of the mathematical procedure explained in Section 5.1, the foundation
input motions are obtained by the multiplication of the recorded motions in frequency
domain with the translational kinematic interaction factor Iu. Thus, the object of
this section, is to investigate on the differences of the modified motions with respect
to the original ones, in order to derive conclusions upon the actual influence of the
kinematic response of the pile in case of an earthquake to the free field motion. The
approach of the analysis treated in the present chapter is subsequently summarized.
A first post-processing of data has meant to obtain the main ground motion param-
eters of the modified motions and compare them in charts (see figures 5.2, 5.3 and
B.1- B.8) with the original motions. The selected parameters are: maximum Acceler-
ation (or PGA), maximum Velocity, Arias Intensity, Predominant Period and Mean
Period. Based on the comparisons, relevant cases are thoroughly treated. With rel-
evant cases it is referred to those cases where one of the motion parameter of the
FIM is eccessively greater or smaller than the input motion, or viceversa. For these
cases, the response spectra of the motions are shown and commented in relation to
the kinematic factor Iu: if the analysis feeds the thesis, then the approach is proved
and more reliable conclusions can be inferred.
Given the small difference between the two pile solutions, it has been preferred to
focus only on the pile serving as a foundation of a 5 MW Wind Turbine, whose
dynamic impedances are described in section 3.3 and kinematic pile-soil interaction
factors treated in section A.1 and exposed in figures A.1 and A.2 in a dimensional
frequency range ([Hz]). Comments in this section are often referring to results exam-
ined in the the two quoted sections, which it is recommended to read simoultaneously.
The plots of Iu of the relevant cases are also reported herein for a straightforward
comparison.
In figures 5.2 and 5.3 the scatter plots of the predominant periods of the original
and modified motions are exposed. The values of the dots (coordinates of the charts)
are illustrated in table 5.2. It has been decided to carry out an analysis which shows
the peak periods of the motions, being a characteristic parameter which is immedi-
ately recognizable at naked-eye from the response spectra.
The charts with the scatter plots of the other motion parameters are found in the
Appendix B in figures B.1- B.8) and the relative data are illustrated in tables B-B.

at each frequency. If the amplification factor (also called transfer function) of the
motion produced by the multiplication with Iu is plotted against the ratio between

The motion is amplified within a certain range of ωSDOF /ωinput, else, a de-
amplification occurs.
This means that at a specific frequency, the result of the
multiplication of Iu with the original accelerogram, might amplify or not the mo-
tion, depending on the equivalent SDOF motion at that frequency.
In fact, it is
clear that the time domain response is directly increased (or decreased) by the Iu,
depending just on the site conditions (Vs) and on the pile geometry and material.
What happens in the FFT (frequency domain) has to do with the frequency content
of the accelerogram and of the kinematic interaction factor, and the ”in-phase” and
”out-of-phase” matching between the two complex-valued numbers.

5.5
Discussion of Results
Within the chapter, the application of the analytical model formulated and treated
in Chapter 4 is performed on a set of accelerograms representing strong ground mo-
tions recorded in soil strata with different stiffnesses (Vs = 100; 250; 400; 1000m/s).
Due to the presence of the pile embedded in a homogeneous soil layer, the motion
is modified and assumes the name of foundation input motion. The validity of this
model is assessed and discussed herein.
The first trend marks that the effectiveness of the foundation input motion is more
significant in soft soils (Vs = 100m/s) (Figures 5.11- 5.18) as the response acceleration
along the period is generally always amplidied with a minimum increase of 10 - 20 %,
until maxima ≈30%. For increasing soil stiffness, the kinematic factor Iu moves its
peak (1.25 < max(Iu) < 1.35) closer and closer to T →0 and to higher frequency, for
which it becomes less and less relevant for the amplification of the motion. This trend
is proved as the kinematic response factor for stiff soils (whose natural period is very
short) becomes thus barely significant for the seismic assessment (trend displayed in
figures B.17-B.40).
However, it is still significant for all the motions recorded in medium stiff soil (Vs =
250m/s) as it produces an increase in response acceleration in the range of 10 % more
than the free field motion. A modest amplification is observed in the stiff soils with
shear wave velocity Vs = 400 m/s, in the range of 5% higher than the free field motion.
The most striking cases were treated in section 5.3, where it is explained the approach
of the study. Starting with a first preliminary check on the motion parameters, dis-
played in charts where it is seen the contrast between the original accelerograms and
the modified input motions. The cases showing a discrepancy in predominant pe-
riod of the motion are then treated with meticulous attention. First, the kinematic
interaction factors are studied and then the response spectra, in relation with the
kinematic term, are presented and commented. If the analysis feeds the thesis, then
the approach is proved and more reliable conclusions can be inferred.
The kinematic factor shows a behaviour where in correspondence with the first nat-
ural frequency of the soil, it has a peak where, for the cases of piles treated herein
(slenderness ratio 3.5 and steel material, thus a stiff pile) it reaches ≈25 or ≈30%
more than the free field motion, depending on the specific soil condition. In some
cases the peas (or in its vicinity) corresponds exactl ity a peak in response acceler-
ation of the ground motion: hence the kinematic response amplifies the motion and
a more intense response is observed. In some cases the maximum response acceler-
ation (or PGA) corresponds to the natural period of the soil and the PGA changes
predominant period. These were the cases of Earthquake 2, 6, 8 for the soft soil
(Vs = 100m/s) and earthquake 16 for the medium stiff soil (Vs = 250m/s). In other
less relevant cases, change in predominant period does not crop up.

5 Foundation Input Motion
5.6
Conclusions
The kinematic response of a pile embedded in a soil layer determines changes in the
development of the strong ground motions hitting the soil stratum. The main focus
of the chapter is to prove the effectiveness of the so-called foundation input motion.
In stiff soils or rock, the presence of the pile is not detrimental as the response accel-
eration is increased of the 5% or less with respect to the free field motion, since the
1st eigenfrequency of the soil occurs with short natural period, thus an earthquake
amplification becomes remarkably uninfluenced. In softer soils, a dissimilar trend
is marked. Increases in response acceleration can determine an amplification in the
PGA of even the 30%, or produce a change in the predominant period of oscillation
of the motion if the first soil eigenfrequency matches a peak of acceleration of the
response spectrum of the original accelerogram.
Hence, the influence of the predominant period of the soil is essential in the mod-
ification of the motion, as well as the peak magnitude of the kinematic interaction
factor. It is then highlighted the importance of the kinematic interaction assessment
when treating cases of superstructures supported by deep piles, especially in condi-
tions of soft soils. It is concluded that in soils ranging in soft to medium stiffnesses
(up to Vs = 400m/s), the effects of a seismic loading in a stiff monopile can register
dramatic changes in the kinematic response with respect to the free field motion.
Finally, recalling that the amplification of the strong ground motion occur within
the range of frequency (or period) where Iu ranges above 1, a singular trend arises.
It exists a slightly small mismatch on the precise period (and frequency) where FIM
starts the amplification. This, is due to the ratio between the amplitude of an equiv-
alent SDOF motion developing at at that specific frequency and the amplitude of
the input motion at that same frequency. The amplitude ratio (or transfer function)
determines at each specific whether the motion is amplified or de-amplified.

tween a rigid layer and a uniform damped soil is to predict the real motion occurring
at the surface of the earth crust and to assess the site response analysis through a sim-
plified mathematical formulation. To check the validity of such numerical solutions
(showed in section 6.2), a comparison is made with the same past seismic events for
the same soil stiffness. However, lack of precision is expected mainly for two reasons,
as previously mentioned. First, the value of shear wave velocity refers to an average
value of the upper 30 m of soil layers; secondly, seismic events registered in similar
soils but different geographical locations most likely present different motions due to
the epicentral distance and to the many factors influencing the propagation of waves.

6 Ground Response Analysis
6.4
Discussion of Results
On the basis of the response spectra presented in section 6.2, an argumentation of
the results will be assessed herein. A first trend is observed in coincidence with the
increase of stiffness (or shear wave velocity) of the soil layers: the higher the stiff-
ness contrast between the rock and the soil stratum, the higher the amplification of
the motion in terms of transfer function (transfer between rock and soil boundaries).
This is explained by the constant increment in the response acceleration along the
period, which is less and less definite with increasing soil shear wave velocity. This is
also confirmed in the time accelerograms, which mainly present the same trend (see
Appendix C).
Wherever the predominant period of the soil stratum (the magenta dashed line in
the graphs) corresponds to a peak of acceleration in the recorded motion, then the
ground response is notably amplified. In some cases, the ground response of a stiffer
soil is even amplified with respect to the same motion in softer soils, if this occurs
in correspondance of the natural period of the soil stratum.
This is particularly
appreciable in Earthquake 3 (Northridge-01, Rec.Pacoima Dam, 1994 ), where the
response in the 250 m/s shear wave velocity soil is increased of almost 4 times than
the related outcrop motion and it’s 1.6 times higher than the maxima response accel-
erations of the softer and stiffer soils (Vs = 100m/s and Vs = 400m/s). This is also
observable in Earthquake 8 (San Simeon CA, Rec.Diablo Canyon Power Plant, 2003),
where even the stiffest soil ground response (Vs = 400m/s) reaches a maximum re-
sponse acceleration higher than the ground responses in softer soils (Vs = 100m/s and
Vs = 250m/s), observing a 1.13 times higher amplification. In Earthquake 1 (Loma
Prieta, Rec.Gilroy Array, 1989) where the same as just described above applies. In
other cases, which take place generally more often, the softest soil perceive a higher
amplification if the soil predominant period corresponds to a peak of acceleration,
and this is noticeable in Earthquake 2 (Loma Prieta, Rec.SF - Pacific Heights, 1989),
Earthquake 4 (Kobe Japan, Rec.Kobe University, 1995), Earthquake 5 (Chi-Chi Tai-
wan, Rec.TAP065, 1999), Earthquake 6 (Chi-Chi Taiwan-06, Rec.HWA003, 1999)
and Earthquake 7 (Tottori Japan, Rec.YMGH06, 2000) where the ground response
in the Vs = 100m/s soil is 2.5 times higher than the responses in the stiffer soil strata.
The validity of the numerical solution proposed by Kramer (1996) is checked against
the real recorded accelerograms for the different soil shear wave velocities comparing
records of the same past seismic events with the ground responses obtained via the
numerical formulation. Despite the lack of sufficient information about the geograph-
ical location of the recording stations and a possible roughness when comparing a
solution tailor-made for a specific shear wave velocity with data from an average of
soil layers, good agreements in some cases are found.
For softer soils characterized by Vs = 100m/s, the response evaluated by the nu-
merical formulation seems to be in good compliance with the real accelerograms.
Especially, in correspondence to the natural soil peak period, the Loma Prieta and

6 Ground Response Analysis
6.5
Conclusions
The transmission of ground motion waves from the earth outcrop to softer soil strata
is noticeably more amplified for decreasing stiffness of the soil (or shear wave velocity).
The numerical solution of the so-called transfer function provided by Kramer (1996)
was applied to three different soil strata with different stiffnesses excited by the same
strong ground motions.
The results marked that, most commonly, the maximum
response amplification is higher in softer soils.
However, the influence of the predominant period of the soil stratum on the transfer
function and thus on the response amplification of the motion is remarkably signif-
icant. It is seen that, for some cases, stiffer soils observe peaks of response acceler-
ation higher than the ones observed in softer soils (opposite trend to the one above
explained). This situation occurs if the maxima lay in correspondence of the pre-
dominant period of the soil (1st soil eigenfrequency). This points out the importance
of investigating the resonance phenomenon between soil stratum and rock motions
within the field of seismic assessment.
The validity of the numerical solution is checked against the real recorded accelero-
grams for the different soil shear wave velocities comparing records of the same past
seismic event with the ground responses obtained via the numerical formulation. De-
spite the lack of sufficient information regarding the geographical location of the
recording stations and a possible roughness when comparing a tailor-made solution
for a specific shear wave velocity with data from an average of soil layers, good agree-
ments in some cases are found. Especially for the records registered in the soft soils,
the predominant period between the numerical solution and the real record coincides,
and PGAs too, with an error in the range of 4%.
Although it was found a reasonable coherence when checking the efficacy of the numer-
ical transfer function for the analyzed soil stiffnesses, this is not a glaring indication
that always the theoretical formulation can predict with excellent accuracy the site
response.
A complementary seismological analysis considering geographical locations of the
records and the location of the hypocenter of the ground rupture should be assessed
for a more reliable usage of the numerical solution.

7 Conclusions
7.1
Interpretations and final conclusions
The problem of inertial pile-soil interaction in horizontal mode is faced in this work
by means of the continuum solution theoretically investigated in Novak and Nogami
(1977and employed directly in the solution of the pile-soil inertial interaction problem.
A beam-on-Dynamic-Winkler-Foundation model was employed to investigate on the
kinematic effect of stressed piles of finite length embedded in homogeneous soil layers,
for clamped tip and free pile-head conditions.
The two analytical solutions build up the necessary framework background for the
analysis of the phenomenon of Soil-Structure-Interaction of a pile embedded in a
homogeneous layer of damped soil subjected to horizontal dynamic harmonic waves
(seismic waves). Validation of the closed-form analytical formulations are carried out
within the respective Chapters 3 and 4 via comparison with other proposed solutions
found in relevant literature. Agreement in the results is found.
7.1.1
Inertial Interaction
From the inertial interaction analysis, it resulted from the analyses regarding solid
piles that, in correspondence with the first natural frequency of the soil, a marked
drop of stiffness is obtained, which is more pronounced for stiffer piles (more rigid
solutions). Stiff piles govern the first minimum of stiffness. Conversely, more flexible
solutions (obtained by either more slender piles or stiffer soils) show a larger amount
of drops of stiffness in correspondence with the higher modes of the natural frequen-
cies of the soil, whereas in the rigid solutions the soil-pile system does not respond to
higher vibration modes.
With regards to the study of the dynamic impedances in the case of the hollow core
steel pile, where a stiffer soil stratum is considered and a stiffer pile is prearranged,
similar trends occur with the difference that stiffer solutions (wider pile diameter or
softer soils) present a minor variation of stiffness at the first eigenfrequency, whereas
in the flexible solutions bigger drops of stiffness are observed.
Valid for all solutions is that very little damping is generated below the first reso-
nance with the soil layer. Above the first resonance, the total damping is mostly due
to geometric damping with little contribution from material damping. In the absence
of material damping, sharp minima in the imaginary part of stiffness are associated
with the sharp minima in the real stiffness. The ”markedness” of these minima de-
crease with increasing order of resonances and increasing stiffness of the pile. With
stiff (short) piles, only the first resonance of the layer is usually marked.
==PAGE:181==
7.1 Interpretations and final conclusions
173
7.1.2
Kinematic Interaction
Out of the analyses carried out on the kinematic response of piles subjected to har-
monic horizontal waves, the following trends were observed.
The kinematic response of slender piles attain higher peaks in the translational mode
(Iu) and lower peaks in the rotational one (Iθ). Stiff piles are governed by the rotation
exposing higher peaks of Iθ and, in some cases, notably constant or even descending
behaviour of Iu. This result is associated with soft soils or high stiffness of the pile
material.
Ordinarily, it is seen that rigid solutions (stiff pile or soft soil) keep the maximum
of Iu to a relatively low value (increase of 20 - 30 % of the free field). In contrast,
flexible solutions (slender pile or rigid soil) yield to a higher maximum of Iu which
ranges between 40 - 50 % in correspondence with the first soil natural frequency.
The behaviour of Iθ is generally opposite, as it accounts for the rotational degree of
freedom of the soil-pile system, which dominates the rigid solution.
The dimensionless frequency term λL is the governing parameter of the solution
for different pile geometries for the same pile-soil stiffness ratio.
This yields the
conclusion that long piles exhibit the same response regardless of actual length. A
mathematical demonstration within the chapter proved that the same is not applica-
ble to equal piles for different soil-pile stiffness ratios, due to non-convergence of the
harmonic solution for specific frequency.
Successive to a comparison with previous analytical formulations found in literature,
it is achieved that Gazetas’ (2013) formulations for the translational kinematic inter-
action factor is strongly reliable, being explicitely formulated in an ”at-hand” simple
formulation depending on both stiffness contrast Ep/Es and slenderness ratio L/d be-
sides the first natural frequency of the soil and the inherent frequency of the harmonic
seismic wave.

7 Conclusions
7.2
Suggestions for further research and practical
application
7.2.1
Inertial and kinematic interactions of the soil-pile system
In the realm of inertial effect of seismic response of end-bearing single piles, sugges-
tions that can apply to realistic scenarios are the following. The variation of real
stiffness with frequency can be very dramatic for stiff piles and very weak soils. Con-
versely, they are remarkably modest for slender piles and stiff soil.
For practical applications, it is seen that stiff piles in soft soils might represent a
drastic solution as the stiffness can reduce to the 80% of the static stiffness value
(for null frequency). It is thus recommended that in a scenario presenting a stratum
of soft soil layers (i.e. ρs = 1.8ton/m3 and Vs ≈100 m/s) design solutions of mod-
estly flexible piles are evaluated. However, the possible risk encountered with stiff
(or short) piles is that at the lowest frequencies the pile rotation might govern the
dynamic response leading to a resonance phenomenon.
In regards with the kinematic effect, based on the important revelation stating that
high values of the complex-frequency dimensionless group λL fully control the kine-
matic solution of slender enough piles only for a certain range of stiffness contrasts,
then a range of realistic values of soil stiffness is derived for practical applications,
associated with kinematic solutions determined a priori (λL > 5). It is valid to af-
firm that, in the modern industry considering the materials available today for design
offshore solutions, whichever slenderness ratio of the pile, the solution is uniquely
determined by L as long as the stiffness ratio lays in the range of the two extreme
bound values found within the diagnosis.
7.2.2
Foundation Input Motion
In light of the analyses carried out on recorded past seismic events modified by the
kinematic response of the pile-soil system (foundation input motion - FIM), the strik-
ing results are outlined subsequently. In softer soils, increases in response acceleration
can determine an amplification in the PGA of even the 30%, or produce a change
in the predominant period of oscillation of the motion if the first soil eigenfrequency
matches a peak of acceleration of the response spectrum of the original accelerogram.
Most commonly, the maximum response amplification is higher in softer soils for
the foundation input motion (applicable to the ground response analysis also). In
fact, in stiff soils or rock, the presence of the pile is not detrimental as the response
acceleration is customarily increased of not more than the 5% with respect to the free
field motion, since the 1st eigenfrequency of the soil occurs with short natural period,
thus an earthquake amplification becomes remarkably uninfluenced in such range.

The influence of the predominant period of the soil is essential in the modification
of the motion, as well as the peak magnitude of the kinematic interaction factor.
It is then highlighted the importance of the kinematic interaction assessment when

soft soils. It is concluded that in soils ranging in soft to medium stiffnesses (up to
Vs = 400m/s), the effects of a seismic loading in a stiff monopile can register dramatic
changes in the kinematic response with respect to the free field motion.
