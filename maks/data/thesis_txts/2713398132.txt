Abstract
As the focus on sustainability increases, it is important to find other substitute methods
that counteract excessive emissions of CO2 and the eradication of raw materials. As
known, concrete is one of the biggest culprits, and at the same time, one of the most
used materials worldwide, but because of the large emission of CO2 through cement
production and sand has become a shortage product, it has been necessary to find a more
sustainable solution. Since the 1970s, coal fly ash has been used as cement replacement
with huge success, but due to the decrease in coal combustion, it has been replaced with
other more sustainable combustion types such as wood. But because wood ash does not
have the same properties as coal ash, it cannot be used as supplementary cementitious
material. So another way to minimize the use of cement is investigated.
A geopolymer is an alkali­activated material there is cement­free, which means the CO2
emission can be reduced by up to 80%, depending on the raw materials. AS sand has
become a shortage product and sustainability also depends on maintaining the raw mate­
rials, it is necessary to find a replacement. This thesis investigates whether it is possible
to valorize wood bottom ash as sand replacement in alkali­activated materials. Where
metakaolin was used as the precursor, and sodium oxide and sodium silicate solution
were used as the activator.
This was done through, a characterization of the bottom ash and metakaolin. Then the
formulation was created based on the 2th Full Factorial Design of Experiments, and af­
terward 59 different batches, 177 samples, and 252 compression tests were created. As
there is no standard for geopolymers was 3 different methods investigated.
Method 1 was a trial batch, there was inspired by the standard mortar mixing method,
DS/EN 206 DK NA:2018. This method proved to not be appropriate for mixing geopoly­
mers because both the long mixing time and vibration table affected the mix adversely.
Method 2 was based on the knowledge from Method 1 and DBI’s previous work with
geopolymers. This method proved that better results were obtained when the binder is
activated correctly and without the use of the vibration table. Mix 5, 6, and 8 got the high­
est compression strength, why Method 3 was based on these three mixes. For Method 3
the same mixing method as Method 2 was used, but here was the effect of temperature
and curing days investigated further.
Overall, the conclusion is that geopolymers with bottom ash as a sand replacement do
have the potential to become superior to conventional concrete in long­term performance
and mechanical properties, but still need more investigation before they can be used as
a potential construction material. Mix 6 obtained the most sufficient results, why it has
the most potential for further development. Based on the use of Design of Experiments
it was not the most effective method for this level of investigation, because it caused an
extremely high workload with too many affecting factors. Through this process it can be
concluded based on the used formulation that high molarity of sodium oxide is preferred,
the ratio between sodium oxide and aluminum oxide is preferred to be 1.5 rather than 0.5,
and the ratio between activator and precursor must be low. Based on Method 3 it can be
concluded that ambient temperature gives higher strength.

Resumé
Efterhånden som der bliver mere fokus på bæredygtighed, er det vigtigt at finde andre
erstatningsmetoder, der modvirker overdreven udledning af CO2 og udryddelse af råma­
terialer. Beton er som bekendt en af de største syndere og samtidig et af de mest an­
vendte materialer på verdensplan, men på grund af den store udledning af CO2 gennem
cementproduktionen og fordi sand er blevet et mangelprodukt, har det været nødvendigt
at finde en mere bæredygtig løsning. Siden 1970’erne har kulflyveaske været anvendt
som cementerstatning med stor succes, men på grund af den faldende kulforbrænding
er det blevet erstattet med andre mere bæredygtige forbrændingsformer som f.eks. træ.
Grundet træaske ikke har de samme egenskaber som kulaske, kan det ikke anvendes
som supplerende cementmateriale. Derfor er det nødvendigt at undersøge en anden
måde hvorpå brugen af cement kan minimeres.
En geopolymer er et alkali­aktiveret materiale, der er cementfrit, hvilket betyder, at CO2­
emissionen kan reduceres med op til 80%, afhængigt af råmaterialerne. Da sand er blevet
et mangelprodukt, og bæredygtighed også afhænger af, at råmaterialerne bevares, er
det nødvendigt at finde en erstatning. I denne afhandling undersøges det, om det er
muligt at udnytte træbundaske som erstatning for sand i alkali­aktiverede materialer. Hvor
metakaolin blev anvendt som prækursor, og natriumoxid og natriumsilikatopløsning blev
anvendt som aktivator.
Dette blev gjort gennem en karakterisering af bundasken og metakaolin. Derefter blev
formuleringen skabt baseret på 2th Full Factorial Design of Experiments, og herefter blev
der skabt 59 forskellige batches, 177 prøver og 252 kompressionsforsøg. Da der ikke
findes nogen standard for geopolymerer blev 3 forskellige metoder undersøgt.
Metode 1 var en prøve batch, der var inspireret af standard mørtelblandings metoden,
DS/EN 206 DK NA:2018.
Denne metode viste sig ikke at være egnet til blanding af
geopolymerer, da både den lange blandingstid og vibrationsbordet påvirkede blandin­
gen negativt. Metode 2 var baseret på viden fra metode 1 og DBI’s tidligere arbejde med
geopolymerer. Denne metode viste, at der blev opnået bedre resultater, når bindemidlet
blev aktiveret korrekt og uden brug af vibrationsbordet. Blanding 5, 6 og 8 fik de højeste
kompressionsstyrke, hvorfor metode 3 blev baseret på disse tre blandinger. I metode 3
blev der anvendt samme blandingsmetode som i metode 2, men her blev påvirkningen af
temperatur og hærdningsdage undersøgt yderligere.
Samlet set er konklusionen, at geopolymerer med bundaske som sanderstatning har po­
tentiale til at blive en god problemløsning i forhold til konventionel beton med hensyn til
langsigtet ydeevne og mekaniske egenskaber, men at de stadig kræver yderligere under­
søgelser, før de kan anvendes som et potentielt byggemateriale. Blanding 6 opnåede de
mest tilstrækkelige resultater, hvorfor den har det største potentiale for videreudvikling.
Baseret på anvendelsen af Design of Experiments var det ikke den mest effektive metode
til dette undersøgelsesniveau, fordi den forårsagede en ekstremt høj arbejdsbyrde med
for mange påvirkende faktorer. Gennem denne proces kan det på baggrund af den an­
vendte blandingsformulering konkluderes, at en høj molaritet af natriumoxid foretrækkes,
at forholdet mellem natriumoxid og aluminiumoxid foretrækkes at være 1.5 frem for 0.5, og
at forholdet mellem aktivator og prækursor skal være lavt. På grundlag af metode 3 kan
det konkluderes, at hærdning ved stuetemperatur giver højere styrke af geopolymeren.

Introduction
Over the past decades, it is no secret that the focus on sustainability has experienced a
major increase. When talking about sustainability, there are several aspects to take into
account in terms of the economic, environmental, and social aspects, where the goal is
to reduce climate change and ensure more efficient use of resources. This can, among
others, be done through recycling, avoiding eradicating materials, and lowering the global
emissions of carbon dioxide (CO2).
Since 1990, the global emissions of CO2 have increased by almost 50 percent. This has
caused an increase in the climate changes in such a sudden way that it affects, among
others, the weather patterns, oceans, national economies, and lives, [1]. If this keeps
developing, it will no longer be possible for humans to live on the earth as they do today.
But, is it too late to prevent further adverse effects? The United Nations (UN) has made a
2030 plan with 17 Sustainable Development Goals to reduce climate change and ensure
more efficient use of resources, [1].
These goals also affect the construction industry with Goals 12 and 13. Goal 12 con­
cerns responsible use of resources and reduction of waste generated and environmental
emissions, and goal 13 concerns saving energy in building operations, decreasing the
CO2, and avoiding aggravating the global climate [1]. When talking about the construc­
tion industry, materials play a significant role. Taking a look at concrete, it is one of the
most used construction materials worldwide, but the use of this material counteracts the
development of sustainability. Sand, which is one of the most important resources in the
construction industry, has become a shortage product. A basic mixture of concrete con­
sists of 33­50% sand, [2] but according to the increase of construction sand has become
a shortage product, does this both affects the world’s population and the environment,
[3]. Extensive sand extraction causes erosion and alters rivers and coastal ecosystems,
which is why there has come a focus on the use of other more sustainable materials as
sand replacement.
The cement industry has one of the most significant emissions of CO2 and produces more
than 7 percent of the global CO2 emission, and economically it costs 10­15 times more
than aggregates, [2], It is this high cost and CO2 emission that means other materials,
such as coal fly ash, have been used as supplementary cementitious material (SCM) in
concrete.
Since the 1970s, coal fly ash has been used as cement replacement with huge success,
[4], but due to focus on sustainability increased has the energy production changed and
the amount of produced coal fly ash has decreased and been replaced with e.g. wood
ash instead. Unfortunately, biomass ash can not be directly replaced as SCM, as it does
not consists of the same properties. Therefore, it is necessary to rethink. According to
UN Goal 12 and 13 there are focusing on responsible use of resources, saving energy in
building operations, decreasing the CO2, and avoid aggravating the global climate [1] it
is necessary to find another way to create a similar product as concrete, but without the
large amounts of cement and sand. It is here geopolymers come into the picture.
Overall, sustainability in the construction industry is based on a combination of building
techniques and experience, which has proven to work for many years, with new evidence­
based knowledge (which can be measured and documented). Depending on the raw ma­
terials, geopolymers can potentially reduce the related CO2 emission by up to 80 percent.

A geopolymer is an alkali­activated material that is cement­free and has the potential to
be superior to conventional concrete in long­term performance, and mechanical and fire
properties, [5]. If wood bottom ash can be used as a sand replacement the geopolymers
have the potential to be a good substitute for concrete.
The aim of the present thesis is to investigate if it is possible to develop and test a geopoly­
mer mortar mix design containing wood bottom ash from Amagerværket (HOFOR A/S)
as sand replacement.
1.1
Problem Statement
In this master thesis will following problem statement attempt to be answered: ”Is it pos­
sible to valorize wood bottom ash as sand replacement in alkali­activated materials?”
To answer the problem statement, the following methods have been used:
1. A literature study is conducted to clarify the current research status within the field
of alkali­activated materials, wood bottom ash as replacement, with focus on mix
designs.
2. A physical and chemical characterization of the wood bottom ash and metakaolin is
conducted to clarify the optimum mechanical and environmental performance.
3. A pre­treatment, where the wood bottom ash is being sifted ≤4mm in terms of
improving material characteristics as sand replacement.
4. A formulation of the mix design is developed for the geopolymers.
5. Samples are created to see how the materials react.
6. Optimizing the formulation to maximize mechanical and physical properties.
7. A temperature controlled batch there is based on the optimized mix design.
8. Determining the wood bottom ash role in the mortar phase.
1.2
Scope of Work
Due to the scope of this thesis, wood bottom ash is chosen because it is accessible. HO­
FOR creates yearly around 9200 tons of coarse wood bottom ash. This thesis investigates
whether the wood bottom ash can be used as a sand replacement because it does not
have the properties as supplementary cementitious material. Molarities of sodium oxide
between 2.6M and 7.0M are used in the activator even though previous experience has
proved that higher molarities of sodium give a higher strength, [6]. The idea of using lower
molarities is to find a more accurate cost efficiency of the products. There are investigated
three different methods because there is no standard for developing geopolymers.
1.3
Structure of the Report
This thesis is structured into seven sections. Following section 1 the preliminary intro­
duction, section 2 will provide an overview of the theoretical background and selected
theories that are relevant to the research subject. Section 3 will elaborate on the used
methodology and related potential methodological concerns, while section 4 will report the
results through graphs and models. Section 5 will provide the discussion of the results
according to literature. Section 6 will summarize the findings in a conclusion of the study
and finally, section 7 will provide the basis for potential future research.

Theoretical Background
2.1
Geopolymer
Geopolymer is an alkali­activated material, there consists of a reaction between an al­
kaline activator and an amorphous solid aluminosilicate precursor, where the alkaline
activator decomposes the precursor into silicate and aluminate units, which then recom­
bine to produce a mainly amorphous and three­dimensional matrix with binding proper­
ties, [5]. Geopolymers are a stable structure, which means they do not collab in contact
with water. A geopolymer is intended to be an innovative and sustainable alternative to
traditional cement­based materials, [7], based on its mechanical performance and lower
environmental impact from the low emissions of CO2.
In general, geopolymers consist of three things: Activator, precursor, and aggregates.
Metakaolin has proved to be a good product used as a precursor, [8].
When using
metakaolin as a precursor it is necessary to use a sodium­based activator as metakaolin
does not contain sodium.
Geopolymers have shown excellent properties against sulfate attack, abrasion, and high
temperatures, which makes them a product with lots of potentials, [9].
Despite geopolymers have been investigated for over 50 years, the adaptation and appli­
cation of geopolymers in construction are still very limited, and there are no international
standards for geopolymers characterization and production, [10], as well as an incomplete
understanding of their durability properties in terms of alkali­aggregate reaction, carbon­
ation and efflorescence, and the underlying physicochemical interactions driving this be­
havior, [8].
Geopolymers do not consist of cement, which makes them a greener material to the envi­
ronment than cement­based materials, but how beneficial it is to lower the CO2 emission
depends on the materials used. When using waste materials from the industries the emis­
sion of CO2 will be decreased further. Experimental studies on geopolymers show an
introduction to the use of waste materials such as fly ash, bagasse ash, etc. [9]. From the
article by K. Singh 10%­40% metakaolin was replaced with bagasse ash. The geopoly­
mers were investigated for oven dry curing and ambient temperature curing for 7 and 28
days. The results showed that the geopolymers with 10% replacement at oven­dry curing
developed good mechanical and durability properties, and reached the highest compres­
sion strength, see Figure 2.1, [9]. From the experimental study, it can be seen that waste
materials do have potential as a replacement in geopolymers, in this case, as metakaolin
replacement.

2.2
Process perspectives for developing geopolymers
2.2.1
Mixing time
The mixing time for geopolymers is approximately around 10 seconds, [10], which is sub­
stantially lower than concrete there according to DS/EN 206 DK NA:2018, [11], are mixed
for around 3 minutes. The mixing time for geopolymers has a significant impact, the more
a geopolymer is mixed, the more fluent and separated it becomes. This is also the situa­
tion according to vibrating the mix. According to the standard, a vibration table removes
air bubbles and improves the samples, but for geopolymers, the vibrations deteriorate the
samples. If the mix is not fluent enough, it creates more air bubbles and if the mix is too
fluent, it will separate the mix, [12].
2.2.2
Temperature
A chemical reaction occurs when geopolymers are formed. This chemical reaction is
accelerated by high temperatures there can happen when the oven dries the geopolymers,
[9]. When elevating the curing temperature an increase in the extent and rate of reaction,
is shown through an increase in mesopore volume, surface area, and an accelerated
setting time. The kinetics appears to be temperature­controlled only before the material
is hardened. When synthesized at higher temperature and silicate ratios, a higher Si/Al
ratio and finer pores are obtained in geopolymers, [13].
Experimental studies on geopolymers showed that oven drying geopolymers 24 hours
before it afterward could be cured at ambient temperature increased the strength see
Figure 2.1, [9]. Another advantage of geopolymers is that they are more resistant to
fire than chemical­based materials. The article by G. E. Arunkumar, [14], resulted that
the compression strength of geopolymers for temperatures up to 400°C was better than
cement concrete cured at ambient temperature, [14].
2.3
Wood Bottom Ash
Wood ash is the solid residue after the combustion of wood, which is separated into two
main parts: wood fly ash and wood bottom ash. Wood fly ash is the ash extracted from
the flue gas cleaning of incineration plant, excluding the condensate sludge, [15], which
has been used as waste material as cement replacement for years, and wood bottom ash
is the ash extracted at the bottom of a plant for the incineration of wood, and not mixed
with fly ash or other ash fractions, [15]. Wood bottom ash is a much coarser material than
fly ash, the particles are typically angular, and the shape is very irregular with observable
pores. The surface texture is rough and gritty and has interlocking characteristics, [16].
The properties of wood bottom ash is varying, as it can contain varying amounts of sodium,
calcium, magnesium, iron and, in small concentrations, many other metals depending on
the combusted wood, [16]. The metals are bound as salts, i.e. carbonates, silicates, etc.
Also, unburned residues such as carbon are very common in bottom ash. However, the
bottom ash typically consists of a lot of materials that cannot be completely incinerated.
This makes the bottom ash a non­homogeneous mass, which normally will require some
processing before it can be further worked with depending on the type of ash. When it
is wood bottom ash from surplus and waste threes, the amount will be relatively small
compared to the process it has to go through, [17].
Different factors influence the physical properties of wood bottom ash as the degree of
pulverization of coal, firing temperature in the furnace, and type of furnace. The wood
bottom ash there is investigated in this thesis is from HOFOR’s Main block 4. The inciner­
ation plant used the Fluid bed technology with a bed temperature of around 860 degrees,
[18]. Around 1 million tons of wood chips per year are burned and converted into 150MW

2.4
Metakaolin
Metakaolin, also known as Al2Si2O7, is an amorphous dehydration product of the clay
mineral, kaolinite, Al2(OH)4Si2O5. Metakaolin is processed by calcination of kaolin clay
at temperatures around 650­800oC in a period of 1­12 hours, where it begins to exhibit
strong pozzolanic activities, [19]. if the temperatures instead rise to over 900oC free silica
and mullite are created, because the metakaolin undergoes will a further reaction happens
and form crystalline compounds, [19].
The particle size of metakaolin is around 1­2µm, which is smaller than portland cement
there has a particle size of around 1­50µm, [20]. Besides both its pozzolanic and micro­
filler characteristic metakaolin also have the benefit that it is the only product so far, that
reduces the CO2 output and accelerates the setting [19].
2.5
Sodium silicate solution
Sodium silicate solution (Na2Si3O7) also defined as water glass, is a transparent aqueous
solution there can be used to waterproof floors, basins, pipes, etc., and as a binder for
geopolymers, [21]. Water glass can also be used to make things fireproof and is a low­cost
alternative to products such as tetraethyl orthosilicate (TEOS), [22].
2.6
Efflorescence
Efflorescence is alkali salts that crystallize on the surface of the geopolymer, see Fig­
ure 2.4. It happens when the leaching of free alkalis reacts with carbonic acid, there
are formed by the dissolution of atmospheric carbon dioxide when exposed to water, [8].
There are two types of efflorescence, primary and secondary efflorescence. Primary ef­
florescence can be seen as a visible phenomenon observed on the surface, whereas sec­
ondary efflorescence, also known as subflorescence alkali salt crystals there are formed
within the pore network underneath the surface, due to diffusion of dissolved CO2
3 ions,
[8].
Efflorescence mainly causes aesthetic damage or superficial deterioration, [8], but the
formation of efflorescence is still not fully understood due to the product formation and
different mechanisms of reaction, [8].

2.8
Design of Experiments
Design of Experiments (DOE) is an analysis tool and powerful data collection. It is the
most effective way to empirically learn about technologies when there are many variables
or factors to consider, [24]. As the DOE pioneer George Box said, [24]:
”If you really want to know what effect a variable has, you actually have to change that
variable”
The DOE is defined as a branch of applied statistics that deals with planning, conducting,
analyzing, and interpreting controlled tests to evaluate the factors that control the value
of a parameter or group of parameters, [24]
When developing DOE following guidelines are used, [24]:
1. Define the problem and the objectives
2. Identify the response variable or variables
3. Determine whether the measurement systems are capable
4. Identify the experimental factors and factor levels
5. Identify restrictions on randomization and other constraints

==PAGE:20==
6. Generate the design
7. Prepare to conduct the experiment
8. Conduct the experiment
9. Analyze the data
10. Draw a conclusion and make recommendations for the next step.
There are different types of experimental designs as Full factorial designs, Screening
designs, Fractional factorial designs, Response surface designs, Definitive screening de­
signs, and Custom designs. These are used based on what is wanted to be studied, in
relation to how many factors there are
DOE is used in many environments among others the industry, marketing, agriculture,
health, life science, etc., and is based on active learning, making changes in controlled
environments, cause and effect, and studying what actually happens, [24]. From the ar­
ticle of S.­J. Lya et al.[25] the DOE is used to investigate the main factors of the geopoly­
mers, as many factors affect their mechanical properties. The article shows broadly, that
OH−(M) affects the mechanical parameters of geopolymers mostly, the polymerization
process and developed microstructure was affected by the ratio of OH−(M) and SiO2(mol),
and the main coordination of a geopolymer, there dominate its mechanical parameters are
Q4(4Al) and Q3(3Al), [25].

Characterization
When creating a geopolymer, different aspects affect how workable and sufficient it is to
use. It is therefore important to understand the materials and make a characterization. In
this characterization, the wood bottom ash and metakaolin have been analyzed through

3.3.2
Casting process
The formulation was based on the conditions of a 1g binder which gave a total mix between
2.9­5.2g, according to the different ranges of factors. This small amount was not enough
to fill out the molds of 768 cm3, why it was necessary to normalize the mixes so the
volumes of the mixes were increased with the normalized factor.
Besides the formulation of mix design, the method used to develop the geopolymer mor­
tars also varied. Several different methods were performed to determine which method
was most sufficient for molding geopolymers due to the physical and chemical properties.
In total, 59 different batches were molded.
Method 1 was a trial batch. When using the DOE method it was a good idea to first make a
trial batch to better understand how the materials reacted together. As there are no stan­
dards for geopolymers yet, was the standard mortar casting DS/EN 206 DK NA:2020,
[11] used. With Method 2 was all of the mixes cast based on the knowledge from Method
1 and DBI’s previous work with geopolymers, [12]. Method 3 was an optimized batch,
where there was a focus on the mixes with the most potential.

Method 1
As geopolymers are compared with concrete, the first method performed was inspired by
DS/EN 206 DK NA:2018, [11]. Where the mix was blended as follows:

• After 30 seconds the sand and ash was added
• After 1 minute the mixer speed was increased to level 2
• After running at level 2 for 30 seconds, the mixer was stopped and the mortar was
scraped off the edges using a dough scraper.
• The mortar was resting until 3 minutes had elapsed and then mixed for 1 minute at
level 2
• The mixtures were distributed in the moulds
• Afterwards, the form was placed on a vibration table at around 50 Hz and vibrated
for 2 minutes
• Excessed mix was scraped off
• The form was covered with a steel plate

4.2.1
Method 1
As mentioned in Section 3.3.2, Method 1 was used to get a better understanding and see
how the materials reacted. The result of this method was that the method according to
standard DS/EN 206 DK NA:2018 could not be used for geopolymers. When creating a
geopolymer, it is important to activate the precursor so that it becomes a binder. With
the standard mixing method, it was more difficult to control when the binder was mixed
sufficiently. Following the standard method, the geopolymers were mixed for around 3
minutes, but the time affected geopolymers negatively. As mentioned in Section 2.2.1,
the more the geopolymers were mixed, the more fluent and separated it became. Accord­
ing to the standard, a vibration table removes air bubbles and improves the samples. But
for geopolymers, the vibration table deteriorated the samples. If the mix was not fluent
enough, it created more air bubbles and if the mix was too fluent, it separated the mix, see
Figure 4.9a. A vibration table was therefore not the most suitable when creating geopoly­
mers. When demolding the samples, it could be seen that the geopolymers reacted with
the steel molds, see Figure 4.9b.

Discussion
Even though the DOE could have been a manageable way to develop experimental work,
it was not the right fit for this type of experiment. Because of the limited knowledge, it gave
an enormous amount of data and too many different factors, why it was more difficult to
determine which factors played which role. In the article from S. Jena and R. Panigrahi,
among others, the effect of NaOH molarities was investigated for 8M, 10M, 12M, 14M,
16M, and 18M. The samples with highest compression strength over 40 MPa has a NaOH
molarity of 14M for both 14 and 28 curing days. In this thesis, the used NaOH molarities
were only 2.6M, 4.2M, 4.3M and 7.0M, which is due to see, whether it could be possible
to both create a more cost efficient and more sustainable product than the ones with high
NaOH molarity.
But how do the mixes with lower values of NaOH molarities perform? Although it can be
difficult to specify which factor is significant, it could be seen from Figure 4.14 that the ones
who performed worst were the ones with lowest molarity, and the ones there performed
best have the highest molarities.
From the characteristic, HOFOR expected BA to have more potential as a precursor than
as an aggregate replacement, [18]. This became apparent through XRF, where it could
be seen that BA, among others have a high amount of silicon dioxide and pozzoland
activity as MK, and also the results of their TGA tests were quite similar, see Figure 4.4.
This thesis investigates BA as sand replacement, and even though some characteristics
proved that BA has some of the same properties as MK, there were several aspects that
contradicted BA’s potential as MK instead of as an aggregate replacement. If comparing
the results of BA, MK and 0­4mm sand, it can be seen on the particle size distribution
and SEM results that BA is more similar to sand. Also through the results of compression
and efflorescence tests it can be seen that BA as sand replacement has potential. The
samples with BA as sand replacement obtains compression strengths close to or higher
than the ones without BA, and the samples with highest amount of BA do not develop
efflorescence, which is desired.
As mentioned in Section 3.1.1 it was not possible to remove all of the burned carbon
through sifting the ash. The total amount of burned carbon in coarse bottom ash is not
known and through this thesis it has not been clear which influence the burned carbon
has on the geopolymers.
Due to the limited time period of only 24 hours to observe the efflorescence, it may have
created a misleading picture of which of the samples experiences the most efflorescence
and which one of them does not develop any. The article from M. A. Longhi proves that
some of the samples first develop efflorescence after 28 days, [8]. It is therefore difficult
to say whether BA is resistant to developing efflorescence or if it just extends the time
before it develops.
From Method 3, it could be seen that the samples with ambient temperature obtains bet­
ter results of compression strength compared with the ones with 60o. According to the
literature, water in the bottom of the oven helps to avoid the loss of water by evapora­
tion, because it provides a high humidity (RH>95%), [8]. This can be an important factor
when comparing the results for ambient temperature and 60o, because the loss of water
by evaporation has not been considered in the samples in this thesis.

6
Conclusion
Through this thesis, wood bottom ash was investigated as sand replacement in alkali­
activated materials and the following can be concluded:
From Method 1 based on DS/EN 206 DK NA:2018, [11] it was discovered that geopoly­
mers were affected adversely by vibration tables and long mixing time, which is why this
method was not the most appropriate for mixing geopolymers.
Method 2 was based on the knowledge gained from the results of Method 1 and DBI’s
previous work with geopolymers. The samples were mixed without a long mixing time
and the vibration table. Afterward they were cured at 60oC in the first 24 hours before
demolding. From this method, it was discovered that better results were obtained when
the mix has a high Al2O3­to­Na2O ratio, a high NaOH molarity, and a low ratio between
activator and precursor. Mixes 5, 6, and 8 reached the highest compression strength.
Method 3 was based on a further investigation of Mixes 5, 6, and 8 according to the effect
of the temperature for both ambient and 60oC, and the curing days for both 14 and 28 days.
It was discovered from the temperature impact that the samples at ambient temperature
obtained higher compression strengths than the ones at 60oC. For the curing days it was
discovered that an increase from 14 to 28 curings days did not impact the samples at 60oC,
the opposite happened where one of the compression strengths decreased according
to an increase in curing days. Whereas, for the samples at ambient temperature, the
compression strength increased as the curing days were increased.
It can be concluded that when developing geopolymers it is important to use molds of
non­reactive materials such as wood because the alkali­activated material reacts with
steel.
Mix 6 obtained the most sufficient results at ambient temperature, and so it has the most
potential for further development. Based on the use of Design of Experiments it was not
the most effective method for this level of investigation, because it caused an extremely
high workload with too many affecting factors. Through this process it can be concluded
based on the used formulation that high molarity of sodium oxide is preferred, the ratio
between sodium oxide and aluminum oxide is preferred to be 1.5 rather than 0.5, and
the ratio between activator and precursor must be low. Based on Method 3 it can be
concluded that ambient temperature gives higher strength.
From efflorescence tests of the 28 days samples, it was discovered that Ref­6 developed
the most efflorescence, while the BA80 samples at ambient temperature provided the
best conditions to avoid efflorescence. It can be concluded that high temperatures lead
to more efflorescence and wood bottom ash counteract the formation of efflorescence.
Overall, the conclusion is that geopolymers with bottom ash as a sand replacement do
have the potential to become superior to conventional concrete in long­term performance
and mechanical properties, but still need more investigation before they can be used as
a potential construction material.

Perspectivation
The work done throughout this thesis proves that there is potential for wood bottom ash
as sand replacement in alkali­activated materials, but it needs more investigation. For the
future, it is recommended to do the following aspects differently:
DOE gave an enormous workload, with some parameters there were given from the start
that were proven not to work. It is not recommended to use this method for the mix design,
but instead focusing on mixes with a high Al2O3­to­Na2O ratio, a high NaOH molarity, and
a low ratio between activator and precursor and then focusing on changing only a few
factors at a time, so that their influence becomes clearer. A method as slump flow could
be used as investigation of the parameters influence and the mixes’ workability.
As the samples cured at ambient temperature obtained the most sufficient results accord­
ing to high compression strength and low efflorescence, it is recommended to go further
with these. Also according to a more sustainable perspective.
The influence of the burned carbon that could not be removed through sifting the ash is
still unknown, so it could be interesting to investigate the impact by either removing it by
washing or vacuuming it, as it was experienced that the ventilation soaked some of the
burned carbon up when measuring the amount of ash needed.
It can be recommended to look at a longer time period for both curing days and for the
efflorescence test. The curing days could be interesting to investigate further as the results
gave varying results as to whether the geopolymers became stronger or weaker with
time according to the 14 and 28 curing days in this thesis. A longer time period for the
efflorescence test could be interesting as other experiments have shown some samples
first developed efflorescence after 28 days, which means that it is not necessarily the most
accurate result given from only 24 hours.
Another thing to do differently is to use some nonreactive forms such as wood forms or
similar, so the geopolymer does not react with the forms and see if this has an impact on
the compression strength.
If geopolymers should be used for something else, it could also be interesting to investi­
gate the fire resistance of the geopolymers and how heat is conducted through them, also
in relation to the effect of wood bottom ash. The industry needs a product that can insu­
late chimneys inside the house as a cylindrical column appearance, so it does not have
to be a large square plaster box. Although the chimney industry has developed pipes
with a diameter of only 150 mm, the packaging easily becomes 500x500 mm with plaster
and insulation. Aesthetically, geopolymers would be a game changer in the construction
industry, provided it can withstand the dissipation of the heat.
