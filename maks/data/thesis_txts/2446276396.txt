Abstract 
 
Peptides are molecules of high interest in the pharmaceutical industry. They have important 
roles in biological processes and show high selectivity and specificity for their. These properties 
make the development of peptide drugs of high interest for the industry. However, peptide drugs 
have limitations for use as drug, including very short plasma half-lifes resulting from fast 
proteolytic degradation. Various chemical modifications have been investigated by researchers, 
with several of them showing successful in order to prolong the half-life of peptide drugs. For 
example, Liraglutide; a fatty-acid functionalized analog of the natural GLP-1 receptor agonist, 
is one of the best selling drugs for treatment of Type 2 diabetes in Denmark.  
 
The use of peptidomimetics, a small protein-like chain designed to mimic a peptide, is a 
promising strategy to enhance stability and avoid enzymatic degradation, hereby obtaining 
higher bioavailability. One broadly used peptidomimetic structure is peptide bond isosters, 
where one (or more) of the peptide bonds are replaced with ‘amide bond look-a-like’ isosters. 
This replacement has been shown to limit the degradation by enzymes. 
 
The aim of this project was to synthesize an oxetane-modified GLP-1 analog, to investigate 
whether this modification could prolong the half-life of GLP-1. The synthesis was planned to 
involve an oxetane containing dipeptide building block and a GLP-1 (10-37) peptide, which 
could then be coupled together.  
The oxetane containing dipeptide building block were synthesized manually in solution and 
several different synthetic strategies investigated to identify procedures giving the desired 
compound in satisfying yield. GLP-1 (11-37) (D) peptides were synthesized by automated 
solid-phase peptide synthesis (SPPS). Afterwards, the oxetane modified dipeptide (C) was 
integrated into the peptide chain to obtain an oxetane modified GLP-1 analogue (A), which 
could be used to study the effect in prolongation of the GLP-1 half-life.  
 
Characterization of the modified GLP-1 analogues were performed by matrix-assisted laser 
desorption-ionization (MALDI) and liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS). 
Data from the MALDI analysis indicated successful integration of oxetane dipeptide building 
block into the peptide chain.  
 
Unnatural amino acids have also been shown as promising building to prolong the half-life of 
peptide drugs. When incorpotated into the peptide drug, they are not recognized be the

proteolytic amino acids, which result in higher life-time. In this project, I also studied the 
synthesis of a novel unnatural γ-amino acid building block. To  synthesize the desired γ-amino

Reaction with an activated alkyne derivative, hereby forming sulfonamide product was

1. Introduction  
 
Peptides play essential roles in the human body and show have a great specificity towards their 
receptors. For that reason, peptides have been of great interest in the pharmaceutical industry 
and today, more than 60 US Food and Drug Administration authorized peptide medicines are 
for sale [73]. Among the many positive effects, there are as well drawbacks and limitations. 
One of them is the short circulating plasma half-life, caused by degradation by metabolic 
proteases, especially DPP4.  
In this thesis, I will develop a novel GLP-1 variant, which hopefully can not be recognize and 
degradated by DPP-4 enzyme, hereby increasing half-life. Specifically, I will synthesize a 
oxetane modified dipeptide moiety and incorporate it in the GLP-1 structure [62]. Oxetanes 
have been found to be good bioisosteres for amide bond. Therefore, it would be interesting to 
study an oxetane modified GLP-1 sequence and study the effect on enzymatic stability and the 
half-life of the peptide.  
Unnatural amino acids could also prolong the plasma half-life, because they can not be detected 
by enzymes. The synthesis of a novel unnatural γ-amino acid will also investigate in this thesis.  
 
1.1 Peptides as drugs  
 
Peptides have many important roles in human body such as hormones, ion channel ligands, 
neurotransmitters therefore peptide therapeutics have a crucial part in pharmaceutical research 
and development. Although they do not not meet any of the Lipinski rules (rule-of-five), which 
again do not make them acceptable for oral administration, they are seen as highly promising 
drugs due to high selectivity and specificity. The peptide drug market is currently growing, and 
they are expected to have even bigger impact in the future [1]. 
Peptides have a lot of advantages over other drugs. Compared to proteins and antibodies, they 
are less costly to manufacture. When comparing with small molecule drugs, they are more 
specific for their targets due to more interactions with the receptor. Generally, peptides have 
higher activity per unit mass, greater organ penetration, greater efficacy, selectivity, higher 
chemical and biological diversity [2]. Due to the fact that they are made from natural 
components, the degradation products of peptides are amino acids, leading to minimized risk 
of systemic toxicity, low likelihood of drug-drug interactions [3]. However, although peptide 
drugs have a lot of benefits there are as well limitations.  
==PAGE:13==
 
2 
In most cases naturally occurring peptides are considered as poor drug candidates because of 
their physicochemical properties and pharmacokinetic profiles, including poor bioavailability 
[4]. They show fast degradation by proteolytic enzymes of the digestive system and in blood 
plasma, which result in a disadvantageous short half-life, as they are very quickly excreted from 
the circulation. In addition, due to their general hydrophilic properties, they have a poor 
capacity to cross physiological barriers. [1,5].  
In figure 1. you can find a SWOT analysis of peptides as drugs.  
To solve the limitations (weaknesses) of peptide drugs, research has been focused on 
developing methodologies for prolonging the lifetime of peptide drugs as well as developing 
alternative formulation strategies and routes of administration. In this thesis, I will focus on 
strategies for prolongation of half-life, as this has been the focus of this thesis.

2. Approaches to increase of plasma half-life  
 
As seen in Figure 1, peptides suffer from a low plasma half-life, due to degradation by proteases 
and elimination. To tackle this problem, research has been focused towards developing methods 
to prolong the half-life. I will highlight some of the most promising strategies that has been 
developed over the years to prolong the plasma half-life of peptide drugs; strategies that could 
potentially allow oral administration of peptide drugs.  
 
Changing the secondary structure of peptides for example through cyclization is a common and 
well-studied method for protection against enzymatic degradation. In addition to the benefits 
of cyclic peptide to prevent proteolytic degradation, they also have greater absorption after oral 
administration, because they are less flexible and have strong H-bonding properties. Typical 
strategies accomplished for cyclization, include amide formation between lysine and aspartic 
acid/glutamic acid side chains and/or the C- and N-terminal, hereby forming lactam bridges. 
Also, cyclization by formation of Cys-Cys disulfide bonds has been investigated [1, 6-8]. 
 
PEGylation is another method that result in a longer life-time. Studies have shown that the 
resulting amphipathic molecule makes a steric barrier, which limit globular filtration and reduce 
clearance by circulating macrophages in the liver. Although this approach is now approved by 
FDA, it is not a frequently used method due to high safety responsibility. Also, it is stated that 
in high parenteral doses, it is toxic [7-10].  
 
Fatty acid conjugates are now also a common technique to obtain a higher bioavailability. 
Addition of fatty acids to the peptide result in higher stability, prolonging the plasma half-life. 
These effects result from the capability of forming oligomers and binding to human serum 
albumin. This makes the fatty acid-functionalization a highly attractive path to prolong the 
blood retention time for low peptide medications of low stability. Studies show that peptides 
conjugated with fatty acids will extend the absorption rate and make them more resistant against 
enzymatic degradation. Liraglutide is one of the many FDA approved fatty acid-conjugating 
peptide drugs. Liraglutide is a long-acting GLP-1 receptor agonist for the treatment of type 2 
diabetes, marketed by Novo Nordisk, Denmark [11-14]. 
 
Replacement of natural L-amino acids with D-amino acids is also a well-researched strategy. 
Studies show that replacement of the N- and C-terminal regions with D-amino acids increases 
==PAGE:15==
 
4 
the stability, because the cleavage by enzyme will not occur in vitro in both human serum and 
lysosomal preparations. D-amino acids are enantiomers of naturally occurring L-amino acids 
which means that L- and D-amino acids are mirror images of each other that have the same 
chemical and physical properties [15,16]. 
 
Lastly, the used of so-called peptidomimetics is an important method, having a crucial role in 
medicinal chemistry. An isosteric replacement of the amino functionality will mean that the 
peptide is not recognized by proteolytic enzymes. Also, it can result in different folding 
characteristic of the peptide backbone [17].  
 
In this project, I have worked in developing to different peptidomimetic structures: (1) Oxetane, 
(2) δ- amino acids. These methodologies will be discussed later in the thesis (section 8.4, 9.3 
respectively).

3. Diabetes Mellitus  
 
Diabetes mellitus is a metabolic disorder with unregulated carbohydrate, fat and protein 
metabolism, resulting in prolonged high blood sugar level (hyperglycemia), brought about by 
insufficient insulin secretion from the pancreas or the cells of the body not responding properly 
to the insulin produced [19]. The high level of glucose in the blood may cause destruction of 
organs in the body. Diabetes mellitus can result from both inherited and/or acquired insulin 
deficiency. Diabetes is a worldwide disease with approximately 150 million people suffering 
from it [20].  
 
Diabetes has several phenotypes, but the main classes are insulin-dependent diabetes mellitus 
(IDDM, type 1) and non-insulin dependent diabetes (NIDDM, type 2). These two types will be 
discussed in the sections below [18].  
 
3.1 Type 1 diabetes 
 
Insulin-dependent diabetes mellitus (DDM) requires lifelong treatment with insulin. T1DM is 
an autoimmune disorder caused by B-cell destruction. The immune system of the body destroys 
B-cells in the pancreas causing insufficient insulin production. This led to lower energy uptake 
in the cells, causing overabundance of glucose in the blood. This type of diabetes most often 
starts before the age of 30 years and the symptoms become severe really fast if untreated 
[18,19].  
 
3.2 Type 2 diabetes  
 
Most of the people who suffer from diabetes have T2DM. It is associated with environmental 
factors, such as overweight, obesity and physical inactivity. It is defined as a heterogenous 
disease caused by genetic and environmental factors, leading to insulin secretion deficiency and 
resistance to the action of insulin [18,19]. Generally, it is appearing after the age of 40 and 
develops slower and the symptoms are not as severe as T1DM.

3.3 Insulin 
 
Insulin is a small protein hormone synthesized in the pancreas as a precursor, pre-proinsulin. 
Proteolytic elimination of a signal sequence generates proinsulin. If glucose level is raised in 
the blood, proinsulin is transformed into active insulin. It has a crucial role in regulating the 
metabolism in target cells, for example skeletal and liver muscles. Insulin provide energy to the 
cells, while preventing the breakdown of stored energy. To achieve these physiological effects, 
it regulates several processes in the target cell. For example, it controls various enzymes and 
regulates glucose transport across the plasma membrane [21,23]. 
 
3.5 Pathophysiology 
 
Food consumption leads to break down of carbohydrates to glucose molecules in the gut system. 
Glucose is absorbed into the bloodstream, resulting in increased blood glucose level. This 
causes insulin secretion from the B-cells of the pancreas. The insulin mode of action (see Figure 
2) is negotiated by specific sell surface receptors. These receptors consist of an extracellular- 
and intracellular domain. Insulin binds to the extracellular domain (a-subunit), resulting in 
cross-autophosphorylation of the receptor, leading to the intracellular (b-subunit) acquiring 
tyrosine-specific protein kinase activity. Insulin triggered tyrosine phosphorylation initiate 
several downstream signaling pathways. The most important in the metabolic actions of insulin, 
is the activation of phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase (IRS-1), leading to an enhanced

4. Glucagon-Like Peptide-1 (GLP-1) 
 
Glucagon-like peptide 1 (GLP-1) is an incretin peptide hormone produced and secreted by 
intestinal enteroendocrine L-cells.  The initial product GLP1-37 is susceptible to proteolytic 
degradation giving rise to two circulating equally potent forms, GLP-1 (7-36) amide and GLP-
1 (7-37). GLP-1 (7-36) amide is the active form after food intake. GLP-1 initiate several 
physiological processes, such as activation of adenyl cyclase and cAMP generation, which 
again trigger several cAMP-dependent pathways, for example PKA, and Epac. It also increases 
glucose-induced insulin secretion, enhance insulin synthesis, inhibits gastric emptying and 
decrease appetite and food intake. Hereby GLP-1 is responsible for control of the blood glucose 
level, which makes GLP-1 an interesting molecule for use in treatment of type 2 diabetes [24-
26].

4.1 Origin 
 
GLP-1 and Glucose-dependent insulinotropic peptide (GIP) are the main incretin hormones 
generated by L-cells of the lower gut and the K-cells of the intestinal enteroendocrine cells. 
Several studies show that in healthy people GIP has an important role in mediating the incretin 
effect and hereby glucose homeostasis; however, the GIP action is decreased in patient with 
T2DM. Contrary to GIP, the insulinotropic properties of GLP-1 are retained in patients with 
T2DM; therefore, GLP-1 has a better therapeutic property compared to GIP [30].

splitting result in two forms of GLP-1: GLP-1(1-37) and GLP-1(7-37) NH2. The latter form is 
the biologically active form in the blood stream [27-29].

4.2 GLP-1 incretin effect  
 
 
The GLP-1 receptor is a class of G-protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs) that mediates the action 
of GLP-1. GLP-1R is expressed in pancreatic  a- and b-cells. Activation of GLP-1R on b-cells 
initiate a complex triggering cascade, leading to increase in intracellular Ca2+ and cAMP, and 
movement of insulin containing granules toward b-cell membranes. The granule structures 
release insulin into the blood stream resulting in lowering of the blood glucose level. At the 
same time, GLP-1 supresses secretion of glucagon from a-cell, resulting in decreased glucose 
production. Overall this contribute to a lower blood glucose level [34].  
GLP-1 has an important effect in the small intestinal lumen. Release of GLP-1 causes 
suppression of gastric emptying and delays food uptake, resulting in  a decreased postprandial 
glucose level [30-33].  
 
4.3 GLP-1 Degradation by dipeptidyl peptidase IV (DPP-IV) 
 
GLP-1 is excreted from the L-cells and after the ingestion of food, high plasma concentration 
of GLP-1 is obtain within a few minutes [30]. However, the plasma concentrarion of GLP-1 is 
decreasing really fast due to degradation by proteolytic enzymes.  
Dipeptidyl peptidase-4 (DPP4) is a proteolytic enzyme known to cleave and degradate a broad 
range of peptide substrates, including GLP-1. The enzyme recognizes the N-terminal end of the 
peptide substrate and rapidly cleaves between the second and third amino acid. In the case of 
GLP-1, this means that the peptide hormone is cleaved between the second- Histidine (His) and 
third-Alanine (Ala) amino acids, hereby creating inactive GLP-1(9-36) NH2. This degradation 
==PAGE:21==
 
10 
results in a short plasma half-life of GLP-1 (7-36) NH2, approximately 2-3 minutes [35,36]. 
DPP-4 are found in many tissues, including liver, kidney, intestinal brush-border membranes. 
DPP-4 degradation is fastest with peptides having proline or alanine residue in the second N 
terminal area [37,38], explaining the short degradation time for GLP-1.  
 
4.4 Drugs on the Market to Treat Type 2 Diabetes 
 
To increase the action of incretin hormones, different strategies habe been investigation to 
prevent DPP4 proteolytic degradation. These strategies comprise the development of stable 
synthetic GLP-1 analogs, small-molecule GLP-1 receptor agonist and DPP-4 inhibitors, all with 
the main goal to develop an efficient treatment of type 2 diabetes. In the following section, 
examples from each of these groups will be discussed.  
 
GLP-1R agonists 
• Exenatide 
Exenatide was the first GLP-1 receptor agonist approved to treat type 2 diabetes. Exenatide is 
a synthetic version of exendin-4, a natural DPP-IV resistant peptide agonist of the glucagon-
like peptide (GLP) receptor that promotes insulin secretion in Gila monster lizard salivary 
glands, hereby lowering blood glucose level. It contains approximately half of the same amino 
acid sequence (53 %) as the human GLP-1 but initiate the GLP1R with the same potency; 
therefore, it was a good candidate to treat T2DM. The drug is injected twice per day using a 
pen device, the plasma half-life is nearly 60-90 minutes after a subcutaneous injection [39,40]. 
Exenatide shows its ability to postpone gastric emptying; after breakfast it has an effect on 
glucose excrusion [30].   
• Liraglutide 
Liraglutide is a GLP-1 receptor agonist, having 97% identical amino acid sequence with human 
GLP-1, having an arginine replacement of lysine in position 34. In addition, it contains a 
glutamic acid C-16 fatty-acid (palmitic acid) on the remaining lysine amino acid in position 26 
[41], which was introduced to increase the half-life (see section 3 for a general description of 
this approach). It has a half-life of about 10-14 hours, allowing administration subcutaneously 
once per day. The prolonged half-life is explained by the fatty acid inducing oligomerization as 
well as inhancing binding to albumin in the subcutaneous tissue. Binding to albumin slows 
degradation and decrease elimination. Mode of action is similar to natural GLP-1; enhancing 
intracellular cAMP resulting in increase in insulin level [42-44].

5. Peptide Synthesis  
 
Natural peptides are made by selective coupling together members of 22 proteinogenic amino 
acids (see Appendix 1.) [47]. Currently, three strategies are used for peptide production:  
chemical synthesis, in vivo expression, and cell-free protein synthesis. All three methods have 
their strengths and limitations, with chemical synthesis being the most practical for the 
synthesis of shorter peptides (up to 30 amino acids).  
Chemical synthesis of peptides can be achieved by solution-phase synthesis, solid-phase 
peptide synthesis (SPPS) or the combination of these two methods [49]. 
Since the introduction of solid-phase synthesis by Merrifield in 1986, this technology became 
a method of high interest. In the last decade many improvements have been made by 
development of polymeric carriers and linkers, reversible protective groups and methods for 
covalent amide bond formation, contributing to the advancement of organic chemistry as a 
powerful tool for peptide research. Furhermore, today solid-phase peptide chemistry can be 
routinely made by automatic peptide synthesizers.   
In the below sections, I will give a more detailed introduction to SPPS because it is the 
technique used in this project for synthesis of peptides.  
 
5.1 Solid-Phase Peptide Synthesis 
 
In 1963 Merrifield developed the SPPS method to prevent time-consuming purification of the 
crude reaction mixture after each synthetic step [50].  
The synthesis starts with attachment of a linker on the solid support (resin), followed by 
attachment of the first C-terminal amino acid, after which the reaction occurs between the C-
terminus of the incoming amino acid and the N-terminus of the forming peptide anchored to 
the resin. Therefore, the synthesis is made from the C-terminal to the N-terminal side [51].  
The following steps are needed for each amino acid addition: 1) cleavage of the a-amino 
protecting group on the growing peptide, 2) remove deprotection side-product by careful 
washing the resin, 3) coupling of the protected amino acid, 4) final washing step for get rid of 
the excess reagents [51].

The main advantages for use of SPPS are as follows [48]: 
• The side-products, excess reagents can be easily removed by washing the resin, since 
the product is attached to the resin.

• Large excess of reagents can be used in order to ensure completion of each reaction. 
• Automatized peptide synthesizers are available.

5.1.3. Orthogonal Protecting Groups

unwanted side reactions. It is crucial to select the proper protecting groups because it should be

in side-product formation. To prevent this, orthogonal PGs (‘temporary’ and ‘permanent’ PGs) 
have been developed, which are selectively removed using different chemical condition

5.1.4. Coupling Reagents 
 
In peptide synthesis, efficient procedures for formation of the amide bond between the 
carboxylic acid functionality of one amino acid and the amino group of the other amino acids 
is really important, as incomplete reaction will lead to peptide truncation in each step, resulting 
in a highly heterogenous product mixture. Scheme 2 is a graphical illustration of a general 
peptide bond formation, which consist of two steps: in the first step, activation of the carboxylic 
acid group occurs (condensation reaction). In the second step, the resulting intermediate is 
attacked by a nucleophile in an acylation reaction. These two steps can be carried out in a one-
pot reaction by the use of coupling reagents [53].

Cleavage of the peptide from the solid support after SPPS gives a crude product mixture, which

6.1 Matrix-assisted laser desorption-ionization (MALDI) 
 
Matrix-assisted laser desorption ionization (MALDI) time-of-flight (TOF) mass spectrometry 
is one of the most powerful soft ionization techniques for analysis and characterization of 
biomolecules. The easy sample preparation and quick data acquisition makes this method 
effective for peptide identification. The principle of the MALDI technique is ionizing and 
vaporizing of the biomolecules, where ions are generated without fragmentation. The 
biomolecule is integrated to a matrix, which is able to absorb energy from a pulsed laser. This 
generates ions in the gas-phase, which allow a mass analyzer to acquire the mass spectra. In 
TOF-MS the ions are separated according to their mass-dependent speed. There are a lot of 
positive benefits to MALDI-based procedure: easy sample clean up, no need for labeling, high 
sensitivity [54,55].  
 
6.2 Liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) for peptide 
qualification  
 
Liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry is another important method, combining liquid 
chromatography (LC) with mass spectrometry (MS). MS/MS means that it is a combination of 
two mass analyzers in one instrument. The main benefit for use of MS/MS is an enhanced 
sensitivity and the achievement of more structural information (see below).  
It is important to understand the difference between LC-MS of small molecules and large 
molecules, including peptides. Small molecules produce a singly charged species, while in large 
molecules the generation of both singly charged or multiply charged species is possible. Often 
large molecules will exist as multiply charged species. The mass signal observed in MS 
corresponds to a m/z ratio, meaning that for large molecules, the mass spectrometry intensity 
will spread out over several different peaks. Peptides are generally more complex, as they can 
form various fragments, due to cleavage of the amide bond between the amino acids. This will 
lead to even less intense MS signals. However, detailed analysis of the fragmentation pattern 
can be used to predict the full structure of the backbone. There are several advantages for using

a broad linear dynamic range [56].

7. Peptidomimetics 
 
As previously discussed, (section 1) peptide-based drugs present important challenges 
according to bioavailability and biostability, because they are not stable against peptidases and 
proteases in vivo. As summarized in section 2, different chemical modifications of the peptide 
has been used to improve the stability, with peptidomimetics being an essential strategy in 
medicinal chemistry. The structure of peptidomimetics are designed to retain (or improve) the 
biological activity of the natural peptide, while circumventing the problems associated with a 
natural peptide. Peptidomimetics are designed to enhance the bioavailability and improve 
stability, avoiding enzymatic degradation. The design of peptidomimetics starts by analyzing 
the core part (pharmacophore) being important for the biological activity; ensuring that you will 
retain activity. This is achieved by performing a structure-activity relationship (SAR).  
One important and widely used type of peptidomimetics is peptide bond isosteres. In peptide 
isosteres, one (or more) of the amide-bonds in the backbone is exchanged with ‘amide-bond 
look-a-like’ isosters. Importantly, the isosters are designed to have control on secondary 
structure, which is ensured through maintainance of important hydrogen bond interactions [57, 
58]. However, a change in the backbone structure of peptidomimetics make them not 
recognized by enzymes, hereby inhibiting their degradation and prolonging their half-life. 
Peptide bond isosteres are powerful methods that has been used in various applications, 
including the study of protease activity, [62] as well as modulating polarity and bioavailability 
[81].

7.2 Oxetanes in drug discovery  
 
Oxetanes are functional groups, which can function as an isoster for the carbonyl group [59].

for each chemical reaction carried out in this thesis. Some of the reactions for synthesis of the

The following methods were used for characterization the oxetane modified dipeptides: 1H-
NMR, 13C-NMR, IR spectroscopy.

To build the desired peptide chain (GLP-1(10-37) (D), the first 8 amino acids was coupled to 
give, Compound 1, which was analyzed by LC-MS. Then 5 more amino acids were added to 
Compound 2, 3 and 4, and 4 amino acids to Compound 5 (see Figure 12 for the 1-5 Compound). 
The first 3 compounds were synthesized on 500 mg resin with a loading of 0.40 mmol/g. For 
compound 4 and 5, the reaction was scaled down to 200 mg resin. It was scaled down to save 
some of the intermediates, as it becomes more difficult to couple when the peptide chain is 
longer. If any inefficient coupling was observed, giving a truncated peptide, we would be able 
to continue from one of the intermediates.  
 
After having finish synthesis of the desired peptide chain (GLP-1(10-37) (D), the resin was 
washed with methanol (MeOH) and dichloromethane (DCM), and were dried for a few hours. 
It was then stored in the refrigerator. 
 
After synthesis, each compound was analyzed by LC-MS and MALDI to prove the formation 
of the desired peptide chain. A small sample of the resin was taken out from each compound 
(1-5) and cleaved from the resin. This was carried out by swelling the resin for 2 hours (h) in 
95 % trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) in the presence of 2.5 % triisopropylsilane (TIPS) and 2.5 % 
H2O as scavengers, which cleave the peptide from the resin and removes all acid-labile 
protecting groups [66]. The resin was filtered off, and the peptide precipitated by addition of 
diethyl ether to the TFA cleavage mixture. The precipitated peptide was washed several times 
with diethyl ether to get rid of excess TFA and sidechain protecting groups byproducts. After 
drying for a few hours, the isolated peptides were dissolved in acetonitrile (MeCN) and 
analyzed by LC-MS. For the MALDI analysis, the peptides were dissolved in a mixture of 50 
% H2O and 50 % MeCN.  
Both LC-MS and MALDI samples confirmed the formation of the desired peptide (see results 
in Figure 13).

8.4 Enzyme (DPP-4) degradation study  
 
It is well known that GLP-1 (7-36) amide is degraded primarily by dipeptidyl peptidase 4 (DPP-
4). It cleaves off the N-terminal dipeptide yielding an inactive metabolite, GLP-1 (9-36) amide 
[35,36]. To investigate whether the oxetane modification had led to higher atability, the 
sensitivity of oxetane modified GLP-1 was investigated and compared to the sensitivity of 
natural GLP-1 (9-36) amide towards DPP IV. 
 
The protocol to measure DPP4 enzymatic activity was performed as described by Prospec 
(provider of the DPP4 enzyme used in our assays).  
DPP IV degradation of GLP-1  
To test the ability of the oxetane modification to protect GLP-1 from DPP IV degradation, 
1mg/ml of GLP-1 in PBS buffer, pH 7.4 was incubated with 1.25mU/ml of DPP IV for 1 min, 
5 min, 10 min and 2 hour at 37°C. The reactions were quenched by addition of DDP IV 
inhibitor, (S)-1-[4-methyl-1-(2-pyrimidinyl)-4-piperidylamino]acetyl-2-pyrrolidinecarbonitrile 
as ordered from Sigma Aldrich. Next, the solutions were filtered through a100 kDa MWCO 
filter. The filtrates containing degraded and un-degraded GLP-1 were analyzed by reverse phase 
HPLC. Unfortunately, it was not possible to analyse the samples by HPLC due to low 
concentrations of GLP-1 in the assay. Therefore, it will require the assay at higher peptide 
concentration. Time-limitations of the current project, did not allow further investigation at this 
time. Peptide samples (GLP-1 (9-36) amide and oxetane-modified GLP-1 (9-36) amide has 
been submitted for further analysis at the DTU Food institute, where they will investigate both 
the activety and the stability of the peptides in new future.

9. Conclusion and Perspective 
This project was focused on the synthesis and investigation of novel GLP-1 variants to avoid 
enzymatic degradation and prolong the plasma half-life.   
 
The experimental work of this thesis was divided into two main parts: (1) synthesis of oxetane 
modified dipeptides and integration into the GLP-1 peptide chain, (2) investigation of a novel 
unnatural γ-amino acid building block.  
 
Initially, work was done to find a reliable strategy to obtain a satisfying yield for the synthesis 
of the oxetane modified dipeptide. 3 different protecting group strategies were tried, with two 
of the strategies leading to satisfying yield of the nitro-functionalized intermediates (40 %). 
These were further reduced to the corresponding amines, followed by Cbz- and Fmoc- 
protection, respectively. The Cbz/tBu/n-propyl strategy gave the highest yield (68 %). Thus, 
this compound was integrated into the GLP-1 peptide chain. Unfortunately, during coupling of 
the oxetane building blocks, the peptide synthesizer broke down and therefore, synthesis was 
done by manually. Fortunately, the coupling was successful, which was observed by MALDI. 
 
The second aim was to investigate and develop an effective strategy for synthesizing a novel   
γ-amino acid building block. The first step was successfully prepared with a yield of 92 % (ee). 
For the second step, small  test reactions were made in order to find successful strategies for 
obtaining the desired sulfinamide product. Several catalysts were tested and reactions analyzed 
by LC-MS. Most of them did not show any product formation; however, when using LDA and 
n-butyllithium bases, a product with the desired mass was observed. However, it was not 
possible to isolate and confirm formation of the desired product. Thus, more work is needed to 
optimize the conditions.  
 
A future perspective of this project is to finish the full coupling to obtain Compound A. We 
showed that the oxetane modified dipeptide was successfully coupled to the growing chain, 
missing only one coupling (His) to get the full oxetane-modified GLP-1 peptide sequence. This 
will be followed by kinetic studies of enzymatic stability, comparing with the natural GLP-1 to 
see whether this modification could prolong the half-life of the GLP-1 peptide. Also, we will 
be investigated whether binding to - and activation of the GLP-1 receptor is preserved. If 
activity is preserved and the peptide is showing higher stability, it would be interesting to do 
more studies in vitro and in vivo, as a potential treatment for T2DM.

The machine was started to swell the resin at 70 ºC in DMF. Initially, the Rink Amide Linker 
was coupled to the resin, which will give the C-terminal amide peptide after cleavage. When 
this was completed, the first amino acid was coupled. After more than 10 amino acids, each 
amino acid couplings were repeated three times at 75 ºC to make sure that the couplings were 
successful.  For the couplings, we used DIC/Oxyma reagents. Deprotection of the Fmoc

synthesis started. Then, the vessels were placed in the right position on the machine along with

separation was repeated several times to make sure to get rid of excess TFA.
