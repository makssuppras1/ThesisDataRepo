Abstract
In a world with an increasing demand for creating smarter and more innovative technologies, the
globe is consuming a more substantial amount of energy every year. Concerns have been raised,
on how to supply the world with energy without compromising for the future generations [47].
As a result, surplus heat sources and surplus heat have gained signiﬁcant attention over the
past decades. Buildings and infrastructures are paying a large part in the contribution towards
the consumption in a developed country, and a change in practise must occur. The research
has examined and compared potential surplus heat sources in a new area to be constructed at
Fornebu, in Norway. Three sources have been further examined and compared. Design 1 ex-
amined the potential of utilizing surplus heat from the local metro station. Design 2 examined
the potential of utilizing surplus heat from the new commercial buildings to be constructed in
the district. Design 3 examined the potential of installing solar thermal collectors on the roof
top of the residential buildings in the new district.
From the results, it was found that design 1 was the best rated design with an overall score of
8.6 / 10. It was found that indicator 1, coverage of estimated heat consumption in the resi-
dential area, was one of the most essential indicators. The results of the remaining indicators
showed a similar trend for the three designs, while indicator 1 showed a varying result. Another
central ﬁnding related to the study is the importance of examining potential local heat sources
in a new area.
Keywords: Surplus heat, Norway, Sustainability, Building Regulation, District heating, PED

1.1
Background
In a world with a greater demand for energy, as a result of dramatically increasing technological
innovations, the world consumes a larger amount of energy annually [28]. In Norway, the av-
erage household uses around 40% of the total energy consumption on heating and cooling[52].
Measures have to be completed on the topic of utilizing renewable energy sources, to reach the
goal of becoming a zero energy area by 2050. [48]
Renewable energy is one of the catalysts to increase the standard of living, while lowering green-
house gas emissions [50]. Norway is one of the leading countries within energy management,
hydropower, solar power and oﬀshore wind power. The expertise on the topic of renewable
energy is highly demanded internationally. To sustain the leading title, the government are
actively investing in renewable energy, making it possible for the country to maintain their
acknowledged ﬁeld expertise.
"BREEAM is the world’s leading sustainability assessment method for masterplanning projects,
infrastructure and buildings." [7]. It is an environmental certiﬁcation that recognizes the value
of a high performed environmental assessment throughout the lifecycle. BREEAM-NOR is
the Norwegian adaption of BREEAM, and is one of the most recognized environmental assess-
ment methods in the country [23]. One of the elements in the assessment method involves the
pre-planning of a project. The evaluation of local heat sources are recognized to be of great
importance to the overall rating of a project.
In the process of creating a more livable present, while not compromising for future generations,
sustainable development has gained signiﬁcant foothold over the past decade. The amount of
support has been overwhelming, leading to a large number of studies and actions completed
on the topic. As a part of the development at Fornebu, Selvaag Bolig is planning to construct

1.2
Problem Formulation
Carbon emissions and air pollution are commonly related to transportation [6]. A highly dis-
cussed topic is the large number of vehicles that realises emission into the atmosphere. However,
the environmental footprint from buildings are higher. It is found that buildings accounts for
around 6% more pollution than transportation. The pollution produced by buildings are both
direct and indirect. Direct pollution are related to equipments that relies on combustion, and
the indirect pollution are related to for example heating that are not produced on site.
An old saying in Norway is "Do not ﬁre for the crows" and was a saying people used if some-
one let a door or window stay open for a longer amount of period. Back in the days, people
understood the amount of work that was put into the short-travelled heat and was leading to a
lower amount of unnecessary heat released. Innovations and modernizations in regards to tech-
nologies have led to a diﬀerent mindset in people. In Norway, the cold seasons are no longer a
problem. The population are installing patio heaters or gasoline to sit outside during the colder
months. Heating and cooling of buildings in Norway are usually extracted from electricity, as a
result of the aﬀordable electricity prices [40]. Energy is long-travelled and people do not have
to work for a satisfying indoor climate. We do not only ﬁre for birds, but we heat up the entire
global greenhouse.
Thermal environment has gained a great attention over the past decades [30], in regards to
heating and cooling. It is acknowledged that these pillars of the thermal environment are not
to change, but the source. The source are related to where the pillars of thermal environment
are getting their supply from.
In the process of creating more sustainable and energy eﬃcient homes, the building regulations
have changed accordingly. Local heating and eﬃcient interactions between buildings is about
to establish a considerable concept. Local heating is energy or heating produced near the end
user. Alongside local heating and eﬃcient interactions between buildings, surplus heat has also
gained a great attention.

Delimitations
One of the main delimitations related to this study is the outcome of the actual design. The
project is yet to be completed, where the diﬀering level of the buildings, ﬁnal roof design and
roof utilization, as well as total m2 per premises are not settled. The available data related
to the buildings are very generic. The information is including the total amount of m2 to be
constructed, divided into commercial buildings and residential buildings. It is expected that
the buildings are going to vary between 5-7 stories, making the outline of buildings diﬃcult
to forecast. This study assumes that all buildings are at an equivilat height, which allows
for the analysis. The simulated energy demand in the area is related to previous studies and
calculations based on the area.
This study examines diﬀerent sources, rather than delivering actual design. This study are
based on diﬀerent upon diﬀerent sources of input. The simulations completed are generic and
are touch the diﬀerent ﬁelds.

2.2
Selection of Surplus Heat Sources and Limitations
The third step of the approach have involved a selection of surplus heat sources. From the data
collection, diﬀerent surplus heat sources was evaluated upon the speciﬁc area. The three most
potential surplus heat sources was further elaborated and studied. At this stage, it was decided
to proceed further with surplus heat from the metro station, which is design 1. Design 2 con-
cluded to be surplus heat in the district and Design 3 was decided to be solar thermal collectors.
At this step, the limitations of the study and scope of work was also outlined. The topic on local
heating and sustainable development involve a range of various subjects. It have, therefore,
been crucial for this study to narrow the work. It was decided to focus on three designs, with a
maximum of 10 indicators. This was concluded to highlight the most valuable indicators, and
to maximize the insights of the research. In order to focus on three sources, it was also decided
to complete an evaluation of execution and suitability, rather than a detailed design approach.
2.3
Selection of Indicator Framework
The next step involved the selection of a suitable indicator framework. To evaluate and justify
the three designs, this study have utilized an indicator framework in the analysis. The indi-
cator framework is established to narrow down the diﬀerent ways of evaluating the designs,
in order to answer the problem formulation. The most common indicator frameworks have
been evaluated, including the domain-, goal-, sectoral-, issue-, casual- and combinations-based
framework [46]. The domain-based indicator framework is very eﬀective to ensure coverage of
the dimension of sustainability. However, it does not directly linked to the indicators to the
sustainability goals.
The goal-based indicator framework is appropriate when examining speciﬁc issues and goals,
as it is evaluates how far in the process the indicators are in terms of reaching the goal. The
framework is also beneﬁcial to evaluate if it is moving towards the right direction. A sectoral
framework is most appropriate when evaluating an issue for a sector with a speciﬁc responsibil-
ity, and are most common for municipalities, governments, policies or staﬀ. The issues-based
framework are usually covering a speciﬁc are of issue. A casual framework introduces cause-
eﬀect relationships, making it possible to identify why certain indicators are rising or falling.
Finally, the combination frameworks has the advantage of combining the above-mentioned

3.3
District Heating and Cooling
District heating is consisting of three main components; the heating plant, the transmission and
distribution system as well as the consumer systems. District heating is a heating system that
supplies heat and DHW, from hot water in insulated pipes through a district heating plant.
District heating can distribute heat to both residential and commercial buildings in an area
or district [53]. The energy source can vary between waste, biofuel, heat pumps, landﬁll gas,
natural gas, propane/butane, electricity, and fuel oil. The heating plant is distributed with two
pipes, one distributor pipe and one return pipe.
The district cooling system is an energy-eﬃcient air-conditioning system that provides cold air
through an air conditioning system. A district cooling system is based on the same principle
as district heating, but the district cooling distributes cold water rather than hot water. A
district cooling system consists of a central chiller plant, transmission and distribution system,
and consumer system [61].
District heating and cooling can also be combined and is more common in commercial buildings.
The district cooling chills down and cleans the air through the air conditioning system during
the warmer days, and the district heating system heats the building during colder days.
3.3.1
District Heating in Norway
As mentioned, district heating in Norway is more common in commercial buildings, in com-
parison to residential buildings. The organization, Norwegian District Heating was established
in 1981[54], with a vision of promoting the development of district heating and central heating
systems. Today, Norway consists of more than 40 companies who are producing and distribut-
ing district heating across the country. The leading company is Hafslund Varme, located in
Oslo, Statkraft Varme located in Trondheim, and BKK located in Bergen [53].
Every district heating plant located in Norway has to follow the Energy Act, which means that
if the heating plant is exceeding a capacity of more than 10 MW and is supplying external
consumers, the distributor of the heating plant must have license obligations. The district
heating licenses was established in 1988, where the majority of the applications was granted
between 2007-2010. Today, the majority of the applications are related to change in previous
applications rather than new developments of heating plants. A company is then connected to
a concession area, meaning that the consumers in the area are obliged to connect [41].

3.9
Sustainable Development, National and Local Goals
Sustainability and sustainable development have gained great attention during the past decades,
as a result of the increasing awareness of global warming. Sustainability origins in the idea that
the services and goods should be produced in a way that does not compromise for the future
generations, by not damaging the environment [63]. Sustainable development covers three main
pillars; the economic-, environmental- and social perspectives. Sustainable development must,
therefore, include social sustainability, environmental sustainability and economic sustainabil-
ity to achieve sustainable development.
The Sustainable Development Goals (SDG) was ﬁrst introduced during the United Nations
(UN) conference on Sustainable Development in Rio De Janerio in 2012. The ambition and
objective of creating the goals were to establish universal goals for the 193 country members
of the UN, including both developing and developed countries. The SDG’s consists of 17 in-
dividual goals, including urgent environmental, political, and economic challenges [65]. Each
goal contains sub-goals and indicators, comprehended to cover a larger amount of sustainable
development in the world. A large amount of the goals are concerning developing countries and
is not relevant for developed countries.

4.1
Selection
In a world with a higfh focus of limiting the energy consumption and improving the energy
eﬃciency, as mentioned, the desire for this study was to examine potential surplus heat sources.
The regulations in Norway are strict, meaning that the buildings constructed today have a low
leakage rate. It was, therefore, decided to focus on a higher utilization rate of heat and the
surplus heat would be an interesting ﬁeld of study.
As a result of the high focus of limiting the energy consumption, mainly based on the construc-
tion materials and structure, buildings constructed in Norway today are very energy eﬃcient.
The trend of constructing low-energy buildings, while selecting sustainable solutions in combi-
nation with awareness of global warming, has aﬀected the building regulation. Thus, Norway
have over the past decades been stricter with a generic goal of having a lower U-value on all
external parts of the building. Today, it can be seen that new buildings are almost utilizing
the maximum amount of energy internally. In correlation with creating the most sustainable
houses, studies on surplus heat sources that are not being utilized or discharged from buildings,
have been completed and highlighted.
As the new district at Fornebu are to be constructed in accordance to the TEK17, it was found
that the focus area for this study should be related to potential surplus heat sources to be uti-
lized in the district. From the research, it was found that a various of surplus heat sources have
been studied and further elaborated into actual concepts. In order to get a better perspective
of suitable surplus heat sources for the speciﬁc district, a larger amount of surplus heat sources
was examined. This allowed insights into the speciﬁc source, and its suitability for the project
at Fornebu.

Chapter 8:
Conclusion
This study desired to tackle the topic of heating and cooling of buildings in Norway. A new
district to be constructed in the suburbs of Oslo has been used as the basis for this case study.
The focus area was to examine potential surplus heat sources in the area, to be potentially used
as local heating for the residential buildings. Three diﬀerent sources were chosen and further
investigated with a chosen indicator framework and suitable indicators.
From the results, it is found that that Design 1- surplus heat from the metro station, was the
best-rated design, with an average rating of 8.6. One of the main reasons why this design stood
out the most is related to indicator 1- coverage of estimated use. It was found in the analysis
that the design has a potential of covering the majority of the estimated heat consumption in
the district. Design 2 was found to cover less than 20% of the estimated heat consumption,
while design 3 is estimated to cover around 30 %.
In sum, it is found that design 1 is a good option for residential buildings in the district. How-
ever, larger adjustments in the underground system, in regards to the projected heating and
cooling system must take place. The project of the metro-line and underground area is at the
ﬁnish line, meaning that there is a little possibility of completing such adjustments. Another
complication related to the installation of a local heating system is to ﬁnd a potential investor
and client for the project.
The results provided in regards to the three designs should be taken into account when con-
sidering the construction of a new district. It is discussed whether the designs are suitable for
the new district at Fornebu. However, the importance of the study has shown the potential of
utilizing surplus heat in a district and is found to be possible. It can, therefore, be argued that
a similar design would be suitable in a similar climate-situation, like Fornebu.
