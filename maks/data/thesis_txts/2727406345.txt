Abstract
The integration of variable renewable energy (VRE) sources into the power system requires increased system
flexibility. Energy Communities has received attention from the European Union and the Danish transmission
system operator, as a way to access more system flexibility. Energy Communities can provide demand-side
flexibility by shifting their consumption in response to imbalances in the grid. Previous research has shown,
that Energy communities can achieve economic benefits through load-shifting. Ancillary services are part of
the system flexibility and play a crucial role in maintaining energy balance in the power grid. Existing studies
on the economic potential of ancillary markets, energy communities and risk modeling in power systems are
explored in a literature review. None of these studies considered risk-taking in the ancillary markets.
This thesis investigates the economic potential for Energy Communities participating in the ancillary markets.
It also investigates how risk-taking in ancillary markets can be implemented in a stochastic optimization model
of an energy community. The energy community include controllable loads (TCL), electric vehicles (EV) and
solar panels.
Ancillary market participation and two types of risk-taking, referred to as delivery risk and
capacity risk are added to the model. Risk-taking is implemented through the use of chance constraints, sample
average approximation and CVaR constraints. Their effect on bidding strategies and model performance is
analyzed. Results show, that delivery risk and capacity risk can be interpreted in different ways. Delivery
risk does not influence bid strategy, only the optimal operation of the energy community. Capacity risk has
significant influence on the bidding strategy. Depending on the interpretation of the capacity risk, different
bidding strategies are achieved. Moderate economic benefits are observed from the implementation of both
delivery risk and capacity risk taking.

Introduction
Power system flexibility is key to the integration of variable renewable energy (VRE) sources [1]. Historically,
system flexibility has primarily been provided by conventional power plants due to their highly controllable
production. VREs like solar and wind are more volatile and unpredictable. As the share of VREs increases,
replacing more flexible production, new ways of providing system flexibility are needed to maintain a stable
power system. The Danish Energy Agency (DEA) lists demand-side flexibility as one of five measures to increase
power system flexibility [2]. With demand-side flexibility, demand is intelligently adjusted to account for minor
and medium system imbalances, typically over a period of days, hours or even seconds [2]. Consumers can
achieve economic benefits simply by shifting consumption to hours when the spot price or energy tariffs are
low, thereby saving money on the electricity bill. Demand-side flexibility can primarily be provided by large
and mid-level companies, aggregators and private household consumers.
Where demand-side flexibility has
shown an economic potential for companies and aggregators, the potential for private households seems to be
more modest [2]. Over the last couple of years, Energy Communities, where private households collaborate
and aggregate their assets, have gained interest. Research shows that, by being part of an energy community,
private households can reduce energy costs and become less dependent on the grid [3], [4].
Energy Communities have also gotten attention from the EU and the Danish transmission system operator
(TSO) Energinet as a way to access more system flexibility. In the EU Directive 2019/944 [5], the concept of
Energy Communities was introduced in the context of EU and is defined as a legal entity that ”has for its primary
purpose to provide environmental, economic or social community benefits to its members or shareholders or
to the local areas where it operates rather than to generate financial profits”. The directive states that these
communities should be able to access all electricity markets, either directly or through aggregation. In 2022,
the Danish Energy Agency (DEA) released a subsidy pool specifically to promote the development of Energy
Communities in Denmark [6].
Another way to contribute with demand-side flexibility is through ancillary services. Where research has already
been done on how energy communities can reduce cost through load shifting, e.g. [3], [7] and [8], little research
has, to this author’s knowledge, been made on the economic potential of energy communities participating in
ancillary markets. In the following, power systems and the role of ancillary services are introduced, as they play
a central role in the work of this thesis.
1.1
Power Systems and Ancillary Markets
Electricity is an instantaneous good, which means that it cannot accumulate in the power system. It is therefore
vital to at all times keep a precise balance between production and consumption. The Danish TSO Energinet
is responsible for maintaining energy balance in the grid. To do this, several ancillary markets look to procure
a pre-determined amount of reserve capacity. Power balance in the grid is measured by frequency, which in
Europe should be kept precisely at 50 Hz. When the frequency drops below 50 Hz, up-regulation reserves are
activated. Up-regulation is done by either increasing energy supply or decreasing demand. When the frequency
exceeds 50 Hz, down-regulating reserves are activated by decreasing supply or increasing demand. To maintain
the energy balance, a line of different technological properties and services are required for different purposes.
Ancillary services are traded in ancillary markets. Different synchronous areas have different ancillary markets
with different technical requirements. Denmark is part of two different synchronous areas: DK1, which is part
of the European continental electricity system and DK2, which is part of the Nordic electricity system. This
report focuses on DK2. There are 5 different ancillary markets in DK2, namely FFR, FCR-N, FCR-D, aFRR
and mFRR. It is the Danish TSO Energinet, who buys ancillary services, as they are responsible for the system
balance. As there is high uncertainty as to what degree and when ancillary services are needed, ancillary markets
are based on reserve power capacity (measured in kW), rather than delivered energy (measured in MWh). The
TSO pays a supplier with the right technical properties to reserve capacity that can be activated in case of a
system imbalance. Depending on the market, an additional payment is given for the amount of energy that
was actually delivered during the reserve activation. In section 2, the four markets, FFR, FCRN, FCRD and
mFRR, are introduced. aFRR is not included as it, until 2023, was traded on a monthly basis, where it seems
more realistic for private households to plan their consumption from day to day.

1.2
Energy Communities and Ancillary Markets
Giving energy communities access to the ancillary markets can potentially increase the available flexibility from
the communities. If we assume energy communities are economically rational, regular load shifting is only done
to the extent where it is economically feasible to do so. If there is a larger profit from participation in ancillary
markets, compared to regular load shifting, this may encourage communities to increase the available flexibility.
The pending article [9] is an outcome of the DTU special course S46 ’Flexibility Service Integration in Citizen
Energy Communities’ which this author participated in, in the Fall of 2022. Here, an economic model was
developed for an energy community participating in ancillary markets. Results showed good economic potential
for participating in ancillary markets. Results also showed that energy imports from the grid increased notably
when participating in the ancillary markets, which may not be desirable. The model had several simplifications
that could be improved to get more accurate results. For example, the ancillary market rules outlined in section
2.2 were not considered.
In [9] ancillary markets was introduced to an energy community but without considering any risk-taking in
the ancillary markets. In section 2.2, this thesis introduces delivery risk and capacity risk, which allows the
community to take risk with regard to having the available capacity and delivering 100% of what is activated.
to allow ancillary bids with only 90% certainty in the markets.
The aim of this thesis is, therefore, to find a convenient way to implement this 90% rule in the energy community
model from [9] and examine if more flexibility can be accessed in the community while still complying with the
market rules in section 2.2. The hypothesis is that allowing risk-taking can increase the accessible flexibility as
well as increase the economic benefits for the energy community.
1.3
Literature Review
This report continues the work performed during the aforementioned special course [9] and takes its main inspir-
ation from the articles [10] and [11]. In [9], a two-stage stochastic model of a collaborative energy community’s
participation in ancillary markets was developed. The energy community consisted of 10 prosumers of 3 types:
1. Non-flexible prosumers, modeled as non-controllable loads, 2. flexible prosumers, who were able to shift
part of their demand and 3. battery prosumers, who were able to adjust battery charging and discharging.
The community participated in the DK2 markets: mFRR, FCRD up-regulation, FFR and FCRN. The model
did not allow for risk-taking, meaning that full capacity was reserved of any bid accepted in the markets, re-
gardless of the chance of activation. Even though the model was very conservative, results showed that the
community could significantly reduce their electricity costs. In some scenarios, a net profit was even achieved
when participating in ancillary markets.
1.3.1
The Economic Potential in Ancillary Markets
Thingvad and co. [12] examined the economic value of multi-market bidding in the Nordic Ancillary market,
specifically for a battery energy storage system (BESS) in the FFR, FCRN and FCRD markets. The authors
conclude that multi-market bidding, where the most profitable market can be chosen hour by hour, outperforms
single-market bidding in both FFR, FCRN and FCRD markets. From market analysis, they find that, even
though FCRN market historically has had the highest revenues, it also comes along with a significantly higher
energy throughput. This results in many battery charging cycles that reduce battery lifetime. In comparison,
FCRD market comes with a significantly lower energy throughput, prolonging battery lifetime, but also a lower
revenue. The authors conclude, that FCRD market may be better for batteries, as it will increase their lifetime.
1.3.2
Risk Modelling
A recently published paper [13] provides a general review of methods and applications which can be used for the
optimization of power systems under uncertainty. The review includes stochastic, robust and chance-constrained
optimization. The article also provides guidelines on how to formulate optimization problems and represent
uncertainty, depending on the uncertainty sources and other available information. For limiting the probability
2
==PAGE:8==
of constraint violations due to generation uncertainty, the authors recommend chance-constrained optimization.
This will become relevant when introducing risk-taking in ancillary markets.
Pacheco et al [10] propose a new method to integrate automatic and manual reserves in optimal power flow (OPF)
models as seen from the TSO perspective. The authors propose a combination of joint chance-constraint (JCC)
and complex redispatch action to model automatic generation control (AGC). Instead of using JCC to limit the
violation of technical constraints, the authors use the technique to control the probability of utilizing different
reserve actions of the TSO. The authors propose to differentiate between the implementation of Automatic
reserves (in DK2, FFR, FCRN and FCRD) and Manual reserves (mFRR in DK2). Chance constraints are
used to model uncertainty in the automatic reserves, where activation of the manual reserve is modeled as a
re-dispatch.
Another study [11] models a simplified energy community as a battery with an uncertain state of charge,
approximating the portfolio’s aggregated flexibility. The authors propose using Conditional value at risk (CVaR)
chance constraints and, in addition, Worst-case CVaR constraints to model the physical uncertain constraint on
the state of charge (SOC) in a scenario-driven stochastic optimization model. Where regular chance constraints
consider only the probability of violation, the authors emphasize that risk-based constraints consider both
the violation magnitude and probability of the violation.
The authors demonstrate that risk-based energy
constraints are good for coordinating distributed assets with unknown costs of constraint violations. They also
use shape-based clustering to capture temporal characteristics of real-life measurement data to maintain a good
representation of the uncertainty distribution. They also propose Worst-case CVaR, which, in out-of-sample
tests, shows to shield significantly better against extreme out-of-sample outcomes, compared to using the regular
CVaR constraint. The authors state that ”CVaR is particularly beneficial over CC in problems where violation
costs are hard to be characterized ex-ante, e.g. for thermal discomfort”. The authors consider the simplified
community participating in the day-ahead and real-time imbalance markets, but not participation in any reserve-
markets. As the authors have simplified the community to a battery, they do not consider independent bidding
strategies of individual community members. [14] proposes the use of distributionally robust chance-constrained
(DRCC) optimization to solve the centralized generation expansion planning problem (GEP), while accounting
for both long- and short-term uncertainties. By implementing distributionally robust chance constraints, the
model accounts for both operation uncertainties, flexibility requirements and adjustable conservativeness of the
solution by varying the desired confidence level of chance constraints. Affine policies are used to analytically
reformulate the distributionally robust chance constraints instead of having a second-stage decision.
1.4
Novelty and Hypothesis
This thesis aims to contribute to the exploration of how energy communities can reduce costs by allowing risk-
taking in ancillary markets. Other studies have investigated the economic benefits of electric vehicle communities
[7] as well as the fair redistribution of benefits through risk trading [8] and [3]. However, these papers only
considered participation in the forward and spot-market, not participation in the ancillary markets. In [9], the
benefits of an energy community participating in ancillary markets were investigated. However, it was assumed
that the energy community was not allowed to take any risk with regard to having the full capacity available
and delivering the full amount of activated ancillary services. It is this author’s hypothesis that this no-risk
approach puts significant limitations on the ancillary capacity available in the energy community.
This thesis explores how the implementation of risk taking in ancillary markets influences the bidding strategy
and optimal operation of the energy community.
The hypothesis is, that by allowing for uncertainty, the
community will be able to increase its available ancillary services while also improving its economic benefits of
offering flexibility. To investigate this hypothesis, the following research questions are considered:
1. How can ancillary markets bidding rules be interpreted in an optimization model?
2. How can we account for the economic risk of not delivering on ancillary bids?
3. To what degree can risk-taking in ancillary markets reduce energy costs for an Energy Community?
4. How do different rule implementation methods influence bidding strategies in ancillary markets?
5. How do different rule implementation methods influence model performance?

The thesis focuses on mFRR up-regulation, FCR-D up-regulation, FCR-N symmetric up- and down-regulation

1. Literature review to cover: 1) How risk modeling, particularly in power systems, has been done by others.

2. Development of a representative model of an energy community, including realistic asset uncertainties.
3. Implementation of ancillary market participation to the energy community model.
4. Implementation of different methods for modeling available capacity risk and risk of not delivering activ-

5. Analysis and comparison of the performance of risk models in 4.

1.5
Terminology
In this section, some central terminology used in the report is explained.
Prosumer
In this report, a prosumer is used to describe an individual member of an energy community who owns any
type of either controllable load or generation. The report primarily considers electric vehicles and heat pumps.
In this report, it is assumed all units live up to the technological requirements of all ancillary markets.
Ancillary services
It is vital to the security of supply that the energy balance between supply and demand is kept at all times. As
both supply and demand are hard to predict when planning energy production, ancillary services are needed to
contain and restore energy balance in a quick and precise way. Different technical qualities are needed to keep
the energy system in balance, including fast response, precise and reliable delivery, and magnitude. However,
all three are hard to combine in one technology. Therefore, different markets exist with different technical
requirements.
Ancillary markets
Ancillary services are traded in ancillary markets. Different frequency areas have different ancillary markets
with different technical requirements. Denmark is divided in DK1 and DK2, with separated ancillary markets.
This report focuses on DK2, which is a part of the Nordic frequency area and contains 5 different ancillary
markets: FFR, FCR-N, FCR-D, aFRR and mFRR. These markets are operated by the Danish TSO Energinet.
As there is a high uncertainty as to what degree and when ancillary services are needed, ancillary markets are
based on reserve capacity rather than delivered energy. Therefore, the TSO must buy reserve capacity in the
market, meaning the TSO pays a supplier with the right technical properties to reserve capacity that can be
activated in case of a system imbalance. Depending on the market, an additional payment is given for the
amount of energy that was actually delivered during the reserve activation.
Reserve and Activation
Ancillary services are used to ensure the energy balance in the grid. This is needed as production and con-
sumption levels cannot be predicted precisely. Therefore, the TSO must be able to make real-time adjustments
to maintain the energy balance. This can happen through up- and down regulation, depending on whether
more or less energy is needed in the grid. As the TSO does not know how much regulation will be needed, the
TSO reserves ancillary capacity which can be activated with very short notice. Ancillary capacity or reserve
4
==PAGE:10==
capacity is bought in the Ancillary reserve markets. If energy regulation is needed, the reserve capacity is then
activated. Depending on the ancillary market, an additional balance price is paid for the energy delivered during
activation.
Delivery and Non-Delivery
The term Delivery is used to describe the share of an activated ancillary which is being delivered by a prosumer
or the energy community. The term non-delivery is used to describe the share of an activated ancillary bid that
is not being delivered.
Demand Response and baseline
In [5], demand response is defined as: ”the change of electricity load by final customers from their normal or
current consumption patterns in response to market signals, including in response to time-variable electricity
prices or incentive payments, or in response to the acceptance of the final customer’s bid to sell demand reduction
or increase at a price in an organized market ”. A baseline is determined as the normal consumption pattern
a unit would have had if it did not take market signals into account.
Risk in ancillary markets
In the context of this report, risk in ancillary markets refers to capacity risk, which is the risk of not having the
full capacity available for ancillary services and delivery risk, which is not being able to deliver the full amount
of activated ancillary service.

Notation
Many model parameters and variables are introduced in this report. Capital letters are in general used for
parameters. Small Latin letters p are used for tangible decision variables. Small, Greek letters are used for
abstract variables associated with risk modeling. θ is used for temperature. The small letter y is used for all
binary decision variables. Big letters Y are used for binary parameters or percentage parameters. Downwards
pointing arrows ↓are associated with down-regulation or import from the grid. Upwards pointing arrows ↑
are associated with up-regulation or export to the grid. The positive direction for energy flow is from grid to
community to prosumer.

Background
As this report goes into detail about modeling ancillary markets, this section elaborates on the technical re-
quirements and market policies of the different ancillary markets.
2.1
Ancillary Markets
Figure 1 and 2 on the next page provide an overview of the basic properties of the four ancillary markets
mentioned below.
2.1.1
FFR
Fast Frequency Reserves (FFR) is a very fast responding primary reserve which is used to secure the dynamic
frequency stability at times when there is low inertia in the energy system. This primarily happens during the
summer, when energy consumption is not used for heating. FFR is only defined for energy deficiency situations,
when there is need for more up-regulation in the grid. As illustrated in figure 2, FFR is activated at frequencies
below 49.5 Hz. Figure 1 shows that FFR must have a response time of less than 1.3s, which is the fastest of
all ancillary markets in DK2. Full FFR support must be able to last 5-30s and must be recovered and able
to deliver a new activation response within 15 minutes [16]. FFR reserves are activated automatically through
frequency measurements at the grid connection point. FFR is rarely activated, as the frequency rarely drops
below 49.5 Hz. It is typically technologies like ventilation, batteries, electric boilers or power plants which live
up to technical requirements for FFR and are able to participate in this market [16].

The deadline for making bids in the FFR reserve market is at 15.00 the day before the production day (D-1).
Bids are accepted on an hourly basis for a 24h day at a time. Accepted reserve bids receive a marginal price
and no additional payment in case of activation. When receiving the marginal price, you receive the highest
offer price of the accepted bids. The minimum bid size for FFR is 0.3 MW and, in 2021, Energinet bought
between 0-23MW FFR reserves [15].
2.1.2
FCRN
Frequency Containment Reserves - Normal operation (FCR-N) is a fast responding primary reserve that is used
to secure the balance between production and consumption during normal operation, between 49.9 and 50.1
Hz (see figure 2. FCRN bids are symmetric, meaning that capacity for both up- and down-regulation is offered
in the same bid. FCRN reserves are activated linearly from 0-100% as the frequency moves from 50-49.9Hz
for up-regulation and from 50-50.1Hz for down-regulation. As FCRN delivers gradual activation, good control
of the frequency response is important. The maximum response time for activating the full bid must be less
than 150s [17]. In DK2, FCRN reserves must be able to continuously deliver up- and down-regulation over
the whole bid period. FCRN regulation is controlled automatically through frequency measurements at the
local grid connection point [18]. FCRN is the most active ancillary market, as the system frequency lies within
the normal area the majority of the time. It is typically technologies like electric cars, heat pumps, cooling
systems, ventilation, batteries and electric boilers which live up to technical requirements for FCRN and is able
to participate in this market [16].
In DK2, FCRN reserves are traded in two separate auctions with different gate closures. The majority of the
reserve capacity is bought in the D-2 auction (two days before), with gate-closure at 15.00. The remaining
capacity is bought at the D-1 auction, with gate closure at 18.00. Bids are accepted on an hourly basis for
a 24h day at a time. Accepted reserve bids receive a pay-as-bid price and, in addition, the marginal balance
price for the energy service delivered during activation (i.e. MWh up and down regulation). When receiving
the pay-as-bid price, you only receive your own offer price instead of the marginal price. FCRN reserves are
traded for both DK2 and Sweden. Energinet procures around 18MW FCRN reserves for DK2. Around 98MW
are procured for DK2 and Sweden in total [18]. The minimum bid size for FCRN is 0.3 MW. [15].
2.1.3
FCRD
Frequency Containment Reserves - Disturbance (FCR-D) is a fast-responding primary reserve, which is used
to regulate the frequency during larger frequency drops caused by larger breakdowns/failures in production or
transmission lines. FCR-D services are defined for both up- and down-regulation. However, in contrast to the
FCR-N market, FCR-D bids are made separately for up- and down-regulation. As illustrated in figure 2, FCR-
D down-regulation is active at frequencies between 50.1-50.5 Hz and FCR-D up-regulation is active between
49.5-49.9 Hz.
As FCR-D has only recently been defined for down-regulation, only FCR-D up-regulation is
considered in this report. FCR-D up-regulating reserves are activated linearly from 0-100% as the frequency
6
==PAGE:12==
moves from 49.5-49.9Hz. Like FCR-N, FCR-D require well-controlled frequency responses [18]. FCR-D services
must be able to deliver 50% of the full bid amount within 5s and 100% within 30s. They must also be able to
sustain a full activation for a period of 15 min. or until mFRR reserves can take over. FCR-D regulation is
controlled automatically through frequency measurements at the local grid connection point [18]. It is typically
technologies like electric cars, heat pumps, cooling systems, ventilation, batteries and electric boilers which live
up to technical requirements for FCR-D and are able to participate in this market [15].
FCR-D reserves are traded in two separate auctions with different gate closures. The majority of the reserve
capacity is bought in the D-2 auction, with gate-closure at 15.00. The remaining capacity is bought at the D-1
auction, with gate closure at 18.00. Bids are accepted on an hourly basis for a 24h day at a time. Accepted
reserve bids receive a pay-as-bid price but, in contrast to FCR-N market, no additional payment is received for
activation. In DK2, Energinet procures around 44MW FCR-D reserves in DK2 and a total of 237 MW when
including the Swedish market. The amount of FCRD reserves needed corresponds to the largest dimension unit
(N-1 rule) [18]. The minimum bid size for FCRN is 0.3 MW, but the bid is allowed to be an aggregation of
several units at different locations [15].
2.1.4
mFRR
Manual Frequency Restoration Reserves (mFRR) is a tertiary reserve that is used to replace or add needed
production capacity. Replacing capacity may be needed if producing units break down, there are interruptions
on transmission lines, or there are last minute deviations in consumption or production forecasts. As illustrated
in figure 2, mFRR is active at frequencies below 49.5 Hz. mFRR reserves are typically activated in larger
amounts at a time, and the response time to activate a full bid is 15 mins. Energinet activates mFRR bids
manually by contacting the operator, which, oftentimes, will be able to activate production with an online
signal. mFRR bids must be able to deliver full activation for a minimum of an hour [18]. It is typically larger
technologies like wind, electric boilers, industry and power plants that participate in the mFRR market, as they
are able to deliver the required magnitude of energy during activation [15].
mFRR reserves are traded in two separate auctions with different gate closures. Around 60% of the reserve
capacity is bought for a month at a time, with gate closure at 10.00 the 26th of the preceding month. The
remaining 40% is bought at a D-1 auction, with gate closure at 9.30. Bids are accepted on an hourly basis for a
24h day at a time [18]. Accepted reserve bids receive a marginal price and, if activated, the additional marginal
balance price for the delivered amount. The minimum bid size for mFRR is 1 MW, but the bid is allowed to
be an aggregation of several units at different locations. In 2021, Energinet procured about 328 MW and 271
MW of mFRR reserves in the monthly and daily markets [15].
2.2
Market Rules
Besides living up to the technical requirements mentioned in section 2.1, ancillary suppliers must also live up
to what I will refer to as Ancillary market rules in the remaining report. The information is from a meeting
between Andrea Gloppen Johnsen and Energinet employee Thomas Dalgas [19].
1. Consumption technologies with inherent intermittency or uncertainty are required to demonstrate a fore-
cast method that is able to accurately predict what amount of ancillary service can be provided.
2. Participants must have a forecasting method with hourly resolution and show that their forecasting method
is 90% accurate. Participants are expected to use the same forecasting method when placing bids in the
ancillary markets.
3. When bidding in the ancillary markets, the 90% quantile of the expected capacity available should be
used. This means, that you must be 90% sure your capacity is available. If continuously and significantly
less than 90% of activated reserve bids are delivered, Energinet may exclude providers from the ancillary
markets.
4. Consumption technologies must provide a baseline for default consumption, such that the amount of
ancillary services delivered can be calculated. The baseline may be provided for an aggregated portfolio
of technologies, but individual baselines must be available for individual technologies in case Energinet
wishes to inspect them.
7
==PAGE:13==
5. In a scenario where ancillary reserve bid is not available after all, Energinet must be informed immediately
and reserve payment is returned to the TSO. Depending on the severity and magnitude of the canceled
bid, the service provider may also be obliged to pay for replacing the lost reserve capacity, at a price of
maximum 3 times the reserve price originally received.

This section covers the methods used to develop a mathematical model of an energy community that is able to
take risks while participating in ancillary markets. First, the overall structure and assumptions about the energy
community is presented in section 3.1. In sections 3.2 and 3.3, methods for the modeling of community assets

I next provide an overview of the energy community structure. The energy community model is a 2 stage
stochastic model, where the first stage decision is bidding in the four ancillary markets for a 24h period. The
second stage decisions are the optimization of the operation of community assets to minimize the community
electricity cost while complying with the ancillary markets rules in section 2.2.

3.2
TCL Modelling
As listed in table 1, four prosumers own a TCL. There are different ways to model TCLs with various advantages
and disadvantages. A common way to model a TCL is by using asymmetric block offers, where sets of response
and rebound blocks are predefined data constructed from well-defined temperature curves of the TCL [20].
Here, binary variables are used to choose the optimal response blocks. This method makes it easy to ensure
well-controlled and defined temperature changes and that the TCL does not reduce costs by using less energy
and maintaining a lower temperature. However, for the purpose of this report, we are interested in modelling the
uncertainty of TCL demand. For a private household, the TCL demand depends on the outdoor temperature
as well as the thermal properties of the house.
To use the TCL for load shifting, we want to allow house
temperatures to deviate from the optimal indoor temperature. At the same time, we want to limit the deviation,
as an indoor temperature of, say, 15oC would be very uncomfortable for the prosumer. Limiting the temperature
deviation is also important with regard to preventing the model optimizer from driving TCL consumption to
zero and saving a lot of money just from reducing consumption, rather than from load shifting.
Following the approach suggested in [21], the TCL is modeled as a generalized battery. This allows the TCL
to take any continuous power setting between 0 and its maximum power capacity, allowing it to fine-tune its
consumption. In [21] the authors successfully model a group of TCLs as a stochastic battery with uncertainty
in outdoor temperature and user-specified limits on acceptable temperature deviations. The relation between
indoor temperature and TCL power consumption is expressed with a continuous power model in (5).

3.4
Ancillary Markets Modelling
This section covers how participation in the DK2 ancillary markets are modeled without considering risk-taking.
The following assumptions are made about the energy community:
1) The community is able to participate in the four ancillary markets: FFR, FCRN, FCRD (with up-regulation
only) and mFRR. The remaining energy demand is covered by importing from the grid at a consumer spot price
(i.e. including tariffs and taxes). Excess energy not used for ancillary service and internal consumption can be
sold at producer spot price (i.e. raw spot price without tariffs).
2) The minimum bid size accepted in ancillary markets is 300 kW [18]. It is assumed that the energy community
is part of a larger portfolio of aggregated ancillary service providers who are able to bid above the minimum
required bid size in the different ancillary markets. The community TCL and EV technologies live up to the
technical requirements (for example, response times) to participate in all four ancillary markets. The available
ancillary capacity for each prosumer, as well as storage capacity, is listed in table 1.
3) It is assumed that the community has a community manager who is responsible for aggregating the capacity
of the individual prosumers and bidding in the ancillary markets. It is also the community manager who has
the obligation to meet requirements for participating in ancillary markets (see section 2.2).
11
==PAGE:17==
4) Prosumers are able to share electricity which is not used for ancillary services. This means that, if a prosumer
has reserved ancillary capacity, another prosumer is not able to deliver the ancillary service. However, through
equation (3b) prosumers are able to share the excess energy from for example PV panels, which would have
otherwise been sold to grid. This helps minimize energy importation and exportation from the grid, thereby
reducing grid dependency.
5) Hourly baseline consumption profiles, including uncertainty, are calculated for the individual TCL or EV
prosumer. A detailed description of how these baselines are constructed are provided in sections 3.2 and 3.3.
6) The community manager is responsible for the community living up to the requirements for ancillary market
participation (see section 2.2). The ancillary service delivery is measured for the community as a whole, meaning
as the average for all community prosumers.

3.5
Risk in Ancillary markets
In this optimization model, the goal is to optimize the bidding strategy in ancillary markets while complying
with the 90% rules for bid capacity and activation delivery introduced in section 2.2. I refer to the risk of
violating these two rules as capacity risk and delivery risk.
Capacity risk is the risk of bidding reserve capacity that you are not certain you have available. An example
could be the EV owner placing a reserve bid in from hour 17-18, yet does not return with his EV before hour
19. Even if there is no activation in that hour, he is still bidding capacity he does not have in the ancillary
market. In section 2.2, participants are required to bid the 90% percentile of the reserve capacity they expect
to have available. Energinet does regular sample controls to check if the capacity is available. The economic
consequence of bidding with less than the 90% certainty that ancillary capacity is available is to be excluded
from the ancillary markets. Implementation of the capacity risk is done through changes in the TCL and EV
capacity constraints in equations 19.1, 19.2, 20a and 20b.
Delivery risk is the risk of not being able to deliver the amount of ancillary service you are supposed to when
an ancillary bid is being activated. An example could be that the EV battery is connected, but not sufficiently
14
==PAGE:20==
charged to deliver the full ancillary service. The economic consequence of delivering less than 90% of the bid
activation is to return the reserve payment to the TSO. Reserve payment is only returned for the part of the
activated bid which is not delivered during activation. It is a simplification to assume that only part of the bid
payment must be returned. It is also assumed that the community is not large enough to risk paying for the
replacement of the lost capacity. If less than 89% on average is being delivered, ancillary market participants
risk being excluded. Delivery risk is implemented by changing the equations 18.1-18.5.
The level of capacity or delivery risk is measured collectively for the community. That is, if one prosumer
delivers less than 90% of their bid activation, the community on average is still able to deliver more than 90% of
the total activated bids in the community. This contributes to minimizing the risk for the individual prosumer.
[13] provide a review of various methods to implement uncertainty in power system models. The following
should be considered to successfully implement uncertainty in an optimization problem:
1. Uncertainty propagation: What are the sources of uncertainty, and how do they combine and propagate
through the model to become uncertainty in the model output?
2. Uncertainty representation: What is known about the probability distributions of the uncertainty in-
put/sources? What data is available, and who has access to it? How can the uncertainty be represented
mathematically?
3. Optimization under uncertainty: How should the risk associated with the second stage be measured and
managed in the optimization model?
4. Tractable approximation and solution algorithms: How can we make the optimization problem tractable
while keeping the uncertainty representation?
3.5.1
Chance Constrained Optimization
Both Capacity risk and Delivery risk are concerned with limiting the probability of a constraint violation while
allowing for some uncertainty. A common method used to limit constraint violation is Chance Constrained
optimization [23]. Here, the probability of constraint violation is first expressed as a continuous probability
function, which is then discretized to apply it to a set of representative scenarios. Chance constraints (CC) use
binaries to count the number of scenarios in which a constraint is being violated. The number of occurrences
approximates the probability of constraint violation.
The advantage of chance constraints is that it allows for second stage decision-making, which is an important
quality since it allows for optimize the operation of the community, after the uncertainty is revealed. Chance
constraints also allow the best exploitation of the historical data available. The downside is that chance con-
straints only limit the probability of a constraint violation, not the size of the violation. In addition, chance
constraints are mostly non-convex, making them challenging to solve. This is partially due to the fact that
chance constraints include binaries, making them hard to scale [13]. The basic form of a chance constraint is
formulated in optimization problem (24).

3.6
Risk Implementation
We now have all the theory needed to introduce chance constraints for the capacity and delivery risk in sections
3.5 to the model. In the community model, the decision variables x consist of the first stage decisions to bid
in the ancillary markets as well as the second stage decisions on how to operate the community. The uncertain
parameters ξ represent the accumulated uncertainties from the community and the ancillary market.
To measure the delivery risk, we must define the risk operator G(x, ξ) such that it measures the amount of
activated bids which is not delivered by the community. In this case, I will present four different delivery risk
operators. We must also construct i.i.d. samples of the uncertainty vector ξ and decide the number of scenarios
N to use, to get a good SAA representation.

Considering the community optimization problem referred to as the no risk model introduced in section 3.4,
including ancillary market participation (The full mathematical formulation can be found in appendix A). All
variables are continuous, and so, the solution space X is a subset of Rn. All constraints are linear, and all
inequality constraints include the boundary in the solution space as they only include equalities or ”less than
or equal to” formulations. All decision variables are bounded by a constraint. This makes the solution space is
a compact set [28]. The objective function is a second order polynomial mapping from Rn →R. The problem
is therefore a convex optimization problem defined on a closed and bounded set (see full model in appendix A).
The uncertainty vector ξ consist of both the community and ancillary market uncertainties from section 3.6.1.
All uncertainties are associated with d unknown but continuous probability distributions. Therefore, ξ is defined
on a set Ξ ⊂Rd. However, only discrete samples ξi are available in the form of historical data. By choosing a
risk operator with the mapping G : Rn × Ξ →R, the community problem is of the type (24) and we are able to
add a chance constraint to the problem.
As we have a continuous solution space X and samples ξi from historical data, G(x, ξi) is a Carath´eodory
function, as it is both continuous in x ∈X and measurable in ξ ∈Ξ (Definition 1.1 in [29]). By choosing samples
ξi that are i.i.d. then from proposition 6.1 in [24] we know, that by reformulating the community problem as an
SAA problem (28), the optimal solution will converge to the optimal solution of the real community problem
with probability 1 as N →∞.
3.6.1
Approximating the uncertainty vector ξ
For an energy community participating in ancillary markets, there are many different sources of uncertainty to
consider. We can divide uncertainty sources into Community uncertainty and Ancillary markets uncertainty.
Not all uncertainties can be included in the model, but the ones with the most importance have been included.
As mentioned in section 3.5.2, it is important, that the samples ξ are i.i.d. Here we take advantage of the
large amounts of historical data that is available for ancillary markets, weather conditions etc. to generate a
sufficiently large sample pool, so we can draw random samples and assume they are i.i.d. In the following I
introduce the historical uncertainty data used to approximate the uncertainty vector ξ.
Community uncertainties are uncertainties that influence the available capacity, energy demand and oper-
ation of community assets. Community uncertainties considered in this model include solar radiation, outdoor
temperature, EV driving pattern and household energy demand. PV production and outdoor temperature are
correlated as they follow the seasons. They also repeat a daily pattern with a peak in the middle of the day.
Where household demand and EV driving patterns are not correlated with other uncertainty sources, they also
have a daily pattern built in. On a weekday, most EVs disconnect in the morning and return in the afternoon
when prosumers return from work. Similarly, household demand typically peaks around dinner time, when
prosumers return home. All prosumer uncertainty should, for this reason, be considered for a 24h period to
preserve the daily pattern.
Ancillary markets uncertainty refer to the uncertainties in the ancillary markets. This includes reserve
market prices, spot and balance prices, bid acceptance, and bid activation. Much of the uncertainty sources in
the Ancillary markets are correlated. The activation in the different markets all depend on the grid frequency.
The balance price is closely related to the spot price: When there is a need for up-regulation in the grid, the
balance price is almost always higher than the spot price and vice versa when down-regulation is needed. Market
prices and activation are also correlated; if a lot of up-regulation is needed, it is likely that the activation price
will increase due to supply-demand dynamics. Spot and balance prices typically follow a daily pattern, the duck
curve, where increased demand in the morning and evening hours results in higher prices in these hours.
The probability distribution of the community and ancillary market uncertainties is unknown. However, plentiful
historic data is available, which can be used to estimate distributions of the uncertainty. For example, solar
and temperature data is available at an hourly resolution for an entire year or more [30]. Hourly data on total
private household consumption in DK2 is available for a year or more at [31]. Most data for Ancillary market
is available at an hourly resolution for several years at [32]. As historical data is not available for EV driving
patterns, they are modeled using Poisson distributions for arrival and departure times. Activation data is not
directly available for FFR, FCRN and FCRD markets. Instead, frequency data for the nordic frequency area is
available at [30] with a 10Hz (10 measurements pr. second) resolution for several years.
18
==PAGE:24==
When sampling scenarios, it is assumed that the ancillary market uncertainty is independent of the community
uncertainty. Uncertainty sources in the ancillary markets are best represented by treating all ancillary market
data (activation, acceptance, reserve prices, balance prices and spot prices) as correlated and part of the
same, multidimensional risk distribution. As there tends to be a daily pattern in, for example, spot prices
(with peaks in the morning and evening), it is important to maintain the pattern during the day. Similarly,
the correlated PV and temperature data is sampled for the same 24h time period to create a sample.
As
a simplification, household data is also considered part of the same sample.
Lastly, EV driving pattern is
represented as a discretized probability distribution. The data processing and construction of the independent
identically distributed samples and the discretized probability distribution for EV driving pattern is further
elaborated in section 4.
3.6.2
Choosing the confidence level γ and the number of samples N
Recalling the ancillary market rules, we want to achieve 90% delivery of activated bids and choose a confidence
level of 95%. This is achieved by choosing δ = 5% α = 10% in equation (29). Choosing the SAA confidence
level γ is a balance between the need for a reasonable sample size N and getting a closer bound on the actual
non-delivery α. In this report, we use γ = 5%. Then we need N = 260 samples to get a good approximation of
the distribution p(x) in equation 27.

4.1
Data with Uncertainty
The uncertainties are represented in a large set of scenarios which are representative of the various uncertainties
in the model. To achieve good representation of the uncertainties, some data processing has been done. In the
following, the data and data processing is introduced.
Whenever historical data is used, data has been taken for the whole of 2021, giving 365 raw data samples. This
is except solar data, which is taken for year 2019 instead of 2021. All historical data is in hourly resolution
except frequency data which is in a 10Hz (10 measurements pr. second) resolution.
As the model consider a period of 24 hours, all historical data is sampled for 24 coherent hours at a time from
midnight to midnight the following day. Solar and outdoor temperature are both seasonally dependent and are
assumed to be strongly correlated. Ancillary market data is often related. Balance price is directly related to
the spot price, activation levels in the different markets are all related to frequency levels.
To create many samples to best represent the many uncertainties in the model while keeping the correlations
mentioned above, raw data was divided in two pools. One with community and one with ancillary market data.
This way correlations between data could be kept within the pool, but where it could be assumed that there
were no strong correlations between data in the two pools. This resulted in two pools with 365 samples in
each, which could be combined to create one pool of 133,225 unique samples with good representation of the
uncertainties faced by the community.
4.1.1
Community Data
Community uncertainty data include solar data, outdoor temperature, EV driving pattern and household energy
demand. Particularly, solar and outdoor temperature are considered to be correlated as they both follow seasonal
changes.
Solar data were taken from [36]. Data is with an hourly resolution for the whole year of 2019. Ambient temper-
atures were taken from [37] with an hourly resolution for the whole year 2021. We assume, that temperature
and solar levels are correlated, and that solar levels were similar for 2019 and 2021. Solar and temperature data

==PAGE:30==
are sampled together, such that solar data from 9-1-2019 and temperature data from 9-1-2021 are always in the
same sample.
Community data also includes uncertain household demand, which is based on the hourly consumption scenarios
of a normal danish household, excluding heat consumption. To achieve the daily consumption pattern, the daily
profile for total demand for private consumer from [31] was used and scaled down to one household scale, using
estimations from [38]. Individual consumption scenarios were generated for the 10 prosumers by shifting the
demand curve and slightly scaling it to create variation in the scenarios.
On workdays, electric vehicles are typically away from home during work hours, leaving in the morning around
8am and arriving back home in the afternoon around 5pm. As a simplification it is assumed that all days are
work days and that cars only take one trip a day. Uncertainty in EV driving pattern was created by sampling
sampling departure and arrival times randomly from two Poisson distributions plotted in figure 5. Departure
times were sampled from the pdf (43a) with a mean at 8am and arrival times were sampled from (43a) with a

4.1.2
Ancillary Market Data
In this report, the four ancillary markets, FFR, FCRN, FCRD and mFRR are considered. To model these
markets, information on reserve market prices, bid acceptance, bid activation, balance prices and spot prices
are all uncertain at the time the community bids in the ancillary markets. All raw data related to spot and
ancillary markets, except frequency data, was retrieved from Energinets webpage energidataservice.dk. Historical
frequency data for the year 2021 were retrieved from [30]. Raw data taken from energidataservice.dk is with an
hourly resolution, raw frequency data from [30] is in a 10 Hz resolution, i.e. 10 measurements pr. second.
Spot prices, tariffs and balance prices
Spot and balance market prices are correlated, as the balance price lies slightly above the spot price at times
when up-regulation is needed, and slightly below the spot price, when down-regulation is needed. Both spot
and balance prices were retrieved from energidataservice.dk at an hourly resolution for 2021. The raw spot
price was used as the price prosumers receive for exporting energy to the grid, whereas 2021 tariffs and taxes
of around 1.5 DKK/kWh were added to get the cost of importing from the grid. The balance price is the price
pr. kW delivered, when FCRN and mFRR bids are activated.
Bid acceptance
When accepted in the FFR and mFRR markets, everyone receives the same marginal price of the highest

5.1.2
Out of sample results
As mentioned in section 3.6.1, it is important to test how the uncertainty in the input data propagates through
the mathematical model and is manifested in the solution output when working with SAA.
An out of sample (OS) test was performed on the five ancillary models in table 12, to check their robustness
and general applicability. For the OS test, the endogenous bidding strategy is fixed and reused as first stage
decisions for a new set of scenarios. In this case, the bidding strategy is identical for all five ancillary market
models. Model robustness and applicability is then assessed from how well the endogenous bidding strategy
is for the new samples. For the OS test, 1300 new scenarios were randomly sampled from a pool of 132,236
scenarios, excluding scenarios used in the original model.

slack variable allows violation of the bid capacity constraint, when the ancillary capacity bid that was fixed in
the original strategy is not available in an out of sample scenario. A penalty term was added to the objective
function (17.1), to minimize the use of slack variables:

5.2
Binding Constraints and Sensitivity Analysis
Shadow prices (i.e. dual values) can be retrieved from LPs (linear problems), but not from MIPs (mixed-integer
problems). The risk models CC, JCC and HJCC are MIPs. To collect shadow prices from these models, the
binaries were fixed to their optimal solution values, and the optimization was run again as an LP to access the
shadow prices.
Binding constraints on the no risk model are primarily constraints on the reserve capacity. The highest shadow
prices are associated with the limitation of TCL reserve capacity for up-regulation in (19.1). The highest shadow
prices for this constraint lie between at 0.386-0.633 DKK/kW. As mentioned, the available TCL capacity depend
on the outdoor temperature (eq. 7a). As the scenarios are sampled from all seasons, some scenarios are summer
days, where the TCL baseline is 0 during the day. As it is not allowed to take any capacity risk in this model,
it is the lowest common denominator of all scenarios which sets the capacity limit for the first stage decision.
This explains why there are no reserve bids during the day in figure 10. Getting more TCL capacity would have
the most impact on reducing energy costs in a no risk setup.
32
==PAGE:38==
The second-highest shadow prices are related to the limitation of EV reserve capacity for up-regulation. The
price is at 0.358 DKK/kW for only four specific constraints (i.e. prosumer, hour and scenario combination).
This is specifically for hour 24, but for four different scenarios and for four different EV prosumers. In this
case, the high shadow price does not have to do with specific conditions in the ancillary markets (high prices,
activation etc.). As this happens in four different scenarios, the high shadow price is rather related to the small
chance of an EV not being at home in hour 24 (see figure 5). In these scenarios, four of the EV prosumers have
a scenario, where they are not home at 24. As it is not allowed to take any capacity risk in this model, it is the
lowest common denominator of all scenarios that sets the capacity limit for the first stage bid decision for the
individual prosumer. Similarly, many constraints for EV reserve capacity for down regulation are also among
the higher shadow prices at around 0.335 DKK/kW. However, most of the binding constraints are concentrated
around hour 5, just before the EVs leave for work. In these hours, the EVs are not able to discharge, as they
are required to have a SOC of 80% at the time of departure.
High shadow prices are also found for the line constraint of prosumer 7 and 10, specifically for hour 24 and only
in a few constraints. These two EV prosumers are owners of a supercharger, and their capacity is therefore
limited by line capacity and not charging capacity.
Other binding constraints include limitations on EV charging and discharging and EV SOC demand at the time
of departure. To summarize, by far the most binding constraints are related to capacity rather than delivery
for the no-risk model.
CC, JCC and HJCC binding constraints
Many of the binding constraints for CC, JCC and HJCC are very similar and will be described together. The
delivery risk models share many of the same binding constraints as the no risk model, namely limitations of the
TCL and EV reserve capacity for up-regulation starting at around 0.633 DKK/kW.
For the CC, JCC and HJCC models, there are also several of the non-delivery constraints are also binding.
FCRD non-delivery has the highest shadow price of 0.01 DKK/kWh. In these cases, the community is better
off not delivering the FCRD service, but because they are required to deliver 90% of the activation, they are
forced to deliver FCRD up-regulation these hours. It also means, that employing the non-delivery elsewhere
was worth more than the 0.01 DKK/kWh. Binding constraints are also found for non-delivery of FCRN up-
regulation, with the highest shadow prices starting from 0.013 DKK/kWh. Again, the models would here be
better off not delivering, but must live up to the 90% delivery rule. That the non-delivery is primarily employed
for FCRN up-regulation is also reflected in table 11.
CVaR binding constraints
Binding constraints for the CVaR model stand out in comparison to the three chance based models. The shadow
price for FFR non-delivery (see CVaR reformulation in table 4) is a 890934 DKK/kW. Which is incredibly high,
compared to any other shadow price. An explanation could be, that the reserve price in the FFR market is
generally high, in combination with very little chance of activation, and a very small energy amounts associated
with an activation. The highest FFR reserve price observed in the data was at 0.1 DKK/kW during a summer
night. This will be further discussed in section 6. CVaR constraints for mFRR, FCRD and FCRN-up were the
next highest shadow prices at around 0.1 DKK.

Discussion
All four delivery risk models perform well in the out of sample test. As all four models result in the same optimal
bidding strategy, the models only differ from each other, is how risk is distributed during the second stage
decisions on community operation. CVaR is nice to use in combination with Sample Average approximation, as
an alternative to Joint chance constraints, as no binaries are needed, and it is convex, making it computationally
faster. It also considers both magnitude and probability of violation, in contrast to JCC, which only considers
probability of violation.
No binaries, makes it better for scalability, and it is thereby possible to consider
constraint violation on an hourly basis, rather than on (24-h) scenario basis, allowing for much more flexibility
in the optimal solution.
In the out of sample analysis in section 5.1.2, a slack variable ϕcts was added and penalized with a penalty of
50 DKK/kW. This resulted in a long right-skewed tail in the OS histograms 16. Here, the worst case scenario
is an objective value of around 2,700 DKK. Considering, that the economic consequence of delivering less than
90% is merely to be excluded from the ancillary markets, the worst case scenario should be to return to a no
market scenario as in figure 13. Considering, that the worst case OS scenario for the no markets model is only
around 1,500 DKK, the penalty Cpen was set too high.
The risk operators in section 3.7 are only some of the ways to interpret the 90% rule for ancillary delivery.
Considering the risk for each individual hour, for each individual market (i.e. extending the CC operator to
consider the risk for each individual hour) would be a more accurate way to express the risk of non-delivery.
More flexibility would also be achieved, as this operator would have a higher degree of freedom, compared to
the operators in 4. However, this would result in |AM| × |T | × |S| binaries, which is significantly more than
HJCC in table 13. Choosing the right risk operator is a balance between having a reasonable computation time
and having precise representation of the risk you face. In case of the delivery risk results however, we also see,
that the results are very similar, because the confidence level (1 −γ) is so high, that there is little room for
uncertainty.
In section 2.2 it was described, how consumption technologies should have a baseline for their consumption, and
that the ancillary services delivered should correspond to this baseline. Where the ancillary reserve capacity
in equations (19.1)-(20b) is limited by the baseline, the ancillary service delivery as implemented in equations
21, 8a and 13 is not limited to the actual deviation from baseline, as illustrated in figure 3. Adding this to the
model would decrease the amount of ancillary delivery, particularly TCL, as any larger deviations from baseline
are penalized in equation (9).
Consumption technologies must provide a baseline to participate in ancillary markets (section 2.2). How the
baseline is defined plays a role in how much ancillary service can be delivered. The baseline could be defined
as the unintelligent consumption level, where changing energy prices are not considered. The baseline could
also be defined as the intelligent consumption levels, optimized with regard to spot prices and tariffs. As spot
and balance prices are related, using the intelligent consumption as a baseline will reduce the available ancillary
services in the community, compared to the unintelligent baseline.
In section 5.4, I introduced preliminary results for two capacity risk models. There were significant differences
in the bidding strategies, compared to the no risk model. Further exploring capacity risk results as done for the
delivery risk models in section 5.1 would be interesting future work as we observed different bidding strategies
in these models.
In the CVaR delivery risk model, the shadow price for the FFR non-delivery constraint was extremely high,
compared to any other shadow price. A possibility is, that the combination of a very activation probability and
very little amount of energy being delivered, when FFR is activated could cause some numerical issues in the
constraint. Looking into an explanation for this unusual shadow price could be something to look into in future
work. The FFR shadow price may be an indication, that FFR activation should be modelled differently than
by integrating over the frequency in the FFR active area (see figure 2 for an example).
The results indicate that ancillary market participation can be profitable. However, investments in the right
technology is needed to comply with the technical requirements for ancillary services. It may be worth looking
into the cost of installing the necessary technology and see if it outweighs the profit from ancillary markets.

Conclusion
Two types of economic risk were found, which should be taken into account when optimizing the ancillary
bidding strategy. One is the delivery risk of not being able to deliver the full amount of an activated ancillary
bid. The economic consequence for small units like an energy community is, that the reserve payment is returned
to the TSO. This risk was accounted for in the model, by adding a non-delivery variable, ϵam
c
ts as in the problem
(35). The other is the capacity risk, where the average of realized available capacity over a period of time is
less than 90% of the reserve bid. Here, the economic consequence is, that you may get excluded from market
participation and loose the revenue that comes with it. This risk is accounted for in the out of sample test
through equation (44) and (45).
In section 3.7, four different delivery risk operators and two capacity risk operators were introduced and im-
plemented in the energy community model. The methodology behind include chance constraint optimization,
where sample average approximation and CVaR approximation was used to make the constraint tractable. The
operators were used to allow for risk taking with regard to delivering the full activated ancillary service in
equations (35.3)-(35.7).
By introducing delivery risk, a small reduction in energy costs were achieved of 5-8 DKK/day for the entire
community, compared to not taking any risk.
The cost reduction varied slightly, depending on which risk
operator was used. Examination of the binding constraints indicate, that capacity is the main limitation to
gain more revenue in the ancillary markets.
The four delivery risk operators had very little influence on the bidding strategy, as it was identical to the no
risk strategy. However, the delivery risk operators did influence the operation of the energy community and
distribution of non-delivery. There were some differences to what extent the operators allowed for non-delivery.
CVaR operator proved to be the most conservative operator that allowed for the least non-delivery. HJCC was
the least conservative. When comparing the out of sample performance of the four risk operators, the CVaR
operator performed poorly with regard to the joint chance of constraint violation. This is even though CVaR
is the most conservative when considering the expected objective values.
The two capacity risk operators had significant influence on the bidding strategy. Depending on the interpreta-
tion of the capacity risk, different bidding strategies were achieved. In none of the risk models does the number
of binaries depend on the size of the community. This makes it possible to apply them to larger communities.
8
Future Work
As all delivery risk models perform well in the out of sample test for the SAA sensitivity level γ = 5% and all
had the same first stage solution, it might be worth exploring model sensitivity with regard to the value of γ.
Running the models with γ = α = 10% would be interesting to see, if we start seeing any differences in the
bidding strategy.
The available TCL capacity for ancillary services strongly depend on the outdoor temperature. Where temper-
ature scenarios were sampled from all seasons, it will likely have significant influence on the TCL capacity, if
temperature samples were narrowed down to a particular season. Future work could be to run the model with
e.g. winter days only, as temperature predictions are usually reliable.
The four delivery risk operators in table 4 actually allow for different types of heterogeneous risk preferences
with regard to the different ancillary markets and the different hours of the day. As the CC operator is defined
individually for each ancillary market, choosing different values of γ would allow for different risk preferences
in the individual markets. Similarly, the HJCC operator is defined individually for each hour, which allow
the community to adjust the risk preference during the day. The risk operators could also be defined for the
individual prosumer, allowing each individual to be more or less risk-averse. However, for the CC, JCC and
HJCC operators this would require a significant amount of binaries which may cause computational issues.
Adding heterogeneous risk preferences for the individual prosumer would also put a limit on the scale of the
community, as the amount of binaries in table 14 would depend on the amount of prosumers. As CVaR does
not have binaries, this operator would be better suited for the implementation of heterogeneous prosumer risk
preferences. Heterogeneous prosumer risk preferences in ancillary markets could be combined with risk trading,
37
==PAGE:43==
as done in [3].
In future work, it could also be interesting to investigate the introduction of risk-taking with regard to utility
such as indoor temperature or EV demand for driving. Instead of taking risk in the ancillary markets, community
members could instead choose to tolerate lower indoor temperatures or a limited driving range.
In the current community model, the individual prosumer is responsible for delivering any activation of their
ancillary bids. However, there could be several advantages to allowing prosumers to deliver ancillary services
for each other. Reserve trading would likely reduce the risk of non-delivery and improve cost reductions. An
ex-post re-distribution of reserve payment could be developed to investigate, how an economic advantage of
having a local ancillary service market might look. Another possibility is to model the community as a fully
collaborative model, where a community manager has full control over the management of the community.
In the current 2 stage model, ancillary activation is revealed for the whole 24h period at a time. In reality,
ancillary services are only activated with a very short notice. This means the model has the opportunity to
plan its operation hours ahead of an activation, which is not how things work in reality. The model has time
to ensure the needed capacity hours ahead in scenarios where there is activation, and knows which scenarios it
does not need to prepare for activation. The question is, how much benefit there would be from participating
in ancillary markets, if the activation is revealed the same hour of operation.
