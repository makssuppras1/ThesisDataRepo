Real-Time Rendering with Area Lights
Davide Sismeiro Giuffrida s233204
Abstract
Area lights have long been a challenging topic in real-time rendering. While video-game engines
and other computer graphics applications handle point lights efficiently, as they can be approxi-
mated as simple directional vectors, area lights cannot be simplified in the same way due to their
spatial extent. Building on prior research, this thesis presents a practical implementation of area
lights using an approximation derived from Linearly Transformed Cosine (LTC) functions. The
discussion focuses on the system’s underlying mechanics, the modifications and improvements in-
troduced during development, and key pitfalls to avoid when integrating this technique into a
commercial game engine.

Introduction
Physically based rendering of area light sources presents significant computational challenges in real-
time contexts, primarily due to the expensive numerical integration of the illumination equation. The
core difficulty lies in evaluating the integral of the product between the Bidirectional Reflectance Dis-
tribution Function (BRDF) and the incident radiance over the spherical domain defined by the light
source. While complex, polygonal area lights are particularly important as they model the spatial ex-
tent typical of real-world light sources. Unlike point or directional lights, area lights naturally capture
the soft shadows and gradual falloff observed in physical illumination, leading to more photorealistic
results. While modern ray- and path- tracing frameworks can handle these lights effectively in offline
rendering, real-time applications face fundamental limitations due to hardware constraints.
As we just said, the central computational bottleneck arises from the need to integrate the BRDF
over spherical polygons, a mathematically complex operation even for simple reflectance models. For
instance, Arvo’s analytic solution for Phong BRDF integration over polygonal domains [10], while
theoretically elegant, proves prohibitively expensive for real-time usage. These challenges are further
compounded by modern physically-based materials that exhibit complex anisotropic reflectance prop-
erties and skewness, requiring accurate representation of directionally varying specular lobes. This
tension between physical accuracy and computational efficiency motivates the development of special-
ized approximation techniques for real-time area light shading, particularly those that can handle both
isotropic and anisotropic material responses while maintaining interactive frame rates.
Extensive research has been conducted to approximate area light shading in real-time, with Linearly
Transformed Cosines (LTCs) by Heitz et al. [32] emerging as one of the most robust and adaptable
solutions. Simply said, these are a new kind of spherical distributions derived from applying a linear
transformation to the direction vectors of clamped cosine distribution.
This parametrization is not only easy to understand, but can also be applied to any spherical
distribution we desire. On top of this, the derived distribution inherits several properties from the
original one such as equivalence in normalization and integration, as well as importance sampling.
These properties make it possible to integrate LTCs accurately over spherical polygons, as the
clamped cosine distribution admits an analytic solution for this integral. Furthermore, since LTCs
can approximate a wide range BRDF characteristics by adjusting the transformation matrix, we can
effectively approximate our target BRDF using appropriately transformed LTCs.
Overall, LTCs offer an elegant combination of simplicity and robustness, enabling efficient shading
of different light shapes within a unified framework.
In this thesis, we present a practical imple-
mentation of LTC-based area light shading in Glacier, the commercial game engine developed by IO
Interactive (IOI). Our implementation entirely replaces the engine’s existing rectangular area lights,
which lacked both specular highlights and shadow casting capabilities; deemed as critical features for
production use. We document the complete implementation process for LTC area lights, analyze the
technique’s limitations, and showcase optimizations for performance-critical bottlenecks. Furthermore,
we provide comprehensive performance metrics and qualitative comparisons to facilitate thorough eval-
uation of our approach in real-world production scenarios.
The second part of this work addresses shadow rendering for polygonal lights. Despite the abun-
dance of shadowing techniques, we prioritize approaches that balance visual quality, computational
efficiency, and seamless integration into existing game engine workflows. While we haven’t found a
universally robust solution, we evaluate several candidate methods, discussing their implementation
challenges, trade-offs, and suitability for production environments. We also introduce a novel shadow
approximation which is able to reproduce plausible soft shadows and is comparable in terms of per-
formance to existing methods.
Finally, we will also provide performance metrics and figures to showcase and compare the different
implemented methods both in terms of lighting models and shadow techniques.

Related Work
2.1
Polygonal-Light Shading
In rendering contexts, unlike point or directional lights which are modeled as infinitesimal sources
emitting light from a single location or direction, area lights emit light across a finite surface, en-
abling the simulation of soft shadows, penumbra regions, and more physically plausible lighting. This
type of lighting plays a critical role in achieving photorealism, which is why correct techniques are
particularly sought after in the computer graphics field. Accurate simulation of area lights requires
integration over the projected area of the light’s surface onto a hemispherical domain, a process that is
computationally expensive and traditionally reserved for offline rendering applications such as movies,
pre-rendered cinematic sequences in games or architectural visualizations. Nonetheless, there is much
active research to analytically approximate area lights for real-time applications.
Monte Carlo Integration
When tackling this problem, we are not restricted to finding an analyt-
ical solution. Instead, we can leverage techniques typically reserved for offline rendering or high-end
hardware.
Distributed ray tracing [6] and path tracing [7] are among the most general numerical
methods for evaluating complex lighting integrals through stochastic sampling. Currently, we are in a
transitional phase where high-end gaming hardware can support these techniques, while mid-to-low-
end systems remain incapable of doing so. These frameworks offer significant advantages, as they
produce theoretically unbiased and physically accurate renderings. Additionally, area lights integrate
seamlessly into such systems, as they tend to rely on simple ray-triangle intersections, precisely what
is needed for area light evaluation. However, one of the most challenging aspects of implementing
these techniques is sample (ray) generation, which may initially seem straightforward but in reality it
is not. In the context of direct area light illumination, samples are typically generated using one of
three strategies:
• Solid-angle sampling based on the light’s spatial extent [11, 27, 38],
• BRDF importance sampling to account for view-dependent specular effects [7, 2], or
• Hybrid approaches combining both techniques to reduce variance.
All the aforementioned methods face practical challenges in real-time rendering.
They require an
impractically large number of samples (often hundreds per pixel) to achieve noise-free and unbiased
results. This computational demand makes pure Monte Carlo methods feasible only on high-end hard-
ware. Since the focus of this thesis was on solutions compatible with most gaming systems, rather
than a select few, we opted for alternative methodologies discussed in the following paragraphs.
Most Representative Point
Most Representative Point (MRP) techniques provide an efficient
alternative to costly sampling methods by approximating area light illumination with a single rep-
resentative point on the light surface. Early work by Picott [9] introduced this concept for Phong
area lighting, defining the MRP as the point on the light source closest to the reflection direction.
Wang et al. [24] then extended this method by further allowing textured area lights to work with this
technique. Others, instead, focused on finding a more efficient and robust representative point such
as Drobot [28] which considers a point in the area of intersection between the area light and a cone
with aperture parametrized by the surface roughness. Karis [26] further enhanced this technique by
accounting for energy normalization when shading sphere lights while still considering the MRP as
the point on the surface of the light closest to the reflection ray. The energy normalization accounts
for the widened specular distribution caused by the sphere’s subtended angle. Karis also adapted this
technique to work with tube lights by similarly identifying the nearest point on the cylindrical surface.
While these approaches produce convincing results and maintain compatibility with standard BRDF
models, leading to adoption in game engines like the Glacier engine, they exhibit several limitations.
The highlight approximation works reasonably well with Phong BRDFs but becomes less accurate for
microfacet BRDFs. Horizon handling presents another challenge, as the method assumes a sphere
light’s irradiance equals a point light’s when above the horizon, an approximation that fails at grazing

Irradiance Tensors and its application developed by
Arvo [10] managed to provide an exact analytic solution for the shading of glossy surfaces lit by
a polygonal light source. At the same time, this implementation does rely on expensive countour

for approximating and evaluating the edge countour integrals in O(1) instead of O(n). They also
managed to overcome the Phong restriction enabling support for microfacet BRDF with highlight
shape preservation. Lastly, they also proposed a novel technique to shade disk lights which involved
spinning quads. While employable in real-time and capable of approximating a variety of shapes this
technique is still limited to the rotationall symmetric shapes of Phong lobes. Furthermore, visible
artifacts are present both when they try to recover anisotropy and when shading disk lights using the

framework for polygonal light shading that remains robust under diverse rendering conditions while
exhibiting minimal artifacts. The key theoretical contribution was the recognition that linear trans-

Spherical Pivot Transformed Distributions
It’s important to mention another technique which
builds upon Linearly Transformed Cosines (LTCs). Shortly after the publication of the aforementioned
paper on LTCs, Dupuy et al. [37] introduced Spherical Pivot Transformed Distributions (SPTDs). This
a novel family of parametric spherical distributions designed for efficient rendering of spherical lights.
Similar to LTCs, SPTDs leverage the Jacobian of a geometric transformation, specifically, a restricted
set of 4D M¨obius transformations, to achieve closed-form solutions for spherical cap integrals. The key
innovation lies in their pivot transformation, which maps directions through an interior pivot point,
preserving spherical caps and enabling analytic integration and importance sampling. As stated by
the authors, this approach extends the rendering toolbox by supporting arbitrary frequencies while
maintaining exact spherical-cap properties, addressing limitations of prior methods like uniform distri-
butions or clamped-cosine lobes. Their work demonstrates applications in both real-time sphere-light
shading and variance-reduced Monte Carlo sampling. While proving to be an effective technique, for
the purpose of this thesis, we decided not to pursue such method as we wanted a unified solution and
spherical area lights could be obtained even through the LTC framework.

Shadows
Although it may not be immediately obvious, shadows play a crucial role in rendering by providing
essential depth cues that help viewers accurately perceive spatial relationships between objects. This
effect is clearly illustrated in Fig. 3.

While the spheres occupy the same positions in both images, the shadows they cast lead us to
perceive them as being placed quite differently with respect to the underlying plane. Even knowing
this, most of the aforementioned lighting techniques do not inherently account for shadows. For this
reason, we decided to explore the best possible solutions for making shadows for area lights work in a
commercial engine.
Unfortunately, as we will explore in later sections, achieving realistic area light shadows in real-time
rendering remains a significant challenge. The primary difficulty stems from the fact that area lights
possess spatial extent, which means that points in the scene can be partially occluded, resulting in
what we refer to as soft shadows. For obvious reasons, this could never happen when shading with
Point or Directional lights. Area light shadows are typically composed of two distinct regions: the
umbra, which encompasses points that are fully occluded by the light blocker, and the penumbra, which
includes points that are only partially shadowed by the light blocker. To approximate this behavior
numerous techniques have been developed, but most come short when compared to ray-traced refer-
ences. Even so, we opted to explore the various shadow methods previously introduced to understand
whether any of these could prove a viable solution. Among the most prominent shadowing methods is
the widely adopted Shadow Mapping method. Ubiquitous in most video-games and gaming engines,
this technique was originally introduced in 1978 [5] for offline rendering and later adapted for real-time
applications due to advancements in GPU technology.
In the following sections we will explore this technique and the various other ones that have been
developed separately or as an evolution of Shadow Mapping. As we will see, despite their utility, most
approximations we will examine usually fall short of accurately reproducing the complex light behavior
of true soft shadows cast by area lights. Nevertheless, several of these methods deliver visually ac-
ceptable results for the practical purposes of this thesis. In the following sections, we will analyze how
these techniques operate in practice starting with ray-traced shadows, and also provide a theoretical
overview of more advanced and physically accurate approaches.
Ray-traced Shadows
Unsurprisingly, the most physically accurate shadows are achieved through
stochastic sampling in ray- and path-traced frameworks. Unlike rasterization-based techniques such
as shadow mapping (discussed in subsequent sections), ray tracing computes shadows by explicitly
testing visibility between surface points and light sources via ray-scene intersections. For each shaded
point, one or more shadow rays are cast toward the light source; if a ray intersects geometry before
reaching the light, the point is considered shadowed. To simulate area light shadows, the light source
is typically approximated as a geometric primitive (such as a rectangle or a disk), and shadow rays are
jittered across its surface. A common implementation samples random points within a cone subtended
by the light’s angular extent at the shaded point as detailed in the following practical implementa-
tion [44]. This distribution naturally produces soft shadows with physically correct penumbrae, where
the occlusion level is determined by the ratio of blocked rays. However, this raw stochastic sampling
results in significant noise, requiring robust denoising pipelines for practical use.
8
==PAGE:10==
A key denoising technique is spatiotemporal accumulation [41], which reuses shadow data from pre-
vious frames to improve temporal stability. Instead of storing multiple historical frames (which would
be memory-prohibitive), an exponential moving average blends current and past results, preserving
high-frequency details while reducing noise. Further spatial denoising, such as edge-aware bilateral
filtering, can then be applied to smooth residual noise while maintaining shadow boundaries. This
combination yields the characteristic softly blurred shadows expected from area lights.
Despite its visual fidelity, this approach remains computationally expensive, particularly for real-
time applications on lower-end hardware. Consequently, while ray-traced shadows represent the gold
standard for quality, their performance constraints made them impractical for the scope of this thesis.
Hybrid Solution
An alternative to the fully ray-traced framework described above is the use of
hybrid solutions, which were introduced to shadow accurately when occluders are illuminated by area
lights. One of the most effective techniques in this domain is presented by Heitz et al. in their paper
Combining Analytic Direct Illumination and Stochastic Shadows [43]. We also had the opportunity
to consult Eric Heitz, the original author of the Linearly Transformed Cosines (LTC) area light tech-
nique used throughout this thesis. He confirmed that this hybrid method is currently considered the
state-of-the-art solution for area light shadows and is the one implemented in the Unity engine.
This approach combines analytic LTC-based illumination with stochastic shadowing. The key steps
in the method are as follows:
• Casting sparse shadow rays using multiple importance sampling,
• Accumulating results into a high-noise shadow texture,
• Applying aggressive filtering to denoise the result,
• Combining the denoised shadows with the analytic LTC illumination result.
The original paper provides mathematical derivations explaining the validity of this method and
includes comparative results to support its effectiveness. While the above summary is simplified, it
captures the core concept behind the technique.
Despite the high visual fidelity achieved by this approach, its implementation exceeded the scope
and timeline constraints of our project, both in terms of integration within Glacier and computational
overhead. Therefore, we decided to enhance Glacier’s existing shadow system by developing a more
practical solution for area light shadows. This enhancement was built on top of the engine’s founda-
tional shadowing methods, primarily based on the standard shadow mapping technique.
Shadow Mapping
Shadow Mapping was first introduced in 1978 by Lance Williams [5] and re-
mains, even today, a widely adopted technique in computer graphics and video games. Its popularity
stems from its simplicity, computational efficiency, and the numerous derivative techniques developed
to improve its visual fidelity (which we will discuss in later sections).
The algorithm itself is conceptually straightforward. First, we render the scene from the light’s
perspective, storing the depth values of the nearest occluders in a texture called the shadow map.
The projection matrix used depends on the light type: we employ either an orthographic projection
for directional lights or a perspective projection for point/spot lights. These depth values are then
normalized to the range [0, 1]. During the shading pass, each fragment’s depth is compared against the
corresponding value in the shadow map to determine visibility. Following the convention where zero
represents surfaces closest to the light and one represents those farthest away, a fragment is considered
in shadow if its normalized depth value exceeds the value stored in the shadow map. Of course, this
comparison can be swapped if we utilize the opposite convention and represents surfaces closest to the
light with a 1 instead of zero (as done in Glacier).
9
==PAGE:11==
The significant advantage of this technique lies in its image-based nature. Since shadow mapping
depends only on the rendered depth texture rather than scene geometry, it scales efficiently with
complex scenes. The primary performance constraint is shadow map resolution, as it may be unfeasible
to store so many high resolution shadow maps for a single scene (as we need at least one per light).
On top of this, shadow mapping presents some severe limitations which include:
• Production of only hard shadows due to the binary visibility test
• Aliasing artifacts (jagged edges) caused by limited shadow map resolution
• Perspective aliasing (particularly for directional lights)
• Depth precision issues
Various techniques, which we will now discuss, have been developed to address these limitations
while maintaining shadow mapping’s core strengths.
PCF
One of the first techniques introduced to address the aforementioned limitations of shadow map-
ping is Percentage-Closer Filtering (PCF) [8]. This technique integrates seamlessly with the shadow
mapping framework by applying a form of post-filtering. While one might initially consider applying
a simple pre-filter to the shadow map itself in order to blur it out and achieve softer shadow edges,
this approach has been shown to corrupt depth comparisons and introduce artifacts. Instead, PCF
applies filtering to the binary visibility results. In practice, when evaluating a specific pixel, PCF also
evaluates neighboring pixels by performing multiple shadow map lookups and depth comparisons. The
results are accumulated into a running sum, which is then averaged by the total number of samples
checked. This technique gained widespread adoption after being hardware-accelerated by NVIDIA [13].
The choice of filtering method is crucial. Various filtering approaches are discussed in the litera-
ture [21]. The simplest option is a standard box filter with variable size. However, due to its uniform
nature, this filter tends to produce banding artifacts and visible regularity in shadows. A Gaussian blur
with variable kernel size offers improvement, though it still exhibits similar issues to a lesser degree.
Through experimentation, we found that random sampling based on a probability distribution yielded
the best results. Specifically, we implemented Poisson disk filtering by precomputing an array of 64
points (easily extensible if needed) within a unit circle, distributed according to Poisson disk sampling.
During sampling, we index this array and use the indexed sample value as directions combined with a
specified radius to determine shadow map lookup locations. Further improvement can be achieved by
applying small random jittering, for example through a GPU-computed pseudo-random angle.
These methods offer several advantages. They effectively blur shadow edges while minimizing arti-
facts and, as a beneficial side effect, they naturally produce soft shadows; addressing a key limitation
of standard shadow mapping. Overall, this approach not only reduces aliasing but also approximates
realistic penumbrae by smoothing shadow boundaries.
Despite its benefits, PCF becomes computationally expensive for large filter kernels, as each sample
requires a separate texture lookup which are very expensive operations to perform on the GPU. Large
kernels are necessary to achieve softer shadows, and while Poisson disk filtering can achieve similar
softness with fewer samples than uniform filters, the computational costs can still become prohibitive.
PCSS
As we have just explained, when we apply PCF the softness of the shadows depends on the
kernel size we choose for filtering.
More specifically, larger kernels produce softer shadows.
This
means, that if we had some information about how far the occluder was from the shaded point, we
could potentially enable an approximation of contact-hardening effects, where shadows appear sharper
near occluders and gradually soften with distance. This concept was first formalized by Randima
Fernando in the NVIDIA whitepaper Percentage-Closer Soft Shadows (PCSS) [14, 15]. The PCSS
algorithm operates in three stages:
10
==PAGE:12==
1. Blocker search: The shadow map is sampled to identify and average the depths of nearby
occluders.
2. Kernel scaling: The filter size is dynamically adjusted based on the blocker distance, light
distance, and light source size (derived via triangle similarity under the assumption of parallel
occluders and receivers).
3. Filtered visibility: PCF is applied with the computed kernel to produce soft shadows.
If correctly applied, this method works great in simple scenes and there are examples of the PCSS
technique being used for commercial games like Hellgate: London [22].
The main limitation of this technique comes from its assumption and setup. First, as we already
mentioned, this approach relies on the parallel-plane assumption to simplify depth calculations. This
means that if occluders are not set up parallel to each other some artifacts may become visible. From
our testing though, these type of artifacts seem to be negligible. The biggest issue comes from how
the shadow maps themselves are created and then utilized. As we previously explained, shadow maps
only store the depths to the closest surfaces. This means that if there is an occluder set very close to
the light, another occluder set very close to the shadowed point and their penumbras intersect, we will
have some very noticeable artifacts where shadows become lighter than what they are supposed to be.
On top of this, because of the elevated number of texture samples performed, this technique is very
performance heavy. Figure 4 demonstrates this problem in our own application. For this reason we
had to think of some other approximations which can simulate this contact hardened shadows without
incurring in these type of artifacts. By the end, we did manage to find a plausible approximation that
took inspiration from PCSS, but did not present any visible artifact typical of this technique as well
as being much more computationally efficient. We will fully explain this method in later sections.

Pre-filtering Techniques
We now focus on another branch of technique that while not directly
implemented in this thesis, are important to mention as they refine and build upon the concept of
shadow maps. As previously mentioned conventional shadow maps resist direct pre-filtering because
naive blurring corrupts depth comparisons. This does not mean it is impossible to perform though.
Advanced techniques have been proposed which successfully enable this pre-filtering capability while
producing plausible soft shadows. While not extensively investigated in this thesis, some of the most
noteworthy are Variance Shadow Maps (VSM), Convolution Shadow Maps (CSM), and Exponential
Shadow Maps (ESM). Each approaches the pre-filtering step through distinct mathematical represen-
tations. While these techniques look promising, the reason we have not actively pursued them in this
thesis was because changing the way shadow maps were being generated in Glacier was not straightfor-
ward and we also wanted to understand how far we could push techniques based on a standard linear
depth shadow map.
VSM
First proposed by Donnelly and Lauritzen [17], VSM transforms shadow mapping into a sta-
tistical visibility problem. The technique stores two values per texel in a two-channel texture: the
depth mean (z) in the first channel and the squared depth (z2) or the sum of the mean and variance

mipmapping or Gaussian blurring, as the statistical moments remain valid under interpolation. On
top of this, by using a bilinear texture look up we can directly fetch E(z) and E(z2). We could of
course also use a trilinear or even more filtering after the texture lookup to further reduce aliasing.
This technique still presents some limits. Firstly, we need to render the entire scene into the VSM
shadow map.

receiver plane for example we won’t end up with the shadow approximation.

of most pre-filtering techniques including CSM and ESM. Finally, the main limitation of VSMs, is
the fact that this statistical approximation introduces known light bleeding artifacts which become
particularly noticeable in regions with complex occlusion where multiple depth discontinuities cause
variance overestimation. While there are multiple approaches that minimize this issue such a threshold
to remap shadow intensity or even a novel technique called Layered VSM.
CSM
Introduced by Annen et al. [18], Convolution Shadow Maps (CSM) reformulate shadow map-
ping as a convolution between a prefilterable shadow function and a reconstruction kernel. To un-
derstand this method, let us first consider the classical shadow mapping technique. Shadow mapping
essentially encodes a function z(p), representing the depth of the closest occluder to the light source
at each shadow map pixel p. The standard shadow test can then be written as the following binary
shadowing function s:
s(x) := f(d(x), z(p))
(4)
where f(d, z) returns 0 if d > z and 1 otherwise, and d(x) is the depth of the fragment at world-
space position x.
The authors demonstrate why convolving this function directly is not feasible, even under simpli-
fying assumptions, primarily because z(p) is not directly linear and thus cannot be prefiltered using
standard convolution techniques (as explained in previous sections). To address this, they propose an
approximation that transforms the depth values so that the shadow test becomes a linear combination.
Specifically, the binary function f(d, z) is approximated by a sum of basis functions:

allows the shadow function to be expressed in a form suitable for convolution, since the convolution of
the shadow function becomes the sum of convolutions of the basis images.
The authors opt for a Fourier expansion of the shadowing function f. Consequently, instead of
storing the linear depth values, the shadow map stores sin(ckz) and cos(ckz), where ck = π(2k −1)
and k is the index of the basis function. This leads to multiple CSM textures per light source. For
instance, truncating the Fourier series to 16 terms requires storing 16 sine and 16 cosine components,
resulting in substantial memory usage.
To reduce the memory footprint, these components are efficiently packed into 8-bit RGBA tex-
tures. This packing introduces negligible quality loss compared to storing each coefficient in 32-bit
floating point format. Once the Fourier coefficients are stored in textures, they can be prefiltered using

While CSM suffers much less the light bleeding artifacts common to statistical methods like Vari-
ance Shadow Maps (VSM) and produces more accurate penumbrae, it introduces significant memory
bandwidth requirements and computational overhead for projection and reconstruction of basis func-
tions. Furthermore, despite optimizations, dynamic scenes with complex shadow interactions (e.g.,
overlapping occluders) can still suffer from some light bleeding. Due to its implementation complexity
and performance costs, CSM is rarely used in practical real-time applications.
ESM
Exponential Shadow Maps (ESM) were introduced independently by Annen et al. [20] and
Salvi [23], drawing conceptual inspiration from Convolution Shadow Maps in their use of an approx-
imated shadow test. However, rather than relying on basis function expansions, ESM employs an
exponential function to approximate visibility.
From the definition of the shadow map, we know that for each world-space point x, its distance
d(x) to the light source must always be greater than or equal to the corresponding depth value z(p)
stored in the shadow map (where p is the projection of x in shadow map space). This is because the
shadow map stores the minimal depth (closest surface to the light), thus the inequality d(x)−z(p) ≥0
should hold. In practice, however, this relationship may fail due to floating-point precision limitations.
Assuming the inequality holds, we can define the shadow test f(d, z) as:

that c = 80 works well. This value represents a compromise - while larger values would provide better
theoretical results, they are constrained by the precision limits of floating-point representation. The
authors demonstrate how this approximated shadow test can be properly convolved.
In practice, the method uses a standard 32-bit floating-point depth texture, but stores exp(cz)
instead of the conventional depth z. Similar to Convolution Shadow Maps (CSM) or Variance Shadow
Maps (VSM), this representation allows for pre-filtering of the shadow texture. The shadow test then
simplifies to evaluating Equation 7.
According to the authors’ evaluation, ESM achieves an effective balance between quality and per-
formance, exhibiting fewer light-leaking artifacts than VSM and more consistent penumbra rendering
compared to CSM. Additionally, ESM has lower memory and bandwidth requirements, as it only re-
quires a single-channel texture compared to CSM’s multi-texture storage.
Nevertheless, artifacts can still occur if the parameters are not properly tuned. While all three
methods (VSM, CSM, and ESM) offer distinct advantages, their known limitations and artifacts led
us to exclude them from implementation in this thesis. Although various mitigations have been pro-
posed, even ESM, which generally performs best among the three, is not entirely free from issues.
Consequently, we focused on alternative approaches that better aligned with the goals and constraints
of this work.

3.1
The Rendering Equation and BRDF
When rendering a physically accurate three-dimensional scene, whether for video games, simulations,
or visual effects, each pixel, which corresponds to a point on a surface in the scene, must take into
account the contribution of all light sources. The mathematical framework that governs the transport
of light in such a setting is known as the Rendering Equation, first formally introduced by Kajiya
in 1986 [7]. This equation describes the outgoing radiance at a surface point as the sum of emitted
and reflected radiance.
In our case, we simplify the evaluation of the rendering equation by excluding the emitted radiance
term, since we are currently only interested in the reflected light. The reflected component is computed
using the Bidirectional Reflectance Distribution Function (BRDF), which characterizes how
incoming light from a direction ωi is scattered toward an outgoing direction ωo, typically the direction
of the camera.
Mathematically, the outgoing radiance Lo, omitting the emission term, is expressed as:

In real-time rendering, however, ray-tracing frameworks encounter hardware limit and are only able
to run on higher-end graphic cards. This is why a different approach is needed to address this problem.
One of the most versatile and robust solutions researched is the technique presented by Eric Heitz
et al. [32] which involves the use of a new family of distribution functions called Linearly Trans-
formed Cosines (LTC) which efficiently approximate how different shapes of area lights shade a
specific surface point.

3.5
Trowbridge-Reitz (GGX) Microfacet Model
Having understood the need to approximate the rendering equations with an approximated BRDF, we
must now choose a specific physical model for computing the outgoing radiance Lo. This model will
be approximated in our pre-computation step outlined in the following section. Among the available
options, we utilize the Trowbridge-Reitz (GGX) microfacet model due to its physical accuracy and
widespread adoption in modern rendering applications and academic papers.
This model was originally introduced by Trowbridge and Reitz [3], and was later rediscovered
and popularized by Walter et al. [19].
Today, the GGX distribution is widely regarded as one of
the most physically accurate parametric BRDF models for simulating specular highlights. It exhibits
several desirable properties, including physically plausible energy conservation, a realistic heavy-tailed
reflectance distribution, and a strong correspondence with measured material behavior. For the sake
of conciseness, we will refer to this distribution simply as GGX throughout the remainder of this thesis.
In general, microfacet models provide a framework for constructing physically-based BRDFs by
modeling surfaces as collections of microscopic facets, each behaving as an ideal mirror reflector. The
combined surface reflection emerges from statistically averaging these microfacets, taking into account
three key factors:
• Distributions of orientations: The statistical orientation of facets, described by a Normal
Distribution Function (NDF)
• Geometric occlusion: Mutual shadowing and masking between facets
• Fresnel effects: How reflectivity changes with viewing angle.
These components combine in the microfacet BRDF formulation:

Polygon Clipping Approximation
The final precomputed value accounts for horizon clipping of
polygonal lights. Since the physically correct result requires integrating only over the upper hemisphere
Ω+, we must ensure that the projected polygon is clipped to the horizon to exclude contributions from
below the surface (which would introduce non-physical energy loss and artifacts).
This clipping step was not mentioned in the original work by Heitz et al. [32], but was first in-
troduced in the SIGGRAPH presentation slides [29], and later refined when disk and ellipsoid lights
were incorporated into the framework [31]. The authors’ initial solution involved evaluating a large
set of branching conditions, manually handling all possible configurations of edges with approximately
16 distinct if-else cases. While this approach was exact and, at the time, the fastest solution they
identified, the excessive branching significantly impacted performance.
As a result, a more practical solution was introduced: approximating the polygonal light by replac-
ing it with a proxy sphere of equivalent form factor. This approach offers two main advantages: the
form factor of a polygon can be easily computed (making the substitution easy), and the clipped form
factor of a sphere admits a closed-form analytical solution [12].
20
==PAGE:22==
To apply this approximation, the authors computed the angular extent and elevation angle of the
polygon’s form factor to determine the corresponding proxy sphere parameters. During the fitting
stage, an additional precomputed parameter was introduced: for each combination of these two val-
ues, the appropriate multiplier derived from Snyder’s analytical formulas was stored in a lookup table
(LUT). At runtime this needed to be indexed and simply applied to the form-factor to end up with
the correctly clipped form-factor.
Later, with the introduction of disk and ellipsoid lights, the authors slightly modified the index-
ing of the LUT for this clipping term. Instead of using the solid angle directly, they adopted the
cosine-projected solid angle. This adjustment produced improved accuracy for ellipsoidal shapes, as
this formula admitted closed-form solutions even when the ellipses were oriented parallel to the differ-

3.8
Shadows
In the second part of the thesis, we have dedicated much effort and attention in implementing shadow
casting for our newly developed area lights. This was identified as a critical requirement following
discussions with IOI’s art department, who confirmed that area lights without shadow support would
be impractical for production use. The primary challenge lies in the computational expense of accurate
shadow rendering, which typically requires ray-tracing or hybrid approaches as discussed in Section 2.2.
These expensive techniques were soon deemed unfeasible to try and implement due to the time
constraints and scope of this thesis. Therefore, our focus shifted toward enhancing Glacier’s exist-
ing shadowing techniques and exploring alternative methods that could more effectively approximate
shadows cast by area lights.
In the following sections, we start by examining what properties we wish our shadow method to
have. We then understand what has been already implemented in Glacier and describe how we it-
eratively improved upon this system. Throughout the development process, we engaged in ongoing
dialogue with stakeholders from IOI’s art department to assess existing workflows, understand the
limitations of current shadow techniques, and gather feedback on our solutions. Ensuring that our
approach offered practical benefits was a top priority, and this continuous feedback loop was instru-
mental in guiding our work. A summary of the final feedback discussion can be found in Appendix B
(8).
Finally, we introduce the novel shadow casting approximation we developed which offers greater
visual fidelity for a similar computational cost when compared to the solution previously implemented
in Glacier. We will explain how we arrived at this solution and how it works. In later sections we will
also provide comparative metrics and figures to showcase the difference between the shadow techniques
and how our novel approximation holds up in test scenes against the pre-existing methods.
3.8.1
Desirable Properties of Area Light Shadows
Unlike point or directional sources, area lights generate soft shadows with gradual transitions from
umbra (fully shadowed) to penumbra (partially shadowed) regions. Any convincing approximation
must balance four key qualities:
• Soft edges: Shadows should softly fade out along the borders.
• Contact hardening: Shadows should appear sharper near occluders and blur with increasing
distance.
• Visual stability: Avoidance of visual artifacts such as jagged edges, shadow acne or light leaks.
• Efficiency: The method should be lightweight enough for use in real-time applications.
When assessing previous shadow techniques and their potential improvements these are the visual
aspects and metrics we need to consider to offer valid comparisons across methods.
3.8.2
Glacier shadow techniques
Glacier already supported shadow casting for most light types: Spot, Directional, and Omni (Point)
lights. These implementations utilized shadow maps suited to each light type’s emission characteris-
tics. However, the rectangular area lights previously implemented (unrelated to the LTC methodology
adopted in this thesis) lacked any shadow support and were therefore unused in production due to the
unrealistic lighting they produced.
Among the existing systems, Omni lights proved to be the most promising reference for area light
shadowing. Omni lights emit light in all directions and utilize a cubemap consisting of six shadow maps
to capture depth from all angles. During shading, the appropriate shadow map is selected based on the
31
==PAGE:33==
fragment’s position relative to the light, and depth sampling is performed accordingly. Considerable
care must be taken to avoid artifacts at cube face boundaries and ensure seamless transitions between
shadow maps. This solution, while more realistic, is very computationally expensive, which is why the
artist has the option to cast shadows in a single direction to make the lights much cheaper to render.
This means that the shadows won’t be cast in all directions and instead of six low-resolution shadow
maps, a single high-resolution shadow map will be utilized effectively rendering spot light shadows.
While this may not seem optimal, in a videogame production setting, having this option was critical,
as we discovered during one of the several conversations we had with the art department.
This means that, although the shadow mapping algorithm for Omni lights could present an extra
layer of complexity when casting shadows in all directions, the method boils down to the advanced
shadow mapping techniques utilized for the other light types such as Spot lights. Thus, we can con-
sider the simplest case of a single shadow map technique and use it as a foundation for developing new
approximations targeting the soft shadow behavior typical of area lights.
At the end, we decided to focus primarily on this single directional shadow casting technique as the
current implementation of the cubic-shadow-map available in Glacier presented several issues at the
time of development. Because of this and performance reasons artists were entirely avoiding utilizing
them in practice as well, which is why we focused on making this system as lightweight as possible
while sacrificing realistic and accurate shadowing.
3.8.3
Dilated Percentage Closer Filtering
The shadow map technique implemented in the Glacier engine and applied on all light types’ shadows
is a method used in the game Call of Duty: Black Ops Cold War, presented by Treyarch at GDC [45].
This method builds upon Percentage Closer Filtering (PCF) by introducing a variation that approxi-
mates the effects of Percentage Closer Soft Shadows (PCSS) 2.2. Known as Dilated Percentage Closer
Filtering (DPCF), the technique simulates plausible soft shadows by blending between a standard PCF
result and a curved PCF result. The transition depends on the proximity of the occluder from the
shadowed point. This technique correctly approximates contact-hardening soft shadows with minimal
visual artifacts. On top of this, we can estimate the penumbra radius required for the final PCF step
on the CPU rather than per pixel, avoiding additional computational overhead and texture sampling
costs typically associated with PCSS.
Although this method provided sufficiently realistic results for production use (as it was the default
shadow method in Glacier), we aimed to further improve shadow quality, especially under area light
illumination. We wanted certain characteristics such as contact hardening to be even more pronounced
than what they currently where. In order to achieve this, we initially chose to experiment with a full
implementation of PCSS. While it is generally more computationally expensive, our goal was to eval-
uate whether the visual benefits would justify the increased performance cost.
3.8.4
PCSS evaluation and limitations
Since the underlying principles of PCSS have already been discussed in Section 2.2, this section will
focus on the limitations that ultimately led us to discard it as a viable option for our advanced shadow
mapping solution. While PCSS initially appeared promising in addressing the soft shadow charac-
teristics expected from area lights, such as soft penumbras and contact hardening, our preliminary
evaluations revealed several critical drawbacks that rendered it unsuitable for practical use.
Before explaining these limitations, we would like to make a brief practical note: after estimating
the penumbra radius during the blocker search, we applied a Poisson disk PCF step. This choice
was made to trade banding artifacts for a controlled amount of random noise, which results in more
visually appealing shadows due to the reduced regularity. This approach was selected after empirical
experimentation with various PCF algorithms, where we found the Poisson disk PCF to yield the most
32
==PAGE:34==
satisfactory results.
The first limitation, common to PCSS as well as DPCF (and even our own approximation), is the
necessity of applying filtering operations across all points in the evaluated region, not just those in
shadow. Ideally, we would like to avoid unnecessary computations when a surface point is fully lit. In
the PCSS pipeline, the blocker search phase already reveals whether a point is occluded or not. Logi-
cally, this would allow for an early return and avoid the costly PCF filtering stage by simply returning
a value of 1.0 (i.e. no shadow). However, doing so introduces a major visual drawback: shadow edges
become unnaturally sharp, eliminating the soft falloff typically seen in penumbral regions. Despite
multiple empirical attempts to enhance the blocker search to account for this issue, we were unable to
fully resolve it. As a compromise, the PCF step must be applied uniformly across all fragments, lit or
shadowed, which, while restoring proper edge blending, significantly increases the presence of shadow
acne. This is largely due to the resolution mismatch between the shadow map and the rendered image,
a well-known problem that remains difficult to fully eliminate.
While not optimal, the previous limitation is common to most soft shadow techniques, meaning
that it was not the main reason why we opted out of using PCSS as a potential improvement of the
current shadow technique. The most severe limitation of PCSS is illustrated in Figure 4. This artifact
is intrinsic to the PCSS algorithm and arises when penumbra regions from multiple occluders intersect.
If the occluders are spatially distant, the blocker search fails to account for all relevant depth discon-
tinuities, since the shadow map records only the nearest depth values. Consequently, the evaluated
average blocker distance will lead to artifacts. In practice, when this occurs, the penumbra radius,
used in the subsequent PCF step, is misestimated, resulting in partially shadowed regions appearing
more lit than what they are supposed to be. This leads to highly visible discontinuities and incorrect
lighting, even in relatively simple test scenes, severely limiting PCSS’s robustness in real production
scenarios.
Given these issues, combined with the method’s inherently higher computational cost, we concluded
that PCSS was not a viable candidate for our shadowing technique. These limitations motivated us to
explore alternative approaches. Our efforts ultimately led to the development of a lightweight approx-
imation that avoids the multiple-occluder artifact and still produces visually plausible soft shadows
with no performance difference compared to the original Glacier DPCF method.
3.8.5
Fake-PCSS approximation
During our empirical research in addressing the limitations of PCSS, we discovered a very simple ap-
proximation that provided visual improvements over the default DPCF method, without suffering from
the most problematic drawbacks of PCSS. We named this method Fake-PCSS, and its functioning is
quite straightforward in practice.
In standard PCSS implementations, the primary value we are interested in is the radius which we
then use in the subsequent PCF step. In classic PCSS this is estimated as:
radius = AvgBlockerDistance −Depth
Depth
· LightRadius
(72)
Here, Depth refers to the distance from the light source to the shadowed point, AvgBlockerDistance
is computed by averaging the depths of blockers found during the blocker search, and LightRadius de-
notes the size of the light source (larger values result in softer shadows). The multiple-occluder artifact
we previously discussed originates from the computation of AvgBlockerDistance, which often leads
to sudden mismatches in the estimated radius. Motivated by this, we explored potential alternatives.
By coincidence, we found that setting AvgBlockerDistance = 1 yielded unexpectedly good results.
Soft shadows were still produced at high quality, but without the need to perform the blocker search
step. This also effectively eliminated the multiple-occluder artifact. When comparing the resulting
shadows to those produced by the default DPCF method used in Glacier, we observed significant visual
improvements without introducing new artifacts. This motivated a deeper investigation into simpler
33
==PAGE:35==
yet plausible approximations for soft shadow rendering.
Our final approximation computes the shadow radius as the average of two values. The first is a
depth-based radius obtained by linearly interpolating between a minimum and maximum penumbra
radius, using the distance from the light as the interpolation factor. This ensures that points closer to
the light receive a smaller radius.
The second value is derived from the modified PCSS equation with AvgBlockerDistance = 1,
clamped between the same minimum and maximum penumbra radii. These two values are then aver-
aged, and the resulting radius is used in a standard Poisson disk PCF step.
The two primary limitations of this technique are: it offers less artistic control and is harder to
fine-tune, and it suffers from reduced contact hardening when shadowed points are far from the light
source. However, since the second limitation is also present in the DPCF method, Fake-PCSS re-
mains a compelling alternative. It delivers noticeable visual improvements, especially in rendering fine
shadow details, while avoiding the computational overhead and artifacts of traditional PCSS.
In following sections, we will present comparative metrics, both in terms of performance and visual
fidelity.

4.1
LTC Area Light Implementation
As discussed in earlier sections, the authors themselves iterated extensively on their implementation
to improve consistency across different light types and to enhance performance. In this section, we
delve deeper into some of these author-provided improvements and explain how they work. On top
of this, we further explain our own improvements to this process after identifying critical aspects that
required to be fixed.
Fitting Implementation
In the pre-computation step, where we fit the LTC distribution to the
GGX microfacet distribution (as detailed in Section 3.6), our initial goal was to fully reimplement
the fitting procedure within Glacier. This approach would allow us to easily trace and debug any
discrepancies that might arise in later stages by verifying whether they originated from this step.
After implementing the full fitting routine in Glacier, we exported the resulting textures to ex-
ternal files, enabling direct comparison with the reference textures provided by the original authors
in their GitHub repository [36]. While we successfully reproduced the second LUT containing the
Fresnel term, geometric attenuation, and clipped proxy sphere form factor multiplier, we encountered
difficulties reproducing the first LUT, which stores the elements of the inverse linear transformation
matrix M −1.
35
==PAGE:37==
These minor discrepancies were traced back to Glacier’s default use of C++ fast math mode. This
mode accelerates computation by sacrificing numerical precision, which proved problematic for com-
puting accurate inverse matrices. Since disabling fast math in Glacier is non-trivial and could have
widespread effects on the engine’s pipeline, we opted to retain our fitting code for future use but instead
imported the original lookup tables provided by the authors. This proved simpler and more robust.
The data was stored in a float4 array inside a header file, which could be directly accessed by the
engine to ensure proper GPU texture binding.
Due to time constraints, we were unable to thoroughly investigate alternative solutions, such as
relying exclusively on double precision arithmetic, which is not affected by fast math. However, given
that importing the table itself worked reliably and matched the expected output, we were satisfied
with the adopted solution.
Specular and Diffuse components
Focusing on how the LTC light types are generally set up we
can start by emphasizing the need to differentiate when we are computing the diffuse and the specular
component. In the previous sections we have always referred to how the LTC technique approximates
the BRDF GGX by pre-computing a LUT where the inverse linear transformation matrix is stored.
What we have not mentioned until now is that this inverse linear transformation is required only when
computing the specular component and not the diffuse one. This is because only the specular term
depends on the view direction and material roughness while the diffuse is independent of these values.
Given this difference, an easy solution to avoid having duplicate code and unnecessary if-else
branches is to utilize as M −1 a simple identity matrix so that no transformation is being applied when
computing the diffuse term. We also tried adding a simple boolean flag to avoid performing unnec-
essary matrix operations when integrating the diffuse component, but from preliminary tests, we did
not witness any significant improvements in terms of performance. For this reason we kept the same
function when evaluating both components for the sake of code simplicity.
In practice this means that we call the same function with a different M −1 parameter twice, once
for specular and once for diffuse. We compute these values separately as most rendering pipelines
(like Glacier’s) tend to utilize them this way. While we don’t apply any extra formulas to the diffuse
component per se, we still need to account for the Fresnel and Geometric attenuation terms we pre-
computed in the fitting step 3.6 to properly distort and attenuate the specular highlight. The final
specular component is then obtained by being multiplied with the Fresnel/Geometric approximation
equation:
spec = spec(R0 nD + (1 −R0) fD)
(73)
where nD and fD are indexed from the LUT, while R0 was a value readily available in Glacier.

Ellipsoid light implementation
During the testing phase of our ellipsoidal light implementation, we observed visual artifacts when
objects intersected the ellipsoid volume. These artifacts manifested as extremely bright spots, caused

inside the ellipsoid. Specifically, we examined the transformed center of the ellipsoid in the unit sphere

and terminated the shading function early, returning empirically chosen fallback values:

same technique used for the disk light case, which involved computing the eigen-decomposition of √Q

To resolve this, we adopted a revised version of the same eigen-decomposition method. We be-
gin by checking whether the matrix is diagonal, which we determine by evaluating the orthogonality
of the input vectors. If the matrix is diagonal, we simply return the reciprocals of its diagonal ele-
ments as eigenvalues. Otherwise, we compute the eigenvalues and eigenvectors using a more stable
formulation, closely related to the one suggested by the original authors. This involved fine tuning spe-
cific calculations, resulting in a more robust implementation (at least based on our preliminary testing).
One value we had to carefully fine tune was the threshold for determining whether the input vectors
are considered orthogonal. Through empirical testing, we set this threshold to 0.001, as it was the
only value that consistently prevented errors for both disk lights and ellipsoid lights. This robustness
allowed us to apply the improved method to disk lights as well, thereby simplifying the code base by
avoiding redundant functions. The complete implementation is provided in Appendix A (7).
4.1.4
Line Light implementation
While implementing and testing the performance of Line lights, we encountered a significant pitfall
that required careful attention. Using SCUI, we observed a substantial spike in VGPR usage whenever
the Line light was compiled alongside the other area lights. Given that the lighting shader was al-
ready a critical performance bottleneck, this increase in register usage clearly necessitated optimization.
Our investigation revealed that the spike was caused by a matrix inversion operation performed to
compute the width factor necessary for evaluating the line’s irradiance, as described in Equation 68.
Although the inverse matrix M −1 was already stored and accessed in earlier parts of the code via the
LUT, this specific operation required a second matrix inversion, leading to additional overhead.
As a potential solution, we explored the idea of storing the original matrix M in a third LUT,
in addition to its inverse. After configuring the pipeline accordingly, we found that the VGPR spike
persisted. This suggested that the issue did not stem from the matrix inversion computation itself,
but rather from the temporary allocation of a new 3 × 3 matrix used during the operation.
To resolve this, we restructured the computation to avoid allocating the additional matrix al-
together.
After several iterations, we derived an alternative formulation that preserved the visual
fidelity of the original method while eliminating the VGPR spike.

Shadows (Fake-PCSS)
Shadow cone attenuation
In order to make our shadow solution very lightweight and usable in
productions settings we have chosen to have it cast shadows in a single direction. While sacrificing
visual accuracy, this allowed us to leverage a single shadow map. This was already implemented for
Omni lights inside the Glacier engine where you could choose whether you wanted shadows being cast
in all directions, using the six shadow maps arranged in a cube map, or using a single shadow map.
We are essentially creating a cone inside which shadows are cast. The extents of the cone also define
the extents of the shadow map. Everything outside this cone won’t be shadowed, which introduces
a problem we need to carefully address. If a shadow intersects this cone there will be a sharp cutoff
which is not desirable. In order to implement proper blending an angular attenuation is applied on top
of the shadow attenuation depending how close to the edges of the shadow cone the shadowed point
is. We initially used the angular attenuation formulas used also for the omni lights. During testing
though, we realized a problem, which only after further examination we understood was connected to
this attenuation formula.
Essentially, if the camera view was placed in such a way that the specular highlight of the area light
would overlap the shadow we would see light leaking even when it shouldn’t. This was very confusing
initially as it seemed that the values of the shadows were never reaching zero for some reason. Thus,
the specular highlighted was never being fully attenuated. Only after some time did we realize that
the aforementioned angular attenuation was causing this issue.
In order to fix it, we completely changed the angular attenuation function. The way it worked for
the omni light, was to utilize the shadow cone’s extents and compute a quasi-smoothstep function that
nicely blended between the center of the shadow cone to the edges uniformly. We realized this was not
ideal behavior. Ideally we only wanted this value to affect the shadow attenuation value when close to
the borders. Which is exactly what we did.
The new formula now takes a threshold value, checks whether the current angular attenuation value
is above or below this threshold. If above, it simply returns 1. If below it means the shadowed point is
now close to the edges of the cone and we need to uniformly blend it out using a linear interpolation
function readily available in HLSL.
After implementing this, all the shadows were now darker, which was what we initially wanted,
and also correctly accounted for the specular highlight not causing light leaks anymore.
Dynamic vs Static Shadow Radii
When implementing shadowing for our area lights, an im-
portant consideration to keep in mind is the distinction between dynamic and static shadow maps.
The rendering system transitions seamlessly between these two types based on the distance from the
camera to the shaded point. This functionality was already in place for Omni lights, and we extended
the same logic to support area lights.
The main difference between the two shadow map types lies in resolution and update frequency. Dy-
namic shadow maps are rendered at a high resolution (typically 1024×1024) and updated every frame,
allowing for accurate shadows from both static and moving objects. In contrast, static shadow maps
are precomputed once at application startup with significantly lower resolution (typically 128 × 128),
and therefore only capture shadows from static geometry.
While this system provides a good balance between performance and quality, it introduces a crit-
ical issue when using our Fake-PCSS shadowing technique. Specifically, the penumbra radius in the
Fake-PCSS function is resolution-dependent. If the same radius used for dynamic shadow maps is
applied to the static maps, the result is an overly large sampling radius, leading to artifacts such as
overly soft or faded shadows. Additionally, performance can degrade due to ineffective texture cache
usage as samples become too scattered to benefit from spatial locality.
40
==PAGE:42==
To mitigate this, we categorize shadow radii into two groups: dynamic and static. These values can
be artist-defined from the editor, offering flexibility and artistic control. Empirically, we have found
that dividing the dynamic radius by a factor of 5 yields a visually satisfactory result for static shadows,
though this value can be tuned as necessary for specific scenes or assets.

• Light extent
• Two-sided flag (for Rect and Disk lights)
• Use end-caps flag (for Line lights)
While the area light size is straightforward, the other parameters offer more nuanced control and
are worth exploring in detail.
Light extent is a two-element vector: the x component defines where the light falloff begins, while
the y component determines where the falloff ends and light becomes invisible. This second value also
affects the bounding box size of the light. A larger extent results in more affected shading points,
increasing the rendering cost. For our performance testing, we intentionally set large y values to stress
the system. The visual difference is shown in Figure 17.
The Two-sided flag controls whether light is emitted from both sides of planar shapes like Rect
and Disk lights. When the flag is disabled, lighting is only evaluated on the front face, reducing compu-
tation time. This is especially noticeable for Disk lights as we will later demonstrate. Obviously these

The close-up comparisons highlight several key differences. Fake-PCSS better preserves fine geo-

with the results, possibly leading to an integration of this system also in the other light types that

Occlusion limitation
Our current implementation supports area light shadowing via a single shadow
map, effectively casting shadows in one main direction, and thus forming what we named a ”shadow
cone”. This approach offers a notable advantage by introducing some degree of shadowing without in-
curring a significant performance cost. However, this simplification comes with an inherent limitation:
both diffuse and specular lighting components are always visible from points outside the defined cone,
even when there is geometry occluding the light source. For example, placing a light inside a closed
room can lead to unrealistic light leakage into adjacent spaces, despite complete occlusion by walls or
other structures. We can visualize this problem in Figure 22.
Figure 22: No occlusion present on the right leads to light leaking through geometry
While this may seem an obvious limitation based on how we have set up the system, it is important
to stress this flaw as it needs to be fully taken into account in production settings.
In practice, this limitation can be mitigated through Glacier’s per-room lighting system, which
restricts light influence to explicitly defined zones. In this setup, a light source placed in one room will
not affect geometry located in other rooms, effectively preventing light leakage and preserving spatial
coherence.
Although this workaround does not fully resolve the issue of physically accurate area light shad-
owing, it provides a practical and efficient compromise that integrates well into the existing rendering
pipeline. Given its low implementation complexity and its ability to address the most prominent arti-
facts, this solution is suitable for many production scenarios.

Discussion
In this thesis, we have thoroughly investigated the implementation and optimization of Linearly Trans-
formed Cosines (LTC) for area light rendering in real-time graphics applications such as a commercial
gaming engine like Glacier. The LTC framework, confirms to be an elegant and efficient approximation
to the physically-based BRDF area light integration problem, offering a compelling solution both in
terms of visual fidelity and computational cost. Our work builds upon this foundation, adapting and
extending the original methodology into a performant, production-ready lighting system for a commer-
cial game engine. This process required deep understanding not only of the mathematical foundations
of the technique, but also of the practical constraints imposed by real-time rendering pipelines and
hardware architectures.
One of the core strengths of the LTC approach lies in its modularity, allowing for scalable support
across various geometrical light types. In our implementation, we adapted all of the shapes currently
researched such as rectangular, disk, ellipsoid, and line lights. Each of these introduced unique chal-
lenges in terms of both shader logic as well as optimization and robustness techniques. In particular,
the line light proved particularly tricky compared to the original solution, as we were required to in-
clude optimized matrix inversions to properly account for VGPR (Vector General Purpose Register)
usage and ensure acceptable performance. Throughout the thesis, we highlighted and addressed sev-
eral of these challenges, some of which were not adequately handled in the authors’ original reference
implementation or subsequent demos.
A key contribution of this work is the introduction of a lightweight yet visually effective shadowing
mechanism, namely the novel Fake-PCSS algorithm. While not physically accurate in its simulation
of area light shadow penumbrae, this method provides a compelling visual approximation at minimal
computational cost leading to plausible soft shadows. Its introduction enabled us to avoid the pro-
hibitive performance penalties typically associated with true area light shadowing, thereby preserving
real-time frame budgets. We further demonstrated how this method could be dynamically tuned to
reduce artifacts across shadow map resolutions, reinforcing its adaptability and robustness in diverse
scene configurations. On top of this we also managed to gather positive feedback from the key stake-
holders who would then make use of this system.
From a development and engineering standpoint, a considerable portion of our effort was dedicated
to performance profiling and iterative optimization. Utilizing tools such as SCUI and Tracy, we con-
tinuously monitored GPU register pressure, LUT image fetches, and branching behavior within the
shader code. This allowed us to catch performance bottlenecks early on and to fine-tune our imple-
mentation to fit seamlessly within the constraints of the pre-existing lighting system in Glacier. Our
changes were made with the intent of minimal intrusion, ensuring compatibility with existing render
passes and lighting techniques.
Additionally, this thesis emphasizes the importance of implementation awareness. While the the-
oretical formulation of LTC is well-documented in literature, the practical considerations involved in
translating this theory into robust, scalable code are often understated. By detailing our process, pit-
falls, and decision-making process, we hope to provide a valuable guide for developers seeking to bring
advanced lighting models into real-time graphics applications, especially under hardware or engine-
specific limitations.
In conclusion, this thesis demonstrates that with careful mathematical formulation, shader design,
and attention to hardware constraints, high-quality area light rendering can be achieved in real-time
without sacrificing responsiveness or compatibility. Future work could explore several promising di-
rections:
• Enhancing BRDF fitting strategies to accommodate complex material models such as anisotropic
surfaces [46],
• Implementing support for textured or emissive masked area lights as originally proposed by the
LTC authors but omitted here due to scope limitations,
50
==PAGE:52==
• Integrating hybrid shadowing approaches [43] to achieve more physically plausible penumbrae
while maintaining interactive performance.
These improvements would contribute to making this implementation for real time area lighting
even more robust and reliable. Several of these enhancements are well documented in the existing
literature, and the primary reason they were not incorporated into this work was the limited time
available. Nevertheless, our contribution establishes a strong and flexible foundation for area light
rendering. We hope to see this work further refined and eventually adopted as a standard approach
for illuminating interactive scenes in future production environments at IOI.

Appendix B: Art Department feedback
Throughout development we kept ongoing conversation with the IOI’s art department in order to
understand what where the requirements for a proper lighting system and get some extra feedback
along the way. In particular, towards the end of the project, we presented our progress to key stake-
holders from the art department, showcasing the functionality of the light types we had implemented:
Rectangular, Disk, Line, and Ellipsoid lights. We presented them in a very simple test environment
highlighting their interactivity and showcasing how easily they could be manipulated in terms of trans-
form, size, and the other area-light-specific parameters we have discussed in previous sections.
Next, we introduced the practical implementation of the Fake-PCSS shadow technique.
While
they were already impressed by the fact that some form of shadows were available (even if cast in a
single direction), what truly stood out was the novel shadow technique we implemented. They were
impressed by the noticeable visual improvement over the default method when it came to preserving
finer shadow details, such as letters placed in front of the light source. Where the original technique
blurred these shadows entirely, our new approach rendered them clearly discernible on the shadow
receiver.
The team acknowledged that these lights would be highly useful in production, especially now that
shadows were functional. They praised the many customization options and user-friendly tweaks we
had made. However, they emphasized the need to ensure computational feasibility. If the novel shadow
technique or the overall lights were too expensive they would also be avoided in actual production en-
vironments.
On top of this, they also re-iterated on the fact that further tests needed to be conducted on
more complex scenes with different characters, more complex geometry and various materials. They
also raised an intriguing question: Could this technique be extended to arbitrary meshes? While we
explained that Linearly Transformed Cosines (LTCs) are a purely mathematical representation of
shapes, their interest in its potential applications was a promising sign of its broader utility.
