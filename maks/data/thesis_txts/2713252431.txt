Abstract
Demand response has been a growing field of study in recent years, and a rapidly changing energy sys-
tem is expected to have an increased penetration of demand response to increase the overall flexibility
of the energy system. This thesis demonstrates both how an aggregator can deliver the most profitable
signals to consumers, signalling them to free up energy, and how consumers deal with these signals
using a Home Energy Management System. Both programs were successful , the aggregator program
being done in three different ways, to showcase some of the challenges of demand response, and the
consumer program showcasing one type of consumer. Time granularity, being one of the challenges, is
crucial when it comes to capturing the maximum amount of flexibility, allowing DR to capture even
more benefit. The rebound effect, another possible challenge, is an unintended effect of activating
demand response, which is also demonstrated, and is something aggregators will need to be mindful
of.
For the consumer side it was demonstrated how the Home Energy Management System is capable
of scheduling various types of loads and accept/reject the aggregator’s signal based on the preferences
of the consumer. Having such a system is what enables consumers to be responsive to both fluctuating
energy prices and aggregator signals, in the short term.

1.
Introduction
This thesis aims to provide a comprehensive review of residential demand response (DR) via aggre-
gation regarding technical, regulatory, and economic facets. The first part deals with the emergence
of DR, what makes DR a candidate for dealing with the problems increasingly plaguing the electrical
grid, and what difficulties are associated with its introduction into the market, all from a Eurocentric
perspective.
The second part looks at the operations of an aggregator and an enlisted customer in sending and
dealing with DR activation signals. Two optimization programs, one for picking the most profitable
signals to send to the consumer and one for controlling electricity use at the consumer’s premises,
according to individual preferences.
This first chapter introduces the power system as it has been managed traditionally and high-
lights some significant aspects of the modern power system that challenge the modern electrical grid’s
adequacy, reliability, and efficiency.
1.1
Traditional power system
The power system started as a monopoly, and it is only within the last 30 years that the shift over to a
liberalized energy market has taken place. Before that, large monolithic entities were in control of both
generation, transmission, distribution and retail, allowing them to determine the price of electricity
without competition. As part of a general shift in policy, introducing competition into areas that
heretofore were not exposed to such, the EU launched measures in 1996 that formed the basis of an EU-
wide liberalized and integrated electricity market[1]. Achieving competition required the unbundling
of vertically integrated companies, among other actions. The unbundling process happened gradually,
separating parts that were primed for exposure to competition from the grid, which is a natural
monopoly1. These regulatory changes engendered a new environment that encouraged competition,
ameliorating the conditions for consumers.
1.2
The modern power system
The modern power system distinguishes itself from the traditional one in many ways. Mainly it has
done away with the top-down structure and instead increasingly relies on variable renewable energy2
(VER). This shift has taken place to halt the effects of global warming and heighten energy security by
decreasing reliance on foreign nations. The legacy electrical system, encompassing the grid, regulations
and primarily large central base-load providers, were not developed to fair well in this new environment.
Therefore, new innovative additions to the regulatory body, improvements to production units, and
novel market mechanisms arose. The transition has been a gradual change, steadily increasing the
share of renewables until some places have come close to a ’tipping point’. Grids are having trouble
absorbing the energy being produced, and new additions to the system must be conceptualized to
successfully integrate the increasingly larger VER shares.

A recent addition to the grid, advanced metering infrastructure (AMI), equips the grid with 2-way
data transferability. AMI integration is a major enabler of DR. It is an integral part of a modern grid
and is increasingly adopted globally. EU member states are required to implement AMI under EU
energy market legislation in the Third Energy Package[2]. AMI is an essential addition to the modern
grid since it allows for near real-time communication between the consumer and utilities, allowing
the consumer to experience Real-Time Pricing (RTP). Conversely, it also allows for an extensive data
collection of customers’ load patterns down to 15-minute blocks, which are crucial for DR programs[3].
1.2.1
Characteristics of renewable energy resources
The thing that mainly sets apart VER is that they are non-dispatchable, which coupled with the clas-
sical notion of supply following an uncontrollable demand causes difficulties. Wind power production
does not necessarily coincide with demand, and both wind and solar can experience dramatic ramping
events in the case of, e.g. cloud covers or storms. This requires systems with high amounts of VER to
employ a larger amount of reserves at a considerable cost. The reserves needed range from frequency
stabilizing, which help even out the minute by minute fluctuations in wind power output, to ramping
capabilities.
The adverse effects can be abated through several means. Increasing the balancing region flattens
out the variability of the VER, adding interconnectors to distant areas and allowing for sub-hourly
scheduling of generation are all ways of aiding grid operators and balance responsible parties3 (BRP).
However, as the VER penetration increases, new operational practices such as DR are poised to become
one of the mechanisms used to integrate VER further.
1.2.2
Need for reserves
The increasing cost of reserves alluded to in the previous section arises from the need to ready the
grid in case of unpredictable perturbations in VER output. The load also contributes to this need
increasingly in recent times, due to a drop in residential load factor4 caused by the proliferation of,
e.g. electric cars. The difference is that demand is, to a great degree, predictable seasonally, weekly
and daily, with typical day-ahead load forecast errors below 1%. Day-ahead wind power forecast, on
the other hand, depend on geographic diversity, terrain and number of turbines for accuracy, with a
mean absolute error5 of 15%-30%[4]. However, constant improvements are made in this field.
1.3
System flexibility
Achieving a more flexible power system is of utmost importance for planners who manage an ever-
increasing variable and intermittent generation portfolio. The difficulty lies in the fact that capital
intensive investments with payback times spanning decades must be put in motion long before anyone
can say what state the system is in at the time of launch. Certain areas in Europe has experienced
a surge in the prevalence of negative prices in recent times. This is an attestation to the fact that
the grid lacks the necessary flexibility to absorb the power being produced. This periodic inability to
absorb the energy is not solely caused by wind but equally by the large base-load plants that cannot
throttle generation further because of technical reasons or because of economic considerations. Another
example of this is the California electrical grid which struggles to integrate a large amount of solar
energy since the bulk of the energy production happens at midday, where the demand is relatively
low[5], as well as toiling with the issue of a rapid ramp-up of net-load6 at sunset, when power from
solar dies down, while the demand has its peak[6]. Curtailment of VER can become necessary but is

Fast-acting generation units such as gas- and hydro driven generators have played a vital role in
the energy system so far and will for years to come. Hydropower is renewable, has a low marginal

The upper limit seems to be all but reached in Europe, where the

Batteries are another way of bolstering the grid against the variable output of renewables, but

goals[10]. The problem with relying on batteries becomes exacerbated during wind droughts, which
can last for weeks[11]. The most recent one caused a spike in energy prices and had coal- and gas-fired
plants needing to step in and cover the short fall[12].
DR has seen an increase in interest as a means to provide the grid with flexibility in a cost-effective
way. Although DR is exceedingly complex to pull off for the aggregator given the prodigious data
needs and the byzantine regulations needed to integrate the aggregator into the market, it could prove
to be an essential resource/operational tool for the electricity market.
Moving forward, it seems most likely that a combination of the mentioned alternatives will serve
the grid best, each with its trade-offs.
Through prices, the liberalized energy market will impose
beneficial levels of market share for each contender. However, DR by way of an aggregator is exciting
because it is innovative, engages consumers for the first time, and requires very little in terms of capital
expenditure to support the grid in a myriad of ways.
1.4
Demand response
There are two main categories of demand responsiveness. Both are described to identify how they
differ and to finish up the introductory part, a brief look at the price elasticity of demand.
1.4.1
Types of demand response schemes
Dynamic pricing schemes, referred to as price-based DR (PBDR), is one way of making consumers
responsive, and there are various types to choose from, ranging from the static flat rate to the real-time
pricing (RTP). Common for all of them is the idea of passing through the price variability to the end
consumer in order that they have better information on the actual cost of their expenditure and can
react accordingly. The norm has been that the retailer absorbed all volatility, causing consumers to
be indifferent about their time of use, even though different times carried vastly different costs, either
because of grid overload or an absence of cheap generation.
The other way of incentivizing consumers to change consumption patterns is via incentive-based
DR (IBDR), which comes in various forms as well. The type discussed in the case of an aggregator
managing an extensive portfolio of smaller residential units is direct load-control (DLC). In this case,
signals from the aggregator directly control specific loads at the customer’s premises[13].
The two types are not mutually exclusive. A consumer can engage in both types, thereby saving on
the electricity bill and be receptive to signals and receive direct payments. In that case, however, the
aggregator and BRP/supplier will need to be attentive when separating which of the two influences
has caused a change in the load pattern. The two types differ because IBDR constitutes a flexible
resource that can be sold on the market, comparable to generation, and PBDR does not. This resource
is applicable for a multiplicity of uses, centered around balancing and system adequacy. Examples

of these uses are congestion management, portfolio optimization and generation capacity adequacy,
procured by transmission/distribution system operator (TSO/DSO) and BRPs[14].
1.4.2
Price elasticity
For DR to even be an option, consumers must be willing and able to suppress their energy usage. AMI
has made them able, but the willingness is still debated. The level of response to either dynamic price
changes or incentive payments depends on the type of customer, whether comfort or potential savings
matter the most and to a large extent, what type of appliances are at the residence.
DR is quantified differently depending on the type of DR scheme. Starting with PBDR, the response
to changing prices is measured by own-price elasticity7. Electricity has historically been very price
inelastic, especially in the short run by virtue of a lack of substitutes. However, with the prevalence
of new load types, the shifting of loads and the ability to store energy has become more practicable,
supposedly turning it more elastic.
For IBDR, the appropriate approach is to measure the absolute or relative impact on the load
from a signal. Figure 1.1 displays measurements of impacts from different DLC programs, primarily
targeting A/C and water heaters. The impacts measured ranged from 0.4 kW to 1.5 kW per customer.
The reduction is contingent on several factors, such as the size of load absent a control signal. The
data is from 2004 and originates from utilities across the USA. In a modern European context, one
might expect these impacts potentially be higher, depending on the customer, given that, e.g. electric
cars are far more prevalent.

2.
The aggregator
This chapter deals with introducing the aggregator into the market. An existing market player can
perform this new function, or a new actor can take on the role of the aggregator. Depending on the
model used to implement aggregators, an aggregator will have varying interactions with other market
actors. The main contention is balancing, which has different degrees of complexity, depending on the
model chosen. The chapter then touches upon the data needed to use aggregated resources successfully.
Finally, it delves into the economic benefits of DR and enters a conceptual discussion on what precisely
constitutes a societal benefit as opposed to a rent transfer, two concepts often confused.
2.1
Benefits of aggregation
Historically, only large consumers could opt into demand response programs, which entailed the TSO
having the choice of curtailing these large consumers in the case of a contingency[16]. Aggregation,
however, allows smaller consumers, who otherwise could not manage the complexity, to interact with
the electricity market.
An aggregator assumes all the risk, pools together the flexibility of many
households, and sells it as a singular resource. Because of its decentralization, this resource is, in
turn, more robust than generation contingent on a single generator, in some respects. The flexibility
services that an aggregator can offer already exist as defined products in some cases, such as the primary
frequency control. Depending on regulations, aggregated flexibility may or may not be eligible for these
services[17]. Figure 2.1 is a non-exhaustive overview of services that aggregators might offer.

2.2
Market models
There is an ongoing discussion regarding whom the most suitable candidate is for offering aggregation
services to the consumers. The choice is mainly between the already established supplier or a new
independent actor. The third option of a regulated actor does not go hand in hand with the EU phi-

aggregators are offering is high and on the rise[18]. A supplier/BRP1 could also offer an aggregation
service and is in a favorable position to do so since it already has a customer base, access to data and
is established in the market. Furthermore, this arrangement would drastically reduce the complexities

In the European electrical grid, every market actor is responsible for their balancing.
Smaller
actors, such as households, typically outsource that responsibility to a BRP. All BRPs attempt to
maintain a balance in their portfolio, lest they be penalized by the TSO. The TSO’s responsibility is
the balance of the entire grid. The balancing market is realized right at the cusp between the financial
markets and the physical exchange of power[20]. Using this model, compensation for imbalances is
unnecessary, and the aggregator does not need its own BRP nor a contract with the supplier.
Nevertheless, suppliers are not necessarily suited for this new market because of a conflict of interest.
Their core business is to sell energy to consumers, whereas DR primarily curtails energy usage[21].
Despite these caveats, some believe that suppliers are apt because of the lack of clear regulations on
the roles and responsibilities of third-party aggregators towards other market players, opening up the
possibility of free-riding[19].
Another model, known as the uncorrected model, also does not require that the aggregator has a
BRP nor a contract with the supplier. In this model, DR activation will result in an imbalance for
the supplier, which gets remunerated through the regular balancing mechanism. For this to work,
passive contributions to balance restoration have to be incentivized by the TSO. If an aggregator is
operating in the balancing market, the imbalance of the supplier will contribute to restoring system
balance. However, it carries additional risk for the supplier, which becomes dependent on the balancing
price. In cases where the aggregator operates outside of the balancing market, DR that aggravates
the imbalance can get activated, in which case the model fails. Such a model is thus not pertinent if
those conditions betide. In figure 2.3 an example of an aggregator operating in the balancing market
is displayed, in which a supplier has sourced 100 units of energy, and the aggregator sends out a signal
which depresses the consumer’s usage by 25 units. This results in the BRP/supplier having a surplus
of energy, and the grid a shortage, due to the actions of the aggregator. The supplier is then passively
contributing to the restoration of balance.
The last model to be highlighted is the contractual model. In this model, the aggregator has its
own BRP and a contract with the supplier. The energy offered by the aggregator is sourced ex-post
from the supplier/BRP, according to contract specifications. In figure 2.4 the different interactions
are on display. A baseline that is agreed upon bilaterally forms the basis for calculating the volume of
flexibility that the aggregator procures from the consumer.

aggregator sends a signal to the consumer which lowers its energy use by 25 units. The supplier has

2.2.1
Other considerations
The supplier needs to know when DR is activated to prevent it from taking corrective measures upon
seeing a change in consumption. The distinction between regular changes in consumption and ones
that are initiated as a result of an aggregator signal is crucial since potential corrective measures in
case of the latter would render the DR ineffective[19].
The rebound effect is when consumers who have responded to a DR signal subsequently increase
their consumption. Depending on when a signal is known to come, the energy use can also increase
in advance. This secondary effect can lead to consumption levels beyond the ranges forecasted by the
supplier, and depending on the market model, it must be taken into account.

Neural networks, fuzzy logic and statistical techniques[methods]. Costumers are assigned to different
clusters depending on several variables, both qualitative and quantitative, of which a shortened list is
presented in table 2.1[segmentation].

since it forms the reference point from which incentive payments are made[19], as well as determining

2.4
Economic benefits of demand response
Introducing DR into the market enhances efficiency insofar as it helps bridge the gap between the
cost of producing power and the utility it provides for consumers. This is achieved when decisions
regarding consumption and production are made on the basis of marginal costs, which is defined by
the intersection of the supply and demand curves. Prior to advances in DR, most consumers have paid
a constant average price. In figure 2.7 the inefficiencies of average pricing are illustrated.
Q* and Q∗peak is the quantity of electricity used at the average price in the off-peak and peak
situations. However, the optimal quantity is higher in the off-peak hours and lower in the peak hours.
The forgone quantity in the off-peak hours is advantageous economic activity that did not occur. In
the peak hours, the cost of providing electricity past the quantity Qpeak exceeds the utility gained by
consumers. Reducing or eliminating these market failures would result in societal benefits.
In addition to general improvements in efficiency and usage of generation, transmission and distri-
bution capacities due to prices conveying more accurate information, DR can also be deployed as a
resource. Market actors such as the TSO can utilize this resource, explicitly targeting the improve-
ments mentioned above. The capacity of the electricity system needs to be such that it can serve the
maximum demand plus an additional reserve margin. These peak demands only occur for a number
of hours throughout the year. Being able to shift peak loads allows for the deferral of new generating
units and transmission and distribution lines by simply utilizing the already existing capacities more

2.4.1
Cost reductions versus rent transfer
In discussions pertaining to the benefits of DR, one can easily confound actual societal benefits with
rent transfers2, if one is not careful correctly distinguishing improvements in efficiency constituting
actual benefits, with transitory rent transfers. The former should incentivize policymakers to invest
in initiatives that promote its advancement. In contrast, pursuing the latter leaves one market actor
better off, to the detriment of another.
The net benefit to society is the net decrease in supply- and demand-side costs of providing a
certain level of utility. DR decreases supply-side costs by, e.g. lowering the need for running expensive
peak units, deferring or avoiding investments into generation or transmission etc. The demand side
might have an increase in costs in the form of, e.g. lower comfort or production output.
The steep fall in consumer prices that can occur following a relatively small decrease in demand
during peak periods happens because of the inelasticity of supply in the short run. This phenomenon
can be mistaken for a societal benefit but is, in reality, only a transitory rent transfer from suppliers
to consumers. The crux of the matter is the conflation of the notions of cost and price. The fact that
prices drop does not imply that costs have gone down, and thus there can be no societal benefit. While
it might seem expedient to lower consumer prices, it is not sustainable in the long run. Any lowering
of consumer prices beyond the reduction in supply-side cost is ultimately a decrease in the economic
rent of generators, which are used to pay off capital investments. For the large amount of capacity still
needing to stay profitable, prices must return close to the origin, barring a slight improvement caused
by whatever gave rise to the change, possibly the improved DR. Essentially, the supply curve would
shift to the left until the weighted average prices were almost at their previous level, now servicing a
demand with an improved load-factor.
It is not a goal in itself to reduce price spikes. Price spikes in any commodity market can be
necessary, signalling the need for increased capacity in the long run, which helps moderate future price
spikes. Moreover, generators cover their capital expenses with the help of the economic rents that
result from the spikes. This can be demonstrated by looking at figure 2.8. In this example, SN is the

3.
Aggregator optimization program
The aim of the program is to optimize the aggregators’ profit, by choosing which signals to send to
its consumer clusters. These clusters are all located in such a way that energy procured at either
one is fungible.
The program is built on the premise that the aggregator operates under a single
BRP structure, and therefore imbalance payments are not considered. Only downward shifts in energy
are included, but the mechanism for including upward flexibility is similar in implementation, only
opposite in nature.
The aggregator’s profit is the revenue earned by selling flexibility, less the incentive payments made
to consumers. The term flexibility covers all possible uses of the energy. Although the aggregator’s
product could be procured by the TSO as part of ancillary services, or be purchased through the
market, no attempt has been made to separate the two. The program is limited to a 24 hour period,
equivalent to a day-ahead market decision horizon. There are 3 different iterations of the same program,
with minor changes differentiating them.
The first version, written in aggregator15.jl, operates with a 15 minute time resolution in the mar-
ket side as well as at the consumer premises. This entails a more efficient and responsive suite of
signals, as well as the ability to utilize them to the fullest. The second, aggregator1.jl, has a 15 minute
resolution at the consumer side, but a 1 hour flexibility-market resolution, not allowing the flexibility
of the DR to reach the market. This is pursuant to traditional market products. The final version,
aggregatorpb.jl, has a rebound effect added onto the 15 minute version, to showcase one of the possible
ramifications of altering the load curves of consumers.
The input into the program consists of:
– Data on clusters. This includes their baseline electricity usage and number of houses in a cluster.
– Signal data. This is the characteristics of the signals that the aggregator has at its disposal. A
signal consists of three parts. An incentive payment, the size of which is known to elicit a certain
response, a requested amount of power reduction and a duration. The duration of the signals are
in the range of four to eight hours[25] and the power reduction ranges are adapted from the data
presented in figure 1.1 chapter 1.4.2. The full suite of signals consists of all possible time-spans
for each signal type.
– Flexibility price forecast. The price an aggregator gets for the energy that has been freed up as
a result of a signal.
The program will return:
– A list of clusters with their designated signals.
– The payout to consumers.
– The income of the aggregator.
– The profit obtained by the aggregator.
– The total energy sold to the market.

3.3
Time granularity
As new technology both enables and requires operation that resembles real time, given the more erratic
market brought on by the introduction of intermittent energy sources, markets are pivoting towards
products with lower time granularity. The time refers to the span of time over which the physical
delivery of the electricity traded is set to take place. Traditionally the dominant product was that
of 60 minutes of physical delivery, but recently products of 15 or even 5 minutes of delivery have
been introduced into markets around the world. This has the effect of facilitating an environment
where flexible power sources are better remunerated for their ability to more accurately follow the
demand ramps, illustrated in figure 3.4. In conjunction with this change, a gate closure closer to real
time operation allows market players that might otherwise not be able to commit with longer lead
times[26].
In this version, to demonstrate the effect of increased granularity, the market resolution was in-
creased to 60 minute settlement periods. This means that the aggregator will have to provide a stable
power over the course of that entire delivery period, and not in 15 minute intervals as previously.

3.5
The rebound effect
The rebound effect, briefly mentioned earlier, is a result of certain types of loads coming back online.
Mainly curtailable, which cover EVs, freezers, water heaters and room heating/cooling equipment.
Curtailable loads are defined by their ability to be turned off for a certain period of time without
incurring any adverse effects. Alternatively, in the case of room heating/cooling the set temperature
can be temporarily raised/lowered to free up some energy in response to a signal. When the signal
has ended these loads must now make up for dissipated energy, causing their energy expenditure to go
above regular levels. Shiftable loads might also come online disproportionately after a signal. These
are flexible loads such as washers and dryers, that have to run within a certain time frame. Having
prevented these from turning on for en extended period of time increases the risk that a large amount
of such appliances will turn on around the same time. Depending on when the signal is known to the
customer, energy might also be drawn prior to the signal, and stored for use during the signal period.
Signals can be sent with hours of leeway or next to none. Only rebound as result of a sudden signal
is treated in this chapter.
Depending on the market model, the rebounded energy constitutes an expense in addition to the
incentive payments. In the case of a third party aggregator, the unexpected rebound might lead to an
imbalance for the BRP or a loss of revenue because of increases in demand at times with higher prices,
which the flat price the consumer is charged might not cover.
In the case of a single BRP where there is a contract, the contract would specify how the imbalance
should be handled, and for the uncorrected model the imbalance would get remunerated through the
regular balancing mechanism.
In this case the undesirable rebound is incorporated into the program with the cost equal to that

Related work
Throughout the literature many approaches to the subject of DR are found. Various DR schemes have
been proposed as well as optimization methods. The common denominator across all DR schemes is
the goal of transitioning into a more flexible electrical grid, with an increase in the efficiency of the use
of the increasingly intermittent share of resources. A paper[28] has done a review on the optimization
methods applied and categorized them according to their methods, as seen in figure 3.7.
Figure 3.7: A listing of the various combinations of optimization methods and target of optimization[28]
The papers are tackling DR from different perspectives, such as homeowners looking to minimize
electricity payments, power utilities maximizing profit, while taking consumer’s reaction to electricity
prices into account or micro-grids minimizing electricity cost across the entire grid. Both linear and
non-linear approaches, heuristics and game theory have all been used. These different approaches
all vary in their ability to scale and include uncertainties among other things. The paper concludes,
among other things, that the majority of DR schemes rely on a baseline from which reductions are
calculated from, essentially serving as the basis of DR.
Another paper[29] describes a process whereby consumers are aggregated and treated like a virtual
power plant (VPP). This work is closely related to what has been presented in this chapter. The aggre-
gator in both cases serves as a middleman between the customers and the market. This paper focused
solely on customers’, both residential and commercial, appliances with thermal storage, specifically
A/C units, while optimizing for maximum load reduction over a certain control period. Some of the
major similarities that were identified were as follows:
– Establishment of a baseline consumption profile to correctly identify changes brought on by
signals.

Consumer optimization program
On the receiving end of the aggregator’s signals is the consumer. Consumers preside over a range
of different loads and a Home Energy Management System (HEMS) is tasked with coordinating the
energy use, with the goal of increasing the consumer’s utility. This is done by using weather and
electricity price forecasts, user behavior, aggregator signals etc., to optimally schedule energy use, on
the basis of user preferences. It acts as a central hub for all the house’s loads and potential DERs,
visualized in figure 4.1. The HEMS is what allows consumers to be responsive to short term energy
price fluctuations, thereby allowing DR to happen. For this demo both price- and incentive based
DR is in force, with the rate structure being RTP. This is done so placement of loads absent any
aggregator signals is not arbitrary, and because this finer time granularity most likely is the prospect
for consumers[30]. The HEMS will plan out the load profile for a 24 hour time span, optimizing on
three dimensions: Consumer comfort with respect to indoor temperature, minimizing cost of energy,
which includes obtaining the incentive payment if deemed advantageous and lastly, maximizing the
shiftable appliance startup-time preferences. The program is written in consumer.jl.

to coordinate loads and DERs optimally.
The household modelled in this chapter has a number of different loads in the respective categories.
Appliances such as a dishwasher and washing machine which fall under the shiftable profile category.
They should be run for a given number of cycles throughout the planning horizon and a cycle has a
known profile, but the HEMS is free to schedule them when advantageous, subject to electricity price,
user preference etc. A shiftable volume is similar, but the energy it requires can be drawn arbitrarily
within a certain time range. In this category falls the houses’ water heater.
the freezer and fridge both fall under the disconnectable category. A freezer can be disconnected
for a stretch of time and still maintain an acceptable temperature, before it has to come online again.
The houses’s climate control is reducible. The heating/cooling can make compromises on the ideal
indoor temperature, depending on user configuration in order to maximize utility, by modifying the

inflexible load is not controlled by the HEMS, and encompasses loads such as TVs and ovens, for which

4.0.1
Multi objective optimization problem
The goal of the program is to maximize temperature satisfaction, minimize electricity cost and maxi-
mize appliance schedule satisfaction, subject to the various constraints. These three goals are compet-
ing, and therefore no single solution can satisfy them all at once. Instead, when dealing with a multi
objective optimization problem (MOOP) the solution is a point at which no other feasible solution
exists, such that one objective could be improved, without it being to the detriment of another. This
notion is known as Pareto optimality.
It is the decision maker’s (DM) job to express the relative
importance of the objectives. To this end, there are different methods available. A popular choice,
and one that results in a single objective function consisting of the weighted sums of all the objective
functions, is the weighted sum method. The weights must be non-negative to ensure Pareto optimality.
It should be noted that there is no single solution to a MOOP, instead the solution lies on the so-called
Pareto face, which is linear, in this case, given the linear program. Normalizing via weighted sum
can therefore leads to sensitivity issues, since optimal solutions are corner point solutions, and slight

Conclusion
The report successfully expounds upon DR, by first laying down the ground work necessary for a
thorough understanding of the subject matter, and then demonstrating how DR is done practically
from the aggregator’s perspective and from the perspective of a consumer.
DR is likely going to play a bigger role in the coming years, and the introduction of the aggregator
will progress and regulations and policies will get more fleshed out. Regarding the economic benefit of
DR, it is important to differentiate between societal benefits and rent transfers. Ideally, public policy
should be made on the basis of societal benefits only.
The aggregator program was successful in picking out the optimal signal to send to the consumer
clusters.
Conversely, the consumer program showed how a HEMS at the consumer premises can
coordinate all the loads based on consumer preferences, prices and possibly accepting the aggregator’s
signal.
