Abstract
The sustainable energy development is a sensitive topic in nowadays society. The need for new sources
of energy is urgent now more than ever. Even though the research in this branch of engineering is mainly
oriented toward finding alternative solutions, a lot can still be improved in the already existing developed
designs. The wind energy sector is the example that matches perfectly with what was just stated. The
exponential growth of this renewable source of energy is a consequence of the strong interest in this field.
Considering this, it is obvious how wind energy will play an essential role in matching the global energy
production requirements in the following years.
The phenomenon described will soon translate into the necessity to be able to build wind power plants in
areas with adverse weather conditions, in order to not disturb the population in terms of noise and visual
impact. The purpose of this thesis is to help analyse how wind energy production can be affected by an
extreme cold climate environment. To be able to do that a well tested ice accretion investigation method
is needed. Thanks to this report the reader will be able to have a detailed view of the procedure developed
during a 5 months thesis period.
The following paper will describe the study conducted in a collaboration between DTU and FORCE
Technology. Different tests were done with a scaled wind turbine in the Climate Wind Tunnel at FORCE
Technology (Lyngby DK). This particular wind tunnel enables the simulation of meteorological phenom­
ena even at very low temperature. The tests took place, then the iced sample blade was scanned allowing
the team to obtain different 3­D models with the photogrammetry process. The 3­D models of the blade
have then been post­processed with different software to ultimately obtain different sections. This ensures
a more detailed analysis.
The goal is to be able to have a prediction model to analyse how the ice will accumulate on the surfaces
of wind turbines’ blades. To do that, this thesis aims to observe the effects of wind turbine’s operation
parameters on the ice accretion phenomenon. This could be relevant study that hopefully will have a
positive impact on the future generation of wind turbines.

Introduction
The increasing interest in sustainable sources of energy creates new challenges in the field of engineering.
In the renewable energy development, wind energy plays and important role, since it is one of the most
prolific ways to produce clean power. The Global Wind Energy Council confirms that one of the most
prolific years for wind energy was 2021, with a rise of 93.6 GW of new capacity installed. This value is
only 1.8% lower than 2020’s record year [1]. This growth builds up to make the 837 GW of wind power
capacity worldwide in 2022 which helps avoiding 1.2 billion tonnes of CO2 emissions [1]. Albeit the
sustained growth in the past few years, more capacity needs to be installed to meet the net zero targets of
2050. It is then crucial to find new areas to harness the wind resource for the production of energy.
Figure 1.1: 2020­2030 wind energy growth [1]
The exponential increment needed in this energy branch requires the exploitation of the wind resource
in extreme environmental conditions too. Remote areas are considered ideal to install wind farms in
order to not compromise the landscape and avoid disturbance to the population. For these reasons, cold
climate sites are some of the most attractive locations. It is also important to consider that winter is the
season that typically yields most wind energy thanks to average higher wind speeds and increased air
density. The same applies during the night when the temperature levels drop more easily below 0 °C
[2]. Besides, such conditions are not restricted to the Nordics or mountainous regions anymore. Newly
designed turbines can be high enough to experience cold weather and in­cloud icing even in Southern

countries and at low altitude areas because of the increased hub height and rotor diameter [3]. Despite
its advantages, wind farms built in these non­conventional areas have to endure harsh climate conditions
that can affect the structure and components of the wind turbines. In particular, in­cloud icing can cause
structural load increment to the wind turbine, compromise the aerodynamic performances, and create

1.2
Background
Cold climate is defined, in a wind energy context, both as the condition which allows the formation of
atmospheric icing; and as the state where the temperatures drop below the operational limit of a standard
turbine (usually ­10 °C for operation, ­20 °C for survival). These conditions are strictly related to the
location of the site and the season. Even if the annual average temperature might be mild, during the
winter some serious ice accretion can compromise the wind turbine. A more detailed definition of this
concept has been given by the IEA Wind (Task 19 Wind Energy in Cold Climates):
”Cold climate locations are sites where the temperature can go lower than the operational tempera­
ture of the wind turbine (Low Temperature Climate ­ LTC), or icing events occur (Icing Climate ­ IC).”
Figure 1.3: Cold Climate definition with Low Temperature Climate and Icing Climate
IC is correlated to the atmospheric icing. This can have both effects on the wind turbine and on the
meteorological instruments. LTC instead is when extremely low temperatures occur, this instead can
affect the maintenance of the turbine. Furthermore, it is important to specify there are two categories
of atmospheric icing: in­cloud icing (glaze, rime and mixed) and precipitation icing (caused by freezing
rain or wet snow) [6]. The mechanical characteristics of the ice depend on different parameters related to
meteorological and atmospheric conditions. As instance, the liquid water content (LWC), median volume
droplet diameter (MVD), wind speed, atmospheric pressure, air temperature and humidity can all affect
the ice formation process. These parameters also influence the ice accretion process which is further
categorized as follows:
• In­cloud icing: related to temperatures below 0 °C and clouds on low altitude.
• Precipitation icing: dependent on precipitation. It can also happen at positive temperatures in form
of wet snow.
In order to better understand the concept, a short explanation of the different types of ice and accretion
process is given in the following sections.

1.3
Icing types
Icing is a widespread issue that can critically affect many structures such as aircrafts, bridges, power lines,
vessels, offshore oil platforms and wind turbines [7]. Depending on the meteorological conditions, ice
can feature different shapes, content and appearance. The main in­cloud icing categories are: glaze ice,
rime ice and mixed ice. All these types can affect the blade surface roughness within seconds and deform
the blade shape within several minutes to hours. Figure 1.4 reports the relationship between different
types of ice accretion, temperature and wind velocity.
Figure 1.4: In­cloud icing classification [6]
Glaze ice
Glaze ice, is an ice coating characterized by a high density (900 kg/m3), transparency, smoothness and
homogeneous appearance similar to clear ice. Glaze ice is caused by large droplets of freezing rain or wet
in­cloud icing between 0 °C and ­4 °C. The accretion rate of glaze ice depends on: rate of precipitation,
wind speed and temperature. Glaze ice is also typically hard to remove because of its strong adhesion
and cohesion forces [8]. From previous studies, it was found that its presence on an airfoil results in
the formation of double or multiple horns ice which cause decrease of lift, increase of drag and increase
of pitching moment. Furthermore, the formation of horn ice on the surface leads to increased projected
height of the airfoil causing higher turbulence intensity [9]. Based on flight test data comparison with
wind tunnel experiments, it has been concluded that the extent of drag increase is larger (up to 230 %)than
that of lift increase (35 %) [10].
Rime ice
Rime ice is white ice with in­trapped air which is formed when supercooled water droplets (liquid water
below 0 °C) freeze on surfaces causing windward icing structures. Because of its crystalline structure
rime ice is brittle; moreover, depending on the temperature, rime ice can be further classified as soft rime
or hard rime. Soft rime is similar to hoar frost, it forms below ­8 °C, it is less dense (200­600 kg/m3)
and more crystalline than hard rime. Conversely, hard rime forms between ­2 °C/ ­8 °C, it is more dense
(600­900 kg/m3) and has a more opaque appearance. The accretion rate of rime ice depends on many
parameters: dimension of the object exposed, wind speed, water content in the air and temperature [8].
The aerodynamic effects of rime ice accretion on an airfoil consist in a reduction in lift and an increase
in drag [11]. Because of its streamlined shape, the impact of rime ice on the airfoil aerodynamics is more
limited compared to glaze ice and mixed ice.
Mixed ice
Mixed ice is a mixture of glaze ice and rime ice and is one of the most common icing forms in real­life
scenarios. Its appearance consists of layers of clear and opaque ice as a result of variation in liquid water
content, air temperature and droplet size. This ice type appears clear near the stagnation line and turns to

to remove than rime ice. Previous papers determined that, similarly to glaze ice, mixed ice formations

1.4
Icing accretion process
When supercooled water droplets contained in the air hit the structures of a wind turbine, the ice accretion
process starts. The process is a time­dependent mechanism described in Equation 1.1 formulated by
Makkonen [15]. In particular, the equation calculates the icing rate dependency with the parameters
presented below.
dM
t
= α1 ∗α2 ∗α3 ∗w ∗v ∗A
(1.1)
Where:
• α1: collision efficiency, refers to the flux density of the particles.
• α2: collection efficiency, describes the rate of collided particles that stick to the surface.
• α3: accretion efficiency, refers to the ratio of icing particles on the body and the flux density of the
particles.
• v: particles’ velocity relative to the airfoil,
• w: mass concentration of the water droplets in the air,
• A: cross section area of the airfoil.
The three efficiencies, with a value from 0 to 1, are correction factors that may reduce the ice mass rate
from its maximum value [w ∗v ∗A]. True values of collision efficiency α1 will always be lower than 1,
however, α1 increases proportionally with the size of the droplets, or the air velocity.

is important to specify that ice rate and type are also dependent on other variables related to the blade
geometry (chord, airfoil and angle of attack).

LWC ­ Liquid Water Content
The liquid water content is the measure of the mass of water in a cloud at a specific amount of dry air
[g/m3]. High values of LWC suggest formation of glaze ice; on the other hand, low LWC values are an
indicator of rime ice accretion.
MVD ­ Median Volumetric Diameter
The median volumetric diameter defines the median size of water droplets in the cloud [µm]. Usually,
this value decreases when the LWC value increases. α1 and α3 efficiencies depend proportionally on
median volumetric diameter. High MVD increases the droplet rate hitting the surface.
Wind Speed
The velocity of the air affects both the rate of ice accretion and the ice type formation. Faster droplets
also rise the collision efficiency.
Air Temperature
One of the most determinant parameters in the icing process is the air temperature. By changing the
convective heat, the air temperature affects the liquid water content and the accretion efficiency (α3).

1.6
Effects of ice on wind turbines
1.6.1
Power losses
Based on the blade element momentum method, the aerodynamic loads for the affected chord lengths
can be solved to determine the torque and power loss [17]. Depending on the frequency, duration and
intensity of the icing phenomenon the wind turbines may be forced to curtail the power production. The
effects of icing on wind turbine blades can lead up to a 17% loss in AEP and reduce the power coefficient
from 20% to 50% [18].

1.6.2
Mechanical problems
Icing phenomenon is characterized by uneven distribution along the blade which can lead to imbalances
and quicker wear of the wind turbine’s components. Moreover, ice accretions are not limited only to the
blades of the turbine but can also form on the nacelle and tower adding mass to the overall structure and
causing vibration phenomena.
1.6.3
Measurement errors
Ice accretion can compromise the performance of meteorological instruments such as anemometers and
wind vanes. For example, the additional mass on the anemometers makes the instrument act like a fly­
wheel which responds more slowly to changes in wind speed. Wind speed prediction errors caused by
ice accretion can then be as large as 10% to 30 % [19]. This can generate overloads in the turbines which
rely on the wind speed parameter read by the meteorological instruments.
1.6.4
Safety problems
The most common safety issue of iced wind turbine blades is the ice shedding phenomenon which typ­
ically takes place when the air temperature is just above the freezing point. Usually, the risk is circum­
scribed to a small radius around the tower of the wind turbine, however, there is still a significant risk for
the public, surrounding structures and employees responsible for constructing and maintaining the wind
farm. Ice chunks shed from the turbine can weigh up to several kilograms and can potentially impact and
critically damage other parts of the turbine during the impact.
1.7
Anti­icing and de­icing techniques
Based on the type of ice accretion, there are potential solutions such as de­icing and anti­icing to prevent
critical failures. De­icing systems (mechanical or thermal removal) aim at removing the ice once it is
formed; whereas anti­icing systems (coating or re­design of the surface’s shape) aim at preventing icing
phenomenons. These systems can be further categorized into active or passive. On the one hand, active
systems require an external energy supply; on the other hand, passive systems can operate without an
external energy supply. Passive anti­icing systems represent the best solutions in terms of energy effi­
ciency however, they cannot completely prevent icing phenomenons therefore active solutions must be
integrated too [20]. Current solutions offered on the market vary depending on the blade manufacturer.
Some valid options include black paint, pitching of the blade, chemical coatings, microwaves [21] or
shaking the blades [22]. Even so, all these solutions have some drawbacks and require major invest­
ments; it is therefore crucial to implement reliable icing estimation strategies.

Spray system
The CWT is a closed­circuit wind tunnel, which allows the test section to be completely isolated. It has
all the components of a common wind tunnel, however it also includes a cooling and a spray system to
be able to perform atmospheric events. Thanks to these, clouds, and ice can be recreated artificially. A
picture of the spray system is reported below.
Study of icing shapes on rotating HAWT and evaluating its performances
14
==PAGE:24==
Figure 3.2: CWT spray system
It is made of 15 nozzles equally distanced one to each other and located on an airfoil shape support to
not disturb the airflow. The working principle consists of two concentric tubes where water and air flow
separately at different pressure. They are then mixed in the cap to obtain the desired type of mixture.
This setup is very delicate to the temperature, for this reason, a cooling system is also needed to avoid
the water to freeze inside the tube. This consists of an electric wire wrapped around the tube bringing the
water inside the smaller tubes located in the spray system. This wire ensures that the water stay warm
enough to flow properly inside the nozzles even at an environmental temperature lower than 0 °C. Despite
these precautions, in some preliminary tests, the system got stuck quite often. The routine procedure was
to remove all the nozzles and clean them in order to be sure that no ice was formed inside. However,
after some minutes of working the spray system stopped again. A more detailed inspection reveals some
air bubbles were found at the very top edge of the water tube, even if the water system was running.
This suggested that some part of the water system got frozen. An accurate analysis revealed that a small
section of the water tube inside the tunnel wasn’t covered by the heating wire, causing the water inside to
freeze and block the whole flow. For this reason, some measures were taken. In particular, the warmed
cable was extended to the section uncovered. Additionally, the portion was coated with bubble wrap as

Photogrammetry Method
In this section, the photogrammetry method used during the experiments will be explained. The reason
behind the need for photogrammetry stands in the will to compare the different cases analysed. The long­
term idea is to have an assortment of different scenarios in order to predict the icing accretion, depending
on the input parameters, and know how to act.
A fundamental contribution to the research for a proper and systematic photogrammetry method has
been given by the BSc students Jan Jastrzebski and Carlos Prieto Velazquez. In the work done in their
thesis ”Optimizing 3D techniques for capturing the build­up of ice on wind turbine blades” a consistent
improvement was obtained compared to the previous method developed in Berat MSc thesis [23]. Despite
the working principle is almost identical, the addition of more professional tools allowed a better result.
For instance a proper green screen, light sources with adequate diffuse boxes, and a 28 mm Sigma f/1.4
prime lens helped a lot in obtaining a more accurate result alongside with the experience showed by Jan
and Carlos. They were able to identify the ideal distances and spatial distribution of the setup component
inside the wind tunnel, which allows the method to be consistent and systematic. A relevant help has
also been given by the MSc student Marian Petroc. Thanks to his mechanical knowledge he was able to
build a working turning table essential for the whole procedure. Figure 3.11 shows the complete setup
for photogrammetry assembled inside the CWT at FORCE Technology.
Figure 3.11: Final photogrammetry setup inside the CWT

• The test was performed at the CWT according to the determined initial parameters
• Once the test was done the whole instrumentation for photogrammetry was displaced properly
inside the CWT and power supplied;
• The designated blade for photogrammetry was carefully unmounted from the wind turbine and
connected to the turning table;

4.1.2
Test conditions
The main requirement for the analysis developed in this MSc thesis was to obtain rime ice accretion on
the wind turbine blades. Being able to achieve this consistently required a variety of preliminary tests.
Thanks both to personal experience at the CWT and to Berat [23] studies, the ideal test conditions were
outlined.

these considerations have been taken into account, the ideal Ap, Wp have been selected. In particular
with Ap=3 [Bar] and Wp=2.7 [Bar] at ­8 [°C] the best rime ice accretion has been obtained.

• 3 stand­still cases (λ = 0) for pitch θ = 0°, 5°, 90° (θ = 90° parking mode of the wind turbine);

For the stand­still cases, the differences between θ = 0° and θ = 5° are very small all over the blade. This

blade. In fact, as reported before, the measurement of the pitch angle during the test has been done on

free wind, which causes more ice to accrete on the edge close to the tip. Another remarkable aspect is
that the suction side is almost completely clean from ice. This happens because the wind flow through
the blade, which is not rotating, fully separates and doesn’t compromise the back of it.
What happened for case 3 (parking mode) is different. Because of the high pitch angle, is now the leading
edge of the blade to face the free wind. For this reason, the impingement area is the leading edge, so the
ice accumulates more in this region. Again the ice accreted more going to the tip. The cause is the same
described for case 1 and case 2: the measurement of the pitch angle. On the other way around, the leading
edge of the blade close to the root is not directly perpendicular to the free wind direction.
5.2.2
RPM Cases
Because rotational speed is now a parameter, some specifications are required. ωr component is now
present and so Vrel. This causes the wind flow to move in a smoother way around the blade, compared
to the stand­still cases. This is the reason why a thin layer of ice is accumulating also on the suction side
while it is not in the stand­still cases (see Appendix section A.3).

This behaviour can be seen all over the blade, more evidently in the edge close to the tip. A lower pitch
angle causes the ice to accrete on the leading edge but slightly more orientated on the pressure side. On
the other hand, a higher pitch angle induces the ice to accumulate more uniformly, between the suction
and pressure side, on the leading edge. The reason behind that is that with θ = 5° there is less flow
separation compared to θ = 0°.

Some common behaviours can be observed between all the RPM cases even if the LWC value has a
constant value along the span of the rotor:
• The ice thickness variation along the span of the blade (thicker on the tip, thinner on the root).
• Rime ice shapes can be found at the root region of the blade, while the tip of the blade presented
ice closer to mixed ice.
These phenomena happen as explained before because of the twist of the blade. However, this is not
the only cause. A big contribution to that comes from the impact velocity. From Makkonen’s equation
(Equation 1.1) it can be seen how v (particles’ velocity relative to the airfoil) is proportional to the mass
of the ice accumulated. Because this factor increases going to the tip of the blade, the outcome is a thicker
layer of ice on the tip compared to the root.
The impact velocity, however, is inversely proportional to the accretion efficiency (α3), which decreases
going to the tip (see Equation 1.1). This is the consequence of a higher value of the local kinetic heat
which rises with the local TSR. Consequently, the ice accreted closer to the tip has characteristics similar
to mixed ice, on the other hand going to the root the ice looks almost perfect rime.

cases is even more visible from this plot: the higher is the rotational speed, the more is the ice accreted.

• For the stand­still cases (c1, c2, c3) the curve is even steeper going to the tip of the blade. This

Conclusions & Future Work
This study aimed to analyse the ice accretion process on a horizontal axes wind turbine, alongside an
evaluation of its performances. To do that the previously existing wind turbine setup was improved
to avoid any vibration during the test, and to obtain an overall better result. At Climate Wind Tunnel
(FORCE Technology), a set of preliminary tests were taken before running the actual ones. This has
been done in order to understand how to be able to perform the tunnel, calibrate the force transducer, and
to check the wind turbine was properly functioning. Also, it allows the team to identify a problem in the
water system. A section of the water tube inside the wind tunnel wasn’t wrapped into the heating system
cable, causing the whole water system to freeze.
After the issue was fixed, and the tests’ schedule was set, it was possible to perform the main tests.
During every test, data from the force transducer were taken thanks to LabView software. After each
test was terminated the whole setup for the scanning had to be carried inside the tunnel. At this point the
photogrammetry process took place. Both the data from the force measurement and the scanning were
then post­processed with different software. Unfortunately the first turned out to be unreliable. On the
other hand, the photogrammetry process gave accurate results. Because of that, a detailed discussion
was possible on the icing shape but not on the performances of the wind turbine related to the accretion
phenomenon.
Different comparisons have been made between stand­still cases, same TSR cases, and same pitch angle
ones. The idea was to find a pattern in the ice formation process. That’s the reason why the ending part
of the chapter 5 analyses the common behaviour in the ice accretion phenomenon.
With this being said, the following conclusions were found:
• The location of where the ice is accumulating on the blade depends on the pitch angle of the blade
itself.
• The thickness of the ice depends on the impingement area being perpendicular to the free wind
direction (for the stand still cases), or on the impact velocity of the droplets and it is then related to
the rotational speed of the wind turbine (for the rotational cases).
With the experience gained during this master project thesis, some ideas are listed below as future work
to improve the outcomes’ reliability.
• Further verification on the force transducer (AMTI MC3A­500 six­axis) accuracy is needed. From
the calibration process, cross talk phenomenon was observed and solved with polynomial function.
However obtained tests’ measurement were indicating that some other issue was effected the data.
The results were, for this reason, corrupted and useless. A detailed analysis of this sensor is needed
before using it for other studies.
• Measurements of the cloud characteristics inside the CWT, with a CDP (Cloud Droplet Probe), are
essential to provide more accurate input parameters during the tests. Ideally, a correlation between
the water pressure, air pressure (Wp, Ap respectively), and wind speed with the cloud characteristics
could be found. This would help to have a better knowledge of the test’s conditions.
Study of icing shapes on rotating HAWT and evaluating its performances

==PAGE:44==
• More tests can be run by increasing the exposure time to observe how the outcomes of ice accretion
is affected by this. Also, a wider analysis for different pitch angles might show interesting results.
• This all project focused on rime ice formation, however, a detailed analysis for mixed and glaze
ice types would be interesting considering the frequency of the phenomenon in a real environment.

BEM Theory
The Blade Element Momentum theory, known as BEM theory, is the most used theory to analyse wind
turbines’ aerodynamic performances. It is important to specify that this is the most common method
because of the low cost of operation and the reliability of the results. It was introduced by Glauert who
combined the 1D momentum theory and the blade element theory. Different variations of the BEM codes
can be developed, in fact, it can be both steady or unsteady and even some correction methods can be
implemented.
For the purposes of this thesis, the BEM code has been used with two basic corrections as shown in this
appendix section.

• The flow is stationary, uniform, and incompressible.
