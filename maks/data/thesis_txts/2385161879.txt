Abstract
Scaling deposition within different parts of the oil and gas production, causes massive economical
impacts and safety hazards. The trend in hydrocarbon production moves towards development of deeper
wells, with high temperature and high pressure. Development of subsea production units with long tie
backs, introduces new challenges regarding scaling occurrence.
Prevention and inhibition of scales, are considered preferable in comparison to removal. This require
reliable thermodynamic models, and experimental data.
Determination of the solubility of sparingly soluble salts, are a crucial step in the process of scale predic-
tion. The work conducted in this study will be a part of the development in effective scale prediction and
prevention. Enhanced production efﬁciency, relies on accurate modelling of scale precipitation. Experi-
mental data of sulﬁde solubility are found to be of old origin, originating from extended experimental work
throughout the 20th century. The overall work within this ﬁeld shows great confusion, and contradictory
results. The experimental work of this study, will be compared with the one found in open literature.
This thesis work is a joint in the new research conducted at DTU(AT CERE), which are aiming towards
enhanced understanding of scaling minerals.
An extensive study of available experimental data has been conducted. The strengths and weaknesses
of these studies, has been discussed. The goal for this experimental study, has been to avoid experi-
mental uncertainties and error. In addition, implementing a modern analytical method for concentration
measurements.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This thesis work is covering the experimental determination of solubility, of the scale mineral
Lead(||)sulﬁde. The study are conducted in order to expand the knowledge of scale formation.
Formation of scale poses a general challenge for the production equipment in the oil and gas industry.
Through scale precipitation in production and interior piping, great loss of production may occur. Ex-
amples of worst case scenarios, are production stops and downhole safety valve failure. The need for
enhanced understanding of the scaling problem, is critical. Especially considering the enhanced focus on
cost efﬁciency, and safety concerns in the oil and gas industry.
Prevention of scaling in contrary to scale removal, represents large savings related to cost. A better base of
information provided by modern experimental studies, will improve the ability to model scale precipitation
occurrence. The companies are using thermodynamic models, which are based on experimental data of old
origin. These data are representing a time where experimental studies were prioritized. The experimental
work conducted in the early 20th century are extensive. Nevertheless, characterized by the technology
available at that time. The measurement methods were inaccurate, hence the results show large deviance
in magnitude. This leads to erroneous prediction of scaling to day, with the vast economical impact that
inﬂicts.
The following chapter aims for an unveiling of the scaling problem, and its impact on the oil and gas
industry.

1.1. SCALING
3
1.1
Scaling
Scaling are inorganic deposits(crystalline) which may occur within both the source rock, and equip-
ment of hydrocarbon production. The species may be both water-dispersed or water-soluble(Amjad and
Demadis (2015)). Depositions of scaling is due to precipitation of mineral components, within formation-
or injection/drilling-ﬂuid during oil and gas extraction(Crabtree et al. (1999)). This problem is extensive
within the production of hydrocarbons, and other processes where water are produced. The mineral
precipitation are induced by changes of properties in the produced ﬂuid. When producing hydrocarbons
from a source rock, the transported ﬂuid are subjected to a series of environmental changes. The tempera-
ture is decreasing, as the ﬂuid travels towards the topside production unit. The geothermal gradient is
approximately 25-30 ° C/km (Schlumberger - Oilﬁeld glossary (2017)). An approximation of pressure
conditions in a certain part of a well, is given by multiplication of mud weight and depth. Therefore, the
pressure reduces as the ﬂuid travels towards topside. The solubility of constituents within the brine may
decrease, hence precipitate as a result(Bai and Bai (2010)).
The ﬂuid of the source rock has achieved chemical equilibrium through millions of years. Sudden
interaction with injection ﬂuid causes distress, and reaction products may occur. Pressure support by
injection or drilling(mud), introduces ﬂuid to the source rock. The formation water and injection/drilling
ﬂuid meet, and may be incompatible. This cause reactions, and disturbance in the chemical equilibrium
of the formation water. The produced water is therefore in risk of getting oversaturated with scaling
components(Crabtree et al. (1999)).
Additional factors deciding location and amount of scaling can be kinetics, pH, composition and CO2
content(Garcia et al. (2005)).
1.2
Concepts and mechanisms
1.2.1
Saturation ratio
As described, the formation of scales depend on several factors. The solutions degree of saturation,
plays a large role. This due to the ion concentration, hence the source of scaling. The saturation ratio, is a
way to express the degree of saturation within a solution. Equation 1.1 shows the relation between the
product of cat- and anion activity constants, versus the solubility product.
SR = [Mx+][Nx−]
Ksp
(1.1)
[Mx+] and [Nx−] represents the activity constants of the cation and anion in the solution. Ksp represent
the solubility product, which describes the amount of moles of ions(per unit volume of solvent) the ﬂuid
may contain before risk of precipitation. Another way to describe equation 1.1 is "What you have/What
you can have at t = ∞", according to Amjad and Demadis (2015).
Interpretation of the saturation ratio, may lead to prediction of the solutions grade of saturation. If
SR<1.0 the solution is undersaturated. Scales do not tend to form, and existing scale tend to dissolve. If
SR=1.0, the solution is saturated. In this state of saturation, scale neither tend to form or dissolve. Lastly,

1.2.2
Nucleation
Driving forces regarding formation of scales may be change in pressure, temperature, varying pH
values and incompatible water. Precipitation of minerals in water, happens when the solubility limit
of its components are surpassed (Crabtree et al. (1999)). Forming of scales are not always occuring in
oversaturated ﬂuids, even though the presence of driving forces are a fact. The scale has to "grow from
solution", through the mechanism of nucleation. Nucleation processes are divided into homogeneous and
heterogeneous nucleation(Crabtree et al. (1999)). Unstable atom cluster formation within a saturated ﬂuid,

1.2.3
Precipitation
Precipitation is according to Hefter and Tomkins (2003) nucleation and growth of a phase 2 from
another phase 1. Phase 1 has a different composition, than phase 2. Precipitation, leading to scale
deposition, is only possible if phase 1 is in thermodynamic instability. Phase 1 may be a supersaturated
solution of phase 2, given that phase 2 is a pure compound. Precipitation of the pure compound may not
occur, given that the supersaturated solution may be stable for long periods of time. This is related to
surface tension of phase 2 and the magnitude of supersaturation of phase 1. Stability may be kept when
surface tension(Gibbs surface energy) is high, and the supersaturation is low(Hefter and Tomkins (2003)).
1.2.4
Solubility
The solubility phenomena is deﬁned by Hefter and Tomkins (2003) as:
"The analytical composition of a mixture or solution which is saturated with one of the components of the
mixture or solution, expressed in terms of the proportion of the designated component in the designated
mixture or solution."
Here "saturated" is related to the equilibrium between dissolution and crystallization. The solution can
be in both stable and metastable state. Metastable state would refer to supersaturation of the solution, as
described in section 1.2.1
With simpliﬁed words, the solubility is the maximum amount of solute dissolved in a solution at equilib-
rium. The solubility measurements of a mineral in solution, are valuable for predicting scale deposition.
1.2.5
Scenarios
According to Crabtree et al. (1999) there are four common scenarios, in which precipitation of scaling
occur. The ﬁrst scenario is Autoscaling, or self-scaling. This happens when the formation water of the
reservoir experiences change of temperature and/or pressure conditions during production. Precipitation
occurs when these changes makes the ﬂuid composition surpass the solubility limit of a mineral con-
stituent(Crabtree et al. (1999)).
The second scenario involves mixing of incompatible ﬂuids. Incompatible mixing ﬁnds place typically
when recovery methods are utilized in the well. Both secondary- and enhanced oil recovery(EOR) involves
injection of ﬂuid, for pressure support and waterﬂooding. The injection ﬂuid is usually seawater, which is
rich in anions. Water within the formation are rich in cations, dissolved from the formation. This mixed
ﬂuid has a new ion composition. An example is the calcium sulfate scaling which may precipitate in a
well. SO−2

1.3
Types of Scaling
According to Olajire (2015) the oilﬁeld scales may be classiﬁed into two groups, depending on their
sensitivity to pH. The "pH sensitive" group are scaling minerals were the pH of the brine have a strong
effect on their precipitation tendency. This group includes carbonates and sulﬁdes, and the prediction
of deposition is complex. The "pH independent" group includes scales where precipitation tendency are
little, or not affected of the pH(Olajire (2015)). This group contain scales such as sulfates.

1.6
Economical Impact
The formation of scale deposits are inducing the risk of economical losses, due to both decrease in
production and operational costs(I.E well workovers). According to Mackay (2008), the scaling treatments
introduces costs related to I.E rig hire(100-400 thousand $ a day), and installment of interval control
valves(ICV) (up to 1 million $). In addition to decreased production and downtime, occurrence of scaling
represents a major economical impact to the NPV of a ﬁeld.
There are several examples of oilﬁelds where scaling creates large problems regarding production. The
Daqing ﬁeld(China), experienced scale in 76.9 % of their wells(ﬂooded with ASP)(Jiecheng et al. (2011)).
In the Nort Sea, one of the most critical effects of scale deposition has been reported. A well within the
Miller ﬁeld, experienced total stop in production, over a period of only 24 hours. The restriction in ﬂow
represented a drop in production of 30000 bbl per day, to full stop(Gabra and Sulaiman (2014), Brown
(1998)). This shows how rapid scale may deposit, and the vast consequences.
Another example is scale occurence in the Veslefrikk ﬁeld in the North Sea(Norwegian sector). Both
CaCO3 and BaSO4 has been found. Even though the ion content of the formation ﬂuid is fairly low,
there have been encountered substantial scale deposition problems. The use of scale control measures in
this ﬁeld, has enhanced both production and lifetime. This is illustrated in Figure 1.15, where it is possible
to see the increased oil rate, after applying scale control(I.E re-perforation, squeeze and well stimulation).

1.7
Scaling management, prevention and removal
After establishing both the mechanisms of scale formation and related problems, this section is
devoted to explaining the measures of scale management. As discussed in the previous section, the savings
related to scaling control are vast. In order to maintain the production, and prevent losses due to scaling,
there are applied several methods. These methods are aiming for removing the scaling potential, prevent
nucleation and removal of scaling(where deposition has already occured). According to Crabtree et al.
(1999) methods for scaling removal has to be efﬁcient, quick and non destructive towards production
equipment and tubing. Even though this is rather self explanatory, the emphasis has to be on these factors,
due to substantial economical scopes in this industry.
1.7.1
Reducing scale potential
The scaling potential is dependent on change in temperature and pressure, in addition to ion concen-
tration. By limiting the presence of these factors, the total scaling potential will naturally decrease.
Well production strategies with embedded consideration of scaling precipitation, may lead to less scaling
problems. Meaning active control of well properties. With careful controlling of the production, sudden
changes in pressure can be avoided or minimized. The mixing of incompatible brines, can be controlled
by steering of chokes in water producing wells(Olajire (2015)).
The concentration of ions, may be lowered and therefore reduce the scaling potential. This is a costly
method, related to high capital expense(CAPEX). This due to building and installation of sulfate removal
plant(SRP). The plant is designed to remove sulfate ions, and proven effective of desulfating seawater.
This may be worth installing, when aging ﬁelds are entering extensive EOR and ﬂooding operations. As
an example of its efﬁciency, sulfate removal plants are reported to lower the concentration of sulfate ions
from 2700 ppm to below 100 ppm(Olajire (2015)).
Injection of other water types(besides ion rich seawater), would also lower the scaling potential. Produced
water and aquifer water have sulfate concentrations, which are considered very low or negligible(<20
mg/L)(Olajire (2015)).
1.7.2
Inhibition
Inhibition is a method often applied through squeeze treatments. Injection of chemical inhibitors, are
designed to prevent precipitation of scaling. This is done by reducing, or slowing down of the nucleation
and growth of scales. The injection is located near the wellbore of the production well, and thereby
subjected to the formation brine. Adsorption of inhibitor in the formation matrix, through squeeze,
prevents wash out of the inhibitor. The inhibitor are either hindering the formation of scaling crystals, or
stopping the crystal growth when absorbed into the crystal surface(Olajire (2015)).

Conventional scale inhibitors dissolve in water, hence have hydrophilic properties. These types are
according to Olajire (2015) inorganic phosphates, organophosphorous compounds and organic polymers.
The focus on preventing hazardous chemicals discharge has increased within the industry, limiting
inhibitor chemicals. The green scale inhibitors has to ﬁt the limitations set regarding toxidity, biodegrad-
ability and bioaccumulation. Some less toxic inhibitors are dibutylphosphorodithoic acid and carboxyhy
dromethylphosphonic acid.
Lastly the naphtenate inhibitors are for hindering of naphtenate deposition. Injection of acid(I.E acetic
acid) lowers the pH, hence inhibits the formation of naphthenate.
1.7.3
Scaling removal
The removal of scaling involves high emphasis on identifying the scaling type, and the magnitude
of the deposition. This is due to the varying properties, related to different types of scaling minerals.
Choosing the wrong method for a speciﬁc scaling removal job, may actually create more damage than
improvement. Less suitable removal treatments, may induce relapse of the scaling problem(Crabtree et al.
(1999)).
There are two main types of scale removal, divided into chemical and mechanical removal. These types
are described below.
1.7.3.1
Chemical methods
According to Olajire (2015) the chemical removal are considered the least expensive method in many
cases. These approaches are often utilized in locations which are difﬁcult to access, or mechanical methods
are considered less effective. The type of chemical reactant has to be wisely chosen, according to the
scaling type and location. An example is the use of hydrochloric acid for carbonate removal. Carbonate
scaling are very soluble in this acid, hence effective. This is known as acid stimulation. Acid stimulation
with hydrochloric acid are widely used, due to low cost and efﬁciency. The downside of the treatment
is the corrosive property of the acid, which may induce corrosive damage on tubings and equipment.
Corrosion inhibitors are often added to the corrosive solution, to lower the risk of corroding metal(Olajire
(2015)).
For other types of scaling, as sulfate scaling, hydrochloric acid are not effective. The sulfate scaling with
its low solubility, has to be treated with other means. An example of treatment for sulfate scaling, may be
chelating agents, which isolates and breaks up the ions from their structure(Crabtree et al. (1999)). The
ions are either lead to the surface through the production ﬂow, or driven into formation. These agents are
able to dissolve scaling more evenly, due to slower dissolution rate than I.E hydrochloric acid. Examples
of these agents are NTA and EDTA, nitrilotriacetic and ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid respectively. The
chelating agents are related to low corrosion rates, and the need for corrosion inhibitor additives are less
needed. However, the cost in relation to the use of chelating agents are relatively high. Chelating agents
are succesfully used in combination with acids as citric acid. This blend is proven to be effective on iron
and copper oxides (Olajire (2015)).

1.7.3.2
Mechanical methods
Mechanical removal of scaling are done by a set of different available tools. The down side with
mechanical removal is cost(I.E rig hire), and risk of damage to production tubing and equipment. In

Weigels data included measurements of both precipitated, and crystallized PbS mineral(Weigel (1907)).
The solubility of precipitated PbS, is higher than crystallized PbS. The solubility values are very low,
as illustrated in Figure 1.17. This study utilizes conductivity measurements as analytical method. The
method is not preferable, since the conductivity levels of these solutions are very low. The magnitude of
this conductivity, is likely to be lower than the conductivity of the used water. Weigels measurements are
therefore considered to be erroneous (Kolthoff (1931)). In addition, the measurement of conductance by
Weigel is likely to be of impurities within the samples (Kolthoff (1931)). The data may be measurements
of oxidized sulphide species, which could occur in the oxygen rich solution water. In a search for equili-
bration time, the only time span mentioned is 20-23 hours. This is described as rotation period, related to
the conductivity measurement. Whether the solution has been given sufﬁcient time to achieve equilibrium,
is uncertain. Kolthoff (1931) states that the calculation of solubility from electrical conductance done by
Weigel (1907), are wrongly executed. This due to wrong assumptions regarding complete hydrolysis of
the sulﬁde ion.
Nims and Bonner (1928) used a concentration cell method, for determination of solubility. They used
natural Galena, in which they removed surface oxidation products with ammonium acetate(in dilute
solution). Electromotive force(E.M.F) was measured, and equilibration times were not listed in the study.
When the E.M.F became constant, they assumed reaching of equilibrium state. The solubility were
calculated from the measured E.M.F values. The data of Nims and Bonner (1928) were slightly higher in
value than Weigel (1907). Nims and Bonner (1928) concluded that their solubility measurement, were
certain within a range of 30 . This statement did not have any following elaboration.
Hamann and Anderson (1978) experienced low reproducibility of measurements in their 1978 study. The
measurements of PbS were conducted in sulfur-rich NaCl solutions. They ran samples in two different
temperatures, hence 25°C and 90°C. The large deviance in measurements are due to several reasons. Fine
particles getting through their ﬁlter, was believed to be a large source of error. They used a series of ﬁlters,

2.2
Experimental method
Hefter and Tomkins (2003) describes several ways of differentiating experimental determination
of solubility. Firstly, the two main categories are direct and indirect methods. Where the former are
determining solubility from analysis(chemical), and the latter ﬁnds solubility through deduction of
solubility product. The direct methods are again divided into analytical and synthetic. Synthetic method
measures a characteristic property(of sample with known composition), while changing of an equilibrium
parameter. The equilibrium parameter may be I.E temperature or pressure. The varying characteristic
property is plotted with the modifying equilibrium parameter. The analytical method introduces the use of
chemical analysis, in order to determine solubility. There are several ways to determine solubility, and
its use are depending on several factors. The experiments conducted in this study, are utilizing a direct
and analytical method. The experimental procedure consists of preparation, ﬁltration and analysis of
sample solutions.
The before mentioned studies (section 1.8) had a series of weaknesses, in which this study has emphasized
to learn from. The experimental procedure is designed to maintain constant temperature throughout the
process. This is to reduce the risk of precipitation of solids, within the experimental equipment. In order
to prevent the chance of precipitation of solids before dilution, this study is introducing immediate dilution

2.3
Analytical method
The analytical part of this study includes the modern method of Inductively Coupled Plasma Optical
Emission Spectroscopy(ICP-OES from now on). This method is chosen in order to produce valid experi-
mental data, that can detect the low concentration levels in question. This is very important for the study,
which are aiming to avoid uncertainties that have been introduced in the past(discussed in section 1.8).
The method represents low detection limits for a large range of elements. The method is speciﬁc, which is
preferable to the methods described earlier.
2.3.1
ICP-OES
Inductively coupled plasma – Optical Emission spectroscopy have the ability to analyze liquid samples.
The samples are subjected to the core of inductively coupled argon plasma. The heat generated in the core
of the inductively coupled argon plasma may reach 8000 °C. The samples are nebulized in these high
temperatures. A spectrometer is collecting light, emitted from the thermally excited elements within the
sample solution. A diffraction grating resolves the surpassing light. The spectrum of wavelengths are
collected, ampliﬁed and measured by intensity. The intensity measurement are compared with standards,
hence converted to elemental concentrations(EAG (2017)). Figure 2.4 shows the liquid sample hitting the
argon plasma, and the emitted light.
Figure 2.4: Schematics of the ICP-OES(EAG (2017))
The method is capable of determining up to 70 elements dissolved in solution. The equipment used
in this study are an Avia 200 ICP-OES, from the fabricant Perkin Elmer. The equipment are utilizing
both radial and axial view of its optics, leading to a wide range and delivering low detection limits. The
detection limit for Pb and S are 1-10 PPb(10-100 µg/L), which is very low. This method are therefore
suitable for detecting the low solubilities expected of the exotic scale PbS.

Purpose of experiment
Determination of the Solubility of sparsely soluble salts at atmospheric pressure, and temperatures up

3.2
Summary of Method
The process consists of three steps before analysis of the samples is conducted. The preparation
consists of creating a solution with a known content of precipitated mineral. The samples are created in an
environment which is ﬂushed with Nitrogen(as well as bubbling of the solution water), in order to remove
Oxygen. This is to avoid oxidation, which may lead to invalid solubility measurements.
Equilibration of the samples are conducted in order for the solution and the mineral solid to reach
chemical equilibrium. This process is closely monitored regarding temperature and time. Filtration is
the last step of the experimental method, and is conducted in order to remove mineral particles. The
solution(which is immediately diluted) is then analysed, in order to ﬁnd how the mineral has dissolved
into the solution.

CHAPTER 4
Results and Discussion
The extensive experimental work conducted in this thesis, has lead to a rather large amount of new
solubility data. This chapter will enlighten the results, and its magnitude relative to earlier work.
The experimental data in this study measure 79 separate samples in total, which has produced 233 valid
data points. The experiments are conducted in different temperatures and equilibrating times. It has
been emphasized to avoid sources of error, as much as possible. Other studies showed several types of
anomalies, regarding experimental methods(section 1.8). Every anomaly that has occurred during this
study, has been thoroughly noted. Examples are equipment failure and temperature differences.
The different temperature stages chosen in the study are 25°C, 40°C, 60°C and 80°C. All of the runs
has been set to 1, 3, 7 and 11 days of equilibrating time. The different conditions has been tested four
separate times(or more), to ensure reproducibility. Additionally, prolonged equilibrating times(14 days)
were conducted for the temperature extremities(25°C and 80°C). Introduction of NaCl in the solution
was tested to some extent. This was executed in order to compare the new method against the previous
mentioned work(section 1.8).

erroneous.

4.3
Limiting elements
Through the work conducted on this experimental study, there have been some limiting elements.
These elements are found through thorough literature study, and experiences in the laboratory activities.
H2S partial pressure is the ﬁrst limiting element. The presence of H2S were detected in early stages of
laboratory work, conducted at DTU. The adding of silicone oil in the samples, hindered the evaporation
of H2S. However, any means of measuring H2S partial pressure has not been possible. This due to
limitations in laboratory facilities. The presence and magnitude of H2S partial pressure, may have a large
impact on the solubility.
Equilibration time has been discussed signiﬁcantly in this thesis work. The introduction of varies run
times, were introduced to detect the reaching of equilibrium solubility. However, for three of the run
temperatures(25 °C, 40 °C and 80 °C) the set run time proved insufﬁcient. Due to limiting time, this
aspect of the study were not possible to prolong.
Colloids were a concern early in the study. This due to the large presence of ﬁltration problems in
the available work. The presence of colloids, may cause problems during ﬁltration. These particles
may surpass the ﬁlter, causing erroneous measurements. In order to test this, Dynamic Light Scattering
Analysis were suggested. Arrangement of this analysis, were not possible. Without this analysis, the
presence of colloids could neither be conﬁrmed nor disproved.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion and future work
The solubility of lead(||)sulﬁde in aqueous solution, has been decided experimentally. The solubility
of "exotic" scales are investigated, due to the increasing problem these insoluble salts pose to the oil and
gas industry. This challenge is growing, due to the frequent development of HPHT wells. The thesis is a
part of the work conducted at CERE, providing experimental solubility data. This data are much needed
for further improvement of scaling prediction.
The work has been focused on revealing the effect of temperature and time, on the solubility of PbS
in aqueous solution. The goal was to create as much data as possible, in the given time period. With
emphasis on excluding the mistakes and uncertainties, found in the available literature.
As experimental work is done, several discoveries are made along the way. This resulted in additional
tests, beyond the scheduled program. This including the effect of salinity on solubility of PbS, and pH
values. The discoveries are concluded as follows:
• The solubility of the saturated aqueous solution of PbS, is found to be dependant on temperature.
The trend shows increasing concentrations of both Pb and total S ions with increasing temperature.
• The equilibration times are different, for the tested run temperatures. Only the 60 °C run series,
tend to reach the equilibrium solubility within the limiting time aspect.
• The introduction of NaCl into the sample solution, gives indications of contradictory trends with
temperature. The sparse data acquired, are not considered sufﬁciently in deciding a deﬁnite trend.
However, the results obtained indicate the effect of "salting out" at 80 °C and "salting in" at 25 °C.
• ICP-OES has proven to be an analytical technique, providing accurate concentration measure-
ments(error 1.4 % -3.5 %). Available studies, describes the use of unspeciﬁc analytical methods.
The use of an analytical method, capable of measuring a series of elements at low concentrations,
pose advantages. This is further substantiated by the acquired results in this study. The deviance in
measured concentration of Pb and total S, has been a good example.
• The deviation in measured concentration of Pb and total S, supposes the presence of another com-
pound. Through ﬁtting of parameters in the Extended UNIQUAC model(Thomsen and Rasmussen
(1999)), the presence of Pb(OH)2 is suspected.

• pH measurements evidence of stabilized pH values within the samples. Additionally, there is
measured an increase in pH, in the samples containing NaCl. Reported increase in pH when
forming of PbOHCl and 3Pb(OH)2 · PbCl2(Toptygina et al. (1984)), implies the possibility of

These conclusions lead to several recommendations for future work. The additional tests described,
are a good base for further full scale experimental programs. Establishing sufﬁcient data to foresee the
effect of both salinity and pH on solubility, would be advantageously. The limiting aspects described in
Section 4.3 are also recommended for further improvement of experimental work.
Additionally, further work should be conducted on adjusting the Extended UNIQUAC model. This to
include the ability to predict PbS scaling occurrence. This model were just brieﬂy introduced in this
thesis, and not a main objective. The model has proven capable of predicting a numerous group of scaling
minerals, hence a valuable asset.
The standard operating procedure provided in this thesis, is addressing continuous work on solubility
measurements. Ensure that the SOP are updated as the development moves forward, and make use of the
experiences discovered in this study.
