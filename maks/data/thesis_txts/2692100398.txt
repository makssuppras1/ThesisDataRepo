Abstract
This master’s thesis examines the factors that influence individuals’ preferences towards alternative
fuel vehicles and whether an individual’s location has an impact on preferences. This project is based
on the need to increase the share of alternative fuel vehicles. As we live in a world where the increasing
emissions that conventional cars put into the atmosphere and the high correlation between greenhouse
gas emissions and health problems have created the need for further research. This project will explore
the factors that influence ICV, BEV and PHEV preferences. This is done based on a stated choice
experiment conducted by DTU professors with 2961 individuals. Each participant responded to 8
different constructed scenarios on which the estimation of this project will be based. The estimation
will be based on a panel mixed logit model from which the values of the estimated parameters will
be derived. The main results show that cost attributes have a significant negative effect on vehicle
preference. Similar effects are shown for the acceleration of a vehicle, while individuals seem to prefer
cars with larger boot size cars. Regarding the categories of charging infrastructure, it seems that
its wide presence and availability would act as a driving force for respondents to choose alternative
fuel vehicles. In terms of socio-demographic factors, higher education level plays an important role in
BEV and PHEV preference, but gender is not considered as one of the most important factors. One
of the main conclusions that emerge from this research is that regional differences were not identified
as significant factors influencing preferences for alternative fuel vehicles.

Introduction
1.1
Scope and Goal
The purpose of this master thesis is to build a discrete choice model based on stated preferences and
to identify the factors that affect individual preferences for battery electric vehicles or plug-in hybrids
and whether there are differences between the five unique regions that Denmark consists of.
When fossil fuels such as gasoline and diesel are burned, carbon dioxide, a greenhouse gas, is released
into the atmosphere.1According to the European Commission, cars are responsible for about 12%
of total EU emissions of carbon dioxide (CO2), the main greenhouse gas. As stated by Haustein
& Jensen [2018] there is a significant link between emissions and health problems. In addition, as part
of the EU Climate and Energy Framework 2030 and the current contribution to Paris Agreement,
the EU has introduced legislation to reduce emissions by at least 40% by 2030.2 To achieve this
goal, many countries are promoting alternative fuel vehicles. Battery Electric Vehicles (BEVs) and
plug-in Hybrid Vehicles (PHEVs) can help reduce greenhouse gas emissions and local pollution by
replacing transportation that would otherwise be provided by diesel or gasoline vehicles (Figenbaum
& Kolbenstvedt [2016]). The Danish parliament has agreed to have 775000 electric or hybrid cars
by 2030 to achieve a 70% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions.3
The three different fuel technologies to be mentioned in this project are the Internal Combustion engine
Vehicles (ICV’s), the Battery Electric Vehicles (BEV’s) and the Plugin Hybrid Vehicles (PHEV’s).
Internal Combustion engine Vehicles are named for their use of an internal combustion engine.
In a basic sense, an internal combustion engine converts the chemical energy available in the fuel
into thermal energy through combustion, and by expanding the working fluid, it converts the thermal
energy into mechanical work with the crank slider mechanism, converting linear force/work into
rotating torque/work on the output shaft (Naber & Johnson [2014]).
Battery-electric vehicles use a battery to store electricity, an electric motor to provide acceleration
and braking torque, and a motor controller to manage torque from moment to moment. Compared
to conventional vehicles, BEVs have limited range and long recharging times (Reuyl [2000]). They
have advantages in that they are highly efficient, produce no tailpipe emissions, and thus can improve

Plug-in Hybrid-electric vehicles typically have two or more sources of energy and/or power.
They use fuel (e.g., gasoline, diesel) to provide greater range and convenient refueling. They combine
an internal combustion engine with an electric motor and electrical energy storage (e.g., battery, ultra

Although there are many advantages for alternative fuel cars, there are still some disadvantages
compared to internal combustion engine vehicles that act as barriers to their higher market penetration.
Firstly, one of the problems is that the purchase price is relatively higher than the internal combustion
cars, and secondly, that the limited range can create problem on long distance trips (Christensen et
al. [2010]). Bansal et al. [2021] highlights the chicken-and-egg problem for the supply-demand balance
for the alternative fuel vehicle market. That is, while investments in increasing charging infrastructure
and electric vehicle range depend on BEV and PHEV demand, customer preferences for alternative
fuel vehicles are highly influenced by the absence of the two specific factors mentioned above.
The following report is based on data obtained from a previously conducted research project called
ELISA. As explained on Jensen et al. [2020], the ELISA project was founded by four DTU professors
(Danish Technical University) who specialize in the department of Technology Management and
Economics. The experiment involves choosing between three different vehicle technologies (ICV, BEV,
PHEV) and six different vehicle segments (Mini, Small, Medium, Large, Premium, Luxury/ Sport).
1.2
Research Question
The aim of this project is to answer the research question of how individuals are influenced in their
preferences for alternative fuel vehicles and whether location is an important aspect in their choice.
The following research questions will focus on answering the above problem statement. Concretely,
it will be examined:
• What factors affect the market shares for electric vehicles?
• How do preferences for electric vehicles differ across individuals?

1.3
Research Structure
The paper begins with an introduction to the topic and a statement of the research question. Then,
a literature review is presented that serves as the basis for this project and mentions the previous
modeling approaches taken by researchers as well as the results of previous studies. Previous literature
indicates that young men are more likely to choose an alternative fuel vehicle and that high income and
environmental sensitivity are two other reasons for choosing an electric car. In addition, several studies
point to the importance of expanded charging infrastructure to support electric car adoption, as this
would be a response to the "range anxiety" that many drivers experience. One limitation is in relation
to spatial effects, as the studies to date do not have a clear answer as to how population density affects
preferences or whether there are regional differences. The subsequent methodology chapter presents the
methods and tools used to answer the research question. In the fourth chapter, the description of the

In addition, the second part of the chapter presents the model construction and the progress towards
achieving the final utility function. The fifth chapter presents the estimation results and the predicted
market shares, willingness to pay for the attributes and elasticities. The final chapter is the conclusion
of this project, which summarizes the overall goal of the project and reflects on the key observations. It
also identifies the limitations of the project and provides further recommendations for future research.

Literature Review
The subject of this research is closely related to an issue that has arisen in recent years from the
gradual increase in the need for more ecological alternatives in our lives. There are several researches
that deal with the analysis of alternative fuel vehicle preferences, and this chapter will serve as a
foundation and link that connects the already conducted literature with this project. Existing research
on discrete choice modelling for alternative fuel vehicles is reviewed and potential research gaps in
the research field are explored. The following part consists of the modelling approaches based on
the existing literature and other researchers’ findings from studies on alternative fuel preferences.
2.1
Modelling Approaches
In most studies, the data-set used comes from the data of stated preferences (SP). This type of
data-set is used due to the fact that the market penetration of BEV or PHEV is still low (Liao et al.
[2017],Bansal et al. [2021]). This may suggest the need for further investigation based on the fact that
many of the participants in these studies have no prior experience with alternative fuel cars. (Rezvani et
al. [2015],Smith et al. [2017]). Jreige et al. [2021] also supports that people’s preferences may fluctuate
after electric vehicles become widespread in society. The method used by many researchers was first the
multinomial logit (Musti & Kockelman [2011],Orlov & Kallbekken [2019],Zhuge & Shao [2019],)which
assumes independence of irrelevant alternatives, which will be analyzed later in the chapter 3.2.1. In
reality, preferences vary from individual to individual, so later studies often used the mixed logit model
for discrete choice modelling to account for this variance (Liao et al. [2017],Langbroek et al. [2016]).
2.2
Previous Studies on Alternative Fuel Types
Existing literature has examined various attributes that may influence people’s preferences for vehicle
fuel type. As it is mentioned on Haustein & Jensen [2018] and Jia & Chen [2021], one specific attribute
is the education of the user, which highlights that highly educated individuals have a higher preference
for alternative fuel vehicles than others. By examining previous research, it can also be seen that
people who choose BEVs or PHEVs are more likely to be male, have a higher education, and earn a
high salary. Brückmann et al. [2021] found that older people over 65 are less likely to adopt a battery
electric vehicle. In addition Figenbaum & Kolbenstvedt [2016] on a survey identified that people who
own battery electric vehicles are young (typically 35-54 years old) with an average age of 47, while

The previous characteristic goes hand in hand with the "range anxiety" experienced by people who
drive battery electric vehicles. "Range anxiety" is the fear of being stuck with an uncharged battery

are affected by battery range and fast charging infrastructure, while ICV owners are mainly affected
by monetary attributes. Carley et al. [2016] suggests that "range anxiety" can be reduced by installing
new charging stations and making them more visible to users.

3
Methodology
This chapter presents and explains the methods used to carry out the project. First, the method of
collecting data through a stated preference experience is presented and the advantages or disadvantages
of using this method are explained. Then, the theory of discrete choice modelling is introduced and
the two methods that will be used in this project are presented. In addition, the theory of willingness
to pay and the theory of elasticities will be presented.
3.1
Stated Preference Method
In order to complete this project, it was necessary to collect people’s preferences. There are two ways
to do this, with revealed preference or stated preference. In this project, stated preference is used.
Stated preference data is data that is collected in a survey where the people participating have to
choose different hypothetical scenarios (Train [2009]). In this case, a person participating in the Eliza
project must give 8 different answers to 8 different scenarios between different car types and segments.
One of the main advantages of the stated-preference method is the variability that a researcher can
include in the experiment. This means that a researcher can create a variety of variables or scenarios
to achieve their goal. Also, a variety of responses can be obtained from the same person based on
different experiments.
One of the limitations of the stated preference method is that the stated choice that individuals
make may differ from their actual choice. One reason for this could be that while filling out the
questionnaire, people are not completely sincere about how they would act in a real situation, or
they do not think about it thoroughly. This could lead to invalid results that are different from the
researcher’s expectations.
3.2
Discrete Choice Modelling
People make a variety of decisions throughout their lives. To understand these decision-making
processes, researchers have developed various frameworks called choice models. These frameworks
assume that the choice environment and the decision maker provide a mathematical representation
to study choice behavior (Iraganaboina et al. [2021]).
In this case, to estimate the preferences of individuals regarding the type of car technology or different
car segments, discrete choice modelling is used. To delineate the general framework for discrete choice

models, the four general assumptions for these models are presented below, based on Ben-Akiva &
Bierlaire [1999]:
1. The decision maker, that makes the choice between the various alternatives. In this project,
the decision makers are the people who participated in the survey.
2. The alternatives, which determine the available options for the decision maker, called the choice
set. Based on Train [2009] the choice set needs to obey to three characteristics:
• The alternatives must be mutually exclusive.
• All possible alternatives must be included in the choice set. This means that the choice set
must be exhaustive.
• The number of alternatives must be finite.
In this case, the choice set is formed by 18 different combinations of vehicle types and vehicle
segments. A combination of three vehicle types and six vehicle segments.
3. The attributes, which represent the advantages or disadvantages of a particular alternative and
affect the decision maker’s choice. In this project, the attributes consist of the car attributes that
are provided to the participants.
4. The last assumption is the decision rule. This describes the process of the decision maker to
choose one of the alternatives. In this case, the decision rule will be based on random utility theory,
which is a value that represents the decision maker’s preference for a particular alternative. The
choice will be made according to the highest utility in the entire choice set.

4.1
Stated Preference Survey Description
The stated preference data-set includes 23688 answers from 2961 Danish citizens. The answers
were collected with the help of a survey through the Danish official digital mailbox. All the details
about the survey are provided by the initial analysis conducted from professors of Danish Technical
University’s management department. As Jensen et al. [2020] described on the corresponding research,
the survey consists of four different segments:
• Intro Questions
• Stated Choice Experiment
• Background Characteristics and Car Usage
• Attitudinal Statements
On the first part of the questionnaire some basic information of the user are collected. Specifically
the text appears to the respondent is the following.
‘In this part of the survey, we ask for information about your occupation and cars that you have access
to in your household. Besides cars that you may own, this can also be a company car, a leased car,
or a car that you often borrow from somebody else.’
The second part of the questionnaire contains the stated choice experiment. Two scenarios are created
to accommodate differences on the accommodation of the respondents, whether they are living on a
house or a flat. This would possibly affect the charging possibilities on the users so for the individuals
living on a flat two new variables included which are:
• Distance to home charging [m] for PHEV and BEV
• Home charging availability for PHEV and BEV
On the variable Home charging availability the user answers on the possibility that the nearest to
home public charger is available when needed. The answers vary from 1-4 which is corresponding
to 25%, 50%, 75%, 100% of the nearest public charger availability respectively.
Two distinct categories created, one for those who are able to charge at their residence and one for
those who are not. Also, two identical designs created, the first of which dealt with the choice between
2 ICVs and 2 BEVs, while the second dealt with the choice between 2 ICVs, 2 BEVs, and 2 PHEVs.
An answer must be given for 4 cases of the first design and 4 cases of the second design, for a total of 8

stated choices. Through these 8 choices of the same individual, different scenarios were presented with
a variety of vehicle types and vehicle segments with different characteristics. The vehicle attributes
that were provided to the survey are presented below as they were presented on Jensen et al. [2020]:
• Purchase price: The purchase price of the vehicle on DKK.
• Yearly Cost: A yearly cost according to the expenses of annual taxation and insurances. Presented
on DKK per year.
• Operation cost: The cost of operation of the vehicle presented on DKK per km.
• Range: The driving range of the vehicle with fully charged battery. Presented only for BEV and
PHEV on km.
• Acceleration: The acceleration time from 0 to 100 km and it is presented on seconds.
• Boot Size: It presents the vehicle boot size according to four categories.
• Carbon Emissions: It presents the CO2 emissions on gramms per km.
• Distance to Home Charging: Presents the distance to the nearest public chargers from home in

• Home Charging Availability: Indicates the probability that a nearest public charger is available.

• Distance Between Fast Chargers: Presents the average distance between public chargers on meters,
only for BEV vehicles.
• Charging Speed: Indicates the charging speed of public chargers. It is presented as the km that
a BEV vehicle can travel with 10 minutes of charging.
After the stated preference the background characteristics are collected. That includes the personal
socio-demographic characteristics of the participants and also information about their household and
its members. Finally the last part of the survey collects information based on attitudinal statements.

4.5
Additional Variables
After testing the panel mixed logit model, in which only the car attributes included, other variables are
added according to the existing literature. Since one of the main questions in this project is to identify
the differences at the regional level for the country of Denmark and to determine whether the location of
individuals affects their preferences for BEVs and PHEVs, the geographical characteristics were included
in the model. More specifically, the population density for each municipality is included in the model and
divided into three categories of low, medium and high density areas. The category of medium density ar-
eas was set as the reference. In addition, two variables were modified and divided among the five regions.
The two modified variables are range for BEVs and PHEVs and CO2 emissions. These new variables will
provide the differences to the five regions of Denmark with respect to these variables. This is done by
setting a Danish region as a reference for the two parameters, range and CO2 emissions and estimating
with the rest of the region to see if there are any significant differences. Thus, these variables will answer
the question how important the range of BEV or PHEV is for the 5 Danish regions in general and what
differences there are between them on the same topic. The same applies also for the CO2 variable.
One of the sociodemographic characteristics of the users that is inserted in the model is the age of
the user. Three segments created according to the age group that each respondent indicated, so the
categories are:
• Young: From 18 to 40 years old.
• Middle: Above 40 years old and until 60 years old.
• Old: Above 60 years old.
The age characteristic is used in the estimation as a characteristic of an individual with only two coeffi-
cients for each age category as the old category was used as the reference. It must be noted though, that
by inserting to the already existing model the age characteristics were insignificant and were not used.
The next sociodemographic characteristic of the users was the gender of each individual. The same
procedure is followed as for the age characteristic, but only two categories are formed for males and
females. Since males are used as reference, only two coefficients are included in the model, those

for females for BEV and PHEV. The effect of the characteristic is significant for the estimation and
is therefore included in the final model.

fuel vehicles according to the literature. So by following the same procedure, four income categories
were created for the income category that each respondent stated.
• Low Income: Individuals on the first four income categories. (0 kr. to 399.000 kr.)
• Middle Income: Individuals between income categories 5 to 8. (400.000 kr. to 799.000 kr.)
• High Income: Individuals on income categories 9 to 11. (800.000 kr. to 1 Million kr. or more)
• N/A Income: Individuals on income category 12. (Did not provide income information)
The income category set as reference is the low income category and by inserting the the income
categories on the estimation the result is insignificant and they are not used further.
In addition to the sociodemographic characteristics examined, the education of the user is also inserted
in the estimation. Six categories are created according to the highest completed education of each
respondent. The categories can be seen below:
• Folkeskolen
• Gymnasieuddannelse
• Erhvervsfaglig
• Videregående Uddannelse 1
• Videregående Uddannelse 2
• Higher Education
Initially folkeskolen was set as a reference for the education categories but the results for the estimation
showed that the contribution of the educational categories were insignificant. So different structure
of the categories were used and finally the one that was used in the model is that only the higher
education is included in the model while the rest categories are used as a reference. In that way the
difference between individuals with completed higher education compared to the rest will be shown
with respect to BEV and PHEV preference to further validate the literature outcome that highly
educated people have higher preference on BEV or PHEV. The contribution of the characteristic
in the estimation was significant so the last structure is used in the final utility specification.

Alternative Specific Constants
The alternative specific constants are used to cover the unobserved effect of utility. All of them
are significant at a 1% level, except for the alternative specific constant related to PHEV vehicle
type, which is significant at a 10% level. From the estimation in Table 12, it can be seen that both
alternative specific constants for BEV and PHEV have negative signs. This means that the average
unobserved utility for the vehicle types has a negative effect on the preferences for alternative fuel
vehicles compared to the reference, which is ICV. As for the vehicle segments, the reference is the
middle segment and it can be seen that all other ASCs for the vehicle segments have a negative sign
on their coefficient. Moreover, the heterogeneity in the different vehicle types and vehicle segments
expressed by the S values seems to be very significant for all vehicle types and segments except the
one for large vehicles. Being significant means that there is significant heterogeneity among the choices
of the individuals who participated in the experiment.

The purchase price of the vehicle is significant at a 1% level for the estimation. It has a negative
sign, as expected, because individuals prefer to spend less money when buying their vehicle. Note that
the result is scaled by dividing by 100000, so the value for buying for a unit of 1/DKK is -0.00000669.
Similarly, the yearly cost of a vehicle is significant at a 1% level for estimation, but less significant than

analogous. As expected, the value of the coefficient is negative, since higher operation cost would lower

Conclusion
There is a strong need to increase the market share of alternative fuel vehicles in cities. This need
stems from the correlation between greenhouse gas emissions and health problems and the fact that
car use is responsible for a large proportion of these emissions. Based on the agreement that the
Danish Parliament has made regarding the reduction of greenhouse gas emissions, this project is
considered to be of high relevance to support this decision. This is done by identifying the factors
that influence the preferences for BEVs and PHEVs, the variance of preferences across individuals,
but also across different areas throughout Denmark. This project sought to answer the question of
how individuals are influenced by their preferences for alternative fuel vehicles and whether location
is an important consideration in their choice. This was addressed with an estimation from a panel
mixed logit model based on a stated preference experiment. By conducting the estimation, the factors
that influence the preferences of BEVs and PHEVs are identified as the reference of the estimation
was the ICV. The identified factors were divided into 5 categories which are:
• Cost Attributes
• Car Attributes
• Battery Characteristic and Charging Infrastructure
• Sociodemographic Characteristics
• Geographical Characteristics
More analytically, cost attributes clearly appear to have a negative influence on vehicle choice, with
purchase cost being the most significant. Operation cost and yearly cost are not as important as
purchase cost but they are still parameters of high importance for people selecting a vehicle. Among
the vehicle characteristics, the parameter describing the acceleration of the vehicle has a negative effect
on vehicle preference and it is found to be significant for estimation. For the boot size of a vehicle,
it can be inferred from the estimation that vehicles with larger boot sizes consisting of large and very
large boot sizes tend to be more preferred than smaller boot sizes. The next category of parameters
used is charging infrastructure. For this category, the effect that charging infrastructure has on BEV
preferences was not found to be significant, in contrast to the literature review. The parameter "
distance of charger from home" was found to be significant and as expected, the parameter is negative,
which means that the preference for BEV and PHEV decreases when a charger is far from their place of
48

Danish Technical University
residence. The charging speed concerning BEV and availability of charger for BEV and PHEV were also
found to be significant with positive signs for both. The next category analyzed is the socio-demographic
characteristics which is the gender and education of the user. Through the estimation, the parameters
for females are calculated according to BEV and PHEV but the differences between males and females
in vehicle preference are not significant. Education preference for BEV and PHEV was included in the
model with only two parameters, higher education for BEV and PHEV, as the other education categories
were used as reference. The estimation showed that higher education has a significant and positive
effect on preference for alternative fuel vehicles compared to individuals with other education status.
Overall, the study provides additional results on the factors of preference for alternative fuel vehicles.
In addition to the information that exists in the existing literature on what influences car users’
preferences, this report provides information at the regional level for two of the parameters. Based
on the estimation for CO2 emissions, it can be seen that these can generally have a negative effect on
vehicle preferences. When it comes to the regional effect of the parameter, the regions of Midtjylland
and Nordjylland initially had a different effect than the other regions, namely that these two regions
had significant parameters with a negative sign. But by the final estimation, they became insignificant
when more socio-demographic characteristics were included in the estimation. This proved that
the regional differences captured not only spatial differences but also demographic characteristics.
On the other hand, it seems that there are no significant differences between regions in terms of
the range effect on BEV and PHEV preferences, except for two regions. The regions Midtjylland
and Nordjylland seem to have a positive effect on PHEV compared to the reference for the range
parameters. Similarly, the estimation of the final model reveals that the regional parameters also
include information from socio-demographic categories.
Additionally, the influence of population density on BEV and PHEV preferences was included in
the model for the country of Denmark. Although population density has been analyzed by researchers
before, as can be seen in the literature review, the results were not significant and there is no
previous research that has been done specifically for Denmark. Although in this project the "high
population density" parameter was not significant, the "low population density" is significant and
provides information that areas with low population density have less preference for alternative fuel
vehicles than other areas.

Willingness to pay for the estimated parameters is also calculated and the amount an individual
would be willing to pay for each specific unit change on each parameter in terms of purchase price is
measured. Differences in WTP across regions and socio-demographic characteristics are also measured.
Most of the results have plausible signs, except for the differences between income categories on CO2
emissions, which require further investigation.
This project has a number of limitations. First, all results are dependent on a dataset obtained through
a stated preference experiment. By using a stated preference experiment, some problems may arise.
One of them is the validity of the participants’ responses. It could be that the participants are either not
completely honest with their choice or that their statement does not represent a real choice. In addition,
survey participants may not have experience with alternative fuel vehicles, so their responses may be
insufficient because they may not perceive the importance of battery range or charging specifications.
To avoid these limitations, a project with combined stated and revealed preferences is proposed instead.
Another limitation would be the lack of data on charging infrastructure as a function of respondent
location. Stated preference data shows how an individual’s choice would turn out if the charging
infrastructure at the respondent’s location is in a certain state. It would be interesting to investigate
how the effectiveness of charging parameters on BEV and PHEV preferences would change if the
respondent had to provide charging data as an input to the survey. Another suggestion would be
to have an external database of public charging stations, including their location and specification,
to examine how the density of charging stations affects the preferences of individuals in the region.
Based on the above limitations, further research could be conducted to identify the factors influencing
BEV and PHEV preferences and regional differences based on revealed preference data. An additional
project could also be based on the effect that urban, rural, or suburban areas have on preferences,
as this project was based on population density only and the result could be different.
