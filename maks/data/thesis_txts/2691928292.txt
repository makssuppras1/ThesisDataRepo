Introduction
3.1
Project Descriptions
Ovarian follicles guide female reproduction and health. The follicles release eggs for potential fertilization
and hormone production guides the menstrual cycle and impacting female health. Follicle growth and
hormone secretion is regulated tightly in vivo in a speciﬁc order. Inter-follicular communication ensures
only one follicle at a time grows, secretes hormones, and releases oocytes. Follicles are limited in number
and females have a max number before birth. When follicles are exhausted in menopause or removed
in surgery, the loss of hormone production leads to osteoporosis, cardiovascular disease, obesity, and
cognitive decline. Menopause happens at approximately the same age for all women. With female life
expectancy increasing [1], it increases the period of hormone loss, inﬂuencing signiﬁcantly female life. The
only option for treatment is hormone replacement therapy (HRT), which do not accurately replicate the
female physiology. The therapy must be individually designed around the female patient with speciﬁc
product, dose, and dosing time. Even with individually designed therapy, the results are inconsistent
with varying eﬀect.
Transplanted ovarian tissue theoretically can restore hormone production and create a physiological HRT.
However, allotransplantation of donated or stem-derived tissue creates a rapid immune response, clearing
the tissue rapidly and violently. In addition, negative The immune response can be reduce or eliminated
if the tissue is enclosed by an immune-restrictive housing permeable to nutrition and hormones, but
impermeable to host immune cells. This therapy has the potential to provide women a better quality of
life.
3D printing has greatly evolved over the last twenty years, providing new, state-of-the-art approaches in
the ﬁeld of tissue engineering. The reduction of 3D printer cost and size increases the usability, repeatably,
and functionality. However, each technology has variation in bioprinting methodology, software, and
hardware. This project aims to merge technology and biology to establish a 3D printing technique for
generating follicle-laden mimics of the native ovary. Speciﬁcally, we will develop a print procedure for
gelatin-based bio-inks for embedding follicles at high densities in porous 3D dimensional constructs. The
speciﬁc aims are:
1. Establish a general 3D printing protocol for hydrogel extrusion on a low cost desktop bioprinter
2. Formulate and optimize gelatin-based bioinks
3. Investigate follicle survival and maturation in a 3D bio-printed construct
As a basis for the work, a discussion of follicle biology combined with technological and biological 3D
printing requirements. Further, essential background will be conducted on follicle biology, gelatin chem-
istry, DIW 3D printing principles, G-Code and rheological method.

4
Background
4.1
Biology
The ovary has two main functions: development of oocyte for fertilization and regulation of reproductive
and non reproductive health through sex steroid hormone production and secretion. The ovarian follicle
controls these functions, composed of an oocyte and supporting somatic cells (granulosa and theca).
Folliculogenesis is a process through multiple irreversible stages where a follicle will transition from
an immature inactive follicles to released ovum for fertilization.
These follicles go through continual
recruitment for slow growth (initial) and cyclic recruitment to choose the follicle for release of the ovum
[2]. In the female reproductive lifespan, less than 1% of follicles ovulate for fertilization, the rest proceed
through a controlled cell death called atresia [3].
4.2
Folliculogenesis
The follicular phase of the menstrual cycle provide follicle recruitment, growth and release from the
ovary. Folliculogenesis begins in fetal development as primordial germs cells peaking at the 20th week
of gestation [3]. Females are born with a ﬁnite reserve of primordial follicles called the ovarian reserve.
Apoptosis of an estimated 90% of ovarian reserve occurs during infancy and early adulthood [4]. These
follicles have a continuous initial recruitment, transitioning from inactive primordial follicles to primary
follicles. The initial recruitment begins in utereo and ends in the later stage of menopause. Factors
than inhibit initial recruitment include Anti-Mullerian hormone (AMH) and Forkhead box L2 (FOXL2).
Paracrine factor AMH secrets from maturing follicles and limit recruit and formation of primary follicles.
Transcription factor FOXL2 expressed in granulosa cells thought to mediate transition of squamous
to cuboidal granulosa cells.
Granulosa cell mitosis transition the follicle from single layer (primary)
to multiple layers that can produce steroids (preantral). The preantral follicle then forms a basement
membrane and theca cells with vascularization to access nutrients and hormones in the bloodstream. A
small number of antral follicles develop into preovulatory follicles through cyclic recruitment occurring
every menstrual cycle [2]. The remaining antral follicles proceed through atresia. The development of a
single dominant follicle from resting follicles during a menstrual cycles occurs in six stages [5] [6] [7] with
Figure 4.1 showing the physiology and average size of each stage:
1. Resting/Primordial stage.
A large reserve of inactive primordial follicle with one layer of non-
dividiving squamous pregranulosa cells surrounded by basil lamina.
2. Primary stage. The follicle is activated, transitioning from squamous pregranulosa to a single layer
cuboid granulosa cells.
3. Secondary stage. The cuboid granulosa proliferate (mitosis) into multiple layers with basil lam-
ina expansion. Additionally, precursor theca cells are recruited arguably from the stromal layer
surrounding the granulosa cells and oocyte.

4.2
Folliculogenesis
4. Antral Stage. Fluid ﬁlled cavity (antrum) formation begins. Follicles form through granulosa cell
secretions and thecal vascularization ﬂuid. The ﬂuid ﬁlled cavity (antrum) forms in the granulosa
cell area of the follicle. At this stage the follicle development becomes gonadotropin-dependent with
miotic division and polar bodies creation.
5. Preovulatory Stage. Characterized by a large antrum and oocyte capabile of ovulation. Contains
two types of granulosa cells: cumulus and mural. FSH matures the preovulatory follicle and LH
communicate ovulation.
6. Ovulation. The ovum is released, basil lamina degraded, and remaining granulosa cells diﬀerentiate
into luteal cells.
Figure 4.1: Folliculogenesis classiﬁcation and average size of follicle [8]
These phases are guided guided by hormones described in Section 4.2.1 in two distinct stages:
gonadotropin-independent and gonadotropin-dependent. The early stage is independent of gonatropins
and dependent of on intrafollicular oocyte-somatic cell signalling. Functional gonadotropin recepters have
not developed, so endocrine, paracrine and autocrine factors inﬂuence the growth and development of the
oocyte and the granulosa cell and theca cell layers [2] [9]. The interfollicular crosstalk is thought to be
dependent on the 3D morphology and cell positioning. As folliculogenesis progresses, the follicle expands
and creates distinct phenotype characteristics [9].
4.2.1
Follicle Hormonal Regulation
One of the major functions of the ovary is to secrete and regulate hormones in the hypothalmic-pituitary-
ovarian (HPO) axis (Figure 4.2a). The menstrual cycle and the follicular phase has a complex hormonal
release through the HPO axis that varies through the cycles to guide the development of the follicle
for impregnation. The main hormones involved in folliculogenesis include: hypothalamic gonadotropins
releasing hormone (GnRH), two pituitary hormones: follicle-stimulating (FSH) and luteinizing (LH), and
the steroid hormone estradiol-17-β (estradiol).
GnRH is the main hormone guiding reproductive function. The hypothalamic GnRH neuronal network
pulsatile secretes GnRH into the hypothalamo-pituitary portal circulation. These pulsations travel to

The G-Code programming language is the primary coding language in computer aided manufacturing

material being used for each individual print. The G-Code language allows a ﬂexible code with the ability
to be used for a variety of materials, 3D architectures, and technologies.

Shear Thinning
Shear thinning is a type of non-newtonian behavior where viscosity decreases under shear strain. This is
caused through polymer chain reorganization (Figure 4.7a). This reorganization is a signiﬁcant beneﬁt
for bioprinting. This reorganization improves resolution and reduces cell damage by allowing a smoother
extrusion through the nozzle [24] [25] (Figure 4.7b). The bioink must overcome yield stress to allow
begin the ﬂow from nozzle to the build platform (4.7. The ﬂow begins by shear forces breaking fragile,
physical crosslinks. The crosslinks slowly reform after extrusion, with the time length inﬂuences the
shape ﬁdelity. Printing a hydrogel, the bioink must extrude as a viscoelastic liquid to allow extrusion
then quickly become a viscoelastic solid to retain shape ﬁdelity and structure. This transition of states is
called the gel point, dependent on the bioink design, with potential pH, temperature, and ionic strength
factors.

4.5.1
Gelatin
Gelatin is a protein based natural hydrogel.
Gelatin is desirable as a hydrogel due to its strengths
including: natural material, biocompatibility, possessing signaling molecules that favor cell adhesion,
relative ease of acessibility, high cell aﬃnity, and low cost [31] [32].
Gelatin is a widely used as a
hydrogel component due to water-binding capacity, hydration properties, gelling behavior, and surface
behavior.
Gelatin is a biodegradable mixture of polypeptides derived from the collagen, with variation based on
collagen type and hydrolytic method [33]. Collagen I hydrolysis can be physical, chemical, and enzymatic
with three phases: raw material pre-treatment, gelatin extraction, and product puriﬁcation or drying.
The processes break down the collagen I triple helix into a mixture of polypeptides, with properties based
on material and preparation. The preparation method, temperature, pH and concentration inﬂuence the
gelatin solution. Gelatin is commonly prepared in PBS or media to provided a good environment and
prevents the pH inﬂuence to the gelatin. Gelatin can be printed as fugitive or as a viscoelastic solid
with slight variations of gelatin hydrogel composition can signiﬁcantly alter mechanical properties. One
signiﬁcant factor that inﬂuences the mechanical properties of gelatin is its concentration. The higher
concentration of gelatin creates a faster gelation point time, increased viscosity, a stiﬀer hydrogel, and an
increased diﬃculty in printing.
Gelatin transitions between a viscoelastic liquid and viscoelastic solid based on temperature. Gelatin
transitions to a viscoelastic liquid through increasing the temperature to weaken the bonds with relatively
high viscosities at 37 °C [34]. When temperature decreases, gelatin transitions to a viscoelastic solid
through physical crosslinking. This transition between viscoelastic solid and viscoelastic liquid is called
the gelation point and is a thermo-reversible process.
Gelatin has varying stiﬀness, measured by the weight required to press on a gel 4mm without breaking
(Bloom) [?] An increased bloom value will create a higher melting and gelling point with an overall shorter
gelling time. Typically a gelatin has a gellation point about 28 ◦C, ordering the gelatin molecules by non-
speciﬁc bonds (hydrogen, electrostatic, and hydrophobic). The tensile strength reduces sharply above the
gellation point of 28 °C. The rigidity is inﬂuenced by temperature, concentration, pH and additives.

4.5.2
Cross-Linking
Physical crosslinking of gelatin occurs when gelatin below 35 Celcius through transformation from ran-
dom coil gelation molecules to an ordered triple helix conformation. Gelatin by itself has poor mechanical
stability and durability, so additional cross linking methods are common to improve mechanical proper-
ties.
The mechanical properties of gelatin can be improved through multiple approaches before, during or after
bioprinting to improve mechanical strength and structural integrity. Other materials may be integrated
into gelatin (e.g methacrylate) to improve viscosity and printability of the bioink. Photopolymerization
with UV light can increase the mechanical strength and shape ﬁdelity. However, this method has limited
applications due to gelatin becoming non-biodegradable. Chemical crosslinkable polymers (e.g alginate,
chitosan, ﬁbrinogen, or hyluronan) may be added to the gelatin polypeptide chains to form larger macro-
molecules. This can improve the properties of gelatin, however this can create toxic crosslinker in the
hydrogel causing potential toxic reagents release during in vivo degradation [33].
Finally, enzymatic cross-linking may be used to improve the gelatin’s mechanical properties. Microbial
transglutaminase (mTG) is a common be used as a protein crosslinking enzyme.
Crosslinking is a common process that stabilizes a structure through bonding one polymer chain to an-
other. Crosslinking can be physical, chemical, or enzymatic. Physical crosslinking (also called temporary
gel or thermoplastic hydrogels) are formed by weak interactions that are a reversible interaction. Physical
crosslinking has small mechanical property changes due to the weak interactions. Additional crosslinking
or post processing procedures may be used depending on the application to obtain the desired biomaterial
properties. Chemical crosslinking (also called thermosetting hydrogels), including ultraviolet (UV) and
wet-chemical, forms hydrogels through covalent bonding. This creates a non-reversible, more mecanical
stable biomaterial than physical crosslinking. However, UV can damage cells or printed architecture and
wet-chemical may use a potentially harmful crosslinking agents. Enzymatic crosslinking uses a protein
speciﬁc enzymes to create bonds between protein molecules. The crosslinking type combined with the
polymer and methodology create a crosslink density (number of bonds formed between polymer chains),
inﬂuencing elasticity, viscosity, glass transition temperature, solubility, and polymer type [35]. The in-
crease in crosslink density increases rigidity and glass transition temperature, decrease viscosity, chain
movement space, and free volume and can become brittle [35]. Controlling the degree of crosslinking
changes the mechanical properties of the hydrogel and allows optimization of rheology for the speciﬁc
biomaterial application.
Gelatin can be reversibly physically crosslinked by temperature. Gelatin solid that is heated in a liquid
will dissolve and the protein will be homogeneously mixed in the liquid. As the liquid cools, the protein
interacts with the water molecules, forms hydrogens bonds, and arranges into triple helix structures. This
is a reversible process due to the weak hydrogen bonds are broken and are not stable at body temperature
[29]. Chemical crosslinking with complementary groups like aldehydes and condensation reaction [29] [36]
and/or enzymatic crosslinking of the unmodiﬁed gelatin have been used to modify gelatin’s mechanical
properties [37].

Start-up G-Code modiﬁcation
The start-up G-Code prepares the FELIX BIOprinter to extrude the material. This sets the coordinate
system, turn on necessary components, home the system, and sets the print head. The coordinate system
and extrusion can be set to either an absolute or relative system which signiﬁcantly inﬂuences the G-Code.
In an absolute system, all coordinates space is based from one speciﬁc coordinate position (commonly
the origin position). In a relative system, the coordinate space is based on the previous local coordinate
position. The absolute extrusion position is based on the starting syringe material level compared to the
relative system where it extrudes based on the previous extrusion distance. Two G-Code were exported,
one in absolute extrusion and one in relative extrusion settings. This was to observe the diﬀerences and
select which version would be most eﬃcient to G-Code.

Amplitude Sweep
The amplitude sweep shows how the material behaves to strain with measured with storage and loss

being independent of the strain. The 8% gelatin shows a yield point at the maximum oscillatory strain
of 12.1 %, where the storage modulus decreases and equals the loss modulus. The decrease in storage
modulus disturbs the viscoelastic solid structure and when the storage modulus is below the loss modulus
the material starts to behave like a viscoelastic liquid.

Discussion
8.1
Software
The Simplify3D software provide a Simplify3D base G-code that allow modiﬁcation for bioink printing
in well plates. Simplify3D exported G-code describes what G-Code is used by the FELIX Bioprinter
hardware combined with FELIX designed a successful G-Code for a 0.410 mm diameter needle. However,
Simiplify3D did not functionally work for easy direct printing into well plates. This is due to Simplify3D
being a universal software. A general software issue with universal software is that it is not designed to
one speciﬁc technology and/or originally designed based on a diﬀerent technology. Making an universal
software functional to a new technology, the software is made to work with the hardware the best it
can. The Simplify3D was originally designed in 2013 based on a ﬁlament 3D printer, which has many
diﬀerences than an extrusion bioprinter. A ﬁlament 3D printer prints direction on the build platform.
This explains why the coordinate system was at the lower left corner of the build platform and why there
were no program or special settings for building on a well plate system. An additional discovery was that
to draw a circle, Simplify3D drew multiple small lines to create a circle. This was due to the G2/G3
arc function being removed due to particular 3D printing hardware not allowing that function [58]. A
universal software provides the potential to work for multiple 3D Printing technologies, material, and
ﬁrmware. The downside of universal software is that it not designed around the individual 3D printer,
but designed to work with the 3D Printer.
There are four factors that lead to the necessity of validating the Z origin position (Z=0) and Z Height for
well plate extrusion: lack of well plate speciﬁc build platform, ineﬃcient FELIX calibration wizard for well
plates, lack of Simplify3D settings for 0.630 mm or 0.840 mm needle sizes, and material drying/shrinking.
The Z origin position was determined by obtaining coating thickness through the calibration wizard for
known equipment and translate to the well plate equipment. The Z-height was obtained through the
0.410 build setting and translated to the diﬀerent needle diameters.
8.2
Bioink Printing
All experimental bioink print methodology extruded follicles from the syringe into multiple well plates.
Follicle up to 300 µm were capable of being extruded out of all needle diameter, including the 410 mm
needle diameter. I think this is due to a combination of factors. The follicle is an extremely stretchable
and expandable cell structure due to folliculogenesis and its physiology development. This most likely
allows it to stretch without breaking overcoming the stress stress and yield stress to extruded from
the needle to the well plate. Second, we used a low bloom gelatin without additives in our hydrogel.
This means our hydrogel has comparatively weak physical cross-links and low stiﬀness during extrusion.
This creates a lower stress environment to extruded the hydrogel. Third, the large syringe creates a
minimum/negligible extension stress on the follicles. Fourth, the 3D printing settings were idealized to
minimize the forces on the follicles. However, the percent output of the follicles from the syringe to well
plate was low.

8.4
FELIX Limitation in Bioink Printing
Limitations in the design of the FELIX BIOprinter created technological challenges in the extrusion of
a bioink. The FELIX is designed to load a 5 mL syringe with a rubber plunger. This syringe combined
with the extrusion bioprinter technology does not allow complete extrusion of all material. There is still
residual material left in the syringe and needle when the piston has reached the inferior extent. This is a
signiﬁcant limitation in the extrusion of extrusion of smaller volumes of material. For example, if 500 µm
of bioink is desired to print, there is 150 - 200 µL of material in the conical needle leaving 300-350 µL of
material in the syringe. In this example there potentially 30 - 40 % of bioink left in the syringe and needle
when the hardware is incapable of mechanically extruding any more distance. This can be potentially
mitigated by adding biomateral before the ﬁnal print to force the bioink out of the needle. This was a
diﬃcult method to prevent loss of bioink and follicles and during the loading of the biomaterial. The
other option is to manually force air through the syringe but that is removing all shape ﬁdelity and the
beneﬁt of using a 3D printer.

Creation of a 8.5% gelatin 1 mL stock solution:
1. Weigh 0.085 g gelatin powder per mL of media
2. Mix dry gelatin powder with media avoiding aggregation of gelatin powder
3. Place solution in 37°C water bath for 1 hour
(a) Mix throughout process to break up gelatin aggregation to ensure homogeneous mixture and
correct concentration
4. Pipette solution desired volume into a 5 mL syringe
5. Add 350 µL follicle media solution
(a) 1:1 mixture gives 5% gelatin bioink
6. Remove air from syringe
7. Rotate syringe 20 minutes in room temperature water bath to decrease gelation time
8. Load syringe into FELIX BIOprinter
9. Extrude gelatin in 24 wells (A1-C8) of 48 well plate
10. Wait ﬁve minutes after ﬁrst well extrusion
11. Add 500 µL mTG into each well plate
12. Wait ten minutes for cross-linkage
13. Insert 48 well plate into Incubator for 1 hour
14. Remove mTG from 48 well plate
15. Pipette media in gelatin wells
