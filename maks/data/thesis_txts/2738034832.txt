Abstract
Small and powerful micropumps are important for supporting the health of diabetes pa-
tients throug easy and regular insulin infusion. Gadolinium-doped Cerium oxide (CGO)
Has shown itself to be a promising candidate as an electromechanical actuator due to
its remarkably strong electrostriction with a longitudonal electrostriction coefficient of
|M33| > 10−16m2/V2. It has a number of advantages when compared to conventional
piezoceramics such as being lead-free and compatability whith polymers.
The efficacy of CGO has been investigated in a number of experiments provided by
Victor Buratto Tinti and Jin Han at DTU Energy. In this thesis, these are compared
with simulated experiments through finite element methods. This thesis also hypothesises
the existence of velocity matching acoustic modes though fluid-filled microcapillary tubes.
In these modes, the fluid and the wall oscillate synchronously without generating viscous
boundary layers. The last part of the thesis investigates using these velocity matching
modes in conjunction with CGO actuators for an effective micropump with the use in
insulininfusion in mind.
The transverse electrostriction coeffictient of CGO is found to have a value of |M31 ≤
2 × 10−20 according to the provided experiments, a value much lower than expected. The
velocity matching modes show to viscous dissipation which is orders of magnitude lower
than other modes described in the literature of pulsatile flow. These modes do however
turn out not to be useful in pumping systems due to their incapacity to generate time-
averaged flow.

1.1
Micropumps for diabetes
Much of the motivation in the field for the work with micropumps was to help people
suffering from diabetes.
Hyperglycaemia, also called raised blood sugar, is a common
effect of uncontrolled diabetes. It is unpleasant to experience, and may over time severely
damage many of the body’s systems in general and the nerves and the blood vessels in
particular;[3] increasing the risk of heart decease and stroke.[4] In spite of major advances
in the treatment of diabetes such as the development of insulin pens, insulin pumps, and
inhalers,[5] work is still being done to make insulin infusion more convenient.
An artificial pancreas is a newly developed type of insulin pump promising a safer and
more reliable way of supplying insulin. By monitoring blood glucose continuously it is

able to infuse the appropriate dose of insulin whenever necessary.[5] A 2017 meta analysis
[6] found that continuous insulin infusion reduces glycosylated hemoglobin (HbA1c) by

1.2
The giant electrostrictor CGO
In 2013 a novel family of materials based on gadolinium-doped ceria (GdxCe1−xO2−y
(CGO)) was discovered to exhibit an effect known as giant electrostriction.[8] Whereas
piezoelectricity is the tendency of materials to produce a mechanical response that is
linear in the applied electric field, electrostriction is the tendency of materials to produce
a mechanical response proportional to the square of the applied electric fields.
Giant
electrostrictors such as CGO are interesting for manufacturing of electromechanical devices
for a number of reasons.
They are able to do high amounts of work with very little
material; their isotropic nature make them applicable in novel geometries, and their low
deposition temperature means that they can be applied directly in top of certain polymers;
allowing for more actuation of more flexible materials. [9] Another benefit of CGO in
comparison with piezoceramics in the context of insulin pumps comes from the fact that
the most common type of piezoceramic, Lead zirconate titanate (PZT) contains lead,
which according to a 2011 EU directive must be phased out in all new medical devices by
2017 wherever possible [10].2
The first half of this thesis investigates the versatility of CGO as an actuator for micro-
electromechanical (MEMS) systems. It contains a numerical study of three experiments
carried out by Victor Buratto Tinti, Jin Han and Vincenzo Esposito from DTU Energy.
The experiments were partly made to characterize the CGO, partly to exemplify the po-
tential applications for optics and fluidics.
Simulations are carried out to analyze the
properties of the CGO and to determine the performance of the primitive devices made
for the experiments. The main results from these simulations are included in our paper
”Oxygen Defective Electrostrictors for Soft Electromechanics” which was submitted to the
journal Science Advances on May 9th 2024[11], and was accepted today, on the 17th of
June 2024.

Ye et al. [13] have in 2014 made a TWµPs with a maximum water flow rate of Q =

Mechanical metamaterials with tunable elasticity tensors have been designed and de-
veloped in the preceding decades,[17–20] and have demonstrated a huge variety in terms
of their mechanical properties going far beyond the range of conventional (non-meta) ma-
terials. 2D matamaterials in particular have demonstrated Poisson’s ratios ranging from
-10.24 to +13.79.[17]
In the second part of this thesis, I shall argue using the traditional Womersley theory
of pulsatile flows in cylindrical pipes [21, 22] 3 together with the known properties of
2D metamaterials, that it is possible in theory to induce a kind of acousto-elastofluidic
wave similar to those of blood pressure pulses, but with the unique property that the
viscous boundary layer and the associated attenuation is suppressed completely. I shall
then propose and analyze a micropump designed to utilize these special acoustic modes.

Theory of Electrostriction
In this chapter, we shall try to go through all of the electrostriction theory necessary
for the finite-element simulations made for the paper ”Oxygen Defective Electrostrictors
for Soft Electromechanics”. This shall be done without skipping too many steps, such
that my entire train of thought may be found in one place. I shall start by laying out

2.1

applied electric field. Unlike piezoelectricity, electrostriction is a property found in all
materials, even in isotropic materials. It was discovered all the way back in 1877 by E. J.

in most materials. If a non-piezoelectric material is subjected to an applied electric field

Modelling CGO as an electrostrictor

In this chapter, I shall show how the theory of electrostriction is implemented in finite

One setup is an elastic cantilever with thin film CGO actuator attached to the top as
depicted in figure 4.1. The purpose of the cantilever is to measure the M31 electrostriction

cantilever that is due to the expansion/contraction of the CGO at a certain voltage. Also,
the cantilever is meant to demonstrate the capabilities of CGO, when applied directly on

The second setup is an optical device consisting of a flat piece of PET with a layer of

to show that the CGO works as expected. If this is the case, it will demonstrate a benefit
of CGO as an isotropic electrostrictor, which unlike piezoelectric crystals can be made to
work in many different kinds of geometries.
The experiments are similar in the sense that an alternating electric field was applied

Chapter 6
Theory of pulsatile Flow in Tubes
with Anisotropic Stiffness
The motivation for this chapter can be summerized in the following thought experiment:
1. Imagine a long tube with a thin, light, isotropic and compliant wall. The tube is
filled with an incompressible fluid.
2. Assume a travelling sinusoidal pressure wave is propagating along inside the tube.
The wavelength is considered to be big compared to the radius of the tube, and the
viscosity is assumed to be low. See Fig. 6.1.
3. Such a wave in such a fluid will have a uniform velocity profile across its cross section,
with a tiny viscous boundary layer at the wall defined to fulfill the no-slip boundary
condition.
4. The compliant tube will tend to expand within regions of high pressure, and contract
within regions of low pressure. Crucially, a Poisson effect will also cause the wall to
contract by νp% axially (along z) as it expands by 1% radially, and vice versa for
contraction. νp is the Poisson’s ratio. See Fig. 6.2.
5. The incompressible fluid will preserve its volume. Thus, as each short cylindrical
fluid element with volume V ol = πR2L expands by 1% radially, the fluid must
contract by 2% axially.
6. Due to the waves being long compared to the radius, as we integrate these contrac-
tions to obtain the velocity of the fluid, the axial velocity must be much higher than
the radial velocity.
7. Integrating along the wall, the axial velocity of the wall must be exactly νp
2 times
that of the fluid.
8. Thus, if one had νp = 2; the bulk velocity of the fluid would be exactly equal to that
of the wall, and the no-slip boundary condition would be fulfilled without a viscous
boundary layer.

Chapter 7
A Micropump with Suppressed
Viscous Dissipation
Now that we have found the criteria for supression of viscous dissipation, it may be in-
teresting to investigate the utility of these modes in the context of acoustofluidic devices.
One may imagine many possible applications:
• High-quality factor acoustic cavities.
• High-precision acoustic time-of-flight spectrometers.
• Low-loss acoustic energy transmission.
• Tiny, powerful pumps.
In this chapter we shall however focus our efforts on investigating the last of these options:
A tiny, CGO-driven travelling-wave micropump. The micropump considered is sketched
in Fig. 7.1b. It consist of a micro-capillary tube which loops around to end in itself, giving
it the topology of a torus. The micro-capillary tube is filled up with a fluid. It is expected
to work in the following way:
1. First, AC voltages are applied at either end of the CGO segments. This induces
uniform axial electric fields E inside each CGO film. The AC-voltage applied to one
of the CGO films is a phase π/4 ahead of the AC voltage applied to the other CGO
film.
2. Since CGO is an electrostrictor, the AC-field along the CGO will produce an internal
stress σel inside the CGO. Since σel is quardratic in E, the phase difference between
the induced stresses will now be π/2 (a quarter-wave).
3. The stress will cause CGO-covered tube segments to expand and contract periodi-
cally, sending acoustic pressure waves out to their sides.
4. These waves propagate along the tube. However, by placing the two CGO-segments
exactly one a quarter-wave apart, the counterclockwise-travelling waves will interfere
destructively, leaving only the clockwise-propagating waves.

Modelling results and discussion
The frequency response of the average pressure amplitude is plotted in Fig. 7.8. A single
resonance frequency can be seen at fres = 3279 Hz. The fact that we only are observing
a single resonance frequency rather than two resonances at slightly different frequencies,
indicates that the two lowest eigenmodes of the system are degenerate.
This I shall
interpret as an artifact of the design decision to place the two CGO-segments exactly one
quarter-wave apart. As can be seen from the response at fres illustrated in Fig. 7.9,
the two lowest eigenmoedes largely resemble the functions cos(z/L) and sin(z/L) with
localized perturbations in the two active regions. Due to the quarter-wave separation, no
matter how one of the CGO-segments perturbs the frequency of the sine-mode, the other
of the CGO-segments will perturb the cosine mode in exactly the same manner. It is thus
possible for the system to support coherent superpositions of the two modes, allowing for
a travelling wave around the loop.

Chapter 8
Conclusion and Outlook
In this thesis, we introduced a way to simulate three CGO-experiments and found some
interesting results in terms of the properties of CGO. Next, we looked for a general solution
to the problem of pulsatile flow through tubes with anisotropic compliance, and lastly we
combined these theories in an analytical study of a hypothesized ”micropump”. I have a
few remaining thoughts on this investigation to leave you with.
8.1
CGO and electrostriction
The simulated experiments seem to indicate a far lower transverse electrostricion coefic-
cient M31 than what has been reported. If this result persists, it has a number of con-
sequences depending on the context within which the result is considered.
At a very
fundemental level, the result is rather interesting, as we have a material that expands
in the same way as any other material in the sense that it expands when squeezed me-
chanically with a Poisson’s ratio of νP = 0.313; a rather typical value on par with many
other kinds of oxides [36]. When it is squeezed with electric fields however, it doesn’t
seem to contract nor expand in the transverse direction at all. I do not understand why
this is happening. It may simply be a coincidence, it may have some deeper microscopic
explanation, and for all I know it may be related to why gadolinium doped CGO exhibits
giant electrostriction in the first place.
From a technological point of view, it means that it may not be feasible to manu-
facture cross-plane devices made to utilize the transverse electrostriction in CGO. Our
experimental collaborators Victor Buratto Tinti and Vincenzo Esposito expressed great
satisfaction with this result, and will design their future CGO devices with this in mind.
The question now is if the CGO can be altered in a way as to enhance the transverse
electrostriction coefficient sufficiently for technological applications, or if one should work
around this problem by placing the electrodes such that the electric field is aligned with
the desired direction of strain. Finally, it is of course also fun to think about whether this
unique property of CGO could be utilized for certain applications, although I do not at
the moment know what these should be.
With regard to the simulations, the developed method of simply establishing the gov-

of handling the effects of the electrostriction around the electrode edges, due to the huge

For the practical use of CGO as an electromechanical actuator, one still needs to show

of the maximum amount of work the CGO will be able to perform without breaking.
8.2
Velocity matching modes
I am not very happy with the design of the metamaterial suggested in Chapter 7. The
mixing between polymer and metal turned out to restrict the design more than expected
from the general intuition that the stiffer metal surely would dominate the elasticity of
the tube while the soft polymer then could keep the fluid inside. The balance between
keeping the anisotropy, the weight of the wall and its flexibility high while keeping acoustic
damping and nonlinearities low would benefit from a design with more degrees of freedom.
To meet all these criteria, I speculate that a tube with a folded wall as inspired by origami-
based mechanical metamaterials like the one presented by Lv et al.[19] would be optimal.
It may however also be the case that adding periodic reinforcement rings and dead-weights
along the tube would be enough depending on the application.
Since these velocity-matching modes do not seem to have any use in terms of a pump,
the next question is of course whether they may be good for anything at all. To elaborate
a bit on their capabilities in terms of sorting particles in a time-of flight kind of way, the
ability for the current ”pump” design to create a uniform stream of acoustic waves with
very little streaming is interesting, since it removes a lot of noise from the measurements
to only leave the waves themselves to interact with the particles.
The capability of the dissipation-free modes to transmit acoustic energy can be sum-
marized from the fact that a travelling mode with at mean velocity of vz = 20 m/s and a
mass per unit length of 2 × 10−5kg/m propagating at a speed of c = 663 m/s m/s carries
a lot of acoustic energy. Estimating the power carried along the tube as P = 1
2 ¯ρ|v|2c one
ends up with P = 2.7W, making it comparable to the power carried by a small wire in a
tabletop electric circuit.
Also, I am aware that acoustic cavities appear in devices for optical applications[37].
Having briefly met with one of the developers from the company OCT light, anecdotally
he explicitly expressed having issues with the air in their optomechanical cavities causing
severe damping due to the viscous dissipation due to the air inside the cavities. Maybe
this theory can be used to help with such issues as well.
At the time of writing, I am still baffled that the acoustic streaming from these velocity

corresponding to ”The relative change in cross section squared times the speed of the

In the end, I suppose the results of this project illustrate some of the various ways
