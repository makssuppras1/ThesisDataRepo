2 Abstract
In recent decades, there has been a growing interest in cultural transformation and
innovation management. The need for effective tools and practices in these areas has only
been heightened by the outbreak of COVID-19 and by the global political instabilities of the
past few years. This thesis addresses such increased need by introducing a novel cultural
mapping
framework
designed
for
large-scale organisations. Grounded in theoretical
foundations, the model presented draws inspiration from existing tools in business and
product development whilst offering better usability, adaptability, and practical insights.
The research methodology involves an exploration of cultural theory, focusing on the
definition of culture and the identification of key cultural indicators. Based on this theoretical
frame, the study has developed a cultural mapping model that encompasses three critical
areas of interest: behaviour, values, and meanings. These three facets provide a holistic
view of an organisation's culture and provide actionable insight by comparing the culture
embodied by operational staff with the one desired by leadership.

6 Introduction
6.1 On my personal motivation
During the Spring of 2019, I worked as an intern in the London offices of a large trade
insurance company which, at that time, was embarking on two significant transformational
projects.
The first project was a technological overhaul, moving from an outdated intranet system
created in the early 2000s to a modern platform inspired by social media. This shift was
accompanied by the introduction of new communication tools and work practices, including
enhanced work-from-home policies, open offices, and flexible workstations.
Simultaneously, the company's leadership sought to prepare and, if necessary, adjust the
company culture to embrace these changes. This initiative involved a culture mapping
process, in which I participated. We used the Culture Mapping Canvas, a tool developed by
Dave Gray and Alexander Osterwalder, and published in 2015 (Osterwalder et al., 2020).
This canvas was employed in workshops with each department, enabling us to track the
culture within each of them.
Leadership and managers were not involved in these workshops to create a safer
environment for the employees to express themselves without fear of repercussions.
Instead, they underwent a similar workshop, to ideate and map the ideal culture they
envisioned for the company.
My involvement in this project led me to two key realisations: firstly, the profound impact of
culture on employees' work and daily life quality; and secondly, the limitations of the Culture
Mapping Canvas. While effective in mapping certain cultural aspects, such as the
behaviours acted by the individuals in a company, it fell short in capturing others, like values
or symbols and their meanings. These insights prompted me to look for a new model that
could more comprehensively map culture, ultimately improving the daily lives of workers.
6.2 Background and research question
Cultural transformation and innovation management have witnessed a remarkable surge in
recent decades, as organisations endeavour to adapt to the ever-changing business
landscape. Technological advancements have bestowed new opportunities upon employees
and leadership alike, revolutionising the way we work and collaborate (Bostrom, 2006). The
outbreak of the COVID-19 pandemic further accelerated this transformation, propelling
innovative approaches such as remote work, distributed collaboration, and asynchronous
communication to the forefront of organisational practices (De’ et al., 2020).
In this dynamic context, large organisations have been proactively adopting innovative tools
and procedures, aiming to enhance their adaptability and agility. Nonetheless, individuals
often exhibit reluctance towards change unless change itself is woven within the overarching
organisational
culture
that
envelops
them.
Initiatives
geared
towards
reshaping an
organisation's culture play a significant role in mitigating resistance to change (Jain et al.,
7
==PAGE:9==
2018). Consequently, businesses are progressively allocating resources to grasp and
execute successful culture transformation and administration strategies.
While research has been conducted in the past decades on cultural mapping many aspects
remain
inadequately
explored.
Specifically, Duxbury highlights the need for “further
comparative analyses of case studies, toolkits, and good practices”, “stronger theories of
cultural mapping”, and, generally, for “better methods for cultural mapping” (Duxbury et al.,
2015), while Jung concludes that “there is no ideal instrument for cultural exploration” (Jung
et al., 2009).
Building upon the insights of Duxbury and Jung, a gap emerges within the realm of cultural
studies, particularly concerning the development and application of cultural mapping models
and tools. This lacuna is underscored by my aforementioned personal experience, and the
transformative impact of recent global events and technological advancements, such as the
Covid-19 pandemic and the proliferation of generative artificial intelligence models. These
factors collectively underscore the need for tools that facilitate the successful navigation and
management of cultural shifts within large-scale organisations.
These observations guide us to the research question this thesis seeks to answer: how can
organisational culture be effectively mapped to generate actionable insights?
This research question can be divided into two smaller subquestions:
1.
How can organisational culture be mapped?
2.
How can we generate actionable insights?
In this context, actionable insights refer to clear and practical information that organisations
can directly utilise to make informed decisions or implement changes. These insights will aid
decision-makers in identifying points for action to align the culture mapped within the
organisation more closely with that desired by leadership.
In response to the research questions, I aim to bridge this gap in research by developing a
new framework for cultural mapping. This framework is designed to be both grounded in
theory
and
pragmatically
relevant,
addressing
the
necessities
of
contemporary
organisations.

6.3 Structure
As previously outlined, the primary objective of this manuscript is to present a model for
mapping the cultural landscape within large-scale organisations. This model will be firmly
rooted in theoretical foundations while also offering practical insights for implementation.
After discussing the methodology employed, the first main section of the thesis will be
dedicated to an in-depth exploration of the relevant literature. This involves an analysis of
various definitions of organisational culture put forth by eminent scholars over the past
decades. Throughout this analysis, I will strive to identify commonalities and disparities while
taking note of the key criticisms associated with each theory. By comprehensively surveying
the spectrum of cultural definitions, I will subsequently delve into the identification of cultural
indicators, examining what researchers seek when assessing the culture of a specific
organisational environment. These identified indicators will serve as the building blocks upon
which the proposed framework will be constructed.
The latter part of the thesis will centre on the framework itself. Firstly, I will elucidate the
methodology employed in its development, which draws inspiration from tools utilised in
product and business development. Secondly, I will provide a comprehensive breakdown of
the framework's distinct modules, outlining the theoretical underpinnings of each and offering
practical guidance on their utilisation through workshops in real-world settings.
Concluding the thesis, the discussion and conclusion section will consolidate the insights
gathered throughout the document. It will provide a sort summary of the entire thesis,
followed by a discussion of relevant considerations about the results from the tentative
employment of the model, the model itself, and its limitations. Lastly, I will conclude the
thesis by circling back to the start, checking if the research questions I sought to answer
were addressed.

7 Methodology
7.1 Introduction
This chapter addresses the philosophical and methodological approaches.
Drawing on the research methodologies described by Saunders et al. (2015), I will firstly
discuss any conscious assumptions I can identify; secondly, I will describe the research
philosophy and approach I adopted; and lastly, I will address the methodological choice.
7.2 Assumptions
In conducting research, implicit or explicit assumptions are always present. We therefore
need to address them (Burrell & Morgan, 2019).
Expanding on the work by Burrell and Morgan, first published in 1979, Saunders et al. (2015)
categorise these assumptions into three primary groups: ontological, axiological and
epistemological. This chapter aims to elucidate these assumptions and their implications for
the research methodology.
Ontological assumptions pertain to the nature of reality as it is encountered in research. In
the context of this study, two key ontological assumptions must be acknowledged: the first
one is that organisational culture not only exists but can also be mapped and analysed; the
second is that, although with many difficulties and resource employment, culture can also be
changed or influenced in a desired direction. These assumptions ground the relevance of the
present study, as it specifically explores these areas: how to map organisational culture, how
to analyse these mappings, and how culture can be intentionally shaped.
Axiological assumptions reflect personal values and beliefs underpinning the research
approach. I place significant value on personal connections and interactions for exploring
cultural phenomena, as they allow for a more direct and engaged participation of the
individuals living the culture inside the organisation with the study itself and with the map
maker.
This belief has guided the development of a cultural mapping tool designed for workshop
settings, where the map maker employs the tool with direct participation from individuals
within the organisation. The map maker should not only passively conduct these workshops
but also engage in discussions with participants to deeply understand the reasoning behind
their responses. Through this, the map maker gains a deeper understanding of the culture.
Epistemological assumptions define what constitutes valuable and acceptable knowledge.
This study acknowledges the multifaceted and subjective nature of culture, where diverse
perspectives coexist and enrich the understanding of the cultural object under study.
Surveys, though useful for gathering quantitative data, are considered limited in capturing
the complex realities of culture due to their lack of interactivity, as previously discussed.
Preferably, qualitative methods are used. An ethnographer immersed in the cultural
environment can experience, observe, and record subtle details in people’s behaviours and
traditions; an external observer might achieve similar results, albeit with a less immersive
understanding of the culture; an interviewer, by posing questions to an individual, might
10
==PAGE:12==
prompt them to elaborate on their thinking. All these examples are possible only within
interactions between individuals, absent in questionnaires.
If we accept that the methods to study an object are designed based on its nature, then,
whilst studying culture, the methods should be designed on our understanding of what
culture is. As stated before, we are acknowledging culture as multifaceted, subjective, and
ever-changing. None of these aspects can be fully described with the use of quantitative
variables. At times, some aspects even contradict each other, leading to the impossibility of
drawing clear and direct consequences. Hence, to study culture we need methods that are
designed to account for these complexities and describe them in more elaborate ways.
Thus, in this thesis, surveys are not considered a valuable method of cultural analysis.
7.3 Research philosophy and approach
The aforementioned assumptions all feed into the decision to approach this study from a
perspective
that
is
both
interpretative
and
pragmatic,
supported
by
an
inductive
methodology.
The interpretative aspect is driven by the belief that individuals create diverse meanings
based on their unique circumstances, backgrounds, and experiences; in this case, the study
of culture becomes an exploration of these varied meanings and interpretations, and how
they come into existence. The research methods are thus designed to delve into the
subjective experiences and perspectives of individuals, acknowledging the multiplicity and
complexity of cultural interpretations
Concurrently, the research adopts a pragmatic approach due to the practical constraints,
such as time and scope, given by the nature of the project itself. Echoing Saunders et al.
(2015), by pragmatic, I mean that I consider concepts as relevant only when they support
action. This has led to some arbitrary choices throughout the research process, from the
selection of specific authors in the literature review to the development of the cultural
mapping model. Each choice was influenced not only by the research questions stated in the
introduction but also by observed market needs, gleaned from personal conversations with
individuals in culture-change roles within large organisations, as well as my own work
experience in similar environments. This pragmatic approach ensures the study remains
focused and relevant, while adaptable to the practical realities of conducting research.
The research approach is abductive. From interviews, and the analysis of a limited number
of
authors,
I developed a cultural mapping framework able to provide users with
action-oriented recommendations. This is done employing qualitative data gathered through
personal interactions of the map maker with the individuals living the organisation. It is
important to note the intentional correspondence between the organisation and its culture, as
it underscores the personal belief that an organisation is its culture. This similarly implies that
changes in one mean changes in the other, with all the implications that can be derived
concerning values, meanings, behaviours, and all the other components of culture itself.

8 Theoretical analysis
8.1 Introduction
Despite its significant influence on human interactions and societal structures, pinning down
a definitive description of “culture” has remained a complex challenge for scholars,
anthropologists, and professionals alike. This complexity arises from culture's intrinsic
multifaceted nature, which offers a vast canvas of interpretations. These interpretations often
diverge, sometimes subtly and at other times significantly, leading to a tapestry of conflicting
viewpoints. While there is a vast expanse of literature dedicated to understanding culture,
the quest for a universally accepted definition continues uninterrupted.
As alluded to earlier, the first half of this thesis navigates the labyrinth of cultural definitions
by examining the insights offered by seminal authors. These insights are further enriched by
juxtaposing them with critiques from their contemporaries. An overview of the authors
discussed and their key teachings can be found in the table in Appendix A. After cementing
a foundational understanding of the multi-dimensional concept of culture, our exploration will
transition into identifying the markers that signify culture.
This foundational theory will serve as the bedrock from which I will derive a comprehensive
mapping model, one that not only recognizes the diversity and depth of culture but also
facilitates its interpretation and the generation of valuable insights

8.1.1 Edward Burnett Tylor
Edward Burnett Tylor, an English anthropologist, significantly influenced cultural studies with
his works "Primitive Knowledge" (1871a, 1871b) and "Anthropology" (1889).
His inclusive view of culture is defined in the first lines of Primitive Knowledge, as “that
complex whole which includes knowledge, belief, art, morals, law, custom, and any other
capabilities and habits acquired by man as a member of society” (Tylor, 1871a, p. 1).
One of the main concepts of his theory is "survivals", remnants of older cultures within
modern societies. He viewed these survivals as “mines of historic knowledge” (Tylor, 1871a,
p. 64), as they tell us about the past and shape the future while preferring this term over
"superstition", as the latter is spoiled by negative connotations (Tylor, 1871a, p. 65).
An example of this is how the English people consider marriages in May as unlucky. This is
a survival in culture from the Roman who, eighteen centuries before, would not marry in May
due to the annual rites of the Lemuralia happening in the same month. During these rites the
Romans would exorcise any malevolent and fearful spirits of the restless dead from their
homes (Tylor, 1871a, p. 63).
Discussing culture and survivals, Tylor also explains his view of the ethnographer’s role,
which is that of a reformer aiding mankind’s progress, by removing hindrances and exposing
remains of older cruder cultures to be destroyed (Tylor, 1871b, p. 410).
Furthermore, Tylor believed in the evolutionary nature of culture, arguing that all cultures
evolve from primitive states and become, “in the course of ages, more intelligent, more
systematic, more perfectly arranged or organised, to answer their purposes” (Tylor, 1889, p.
15).
As cultures go through the same stages of development, what distinguishes them is the
state of development of their education, seen as the cumulative knowledge acquired in the
span of centuries (Stringer, 1999).
While seminal, Tylor's views have been criticised for paternalism, often categorising some
cultures as "lower" due to their developmental stage and associating “primitives” or
“savages” with “children”. Despite this, his theories should be contextualised within the 19th
century's understanding and approach to cultural evolution, and remain coherent with his
evolutionary framework (Stringer, 1999).

8.1.2 Geert Hofstede
Geert Hofstede, a renowned Dutch social psychologist and anthropologist, had a career that
spanned diverse fields from engineering to social sciences (Sent & Kroese, 2020). He
famously compared culture (intended as patterns of thinking, feeling, and acting) to "the
software of the mind", as, in his perspective, culture represents the "collective programming"
distinguishing one group of people from another. However, he quickly emphasised that
humans aren't programmed as rigidly as computers. While certain behaviours might be
predisposed due to cultural "programming", deviations are natural and expected; this
"software only indicates what reactions are likely and understandable, given one’s past"
(Hofstede et al., 2010, p. 5). For instance, if we are to ask an employee whether they
preferred time off from work in place of higher pay, their answer would indicate their
belonging to a group with a different culture (Hofstede et al., 2010, p. 28).
Much of Hofstede's seminal work draws from a survey conducted by IBM between 1967 and
1973.
His
analysis
of
these
findings culminated in his 1993 book, "Cultures and
Organizations: Software of the Mind", (2010), where he introduced several dimensions to
elucidate specific cultural variations at a national level elucidate cultural variations at a
national level (Individualism vs. Collectivism, Power Distance, Uncertainty Avoidance,
Masculinity
vs.
Femininity,
Long-Term
Orientation
vs.
Short-Term
Orientation,
and
Indulgence vs. Restraint). I will not go into a detailed explanation of these dimensions, as
they are not relevant to my study, but it is important to note their importance for two reasons.
Firstly, through them, Hofstede was the first one to conduct an empirical cross-country
research with quantitative cultural variables, making culture measurable; secondly, his
cultural dimensions have been well-received in many fields, such as psychology, marketing,
business, and economics (Sent & Kroese, 2020, p. 22).
While Hofstede's theories have had a profound impact on the study of culture, they have not
been without their critics. Acknowledging these criticisms, Hofstede himself engaged with his
detractors in several publications (Harzing & Hofstede, 1996; Hofstede, 2001; Hofstede et
al., 2010). He categorised the primary objections to his work into five standard criticisms:
1.
Surveys are not a suitable way of measuring cultural differences
2.
Nations are not the best units for studying cultures
3.
A study of the subsidiaries of one company cannot provide information about entire
national cultures
4.
The IBM data are old and therefore obsolete
5.
Four or five dimensions are not enough
Addressing these points of contention, Hofstede provided a defence of his methodologies
and conclusions, without amending them.

8.1.3 Edgar Schein
Edgar Schein, a Swiss-born American psychologist, made significant contributions to our
understanding of organisational culture. His magnum opus, Organizational Culture and
Leadership, first published in 1985 and updated multiple times since then, has been adopted
by countless organisations worldwide as the theoretical bedrock for their change and culture
management processes, and many organisational culture scholars have been heavily
influenced by his theories. At the core of Schein’s perspective on organisational culture is
the idea that culture can be seen as the “accumulated shared learning of a group as it solves
problems of external adaptation and internal integration” (Schein, 2017, p. 21). This denotes
how groups within an organisation navigate external pressures and changes, and how they
foster unity and coordination internally.
Furthermore, cultures are also defined as “learned patterns of beliefs, values, assumptions,
and behavioral norms that manifest themselves at different levels of observability” (Schein,
2017, p. 18).
These levels of observability are the core of his cultural theory. Per Schein, there are three
distinct layers based on the degree of visibility of the cultural phenomenon to an observer:
artefacts (visible phenomena and behaviours, easy to identify, but difficult to decipher),
espoused beliefs and values (ideals and principles that members of the organisation profess
or claim to hold), and underlying basic assumptions (unconscious, often unspoken beliefs
and values that underlie everyday operations and interactions) (Schein, 1984, 2017).
Figure 1. Layers of Culture in Schein’s Theory
Schein himself provides some examples to clarify these layers. Concerning the artefacts, he
mentions the Egyptian and Mayan pyramids, which are very easy to spot, and of similar
shape, but have very different meanings in the two cultures (tomb in the former, temples in
the latter). Regarding the espoused beliefs, he mentions how a company may declare to
value people and a high quality for their products. Finally, about the underlying basic
assumptions, he argues that if we assume it is human nature to be lazy, then we will
15
==PAGE:17==
interpret someone’s absence from the office as taking a leave rather than working from
home.
While Schein’s theories have been extremely influential for both scholars and professionals,
this success has brought many critiques as well.
One primary criticism levelled against Schein’s work is his notable emphasis on values.
Detractors argue that he may have disproportionately prioritised values over meanings and
symbolism, which are often regarded as much more important, particularly by anthropologist
researchers (Alvesson, 2002; Geertz, 1973).
Similarly, there's a sentiment among some critics that Schein might have placed too heavy
an emphasis on the role of leadership in shaping and moulding culture. While leadership is
undoubtedly pivotal, this might risk overshadowing other essential influencers within an
organisation, such as long-lasting employees, new ones, and external societal shifts
(Alvesson, 2002).
Another concern is the potential assumption of regularity in individuals' behaviours within
organisations. Schein’s model, some believe, paints a picture of predictability and uniformity
in how individuals act, which may not always reflect the varied behavioural patterns seen in
real-world settings (J. Martin, 2002).
Lastly, a prominent critique of Schein's model centres on its potential neglect of the presence
of subcultures within larger organisational entities. By suggesting a singular, cohesive culture
for an entire organisation, the model may overlook the intricate network of subcultures and
unique subunits frequently present within such entities. These subcultures can exert
substantial influence, often operating under cultural norms distinct from the prevailing
organisational culture. This divergence is so pronounced that several academics have
highlighted the topic, illustrating how culture delineates patterns of behaviours, values, and
meanings uniquely manifested in these subunits (Becker, 1982; J. Martin, 2002).

8.1.4 Clifford Geertz
Clifford Geertz was an American anthropologist, whose work moved cultural studies from
sweeping, overarching theories aiming to explain entire cultures in broad strokes, towards a
more intricate examination of the myriad complexities and nuances that characterise specific
societies and their practices.
Central to Geertz's understanding of culture is the idea that culture is both manifested in and
transmitted through symbols. For him, culture is a “historically transmitted pattern of
meanings embodied in sym bols, a system of inherited conceptions expressed in symbolic
forms by means of which men communicate, perpetuate, and develop their knowledge about
and attitudes toward life” (Geertz, 1973, p. 89). Rather than being a mere backdrop against
which to attribute events, behaviours, and the like, culture is a context within which symbols
can be thickly described (Geertz, 1973, p. 14).
The
term
"thickly"
holds
significant
weight
in this context. Geertz indeed devotes
considerable time explaining the difference between thin and thick descriptions, to the point
where it becomes central to theory. By “thick description”, Geertz refers to the deep, detailed
interpretation of the underlying meanings and significances of cultural symbols, as opposed
to mere factual accounts devoid of interpretation, which he terms “thin description” (Geertz,
1973; M. Martin, 1993).
His perspective on cultural analysis is inherently interpretative. Geertz challenges the
inflexibility of causal explanations, contending that genuine understanding springs not merely
from causation but from nuanced interpretation (Geertz, 1980; M. Martin, 1993). Such an
approach resonates with his vision of, in his own words, “nook-and-cranny anthropological
work”, where the objective is to “connect local findings with general considerations” (Geertz
et al., 1979). An example here is provided by his studies of Balinese culture through a deep,
extremely detailed exploration of the traditional Balinese cockfights observed in the village
he stayed in April 1958 (Geertz, 1973).
Nevertheless, despite its innovative nature and its success within anthropologists and
ethnographers, Geertz's methodology is not without critique. One significant contention
against his method is the challenge it poses in drawing generalizable conclusions
(Shankman et al., 1984). The focus on deep interpretative analysis implies that the
anthropologist is entangled in a web of particulars, rendering broader generalisations
potentially elusive. As Geertz himself acknowledged, this depth often requires one to

8.1.5 Howard S. Becker
Howard S. Becker was an American sociologist whose insights, especially in the domain of
labelling theory, offer a fresh perspective on the intricacies of deviance and subcultures
within the broader cultural framework.
Becker's conceptualisation of culture is particularly innovative. Contrasting with traditional
anthropological views, he posits that culture emerges as a byproduct of group interactions
(Becker, 2008). In Becker's view, culture doesn't exist as an independent entity but is rather
continually forged and reshaped by the collective actions of individuals within a group. He
also regards culture as a resource that individuals tap into to coordinate their activities,
emphasising its dynamic nature (Becker, 1982).
His conceptualisation appears clearer once we observe the example from which he starts
describing his theories: musicians being able to play instruments together despite meeting
only minutes before a concert. Culture, in this case, explains this concerted action as people
share understandings that allow them to behave collectively.
Another key theme in Becker's theory is, in fact, this continuous interaction. He underscores
this continuous process in which people create and change culture continuously adapting
their set of understandings to new situations.
Becker also incorporates Robert Redfield's definition of culture into his analysis. Redfield
describes culture as "conventional understandings made manifest in act and artifact"
(Redfield, 1940, as cited in Becker, 1982). This definition brings into focus the tangible
expressions of culture, conveying the idea that cultural norms and values are not merely
theoretical constructs but are manifest in everyday practices and material culture.
An additional innovative aspect of Becker’s theory is his recognition of subcultures. He
defines these as distinct cultural groups that “operate within, and in distinction to, the culture
of the larger society” (Becker, 1997).
While Becker's contributions to the field of culture are significant, it is important to note that
critiques of his work often centre around his labelling theory and studies on deviance.
However, these aspects are not the focus of this thesis and will be left aside in our
discussion.

8.1.6 Joanne Martin and Debra Meyerson
Joanne Martin is a scholar in the field of organisational studies, renowned for her extensive
work on organisational culture, and her contributions to gender and feminist research. Debra
Meyerson, similarly, is an academic, recognised for her work in education and organisational
studies. Meyerson's research particularly emphasises the role of diversity as a catalyst for
positive transformation within organisations.
In their seminal article, "Cultural Change: An Integration of Three Different Views" (1987),
Martin and Meyerson articulate their perspective on organisations and culture. For them,
cultures are patterns of meanings, values, and behaviours. Moreover, organisations are
considered cultures. Consequently, organisational change entails a transformation in these
underlying patterns. For instance, the structure of an organisation is considered “both a
manifestation of and constraint on organizational cognitions, values, and behaviour”
(Meyerson & Martin, 1987). While their initial discussion sets the stage by outlining their
perspective on culture, the core focus of the article shifts towards the methodologies through
which organisations navigate cultural change. It is within this exploration that the originality
of their approach shines through.
Martin and Meyerson delineate three distinct paradigms through which organisations
address cultural change, set apart by each paradigm's approach to ambiguit: the first
paradigm seeks to negate ambiguity, prioritising consistencies as the foundational elements
of culture; the second paradigm, in contrast, harnesses ambiguity, concentrating on the
dynamics that facilitate the emergence of both consistencies and inconsistencies; the final
paradigm embraces ambiguity, holding a singular value: the recognition of ambiguity as an
inextricable aspect of organisational life.
In their collaborative work, Martin and Meyerson posit that these perspectives are of equal
importance, with none superseding the others. Therefore, a holistic understanding of an
organisation’s culture necessitates the consideration of all three perspectives.
However, a primary critique of their framework centres on Martin's methodological approach.
While she adopts a subjectivist standpoint, there is a tendency in her work to revert to an
objectivist approach, particularly when she employs different perspectives to construct a
more objective depiction of culture (Mumby & Martin, 1994; Taylor et al., 2006).

8.1.7 Majken Schultz
Majken Schultz, a Danish academic, has made significant contributions to the field of
organisational dynamics, being involved both in the academic world and in the Danish
business community. Her research primarily focuses on branding and time management in
corporate environments (Hatch & Schultz, 2008).
Although Schultz does not provide her own definition of culture in the broader sense, she
distinguishes between organisational culture, identity, and imag. Organisational culture, as
she interprets it, comprises the collective values, beliefs, norms, and practices that guide
and regulate an organisation. On the other hand, organisational identity refers to the shared
perceptions among an organisation's members regarding its defining characteristics. This
identity is closely tied to the organisation's self-perception and the process of sensemaking
within it. Lastly, images represent the way the organisation is seen by external stakeholders,
and are considered projections of the organisational identity onto others (Ravasi & Schultz,
2021).
Moreover, Schultz elucidates how these notions interconnect and influence each other. She
explains that “when organizational members reflect on their identity, they do so with
reference to their organization’s culture and this embeds their reflections in tacit cultural
understandings. This embedding, in turn, allows culture to imbue identity artifacts with
meaning”. She also argues that claims made by organisational members about the
organisation itself are charged with cultural meanings. Additionally, she observes that
images
can arise both intentionally, through official communications like logos and
brochures, and unintentionally, via everyday behaviours such as gestures and appearances
(Hatch & Schultz, 2002).
These notions are especially important for this thesis, as they further underscore the
importance of detailed descriptions and if the analysis of communications in gathering
information about an organisation's culture.
Given that Schultz’s publications are relatively recent, a comprehensive literature review or
critique of her work is yet to be conducted. Nevertheless, it is noteworthy that her research
predominantly focuses on organisational culture within large entities, which may pose
challenges when directly applying her theories to smaller organisations, such as startups or
newly established companies.

8.1.8 Concluding remarks
At the end of this analysis, there is one question that is worth discussing: is culture the same
thing for all these authors, and they are giving different descriptions of it, or are they actually
talking about something different each time, but giving it the same name?
In my opinion, the culture they are describing is the same human phenomenon, but they are
analysing it from different points of view, from which they then derive different descriptions of
it.
This conclusion is particularly apparent at the moment we try to get a holistic view of it: we
can notice patterns between their descriptions, some characteristics are described in
different authors, and with them, some critiques are similarly shared.
Meyerson and Martin (1987) make it even more apparent, as in their analysis they do not
provide a different view of culture, but group and categorise the ones provided by fellow
researchers. Their different descriptions are the proverbial different faces of the same medal.
The importance of these authors therefore resides in the novelty they provide with their
analysis: Tylor highlights how cultures are built throughout time, Hofstede makes culture
measurable, Schein shows how some aspects are more observable than others; Geertz
underlines how important it is to explore in depth the community object of study, while
Becker further explores the subcultures enclosed in the main one. Finally, Schultz explores
the importance of how the community perceives itself and its communication with outsiders.
Furthermore, each scholar also brings new key concepts that are characteristic of their
different point of observation, and contribute to defining different aspects of culture. These
are what we will call for the rest of the thesis “cultural indicators”, which will help us describe
the culture in a specific environment, and are what we will be looking for while studying one.
The following chapter will focus on these identified indicators; while I just discussed each
author, and what their key teachings are, I will now study each indicator separately,
analysing them from the points of view of those same authors.

8.2.1 Behaviours
In the context of organisational culture, behaviours are the observable patterns in actions
and interactions among members of an organisation. These behaviours are deeply
embedded in the organisation's cultural fabric, influenced by a multitude of factors.
When examining behaviours, it is crucial to distinguish between those driven by external
forces, like pieces of legislation, and those rooted in the organisation's culture. However, this
distinction can often be subtle. For instance, construction workers wearing personal
protection equipment (PPE), such as helmets, might reflect adherence to safety regulations
rather than a cultural trait. Conversely, a widespread neglect of PPE use could suggest a
prevailing organisational culture of disregard for employees’ safety.
Geert Hofstede theorised various dimensions of culture that profoundly shape people's
behaviour within organisations. While Hofstede primarily focuses on describing and
predicting behavioural patterns, his dimensions – such as power distance, individualism
versus collectivism, and uncertainty avoidance – offer valuable insights into the underlying
cultural dynamics that drive these behaviours.
Edgar Schein positions behaviours within the “artefacts” layer of an organisation. According
to Schein (2017), while these behaviours are easily observable, they are significantly
influenced by deeper, underlying layers of an organisation's culture. This perspective aligns
closely with that of Clifford Geertz, who posits that “it is through the flow of behaviour that
cultural forms find articulation” and emphasises the importance of inspecting events to
comprehend the symbolic systems at play (Geertz, 1973, p. 17).
Howard Becker, on the other side, focuses more on how culture is constantly evolving due to
the collective actions of the members of a group. Furthermore, he argues that culture
emerges from group interactions (Becker, 2008). This is notable, as Schein’s and Becker’s
positions are opposite: Scheins sees behaviours as a consequence of culture, whereas
Becker explains how culture is born from collective behaviour.
While the study and mapping of behaviours necessitate direct observation of individuals
within the organisation, such analysis can lead us to infer another critical cultural indicator:
the acted values, which represent the principles and standards that are enacted in the daily
operations of an organisation.
8.2.2 Stated Values
Stated, or espoused, values are the explicitly expressed principles and standards that an
organisation claims to uphold. These values are typically articulated in corporate documents,
such
as
mission statements, vision statements, codes of ethics, and other formal
communications, serving as a public declaration of the organisation's ideals and aspirations.
Schein places stated values as the intermediate layer in his three-tier model of culture,
positioned between artefacts and underlying assumptions. According to him, understanding
these values requires either a significant duration of immersion within the culture, where the
values reveal themselves organically, or a more direct approach of asking members of the
organisation the reasons behind their actions (Schein, 2017, p. 29).
24
==PAGE:26==
Martin and Meyerson provide a critical perspective on stated values. They observe that, in
many cases, there is a noticeable discrepancy between the values publicly espoused by an
organisation and the behaviours that are actually observed within it (Meyerson & Martin,
1987, p. 630). This inconsistency often leads to the manifestation of acted values, the ones
that are truly practised and evident in daily operations. When employees perceive a gap
between stated and acted values, especially if the stated values are perceived as
inauthentic, it can result in disengagement and disillusionment.
The stated values of an organisation, much like its behaviours, cannot be completely inferred
from other indicators and need to be mapped by engaging directly with the individuals who
work in and represent the organisation. This involves not only conversations and interviews
but also careful observation of the messages conveyed to outsiders and the symbols used
for self-representation. This process reveals the interplay between stated values and other
cultural indicators, such as symbols and communication.
8.2.3 Acted Values
Acted values represent the values that are genuinely exhibited in an organisation's daily
operations, observable solely through these activities. As previously discussed, there is often
a disparity between these values and the ones explicitly espoused by a company, a
discrepancy that can be a source of internal tension.
Edgar Schein, views acted values as a critical lens through which the deepest layer of
culture can be discerned. These values are reflective of the underlying, often unconscious,
values held by employees. As such, they are a powerful indicator of the true cultural fabric of
an organisation.
The study of acted values requires a close observation of individual behaviours within the
organisation. This approach involves inferring acted values from the behaviours we have
previously studied. While they are closely related, acted values and behaviours are distinct
concepts: acted values are the foundational reasons behind the observed behaviours.
Furthermore, acted values become particularly salient when juxtaposed with stated values.
The incongruence between what is claimed and what is practised can lead to tension and
disengagement among employees, making this a critical area of study in organisational
culture.
In the context of this thesis, understanding the divergence between stated and acted values
is therefore crucial. By highlighting this dichotomy, the goal is to develop a model that
provides actionable insights for organisational leaders to foster an authentic and cohesive
organisational culture.
8.2.4 Symbols
Symbols are the tangible or visual expressions of an organisation's culture, encompassing
elements like logos, language, and rituals. These symbols carry specific meanings and are
critical in representing and communicating the underlying values and beliefs of an
organisation.
25
==PAGE:27==
Clifford Geertz places significant emphasis on symbols in his theory of culture, asserting that
“culture is the context within which symbols can be thickly described” (Geertz, 1973). This
perspective positions symbols as not just representations, but as rich sources of insight into
the cultural context. Majken Schultz further expands on the nature of symbols, stating that an
entity “becomes a symbol when it represents something more than itself” (Schultz, 1995, p.
76). Schultz also links symbols with meanings, noting that meanings are infused into
symbols during their creation by members of the organisation (Schultz, 1995, p. 77).
Finally, Edgar Schein views symbols as visible artefacts, which, to be fully understood,
require an in-depth comprehension of the cultural context they inhabit.
In my model, symbols serve as a key cultural indicator, aiding in the definition of other
indicators such as stated values and meanings. Symbols are utilised extensively both within
and outside the organisation as mediums of communication, targeting both internal members
and external audiences. Thus, deciphering the significance of the symbols employed by the
organisation under study is instrumental in understanding the espoused values and the
distinctive meanings that characterise its culture. This understanding can provide valuable
insights into how the organisation perceives itself and wishes to be perceived by others.
8.2.5 Meanings
Meanings within an organisation are patterns of interpretations and understandings that
individuals attribute to their experiences and to symbols used by the organisation and its
members. These interpretations are dynamic, continually evolving over time, and are shaped
by both external and internal influences.
Clifford Geertz, in his anthropological approach to culture, posits that the essence of cultural
analysis lies in deciphering these meanings (Geertz, 1973, p. 5). Joanne Martin and Debra
Meyerson further elaborate on this concept by characterising cultures as patterns of
meanings, values, and behaviours. They assert that organisational change is fundamentally
a transformation of these patterns (Meyerson & Martin, 1987, p. 624). From their
perspective, culture is primarily constituted by these shared meanings, making their
comprehension a pivotal aspect of understanding an organisation's culture.
In the context of my thesis, the concept of meanings emerges as a vital indicator. The
process of uncovering these meanings can be facilitated by examining the organisation's
symbols and engaging in sensemaking — the process through which individuals interpret
and make sense of their environment. Understanding this process of sensemaking is key to
comprehending
how
people
within
an
organisation
perceive
and
think
about their
environment and the broader cultural context.
8.2.6 Sensemaking
Sensemaking is the intricate process through which meanings within an organisation are
constructed and understood. Studying this process involves delving into the interpretations
and associations that employees have with their organisation, identifying the elements they
associate with these meanings, and understanding the resources they utilise to interpret the
symbols around them.
26
==PAGE:28==
In the framework of Clifford Geertz’s theory, where meanings are central, the process of how
these meanings emerge is equally crucial and needs to be studied thoroughly. Majken
Schultz, approaching from an organisational standpoint, underscores the significance of
sensemaking in its impact on decision-making processes (Schultz, 1995, p. 87), highlighting
the practical implications of sensemaking in everyday organisational operations, such as
legitimating the members' choice of action, helping the organisational culture structure
individual and social behaviour, and helping the organisational culture maintain its world
view.
As an indicator in my model, understanding sensemaking not only helps to identify the
existing meanings within the studied culture but also illuminates the processes and
resources involved in the formation of these meanings.
8.2.7 Storytelling and communication style with outsiders
Storytelling and external communication are pivotal in shaping the broader community's
perception of an organisation. Through the analysis of these elements, we can gain insights
into how an organisation intends to present itself to the outside world and into how it wishes
to be perceived.
Majken Schultz has extensively researched corporate branding, culture, identity, and their
interplay, including the role of communications. Her studies reveal that companies craft and
convey their desired image through both internal and external communication channels.
Schultz highlights the necessity of managing these communications effectively to foster a
positive company culture and create synergistic effects between individuals and departments
(Hatch & Schultz, 2002, 2008).
The communication style of a company is a significant cultural indicator. It reveals much
about the organisation's stated values, symbols, and meanings. This style encompasses not
only the content of communication but also its tone, medium, and context.
Like the other cultural indicators discussed in this thesis, the communication style cannot be
interpreted in isolation. It must be considered in conjunction with other indicators such as
stated values, symbols, and meanings to fully grasp its implications and the narrative it
weaves about the organisation. Understanding this interconnectedness is essential for
comprehending the complete picture of an organisation's culture and its impact on both
internal dynamics and external perceptions.
8.2.8 Patterns in subcultures
Organisations, particularly large ones, often encompass a diversity of subcultures, each with
its own distinct norms, values, and behaviours. By examining these subcultures collectively,
one can discern patterns and similarities that contribute to the understanding of the
overarching organisational culture. The interaction between these subcultures can foster
innovation and cultural change within the organisation.
Joanne Martin and Debra Meyerson's research is particularly insightful in the study of
subcultural patterns. They argue that organisational culture is not a monolithic entity but is
27
==PAGE:29==
composed of multiple, often intersecting subcultures. These subcultures can emerge from a
variety of factors, including professional backgrounds, departmental functions, or geographic
locations.
Edgar Schein echoes this perspective, noting that any group with a shared task or history
will develop its own subculture (Schein, 2017, p. 34). Clifford Geertz further elaborates that
each subculture possesses its unique set of symbols and meanings. Howard Becker also
acknowledges the presence of distinct internal groups, which operate both within and in
contrast to the broader organisational structure (Becker, 1997).

Ideally, espoused and acted values should align. However, as my theoretical analysis
highlighted, discrepancies between these two may be observed. Such discrepancies need to
be addressed as they can lead to employee disengagement and disillusionment, negatively
impacting both the quality of life and work performance.
In the initial design of the Values Segment, I included a section for mapping symbols and
meanings. Ultimately, I decided to remove this component and dedicate a separate segment
to it. This decision was made to ensure that symbols, meanings, and the process of
sensemaking, receive the attention they merit within the cultural mapping process as crucial
cultural indicators.

==PAGE:37==
9.3.3 Segment 3: Meanings
Figure 7. Meanings Segment Canvas
The final segment (Figure 7) of my cultural mapping model, referred to as the Meanings
Segment, is dedicated to mapping symbols, their associated meanings, and the processes
through which these meanings are formed and understood.
Drawing inspiration from the Culture Map Canvas developed by Dave Gray, which I also
employ in the Behaviour Segment, this segment allows users to identify and visually
represent relationships between different elements across its three layers. Each of these
layers is named as the cultural indicator it maps.
The top layer of the Canvas is reserved for symbols present within the organisation. These
can range from elements in official communications, such as logos and colour palettes, to
more informal internal practices, such as abbreviations of department names or role titles.
The middle layer delves into the meanings attributed to these symbols. A single symbol can
have multiple meanings, and often, the official interpretation proposed by the organisation
may differ from the perceptions held by individual members. This layer explores the diversity
of interpretations and understandings of the symbols identified.
Finally, the bottom layer focuses on sensemaking. This layer examines how individuals and
groups within the organisation construct and negotiate meanings, thereby contributing to the
overall cultural narrative.

9.4 How to use the model
9.4.1 General notes
The development of the model was specifically designed for use in workshop settings to
maximise employee engagement. Conducting these workshops with individual departments
ensures that the experience for the participants does not become too dispersed, enabling
each one to express themselves. At the same time, it allows for the collection of detailed
data about specific sections of the company. However, the application of this model goes
beyond these workshops, requiring the mapper to undertake additional analysis of the data
gathered from these sessions to craft a comprehensive overview of the organisation's
culture.
Moreover, to foster a safe environment where organisational members feel encouraged to
engage in an open and honest dialogue, the workshops should be conducted within
individual departments, intentionally excluding direct managers to eliminate any fear of
repercussions among participants. Leadership should participate in separate workshops,
enabling an understanding of the official vision for the company's future culture.
The distinction between leadership and operational staff in these workshops is crucial for two
primary reasons. Firstly, it facilitates the collection of more accurate data. Secondly, it
enriches the model's comparative aspect, offering actionable insights for potential cultural
change initiatives. By comparing the culture aspired to by leadership with the culture actually
present within the organisation, the mapper can pinpoint areas requiring action.
One last note needs to be made about what should be recorded into the canvases. This
study assumes that all observations made during the cultural mapping process constitute
valid knowledge, even if these observations are at times contradictory. This reflects a belief
in the multifaceted and subjective nature of culture, where differing perspectives can coexist
and provide a better understanding of the whole culture object of study.
37
==PAGE:39==
9.4.2 Segment 1: Behaviours
The first segment of the cultural mapping model utilises the Culture Map Canvas. My
approach to using this canvas reflects my personal experience and insights gained from its
application in various settings.
To begin using the Culture Map Canvas, start with a brainstorming session focused on the
work environment. Encourage participants to freely share anything they perceive as relevant,
be it specific behaviours, observed outcomes, or challenges they face. It's important to
ensure that contributions are specific rather than general statements, as this will aid in
identifying possible opportunities for improvement.
Following the brainstorming, examine the collected ideas and categorise them into the three
layers of the Canvas: Behaviours, Outcomes, and Enablers/Blockers. This step involves a
discussion with participants to understand the connections between these elements. The
goal is to not only classify the information but also to stimulate further discussion that might
reveal additional insights.
The outcome of this process should be a Culture Map Canvas filled with detailed
information, often illustrating direct relationships between behaviours, their causes, and their
effects. While not every piece of information will necessarily show a clear connection, most
should reveal some type of relationship that can be acted upon for future improvement.
In parallel to this process, leadership needs to use the Canvas to outline the behaviours they
aspire to see within the organisation. Leaders should fill in the Canvas based on their vision
for the future, which will then serve as a guide for identifying areas that need attention in the
present to achieve the desired future state.
9.4.3 Segment 2: Values
The segment dedicated to mapping values within the organisation is structured into two
distinct sections: Acted Values and Stated Values. Each section requires a different
approach to filling it out, and they are designed to be completed separately to enable a
comprehensive analysis of the organisation's values.
Located on the left side of the canvas, the Acted Values section should be filled out after
completing the Behaviour mapping segment. The behaviours observed during the previous
workshop are instrumental in identifying the underlying values that drive the actions of
individuals in the organisation. This section is completed by paying close attention to how
individuals describe situations or observations, especially during discussions in the previous
workshop. These self-descriptions are key in unveiling the values that are enacted in their
day-to-day interactions and decision-making processes.
On the right side of the canvas, the Stated Values section is filled by examining official
communications from the organisation and its leaders. This can include public statements,
internal memos, marketing materials, and any other form of communication that reflects the
organisation's officially endorsed values. Additionally, working directly with leaders to
38
==PAGE:40==
understand their perspective on the organisation's values is crucial. This collaboration helps
to ensure that the stated values are accurately captured and represented.
The critical step in this segment is the comparison between the two sections, which should
be conducted layer-by-layer (Acted Values against Stated Values, Self-Descriptions against
Desired External Perception). This comparison serves to highlight any discrepancies or
misalignments between the values that are openly declared by the organisation and its
leadership, and those that are actually practised and observed among its members.
Identifying such misalignments is key to understanding the dynamics of the organisational
culture and can provide a basis for targeted interventions to align these values more closely.
9.4.4 Segment 3: Meanings
The final segment of the model, focused on meanings, is structured into three
parts: Symbols, Meanings, and Sensemaking. This segment mirrors the design of the
Behaviours Segment, with layers that allow users to interrelate multiple elements.
Although users can begin filling out this canvas from any of the three sections, it is generally
advisable to start with the Symbols section as Symbols are typically the most readily
identifiable of the three indicators in this segment. Users should populate the Symbols
section by identifying a range of elements such as logos, imagery, abbreviations, and signs
commonly used within the organisation. These symbols are often designed to convey
messages or values beyond their immediate appearance.
The middle layer, Meanings, involves capturing the interpretations and explanations that
both individuals and the organisation as a whole attribute to the symbols previously
identified. A single symbol can have multiple meanings, reflecting the diverse perspectives of
different members of the organisation. Furthermore, this layer should annotate both the
meanings attributed by individuals working in the organisation to the identified symbols, and
the meanings originally intended by leadership.
Finally, the Sensemaking layer delves into the processes through which the meanings of
symbols are formed and understood. Each meaning identified in the previous section should
have a corresponding entry in the Sensemaking section, explaining how that particular
interpretation came to be.
In this segment, actionable insight is provided through the comparison between the official
intended meaning of the identified symbols, and the explanations provided by members of
the organisation. If discrepancies emerge, leadership can then decide to tackle them by
acting on factors highlighted by the sensemaking layer.

As previously acknowledged, the main limitation of the model I developed is its lack of
testing and feedback from real-world users. Since the inspiration for developing this model
came from my experiences at the London office of a multinational trade insurance company,
I have chosen to apply it retrospectively to this particular setting. Although this approach
introduces its own set of limitations, it provides a starting point for reflection and offers
examples of how the model might be used.
One significant limitation is that this analysis is being conducted years after the original
workshops took place, which impacts its immediacy and level of detail. Most of the analysis
relies on my personal recollections, inherently limiting the accuracy of the information.
Additionally, the model was designed to be used with individuals actively involved in the
organisation. However, in this case, the application is based on personal notes and
memories from past cultural mapping exercises, rather than direct engagement with
participants. Consequently, any results of this analysis will not have a tangible effect on the
organisation under study, as they are retrospective and meant for exploratory purposes.

From the Behaviours Segment, we observe discrepancies between the behaviours currently

retain talent.
However, the behaviours presently observed in the company's culture starkly contrast with

managers seemingly more concerned with "ticking boxes" than engaging with feedback.

10 Discussion and conclusion
10.1 Short model’s summary
This thesis introduces a novel model for cultural mapping, drawing upon the seminal works
of scholars within both organisational culture (Hofstede, Schein, Martin, and Schultz) and
broader cultural studies (Tylor, Geertz, Becker).
Each of these scholars brings an innovative point of view to the table, which leads them to
define culture in different ways. Through the analysis of these different cultural definitions, I
have identified a list of cultural indicators. These indicators not only guide the mapping
process by highlighting key aspects to observe but are also interrelated, allowing for the
inference of certain indicators through the analysis of others.
The model is structured around the identification and mapping of these cultural indicators,
utilising a series of canvases designed to capture them.
The first canvas (developed by Dave Gray in collaboration with Alexander Osterwalder and
Yves Pigneur) focuses on mapping individual behaviours within the organisation, their
causes, and their effects. It specifically addresses the behavioural indicator.
The second canvas explores the values that define an organisation's culture and has been
designed by me based on the Value Proposition Canvas. Juxtaposing the acted and stated
values of an organisation, it maps four different indicators: acted and stated values,
self-descriptions, and storytelling.
The third and last canvas maps the meanings that are lived within the organisation. The
Meanings Canvas has been designed by adapting the first canvas, as it provides an easy
way of visualising the relationship between elements. Dividing them into three layers through
which the user can draw relationships, this canvas maps the following indicators: symbols,
meanings, and sense-making.
The essence of this model lies in its comparative approach, present across all canvases. It
seeks to highlight discrepancies between the leaders' aspirations and the reality as
perceived by organisational members. By doing so, the model offers actionable insights,
enabling organisations to bridge gaps between desired and actual culture, and fostering a
deeper understanding of their internal cultural dynamics.

own insights, highlighting the model's reliance on direct engagement with organisation
members for comprehensive and meaningful cultural mapping.
Moreover, this exercise prompts further inquiry into alternative data sources for mapping and
the potential impact of recent shifts towards hybrid and remote work environments.

changing work dynamics and exploring ethical data use.
10.3 Practical considerations on the model and its limitations
As this thesis draws to a close, it is crucial to reflect on the practical implications and
limitations that characterise the cultural mapping model presented, alongside considerations
for its future enhancement.
The model is primarily designed for workshop settings, offering the greatest value when
utilised as intended. Its application in alternative contexts, as demonstrated by my
retrospective analysis, suggests flexibility but also underscores the need for further testing.
Such testing should aim to validate both the model and the identified cultural indicators’
effectiveness.
Furthermore, the model targets medium to large-scale organisations, given its structure
around conducting multiple workshops across various departments to achieve a holistic view
of an organisation's culture. This renders the model less suitable for smaller organisations
like startups, which typically exhibit flatter hierarchies and less defined departmental roles. In
these contexts, the comparison element integral to the model's canvases may not yield
significant insights due to the overlapping nature of leadership and operational functions.

Despite these considerations, the model's division into three distinct canvases focusing on
behaviours, values, and meanings offers flexibility. This design allows users to either employ
the canvases collectively for a comprehensive cultural assessment or individually to map
specific aspects of culture. This adaptability caters to varied research interests and the
potential integration with other tools.
Regardin the model's limitations, the most significant is its current lack of real-world testing
and
feedback.

to

basis of the feedback gathered.
Moreover, the model suffers from the inherent limitations of the mapping process itself.
As outlined in the chapter on cultural indicators, mapping inherently involves subjectivity, as
the map maker's depiction of reality is influenced by their personal values, beliefs, and
biases (Blomkamp, 2015; Harley, 1989; Wright, 1942). Furthermore, the map maker chooses
how to create the map, and what to include or exclude in the creation process. This is
significant because, as previously mentioned, in this study there are two map makers
involved, and both need to acknowledge what is being excluded from the map and why
(Harley, 1989). Despite this, the value of cultural maps in facilitating strategic planning,
community engagement, and cultural inventory creation remains undiminished. These
limitations do not detract from the mapping’s utility but rather highlight the complexity of the
cultural analysis being done.
Lastly, the selection of scholars and cultural indicators, while informed by academic and
practical considerations that emerged from interviews with industry professionals, is
inherently limited and influenced by personal choice. Future research could expand upon
this foundation by exploring additional or alternative academic works, leading to the
identification of new indicators and potentially inspiring the development of models better
suited to different contexts, such as small-scale organisations, or approaches focused on
quantitative data analysis.

10.4 Conclusion
This thesis set out with the aim of exploring how can organisational culture be effectively
mapped to generate actionable insights.
As mentioned in the introduction, this research question can be split into two subquestions:
1.
How can organisational culture be mapped?
2.
How can we generate actionable insights?
The answer to these questions, summarised here, is implicitly answered in detail throughout
my thesis.
Organisational culture can be mapped with the newly developed model I developed as part
of this thesis. Particularly, while mapping organisational culture (and, more generally, culture)
we are looking for specific cultural indicators, identified by looking at patterns observable in
the definition of culture given by seminal scholars. The model developed provides a
framework to map these indicators in a workshop setting, visualising them in easily
understandable canvases on which the various cultural elements, and their relationships,
can be drawn.
Actionable insight is generated by the element of comparison present in all three canvases I
described as part of the model: the Behaviours Canvas compares behaviours observed in
operational staff to the ideal behaviours envisioned by leadership; the Values Canvas
reveals the gap between the values enacted by individuals in their daily interactions and
those proclaimed by the organisation through its formal communications; lastly, the
Meanings Canvas juxtaposes the interpretations employees ascribe to workplace symbols
with those intended by leadership, alongside the processes and resources through which
those interpretations come into existence.
I began this thesis with an epigraph from a book written by Frank Herbert, "Dune". This
quote is from a passage that describes a dialogue between two characters, where one
explains to the other that a competent leader "must learn to persuade and not to compel",
and to understand "the language of the rocks and growing things, the language you don’t
hear just with your ears".
The initial motivation behind this study was to explore strategies for better managing culture,
with the aim of enhancing the conditions for those working within organisations. We have
observed how an organisation’s culture – its own "language of the rocks and growing things"
– pervades every aspect of its members' daily lives. A healthier working environment leads
to improved quality of work and life, which are intrinsically linked and mutually reinforcing.
This positive cycle must begin somewhere, and it is my hope that this study can contributes
to that.
