Abstract 
Finding differences in the gene repertoire among bacteria in a strain collection of interest, is important for 
understanding fitness and adaption during a hospital outbreak in a selective hospital environment. In this 
project we studied two outbreak populations with the aim of evaluating the application of combined Illumina 
and Nanopore sequencing data with hybrid assembly, for more precise determination of gene content 
changes in a hospital setting, and to gain better detection of events of transmission between species in 
outbreak situations.  
The two strains collection included MRSA (n=20) and LRSE (n=13) strains obtain for clinical samples at 
Rigshospitalet, Copenhagen Denmark. 8 strains MRSA (n=4) and LRSE (n=8) were selected for Nanopore 
sequencing.  
The results showed the hybrid assemblies data could resolve doubt of whether genes were located on 
plasmid or chromosomes, better than Illumina data alone and gave a better detection of transmission events 
during these outbreaks.

Introduction 
 
Nosocomial infections  
Large quantities of antibiotics used in human medicine and agriculture have accelerated the development of 
antibiotic resistance, due to selective pressure. The emergence of antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is an 
aspect of bacterial evolution due to excessive consumption of antibiotics, and together with the lack of 
discoveries of new antimicrobials, makes the fight against AMR one of the biggest challenges facing modern 
medicine. Antibiotic resistance is increasingly threatening treatment of severe bacterial infections, and 
infections caused by drug-resistant bacteria is a major threat to public health in modern medicine (1)(2). 
Nosocomial infections are defined as infections that appear in patients under medical care that are absent at 
the time of admission to the hospital (3). Pathogens causing nosocomial infections include bacteria, fungi, 
and virus, of which antibiotic resistant bacteria have a high prevalence. The most frequent types of 
nosocomial infections include bloodstream infections, catheter associated urinary tract infection, skin and 
soft tissue infections at surgical site, diarrhea, and ventilator associated pneumonia. Infections can either be 
caused by the patients own microbiota (translocation), occur between patients (patient-to-patient 
transmission) or be contracted from the hospital, either from a colonized healthcare worker or the 
environment. The recovery and outcome of the patient varies, dependent on the pathogen and the severity 
of illness, but nosocomial infections are associated with prolonged hospital stay, long term disability, and 
increased mortality rate. Many nosocomial infections can be prevented by infection prevention practices, 
such as increased hygiene, stringent decontamination of medical devices, and with antimicrobial 
stewardship (3)(4). 
 
Surveillance  
Monitoring the spread of antibiotic-resistant bacteria is an essential tool to reduce the spread of multi-
resistant strains in hospital settings, and understanding the bacterial community structure in the hospital 
environment, is fundamental for formulating new approaches to reduce the spread of nosocomial 
pathogens.  
A hospital acquired infection outbreak is defined as a significant increase of infection rate or as two or more 
related cases. Furthermore, it is characterized by many episodes of common infections, such as surgical site 
infections or a higher rate of infection compared to background rate (5). Fast identification of the infecting 
pathogen and source of the infection is important in outbreak containment. Surveillance protocols include 
data collection, demographic risk factors, patients’ history, and diagnostic test (3). The antibiotic resistance

surveillance is conducted by systematic screening, collection and typing of predefined resistant phenotypes. 
At the Department for Clinical Microbiology, Rigshospitalet, screening is initiated by detection of certain 
resistant phenotypes in clinical samples that activates screening of patients and staff at the affected ward.  
Whole-genome sequencing (WGS) is a broadly used approach to determine strain relatedness in outbreak 
detection. This sequencing technique allows for high resolution typing of bacteria strains, providing 
information on genetic relatedness of isolates in epidemiological studies (6). At the Department for Clinical 
Microbiology, Rigshospitalet, Illumina sequencing is implemented for surveillance of antibiotic resistance 
genes and genotyping of strains.  
There are different approaches to analyze genetic relatedness between isolates and currently the approach 
that provides the highest discriminatory power is WGS (7). The WGS data is used to construct phylogenies 
either based on Core Genome Multi-Locus Typing (cgMLST) or Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNP) 
variant calling (8). Both methods can be used to distinguish outbreak-related isolates from isolates that are 
not a part of the outbreak. However, SNPs offer a more sensitive and accurate distinction. (8). 
cgMLST is a gene-by-gene typing method that assesses the loci of a fixed set of conserved genes that are 
present in all isolates in a particular genus or species (7). New isolates are compared against a database of all 
known gene variants to assess the relatedness of all isolates belonging to a given species, and the strains are 
characterized by a profile of allele number (7) and differences between isolates are calculated as allele 
differences.  
SNP analysis is based on observed number of single nucleotide differences between isolates in comparison 
to a single reference genome (8), and observed number of SNPs determine strain relatedness in outbreak 
studies. By comparing nucleotides of different strains, it is possible to construct a distance matrix and 
determine numbers of SNPs between isolates that can be used to generate a phylogenetic tree (9). In 
investigations performed in multiple locations, SNP analyses need to be performed with a single reference 
genome (7) whereas results from cgMLST can be compared from different locations.  
Phylogenetic trees are a visual representation of the genetic relationship between species. Whole genome 
data can be used to create trees based on SNP calling, K-mer overlap, or alignment of genes and genomes. 
For outbreak investigation, SNP and cgMLST is used to create phylogenetic trees (9).

making S. epidermidis an important nosocomial infection in immunocompromised patients and in patients 
with catheters or implants (10). The prevalence of linezolid resistance is low, but several outbreaks and

Staphylococcus aureus  
S. aureus is a Gram-positive, catalase- and coagulase positive cocci, arranged in clusters, and it is a part of 
the normal flora of the skin and mucosa. It is estimated that over 50% of the population at one point will be 
colonized with S. aureus in their nose or throat (12) (13). S. aureus can cause a broad range of infections, 
from superficial skin infections to more complicated infections such as bacteremia, endocarditis, and 
pneumonia (13). Methicillin resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) is resistant to all beta-lactam antibiotics, 
including methicillin (13). Hospital settings provide good conditions for efficient spread of MRSA, mostly 
through physical contact with infected individuals, by surfaces, and other objects. MRSA infections are 
routinely detected among hospitalized patients and it is an important healthcare associated pathogen

Genomic adaption  
There is a significant variation in genome size within prokaryotes genera and species, and acquiring novel 
genes is a major event that drives bacteria adaptation and evolution (15)(16). The core genome includes all 
essential genes needed to maintain bacterial life and are shared by all strains of the same species. Therefore, 
these genes are likely to encode for functions related to basic cellular biology. Pan- or accessory genome, 
provide diversity of additional traits for the bacteria and includes non-essential genes. These genes are 
therefore likely to include functions that confer specific advantages under certain environmental conditions 
(7)(15)(17). A comparative analysis based on genome sequencing of 64 strains of S. aureus revealed a core

genome composed of 1.441 genes, and a pan genome of 7.457 genes. Furthermore, on average for each 
strain, 56% of the genes were part of the core genome (17). 
Horizontal gene transfer (HGT) 
 
Acquiring new genes is accomplished by horizontal gene transfer (HGT). HGT is the transfer of genetic 
material between two or more organisms that can occur continually throughout the organisms’ lifetimes. 
Gene function may also be lost by modification of existing genes (18). Mobile genetic elements (MGEs) are 
DNA sequences that can jump or be mobilized from one strand of DNA to another. There are different types 
of MGEs involved in HGT, including Gene Cassettes and Integrations, Insertion sequences (IS) and 
Transposons, plasmids, genomic islands, and phages (18). IS elements are the smallest and simplest MGEs 
with a length of 1-3 kb and always encode a transposase protein required for transposition (18). 
Genes on plasmids constitute a major part of the variable bacterial genetic content. They are small circular 
strands of DNA that replicate independently from the host. A plasmid contains an origin of replication (ORI) 
and encode proteins that are beneficial for the host, such as resistance genes or genes encoding virulence 
factors. Plasmids vary in size from a few thousand to hundreds of thousands of base pairs (bp), and they lack 
extracellular form (19)(20). 
There are three main mechanisms for horizontal DNA transfer in bacteria; conjugation, transformation, and 
transduction (19). 
 
Conjugation, mediated by plasmids. Bacterial conjugation is the process where a bacterium transfers genetic 
material to another cell by direct cell-to-cell contact. The donor cell produces a pilus that attaches to the 
recipient cell, bringing the two cells together (19)(16), thus allowing for the plasmid to be transferred to the 
recipient cell. Conjugation occurs with high frequency among the same species and can transfer many 
resistance genes and virulence factors at the same time.  
 
Transformation, uptake of extracellular DNA. In transformation, a bacteria cell takes up DNA from the 
environment. Transformation occurs inter-species and can transfer many resistance genes and virulence 
factors (16). This type of gene transfer requires a competent host (19). Free extracellular DNA (exDNA) can 
persist after disinfection and promote horizontal transfers of resistance genes from wastewater (21).  
 
Transduction mediated by bacteriophages. In transduction, viruses infect bacteria and move chromosomal 
DNA from one bacterium to another. This type of gene transfer only occurs intra-species as phages are

species specific, and this type of gene transfer normally includes few resistance genes and virulence factors 
(19).  
Incorporation of genes from mobile elements to the host organism genome occurs via recombination. There 
are two types of recombination: Homologous and Site-specific. Homologous recombination occurs between 
sequences that are nearly identical, and Site-specific recombination occurs between sequences with a 
limited stretch of similarity and involves a specific site on the genome and an enzyme called a transposase to 
catalyze the process. Transposons cannot transfer by themselves and therefore require transfer by 
transformation or transduction. Transposons can also by insert into plasmids and from there they can be 
transferred to recipient cells by conjugation (19).  
Homologous recombination requires a high degree of homology, or at least 500 bp on each side with 95-100 
% DNA homology. This type of gene transfer therefore requires closely related bacteria with highly identical 
genes. Transduction requires homologous recombination with the bacterial chromosome (19). IS elements 
can through insertion either increase the expression of intrinsic resistance genes or inactivate a gene that is 
required for antibiotic susceptibility (18) and they move by site-specific recombination. 
Gene gain and gene loss analysis by GenAPI 
 
GenAPI is an online command-line tool designed to identify and compare the gene content in bacterial 
clones and identifying genes that are lost or acquired in the genome. It works by finding open reading frames 
in annotated genome assemblies. The program is designed to account for highly fragmented assemblies or 
when gene assembly is split to more than one contig. This is obtained by lowering the requirement for 
sequence alignment length, thereby including partly assembled genes. This lowers the risk of false gene 
absence predictions, while maintaining true gene absence call rates. A gene is defined as present if there’s 
detection of a minimum of 25% coverage with 98% identity or with 50% coverage and 90% identity. This 
assumes that there is a low likelihood of having random, high identity alignment on an average length gene. 
The minimum length requirement for the gene to be included in the gene presence-absence matrix is 150 bp 
(22). GenAPI generates a heatmap visualization of gene presence-absence across the included genomes. 
 
Whole genome sequencing (WGS) 
Understanding colonization, infections, and transmission routes of AMR in hospital settings is a crucial step 
toward implementation of strategies to limited nosocomial infections. The genomic structure is relevant 
when studying antimicrobial resistance in outbreak investigation as the location of resistance genes can have

important epidemiological implications (20). Data from Illumina short reads sequencing can reveal details 
about the genomic content of at given bacteria isolate, but the read lengths are insufficiently large to cover 
mobile elements in the genome, including ribosomal RNA operons and multicopy transposons, especially due 
to repetitive sequences of transposases and IS elements (23). 
Fully resolving the bacterial genome requires long-read sequencing. Hence, combining Illumina and ONT 
reads can fully resolve most genomes (24).  
Illumina sequencing 
Illumina sequencing uses a principle called sequencing-by-synthesis, which means that the sequence of the 
DNA strand is detected while the complementary DNA strand is being synthesized. The detection of the 
nucleotides is accomplish by the incorporation of modified fluorescently labeled deoxyribonucleotide 
triphosphates (dNTP), known as reversible terminators, during sequential cycles of DNA synthesis (25). 
During each cycle, the nucleotides are identified by fluorophore excitation and the color of a dNTP is 
recorded before the fluorophore is washed away (25).  
Illumina sequencing workflow involves four steps; library preparation, cluster generation, sequencing, 
alignment, and data analysis. A library is prepared by fragmenting DNA and ligating adapters to both 
fragment ends. The adapters contain sequences that allow for binding to the flow cell, the sequencing 
primer, and barcode sequence. During cluster amplification, a library is loaded into a flow cell and the 
fragments hybridize to the flow cell surface, and then the fragments are amplified into a clonal cluster 
through bridge amplification. Sequencing is conducted at described above, where sequencing reagents are 
added (including fluorescently labeled modified dNTPs), and the first base is incorporated. Subsequently, the 
cycle is repeated as many times as needed to create the read length of choice.  
Illumina produces read sizes of up to 300 bases, with an average coverage of 30 to 100 reads per base and is 
currently the most widely used sequencing platform for genome sequencing (23). 
Nanopore sequencing 
The nanopore consists of transmembrane cellular proteins inserted into a synthetic polymer membrane 
which exhibits a high electric resistance. The system contains an electrolytic solution and when an electric 
field is applied, the solution generates a current and the solution flows though the pore. Single molecules 
entering the nanopore cause characteristic disruption in the current, proportional to the size and chemical 
property of the nucleotide base. By measuring that current, the molecule can be identified, and the base 
sequence of the strand can be inferred. To ensure a controlled passage of the DNA strand through the 
nanopore, DNA fragments need to be modified by ligation of sequencing adapters. This process involves a 
loaded enzyme, also referred to as a motor protein. The sequence adapter contains a domain for tether

binding, which is required for attaching the DNA fragment to the membrane (25). Oxford Nanopore 
Technologies (ONT) platforms generate long DNA fragments with average read length from about 10 kb or 
longer, and can in theory produce complete genome assemblies, revealing the full structure of a bacterial 
genome (25), but the reads are more error-prone than Illumina sequencing. Data from ONT are therefore 
generally not used alone for a reliable identification or mapping outbreaks of AMR bacteria in outbreak 
investigation (7). 
Hybrid assembly  
 
Unicycler is a hybrid assembly tool for bacterial isolate genomes that produces accurate assemblies from 
sequencing data from both Nanopore and Illumina sequencing approaches (26). Short reads are first 
assembled into an accurate and connected assembly graph, and then the long reads are used to correct the 
graph. The program uses long reads of any depth and quality in hybrid assembly. Ten times or more read 
depth may be required to complete a genome, but Unicycler can make nearly complete genomes with far 
fewer long reads. This results in effective use of fewer long reads that are more error prone than short reads. 
With a fully closed genome, genetic adaption or rearrangement can be studied in detail, for example in 
longitudinal studies or in closely related isolates where adaption is suspected over time.

understanding fitness and adaption during a hospital outbreak in a selective hospital environment.  
In this project we studied two outbreak populations with the aim of evaluating the application of combined 
Illumina and Nanopore sequencing data with hybrid assembly, for more precise determination of gene 
content changes in a hospital setting, and to gain better detection of events of transmission between species 
in outbreak situations. Sequencing data from short reads alone, were also compared with the results from 
hybrid assembly data.

Bacterial isolates, when received were kept in a 15% glycerol stock at -80 C, were cultured and incubated for 
24 or 48 hours, on 5 % blood agar, under aerobic condition, at 37 °C. Approximately 10 µl colony material 
from each culture was harvested and DNA extraction was conducted.  
Illumina library preparation and sequencing  
Illumina: Total DNA was purified using DNeasy Blood and Tissue DNA kit (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany) following 
manufacturer’s instruction. The genome of all the isolates were subjected to whole-genome sequencing on a 
Miseq Instrument (Illumina), based on paired-end 2X250 bp Illumina libraries.   
In total, 12 isolates (8 LRSE- and 4 MRSA) were identified for further investigation with Nanopore 
sequencing, based on Heatmap analysis for gene absence/presence, using GenAPI on the annotated Illumina 
genomes (22).  
MinION library preparation and sequencing  
Nanopore; Total DNA was purified using Circulomics protocol Nanobind UHMW DNA Extraction-Gram-
Positive Bacteria Protocol (document ID; EXT-GPU-001) according to manufacturer’s instructions with 
modifications. These modifications included using only halve the amount of PBS at the beginning of the 
protocol. All other substrates were halved for the remainder of the protocol, including EB buffer at the end 
of the protocol. Briefly, approximately 10 µl colony material was resuspended in PBS and thoroughly mixed. 
Hereafter, enzymes were added, and the suspension was gently mixed in between steps of adding and 
incubating, by pipetting to ensure homogeneous samples. After the cells were lysed, a Nanobind disk that 
captures free DNA was added and hereafter isopropanol, and the tubes were mixed by inverting for 10 min. 
The tubes were then placed on a magnetic tube rack for washing the solution. Finally, the DNA was eluted in 
EB buffer and incubated with the Nanobind disk overnight. Next day the eluate was transferred into a new 
tube and left for 2 hours to rest before the DNA could be used for further investigation.

Before sequence library preparations, DNA quantity was assessed using Qubit fluorometer (Thermo Fisher 
Scientific). Briefly, the eluate was measured, using 2 µl DNA in 198 µl Qubit working solution. For a more 
precise measurement of DNA quantity, triplicates (X3) of a 1:10 dilution of eluates was measured. The three 
measurements were used to obtain the exact quantity to transfer to a new tube to obtain 400 ng of DNA 
template.    
Sequencing library preparation was carried out with Rapid Barcoding Sequencing kit (SQK-RBK004, Oxford 
Nanopore Technologies, Ltd. Oxford UK) with the native barcoding expansion kit (EXP-NBD103), according to 
manufactures instructions. Briefly, a total of 7,5 µl of 400 ng template DNA for each sample was mixed with 
2,5 µl barcodes. After adapter ligation, 9 µl barcoded DNA from each isolate was pooled, and the reactions 
were purified using AMPure XP beads. A total volume of 11 µl of pooled barcoded library was loaded onto a 
MinION Flow cell and sequenced for 24 hours on a MinION Mk1C device with default settings. The base 
calling was performed on Mk1C with Basecall Model “Fast basecalling”.  
Hybrid Assembly 
For combining the Illumina data with Nanopore data we used a pipeline developed by Department of 
Genomic Medicine called Nanogem. Nanogem first assembles the genome with Unicycler 
(https://github.com/rrwick/Unicycler) (26) applying Spades V.3.13.0 followed by bridging over contigs with 
long reads.  The genome is finished by 10 rounds of polishing using Pilon. Nanogem then performs BLAST in 
order to confirm the species, annotates the genome with Prokka V. 1.14.0 and runs abricate V.0.9.8 with 
Resfinder database and finally visualizes the genome assembly with Bandage, a bioinformatic tool for 
creating interactive visualizations of assembly graphs, using default parameters 
(https://github.com/rrwick/Bandage)(27) 
A 100% complete genome assembly results in one contig per chromosome or plasmid (26). Unicycler 
performs a SPAdes assembly (28) of the Illumina reads and then scaffolds the assembly graph using long 
reads. Briefly, assembly graph construction (De Bruijn graph), followed by k-bimer (pairs of k-mers) 
adjustment, then, construction of paired assembly graph, and lastly, construction of contigs (28). Unicycler 
was run with default settings, no filtering of reads and in conservative mode. Finally, the genomes were 
polished.  
Bandage, a bioinformatic tool for creating interactive visualizations of assembly graphs, was used for 
visualization, using default parameters (https://github.com/rrwick/Bandage)(27).

Analysis of shared and unique genes  
 
Illumina sequencing results were assembled and heatmaps were created for analyses of gene 
absence/presence, using GenAPI with default settings (https://github.com/MigleSur/GenAPI) (22). Heatmaps 
were visually inspected for variations in gene content, respectively, for the two strains’ collection. 
Geneious Prime, a bioinformatics software platform for molecular biology and sequence analyses, was used 
for inspecting the gene content. It applies Basic Local alignment Search Tool (BLAST) to search GenBank files 
(protein sequences) against sequences stored in various databases at National Centre for Biotechnology 
Information (NCBI). (http://www.ncbi.nih.gov/GenBank).  
Genes that varied in the two strain collections were inspected with annotated files to determine function 
and placement of the gene for being either on plasmid, transposons, or chromosomes, using  Geneious 
Prime, using BLAST of loci tags, where genes were present in some but not all strains.  
In total 12 isolates (8 LRSE and 4 MRSA) were identified for further investigation with Nanopore sequencing, 
based on heatmap analysis. This isolate represented the different variations in gene content, detected in the 
strain collection, revealed by WGS data analysis.  
Hybrid assembly sequencing results were assembled and new heatmaps were created for the respective 
collections, using GenAPI with default settings (22) and gene variation was investigated as described above, 
by visualizing and using BLAST of genes for functions and placement either on MGE or chromosomes.  
And lastly, the result from the two, gene content analyses, was compared.

Discussion 
Understanding the evolution and spread of resistant bacteria during hospital-acquired outbreaks, is 
important for understanding transmission of AMR within a selective hospital environment.  
Novel diagnostic methods have enabled a more efficient determination of transmission events among 
bacteria. Here, we evaluated the application of a hybrid assembly tool, Unicycler, to obtain finished genomes 
and circularized plasmids, using both short reads from Illumina sequencing and long reads from nanopore 
sequencing. 
For the MRSA outbreak, investigation revealed two main genomic events, one in February 2021, where 
strain nr. 11 acquired a plasmid that is shared by all strains in the collection hereafter, except strain 15. The 
second event is an incorporation of an IS6 element into the chromosome, found in three stains. Only one 
strain in the dataset contained genes from both events. 
For the first event, a stretch of about 30 genes is apparent in nine strains, and the results from analyzing the 
Illumina assembly data, shows two possible explanations, either a mobile element is incorporated into the 
chromosome or a plasmid is gained.  
The hybrid assembly data resolved this uncertainty because it shows that all the genes are located on one 
contig and the hybrid assembly graph shows a closed plasmid. Thus, the hybrid assembly supports the 
conclusion that these genes are located on a plasmid, and that a plasmid is gained in the population in 
February 2021.  
For the second event of the MRSA collection, a segment of 20 genes was located on 3 of the 20 strains, 
strain 7, 15, and 20. These genes were located on contig 1, and BLAST hits gave best results with 
chromosomal genes, including the (aph(3) Illa) gene that confers kanamycin resistance. Additionally, an IS6 
like element was found at the beginning and end of the segment of 20 genes, consistent with the likely event 
of a transposon getting incorporated into the chromosome. Illumina data showed ermC, conferring 
kanamycin resistance, and the segment of 20 genes on two different contigs. However, the hybrid assembly 
showed ermC and the 20 genes to be located on the same contig. Thus, the hybrid assembly data supports 
the notion that that these genes were acquired together.  
Strain 15 does not have a plasmid according to hybrid assembly graph, thus further supporting the theory 
that the mobile element found in segment 2 is located on the chromosome and that this event is not linked 
to the gain of the plasmid revealed in segment 1. The timeline for the uptake of this transposon is not as 
clear as with the plasmid because strain 7 and 15 are collected almost one year apart. Furthermore, 7

Strain 20 the latest isolate in the dataset, and is the only strain containing genes from both events.  
Many of the same genes were found by GenAPI, but the heatmap has resolved many of the mosaic with 
single genes missing across the dataset. Furthermore, the hybrid assemblies have resolved the doubt of 
whether these genetic events were plasmid or transpositional events.  
Consistent with our findings, MGEs carrying antibiotic resistance genes are known to be acquired by MRSA 
on multiple occasions (13) and clinical strains of Staphylococci spp. frequently harbor one or more plasmids 
that confer resistance to antibiotics, heavy metal ions, or antiseptics (18). The gain of the plasmid encoding 
erythromycin resistance is mainly found to be linked to selective pressure, and in this study the latest strains 
have gained this plasmid, thus likely reflecting high levels of macrolide use in the hospital (33). 
The LRSE dataset investigation revealed different genetic events; a gain of a resistance island carrying a 
fusidic acid resistance gene, and a loss of a plasmid with erythromycin resistance. Furthermore, there is a 
loss of a transposon in the newest isolates. 
The first segment located in the heatmap for the LRSE strains identified genes that are contained in an 
antibiotic resistance island located on the chromosome. The genes detected included a (fusB) gene referring 
to fucidin acid resistance. These findings were confirmed by hybrid assembly results, where genes were 
located on contig 1.  All strains, except for strain 2, carry this resistance island, indicating that it has either 
been lost in strain 2, or simply never gained.  
For the second event, a mobile element, which is an IS6 family transposon, is lost in in strain 10, 11 and 13. 
The genes on the transposon included metabolic enzymes and enzymes involved with cell wall synthesis. 
Here, the hybrid heatmap revealed a longer stretch of genes for this segment than the Illumina heatmap. 
Thus revealing an IS6 family transposase on both ends of this section. This supports the conclusion that of 
the genes belong to the same MGE, and to have been lost in the newest strains in the outbreak.   
Findings on segment 3 is consistent with a plasmid carrying an antiseptic resistance protein and an 
erythromycin resistance gene. According to the heatmaps, these genes are shared by tree strains in the 
collection, strain 1, 2, and 8, which were collected in 2017, 2018, and 2019, respectively. This finding 
indicates that a plasmid has been lost in the population in November 2019.  
Strain 1 was used for investigating the genes on segment 2 and 3 in the hybrid assembly. The investigation 
revealed a poor quality hybrid assembly graph with many unresolved bridges (see appendix #), and therefore 
supports the notion that a plasmid has been lost in segment 3 only based on genomic data from strain 1 is

Epidemic clones of S. epidermidis are known to evolve quickly through recombination and exchange of MGEs 
(34). Frequency of MGE loss and acquisition events contribute to a successful persistence of clones in 
hospital outbreaks (33). In this study, two events are observed where MGEs are lost during an outbreak, and 
this is consistent with the well-established fact that that harboring resistance genes come at a fitness cost for 
the bacteria. Strains carrying fewer genes can grow quicker and genes that are not essential for survival are 
often lost in bacteria populations (33). 
There are more genetic events in the LRSE outbreak and less clear timeline for the lost and gained of MGEs 
than in the MRSA outbreak. This may be due to the fact that the LRSE outbreak has been maintained for a 
longer period, starting in December 2017 with the first isolate and the latest from December 2020. The 
sample site of these strains also indicates more severe infections, than the isolates found in the MRSA 
outbreak. Severe infections are harder to treat and patients are often hospitalized for longer. The isolates in 
the MRSA collection, on the other hand, are from samples collected over at shorter time, from February 
2020 until July 2021. Amongst the collected strains for MRSA, are screening samples, indicating carrier status 
rather than infection. In this outbreak, only two main genetic events are detected with a clearer timeline 
than in the LRSE outbreak.  
The biggest advances when analyzing the gene content of variants, was that with good hybrid graphs, 
determining the location of genes either on chromosomes or plasmids, was much clearer compared to 
Illumina data. 
Our findings also showed that creating heatmaps from Illumina data showed strong discriminatory power to 
segregate strains into variant groups, and thereby allowing selection of relevant strains for further analyses 
possible. Conversely, Illumina data was overall not able to complete any of the mobile elements and place 
them in the full genomic context. Furthermore, heatmaps created from hybrid assembly data, also has fewer 
positive genes in the heatmap, as illustrated from the missing mosaic structure in the presence/absence 
heatmap. False positive gene calling by GenAPI could be due to partially assembled genes that are included 
in the analysis.

The need for long stretches of DNA to be able to create good hybrid data, makes the DNA extraction 
challenging, compared to other extraction protocols. Measuring correct DNA input for libraries is also 
difficult due to the sticky nature of highly concentrated DNA. The challenge in obtaining good Nanopore data 
experienced in this study may be due to the fact that it is still a novel technique in the department. For these 
reasons, Nanopore is not suitable for big scale analysis with the current workflow.  
Implementing nanopore as a part of the surveillance program is feasible for a few selected isolates, for 
better and faster attainment of knowledge regarding the genomic events that take place during outbreaks.  
Biological differences between the two datasets may have arisen because DNA was extracted from different 
subcultures of the same isolate, thus possibly introducing biological variability. Hence, we cannot exclude 
that this variation has taken place outside the patient and not during the infections and transmissions.  
Due to small amount of nanopore data, not all hybrid assemblies were fully closed. This can be overcome by 
normalizing the samples as described.

Perspectives  
Data for Nanopore sequencing can currently not be used alone for reliable identification or mapping of AMR 
in bacteria in outbreak investigation, due to high error rate in base calling. New chemistry reducing the error 
rate for base calling in Nanopore sequencing has been shown to generate near perfect sequencing of 
bacterial genomes for isolates without short-read polishing (35), thus possibly making Illumina sequencing 
superfluous. 
Conclusion 
The novel Technik of Nanopore sequencing has presented an opportunity for more accurate analysis on 
genetic events in bacterial populations, thus producing long reads of the genome. For now, the error rate for 
base calling for this sequencing technique is still too high to apply as a single approach for determining 
bacteria isolates in outbreak investigation.  
Creating hybrid assemblies using Unicycler, enables the obtainment of closed genomes and circularized 
plasmids. This study investigated the use of Unicycler to combine the short reads of Illumina reads together 
with long reads from Nanopore technology to more accurately elucidate the genomic events among several 
bacterial strains within a two separate hospital outbreaks, MRSA and LRSE. The data in this study showed 
that hybrid assembly data provided more accurate depictions of genomic events than Illumina data alone. 
The hybrid assembly data showed that the MRSA outbreak had 2 main genomic events and the LRSE 
outbreak had 3 main genomic events.
