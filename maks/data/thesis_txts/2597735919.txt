Abstract
Colored building-integrated photovoltaics (BIPV) are deemed to play a large role in achieving
the targets for renewable electricity generation and thus the mitigation of climate change. Further
development of coloration technologies for use in BIPV requires reliable methods for the assessment
of both color appearance and eﬃciency, including their angular dependency.
This thesis presents a laboratory measurement setup for the spectral bidirectional reﬂectance
distribution function (BRDF) of BIPV products in a single plane, allowing the determination
of the angular dependent color appearance. In addition, the setup allows measurements of the
incidence angle modiﬁer (IAM), corresponding to the relative angular dependent eﬃciency of
electricity production.
Furthermore, samples of three diﬀerent coloration technologies are investigated, including spec-
trally selective coatings, ceramic inkjet printing on glass and colored interlayers between glass
and PV cells. Further measurements of transmittance, external quantum eﬃciency (EQE) and
IV-curves are performed for a more detailed analysis of the samples.
Results show very distinct relationships for color saturation, eﬃciency and view angle between the
diﬀerent technologies: Samples with spectrally selective coatings show the highest transmittance
and thus highest eﬃciency, but low color saturation far from specular reﬂectance angles as well
as signiﬁcant iridescence for certain colors. Inkjet printed samples show the highest transmittance
losses as well as the most uniform color appearance. For samples with colored interlayers, the color
saturation as well as the transmission losses increase with the number of colored layers in the
sample.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
With the increased focus on mitigating global warming, an increasing number of countries aim
to transition towards renewable electricity generation. The International Energy Agency (IEA)
expects solar photovoltaics (PV) to become the power generation technology with the highest
installed capacity by 2035 [1]. This prognosis however will not be achieved by utility scale instal-
lation alone, but will also require installations on buildings. In this context, building-integrated
photovoltaics (BIPV) will play a large role, as they oﬀer higher visual attractivity compared to
building-applied photovoltaics (BAPV).
BIPV proposes to match the appearance of PV modules to the surrounding facade, however this
color matching is currently not possible in an eﬃcient, cheap and accurate manner. While the IEA
has identiﬁed several viable technologies, these are usually limited to a small selection of colors,
hampered by large eﬃciency losses due to coloration or limited to certain applications due to their
iridescent appearance. [2]
In recent years, private companies as well as research institutions have developed a number of col-
oration techniques, resulting in prototype products that are now slowly reaching the market. While
the assessment of power conversion eﬃciency is straightforward, deﬁned by the common PV testing
procedures, assessment of color appearance is signiﬁcantly more diﬃcult and not standardized.
This thesis aims to provide a reliable method to assess the appearance of colored BIPV products
as well as the eﬃciency of coloration by means of a laboratory measurement setup. Based on the
presented setup measuring the bidirectional reﬂectance distribution function (BRDF), a variety of
colorimetry parameters can be determined to characterize the angular dependent color appearance
of BIPV products.
Additionally, samples based on several existing coloration technologies are measured and compared
in order to outline the capabilities of the measurement setup. Using the determined colorimetry
parameters as well as angular dependent power conversion eﬃciency, the aim is to highlight and
quantify the diﬀerences between the coloration technologies. This then allows the determination
of the most promising candidates for further research in colored BIPV.

2.1.2
Specular and diﬀuse optical properties
Reﬂections from the surface of a material can be separated into several categories [4]:
Specular reﬂections, or regular reﬂections, occur when the angle of reﬂection is equal to the angle
of incidence. For parallel rays of incident light, this results in parallel rays of reﬂected light. In the
same manner, specular or direct transmittance refers to transmitted light following Snell’s law,
thus the angle of refraction depends only on the refractive indices of the materials involved, again
leading to parallel rays of transmitted light.
Diﬀuse reﬂectance and transmittance, on the other hand, refer to light which experiences scattering
on the material surface or in the material itself. This scattering can result in a variety of angular
intensity distributions, with materials with constant radiance in all directions being referred to as
Lambertian surfaces.
Retroreﬂection describes the phenomenon of light being reﬂected back in the direction of the
incident light.

2.3
Colorimetry
When describing the color of objects, the goal is to link a physical phenomenon (i.e. the spectrum
of a light source or reﬂection) to a mental one, in this case the human perception of color. In this
thesis, the focus lies not on unrelated colors, which can only be perceived in illuminant mode (e.g.
colored light sources), but on related colors perceived in object or surface mode, reﬂecting light
from an external illuminant. To allow an accurate description, the following key terms have been
deﬁned [7]:
Hue is the aspect diﬀerentiating a color from white. There is not one scale describing hue, but all
colors in the visible range can be referred to by combination of four unique hues, which are red,
blue, green and yellow. As the two combinations red-green and yellow-blue are not observable, hue
can be described by two bipolar dimensions, with red and yellow being positive by convention.
Alternatively, polar representation with hue as an angle is possible.
Saturation describes the degree to which a color diﬀers from white, regardless of brightness or
lightness. Chroma, on the other hand, corresponds to the diﬀerence between a color and an
achromatic percept of the same lightness, thus is relative to a reference. This reference is usually
gray, corresponding to the external illuminant. Perceived saturation is not stable over wavelength,
with colors of equal luminance appearing noticeable less saturated at medium wavelengths (green)
than at short or long wavelengths (blue and red). Additionally, saturation depends on light level,
appearing most saturated at intermediate light levels.
Brightness gives the perceived overall level of light emitted by a color, while lightness is the
perceived level of a color relative to a stimulus appearing white. This allows the perception of
dark colors, such as brown, however lightness only exists for related colors. Brightness itself is not
directly related to radiance or luminance, instead colors at medium wavelengths (yellow) appear
brightest, followed by those at long (red) and short wavelengths (blue).
Many additional factors inﬂuence the appearance of colors, most importantly the presence of other
colors nearby, which leads to contrasting or assimilation. Speciﬁc patterns may also cause percep-
tible color diﬀerences in otherwise identical objects. Furthermore the so-called color constancy
phenomenon refers to the relative stability of color perception under changing illumination due to
adaptation. These eﬀects are neither easily described nor completely understood in all their details
and thus go beyond the scope of this thesis. [7]
2.3.1
Color perception
Accurate representation of the diﬀerence between colors is notoriously diﬃcult due to a per-
ceived disconnect between spectral composition and color appearance. This means, that two spec-
trally diﬀerent samples may be indistinguishable by a human observer, a principle referred to
as metamerism. In fact, every color perceivable by the human eye can be replicated using three
independent light sources, called primaries - a principle which is referred to as trichromacy.
As a result, the International Comission on Illumination (CIE) developed both the RGB and XYZ
color spaces in 1931, which use three variables in order to describe the relationship between the
three primaries. In this thesis mainly the XYZ system will be employed, as it is most widely used in
industry and can easily be converted to other color spaces. The conversion of a measured spectrum
to XYZ tristimulus values uses a so-called standard observer, idealising human color perception.
The 1931 CIE 2° standard observer assumes a view angle of 2°, which is relevant due to perceptive
diﬀerences between cones and rods in the human eye. [8]

Initially the CIELAB color space was designed in such a way, so that the color diﬀerence between
two points relates to their Euclidean distance. Comparing to measurements of JND by MacAdam,
commonly known as MacAdam ellipses, however, does not show good agreement. Thus more

previous formulae especially in regards to diﬀerences in lightness (L∗) as well as diﬀerentiation in
the blue region of the CIELAB color space [12].
2.3.4
Color matching systems
While accurate and device independent description of colors is possible using both the CIELAB
and CIELUV color space, in industry and design more commonly known color matching system
are used. These databases of well-deﬁned colors allow for color constancy through diﬀerent steps
of the design and production process of colored materials.
The most widely used, proprietary color matching systems are produced by RAL and Pantone,
which oﬀer a number of individual systems for diﬀerent applications. While Pantone covers a large
number of color standards across a variety of disciplines [13], RAL focuses on a smaller selection
of well-deﬁned colors mainly used for paints and coatings [14].
Using the earlier mentioned color diﬀerence functions, the accordance of the measured color of a
speciﬁc product to the color deﬁned in the design stage can be measured. This, however, is outside
the scope of this thesis.
2.4
State of the art in colored BIPV and literature overview
The International Energy Agency (IEA) has identiﬁed a number of coloration technologies among
currently marketed BIPV products and under research [2]. They identiﬁed the following ﬁve cate-
gories:
• colored anti-reﬂective coatings on solar cells
• colored or semi-transparent PV-active layers (transparent thin ﬁlm PV, organic solar cells,
etc)
• special colored or patterned layers, coatings or interlayers as solar ﬁlters
• colored polymeric ﬁlms (encapsulant, backsheets)
• printing, coating or other modiﬁcation of module front glass
The last group includes a variety of diﬀerent technologies, ranging from multi-layered, spectrally
selective coatings to various means of printing, enamelling or mass coloring of solar glass [2].
One of the most common methods for coloration of BIPV products involves the printing of colored
dots, which become imperceptible at higher viewing distances. Studies have been performed re-
garding the impact of dot size, coverage and transparency on the power losses, showing signiﬁcant
power losses for higher coverages. [15]
A promising recent development has been the experimental realization of colored BIPV modules
inspired by the Morpho butterﬂy eﬀect. This coloration technique depends on a multilayer Bragg
stack of thin layers on the rear side of the glass and creates a saturated color appearance with
low losses. The colored samples do however show some iridescence, meaning angular dependent
changes in hue. [16]

2.4.1
Theoretical color eﬃciency
In recent years, a few studies have investigated the theoretical eﬃciency limit of coloring PV
products [17]–[19]. In these investigations, ideal reﬂection spectra were constructed for diﬀerent
colors, namely thin, rectangular (“pill box”) reﬂection spectra. In some of the studies, the “pill
boxes” were ﬁxed in width while the reﬂectivity was adjusted, while in others reﬂectivity was ﬁxed
and the width changed. It was shown that all visible colors can be achieved through combination
of two “pill boxes”, which is in good agreement with earlier theories [20]. It has also been shown,
that using two “pill boxes” is the optimal way to produce all colors in the visible spectrum, as the
sum of the color matching functions has only two peaks. By placing the “pill boxes” close to these
maxima, the lowest amount of photons have to be reﬂected in order to produce the desired color,
thus giving the optimal reﬂection spectrum [19].
For monochromatic colors, constructed from single “pill boxes” of 40 nm width, power losses
amounting to below 8 % have been found. Blue reﬂection spectra achieve the lowest power losses,
followed by green and red monochromatic colors. This is due to the shape of the solar spectrum
and the spectral response of photovoltaics (PV) cells, making long wavelength (red) photons the
most important ones for power generation. [17]
While this conclusion seems straightforward, “pill boxes” of ﬁxed width do not result in equally
saturated colors, as is easily apparent from the color matching functions in Section 2.3. Using “pill
boxes” of varying width, however, allows the optimal matching of all visible colors [19].
A further question addressed in these studies regards the optimal way to quantify and describe
coloration eﬃciency, with diﬀerent results: Peharz and Ulm chose to deﬁne the color eﬃciency as
the sum of the X, Y , and Z color coordinates, divided by the loss in photocurrent [17]. Both Røyset,
Kol˚as, and Jelle as well as Halme and M¨akinen only related the Y color coordinate (luminosity)
to the power loss [18], [19]. These deﬁnitions, however, lead to contradicting theoretical eﬃciency
limits for diﬀerent wavelengths.
It is believed that a better descriptor for the coloration eﬃciency should take the color saturation
into account, as it is more relevant for the color appearance of a BIPV product than the individual
color coordinates. Alternatively, a color eﬃciency metric could assess the color diﬀerence to a
reference sample. The formulation of an accurate descriptor for the coloration eﬃciency, however,
goes beyond the scope of this thesis.

BRDF measurement setup
In order to assess the angular dependent appearance of colored building-integrated photovoltaics
(BIPV) products, measurements of the spectral reﬂectance at diﬀerent angles are required. As
described in Section 2.2, the relevant parameter is the spectral bidirectional reﬂectance distribution
function (BRDF).
This chapter describes the laboratory setup at DTU Fotonik for measurements of single-plane
spectral BRDF. While developed speciﬁcally with PV products in mind, the applications of the
setup are limited only by the sample size and the required accuracy of measurement.
Section 3.1 describes the BRDF setup in its entirety, including improvements made to the light
collimation setup as well as the typical measurement process. The calibration of the measurement
setup using a white reference sample as well as the process to obtain the BRDF from the raw
measurement data is presented in Section 3.2. Finally, results from measurements of the white
reference sample are shown in Section 3.3, which serve as characterization of the measurement
setup.

3.1.3
Improvements of light collimation
While the setup has been shown to give adequate results, some issues regarding the light collimation
that may limit the measurement accuracy can be identiﬁed:
First of all, the light beam from the OAP mirror is not very well collimated, as several patterns of
divergent light can be observed around the primary light spot. Secondly, the intensity distribution
within the collimated light spot is easily visible. This is unavoidable when using OAP mirrors for
collimation of light, as shown in Section 2.1.4.
Choosing a diﬀerent OAP mirror, however, changes the intensity distribution. As for the divergent
light, this can be considered a consequence of the LDLS not being a perfectly punctual light source.
Refocusing the collimated light through a small aperture allows these divergent components to be
eliminated, thus allowing an improved collimation afterwards.
As shown in Section 2.1.4, using an OAP mirror with diﬀerent RFL but otherwise identical dimen-
sions also leads to a reduction in overall intensity, as it subtends a smaller solid angle. Therefore
a compromise has to be found between the uniformity and the magnitude of the intensity in the
collimated beam.

3.1.4
Measurement process
For all BRDF measurements, samples are typically measured at sample angles θ ranging from 0° to
60° in 15° steps. Measuring only in direction of positive angles is suﬃcient, since the BRDF follows
the Helmholtz reciprocity (see Section 2.2). Regarding the sensor angle αx, measurements are
typically taken from −90° to 90°, though angles above ∼80° show high measurement uncertainty.
For every measurement conﬁguration, ﬁrst a measurement of the background noise is taken which
is then subtracted from the actual measurement. For these “dark signal” measurements, the light
beam from the LDLS is blocked and measurements are taken at all αx in order to identify any
ambient reﬂections. Additionally, the spectrometer integration time is adjusted to avoid saturation
of the detector. Typical integration times range from 0.1 s to 10 s and for most measurements data
averaged over 5 measurements is used to reduce signal noise. The spectrometer is kept at room
temperature throughout all measurements.

Colored BIPV samples
In order to assess the impact of diﬀerent coloration technologies on both angular dependent color
appearance and eﬃciency, 26 diﬀerent samples using three diﬀerent coloration technologies are in-

visual appearance.
While all samples use monocrystalline Si cells, the coloration technologies as well as glass used are
diﬀerent for all three groups, as they are produced by diﬀerent manufacturers. Dansk Solenergi
ApS provided samples based on stacked colored layers in the laminate, presented in Section 4.1.
The remaining two groups both achieve coloration through the glass itself, either through inkjet
printed ceramic colors (see Section 4.3) or through multilayered coatings. This last group, under
the name Kromatix™, shows a high variance in color appearance depending on viewing angle and is
presented in Section 4.2. For both of these last two groups, samples were laminated using identical
components, to allow for better comparability.
Prior to the measurements presented in Chapters 5 and 6, all samples are investigated using
electroluminescence (EL) imaging, to conﬁrm that the area between the busbars is free of cracks
or other defects.

5.1
Analysis process
As described in Section 2.3.2, the spectral reﬂectance is converted to XYZ tristimulus values using
Equations 2.15. Since the angular dependant color appearance is of interest, the spectral BRDF
is used instead of the spectral reﬂectance. In turn, the normalisation factor k in Equation 2.16 is
modiﬁed by a factor of π, to take the BRDF of a perfect lambertian reﬂector into account. Thus,
Y = 100 corresponds to the reﬂection from a perfect lambertian reﬂector with 100 % reﬂectance.
At angles with high specular reﬂections, this can therefore result in XYZ coordiantes outside the
feasible color gamut.
The XYZ tristimulus values are then converted into the diﬀerent color spaces described in Sec-
tion 2.3.3. The color appearance is then visualised in the CIELAB color space for all measurement
points, colored in their corresponding RGB colors. For better readability, all ﬁgures also show
projections on the diﬀerent planes of the CIELAB color space.
As a ﬁnal step, the CIELUV chroma and saturation parameters are determined and compared
between the diﬀerent samples, as described in Section 2.3.3.

Chapter 6
Supplementary measurements
Contrary to the appearance of building-integrated photovoltaics (BIPV) samples, which depends
on the spectral reﬂectance, the power production of such devices is determined by the spectral
transmittance of the glass cover and encapsulant. The addition of special coloration agents, in
the form of additional layers between glass and cell or by treating the glass itself, can modify the
transmittance through the laminate. This chapter shows 4 diﬀerent methods that investigate the
performance of the BIPV samples presented in Chapter 4.
In Section 6.1, the spectral transmittance of the diﬀerent cover glasses is analysed, both regarding
linear and total transmittance. While the transmittance does not directly correspond to the power
production of the laminated samples, it can give some indications towards it. Furthermore, by
comparing linear and total transmittance, the diﬀusivity of transmission is determined.
Possibly even more interesting is the quantum eﬃciency (QE), described in Section 6.2, as it
also takes into account the spectral response of Si photovoltaics (PV) cells. With the additional
measurements of raw cells, the relationship between transmittance measurements before lamination
and QE measurements after lamination is investigated. Additionally, Isc can be calculated from
QE measurements, allowing eﬃciency comparison between the diﬀerent coloration technologies.
Section 6.3 shows the results from dedicated IV-curve measurements using a solar simulator.
Similar to the Isc obtained from QE measurements, comparing the Isc obtained from these IV-
curves allow comparison of power production and eﬃciency.
Finally, also the angular dependent power production is investigated, by measuring the incidence
angle modiﬁer (IAM) in Section 6.4. It shows the current produced at diﬀerent sample angles,
relative to an incidence angle of 0°, thus corresponding to the total transmittance. Combined with
the earlier measurements of spectral reﬂectance (see Chapter 5), this allows for the comparison
between angular dependent apperance and power production.

6.1.1
Measurements & Analysis
In order to put the eﬃciency measurements into perspective, spectral transmittance measurements
are performed on all available glassses used to assemble samples. For the inkjet printed glasses,
duplicates are used, which are manufactured identically to the glasses used in lamination. For the
Kromatix glasses, transmittance measurements are performed before lamination.
As a ﬁrst step, linear transmittance measurements are performed using a Cary 50 UV-Vis spec-
trophotometer. Samples are aligned to the focal point according to the manufacturers instructions
using a custom sample holder. This linear transmittance (Tλ,l), however, cannot be deemed rep-
resentative of the total transmittance of the samples, as some diﬀusivity is observable even with
the naked eye. This is especially true for the Kromatix samples, which indicate highly diﬀusing
properties.
As a second step, therefore, total transmittance measurements are performed using an integrating
sphere. As a broadband lightsource the laser-driven light source (LDLS) is used, together with the
collimating setup described in section 3.1.3. In order to compensate for reﬂections from the sample
back inside the integrating sphere, due to the relatively large sphere port, a compensation light
source (CLS) is used, following best-practice recommendations [32]. Figure 6.1 shows a diagram
of the experimental setup for the transmittance measurements.
Figure 6.1: Experimental setup for total transmittance measurements

port is reduced from 2.5 in to 2 in diammeter using a port reducer and further to the light beam

equator is connected to the spectrometer using a 600 µm optical ﬁbre. The top port of the sphere
is connected to the CLS using another 600 µm optical ﬁbre. The CLS itself is an OceanOptics

6.2
Quantum Eﬃciency
External quantum eﬃciency (EQE) describes the capability of a photovoltaic device to convert
incident photons into electrons. As it includes both the spectral transmissivity of the glass and
encapsulant and the spectral response of the PV cell, the EQE can be a valuable parameter when
assessing the performance of BIPV devices.
6.2.1
Measurements & Analysis
For all samples otherwise investigated in this thesis, EQE measurements are performed using
a “QEXL Solar Cell Quantum Eﬃciency Measurement System” by “PV Measurements, Inc.”.
Measurements are taken in the range of 300 nm to 1200 nm, including a calibration with a Si
photodiode for wavelengths up to, and including, 1100 nm and a Ge photodiode beyond. In order
to take diﬀerences in calibration into account, measurements above 1100 nm have to be corrected
by a factor of 0.6365. All samples are measured at 10 nm intervals, with a chopping frequency of
110 Hz and without any bias light or voltage bias.
In addition to the measurements on laminated samples, EQE measurements of raw cells used for
both the inkjet printed and Kromatix samples are taken.
According to the standard for spectral response measurements of PV devices [33], the entire cell
should be illuminated with monochromatic light. In this particular setup, however, the measure-
ment light spot is much smaller, covering only few mm2. Additionally, the investigated samples are
not particularly uniform regarding the coloration material, especially in case of the Dansk Solen-
ergi samples. This in turn could lead to the appearance of the measured area not corresponding
to the entire sample.
To mitigate the impact of the sample non-uniformity and the small light spot, 5 measurements are
taken at diﬀerent positions over the entire cell. These measurements are then ﬁltered by excluding
all measurements with a diﬀerence of more than one average standard deviation from the maximum.
The ﬁnal EQE spectrum is then obtained as the average of the remaining measurements.
In addition to the measured EQE, the measurements system directly calculates Jsc from the raw
data. Using the known cell dimensions of 156 mm · 156 mm, the Isc is calculated for comparison with
the IV-curve measurements. The results of these calculations are shown in Table 6.1 in Section 6.3.
In order to assess the eﬃciency of the coloration material alone, an additional parameter named
coloration quantum eﬃciency (QEc) is calculated by dividing the measured EQE of each sample
by the measured EQE of the reference sample. This correction takes both the glass, encapsulant
and cell characteristics into account, thus the QEc only describes the properties of the coloration
material. This is of course only feasible if the reference glass is identical with the glass used in the
samples, apart from the coloring agent. As this is only the case for the inkjet printed samples, the
QEc is only calculated for these samples.

6.3.1
Measurements & Analysis
IV-curves of all laminated samples are measured using a PASAN solar simulator, which performs
ﬂash tests with voltage sweeps close to standard test conditions (STC). The light source produces
a spectral distribution close to the AM 1.5 solar spectrum (AAA class) with an irradiance close to
1000 W/m2, conﬁrmed with a calibrated reference cell. The module is heated close to 25 ◦C prior
to measurements. Additionally, a temperature and irradiance correction is performed by the tester
software for the remaining small deviations.
For each sample, 3 measurements are taken and averaged to obtain their IV-curves. For intercom-
parison, the Isc is determined for each sample from the y-axis intercept, and by dividing it by
the Isc of the respective reference sample, a Isc,rel is calculated, which relates to the impact of
coloration.

6.4
Incidence Angle Modiﬁer
The IAM describes the relationship between the angle of incidence (AOI) on a PV device and
its produced power, relative to an AOI of 0°. Alternatively, it can also be used to describe the
performance of solar thermal collectors at diﬀerent AOIs. As it is mainly determined by the surface
of the glass cover on a device as well as the properties of all interfaces in front of the PV cell,
diﬀerent coloration techniques may have an inﬂuence on the IAM. While light absorption by
coloring agents can be considered to remain constant regardless of AOI, the reﬂections at the glass-
encapsulant interface are likely to change. Additionally, reﬂectance of glass, and all translucent
surfaces, is spectrally sensitive, an aspect that can change with angle as well. Overall the IAM can
be interpreted as the angular transmissivity throughout the laminated compound.
Diﬀerences of IAM have a more signiﬁcant inﬂuence on energy yield for installations with a higher
average AOI. This certainly is the case for fa¸cade mounted installations, which is a primary usecase
for BIPV. Thus the goal is to create a colored PV module without lowering the IAM through col-
oration, as this would disadvantage vertical installations beyond the already diminished irradiance
when compared to tilted installations [34].
6.4.1
Measurements & Analysis
The setup for measuring the IAM is largely identical with the setup used for reﬂectance measure-
ments, as described in Section 3.1. However, in order to allow for higher AOI, while keeping all
incident light within the cell area, the light spot is reduced by using a mask with a hole of 12 mm
in diammeter. This allows for measurements up to ±80°. For measurement, the samples are con-
nected to a 0.5 Ωshunt resistor, allowing the current to be determined from voltage measurements.
Additionally a thermocouple is used to conﬁrm the stability of device temperature throughout the
measurement.
For each angle, in 5° intervals, 20 data points of current are acquired and averaged over, as well as
over three overall measurements per sample. Since the amount of incident light remains constant,
due to the light spot continuously being contained in the sample, no cosine correction is necessary
and the ratio between the measured current at sample angle (θ = θ) and at 0° results in the IAM
(Kθ).
In order to isolate eﬀect due to the coloration material from eﬀects inherent to the ﬂoat glass,
an IAM of coloration is deﬁned as the fraction between the Kθ of a sample x and the Kθ of the
reference sample x0 (see Equation 6.3). However, since not all reference samples actually use the
same glass as their respective samples, intercomparison between the diﬀerent technologies has to
take the diﬀerences in reference glass into account.

Chapter 7
Results intercomparison and
discussion
In this chapter, results from the reﬂectance measurements and colorimetry calculations are com-
bined with the supplementary measurements to give a holistic assessment of the diﬀerent coloration
technologies used for the samples in this thesis.
Additionally, recommendations for further development of the measurement system as well as
further research on coloration technologies for building-integrated photovoltaics (BIPV) are pre-
sented.
7.1
Dansk Solenergi samples
The ﬁrst group of samples uses colored layers between the glass and photovoltaics (PV) cell to
achieve coloration, as described in Section 4.1. As has been noted in previous sections, a clear trend
between the number of colored layers can be observed for both color appearance and eﬃciency:
Section 5.5 showed relatively ﬂat reﬂectance spectra from 300 nm to 900 nm for all samples, ac-
cented by peaks at ∼400 nm or ∼600 nm with a smooth decline towards longer wavelengths, leading
to a dark gray or red appearance. While for gray samples, additional colored layers show little in-
ﬂuence, for the red samples an increasing number of layers leads to sharper peaks, and in turn
more saturated colors.
Measurements of the Isc,rel show a similar behaviour (see Section 6.3), with an increasing number of
colored layers leading to a larger reduction in current, regardless of color. This is also conﬁrmed by
measurements of the external quantum eﬃciency (EQE) (see Section 6.2), which show a reduction
in EQE with an increasing number of layers. While this reduction aﬀects all wavelengths equally, for
the gray colored samples, the red samples show a more signiﬁcant reduction at short wavelengths,
with longer wavelengths remaining unaﬀected.
From the reﬂectance spectra and the spectral EQE, it can also be concluded that the samples
achieve their coloration mainly through absorption of light with speciﬁc wavelengths by the colored
interlayers. As the gray layers absorb equally across the entire spectrum, this only leads to a reduced
transmittance, without leading to increased reﬂectance. Thus, the number of layers has a lower
eﬀect on the gray colored samples than on the red samples.
81
==PAGE:95==
Master Thesis
Markus Babin
Additionally, samples “DS Red 2” and “DS Red 3” as well as “DS Gray 2” and “DS Gray 3” can
be compared directly, with the samples being distinguished only by the addition or omission of an
additional layer of encapsulant (i.e. polyoleﬁn elastomer (POE)) in between two colored layers. As
there is no signiﬁcant observable diﬀerence in either color appearance or Isc,rel, it can be concluded
that these additional layers of encapsulant do not aﬀect the optical or electrical properties of the
samples. There potentially may be an eﬀect regarding the cohesion of layers in the sample, however
this is not assessed in this thesis.
Regarding the angular dependence of color appearance, this is dominated mainly by the structured
glass surface of the samples, leading to high reﬂectivity close to specular reﬂectance angles, without
causing specular reﬂections directly. This leads to the samples appearing very light in color at
these angles. At angles further away from specular reﬂectance angles, however, the samples show
signiﬁcantly higher color saturation, depending on the contained coloration layers.
As shown in Section 6.4, the structured glass also leads to higher reﬂectivity at low incidence
angles and lower reﬂectivity at high incidence angles, compared to the reference sample with plain
glass. For the incidence angle modiﬁer (IAM), also a dependence on the number of layers can be
observed, with a higher number of layers generally leading to lower values.
Concluding on the Dansk Solenergi samples, it has to be stated that due to the large inﬂuence
of the structured glass, a proper comparison of the coloration technology to other technologies is
not possible. It can be assessed, however, that a larger number of colored layers leads to a higher
saturation as well as lower eﬃciency. For samples with mostly achromatic appearance (i.e. gray
samples), the increase in saturation is irrelevant, thus only leading to additional transmittance
losses without improving the color appearance.
7.2
Inkjet printed samples
This group of samples achieves their coloration through a ceramic ink printed on the inside of the
glass before lamination, possibly the simplest of the investigated coloration techniques. Similar to
the Dansk Solenergi samples, this technology achieves coloration mainly through the absorption
of light leading to very similar transmittance and reﬂectance spectra.
Section 6.1 shows that the ceramic inks used, however, are quite diﬀerent from each other: While
most colored glasses show low total transmittance at low wavelengths, rising gradually to medium
transmittance at higher wavelengths, the blue sample shows relatively high transmittance through-
out the entire spectrum, with reduced transmittance from ∼530 nm to ∼650 nm. This also shows
when comparing the Isc,rel of the samples, with the blue sample alone reaching a current compa-
rable to the other technologies.
Similarly, large diﬀerences between the diﬀerent reﬂectance spectra can be observed (see Sec-
tion 5.3): While the white sample shows very high reﬂectance, all other samples show reﬂectance
spectra similar to their transmittance spectra, though at diﬀerently scaled intensity. Additionally,
lower reﬂectance than transmittance is measured for longer wavelengths, and all samples show
various degrees of specularity of reﬂectance.
Most samples show a relatively uniform color appearance, regardless of angle of incidence (AOI)
or view angle. This is not true for the blue sample, however, as due to its low overall reﬂectance
but high specularity of reﬂectance, it only appears blue at angles close to the specular reﬂectance
angles. For all other samples, the average color saturation is the highest among all samples, mainly
due to the relatively uniform appearance over angles.
82
==PAGE:96==
Master Thesis
Markus Babin
The choice of color also has a signiﬁcant eﬀect on the IAM, with higher specularity of reﬂection
correlating to a higher IAM.
In summary, this coloration technology achieves the highest average color saturation with no
signiﬁcant change of hue over diﬀerent AOIs or view angles. The impact on power conversion
eﬃciency is signiﬁcant, however, with Isc,rel falling below 70 % for most colors. Compared to
theoretical calculations [17]–[19], signiﬁcantly higher losses are observed, with the blue colored
sample showing the highest eﬃciency. This, however, is rather a result of the properties of the
speciﬁc ceramic ink used than a fundamental limit.
7.3
Kromatix samples
The Kromatix samples are diﬀerent from the other two groups in the sense, that they do not achieve
coloration mainly by absorption of light, but rather by selective transmittance and reﬂectance
through thin layer coatings. Thus they show signiﬁcantly diﬀerent behaviour, both regarding color
appearance and eﬃciency, than the other technologies:
Both the transmittance and reﬂectance measurements in Section 6.1 and Section 5.4 show smooth
spectra with multiple peaks and valleys, which are directly opposed. For example, samples showing
a low reﬂectance at ∼400 nm show a high transmittance at the same wavelength, and vice versa.
The transmittance, however, is relatively high for all wavelengths, leading to a high Isc,rel (see
Section 6.3). In fact, with a Isc,rel above 90 % for all samples, these are the highest eﬃciencies
among all three investigated technologies.
The reﬂectance, however, is highly dependent on view angle, with only angles close to the specular
reﬂectance angles resulting in high lightness and saturation. This in turn means, that at most
angles, reﬂections from the samples appear dark and unsaturated. Additionally, the hue changes
with AOI and view angle for the green, blue and terracotta sample, leading to iridescent color
appearance. The other samples, e.g. the gold and bronze sample, however, do not show any irides-
cence.
Additionally, due to the anti-reﬂective treatment of the glass surface, the Kromatix samples also
show the highest IAM of all technologies (see Section 6.4). The gold and bronze samples even show
a little higher IAM than the green, blue and terracotta samples, possibly indication a relationship
between iridescence and IAM.
In conclusion, the Kromatix samples show the highest Isc,rel and IAM of all technologies, while
suﬀering from iridescent appearance and generally low reﬂectance at view angles far from specular
reﬂectance angles. This sadly leads to a low average color saturation.
7.4
Recommendations and further research questions
As described in Chapter 3, improvements have been made to the light collimation of the bidirec-
tional reﬂectance distribution function (BRDF) measurement setup. While a characterisation of
the setup has been performed using a white reference sample, this reference sample itself is not
well characterised. For a more accurate assessment of the measurement setup and its uncertainty,
a characterisation using a larger, well characterised reference sample is recommended.

Additionally, the setup measures BRDF only in a single plane. Thus adding the capability of ro-
tating the sample along the horizontal rotational axis would allow for a more accurate assessment
of the hemispherical reﬂectance at higher AOIs as well as the anisotropy of the BRDF. Further-
more, measurements at lower intervals of AOIs and view angles would allow for a more accurate
determination of the angular dependence of the BRDF.
While the samples investigated cover diﬀerent coloration technologies, the diﬀerences in glass
texture and surface treatment signiﬁcantly reduced their comparability. For a more detailed inves-
tigation, especially regarding the Dansk Solenergi, it is recommended to use samples with either
a smooth glass surface or an anti-reﬂective treatment.
In addition, measurements of the angular dependent color appearance can only be a step in the
assessment of colored BIPV products. Depending on the application, e.g. mounting on roof or
fa¸cade, common AOIs and view angles should be taken into account for the applicability of speciﬁc
products.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to develop and improve an experimental method for the determination
of angular dependent color appearance and eﬃciency of colored building-integrated photovoltaics
(BIPV) products. This has been achieved using a measurement setup for the spectral bidirectional
reﬂectance distribution function (BRDF) in addition to a number of supplementary measurements.
As a secondary focus of this thesis, three diﬀerent coloration technologies for BIPV products were
measured and analyzed.
First of all, the existing setup at DTU Fotonik for the measurement of the spectral BRDF in a
single plane was improved regarding the light collimation. Additionally the experimental setup
was characterized using a white reference sample to determine correction factors for more accurate
measurements. The improved setup allows the determination of the spectral BRDF over a variety of
angles of incidence (AOIs) and view angles which can then be converted to various color coordinates
in the CIELAB and CIELUV color spaces.
Secondly, three sets of samples were analyzed, using measurement from the BRDF measurement
setup as well as transmittance, external quantum eﬃciency (EQE), IV-curve and incidence angle
modiﬁer (IAM) measurements. Comparing the individual angular dependent color appearance and
power production, the following conclusions were made:
The ﬁrst group of samples, provided by Dansk Solenergi ApS, is based on colored interlayers
between glass and PV cell and showed high color saturations mainly at angles far from specular
reﬂectance angles. An increasing number of colored layers was shown to give an increasing color
saturation. The second group, based on Kromatix glasses, showed an opposing behaviour, where
high lightness coincides with high chroma. Some of these Kromatix samples (K Blue, K Green and
K Terracotta) also showed signiﬁcant iridescence. The ﬁnal group, based on glasses printed with
ceramic inks, showed a rather uniform color appearance across all angles.
Regarding the eﬃciency, the Kromatix samples showed the highest transmittance, resulting in the
lowest loss of Isc,rel, regardless of angle. The inkjet printed samples, on the other hand, showed the
highest losses due to light absorption in the ink. The Dansk Solenergi samples range in between,
with an increasing number of colored layers leading to increased losses.
Due to the diﬀerences in texture and surface treatment of the glass, a detailed comparison of the
diﬀerent technologies was not possible, warranting further research. Additionally, extension of the
BRDF measurement setup to perform proper hemispherical measurements would allow for a more
accurate assessment of the hemispherical reﬂectance of colored BIPV products.
