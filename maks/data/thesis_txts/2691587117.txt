Abstract 
 
 
 Cohesive zone model has been widely used to predict and analyze the behavior on the interface area when load is 
applied. A constitutive equation for the cohesive zone model describes the relationship between traction and 
effective separation. The phenomena for crack such as crack initiation, propagation and final fracture can be 
explained theoretically with the constitutive equation. Due to the limitation of using the default models in a 
commercial software, ABAQUS UMAT (User MATerial) interface is used to implement Tvergaard’s 1990 
debonding model. Hill48 yield models are implemented to describe the behavior of anisotropy. The research was 
extended to model HCP materials such as AZ31. HCP materials generally show the plastically asymmetric behavior, 
which shows strong strength differential (SD) effect with distortion hardening and it is not able to be predicted by 
Hill48 models. The yield function proposed by Yoon et al. (2014) was implemented to predict the behavior of 
cohesive layer more exactly for the asymmetric behaviors of HCP materials.

Chapter 1. Introduction 
 
In order to make the material have higher performance, several methods are conducted. One of the methods is 
using the second phase particle to reinforce the material. The particles added into the homogeneous material 
strengthen the materials. The material properties such as the stiffness, the yield strength and the ultimate tensile 
strength can be improved. However, adding the second phase particles cause the void nucleation on the interface 
between the material and inclusion (particle), limiting the ductility and the fracture toughness. Divecha et al. [1] 
and McDanels [2] showed it by investigating the aluminum reinforced by SiC. There are papers about the perfectly 
bonded particles such as Christman et al. [3,4] and Tvergaard [5], but these researches cannot explain the ductility 
and the fracture toughness problem. 
To model the behavior on the interface between material and particle, cohesive zone model can be employed. 
Barenblatt [6] first proposed the concept of cohesive zone. According to Needleman [7], cohesive zone model is 
appropriate to analyze the decohesion when interfacial strength is weaker than the material In the commercial 
finite element programme ABAQUS, there are several cohesive zone models which are developed from the works 
by Chen et al. [8], Alfaro and Crisfield [9], and Davila et al. [10], and these models are based on the traction-
separation law. The model employing the traction-separation law is proper when the thickness of adhesive layer 
is negligible. 
Needleman [7], Nutt and Needleman [11], Povirk et al. [12], and Xu and Needleman [13] researched the void 
nucleation by an inclusion. Some authors proposed several cohesive zone models that are specialized for analyzing 
the debonding of the material and the inclusion and applied for some problems. Needleman [7] proposed the 
cohesive zone interface model for void nucleation by inclusion debonding, which describe the debonding behavior 
of only normal separation. Tvergaard [14] extended the Needleman’s [7] cohesive zone model to work for both 
the normal and the tangential separation. The model proposed by Tvergaard can also describe the behavior under 
compressive loading. 
Legarth [15] employed the cohesive zone model for the study about the effect of anisotropic plasticity on failure 
by debonding, by comparing with isotropic plasticity. Legarth used a unit cell model and implemented the yield 
function of Hill [16,17] and Barlat et al. [18] to model the plastic anisotropy of the material. Legarth [19] showed 
the predicted failure strain is affected by the orientation of plastic anisotropy. Using the same unit cell model, 
==PAGE:6==

Legarth [20] researched the effects of anisotropic configurations of the inclusion. By changing the ratio of the 
semi-major axis and semi-minor axis of the inclusion and the ratio of the horizontal length and vertical length of 
the unit cell, Legarth [20] showed the effects of anisotropic configurations on the failure by debonding. Legarth 
[20] also studied the effects of the interface imperfection between matrix and particle by changing the parameters 
of maximum interfacial stress. 
Legarth [20] tested only for the biaxial tension to research the effect of plastic anisotropy because the yield 
functions proposed by Hill [16,17] and Barlat et al [18] which were implemented for the matrix surrounding the 
particle(inclusion) can describe the behavior of only Body-Centered-Cubic (BCC) and Face-Centered-Cubic 
(FCC) materials. However, these yield functions cannot describe the behavior of Hexagonal-Closed-Packed (HCP) 
materials such as AZ31. HCP materials show the plastically asymmetric behavior, which shows strong strength 
differential (SD) effects. This thesis investigates the SD-effect of the anisotropic-asymmetric plasticity on the 
failure by debonding. The yield function proposed by Yoon et al. [21] is implemented to describe the plastically 
asymmetric behavior of HCP materials. To show the influence of SD effect, the term related to the pressure 
sensitivity was included to the isotropic material and conducted the same test with Legarth [15]. 
In chapter 2, the theoretical backgrounds about the cohesive zone model and yield models are reviewed. When 
the cohesive element is used, a simple example of cohesive zone model which ABAQUS offers and the reason for 
not using the basic cohesive zone model in ABAQUS is written. In chapter 3, implementation and verification of 
constitutive model for the cohesive element is explained. Brief explanation of the algorithm of UMAT for cohesive 
zone model is included. In chapter 4, the unit cell model used in this research is introduced. Conditions applied 
such as periodic boundary condition, force equilibrium, or material orientations are written. In chapter 5, the result 
of Legarth [15] is duplicated. Because the number and location of integration points of cohesive element is 
different between ABAQUS and Legarth, this procedure is necessary to check if ABAQUS and UMAT can used 
to do the analysis for debonding. In chapter 6, the influence of the SD effect is considered by implementing 
Yoon2014 yield function. There is conclusion in chapter 7.

Chapter 2. Theoretical Background 
 
To describe the behavior of the material, some constitutive models such as the cohesive zone model proposed by 
Tvergaard [14], yield functions proposed by Hill [16,17] and Yoon et al. [21] are implemented. In this chapter, the 
constitutive models are explained. 
 
 
2.1 Cohesive element 
Cohesive zone model is used to predict and analyze the behavior on the interface area when load is applied. 
Constitutive equation for the cohesive zone model describes the relationship between traction and separation. In 
ABAQUS, there’s two method to model the adhesives. First method is using the cohesive element and second is 
using the cohesive interaction. In this paper, cohesive element is used among two methods because ABAQUS 
UMAT (User MATerial) interface is used to implement the cohesive zone model. 
 To analyze the behavior on the interface, the cohesive element is inserted between two components as shown in 
the figure 2.1. As the figure shows, two sides of cohesive element are bonded with the components. The thickness 
of the cohesive element should be set almost zero because it is used to model the adhesive.

2.3.1. The reason for not using cohesive zone models ABAQUS offers 
 There are several cohesive zone models which describes the behavior of cohesive elements using traction-
separation law in ABAQUS. For example, ABAQUS offers 6 basic damage initiation criterions: maximum 
nominal stress criterion, maximum nominal strain criterion, quadratic nominal stress criterion, quadratic nominal 
strain criterion, maximum principal stress criterion and maximum principle strain criterion. Damage evolution 
can be defined based on the displacement and energy. Each type can be divided in three different types of softening 
for damage evolution laws: linear, exponential and tabular. The displacement for final fracture is decided on two 
methods: based on energy and traction. It is convenient to use the basic cohesive zone models in ABAQUS. 
However, the cohesive zone models in ABAQUS have a problem. If at least one of the cohesive element’s 
integration points are under normal compressive loading, the cohesive element is not removed even if the 
degradation value reached 1.0. The element does not reach to the fully debonded state, so the thickness of the 
element can reach to almost zero, and it can leads to the convergence problem in ABAQUS. 
 The cohesive zone model to be used in this paper should be able to eliminated when at least one of the integration 
points’ degradation value become 1.0. This condition needs to be work even one of the integration points is under 
compressive loading.

2.4 Yield functions 
In this research, Hill-48 [16,17] and Yoon-2014 [21] yield models are implemented to describe the behavior of 
materials. Both Hill-48 and Barlat-91 yield models are implemented to describe the behavior of BCC and FCC 
metals, respectively. HCP materials, however, shows the plastically asymmetric behavior, which shows strong 
strength differential effect with distortion hardening, which is not able to be predicted by Hill-48 and Barlat-91 
models. Yoon et al. [21] is implemented to predict the behavior of cohesive layer more exactly for the asymmetric 
behaviors for HCP materials.

3.5. UMAT of cohesive zone model 
 The overall algorithm of UMAT of the cohesive zone model is shown in the figure 3.7. The state variables that 
are updated is 𝜆௡ , 𝜆௠௔௫ and ‘id_fail’. 𝜆௡ is the degradation value for 𝑛 -th time increment, 𝜆௠௔௫ is the 
==PAGE:28==

maximum degradation value during whole loading history and id_fail is the variable which is used to check 
whether the cohesive element is fully debonded or not. 
 The input values are the state variables, nominal strain in normal and tangential directions and material properties 
such as the critical interfacial separations and maximum traction. First of all, check whether ‘id_fail’ is 0 or 1. If 
‘id_fail’ is 1, it means that the cohesive element is fully debonded. All integration points in a cohesive element 
share one ‘id_fail’ variable. Thus, the element is removed when the degradation value at one of the integration 
points becomes 1. When the element is fully debonded, the traction on the integration points is zero and returns 
the identity matrix as a Jacobian. 
 If ‘id_fail’ is 0, the UMAT subroutine calculates λ value for the 𝑛-th time increment. If 𝜆௡≥1, the ‘id_fail’ 
of the element becomes 1. The traction is zero and returns the identity matrix as a Jacobian. If 𝜆௡≥𝜆௡ିଵ and 
𝜆௡≥𝜆௠௔௫, it is the loading case. The traction change follows the ① in the figure 3.6. If 𝜆௡< 𝜆௠௔௫, it is the 
unloading case. The traction change follows ② if 𝜆௡≥𝜆௡ିଵ and ③ if 𝜆௡< 𝜆௡ିଵ. For case ①, the traction 
change follows the eq. 3.1 and eq. 3.4 and returns the 𝑇௢𝐴௟௢௔ௗ௜௡௚ as a Jacobian matrix. For the unloading, the 
traction is calculated as eq. 3.12 and returns the 𝑇௢𝐴௨௡௟௢௔ௗ௜௡௚ as a Jacobian matrix. 
 After all, the UMAT updates the state variable 𝜆௠௔௫.

Chapter 4. Unit cell model 
 
Legarth [15,19,20] has studied about the effects of anisotropic plasticity on failure by debonding. Legarth [15] 
showed that anisotropic plasticity affects the failure by comparing with the isotropic von-Mises yield function. 
Legarth [19,20] also researched about the effect of the orientation of the plastic anisotropy and anisotropic 
configurations on the failure. The present paper investigated the effect of plastically asymmetric behavior. In order 
to research this topic, this paper compared the result with the paper of Legarth. To make the comparison possible, 
the unit cell model proposed by Legarth was used. In this chapter, the unit cell model is introduced. 
 
Figure 4.1. Periodically located cells 
 There are some assumptions for the unit cell model. First, the inclusion is located periodically in the matrix 
material as figure 4.1. Thus, only a unit cell is considered for the analysis. Second assumption is that the inclusion 
is significantly stiffer than the material. Therefore, the inclusion is assumed as the rigid body. In addition, the 
model is assumed as plane strain. Full three-dimensional study analysis is required to describe the behavior of the 
composite material, but because of the complication of the analyses, Legarth decided to use simpler model. One 
of the possible models was the model that Niordson and Tvergaard [24, 25] used, but they used axisymmetric 
calculation which means it cannot describe the asymmetric behavior, so it is not proper for this research. Thus, 
==PAGE:31==
27 
 
another unit cell model is required. Legarth simplified the problem as the plane with in-plane loading. For this 
simplified problem, plane stress or plane strain model can be considered. Legarth chose the plane strain model for 
the research.

7. Conclusion and Future works 
 
 Composite was used to make the material performance better. One of the methods is inserting the inclusion inside. 
When the composite is loaded, there is possibility that the debonding of the matrix and inclusion occurs. This 
leads to the void nucleation and it leads to the reducing of the fracture toughness. Therefore, analysis of debonding 
in composite is important. 
 There were previous papers that researched about debonding with anisotropic effect. This research extended the 
region for debonding analysis to the material which shows the asymmetrical behavior. To show the SD effect well, 
an isotropic material is used. By using this material, the difference for the isotropic and anisotropic material was 
shown. When the tensile yield stress is bigger than compressive yield stress, the ultimate stress before debonding 
was higher than isotropic. However, the strain was smaller than the isotropic case. 
When the cohesive element is used to describe the adhesive behavior, the decreasing of strain might be observed 
on the debonding point. This phenomenon is called snap-back. When the snap-back occur, the result diverges. 
With this convergence problem, the analysis cannot be proceeded until ultimate fracture of the composite. 
Therefore, solving the snap-back problem is one of the future works. In addition, this research is performed for 
plane strain condition. Therefore, it can be only applied for the composite with long fiber. In the real world, 
however, there are various shapes and size of inclusions. To analyze the debonding for that case, the debonding 
between cube-shaped unit cell and sphere-shaped inclusion can be used.
