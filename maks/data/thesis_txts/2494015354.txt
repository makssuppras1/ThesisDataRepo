Abstract
In this project we have established a coupled-mode formalism to model transverse modal instabilities
in high-power ﬁber ampliﬁers. We speciﬁcally included the eﬀect of pump noise in the coupled-mode
formalism. We found that pump noise as both harmonic ﬂuctuations or white noise can greatly
reduce the TMI threshold. However, it is mainly frequencies of the pump noise that overlap with the
peak of the thermo-optic nonlinear eﬀect that decrease the TMI threshold. This will usually be at
a peak of a few kHz (8.4 kHz in our case) and a width of a few kHz depending on the ﬁber. We also
compared the eﬀects of increased initial signal noise versus pump noise. The consequences of pump
noise is an increased signal noise. Thus, if the initial signal noise was at the same level or larger
than what the pump noise would cause, the pump noise would not decrease the TMI threshold. This
can serve as guidelines to how much the pump noise should be decreased. Additionally, we found
that harmonic pump ﬂuctuations of a few hundred hertz can greatly enhance the four-wave mixing
(FWM) eﬀect of the thermo-optic eﬀect.

Introduction
The ﬁber ampliﬁer is an important component which can amplify the power of desired signals, which
is for instance highly useful in telecommunications [1]. It is also a vital component in the ﬁber laser
characterized by producing a diﬀraction limited beam. High-power ﬁber lasers have a wide range of
applications including material processing and spectroscopy. The short conceptual description of the
ﬁber ampliﬁer is that the core of the normal ﬁber is doped with rare-earth ions such as ytterbium
hereby creating a gain medium. Then pump light is coupled into the ﬁber which is absorbed by
the rare-earth ions which are then excited to a higher energy state.
The signal light is hereby
coupled into the ﬁber where the signal will be enhanced by stimulated emission. Various limitations
to increase the power scaling of ﬁber ampliﬁers include over-heating due to the high power and
diﬀerent nonlinear eﬀects such as Raman gain [2, 3]. To compensate for these eﬀects large mode
area ﬁbers are used [2,3]. However, in such multi-moded ﬁbers transverse modal instabilities (TMIs)
occur and disturb the signal such that it no longer is diﬀraction limited [2]. What happens is that the
transverse intensity of the signal will start to strongly ﬂuctuate in time. TMI is one major limitation
to increase the signal power and still obtain a diﬀraction limited signal [2]. The main source for TMI
is that a signiﬁcant amount of power in the signal that initially would be in the fundamental mode
(FM) is transferred to a higher order mode (HOM) at a shifted frequency, i.e. the noise level in
the HOM has been signiﬁcantly increased. This process is caused by thermo-optic nonlinear eﬀects
due to heating caused by the quantum defect (the energy diﬀerence between the pump light and
signal light) and the TMI occurs at a certain power threshold. Several parameters can inﬂuence this
threshold such as ﬁber dimensions [2,4], ﬁber cooling [5], signal noise [4] and pump noise [6,7]. The
general purpose is to design and choose these parameters such that the TMI threshold is increased
as much as possible.
The scope of this project is to establish a coupled-mode formalism to model transverse modal in-
stabilities in high-power ﬁber ampliﬁers. The model will diﬀer from previous eﬀorts by explicitly
including low-frequency pump noise eﬀects. The overall goal of the project is to quantify the impact
of pump noise relative to other sources of technical noise such as signal noise.

Theory
3.1
Theoretical considerations and purpose
In this theory section we will give a general description of the optical ﬁber, how the gain medium
works and then derive the coupled-mode equations (we will also refer to this as the propagation
equation) for our ﬁber including the thermo-optic eﬀect. There are some eﬀects we are neglecting in
our propagation equation and also a number of assumptions. We neglect the dispersion of the ﬁber
as we will only consider frequencies a few kilo hertz away from our center frequency. In this case
the eﬀects of dispersion should be very small. We will also neglect other nonlinear eﬀects intrinsic
to the ﬁber material. These eﬀects are in general proportional to the intensity of the light and
will increase as we propagate the signal [2]. Therefore larger core ﬁbers are used to reduce this
eﬀect [2]. Furthermore, the length of the ﬁber with a gain medium, that we will be studying, will
only be a few meters long. Therefore we will assume that the nonlinear eﬀects intrinsic to the ﬁber
material will not be the dominant eﬀect compared to the thermo-optic eﬀect. Therefore they have
not been included. We have also neglected losses intrinsic to the ﬁber material such as absorption
and scattering. We also assume that ﬁber parameters such as the thermo-optic coeﬃcient, thermal
conductivity and heat capacity are temperature independent. These parameters will be introduced
later. Furthermore, the eigenmodes are also assumed to be constant and therefore independent of
the temperature.
Our goal is to describe the propagation of a light signal through a ﬁber with a gain medium. More
speciﬁcally we wish to study the thermo-optic nonlinear eﬀect and how it aﬀects the signal. The
thermo-optic eﬀect is due to the heating of the gain medium which in return changes the refractive
index. This can be represented as a small perturbation to the polarization, δPth(r, t). The Maxwell’s
equations in time domain will then have the following expression:

3.2
Optical ﬁber and eigenmodes
The optical ﬁber is the primary product used to transmit light. An optical ﬁber can be made with
many diﬀerent geometries. In this project we will be considering a cylindrical step-index ﬁber, SIF.
The SIF has a core with refractive index nc and radius Rc and a surrounding cladding of refractive
index ncl with radius Rcl. The core refractive index is larger than the cladding refractive index such
that the core can conﬁne the light. The pump light, which we will introduce later, is conﬁned within
the cladding. In our case we will also have an outer cladding of refractive index nouter with radius
Router. The ﬁber also has an outer coating, which protects the ﬁber. The material used for such
optical ﬁbers is usually silica glass. The core could then be doped with another material to increase
the refractive index slightly. The optical ﬁber is also referred to as a waveguide and a sketch can be
seen in Fig. (1).

3.3
Gain medium and rate equations
As we are studying the ﬁber ampliﬁer, we have to understand the basics of the gain medium that
is responsible for amplifying our desired signal. We will thus give a simpliﬁed description of this
process. This simpliﬁed model has been used in the implementation.
The way our normal ﬁber is changed is that the ﬁber is doped with another material. This will be
in the ﬁber core and the material would usually be rare-earth ions such as erbium or ytterbium. In
this project we will only be studying ytterbium doped ﬁbers. When ytterbium is used as a dopant in
silica ﬁbers it is in the form of ytterbium ions, Yb3+ [4]. These will then interact with the silica host
and form diﬀerent energy levels. The ﬁber core will then contain a certain concentration of these
ions NY b(r) which could be position dependent, however we will assume a uniform concentration
throughout the ﬁber core NY b(r) = NY b.

population inversion so that our signal is ampliﬁed by stimulated emission and not absorbed. Thus
the pump light need to contain suﬃcient power.

Here we will give a description of TMI and how to quantify it. Ideally, when our signal has propagated
through our ﬁber gain, the output signal has an identical frequency distribution except an increased
power at our carrier frequency. However, due to the diﬀerent nonlinear eﬀects such as the thermo-
optic eﬀect, the signal will now both have diﬀerent frequency components and possibly be distributed
over more than one mode. Therefore the transverse output of the signal intensity will possibly have
many ﬂuctuations as a function of time. These ﬂuctuations will disturb the signal and it may no
longer be useful to the desired application. The signal may no longer be diﬀraction limited. We
now need to quantify the TMI. It has been shown that the primary cause for TMI is an increased
frequency component in the ﬁrst higher order mode [4]. Therefore the power fraction of the ﬁrst
higher order mode will be a good indication for how much our signal has been disturbed. We deﬁne
it as [4]:

4.1
Numerical implementation
Our full propagation equation, Eq. (128), is an initial value problem (ODE with initial conditions).
We can not solve this analytically besides simple cases, so we have to solve it numerically. This means
that all Fourier transforms will be transformed into discrete Fourier transforms. Equivalently, all
integrals will be transformed into Riemann sums. This means that we have to deﬁne a frequency
domain. First we will chose a number of grid points, N = 2x, where x is an integer. Then we will
chose a frequency spacing ∆Ω. Our frequency domain will then be a vector of the form: Ωlist =
[−N/2 : 1 : N/2−1]∆Ω. The notation means that the ﬁrst element has the value −N/2∆Ωand then
in steps of ∆Ωthe ﬁnal value will be (N/2−1)∆Ωresulting in a vector of length N. Correspondingly
our time spacing will be ∆t = 2π/N/∆Ωand time vector tlist = [−N/2 : 1 : N/2−1]∆t. We will end
up with 2N ODE’s for our propagation equation as we are only looking at LP01 and LP11. We will
solve this in Matlab using ode45, which is based on a Runge-Kutta (4,5) method [14]. All Fourier
transforms will be carried out by Matlab’s fast Fourier transform (FFT) [15] which is most eﬃcient
when N has the expression N = 2x. It should be taking account that Matlab’s deﬁnitions for the
Fourier transforms are a bit diﬀerent than in this project, so this has to be taken into account. It
is important to choose a suﬃcient amount of grid points and small enough frequency spacing such
that the problem is described correctly. Therefore one has to perform a convergence study to make
sure the problem is correctly described. This study can be found in the appendix.
We also have to implement the time dependent gain and the time dependent term in the thermo-
optic contribution. To do this we use our deﬁned time vector, tlist, for the pump power. We then
solve the rate equations for each time step. This means that we will solve 2N ODE’s for our rate
equations. The propagation equation and rate equations are solved simultaneously and thus we solve
in total 4N ODE’s. Alternatively one could solve the rate equations independently and simply ﬁt an
expression to the time dependent terms. This would reduce the number of ODE’s that would have
to be solved simultaneously, however this is not how the implementation has been made.

and the dopant is ytterbium.

4.2
Green’s functions
In this section we will have a small discussion about the Green’s function overlap integrals, Gmnpq(Ω)
and Gmn(Ω). The ﬁrst term can be understood as the polarization contribution to the ﬁeld of mode
m due to the ﬁeld of mode n and the intensity overlap of modes p and q. The last term, Gmn(Ω), is
then the polarization contribution to the ﬁeld of mode m due to the ﬁeld of mode n and a constant
intensity contribution. This term arises due to the constant term (Is,0(z, t)) in our expansion of the
intensity: Is(r, t) ≈Is,0(z, t) + δIs,th(r, t). As said before the only non-zero terms are: Gmmmm(Ω),
Gmmnn(Ω), Gmnmn(Ω), Gmnnm(Ω) and Gmm(Ω). Each term has a real part and an imaginary part.
The real part of the ﬁrst term Gmmmm(Ω) is responsible for intra-modal eﬀects such as self-phase
modulation (SPM [19]) and four-wave mixing (FWM [19]) [4]. We will later ﬁnd that the FWM can
have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the signal. The imaginary part is responsible for the nonlinear gain
on the Stokes side of the spectrum [4]. The term Gmm(Ω) will have similar eﬀects as Gmmmm(Ω).
The term Gmmnn(Ω) gives rise to cross-phase modulation (XPM [19]) [4]. The imaginary part of
Gmnmn(Ω) is responsible for the power transfer between the modes [4]. This is the term that largely
will be responsible for TMI. The term Gmnnm(Ω) is also responsible for power transfer between the
modes, however it comes with a phase e2i(βm−βn)z. Depending on the ﬁber parameters, this will in
most cases be a fast oscillating term and therefore most likely not have a signiﬁcant contribution to
the power transfer [4]. One detail is that some of these expression are similar, such that:
Gmnmn(Ω) = Gmnnm(Ω) = Gnmnm(Ω) = Gnmmn(Ω).

4.4.3
Summary
We found that the harmonic pump noise had a large inﬂuence on the TMI threshold when the
harmonic frequency is at Ωp = 8438 Hz, which corresponds to the maximum peak of the coupling
term for the Green’s function Gnmnm(Ω). The peak is positioned at ΩG and the width of this peak
is a few kHz. In general pump noise away from this frequency does not have a large inﬂuence on
the TMI threshold.
When the initial signal noise was quantum noise a relative RMS of 1% for
the pump noise would halve the TMI threshold. The dependence of the relative RMS on the TMI
threshold was logarithmic. The pump noise couples power at Ω= 0 to the frequency of Ωp. Thus,
the initial coupling of the signal into the HOM will be distributed to Ωp where the noise experiences
the nonlinear gain and causes the TMI. The dependence of the initial HOM content, f11, on the
TMI threshold was also logarithmic. However, changing the relative RMS one order of magnitude
would change the TMI threshold much more than changing the initial HOM content.
This was
approximately a factor of 2 larger.
We also combined an increased initial signal noise of values
around −100 dBc/Hz with pump noise. We found that the pump noise does not simply decrease the
TMI threshold independent of the signal noise. The pump noise serves to increase the signal noise
by coupling power from Ω= 0 to Ωp. If the signal noise is already on the same level or larger than
what the pump noise will contribute, we will not see an increase in the TMI threshold by lowering
the pump noise. The opposite also holds true, i.e. that changing a small signal noise, when the pump
noise is large, will not aﬀect the TMI threshold. We also found that harmonic pump noise of a few
hundred hertz could greatly enhance the FWM eﬀects of the thermo-optic eﬀect. The power would
be distributed into many peaks at frequencies above and below Ω= 0 determined by Ωp. However,
the peaks were out of phase such that the output power of the signal still would follow the frequency
of the pump, Ωp, and not be something chaotic. This behaviour was slightly represented when using
the relative RMS of the intensity of the signal as a measure of the TMI threshold. However, the
sudden increase in the relative RMS of the intensity was still governed by the HOM at the frequency
ΩG. The consequences of splitting the signal into many peaks can be diﬃcult to say something about
and it may depend on the application.
Using white noise as pump noise we found very similar tendencies as for the harmonic pump noise.
The only thing that will not happen is the distinct splitting of the power into diﬀerent peaks with
diﬀerent frequencies.
We have only studied one kind of ﬁber and as such we have not studied how ﬁber parameters will
inﬂuence the eﬀects of pump noise. For instance it could be that the ﬁber parameters could eﬀect
how strong the FWM eﬀect would be.
This will mostly likely be through the Green’s function
overlap. It has already been shown how ﬁber parameters change the coupling term between the two
modes [4].

==PAGE:55==
Conclusion
In this project we have modelled thermo-optic modal instabilities in high-power ﬁber ampliﬁers with
pump noise. We have derived coupled-mode equations that include a time dependent gain due to
pump noise. We have then implemented and veriﬁed a numerical model which solves these equations.
Using this model we have found that pump noise with frequency components overlapping with the
main frequency of TMI ﬂuctuations, due to the thermo-optic eﬀect, can greatly decrease the TMI
threshold, which has also been found in experiments [6]. Pump noise with frequency components
outside this interval will in general not have a large inﬂuence on the TMI threshold. For the speciﬁc
ﬁber studied in this project this main frequency is positioned at Ω= −8438 Hz with a width of a few
kHz. These values are of course ﬁber dependent, but it will mainly be in a frequency range similar
to these values. The origin for why the pump noise eﬀects the TMI threshold comes from the initial
HOM input of the signal at the carrier frequency. The pump noise can then couple power at the
carrier frequency to the main frequency of TMI ﬂuctuations and thereby enhance the thermo-optic
eﬀect causing the TMI threshold. We found a logarithmic dependence for the initial HOM content
on the TMI threshold for values in the range 1% down to 0% for the initial HOM content. Similarly,
we found a logarithmic dependence of the relative RMS of the pump noise on the TMI threshold for
values in the range 10% down to 0% for the relative RMS. However, decreasing the relative RMS
one order of magnitude yielded a larger increase in the TMI threshold compared to decreasing the
initial HOM content one order of magnitude. All these ﬁndings were done under the assumption that
the signal noise was the lowest possible i.e. quantum noise. Therefore we also increased the relative
signal noise to values around −100 dBc/Hz. As has already been shown before [4] and also replicated
in this project we have a logarithmic dependence of the signal noise on the TMI threshold. Now
combining the pump noise with the increased signal noise we found that the eﬀects do not simply
decrease the TMI threshold independently of each other. In fact, we found that combining pump
noise and signal noise, that independently would decrease the TMI threshold by a factor 2 compared
to pure quantum noise, had the same TMI threshold as the eﬀects independently. The reason for
this is that the pump noise will add signal noise to the initial signal noise. In the case described
the pump noise would approximately double the signal noise. Due to the logarithmic dependence of
signal noise on the TMI threshold, we will not see a large diﬀerence. Therefore either pump noise
or signal noise can be the source for TMI. Having only decreased one of these will therefore not be
expected to have a large inﬂuence on the TMI threshold. All of these ﬁndings of course depend upon
what the actual circumstances are in real applications and how these can be controlled, i.e. signal
and pump noise. However, the results in this project can serve as guidelines for what the expected
inﬂuence will be when changing pump noise, signal noise and initial HOM content.
One additional thing we also found is that a harmonic pump ﬂuctuation with a frequency in the
range of a few hundred hertz with a relative RMS of around 1% could greatly enhance the FWM
eﬀect due to the thermo-optic eﬀect. The FWM eﬀect of the thermo-optic eﬀect has a range around
a few hundred hertz around the carrier frequency. In fact, the signal was completely split up into
many peaks and we no longer had a speciﬁed carrier frequency. However, these peaks were out of
phase such that the output power of the signal still followed the harmonic ﬂuctuations of the pump.
Decreasing the relative RMS of the pump noise would accordingly suppress this eﬀect.
