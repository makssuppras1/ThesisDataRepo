Abstract 
 
Nucleic acids in the form of DNA and RNA are important targets in pharmaceutical and 
biological research. Several optical methods such as FISH and FIT have been developed in 
order to investigate these molecules. Central to such methods is the use of a fluorescent 
nucleobase attached to a complimentary strand of a target.  
 
The synthetic work presented herein was directed towards designing novel fluorescent 
molecules for subsequent post-synthetic attachment to oligonucleotides using CuAAC click 
chemistry. An alkyne was incorporated at the 5-position of the nucleobase uracil in 2'-
deoxyuridine, followed by conversion to a phosphoramidite. This phosphoramidite was used 
in oligonucleotide synthesis of a probe with a sequence directed towards mus musculus 
(house mouse) low-density lipoprotein receptor-related protein 1 (Lrp1). 
 
Syntheses of fluorophores with azido-groups proceeded along two different tracks. Project A 
and B, were unfortunately not finalized due to synthetic challenges and time constraints. In 
Project A, a furanyl-substituted indole, 5-(3-(4-aminophenyl)-1H-indol-2-yl)furan-2-
carbonitrile (A-7), was synthesised using Larock heteroannulation, but in too low amounts for 
further transformation to the desired azide. In project B, furanyl-substituted anthracene with 
various azide-baring linkers was planned. The key intermediate, 3-(10-(furan-2-yl)-
anthracen-9-yl)-prop-2-yn-1-ol (B-3) was synthesised, but there were problems with the 
solubility of the starting material, 9,10-dibromoanthracene 
 
The two prepared fluorophores ((A-7) and (B-3)) were sent for FLIM analysis by Rulan 
Dmitriev (University College Cork, Ireland). The fluorophores were analysed in living cells 
(HCT116 cells) and the result showed that (A-7) had the best fluorescent profile. It stained 
cells quickly and localized to the cytoplasm, with some punctuate pattern.

Resumé  
 
Nukleinsyrerne, DNA og RNA, er vigtige molekyler i farmaceutisk og biologisk forskning. 
Optiske metoder, som FISH og FIT er blevet udviklet for at undersøge sekvenser og 
funktioner af disse nukleinsyrer. Disse metoder er baseret på inkorporering og brug af 
fluorescerende nukleobaser, som kan visualisere binding til en komplementær streng af et 
ønsket oligonukleotid.  
 
Den eksperimentelle del af dette arbejde var at syntetisere nye fluorescerende molekyler til 
efterfølgende syntetisk kobling til oligonukleotider under anvendelse af CuAAC klik-kemi. 
Der blev inkorporeret en alkyn i 5-positionen på nucleobasen uracil i 2'-dexyuridin, som 
efterfølgende blev omdannet til en phosphoramidit. Denne phosphoramidit blev anvendt i 
oligonukleotidsyntese af en sekvens rettet mod mus musculus low-density lipoprotein.   
 
To klasser af fluoroforer indeholdende en azido-gruppe blev forsøgt fremstillet, projekt A og 
B. Desværre blev disse synteser på grund af vanskelige synteser og tidsbegrænsning ikke 
afsluttede. I projekt A blev der syntetiseret en furanyl substitueret indol, 5-(3-(4-
aminophenyl)-1H-indol-2-yl)furan-2-carbonitrile (A-7), ved brug af Larock heteroannulation 
men i for lavt udbytte til videre syntese af det ønskede azid. I projekt B var furanyl-
substituerede anthracener med forskellige azid-linkere planlagt. Der blev kun fremstillet 
nøgle intermediatet, 3-(10-(furan-2-yl)-anthracen-9-yl)-prop-2-yn-1-ol (B-3), da der var 
problemer med opløselig af udgangsstoffet (9,10-dibromoanthracen).  
 
De to fremstillede fluorescerende stoffer ((A-7) og (B-3)) blev sendt til FLIM analyse hos 
Ruslan Dmitriev (University College Cork, Irland). Fluoroforene blev testet i levende 
HCT116 celler. Her viste resultatet, at (A-7) gav det bedste resultat, da fluoroforen farvede 
cellerne hurtigt og var lokaliseret i cytoplasmaet med et punktformet mønster.

1. Introduction  
The nucleic acids DNA and RNA are very important to the function of the cell and are key 
molecules in all living organisms. The central dogma describes the flow of genetic 
information. There is a simple linear correspondence between DNA, RNA and protein, where 
the genetic information always flows from DNA to RNA, and then into protein. 
Understanding of structure and function of biomolecules such as DNA and RNA requires a 
wide range of different tools [1].  
 
Replication of DNA is a biochemical process that happens during mitosis (cell division). The 
process is carried out in the cell nucleus for eukaryotes and in cytoplasm for prokaryotes. The 
replication is catalysed by the enzyme DNA polymerase. Transcription is the first step of 
gene expression, where DNA is transcribed into a single RNA strand, a process catalysed by 
the enzyme RNA polymerase. In that way, a complementary antiparallel RNA strand will be 
formed (primary transcript). Translation is the second step - in this step RNA is translated 
into protein [1]. 
 
  
Figure 1: The Central Dogma. The first step is the transcription of DNA to RNA, and the second step is the 
translation of RNA into protein 
Figure 1 shows the three steps in the central dogma. During the last decades, there has been 
continuing efforts toward finding new and more optimized methods for investigation of 
nucleic acids [2]. The use of synthetic DNA/RNA sequences and their analogues for 
recognition of and binding to messenger RNAs (mRNA) encoding disease-causing proteins 
has led to the production of nucleic acid-based drugs capable of inhibiting the expression of 
specific proteins through either an antisense or RNA interference (RNAi) pathways [2]. 
 
Fluorescent nucleobases that can be incorporated into nucleic acids have proven a powerful 
technique to investigate the nature of DNA and RNA. These DNA and RNA analogues are 
chemically modified, but they retain their biological and physicochemical properties and 
display improved fluorescence properties for optical analysis of nucleic acids [3].

The natural nucleobases are essentially non-fluorescent. The implementation of a fluorescent 
part in the nucleobases can change the base-pairing ability of the nucleobase. In the cases 
where Watson-Crick base paring is preserved after modification of the nucleobase, it is called 
a “canonical pairing”, while a more drastic change in the nucleobases, where the base paring 
is affected, results in a non-canonical design. Therefore, canonical nucleobase analogues are 
those where the modification is limited to specific regions of the bases [3]. 
 
The implementation of fluorescent molecules in nucleic acids can be divided into three 
categories [3]:  
1) Direct chemical oligonucleotide synthesis where a fluorescently labelled nucleotide is 
introduced into the sequence (Figure 2, A).  
2) Post-synthetic modification of nucleotides containing a reactive handle with which a 
fluorescent probe, entailing a compatible functional group, can react (Figure 2, B).  
3) Enzymatic incorporation using primers that contain florescent nucleobases.  
 
Each of these categories has advantages and disadvantages. The advantages of the direct 
implementation are the specific incorporation of virtually any probe at any position, and little 
or no constraint on fluorophore structure, but this method has a high cost due to synthesis of 
advanced probe-modified phosphoramidites. Similarly to the direct implementation, the post- 
synthetic approach can incorporate a fluorescent probe in principle at any position. However, 
only (usually mild) reaction conditions compatible with oligonucleotides may be used, 
thereby limiting the structural diversity of the linking moieties to the fluorophores. The post-
synthetic modification can also enable a divergent screen for new fluorophores. The last 
category is enzymatic incorporation; this approach has low cost but requires access to 
labelled DNA/RNA and imposes constraints on positional labelling [3].

Since the 1980s, there has been a growing interest in developing technologies for localization 
of mRNA in the cell. Back then the state-of-the-art method to visualize RNA was ISH [4]. 
Labelling of mRNA has high interest, as mRNA localization has been shown to be important 
tool for exploring fundamental molecular biology and disease development on a cellular 
level. The probe sequence targeted for mRNA bears features that enable visualization upon 
hybridization, which could be a fluorescent tag. This concept is called fluorescence in situ 
hybridization (FISH) [4].  
 
The goal of this thesis is to chemically design novel fluorescent nucleoside analogous for 
testing in living cells. It will be attempted to synthesise different fluorophores that can be 
coupled to a modified oligonucleotide. The oligonucleotide is planned to be tested in living 
cells.  
 
In this thesis the theory of nucleic acid, oligonucleotide synthesis and the properties of 
fluorescent molecules and associated analytical methods will be described. Furthermore, 
synthesis of functionalized phosphoramidites and strategies to obtain novel fluorescent 
probes are also described. This is followed by the results and discussion section, which ends 
with a conclusion. Every synthesised compound has been characterized with NMR.

When single-stranded DNA pairs up with a complementary DNA-strand following the 
Watson-Crick base pairing rules, the result is a double-stranded DNA that forms a double 
helix as shown in Figure 8. In a DNA double helix, the strands run in opposite (antiparallel) 
directions thereby permitting base pairing between them, which are essential for replication 
or transcription of the encoded information. 
 
 
Figure 8: DNA double helix [8] 
Hybridization of nucleic acids is a reversible process. In this process single-stranded nucleic 
acids are allowed to interact in a sequence-specific manner (Watson-Crick) to form 
complexes or hybrids [9]. 
The difference between DNA and RNA is that DNA exists as a double helix, where RNA 
mostly only exists naturally as a single-strand. The hybrid between RNA and DNA also exist 
as a double helix. Hybridization is a phenomenon in which single-stranded DNA or RNA 
molecules anneal to complementary DNA or RNA [10]. These can either be two DNA-
strands, two RNA-strands or one DNA- and one RNA-strand. These strands are 
complementary to each other but may also be complementary to other sequences present in 
their surroundings [11]. A double-stranded DNA sequence or DNA/RNA sequence are stable 
under physiological conditions due to low content of mismatch. These can be denatured to 
give two separate single-strands by heating [11].  
1.2. Fluorescence  
In order to easily analyse oligonucleotides, fluorophores can be coupled to the 
oligonucleotide. Biophysical studies are often used to analyse fluorescently labelled 
oligonucleotides. High-resolution optical methods represent powerful tools able to monitor 
minute amounts of interacting oligonucleotides tagged with fluorescent probes. These 
methods are non-destructive, have no side effects, give high resolution and are very cost 
efficient [12].  
 
Fluorophores are molecules, which interact with light in a way that can be detected. The π- 
and n-electrons of fluorophores enable them to absorb light at a certain wavelength and emit 
it at a longer wavelength (i.e. fluorescence). The wavelength at which the molecule emits, 
will be longer if there are more of either π- or n-electrons [13].  
 
The fluorophores do not spontaneously produce light. The light is absorbed at one 
wavelength whereby the fluorophore reach an exited state. Here the fluorophores will decay 
back to their ground state. The energy is emitted as a photon, when the fluorophore decays. 
The decay takes a short amount of time (nano seconds) [13], [14].

1.2.1. Fluorescence Resonance Energy Transfer (FRET) 
Fluorescence (Förster) resonance energy transfer (FRET) is a non-radiative, dipole-dipole 
coupling process whereby energy from an excited donor fluorophore is transferred to an 
acceptor fluorophore in close proximity [15].  
 
It is an extremely useful technique used to study molecular interactions inside living cells. 
The technique can for example be used to study protein-protein interactions, ligand binding to 
a receptor and molecular dimerization. In order to record FRET, the analyte needs to be 
tagged with different fluorophores, enabling the emission (λem) of one fluorophore (donor) to 
overlap with the excitation (λex) of another fluorophore (acceptor). FRET occurs, when the 
donor receives energy from a laser or a lamp, and transfers the energy to the acceptor, which 
then emits light at a longer wavelength [16]. The energy that can be transferred using FRET 
decreases linearly with the distance between the two fluorophores to the sixth power. This 
means that in order for FRET to occur the two fluorophores need to be in close proximity to 
each other [17].  
1.2.2. Live Cell Dyes 
It can be difficult to analyse a living cell for its function, because it is not possible to extract a 
part to analyse while keeping the cell alive. Therefore, it is desirable to analyse living cells 
with optical methods. As stated before, the optical methods do not destroy the analyte or 
living cells. Bioimaging using fluorescence microscopy can be used to study the function of 
the newly created fluorescent molecules. This approach does also have an advantage over 
other methods such as in vitro reconstitution of cellular processes, in that it provides an extra 
time dimension to the study, rather than a snapshot of the cell [18]. 
 
These methods are not without flaws, and while fluorophores can be used multiple times for 
studies outside of the cell, to study the fluorophores inside the cell, it is necessary to use very 
intense light to get high-resolution results from the study. This can lead to difficulties with 
the longevity of the fluorophores since they are prone to degeneration due to photoinstability. 
Furthermore, the fluorophore can generate free radicals upon photobleaching that can further 
destroy the cell [19]. 
 
A prerequisite for such studies is that the technique and/or the added reagents (fluorophores) 
do not compromise the cells. It is therefore necessary to test the cells for viability to exclude 
potential cytotoxicity of the probe [20]. 
 
Several types of studies can be conducted. It is possible to study naturally occurring 
fluorescent molecules like NAD(P)H as well as proteins made fluorescent by fusing green 
fluorescent protein (GFP) or its red variant on to the protein of interest [18].  
 
Other types of studies that can be performed in living cells are studies to determine the 
presence (or not) of specific enzymes. Such enzymes could be biomarkers of disease-related 
proteins where early detection (diagnosis) is of outmost importance. One such example is 
tyrosinase, an enzyme that is constitutively expressed in melanocytes and in malignant

melanoma cells. The enzyme hydroxylates tyrosine as the first step in melanin formation. By 
using a dye that becomes fluorescent after tyrosinase-mediated hydroxylation and 
spontaneous cleavage of a masking group, it is possible to image specifically the tyrosinase 
activity in living cells (and in vivo) [20], [21]. 
 
Many other analytes can be detected and imaged in live cells. The principle is that presence 
of the analyte will trigger the release of a fluorescent signal by the reporter.  For example, 
potassium ion fluxes can be imaged by a BODIPY (boron-dipyrromethene) dye with attached 
crown ether. When no potassium is complexed within the crown ether, the molecule is not 
fluorescent, whereas when potassium is complexed, a strong red fluorescent signal is 
obtained [22]. Another example is to test for presence of hydrogen sulphide in live cells. 
When present, hydrogen sulphide will unmask a hydroxyl group in a BODIPY dye making 
the dye fluorescent [23].   
 
Another type of study, as described in this thesis, is where oligonucleotides are fluorescently 
labelled. These can then be used to detect mutations in DNA/RNA or localise specific genes 
on chromosomes in live cells. 
1.2.3. Fluorescence-Lifetime Imaging Microscopy (FLIM) 
Fluorescence-lifetime imaging microscopy (FLIM) is a challenging technique. It produces an 
image based on differences in the excited state decay rate from a fluorescent sample. In FLIM 
the rate of decay of the emission is measured, and that is the property of the individual 
fluorophores [16]. It is defined as the average time in which a molecule remains in an excited 
state prior to returning to the ground state by emitting a photon [24].  
 
FLIM depends on a wealth of environmental parameters such as pH, ion or oxygen 
concentration, molecular binding or the proximity of energy acceptors making it the 
technique of choice for functional imaging of many kinds [24].  
 
It is a stronger method than intensity methods, since FLIM does not depend on concentration, 
absorption of the sample, sample thickness, photo-bleaching and/or excitation intensity [24].

1.3. Diagnostics of Point Mutations in Genes 
A mutation is a change in the nucleotide sequence in coding portions of the DNA. A point 
mutation is characterized by a single nucleotide alteration. The single nucleotide can be 
switched to another nucleotide, deleted or inserted into the DNA chain. Point mutations can 
happen spontaneously during DNA replication.  
Many different methods have been used for detection of mutations for diagnostic purposes. 
Usually the process is starting with the polymerase chain reaction (PCR) to amplify the 
specific DNA region. Examples of fluorescence-based techniques used to investigate point 
mutations are described below. 
1.3.1. Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) 
PCR is a gene amplification technique, which has many applications in molecular- and 
microbiology. The technique is used to amplify the copy number of a specific DNA region of 
interest. The principle of the technique is to have two species of DNA, which are 
complementary to the ends of the gene that is to be amplified [25].   
The technique is a cycle method, which is divided into three steps: 1) thermal-induced 
separation of the double-stranded target DNA (denaturation step), 2) annealing of synthetic 
oligonucleotide primers to the target sequence, 3) extension of the annealed primer-target 
sequence by a DNA polymerase [25]. The cycle is repeated 25-40 times. Here the number of 
cycles required is inversely dependent on the starting copy number of the target DNA. PCR 
products can then be analysed and/or quantified different methods [25]. 
1.3.2. Fluorescence In Situ Hybridization (FISH) 
FISH is an important tool in genetic analysis. More specific, FISH is a molecular cytogenetic 
technique that uses fluorescent probes to detect specific DNA/RNA molecules, which differ 
in only a single base [4]. The technique is used to detect and localize the presence or absence 
of specific DNA/RNA sequences.  
 
In the 1960s Joseph Gall and Mary Lou Pardue realized that molecular hybridization could be 
used to identify the position of DNA sequences in situ. The two researchers published a 
landmark paper in 1969, where they demonstrated that radioactive copies of a ribosomal 
DNA sequence could be used to detect complementary DNA sequences in the nucleus of a 
frog egg [26]. After the discovery by the two researchers, radioactive labels were replaced 
with fluorescent labels in hybridization probes, since these have greater safety, stability and 
ease of detection [26].  
 
The target and the fluorescently labelled probe are mixed and denatured to obtain single-
stranded oligonucleotides. In the denaturation step the hydrogen bonds between the 
nucleobases are broken, and the single-stranded oligonucleotides are ready to find new 
hybridization partners. Upon annealing, the nucleotides will re-hybridize according to the 
Watson-Crick base-pairing rules, while maximising the overlaps. In this way, the fluorescent 
probe and the target will hybridize if the sequences match, and a new double-stranded 
fluorescent oligonucleotide will form. This can be detected for example using a fluorescent 
microscope. The principle of FISH is shown in Figure 9.

for example be using non-fluorescent labels that can be detected using immunological or 
enzymatic methods. Although this requires an additional step, this method has a build-in 
amplification of the signal that can be beneficial.

1.3.3. Molecular Beacons  
Molecular beacons consist of single-stranded oligonucleotide probes with a stem-and-loop 
structure, which recognize and report the presence of specific nucleic acid sequences in a 
homogeneous solution [27]. The stem has complimentary arms on each end of the sequence. 
The loop consists of a probe sequence that is complementary to a target sequence and it is 
analysed in vitro. At one end of the stem, a fluorophore is covalently attached and at the other 
end a quencher is attached [27].  
A quencher is a molecule, which when interacting with a fluorophore can decrease the 
fluorescence of a given molecule, due to the mechanism that utilizes FRET (see section 
1.2.1.). Different quencher molecules have different quenching ranges to cover most of the 
visible spectrum. The molecule needs to be in close proximity to the fluorophore, often 
chemically linked, to get the quenching effect [28]. 
 
Molecular beacons do not fluoresce, when they are free in solution, since the stem places the 
fluorophore and the quencher close to each other. If the target sequence is present, the 
molecular beacon will hybridize to a nucleic acid (probe target) and then form a probe-target 
hybrid that is longer and more stable than the stem hybrid [27]. This will move the 
==PAGE:18==

fluorophore away from the quencher and thus, the beacon will emit fluorescence. Figure 10

1.3.4. Forced Intercalation (FIT)  
Another method to increase a specific fluorescent signal binding is to use forced intercalation, 
also called FIT, probes [4]. FIT probes are peptide nucleic acids (PNA) or DNA single 
strands. These probes contain a visco-sensitive cyanine dye for example from the thiazole 
orange (TO) family. This dye is linked to the backbone of DNA or PNA as a canonical 
surrogate nucleobase [30]. The TO maintains the duplex stability and hybridization 
selectivity regardless of the corresponding base. TO fluorescence increased up to 30-fold 
upon hybridization if there is no mismatch in the surroundings. This makes FIT probes an 
attractive improvement of ISH. FIT probes have been expanded to contain dyes that emit in 
the blue and green ranges [4]. The principle of FIT is shown in Figure 11.

1.4. Gene Therapy 
Genes represent potential targets for gene therapy. Gene therapeutics can modulate (up- or 
down-regulate) disease-associated proteins [31].  
Transporting the therapeutic oligonucleotide to the target cells can be difficult, since the 
exogenous genetic material must be transported into the nucleus of the target cells, and 
ideally, only to the target cells. Furthermore, cell permeability is also an issue due to the size 
and the charge of oligonucleotides. In drug discovery the aim is to find potential drugs, which 
==PAGE:19==
19 of 82 
are potent, selective, and preferably bioavailable to reach its target at sufficient concentration 
[32]. 
 
Protein up-regulation can be achieved via incorporation of a gene coding for a specific 
protein on a plasmid. A vector used for transferring genetic material is defined as the vehicle. 
The ideal vector would transfer a precise amount of genetic material into the target cell, and 
secure expression of the gene product without major side effects [33]. 
Protein down-regulation can be done by altering or reducing expression of the target RNA by 
breakdown the target transcript [31]. Different methods like antisense oligonucleotides 
(ASO), transcriptional gene silencing (TGS) and RNA interference (RNAi) can be employed 
when the genetic sequence of a particular disease gene is known. 
1.4.1. Antisense Oligonucleotides (ASO) 
ASO are synthetic single-stranded oligonucleotides (8-50 nucleotides) able to bind to a 
complementary mRNA and inactivate it [32]. The antisense oligonucleotides can alter the 
RNA function through different mechanisms either by restoring protein expression, reducing 
expression of a toxic protein or by modifying mutated disease-associated proteins to cause 
regains of function [32]. 
Standard DNA-based oligonucleotides suffer from pharmacological shortcomings, such as 
short plasma half-life due to degradation by exo- and endonucleases. Consequently, improved 
oligonucleotides have been developed with reduced susceptibility to these nucleases by 
chemical modification. These modifications are for example [32]: 
 
• Replacement of phosphodiester linkage by phosphorothioate 
• Locked nucleic acid (LNA) sugar units 
• 2'-methoxy or 2'-fluoro 
 
The name “antisense oligonucleotide” originates from the fact that the nucleotide sequence is 
complementary to the gene's mRNA sequence, which is called the “sense” sequence [34].

1.5.1. Activation & Coupling 
Afterwards, the support-bound nucleoside is ready to react with the next nucleoside, which is

The nucleoside phosphoramidite is mixed with an activator (tetrazole or derivative). This will 
protonate the diispropylamino group, which then will be substituted by the tetrazole anion. 
This tetrazole is a good leaving group, and it is rapidly displaced by attack of the 5'-hydroxy 
group of the support-bound nucleoside on its neighbouring phosphorus atom. This reaction 
forms a new phosphorus-oxygen bond, which is energetically very favourable and creates a 
support-bound phosphite triester.

1.6. Labelling of oligonucleotides  
Oligonucleotide can be labelled with fluorophores by using different kinds of methods. Three 
of these methods are click chemistry (CuAAC), Diels-Alder reaction and N-

1.6.1. Click Chemistry  
Barry Sharpless introduced click chemistry at the American Chemical Society annual 
meeting in 1999, and since then it has proven both versatile and expansive in use [36]. Click 
chemistry is used in a wide range of different chemistries, for example organic synthesis, 
medicinal chemistry and bioconjugate chemistry. The field of click chemistry is growing at 
an exponential rate [37].  
 
Click chemistry has some requirements, which must be satisfied before a reaction is 
considered a click reaction. The reaction must be modular, wide in scope, give very high 
yield, and generate only unreactive by-products [37]. One click chemistry reaction is a Cu(I)-
catalysed azide alkyne cycloaddition (CuAAC) reaction, which will be used in this project. 
Only a few years after its discovery [38], the CuAAC was established as one of the most 
valuable means for covalent assembly of complex molecules [37].

1.6.2. Diels-Alder Reaction 
Another way to label the oligonucleotides aside from click chemistry is with a Diels-Alder

fluorescent compound [39]. The principle in a Diels-Alder reaction is that a reaction will

be a cyclohexene structure [39].

conjugation in addition to the other π-electron systems. Furan was chosen, since it is the most

sensitivity to its microenvironment [41].

features compared to either furan or phenyl as fluorescent molecules on nucleotides [41]. The 
target molecules of this thesis are compounds that contain conjugated furan groups.

electrons. The indole is substituted with a 5-cyano-substituted furan and a phenyl group. The 
cyano group will pull the electrons from the indole and it that way; the π-electron conjugation 
will be extended. The phenyl group contributes with an additional 6 π-electrons, and the total 
number of conjugated electrons will be 22.

furanyl group, giving a total of 20 conjugated electrons. Furthermore, the anthracene has

4. Reaction Mechanisms 
One of the most difficult challenges in organic synthesis is to construct new C-C bond. One 
way to make these bonds is to use palladium as a transition metal catalyst. By using a 
transition metal catalyst it has provide chemists with new tools to construct C-C bonds [60]. 
In 2010 Richard Heck, Ei-ichi Negishi, and Akira Suzuki received the Nobel Prize in 
Chemistry for their contributions for developing of Pd-catalysed cross coupling [60].  
 
In this project three different transition metal catalysed cross-coupling reactions are described 
and used, specifically the Suzuki-Miyaura and Sonogashira cross-coupling reactions. 
Furthermore, the Larock heteroannulation will be described, since this was used in project A.  
4.1. The Suzuki-Miyaura Reaction 
The Suzuki-Miyaura cross coupling reaction dates back to 1979 [61] and represents a facile 
method to install new C-C-bonds [62]. The Suzuki-Miyaura is a coupling of boronic 
acids/esters with vinyl/aryl halides. The Suzuki-Miyaura coupling is a widely used cross-
coupling reaction, which proceeds under basic condition and in the presence of a Pd(0) 
(catalyst). Pd(0) can be generated in situ from PdCl2 or Pd(OAc)2 [62] or added directly in 
form of Pd(PPh3)4. The reaction was used in this project, and the mechanism with 2-furanyl 
boronic acid and 9,10-dibromoanthracene is shown in Figure 34.

second step is a ligand substitution. The bromide is exchanged at the palladium for a

4.2. The Sonogashira Reaction 
Another cross coupling reaction is the Sonogashira reaction. The advantage with Sonogashira 
is that it does not require activated substrates, compared to Suzuki-Miyaura and other cross 
coupling reactions [64]. A Sonogashira will need a base (e.g. Et3N), a Cu(I)-species (e.g. CuI) 
and a catalyst (e.g. Pd(OAc2), or similar in presence of PPh3) to occur and form a new C-C 
bond. The new C-C bond will be formed from a terminal alkyne and an aryl halide [64].  
 
In this project, the Sonogashira reaction was successfully used to synthesise nucleoside (2), 
compound (A-3) and compound (B-3). The mechanism for a Sonogahira cross coupling is 
shown in Figure 35 for formation of (A-3).

the terminal alkyne will be introduced, and a new square planar Pd(II) intermediate will be 
generated. It is important that the two PPh3-groups are cis, and likewise, the furan- and 
alkyne-groups are cis. To finish the cycle, a reductive elimination will occur. Here the new C-
C bond will be formed under regeneration of Pd(0) [65].

The mechanism is shown in Figure 36. Larock heteroannulation is a convenient and effective

5. Conclusion  
The goal of this thesis was to design and synthesise an oligonucleotide labelled with novel 
fluorophores on some of the nucleobases. An oligonucleotide with alkyne groups as coupling 
sites for the fluorophores was synthesised together with key intermediates for novel azido-
substituted fluorophores.  
 
The strategy for the synthesis of the fluorophores involved synthesis of different furan- and 
azido-substituted fluorophores with indole and anthracene backbones. The synthesis was 
based on known reactions and commercially available chemicals.  
 
The strategy for oligonucleotide was synthesis of a uracil phosphoramidite with an alkyne 
group on the nucleobase. The phosphoramidite was used in oligonucleotide syntheses of the 
following sequence (based on the sequence of mus musculus low density lipoprotein receptor-
related protein 1 (Lrp1)):  
 
5'-GAC UCT AAT TGA GGG CAA GAU GAC-3' 
 
where the phosphoramidite with alkyne was incorporated as U, and C and G (in bold) were 
incorporated as LNA.  
  
For the fluorophore with an indole as backbone, the synthesis involved five steps. The third 
step was a Larock heterannulation reaction where the yield of the product was poor due to 
difficulty with the purification and separation of regioisomers by column chromatography. 
For future work, isolation both of regioisomers and for obtaining better yield would be to 
carefully make a gradient purification column chromatography with re-purification of impure 
fractions. The remaining steps are well known reactions, with deprotection and formation of a 
diazonium ion and then finish with NaN3 to get the desired novel fluorescent furanylindoles. 
The isolated intermediate 5-(3-(4-aminophenyl)-1H-indol-2-yl)furan-2-carbonitrile (A-7) was 
analysed by FLIM in living cells by Ruslan Dmitriev  (University College Cork, Ireland).  
 
For the fluorophore with an anthracene as backbone, the synthesis involved two initial steps 
to the key intermediate (B-3) and different steps to form azide analogues with different 
linkers. More work should be done to optimize the synthesis of the key intermediate (B-3). 
The main problem with this strategy was the first step to couple 2-furanyl boronic acid to the 
bromoanthracene. Several methods were tried but none gave an acceptable yield. The isolated 
intermediate (B-3) was analysed by FLIM in living cells by Ruslan Dmitriev  (University 
College Cork, Ireland). 
 
The FLIM results looked far more promising for (A-7) than for (B-3).   
  
The synthesis of the oligonucleotide with the alkyne group as coupling site went as expected 
however, the oligonucleotide was not purified due to time constrains.  
==PAGE:43==
43 of 82 
 
During the experimental work, the intermediates were isolated and all the isolated products 
were purified with column chromatography and characterised by 1H- and 13C-NMR.  
 
It has not been possible to complete the synthesis of the planned fluorophores and thereby not 
couple them to the prepared oligonucleotide. One of the reasons for this is that it took some 
time to receive the ordered starting materials resulting in time constrains at the end of the 
project. Furthermore, the project has shown that even though syntheses have been described 
in the literature and are reported to give good yields, it can be difficult to replicate the work 
and isolate the products.  
 
There are still many pathways to investigate before it is possible to synthesise an 
oligonucleotide with the novel target fluorophores attached. This project has shown the way 
forward regarding the furanylindoles (project A) and this project could be completed within a 
limited timeframe. Regarding the furanylanthracenes (project B), this has proven to be more 
difficult.

6.6. DNA Oligonucleotide Synthesis 
The oligonucleotide synthesis was performed on an ABI Expedite 8909 Nucleic Acid 
Synthesis System by the phosphoramidite approach. Synthesis was carried out in a 0.2 µmol 
scale. The applied phosphoramidites were DMT-dC(Bz)-Phosphoramidite, DMT-dA(Bz)-
Phosphoramidite, DMT-dG(iBu)-Phosphoramidite and DMT-dT-Phosphoramidite. The 
following sequence was carried out from 3' to 5':  
 
5'-GAC UCT AAT TGA GGG CAA GAU GAC-3' 
 
The modified phosphoramidite (5) was manually coupled twice in the sequence, which is 
marked with U in the sequence.  
 
LNA phosphoramidites were manually coupled to the oligonucleotide. G-LNA (LNA-
G(dmf)-Phosphoramidite) (7.0 mg) was dissolved in 200 µL dry MeCN mixed with 300 µL 
Activator 42 (5-[3,5-bis(trifluoromethyl) phenyl]-1H-tetrazole). The G-LNA mixture was 
added to the column over 20 minutes.  
 
At the same way, C-LNA (LNA-C(Bz)-phosphoramidite) was manually coupled to the 
oligonucleotide. C-LNA (7.1 mg) was mixed in a syringe with 200 µL dry MeCN and 300 
µL activator (5-[3,5-bis-(trifluoromethyl) phenyl]-1H-tetrazole). The C-LNA mixture was 
added to the column over 20 minutes.  
 
Phosphoramidite (5) (60 mg) was dissolved in MeCN (800 µL) and dry DCM (400 µL). 200 
µL of this solution was mixed with 300 µL Activator 42. The activated phosphoramidite (5) 
was added to the column over 20 minutes. To ensure the coupling of the modified 
phosphoramidite (5), the coupling was carried out twice (reaction solution 200 µL + 300 µL 
activator).  
After the C-LNA, the modified phosphoramidite (5) was added again. The same procedure 
was followed (reaction solution 200 µL + 300 µL activator) and the manually -coupled was 
also carried out twice.  
 
Due to time reasons, solely the crude product could be analysed. The concentration was

The solution (20 µL) was used for HPLC analyse. The HPLC spectrum is shown in Appendix
