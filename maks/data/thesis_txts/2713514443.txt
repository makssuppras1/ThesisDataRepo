Abstract
The project investigates the ability of blood transcriptomics and machine learning to
discriminate the salinity habitat of adult European eels through their physiological con­
dition. A longitudinal study is conducted on 20 individuals collected in freshwater and
raised in saltwater over four weeks. During this period, blood is collected at capture
every second week, resulting in a total of three samples per individual. These samples
are sequenced, preprocessed and analysed using the most suitable pipelines. Physiolog­
ical changes are investigated through differential expression analysis of the blood. This
is the baseline to perform three machine learning approaches for the classification of
salinity condition of the individuals. Potential biomarkers are identified and extrapo­
lated to additional datasets from eels collected separately. Overall, the thesis combines
a longitudinal study and transcriptomic­based machine learning for the development of
a non­lethal sampling method to predict eel salinity habitat.

Chapter 1. General introduction
1
General introduction
1.1
European Eel
The European Eel is a critically endangered species with a complex life cycle. Eels live
on average 5­20 years in fresh­ (FW), brackish (BW) or coastal saltwater (SW) habitats,
before crossing the Atlantic Ocean to spawn in the Sargasso Sea in the Caribbean. The
larvae migrate back towards European and north African shores drifting with the Gulf
Stream current. This migration is of high interest but not well understood yet, previous
studies have described eel navigation using magnetic fields in BW and identified lunar
cues as possible guidance at sea [2].
Figure 1.1: Major life stages of European eel life cycle. From [2]
The journey of the eels begins as leaf­shaped larvae drifting from the Sargasso Sea near
Bermuda to the Gulf Stream across the Atlantic Ocean in Western Europe. At the end
of this one to two­year crossing, they metamorphose into transparent glass eels, at a
size of about 7cm. Navigating up estuaries toward inland rivers, glass eels become
pigmented (elvers) then grow into yellow eels. They live from 5 to 20 years in the same
river, growing from immature yellow eels into silver eels. At this stage they are ready
to start their migration of about 10.000 km, returning to the Sargasso Sea to spawn and
1
==PAGE:15==
eventually die [2]. Figure 1.1 represents the major stages of their life cycle and the water
type corresponding to each of the stages.
As a catadromous species, eels are able to adapt to FW, BW, and SW, which complicates
stock assessment and management of this species. In the last 20 years, the number of
eels reaching European river systems has decreased dramatically . This critical situation
is due to the interaction of many factors including over­fishing, habitat loss, barriers to
up­and downstream migration, and pollution [3].
Salinity is one of the main drivers of differences in the biochemical composition of eels,
with the greatest variation occurring between SW and FW eels [4]. This variation can be
indirectly observed in the otolith. Otoliths are bilaterally symmetrical structures found in
the inner ear, specifically in the vestibular labyrinth of all vertebrates. They allow fish
to perceive gravity, and contribute to their balance and provide directional indicators
during movement. Moreover, in aquatic vertebrates they have a secondary function in
sound detection. The morphological and elemental composition of otoliths, such as the
otolith shape index values and microchemistry composition are a promising tool for fish
stock identification [5]. An example is shown in Figure 1.2.
Figure 1.2: Scanning electron microscope photographs of the otolith micro­structure of glass eels of A.
anguilla (left panels) and A. rostrata (right panels). From [6]
While otolith analysis provides reliable information about the salinity habitat of a fish, it
requires the sacrifice of the animal. As eel is an endangered species, non­lethal methods
should be prioritized. Blood may offer such alternative. Blood transports molecules
throughout the body and can therefore reflect different characteristics such as health,
nutrition, reproductive development, stress, and metabolism. Transcriptomic analyses
allow researchers to analyze thousands of genes at a time to identify expression changes

in response to salinity exposure [7]. Therefore, the use of blood emerges as an ideal tissue
to assess changes in physiology in vertebrates caused by external environmental factors,
particularly the non­mammalian vertebrates as their blood contains both nucleated red
and white blood cells. Blood then, can provide abundant RNA from a small sample
and has the potential to quantitatively assay the response of a larger set of genes and
pathways than those limited to white blood cells [7].
Aside from the method not being lethal, it allows repeated sampling from the same in­
dividual. Therefore, longitudinal studies can be performed, using the same individuals
through the different stages of their life cycle, offering an important and new insight into
eel habitat physiology.
1.2
Project objective
The work performed in this thesis aims to further understand eel habitat and salinity his­
tory. For this purpose, data modeling tools were used to investigate whether whole blood
transcriptomic profiles can infer salinity habitat. Machine learning (ML) approaches
were implemented to test the ability of salinity classification. This thesis aims to:
RO 1 Implement a cost efficient pipeline for read mapping of RNA­seq data for non­
model animals.
RO 2 Identify potential biomarkers that can be used for eel salinity life history classifi­
cation.
RO 3 Understand the underlying biological processes that occurs during environmental
salinity changes.

Chapter 2. Background
2
Background
2.1
Monitoring of eel habitat
Anguilla anguilla is able to colonize a wide range of aquatic habitats. Glass eels recruit to
various marine–seawater (SW), brackish (BW), and/or freshwater (FW) habitats, where
they spend several years to decades feeding and growing as “yellow” eels. Although the
details are not yet fully understood, the current consensus is that yellow eels go through
their final transformation, i.e., “silvering”, once they accumulate sufficient energy stores.
Silver eels become sexually mature while migrating back to the Sargasso Sea where they
spawn [4]. Figure 2.1 shows the relationship between muscular lipid content and weight
in the eel throughout migration; the metamorphosis from the yellow to the silver phase.
Figure 2.1: Relationship between muscular lipid content and weight (age, length) in the eel.The growth
and migration areas are indicated by the light­ and dark­shaded fields, respectively. From [8].
During their continental residency, eels adopt different life history strategies. Some
individuals spend the entire period in either SW or FW habitats, while others exhibit
a habitat­shifting strategy, moving back and forth between SW and FW habitats. The
reasons for this plasticity in life history strategy remain unknown. The factors suggested
include competition within and among species, predation, food availability, habitat use,
the general abiotic conditions in each environment (light, current, temperature, substrate
and chemical composition), eel age, and body size (including length) [8].
Studies related to salinity and habitat selection of eels have taken a variety of forms
to date. These studies have been mainly performed thanks to biological traits such as
eel growth and weight, and biomarkers like fatty acid consumption (FA), stable isotope
ratios (SI), and otolith microchemistry.
5
==PAGE:19==
Fatty acids and stable isotope ratios
Fatty acid (FA) analyses are useful because most aquatic consumers cannot synthesize
some FA in sufficient amounts to meet their physiological needs and they must be ac­
quired through diet. Therefore, it is possible to study consumer feeding habits and infer
habitat use by looking at the FA composition of their tissues. In terms of stable isotopes
(SI), nitrogen and carbon isotope ratios are the most common biomarkers. The step­wise
increase or decrease of N between the consumer and its source can be used to study the
trophic position [7]. For instance, FW have usually lower N values than SW due to the
proportionally greater influence of terrestrial inputs on these systems [7].
Significant patterns in eel biochemical composition have been found in association with
increasing size and stages, regardless of the salinity zone. Later stage eels had higher
total lipid content (TL) and mono­saturated FA (MUFA) contents as well as higher SI ra­
tios than early stage eels. Moreover, the MUFA content observed might be related to the
accumulation of energy stores (in the form of triacylglycerols) in eel muscle tissue prior
to migration [9]. As the patterns are independent of the salinity zone, it reinforces the
idea that factors other than diet should be considered when characterizing eels´ ecology
and habitat use [4].
Figure 2.2: Trophic positions (TP) of European eels (A. anguilla) across different salinity zones (seawater
= SW, brackish water = BW, and freshwater = FW). TP were calculated using the formula by Cabana and
Rasmussen (1996) [10], while isotopic information of potential food sources was provided by Østbye et
al,. (2018) [11]. From [4].

ecology and habitat use of the populations of European eels. The biochemical compo­
sition of eel muscle tissue varies significantly across salinity zones (i.e., SW, BW, and

Otolith microchemistry
Otolith microchemistry is routinely used to study the migration and movement patterns
of teleost fishes [12]. The method assumes that certain trace elements, such as stron­
tium and barium, are incorporated into otoliths in proportion to their environmental
availability [13]. Therefore, the differences in water chemistry remain in the individ­
ual’s otoliths. The time recording property of these organs, can be used to identify a
retrospective chemical habitat use profile.
Pioneer studies were performed by Daverat et al,. 2006 [14]. The aim of this compar­
ative study was to explore the diversity of the different life histories of three species of
temperate eels (A. anguilla, A. japonica and A.rostrata) using variations in otolith Sr:Ca
ratios. Additionally, they investigated the influence of geographic location and species
on the distributions of different eel life histories.
For all studies, sagittal otoliths were extracted, cleaned, and embedded in resin. Otoliths
were sectioned either on the sagittal plane or the frontal plane until the core was exposed
and their surface was smoothed with diamond paste. The Sr:Ca analysis was performed
with a wave­length dispersive X­ray electron microprobe which performed maps of Sr
and Ca intensity on the whole otolith surface. Furthermore, it measured a transect along
an axis from the core of the otolith to the edge (so­called ‘life history transect’). Fre­
quency distributions of Sr:Ca were compared for fish collected in FW, BW, and SW to
verify that the gradient of Sr:Ca values was positively correlated with salinity gradient.
Results confirmed that Sr:Ca measurement maps and line transects of Sr:Ca measure­
ments can be interpreted as habitat use.
More recently, this practice was performed by Milošević et al,. 2021 [5] who studied
the morphology and microchemistry fingerprints of European eels. They managed to
distinguish between European eel stocks from different habitats thanks to the form factor
and the circularity of the sagittal otolith. The process of the study of the otoliths was
as followed; otolith pairs from each fish were removed, cleaned and stored. The right
otolith was used for morphometrical analysis and age determination, while the left otolith
was used for microchemistry analysis.
7
==PAGE:21==
On the one hand, the morphometrical analysis consisted in the measurement of four
morphometric characteristics: length, width, area, and perimeter. These measurements
allowed for the calculation of different shape indices, being one of them the form factor.
On the other hand, for the microchemistry analysis, each sample was treated with a
mixture of concentrated nitric acid and concentrated hydrochloric acid. After complete
dissolution of the sample, the element content in the sample (metals and non­metals) was
determined using an induced plasma emission spectrometer. A total of 10 elements were
measured (Cr, Cu, Fe, Mn, Zn, Ba, Sr, K, Mg, Na) as well as the ratios element:Ca. The
trace of the elements’ concentrations and the ratios were used to define stock differences
[5].

Genomic resources of eel
In the past, the generation of reference genome assemblies was prohibitively expensive
and labour­intensive, so they were only produced for human and the most important
model organisms. However, for a complete understanding of all fundamental questions
in biology, high­quality reference genome assemblies and annotation of all species are
required. The arise and improvement of Next generation sequencing (NGS) technologies
have helped reducing time and cost of genome assembly and annotation.
NGS is a massively parallel sequencing technology that enables rapid sequencing of the
base pairs in DNA or RNA samples that supports a broad range of applications, includ­
ing gene expression profiling, assessment of epigenetic changes, detection of genomic
variations, and several other structural changes at DNA and RNA levels [15]. There are
three generations of NGS; the first one is the Sanger sequencing method developed in
1977.
These Sanger techniques adapted to more developed ones. Second­generation sequenc­
ing, also known as amplified molecule sequencing, relies on massive parallel sequenc­
ing methods to decode the information contained in different molecules such as DNA
or RNA. It uses DNA sequencing technologies capable of processing multiple DNA
sequences in parallel. The sequencing requires three steps; library preparation, amplifi­
cation and sequencing. The newest generation, third­generation sequencing (also known
as single molecule sequencing), is able to produce substantially longer reads and does
not require the amplification step. It does not break down or amplify the DNA; it is able
to directly sequence a single DNA molecule.
The first assembly of the European eel genome was performed by Henkel et al,. 2012
[16] using 2nd generation sequencing. Here, genomic DNA was isolated from the blood
of a female yellow European eel and sequenced with Illumina platform. The initial con­
tigs were built with the de Novo assembly (Figure 2.3) by identifying the Hox clusters,
a group of related genes that encode transcription factors and are used for assembly of
new complete genomes [17]. The contigs were compared with Hox­like sequences using
8

Blast, leading to ten genomic scaffolds. Then, they were oriented into larger supercon­
tigs (scaffolds) using SSPACE for eventually, obtaining one final scaffold set. Once the
full­length base sequences were obtained, the annotation step tool place. AUGUSTUS
was used to predict genes, which were provisionally annotated using Blast2GO.

The continuous technological advances, the evolution of platforms, the improving of
computational methods and the ever decreasing cost of sequencing allow to develop
projects that aim to provide high quality genomic resources for all organisms. One of
the most ambitious is the Vertebrate Genomes Project (VGP) [19], launched in 2017.
It pursues the goal of producing a reference genome assembly for each of the extant
vertebrate species on Earth [20]. VGP introduces a new era of genome sequencing using
a wide array of tools and working with several industry partners.
It aims to generate near error­free, 3rd generation reference genome assemblies of more
than 70.000 extant vertebrate species. The VGP genomes will be publicly available
and integrated with international public genome browsers and analysis databases. The
Species selection is based on; the ones with existing draft genomes in need of improve­
ment, those with specialized traits that inform about human biology, those in immediate
danger of becoming extinct, and those with prominent use in biomedical research. The
VGP aims to map the DNA of these at­risk species to not only preserve blueprints of
their genomes but also to help identify genetic variants that might protect these species
from a total extinction [20].

2.3
Blood transcriptomics
Transcriptomics is the study of the transcriptome, the complete set of RNA transcripts
that are produced by the genome under specific circumstances using high­throughput
methods. Comparison of transcriptomes allows, among other applications, the identifi­
cation of genes that are differentially expressed in distinct tissues and cell populations.
In order to measure the abundance, RNA­seq is used; a sequencing technique that uses
NGS to reveal the presence and quantity of RNA in a biological sample at a given mo­
ment. Once the RNA is sequenced, it must be mapped to the reference genome (aligners)
or the reference transcriptome (pseudo­aligners).
The alignment procedure tries to map the reads obtained from the sequence using a
reference genome. For this, aligners need to consider that each gene has several exons
and the read cannot cover the exon junction between two exons. Therefore, the standard
alignment is only able to cover reads that are part of an exon (Figure 2.5a). When a
particular read covers two exon junctions, it is indispensable to work with splice­aware
aligners which consider these types of reads, for instance TopHat2. The Splice Aware
Aligners first map the reads to the whole genome with Bowtie [23] and then generate
possible splices between neighboring exons (Figure 2.5b). Simultaneously, they collect
the initially unmappable reads and build a junction database for the possible junction.
Finally, the reads are mapped to possible splices.

(b) Simplified overview of the splice­aware alignment process.
Figure 2.5: Simplified overview of genome alignment vs. splice­aware genome alignment. From [24].

Another approach that is gaining more importance is the pseudo­alignment. The most
known approach, Kallisto, is a rapid k­mer based algorithm that uses pseudoalignments
to quickly quantify the abundance of transcripts from RNA­seq data with high accuracy.
Instead of performing a full alignment, the raw sequence reads are directly compared to
transcript sequences and then used to quantify transcript abundance [25].
In fish, few transcriptomic studies have been conducted. Investigated by Jeffries et al,.
2011 [26], it was assessed that fishes respond to different stressors through changes in
gene expression as part of an integrated response. The quantification of these expres­
sion patterns could be approached with transcriptomics and therefore it was possible to
assess the physiological status of wild fishes. They argue that non­lethal sampling for
transcriptomics should become the norm for assessing the physiological status of wild
fishes, especially when there are conservation implications. In the review, they dis­
cuss the different tissues that can be successfully incorporated into non­lethal sampling
strategies (Figure 2.6), which is particularly useful in the context of the emerging field
of conservation transcriptomics.

for transcriptional profiling and the types of responses that can be characterized by each tissue. From [26].
Blood is a promising tissue since it is cheaper and easier to handle. It can be sampled
faster and repeatedly from the same individual. However, blood is not the same and does
not contain the same amount of information among all species. The biggest distinction
can be made between nucleated, cells that contain a cell nucleus, and anucleated blood
cells, cells that do not. The red blood cells in mammals lack nuclei and other internal
structures found in most animal cells. Non­mammalian vertebrates, on the other hand,
have transcriptionally active nucleated red blood cells [7], making their blood potentially
more informative and certainly useful. Its study, allows the identification of expression
changes in response to environmental exposures, helping to better understand genotypic
variation between individuals and populations. Moreover, it can reveal genes and path­

responses to environmental exposures [27].
While these investigations are performed more and more often in human and terrestrial
animals since the beginning of NGS back in the mid­2000s, their potential is still under­
explored in vertebrate non­mammals physiology. If blood transcriptomes can provide
quantitative data to address relevant questions, it could reduce the need to euthanize
animals to assay tissues. To do this, the first step is to validate blood as a potential source

vs 372 differentially expressed genes that revealed that the urban and rural dwelling great
tits exhibited significantly different expression profiles thus proving that blood offers the
same amount of information as liver tissue, and that blood transcriptomics is an efficient
method for the study of non­mammal species.

[26]. Non­lethal blood sampling has been widely used to quantify several physiological,

tool for exposure to endocrine­disrupting compounds in fathead minnows [7]. Further­

a new way to face questions that still have no answer in the animal world. Therefore,

Machine Learning
Machine learning (ML) is a type of artificial intelligence (AI) that allows software ap­
plications to become more accurate at predicting outcomes without being explicitly pro­
grammed to do so. ML algorithms use historical data as input to predict new output
values. There are different types of ML, being the most common, supervised and unsu­
pervised learning. For supervised learning, the algorithms must be supplied with labeled
13
==PAGE:27==
training data and the variables must be defined. Both the input and the output of the algo­
rithm are specified. Nevertheless, unsupervised learning involves algorithms that train
on unlabeled data. It is the algorithm itself the one that scans through sets looking for
meaningful connection. The data that algorithms train on as well as the predictions or
recommendations they output are predetermined [30].
ML algorithms can be also classified depending on the forms of data analysis. Classifica­
tion is the process of identifying which category a new observation belongs to, based on
a training dataset containing observations whose category membership is known. Pre­
diction is the process of identifying the missing or unavailable numerical data for a new
observation [31]. Since very different ML models exist, choosing the right model be­
comes very important. This decision should be made based on the problem to solve and
the data available. For this thesis, three different classification algorithms were used.
These are explained below.
Random Forest
Random Forest (RF) is a tree­based model that can be used for both prediction and
classification, this type of model involves recursively partitioning the given dataset into
two groups, based on a certain criterion until a predetermined stopping condition is met.
A committee of trees each cast a vote for the predicted class i.e. the stopping points
which are called leaf nodes or leaves [32]. An schematic of RF is shown in Figure 2.7.
Figure 2.7: Schematic of Random Forest. From [33].
Although this algorithm is usually used for supervised learning, it can also be employed
for unsupervised learning. This ML technique is able to work on unlabeled and un­
categorized data by allowing the model to work on its own to discover patterns and
information from the data. These types of models are always more challenging since
the interpretation of the cluster always comes back to strong subject matter knowledge
and knowing the data. The profiling of the clusters is the most challenging aspect of the
work and therefore these techniques are computationally more complex and less accurate

and trustworthy [34]. However, it resembles more closely the real­world, where the
input data does not always have the corresponding output and unsupervised learning is
necessary to solve such cases.
K­Nearest Neighbors
K­nearest neighbors (KNN) is a supervised learning algorithm that tries to find a rela­
tionship between k data points based on the distance between each other (Figure 2.8). It
consists of three steps; in the first place the k parameter (the number of data points to
consider near observation) has to be chosen. If k = 1 there can be a potential under­fit,
whereas very large values of k are very computationally expensive and may also over­
fit the data since all the relationships within the data are being calculated. Therefore,
a good starting point for k is the square root of the number of observations, this value
should be odd to ensure that there are no ties in the voting.
Figure 2.8: Schematic of K­nearest Neighbors. From [33].
Secondly, the distance matrix that algorithm is going to use needs to be chosen. The
algorithm computes the distance values based on the distance algorithm chosen. This
algorithm depends on the type of data that the model has to work with, however, the
standard formula is the Euclidean distance. The third step consists of the classification,
the data point is assigned to whichever group has the most neighbors within the vicinity.
Naïve Bayes
Naïve Bayes (NB) is a simple machine learning algorithm that utilizes Bayes’ rule to­
gether with a strong assumption that the attributes are conditionally independent given
the class. It first converts the dataset into a frequency table, then it creates a likelihood
table by finding the probabilities of the different variables, and last it uses the Naive
Bayesian equation to calculate the posterior probability for each class. The class with
the highest posterior probability is the outcome prediction [35]. (Figure 2.9).

other models. However, this method requires splitting the data into training and testing
set and if a categorical variable is not observed in the training set, the model will assign
a zero probability and will be unable to make a prediction. This is often known as “Zero
Frequency”.

Transcriptomic­based Machine Learning
Advances in ML algorithms and transcriptomics have propelled Transcriptomic­based
ML into a promising approach. In the past years, it has been widely used in the investi­
gation of diseases that have increased their incidence and that have a significant health
burden associated. Besides these approaches being highly scalable with low marginal
cost [36], they can predict/classify stages of the disease without requiring expert input.
The majority of the investigations have been performed for humans.
Warnat­Herresthal et al,. 2020) [36] focused their study on acute myeloid leukemia
(AML). The primary diagnosis of this diseases relies on classical approaches and al­
though it is undoubtedly effective, it requires large investments in human expertise and
physical infrastructure. Since leukemias are characterized by strong transcriptomic sig­
nals, a new window opens to approach this issue. Analyzing more than 12,000 blood
samples from more than 100 individual studies, the study provided evidence that com­
bining large transcriptomic data with ML allows for the development of robust disease
classifiers. These results showed that with existing technologies it is possible to achieve
good performance in a near­automated fashion that can be run at a very low marginal
cost. (The RNA assays can already be done for less than 100$) [36].
Pantaleo et al,. 2022 [37] focused their study on Parkinson’s disease (PD), which is often
diagnosed a long time after the onset, when irreversible damage has already occurred.
Blood transcriptomics represents a potentially disruptive technology for the early diag­
nosis of PD. The first question addressed by their work concerned the evaluation of the
informative content provided by blood transcriptomic data. The number of significant
DE genes was approximately 1000. The second question addressed was the capability

of a robust ML model to discriminate between PD in early and late stages, which was
accomplished with an accuracy of 73%. Additionally, 500 genes were identified to have
the most discriminatory power for PD prediction.
Even though this approach has been demonstrated in humans, it has hardly been used
in animals and, to my knowledge, never used in fish. The first study that contemplates
blood transcriptomics and ML in fish has been performed by Bertolini et al,. 2022 [38].
This study has tried to give some insight into one of the enigmas of the aquatic world, the

3.3.1
TopHat2 pipeline
The alignment was performed with TopHat2 [44], the code of the pipeline employed
for the alignment is provided in Listing A.1. In contrast to DNA­sequence alignment,
23

RNA­seq mapping algorithms face more challenges as eukaryotic genomes contain in­
tros. Hence, TopHat2 must be able to handle spliced alignment. In order to do so, the
algorithm follows a two­step procedure, it detects potential splice sites for introns and
then uses the candidate splice sites to correctly align the reads [44].
Figure 3.7: (1) Genome alignment. (2) Spliced alignment. (2a) Segment alignment to genome, reads
are split into smaller segments which are then aligned to the genome. (2b) Identification of splice sites,
segment mapping are used to find potential splice sites usually when the distance between the mapped
positions of the left and the right segments are longer than the length of the middle part of a read. (2c)

segments are aligned to them. (2d) Segments alignments stitched together form whole read alignments,
mapped segments against either genome or flanking sequences are gathered to produce whole read align­
ments. (2e) Re­alignment of reads minimally overlapping introns, genome mapped reads with alignments
extending a few bases into introns are re­aligned to exons instead. From [45].
TopHat2 takes as inputs the raw reads and the assembly reference genome, NCBI fAn­
gAng1.pri (NCBI ref: GCF_013347855.1) [46]. First, it indexes the reference genome
to speed up the alignment process with Bowtie2 [23], an internal aligner. The reads are
then mapped to the genome, the ones that have not been aligned go to the next step where
the spliced alignment takes place. Then, the reads are segmented and tried to be mapped

tries to map them. (Figure 3.7).

The pseudo­alignment of the reads was conducted with Kallisto [49], the code of the
pipeline is provided in Listing A.2.
An index must first be built. Kallisto indexes are constructed with the reference tran­

size of 31, these k­mers are going to be used for the quantification, the second step.
The comparison of the sequencing reads to the transcripts is done using a transcriptome
de Bruijn graph (T­DBG). Then, the k­compatibility of the read contigs is determined
to match the transcript k­mers and finally, the abundance of the contigs in each read
is estimated. An overview of the Kallisto workflow can be seen in Figure 3.8. Two

3.4.1
Differential expression analysis
The differential expression (DE) analysis was performed in Rstudio [51] with the pack­
age Deseq2 [52]. Once the expression counts tables were obtained, the following step
of the analysis was to detect the differentially expressed isoforms/genes. The analysis in
question was the quantification and statistical inference of systematic changes between
conditions within the samples. In this particular case, the reference condition was always
week 0. Deseq2 uses negative binomial generalized linear models to test for differential
expression [52].
In order to perform and design the analysis, the un­normalized count matrix was re­
quired; Deseq2 performs the correction of the individual counts using the geometric
mean and the negative binomial distribution. A table containing the samples’ informa­
tion like sample id, week of sampling, group and stage is also required and it is usually
called metadata. The samples are then modeled according to the variable(s) of choice,
in this case, the changes over weeks. Results were generated, and among them, par­
ticular attention was paid to the P.value and the Log2FoldChange (LFC). The P.value
parameter is going to determine whether the isoform/gene is statistically differentially
expressed. The threshold used is 0.05 i.e. an isoform/gene is determined to be DE if its
P.value is lower than 0.05.
Once the list with DE genes is obtained, the Log2FoldChange parameter was used to
determine the regulation of the isoforms/genes’ expression. In order to compute the
LFC parameter, a reference group is needed. Kallisto counts from G2W0 were chosen
as reference group. When this parameter is positive, the gene is considered to be up­
regulated when compared to the reference week, and when it is negative, the gene is
considered to be down­regulated when compared to the reference week.
Data visualization
Principal component analysis (PCA) [53] is a technique used to emphasize variation and
bring out strong patterns within a dataset. This method allows visualizing the data in a
subspace where the maximum variability between samples is presented. The first prin­
cipal component is the dimension where the variability is the greatest among samples.
Hence, the standard procedure to visualize this information is to plot the first two PCs,
the two that show the highest variability. The axes of the plot do not have any physical
meaning, for this particular case, they were interpreted as variability between weeks.
The data used for the PCAs was the output of the DE analysis performed with DESeq2
[52] for the Kallisto isoforms and Kallisto genes. The PCAs were performed in Rstudio
[51] with the function plotPCA from the same package, DESeq2.
Heatmaps represent another type of graphical representation. It is a visualization tech­
nique that can be applied to a large variety of data. The data used for the heatmaps were
28

the distance matrix of the Kallisto isoforms and Kallisto genes generated by DESeq2. A
distance matrix was calculated between each pair of observations using the Euclidean
method. The package Heatmap.2 [54] in Rstudio was used to plot the heatmaps. When
plotting the data, each of the columns and rows represent a sample. The intensity of
the intersection’s color translates into the similarity in RNA expression between the two
samples. When plotting the data, each of the columns and rows represent a sample.

Random Forest
Two parameters need to be optimized in order to build the model; the number of trees
(ntree) and the number of variables at each internal node (mtry). For the correct number
of trees an initial random forest with ntree = 1500 was performed and the OOB errors
for all the classes were plotted. Figure B.4 shows an example of the OOB errors plot.
From it, it was possible to identify from which number of trees on the errors stabilized.
That number was used as ntree for the final RF model.
To choose the optimal number of variables at each internal node (mtry), cross­validation
using a range of mtry parameters was performed. The range varies from the half of
the square root of the total number of predictors to twice the square root of the total
number of predictors. These variables depend on the number of isoforms/genes used
for the model. The mtry parameter that gave a better performance, (the model with the
lowest error) was set as the accurate mtry. The models were then run with these two new
parameters set.
As often in predictive modeling, one of the main objectives is to reduce the number
of variables needed to improve efficiency. In order to know the importance of each
variable in the node, the Gini index was retrieved. This is a measure of how each variable
contributes to the homogeneity of the nodes and leaves in the resulting RF [32]. The
higher the value, the higher the importance of the variable in the model. The variables
were then ordered according to this parameter and subsets of the dataset were obtained
and used to re­run the models until the lowest error was reached. The error and standard
deviation were calculated and the misclassified samples were examined.

K­nearest neighbors
When running KNN, the dataset is split randomly into two sets, training set and testing
set, 80% and 20% of the total dataset respectively. In this thesis, 47 samples for the
training set and 12 for the testing set. Parameter k needed to be optimised. Therefore,
a sequence of potential k parameters was chosen starting with k = round root square
of the training dataset number of variables (depends on the number of isoforms/genes
used as variables). Then, cross­validation was performed for all the potential k’s. A plot
between error rate and K denoting values in a defined range was derived and the errors
of the models were calculated. The k of the model with the highest accuracy, i.e lowest
error was chosen for the final model.
With the parameters set, the model was run. The number of times it was run depended
on the number of variables (isoforms/genes) used since this number sets the computation
expenses required. The higher number of variables employed, the higher the expense.
Finally, the output of the models were generated, the overall accuracy was averaged and
the respective error were calculated. The standard deviation of the error was also calcu­
lated and the misclassified samples were examined to evaluate the consistency between
the models.

3.5
Gene ontology enrichment analysis
Gene enrichment is a knowledge­based approach for interpreting genome­wide expres­
sion profiles. The method focuses on gene sets, groups of genes that share a common
biological function, chromosomal location or regulation [55]. The gene sets are defined
based on prior biological knowledge, for instance, the Gene Ontology (GO) database.
The goal of the method is to compare trends from members of a gene subset and the
top (or bottom) part of the list of the ranked genes. Then the enrichment score is calcu­
lated, reflecting the degree to which a subset is over­represented at the top (or bottom)
part of the ranked genes list. Finally, the statistical significance from the enrichment
score is estimated (nominal P.value), and a threshold can be set to differentiate between
significantly and non­significantly over­represented genes.
Four gene datasets were obtained containing the differentially expressed genes (P.value
< 0.05) from:
• Group 1 week 0 vs. Group 1 week 1.
• Group 1 week 0 vs. Group 1 week 3.
• Group 2 week 0 vs. Group 1 week 2.
• Group 2 week 0 vs. Group 1 week 4.
Week 0 was used as a reference in all of the gene datasets as it was expected to give
more differential expression when compared with the rest of the weeks since more bi­
ological change is expected to happen over time. Each dataset was later split into two;
up­regulated and down­regulated genes. An initial enrichment analysis was performed
using all datasets (8 in total) but the datasets that were further looked into were the ones
that gave better results in the machine earning models. As one of the main focuses of this
project is to understand the underlying biological processes, different enrichment anal­
ysis web­based tools were used to obtain a higher amount of information and to contrast
the results.
• Panther [56]: the different parameters were set; the statistical over­representation
test was selected as analysis, GO biological process complete as annotation set,
and Danio rerio as reference gene set since it is the most similar to A. anguilla with
the best annotation. Once the analysis was performed, the biological processes
31
==PAGE:45==
(BP) and their respective P.value were obtained as part of the output. The P.value
is calculated using the binomial statistic. It assumes that under the null hypothesis,
genes in the uploaded list are sampled from the same general population as genes
from the reference set, i.e. the probability of observing a gene from a particular
category C in the uploaded list is the same as in the reference list [56]. Only BP
with a significance < 0.05 were determined as significant.
• FishEnrichr [57]: an integrative web­based software that includes a broader num­
ber of gene­set libraries as it is a derivation from Enrichr. It supposes an alternative
approach to rank enriched terms and it is specific for Danio rerio. The concept
follows the same line, a list of genes is introduced on the web and the enrichment
score is calculated. The P.value of each of the processes is computed from the
Fisher Exact test, a test that assumes a binomial distribution and independence of
probability of any gene belonging to any set. The output includes ontologies and
pathways, however, the main interest lies in the GO Biological Process that have
a P.value < 0.05.
• David [58]: the database for annotation, visualization, and integrated discovery.
A combination of functional annotation tools that help understand the biological
meaning behind large lists of genes by identifying enriched biological themes. In
this project, the tool used was going to be the functional annotation. The list of
gene ID’s was then introduced and initially the species chosen was A. anguilla.
Since the output only included three BP, the species was then set as Danio rerio,
finding more than 500 BP. The P.value is calculated using the Fisher Exact test
and only the BP with a significance< 0.05 were kept for the study.

4.1
Kallisto vs. Tophat2
The sixty samples were aligned with Tophat2 and pseudo­aligned with Kallisto. It is
necessary to emphasize the difference in memory and computation speed. One entire
Tophat2 run took approximately 22 hours and the memory used was about 4GB, de­
termined mostly by the size of the reference genome. This memory is not commonly
available on a standard desktop computer, therefore the need of utilising an external
cluster arises. For the alignment of the 60 samples, not including the previous and sub­
sequent steps, a total of approximately 1.320 hours and 240 GB were required. On the
contrary, Kallisto required about 25 minutes per run, adding up to 25 hours considering
all the samples used in the project. In terms of memory, this process can be performed on
a standard desktop computer. This can be considered a major advantage when it comes
to routine procedures.
The output of Kallisto consisted of an isoforms count table and a genes count table
and the output of TopHat2 consisted of a genes count table. The number of isoforms
obtained with Kallisto was 64.130 which refer to 28.788 genes, whereas the number of
genes obtained when aligning with TopHat2 was 25.890. Regarding the genes; 24.441
appeared in both, 4.347 appeared only in Kallisto, and 1.449 only in TopHat2.

Chapter 5. Conclusion and future perspective
5
Conclusion and future perspective
This research aimed to investigate the ability of blood transcriptomics and machine
learning to assess the physiological condition of adult European eels under different
salinity conditions. Based on the results obtained, it is possible to ensure the following
statements.
It has been shown that blood transcriptional profiles are influenced by salinity habitat
and allow to detect long term salinity behaviors. Therefore, blood has been proved as an
informative tissue which makes non­lethal sampling methods promising for the replace­
ment and reduction of other lethal analyses in fish research, especially for endangered
species. Additionally, this type of sampling allows longitudinal studies to be carried
out, which opens a new line of research and can lead to great advances in non­mammals
investigation fields.
In terms of implementing efficient tools for the processing of the raw reads, Kallisto
pseudo­alignment has proved to be a fast, powerful, and accurate tool for reads map­
ping. It showed better results than TopHat2 both in time and memory consumption.
In addition, it also yielded a higher percentage of mapped reads; 80.94% vs. 75.89%
respectively on average.
Machine learning algorithms are able to classify the habitat salinity of European Eel
using blood­based transcriptomics with significantly high accuracy (96%) with only 10
isoforms. For this particular case, the most suitable algorithm for the classification has
been random forest.
Last, isoforms have proved to be more accurate than genes for the ML classification.
Therefore, these isoforms can be defined as potential biomarkers for salinity classifi­
cation and it opens the possibility to study isoforms instead of genes in blood­based
transcriptomics. Moreover, these biomarkers were obtained from a longitudinal study
with samples taken from individuals in a controlled environment. It has been proved that
these results can be extrapolated to field samples from a different study resulting in even
better outcomes. This demonstrates that different eel populations share transcriptomic­
based biomarkers that are able to classify habitat salinity.
5.1
Future perspective
The study has been performed with eels collected in a specific part of Norway, raised
in a controlled environment and without being fed. Therefore, it would be of interest to
replicate this study under regular conditions where the individuals can eat, to expand the
geographical range of sampling and to increase the amount of specimens.
53
==PAGE:67==
Even though the results have showed a clear distinction in gene expression during the
four week period, it remains unknown if this has been enough time for the animals to
completely adapt to full seawater salinity. Future research should consider increasing
this period of time.
The ML results have shown consistency and reliability, whereby an increase in the num­
ber of samples and therefore the amount of data would do nothing but improve the current
results. These data would include, as mention, an extension of locations, populations and
samples per individual. These studies could be conducted separated in order to compare
results, biological processes and potential biomarkers between populations or together
in other to look for a consensus in the species.
New variables could be introduced into the ML studies. Biological traits from the otolith
study, sex, age, length, weight, FA content, silvering stages... Looking for patterns that
help understand the underlying biology that drives the eels’ migration and metamorpho­
sis.
Finally, since it has become a possibility to perform longitudinal studies and it is still
unknown what drives the individuals to shift habitats, a study focusing on inter­habitat
shifters could give the necessary insight. The study of these particular eels could give
deeper information about the plasticity and memory of the gene expression.
