Abstract
Concrete buildings from the 60s and 70s with load bearing inner walls and high
energy consumption are especially suited for façade renovation, which would
provide the buildings with a better energy performance and a more modern
appearance. Therefore, the aim of this project is to develop a multifunctional
façade element that exploits the solar heat gain to reduce the energy demand of
the concrete buildings. The multifunctional façade element should be applicable
for energy simulations in the program IDA ICE, why a code for the program of
the façade element is developed as a result of this project.
The concept has been assessed by modelling a conventional façade from the
1960s, a typical re-insulation of a conventional façade, a ventilated façade and
ﬁnally a multifunctional façade. The multifunctional façade component is based
on a ventilated façade component, which has been calibrated and validated
against a COMSOL model. The multifunctional façade component has been
created by merging the NMF code for the ventilated façade component with the
existing double façade component from IDA ICE. Lastly, the multifunctional
façade model has been validated against the double façade model as well as
tested in diﬀerent weather conditions and parametric setups.
For the reference façade, the total heating demand from September to May
is 219.5 kWh/m2 and the yearly cooling demand is 19.8 kWh/m2.
The re-
insulation of the reference façade lowers the heating demand from September
to May to 210.3 kWh/m2 but increases the cooling demand to 20.2 kWh/m2.
The ventilated façade lowers the heating demand from September to May
to 186.2 kWh/m2 and increases the cooling demand to 41.1 kWh/m2.
The
multifunctional façade lowers the heating demand from September to May to
170.5 kWh/m2, while the yearly cooling demand increases to 64.1 kWh/m2. An
improved conﬁguration of the multifunctional façade lowers the heating demand
from September to May to 130.1 kWh/m2, while the yearly cooling demand
decreases to 16.3 kWh/m2. The results show that the multifunctional façade
would be a possible solution for energy renovation of a conventional façade in a
concrete building.

Part I
Preface
In the beginning of the 1950s, it became possible to produce prefabricated con-
crete elements, which facilitated the construction of new buildings. When the
need for housing increased signiﬁcantly in the 1960s and 1970s, the accelera-
tion of the construction process resulted in the erection of numerous concrete
apartment buildings in short time.
Typical examples of these buildings are
shown in Figure 1. Many of these buildings appear to be unattractive due to
aesthetics and high energy consumption, which makes the building operation
both expensive and non-environment-friendly. These kinds of concrete build-
ings typically have load bearing inner walls and light outer walls, why they are
especially suited for façade replacement. By replacing the existing façade with
modern multifunctional façade elements, instead of demolishing and rebuilding
the entire building, it is possible to obtain a better energy performance and a
desirable façade without compromising with the environmental impact.
Figure 1: Example of existing building facades [2]

Chapter 1
Introduction
The aim of the project is to develop a multifunctional façade element that
exploits solar heat gain to reduce the energy demand of existing buildings. The
multifunctional façade system in this project is a possible enhancement of a
ventilated façade system.
Several studies of ventilated façade systems show
that it is possible to improve the energy performance of a façade system by
forcing air through a cavity to the inside of a room [10][8][5]. The reduction
of energy consumption is obtained as the heat loss through the external wall is
reused for pre-heating of the inlet air in the cavity during the heating season.
If the outer wall component is a transparent material, it is possible to exploit
the solar heat gain to further pre-heat the inlet air [13][16][17], thereby making
it a multifunctional façade system (see Figure 1.1).
Figure 1.1: Concept of the multifunctional façade
==PAGE:20==
6
Introduction
The main challenge of working with multifunctional façades with a transparent
cladding is to avoid overheating during cooling season since transparent mate-
rials can cause high temperatures. Therefore, the purpose of this project is to
answer the following questions;
• To what extent is it possible to exploit the solar heat gain by a multifunc-
tional façade?
• How does the multifunctional façade perform in both the heating and cool-
ing season regarding energy demand and overheating?
The concept will be assessed by modelling a conventional façade from the 1960s,
a typical re-insulation of a conventional façade, a ventilated façade and ﬁnally
a multifunctional façade. The performance of the conventional building façade
as well as the conventional re-insulation of the same are presented in Part II
in order to evaluate the performance of the ventilated and the multifunctional
façade systems, which are presented in Part III. Part IV contains the discussion
of the diﬀerent façade systems as well as the conclusion.
Method
To assess the building performance of the diﬀerent façade systems, the building
simulation software IDA ICE will be used. IDA ICE is a dynamic simulation
tool for analysis of energy consumption and indoor climate, which is commonly
used for the design of energy eﬃcient buildings.
The multifunctional façade
element should be applicable for energy simulations in IDA ICE, why a code
for the program of the multifunctional façade element will be developed as a
result of this project. The creation of a multifunctional façade component for
IDA ICE enables parametric studies of the multifunctional façade’s impact on
the indoor environment and energy performance. In that way, it is possible to
implement the multifunctional façade element as a decision tool in the design
phase of energy eﬃcient building and renovation.
The two cases with the conventional façades will be assessed by simple building
simulation in Chapter 2 and 3.
To assess the performance of the ventilated
façade, the creation of a new component model for building simulation is re-
quired. The model generation, validation and performance assessment are de-
scribed in Chapter 4. The multifunctional façade is a further development of the
ventilated façade model, and the model generation, validation and performance
assessment are described in Chapter 5. The multifunctional façade is further
conﬁgured to lower the heating and cooling demand in Chapter 6. Finally, the
diﬀerent façade systems are assessed and compared based on the simulation
results regarding energy demand and overheating in Chapter 7.

Part II
Conventional façades
In this part of the project, a reference façade from the 1960s and a typical re-
insulated façade are introduced and assessed to investigate their performance
regarding energy demand and overheating.
The high energy consumption of older buildings with conventional façades are
partly due to leaks and heat loss through a poorly constructed building envelope.
The basic concept of a conventional façade is presented in Figure 1.2. As shown,
the inlet air enters the room through leaks in the façade forced by an exhaust
fan inside the room. This means that the temperature of the inlet air (Ta)
is the same as the external temperature (Te).
The temperature inside the
room is denoted Ti and the surface temperatures are denoted T1 and T2 for
the internal and external surface respectively. The conceptual model for the
reference façade further describes that the heat ﬂux (Q1 and Q2) is positive
when going from inside the room to the outside through the façade.
Such
a conventional façade represents the reference façade in this project and the
potential for energy renovation is investigated by performing a room model
simulation in IDA ICE which will be described in the following chapter.
Figure 1.2: Basic concept model of the reference and re-insulated façade
==PAGE:24==
10
The façades from the 60s to 70s in need of façade renovation could go through
diﬀerent extends of renovation. One of the most common renovation types is
re-insulation of the outer wall and application of a new façade cladding to make
the building perform better and change the expression. Often, re-insulation is
implemented on the external part of the outer wall to avoid thermal bridges and
make the renovation easier. However, since many of the buildings considered
have balconies, it is inconvenient to perform an external re-insulation. There-
fore, the typical re-insulated façade in this project is an internal re-insulation
of the reference façade. Chapter 3 describes the room analysis of re-insulated
façade.
Both the reference and the re-insulated façade model have been created in the
standard level of IDA ICE, but to create a schematic view and log the desired
outputs, the models have been built into an advanced model. The schematic
setup of the two IDA ICE models are presented here, since it is the same for
both models (see Figure 1.3).

Chapter 2
Reference façade
This chapter presents the construction of the reference façade and the room
model, that function as the reference case throughout the project, as well as the
simulation results related to the reference façade.
The buildings considered in need of renovation have light outer walls and a study
from the Danish Building Code from year 1850-2000 [6] states that buildings
with light outer walls are predominated by wooden frame structures. Therefore,
the reference façade is assumed to be built up by a 13 mm gypsum board, 75 mm
insulation with wooden frame, a 30 mm air cavity and a 8 mm ﬁber cement board
on the outside as shown in Figure 2.1. This façade structure is given the code
|Gy13|I75|A30|FC8|. The codes will be used throughout this project to make it
easier to separate and recognise the diﬀerent façade systems.

2.1
Room model
To provide a reference case for the room analyses, a room unit is modelled in IDA
ICE as shown in Figure 2.2. To limit the model variations and the amount of
results, the assessed room unit is oriented with a window facing east and located
in the middle of the building. In this way, the reference room has only one outer
wall with direct contact to the outside. The room model simulation is computed
using the weather ﬁle "Denmark DRY 2013_Building Permit_BSimogDMI_m
skudår" that consists of weather data from Denmark over a year.
Figure 2.2: Location and dimensions of the room unit
The reference model inputs are mainly based on a case study from Balleruppla-
nen provided by the Danish Building Regulations [2] which presents a typical
example of an apartment building from the 1960s. The room unit is assumed
to have a window with a 2 pane glazing with an emissivity of 0.84 (see Table
2.1 for glazing properties). The remaining building components are modelled as
listed in Table 2.2. The wall emissivity and absorptance are assumed to be 0.9
and 0.6 respectively.
It would be more realistic to apply a radiator to a building from the 1960s, but
since the room model is used for comparison of the models presented in the
following chapters, an ideal heater and cooler is added for the simulations. The
setpoint is 20 ◦C for heating and 25 ◦C for cooling. The internal heat gain from
equipment is assumed to be 5 W/m2. The Danish Building Regulations states
that there must be an airﬂow of 20 l/s in the kitchen and 15 l/s per bathroom.
As it is assumed that there is one kitchen and one bathroom in the apartment,
the room unit is applied with a total airﬂow of 35 l/s. The same room unit and
model inputs apply to all the room model analyses performed throughout this
study unless otherwise stated.

2.2
Results
The performance of the reference façade has been analysed and compared on
a sunny and on a cloudy winter day as shown in Figure 2.3. Graph a) and
b) show the heating demand from the local heating unit, the radiation from
the sun and the total heat ﬂux through the façade. Graph b) shows how the
clouds aﬀect the radiation on the façade. The sun rises at 8.30 which is when
the radiation starts to increase and keeps increasing until 12.00 on the cold and
sunny day and until 13.00 on the cloudy day. The radiation goes back to zero
around 16.00, which is when the sun sets. The amount of radiation is quite
diﬀerent depending on the clouds, as there is up to 100 W/m2 on a sunny day
and only 35 W/m2 on a cloudy day. The heating demand is more stable on the
cloudy day compared to the sunny day where it drops in the middle of the day
and then increases after the sun sets.
Graph c) and d) describe the heat ﬂux on each side of the façade, where Q1 is
the heat ﬂux through the internal surface and Q2 is the heat ﬂux through the
external surface. The heat ﬂux is positive going from the room to the outside as
shown in Figure 1.2. In both graph c) and d), Q2 has greater deviations than
Q1 due to the radiation going into the external surface during the day. Q1 is
more stable due to the constant room temperature on 20 ◦C and the relatively
stable outdoor temperature around -5 ◦C to 1 ◦C.
Graph e) and f) visualise the temperatures on the interior and exterior surface
of the façade (T1 and T2) and the internal and external room temperature (Ti
and Te). Ti is kept at a constant of 20 ◦C by the ideal heater and the other
temperatures vary depending on the weather conditions the given day. The ex-
ternal surface temperature T2 has a peak in the middle of the day corresponding
to the radiation as expected when the sun is out. The outdoor temperature Te
increases from -5 ◦C to just below freezing during the day when the weather
condition is cold and sunny. When it is cold and cloudy, Te is more stable and
constantly increasing to just above freezing as would be expected, since clouds
cause less dissipation of heat.

Ventilated façades
Several studies of ventilated façade systems show that it is possible to improve
the energy performance of a façade system by letting air through a cavity to the
inside of a room. The reduction of energy consumption is obtained as the heat
loss through the external wall is reused for pre-heating of the inlet air from the
cavity during the heating season. If the façade system is constructed by materi-
als with high thermal capacities, it is also possible to pre-cool the inlet air during
cooling season. Salah-Eldin Imbabi refers to the ventilated façade concept as
a dynamic insulation technology [10]. The study is performed by simulation
in Solidworks Flow and assesses diﬀerent wall sizes, inlet/outlet conﬁgurations,
climatic conditions as well as a small single-zone building with diﬀerent ambient
environments and active versus passive ventilation. The ﬁndings of the study
conﬁrms that the technology works in both hot and cold climates and that both
active and passive ventilation can provide signiﬁcant reductions in energy con-
sumption. It is however noted, that the resulting indoor environment do not
comply with normal comfort conditions. Another study by Iribar-Solaberrieta
et al. investigates the energy performance of opaque ventilated façades [8]. By
performing an experiment with a sample exposed to outdoor conditions and com-
paring it with dynamic building simulation performed in TRNSYS, it is found
that there is a high potential for reducing heating demands, when refurbishing
buildings with ventilated façades. Astorqui and Porras-Amoras perform a com-
parative study between a conventional ventilated system and a two-chamber
system, which is found to be even more eﬃcient in terms of energy performance
[5]. The study further shows that there is great potential in improving the energy
performance by implementing enhanced façade systems and the incorporation of
computer simulation tools for ventilated façade systems is recommended. The
ﬁndings in all of these studies show that there is potential in using ventilated
façades for energy renovation and emphasise the need for simulation tools to
further assess the comfort conditions in the indoor environment.
Therefore,
this project concerns the generation of a ventilated façade component for model
simulation in IDA ICE, which will be described in the following chapter.

Multifunctional façades
The multifunctional façade in this project is a ventilated façade system with
a heat absorbing material facing the exterior to gain heat from the sun. Pre-
vious studies of multifunctional façades, also known as solar walls, have been
performed by using diﬀerent approaches. A recent state of the art study from
2013 presents the results of several studies, highlighting the potential of solar
walls to reduce the energy demand and fossil fuel consumption [13]. Another
project regarding hand calculations and physical models of an active solar wall
states that the façade system is capable of improving the thermal insulation and
collecting heat that can be reused for either heating, ventilation or cooling. The
space state model in that project has been validated by an experimental model
[16]. A numerical study has been performed to create a comparative study of
four diﬀerent solar walls; Composite solar wall, Trombe wall, Insulated Trombe
wall and Non-ventilated solar wall. These four solar walls have been built and
monitored to validate the numerical model used to calculate the energy eﬃ-
ciency of the solar walls [17]. A full scale study focusing on three diﬀerent types
of solar walls has been carried out in Denmark. It was found that the cost of
solar walls are at the same level as an external re-insulation of a façade, while
the solar walls lower the heating demand with 15 %. Furthermore, it was found
that the calculated values correspond to the measured values [9]. All of the
mentioned studies are based on physical experiments which conﬁrm that there
is potential in using multifunctional façades for energy renovation. This project
takes a new approach to the previous studies by generating a multifunctional
façade component for model simulation in IDA ICE, which will be described in
Chapter 5.

Project background
Currently, IDA ICE does not contain a ventilated façade component, why it is
necessary to create such a component to be able to analyse on the performance
of it. Petros Ampatzidis, an intern at DTU, has developed a preliminary com-
ponent model for a ventilated façade, which will be further developed in this
project. The model describes the heat transfer from convection in the air gap
between two wall components. The concept model for the ventilated façade is
shown in Figure 4.2, where Ti is the internal temperature, Ta is the temperature
of the inlet air and Te is the external temperature. Q1 - Q4 denotes the heat
ﬂux through the wall parts from inside to outside.
Figure 4.2: Basic concept model for the ventilated façade
The ventilated facade component developed by Petros Ampatzidis has been writ-
ten in the Neutral Model Format (NMF), which is the main modelling language
in IDA ICE. A NMF model is based on equations, parameters, variables and
links that connects the model with the existing application, e.g. IDA ICE [14].
Before the NMF code can be used, a NMF Translator is used to translate the
code to Fortran format. The Fortran code then needs to be compiled to a dll-
ﬁle and this dll-ﬁle can then be added to IDA ICE [7]. The IDA ICE model
will be validated against a previous study by Jakob Spiegelhauer, that assesses
the potential of pre-heating and -cooling the inlet air through ventilated façade
elements [15]. The study is computed using the simulation software COMSOL
Multiphysics, which is a well-reputed tool for ﬂuid dynamic assessment. The
study begins by evaluating and verifying the performance of COMSOL by ana-
lytic hand calculations, which qualiﬁes the ﬁnal results as reliable.

transfer inside the cavity.

Discussion
Overall recapitulation of the façade systems
For the reference façade, the total heating demand from September to May was
found to be 219.5 kWh/m2 and the yearly cooling demand was found to be
19.8 kWh/m2 (values recapitulated in Table 7.1). The cooling demand is not
critical, as it should be possible to remove the cooling demand with the use of
shading or by opening the window, which makes the high heating demand the
main reason for the renovation.
The re-insulation of the reference façade lowered the heating demand from
September to May by 9.2 kWh/m2 but increased the cooling demand by
0.4 kWh/m2 compared to the reference façade. The cooling demand can possi-
bly still be removed with the use of shading or by opening the window. However,
the reduction in heating demand is not considered a signiﬁcant improvement.
It neither meets the Building Regulation requirement of reducing the energy
demand by either 70 kWh/m2 or 52.5 kWh/m2 to qualify for Renovation Class
1 or 2 respectively [3]. This means that the re-insulated façade would require
other measures, such as an improved lighting or ventilation system, to reduce
the energy demand suﬃciently.
The ventilated façade lowered the heating demand from September to May by
33.4 kWh/m2 compared to the reference façade. This is a signiﬁcant reduction
compared to the re-insulated façade. However, the cooling demand has increased
by 21.3 kWh/m2 compared to the reference façade, which indicates that there is
a greater risk of high room temperatures and thereby a need for manually cooling
and/or shading. The analysis showed the inlet temperature increases with 5 ◦C
on a cold and sunny day, which conﬁrms that there is pre-heating potential in
using ventilated façades. The validation case showed that the ventilated façade
is also capable of pre-cooling the inlet air when it is constructed with a thick
concrete sandwich element (see Figure 4.16, graph b)). However, the increased
cooling demand from the room analysis shows that the ventilated façade has
no pre-cooling potential, when the room is applied with a window and a thin
sandwich element.

by 49.1 kWh/m2, which is close to meeting the requirement for Renovation Class

cooling and heating demand. By turning oﬀthe façade in the warmer months,

multifunctional façade with the façade turned on all year. It is expected that
the residents would open the window when the room temperature is high. This
is found to reduce the cooling demand by 27.2 kWh/m2 compared to the mul-
tifunctional façade with a closed window all year. Since it is likely that the
windows would be replaced during the façade renovation, it is found that re-

cooling demand by 10.8 kWh/m2 and the heating demand by 36.9 kWh/m2.

heating demand from September to May is reduced to 130.1 kWh/m2 and the
yearly cooling demand is reduced to 16.3 kWh/m2. This is a reduction in heat-
ing demand of 89.4 kWh/m2, which meets the requirement for Renovation Class
1. This shows that the conﬁguration of the multifunctional façade results in an
improved and acceptable energy demand compared to the reference façade.

Case setup
This project is a case study based on a room model with the dimensions 4 m x
3 m x 5 m (WxHxL) placed in the middle of the building. The room is applied
with an ideal heater with a setpoint on 20 ◦C to maintain a realistic indoor
temperature. In practice, a more realistic heating system, e.g. a radiator system,
would be applied, which might lead to slightly diﬀerent results. The room is also
applied with an ideal cooler with a setpoint on 25 ◦C to simulate a more realistic
indoor environment with no extreme overheating in the summer months. Since
it is uncommon to have a cooling system in a residential building in Denmark,
the cooling demand from this should be seen as a value for overheating in the
room. With diﬀerent dimensions, a diﬀerent location or other preliminary setups
would result in diﬀerent simulation results.
Façade structures
The reference façade matches a conventional 1960s building with a thickness of
126 mm distributed on 13 mm gypsum, 75 mm insulation, 30 mm cavity and a
8 mm ﬁber cement board on the inside. The ventilated and the multifunctional
façade have the same thickness to avoid changing too many parameters and
make the results more comparable. The limitation of not being able to expand
the façade externally due to balconies is another reason not to change the façade
thickness. Due to the set façade thickness of 126 mm, there is limited space for
the ventilated gap when there has to be a sandwich element consisting of 30 mm
concrete on each side of a minimal layer of 32 mm insulation. The remaining
space is therefore 30 mm for the ventilated gap giving an airspeed of 0.3 m/s
which might not be the most eﬃcient for pre-heating the inlet air. The study by
Jakob Spiegelhauer shows that a lower air speed gives higher inlet temperatures,
which indicates that a wider air gap might be more eﬃcient for pre-heating the
inlet air. This study shows the same tendency for the ventilated façade but
has not been tested for the multifunctional façade. The chosen properties and
materials are crucial for the energy demand and changes in these assumptions
would change the results.
The weather conditions
The performance of the diﬀerent façade systems have been analysed on diﬀerent
weather conditions to track the inﬂuence from the radiation. In particular, it
was found that the solar radiation during winter increases the inlet tempera-
ture. The results for the summer period highlighted the need for a conﬁguration
of the multifunctional façade, since the solar radiation causes an unwanted in-
crease of the inlet temperature. In this way, the diﬀerent weather conditions
are considered to give a reasonable overview of the performance of the façade
systems.

Discussion
Residents in simulation rooms
It is diﬃcult to predict the behaviour of the residents of the apartment. Firstly,
the added heat to the room can vary if the resident lives alone or more people
are living together or depending on how many electrical devices and lamps are
installed in the apartment. Secondly, the residents can open their window and
take in the air from outside instead of taking in the air from the cavity. This
is most common in the summer time when people have a partly open window
at all times. The eﬀect of this have been assessed in Chapter 6. The eﬀect of
an open window during winter has however not been assessed. For the room
analyses, it is generally assumed that the windows are not opened to account
for the cooling demand, and that there is an internal heat gain of 5 W/m2 to
account for residents and equipment.
Validation of the ventilated façade
The NMF model for IDA ICE underestimates the inlet temperatures and the
U-value compared to results from COMSOL, while the heat ﬂux through the
surfaces does not match in the two models. If the U-values are lower, it implies
that the heat ﬂux through the façade is lower meaning that the air in the
ventilated gap is not pre-heated as much. In this way, the diﬀerence in U-value
and inlet temperature is related.
Nusselt number
The original IDA ICE code for the double façade model did not provide accurate
results for the presented multifunctional façade. Therefore, the mathematical
description of the airﬂow in the cavity was changed to resemble forced internal
convection in a duct. This change further required a replacement of the height
parameter with the hydraulic diameter and changing the Nusselt number. The
Nusselt numbers found in literature underestimated the resulting inlet temper-
atures, why the Nusselt numbers used for the ventilated façade component were
instead estimated from the convective heat transfer coeﬃcient, HConv, that is
used in COMSOL. This estimation resulted in a Nusselt number of 17 for all
façades with external gaps. For the façade with an internal gap, the internal
wall part has a Nusselt number of 15.22 and the external wall part has a Nusselt
number of 14.18.
Layering
The layer analysis showed justiﬁcation in having 10 vertical sublayers and 7
horizontal layers to achieve accurate inlet temperatures and heat ﬂuxes through
the façade. 10 sublayers are the maximum amount if the façade consists of 5
materials. It would be possible to apply more than 7 horizontal layers, but to
==PAGE:93==
79
avoid errors when adding the layers manually in IDA ICE, it would be ben-
eﬁcial to develop a NMF code, where the layering is an implemented part of
the component. Since the air gap for the IDA ICE model is only horizontally
layered, the mesh is more simple than the mesh used in COMSOL. As it is only
possible to make layers in the wall components and not in the air gap, it would
improve the understanding of the temperature development to have layers in
the air gap in the ventilated and multifunctional façade. This would require
further development of the NMF code.
Validation of the multifunctional façade
The validation of the multifunctional façade showed that there are minor diﬀer-
ences between the original double façade model from IDA ICE and the created
multifunctional façade model. The model validation showed that the multifunc-
tional façade absorbs less radiation than the double façade model. This would
cause a lower inlet temperature as well as a higher heat ﬂux. This was shown in
the results, which means the diﬀerence in heat ﬂux, radiation and temperatures
for the multifunctional façade model is related.
Buildability
This project has focused on the theoretical aspect of developing a multifunc-
tional façade. It has not been investigated how the multifunctional façade should
be built. Further considerations could include how to construct the horizontal
leakages and the practical design of an "on/oﬀ-switch" of the ventilated gap.
Detailed drawings should be created as well before full-scale testing and con-
struction.
Solar walls as future way of building
This study shows that the multifunctional façade has great potential of pre-
heating the inlet air through the façade to reduce the heating demand.
A
downside to this façade system is the additional cost compared to a normal
energy renovation of a building, as adding a glazing to the outside of a normal
façade will be more expensive. The energy savings should therefore outweigh
the additional cost.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
As a result of the project, a multifunctional façade element that exploits the so-
lar heat gain has been developed for simulation in IDA ICE. The multifunctional
façade model is based on a ventilated façade model, which has been calibrated
and validated against a COMSOL model.
The multifunctional façade com-
ponent has been created by merging the NMF code for the ventilated façade
component with the existing double façade component in IDA ICE. Lastly, the
multifunctional façade model has been validated against the double façade model
as well as tested in diﬀerent weather conditions and parametric setups.
The multifunctional façade element has been tested in a room model.
The
analysis of the room model showed that the inlet air is pre-heated from -3.0 ◦C
to 3.7 ◦C on a cold and sunny day and from 0.6 ◦C to 3.1 ◦C on a cold and
cloudy day. This means that the solar heat gain is exploited in the extent of a
temperature increase of 6.7 ◦C and 2.5 ◦C respectively, which proves that the
multifunctional façade has potential for pre-heating in the winter. The room
analysis further showed that the multifunctional façade lowered the heating
demand from September to May to 170.5 kWh/m2 compared to the reference
façade with a yearly heating demand on 219.5 kWh/m2, while the yearly cooling
demand was increased to 64.1 kWh/m2 from 19.8 kWh/m2. This is a signiﬁcant
decrease in heating demand of 49.1 kWh/m2, but the increase in cooling demand
of 44.4 kWh/m2 implies that it is not worth implementing the basic conﬁguration
of the multifunctional façade for energy renovation. The room analysis showed
that with an external temperature of 26 ◦C, the inlet temperature is 47.6 ◦C on
a warm and sunny day. In this way, the initial concern regarding overheating has
been conﬁrmed but a solution to the problem has been investigated. Since the
multifunctional façade causes a greater need for cooling in the summer months,
a solution where the room takes in the external air instead of the preheated air
from the ventilated gap has been analysed. Furthermore, opening the window,
replacing the window glazing and adding an extra layer of external glazing was
included in the model to lower the heating demand from September to May to
130.1 kWh/m2 and reduce the cooling demand to 16.3 kWh/m2.
In conclusion, the project has developed a multifunctional façade for building
simulation and presented a possible conﬁguration of the multifunctional façade
with an improved and acceptable energy demand as was the aim of the project.
