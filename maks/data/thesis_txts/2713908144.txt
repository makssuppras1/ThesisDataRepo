Introduction
Understanding the spatio-temportal variability of carbon balance in forests and its driving
mechanisms is substantial to evaluate the vulnerability of terrestrial carbon pools in light
of the future climate conditions [1][2][3]. Studying ecosystem carbon dynamics requires the
investigation of interannual variation of net ecosystem exchange of CO2 (NEE) with long-
term eddy covariance measurements [4], in order to identify the key factors and processes
that determine the ecosystem carbon balance [3].
The eddy covariance (EC) method, used to measure mass and energy exchange along a
horizontal surface between the vegetation and the atmosphere [5], represents a widely
approved method for the estimation of CO2, H2O and sensible heat fluxes, and for the
calculation of global carbon budgets in forests, among multiple other ecosystems and
surfaces.
EC achieves the measurement of atmospheric gas fluxes in an ecosystem in
a direct, precise, and continuous manner, and is the most eﬀicient and commonly used
method to study the atmosphere-biosphere interactions [5][6].
However, the theoretical assumptions underlying the EC method are not often met in
practice since complex ecosystems, such as tall forest canopies, can present a large sur-
face heterogeneity and since varying atmospheric conditions may occur. These non-ideal
conditions lead to large and systematic bias errors in the EC estimations [7][8][9].
Therefore, a variety of quality control and assurance criteria are routinely applied to flux
measures [10][11]. In doing so, gaps are introduced in the data sets, generally reaching
30–40% on an annual basis [12][13]. Thereafter, gap-filling corrections must be applied to
obtain continuous data, and calculate daily and yearly flux averages [5]. Various gap-filling
algorithms exist, with specific advantages and disadvantages [12][13][14][15][16][17].
The friction velocity correction, applied to night-time respiration measurements, is one
of the most controversial correction method [5]. This correction is applied to account for
the night time stable stratification resulting in an air flow decoupling between the vertical
zones inside and outside a vegetative surface [18], which may result in a CO2 drainage out
of the investigated control volume and an underestimation of EC fluxes [18][19].
Another commonly discussed part of the EC flux estimation consists in one of its compo-
nent called the storage change. Historically neglected due to its compensation on a daily
scale [20], the storage change has in the recent years been widely investigated, due to its
significant contribution to the NEE of CO2, specifically in tall canopies forest ecosystems
[21].
The present report aims at identifying the optimal storage change method for the esti-
mation of carbon surface fluxes in a Danish forest, using EC measurements and auxiliary
meteorological data. It is hypothesized that the optimal storage change estimation im-
plies a refined velocity threshold value for an accurate interpretation of turbulent flux
measurements from vertical CO2, H2O, and air temperature profiles.

2.1
Technical set-up
2.1.1
Site Description
The observations were made at the Danish ICOS station, DK-Sor, located in the forest
‘Lille Bøgeskov’ near Sorø on the island of Zealand at 55°29’13’’N, 11°38’45’’E, 40 m above
mean sea level [22]. The meteorological station contains a 45 m mast, with a concrete
base, and a hut with the digital equipment.
The temperate maritime climate is characterized by cool summers and mild windy win-
ters. The soils are brown soils classified as either Alfisols or Mollisols (depending on a base
saturation under or over 50%) [23]. The average annual temperature of the study period
for this work (January to December 2021) was 13°C. Located on a flat terrain, the main
part of the forest is covered by European beech (Fagus sylvatica L.), but there are about
10% conifers, including scattered stands of Norway spruce (Picea abies(L.) Karst.), and
single trees of European larch (Larix decidua). The forest is managed and thinned about
20% every 10th year [22]. The average tree height around the mast was 28 m in 2018 [23].
In 2001, the roughness length and displacement height were, 1.8 ± 0.7 and 20.6 ± 4 m,
respectively [24].
2.1.2
Instrumentation and data collection
Eddy Covariance System
The Eddy Covariance (EC) system is running at the station since June 1996. The EC
sensors are mounted at height 43 m (16 m above the canopy) of the 45 m mast.
An
enclosed-path infrared gas analyser (IRGA) measures the fluctuations of CO2 and H2O,
and a 3D sonic anemometer measures the fluctuations of the wind velocity components
(u,v,w) in horizontal, mean and perpendicular to the mean and in vertical direction,
respectively, and of the sonic temperature, which is a close approximation to virtual tem-
perature (Tvirt). The measurements are sampled at 10 Hz. During the study period, the
IRGA was a LI-7200 (LI-COR Lincoln, NE, USA) and the 3D sonic anemometer, a Gill
HS-50 (Gill Instruments, Lymington, UK). The data acquisition was done by the Smart-
Flux2 system (LI-COR Lincoln, USA) [23].
Profile System
The profile system was installed in 2021 and measures CO2, H2O and air temperature
(Tair) using closed-path IRGA, LI-7000 (LI-COR Lincoln, NE, USA), and PT100/3 air
thermometers (Campbell Scientific, Logan, USA) ventilated by 43502 Young (Campbell,
Logan, USA ) at 12 heights along the 43m tower. The datalogger is a CR1000X (Campbell
Scientific, Logan, USA). It has to be noted that the data from the profile system was
available only from 22th of February in 2021.
The measurements of Tair are in situ.
However, there is a spatial separation between
the gas inlet tube and the fast response analyzer, kept in the air-conditioned hut at the
base of the tower. The gas samples are carried down with a 50 m long tube (6 mm inner
diameter) at approximately 4 stdl min-1 to the IRGA, where it is analysed with a flow rate
of 3-4 stdl min-1. This spatial separation and the buffer tanks introduce a high spectral
attenuation [25], but possess the advantage of keeping the IRGA in controlled and stable
conditions, which is essential for the robustness of such long-term measurement systems

2.1.3
Characterization of the buffered sequential sampling system
In order to quantify the filtering effect of the sequential sampling profile system, the phys-
ical properties of the buffer system were determined in a simple step change experiment
conducted at the study site between 10h30 and 12h00 (local time) on the 20th of June
2022. The gas inlet at the 2 m height of the profile system was temporarily disconnected
to perform the experiment. All heights are connected to the IRGA with a 50 m tube. It
is assumed that the obtained results will be representative for any level.
First, to create a controlled reference flow, the inlet tube was injected with a standard gas
mixture, stabilizing the CO2 concentration at 410 ppm. Then, an approximate dilution
factor of 10% was introduced by the injection of N2 with a flow rate of 0.5 L min-1 through
the same inlet tube. The timings of the successive injections were carefully recorded. This
experiment was repeated four times. The experiment start times were recorded with an
uncertainty of about 0.5 s and with possible human mistake.
The infinite impulse response filter (IIR) was used to simulate a low-pass filter, which
represents the behaviour of an ideally mixed stirred reactor, i.e. the buffer tanks [25]. It
is defined in the time domain as

constant. For a given sampling frequency, fs, the relation between the cut off frequency

with fc, the frequency at which the filter reduces power by a factor two.

The tube travel time (TT) is associated with the spatial separation between the gas inlet
and the IRGA. The filter constant (A), defines the weight of additional gas input into
the buffer, compared to the weight of the gas in the buffer volume. Finally, the time
constant (τ) represents the decaying tail of the concentration time series, and especially,
it represents the time at which the spectral time series reaches 1 over the cut-of frequency
(fc) [25].
The tube travel time was optimized using R squared (rsq) of the non-linear fit, based on
a chosen (TT) range of 5 to 100 s. Non-linear least square fitting (nls) was then used to
compute the three parameters of the low-pass filter (A,fc,τ). Those values were obtained
in order to calibrate and characterize the instrumental installation.

2.2.2
Turbulent flux processing
The Software EddyPro version 7.0 [33] was used to compute the raw EC data in LI-COR
GHG format. Mass and energy flux covariances between the high-frequency measured
vertical windspeed w and the scalar of interest s were calculated. The selected averaging
interval (AI) was 30 min. This is the widely accepted length that includes eddies in the
low frequency range of the spectrum and is short enough to avoid the inclusion of non-
turbulent fluctuations [34].
Additionally, corrections were performed to the raw data to correct for instrumental lim-
itations and deviations from the theory of the EC concept [32].
Indeed, even though
measured continuously, the EC data contains gaps due to instrumental malfunctioning
or records incorrectly representing the ecological processes, due to micrometeorological
conditions outside of the assumptions of the EC technique [35].
Data quality control (QC) of the EC raw data is described in Sabbatini et al., 2018 [31].
The various tests eliminate unrealistic values, spikes and check for completeness of the
AI, among other. The tests with elimination of bad data produce one of the three quality
4
==PAGE:11==
flags, according to the percentage of missing records (with 0 being good data, 1, data of
moderate quality, and 2, data of bad quality). The resulting gaps are either filled with
linear interpolation of neighbouring values or replaced with NA (if the gap number of
consecutive gaps is higher than 3).
The default settings of the EddyPro software were selected, unless specified otherwise.
The detrend method selected was linear detrending with a time period of 30 min. The
correction of low-pass filtering effects was done with the in situ/analytic method (Ibrom et
al. 2007) [25], while the correction for instruments separation was done with the method
by Horst and Lenschow (2009), only crosswind and vertical [36]. A spectral correction
for low frequency range was also performed with the analytical method by Moncrief et al.
(2004) [37]. The data was filtered according to the QC test results (Mauder and Froken,
2004) by low data quality (flag value = 2) [38].
2.2.3
Time lag compensation and filtering
The correlation between the gas concentration time series of the upper level of the profile
system and the EC system was maximized with a cross correlation analysis yielding the
optimal time lag. Time lags were computed for CO2, H2O and Tair. For simplification, the
time frame for the time lag estimation was only one full day of measurements, on 1st July
2021. The output value was assumed to be valid all year long, because the flow through
the sampling system was approximately constant. The EC measurements were assumed
to be instantaneous, for the sake of simplification and the obtained time lags were used to
correct the time series of the profile system.
The time lag was applied to the storage change (SC) time series of the corresponding
scalar using spline interpolation. The time lag value was subtracted from the start time
of the time series, signifying that the measured variation had occurred in nature before
the time of the measurement on the IRGA.

Delta concentrations (DC)
The DC method (also indicated as ∆c) follows the principle described by Greco and Bal-
docchi (1996) [20]. For each half hour, an end and a start concentration were defined from
the measured data by using spline interpolation to the margins of the interpolation period
(30 min). Then, the storage term for the AI was calculated as the difference between the
end and the start concentration, as follows

where ρd is the air molar density calculated as a function of air moisture, temperature
and pressure, ∆z is the vertical extent of the sampling point (m) and ∆cs(z) is the scalar
molar fraction difference at the height of the sampling point over the time period ∆t.
Slope method (SL)
In the SL method, the value for each AI was derived using a simple linear regression with
R’s lm(). In particular, the slope of the linear regression was considered, which yields the
same units as the r.h.s term of Eq. 2.5. The regression function was defined to avoid data
scarcity effect on lm(), by setting the output to NA if there were less than 3 consecutive
rows of data.
1 point method (1p)
The 1p method is a discrete storage representation that assumes that the concentration
change within the AI at the top of the tower is representative of the whole profile. A study
by Hollinger et al. 1994 [40] justified the use of this method over a tall forest, where CO2
concentration profiles tend to be well mixed. Similarly, Greco and Baldocchi (1996) [20]
demonstrated that potential errors from the so-called discrete estimate of CO2 SC should
be small.
Full profile (f)
In the full method (equally abbreviated to f), the gas measurements at each level con-
tribute, according to the corresponding layer depth, to the total value of storage change
for the 43 m height CV, over the AI. This method is closer to the integration over height
described in Eq. 2.4, and is, in the case of this study, a 12 points vertical system.

2.2.6
Gap-filling and flux partitioning
REddyProc package provides standard CO2-focused post-processing routines for reading
30 min data from various formats : estimating the u* threshold, gap-filling, flux parti-
tioning and output visualization. The package loads time series of quality-checked and
storage-corrected fluxes, with the standard meteorological parameters [35].
The moving point method [14] was used for the estimation of the u* threshold, to dismiss
non-turbulent conditions data. The gap-filling was executed with the marginal distribution
sampling (MDS) developped by Reichstein et al., 2005 [41].
Finally the daytime flux
partitioning of Lasslop et al., 2010 [42] was used for flux partitioning. One long data gap
occurred from January to end of February 2022, where the profile system was not yet
operational.

3
Results
The first part of this section presents the outcome of the system characterization. The
second part shows the observations of the diurnal variation of the storage change (SC)
term for the different estimation methods, and their relation to the friction velocity. The
main objective of this report is to study the CO2 exchanges, but the sensible heat (H) and
latent heat (LE) SC estimations will also be shown and briefly discussed. Note that if the
different storage methods lead to similar results, only the DC is presented as an example,
while the results from the other methods are available in the Annex. Finally, the impact of
the different storage methods will be observed on the surface fluxes, namely Net Ecosystem
Exchange (NEE), predicted Respiration (RECO) and Gross Primary Production (GPP),
analyzed at the seasonal and daily scales.
3.1
System Characterization
3.1.1
Buffered sequential sampling system properties
The step change experiment was conducted as described in Section 2.1.3, in order to

3.1.2
Time lag compensation
The time lag values for CO2, H2O and Tair obtained by covariance maximisation were 328.4
s, 318.4 s and 246.6 s, respectively. For CO2 and H2O, the obtained values were slightly
increasing with each covariance maximisation iteration. The first value was kept and used
further as starting point to set the lag search window of the cross-correlation function.
These results are not in fit with the experimental time lag from the step change experiment,
showing that the instrumental calibration can be a source of error if not estimated correctly.
Besides, the lower time lag value for H2O than for CO2 is in contradiction with the
estimations by Ibrom et al., 2007 [25]. The result of the gas fluxes and temperature time
series shifts can be observed in Figure A.2 in the Annex.

3.2
Storage term
3.2.1
Diurnal course
The storage terms for CO2, H and LE have been calculated for the whole year and their
impact on the surface flux estimates and annual sums will be shown in Section 3.3. Here,
the storage term for the month of July has been selected to demonstrate the diurnal course
of the SC during the season with the highest photosynthesis rates. Being in the growing
period, this month is also the period when much greater magnitude of NEE and CO2 are
expected (both at night and during the day), compared to the dormant season [44].
Figure 3.2 shows the mean hourly course of CO2 over the month of July 2021 for the four
SC methods. Figure A.3 in the Annex depicts each method separately, with the average
values between methods presented in light grey. The results of SC for H (Figure 3.3) and
LE (Figure 3.4), follow the same structure. These figures show that the SC estimates for
all three parameters follow diurnal variations, with peaks happening in the late afternoon
and/or in the morning.

The mean diurnal pattern of CO2 storage comprises five phases: late night variable positive
storage at low level (23h00-04h00), transition to daytime after flush of CO2 from the
canopy air space around sun rise (04h00-06h00), relaxation of CO2 flush (07h00-09h00),
transient increase of storage over the day (09h00-17h00), crossing the zero line at 12h00,
i.e. the photosynthesis maximum, and early night CO2 accumulation (17h00-22h00).
The CO2 storage term fluctuates around 0-2 µmolm-2s-1 until 4h00 and then peaks to
values between -5 and -10 µmolm-2s-1 at 6h00, before progressively increasing until 12h00,
when it fluctuates around 0 µmolm-2s-1 until 16h00. Then, the values increase until about
3 µmolm-2s-1 around 21h00.
This morning peak has been widely documented and usually occurs when the incoming
radiation reaches daylight values. This is the moment when the nighttime stable stratifi-
cation is broken, and the air mixing in the convective boundary layer causes the storage
values to decrease. This phenomenon is of greater importance after a stable night with
low wind speed [45].
Another notable element in Figure 3.2 is the low CO2 storage values in the second part of
the night (00h00-05h00), compared to the first part of the night (20h00-00h00). Finally,
the gradual increase of storage values in the afternoon, from negative to positive values,
will also be discussed.
Figure 3.2 shows that the SL and DC methods yield similar results for the CO2 storage.
However, the 1p underestimates the morning peak in both SL and DC, while full reaches
a more negative storage value. The same can be observed during the afternoon increase,
where 1p values are slightly lower than the average (in light grey on Figure A.3 in the
Annex) and full values tend to be higher, both in DC and SL. The latter can also be
observed during the night (21h00 to 4h00).
The mean daily values (shown on Figure A.3), with the average standard errors, present
variations between the four SC methods. The values can be observed in Table 3.2.

3.2.2
Vertical gradient
The storage change also presents variations among the different measurements levels.
When calculating the SC over a reference depth of 1m for all levels (Figure 3.5), the CO2
storage term presents higher hourly fluctuations in the lowest levels. At the upper level,
only the morning peak can be observed and a slight afternoon increase. However, when
the storage change is calculated over the respective layer depths, as stated in Table 2.2,
the CO2 storage term (Figure 3.6) shows higher fluctuations in layer 2 (30.05 m to 38.45
m). This layer corresponds to the top of the canopy, where most CO2 exchanges with the
atmosphere occur, but also the layer with the greatest depth (8.4 m).
Similar results are obtained for H and LE (Figures A.6 and A.7 in the Annex). When
looking at the vertical storage gradients for H and LE, it can be seen that with the 1m
depth values (l.h.s.
on Figures A.6 and A.7, respectively), the storage terms presents
similar hourly fluctuation values over the layers. However, looking at the SC calculated
with height-specific depths (r.h.s. of Figures A.6 and A.7, respectively), it can be observed
that the upper levels contribute by far to the storage change term. The highest fluctuations
can be seen in layer 2, while the layer 12 shows a change fluctuations close to 0, both for
H and LE.
3.2.3
Monthly course and Impact on surface fluxes
The monthly variation of SC (in Annex, Figure A.8) shows that the mean CO2 SC was
slightly negative during May-July and slightly over 0 µmolm-2s-1 during the other months.
In the case of H, the monthly values fluctuate around 0 Wm-2, with a stronger negative
peak in May of almost -1 Wm-2. For LE, the SC is close to 0 Wm-2 and slightly positive
all year, except from March to July, where it reached a peak in May with a mean value of
2 Wm-2.
Figure A.9 in the Annex shows that when looking at the seasonal fluctuations of of NEE,
LE and H surface fluxes, the impact of the storage term can not be clearly observed,

Relation to the friction velocity threshold
The friction velocity threshold is applied to avoid using data that fall under meteorological
conditions that do not fulfill the basic assumptions of the Eddy Covariance method, such
as low turbulence conditions, where other transport processes might dominate [46]. As
seen in Section 3.2.4, an overestimation of the u* threshold might result in the overlooking
of considerable values of the storage change term in the calculation of annual budgets.
The u* thresholds have been estimated with the moving point method [14] and the esti-
mates for the different SC methods, and the turbulent flux can be seen on Figure 3.16.
The DC full method yields the lowest threshold (0.23 ms-1), followed closely by SL full
(0.24 ms-1), and the turbulent flux alone gives the highest estimation of 0.29 ms-1.

4
Discussion
4.1
Relevance of accurate storage estimation
The importance of the storage change around sunrise and sunset has been documented,
especially over short time scales of 1h or less [47] [48]. However, over larger periods, the
storage has often been assumed to average to zero [20] [49] and was omitted from long-
term budgets [5]. This assumption becomes false under conditions where non-turbulent
process prevail, namely, in the nighttime stable boundary layer [18] [32] [50] [51], in tall
canopies [52] and during day-night transition times [53]. Fluxes attaining those conditions
are often filtered out in surface flux estimations, using steady state tests and u* filtering,
as part of the standard EC processing [10] [54]. Thereafter, the created gaps need to be
filled [55], which propagates an uncertainty [14] and might lead to an inaccurate depiction
of net surface atmosphere exchanges, and energy, water and carbon diurnal cycles [49].
This indicates the need for an accurate and precise estimation of the storage term and
especially a deep understanding of the driving and counteracting processes.
4.2
Daily course of storage change
The average daily course of CO2 showed a comparable evolution for all four storage meth-
ods: a positive storage at night, when CO2 builds up in the canopy under stable conditions,
and a negative storage in the morning, with a turbulence or leaf assimilation onset leading
to reduction in stored CO2 [32][20].
Xu et al., 2019 explained the peaks of storage term between 8 and 9 am and around 4pm
under the canopy height by the canopy acting like a ”lid”, trapping the surface flux in the
form of storage, instead of registering it as a turbulent flux within the averaging interval
(AI). The morning peak lies in the transition period between the onset of radiative heating
and the the full formation of a strong CBL, while the afternoon peak corresponds to the
onset of radiative cooling and the beginning of the stable boundary layer [49].
Additionally, Aubinet et al., 2012 suggested that the lower values during the 2nd part
of the night could be due to a reduction in source intensity or in the presence of other
CO2 transport processes such as advection [32]. Van Gorsel et al., 2007 also observed
lower values in the 2nd part of the night in a Eucalyptus forest, and explained those by
a suppression of turbulence, stability of the flow regime within the canopy and gravity
currents that drain CO2 enriched air away [53].
The range of values obtained for the SC of CO2 (-11 to 6 µmolm-2s-1) and LE (-18 to 20
Wm-2) were in accordance with the values of ±10 µmolm-2s-1 and ±20 Wm-2 obtained by
Xu et al., 2019 over three forest sites. Only the SC for H showed a much lower range (±1.5
Wm-2) compared to the ±30 Wm-2 in the aforementioned study [49]. However, previous
studies over temperate forests with a height of 30 m showed contrasting ranges of SC with
−1.8 to 5.2 Wm-2 for LE, −9.2 to 11.6 Wm-2 for H [56], and ±3 µmolm-2s-1 for CO2 [57].
These results confirm that storage in tall canopies is a complex process, influenced by a
number of physical, climatic and ecological interacting factors and requires a site-specific
approach [21] [51] [49], which has to be considered in its calculation.
28
==PAGE:35==
4.3
Vertical distribution of storage change
A vertical profile of contributions to the storage term was demonstrated, with a higher
contribution of the lowest profile levels for a normalized layer depth of 1m. This non-
uniform vertical distribution was also demonstrated in Yang et al., 2007 (EC tower of 30m
with 12 levels profile, in a forested site). This study suggested that a level in the lower
canopy contributes more to the seasonal mean CO2 SC than an upper canopy level with
the same depth [44].
When it comes to storage per unit level-specific height, the study by Yang et al., 2007
showed that the level below the thermal inversion layer (the latter was 6.10 m in that
case) contributes to a greater extent to the seasonal mean CO2 SC from sunset to 10 am,
concluding that there is a disproportional relation between the section’s vertical volume
and its contribution to the CO2 SC [44]. This was also shown in the present work, where
the 2nd highest level (2), corresponding to where the inversion layer in stable nights is
located, showed a dominant contribution to the SC for CO2, LE and H.
4.4
Surface flux estimations
The annual sums of NEE for all methods were within 3.25-4.1 metric tons of C ha-1, which
is similar to 3.7 ±0.7 t C ha-1, the value obtained by Wofsy et al., 1993 investigating a
deciduous forest in a humid continental climate [58]. The values of annual respiration and
gross ecosystem production, however, were almost two times higher in the present work.
Pilegaard et al., 2001, obtained over 2 years (1997-1998) an average NEE value of 2.95 t
C ha-1, for the same forest site in Sorø [59].
The mean monthly diurnal course of NEE fluxes during the morning transition period,
showed a clear underestimation of the NEE by the turbulent flux alone, and a slight
underestimation by the 1p method, compared to the full method (Figures 3.12, and 3.13),
i.e. turbulent flux values and full differed by 75% at 6h00. This difference represents the
storage term and the differences between the storage methods can be clearly observed for
this particular time of the day.
The non-linear relation between NEE and Rg during that period, and the earlier peak
of the NEE fluxes taking into account the storage term, compared to the turbulent flux,
might be due to the fact that the column underneath the sensor is more sensitive to the
flux changing, since the canopy acts as a virtual lid [49]. This morning air decoupling leads
to a buffering of fluxes in the below-canopy levels, and to more negative NEE fluxes when
storage is taken into account. The relative difference will be influenced by meteorological
conditions such as wind velocity [44][59].
The observation of GPP showed ± 5% difference between the turbulent flux and the flux
with storage terms, with DCfull presenting the greatest difference. This variation was
concentrated around 12h00.
The analysis of predicted RECO showed a range of 1-20% difference between the turbulent
flux and the flux with storage terms, with DCfull presenting the greatest difference. The
predicted RECO has been calculated based on NEE and GPP using the daytime method.
There is no great diurnal variation in this difference.

==PAGE:36==
4.5
Relation to friction velocity
Similar to the results in Section 3.2.4, many studies have demonstrated that the SC is
usually large when u* is small, especially during stable conditions when turbulence is not
suﬀicient to connect air spaces below and above the canopy [34][49].
A study on the optimal number of vertical sampling points along a profile in a forest
by Whang et al., 2016 demonstrated the relevance of the alternative method of friction
velocity threshold for filtering the nighttime NEE [21], as described by van Gorsel et al.,
2007, which uses data in the early evening period as representative for the whole night to
reduce underestimation of NEE on the morning hours in calm nights [53].
The u* results of this report are also in accordance with the graphs of storage term as a
function of u* obtained by Xu et al., 2019. That study demonstrated potential limitations
of the univariate filtering method of u* thresholding [21]. Similarly, Yang et al., 2016
demonstrated that in 67% of measurements, the nighttime storage in the growing season
accounts for at least 50% of the NEE when u* is lower than 0.3ms-1, and only for 13%
when u* is higher than 0.3ms-1 [44].
These results are equally observed in the SC of DC and SL methods, however, the differ-
ence presented between the 1p and full methods with the binned u* plot, explains why
the the u* treshold is slightly lower for the full methods in both cases.
When it comes to the impact on surface fluxes, Pilegaard et al., 2001 showed that the
substitution of night-time fluxes at u* lower than 0.25 ms-1 with calculated values with
the night-time partitioning method reduced the NEE by 23% [59].
4.6
Comparing storage change methods
Many studies over forested sites in temperate conditions suggested that 1p method and full
profile methods are interchangeable [60][40][61]. Other studies demonstrated the underes-
timation of surface fluxes using the 1p method [62][63][64][44]. The results of the present
work indicated that NEE, predicted RECO and GPP fluxes were underestimated when
using the 1p method in most diurnal transient conditions, compared to the full method.
However, a majority of studies on the optimal number of vertical and horizontal sampling
points, and/or on the optimal temporal resolution for SC estimation, concluded that local
climatic, topographic and surface cover characteristics are great drivers for the difference
in results [49][21][51][65][26], which makes the definition of a standard optimal procedure
for SC estimation a challenge.
When it comes to the comparison of DC and SL methods, there was no large consistent
difference in the resulting surface flux estimations.
30
==PAGE:37==
5
Conclusion
The present work demonstrated the importance of the storage change term in the estima-
tion of surface fluxes, since the turbulent flux alone was systematically presenting a bias,
especially in night-day transition meteorological conditions.
The four studied SC methods presented the typical diurnal course for the SC of CO2, LE
and H. However, a notable difference between the full and 1p method was observed for
CO2 and LE, especially in day-night transitions, i.e. morning and late afternoon peaks.
For all three storage fluxes, a vertical distribution along the profile was observed, with a
higher contribution of the layer close to the canopy, where the inversion layer is located.
The distribution of large storage values at low u*, indicated that a considerable amount of
potentially influencing storage flux data might be omitted from the calculation of surface
fluxes, especially if only the turbulent flux is considered. This was also demonstrated by
the lowest u* threshold in full methods and the overestimation of the u* threshold in the
turbulent flux alone.
It has been demonstrated that different methods for SC estimations can influence the
surface exchanges at annual scales in different manners. From the four methods, the full
methods were shown to result in less underestimations, probably due to a more optimal
calculation of the u* threshold.
Further work includes the exploration of the impact of different storage methods on the
energy-balance closure. The latter constitutes a recommended data quality assessment
metric of EC measurements [5][66][67]. Finally, further investigation on the different pos-
sible algorithmic choices along the computation of the EC storage term, including the u*
filtering, would also contribute to a better understanding of long-term carbon budgets and
forest ecosystem modeling.
