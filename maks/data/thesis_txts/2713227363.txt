III - Abstract 
 
 
Overheating in buildings is growing problem. In Northern European climates, where active cooling is 
less common, future climate projection into the middle of this century suggest that historical weather 
data is woefully inadequate for optimizing the indoor climate and energy performance of future 
buildings. High efficiency modular dwellings, which typically employ lighter airtight constructions, 
are at elevated risk of overheating due to their low thermal mass.  
In this paper, several thermal adaptation strategies are explored using multi-objective optimization 
to achieve an energy efficient, low-cost and climate resilient design which guarantees a baseline 
indoor environmental quality. IDA ICE building performance software is used to simulate a modular 
near-zero energy apartment dwelling which represents a high occupancy, high risk scenario for 
overheating. A lifecycle cost minimization function which can be adapted to the future energy prices 
enables designers to balance upfront investment cost with future energy savings, while achieving 
target IEQ metrics for adaptive thermal comfort, daylight autonomy and air quality. The variables to 
optimize were envelope parameters, thermal mass, shading devices, and natural and mechanical 
ventilation strategies with varying degrees of user intervention.

1. Introduction 
 
1.1. Context 
 
Buildings in the 2020s will need to rapidly adapt to the changing climate and energy context of this 
century. As many of the local climates of the world will be changing predictably toward warmer and 
potentially more extreme weather, fueled by higher temperatures and atmospheric humidity, this 
presents an enormous challenge to the existing housing stock. Higher summertime temperatures and 
shorter heating seasons change the economics of heating and cooling buildings, while time-tested 
traditional constructions may become unsuitable for these new climates. This calls for a fundamental 
review of how we see the built environment, from static and permanent, to adaptive and 
impermanent. Modular construction may present an effective means to produce high quality 
buildings that can be quickly deployed, but they also have certain characteristics which make them 
more prone to overheating. Chief among them is the lower thermal mass of their construction 
materials, namely wood and light steel, as compared to traditional concrete and masonry 
constructions. The restricted internal dimensions typical of modular houses also reduce volume of 
air and the internal surfaces which can absorb and disperse thermal gains (Ozarisoy & Elsharkawy, 
2019). 
 
The case for climate resilience 
 
The problem of overheating in buildings goes beyond concerns of mere thermal comfort; heat stress 
can lead to premature mortality and exacerbate morbidity, especially among the most vulnerable 
members of society. Yearly heat-related deaths could triple by 2050(Ozarisoy & Elsharkawy, 2019) . 
On the extreme end,(Raymond et al., 2020) argues that the increases in wet-bulb temperatures 
nearing the 35⁰C threshold is associated with extreme risks to health, and since 1979, occurrences of 
dangerous “extreme humid heat” have more than doubled globally. In northern European climates, 
risks of such extremes are remote; however, (Rydborg & Brunsgaard, 2021) argues that while serious 
heatwaves have been uncommon in Denmark, they will become a standard occurrence. In oceanic 
climates, precipitation will shift to the winters causing an elevated risk of drought conditions in the 
summer. 
 
(Schünemann et al., 2020) identifies multifamily housing, as an under-studied housing type with a 
vulnerable demographic and a particularly high overheating risk, arguing that confronting this gap 
in research is a matter of climate justice. The case of multi-family homes with more individuals per 
square meter highlights the problem with calculating internal gains based on floor area as is the 
norm. As the trend of rising housing prices continues in Europe, the floor area per occupant is 
decreasing. Smaller dwellings are also much more concentrated in urbanized areas which 
incidentally suffer from the heat island effect. More constrained building sites will also lead to less 
optimal orientations; apartments, for instance, are more likely to have only one exposed façade, 
leading the south facing dwellings to experience significant daytime thermal gains. 
 
Thus, addressing the worst-case overheating scenarios and targeting the needs of our most 
vulnerable populations is one of the most pressing challenges in maintaining a safe and hospitable 
built environment in the face of a warming climate.

2. Review of Literature 
 
2.1. State-of-the-Art 
 
Overheating is a widely studied issue in indoor climate, but it is less studied in Northern climates. 
(Artmann et al., 2008) demonstrates that throughout Europe the ability of buildings to harness cooler 
night-time temperatures to cool buildings will decrease because of climate change; however, unlike 
in South and Central Europe, a reduced but significant passive night cooling potential will remain in 
Northern climates for the coming decades. (Grygierek & Sarna, 2020) argues that using current 
weather data is no longer a reliable method to anticipate future building thermal performance and 
as it does not reflect the future weather extremes, even characterizing Northern and Central Europe 
as “doomed to dramatic changes as a result of global warming”. 
Climate change will significantly alter the economics of a building envelope. According to the 
International Energy Agency (IEA, 2021), global energy demand for cooling may triple between 2020 
and 2050, driven primarily by growth in emerging economies as air conditioning becomes more 
affordable and the need for it increases due to climate change. Avoiding the need for air conditioning 
altogether thus should be a primary goal. (Wang et al., 2021) estimates that due to climate change, 
energy use will decrease overall in colder regions, but this will be partly offset by cooling due the 
lengthening of warmer seasons. Similarly, (Dodoo, 2020) estimates that cooling energy demand may 
increase upwards of 60% to 192% in Sweden, depending on the chosen representative concentration 
pathway (RCP) of atmospheric CO2, as defined by the IPCC. This is a small portion of the total energy; 
however, the mere fact that cooling is installed may drive up its usage and devalue zero-energy 
cooling strategies.  
 
This presents a challenge for Denmark and other northern countries to maximize their capacity to 
passively cool spaces before resorting to active systems. (Gupta & Gregg, 2012) warn that 
deployment of air conditioning on a large scale may run counter to climate mitigation and adaptation 
goals, contributing to higher CO2 emissions and local heat islands. They add that mitigation and 
adaptation measures must not be contradictory or carry negative consequences for future 
generations.  
 
A large amount of research exists on the effects of climate change on residential and many papers 
that propose solutions; however, few papers analyze lifecycle costs, and no research found to date 
accounts for future energy pricing. 
 
(Simson et al., 2015) simulates a series of 16 recently built apartment buildings in Estonia, using IDA 
ICE simulation software, while monitoring temperatures on location. Despite their new 
constructions, over 80% of apartments failed the design performance requirements due to 
overheating. 
 
Several recent papers explore strategies for cooling buildings without the use of mechanical cooling. 
(Rydborg & Brunsgaard, 2021) attempts to resolve overheating in sustainable housing in Denmark 
by comparing the effectiveness of four thermal adaptation strategies in regard to thermal comfort 
and energy efficiency using future climates in 2050 and 2080. The authors argue that many low-
energy buildings are at higher risk of overheating because their focus on energy savings fails to 
==PAGE:14==
12 
 
account for the effects on heat retention. The paper recommends increasing ventilation to twice the 
minimum regulation of 0.3L/s/m2 in addition to window shading and strategic planting of deciduous 
trees. The use of whole building strategies has its advantage over testing individual adaptations 
isolation. The paper finds a balance between thermal comfort and energy efficiency but fails to 
quantify the factor of cost. 
 
Similar to (Rydborg & Brunsgaard, 2021),  (Naji et al., 2021) optimizes a residential design based on 
multiple objectives, namely Lifecycle Cost, thermal comfort, daylighting, air quality and energy. This 
multi-objective optimization (MOO) sought to minimize three objective functions: Thermal 
discomfort hours, daylight unsatisfied hours and lifecycle costs, while constraining indoor air quality 
and acoustic comfort to acceptable levels. The optimization was carried out for 8 locations, covering 
6 climate zones in Autralia, with optimal results found for each location. Unlike (Rydborg & 
Brunsgaard, 2021), future climate was not taken into account. Nevertheless, the ability to find 
optimal solutions in different scenarios shows that the approach is adaptable. Less ideal is the need 
to select a preferred solution based on balancing objective that are unfamiliar to the average person. 
What is an acceptable compromise between ‘thermal discomfort hours’ and ‘daylighting unsatisfied 
hours’? The TRNSYS simulation software used was not able to simulate more complex shading 
devices such as overhangs and external shades. 
 
(Ascione et al., 2018) carries out a cost-optimal analysis to identify the most cost-effective solutions 
for an energy retrofit project. The analysis couples MOO optimization with neural network in a multi-
stage framework. Sensitivity analysis is also used to optimize the neural network. This novel method 
was used to optimize a baseline model of an office building in Italy. The novelty of this approach is 
the reconciliation of private financial interests with the public perspective through global cost 
minimization, as well as the assessment of the contribution to urban heat island (UHI). 
 
A few studies, among them (Chvatal & Corvacho, 2009), have argued that excessive insulation is to 
blame for the overheating issue in low-energy buildings; however, reducing the airtightness or 
insulation level may be a very inefficient strategy to reduce overheating. According to Chvatal, the 
inability of these homes to release heat is also due to a poor ventilation strategy and a lack of solar 
control.  
 
(Rodrigues et al., 2016) investigates the potential for adding thermal mass in a low-energy timber 
dwellings using high-density fiberboard and phase change materials. The results showed that these 
easy-to-integrate solutions could help to regulate indoor temperatures. Rigips brand plasterboard 
with encapsulated PCM was used with melting points of 23 and 26⁰C.  
 
A failure to address overheating may cause occupants to take actions which undermine energy 
efficiency gains, such as opening windows during the heating season. Therefore, ensuring that IEQ is 
maintained, is also important for energy efficiency. In hot weather, occupant behavior is highly 
important when affecting the level of overheating (Gupta & Gregg, 2018). Stochastic behavioral 
models of window opening by residents allow for realistic predictions of one variable which has an 
outsized impact on thermal comfort and energy performance in dwellings (Andersen et al., 2013). 
Implementing these models may result in higher simulation runtimes. 
 
Simulations must be validated for their accuracy.  From (Symonds et al., 2016), there is great concern 
about modelling accuracy, particularly in predicting daily extreme temperatures indoors, and in 
predicting occupant behavior. The paper attempts to validate simulation results against actual 
monitored data of hundreds of homes in the UK. The many unknowns and potential inaccuracies in

3. Objectives 
 
The purpose of this thesis is to anticipate and quantify the future overheating potential in an existing 
modular building design and to implement cost-effective strategies that ensure an adequate indoor 
environmental quality, while also retaining the high energy performance characteristics of near-zero 
energy buildings (NZEB). Using future weather files derived from the United Nations’ climate models, 
this paper employs a multiple-objective optimization (MOO) algorithm with the help of IDA ICE 
building performance software. 
 
➢ An initial sensitivity analysis is done to select the most effective interventions within several 
categories, including solar control, ventilation, thermal mass, and envelope adjustment.  
 
➢ Following a development step to further refine key aspects of the model, a Multi-Objective 
Optimization is carried out evaluation six whole-building strategies. 
 
➢ A final scenario analysis is done to validate the optimal strategy in three climate periods. 
 
➢ The aim is to offers actionable recommendations to designers to create climate resilient 
dwellings that do not rely on mechanical cooling. 
 
➢ One key question to be answered: “Will mechanical cooling be required from 2050 under the 
future climate scenarios, or will other strategies be cost efficient?”

3.2. Scope 
 
The scope of this paper is limited to Near-zero energy residential apartments in Northern climates, 
with special consideration given to modular construction constraints. 
• 
The thesis studies a 50m2 dwelling with two adult occupants located near Copenhagen, 
Denmark. 
 
• 
The building’s principal façade (with the largest glazing area) oriented towards the South. 
 
• 
A moderate-to-severe climate scenario, based on the ‘A2’ family of emission scenarios 
developed by the IPCC, was selected for the future climate simulations in 2050 and 2080. 
The orientation, position and occupancy level of the apartment covers a worst-case scenario to 
ensure the results are robust.  As such, the conclusions drawn from daylight and solar control must 
take into account the south orientation of the principal façade. Results in other orientations may vary. 
 
4. methodology 
 
4.1. Energy Requirements: 
 
Near-Zero Energy Buildings (NZEB) are defined on a country-by-country basis in the EU as each 
territory has a unique climatic and energy context. According to Danish building regulation BR18, the 
energy frame requirements for residential ‘Low-Energy Class 2020’ building, the Danish equivalent 
of an NZEB, is 27 kWh/m2/year. For the reference dwelling in this thesis, which has a 50m2 gross 
floor area, this amounts to 1350 kWh/m2 per year for low energy class buildings. 
The energy frame is defined, according to BR18 regulation #259, as the total primary energy demand 
for heating, cooling, ventilation and domestic hot water per m2 of heated floor area. This excludes 
lighting and other domestic electric uses.

4.2. Thermal Comfort Requirements:

According to (de Dear et al., 2013), the adaptive approach to thermal comfort as defined by the 
standards ASHRAE 55 and EN16798, was first made into a standard in 2004 and has since been 
widely adopted as an optional standard for buildings without mechanical cooling. The original 1998 
paper makes use of the effective temperature; however, to enable wide adoption, this was simplified 
to dry-bulb temperature. The EN16798 differs from the ASHRAE 55-2004R standard in that the 
surveys used to derive the standard were located throughout Europe, rather than globally. Thus, the 
EN standard is likely more appropriate for the Danish context.  
 
Several thermal comfort metrics were used in this paper, some using relative values accounting for 
adaptive comfort, others showing more absolute values. For the sensitivity analysis absolute metrics 
such as ‘maximum operative temperature’ and ‘hours above 24⁰C’ were more comparable since they 
did not depend as much on the baseline model’s performance around the comfort threshold. 
Conversely, measures such as ‘degree-hours outside of Category II’ were highly sensitive to small 
shifts above the adaptive comfort threshold.  
 
Internal Heat Gains: 
 
Quantifying internal heat gains accurately in a representative manner is a challenge. Typically, 
internal heat gains are quantified as a constant W/m2. This is problematic as electric consumption 
depends on occupant numbers, ages, whether adults work from home and varies widely based on 
daily activity of the residents. A typical home will draw 600watts of power as a daily average, 
however a gaming computer may draw 400 Watts on its own. 
According to SBi guide for BE18, internal heat gains from occupants’ bodies should be 1.5W/m2 
with a maximum of 360 W (corresponding to a four-person household). Equipment heat gains, 
including lighting should be set at 3.5W/m2 with a minimum of 210W and a maximum of 840W, 
corresponding to the usage of a single individual and a four-person household, respectively. In the 
test dwelling, this corresponds to 1.5W/m2 × 50 m2 gross floor area, or 75W for persons and the 
210W minimum for equipment, for a total of 285W. Alternatively, for a two-person household it 
may be calculated using half of the body heat for a four-person household or 180W, and half the 
equipment energy or 420W, totaling 600W. 
[Figure 9, Barthelemy 2018] shows there are significantly higher occupancy rates in 1-2 person 
households during the day than in 3-5 person households. In addition, economies of scale do not exist 
in smaller households for cooking and washing, resulting in higher energy per person. Residents in 
smaller dwellings will perform many of the same activities as those in larger dwellings resulting in 
similar heat gains, but with a smaller volume to disperse the heat. This suggests that smaller 
households may have higher heat gains per m2 than a typical detached house; however, there is 
insufficient information found on this topic, presenting an opportunity future research to improve 
upon the internal heat gain model in this paper.

4.3. Daylight Requirements: 
 
A number of daylight requirements were explored which would ensure sufficient and uniform 
daylight for residential buildings. A key consideration was the ability for residents to use their 
apartment as a work-from-home space.  
In the Building Regulation 10 (BR10) Annex 1, subsection 6, The NZE Building Class 2020 specified 
a minimum window area equivalent to 15% of the heated floor area for windows having a 
transmission over 0.75 with adjustments for transmittances lower. This was higher than the 10% 
baseline BR18 requirement, but the requirement ensured that the stringent regulation did not drive 
the construction of homes with insufficient daylight. This requirement was later dropped to the 10% 
figure in BR18, though it is a good starting point for daylight optimization.  
The BR18 regulation #379 has special provisions for working rooms requiring the relevant floor area 
to conform to the (sDA30050) standard. The spatial daylight autonomy (sDA30050) requirement, 
developed by the Illumination Engineering Society in IES ‘LM-83-13’, requires 50% of the useable 
floor area in a zone to have a daylight illumination level of 300 lux for at least 50% of daylight hours 
on a plane at a height of 0.5m above the floor. The requirement utilizes existing weather data from 
the location to determine this figure via a simulation. This is in contrast to the conventional ‘daylight 
factor’ which assumes a uniform illumination equivalent to an overcast sky and does not require a 
year-long simulation.  
 
4.4. Air quality requirements: 
 
Air quality in an apartment is important for two reasons; firstly, higher temperatures cause a release 
of higher concentrations of volatile organic compounds. Secondly, discomfort stemming from poor 
air quality leads to window opening, even at the expense of thermal comfort and energy efficiency. 
(Fabi et al., 2013) find that the three main factors that are most correlated with window opening are 
indoor CO2 concentration, outdoor temperature, and indoor illumination. CO2 is a good measure of 
indoor air quality as it correlates highly with indoor pollutants generated by occupancy.  
To achieve satisfactory air quality, the EN 16798 standard recommends indoor CO2 concentrations 
no higher than 950ppm. Achieving this low concentration will decrease the likelihood of window 
opening. Thus, the air quality in the apartment shall be constrained to 950 ppm above background 
concentrations, corresponding to a minimum Category III comfort levels according to standard 
EN16798, table B.12. reproduced in Figure 4.

4.5. Cost-Effectiveness Requirement:

solution should be selected. As investment decisions in this paper were based on their performance 
relative to an already low-energy baseline, additional investment in energy efficiency has a lower 
marginal return and even minor inefficiencies, such as user behavior or a non-optimal setpoints, can 
have significant downsides. The focus was thus placed on solutions which improved thermal comfort 
with minimal investment and energy cost.

between energy, thermal comfort and investment cost existed.

IDA ICE v.5.0 (beta 22) building performance software was selected because of its high degree of 
customizability and its unique ability to realistically simulate complex shading, and detailed window 
constructions. The software also enabled the development of novel control strategies and to capture

4.7. Baseline Model 
 
A baseline house, modelled after the Domino modular apartment concept developed by Scandibyg 
(Scandibyg, 2021), was created which ensured all requirements for thermal comfort and daylight 
autonomy are fulfilled for the occupant in the present year 2020 using typical year weather files. 
These requirements were fulfilled by altering the original design as little as possible, and with 
minimal occupant intervention. A building information model (Revit BIM) of the property at 
Lundtoftevej 162, constructed by Scandibyg in 2021 with the same modular prefabrication method, 
was used as a basis for the materials and dimensions of the baseline model. The building wall, floor 
and roof cross-sections, details of which can be found in Appendix A: Building Sections’, were all 
modelled faithfully to the Revit BIM model supplied by Scandibyg. The dimensions of the modules 
were also copied as the overall thermal mass of the building is a key design criterion, and 
transportable weight and module size are a hard constraint.

Baseline adjustment: Daylight autonomy  
 
The lighting levels in the bedroom of the baseline model were found to be low and inappropriate with 
a bare-minimum 10% window to floor area ratio and a spatial daylight autonomy (sDA30050) of 
below 20%. This would make for poor working conditions for working-from-home. Conversely, the 
window area in the living room was deemed excessive due to the difficulty of controlling overheating 
in this zone. 
In an earlier version of the low-energy class 2020 specification, a minimum 15% ratio requirement 
was included. This requirement was dropped back down to the baseline 10%; nonetheless, it offered 
a good starting point for daylight autonomy. It was discovered by simulating the domino apartment 
that 15% glazing achieved the spatial daylight autonomy (sDA30050) in more than 50% of the floor 
area.

devices and ventilation even during a portion of the heating season.  
The standard of sDA30050 above 50% was set as a constraint for both the bedroom and living room 
to allow appropriate conditions for working from home. Extensive simulations were done to 
determine windows which improved daylight autonomy. Beside the area of the window, the distance 
of the top of the window from the ground-level was the greatest determinant of daylight autonomy 
and daylight uniformity. The height to width ratio had the second greatest effect on daylight 
uniformity. Wide windows which sacrificed height for width fared as poorly as tall-narrow windows.

“wasted” daylight.

Baseline Development: Air Handling Unit Control Strategy 
Heat recovery ventilation is central to the energy efficiency of a highly airtight building. An early 
iteration of the baseline model had a simplified Air handling unit (AHU) with heat recovery and a 
constant air volume, illustrated in Figure 7. 
==PAGE:29==
27 
 
Setpoints in the AHU were adjusted by use of parametric runs, varying the setpoints around the 
default values to ensure that all the zones achieve basic air quality and thermal comfort, and the 
dwelling consumes the least energy possible. 
The heat recovery bypass setpoint was set to a default value of 24⁰C and allowed the supply 
temperature to vary between 16-21⁰C.  
Below return-air temperatures of 24⁰C, the heat recovery ventilator keeps the supply air 
temperature at 21C using the heat exchanger bypass to regulate the incoming air using recovered 
heat. Having such a high setpoint of 24⁰C is energy efficient, allowing the building to accumulate 
thermal energy and only releasing excess energy that may cause overheating. This is beneficial in the 
winter, but when ambient temperatures are high or internal gains become excessive, this 
accumulation of heat becomes a liability in the warmer months.  
Furthermore, the post-heater, which elevated the temperature of the incoming air to acceptable 
temperatures, limits the supply temperature to 16⁰C. This is done to avoid local discomfort from a 
cool diffuser and to limit the potential for condensation; however, reducing the minimum supply 
temperature benefits both the energy efficiency and summertime overheating. This problem can be 
eliminated, according to (Eppelheimer, 2000), with a diffuser design which disperses the cool air and 
reduces air speeds. The temperature can be lowered to 10⁰C, and even less, without the risk of 
condensation or local cooling discomfort.

4.8. Model Assumptions 
 
Selection of orientation and position of apartment: 
 
A southern orientation for the side with the largest windows was selected to simulate the worst-case 
scenario. An upper-level apartment was selected for the same reason as it had a larger exposed 
envelope, and the upper level is not shaded by a balcony. As many sites have little choice with respect 
to orientation, designing for this extreme case becomes necessary when validating the design for all 
scenarios.  
 
Window Open Behavior: 
 
Modeling the behavior of residents is crucial to accurately predicting energy and thermal comfort 
impacts; however, residents do not behave in a deterministic manner. Many papers, namely (Haldi & 
Robinson, 2009)(Jones et al., 2017) (Andersen et al., 2013) (Fabi et al., 2013) attempt to model this 
behavior using a stochastic model. The energy performance is then run in a randomized series of 
runs with the results converging toward an average value. The stochastic models, however, require 
as many as 10 runs to get one results. This would multiply the computing time, so a deterministic 
model which approximates human behavior will be employed. According to (Fabi et al., 2013) the 
three most important variables that predict window opening behavior are CO2 concentration, 
outdoor temperature and indoor illumination levels. This paper outlines a deterministic model which 
opens windows when indoor temperatures exceeded a set value between 23⁰C and 27⁰C and when 
indoor temperatures are above outdoor temperatures. The second requirement that outdoor 
temperature must be lower than indoor temperature must be dropped in an adaptive model since 
natural ventilation is required to keep thermal comfort in check.  
A window opening macro was developed (see Figure 11) based on this deterministic model with a 
proportional controller that increases the opening linearly between 23⁰C and 27⁰C. In addition, night 
cooling behavior was added which scheduled night cooling between June 1 and August 31 if outdoor 
temperatures remained above 18⁰C.

Dry Steam emissions: 
 
According to IEA annex 14 [IEA,1990], the estimated steam emissions from showers is 0.2kg and 
0.8kg for a 3-minute and 15-minute shower, respectively. The average shower length being 8 
minutes, at the respective emission rates, this translates to between 0.43kg and 0.53kg for one 
shower. Assuming one shower per resident, the average emission per shower (0.48kg) is then 
distributed over the occupancy duration in the bathrooms. 
IEA also estimates in Table 6.1 of the IEA annex that emissions from “cooking and working” in the 
kitchen produces between 600-1500 g/h of water vapor, or 100g/h daily average. The average value 
of 1000 g/h was assumed during kitchen occupancy.

4.10. 
Solution Space 
 
The list of overheating prevention measures considered in this paper largely fall into 5 categories: 
envelope adjustments, solar control, thermal mass, natural ventilation and mechanical ventilation. 
A number of promising and novel solutions were added which included dedicated ventilation 
hatches, phase-change materials, albedo cooling and a demand-controlled ventilation system with a 
ventilative cooling boost mode. 
 
Envelope Adjustments: 
 
Several papers have noted insulation as a culprit for overheating. Thus, reducing the level of 
insulation may have benefits for cooling, but these must be balanced against the winter heat loss. The 
sensitivity analysis is used to quantify the tradeoffs between the cooling potential versus the energy 
losses related to removing insulation. 
The cooling effect of a white roof, also known as the albedo effect, has been a time-tested method of 
keeping buildings cool in southern climates. IDA ICE accounts for the absorbance of surfaces to 
calculate thermal gain, making it possible to check the effectiveness of the albedo effect in a northern 
context. 
 
Solar Control 
 
Solar control strategies were chosen based on their varying ability to selectively shade direct 
sunlight, allow in diffuse light, and preserve the view toward the outside. Solar control glazing was 
the least selective, followed by external overhangs. While internal and external shades are the most 
selective, they require user activation. In the case of screens shades, they also block the view, which, 
as noted earlier, severely limits use usefulness when residents are at home. External venetian blinds 
are the most effective at preserving the view but come at a cost premium. The final cost optimization 
will be able to determine whether the thermal performance advantages of an external shading 
strategy justify their cost. 
 
Thermal Mass 
 
The placement and thickness of a thermal mass largely determines its effectiveness. Other factors 
such as the conductivity and specific heat capacity of the material are also key determinants. Thus, 
three types of thermal mass were selected for assessment: concrete, gypsum and a hypothetical 
hybrid material based on crushed concrete waste. This material would retain similar advantages to 
concrete without the high embodied carbon.  
 
==PAGE:36==
34 
 
Phase-Change Material 
 
In addition to the conventional thermal mass, Phase-Change Materials were also tested. PCMs are a 
novel approach to temperature management in buildings. They are materials with a melting point 
around room temperature which use their ability to absorb latent heat during melting and release 
heat during freezing to dampen temperatures fluctuations in a room. Although used extensively in 
the refrigeration industry, PCMs are currently not common in buildings, however there is no shortage 
of research on the matter. 
Among the two major types of PCMs, paraffins and salt hydrates, the latter category does not present 
a fire safety hazard. However, many have a lower freezing point, known as freezing point hysteresis, 
which requires the material to be supercooled to a few degrees below the melting point to solidify.  
The company Rubitherm in German produces a line of salt hydrate PCMs known as the SP line. Early 
simulations using the standard PCM available in the IDA ICE software, with principal melting and 
cooling point of 23 and 21(respective), revealed that in warmer weather these cooler temperatures 
could not be reach and the material remained in a liquid state. This meant that it was never fully 
utilized on the hottest days of the year. Its sister compound SP26E has a 25-27⁰C melting area and a 
congealing area of 25-24⁰C (Rubitherm, 2020) . Based on tests, this material had a temperature range 
which was well suited for preventing overheating the current context. At 180 kJ/kg for a temperature 
range of 17-32⁰C, this material has a very high specific heat capacity compared to its mass, which is 
advantageous for lightweight construction.  
As will later be discussed in the sensitivity analysis results, a layer of 2.5mm was revealed to have a 
higher thermal damping effect than a 60cm layer of concrete. Thus, Rubitherm SP26E was selected 
as one of the novel materials to be tested. However, being a material that is not currently packaged 
for use in construction projects, it was not included in the final optimized solution. 
 
Natural and Mechanical Ventilation  
 
Two natural ventilation strategies, conventional window opening and ventilated hatches, were 
weighed against a variable air volume (VAV) ventilation system which employs a heat recovery 
bypass to bring cool air from the outdoors and a “boost” mode which pulls additional cool air and 
exhausts excess heat.  The VAV system was compared alongside a constant air volume (CAV) system 
to determine its added value. 
The Ventilated Hatch, and the VAV system with a “boost mode” were novel proposals which required 
further development, found in section 4.1 ‘Step 1.5: ’, following the sensitivity analysis.

4.11. 
Design Methodology 
 
The methodology to arrive at an optimized design followed three-and-a-half steps: 
 
➢ Step 0: Scenario Analysis of the baseline model 
 
➢ Step 1: An initial sensitivity analysis to select the most promising interventions out of each 
category of intervention.  
 
➢ Step 1.5: A development half-step to refine key aspects of the ventilation strategy 
 
➢ Step 2: A Multi-objective optimization that selects the best solution from a series of strategies 
 
➢ Step 3: A final scenario analysis to validate the optimized solution  
 
4.12. 
Step 1: Sensitivity Analysis 
 
Nearly every solution to overheating comes with a tradeoff in terms of cost, energy efficiency or other 
IEQ measures such as daylight quality or the view out. Sensitivity analysis allows one to explore the 
marginal advantages and disadvantages of a design direction and thereby quickly explore the design 
space. The method comes with two major limitations: the interdependency of parameters, such as 
the interaction between the thermal mass and the cooling effect of window opening, is not fully 
explored, and the marginal changes in the parameter will not necessarily create a linear response. 
Phase Change Materials are a good example of both shortcomings. A PCM may not be fully utilized 
when exposed to milder conditions. In such cases, if the PCM does not change phase, it no longer has 
a thermal storage advantage through its latent heat of freezing/melting. This non-linear behavior can 
cause sudden state changes that exacerbate thermal comfort, which a sensitivity analysis will detect. 
Keeping the limitations in mind, the sensitivity analysis was carried out based on the test plan found 
in Appendix B. 
The results were first plotted in terms of their absolute effect in comparison to each other.  
Following this, in the two-factor analysis, the solutions were plotted in terms of the energy 
consumption and hours (or degree-hours) of overheating as compared to a baseline solution. With 
each parameter being varied independently. The variation about this baseline shows the sensitivity 
of these metrics to a marginal change in these parameters.

ensure the final output is as desired.

constraints which set hard limits on thermal comfort, daylight and air quality criteria.

4.3. Step 3: Validation of Results using different scenarios 
 
For the final scenario analysis to validate results, setpoints were adjusted to fit the future weather 
conditions of 2080. 
This included increasing the Boost Ventilation maximum flow to 60L/s, and increasing the ΔT (indoor

requirements while offering significant indoor temperature improvements needed in 2080. The 
control strategy for the blinds was amended to fully close when residents were away on the hottest 
days. 
With the adjustment, however, the simulation failed to achieve the category III limitation with 3 
hours outside CAT II, prompting the addition of solar control glazing. This addition brought the 
simulation well within the thermal comfort requirements with 0 hours outside category II and a mere

The graph in Figure 24 shows the relative effects each measure has on the baseline model. Solar 
control measures and natural ventilation predictably had the greatest improvements on thermal 
comfort. Solar control glazing (low G-value) prevented overheating at a lower cost for energy, 
however it was not as selective as the exterior blinds and exterior screen shades. Selective window 
shading, which employed summertime schedules, had a high potential to reduce overheating hours, 
between 150-350 hours/year, with a near-zero impact on energy. 
Nearly all thermal mass solutions resided in Q3 and were “ideal” solutions but were modest in their 
effects on both energy and comfort. Thus, cost became the key factor in determining the marginal 
cost-benefit of adding mass. However, as it will later be discussed, additional thermal mass had a 
greater thermal response at more extreme temperatures than the other measures, providing a 
safeguard against these extremes. 
The insulation and CAV ventilation showed a high impact on energy for a modest change in thermal 
comfort. The VAV implementation which uses a boost mode of 45L/s to remove excess heat on the 
hottest days outperformed the CAV system per kWh by 80 hours/year on average. It also showed 
nearly the same cooling performance of a 45L/s CAV system for 360 kWh/year less. 
 
Ventilation 
 
 Prior to implementing the VAV boost mode, the baseline house suffered from chronic overheating 
on the hottest days. Using the VAV system to draw out the accumulated warm air on the worst days 
effectively eliminated this problem at a modest cost to energy. Further increasing the boost rate, 
however, carries a high energy cost and must be used as a last resort to prevent overheating. 
Increasing the VAV boost rate from 35L/s to 45L/s showed a similar marginal cost-benefit to 
reducing the insulation. It was found this could be mitigated by changing the (indoor/outdoor) air 
temperature gradient at which the VAV Boost is activated. Boosting the night ventilation during the 
coolest hours of the summer nights maximizes the cooling effect per kWh of energy spent. The 
sensitivity analysis also highlights the Specific Fan Power of the ventilation system as a key area to 
improve. For the baseline model an SFP of 1.0 kW/(m3/s) was used. The highest allowable baseline 
SFP for a residential ventilation system is 1.2 kW/(m3/s). As the AHU heat exchanger is bypassed 
during cooling boost mode, there is opportunity to entirely bypass the ducts which bring air to the 
AHU and reducing the system pressure losses.

Shading overhang: 
The addition of a shading overhang was necessary for the baseline model to fulfill the stringent 
adaptive thermal comfort requirements of zero hours outside of Category II in the year 2020, without 
user intervention. Without the overhang, the apartment reached Category IV thermal comfort. 
Window opening during the daytime while away from the apartment and nighttime ventilation 
became necessary to ensure overheating did not occur. 
The addition of a shading overhang on the south facade reduced the hours of unmet cooling (>24⁰C) 
by a minimum of 58 hours/year for a shading structure of at 2.45 m above the floor level and 1 m in 
depth. Lowering the shade by 15 cm or lengthening had a 35% improvement in this number while 
extending the overhang depth to 1.3m doubled the effect on unmet cooling hours.  
The bottom of the 2-meter-high windows in the living room was not sufficiently shaded by the 
overhang, accounting for performance. This is evidenced by the improvement in performance 
achieved by the overhang in the bedroom, the reduction in unmet cooling hours is double in most 
instances. 
In 2050 and 2080 the effectiveness of the overhang in reducing overheating remains constant in the 
living room, while in the bedroom it increases further. This shows that an overhang is most effective 
when the entire height of the window is shaded at high sun angles. 
 
Mechanical Ventilation 
Using the heat exchanger bypass, Ventilation rates of 35L/s (0.7L/s-m2), which corresponds to the 
minimum boost ventilation, were very effective in reducing the risk of overheating throughout the 
year, with 1 hour outside of category II and none outside of category III thermal comfort. This 
translated to 50 fewer hours/year above 24⁰C as compared to 25L/s (0.5 L/s-m2). Ventilative cooling 
saw the largest improvement by -83 hours/year above 24⁰C between 45L/s (0.9 L/s-m2) and 60L/s 
(1.2 L/s-m2).  
The advantages of increasing the ventilation rates compounded in 2050, doubling the relative 
reduction in unmet cooling hours. By 2080 the advantage of ventilative cooling is in preventing 
extremes with a 2-times reduction in weighted degree-hours (>24⁰C) for 60L/s ventilation rate in 
2080 as compared to 2050. 
However, this came at a proportionally higher cost for electrical energy. Mechanical ventilative 
cooling is shown to be effective, but a constant air volume system is inadequate because of high 
energy demand.

5.3. Multi-Objective Optimization (MOO) Parametric Runs 
 
Taking the results from the sensitivity analysis, an optimization was carried out combining the most 
effective solutions into a series of strategies. 
Some solutions deemed effective in the sensitivity analysis were excluded for reasons of practicality. 
The external screen shades, for instances, cannot be fully deployed if residents are home during the 
hottest days, rendering them ineffective. The thermal mass beyond 30mm in thickness is considered 
too heavy and would likely increase structural load requirements. Other strategies, which are 
affected by user choice, such as reducing internal gains or a white-painted colored exterior, is also 
outside of the scope. 
The Multi-objective optimization described in the methodology section was carried out based on the 
6 strategies identified in Appendix C. 
• 
Strategy 1: Passive Strategy with user night cooling and seasonal shading:  
Comprises a series of 48 parametric runs which combined overhang and external venetian 
blinds, the VAV Boost mode and the conventional thermal mass in the floors and walls. It is 
characterized by high user intervention. 
 
• 
Strategy 2: Passive Strategy with no exterior shades: 
Like Strategy 1, it relies on conventional passive strategies, but without relying on users to 
activate exterior venetian blinds seasonal, instead relying on a larger and lower overhang 
optimized for thermal comfort. 
 
• 
Strategy 3: Solar Control Glazing 
Employs solar control glazing but excludes the overhang while varying the use of 
conventional thermal mass and external venetian blinds 
 
• 
Strategy 4: Active Cooling 
 This is meant as a comparison with the passive measures. It relies entirely on active cooling 
with no user intervention for shading or night cooling, instead relying on either an overhang 
or solar control glazing.  
 
• 
Strategy 5: Phase-Change Material 
Follows strategy one, but employs 2.5mm of phase change material within the interior walls 
and under the floor finish.   
 
• 
Strategy 6: Ventilated Hatch Automation 
Employs a ventilation hatch with an automated louver system, in addition to conventional 
shading strategies and thermal mass. 
 
The aggregated results from the optimization runs, in Figure 30, show strategies 6, 5 and 1 are the 
most successful. Other strategies were excluded as they did not meet the minimum constraints of 
fewer than 3 hours outside of category II.

6. Discussion 
 
This section reflects on the process of assessing the many interventions tested in the sensitivity 
analysis, developing the optimized solution and further improvements, and whether the initial 
objectives were achieved.  
 
6.1. Reflection on baseline model development: 
 
The multi-objective optimization methodology presented in the paper is intended to offer designers 
a method of understanding the magnitude of the overheating problem faced by modular NZE 
dwellings, identify effective thermal control strategies, and quickly explore the solution space. 
Ultimately it must arrive at the most cost-efficient strategy which meets target IEQ performance 
requirements. 
This paper attempted to offer highly relevant and actionable results that could be used to guide 
modular apartment design and inform future research.  For this reason, much emphasis was placed 
on ensuring that the many assumptions that comprise a building simulation were justifiable and as 
close to reality as possible. This presented an enormous challenge because of the sheer number of 
assumptions about occupant behavior, construction detailing, HVAC system setpoints and, future 
climate. 
The choice of modelling Domino house, as outlined in the methodology section, was intended to as 
simple case study with a limited number of indoor climate zones. Small modular apartments were 
identified as valuable case because their high occupancy per square meter make them more prone to 
overheating. Selecting the worst-case scenario with a south orientation and a couple living in with a 
modest floor area reflects a common living situation which is infrequently studied.  
Modular constructor Scandibyg offered critical real-world information related to the construction of 
a modular building. The building cross section and the floor plan dimensions thus developed from 
the BIM model data from design of Lundtoftevej 162 created high-confidence in the robustness of the 
building physics and the available thermal mass. It also validated the earlier statements that modular 
buildings do indeed employ light-weight constructions.  
On developing the model, very little real-world data was found on occupant behavior. Many of the 
assumptions like internal heat gains and ventilation rates, are based on units per floor area. As such, 
the internal heat gains may vary widely. A conservative approach was taken in selecting the 4.2 
kWh/m2 which was ultimately used in the final optimization. 
User behavior related to window opening is also a problematic area. A number of studies described 
in the literature review attempted to develop a stochastic model which predicts window opening 
based measurable factors. Because the models need to be run many times for the results to converge, 
this method was not practicable for an optimization with thousands of potential outcomes. A 
deterministic model was thus used which approximated user behavior based on the data. Further 
==PAGE:74==
72 
 
work would be needed to validate this. The potential for inaccuracy, however, was mitigated by 
ensuring that indoor air quality was sufficiently high and indoor temperatures comfortable during 
the heating season to avoid the need for window opening. 
 
6.2. Reflection on Step 0: future climate sensitivity of the baseline model 
 
The initial scenario analysis of the Baseline file revealed the extend of overheating in 2050 and 2080. 
With hours in category III and IV, home becomes uninhabitable for 29 and 67 hours out of the year 
without any modifications. This highlights the extent of the change and the degree to which the future 
climate will require major changes in the way we design. 
 
6.3. Reflection on Step 1: Sensitivity Analysis 
 
The sensitivity analysis was used to great effect in guiding the initial stages of the design. The method 
quickly identified the tradeoffs between the multiple objectives of energy, cost, cooling potential and 
the impacts on daylight.  
However, the limitations of the analysis must be well understood. Some key findings were: 
• 
Results are highly dependent on the choice of the baseline 
• 
The effects of a parameter can be non-linear in natural, creating sudden changes at certain 
threshold values. This was observed in the case of the phase change materials and more 
generally in the thermal mass sensitivity analysis 
• 
Comparing of the effects of gradual change in a parameter to the deployment of an 
intervention, such as a shading device was a flawed approach. For this reason, the two-factor 
analysis was much more informative. 
• 
The analysis is not capable of capturing the effects of varying more than 2 parameters. This 
was indeed the main motivation behind the multi-objective parametric optimization. 
• 
The interventions needed to be understood in terms of their combined effects. While some 
interventions such as solar shading had diminishing returns when added together, combining 
them with other categories had a multiplying effect. This was recognized in the case of the 
thermal mass which needed to be combined with a heat dissipating strategy such as 
ventilation to achieve the benefits. 
• 
The metrics found to be most useful in quantifying the comfort in the space were the hours 
of unmet cooling, corresponding to the number of occupied hours above the 24⁰C setpoint, 
and the weighted degree-hours above 24⁰C metric which adds more importance to the 
extreme temperatures.  
• 
By contrast, the number of hours in each adaptive thermal comfort category were not as 
useful because they lack granularity, only changing due to large variations in thermal comfort. 
Unlike the former two metrics, the response is also non-linear and highly dependent on the 
baseline choice. 
==PAGE:75==
73 
 
 
 
Some important findings during the sensitivity analysis: 
 
- 
Reducing thermal gains from household appliances and lighting had the double advantage of 
saving energy and preventing overheating. The indiscriminate production of heat during 
summer and winter was a major contributor to overheating; however, it was found that little 
could be done to limit this heat source beside ventilation and encouraging the use of energy 
efficient appliances.  
- 
The Albedo effect of painting surfaces white reduced overheating while incurring a minor 
penalty in winter heating demand but the effect was marginal. This was attributed to the high 
level of insulation which reduced the effects of heat gain through the envelope. Especially in 
the summer, when the change of temperature (ΔT) between indoors and outdoors was no 
greater than 5 K.  
- 
The Air Handling Unit played a surprisingly large role in keeping the building cool during the 
summer, when optimized. Prior to this optimization, overheating was difficult to control in 
the middle of the day when residents were not home, requiring the windows in each room to 
be opened slightly to keep the house within Category II. It also required residents to fully 
open the windows when they arrived home. This user intervention is not ideal, especially in 
houses with elderly or limited-mobility residents. 
 
Thermal mass effectiveness: 
 
According to (Ponechal, 2015), the time necessary to release heat from a material, known as thermal 
lag, depends on its heat capacity, conductivity, and thickness of the mass. Furthermore, the effective 
thermal mass thickness in a diurnal cycle can be 100-150mm for each surface of a mass which is 
exposed. As such, the maximum thermal mass depth of 60mm explored in the dwelling, would have 
been fully utilized. The key finding on thermal mass were that beyond a layer depth of 1.5-3cm 
diminishing returns could be found. The same was found for the tested Phase-change materials 
Rubitherm SP26E beyond a thickness of 2.5mm.  
Spreading the mass over a large internal area was more effective at activating the mass quickly to 
dampen temperature increases. The concrete, with a higher conductivity of 1.7 W/(m K), fared better 
than Gypsum with a conductivity of nearly one-tenth that of concrete. Despite having a similar 
thermal mass on a volumetric basis, the insulating properties of gypsum limited the availability of 
the mass. 
On a volume and weight basis, Rubitherm SP26E PCM outperformed conventional thermal mass 
options, but as was noted earlier, the product is not widely available on the market for construction 
application, with one known brand “Rigips”, however, this paper demonstrates that it may be a viable 
option for modular buildings. 
==PAGE:76==
74 
 
The combined effects of thermal mass and ventilation were validated by Figure 23 which showed 
that in addition to thermal comfort improvements, thermal mass also imparted energy savings.  This 
was explained by the mass’ ability to store thermal energy during the daytime and release it during 
the night. During the shoulder seasons, it was found that because of the low thermal mass of the 
original baseline model the building was releasing heat through the heat recovery ventilation bypass 
to avoid overheating, thus wasting it 
thermal mass most effective in lowering the radiative temperature, rather than keeping air 
temperatures low. For this reason, the work-from-home scenario in the final scenario analysis in 
section 4.3 revealed that 24-hour occupancy may require more thermal mass as ventilative cooling 
during the day raises the temperature of the thermal mass. An effect which was not present when the 
rooms was shut. 
 
Effectiveness of Phase Change Materials (PCM): 
 
Phase change materials seem to show good promise in terms of cost effectiveness and controlling 
overheating at a layer thickness of 2.5mm. Placing the PCM in the internal walls showed the highest 
effectiveness per quantity employed, followed by the floor and, lastly, the external wall; However, 
doubling the PCM in the internal walls is less effective than placing this additional material in the 
floors and external walls. In effect, beyond the PCM layer thickness of 2.5mm, the energy transfer is 
mostly limited by the surface area available, and less by the thickness of thermal mass employed. 
It should be noted that the method of encapsulating the PCM will have a significant effect on the 
surface area and thus the effectiveness of the material. Placing the PCM closer to the surface of the 
wall is also crucial, as the finishing material may prevent the transfer of thermal energy. 
 
6.4. Reflection on Step 2: MOO 
 
The multi-objective optimization (MOO) methodology was found to be a flexible approach that can 
be adapted based on, investment strategy, future energy cost, future climate scenarios and target IEQ 
objectives set by designers. As described in depth in the methodology section. The focus of the MOO 
analysis was on selecting objectives which were easy to interpret designers.  Previous studies such 
as (Naji,2020) and (Ascione,2021) used objectives which were no accessible to ordinary designers.  
In contrast the optimization in the paper used basic financial metrics to develop lifecycle cost. The 
use of the Cost of Conserved energy as a ‘conversion’ between Energy usage and cost is simple, but 
also allows designers to quickly explore future energy cost scenarios to determine the financial 
viability of an investment if energy climbed steeply, as they have in early 2022.  
For the IEQ metric which were not part of the ordinary decision making of investors, the 
implementation of comfort limits allows designers to select a “standard” of comfort that they should 
guarantee in the future climate scenario.  
==PAGE:77==
75 
 
The adaptive thermal comfort limit of 2 hours outside of category 2 was a practical decision that was 
made when it was found that achieving 100% was no feasible. Had designers been faced with the 
choice of selecting between cost and thermal comfort, there is no framework for understanding the 
tradeoffs.  
Another conclusion is that the MOO analysis and the subsequent scenario analysis showed that the 
objectives selection can have a very significant impact on the chosen optimal solutions which is why 
fully understanding the objective functions and their effects are critical. 
A cost of conserved energy of 0.07 EUR/kWh, for instance, may yield results which value upfront cost 
and discounted energy costs on an even playing field, but from an environmental perspective, the 
carbon impact of such a decision may be suboptimal.  
Indoor thermal comfort metrics are also very sensitive as it was difficult to identify a single robust 
metric. Constraining the hours outside of category II, for instance, is a stringent criterion to ensure a 
safe indoor environment irrespective of the region or year of study, but the hard cut-off devalued 
thermal comfort in the ordinary comfort ranges represented by Category 1 thermal comfort. The 
“weighted degree-hours” metric better captured the day-to-day ability of a home to maintain comfort 
levels. 
Attempts were made to create a hybrid metric which calculated the degree hours outside of category 
1, but implementation in the software ran up against time constraints. This strategy, however, may 
have yielded a more forgiving metric which made allowances for extreme weather, but kept a 
stringent indoor temperature. 
 
Strategy 1: conventional thermal mass and solar control 
 
The case of strategy 1 is highlighted to elaborate on the findings from the MOO.  
The first strategy optimized for 9 initial parameters focused on solar control, thermal mass and night 
cooling. Notably absence was the use of PCMs, or a ventilated hatch. Setpoints for the VAV system’s 
cooling-boost ventilation, and the heat exchanger bypass temperatures were also optimized in the 
same run. The climate weather file was set to 2080 to test for the worst-case thermal comfort 
conditions. In nearly all runs, overcooling occurred during a period in august with unseasonably cool 
temperatures. This was eliminated in future optimizations by increasing the setpoint in august to 
22⁰C, thus penalizing the overcooling. 
In the final optimization, the winning solution with the lowest lifecycle cost did not require the use 
of the overhang or solar control glazing and instead relied on external venetian blinds. The demand 
control ventilation (VAV) boost was minimized to 45L/s, very close to the 35L/s minimum 
requirement set by the BR18 standard to ensure adequate extraction from the kitchen and bathroom. 
The thermal mass parameters for the wall and floor of the apartment were maximized, showing that 
they were necessary to ensure thermal comfort without the solar control glazing.

6.5. Scenario analysis of final results: 
 
The energy and cost performance comparison revealed that the active cooling strategy had a lower 
upfront cost but failed to reach the 27 kWh/m2 standard for an NZEB building from the year 2020. 
Following this, energy demand increased further for the active cooling while strategy 6 decreased its 
energy usage. This reveals the ballooning cost of relying on active cooling. Further still the use of 
active cooling may reduce the occupants’ awareness of the link between direct solar radiation and 
thermal gain, rendering them more complacent and less likely to draw curtains or shade. 
The difference in energy usage by 2080 reaches 600kWh, or half the budget of an NZEB of this size. 
All of this will contribute to heat island, the cumulative effect of which is well known to cause 
problems in urban areas. 
 
Work-from-home schedule: 
 
Work-from-home presents a particular challenge to thermal comfort as occupants are present in the 
home during the hottest hours of the day and must rely on natural ventilation to cool down. It is 
assumed that windows and hatches are opened above 23⁰C. During the hottest days, when residents 
are away, the heat recovery works to keep the incoming air cool, but when residents must rely on 
natural ventilation the cool air is lost, and the building accumulates thermal energy quickly.  
As a result, the building optimized for residents working away from home fails the thermal comfort 
criteria. A subsequent optimization of the hatches and setpoints failed to control the large 
temperature swings, suggesting that the thermal mass level must be increased further for 24-hour 
occupancy if Category II thermal comfort is to be maintained.

7. Conclusion 
 
The analysis of the Domino apartment ‘dwelling type 4’ developed by Scandibyg revealed the 
absolute necessity to look beyond present-day indoor climate performance to assess the suitability 
of new constructions. Using historical weather data is no longer a viable strategy to design building 
that will be in operation until 2080 and beyond. 
In the objectives, the question was asked: “Will mechanical cooling be required from 2050 under the 
future climate scenarios, or will other strategies be more cost efficient?”. The paper demonstrates 
that options do exist; however, a robust whole-building strategy is necessary which accounts for 
solar control, generous and consistent ventilative cooling, and an adequate thermal mass.  
The key problem is the ability of a building to limit thermal gains, absorb them without exceeding 
comfort limits and efficiently exhaust these excess gains. The Danish climate is and will likely remain 
suitable for night cooling into 2050 and even 2080, but with increasing difficult. 
Key findings from the project have been: 
• 
The multi-objective optimization (MOO) methodology employed in this paper was found to 
be effective at arriving at an optimal solution from a range of viable options. It was also shown 
to be a flexible approach that can be adapted based on: 
o Investment strategy 
o Future energy cost 
o Future climate scenarios 
o Target IEQ objectives set by designers 
 
• 
As peak summer daytime temperatures increase, 24-hour occupancy is a critical scenario for 
which to design. The external shades cannot be fully closed and the use of ventilative cooling 
by occupant during the peak hours causes the thermal mass to heat up more quickly, thus 
increasing operative temperature in the zone.  
 
• 
A VAV ventilation systems with a heat exchanger bypass and low-pressure losses was shown 
to be effective in controlling the worst overheating in the absence of user intervention. The 
optimization revealed that pressure losses within the ventilation system were a large source 
of energy use in low-energy dwellings raising the need to focus energy-saving efforts on this. 
 
• 
Ventilated hatches with an automated damper system were a novel strategy that was shown 
to be robust in consistently cooling the apartment using night cooling. 
 
• 
The optimization revealed the need for dedicated and inexpensive and low-carbon thermal 
mass options for integration into lightweight constructions. Lightweight Phase-change 
materials (PCM) integrated into the internal surfaces showed future potential, but market 
availability is limited.

7.1. Additional Recommendation to Scandibyg 
 
 
Designing a building for thermal comfort requirements in 2050 or 2080 has immediate advantages 
for thermal comfort in 2020. Investors and landlords may not be willing to pay the initial premium 
for such installations if the risk of overheating will occur 20 years into the future. Nevertheless, while 
some systems may be installed in the future, other interventions, like the addition of thermal mass 
and ventilated hatches, are much more cost-effective to install during construction rather than 
retrofitted afterward. The shading and ventilation strategy of the building must be taken as integral 
to the heating, ventilation and cooling strategy of the building. This can be done by installing shading 
devices into the finished modules as part of the HVAC system. 
 
The key components or any strategy are the ability to ventilate, solar control, and thermal mass. 
Thermal mass was the weakest link because of the constraints imposed by modular construction. It 
is clear that in apartments that favor southern orientations, special provisions need to be made for 
solar control and thermal mass.  
 
Cross-ventilation was shown to be the most cost-effective strategy to cool an apartment like Domino 
if at least two sides are exposed with an unobstructed path for air movement between them. There 
is a high risk with manual strategies if, for instance, only windows on one side of the dwelling are not 
opened. In these cases, the benefits of ventilation are reduced substantially in the modelled 
apartment. The risk is higher when other considerations supersede thermal comfort, like street noise 
from one side of the apartment, driving rain and security concerns related to unattended windows. 
Windows may suffer from all these concerns which only can be mitigated to a limited degree. A well-
designed ventilation hatch can bypass all these concerns; integrated noise isolation can reduce 
transmission to the interior. Integrated louvers can provide protection from driving rain, wind gust 
effects and prevent unauthorized access.  
 
Automating the ventilated hatch opening was shown to maximize night cooling without incurring the 
risk of overcooling at night. An active damper system integrated into the hatch would only require 
the residents to open and close the hatch once a year; full closure of the hatch door provides maximal 
insulation and airtightness in winter. Such a system should be seen as an integral part of the HVAC 
system, creating a robust whole-building strategy to keep indoor temperatures comfortable.

7.2. Limitations 
 
Great care was taken to ensure that the results were reliable, and that the proposed solutions were 
actionable for industry practitioners, but it is important not to overstate their general applicability. 
The results of the study are relevant for near-zero energy residential buildings in northern climates. 
The conclusions drawn, in particular, from daylighting and solar control must take into account the 
south orientation of the building.  
- 
The model took the worst-case scenario to study the overheating in apartments such as 
two occupants in a one-bedroom apartment, southern glazing, an upper-story apartment 
with no shading from adjacent buildings or vegetation. 
- 
The open floor plan offered ample opportunity for cross ventilation, with two exposed 
facades 
Additional factors that contributed to the uncertainties were as follows: 
- 
A moderately severe climate scenario was selected, however this comes with the caveat 
of uncertainty relating to the IPCC’s HadCM3 climate models. The IPCC states that the 
Representative Concentration Pathways are equally likely, and no single scenario should 
be seen as more likely. 
- 
 The morphing methodology employed further adds to the uncertainties. 
- 
The average internal gains of 4.2W/m2 was major assumption with a large impact on the 
results. This assumption did not necessarily account for the behavior of two occupants. 
- 
User intervention such as window opening behavior was also difficult to model 
accurately.  
- 
Validation could not be carried out with a real-world example to validate the modelled 
temperatures and is entirely reliant on the software.

7.1. Future Development 
 
A robust ventilation strategy 
 
Beside orientation and reducing window size, implementing a robust ventilation strategy is the single 
most effective means to reduce overheating and avoid the need for active cooling by 2050 in Danish 
low-energy buildings. This paper demonstrated that ventilated hatches offer a means to ventilate 
without many of the downsides, while a variable ventilation system with a cooling boost mode 
reduced the risks in the absence of occupant intervention.  
Other strategies not explored in this paper included solutions to increase airflow to cool occupants 
directly. Ceiling fans are less common in Danish homes but offer effective localized cooling. Despite 
the restrictive dimensions of modular homes, a ceiling height of 2.5m is sufficient to install some 
ceiling fans.  
Additionally, it was found that many AHU systems with a bypass have in-built pressure losses which 
increase the specific fan power. As low-energy homes use less energy on heating, fan losses within 
the AHU itself are now a more significant contributor to yearly energy usage. Other solutions were 
explored such as shortening the bypass duct length or even installing fans directly into the ventilated 
hatches to bypass the VAV system entirely.  
 
Inexpensive thermal mass 
 
1.5 to 3 cm of thermal mass under the finished floor was shown to offer a significant cooling 
advantage as compared to thicker masses with diminishing returns for each additional cm. Spreading 
the mass more thinly and up to 3cm was shown to have the greatest effect. 
Phase change material also has a potential to retrofit the existing building stock with a low-weight 
thermal mass which can be integrated into the floor and wall underlayers. It was shown that small 
amounts of thermal mass of up to 5mm in average thickness, spread over a large interior surface area 
is sufficient to dampen the effects of summertime thermal gains and keep temperatures within 
reason.
