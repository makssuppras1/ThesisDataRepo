Abstract
This thesis is a product of a joint effort between DTU Health Tech and GN Audio/Jabra
to develop a bio-sensing headset. The main focus point as regards the device is the EEG
sensors it encompasses. The first and foremost objective of this study was the validation of
the signal quality of the Hugin headset, and the evaluation of its performance on standard
EEG protocols like eyes-closed invoked alpha rhythms, and auditory evoked responses,
comparable to an already established EEG platform. Various explorations led to hardware
updates and upgrades of the recorded signal. Following this, an experimental protocol
to detect cognitive fatigue and track attention through EEG signals was developed and
implemented. The intention was to quantify and evaluate cognitive changes that reflect the
generation of mental fatigue. For this purpose, two classic computer games were chosen
as fatigue-inducing factors and two other tests were opted to evaluate the cognitive state
before, in the middle, and at the end of the experiment. The results show that EEG
features reflect the underlying cognitive changes that different tasks provoke and thus
we can classify them. At the same time, the high inter-subject variability, and inherent
complexity of the cognition and neural activity showed that the experimental paradigm
was not appropriate for detecting cognitive fatigue or monitoring attention in the course of
each task. Finally, the results, although inconclusive, provided a lot of insights regarding
the improvement of the product’s design, as well as the limitations of an experiment as
such can entail.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Since its inception in 1924 by Hans Berger, electroencephalogram (EEG) trials on human
subjects have come a long way. Traditionally a research and medical diagnosis tool, but
due to recent advancements made in electrode design and user comfort this method of mea-
surement is opening up for more consumer-related applications. With the ever-evolving
digital landscape, the integration of biosensors in everyday products has changed various
aspects of everyday life in unprecedented ways, offering the opportunity to track one’s
performance, well-being, and safety.
This study is motivated by the multidimensional
applications of biosensor data, and particularly its employment to delve into the intri-
cate realms of cognitive fatigue and attention. From the demanding world of professional
gaming to the dynamic environment of call center operators, the need to comprehend and
optimize cognitive states becomes paramount.
Unveiling the potential of biosensor data for gamers extends beyond mere performance
metrics; it becomes a gateway to understanding the cognitive nuances that contribute
to excellence in competitive gaming.
Similarly, the application extends to call center
workers, where productivity and the quality of customer service are deeply intertwined
with cognitive well-being. Insights derived from biosensor data can illuminate the impact
of working conditions on cognitive states, fostering a healthier and more eﬀicient work
environment.
However, applications of such devices extend far beyond that. Especially in this day and
age, understanding how our brain is affected by the million simulations reaching us every
minute, and addressing potential concerns related to sedentary lifestyles and screen time
management can be extremely beneficial to our mental health.
Furthermore, extending the inquiry to front-line workers, particularly those involved in
safety-critical tasks, opens avenues to harness biosensor data for fatigue detection, thereby
bolstering road safety and overall job performance.
In the realm of education, our study can open new doors toward the use of biosensor
data for learning. By unraveling insights into attention patterns and focus, we hope to
contribute to personalized learning strategies, enhancing educational experiences for those
facing cognitive challenges and attention deficits.
In collaboration with GN Audio/ Jabra, this thesis aims to navigate through diverse
methods of detecting cognitive fatigue and cognitive attention using biosensing headset
and create machine-learning models that can classify and predict such states not only
in the controlled laboratory environment but ultimately in everyday life. This research
endeavors to advance existing knowledge by offering practical applications that resonate
with the complexities of our modern, technology-driven lives.

Theoretical Background
2.1
Brain Physiology
The brain, a complex organ orchestrating cognitive functions through an intricate network
of neurons, exhibits distinctive physiological activity. These specialized cells communicate
through a combination of chemical neurotransmitters and electrical gradients. The elec-
troencephalogram (EEG) captures these electrical gradients to provide insights into brain
activity. It is essential to note, however, that the electrical activity of an individual neuron
is too minute to be directly detected by scalp EEG. Instead, what is observed on EEG re-
flects a collective summation of activity from numerous neurons. EEG waveforms exhibit
diverse characteristics, including location, amplitude, frequency, morphology, continuity,
synchrony, symmetry, and reactivity[1]. While multiple parameters contribute to their
classification, the predominant method is based on frequency, with EEG waves named ac-
cording to Greek numerals representing different frequency ranges. This frequency-based
categorization mirrors the rhythmic patterns of electrical activity produced by neurons.
Each distinct frequency band captured in the EEG signal is associated with specific cog-
nitive states, offering a nuanced insight into the intricate relationship between neural
oscillations and brain function as shown in Table 2.1.

The alpha rhythm exhibits maximal distribution in the occipital regions, and it transitions
anteriorly with the onset of drowsiness[2]. It is more prominent when closing the eyes and
relaxing and is masked when opening the eyes. This effect, usually referred to as the
‘Berger effect’, will be investigated in more detail afterward.
Theta rhythms entail frequencies from 4 to 7 Hz that vary in amplitude and morphology
and are maximal in frontal and fronto-central areas. Their appearance is promoted by
emotions, focused concentration, and engagement in cognitive tasks, while it is usual to
be reinforced with hyperventilation, drowsiness, and sleep[2].
The cerebral cortex, an outer sheet of neural tissue enveloping the mammalian cerebrum, is
pivotal for cognitive functions. With up to six layers of nerve cells, this highly convoluted
”gray matter” constitutes about 80% of the brain’s mass. It has been anatomically divided
into four lobes—frontal, parietal, temporal, and occipital—marked by consistent sulci.
The cerebral cortex operates as an intricate network of distinct functional areas, each
intricately dedicated to specific tasks and sensory processing. Notably, the temporal lobe
emerges as a critical region, contributing significantly to auditory processing, memory
formation, and emotional regulation[3].
The temporal regions are of particular interest to this study, since the prototype headset
has embedded EEG sensors placed in the area around the ear, thus laying in the temporal

2.2
Physiological Measures
Changes in our cognitive state are reflected in changes in our body.
Body-related re-
sponses or physiological measures can be monitored continuously or discreetly in specified
intervals and correlated to mental workload. Some common physiological measures are
cardiovascular (e.g., heart rate variability and heart rate), ocular (e.g., pupil dilation, eye
movement measures), and measure of brain activity (e.g., electroencephalography mea-
sure). Each metric has its advantages and disadvantages depending on the measuring
environment, the cognitive change in question, and other factors[4].
2.2.1
EEG
As mentioned above, EEG is a measure of the brain’s spontaneous activity on the scalp,
and it can provide insights into not only the underlying physiological neural functioning
but also the reaction to any external visual, auditory, or tactile stimuli, as well as the
execution of real or imaginary tasks such as body movements or speech. The amplitude
of an EEG signal is about 100µV measured on the scalp and about 1 −2mV measured on
the surface of the brain[5].
In the standard EEG systems, like the OpenBCI Ultracortex, the scalp electrodes are
placed at standard positions according to the guideline of the International Federation
of Clinical Neurophysiology (IFCN)[6], an extension of the so-called ”international 10-
20 system”, which uses stable anatomical points of the skull like nasion (point between
forehead and nose) an inion (bump at the back of skull) and places the electrodes at 10%
or 20% of the total distances between them as depicted in Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: A-C: Placement of the standard electrodes of the 10–20-system. A: lateral
view, B: frontal view, C: from the top. (from Seeck et.al (2017)[6])
The voltage recorded by the EGG in each channel is the difference in electric potentials
between the electrode in question and a reference electrode. This analog electric signal in
each EEG channel is measured during very small time windows and saved in a digital form
(computer). Thus, each channel shows the change in time of the voltage in each location,
with a resolution dictated by the sampling frequency of the recording device. Thus, in
order to be able to accurately represent this rhythmic signal and detect any activity of
interest, the sampling rate has to be at least twice the value of the highest frequency
component following the Nyquist–Shannon sampling theorem to prevent aliasing.

Electrode impedance
The impedance of electrodes reflects the electrical resistance of the skin. An impedance
higher than 5 kOhms can introduce noise artifacts to the signal, however, any modern
EEG equipment has high-input impedances, and thus ideally electrode impedances up
to 10 kOhms are acceptable to preserve the biosignal’s originality. On the other end, a
contact impedance less than 1kOhms indicated a possible shortcut between neighboring
electrodes[7]. For this purpose, in systems with wet electrodes conductive paste with a
high concentration of NaCl is applied between each electrode and the scalp to create a
better connection. In our case, however, both the OpenBCI EEG system and the prototype
headset comprise dry electrodes. The characteristics of each device will be discussed later
in more detail, but it can be noted here that when recording with the prototype headset, it
was placed in its appropriate position in advance to create a sealed environment and allow
for sweat to develop on the interface of the skin and electrodes to improve conductivity
with its concentration in salt.
There are two different types of electrodes employed in EEG: passive and active. Passive
electrodes, considered the conventional EEG electrodes, convey voltage fluctuations to
the amplifier via a conductive wire. Despite being cost-effective, they necessitate skin
preparation to attain suﬀiciently low impedance. In contrast, active electrodes are passive
electrodes with an inherent buffer or amplifier, thus amplifying the EEG signal directly
at the scalp before transmitting it to the amplifier. While active electrodes come with a
higher cost, they provide superior signal quality[8].
Background noise and artifacts
EEG electrodes as previously discussed measure the summed electrical activity in a spec-
ified location and therefore are prone to pick up electrical activity from various sources
which results in the induction of artifacts in the signal. An artifact is characterized as
anything that does not reflect brain activity and can generally fall into one of the following
categories: physiologic, electric, or environmental in etiology.
Common physiologic artifacts in EEG include electrooculography (EOG), electromyogra-
phy (EMG), and electrocardiography (ECG).
The eye, alike other biological tissues works as an electrical dipole. Thus any movement
generates electrical potentials that are higher in amplitude (in mV ) than the potential of
brain activity (in µV ). Different eye movements generate deflections in the EEG signal
of different polarity in different electrode locations[2]. The blink artifact arises from the
eye’s electrical potential difference, originating from the positively charged cornea and
the negatively charged retina. When blinking occurs and the eyeball undergoes rotation,
this potential difference undergoes inversion, leading to the creation of a positive current
directed towards the scalp. This artifact is particularly noticeable in frontal channels near
the eyes, with amplitudes exceeding a hundred microvolts. In terms of frequency, it pre-
dominantly encompasses EEG delta and theta bands[9]. Similarly, lateral eye movements,
such as saccades, generate a current moving away from the eyeballs, directed toward the
sides of the head. This results in a box-shaped deflection with opposite polarity on each
side. While these artifacts are most pronounced in channels near the temples, their effects
extend to channels beyond these regions[9].
Muscle artifacts include high amplitude disturbances due to the movement of some part
of the body of the subject or muscles in the scalp and the face (frontalis and temporalis
muscles as depicted in Figure 2.2.
These artifacts have higher frequency components
than most EEG signals and can be removed with low-pass filtering, or higher amplitude
than normal EEG activity and can be removed with amplitude thresholding. Movement

Chewing and tongue artifacts are very prominent when they exist in an EEG recording.

2.3
Attention
A commonly used definition of attention is given in 1890 by the American Philosopher
and psychologist William James:
“It is the taking possession of the mind, in clear and vivid form, of one out of what seem
several simultaneously possible objects or trains of thought. Focalisation, concentration
of consciousness are of its essence. It implies withdrawal from some things in order to deal
effectively with others.”
This definition clearly illustrates the cornerstones of attention, that is its voluntary aspect,
its limited capacity, the selection of relevant information, and disregard of irrelevant in-
formation. The fields of psychology and cognitive neuroscience have extensively explored
various facets of attention. In different contexts, we characterize distinct types of atten-
tion based on the specific nature of the tasks employed for their investigation. Sustained
attention, also known as vigilance, involves the ability to maintain focus over an extended
period. Divided attention tasks assess individuals’ capacity to attend to multiple sources
of information simultaneously. Spatial attention pertains to the ability to concentrate on
a specific region within our environment and shift attention to other locations. Visual
attention and cognitive attention are additional aspects that contribute to the broader
understanding of how individuals engage with and process information in their surround-
ings. Temporal attention is directing one’s focus towards a specific point in time or a time
interval and is highly related to expectation and anticipation [11].
Attention can be classified into external and internal attention. Externally-oriented at-
tention is directed towards stimuli in the environment. This external attention can be
intentionally guided by task requirements in a top-down manner, such as when we con-
centrate on a specific spatial location or feature of sensory stimuli that is relevant to our
goals. Additionally, external attention can be involuntarily drawn to an object or event
in a bottom-up manner, even in the absence of any intentional goal to attend to them.
Internally-oriented attention, on the other hand, is directed away from external stimula-
tion and towards internal mental representations and thoughts. Instances of internally
directed cognition encompass activities like episodic memory retrieval, working memory,
planning, mental imagery, and mental calculation[12].
2.3.1
EEG as attention biomarker
EEG activity in the different frequency ranges has been associated with different types
of attention[11]. Spatial attention has been proven to modulate oscillatory alpha (and in
somatosensory tasks also beta) activity with the introduction of a spatial cue, signifying an
engaged process, by suppressing sensory distractors on the unattended side, or promoting
stimulus processing on the attended side, or both. In cases of selective attention, the focus
on a specific sensory modality leads to a modulation of alpha power in primary sensory
regions.

2.4
Fatigue
Fatigue has been one of the fundamental constructs in a great many research fields such
as cognitive psychology, exercise physiology, human factors and engineering, as well as
medical practice. However, despite the extent of the research that has been conducted
since the 1890s[4], there is yet no consensus for a standard definition of fatigue. In the
progressively multidisciplinary research of the last decades, it is an experimental notion,
a symptom (e.g., of chronic illnesses), a risk, a cause (e.g., impaired performance), and a
result (e.g., sleep deprivation or physical labor)[19]. Clinically, fatigue is best defined as
the physiological state of reduced mental or physical capability, and low alertness leading
to diﬀiculty in performing voluntary activities.
Its primary physiological and psychological symptoms encompass subjective sensations of
reduced energy and motivation, potentially coupled with physical debilitation. The idea
that fatigue consists of various distinct components is well-accepted in the literature. These
are mental, motor, metabolic, endocrine, sensory, and other physiological or environmental
components that make it distinct from drowsiness, or sleepiness, although it is often the
case that they are felt at the same time[20]. It is considered that the phenomenological
experience of mental or cognitive fatigue is universal. In contrast, performance effects
associated with these symptoms are not ubiquitous in the same way, as many studies have
proven that under fatiguing conditions, performance can either decrease, stay the same,
or occasionally improve with increased time spent on a task[4].
New techniques have been developed in an attempt to link the task characteristics and
person characteristics that lead to cognitive fatigue, as well as the underlying neural
mechanisms and the loci of cognitive fatigue effects[4].
Approaching it from an alternative standpoint, fatigue can arise from a lack of motiva-
tion to continue a task that is becoming increasingly less rewarding over time and/or
monotonous[21]. The isolation of mental fatigue from general fatigue is particularly cru-
cial in the context of intricate, high-risk tasks like piloting aircraft and operating vehi-
cles. These activities impose minimal physical demands but place substantial cognitive
burdens on human operators. The distinct nature of mental fatigue in these scenarios
significantly compromises performance and safety, leading to a spectrum of cognitive im-
pairments[20][22].
As discussed above, numerous complex, high, and low-risk tasks require minimal physical
stain but impose considerable mental effort. In the present work, we attempt to isolate
mental or cognitive fatigue from general fatigue and correlate it with task performance and
8

reaction time. Nevertheless, fatigue is a very complex and broad subject, and clinicians
and neuroscientists have diﬀiculty in establishing a ground truth in terms of specific phys-
iological processes. Therefore, cognitive fatigue is often defined by a subjective evaluation,
associated with various behavioral and physiological symptoms, like reduced alertness,
unwillingness to perform a task, or reduced mental capacity[23][24].
In view of the pressing practical need for an objective evaluation, estimation, and detection
of mental fatigue, in the current study, we measure cognitive fatigue as a state occurring
from a high mental workload. We aim for our study to be focused on inducing mental
fatigue through sustained effort in computer games, and differentiate it from drowsiness
or sleepiness.

2.5
Berger Effect
The ‘Berger effect’ refers to a phenomenon wherein the presence of alpha brainwave ac-
tivity becomes more prominent when individuals close their eyes, and it diminishes upon
reopening them[26]. As highlighted previously, the alpha rhythm, characterized by fre-
quencies between 8-12 Hz, is linked to a state of relaxation, meditation, and reduced
alertness. Closing the eyes reduces visual input, resulting in an increase in alpha activity.
Conversely, opening the eyes increases visual input, leading to a decrease in alpha activity.
The phenomenon takes its name after Hans Berger who initially discovered it, and since
then, it has been extensively researched and proven to be a consistent indicator of shifts in
brain activity. Despite extensive study, the precise mechanisms driving the Berger effect
remain incompletely understood hypothesizing that there are both linear and non-linear
mechanisms that generate it.

2.6
Auditory Steady-State Response
All sounds vary in terms of frequency and amplitude over time, thus making time a corner-
stone in the context of hearing. When dealing with intricate natural sounds like speech
or music, they can be decomposed into two components: a slowly changing ‘envelope’
and a rapidly oscillating ‘fine structure’. Between the two, the envelope holds a greater
significance in understanding speech as temporal cues have been proven to help in distin-
guishing phonemes, syllables, and words[27][28]. Auditory temporal resolution is defined
as the ability to detect small and sudden alternations in sound stimuli and is important
for both verbal and non-verbal stimuli, as well as in the perception of music, rhythm,
and periodicity[29]. It is closely connected to the envelope of a sound stimulus because
the envelope represents the slower, modulating changes in the amplitude (loudness) of the
sound over time.
The Auditory Steady-State Response (ASSR) is an electrophysiological measurement used
in audiology and neurophysiology to asses the auditory system’s response to periodic au-
ditory stimuli, typically in the form of amplitude-modulated sounds at specific frequen-
cies[30]. In practice, a carrier tone (often 1000Hz) is amplitude modulated with a frequency
that we would like to detect. The 40 Hz amplitude modulation evokes ASSR that origi-
nate in the primary auditory cortex and has a higher signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) when the
subject is awake[31]. In other cases, different, close to 40 values are picked. One example
is 37 and 43 [32]. Traditionally, this measure is used clinically to estimate the hearing
thresholds in infants or subjects for whom is diﬀicult or impossible to communicate what
they hear[33][31].

2.7.1
Filtering
EEG recordings are often contaminated with various artifacts and unwanted signals, ne-
cessitating the use of signal processing techniques. Filters play a crucial role in EEG signal
processing, enabling researchers to enhance signal quality, remove noise, and extract rel-
evant information from the data. FIR, or Finite Impulse Response, filters are a class of
digital filters widely used in signal processing. Unlike IIR (Infinite Impulse Response)
filters, FIR filters only consider a finite number of past input samples to compute the
current output. The impulse response of an FIR filter is finite in duration, making it
inherently stable and with desirable characteristics for certain applications. Their linear
phase response is particularly valuable in preserving the temporal relationships of neural
signals, making FIR filters a popular choice in neuroscience applications[34].
In the current study, we used MNE in the context of EEG analysis, a widely used software
package that provides comprehensive tools for processing and analyzing neuroimaging
data. Their characteristics and specifications are illustrated below in more detail.
Band-pass
The band-pass filter applied to the raw data is designed to selectively allow frequencies
within a specified range, in this case, from 1 to 35 Hz, to pass through while attenuating
frequencies outside this range. It employs a one-pass, zero-phase, non-causal bandpass
filter design using the firwin method with a Hamming window. The filter specifications
include a passband edge from 1.00 to 35.00 Hz, a lower transition bandwidth of 1.00 Hz,
and an upper transition bandwidth of 8.75 Hz. The resulting filter has a length of 825
samples (3.3 seconds). The Hamming window introduces a passband ripple of 0.0194 and
achieves a stopband attenuation of 53 dB, ensuring effective frequency discrimination and
minimizing phase distortion. This band-pass filter is well-suited for isolating frequency

Filter characteristics
In the context of digital signal processing, the application of a one-pass, zero-phase, non-
causal filter involves several key characteristics designed to optimize the filtering pro-
cess[34]. The term ”one-pass” signifies that the filter processes the data in a single iter-
ation, contributing to a reduction in computational complexity and enhanced processing
speed. Additionally, the filter is zero-phase, indicating that it is applied twice—once in
the forward direction and once in the backward direction—to eliminate any phase shift
introduced during the filtering operation. This zero-phase attribute is crucial for main-
taining the integrity of the signal’s phase relationships, particularly in applications where
phase distortion is undesirable.
Furthermore, the filter is non-causal, leveraging future samples during the filtering process.
This non-causal nature allows for a more pronounced transition between the passband and
the stopband, ultimately contributing to an improved frequency response. The advantages
inherent in this one-pass, zero-phase, non-causal filter configuration include the sharper
transition between frequency bands, resulting in a superior frequency response.

In consideration of the inherent constant variation in EEG signals attributed to sponta-
neous voltage fluctuations, it is customary to designate a pre-stimulus period during which
subjects are assumed to be in a state of relaxation, reflecting a baseline characterized by
normal neural activity. The baseline activity is subsequently averaged, and the result-
ing profile is subtracted from the overall recording. This procedural step is particularly
prevalent in Event-related Potential (ERP) analysis, where a specific time window pre-

The rationale behind this practice is to isolate and emphasize signal deflections pertinent

Theoretically, the adoption of an extended reference

2.7.3
Power Spectral Density Estimation
Spectral density estimation plays a crucial role in any signal processing application since it
offers insights into the distribution of the frequency components of a signal. In its essence,
it requires a transformation of the EEG time series from the time domain to the frequency
domain. For this purpose, the PSD is estimated by using a discrete Fourier Transform
(DFT)[37] to extract information regarding the power of each frequency component. This
estimation depends on multiple parameters like the type and the length of the window
used, the percentage of overlap between the windows, and the number of DFT points, and
the choice of each parameter can impact the final result. In this work, we utilized two
common techniques of estimating the power spectra, namely the Welch and the Multitaper
method.
Welch Method
The basic idea behind the Welch method[38] is to use a moving window and calculate the
FFT in each window. Then the periodogram for each segment is calculated by taking
the squared magnitude of the FFT output and the final estimate of the PSD is obtained
by averaging the periodograms over all the windows. The key feature of this method is
the segmentation of the signal into overlapping segments of a specified length and the
application of a window function in the time domain. The Hanning window that was used
in the current study is a usual choice that offers a good frequency resolution with reduced
spectral leakage.
The number N of FFT points is conventionally set to the power of 2 that is next above
the length of each segment for an eﬀicient and faster implementation of the algorithm.
Reducing the window size in spectral analysis leads to an increased number of windows
into which the data is segmented.
This augmented segmentation proves beneficial for
achieving smoother Power Spectral Density (PSD) estimates by averaging out the random
effects of noise. However, the trade-off associated with a smaller window size, determined
by the parameter N, is a compromised frequency resolution. As the distance between two
frequency points increases, there is a consequent decrease in frequency resolution, given
the relationship freq_res = fs
N .
By subsequently averaging these estimates, Welch’s method reduces the variance of in-
dividual power measurements. This provides a compromise between high-frequency res-
olution and reduced noise, making it suitable for analyzing signals with non-stationary
characteristics like the ones encountered in EEG signals[39].

Multitaper Method
The Multitaper Method, introduced by Thomson [40], offers an alternative approach to
Power Spectral Density (PSD) estimation. It is an improvement over conventional methods
such as the standard periodogram spectrum and Welch’s method by concurrently reducing
noise in estimated power spectra while preserving high-frequency resolution. Unlike other
methods employing a single data taper or spectral window, the Multitaper Method em-
ploys multiple orthogonal tapers to estimate the power spectrum of a signal. These tapers
are Slepian discrete prolate spheroidal sequences (DPSS)[41], chosen for effectively min-
imizing spectral leakage and concentrating power within narrow bands[42]. The process
involves computing a set of independent power spectrum estimates, where each data taper
is multiplied element-wise by the signal, generating a windowed trial for power estimation
at each component frequency. The pairwise orthogonality of each taper ensures that the
window functions are uncorrelated.
The final spectrum is then obtained by averaging
over all the tapered spectra, resulting in a more robust and stable estimate with reduced
variance.
The number K of tapers along with their exact bandwidth represents the classic trade-
off between spectral resolution and stability of the estimate. The Multitaper Method is
capable of providing estimates for both the singular components, referred to as the ”lines,”
and the continuous component, known as the ”background,” within the spectrum. After
calculating the tapers for a specified frequency bandwidth, the overall power spectrum
is obtained by averaging the individual spectra derived from each tapered version of the
dataset. The high-resolution multitaper spectrum is then derived as a simple weighted
sum of the K eigenspectra.
Comparison
Both methods aim to estimate the PSD, but they differ in their approaches. The Welch
Method emphasizes overlapping segments and windowing, providing a trade-off between
frequency resolution and reduced variance, and is advantageous in its simplicity. However,
it offers a reduced frequency resolution when shorter overlapping segments are chosen and
it is very sensitive to the window choice, possibly resulting in the introduction of artifacts.
The Multitaper Method, on the other hand, provides high-frequency resolution in the
spectral estimates and a reduced variance for more stable and accurate results. Addition-
ally, the use of orthogonal tapers allows for uncorrelated window functions, minimizing
spectral leakage. Nevertheless, it is a technique that contains a lot of complexity and
computational intensity in the process of computing the multiple tapers and selecting the
optimal bandwidth for optimal performance.
The choice between the Welch and Multitaper methods depends on the characteristics of
the signal and the specific requirements of the analysis. The Welch Method is commonly
used for its simplicity, while the Multitaper Method is favored in scenarios where higher
precision and reduced variance are crucial since this classic trade-off is balanced at the
expense of computational load.

Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA) is a popular machine learning and dimensionality

• the values in every feature is normally distributed
• the data is linearly separable
• the covariance matrices are the same for the classes
It finds the inter and intra class variance and tries to maximize the inter class variance,
and minimize the intra class variance by projecting the data into a lower dimension by
finding the discriminant features. A new datapoint is transformed to this space using the
found transformation matrix. Then, in this new feature space the algorithm predict the
score of a new datapoint being in a class using the Bayes theorem 2.1.

3.1.2
State of the art
In section 2, the nature of mental fatigue and how it is associated with our attentional
resources was introduced. Furthermore, the changes in the brain during different cognitive
states were discussed to evaluate the role of EEG as a biomarker of mental fatigue and
attention.
Throughout the years, there has been extensive research involving mental fatigue and
cognitive attention employing a plethora of approaches and methodologies regarding de-
tection. Here, we delve more into the current state-of-the-art using EEG for cognitive
fatigue detection and assessment guided by a systematic review with meta-analysis pub-
lished in 2020 [45], which assesses the validity and reliability of EEG spectral bands as
an indicator of the onset of cognitive fatigue. In this review, the change in the four main
EEG bands, along with the change’s size and direction are explored in detail. The results
they reached were the following: δ activity change has a moderate non-significant effect
size (change in 9 of the 21 studies), θ activity change has a large significant effect size
(change in 19 of the 21 studies), α activity change has a moderate to large significant effect
size (change in all 21 studies), and β activity change has a small non-significant effect size
(change in 12 of the 21 studies). However, the high heterogeneity that was found for the
overall EEG activity implies varied populations, that is variation in methodology, and
thus further meta-analysis is required. A sub-group meta-analysis by brain region showed
that the change in θ activity was large in frontal, central, and posterior sites, while for α
the change was moderate to large in central and posterior sites, and moderate in frontal.
There is no mention of temporal sites in this meta-analysis. A sub-group meta-analysis
by type of fatiguing task divided the studies into two groups, driving (simulated or real,
including simulated flying) and cognitive tasks. The results showed that for both θ and
α activity there were no significant differences between the two groups.
Additionally,
they hypothesized that inconclusive outcomes in δ activity might be due to ocular arti-
facts, which often fall within the same frequency range especially when recorded from the
frontal region. Regarding the variability in the changes in β activity, since beta activity
is believed to coincide with heightened cortical arousal linked to increased mental engage-
ment or effort, elevated beta during simulated driving/flying tasks may be connected to
the exertion of mental effort to maintain vigilance [47]. Conversely, the reported decrease
in beta activity may be a consequence of cognitive fatigue.
Only in 2 out of the 21 In studies[48][49], there was a decrease instead of an increase found
in the θ activity, but the justification is not evident. In the former [48] a monotonous
driving session was assessed through the four frequency EEG bands: δ, θ, α, and β, along
with four other algorithms - combinations of the frequency bands: (θ + α)/β, α/β, (θ +

Materials and Methods
In this study, our research endeavors revolve around two principal aims. The primary focus
lies in the validation of data acquired from the prototype device employed for our investiga-
tion. Additionally, we aim to gauge cognitive fatigue and attention through physiological
measures. The following section delineates the methodological approach undertaken to
achieve these dual objectives.
4.1
Participants
A cohort of 13 healthy individuals (11 male, 2 female, mean age 26.4) partook in the ‘Cog-
nitive fatigue and attention’ experiment. Out of these, 5 participated in the ‘Validation’
experiments as well. Their involvement was entirely voluntary, with each participant being
provided detailed information through written informed consent, aligning with the princi-
ples outlined in the Declaration of Helsinki by the World Medical Association (WMA)[67].
A copy of the informed consent is attached in the Appendix C.
All participants demonstrated either normal visual acuity or corrected-to-normal vision
and reported an absence of symptoms or signs related to neurological impairments. It is
essential to point out that Participant No. 12, involved in both experiments, possesses a
hearing impairment, while Participant No. 7 experiences cardiomyopathy. Both individ-
uals volunteered to participate, and their inclusion was maintained, as the experimental
procedures presented no inherent risk to their well-being. Furthermore, throughout the
entire experiment, one of the two investigators was consistently present by their side,
ensuring a supportive and secure environment for their involvement.
The subjects were requested to abstain from caffeine and other stimulants on the day of the
experiment. All experiments were conducted within the time frame of 9 a.m. to 16 a.m.
To mitigate potential post-meal energy crush and drowsiness[68], individuals partaking in
afternoon experiments were advised to have a light lunch. It is important to note that two
participants admitted to consuming coffee two and four hours prior to the experiment.
Despite this, their contributions were not included in the analysis.
4.2
Experimental environment
The experiments were conducted within a shielded room. This is a Faraday cage, effec-
tively mitigating electromagnetic interference and significantly enhancing the signal-to-
noise ratio. To ensure optimal data quality, participants were instructed to maintain a
comfortable seated position, minimizing unnecessary movements or contact with the EEG
acquisition devices.
Conducted from a computer situated 0.5 meters away, the experiment’s instructions and
guidance were presented on the screen, fostering a seamless interaction with the partici-
pants. During the ‘Cognitive fatigue and attention detection’ experiment, a keyboard and
a mouse were placed in front of the participant to engage in the experiment, in a position
that required minimal movement. Throughout each session, one of the two investigators
oversaw the experiment, guaranteeing the meticulous recording of the experiment and
providing prompt guidance when necessary. It is noteworthy, however, that this setup
posed certain limitations, a topic we delve into in a subsequent section.

4.3
Data acquisition devices and software
4.3.1
EEG headset prototype - Hugin
Hugin is a Bluetooth over-ear headphone specifically engineered for biosignal recording.
Equipped with 10 dry electrodes placed around the ear pads that record EEG signals, it
operates at a sampling rate of 250 Hz. Among these electrodes, eight function as input
electrodes, while the remaining two (4N and 8N) serve as bias or ground electrodes. This
configuration aims to center the differential amplifiers on each channel, effectively mitigat-
ing common-mode mains noise, that is artifactual signal common to multiple electrodes.
Figure 6.2 illustrates the placement of these electrodes, which are crafted from a conduc-
tive mesh wrapping around the ear pads. While the electrodes themselves are not active,
their cables are shielded to mitigate the potential for noise interference among them.
The electrodes cover some part of the temporal area but also some area close to the jaw
making it susceptible to potential noise and motion artifacts. This poses a limitation that
will be elaborated upon in a subsequent section.
Throughout the recording process, the acquired signals undergo amplification with a gain
of x24. Prior to each recording session, impedance checks are performed on each channel,
and the railing is assessed to ensure that the amplified voltage of the signal from the
measuring electrode to the reference electrode adheres to specified boundaries.
Powered by a rechargeable lithium-ion battery, Hugin offers a continuous usage dura-
tion of up to 8 hours.
This headset is a modified iteration of the company’s existing
consumer-grade headsets, boasting a lightweight and adjustable headband designed for
comfort during extended usage periods.
Notably, Hugin facilitates real-time data streaming through the utilization of the Lab-
StreamLayer (LSL)[69] software framework. LSL serves as an open-source middleware
ecosystem designed to seamlessly manage the streaming, collection, synchronization, and
recording of time-series data, such as neural, physiological, or behavioral data streams
originating from diverse devices, software applications, and event-generating systems. LSL
provides a standard protocol for real-time data streaming and synchronization, making
it easier to integrate various sensors and devices into a unified data acquisition system,
along with cross-platform compatibility facilitating the data processing and visualization.

4.4.1
Impedance measurement
The impedance of individual channels over time was assessed as an additional measure to
ensure the quality of the signal in both systems. These were measured before and after the
experiment for every participant to evaluate how they evolved in the span of 15 minutes.
As discussed in detail in a previous section, the impedance serves as an indicator of
the degree of coupling between the channel and the skin, with higher values suggesting
suboptimal contact and, consequently, a less accurate recording of neural signals. Our
observations revealed that the impedances achieved satisfactory levels (< 300kOhms) after
a settling period lasting several minutes and allowed for the recording to begin. Notably,
an impedance threshold of 100kOhms has been employed in various EEG studies[80] but
it was deemed impractical for the current developmental stage of the prototype headset.
In contrast, the OpenBCI system achieved impedance values below 100kOhms for certain
participants and specific electrode locations, contingent upon factors such as hair density
and the headset’s fit on the individual’s head. A detailed representation of the impedance
values for each participant and each device is illustrated in the appendix.
4.4.2
Eyes open-closed
The eyes open-closed paradigm constitutes a fundamental assessment in EEG-based and
BCI applications, wherein participants are instructed to alternate between periods of eyes
closed and eyes opened.
In this study, the participants were requested to keep their eyes closed and relax for 30
seconds and continue with keeping their eyes open for another 30 seconds while attending
to an auditory stimulus. The termination of the ‘Rest/Eyes Closed’ phase was demarcated
by an auditory cue signaling the commencement of the next experimental phase.
During the ‘eyes closed’ interval it is expected that the alpha activity in the brain is more
apparent and is suppressed in the ‘eyes open’ interval. For this purpose, the spectrogram of
the continuous signal was computed to examine the changes in the alpha band, frequency
range from 8 to 13 Hz. Given the predominant localization of alpha waves in the visual
cortex of the occipital lobe[2], their detection is anticipated to be more discernible in
this region compared to the temporal region where the prototype headset electrodes are
situated. Detailed results are expounded upon in Section 5.
4.4.3
Auditory Steady-State Response
The auditory steady-state response (ASSR) is a generated potential in reaction to mod-
ulated tones. When an individual perceives such a tone, the EEG will mirror the tone’s
envelope. In essence, if the tone underwent modulation at a certain frequency, we antici-
pate observing an augmentation of that frequency in the EEG signal. This augmentation
is particularly noticeable in electrodes situated on the auditory cortex.
The ASSR phase of the experiment comprised the participants having their eyes open and
looking at the fixation cross on the screen while a modulated tone was played through the
headphones, in the Hugin case, and through the speakers, in the Ultracortex case. The
participants were requested to be as focused as possible on the auditory stimulus. An
initial test with each participant can conducted beforehand to determine the maximum
volume level with which each participant felt comfortable. The auditory stimulus was a
40 Hz amplitude-modulated tone of a 1 kHz carrier tone, which was generated for the
experiment.
4.4.4
Validation test preprocessing and analysis
For each participant, the continuous EEG signals collected were filtered with a one-pass,
zero-phase, non-causal band-pass FIR filter from 1 - 50 Hz and a one-pass, zero-phase,

non-causal band-stop FIR filter at 50 Hz to remove the power line interference. The dis-
tinct eyes-closed and eyes-open phases were separated and each one was further segmented
into 5 or 30-second epochs. The rejection criterion was different for each measurement, de-
pending on the ‘success’ of the measurement in acquiring as noise and artifiact free data as
possible. Overwhelmingly, 150 or 600µV in amplitude was applied to each epoch to elim-

then averaged within each condition, namely ‘ASSR/Eyes Open’ and ‘Rest/Eyes Closed’,
as part of a concerted effort to mitigate the impact of random noise. This assumption is
grounded in the belief that such noise is unrelated to the anticipated deflection in the EEG
signal induced by the auditory stimulus. The duration of the epochs was chosen as such
to be able to capture multiple cycles of the 40 Hz modulation. The power spectral density
(PSD) was computed using the multitaper method to attain high frequency specificity
while maintaining low variability.

Bluetooth Interference
When developing a device, it is crucial to consider potential electromagnetic interference
(EMI) caused by an electromagnetic disturbance that can potentially affect the perfor-
mance of the device. One of the most common types of EMI is Bluetooth (BT) interference
which may arise when streaming data within similar radio frequency ranges. Previous
studies [81] have highlighted the susceptibility of EEG data transmission to disruptions
caused by Bluetooth interference from other devices or wireless transmitters at testing
sites [82].
To assess Bluetooth interference, a controlled experiment was conducted. The Bluetooth
audio streaming functionality was isolated by disconnecting the headset from the com-
puter’s sound system. During an auditory steady-state response (ASSR) test, an inves-
tigator wore cabled earphones to receive auditory stimuli, and the Hugin headset was
positioned atop these earphones, ensuring proper electrode placement. Subsequently, the
same ASSR experiment was repeated without cabled earphones, with the headset’s audio
directly connected to the computer’s sound system. Power spectral density (PSD) analysis
was employed during the ASSR condition, with the same methodology that was applied
in the validation experiment, to evaluate if additional noise was introduced into the EEG
signal. The continuous EEG data were segmented into 5-second epochs and a rejection
criterion of 150µV was applied to discard the noisy ones.

4.4.7
Exploring Signal Anomalies
Unsuccessful Auditory Oddball
In the initial development of a validation experiment, we attempted to recreate an audi-
tory oddball paradigm and perform an ERP analysis. This was inspired by the valida-
tion scheme followed by the competitor company Neurable [32], which recreated a Visual
Oddball paradigm and analyzed the P300 response[64]. The visual oddball experiment
involves the presentation of some non-target visual stimuli intercepted by an infrequent
target. Analogously, the auditory oddball paradigm that we aspired to recreate involved
the presentation of auditory stimuli. The participant is requested to respond to the target-
oddball auditory cue by pressing a button. This is expected to evoke a positive deflection
300 msec after the stimulus presentation. The inter-stimulus intervals are varying. The
Evoked Response Potential (ERP) is calculated by averaging across the trials for each
condition. The oddball ERP is expected to have a difference from the baseline of a couple
µV , and the non-target condition should present no difference from the baseline. The
experiment was designed in PsychoPy and event-markers were streamed to time-lock the
presentation of each auditory cue and its corresponding label.
Regrettably, attempts to reproduce this phenomenon proved unsuccessful. The baseline
exhibited unexpected variability and amplitude, and no discernible component was evoked
around the expected 300-millisecond interval. Possible explanations considered included
a diminished signal-to-noise ratio, an insuﬀicient number of trials, inadequacies in the
34
Performance evaluation of EEG headset for Brain Computer Interface (BCI) applications
==PAGE:43==
experimental design, or errors in data processing and analysis. Additionally, there was a
reasoned guess that the elicited signal might have been too subtle to be captured at the
current stage of prototype development.
Despite the outcome, this circumstance turned our research interest into a series of sub-
sequent investigations that yielded valuable insights:
• Sample-to-sample jitter analysis
• Detection of lost samples during high BT audio load, or other radio interference
• Validation of the signal processing chain (time-stamping in MNE-Python)

4.5
Attention and fatigue detection
4.5.1
Ground truths
In order to be able to design an experiment capable of detecting attention and fatigue,
certain ground truths were established in the context of EEG features. Literature sug-
gests that beta activity shows active thinking and alertness, while alpha shows relaxed
awareness and tiredness, and theta indicates idleness and the state between awake and
asleep. However, there is more to it, regarding the cognitive state of the individual under
examination.
Mental Fatigue:
There is a consensus that the most prevalent indicators of fatigue
are large increases in theta activity in frontal, central, and posterior sites, and moderate
changes in alpha activity in central and posterior sites [45]. The beta band also increases
in the frontal lobe during fatigue states[84].
Attention:
Relative to rest, alpha activity decreases during both concentration and im-
mersion. As it is primarily related to the inhibition of task-irrelevant information, alpha
activity increases in the task-unrelated regions and decreases in the task-related ones. It
illustrates a decrease in time on task and task performance because of the greater atten-
tional demand. At the same time, theta activity showcases a rise in sustained attention
especially in high cognitive load tasks. Theta waves also show a decrease in almost all
regions during concentration and, on the other hand, an increase in all regions during
immersion. Finally, beta activity drops in concentrated states but increases during im-
mersion. Beta waves increased during concentration and immersion in the frontal and
occipital lobes, with a higher increase in immersion. In the temporal lobe, Beta waves de-
creased during concentration and increased during immersion. In the central region, Beta
waves decreased during concentration and immersion, and the decrease during immersion
was larger[18].
4.5.2
Detection methods
For the purpose of detecting cognitive fatigue and attention, one should design an exper-
iment suitable for inducing fatigue in the participants, while keeping them engaged and
focused throughout the procedure. It is clear by now, and as the literature suggests, this
is no easy task, especially because of the subjectiveness of these phenomena and the way
each experiences them. The initial scope of this work was to endeavor to detect both
cognitive fatigue and attention through physiological measures and more specifically the
brain activity of the participants. However, previous studies suggest other measures that
can be additionally monitored, both objective and subjective, to correlate with the suppos-
edly objective metric of EEG signals. Despite their very personalized nature, EEG signals
are considered an objective measure from the aspect that they quantify spontaneous brain
activity that follows specified known patterns for particular conditions. These measures,
along with their corresponding testing tasks are described systematically down below.
Electroencephalography (EEG)
The EEG signals of the participants were recorded continuously throughout the whole
duration of the experiment. However, extracting significant features from EEG signals
is a challenging task considering their intricate, unstable, non-stationary, and non-linear
nature. The spectral band powers and their combination in ratios were utilized as features
to evaluate fatigue and attention in this study due to their widespread employment in
fatigue-related and attention-related studies.

4.5.4
Processing and data analysis
The analysis of the recorded EEG signals was performed using the MNE-Python toolbox
(v.1.6) and custom-made routines in Python 3.9 based mainly on the scipy.signal library
for signal processing.
The raw EEG data measured from Hugin are streamed from BioGenie with LSL to the
LabRecorder framework where the event markers are also parsed from PsychoPy during
the experiment.
The final recording is in XDF format and contains the two synchro-
nized streams. The raw EEG signals, sampled at 250 Hz, are processed offline for every
participant.
Initially, they are filtered with a Band-pass filter from 1-35 Hz and a Notch filter at 50
Hz with characteristics that were described in detail in Section 2.7, thus removing the
power line interference and retaining only the brain activity of interest. A preliminary
visual inspection is conducted to verify the quality of the signal, remove the noisy-railed
channels, and decide upon a rejection threshold in the amplitude of the signal, above which
this part of the signal is considered artifact-contaminated.
Afterward, based on the event markers, the continuous signals are divided into different
task repetitions to be further processed and to extract features.
During this step, it
was acknowledged that during the Math task the constant looking back and forth from
the screen to the keyboard corrupted our signal with artifacts to a great extent. This
instigated an extensive investigation and attempt to perform artifact removal on these
segments of the recording, to render them usable for feature extraction and classification,
in which we will delve later in this section.
Thereafter, each task is further segmented into 5-second epochs and the rejection criterion
is applied to remove epochs contaminated by noise. For the majority of the participants,
the rejection criterion for the peak-to-peak distance was set at 200µV , with only a couple
of exceptions where a 100µV rejection was possible. We assume that the baseline displays
steady-state characteristics in these small intervals and accounts for the background neural
activity. Thus the Baseline epochs are averaged out and removed from the task-related
epochs to enhance the Signal-to-Noise ratio and accentuate the components of interest.
Then, the feature extraction succeeds in the preprocessing of the signals, and the relative
band and ratio features are computed. The scipy.signal.welch function is employed
with a Hann window, 75% overlap, and a segment length and FFT length of 512 to achieve
adequate frequency resolution and accuracy of our feature values. The final features are
extracted after being smoothed with a moving average filter and a smoothing factor. This
procedure is expounded later in this section.

In conventional artifact removal algorithms, periods containing artifacts are located and

between saccades revealed characteristics resembling neural activity. An initial approach
involved isolating these intervals and performing power spectral analysis separately. How-

An eye_artifact_removal function was designed to remove the eye artifacts in question
from the EEG data using a combination of methods. A breakdown of the main steps
followed to identify and correct the artifactual segments are listed below:
1. Data Normalization:
• This helped in making the data comparable across different channels and par-
ticipants.
2. Rectangular Pulse Detection:
• For each point in the normalized EEG data, a local section of 25msec is con-
sidered, and a line is fitted to the data in this section.
• The slope of this line is raised in the 10th power while maintaining the ± sign.
3. Regions-of-interest Identification:
• The identified slopes set transition periods where eye artifacts are likely to be
present.
• The transition periods are evaluated on a threshold value of ±5 and their du-
ration (transitions of less than 5 samples are discarded).
• The beginning and the end of each transition period along with their counter-
part points in the original data are extracted and labeled as low/ high transition
and low/ high interval.
4. Artifact Correction:
• The interval periods that mark the brain activity are updated in the original
data by subtracting their mean value
• The transition periods that mark the saccade artifacts are updated in the orig-
inal data by smoothing.
• A line is fitted to the transition period and then subtracted from it.
• From the boundaries of the transition periods, two middle points are extracted
along with their relative value in the original data.
• A new line is then fitted at those points and subtracted from the transition
periods to fit it more accurately to the new values of the interval boundaries.
The function is applied to each channel independently, and the final corrected data is
returned for further analysis. Figure 4.18 illustrates the correction of the artifacts. It is

of the evaluation tasks, considering the overall variability in each task. The evaluation

a pairwise comparison among the three repetitions.

Feature selection
As stated in the EEG feature list 4.5.2, there were 3 bands that were extracted as features,
and 4 more band ratios made our of these 3 bands. These features are:
• Alpha band: spectral components 8-13Hz
• Beta band: spectral components 13-30Hz
• Theta band: spectral components 4-7Hz
• Alpha / Beta
• Theta / Beta
• (Theta + Alpha) / Beta
• (Theta + Alpha) / (Alpha + Beta)
In total we have these 7 features, and per each feature 5 channels gave us the features
to use in classification, totalling 35 features. We ended up with 5 channels instead of
the origial 8 because we had 2 constantly noisy channels we removed, and one more was
removed as it had to be selected as the reference channel. If a channel is selected as a
reference channel, it does not supply any usable data.
These channels were further divided in a feature selection process to try to see how much
do they contribute in our classification task, which resulted in the following datasets:
• All: In this combination all features are used to train and test a model.
• Without Alpha: In this combination all features that does not contain the Alpha
band in any shape or form. These are: Beta, Theta, Theta / Beta
• Without Beta: In this combination all features that does not contain the Beta band
in any shape or form. These are: Alpha, Theta
• Without Theta: In this combination all features that does not contain the Theta
band in any shape or form. These are: Alpha, Beta, Alpha / Beta
Then, this feature selection was done for all the participants individually.
4.6.3
The training and testing process
For each participant’s data individually, the signal processing steps were done, then the
resulting dictionary was saved as a pickle file, for ease of use later. It was also ensured
with a function that pickle serializing does not modify the structure or the data itself in
any way or form. The classification problem that we trained models for are the following:
• distinction between tasks.
Though there are 3 repetitions of each task, we have
merged them for this classification. Each participant’s data was individually used.
• distinction between repetitions. As each task has 3 repetitions we made models to
classify between the three repetitions. Each participant’s data was individually used.
• generalize task distinction between participants. Here, the train set would be all the
participants except one, while the test set is the one participant who was not in the
training set.
Performance evaluation of EEG headset for Brain Computer Interface (BCI) applications
53
==PAGE:62==
With the 3 above goals in mind, the following training and testing pipeline was imple-
mented an used. A model was trained for each participant (11), each feature band (44.5.2),
and each task(5: all, pvt, math, tetris, snake). This means that each of the 297 models
specified above were trained for each of the 220 datasets, so the best models were selected
from 65340 models.
The training and testing process was done as follows:
• load in a dataset, assign correct labels to the dataset
• then, the train and test set was split in a stratified random shuffle. A stratified
random shuffle first reorders the data, then randomly selects the test points in a way
that maintains the class balance in the test set, as it was before the split, as defined
in sklearn [101].
• next, a simple preprocessing for the data was done, to bring it to the best shape for
a machine learning algorithm. This essentially only means fitting a standardization
algorithm [102] to the train data and transforming it. This would have the effect of
reducing the mean of the features of the train data to 0, while setting the standard
deviation of the features to 1. Then, we transform, but not fit again to the test data.
This means, that we are using the characteristics of the training data on the test
data. This is done to avoid leaking information into the test set, which would make
the classification unrealistic.
• After this point the grid search for the best hyperparmeters begin for each classifier.
Firstly, the model is instantiated with the hyperparameters tested at that time. A 3
fold cross validation is done on the training set, then the model is fitted and tested
on the test data. To ensure that there is no information leakage by basing decisions
on normally not available data we do not select the model that has the best accuracy
on the test set, but we choose the model that did best in the cross validation with
the training data. The best model by this criteria was selected, and its details and
confusion matrix was saved.

6.1
Validation test
6.1.1
Results
From table 5.1 we can see the following:
• ASSR: Hugin was able to detect it 6 out of 9 times, while Ultracortex was able to
detect it 7 out of 9 times.
• Berger effect: Hugin was able to detect it 5 out of 7 times, while Ultracortex was
able to detect it 6 out of 7 times.
As these are well researched effects it can be an error in the experiment setup that pre-
vented us from finding ASSR and Berger effect in every participants every time. In any
case we can see that though Hugin is not perfect, in majority of the cases it does pick up
on the effects that we are looking for, so as Ultracortex. The concept of this validation
test is to see whether Hugin is capable of picking up on the effects in similar manner that
Ultracortex picks up on. This is the case, as we can see it from the validation results
which allows us to conclude that Hugin is a valid EEG platform.
Especially with ASSR, the resulting activation can be ‘swallowed’ by the noise floor,
if that is too high. With each participant we walked the fine line of rejecting enough
noisy epochs but still having enough data to obtain a representative analysis. To obtain
better results with Hugin, being able to further reduce the noise floor with more advanced
electrode constructions for better recording quality and resiliency to environmental (and
intra device) effects are needed. Otherwise the usecase of Hugin is very promising, as it is
a daily worn device and the sheer amount of data that can be collected could counteract
infrequent artifacts or noises.
Differences in effect magnitude
Though Hugin seems like a valid EEG platform, it must be noted that the magnitude of the
effects that we claim Hugin picked up on is much smaller than its Ultracortex counterpart.
Note on the example images for Hugin 5.1 and for Ultracortex ?? the difference of the effect
magnitude. For ASSR, while with ultracortex the electrodes cleanly pick up on the effect,
with hugin we have a smaller peak at 40Hz. This trend is true for all the measurements,
that the Ultracortex’s 40Hz peak is more clearly visible. Also note how more apparent
the Berger effect is on the Ultracortex’s measurement. These differences will matter if we
would like to apply a classifier to it. But considering that Hugin is a prototype under
development, this is a promising result. This difference of magnitude could be caused by
the measurement locations T5 and T6 are more suited to catch these activiations than
regions around the ear.
6.1.2
Validation test: How do we know what we measured is correct?
Now that we claimed that Hugin is a valid platform we must verify that our experiment
was correct. Some reasons were collected to argue for the correctness of the experiment.
In this case we will be looking at the ASSR test. We examine the claim: Was the 40Hz
peak we caught the result of a speaker operating next to the EEG? We have conducted
tests that were done with earphones then laptop speakers with the same participant. We
could see similar 40hz peak activation. Moreover, when a participant with hearing damage
attempted the test, we could not see ASSR on tests where no hearing aid was used, an we
could see assr where hearing aid was used.

6.2
Data quality
6.2.1
Hardware updates
In the development of electronic devices, ensuring Electromagnetic Compatibility (EMC)
is one of the most important design and engineering considerations.
This imperative
arises from the necessity for a device to operate seamlessly in real-world environments,
where it coexists with diverse electronic devices capable of generating Electromagnetic
Interference (EMI). Beyond the considerable implications for safety and product reliability,
the potential repercussions encompass data loss and signal corruption.
Consequently,
electronic devices must be engineered to function effectively even in the presence of certain
levels of EMI.
Throughout this study, while validating the headset prototype our investigations aided
the developing team in discovering and resolving various issues, pinpointing sources of
interference, and revising the device leading to enhanced signal quality. A notable source
of interference was traced to the ’on-head’ detection sensor embedded in the original head-
set, which introduced artifacts into the power supply. This interference extended to the
Hugin module, sharing the same power supply. While this would be inconsequential in
the original audio device, the introduced noise, oscillating at approximately 25 Hz, signif-
icantly impacted the highly sensitive EEG system. The resolution to this issue involved
the implementation of a dedicated power supply for the Hugin module. However, The
replacement power supply introduced a distinct interference pattern at approximately 10
Hz due to its utilization of a switching regulator as part of its switch mode power supply
design[103], aimed at optimizing electrical eﬀiciency. This issue was subsequently resolved
by transitioning to an alternative power supply, at the expense of a small reduction in
battery life.
Hence, the Validation experiments employed the original headset design, whereas the
Cognitive Fatigue and Attention Detection experiment utilized the revised and enhanced
hardware version.

6.4
Attention and fatigue detection with EEG sensors
During the Attention and Fatigue experiment we expected that the participants would
show signs of cognitive fatigue by the end of it. The initial hypothesis was formulated
around a reduced performance and reaction time at the Evaluation tasks, along with the
self-reporting questionnaires to be able to label the cognitive state of the participants.
However, the performance results during the Math tasks (Table 5.8) did not show a defini-
tive trend with 1 out of 11 participants showing a better performance, 2 out of 11 a
decreasing performance, 3 out 11 a rise from the first to the second repetition and then
a drop, and finally 5 out of 11 the exact opposite. We conclude that the performance
metric in this context is not a reliable marker since the total number of math problems
presented was also varied. This is accounted to the fact that some participants needed
time to get accustomed to the task. An important point to underline is the keyboard
limitation, where some participants found it uncomfortable to input their responses, or
some keys appeared sometimes unresponsive. On the other hand, we were unable to ex-
tract information from the PVT test due to the fact that the PsychoPy files for such large
recordings were corrupted.
The results from the self-reporting questionnaires are showcased in Figures 5.10, 5.11, 5.12.
The majority of participants scored higher fatigue rates at the end of the experiment in
comparison to the onset, while two (Participants 7 and 9) scored lower and one (participant
10) scored the same. The VAS-F questionnaire comprises two subscales, one for ‘tiredness’
and one for ‘energy’. It is interesting to see that 7 out of 11 of the participants reported
a higher energy level, signifying that the experimental session made them more energetic.
Regarding the NASA-TLX questionnaire that quantifies the load induced from a specific
task, our findings indicate that for half of the subjects, the Tetris game generated a higher
task load, while for the other half the Snake. Finally, taking a closer look at the average
scores of the custom questions present at the end of each gaming block, a steady rise is
associated with how tired the participants felt, and a similar decrease is associated with
their ability to further concentrate. On the other hand, the effort required for each task
displayed an accent from block to block in the first game, then the average score resumes
its initial value, only to rise again with a bit steeper trend. These findings align with
our apprehension that even though the participants had a general feeling of tiredness
by the end of the experiment, that does not necessarily constitute cognitive fatigue. It
is noteworthy that for some subjects the Tetris game or the Math task were in reality
stimulating thus rendering the results further away from the initial hypothesis.
These findings are additionally supported by the continuous EEG features visualized in
Figures 5.13 to 5.15 throughout the course of the experiment. Literature suggests α and
θ activation while emerging to a more fatigued state. Nonetheless, we were not able to
detect such a trend for either of the features. This experimental paradigm poses a great
limitation to that end since the complexity and multidimensionality of each task separately
are suspected to be masking any onset of cognitive fatigue. Additionally, the systematic
review[45] connecting mental fatigue with θ and α activity are directed to the mid, frontal,
parietal, and occipital regions of the brain whereas our application was focused on the
temporal region. Only one study[48] investigated the temporal regions, but it involved a
monotonous driving simulation task. A significant difference was discovered for all EEG
algorithms, identical to our ratio EEG features, with each algorithm displaying an initial
steep increase followed by a moderate one. In contrast to these results, our findings on
the ratio EEG features do not display a similar behavior. This is reasonable given the last
variability between the fatigue-inducing methodologies employed in each work.

6.5
Implications for research questions
At the beginning of this thesis, the following research questions were stated:
• RQ1: How can cognitive attention be detected based on EEG?
• RQ2: How can cognitive fatigue be detected based on EEG?
• RQ3: How can cognitive attention and fatigue be evaluated with a headset that
incorporates biosensors?
• RQ4: How is the performance of such a headphones EEG system compared to an
established EEG platform?
6.5.1
Research Question 1: Attention detection
In relation to research question 1, attentional networks are proven to be a complex struc-
ture closely related to the role of alpha activity in inhibiting task-irrelevant information
and their processing (thus displaying a rise in these areas), while promoting the areas
processing the task-relevant information (lower alpha). Following that theta activity rises
with sustained attention and beta activity rises in more concentrated states. In temporal
regions, however, beta activity displays a drop for concentrated states and rises signifi-
cantly the more engrossed you are in a task.
6.5.2
Research Question 2: Fatigue detection
In relation to research question 2, fatigue detection is closely related to a rise in frontal,
central, and parietal mostly in theta and less significantly in alpha. However, it is unclear
how fatigue affects the temporal regions.
6.5.3
Research Question 3: Fatigue and Attention detection with
Headset
Evidently, the large inter-subject variations in the EEG features did not allow for conclu-
sive results. However, there were very prominent changes in neural activity for different
tasks for the majority of the participants signifying that the headset is able to detect
accurately the neural activity, but our methodology and experimental paradigm were not
appropriate to achieve a guided detection.
With the current experiment design we were not able to come up with a classification
problem for attention or fatigue that wouldn’t leave an inconclusive result. With the data
at hand we believed that a good classification problem is task classification and repetition
classification of a task. For both of these problems we found that good performing clas-
sifiers could be created. Good classifiers for data that consists of a single measurement
session. To verify the validity of this classification problem, a further study is needed with
multiple measurement sessions.
6.5.4
Research Question 4: Validation
Hugin was compared in the same tasks against an established EEG platform, the Open-
BCI’s Ultracortex Mark IV. Here, we found that Hugin performed similarly to Ultracortex
in finding the target effects, though they are less apparent in Hugin’s measurements. With
this we can conclude that Hugin is a valid eeg platform.

6.6
Limitations
This section stands as an overview of some limitations encountered that affected the com-
pletion of the goals of this work, as well as the induction of definitive conclusions regarding
the set research questions.
6.6.1
Experimental design
The experiment designed and conducted to detect fatigue demonstrated a range of defi-
ciencies that rendered us unable to draw definitive conclusions. Starting with the general
overview of it, a number of various evaluation and fatigue-inducing tasks were employed,
each with its own characteristics and effect on mental activity. This resulted in subjects
experiencing it differently and creating vast inter-subject variation for the analysis. It
was recognized retrospectively that some of the tasks displayed competitive mechanisms
in some participants and thus even after an hour of the experiment cognitive fatigue was
not induced. Added to that, evaluating the levels of fatigue during a cognitive task was
not a prudent approach since the effects of fatigue could be masked by the underlying
neural mechanisms involved in said task.
An additional limitation point constitutes the different metrics used to detect fatigue. It
was evident that the performance was not showcasing any descending trend during the
course of the experiment, because some participants required an ‘adjustment period’, and
after the diﬀiculty induced by the novelty of it wore off, better results were achieved.
Similarly, having two different games for a short period of 15 minutes each does not allow
the fatigue to build up so that is more evident, and to further support our previous point,
the short length of each game might induce less cognitive load, and thus fatigue, to usual
players resulting to a non-uniform result.
Furthermore, a very constricting factor appeared to be the keyboard used for the partic-
ipant’s engagement in the experiment. The fact that they needed to look down from the
screen to the keyboard introduced a great deal of artifacts which, due to their frequency
and short duration, was not easy to isolate and correct without introducing discontinu-
ities and tampering with the signal. The examination of the accelerometer data from the
embedded PPG sensors did not show any correlation with the artifacts and thus they
could not facilitate the procedure. Of course, this limitation, turned into a blessing and a
curse at the same time, since it provided inspiration for future applications that the time
constraint did not allow the authors to investigate.
Finally, the headset incorporated more sensors like PPG and IMU which were not used in
the analysis of the experiment due to the complexity of the endeavor to include them and
time restrictions limiting this study. There is a possibility that the PPG sensors could have
provided a clearer indication of the cognitive state to facilitate a classification between a
fatigued and a non-fatigued state.
As an end-note to the experimental design, a careful investigation must proceed at the
onset of the experimental series because it can happen that some software, like the Psy-
choPy platform that we used to conduct the experiments, might be crushing often and/or
produce corrupted files or no files at all. The root of the problem was never discovered but
it is reasonable also that personal computers might not be ideal for such lengthy recordings
Regrettably, this is one of the challenges that we faced in this study and obstructed us
from extracting all the metrics that we initially planned from the experiment.

On-head-sensor:
As discussed in section 6.2.1 a detector from the original headset was

Corrosion:
The corrosion of the electrodes is a very crucial issue regarding the design
of a product and its versatility. Throughout the study, we have counted heavily on the
effect of sweat accumulating between the skin and electrodes. While this indeed helped to
reduce the impedance of the electrodes, over time the remains of that sweat built up on the
electrode, and it visibly altered the electrode’s color by the end (Figure ??). This could
result in distorting the signal’s fine structure or adding some DC component. Cleaning the
device after every measurement may have solved this issue, but this was not implemented
in time. The effects of such corrosion on the electrodes were not studied in this project,
so we do not know what and how much of an effect it has on the signal quality.
Impedance instability:
As good signal quality often depended on sweat accumulating
between the skin and the electrode, the participants had to wear the headset for at least
15 minutes before the onset of measurement to ensure good data quality, meaning having
a reference electrode candidate below 500kω and several others in a similar range for
measurements.
However this condition was very limiting for the participants and the
impact was very fragile, with merely lifting the ear cup could throw off impedance levels.
Sudden head movements or a participant lifting the ear cup to scratch the ear in the middle
of the experiment would cause significant changes in the signal quality, and a readjustment
period was necessary.
6.6.3
Environment
The environment of the experiment constitutes a restricting parameter in the whole proce-
dure. All the experiments were held in an electromagnetically shielded room to reduce EM
interference during the measurements. However, this is a very small room that for some
participants might feel suffocating, or it is also prone to become too warm after a series of
consecutive experiments. Additionally, having the experimenter physically supervise the
experiment not only induced psychological effects, but also the air was used up quicker by
2 people than the ventilation system could reasonably handle, leading to stuffy air, which
in turn may have led to more drowsy states than normal. Some participants also noted
that they felt uncomfortably warm at times.

Conclusion
This thesis has evaluated the signal quality and the performance of the biosensing Hugin
headset through various conditions. First and foremost the validity of the data acquired
was assessed while comparing it to an already established EEG platform. The purpose of
this series of validation experiments was to prove the data quality of the device, evaluate
it in standard EEG protocols, and investigate other aspects that could be limiting, like
Electromagnetic Interference and the occurrence of various artifacts. The second point of
interest in this work was the development of an experimental paradigm for the detection of
cognitive fatigue and attention. The aim was to create well-performing machine learning
models to classify between different cognitive states. The choice of the different detection
methods was such in order to have a subjective, through the questionnaires, an objective,
through the EEG features, and quantitative, through math performance and reaction time,
measures of the cognitive state of the participants.
The results of the Validation tests that Hugin, even in its initial hardware design, was able
to perform equivalently to OpenBCI Ultracortex Mark V. Additionally, it was discovered
that the Hugin headset can be used to assess different cognitive tasks and perform a high
accuracy classification among them. Regarding the fatigue-inducing experiment, it has
been found that the cognitive states induced by different tasks are complex enough to
illustrate very high inter-subject variability. The initial hypothesis was that the chosen
games would induce cognitive load and thus fatigue that it could be evaluated during the
evaluation periods, However, the inappropriate experimental design led to inconclusive
results since not all participants reported a rise in cognitive fatigue by the completion of
the experiment. What is more, the inhomogenous variability in the feature values among
the repetitive evaluation tasks did not allow for a statistical analysis of their differences.
Assumptions regarding cognitive states throughout the experiment were drawn for 3 par-
ticipants but again, there was no common trend indicating that a simpler, more con-
trolled, and more ubiquitous experimental procedure should be followed to detect fatigue
and track attention and then generalize it to something more complex. Additionally, the
performance during math tasks did not show any specific trend.
Variability among participants in the findings when focusing on the attention markers
illustrated that our results are inconclusive.
All things considered, it is evident that
there are multiple competitive underlying mechanisms as regards attentional networks
to account not only for the voluntary goal-oriented attention (dorsal) but also for the
orientation to salient and novel distractors (ventral). The current study has shown us
that attention is not a step function, it fluctuates during a task.
The initial proposal intended to implement one more test in this experiment, that is
testing the headset in an end-user environment: in an unshielded room, playing a more
modern, competitively played game. Delays in the project and prioritizing concluding in
a controlled environment led to the decision to reduce the scope of the project, excluding
the end-user environment tests.
However, despite the fatigue and attention detection being undetermined, a great number
of insights were gained along the way regarding the headset, dealing effectively with arti-
facts, and the underlying mechanisms involved in these composite and multidimensional
notions that is cognitive fatigue and attention.

Future Work
Based on the limitations we identified in this work, there are many areas for improvement,
not only as regards the device, but also the fatigue and attention detection, and task
identification.
8.1
Product Design
Our investigations and validation tests provided valuable insights into many aspects of the
product design, as was elaborated in section 6.2.1. One of the limitations of this study
was that the validation experiments on the standard EEG protocols took place before the
hardware and software updates. In the future, repeating these experiments would provide
a qualitative measure for these improvements and a comparison to the SNR of the recorded
signal. Especially with the unsuccessful auditory oddball experiment, implementing it with
the improved headset and extracting the expected result would place Hugin in a definitive
place among other established EEG platforms and similar competitor devices already on
the market.
Exploring the performance of Hugin during daily use, when performing focused tasks like
studying, as well as gaming in an end-user environment would bring forth further design
limitations to be improved and propose new angles of achieving good data quality that
can overcome the noises of a modern everyday environment.
The current prototype is a good place to start, but there could be several improvements
made. One recommendation is a more ergonomic ear cup, that makes better use of the
hairless section of a person behind their ear. The ear could play a bigger role in supporting
the ear cup and the sensors, which in turn would lead to a more stable signal. To that
end, a future idea could be to develop a sticker with embedded electrodes similar to the
design of cEEGrid[104] to be placed directly on the skin on the back of the ear. This ad-
vantageous design would then allow for the headset ear pad to sit on this medium without
the limitation of a participant having too much hair. As an end note, we would suggest
using a specified electrode as a reference, which would be uniform for all participants. It
could be that this reference electrode would be an active electrode, similar to the ones
used by the Ultracortex Mark V, placed on the headband on the midline achieving thus a
signal with superior quality.
8.2
Generalization of models
An interesting and necessary future step would be to generalize our models among partic-
ipants. We were not successful in generalizing EEG data between participants, but that
might have been only because of a lack of time. Given more time, we would approach this
problem in a way that once we have trained a model on every except one participant, we
would give a small amount of the test participant’s data to further train, or calibrate, our
model, where a warm start is possible. Then this resulting model is tested on the rest
of the test data. Then, this accuracy would be compared with a model that was trained
from scratch with the same amount of small data given from the given person, and only
that. If we see that the model trained on everyone’s data proves better than the model
trained only on the small calibration set, then we can conclude that common patterns
among participants are found, and given more data there is a possibility for generaliza-
tion. If the model that trained on everyone’s data does not fare better, then we would

87
==PAGE:96==
still have the possibility to record more data, to see that it is enough for seeing the effects
of generalization.
Additionally, future explorations could include task detection among more everyday ac-
tivities in front of a computer such as studying, watching a movie, and playing different
video games[105][86]. Task detection between very similar but distinct tasks could be an
interesting direction similar to studies comparing concentration and immersion states[18].
An example could be reading a physical book versus an online book. Such an application
can provide insights as regards immersion and deeper engagement, the different aspects of
visual stimulation that can affect cognitive load and fatigue differently. To this end, the
limitation of producing eye artifacts due to the wandering of the gaze could be utilized as
an eye tracker to quantify the effect of fatigue on reading. Similarly, exploring emotion
recognition through movie clips and affecting videos could be a very promising future
investigation, especially given the extent of research on this domain.
8.3
Improved fatigue and attention assessment
As previously discussed in section 6.6, future experiment designs to detect fatigue should
entail more lengthy continuous gameplay.
Previous studies[45] have shown significant
results with time-on-task more than 1 hour. A proposed design would include a playtime
of 2 hours split into 5-minute segments, after each of segment the participant would fill
fatigue-related surveys (VAS-F, currently used questions between games, NASA-TLX) and
attention-related surveys (questions such as, on a scale of 1-10 rate: do you enjoy the game,
were you in the state of flow). Then, interpolate between these points linearly alongside
the EEG features. These subjective markers would then constitute a label associated with
a fatigue/attention level that would distribute the EEG data into (e.g. 5) different classes.
In this way, a dataset that follows at participant’s self-reported fatigue and attention levels
can be acquired, and it can then be considered automated attention and fatigue detection.
These patterns could then be generalized to different measurement sessions of a single
person for a personalized fatigue/ attention classification.
As a future work, we also propose the possibility of developing a thresholding algorithm
much alike [106]. In this work, a warning threshold is set from the alpha band when a
trial’s reaction time is longer than 2.5 times the mean reaction time within the first 4
minutes of the task, so that online closed-loop fatigue detection and mitigation system is
developed.
8.4
Personalized models
Our findings showed good performance on the classifiers but that might be biased because
they each refer to a single measurement session. To ensure that the patterns we claim
to have found do exist, longitudinal studies are proposed as future work, where repeated
measures with the same participants are performed over a prolonged time.
In the realm of personalized applications, a future application of the Hugin headset could
be its employment in Neurofeedback training[107], as well as personalized hearing tests.
As seen in the ASSR tests, some users respond with peaks of different magnitude to
the modulated tone, but in general, raising the volume raised the response as well. It
should be verified whether this is connected to the user’s hearing capability. If it is, then
the detrimental effects of listening in loud volume in a headset could be monitored in a
convenient and daily used device.

A.2
Stanford Sleepiness Scale (SSS)
The SSS[90], serves as a subjective assessment tool for gauging sleepiness at a particu-
lar moment. Comprising a single item, respondents are prompted to choose from seven
statements that most accurately capture their current perception of sleepiness.
Values are assigned as follows:
1. “Feeling active and vital; alert; wide awake.”
2. “Functioning at a high level, but not at peak; able to concentrate.”
3. “Relaxed; awake; not at full alertness; responsive.”
4. “A little foggy; not at peak; let down.”
5. “Fogginess; beginning to lose interest in remaining awake; slowed down.”
6. “Sleepiness; prefer to be lying down; fighting sleep; woozy.”
7. “Almost in reverie; sleep onset soon; lost struggle to remain awake.”
A.3
Visual Analogue Scale to Evaluate Fatigue Severity
(VAS-F)
The VAS-F[91] scale comprises 18 items aimed at assessing individuals’ subjective ex-
perience of fatigue. Respondents are required to mark an ”X” on a visual analog line,
indicating their current feelings, with the line spanning between two opposing descriptors
(e.g., from ”not at all tired” to ”extremely tired”). Unlike discrete Likert-type scales, the
VAS-F allows for a broader spectrum of responses. However, the advantages of employing
a visual analog scale may be counteracted by individuals hesitating to utilize the utmost
ends of the scale. Additionally, some critics argue that the scale is ambiguous and lacks
sensitivity in distinguishing between fatigue and sleepiness.

A.4
NASA-Task Load Index (NASA-TLX)
The NASA TLX[92] is a tool designed for assessing subjective mental workload during
task performance. It evaluates workload across six dimensions:
1. Mental demand: How much mental and perceptual activity was required (e.g.
thinking, deciding, calculating, remembering, looking, searching, etc)? Was the task
easy or demanding, simple or complex, exacting or forgiving?
2. Physical demand: How much physical activity was required (e.g. pushing, pulling,
turning, controlling, activating, etc)? Was the task easy or demanding, slow or brisk,
slack or strenuous, restful or laborious?
3. Temporal demand: How much time pressure did you feel due to the rate of pace
at which the tasks or task elements occurred? Was the pace slow and leisurely or
rapid and frantic?
4. Performance: How successful do you think you were in accomplishing the goals of
the task set by the experimenter (or yourself)? How satisfied were you with your
performance in accomplishing these goals?
5. Effort: How hard did you have to work (mentally and physically) to accomplish
your level of performance?
6. Frustration: How insecure, discouraged, irritated, stressed, and annoyed versus
secure, gratified, content, relaxed, and complacent did you feel during the task?
Participants rate each dimension on a scale ranging from 1 (low) to 20 (high).
Addi-
tionally, a paired comparisons procedure involves presenting 15 pairwise combinations to
participants, who then select the scale from each pair that most significantly influences
their perceived workload. This procedure accounts for variations in workload definitions
and sources between raters and tasks.
The TLX software calculates an overall workload score on a scale of 0 to 100 based on the
participants’ self-ratings and paired comparisons. An online version[108] of the evaluation
questionnaire was employed during the experiment:
