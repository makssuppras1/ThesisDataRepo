Abstract 
IV 
Abstract 
Influenza virus is a disease which represents a major societal cost with hundreds of thousands 
of deaths and millions of severe cases. In addition to its seasonal nature, zoonotic events 
involving influenza A virus (IAV) sometimes result in world-wide pandemics, the deadliest of 
which claimed an estimated 50 million lives in 1918. These factors underscore the importance 
of understanding IAV and especially its interactions with the host immune system. 
This thesis aimed to contribute to this understanding by investigating the virus-host interactions 
between influenza virus and the host innate immune system. The focus of this thesis is the IAV 
non-structural protein 1 (NS1), which is essential for the IAV evasion of the host immune 
response and subsequent successful replication. This was investigated through microfluidic 
real time quantitative PCR targeting known antiviral innate immune genes in human and 
porcine airway epithelial cells infected with IAVs with differing NS gene segments. This data 
was analysed and compared to the viral titers to compare the effects of the different NS 
segments.  
It was concluded that the pandemic IAV NS gene seems to have an advantage over Eurasian 
avian-like swine IAV NS in the human and porcine cells investigated, which is in agreement 
with literature in regard to differences in their NS1 protein sequence. However further, more 
statistically vigorous, studies are required to verify this observation.

1 Introduction 
Influenza virus (IV) is one of largest causes of respiratory tract infections in humans globally, 
causing upwards of 1 billion annual infections, leading to an estimated 3-5 million severe cases 
and 290.000 to 650.000 deaths (World Health Organisation, 2024). IV changes rapidly due to 
frequent mutations and the lack of mechanisms to revert or repair the mutations. Additionally, 
the segmented nature of the IAV genome allows viruses to exchange whole genes by 
reassortment in cross infections (Noda, 2021). Fast paced evolution along with effective 
immunosuppressive mechanisms makes IV re-emerge as both a seasonal and a pandemic virus 
(Troeger et al., 2019). One especially important type of IV is influenza A virus (IAV), which 
both is responsible for annual epidemics and is the only type known to cause pandemics. IAV 
is known for undergoing zoonosis and reverse zoonosis, especially jumping hosts between 
swine, birds and humans (Schrauwen & Fouchier, 2014). Evidence suggests that zoonotic 
events were the origin of at least 3 of the 4 major human IAV pandemics in the 20th and 21st 
century, the inaptly named “Spanish flu” of 1918 the deadliest among them was responsible 
for an estimated 50 million deaths (Schrauwen & Fouchier, 2014). The 2009 IAV pandemic 
was also of swine origin (Jameel, 2010), and although no human-to-human transmission of 
IAV has been observed in Denmark since then, two recent case studies found swine to human 
transmission of IAV (Jakob N. Nissen et al., 2021; Klara M. Andersen et al., 2022).  
The serious seasonal impact on human health and pandemic threat of IAV makes it particularly 
valuable to better understand virus-host interactions, information which is key for developing 
and improving countermeasures such as viral surveillance programs, vaccines and antiviral 
medication. This project aims to explore virus-host interactions of IAV through investigating 
the impact of the immunosuppressive IAV non-structural protein 1 (NS1) on antiviral gene-
expression. Furthermore, the project will investigate whether these immunosuppressive 
capabilities are translatable between different hosts using swine and human airway epithelial 
cells. 
The project will aim to prove or disprove the following hypotheses: 
1) The immunosuppressive capabilities of IAV are related to the viral NS1 protein variant 
and the expression of innate antiviral genes will vary depending on the origin of the NS 
gene segment. 
2) The IAV NS1 protein's capacity to suppress host antiviral responses is transferable 
between swine and humans.

2 Theoretical Background 
2.1 Influenza A Virus Structure 
Influenza A virus is an enveloped, segmented, single stranded, negative-sense RNA virus 
which belongs to the Orthomyxoviridae family (Gao et al., 2019). IAVs consist of a viral 
envelope with surface proteins, containing the genome which is coded in single stranded 
negative sense RNA and segmented onto 8 viral ribonuclear proteins (vRNP) (Dadonaite et al., 
2019). The viral envelope is supported by matrix 1 protein (M1) and consists of lipids from the 
cell membrane the virion budded from, along with 3 main surface proteins, hemagglutinin 
(HA), neuraminidase (NA) and membrane protein (M2) (Figure 1). These three proteins play 
crucial roles in cell entry, endosomal escape and release of newly budded viral particles. HA 
and NA variants additionally subcategorize IAVs into 198 subtypes consisting of 18 HA and 
11 NA variants (J. Jang & Bae, 2018). 
 
Figure 1 IAV virion structure. 8 segments of the IAV genome are attached to nuclear protein and the IAV polymerase. The 
genome is surrounded by the viral envelope, a lipid membrane consisting of the lipids of the previous hosts cell membrane. 
This membrane has nuclear export protein (NEP) attached to the inside, and 3 surface proteins, hemagglutinin, 
neuraminidase and Membrane protein. (Arranz et al., 2012)

The structure of the vRNPs is believed to be as shown on Figure 3, single stranded viral RNA 
is attached to nuclear proteins (NP) which form a double helical rod-like structure with the 
viral RNA-dependant RNA polymerase (RdRp) binding the 3’ and 5’ terminals at one end 
(Tomescu et al., 2014). Some regions of the genome are less bound to the NPs, and form double 
stranded protrusions from the main body of the structure which are believed to be important in 
replication and selective viral packaging (Noda, 2021) and play a major role in host detection 
of IAV.

The IAV RNA-dependent RNA polymerase is made from the 3 proteins: polymerase basic 1 
(PB1), polymerase basic 2 (PB2) and polymerase acidic (PA). These come together to make a 
polymerase capable of producing mRNA molecules from negative sense RNA, but unable to 
synthesize or methylate cap structures. As the capping of mRNA is essential to exploiting the 
host protein synthesis mechanisms, there are alternative mechanisms in place to ensure correct 
capping of the viral mRNA. For the 5’ cap, the IAV polymerase binds to a host polymerase II  
to get viral RNPs close to newly capped host mRNAs. PB2 has a 5’cap binding domain, which 
binds the 5’ regions of newly formed host mRNA, and PA has an endonuclease domain which 
cleaves it off 10-14 bases down from the cap. These caps are inserted into the viral polymerase, 
where they basepair with the RNA template, and are elongated to form 5’ capped positive sense 
mRNA. At the end of the transcription the poly-A tail is transcribed and the transcription 
terminated, resulting in capped and poly adenylated viral mRNA ready for translation (Fodor 
& Velthuis, 2020).

2.2 Influenza A Virus Replication Cycle 
To replicate, the virion must first enter the host cell by endocytosis. This is achieved by the 
two main surface proteins, HA and NA, working together to allow the virion to search the 
surface of the host cell for the correct binding to initiate endocytosis (Dou et al., 2018). HA 
binds terminal n-acetylneuraminic acid (sialic acid) of glycoconjugates on the host cell surface 
(Weis et al., 1988), and NA cleaves unproductive HA-sialic acid bonds to allow further 
exploration of the surface. Once endocytosis is triggered, the virion enters the host cell in an 
endosome which is gradually acidified through maturation and fusion with lysosomes, 
eventually reaching a pH as low as 5 (Dou et al., 2018). The low pH environment triggers a 
conformational change in HA which is then cleaved by host proteases allowing HA to enter 
the endosomal membrane and fuse the virion capsule to it, creating a passage between the 
inside of the virion and the cytosol (Bullough et al., 1994). Matrix protein 2 (M2) acidifies the 
virus particle by pumping protons in from the endosome, which disrupts the protein-protein 
interactions between the viral M1 matrix and the RNPs, allowing the RNPs to leave the virion 
and enter the host cell cytoplasm (Bouvier & Palese, 2008; Dou et al., 2018).

Once inside the cell cytoplasm, certain amino acid sequences on the NPs and RdRp known as 
nuclear localization signals are recognized by importin α, which recruits importin β leading to 
RNPs being trafficked to the nucleus of the host cell (Melén et al., 2003). Once inside the 
nucleus, viral RNA synthesis will take place mediated by the viral RdRp. Three kinds of vRNA 
will be synthesized here, positive sense capped polyadenylated messenger RNA (mRNA) as a 
template for viral proteins, positive sense complimentary RNA (cRNA) as a template for 
synthesizing more negative sense RNA, and the negative sense RNA copies for packing into 
new virions (Bouvier & Palese, 2008). The produced RNA can be transcribed by the RdRp as 
is, but to be exported and translated in host ribosomes, the viral RNA must look like host RNA 
with a poly-A tail at the 3’ end and a primer and cap at the 5’ end. Since IAV has no dedicated 
poly(A) polymerase, the negative sense viral genes simply have 5-7 uracil residues which are 
translated to a tail of adenosine by the RdRp. IAV has no RNA capping mechanism of its own 
but the PB1 and PB2 proteins of the RdRp facilitate binding to host polymerase II and stealing 
of 5’ capped primers from host pre-mRNA to be the starting point of viral transcription and 
serve as the 5’ end of viral mRNA (Fodor & Velthuis, 2020). Once the vRNA is capped and 
polyadenylated (and potentially alternatively spliced), nuclear export protein (NEP) (also 
known as the non-structural protein 2 (NS2)) and matrix protein 1 (M1) mediate export of the 
vRNA from the nucleus to the cytoplasm (Bouvier & Palese, 2008). 
The viral mRNA for the membrane proteins (HA, NA, M2) are translated, glycated and 
matured in the rough endoplasmic reticulum and Golgi complex, and the mRNA for the other 
viral proteins is translated by ribosomes in the cytosol (Han et al., 2019). The RdRp 
components (PB1, PB2 and PA) are then trafficked back to the nucleus due to their NLSs, 
where they can assemble with viral positive sense cRNA to form complementary RNPs (Dou 
et al., 2018). The cRNPs can then synthesize negative sense viral RNA to assemble with NPs 
and RdRp components to make new vRNPs. Newly translated NEP and M1 are also transported 
to the nucleus where they bind RdRps and facilitate recruitment of exportin 1 subsequently 
transporting newly assembled RdRps out of the nucleus (Dou et al., 2018). The PB2 of RdRps 
interacts with the host protein Rab11 and are transported to the cell membrane by the vesicular 
transport network (Amorim et al., 2011).

Once RNPs and the necessary viral proteins accumulate at the cell membrane, viral budding 
and escape can initiate. HA, NA and M1 accumulation at the cell membrane results in 
filamentous protrusions in the membrane (Chlanda et al., 2015), and the vRNPs are packed into 
the budding virion. For the newly formed virions to be infectious, it is essential that they all 
receive one of each vRNP. The mechanism is not fully understood, however research indicates 
a selective packing mechanism bundles 8 unique vRNPs together for packaging and localises 
them to the tip of the viral bud based the secondary and tertiary structure of the non-NP-bound 
RNA on the vRNPs (Figure 5) (Noda, 2021). As the buddings extend from the cell surface, HA 
in the budding membrane can bind sialic acid on the cell, but these are cleaved by NA and the 
newly formed virions are released to infect new cells and repeat the cycle (Dou et al., 2018).

2.3 Host Innate Immune Response to Influenza A Virus 
The host innate immune response to IAV infection begins with recognition of the virus by 
pattern recognition receptors (PRRs). There are several important PRRs for detection of IAV, 
among them Toll like receptors (TLRs) (Lee et al., 2013), Z-DNA-binding protein 1 (ZBP1) 
(Malireddi et al., 2023), and RIG-I-like receptors (RLRs) (Kato et al., 2006). The role of select 
TLRs and ZBP1 will be briefly summarized and the mechanism of RIGI will be discussed in 
more detail. 
==PAGE:17==
2 Theoretical Background 
Page 8 of 40 
 
TLR3 and TLR7 are transmembrane proteins on cytoplasmic vesicles such as endosomes, 
which sense double stranded RNA (dsRNA) and single stranded RNA (ssRNA) respectively, 
and have been shown to be upregulated in vivo in human cells during influenza infection (Lee 
et al., 2013). Upon activation, they initiate a cascade of signals which result in translation of 
inflammatory cytokines and type 1 interferons (Takeuchi & Akira, 2009). In the context of 
IAV, ZBP1 binds Z-RNA, RNA in a left-handed helix rather than the standard righthanded 
conformation. IAV produced Z-RNA in the nucleus of infected cells, where ZBP1 can bind it 
and initiates necroptosis in the infected cell (Zhang et al., 2020). 
Retinoic acid-inducible gene-I (RIGI), is essential for recognition of IAV (Kato et al., 2006). 
RIG-I is activated by dsRNA and is specifically activated by the panhandle structures on the 
IAV genome segments which serve as promoters for viral gene transcription (Liu et al., 2015). 
RIGI has been shown to exist mainly in the cytoplasm, but in increasing concentrations in the 
nucleus as infection progresses (Li et al., 2014). RIGI patrols the cell in an inactivated state, 
binding and releasing RNA until it finds its target. Once a target such as IAV vRNA is bound, 
large conformational changes are induced, and the caspase recruitment domains (CARDs) of 
RIG-I become accessible to E3 ubiquitin ligase (Likely tripartite motif 25 (TRIM25) (Gack et 
al., 2007) or Riplet (Oshiumi et al., 2010)) which ubiquitinates the CARDs, allowing for 
downstream signalling via mitochondrial antiviral signalling protein (MAVS). 
MAVS is a membrane protein found in various organelle membranes which upon binding to 
the CARDs of activated RIGI oligomerises with other MAVS monomers, forming an activated 
complex (Thoresen et al., 2021). Activated MAVS results in activation of nuclear factor kappa-
light-chain-enhancer of activated B cells (NF-κβ) and interferon regulatory factor (IRF) 3, 
which ultimately lead to expression of type-I interferons (Seth et al., 2005). This mechanism 
involves the E3 ligases TNF-α-associated recruitment factors (TRAFs) which are recruited to 
the activated MAVS and make polyubiquitin chains which are released and likely recognized 
by and activate an inhibitor of NF-κβ kinase (IKKϵ) and TANK-binding kinase 
(TBK1)(Thoresen et al., 2021). These kinases phosphorylate the transcription factors interferon 
regulatory factor 3 (IRF3) and interferon regulatory factor 7 (IRF7) which, along with the 
activated NF-κβ, triggers expression of type 1 and type 3 interferons and antiviral cytokines 
(Rashid et al., 2023). 
The interferons can exit the cell and interact with interferon receptors on the outside of the 
same or neighbouring cells membranes. This releases receptor bound tyrosine kinase 2 (TYK2)

and Janus kinase 1 (JAK1), which mediate phosphorylation of signal transducer and activator 
of transcription 1 and 2 (STAT1 and STAT2), making heterodimers that associate with 
interferon regulatory factor (IRF9) which stimulates expression of interferon stimulated genes 
(ISGs) (Majoros et al., 2017). This amplifies the antiviral response in the infected and 
neighbouring cells. 
A major part of the host antiviral response is upregulation of translation of ISGs, making 
hundreds of different proteins which inhibit viral replication through different mechanisms 
(Perng & Lenschow, 2018). Describing all these proteins and mechanisms is beyond the scope 
of this project, however the antiviral ISG proteins for which the translation levels are 
investigated in this project are briefly described.  
ISG15 is a ubiquitin-like protein, which can be bound to host or viral proteins by an E3 ligase, 
in a process called ISGylation. ISGylation of the NS1 protein of IAV inhibits its 
immunosuppressing effects (Tang et al., 2010). ISGylation also targets many host proteins 
involved in the antiviral response such as RIGI, STAT1, PKR, and TRIM25, and broadly 
speaking can limit tissue damage and modulate interferon 1 signalling (Perng & Lenschow, 
2018). Unconjugated ISG15 can also function as a cytokine and regulate release of other 
cytokines (Perng & Lenschow, 2018). 
OAS1 is a part of the oligoadenylate synthases (OAS) family of ISGs which are known for 
synthesizing 2´-5´ oligoadenylates to induce general RNA degradation in the cell. A type 1 
interferon response induces OAS1 translation, which is produced as an inactive enzyme and is 
activated by dsRNA (Melchjorsen et al., 2009). This leads to synthesis of 2´-5´ oligoadenylates, 
activating RNAse L, which degrades viral and cellular RNA supressing viral synthesis 
(Melchjorsen et al., 2009). 
 
OASL is another member of the OAS family, but it cannot synthesize oligoadenylates.  IRF3 
induces OASL transcription (Melchjorsen et al., 2009), which by its ubiquitin-like domain can 
bind RIGI, mimicking the polyubiquitination of TRIM25 or Riplet and thereby enhance 
antiviral signalling (Zhu et al., 2014). 
MX1 (MX dynamin like GTPase 1) is a GTPase which binds vRNPs at NP and PB2 proteins 
and disturbs the PB2-NP interaction through an unknown enzymatic mechanism requiring GTP

(Verhelst et al., 2012). This interaction inhibits viral polymerase activity, which supresses viral 
transcription and replication. 
RSAD2 (Radical S-Adenosyl Methionine Domain Containing 2), also known as viperin, is an 
antiviral protein which inhibits IAV budding and release by decreasing the number of lipid 
rafts in the cell membrane, which have been shown to be the budding site of IAV, through 
interaction with fanesyl diphosphate synthase (FPPS) (Wang et al., 2007). 
EIF2AK2 (Eukaryotic Translation Initiation Factor 2 Alpha Kinase 2), also known as PKR, is 
a protein kinase which when associated with dsRNA phosphorylates eIF2α, inhibiting all 
protein synthesis in the cell, including vRNA, which supresses viral replication (Okumura et 
al., 2013). ISGylation can activate PKR in the place of dsRNA (Okumura et al., 2013). 
2.4 Immunosuppressing mechanisms of IAV 
IAV has evolved in reaction to the selection pressure of the innate immune system, and as such 
many IAV proteins have immune suppressing effects. Especially the IAV polymerase subunits, 
HA and most importantly the NS proteins have been shown to elicit anti-immune effects 
through various mechanisms (Rashid et al., 2023). The polymerase subunits are a good 
example of this, as they target different steps in the previously discussed innate antiviral 
response. PB1 destabilises MAVS and promotes degradation of ubiquitinated MAVS, 
impeding the host antiviral response (Zeng et al., 2021). PB2 promotes the polyubiquitination 
of JAK1 leading to proteasomal degradation of JAK1, disrupting the JAK/STAT pathway 
upregulation of ISGs, meaning the host sensitivity to type 1 interferon signalling is decreased 
(Yang et al., 2022). PA inhibits type 1 interferon signalling, specifically interferon-β, through 
interaction with IRF3 and blocking its activation (Yi et al., 2017). PA-X is a small fusion 
protein originating from the PA gene segment of IAV, which also has been shown to have 
various immunomodulatory functions, especially related to inhibition of host gene transcription 
(Hu et al., 2018).  
The arguably most important, and very extensively studied IAV immune supressing protein is 
NS1. NS1 has been shown to supress the host immune response through many unique 
mechanisms, some of which will be briefly described here. One important mechanism through 
which NS1 reduces host immune response is binding to TRIM25, which blocks its 
ubiquitination of RIGI, thus inhibiting the main pathway of IAV recognition (Gack et al., 
2009). NS1 from human adapted IAV has also been shown to block the RIGI ubiquitination 
through interaction with the E3 ligase Riplet (Jureka et al., 2019; Rajsbaum et al., 2012). NS1 
==PAGE:20==

also has an dsRNA binding domain, which has been shown to be reduce IAV sensitivity to the 
effects of RNAse L, likely because NS1 binding of dsRNA is blocking OAS access, impeding 
the RNAse L activation (Min & Krug, 2006). NS1 also inhibits the reduction of host 
transcription and translation by binding to PKR and blocking its phosphorylation of eIF2α 
(Kim et al., 2024; Lu et al., 1995). 
The list of proposed and confirmed immune suppressing mechanisms of IAV proteins is long 
and ever growing, and the discussed mechanisms is only a small selection of the mechanisms 
most relevant for the present project. In summary, several IAV proteins have immune 
modulatory effects through various unique mechanisms targeting many of the steps in the 
antiviral immune response. Of these proteins, NS1 is the most important for modulating dsRNA 
mediated antiviral immune response (Han et al., 2019), and will be the focus of this project.

3.2 RNA Purification 
The first step of sample analysis from the infection experiment was RNA purification. The 
samples were stored in DNA/RNA Lysis Buffer (Zymo Research), which mainly contains 
guanidinium thiocyanate and some N-Lauroylsarcosine Sodium salt. This is used to lyse the 
cells, inactivates the virions, and denature and solubilises proteins. Using Quick RNA™ 
Microprep Kits (Zymo Research), the samples were mixed with ethanol and spun through 
columns, binding the RNA to the column. The columns were then washed with buffer, treated 
with DNAse to destroy genomic DNA and washed with buffer to remove proteins, lipids, 
sugars and salts. Lastly, nuclease-free water was added to the columns to collect the purified 
RNA. 
3.3 Spectroscopic Analysis (NanoDrop) 
The purified RNA samples were analysed by UV-vis absorption spectroscopy by a NanoDrop 
One/OneC Spectrophotometer (Thermo Fischer Scientific) to assess the RNA concentration 
and purity. The spectrometer analyses the samples by comparing the absorption of the sample 
to a blank at wavelengths associated with RNA and common contaminants. Nucleic acids have 
a peak UV absorption at 260 nm due to absorption from aromatic structures of the bases, and 
proteins and phenolic compounds have high absorption at 280 nm from the aromatic structures 
of phenol and amino acid side chains. Peptide bonds of proteins, some chaotropic salts as well 
as many organic compounds absorb UV at 230 nm.  
The NanoDrop returns 3 measurements for RNA, the concentration, which is based on the 260 
nm absorption (Koetsier et al., 2019), and the 260/230 and 260/280 ratios. The 260/280 should 
be above 1.8 for RNA to be suitable for gene expression experiments (Becker et al., 2010) and 
is 2.1 for pure RNA (Koetsier et al., 2019). The 260/230 ratio should also be above 1.8, and is 
2.1-2.3 for pure RNA. 260/230 ratios can tend to have high standard deviations, especially at 
low RNA concentrations, and should be interpreted with care (Koetsier et al., 2019).

3.4 Electrophoretic Analysis (Bioanalyzer) 
To assess RNA integrity, the samples were subjected to electrophoretic analysis in an Agilent 
2100 Bioanalyzer with an Agilent RNA 6000 Nano Kit (#5067-1511) using the Expert 2100 
software (Agilent Technologies). The Bioanalyzer uses microfluidic electrophoresis, allowing 
for small sample volumes to be used, and measures the RNA via laser-induced fluorescence 
detection (Schroeder et al., 2006). Based on the resulting graph, the software then assigns an 
RNA integrity number (RIN) to the samples from 1-10 with 1 representing very degraded RNA, 
and 10 representing highly intact RNA. The RIN is calculated by an algorithm that takes into 
account the size of the 18S and 28S peaks (for eukaryotic cells) compared to the total area 
under the curve, the height of the 28S peak, the area under the curve in the “fast region” and 
the size of the marker peak (Schroeder et al., 2006). The basic principle is that the 18S and 28S 
fragments make up most of the RNA in eukaryotic cells, meaning highly intact RNA will look 
very similar on the graph, even between very different RNA samples. In essence, un-degraded 
RNA will mainly consist of a large 18S peak and 28S peak along with a small marker peak and 
a small 5S peak, and little to no signal in the fast and inter regions. As the RNA gets more 
degraded, the size of the ribosomal RNA peaks will decrease, and their fragments show up as 
signal at earlier retention times. This is illustrated in Figure 6. A study from 2006 (Fleige & 
Pfaffl, 2006) analysed the importance of RIN in reproducibility of q-PCR experiments, and 
recommend RIN above 5 to be considered good RNA quality and RIN above 8 to be perfect. 
 
 
Figure 6 Typical RNA integrity graphs for RIN 10 and RIN 6. For highly intact RNA, the S18 and S28 peaks are high, and the 
fast and inter regions have little signal. For more degraded RNA, the ribosomal RNA peaks are smaller, and their fractions 
show up as signal in the inter and fast regions and under the marker peak. Adapted from (Schroeder et al., 2006)

3.5 Microfluidic Real Time Quantitative Polymerase Chain Reaction 
Microfluidic real time quantitative polymerase chain reaction (qPCR) is a widely used method 
for quantifying RNA in samples and was used in the present project to quantify mRNA of 
innate immune genes. Microfluidic qPCR utilizes microfluidic chips, such as the ones 
developed by Fluidigm, which through careful control of microfluidic valves allow PCR 
reactions at a 10 nanoliter scale and for thousands of reactions to run simultaneously on one 
chip (J. S. Jang et al., 2011).  
The first step for two step real time qPCR is cDNA synthesis in which nucleotides, random 
primers and oligo-dT primers, and reverse transcriptase are incubated to generate DNA 
complementary to all the RNA in the sample. The second step is the qPCR reaction which 
requires four main components: cDNA samples, primers specific to the genes of interest, 
nucleotides, thermostable DNA polymerase, and a fluorescence dye. The experiment is set up 
with controls including one with no reverse transcriptase in the cDNA synthesis to check for 
DNA contamination in the RNA samples, and one with no cDNA in the qPCR to check for any 
other DNA contaminations.  
 
The number of cycles, temperatures and thermocycling program in general can vary between 
experiments, but for the qPCRs in the present project the microfluidic qPCR chip with all the 
samples and primers was subjected to a thermocycling program as seen on Figure 7. First 
samples were thermally mixed, then the samples were incubated at 50 ℃ for two minutes to 
let uracil-N-glycosylase (UNG) degrade any PCR product contaminations that may have 
been present (Epstein & Hewlett, 1993). The samples were then heated to 95 ℃ for 10 
minutes to activate the hot-start Taq DNA polymerase, which has a reversibly bound 
inhibitor preventing transcription at lower temperatures, but which is released at PCR 
cycling temperatures (Thermo Fisher Scientific, n.d.).  
The samples were then subjugated to 35 cycles of denaturing, annealing and extension. The 
qPCR reactions were kept at 95 ℃ for 15 seconds to ensure nothing is bound to the DNA 
and that it was melted to ssDNA. The PCR reactions were then cooled to 60 ℃ for 60 
seconds where primers and Taq DNA polymerase anneal to the single stranded DNA 
which are then extended to form double stranded DNA.

Figure 7 qPCR thermal protocol used for microfluidic real time qPCR in the present study. Samples are thermally mixed,

The qPCR machine (BioMark HD (Standard BioTools) in the present project) is essentially a 
thermocycler with a fluorimeter and a light source. At the end of each amplification cycle, the 
light source excites the active fluorescent dyes in the PCR reaction, and the fluorimeter makes 
a measurement for all the reactions. There are two common systems of fluorescent dyes, 
dsDNA binding dyes and target specific probes, which are activated when DNA polymerase 
breaks the probe, and when bound to double stranded DNA respectively (Applied Biosystems, 
2014). The number of active dyes will scale with the amount of dsDNA generated in the PCR 
reaction, leading to the fluorimeter measuring an increasingly higher fluorescence signal each 
cycle proportional to the number of DNA molecules. These measurements are output as 
amplification curves, and at a cycle number where the signal is above background noise and 
the fluorescence is growing exponentially, a Cq value is calculated as a measure for the relative 
RNA concentration for the gene of interest in the sample.  
After the last amplification cycle, the PCR reaction products are slowly heated, and exited and 
measured for fluorescence many times during the process, measuring the dissociation of 
dsDNA binding dyes. This results in a melting curve for the PCR products, which is typically 
displayed as a the rate of change in fluorescence as a function of temperature (Applied 
Biosystems, 2014). From the melting curves, uniformity of the PCR product and presence of 
primer dimer, products from unspecific primer binding and contaminants can be seen as peaks 
corresponding to the melting point of the product or contaminant.

RNA Extraction 
An overview of the workflow of all the analysis experiments can be found on Figure 9. 
The RNA was extracted utilizing Quick RNA™ Microprep Kits (Zymo Research). The 
extraction was carried out in accordance with the protocol accompanying the kit with minor 
adjustments. Ethanol (300 µL, 100%) was added to each sample and mixed well. The mixture

The columns were washed with RNA Prep Buffer (400 µL), centrifuged (30 sec, 12,000 g) and 
the flowthrough discarded. RNA Wash Buffer (700 µL) was added, columns centrifuged (30 
sec, 12,000 g) and the flowthrough discarded. RNA wash buffer (400 µL) was added and the 
samples centrifuged (2 min, 12,000 g). The columns were transferred to a nuclease-free tube, 
nuclease-free water (15 µL) was added, and the columns centrifuged (30 sec, 12,000 g). The 
samples were analysed by NanoDrop and stored at -20 ℃.

Pre-Processing and Data Analysis 
The qPCR data was initially pre-processed in Fluidigm Real-Time PCR Analysis Software (v. 
4.8.1) where data with abnormal melting points and amplification curves was removed from 
the dataset. Next, samples with >5% signal from gDNA were identified from comparison to 
the -RT sample and removed. Additionally, the no template control (NTC) samples were 
examined to ensure no DNA contamination had occurred. The dilution curve samples were 
used to calculate the PCR amplification efficiency, and primers with 80% - 120% efficiency 
and R2 above 0.95 were accepted. Amplification efficiency could not be calculated for the 
RPMI-2650 cells due to poor results from the pool samples. 
The Ct values for all the remaining samples after pre-processing were exported to Microsoft 
Excel (V. 2410) where the data was further processed. First, the data was efficiency corrected 
using the PCR amplification efficiency, except for RPMI-2650.  
The technical variation of the cDNA replicates was calculated, and any sample with a technical 
variation greater than 1,5 Cq in more than 15% of the technical replicates had either one or 
both replicates removed. As a rule, both replicates would be removed, unless the melting point 
and amplification graphs in the Fluidigm software clearly showed a problem with only one

replicate, in which case only that one was removed. Then primers with more than 15% technical

Quantitative PCR 
The quantitative PCR was carried out with 1 chip for the porcine cells (NPTr) and one chip for 
the human cells (Calu-3 and RPMI-2650). To simplify further data analysis, one primer pair 
was chosen to represent each gene of interest in the human cell lines. This was possible because 
the correlation between primer pairs for the same gene of interest was high, and the associated 
p-value low. For the selection of the primer pair to represent each gene, the main priorities 
were to select the same primer pair for both human cell lines (Calu-3 and RPMI-2650) and for 
the expected reproducibility of the results to be the highest. Expected reproducibility is hard to 
gauge from a small number of replicates, but the primer pairs which had most samples with 
low technical variation were expected to be more reproducible. Furthermore, the cell line 
showing the most regulated genes of interest was considered the best to judge the 
reproducibility in. This is because upregulated genes have more mRNA in the samples, which 
makes small technical variations more visible between the replicates when comparing Cq 
values. Based on this, first the genes which had only one primer pair remaining after data 
processing and pre-processing in either cell line, had this primer pair was selected for both 
human cell lines. For the remaining genes of interest, the primer with most technical replicates 
below 0.5 Cq technical variation were selected.  
During the data processing, averages were taken of the biological replicates, and they were 
scaled to be foldchanges compared to their control samples. The biological replicates which 
had a p-value below 0.05 from a t-test against their controls, and an up- or downregulation of 
at least 100% compared to their control, were displayed with their standard deviations in Table

Dashes ( - ) were used for replicate averages which did not meet the requirements for regulation 
level and p-value, and N/A was used where up- or downregulation is not estimated due to 
removal of data in pre-processing or processing. For the comparison of cell lines, the focus will 
be on samples from 48 hours post infection because the highest number of regulated genes

Quality, selection, and validity of data 
To assess the validity of the results obtained from the qPCR experiments, the quality control 
results, consistency between replicates, and data processing and selection must be discussed.  
Regarding quality control, the NanoDrop measurements had approximately 30 measurements 
outside the expected range for the 260/230 ratios, but analysis of the samples was continued, 
and the concentrations from the same analysis were used to standardise the amount of RNA for 
each sample in the cDNA synthesis. A concern could be that the 260/230 readings were valid 
and that contaminations significantly influenced the data in the further analysis. This is 
however deemed unlikely based on the following arguments. Firstly, contaminants absorbing 
in the 230 nm region, such as phenolic compounds, ethanol or EDTA, would lower the 
expected 260/230 ratio of approximately 2 - 2.2, but both ratios above 4 and below 1 were 
observed. This would indicate a second contaminant in the 260 nm region, or a contaminant 
such as protein which absorbs at both wavelengths, but consistent 260/280 ratios at the 
expected value of approximately 2 were observed in all the samples, making this explanation 
unlikely.  
An issue with the blank could also influence the ratios, but the strange 260/230 ratios persisted 
through use of different blanks. Finally, the 260/230 ratio could be unreliable below certain 
RNA concentrations. The most extreme outliers are related to very low RNA concentrations, 
but others, especially too high 260/230 absorption measurements, are observed at relatively 
high concentrations. Experiments behind a technical note from New England Biolabs (Koetsier 
et al., 2019) describe 260/230 absorption measurements to be unreliable below 20 ng/mL and 
quite variable between 20-50 ng/mL, usually displaying too high values. It also describes the 
260/230 ratio to be especially variable compared to 260/280 ratios and that concentration 
estimates are relatively accurate down to 5 ng/mL. These observations seem to explain the 
patterns of outliers seen in this project, indicating that the NanoDrop results are not an 
indication of contaminated RNA samples and that the concentration estimates can be trusted. 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:42==
6 Discussion 
Page 33 of 40 
 
Further supporting this conclusion, the main expected result of (non-nuclease and non-nucleic 
acid) contaminations is inhibition of processes such as cDNA synthesis or amplification, and 
poor RNA concentration estimations could result in inconsistent RNA amounts added for the 
cDNA synthesis. Unequally inhibited cDNA synthesis and pre-amplification between samples, 
or inconsistent start RNA concentrations would likely result in large biological replicate 
variations due to varying cDNA concentrations in the qPCR experiment. Contamination issues 
could also potentially inhibit the amplification in the qPCR experiment, and result in low 
amplification efficiencies. Neither unexpectedly large biological replicate variation nor low 
amplification efficiencies were observed, providing further evidence that RNA sample 
contaminations were minimal. In conclusion, the unexpected patterns in the NanoDrop 260/230 
are likely due to variable results at low and medium RNA concentrations as described in 
(Koetsier et al., 2019) and no evidence of significant contamination is observed in the qPCR 
data. 
 
The data which was presented and analysed was selected based on two criteria, which were 
gene regulations of more than 100%, and p-values of less than 0.05, in infected cells compared 
to control cells. The cutoff at 100% gene regulation is commonly used in the literature, and 
successfully removed all putative false positives seen in the reference gene panel.  The p-value 
from the t-test is usually used as a measure of statistical significance, however this is not 
possible in this project. Although t-tests are designed to allow for low sample numbers, they 
require the samples to be normally distributed and independent. The replicates in the infection 
experiment were cells from the same culture, so they cannot be considered entirely 
independent, and since there are only 2-3 of these replicates after data processing, it cannot be 
proven that they are normally distributed. Instead, in this project high p-values are considered 
to indicate low likelihood of reproducibility of the data and are therefore used to further filter 
the presented and analysed data. In practice, only datapoints with fold-changes close to the 
100% regulation cutoff limit are removed, and due to the low number of replicates and 
subsequently high uncertainty of the results, the potential removal of some potentially valid 
datapoints near the cutoff limit is considered uncritical. In summary, the cutoff limit of 100% 
gene regulation and p-value of 0.05 attempt to remove unreproducible datapoints while 
conserving potentially valid ones, despite statistical significance not being calculable. 
==PAGE:43==
6 Discussion 
Page 34 of 40 
 
The almost equal performance of the primer pairs for the same gene resulted in difficulty in 
selecting a metric which accurately reflects reproducibility. However, due to the primer pairs’ 
performance being comparable, the reproducibility is also expected to be similar. This means 
that achieving an objective, consistent way of selecting the primer pair is the most important, 
as both primers likely are equally good choices. In conclusion, primer pairs are selected in an 
objective manner which attempts to prioritise primer pairs showing results with highest 
reproducibility. While it is uncertain whether the primer pair with the highest reproducibility 
is selected, this is likely inconsequential because reproducibility between the primer pairs is 
expected to be highly similar. 
To sum up, it is concluded that absorption spectroscopy and examination of technical variations 
do not show evidence of significant levels of contamination in the RNA samples. It is further 
concluded that although statistical significance is not calculable from the replicates, objective 
metrics have been used to select what qPCR data to present and further analyse. Due to the lack 
of statistical evidence and certainty concerning the exact foldchanges, qPCR data should be 
interpreted accordingly. 
Cell line and virus comparison 
One important factor when comparing the gene regulation of the cell lines is how successfully 
the viruses were able to replicate in them. It seemed that SV179 had an advantage over SV185, 
which was clearly demonstrated in swine cells (NPTr) but less so in human cells (Calu-3 and 
RPMI-2650). This poses the question whether the virus titers 48 hours post infection are 
reflected in the qPCR data. Evidence of different mechanisms could be expected in the data; 
the more successfully replicating virus may have better NS1 driven immune evasion, resulting 
in less regulated innate immune response. Alternatively, the virus which has a higher titer might 
cause a greater innate immune response due to the higher concentration of PAMPs. In the 
second case, the advantage of better immune evasion may be reflected in the higher viral titers 
rather than a lower number of regulated genes. 
The hypothesis that a higher virus titer causes a stronger innate immune response seems to be 
supported by NPTr qPCR data where the high viral titer of SV179 in infected porcine cells was 
accompanied by a higher number of regulated antiviral genes (10 out of 16) and the lower titer 
of SV185 resulted in few regulated genes (1 out of 16) 48 hours post infection. The human 
Calu-3 cell line also follows this trend and has 11 out of 11 antiviral genes regulated after 
infection with SV179 and SV185 alike, matching their high titers 48 hours post infection. In 
==PAGE:44==
6 Discussion 
Page 35 of 40 
 
contrast, SV179 infected human RPMI-2650 cells have fewer regulated genes than when 
infected with SV185 despite the SV185 viral titer being slightly higher. When broadening the 
scope to all the analysed viruses and cell lines in the infection experiment, all the cells showed 
a high number of regulated innate immune genes at high virus titers (>106 PFU/mL) and most 
showed a low number of regulated immune genes at lower titers. In conclusion, the titer and 
qPCR data supports the hypothesis that a larger number of regulated antiviral genes is related 
to high viral titers, and that better immune evasion is potentially better reflected in virus titers 
rather than number of regulated genes. Assuming that higher titers reflect better immune 
evasion, SV179 seems to have an advantage over SV185, especially in porcine NPTr cells. 
When comparing the two viruses, SV179 and SV185, it is argued that host immune regulation 
differences are attributed to the differing NS segments, even though many IAV proteins have 
been shown to have immunomodulatory effects (Rashid et al., 2023). To assess the validity of 
this claim, the sequence differences between the two viruses were compared after genetic 
sequencing and translation into protein sequences. Pairwise comparison, confirmed that the 
main differences between the two viruses were found in the viral NS gene. Although this does 
not guarantee that the observed regulation differences are not a product of changes in other 
genes, it indicates changes in NS to be of importance. This is in part concluded due to the larger 
changes in the NS segment and is further supported by NS1 being considered the most 
important regulator of host immune genes during influenza virus infections (Han et al., 2019).  
The difference was further explored by specifically examining known immune protein binding 
sites on the viral NS1 protein investigating whether any of these were different between SV179 
and SV185. A full analysis of every important binding site is beyond the scope of this project, 
however two interesting differences were discovered. Amino acid 207 on NS1 is a crucial part 
of the binding site for the interferon induced protein IFI35, which is involved in regulating host 
antiviral response (Wang et al., 2023). N207 has been shown to be associated with lower 
virulence in IAV, and NS1s of pandemic IAVs from the 21st century have D207 instead of 
N207, and drastic increases in D207 can be observed in viruses after the pandemic in H1N1 
(2009), H7N9 (2013), H5N1 (1997 and 2003)  (Wang et al., 2023). Consistently with this, the 
pandemic NS1 gene in our virus SV179 has D207, whereas the Eurasian avian-like NS1 in 
SV185 has N207.  
The other interesting difference is in the 21st amino acid position of NS1, which is important 
in binging NS1 to the CARD domain of RIGI, involved in viral recognition and kick starting 
==PAGE:45==
6 Discussion 
Page 36 of 40 
 
the defence against viral infections. It has been shown that a mutation from 21R to 21Q in NS1 
can completely disrupt this binding, nullifying the beneficial immune supressing effect on the 
host (Jureka et al., 2019). This exact mutation is present between the viruses examined in this 
project, with 21R in the pandemic NS1 variant (SV179) and 21Q in the Eurasian avian-like 
variant (SV185). To sum up, the largest difference between SV179 and SV185 is in the gene 
segment coding for the most important viral regulator of host immune antiviral response, NS1. 
Within NS1 two differences in position 207 and 21 both favour virulence in SV179 over 
SV185, by facilitating suppression of antiviral immune responses in the host. This could 
potentially explain the higher virus titers and following higher number of activated immune 
genes observed in the plaque assay and qPCR data. 
The original hypothesis intended to be explored in the second microfluidic qPCR set was 
whether the difference in gene expression regulating properties between SV185 and SV179 in 
swine airway epithelial cells (NPTr) were observable in human airway epithelial cells (Calu-3 
and RPMI-2650). The same regulation properties were not observed in human cells as Calu-3 
had no difference in the number of expressed genes after infection with SV185 and SV179, 
and RPMI-2650 had more expressed genes after infection with SV185 then SV179. This is 
somewhat unsurprising considering the titer and qPCR data comparison, as the number of 
regulated genes seems more corelated to the titer than the strain or viral NS1 sequence. When 
focusing on the viral titers, the data does seem to indicate some replication of immune evasion 
properties between species, although vague. SV179 was shown to have a clear advantage over 
SV185 in the porcine NPTr cells, a slight advantage in human RPMI cells, and no difference 
in human Calu-3 cells. There are very few datapoints for this comparison, and plaque assays 
were carried out with only one replicate, so the reproducibility of the results is uncertain, and 
it would be interesting to carry out the experiment on more cell types and in more replicates to 
verify. 
In the present project, it is not certain how much of the observed difference between porcine 
tracheal NPTr cells and human lung epithelium Calu-3 cells or human nasal epithelium RPMI-
2650 cells is due to the different species, and how much is due to the different organs of cell 
line origin. Although they are all airway epithelial cells, immune responses to the same 
pathogen can vary drastically between organs, and even between parts of the same organ such 
as the upper and lower trachea of swine (Laybourn et al., 2024). To be certain the difference 
observed between cell lines can be attributed to the species, cells from the same part of the 
same organ should be used. If another experiment was to be carried out seeking to reproduce 
==PAGE:46==
6 Discussion 
Page 37 of 40 
 
and validate the apparent effect of NS1 on virus titers in cells, tracheal cells of both human and 
porcine origin could be used, and potentially nasal and lung cells too depending on the size of 
the project. True biological replicates for each cell line cultured in parallel, and potentially 
more than one cell line from each organ would massively bolster the significance of the results. 
 
Immortalized cells as a model 
The ultimate aim of working with the porcine (NPTr) and human (Calu-3 and RPMI-2650) cell 
lines in this project is to cheaply and ethically model what happens in the innate immune 
response of humans and pigs when infected with different IAVs. There are however major 
differences between live animals in their environment and cells in a laboratory, which is bound 
to be reflected in the measured immune response.  
To assess the translatability of the results seen in cells to live animals, the innate immune gene 
regulations observed in porcine cells (NPTr) infected with SV179 in this project were 
compared to the results seen in vivo in pigs (Laybourn et al., 2024). In that study, tissue from 
the trachea of pigs was collected 3 days after inoculation with a swine adapted H1N1 IAV 
(A/Swine/Denmark/3974/2017) (Laybourn et al., 2024). In agreement with the present study 
they observed a significant upregulation of many of the same innate immune genes as seen in 
SV179 infected porcine NPTr cells including RIGI (3.2), ZBP1 (2.8), DDX60 (2.3), RSAD2 
(3.3), MX1 (3.3), OAS1 (3.2) and ISG15 (3.1) (Laybourn et al., 2024). The fold changes 
observed in the present porcine NPTr cells were generally higher for the aforementioned genes, 
between 4 (± 0.9) and 32 (±18). The previously discussed high uncertainty of the NPTr cell 
results makes direct comparison of foldchanges in individual genes difficult, however the 
consistent higher expression levels is interesting. The higher levels are not just seen in the 
experiment from this project, a paper from 2018 included an experiment with porcine NPTr 
cells infected with swine adapted IAV (A/Giessen/06/09) and showed foldchanges of 
approximately 600 in MX1 and 16 in OAS1 48 hours post infection (Petersen et al., 2018) 
compared to the fold changes of 4 (±0.9) for MX1 and 8 (±0.9) for OAS1 in the present study. 
Many factors could explain the observed differences between the cell studies and in vivo study. 
Firstly, the consistently higher levels of gene regulation in the cell studies could potentially be 
explained by the controls. The controls used for the cell studies are in sterile conditions and 
should represent levels of innate immune gene mRNA with no immune stimulation, whereas 
the control pigs are not in sterile environments. This could be speculated to result in some 
==PAGE:47==
6 Discussion 
Page 38 of 40 
 
baselevel of immune stimulation in the control pigs which is not seen in cell controls, leading 
to higher observed foldchanges in cell experiments. The extreme fold changes observed, for 
instance for MX1 (609) in the 2018 cell experiment by Petersen and co-workers, may also be 
unique to cell studies, as no foldchanges above 5 were observed for the swine adapted IAV 
infected pigs (Laybourn et al., 2024). One possible explanation could be the porcine NPTr cells 
existing in a monoculture, void of interactions with other cells or the broader immune system 
of the organism. Extreme fold changes in immune related genes found in vitro would be deadly 
in vivo, as exemplified by cytokine storms caused by the 1918 pandemic H1N1 IAV (Petersen 
et al., 2018), and therefore immune responses must be modulated in living organisms. This 
immune modulation may explain why no extreme spikes in fold changes are observed in the 
pigs but are present both in the Petersen and colleagues cell study (MX1) and the Calu-3 cells 
in the present project (CXCL10 and RSAD2).  
The differences observed between the results obtained in cells and in vivo seem to demonstrate 
that cell studies in immortalised cell lines such as porcine NPTr cells can be useful in showing 
what general tendencies to expect in vivo, but cannot be expected to replicate the exact results. 
This unsurprising conclusion reflects the major differences between sterile monocultures of 
immortalized cells and the complex environment and diversity of cells in vivo. These 
differences can be reduced by employing more complex models, aiming to increase the 
resemblance between the models results and results in vivo. Respiratory air-liquid interface 
culturing can be used to differentiate certain cell types, such as basal cells, into various 
epithelial cell types, better representing the composition of the mucus membrane in vivo 
(Walters et al., 2013). Another layer of complexity can be added by emulating the 3D structure 
of the studied organ by employing “organ on a chip” models (M. Zhang et al., 2021) With 
increasing complexity, the models better emulate the conditions in vivo and therefore are likely 
to have results more representative of in vivo experiments, but the increased complexity comes 
at a financial and time cost. While it is unlikely that such models can completely replace in 
vivo models, their higher resemblance to in vivo conditions may be able to sort out some 
phenomena unique to immortal cell monocultures before investing resources investigating in 
live animals. This, along with improvements in the field of simulation in silico, can hopefully 
help to drastically reduce the number of animals necessary to make and verify immunological 
discoveries. 
In conclusion, monocultured immortalized cells have their place in the research of the innate 
immune system for identifying potential effects in a relatively cheap and ethically sound 
==PAGE:48==
6 Discussion 
Page 39 of 40 
 
manner. Although in vivo experiments will likely always be necessary to verify these effects, 
more complex models may be able to reduce the number of live animals necessary to 
investigate the effects by filtering out effects unique to monocultured cells.  
This project aimed to explore virus-host interactions of IAV through the lens of NS1 and its 
immunosuppressive effect on host antiviral gene expression. This was achieved by the 
investigation of viral titers and regulation of transcription of innate immune genes for human 
and porcine cells infected with swine adapted IAVs with differing NS1 segments. The results 
seem to confirm the first hypothesis, that the immunosuppressive capabilities of IAV are 
related to the viral NS1 protein variant and the expression of innate antiviral genes will vary 
depending on the origin of the NS gene segment.  
The project further aimed to investigate these immunosuppressive capabilities and whether 
they are translatable between hosts. This was investigated in the comparison between the 
number of regulated innate immune genes and titers in human and porcine origin airway 
epithelial cells. The number of innate immune genes regulated seemed to better reflect viral 
titer than NS1 variant, and the viral titers showed SV179 at higher titers than SV185 in the 
porcine NPTr cells, but equal in the human Calu-3 cells and only slightly higher in human 
RPMI-2650 cells. This somewhat supports the second hypothesis that the IAV NS1 protein's 
capacity to suppress host antiviral responses is transferable between swine and humans, but is 
inconclusive and is further complicated by the fact that the cells do not originate from the same 
organ.

7 Conclusion 
NPTr cells infected with SV179 with the pandemic NS1 are observed to have markedly fewer 
innate immune genes regulated than cells infected with SV185 with the EAS NS1. Neither 
infected Calu-3 nor RPMI-2650 show the same trend for SV179 and SV185 infected cells. It 
is concluded that the number of regulations is not well corelated to the NS1 variant of the virus, 
however it is observed to be well correlated to the viral titer measured in the cells. Assuming 
better immune evasion to be correlated with higher viral titers, the pandemic NS1 of SV179 
seems to provide an advantage over the EAS NS1 of SV185, as SV179 displayed consistent 
equal or higher titers in the 3 cell lines. In line with this, two differences are identified in the 
protein sequence of the pandemic and EAS NS1, which according to literature should favour 
the pandemic NS1 (Jureka et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2023).
