Abstract
This thesis describes practical implementation and use of a next-generation prototype for
self-tracking using gesture interfaces. The prototype allows users to capture subjective
experiences with the press of a button, and adds modality to occurrence data, in an ef-
fort to enhance current work on self-tracking devices and reduce burden for users. We
implement a prototype that can associate raw sensor data readings with a phenomena
occurrence on button press. We conduct an experiment where participants assess a
number-based and colour-based stimuli on a physical analogue scale (PAS). We quantify
stimuli responses on the scale, and compare with stimuli responses on a visual analogue
scale (VAS). Additionally, we conduct performance experiments on the prototype. Find-
ings reveal the prototype’s efficacy in capturing data for given stimuli, with the gestures ad-
justing pitch and roll on number stimuli having an overall accuracy of Apitch ≈62.47% and
Aroll ≈48.24%. On greyscale stimuli, we observe an overall accuracy of Apitch ≈33.95%
and Aroll ≈34.95%, suggesting that the subjective nature of the stimuli affects the effec-
tiveness of the input method. Findings show promise for use of gesture-based input meth-
ods on segmented scales, with an accuracy up to Apitch ≈92.24% and Aroll ≈85.93% on
number stimuli, with a count of 2 segments. Stability testing of the prototype indicate very
accurate readings at stable angles, with a low mean variance of Vpitch = 0.000298 and
Vroll = 0.002853. Furthermore, a post-experiment participant survey provided valuable
feedback, underscoring a future device’s potential adoption in both clinical, personal and
research contexts. Based on findings, relevant future work directions are recommended.

Introduction
In recent years, self-tracking or self-quantification [1] has gained popularity [2], from track-
ing routine occurrences like sneezing to complex subjective experiences such as Post-
Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) symptoms [3]–[5]. Recent studies deploying devices
in Experience Sampling Method (ESM) [6] contexts ([7]–[9]) or Ecological Momentary As-
sessment (EMA) [10] contexts ([4], [11], [12]), have demonstrated potential for personal
knowledge gain and therapeutic significance. Similar studies have also been applied in
various health-related contexts [2]. While solutions exist for practical use of self-tracking,
generalised solutions are limited [13].
Self-tracking can be a daunting task, as it requires a great amount of effort from the individ-
ual to remain consistent with tracking. Traditionally, gathering data in ESM and EMA con-
texts have been done with pen-and-paper, or through use of mobile applications. Lately,
more tangible solutions have been approached and explored [12], but more systematic
research on accuracy and viability of these input methods is scarce.
A wearable device allows for data collection that can be perceived as less of a burden
compared to use of a notebook or mobile app; where a single click can represent an
defined action or occurrence, and cause less repeated disruption [8]. Providing a device
that can bridge that gap, where users are less burdened, but still able to confidently and
accurately report on subjective phenomena, can yield valuable information in regards to
the tracked phenomena.
Previous work from Kaminski [14] and Beck [15], suggest use of novel wearable systems.
Beck ultimately falls back on the MetaMotionR device for their data collection, and the use
of this specific device is shared by Volsa et al. [8]. Larsen et al. [3], Riisager et al. [5] and
Arendt et al. [4] make use of a shared realised prototype for their data collection. With
a device built with the principle of continued use in mind, the importance of emphasising
freedom of use and subjectivity of actions becomes apparent, and particularly how the
device is used.
A common measurement for subjectivity on a scale could be use of a Visual Analogue
Scale (VAS), which is typically used to objectify subjective phenomena such as pain [16].
Stieger et al. [7] present a Physical Analogue Scale (PAS) as a novel approach to ESM,
where sensors are used to defer physical movement which is later mapped to a scale.
This thesis work intends to further explore the use of a wearable device, with a generalised
approach to how subjective experiences can be recorded. For the sake of maintaining
subjective interpretation, raw data is collected and stored and later post-processed, rather
than at the time of occurrence. The overall approach aims to enhance current work on
self-tracking devices, and provide further context on physical input methods for minimising
burden on the self-tracking user. The implementation of a prototype ensures an accessible
foundation for a self-tracking device where interpretation of raw data is in focus.

collecting occurrence data on subjective experiences for self-tracking purposes. The pro-

Related Work
With self-tracking or self-quantification becoming increasingly popular, more research has
been done on practical use of it in ESM designed studies, expanding the topic. This
chapter will detail relevant related work and how it relates to this thesis.
2.1
Compliance and burden
Matejka et al. [17] investigated how visual appearence of visual analogue scales im-
pacts responses obtained. The study explored effects of tick marks and labels on sliders
with distribution of responses. They conducted two experimental tasks to evaluate the
performance on sliders. The participants assessed greyscale colours on different slider
designs. These colours span between white and black, as seen in Figure 2.1. Their find-
ings indicated that tick marks significantly create bias in responses. While bias was seen,
ticks also increase precision and reduce response time. The study concluded that visual
design of visual analogue scales is crucial in obtaining unbiased data.
Figure 2.1: Greyscale values between white and black, from Matejka et al. [17]
Kaminski [14] describes development of a wearable system for collecting data on sub-
jective experiences. The study aims to minimise user interaction required for manually
recording data. Performance tests were made and indicated the system’s efficiency in
reducing interaction time was increased compared to alternative mobile applications.
Partially inspired from Kaminski’s work, Beck [15] describe a novel solution for reducing
burden on users in regard to self-tracking. They describe a solution with an IMU, a mi-
crocontroller and a Bluetooth module. The prototype is intended to communicate with a
companion app running on Android, where the data could be viewed and exported. The
author decided the device would be too bulky in practice, and opted for use of a Meta-
MotionR device (see Figure 2.2) for the gesture-based experiment. This device uses a
similar sensor fusion algorithm to their proposed prototype.
Beck performed an experiment using the companion app, and the MetaMotionR device,
where the participants were tasked to assess a colour or number on a slider or using
the device, inspired by the work from Matejka et al. [17]. They employ two different
gestures to give input: an arm elevation and a wrist rotation gesture. They found that the
slider input method greatly outperformed the gesture-based device on both colours and
numbers. They observe a difference between a wrist rotation versus arm elevation use,
where the rotation produces higher responses for lower stimuli, and lower responses for
higher stimuli. They suggest bone structure in the lower arm may impact the results. They
find a more scattered result for colour assessment versus number assessment.
Similar to Beck, Stieger et al. [7] describes use of the same MetaMotionR device pro-
grammed with a PAS. The study includes data from two pilot studies and a 2-week study
on social media ostracism. Participants indicate on a VAS using an app, and subsequently
using the device on PAS. The pilot studies showed that PAS measurements are accurate

that social media ostracism significantly heightened feelings of being offended, especially

Volsa et al. [13] present an open-source solution for self-tracking in the field using smart
watches. The tool aims to provide an easy-to-use solutions for ESM, that ultimately re-
duces participant burden and improves compliance. The described solution includes dif-
ferent response scales, such as the Likert scale, VAS and other inputs. They report on
two groups: a smart watch and a smart phone group. A week-long ESM study using a
T-Watch smart watch show that participants in the smart watch group have higher com-
pliance than the smart phone group. The study demonstrates that smart watch usage in
ESM is a viable option compared to smart phones.
In a later study, Volsa et al. [8] introduce another open-source solution to support wear-
ables in ESM. The study describes use of a device configured via an Android application
that allows seamless data collection and export. They conduct a series of pilot studies to
assess the viability of the MetaMotionR device, demonstrating high correlations between
the collected device data and traditional smart phone assessments. Similarly to the pre-
vious study, they reported higher compliance rates for the wearable device compared to
smart phones. The results suggest that wearables can reduce participant burden, and
provide a low-maintenance alternative for self-tracking.
Van Berkel et al. [12] explore accuracy and resolution of a participants input using tangi-
ble self-report devices. They compare different input types to understand their accuracy.
Participants were asked to input randomised target values using the input types while
holding the device out of sight. The study highlights varying limits to the accuracy of the
input types, and indicated highest accuracy for basic sliders. They suggest that self-report
devices can offer discreet and convenient input, but accuracy varies greatly.
In 2018, Vega et al. [11] explored various methodologies for collecting self-tracking data
on Parkinson’s Disease symptoms, where they compared different digital prototypes to
traditional paper diaries in an EMA context. After testing multiple prototypes, they found
that paper diaries were most effective, achieving the highest compliance and acceptance
among their participants. They emphasise the need to reduce burden and address the
impact of symptom awareness.

2.2
One-Button Tracker in a clinical setting
Larsen et al. [3] conducted a case study involving a veteran with PTSD, using a one-
button wearable device (”One-Button Tracker”, see Figure 2.3) for the purpose of self-
tracking subjective experiences. The study aimed to explore if self-tracked data on expe-
rienced symptoms could aid the therapeutic process. The device allowed the participant
to record occurrences of symptoms in real-time, allowing for engagement in their own
treatment. Findings suggest that introduction of self-tracking tools in therapy can improve
understanding of PTSD symptoms. They also suggest potential for personalised therapy,
where the real-time data can enable and complement traditional therapeutic methods with
detailed insights of the occurrences.
Building on Larsen’s work, Arendt et al. [4] did a case study to explore usage of self-
tracking devices to distinguish between rumination and intrusive memories in patients
with PTSD. Two participants recorded occurrences over two weeks, where the first week
no distinguishing was made between the phenomena, followed by training to help the
participants differentiate between rumination and intrusive memories. After which, the
tracking continued with the differentiated phenomena. Results indicated that participants
could distinguish between rumination and intrusions, and data from the device suggested
that self-tracking tools could be valuable for therapeutic purposes.
Riisager et al. [5] present a case study involving a refugee diagnosed with complex PTSD.
Using the one-button wearable, the participant used the device to track flashbacks, inter-
ventions and anger experiences. The consistent engagement with the device demon-
strated its unique ability to enhance the therapeutic process, and enabled the participant
to gain insights into their experiences, contributing to more effective therapy. Findings
suggest a significant role for self-tracking in tailoring treatment to individual needs.
To assess usability and perceived usefulness of the one-button tracker, Belt et al. [9]
conducted a study where participants used the device to track various phenomena. The
study combined qualitative interviews and quantitative surveys to assess usability. Find-
ings showed that the device was well-received, but showed that data saturation was not
achieved. It was also highlighted that the device had challenges with its wearability. They
suggest further research with a larger pool of participants to understand the device’s full
potential, but concluded that the tool offers promise for personal science and research
within the topic.

Analysis
This chapter analyses related work and describes key concepts in relation to this thesis.
The following sections explain theoretical use of key concepts for the project vision.
3.1
Stimuli on VAS and PAS
A VAS is commonly a 100mm straight line that have the words ”no pain” on the left-end and
”unimaginable” on the right-end of the line [16]. A universal example of a VAS can be seen
in Figure 3.1, where the VASs are either lines, colours or a combination of emojis. Often
the colour green is used as a low-to-mild level of pain, while red signifies severe pain. VAS
can be applied in various contexts to assess subjective experiences, such as pain. In the
work by Matejka et al. [17], performance on traditional slider inputs were evaluated using
greyscale colours. Unlike the VAS, which relies on visual input, the PAS involves physical
input, where an individual would indicate their response through a physical gesture on
an imagined scale. An example of a PAS can be seen in Figure 3.2, where instead of a
visual input, the input is indicated with physical movement instead. The imagined scale
translates to an angle of approximately 0°, 45° and 90°, essentially providing three inputs.
Like a VAS, PAS can be a continuous scale. Stieger et al. [7] found that their wearable
with a programmed PAS had comparable reliability to a VAS, and that they formed linear
relationships.
Figure 3.1: Different universal VASs1. The VASs are depicting a pain scale between no
pain and unimaginable pain, which are translated into a value in the range 0 to 10.
In this thesis, stimuli responses on a slider (VAS) will be compared to physical stimuli
responses (PAS). The intent is to evaluate greyscale (colour) and integer (number) stimuli
on both scales, similar to work done by Beck [15]. The defined continuous scales are
later illustrated in Chapter 6. We imagine the PAS being similar to input as depicted in
Figure 3.2, with the difference being that the physical scale is comprised of 50 shades of
greyscale, inspired by Matejka et al. [17], and numbers between 0-100. This large count
of possible input emphasises the expectation that a physical input device can be used in
place of visual input.

3.2
Therapeutic wearables and their benefit
There is a clear therapeutic benefit of self-tracking in clinical purposes, as demonstrated
in work by Larsen et al. [3], Arendt et al. [4] and Riisager et al. [5]. The One Button
Tracker is a unique device that illustrates how future iterations of gesture-based inputs
can be utilised, though research indicates that traditional input methods (e.g. sliders)
outperform gestures in terms of accuracy. The simplicity of the device emphasises how
intuitive wearables can facilitate real-time data collection, while simultaneously supporting
better compliance and engagement. The effectiveness of distinguishing between different
types of symptoms in combination with therapy, as seen in [4], showcase a great potential
in clinical settings.
3.2.1
IMUs in practice
Intertial Measurement Units (IMUs)2 are a system-in-package combination of multiple
sensors: an accelerometer, a gyroscope, and a magnetometer. Advanced IMUs such
as the BNO08X family3 include sophisticated sensor fusion algorithms [18]. The software
processes raw sensor data to deliver accurate and reliable outputs for calibrated 3D orien-
tation, heading and velocity. In the context of this thesis, the BNO08X family is considered
for use of raw rotation vector outputs.
Previous work lack exploration with different IMUs or other wearables, that may prove
worthwhile and suited for the purpose of self-tracking. Related work by Volsa et al. [8],
[13] and Belt et al. [9] indicate that reducing burden for users is a primary hurdle and
a necessity for achieving consistent data reporting. Exploration with new novel devices
and gesture-based input methods suggest a need for more tangible input devices, that
ultimately reduce friction for the user in their self-tracking.
By making use of raw sensor data from the IMU, we can discern absolute physical orien-
tation and apply it to a PAS. With physical movement, we can define movement that will
reflect a response on the scale. In Figure 3.2 we see three different responses on their
defined PAS. When applied to the thesis context, we envision a continuous scale between
0° and 90°, mapped to the contextual scale values (e.g. the approximate angle 0°, 45°
and 90° would produce the number 0, 50 and 100 respectively, on a number PAS).

closely related methodologies, where both attempt to collect data closer to the occurrence

to present further steps to achieve more precise immediate sampling with use of a device
for self-tracking purposes, ultimately reducing burden and eliminating recall bias.
3.3
Design philosophy: open-source accessibility
Upholding an open-source approach as a paradigm in the expanding research on the
topic, is paramount to continuing an accessible design philosophy. Volsa et al. [8], [13]
set an example with contributing to the topic generously. The design philosophy displayed
with the novel One Button Tracker device [3]–[5], [12] provides:
1. An easy-to-use device
2. Anonymously collected data
3. Accessible data exporting options
4. Optional annotation of collected data points
5. Flexibility in processing collected data
The device universally provides easy integration into a user’s daily routines, while pro-
tecting the user’s identity, creating a space for uninhibited self-tracking. In addition, the
collected data exhibits a high degree of self-defined interpretation and flexibility. The data
is what you make it out to be; effectively, it maintains it’s subjectivity.
This philosophy underscores the importance of creating accessible, ethical and flexible
self-tracking tools, that may be used in a wide range of contexts. The design philosophy
applies in the context of this thesis, where the ultimate goal is to create flexibility that can
advance future self-tracking.

The truth value represents the presented stimuli number or colour. Depending on the ex-

is immediately used to compare against the truth value truth. For device type (gesture-
based) experiments, the value is calculated. Assuming a number stimuli experiment, the
scale is always 0-100. Assuming a greyscale stimuli experiment, the scale is always 0-49.
We then calculate the stimuli response value using the defined method in subsection 5.6.2,
dependent on the gesture used. For pitch gestures, the calculated pitch θ is used. For
roll gestures, the calculated roll ψ is used. The result of the scaling method gives us a
calculated value pitch_truth or roll_truth, that can be used to compare against the truth
value truth.

6.1.4
Design

and roll gesture), and two input types (stimuli; greyscale colour and number). The inde-
pendent variable is the stimuli ground truth, and the dependent variable is the participant
response (input from slider or device).

tasked to complete each experiment type, but depending on their participant index, the
order of the experiment presented to them first will be different from the previous partici-

8
Discussion
This chapter analyses and discusses the work and results of this thesis.
8.1
Highlighted results
The findings from the survey data provide insights into user preferences and experiences
with different physical input methods. Participants generally preferred arm elevation over
wrist rotation, indicating a preference for more extensive movement. Arm elevation was
perceived as easier to estimate accuracy with, likely due to the more extensive range of
motion providing a finer granularity of the physical scale.
While arm elevation was preferred for its accuracy, wrist rotation was noted for its com-
fort. This highlights a trade-off between precision and comfort, which could be crucial in
designing future self-tracking prototypes. Users may benefit from a hybrid approach that
combines the ease of estimation with the comfort of wrist rotation. In the context of this
experiment, the wrist rotation, or roll gesture, was limited to 90° of freedom. In a real-life
scenario, the range of motion is 180°, which could extend the physical scale and possibly
offer a similar perceived accuracy as arm elevation, while preserving comfort.
The results showed that accuracy varied depending on the input type and the stimuli type.
Comparing calculated t-statistics per experiment type, we observe that number-based ex-
periment types are statistically significant, while greyscale-based experiment types are
not statistically significant. For the number stimuli, the slider input method significantly
outperformed gesture-based input methods in terms of accuracy, indicating that visual
feedback plays a role in assessing with high precision. For greyscale stimuli, the differ-
ences in accuracy between input methods were less pronounced and had high spread,
suggesting that the nature of the stimuli (assessing a colour) affects the effectiveness of
the input method. No significant difference was seen on assessing colour stimuli for the
N = 5 highly experienced application designers versus other participants.
This could be affected by many factors. The greyscale stimuli group had more variability
in responses compared to number stimuli, regardless of the input method. This variability
could be due to the subjective nature of colour perception, which may require more nu-
anced control than numerical estimation. The number of outliers in the greyscale stimuli
suggest that the assessment may be too cumbersome for participants. We see a higher
variance for both gesture input methods compared to the results by Beck in 2018 [15].
8.2
Subjectivity
The responses are subjective by nature, as one participant will have a different perception
from the next participant. An interesting follow-up study would be to allow participants to
learn how to use the device prior to evaluating how accurate the physical scale could be. A
learning period where immediate feedback would be available for the response, and then
afterwards, they are allowed to complete the experiment. The comparison with last five
stimuli suggest minimal to no learning effect on 20 stimuli. However, without feedback for
the participant, no adjustments are made to later responses. Participants may consider
their previous response as a correct response, when it may not be, and therefore future
responses may be skewed.
The outliers and high truth differences for valid stimuli suggest human error, but it is hard
to pinpoint how they were produced. The high kurtosis on greyscale stimuli experiment
A Next-Generation Prototype for Subjective Phenomena Tracking using Gesture Interfaces
45
==PAGE:54==
types is consistent with us seeing a high count of outliers. On the contrary, number stimuli
experiment types exhibit a low kurtosis, consistent with the low outlier count. A potential
countermeasure could be if the participant is recorded during the experiment, where be-
haviour could be revisited. Recording can also adjust for potential unwanted rotations,
movement or prototype placement on the arm inferred by the participant.
The stability testing indicate that the sensor reading and conversion to Euler angles is
extremely accurate and stable, with very low variance. While the stability reporting of
the device and by extension the sensor is extremely accurate, this testing is done while
stationary. Introduction of a moving arm inevitably introduces variance.
Participants were not asked to wear the device in a specific rotation (e.g. the device
could face with the USB-connector medial to the body, or lateral to the body). This means
the the reporting can be done in either a negative or positive angle, where the resulting
absolute value is used. The participants were not asked to use their dominant hand,
which may affect stability. Upon pressing the button, minor angle adjustments may be
caused difference due to applied pressure on the device, and by extension, moving the
arm. As known from stability testing the sensor reading is very accurate, and therefore
small adjustments to the positioning of the arm can have great impact on the accuracy. In
addition, natural drift may be present as reported from the sensor board datasheet [18].
We attempt to segment the original scales into equal segments to determine how this
affects the pool of stimuli responses. Due to the inconsistency and high spread seen
with greyscale stimuli experiment types, analysis of accuracy on a segmented scale for
greyscale was discontinued. The results on segmented scales for number experiment
types are present. When transformed to a segmented scale, the stimuli exhibit greater in-
clusion. This suggests that the physical scale could be segmented into variations (e.g. 2,
3, 4, 5, 7) and exhibit a higher accuracy with lower segmentation. This could be useful in
a scenario where lower granularity is needed, for example an imagined VAS on customer
satisfaction (emoji between sad to happy). The standard deviation (SD) and interquar-
tile range (IQR) for each of the physical input methods on number stimuli indicate use
on a segmented scale could be feasible (within ±15). We recognise that the approach
for the prototype and PAS application will not work for every population, and should be
reconsidered when applied in future work.
8.3
Prototype design
It was opted to store raw data as opposed to processed data. This opens up possibilities
for the device scope. By processing the raw data afterwards, we allow for potential ex-
ploration on the data. Raw data allows for freedom of definition: the movement can be
highly subjective and refer to specific things in a real-life usage scenario. Ultimately, the
introduction of physical movement introduces modality, a way of knowing which physical
state (angle) the prototype was in.
Using a device prototype similar to the one in this thesis, one user could define their use
to be limited to two actions: held at a specific angle means YES, and at another angle
means NO. This would represent a scale with only two inputs (or segments). Another user
could define the action and scale with more granularity, where the tracked phenomena
may be an occurrence of each time a car of a certain colour is spotted. A button click at
approximately a) ∼0° is red, b) ∼30° is blue, c) ∼60° is green, d) ∼90° is yellow. The oc-
currences at each a) through d) is easily discerned by reporting at the approximate angle,
even at reported gesture-based input method accuracy. The usage of the gesture-based
device on PAS shows promise, but is highly dependent on what granularity is needed.

For high precision, traditional input methods may be better suited.
Battery consumption data highlighted a practical consideration of using the prototype de-
vice. The mean battery life of approximately 3.5 hours suggests that while suitable for
short-term use, improvements in energy efficiency are necessary for longer studies. Bat-
tery consumption may be impacted by a very high reporting rate. The report rate is at
400Hz and read every 20 ms in the main loop, this likely depletes the battery quickly.
Reducing the reporting rate or introducing deep sleep cycles may prove beneficial in sig-
nificantly improving battery life for a future prototype.
Usage of motor versus no motor in the prototype context was discontinued, as the proto-
type solution with the motor malfunctioned. In hindsight, this may been a positive happen-
ing. In early development, we speculated that the vibrations may affect the sensitivity of
the sensor reading. This theory was never tested. We also recognise the VAS scale that
was used in the experiment app did not comply to a standard measurement of 100mm,
which could introduce unintended variance.
Prototype device size and usage of space in the case models can be made more effec-
tive. The prototypes presented in this project are considered proof-of-concept, and further
iteration into more efficient usage of space is needed for optimal sizing. The survey and
anecdotal participant feedback suggests a need for a similar device, but in a different for-
mat. Participants would self-report that they would prefer to use their mobile phone or
other wearable, to not have an additional wearable. Device type exploration (ring, smart
watch, more commercialised prototype) could be a direction for future work. The open
design philosophy enables future work to utilise and expand on the work made.

Future Work
This chapter intends to present directions for future work on the topic of personal infor-
matics and self-tracking within the context of this thesis.
9.1
Future work
The use of a PAS to classify actions looks promising. With continued research, and ad-
ditional experimentation with self-tracking devices, further insights can be gathered. De-
pending on the granularity needed, conducting experiments with different scales could
yield interesting results. In combination with having participants learn how to use a de-
vice over a period of time, during a case study, practical implementations of the physical
scales could be refined.
9.1.1
Exploring larger space on a different scale
While the current prototype device can be used to potentially put subjectively collected
data into categorised buckets, the results on accuracy are not granular enough for a high
precision scale. An interesting direction could be to expand the scale used. For example,
the roll gesture in the thesis experiment only utilises angles between 0° and 90°. Rotation
of the arm can with ease extend this scale to support 180°, and allow for a greater range
of motion.
The use of greyscale as a stimuli proved cumbersome for participants to assess. An alter-
native could be to revert to a traditional 100mm VAS alternative, such as a traditional pain
scale rating or a customer satisfaction scale (emojis spanning between sad and happy).
A colour may in general be hard to assess, particularly in comparison to the number stim-
uli. The results suggest that numbers are easier to assess on a physical scale, most
likely since they are easier to place on the physical scale mentally. Drawing a scale of
fifty shades of colour in your head may seem close to impossible for some participants.
Offering a scale with fewer options (segmented) could further reinforce the viability of the
input method.
Future work could encompass a case study where a similar prototype is deployed to be
used by a single or multiple participants on a segmented scale with 2 to 7 different inputs.
In practice, the device could be used in a similar manner as the One Button Tracker was
deployed in different studies [3]–[5], [9]. The difference being, with a similar prototype
as presented in this thesis, the authors could define accompanied PASs and participants
would be able to use the physical prototype as a scale, allowing for immediate data anno-
tation with the phenomena occurrence (e.g. severity of a symptom between 0-5, where
the scale is defined by the authors). A phenomena occurrence would be tracked on but-
ton press, at a specific angle to indicate severity, ultimately reducing overall burden on
the participant.
9.1.2
Learning model for individual differences
Another interesting path could be to adjust for participant physical input. With wearing a
physical device comes offsets: device placement and usage in real-time by the participant.
It can be hard for a participant to always be consistent with physical placement, due to
various reasons, with an example being physical disabilities or heavy movement (e.g.
transit, sports).
The results suggest that minimal to no learning effect over the 20 stimuli per experiment on
the participants. Potential future work could consider a number of measures focusing on

==PAGE:58==
learning. The focus could be to have an extended learning period, where the participant
gets immediate feedback on how they are reporting (on the scale that they are reporting),
which would allow the participant to adjust and improve their reporting over time.
Beck [15] suggests future work on a potential mathematical model to adjust for non-linear
movement from wrist gestures. In a similar effort, an approach for a machine learning
model could be developed, where the device learns the participant’s patterns and usage,
and adjusts according to set parameters. A foundation for that could offer a more widely
accessible gesture-based device.
9.1.3
Device exploration and optimisation
The current proof-of-concept prototype could benefit from more efficient use of space
and a reduction in overall size, or making use of other device types, while maintaining
its single-purpose approach. Future iterations should focus on creating a compact case
with optimised hardware space. The survey suggests a need for a subtle device, but
preferably with low-impact wearability and high comfort. This could potentially be explored
as a different device type, such as a ring.
With device exploration, software considerations need to be considered. For the proto-
type in this thesis, focus on battery consumption was omitted, and thus regular use of
the specific prototype is not feasible for longer periods of time. Future work could con-
sider introducing sleep cycles, on-demand reading of the sensor, or reducing reporting
rate to maintain an extended battery life. With optimisation, a good balance between
performance and battery consumption could be achieved.
Finally, embracing an open-source design and development philosophy allows for contin-
uous improvement and potential community contributions. Future work should encourage
collaboration with other researchers and developers to refine the device, share insights,
and expand its usage.

10
Conclusion
This thesis explored development and experimental use of a gesture-based prototype for
tracking subjective phenomena. The research focused on creating a wearable device
capable of collecting sensor data from an IMU, and converting the raw data into Euler
angles, with the end goal of translating pitch and roll values onto a defined PAS. The idea
with the prototype is to adopt physical gestures as an input method.
The prototype successfully demonstrated feasibility with using physical gestures to assess
a stimuli. The survey indicated a participant preference (Nelevation = 35, Nrotation = 5)
for arm elevation over wrist rotation due to its perceived accuracy, despite wrist rotation
being noted for its comfort (Nelevation = 14, Nrotation = 26). The experiments indicated
a highly varied accuracy between number stimuli (Apitch ≈62.47%, Aroll ≈48.24%,
Aslider ≈94.11%) and greyscale stimuli (Apitch ≈33.95%, Aroll ≈34.95%, Aslider ≈
35.56%). Number stimuli are shown to be more consistently reported, albeit with higher
variance between the gesture-based input methods (pitch: SD = 9.854, IQR = 10.134,
roll: SD = 12.083, IQR = 13.285) versus the visual slider (SD = 3.648, IQR = 3.995).
The wrist rotation gesture (adjusting roll) indicates a higher spread of responses com-
pared to the arm elevation (adjusting pitch), suggesting a need for a greater range of
motion, as supported by survey answers. The subjective nature of colour perception
likely contributed to high spread of truth responses on greyscale stimuli, as seen across
all input methods (pitch: SD = 11.516, IQR = 17.960, roll: SD = 9.875, IQR = 14.360,
slider: SD = 11.835, IQR = 18.394). Stability testing confirm the IMU’s ability to accu-
rately report in stationary conditions, with a low mean variance of Vpitch = 0.000298 and
Vroll = 0.002853, though physical motion quickly introduces variance when not stationary.
The results reveal minimal to no learning effects over 20 stimuli, suggesting a need for
extended learning periods, possibly with immediate feedback for the user. The use of
the traditional slider input method reveals a small improvement in truth difference, with
Ttrailing ≈10.59 and Tleading ≈11.58 for greyscale stimuli and Ttrailing ≈3.58 and
Tleading ≈3.86 for number stimuli. Gesture-based input methods did not see improve-
ment over the last five stimuli, when compared to the first 15 stimuli. However, we see
great potential in use of the gesture-based input methods on segmented scales, with an
accuracy up to Apitch ≈92.24% and Aroll ≈85.93% on number stimuli, with a count of
2 segments. Potential use allows for a self-defined granularity based on the segments
used. The choice to store raw data rather than processed data expanded and showcases
the device’s potential applications, allowing for a high degree of flexibility in both clinical,
research and personal contexts. We recommend a number of possible directions to ex-
plore in future work, including expanding the gesture scale, applying potential learning
models and optimisation and exploration of the prototype device implementation.
In conclusion, this thesis laid groundwork for using a practical implementation of a gesture-
based interface in subjective self-tracking, demonstrating a step toward potential future
devices and their challenges. Future research should focus on refining the prototype’s ac-
curacy and usability, and defining context-appropriate scales to aid in various self-tracking
purposes and populations.
