1
Introduction
Oﬀshore wind energy is becoming increasingly more important in the energy industry, and its
goal of reducing the use of traditional forms of energy, with high carbon emissions, in favor
of renewable energy sources. In this regard, the use of bottom-ﬁxed wind turbines limits the
harnessing of the oﬀshore energy potential to shallow or intermediate water depths. The use of
ﬂoating wind turbines opens this possibility. However, the increased complexity of these tur-
bines implies that their cost of production and risk is higher at the present time. Therefore, it
is essential to create fast and eﬃcient tools that are able to simulate the behaviour and perfor-
mance of a ﬂoating wind turbine in order to keep production costs at a minimum.
The design process of any ﬂoating wind turbine is made typically with time domain, state of the
art numerical models. These models tend to be computational expensive when diﬀerent ﬂoaters
designs want to be tested. The use of fast frequency domain tools could help in the preliminary
stages of the design, when an optimal conﬁguration of ﬂoater and turbine is being selected, to
increase the eﬃciency and speed of the ﬂoating wind turbine development.
In the present study, a validation of the Quick Load Analysis for Floating wind turbines (QuLAF)
against a time domain aeroelastic code (HAWC2) and experimental data was performed on the
TetraSpar ﬂoater developed by Stiesdal Oﬀshore Technologies. The main motivation of this
was to further contribute to the building of the eﬃciency in ﬂoating wind turbine design and
advance the use of fast numerical models, eﬀectively bringing down the costs for ﬂoating wind
turbine and ultimately enhance the use of ﬂoating wind farms. This study is a contribution to
the FloatStep project which is funded by the Danish Innovation Fond and its main objective,
is to develop numerical and computational tools for the design and optimization of the future
oﬀshore ﬂoating wind turbines.
The process carried out in order to perform the veriﬁcation and validation against HAWC2 and
the experimental data was:
• Setup of the QuLAF model. This implied ﬁnding all the parameters needed to run the
model and doing diﬀerent simulations in HAWC2 in order to determine necessary external
and non-linear inputs (mooring stiﬀness calculation and aerodynamic loads and damping
coeﬃcients).
• Running the veriﬁcation with HAWC2 for four diﬀerent wind and sea states: regular and
irregular waves, wind only and wind with regular and irregular waves, and below rated,
rated and above rated wind speeds.
• Running the validation by comparing the results obtained with the experimental data
acquired during the TetraSpar tank tests campaign.
At a later stage of the project, second order wave excitation forces were implemented in HAWC2
and QuLAF using the Newman approximation to better replicate experimental results, and an
alternative way to extract the aerodynamic damping coeﬃcients of the turbine was investigated
in order to calculate them faster and easier.

2
Background
This chapter describes and displays in brief the previous investigations and works necessary to
develop the present study, comparison and validation of the QuLAF model.
2.1
Quick Load Analysis on Floating Wind Turbines Model (QuLAF)
The quick load analysis model for ﬂoating wind turbines (QuLAF), is a frequency domain tool
developed at the Technical University of Denmark by the Post-Doc Antonio Pegalajar Jurado
throughout his PhD. studies in conjunction with Henrik Bredmose and Michael Borg. The de-
velopment and initial tests of the numerical tool were part of the LIFES50+ initiative from
diﬀerent institutions, research centers and universities with the main objective to design ﬂoating
wind turbine substructures and the methodologies, numerical and optimization tools which will
reduce its production costs [1].
The maths and diﬀerent methods of the model were derived from the frequency domain model
developed by Signe Schløer from the Wind Energy department at the Technical University of
Denmark [10]. Her main objective was the analysis and validation of the model through the
evaluation of oﬀshore monopile-type wind turbines.
Schløer developed the tool for monopyle-type oﬀshore wind turbines based on the frequency
domain models investigated by Jan Van der Tempel [14] . In his studies, he researched the ap-
plication of frequency domain models in the design, optimization and fatigue analysis of oﬀshore
wind turbines. Also, he developed methods for diﬀerent non-linear inputs to be computed, in
order to insert them in the frequency domain transfer functions like the clamped aerodynamic
loads calculation method which will be explained in further sections. Other works on the de-
velopment and assessment of frequency domain models were carried out by Richard C. Lupton
in his PhD. studies [15] but applied to the design optimization of oﬀshore ﬂoating wind turbines.
2.2
The Scaled TetraSpar Floating Wind Turbine
For the validation and development of the project, a scaled model of the DTU 10 MW reference
wind turbine, designed by Bak et al. (2013) [3], was tested out in a series of tank tests at the
Danish Hydraulic Institute (DHI). The scaled model was mounted on top of a TetraSpar ﬂoater,
designed and manufactured by Stiesdal Oﬀshore Technologies (STO). The test campaign was
carried out by the DTU Wind Energy department and a summary of the layout can be found
in Bredmose et al. (2017) [7].
2.3
Tetraspar Scaled HAWC2 Model
The 10 MW Scaled Tetraspar ﬂoating wind turbine aeroelastic model used in the present study
to develop the coupling and comparison between the frequency domain model and the time
domain model, was based on the scaled DTU 10MW wind turbine numerical model developed
initially by Ahmet Selim Kocaturk in his master thesis project [2]. The Tetraspar ﬂoating struc-
ture, the WAMIT DLL module, its mooring system and the sea environment conditions were
later added to the code by Freddy J. Madsen and Thomas R. Nielsen [6] with the help of Michael
11
==PAGE:14==
Borg who helped with the calibration of diﬀerent physical and damping parameters.
Finally Anders K. Lomholt worked on turbine’s controller and adjusted the tower’s fore-aft
mode shape parameters in order to validate them against the experiments from the campaign
done to the physical scaled wind turbine prototype. Additionally, he developed on Solidworks,
the computer assisted design (CAD) model of the ﬂoating wind turbine. Several physical and
non-linear parameters and inputs acting on the turbine were pre-calculated or extracted from
this model in order to run the frequency domain model QuLAF.

3
1:60 Scaled TetraSpar Floating Wind Turbine
The ﬂoater design in study is a 1:60 scaled version of the DTU 10 MW Reference Wind Turbine
(DTU 10 MW RWT) mounted on a Stiesdal Oﬀshore Technologies (STO) TetraSpar ﬂoater.
A full description of the ﬂoater geometry and features can be found in [7]. A series of tank
experiments were carried out at the Danish Hydraulic Institute (DHI), and a model in HAWC2
was developed and calibrated in order to try to reproduce the experiments results.
In this
chapter a brief description of the scaling will be elaborated as well as the load cases included in
this study.
3.1

HAWC2 Model
HAWC2 (Horizontal Axis Wind turbine Code of 2nd generation) is an aeroelastic code intended
for calculation of the response of onshore, bottom mounted or ﬂoating oﬀshore wind turbines in
time domain [17]. The HAWC2 solver operates through a main .htc ﬁle, where all components
are modelled, oriented and constrained, and where the corresponding input ﬁles and external
DLLs are called. The simulations of a ﬂoating wind turbine comprehends the use of several dif-
ferent modules that interact with each other in order to converge to a solution. This interaction
can be seen in ﬁgure (4).
The HAWC2 model of the ﬂoating wind turbine was developed by Thomas Nielsen and Freddy
Madsen [6]. A picture of how the model is set up can be seen in ﬁgure (5).

4.1
Frequency Domain Models
As exposed in the background section of the report, the main objective of the frequency domain
computational models developed by Pegalajar, Schløer and the previous works from other in-
stitutions, is the saving of simulation time and CPU capacity consumption during the design
optimization process of oﬀshore wind turbines and structures.
State of the art aeroelastic codes like HAWC2 or FAST have proved to deliver precise dynamic
behaviour to real life conditions but unfortunately, they require a considerable amount of time
and computing power to obtain the desired dynamic responses under the required environmen-
tal conditions. QuLAF and the other frequency domain models due to their nature, are able
to compute and obtain similar dynamic responses but in less computation time and without
requiring as much CPU power as a S.o.A. aeroelastic code.
The main tool the frequency domain models implement are the discrete Fourier series trans-
formations which manipulate a time domain input and decompose it in a set of signals each
one, with an harmonic frequency and an equivalent amplitude to the original input [15]. This
mathematical model has the following general expression:

critical value (ζ = 0).

Wind only
The third part of the veriﬁcation was made in wind only conditions. The wind has an irregular
spectrum, modelled with a Mann turbulence box with the same L and αϵ parameters described
in section 4.4.4, and with the same turbulence intensity that was measured in the experiments.
The aerodynamic loads previously calculated for each wind speed also had the same turbulence
parameters. Consequently, both models had the same wind speed time series.
Similarly to the previous section, only one case will be displayed and analyzed here, EC6 (above
rated wind speed). The results are shown in ﬁgure (26), the statistics are displayed in table (16),
the standard deviations in ﬁgure (27) and table (17). The rest can be found in the appendix in
section 10.3.4.
Since QuLAF calculates the viscous damping coeﬃcients using equation (29), it requires a
representative velocity of the ﬂow particles in the horizontal (urep) and vertical (wrep) directions
caused by the waves.
In this particular case, since there are no waves, there will be zero
representative velocity, and thus, no viscous damping.
To account for this lack of damping
in the frequency domain model, a calibration was implemented, by adding to B an additional
matrix (Badd):
B = Brad(ω) + Bstruct + Bhydro + Baero + Badd
(55)
Due to the fact that the viscous damping forces have an inherent non-linear nature, and that
in QuLAF a linear term is used to represent it, the calibration was done iteratively using the
standard deviation error of the responses as a measure to match. The additional damping was
tuned until the relative error with HAWC2 was below 5%.
In general a good match was found between the two models, for the time series, PSDs and
exceedance probabilities. From table (16), the heave mean for QuLAF seems to be minimally
underestimated, similarly to the case for waves only. This is due to a diﬀerence in the mooring
stiﬀness for heave, but again the diﬀerence is small enough to be acceptable. In the PSDs of the
EC6, it is shown that the natural frequencies of the ﬂoater were more excited that any other,
and that the nacelle acceleration is coupled with the pitch natural frequency. Table (16) also
exposes that the 95% percentile has minimum deviation between the two models. This is a
result of the calibration made to the damping matrix in QuLAF.
It was noted that for sea states with slower wind speeds the most excited frequencies for the
ﬂoater motions were the ones within the wind spectrum band. For above the rated wind speed,
the natural frequencies were the ones with the most energy, as observed in ﬁgure (26). This
eﬀect was also noted to happen for sea state EC11. Additionally, the value of the energy in the
PSDs for the lower sea states is quite higher than the ones in EC6 and EC11, suggesting that
the cause for this behaviour might lie in the thrust generated by the turbine. At below rated
and at rated wind speed the mean thrust level is higher than for the above rated cases. This
results in a more important inﬂuence of the wind in the motions of the ﬂoater, meaning that
the response will have the same frequency as the wind. When the wind speed exceeds its rated
value, the thrust diminishes, leaving the ﬂoater to move according to its natural frequencies
more freely. At sea state 11 there is only the aerodynamic drag acting on the turbine, making
this eﬀect even more evident.

Wind and Waves
The last part of the veriﬁcation was carried out with simulations were both wind and waves were

as the previous section were used. So both models had the same wave elevation and wind speed
time series for better analysis of the responses.
As with the other analyses made, in this section only one case will be addressed: EC5 with
irregular waves. The rest of the results can be found in the appendix, section 10.3.6. The results
are shown in ﬁgure (28) and table (18), and the standard deviation bar plot and errors table are
in (29) and (19) respectively.
The presence of both wind and waves in the simulation has a positive impact in the results
since that, at a lower sea state, as discussed in the previous section, the thrust generated by the
rotor will be the predominant inﬂuence in the ﬂoater motions frequencies, as can be seen in the
results for EC5. For more severe sea states, the thrust of the turbine is lower, so the wave and
natural frequencies will dictate how the ﬂoater responds, and, as said in section 5.2, these sea
states have longer periods and, therefore, a higher chance to excite the low natural frequencies
of the ﬂoater. All of this leads to a compensation of the weak points of each situation with the
strong ones of the other, resulting in a good match.
Looking at ﬁgure (28) and at table (18), it can be seen that the response presents in general,
an agreement between HAWC2 and QuLAF. The biggest discrepancies are located in the surge
and heave motion, where the time series show that even though their mean values seem quite
close to each other, they have a diﬀerence in amplitude. This can be better seen in the PSDs,
where the plots show that the frequency domain model has a lower damping for this motions.
Since the wind speed for this sea state is the rated, the thrust will be the highest. This means
that the surge motion will be strongly inﬂuenced by the wind, which in turn suggests that the
diﬀerence in the response could be caused by discrepancies in the aerodynamic damping.
In consideration that the aerodynamic damping will have a non signiﬁcant impact in the heave,
and that the hydrodynamic radiation damping coming from WAMIT is the same for both mod-
els, the overestimation of the maximum values of heave indicates that the viscous eﬀects are not
being entirely represented in QuLAF. One possible source of this diﬀerence could be to choosing
of the drag coeﬃcient in the vertical direction, CDz. Another value that better approximates the
response from HAWC2 would help resolve the problem. This value was set to be 2.05 since most
of the ﬂoater structure is composed of cylinders, and the side tanks could be seen as small heave
plates. Despite this diﬀerences, it was chosen to not calibrate the results since the frequency
domain model takes into account all the physics the SoA code does.

8
Alternative Aerodynamic Damping Extraction
The damping coeﬃcients are generally the most diﬃcult parameters to understand and estimate
in any dynamic model. Floating wind turbines are not the exception, with some of these coef-
ﬁcients having to be determined by trial an error or by iteration until an acceptable solution is
reached, or using a numerical solver to estimate them.
In frequency domain models, the application of the aerodynamic loads and damping are not
simple due to their inherent non-linear nature. The work carried out by the aerodynamic damp-
ing in a ﬂoating wind turbine is a function of the wind speed, rotor speed, turbulence intensity,
oscillation amplitude and above all, motion frequency. As explained in section 4.4.5, the aero-
dynamic damping is described as a linear coeﬃcient that does the same work in one oscillation
cycle. This coeﬃcient was extracted by performing decay tests in every DoF.
In this chapter a simpler method was employed to extract the aerodynamic damping out of the
turbine: the nacelle and rotor are forced to harmonically move against a constant steady wind
with diﬀerent oscillating frequencies. In this manner, a more streamlined process of providing
the aerodynamic loads and damping for the optimization process in the early design of the
ﬂoating wind turbine can be achieved.
8.1

turbine and its controller design. Therefore, extracting the aerodynamic loads and damping
from just the rotor should be comparable as that one obtained using the complete system.

were present and located at the same hub height as the decay tests with no other sources of
damping enabled. The only allowed degrees of freedom were the blades rotation and an active
controller.

the natural frequency of the surge, pitch and tower fore-aft motion, and the amplitude was a

9
Conclusions
A model of the scaled TetraSpar with the DTU 10 MW RWT was setup in QuLAF, veriﬁed
with HAWC2 and validated against experimental data obtained during the test campaign. At a
later stage of the study, second order wave excitation forces were implemented in HAWC2 and
QuLAF using the Newman approximation, and an alternative way of estimating the aerody-
namic damping, using forced oscillations instead of decay tests, was investigated.
The setup of the frequency model was done by implementing diﬀerent methods with outputs
from HAWC2 and Moordyn to obtain the external inputs as the aerodynamic loads, the mooring
line stiﬀness system and the aerodynamic damping. Their implementation and validation with
the model to model comparison results proved to perform as expected in most of the analyzed
cases. Still, it is important to highlight the considerable inﬂuence HAWC2 has over QuLAF
which assures the reliability between both models but can condition the frequency domain’s
assertiveness. If a systematic error in the external input simulations from the aeroelastic code
is ignored, it can aﬀect the outcome of the dynamic response in QuLAF reducing its accuracy.
It is therefore always necessary to maintain a non-dependant from HAWC2 qualitative or quan-
titative accuracy validation test to assure the ﬁdelity of the frequency domain results.
The veriﬁcation of QuLAF against HAWC2 gave an overall match of both responses. The nat-
ural frequencies of the ﬂoater were within acceptable levels, with an error of 8% for heave as its
biggest diﬀerence. The only calibration needed to match these frequencies was made to the tower
bending stiﬀness, since the tower fore-aft mode shape was approximated as a clamped-free beam.
Of all the four degrees of freedom, the tower fore-aft mode shape gave the best results, meaning
the approximation is acceptable. For wind only conditions the damping matrix of QuLAF had to
be calibrated in order to take into account the hydrodynamic viscous damping. The best results
were obtained when the environmental conditions were more severe, due to the longer periods of
the wave spectrum which would aﬀect the natural frequencies of the ﬂoater more. Apart of the
tower bending stiﬀness, and the damping in wind only cases, no further calibration was required.
The steady states responses were compared and validated against the TetraSpar testing cam-
paign done in 2017. As an overall conclusion, it is possible to aﬃrm that the QuLAF model,
although it gives results on the same order of magnitude as the experiments, has considerable
diﬀerences on the lower sea state cases due to the simpliﬁcation of the viscous drag eﬀects and
the omission of the second order wave loads. For the cases were the wind was included, it was not
possible to make a concluding assessment of the behaviour for the models and the experiments
since the turbulence inputs did not paired as initially expected. Even though the calibration of
the damping coeﬃcients done to compensate the omission of the interaction between the ﬂoater
and the water without the wave presence gave accurate results in the model to model compari-
son, the diﬀerences between both models and the experiments support the previous statement.
The second order wave loads were implemented as an additional feature in the scope of the
present investigation. From the results achieved, it can be stated that for the analyzed ﬂoat-
ing structure in this study, the second order eﬀects produce a high impact on both models
and increments their accuracy in comparison to the experiment measurements. Even though
the Newman simpliﬁcation its a potential source of mismatch between the models and the real
life behaviour of the ﬂoating wind turbine, its addition to the calculations of the steady state
responses is crucial to obtain the long period amplitudes observed in the experiments. If the
84
==PAGE:87==
double summation calculation of the forces is improved in computation eﬃciency terms, it will
possible for QuLAF to calculate the loads itself and not depend on an external algorithm which
enhances the overall simulation time to ﬁnd the desired solution.
Regarding the use of forced oscillations as a way of estimating the aerodynamic damping, sub-
stantial discrepancies were found with the decay tests and a deeper study is required in order to
ﬁnd the source of these diﬀerences. The main advantage of the alternative method is its simplic-
ity. The simulations are shorter, there is signiﬁcant less amount of bodies and parts that need to
work in the state-of-the-art code, making it a simpler model and easier to run. Unfortunately,
the current values obtained with this method cannot represent accurately the results obtained
with the classic decay tests at the moment and further investigation will be required.
Despite the aforementioned limitations, QuLAF has proven to be an accurate tool to use for the
preliminary design stages of a ﬂoating wind turbine. It was found that it could give results quick
and comparable to HAWC2 results for the natural frequencies, ﬂoater motions and loads , which
allows it to be an excellent optimization tool for diﬀerent ﬂoater conﬁgurations. Nonetheless, a
full aero-hydro-servo-elastic model will be necessary to fully evaluate the response of the ﬂoating
wind turbine. During the validation there were found large discrepancies between the experi-
mental data and the model results. This was mainly due to diﬀerences in the wind modelling
and to the inability of low ﬁdelity aerodynamic tool of correctly predicting the behaviour of a
ﬂoating wind turbine. Therefore, it is not possible to give a ﬁnal conclusion on this regard, and in
future work the same inputs as for the experimental data should be used in the numerical models.
9.1
Future Work
One recommendation for further development of the Quick Load Analysis for Floating wind tur-
bines would be to create a standard protocol of how to build the setup in order to run the tool.
A standardized way of carrying out the preliminary simulations for the external inputs, such
as the mooring stiﬀness, could reduce the time for running QuLAF signiﬁcantly, by eliminating
errors and giving a quick troubleshooting. Additionally it would reduce the design optimization
times which is the main objective for the creation and implementation of QuLAF.
Furthermore, it is clear that a further calibration of the wind will be necessary to have the same
input for the numerical models and the experimental data in order to have conclusive results
for the validation. Even though an aeroelastic code does not fully simulates the behaviour of
a ﬂoating wind turbine, most of the deviation was due to diﬀerences on the wind seen by the
turbine in the experimental data and the numerical models.
For the second order wave eﬀects over the system’s response, it is recommended to investigate
new CPU eﬃcient algorithmic methods in order to reduce not only the time required to calculate
the quadratic transfer function (QTF), but also the double summation involved in the excita-
tion forces. This feature is imperative since the analysis done on chapter 7 demonstrated the
importance of the frequency interactions and how they aﬀected the ﬁnal solution of the system.
As for the alternative way of extracting the aerodynamic damping, a further look at the dynamic
inﬂow eﬀect inﬂuence on the rotor during the forced oscillations would be a course of study that
could bring a solution to the discrepancy of the results with the decay tests.
85
==PAGE:88==
Finally, further work on expanding the QuLAF must be concentrated in enhancing the model’s
degrees of freedom in order to asses its behaviour when the incident external sources do not
have the same direction as one of the pre-deﬁned directions in this study. This will approach the
model to more realistic case scenarios. When the TetraSpar ﬂoating Wind Turbine is designed
at full scale in the near future, it is recommended that QuLAF implements the inﬂuence of the
ﬂexible blades in the rotor aerodynamic behaviour.
