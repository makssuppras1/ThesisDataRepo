Abstract
Buildings are responsible for the 40% of the whole global energy consumption as 1/3 of the
greenhouse gas emissions. In a world projected towards a more environmentally sustainable
philosophy, it is fundamental to understand the importance of reducing the energy consumes
and the environmental impacts of the buildings.
The renovation process is the main focus of this Thesis, which has the goal of targeting not
only one speciﬁc building, but one type of construction. This construction type was implied
in the buildings erected in Copenhagen between 1850 and 1920.
The aim of the project is to design a renovation solution for the building envelope, considered
as the major responsible for the energy performance. The approach, will be focused not only
on the reduction of the energy consumption, but also to the Life Cycle Assessment of the
diﬀerent components and solutions, a daylight analysis and an economical estimate of the
various scenarios.
The results show that signiﬁcant savings can be reached utilising diﬀerent insulation systems.
For the external wall, it can be saved up to the 25.2% of the energy consumption. In the third
ﬂoor slab, dividing the building from the uninsulated and unheated roof, can be saved up to
the 24.6% of the energy. Finally as a combination of the two aforementioned components, the
renovating solutions save up to the 47.3% of the heating energy consumption.
Finally, the chosen renovation solution was not only found from the energy savings results, but
also from a Life Cycle Assessment point of view, a daylight perspective, an economical analysis
and some feasibility hints. The ﬁnal choice is the solution accounting the Isover Plusλ34,
together with the Papiruldλ39, A mix of satisfactory results among all the aforementioned
analysis.

Chapter 1
Introduction and project aims
Nowadays, in Denmark as in the rest of the world, the building sector is more and more
projected towards a sustainable concept.
All the new building designs are focused on an
energy saving phylosophy. On the other hand, the new buildings account less than the 1% of
the building stock. The other 99%, consists of the existing buildings. Furthermore, the 75%
of the buildings, are of residential type and most of them are yet not renovated. This implies
an over-use of energy, which is reﬂected also in a over-use of the earth resources. Solving this
problem, goes together with the european energy plan, for the reduction of the gas emissions
of the 80% by the 2050. For all this reasons and in order to achieve this European goal, it is
also important to renovate the existing building stock.
This Thesis, is focused on the renovation design of a heritage residential building from the
early 1900s, located in Østerbro, a district in the north-east area of Copenhagen. However, the
object of this renovation design, is to be applicable to all the buildings constructed between
the 1850 and the 1930. This renovation design will aim attention at the weakest elements of
the building envelope, which were found to be in the external wall and in the last ﬂoor slab.
These are the elements through which the heat ﬁnds its easier path to the external, leaving
the room spaces. This causes an increase in the energy consumption, in order to assure an
acceptable indoor climate for the users.
The project faces also diﬀerent limitations, as the impossibility to change the external façade
towards the street. This imposition comes from the Copenhagen’s Municipality, which wants
to preserve the aesthetics of the heritage buildings.
The design, is based on the research of ones of the best insulation products and systems,
available in the Danish market, with a focus on their energy performance as well as their
environmental impacts in a cradle-to-gate perspective.
The selected insulation systems (addressed as components), are simulated together with the
residential part of the building, in the courtyard façade and in the third ﬂoor slab (from the
ﬁrst to the third ﬂoor), in order to give an understanding of their impacts on the building
performances.
Once all the components are evaluated, the selected ones will compose two solutions. These
two solutions will be investigated from both the energy and and the environmental impacts
points of view, in order to establish the best performing ones. Finally, in order to have more

Chapter 2
Literature review
This literature review focuses on the heritage building renovation, seen from diﬀerents points
of view.
• "Facts". It is important to deﬁne the topic in a context.
• "Old-tech". It gives a background on how the old buildings were constructed in the
past.
• "Why renovate?". It analyses the decision making, when and why it is better to renovate
instead of tearing down to rebuild.
• "New-tech". It is relevant to analyse the newer techniques, which are in use nowadays, in
order to renovate the old buildings. It is also divided into the subsections "techniques",
"materials" and "software".
• "Not only energy". It is explained what is essential to keep in mind, further than the
energy-saving matter, when renovating a building.
• "Life cycle assessment". In here a brief introduction to this topic is presented.
As it is substantial to give a boundary to this topic, which could be diﬀerent from one coun-
try to another or even another city, all the literature review is focused on the relevant facts
concerning the Copenhaguese building stock.
2.1
Facts
A big problem humanity has to face, is the climate change. For too many years the human
kind has severely depleted the world’s resources without comprehending which could be the
consequence for this behaviour.

The United Nation Environment Program states that, globally talking, "buildings use 40% of
global energy, 25% of global water, 40% of global resources and emit 1/3 of GHG emissions"
as represented in Figure 2.1. The same results were presented by the European commission [1].

The European Union set two targets for two periods of time. The ﬁrst one is to reduce by 20%
the gas emissions, by 2020. The second one, more challenging, is to achieve an 80% saving by

fuel free by 2050 [2]. In order to achieve this goal, among all the diﬀerent paths, Denmark is
willing to convert the source of heating for buildings, from fossil fuels to renewable energy, by
2035 [3]. It could even be a more terriﬁc jump to the target if this conversion is associated to
minor energy needs for the buildings’ heating, thanks to the nowadays techniques for reducing
their energy consumption.

2.2
Old-tech
After the Copenhagen historic ﬁres in 1728 and in 1795 (represented in Figure 2.4), there was
a need for stricting the rules for what concerned the way the buildings had to be constructed.
The presence of the wood as the main material, showed a strong weakness towards the ﬁres,
turning the buildings in the main ﬁre supply. After the ﬁrst drafts of a complete building
regulation, in the 1856, the ﬁrst detailed version came out which was upgraded later with
minor diﬀerences in the 1871, 1875 and 1889). The book (Figure 2.5) was so detailed, that, it
has been applied for more than a century in the most of the Copenhagen buildings. i.e. there
were the ﬁrst impositions on the ceiling height and in the wall materials [6].
For all the afore mentioned reasons, the buildings erected in Copenhagen in the period between

2.4.1
Insulation
The external envelope of a building, is the most important component when referring both
to energy consumption and indoor climate. It is the element that inﬂuences the most the
insulation of the building from the external weather, reducing the heat loss through the walls.
In the case of a renovation, it is vital to design a continuous envelope, from the foundation to
the roof.
The insulation can be applied in two ways, externally and internally [11].
• External insulation (Figure 2.9a).
This is the most eﬀective measure.
It allows to
easily apply a continuous layer around all the building. This reduces potential thermal
bridges and increases the structural integrity (as an example, avoiding the condensation
in proximity of the structural elements). Moreover, the internal surface temperature of
a wall would be closer to the indoor air temperature, avoiding condensation and possibly
turning the wall in an eﬀective thermal mass which can help in achieving higher indoor
climate quality classes.
Despite all these beneﬁts, the building would loose its old
external identity.
• Internal insulation (Figure 2.9b). With this method more diﬃculties have to be faced. It
is problematic to eliminate thermal bridges. A structural decay is more likely to happen.
There is a great temperature excursion between the added insulation and the existing
wall layers, increasing the chances of condensation, thus the building is more likely to
last shorter due to deterioration. All this, if not designed properly can be reﬂected in a
poor indoor climate and poor living conditions. Furthermore, installing the insulation
on the inside of the building, reduces the useable area, changes the internal identity
of the building and obliges the inhabitants to leave the building until the end of the
renovation process.

2.4.2
Insulation materials
The insulation of a building, can be achieved by using diﬀerent types of materials, each of
them with diﬀerent characteristics. Among the wide variety, in the next sections, the mineral
glass wool, the rock wool and the wood wool insulations are going to be presented.
2.4.2.1
Mineral wool insulation
The mineral wool insulation is made of sand and glass slag. It is produced by melting the
raw material until it transforms into lava. Once the lava state is reached, it is spun into ﬁbers
through a centrifugal wheel system. By letting it rest, the material deposits forming felt,
which is the real composition of the actual ﬁnal product. Among its characteristics, mineral
wool presents a density which can vary between 14 kg/m3 and 60 kg/m3, according to its
performances. Thanks to its relatively low density, it has a lower impact on the environment
when compared to the majority of the other common insulation materials having the same
thermal characteristics. It has a low resistance to pressure. The thermal conductivity varies
from 0.03 W/mK, to 0.041 W/mK. It is a superbly performing sound insulator. It is not
ﬂammable and belongs to the A1 ﬁre rating proof class. It is in general an excellent material
and its wide use proves it [14].
2.4.2.2
Rock wool insulation
The rock wool insulation is made of vulcan origin material, dolomite, diabase and basalt. Its
production procedure is the same of the mineral wool. The density of the rock wool is high and
can be between 30 kg/m3 and 200 kg/m3. This is also a weak point of this material, which,
due to its high density (thus, more material is used per m3), results in higher environmental
impacts than the other common insulation materials having the same thermal characteristics.
On the other hand, this high density results also in a greater thermal mass potential and
better structural properties. It has a high resistance to pressure. Its thermal conductivity
lies between 0.033 W/mK and 0.039 W/mK, according to its performances. As well as the
mineral wool, it has excellent sound insulation properties. It is classiﬁed as non-combustible
and belongs to the A1-A2 ﬁre rating proof class depending on the products [14].
2.4.2.3
Wood wool insulation
The wood wool insulation is composed by surplus and recycled paper, which are resulting
hollow cellulose ﬁbres. In order to increase the ﬁre performances, ﬁre retardant salts are used.
The density of the wood ﬁbres is around 40 kg/m3 and 45 kg/m3. Its thermal conductivity

considered to be combustible with a ﬁre rating of D-s2,D0 which means also it has a limited
smoke production and no droplets [14].
2.4.2.4
High reﬂectance radiators panels
Another tool which can be used when it is not possible to insulate neither from the outside
nor on the inside, is to use a high reﬂectance panel for the radiators. These panels have
the goal of reﬂecting the heat which otherwise would be released to the external, causing a
substancial heat loss. The properties of the material rely on a high reﬂectance, thus a poor
absorptivity. It has been estimated by the British Board of Agreement, that, "from the use
of a Radﬂek sheet reﬂective radiator panel inserted between a radiator and an uninsulated
wall, there is reduction of the heat ﬂow by approximately 45% through the section of the wall,
directly behind the radiator". In order to get this achievements, the panels should not get
dirty, neither oxidate. This study has been conducted by an empiric analysis with thermal
sensors, with and without using this speciﬁc panel from Radﬂek [15].
2.4.3
Windows
The openings are one of the most critic elements to take care of in a building. From the
openings the heat can ﬁnd the easiest path to the outside. For this reason the choice of the
windows is crucial. The preference should be towards the multiple panes windows with an
excellent insulationg performing frame. The frame should be strong and at the same time
containing the least material possible with a hollowed structure, so that the heat can not have
a direct path to the external. The window should reduce the air inﬁltration to the minimum
possible (even though, it is also important not to have a complete air tight external envelope).
It is important to have a clear idea of what is their role and how important they are, in order
to improve the energy building performances. In this study, the windows are not going to
be changed because of a lack of informations concerning the new openings geometries in the
courtyard façade.
2.4.4
Ventilation
The installation of a mechanical ventilation system is often taken into account. It might not
decrease the energy use in the building, but it would enhance the indoor climate quality in
most of the cases. In most of the cases, by reaching an appropriate ventilation rate, also the
condensation in the most critic areas can be avoided. The new HVAC systems are equipped
with heat recovery, in order to transfer the heat from the polluted outgoing air to the clean
one entering the house, reducing the heat losses and the energy spent to heat the air. As
for the windows, in this project the ventilation will not be changed but it is fundamental to
understand the beneﬁts that come with its right implementation [11].

Sustainable materials and resources
The aim of this section is to give a brief up-to-date description of the new researched sus-

When taking in consideration the three aforementioned elements (insulation, windows and

can be. The most impacting on the environment would be the insulation material for its large
use in the construction. Several studies have been conducted in order to understand if it is
possible to ﬁnd a valid alternative to the classic mineral wool or the polystyrene. A substitute
could be a natural or a recycled material, but, unfortunately their use is not widely spread
yet. As mentioned in a study [1] from 2012, in 2011 plastics and mineral wool predominate
the sales accounting for the 93% of the market share. The problem with these materials is the
consumption of non-renewable materials in their production phase as well as their disposal
stage, in particular for plastics.
Between the alternative natural and recycled materials, prominent results have been achieved
by the polyethylene terephthalate (PET) and the recycled textiles [1].
The polyethylene terephthalate is widely used in the bottling and packaging sector. Its dis-
posal stage causes serious environmental impacts. Recycling this waste to produce insulation
material could have two beneﬁts at once. Concerning its properties, it has been tested and
the results gave a thermal conductivity of 0.0355 W/mK and a 30kg/m3 density [1].
The recycled textiles at the same time produce an abundance quantity of waste which could
be recycled to produce insulation. A speciﬁc material produced by Valverde et al. is composed
by polyester and polyurethane from synthetic textiles. It achieves a thermal conductivity of
0.0358 W/mK and a 80kg/m3 density [1].
Further studies have to be accomplished in order to understand the behaviour of these mate-
rials towards ﬁre resistance. All these were mentioned to give an idea on how it is possible to
obtain insulating materials with highly performing properties by recycling waste.

2.6
Life cycle assessment
The Life Cycle Assessment is the study of the environmental impacts of a product on the
environment.
The assessment can be either "cradle to gate" or "cradle to grave".
The
diﬀerence between these two methods lies in the stages which are taken into account for the
study. In the "cradle to gate", the product’s impacts are considered from the extraction of
the raw materials from the earth, until its production in the factory. On the other hand, in
the "cradle to grave" perspective, the product’s impacts are considered from the raw material
extraction, until its disposal stage, also comprehending its use stage [16].
The LCA scope is to make the designer aware of the impacts that each product has on the
environment and perhaps to inﬂuence the decision maker, choosing the product with the lower
impacts. It can be deﬁned as a tool which gives another perspective from where to compare
multiple products.
The assessment covers all the environmental impacts connected to the life cycle of a product, as
the raw material consumption (linked to the availability of the material) and all the emissions
to air, water and soil. Once the assessment is complete, the results of the impact categories
are analyzed and compared among all the diﬀerent scenarios.
Functional unit
The functional unit is a quantitative reference. It helps to diﬀerentiate
between primary and secondary functions. It serves as a link between two diﬀerent products
which have to be compared, as both of them have to rely on the same functional unit. A
functional unit, takes into consideration the function provided by the product in a certain
quantity, for a certain period and with a determined quality [16].
System boundaries
The system boundaries deﬁne which parts of the life cycle and which
processes belong to the analysed system, i.e. are required for providing its function as de-
ﬁned by its functional unit, hence they separate the analysed system from the rest of the
technosphere. At the same time, the system boundaries also deﬁne the boundary between
the analysed system and the ecosphere, i.e. deﬁne across which boundary the exchange of
elementary ﬂows with nature takes place [16].

material can reduce the energy use, thus the environmental impact that would be necessary
to produce that amount of energy. The energy consumed to heat the building is based on a
district heating system and on the results obtained from the IDA ICE software. The lifespan
for the materials was assumed to be 100 years for the mineral glass and rock wools, whilst for
the wood wool it was assumed to be only 50 years.
The energy requirements with the utilization of each material was related to the impacts for
producing 1 kWh with the district heating serving the Østerbro district in Copenhagen [19].
The next step consist in the sum of the production stage and use stage impacts, in order to
ﬁnd the impacts for the cradle to gate perspective.
The ﬁnal results for each impact category was analysed and compared with the others, to
understand which solution impacts more or less than the others.
All the calculations have been done with the spreadsheets software "Numbers" from Apple.

Functional unit
The functional unit for this assessment is: "The insulation of the courtyard façade with a

The building on which this work is focused is located at the 69 of Kildevældsgade, in Østerbro,

In this phase, the building in itself is evaluated in its general conditions and in its energy use.

From the hand drawings to the digital model
The very ﬁrst materials from which the project has been analysed were the original hand draw-

Among all the given documents, two plans, one section and the façade design were selected.

for an hand-drawing not to be perfect. On the other hand it is important to know how to
face these problems. Knowing the building type and the building regulations of that period
could deﬁnitely be a powerful tool.

Building envelope
The building envelope is only made of structural masonry elements and exterior ﬁnishes. At
the age of the construction, the energy saving measures in the building envelope were not a
primary need. For this reason the walls have just one layer, without neither an air cavity to
function as a barrier for the passing of the heat (this technique was going to emerge after

key point in the project, as also this building façade is considered worth-to-preserve. For this

4.3.1
External walls
The external wall core is the brick structure. Its thickness changes according to the height,
as shown in Table 4.1. In the top ﬂoors the external structural wall is thinner, in order to
provide a light weight, not overloading the construction. These structural elements are like
columns integrated in the external boundary of the building [5]. In-between these columns
the windows are located. Right above the windows, the wall has a thickness which is the same
as the structural wall at that height and carries the load of the beams of the upper ﬂoor in
that area. On the other hand, under the windows, the wall is only one brick thick (Appendix
A.3), consisting in the thinnest part of the external wall, thus, one of the areas in which it is
simpler for the heat to be released to the exterior [6].
4.3.2
Roof
The roof has a light wood structure. As it appears from the Figure 4.1 it is not insulated.
Its use is to serve as a storing room. It plays anyway an important role, as, since it is not a
heated space, it works as a bridge between the building (especially the third ﬂoor) and the
external. As a matter of facts, its inﬂuence on the energy consumption of the third ﬂoor will
be analysed.

beams, at half height, there is a wooden plate which separates the air in the lower part from
the sandy clay on top of it, in order to provide some acoustic insulation [6]. The conditions

3rd ﬂoor Slab insulation
As mentioned before, for the energy porpuses, the slabs are not taken into consideration,
except for the one dividing the third ﬂoor from the roof. In this case, as the roof is unheated,
it cannot be considered as fully insulating from the external.
For this reason the roof is
considered as a resistance for the third ﬂoor.
On the contrary of the external walls, for the insulation of the slab, there are not diﬀerent
systems. The method considers the ﬁlling of the air space with insulation. It is not a simple
work. Looking at the roof structure presented on Table 5.5 and at Figure 2.7, it can be seen
that under the ﬁrst layer of Wooden ﬂoor battens, there is a layer of beams together with
the sandy clay. Removing the sandy clay is hard, thus, expensive. For this reason, the most
feasible solution, would be to create a hole big enough to inﬂate the insulation inside the
air layer. Anyhow, it is interesting to understand also the energy performances of a solution
which comprehends the replacing of the air layer with more eﬃcient insulation panels.
In all the cases the volume of material which is going to be utilized is equal to the thickness
of the layer (0.09m), multiplied by the percentage of area in which the material is present
(90%) by the ﬂoor area (402m2), resulting in 29m3.

5.5
Daylight Analysis
The goal of this analysis, was to give another tool through which the thickness of the insulation
could be chose. The thought behind this analysis is that, the aim is to have an insulation
thickness which is not to thin for the energy porpuses, and at the same time not to thick
to obstacle the entering of the daylight inside the building’s rooms. For this reasons, three
layout are analysed, with 100mm, 200mm and 300mm of insulation more than the external
==PAGE:69==
5.5 Daylight Analysis
57
existing brick wall. The windows were positioned always 50mm distant from the edge of the
insulation. The daylight analysis, has been conducted on two steps:
• creation of a simplistic SketchUp model.
• import and analysis of the model of Velux Daylight Visualizer
5.5.1
Creation of the SketchUp model
The aim of this phase, was to create a 3D model of the building, together with the sur-
rounding shading buildings. Three rooms per ﬂoor in three diﬀerent orientations have been
analyzed. The design of the rooms, implied a good detail level with all the diﬀerent com-
ponents assigned to diﬀerent layers (ceiling ﬂoor, frames, glass panes, walls, windowsills and
the external ground). A material has been assigned to each layer with the same name, in
order to create a fully compatible model to import on Velux Daylight Visualizer. The shading
buildings are represented by simple vertical planes.
5.5.2
Daylight Factor Analysis
Once the models have been completed on Google SketchUp, they are imported on Velux
Daylight Visualizer.
To each material, was assigned a determined reﬂectance value, with
the exception for the glass panes in which the transmittance was assigned. The values are
presented on Table on Figure 5.13. The typical surface reﬂectances and transmittance were
used, as the ﬁnishing materials in the rooms were unknown [32].

Chapter 7
Conclusions
The problem of the energy saving is becoming more and more important in the global scenario
and it is fundamental to commit as much as possible towards a world with minor consumes,
thus minor impacts on the environment, without marginalizing other imporant issues as the
inhabitants’ needs.
The goal of the Thesis, was to investigate a feasible and eﬃcient renovation process, which
could be adapted not only to this speciﬁc building, but to all the similar buildings constructed
in the period between the 1850 and the 1930 as they share the same imposed geometries and
characteristics.
Although the aesthetics limitations, as the prohibition to change the external façade towards
the street, thus, the possibility to insulate only one side of the building, the achievements are
more than satisfactory, with an average energy saving of 32%.
At the same time, the LCA, taking as example the "Global Warming Potential GWP", which
is representative also for all the others impact categories, shows in the long term (100 years)
a saving of the 31% in the impacts.
Also the daylight is not signiﬁcantly aﬀected by the adding of 200mm of insulation.
The economic costs, despite the lack of informations concerning the product costs and the
manwork, show a signiﬁcant saving in the cost of the energy necessary the heat the building,
almost 200 euro per m2 in 50 years.
The inhabitants’ needs were also part of the design phase. As a matter of facts, a priority was
to let the users live in the building while the renovation was in its process to be done. For
this reason the internal insulation was not taken into consideration, more than all the other
reasons already explained.
==PAGE:96==
84
Conclusions
7.1
Recommendations and further studies
This study, due to a limited amount of time and resources, could not go more in deep,
broading also the study area to the ventilation and the windows replacing for example. As
the ventilation plays a very important role in the indoor climate, all the results concerning
this subject were found to be not signiﬁcant. For this reason further studies are recommended
in this speciﬁc topic, which, as aforementioned, has to be a priority for a renovation process,
as well as the energy or the Life cycle assessment.
Another issue which could be furthermore investigated relates to one of the weakest area of
this type of buildings, the parapet walls with the radiators. As these are one of the areas
in which the heat ﬁnds the easiest path to the external, it would be deﬁnitely interesting to
investigate how the use of high reﬂectance panels (brieﬂy introduced on section 2.4.2.4) can
reduce the heat ﬂow to the external, reducing the heat loss, thus the energy use.
The daylight analysis could be improved by knowing the real ﬁnishing materials used in the
apartments and correcting the reﬂectaces of the various components.
Furthermore, a more precise cost-analysis, coprehending the products’ costs and the manwork,
could deﬁnitely be another input for the decision making.
