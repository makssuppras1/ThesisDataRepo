Abstract
The goal of thesis was to develop a fabrication process for a fully functional Row-Column anodic
bonded Capacitive Micromachined Ultrasonic Transducer (CMUT). This was done using an insulat-
ing glass substrate and a Poly-Silicon On Insulator (PSOI) wafer as a top plate which was anodically
bonded. This is an alternative approach to fabricating the Row-Column CMUTs to the preferred
LOCal Oxidation of Silicon (LOCOS) based fusion bonding which is more sensitive to particles.
Furthermore, the LOCOS based Row-Column (RC) CMUT fabrication is tedious and they suﬀer
from substrate coupling. The substrate coupling is eliminated in the anodic bonded RC CMUTs
by the insulating substrate and the fabrication process is approximately three times faster than the
LOCOS.
Four fabrication runs were conducted during this thesis, where two wafers without nitride from the
ﬁrst run was realized by the author and one with nitride as an insulating layer on the top plate
was realized by Magnus G. Petersen in a concurrent project [1].
Electrical characterization was
done on both wafer types for both RC 16 + 16 and RC 190 + 190 which showed the expected
electro-mechanical behaviour i.e. a parabola for the capacitance-voltage (CV) sweep up to 80V.
Additionally the current voltage (IV) measurement showed low leakage current in the pA range
indicating a functional device. Acoustic measurements of the RC 16 + 16 showed charging, which
is a well known problem for CMUTs, whereas it was not possible to get a signal for the RC 16 + 16
without nitride as they were operated at 85V far from the pull-in voltage of 408V. Both RC 16 +
16 broke down and malfunctioned at 80-90V.
A new mask design was created in this thesis allowing standard mask set aligning and maskless
aligning while implementing an integrated apodization scheme to optimize image quality. Moreover
a new way of only opening up to the bottom contacts was added to shield the connecting wires of the
bottom electrode with nitride. This design was used for the last three fabrication runs. Unfortunately
these were not realized as a consequence of severe particle problems in the DTU Nanolab cleanroom.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This introductory seek to provide the reader with an overall understanding of ultrasound and the
ultrasound transducers, where the piezo-electric transducer and the Capacitive Micromachined Ul-
trasonic Transducer (CMUT) is presented. A brief description of going from 2D to 3D images and
the Row-Column array along with a comparison of the two types of RC CMUTs made in the MEMS-
Applied group and a perspective to what other research groups have fabricated on the Row-Column
addressed CMUT front. Finishing with the aim for the thesis and an outline of the thesis.
1.1
Ultrasound transducers for medical imaging
Ultrasound is a non-invasive medical imaging technique of the subjacent structures inside the human
body used for diagnostics within a broad variety of diseases, but also as an observation tool in the
progress of pregnancy as an example. An ultrasound transducer is able to emit longitudinal pressure
waves into the human body by actuation of an electric potential being applied to it. The wavelength
used in medical ultrasound applications are typically in the range between 1-20 MHz which allows
it to penetrate into the soft tissue, the pressure wave is gradually attenuated inside the body. The
wave is partially reﬂected on structures with a change in acoustic impedance from the medium it
is propagating in. This reﬂection is what is called an echo and is sent back towards the transducer
which receives the echo. The distance to the reﬂection, hence the scanned structure can be found
from the time of emit to receive of the pulse along with the speed of sound in the scanned medium.
Diﬀerent distances are assigned speciﬁc grey-scale colours, which is what makes up the traditional
2D ultrasound images.
1.2
Motivation
In the MEMS-Applied Sensors group at DTU a cooperation with BK Medical, Rigshospitalet and
CFU aims to create superresolution 3D ultrasound able to track red blood cells and thereby observe
the development of tumours, diabetes and cardiovascular diseases and determine if the treatment
is working. The goal of this project is to create a Row-Column-Addressed CMUT for 3D medical
imaging for a faster and viable diagnosis of tumour growth, diabetes and cardio vascular diseases,
where this thesis project aims to contribute to the optimization of the fabrication and characterization
of the CMUTs.

1.3.
THE PIEZO-ELECTRIC TRANSDUCER
1.3
The piezo-electric transducer
Currently the piezo-electric transducers dominate the conventional ultrasound for commercial med-
ical imaging. Piezo, abbreviated PTZ is a type of ceramics with piezoelectric properties ﬁrst dis-
covered by Jaques and Pierre Curie in 1880 as the piezoelectric eﬀect. Applying a potential to this
type of ceramic will make it contract and contrary straining the material will create collection of
charges setting up an electric ﬁeld. This property can be exploited to emit ultrasound pressure waves
by applying an AC pulse with high frequency forcing the material to contract and expand. PZT
has a relative high stiﬀness causing a high acoustic impedance resulting in an impedance mismatch
with the human skin. To remedy this obstacle matching layers are required before the piezo-electric
transducers can be used for medical imaging. The stiﬀness also makes it more diﬃcult to bring the
actuated transducer to a stop resulting in ringing after an emitted pulse. The ringing eﬀectively
prolongs the pulse and lowers the bandwidth and consequently decreases the axial resolution in an
ultrasound image.
1.4
The CMUT
Capacitive Micromachined Ultrasonic Transducers, CMUTs are the up-and-coming ultrasound trans-
ducers within medical imaging with already commercially available companies products like KOLO
for medical use and Butterﬂy Network’s point-of-care device proclaiming CMUTs as a viable com-
petitor to the state-of-the-art piezo-electric transducer. The CMUT is a micro-scale capacitor with
a movable top plate, see ﬁgure 1.1 applying a potential enables the emission of pressure waves. A
DC bias is applied to the top electrode which forces the top plate towards the substrate due to
electrostatic attraction, this is called spring softening. By superposing an AC pulse with a smaller
amplitude than the stationary DC the top plate will start to oscillate and emit pressure waves. In
receive mode the top plate is bent by the reﬂected pressure wave causing a change in capacitance
which is used to create images. The advantages of CMUTs compared to piezo-transducers is the
broader bandwidth, lower acoustic impedance and smaller footprint made possible by the semicon-
ductor fabrication industry. The drawbacks of the CMUT is that it is diﬃcult to achieve a pressure
output as high as for the piezo electric transducers. The reason for this is the thinner top plate,
a trade-oﬀwhich is made to achieve a broad bandwidth. To mitigate this a DC potential is uti-
lized to exert more pressure from the plate, however the DC potential complicates the electronics.
Another challenges is the charging problem, requiring excellent cleanliness and high quality of the
implemented dielectric.

With a linear array focusing is only possible in one plane, hence 2D images. The quality of the image
scales linearly with the number of elements. Today modern ultrasound requires approximately 100
elements, meaning that N = 100 electrical interconnections need to be made in order to actuate the
individual elements [3]. 3D images are rendered possible by 2D arrays in this thesis the Row-Column
addressed Array is utilized, which simply consist of two overlapping 1D arrays where one is rotated
90◦, see ﬁgure 1.3 in total giving 2N elements. The top elements in the horizontal direction are called
rows and also referred to as top electrodes. The bottom elements going in the vertical direction are
called columns and also referred to as bottom electrodes.
Figure 1.3: Sketch of 2D Row-Column array, showing rows horizontally and columns vertically [3].
Emitting with rows and receiving with columns creates an overlap focusing on a point in the volume,
which enables 3D imaging. The direct translation of a linear array into a 2D array is called a Fully
Populated Matrix, FPM. FPM scales inconveniently with the number of elements required for an
improved image quality. Whereas a Row-Column addressed array scales linearly which is favourable,
since image quality scales linearly with the number of elements.
The electrical connections are
greatly reduced by the row-column conﬁguration which only requires 2N connections while a fully
populated matrix, FPM requires N× N = N2 connections. To appreciate the ingenuity of the Row-
Column-addressed array an example in comparison with the modern linear arrays: 2D arrays would
need at least 100 elements in both directions yielding 10.000 electrical connections for a FPM and
only 200 interconnections for the RC. With the demand for higher resolution ultrasound images
the complexity of the fabrication and the packaging of the electronics increases, this is why the
Row-Column addressed array is an attractive candidate.

1.6
Previous work in the MEMS-Applied group
The favoured fabrication technique of CMUTs has been a double LOCOS, LOCal Oxidation of
Silicon used for deﬁning the cavities performed on a silicon substrate combined with fusion bonding
with a SOI wafer as a top plate. This process has undergone a lot of process development since
the ﬁrst CMUT project began in 2012, however it is still a complicated and lengthy process with a
critical bonding step very sensitive to particles, which makes it diﬃcult to obtain a high yield. An
alternative to the favoured LOCOS process is the anodic bonding method with the cavities deﬁned
in an insulating glass substrate. The huge beneﬁt of this is to avoid the substrate coupling which
dominates the silicon based CMUTs, called cross-talk. A sketch of the substrate coupling for both
the boroﬂoat and silicon based CMUTs, can be seen in ﬁgure 1.4. Another advantage of anodically
bonded CMUTs is the negligible bottom electrode resistance, as this is changed from a highly doped
silicon to a metal. The electrode resistance becomes of importance when producing the large RC 190
+ 190 arrays. Furthermore anodic bonding is less sensitive to particles, increasing the possibility
of a higher yield and the process takes days instead of weeks, see the table 1.1 for a comparison
between the two processes.
Previous work on the anodic bonding process has been executed by Postdoc Mathias Engholm and
master students Lotte Hendriksen, Nicolai A. Michaelsen, Magnus G. Petersen. Their extensive work
will be the foundation and stepping stone of this thesis which will investigate the implementation of
an insulating nitride layer into this already established fabrication process.
Figure 1.4: When characterizing a bottom electrode the alleged substrate coupling are sketched for a silicon
and glass substrate, respectively.

1.8
The aim of the thesis
The aim of this thesis is to produce anodic bonded row-column addressed CMUTs with an insulating
nitride layer for 3D medical imaging. To do so a new masks set have been designed to implement
the nitride layer on the top plate alongside implementation of an integrated apodization scheme to
improve image quality [6]. Realising this requires a renewed processﬂow based on the work previously
done in the MEMS group and a comprehensive process optimization with preliminary testing before
implementing the nitride layer. The process optimization has primarily been focused on the deﬁnition
of the bottom electrode and the anodic bonding as these proved to be the most challenging steps.
A secondary goal was to automatically characterize a full row-column array with 190 +190 elements
with a new characterization technique for Row-Column array developed by Prof. Erik V. Thomsen
[7].

2.6.
ANODIC BONDING
2.6
Anodic bonding
The simplest form of anodic bonding is that of a glass wafer being bonded to a silicon wafer. The
two wafers are put together by hand where they will adhere close to each other as a result of Van der
Waals forces and Newton rings with interferential colours will appear. The wafer stack is placed on a
ﬂat metal plate, used for both heating and applying the bias with the silicon wafer facing the anode
and the glass wafer towards the cathode. The wafer stack is heated to the bonding temperature in
the range of 300-500◦C and then the bias is applied. The glass wafer is negatively polarized while
the silicon wafer is positively polarized. By increasing the temperature the resistivity of the glass
wafer decreases enough for the sodium ions, Na+ to ﬂow through the glass towards the negatively
biased cathode due to their high mobility under increased temperature, see ﬁgure 2.2 from [11].
Figure 2.2: The three stages of anodic bonding showing the initial contact, creation of the depletion layer
due to ion ﬂow enabled by heating and applied potential and the formation of the siloxane bonds.
This ﬂow of Na+ to the cathode leaves behind a negatively charged region near the interface and
thereby creating a depletion layer. There will be a strong electrostatic ﬁeld across the gap of the two
wafers pulling them together. During this close contact the chemical bonding occurs. Exactly how
the bonds are formed is still debated [11]. One theory relies on the oxygen ions left behind in the
depletion layer oxidize the silicon and form the strong siloxane bonds, Si-O bonds see ﬁgure 2.2. The
other relies on the ”water-pump” phenomenon, where water trapped at the interface will chemically
react with the silicon and the H+ ions will drift towards the cathode. Initially due to the surface
roughness the two wafers will only be in intimate contact in a few locations as seen on the ﬁgure
2.2. As the gap is smallest here the electrostatic pulling force will be greatest and the bonding will
initiate from these locations and spread like rings in the water. In the beginning the current of Na+
ion will be largest and the depletion layer will grow until it reaches a saturation and the current will
gradually decrease.
The challenge with anodic bonding is the thermal expansion of the bonded materials, they need to
have thermal expansion coeﬃcients on the same order to avoid a thermal mismatch. The mismatch
of the thermal expansion coeﬃcients between the materials will cause strain in the wafer stack after

Fabrication RCABCMUT2
This is the ﬁrst chapter on the fabrication process of Row-Column anodic bonded CMUTs, RCABC-
MUT in which the ﬁrst run named RCABCMUT2 will be described thoroughly as fabrication was
the main focus of the thesis. RCABCMUT2 is based on the previous work done by Magnus G.
Petersen and is 3 mask process. Then initial testing of the insulating nitride layer before it was
implemented in the RCABCMUT design. All fabrication have been carried out in the DTU Nanolab
cleanroom facilities.
The ﬁrst run was initiated with 8 boroﬂoat wafers. The boroﬂoat wafers was left in the 250◦C oven
to dehydrate for a minimum of approximately 24 hours to drive out water to obtain a better adhesion
during the metal deposition.
5.1
Metal deposition as mask for cavity etch
The deposition of chrome was done with the Temescal which is an e-beam evaporator. The wafers
are loaded into a holder with planetary and rotational motion to ensure uniform coverage. The
loading is done through a load lock to minimize pump down time as the chamber needs to be under
high vacuum, 1 × 10−6 Torr. The high vacuum allows line of sight deposition, as the metal is locally
vaporized by an e-beam and when evaporated it moves unhindered straight to the wafer surface
and condense. The metals are placed in so called pockets where they can be locally heated up by
the e-beam which is magnetically guided to the desired metal pocket. A 150 nm layer of Cr was
deposited with a deposition rate of 10 ˚A/s which should function as a hard mask for the etching of
the CMUT cavities.
5.2
Deﬁning the pattern for the cavities
To create the pattern of the hard mask the ﬁrst lithography process was done, which consist of the
three main steps; Spin coating of the resist, UV-exposure and development of the exposed resist.
First of all there are generally two types of resist, positive and negative. The positive resist when
exposed to UV-light undergoes a process of chain-scissoring where the polymers of the resist are
“cut” into smaller pieces which can then be dissolved by the developer. As for the negative resist
a contrary process takes place where the UV-exposed resist cross-links, making the resist insoluble
in the developer. For the ﬁrst lithography 2.2 µm of AZ 5214E positive resist was spin coated onto
the wafers. Followed by an exposure of 10 s to receive a dose of 110 mJ/cm2 given by the equation;
D = I · t, where D is dose, I is light intensity and t is exposure time. This ﬁrst print exposure was
done in hard contact between the mask and the wafer, the mask used was the Cavity mask. All of
the lithography processes throughout this processﬂow are made with hard contact which leaves the

Wafer bonding
The ﬁnalized bottom wafers needed to be cleaned before bonding to minimize the amount of particles
on the bonding surface. A piranha clean of 2 min was utilized as boroﬂoat is not allowed in the RCA
clean as it is considered dirty. Piranha etch consist of sulfuric acid, H2SO4 and hydrogen peroxide,
H2O2 in the ratio 4:1 and removes traces of organic residues. It’s characteristic in the way that
the acid is poured in ﬁrst, contrary to the rule-of-thumb to always add acid and that the piranha
solution heats up to 70-80◦C within a few seconds.
The top wafers used in this project are PSOI wafers made by postdoc Andreas Havreland. PSOI
stands for Poly-Silicon On Insulator and were processed ﬁrst with oxidation and then LPCVD
deposition of the poly-silicon to obtain a 400 nm oxide and a 3 µm poly-Si layer. The PSOIs are
then polished in a CMP machine to lower the surface roughness and increase the strength in the
anodic bond. The PSOI wafers are cleaned in a RCA clean. The clean bottom and top wafers are
then gently put together by hand under a HEPA ﬁlter so that the two ﬂats align, this is done to
avoid particles settling on the bonding surfaces.

5.14
Insulation layer - Nitride testing for RCABCMUT2
The ﬁrst run of Row-Column Addressed CMUTs, RCABCMUT2 in this thesis was designed without
an insulating layer in the cavities. The function of this insulating layer is to protect the CMUTs
when they reach pull-in so that they will not short circuit. Insulating layers allows for what would
be destructive testing on the ﬁrst run of RCABCMUT2 and enables testing within the operation
range of the CMUTs which is 80% of the pull-in voltage, which usually is around 220V.
Two things are to be tested before the implementation of the insulating nitride layer on the PSOI
these are:
• Anodic bonding with nitride as an intermediate layer
• Etching nitride away from top side of the wafer to gain access to the bottom contact.
Two PSOI wafers of 525 µm thickness for controls on the RCABCMUT2 design were polished and
then RCA cleaned before 220 nm of nitride was deposited by LPCVD. Two DSP silicon wafers and
two SSP silicon wafers were also run together with the PSOIs and deposited with 220 nm LPCVD
nitride. See the boat location in the sketch in ﬁgure 5.23 and purpose of the test wafers in the list
below.
• PSOI - for control with RCABCMUT
• Si DSP - bonding test
• Si SSP - ASE test

As a consequence of the powercut the furnace was left open for almost two hours and as the entire

powercut had forsaken in terms of functionality and cleanliness for the open LPCVD furnace. It

ventilation during the powercut was questionable. Therefore before running the intended batch, a
silicon test wafer was run to see how many particles was deposited, see ﬁgure 5.24. The nitride test
wafer after the powercut revealed that almost no particles were to be found on the surface as all of
the optical images was black under dark ﬁeld illumination. In conclusion the intended batch was run
for 1 hour as this is maximum process time for the LPCVD furnaces. It should also be noted that
the day of the powercut, where the nitride test wafer for the particles was processed another user
did a batch the following day thereby encapsulating possible particles further before the intended
batch was run two days after the powercut lowering the chances of particle contamination.

9.1
Wafer probing
Manual wafer probing is a tedious and ineﬀective method for chips with 190 + 190 elements, hence
automatic wafer level probing is a necessity to ensure that only working arrays are used for probe
production. A new way of characterizing Row-Column chips was presented by Prof. Erik V. Thom-
sen in [7] and described in the experimental project by the author for silicon based CMUTs [16].
The idea is that neighbouring elements in Row-Column chips can be electrically characterized by
measuring the capacitance, current etc. between them.
Linear arrays are simple to characterize with a two probe setup, see ﬁgure 9.1 one probe is placed
on the top electrode and the other on the bottom electrode.
The simplicity comes from the shared common bottom electrode of the linear array allowing individ-
ual elements to be characterized. The hassle with the Row-Column design is that if a single element,
say a row is to be characterized all of the columns need to be short circuited simultaneously. This
task is not possible with a two probe setup, however a special design was previously made in the
MEMS-Applied group [17] to short circuit all columns, called Bottom frame and one to short all
rows, called Top frame to allow for individual element characterization. A sketch of the Top frame
design can be seen in ﬁgure 9.2. After characterization the chip can be turned into a regular RC 16
+ 16 chip by sawing of the frame as marked by the dice marks. The downside of the frame design is
that the metal frame takes up device space lowering the possible yield and is limited to characterize
only rows or columns. Another approach to achieving individual element characterization would
be to use a multiplexer, which is a very advanced and expensive tool that can probe all elements
simultaneously.

9.3
Convention for measurements
The Row-Column array is essentially two overlapping linear arrays where one is rotated 90◦, see
ﬁgure 9.6. Where the elements are arranged in an opposite alternating pattern so that the contact
pad of row 1 will be to the left and the contact pad of row 2 to the right.
The convention used to obtain all data in this report is called Rows odd and Rows even, referring to
the ﬁrst number of the electrode pair being measured. Rows odd consists of measurements from row
1 to row 2, row 3 to row 4 etc. As only half of the connections are characterized in this way, Rows
even will commence with row 2 to row 3, row 4 to row 5 and so forth to ensure that all connections
are probed throughout the array. The same notation applies to columns. In this way with a ﬁxed two
probe setup these four consecutive automated runs are made to characterize an entire Row-Column
chip. The data is presented in such a way that odd and even are merged for rows and columns, see
table 9.1 to give a complete overview corresponding to the same order of the elements as on the chip.

9.4
The measurements
Electrical characterization of the fabricated arrays are indispensable to have any form of progress in
optimizing the design of new CMUTs and choosing functional arrays to be implemented in ultrasonic
transducer probes.
The chosen parameters for probing and the purpose of the characterization measurements are pre-
sented in table 9.2 and 9.3. First a frequency sweep, Cf to ﬁnd a frequency where the capacitance
is stable. Then an IV measurement to verify that the two elements aren’t short circuited, hence a
compliance is set at 100 µA in case of any shorts between the elements so they won’t be damaged
by high current. Then the capacitance vs voltage measurement, CV to ensure the correct electro
mechanical behaviour seen as a parabola. An impedance measurement is done to check that the
impedance is indeed a straight line as expected from a capacitor in a double log plot. All electri-
cal characterization measurements are performed on the Cascade Summit 12000 probe station from
Cascade Microtech for further description of the equipment please refer to [16].

Chapter 10
Conclusion & Outlook
The aim of the thesis was to develop a process for fabricating fully functional anodic bonded Row-
Column addressed CMUTs. This goal has been achieved. The fabricated CMUTs utilizes a PSOI,
Poly-Silicon On Insulator wafer as a top plate and an insulating boroﬂoat substrate.
In the thesis two mask designs have been used for processing, one made prior to this thesis and one
created during this thesis. The new mask design enables regular mask sets, maskless aligning and
implements an integrated apodization scheme to improve image quality, which was added to the
190 + 190 Row-Column arrays intended for medical imaging. A new way of creating access to the
bottom contact pad was realized by only opening up above the contact pad and sealing the rest with
nitride. Implementing the insulating nitride layer allows for testing of the CMUTs in the operating
range at 80% of pull-in voltage, Vpi = 220V without being destroyed in collapse mode.
A total of four fabrication runs were performed to optimize the fabrication process.
From the
ﬁrst fabrication run two wafers called RCABCMUT2 was successfully fabricated and characterised
by the author.
They had a high structural yield of 93% as only 7 out of combined 96 arrays
were visibly damaged.
One wafer with an insulating nitride layer on the PSOI top plate called
RCABCMUT nitride was successfully fabricated by Magnus G. Petersen in another project and
characterized during this thesis. An innovative characterization technique for Row-Column addressed
arrays were performed on Row-Column chips of 16 + 16 elements and 190 + 190 elements from both
the RCABCMUT2 run without nitride and the RCABCMUT nitride wafer. The idea was to probe
between two neighbouring elements. This characterization method enables probing directly on a RC
chip without a frame design or a multiplexer and determines working from non-working elements.
Thereby minimising the amount of elements which needs to be inspected i.e.
the non-working
elements e.g. 5 out of 190 elements from the characterized RC 190 + 190 array. Prior to this it
was not possible to characterize an entire Row-Column array with a two probe setup without short
circuiting either all the rows or all the columns, called the frame design, which is limited to only
characterizing either rows or columns. The 190-190 Row-Column CMUT showed the parabolic CV
shape which is the expected electro-mechanical response for a functioning CMUT for voltages up to
80V and a low current in the pA range, indicating that no leakage current is running in the device.
On the contrary it was observed that the connecting wires to the contact pad could only withstand
certain voltages before they disintegrated and the CMUTs could only be operated up to 80V before
they would break down and stop functioning, which is not supposed to happen as the ﬁrst design
was supposed to have a pull-in voltage of Vpi = 408V.
To overcome these problems the new mask set was designed. Fabrication run 2-4 was initiated with
this design, however due to severe particle problems in the cleanroom it was not possible to realize
these wafers and thus no testing of the new mask design was possible in this project. During the
fabrication two critical problems were identiﬁed: the redeposition of the bottom electrode metal and
the voids in the anodic bonding. The redeposition of metal ﬂakes during lift-oﬀused to deﬁne the
bottom electrode will result in non-uniform bottom electrodes and induce local pull-in resulting in

malfunctioning of the CMUTs. Particles on the PSOI top wafers caused voids in the anodic bond
between the PSOI and the glass wafer, evidently allowing the KOH etchant, used to deﬁne the
top plate, to penetrate in between the wafers and dissolve the silicon-based PSOI and exposing the
bottom electrodes and deteriorating them.
The outlook should investigate why the fabricated CMUTs can not endure higher voltages than 80V,
as they are intended for operating voltages above 300V. In future projects it should be examined if
another metal could be implemented as a bottom electrode to avoid the problems seen with the gold
in this thesis, as it might improve the tolerable voltages. For instance aluminium could be examined,
as it is already used for the top electrode and allows for wet etching.
Furthermore the reason for the charging of the CMUTs should be explored before they can be utilized
for medical imaging. Previous studies suggest that the quality of the dielectric have an eﬀect on
the charging, and hence the LPCVD nitride should be evaluated. Finally acoustic measurements
should be carried out before the CMUTs can be declared fully functional and approved for probe
implementation in an ultrasound scanner.
