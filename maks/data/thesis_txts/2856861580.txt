This thesis was conducted within the requirements for the completion of the Master’s
degree titled ”Advanced Materials and Healthcare Engineering” at the Technical Uni-
versity of Denmark.
The study was executed in collaboration with DTU Energy,
Department of Energy Conversion and Storage from August 2024 to January 2025.

application of Direct Air Capture. Assessment of chemical modification, structure

Abstract
In the present thesis, electrospinning (ES) was investigated for the fabrication of
amine-functionalized nanocellulose (NC) based sorbents for Direct Air Capture (DAC)
and compared with sorbent structures prepared by freeze-drying (FD). In ES, aque-
ous suspensions of cellulose nanofibrils (CNF) were blended with carrier polymers,
including polyacrylonitrile (PAN), poly(vinyl alcohol) (PVA), and poly(vinyl pyrroli-
done) (PVP), at a relatively high ratio of 50 wt.% to produce porous nanofiber mats.
The obtained nanocellulose-polymer nanofiber structures were compared with CNF
aerogels, which were synthesized via conventional FD method after modification with
3-aminopropyl-methyldiethoxysilane (APDES). For different concentrations of 2, 4,
6, and 8 wt.% of APDES and varying temperatures and durations, the eﬀicient graft-
ing of APDES onto CNF was challenging. The APDES-modified aerogels exhibited a
steep increase in CO2 uptake at low pressures, with the best-performing sample achiev-
ing a maximum uptake of 1.66 mmol/g at 430 mbar in a dry 100% CO2 atmosphere,
confirming the potential of amine-functionalized CNF aerogels for DAC. However,
Fourier-Transform-Infrared and elemental analyses indicated that the APDES was
primarily physisorbed onto the aerogels rather than covalently bonded and removal
of APDES by washing was observed. Alternatively, CNF nanofiber mats were fabri-
cated by ES, using PAN and PVP, with PVP showing good results due to superior
CNF-PVP dispersion in the precursor solution. While CNF aerogels were mesoporous
with surface areas ranging from 14.47 to 22.94 m²/g, the nanofiber mats exhibited
a lower surface area of approximately 1 m²/g. However, the electrospun nanofiber
mats showed a finer and more homogeneously distributed fibrous network compared
to the FD aerogels with varying diameters and a more inhomogeneous structure. This
first study highlights the advantages of nanofiber structures produced by ES for ap-
plication in DAC. ES can provide nanofiber mats with a very high surface-to-volume
ratio, low pressure drop and precise structural control. This renders these nanofiber
structures promising candidates for direct implementation as sorbent layers into air
filtration systems for DAC or indoor cleaning applications. In future work, the ES
process needs to be further optimized to increase the amine loading in the nanofiber
structures to achieve competitive CO2 uptake, which could be accomplished by re-
ducing the concentration of polymer carrier or using carrier polymers that provide
CO2 uptake, such as polyamines.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In recent years, the world has been grappling with multiple crises, with the environ-
mental crisis standing out as one of the most critical. Accelerated climate change,
driven predominantly by anthropogenic activities, has given rise to increasingly alarm-
ing phenomena. These include extreme weather events, prolonged droughts, devas-
tating wildfires, intensified tropical storms, heatwaves, and water scarcity, all ex-
acerbated by the relentless rise in global temperatures [1]. Such occurrences pose
an existential threat to ecosystems and life itself, largely stemming from heightened
greenhouse gas emissions fueled by industrialization, growing energy demands, and
intensive human activity [14]. Among greenhouse gases, CO2 is the most significant
contributor, with its atmospheric concentration climbing to approximately 420 ppm—
an increase of around 100 ppm compared to pre-industrial levels of 280 ppm [14–16].
If no action is taken, the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) pre-
dicts that global emissions could double by 2100, causing irreversible damage (Fig-
ure 1.1) [14]. The persistent rise in energy demands underscores the urgency for sus-
tainable solutions. Under the Paris Climate Agreement, almost all nations pledged
to limit the global temperature rise to 2oC above pre-industrial levels, with an aspi-
rational target of 1.5oC. By 2020, the global average temperature had already risen
by 1oC [17]. Transitioning to renewable energy sources like solar and wind, alongside
alternative fuels, is vital. However, despite advancements in renewable resources, cur-
rent energy consumption remains heavily reliant on fossil fuels, particularly in hard-
to-decarbonize sectors. This makes carbon abatement technologies indispensable for
mitigating global warming. Post-combustion carbon capture utilizing absorption is
one of the most mature technologies in this regard [18]. This technology involves con-
centrating CO2 from flue gases at emission sources, such as coal-fired power plants
and other industrial facilities, to prevent atmospheric release [14, 19].
While this
technology is critical for reducing emissions, it is still insuﬀicient for sectors such as
aviation and shipping [14,20]. Furthermore, the IPCC warns that even with net-zero
emissions by 2050, global temperatures are likely to exceed pre-industrial levels, high-
lighting the necessity of additional atmospheric CO2 removal. Direct Air Capture
(DAC) offers a promising negative-emission solution by extracting CO2 directly from
the air. Compared to other carbon dioxide removal technologies such as afforestation
and reforestation (AR) or bioenergy with carbon capture and storage (BECCS), DAC
is significantly more resource-eﬀicient. DAC requires only 0.2 km2 per ton of CO2
removed, versus 862 km2 /tCO2 for AR, and consumes far less water—25 m3/tCO2

compared to 600 m3/tCO2 for BECCS. Furthermore, DAC can be deployed without

the potential to remove 8–50 Gt of CO2 annually by 2100, significantly contributing
to the goal of limiting global warming to within 2oC [21].

1.1
Direct Air Capture
Currently, DAC technologies are capable of removing thousands of tonnes of CO2
annually [17]. To meet climate targets, global CO2 removal must reach 10 billion
metric tons per year (GtCO2/yr) by 2050, increasing to 20 GtCO2/yr after 2050 [17].
These reductions refer specifically to CO2 removed directly from the atmosphere and
do not include emissions avoided through decarbonization efforts [17]. Achieving a gi-
gatonne scale by 2050 will necessitate annual growth rates approaching 50% [17]. The
successful scale-up of DAC systems heavily depends on the development of advanced
materials that enable higher eﬀiciency [22]. Thus far, DAC processes primarily utilize
absorption and adsorption mechanisms [22].
Absorption, is a volume-based process where the absorbate permeates and uni-
formly disperses throughout the volume of the absorbent [22]. This can be achieved
by liquid scrubbing using aqueous solutions of amines such as monoethanol amine or
calcium oxides/hydroxides such as potassium and sodium hydroxide (KOH, NaOH).
This process, although having the potential to strongly bind to CO2, leads to the
challenging detaching and isolation of CO2 rendering the technology energy-intensive
==PAGE:21==
1.1 Direct Air Capture
5
and is associated with excessive water use [1,14,23]. Furthermore, during this stage
amine volatilation may cause leaching to the environment and toxic release [24].
Consequently, adsorption is the alternative approach and is a surface separation
process where a gas, liquid, or solid adheres to the surface of a material known as the
adsorbent. Physical adsorption involves weak Van der Waals forces, while chemical
adsorption occurs through the exchange of valence electrons between surface atoms
and CO2 molecules, resulting in strong covalent bonds [5]. Identifying suitable adsor-
bents for DAC requires testing CO2 adsorption under low partial pressures, within
0.02-105 kPa, given the low concentration of CO2 in the atmosphere. Each mate-
rial’s performance is characterized by isotherms that detail the amount of CO2 ad-
sorbed (mmol CO2/g) within a range of increasing pressure under stable temperature.
Post-adsorption, the sorbent is regenerated, typically through high-temperature and
vacuum conditions (Temperature Vacuum Swing Adsorption). While effective DAC
adsorbents must exhibit strong adsorption capacities within the relevant pressure
range, the adsorbent-CO2 interactions should not be excessively strong to avoid high
energy demands during desorption cycles [3]. Liquid amines and hydroxides typically
require high regeneration temperatures ranging from 400 to 900oC. In contrast, sor-
bents that operate through adsorption are significantly less energy-intensive, as they
can be regenerated at lower temperatures of 80 to 100oC, or even below this range
when vacuum conditions are employed [15,21,25].
Typical sorbents utilized for DAC through adsorption involve porous solid sor-
bents such as carbonaceous particles, zeolites, metal-organic frameworks (MOFs),
microporous organic polymers, and mesoporous silicas [3]. However, as these materi-
als rely on physical adsorption they exhibit limited capture capacity [22]. To enhance
their performance for utilization in ambient conditions, sorbents are functionalized
with amines, which can form covalent bonds with CO2, even at low concentrations
and in the presence of moisture [26].
Consequently, in this way chemisorption is
promoted through the following mechanism [3]:

Amine-functionalized solid sorbents can be categorized into three distinct groups.
The first class consists of sorbents where amines are incorporated via physical im-
pregnation [25].
In this approach, the amines are retained within the pore struc-
tures of the sorbents through weak physical molecular interactions [27]. The second
class involves sorbents with amines grafted onto the surface through covalent bond-
ing [27]. This method is generally favored as it mitigates the volatilization and loss
==PAGE:22==
6
1 Introduction
of amines during the regeneration process of the sorbent [3]. Finally, the third group
features polyamines that are covalently attached to a solid support.
In this case,
the polyamines are introduced by polymerizing amine monomers directly onto the
support material [3,25].
The reported fabricated solid sorbents usually exhibit a CO2 uptake capacity
within the range of 0.5-4 mmol CO2 per gram of sorbent [14, 15, 21].
Out of the
aforementioned materials, silicas are the most frequently applied supports. The mi-
croporous materials (e.g., zeolites) are less utilized as supports for amine loading as
the small pores are unfavorable to achieve high loading amount [28]. MOFs exhibit
structure stability, high surface area and porosity but they have a high production
cost [29].
Given the substantial amount of processed air required for CO2 capture from
the atmosphere, the importance of cost-effective, renewable and non-toxic solvents
is highlighted.
Nanocellulose (NC) presents an attractive alternative owing to its
promising properties and potential [30].
1.2
Nanocellulose-Based Materials for Carbon
Capture
NC is an appealing option for structuring amines due to its renewability, biodegrad-
ability, non-toxicity, widespread availability, low cost, and the ease of surface modifica-
tion afforded by its abundant hydroxyl groups [30]. Cellulose is a natural biopolymer
that forms the main structural component of plant cell walls, providing mechanical
strength and chemical stability. The chemical structure of cellulose is composed of
linear chains of glucan units linked by glycosidic bonds (Figure 1.2), which aggregate
into primary nanoscale fibers and then microfibrils through intramolecular hydrogen
bonding and Van der Waals forces (Figure 1.3). NC, a one-dimensional form of cel-
lulose, can form physically cross-linked 3D gel networks due to abundant hydrogen
bonds [5].
When dispersed in water, these aggregates have the potential to form
highly porous materials that facilitate the physical adsorption of CO2 [5].
Figure 1.2. Chemical structure of cellulose [2]
NC is classified into cellulose nanofibrils (CNF), cellulose nanocrystals (CNC), and
bacterial nanocellulose (BNC) based on synthesis methods and structural form. CNF
is derived from natural cellulose fibers through mechanical processes such as high-
==PAGE:23==
1.2 Nanocellulose-Based Materials for Carbon Capture
7
pressure homogenization, ultrasonication, or grinding, resulting in a flexible, tangled
network of nanofibers (2-60 nm in diameter and several micrometers in length) [5].
Uniform nanofibers (3-5 nm in diameter) can be obtained using TEMPO-mediated
oxidation, which converts glucose ring alcohols to carboxylate groups [31].
CNCs are rod-like cellulose crystals (5-70 nm in width, 100 nm to a few microm-
eters in length) produced by acid hydrolysis, which removes amorphous regions from
crystalline cellulose. Compared to CNF and BNC, CNCs have higher crystallinity
and a shorter aspect ratio (<100) [4].
Figure 1.3. Cellulose fiber microstructure [3]
Recent research highlights various applications of NC materials synthesized for
use within the pressure range relevant to DAC. Most studies focus on aerogels or
membranes, which will be reviewed in the following sections.
1.2.1
Synthesis and Structural Formation of Nanocellulosic
Aerogels
In recent years, aerogels have emerged as a promising alternative to conventional ad-
sorbents. These three-dimensional, highly porous materials are made from organic,
inorganic, or hybrid precursors and are characterized by a large surface area, low
density, and robust mechanical properties, making them highly effective for CO2
adsorption and storage. Traditional aerogels, such as silica gels or poly(vinyl alco-
hol) (PVA) polymers, however, often suffer from poor thermal and chemical stability,
insuﬀicient surface area, and high production costs [5].
NC owing to its bioavailabity (100 mtons of cellulose produced annually) and its
exquisite properties, presents an attractive alternative approach to produce biobased

aerogels. The production of nanocellulose aerogels can be roughly divided in three
steps: 1) nanocellulose dispersion 2) gelation and 3) elimination of solvent (Figure 1.4).
Firstly, the dispersion of NC in water leads to the formation of a 3D linked polymer
network, provoked by active hydroxyl groups in the structure. These groups create
intermolecular and intramolecular hydrogen bonding resulting in self-aggregation and
entanglement enhancing the hydrogel’s strength and modulus [32]. The dispersion is
achieved through ultrasonic or mechanical techniques and the enhancement of gelation
and stabilization of the network can be promoted by chemical modification such as
the introduction of negatively charged groups on the NC surface [5].
Figure 1.4. Process steps for aerogel synthesis [4]
In order to create the porous solid material, it is necessary to replace the liquid
located inside the network with gas while maintaining the geometry. This step is
crucial in the aerogel formation as it determines the final porous structure. Thus, it is
important not to deform, collapse or shrink the network. Collapse is caused by forces
applied on the pore walls in the horizontal direction at the liquid-gas interface during
evaporation. Solvents such as water have a large surface tension that deteriorates the
structure and increases capillary forces [33]. The approaches implemented for drying
include freeze-drying (FD), supercritical drying (SD) and ambient drying (AD) [32].
FD is the most common technique applied for the formation of open pore foam
materials, often referred as cryogels, as the porosity is not mainly framed in the
mesoporous range.
During this process, the liquid contained in the gel is frozen
and pressure is decreased leading to sublimation of the solvent (ice), avoiding the
liquid gas interface and reducing capillary pressure. The NC concentration, shape
and size dominate the sublimation rate but the morphology and pore distribution are
influenced on the cooling rate and crystallization temperature. Thus, the preservation
of the original structure necessitates controlled growth and orientation of ice crystals.
==PAGE:25==
1.2 Nanocellulose-Based Materials for Carbon Capture
9
This is achieved through rapid/homogeneous freezing by immersing the gel suspension
in a liquid nitrogen bath. The unidirectional ice shaping results in small pore isotropic
foams with a high surface area [5].
SD yields ultra light aerogels with lower shrinkage and high surface area. The
method consists of solvent exchange with organic media such as ethanol (EtOH) and
placing the gel in autoclave with CO2. The conditions are supercritical so that the
gas and liquid phase of the solvent are intertwined.
When the pores are drained
from the organic solvent, the autoclave is depressurized to receive the aerogel [5]. SD
better preserves fine network structure leading to the formation of mesopores and
small macropores which is reflected by specific surface area of several hundreds of
m2/g [34]. In contrast, FD results in structures with thicker non-porous walls and
larger pores [34].
Lastly, AD comprises the evaporation of the interstitial liquid of wet gels un-
der atmospheric pressure yielding xerogels (Figure 1.5). The implementation of this
method lacks elimination of the liquid-gas interface inducing collapse and shrinkage
of the structure which results in non porous, low density materials with insignificant,
negligible surface area [34]. This effect can be reduced by the use of solvents with
lower surface tension such as EtOH or acetone, or by increasing hydrophobicity of the
NC surface to reduce the contact angle between the liquid and the pore walls. AD is
cost-effective and energy-eﬀicient but the resulting products still lack the properties
produced by the previous methods [5].

Silane-Based Amine Functionalization
NC adsorption of CO2 relies on weak physical adsorption with limited selectivity.
The abundance of hydroxyl groups on the NC surface allows for functionalization
with active groups to enhance adsorption by chemical covalent bonding [30].
The functionalization of NC can take place either during aerogel formation or sub-
sequently. In order to achieve molecular attachment, the most commonly employed
groups are aminosilanes, which feature an organofunctional group, Y, and three sili-
con functional groups, OR, as presented in Figure 1.6.
Figure 1.6. Typical chemical structure of organosilanes [3]
The OR groups can consist of methyl (CH3) or an alkoxy such as methoxy (OCH3),
ethoxy (OC2H5), among others [3, 6, 35, 36]. The Y group in organosilanes can be
either reactive (amino, epoxy, methacrylate, etc.) or unreactive (methyl, butyl, octyl,
phenyl, etc.) [35]. In the case of functionalized NC sorbents where the organic entity
comprises amines, the Y group can consist of one, up to three amine groups such as :
• 3-aminopropyl (H2N(CH2)3),
• N-(2-aminoethyl)-3-aminopropyl (H2N(CH2)2-HN(CH2)3),
• 3-[2-(2-Aminoethylamino)ethylamino]propyl (H2N(CH2)2HN(CH2)2HN(CH2)3)
Typical aminosilane structures employed for NC functionalization for CO2 capture
are presented in Figure 1.7.
Silicon-based agents in this form are extensively utilized in a broad range of pro-
cesses [6]. Their versatility stems from their ability to act as coupling agents, effec-
tively anchoring surfaces rich in hydroxyl groups to desired functional groups, such as
amines [6,35]. This wide applicability is attributed to the unique chemical properties
of the silicon atom.
Similar to carbon, the silicon atom bonds with four molecules in its most stable
form. However, its higher electropositivity compared to carbon makes it more reactive
and capable of donating hydrogen [35].
Silicon-based agents are most commonly
employed to bind to silica surfaces, forming Si-O-Si bonds between the agent and the
surface [6,37]. This process is often referred to as the sol-gel method, as it involves the
formation of a colloidal system through the development of Si-O-Si macromolecules
[6]. These macromolecules form a dispersed phase, ultimately leading to a gel-like
structure [6,37].
In the case of CNF, there is limited reactivity with silanes in this form due to the
weak acidic nature of CNF alcoholic groups, which are less electrophilic compared

to silica [6, 38]. Consequently, the silanes need to first undergo hydrolysis which re-
sults in silanol formation [3,6,38–41]. Subsequently and also simultaneously with the
hydrolysis reaction the silanes begin to self-polymerize, also referred to as self con-
densation [3,6,37–41] . The kinetics and mechanism of this reaction are complex, as
they entail multiple reactions between monomers, dimers, oligomers and even macro-
molecules [36,42]. Concurrently, the silanes are attracted to the cellulosic structure
through hydrogen bonding and eventually condense, releasing water and creating
Si-O-C bonds [6]. The overall process is depicted in Figure 1.8.
In literature, it has been reported that condensation on the cellulose surface can
only take place by thermal treatment, at high temperature, above 80oC, causing
evaporation of moisture [6, 38, 43]. However, the Si-O-C bonds formed under these
conditions are not stable; they are susceptible to hydrolysis, leading to reversal and
conversion back into silanols, unlike the more robust Si-O-Si networks [6].
As a
result, a Soxhlet extraction with EtOH can effectively remove the Si-O-C bonds [6].
Nevertheless, still some studies have reported that a significant portion of Si-O-C
bonds could be retained even after extraction, attributed to the effectiveness of the
implemented thermal treatment [6,38,43].
To enhance the formation of Si-O-C bonds on the CNF surface, it is crucial to
minimize the self-polymerization reaction, limiting it to monomer and dimer silane
bridges [6]. This not only increases the availability of silanols but also ensures that
their molecular size remains small, allowing for more eﬀicient diffusion to the CNF.
Conversely, the formation of a macromolecular Si-O-Si network on the CNF surface is
reported to provide resistance to water leaching and reduce the likelihood of Si-O-C
bond breakage [6]. However, for carbon capture sorbents, larger Si-O-Si networks
can obstruct CNF pores, diminishing CO2 kinetics and limiting capture capacity [3].
As a result, dialkoxysilanes are the preferred choice for such applications as there are
less alkoxy groups present to polymerize [3].
To manage and influence these competing reactions, it is essential to regulate

several factors that significantly affect the kinetics of each reaction. These factors
include the silane structure, concentration, water-to-silane ratio, reaction medium or
solvent, temperature, pH, and reaction time [6, 36, 37, 44]. The primary objective is
to initiate hydrolysis while simultaneously restricting the self-condensation process,
to a controlled rate.
Regarding the nature of the silane, the organofunctional group can highly impact
hydrolysis.
Most organosilanes exhibit very slow or even no hydrolysis rates and
reactivity without the presence of a catalyst [6,36,42,45]. In contrast, in the case of
silanes bearing amine functions, the amine catalyzes hydrolysis due to the nucleophilic
nature of the group which interacts with the silane, rendering the alkoxy groups more
reactive [6,36,37,46]. Additionally, amine groups increase polarity and hydrogen bond
formation through this interaction, increasing solubility in aqueous media [36]. This
electron interaction and reactivity is also influenced by the alkyl spacer between the Si
and the amine [3,6]. The shorter the alkyl spacer, the stronger the electron aﬀinity by
the amine and the reactivity of the alkoxy groups [3,6]. Due to this reason the order of
the hydrolysis rate for amines is primary > secondary > tertiary aminosilanes [3,6].
Secondary and tertiary amines also increase steric hindrance further slowing down
==PAGE:29==

hydrolysis [36]. Furthermore, primary amines also exhibit the advantage of being
more intolerant to oxidative degradation during DAC applications increasing their
suitability in associated sorbents [3]. Oxidative stability constitutes a crucial factor
in defining the lifespan of amine-functionalized adsorbents. The oxidative degradation
of these adsorbents primarily leads to the chemical breakdown of the loaded amines,
resulting in the formation of new characteristic bonds, including carbonyls, aliphatics,
heterocyclics, and aromatics. These changes occur due to the development of acid,
amide, imide, and/or urea linkages [21].
Furthermore, reactivity is also influenced by the alkoxy groups. The hydrolysis
rate increases when the reaction reduces the steric hindrance around the silicon atom.
Larger and more branched alkoxy groups decrease the hydrolysis rate, following the
trend: methoxy > ethoxy > propoxy > butoxy silane [36,45].
The moisture/silane ratio and the reaction solvent are also important aspects
affecting the reaction kinetics. The complete hydrolysis of one mole of a trialkoxysi-
lane, requires as a minimum three moles of water [6]. According to studies reported
in the literature, the hydrolysis rate and reactivity of the silanes increases with water
content, while condensation reactions rate decreases [36, 42]. However, execution in
aqueous media may reduce solubility of the formed siloxanes and diffusion through
the media, limiting interaction with CNF. For this reason organic solvents such as
EtOH are also widely employed [36,37].
Temperature is a highly contributing factor in facilitating both hydrolysis and con-
densation reactions [36, 40]. Hydrolysis, condensation, and cyclization reactions are
generally observed to be endothermic under varying conditions, whereas solvation and
hydrogen bonding exhibit slightly exothermic behavior [36]. The polymerization of
silanes follows the Arrhenius equation, according to which, the reaction rate increases
with temperature. Consequently, higher temperatures are expected to enhance the
polymerization of silanes [36].
Regarding the pH, both alkaline and acidic media promote hydrolysis [36, 45].
However, only acidic conditions slow down polymerization reactions [6, 45].
This
is because this factor influences not only the reaction kinetics but also alters the
reaction mechanism, as demonstrated in Figure 1.9 [36]. In an alkaline medium, nu-
cleophilic hydroxyl or deprotonated silanol groups attack the silicon atoms in alkoxysi-
lane and/or neutral silanol during hydrolysis and condensation reactions, respectively.
Conversely, in an acidic medium, alkoxide and silanol initially undergo rapid proto-
nation. This protonation increases the electrophilicity of the silicon atom, rendering
it more prone to nucleophilic attack by water or neutral silanol in hydrolysis and
condensation reactions, respectively. The reaction rate is influenced by steric and
inductive effects, similar to base-catalyzed reactions. Studies have shown that proto-
nated silanol preferentially reacts with the least acidic silanol end groups, resulting
in less branched clusters under acidic conditions. In contrast, deprotonated silanol
tends to attack more acidic silanol groups, promoting the formation of branched and
condensed clusters in alkaline conditions [36]. It has been widely reported that acidic
conditions increased silanol concentration and decreased macromolecule silaloxane
formation over a wide range of time [36,39,40,42].

1.2.3.1
Aerogels Primarily Composed of Nanocellulose
The synthesis and functionalization of NC aerogels have been extensively explored in
the literature. In these studies, CNF suspensions are either synthesized in the lab-
oratory or acquired commercially. Gebald et al. developed an CNF hydrogel using
high shear pressure homogenization of fibrous beech wood pulp as a precursor for
aerogel synthesis [3]. Liu et al. chemically processed eucalyptus pulp with potassium
hydroxide (KOH), sodium chlorite (NaClO2), and hydrochloric acid (HCl) to elim-
inate lignin and hemicellulose. This was followed by grinding, ultrasonication, and
titration with calcium chloride (CaCl2) to induce nanofiber gelation [47]. Zhang et al.

employed sulfuric acid hydrolysis on microcrystalline cellulose (MCC) and achieved
gelation through CaCl2 titration [48]. Similarly, Xu et al. used hybrid poplar as
a starting material and subjected it to chemical treatments with sodium hydroxide
(NaOH), EtOH, and toluene, followed by grinding, to obtain a CNC colloid [8].
The grafting of aminosilane groups onto NC structures is predominantly carried
out via liquid-phase reactions, under varying conditions of temperature, solvent, and
reaction time. For example, Gebald et al. functionalized a CNF hydrogel by mixing
it with different concentrations of APTES, APDES, and AEAPDMS. The reaction
proceeded at room temperature for 24 hours [3]. In a study by Wu et al., tertiary
butanol (TBA) was used as a solvent instead of water, and the CNF suspension
was reacted with AEAPDMS at 90oC for 3 hours under acidic conditions [39]. Li
et al. followed a protocol similar to Wu et al. but extended the investigation to
include different solvents (EtOH and water), temperatures (60–90oC), and reaction
durations (3–5 hours) to study their effects on amine loading eﬀiciency [40]. The
study concluded that the use of solvents with lower dielectric constant and polarity
increases amine loading. These properties minimize hydrogen bonding between the
silane and the reaction medium, thereby promoting better adsorption onto the CNF
surface. Diffusion and interaction with cellulose which also relies on hydrogen bonding
in a solvent with high polarity may be limited due to stronger interaction with the
media. Moreover, the highest amine loading was observed at elevated temperatures
with a reaction time of 3 hours. It was further deduced that temperature increased
the reaction eﬀiciency of aminosilanes with CNF.
Other studies have investigated the functionalization of NC using various quan-
tities of AEAPMDS in aqueous solutions of CNF and CNC. These reactions were
conducted at elevated temperatures, specifically 90oC and 100oC, for durations of
10 and 12 hours, respectively [47,48]. Upon completion of the reaction, all reported
methodologies utilized the liquid nitrogen freezing technique (-196.25oC). Drying of
the frozen samples was then carried out using a freeze dryer.
Beyond liquid-phase functionalization, recent studies have adopted gas-phase mod-
ification techniques, which are applied after FD of the unmodified suspension. This
method typically involves vacuum sealing the CNC aerogel with AEAPMDS at pres-
sures below 2 kPa, followed by heating within a temperature range of 60–140oC for
extended periods of 8–16 hours [8,49,50]. In some cases, prior activation of the NC
aerogel is performed to enhance reactivity during the gas-phase modification.
As
described by Xu et al. and Jia et al., this activation involves treatment with triethy-
lamine (TEA), which facilitates the adsorption of AEAPMDS onto the aerogel [8,50].
The adsorbed silane then reacts with hydroxyl groups present on the CNC surface.
The reaction mechanism is illustrated in Figure 1.10.
It has been alleged that the gas-phase modification approach offers improved chem-

issues such as self-condensation of silane and high reagent consumption with low modi-
fication yields. Nevertheless, the adsorption performance remains comparable to that
of AEAPMDS-grafted aerogels prepared via aqueous-phase reactions [49].

Other efforts regarding NC aerogels aim for synthesis of hybrid composite materials,
where the base sorbent is not solely composed of NC but also from other widely used
materials. This serves as a compromise to increase incorporation of NC renewable
materials while retaining high performance offered by the more optimized compounds
of the sorbent. Approaches for synthesis of hybrid aerogels include combining CNF
with materials such as gelatin, silicalite-1 zeolite, and MOFs. For instance, hybrid
aerogels incorporating zeolitic imidazolate frameworks achieved CO2 adsorption ca-
pacities of approximately 1.2 mmol/g at 308 K and 101 kPa, and 0.62 mmol/g, re-
spectively [56,57]. Dassanayake et al. synthesized CNC-mesoporous silica composites
functionalized with double-amidoxime groups, yielding a CO2 adsorption capacity of

3.30 mmol/g at ambient temperature [58]. They further integrated inorganic parti-

capacity to 5.54 mmol/g at 120oC.

investigated CNF aerogels impregnated with acetate-functionalized

1.3
Synthesis of Nanofiber Films via Electrospinning
and the Potential of Nanocellulose
Overall, from the conducted literature review, it can be derived that the fabrication
of NC amino-modified aerogels via FD is the predominantly applied method yielding
promising results for DAC. Although FD is an environmentally friendly and economi-
cally viable technique, it has been reported that for large-scale commercial production
it can become economically unfeasible due to its high energy consumption [3]. Fur-
thermore, the final form of the aerogels structure is defined by the freezing method
and the formation of microcrystals, which turn out to be the dominant structural el-
ement. This alters the fine structure of the gel, increasing the pore size and inducing
collapsed non-porous regions. On the other hand, SD produces ultra-light aerogels
but long processing time, large amount of waste solvent, and high-cost production,
constitute a roadblock to the aerogel manufacture at industrial scale [5].
Consequently, exploring alternative methods that can create a highly fibrous net-
work and high-quality porous NC sorbents, while maintaining scalability and cost-
effectiveness, could be advantageous for DAC. Electrospinning (ES) emerges as a
promising alternative, offering a versatile and straightforward approach for the pro-
duction of porous, high surface-to-volume structures with precision [61,62]. Through
this method the eﬀicient fabrication of polymeric fibers with nano-scaled diameters,
good interconnectivity, and structural stability can be achieved [13,62,63]. The ran-
dom arrangement of nanofibers in ES results in numerous isotropic pores, enabling the
potential to form layered structures for air filters with minimal pressure drop, making
them suitable for filtration and adsorption among other applications [13,54,64,65].
The principle of the method lies in the injection of a polymer solution through a
metallic needle with a controlled flow rate using a pump. Electric field is applied at the
tip of the syringe, charging the outer surface of the polymer droplet that is emanated.
The electric charge overcomes surface tension and due to the viscoelastic properties
of the polymer, the droplet elongates forming a Taylor cone. A stable charge jet can
be formed only when the polymer solution has suﬀicient cohesive force. During the
process, the internal and external charge forces cause the whipping of the liquid jet
in the direction of the collector. This whipping motion allows the polymer chains
within the solution to stretch and slide past each other, which results in the creation
of entangled fibers with diameters small enough to be called nanofibers [61,63,66].
The ES process and the resulting fiber morphology are influenced by several key
parameters, including solution properties, processing conditions and environmental
factors. Solution properties such as viscosity, surface tension and conductivity signif-
icantly impact the stability of the ES jet and the ability to form continuous fibers.
Processing parameters, including the applied voltage, flow rate and the distance be-
tween the needle and collector, control the fiber diameter, uniformity, and surface
morphology. Additionally, environmental factors such as temperature, humidity and
air flow can affect solvent evaporation and fiber solidification, leading to variations

2.5.1
Fourier-Transfom-Infrared Spectroscopy
The chemical composition of the samples was analyzed using FTIR spectroscopy
(Spectrum Two, PerkinElmer, USA) over a range of 4000–500 cm−1 with 13 scans
and a resolution of 1 cm−1.
FTIR is an analytical technique that identifies chemical structures by measur-
ing how a sample absorbs infrared radiation. IR radiation, with wavelengths longer
and frequencies lower than visible light, spans the mid-infrared region of the elec-
tromagnetic spectrum (4000–400 cm−1). When exposed to IR radiation, molecules
absorb energy at specific frequencies corresponding to their vibrational bond energies,
producing a spectrum of peaks that serve as a unique molecular fingerprint [11].
Molecular vibrations are classified into stretching and bending modes. Stretching
(ν) involves changes in bond length and can be symmetric or asymmetric, while
bending (δ) involves changes in bond angles and includes motions such as wagging
(ω), twisting (τ), rocking (ρ), and scissoring.
The absorption of light by various
functional groups allows the determination of a molecule’s chemical structure, which
is recorded using a spectrophotometer [84].
FTIR spectra are typically divided into four regions based on bond types. Single
bonds, such as O-H, C-H, and N-H, are detected between 2500 and 4000 cm−1. Triple
bonds appear between 2000 and 2500 cm−1, while double bonds are found in the
1500 to 2000 cm−1 range. The region between 650 and 1500 cm−1 is known as the
fingerprint region, containing unique patterns that characterize individual molecules
[85].
An FTIR spectrometer, illustrated in Figure 2.1, consists of three main compo-
nents: a radiation source, an interferometer, and a detector. In the interferometer,
light is split into two beams that travel different paths—one reflects off a stationary
mirror, while the other reflects off a moving mirror. The beams are then recombined
==PAGE:47==
2.5 Characterization Analysis
31
to produce an interference pattern, which is directed to the detector. A Fourier trans-
form is applied to the detected signal, converting spatial data (mirror displacement)
into frequency data (wave number). The resulting infrared spectrum plots transmit-
tance or absorbance of radiation against its wave number [11,84,85].

Scanning Electron Microscopy and Elemental Analysis
Sample morphology was examined by SEM (Ultra 55, Carl Zeiss, Germany) at 15 kV
under vacuum conditions. Prior to SEM imaging, the samples were coated with a
thin gold/platinum layer using an ion sputter coater (SC7650, Polaron, UK). Amine
loading was quantified with elemental analysis using SEM microscopy coupled with
energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) (TM3000, Hitachi, Japan). A scanning
electron microscope (SEM) is a powerful imaging tool that uses an accelerated elec-
tron beam (1–40 kV) to scan a sample’s surface, producing high-resolution images
with a three-dimensional appearance. This beam is condensed and directed through
electromagnetic lenses and scanning coils, which raster the beam across the sample
surface in the x and y directions.
The interaction between the primary electron
beam and the sample generates various signals, including secondary electrons (SE),
back-scattered electrons (BSE), Auger electrons, and characteristic X-rays [12,84].

Secondary electrons, emitted from conduction or valence bands due to inelastic
scattering, originate near the sample surface and are detected to provide topographi-

utilizes characteristic X-rays produced when an electron beam ejects an inner-shell
electron from an atom, causing an outer-shell electron to fill the vacancy. The re-

two shells, creating distinct peaks in the EDS spectrum for each element. Addition-

Thermogravimetry
Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was conducted to assess aminosilane modification
impact on thermal stability at a heating rate of 10oC/min from 30 to 800oC under a
nitrogen flow of 50 mL/min (STA 409 PG Luxx, NETZSCH, Germany).
TGA is a technique used to evaluate the thermal stability of materials by monitor-
ing their weight loss as a function of temperature and time. Samples are heated at a
controlled rate, typically in an inert atmosphere to prevent oxidation, and the weight
changes are continuously recorded. The resulting thermogram reveals weight losses
at specific temperature ranges, corresponding to the sample’s molecular composition
and providing insights into the degree of modification or degradation. TGA can also
be used to measure adsorption by detecting changes in weight due to the absorption
of gases or liquids. A TGA system comprises four key components to ensure accurate
thermal stability measurements: an analytical balance for precise mass monitoring, a
furnace for controlled heating, a temperature control program to regulate and elevate
temperatures systematically, and a data recorder to log changes in weight and tem-
perature continuously. This setup enables the detailed characterization of material
decomposition, thermal behavior, and adsorption properties [86].

and equipment availability. CO2 adsorption measurements were also executed using
this equipment for 2 of the modified CNF aerogels to assess surface area and amine

The measurement of BET surface area involves exposing the sample to N2 gas
at liquid N2 temperature (−196oC) and measuring the amount of adsorbed gas at
different relative pressures. As N2 adsorbs on the surface, the volume of adsorbed
gas is measured and used to construct an adsorption isotherm. The BET method
utilizes this isotherm to calculate the specific surface area of the sample, based on
the assumption that adsorption occurs in layers and follows a linear relationship at
certain pressure ranges. The BET surface area is calculated from the linear part of
the isotherm, typically in the relative pressure range of 0.05 to 0.3 [87].
The N2 adsorption-desorption isotherms obtained are analyzed in accordance with
the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry organization (IUPAC) classifi-
cation, which categorizes isotherms into six types depending the relationship between
porosity adsorption. The IUPAC classification of adsorption isotherms is demontrated
in Figure 2.3(a). The six types of isotherms (IUPAC classification) are characteristic
of adsorbents that are microporous (pore size <2 nm, type I), nonporous or macrop-
orous (pore size>50 nm, types II, III, and VI), or mesoporous (pore size within 2-50
nm, types IV and V) . Hysteresis, a difference between the adsorption and desorption
branches of the isotherm indicates the presence of mesoporous structures due to re-
versible capillary condensation. Hysteresis is also classified (Figure 2.3(b)) and it is
widely accepted that there is a correlation between the shape of the hysteresis loop
and the texture (e.g., pore size distribution, pore geometry, and connectivity) of a
mesoporous material [88].

interaction of primary amines with water or silanol groups, respectively [3, 39, 40].
The presented bands account for the APDES modified samples under reaction condi-
tions of 50 oC for 5 hours with 2 washing cycles employed after reaction completion
(APDES-4-50,5, APDES-6-50,5, APDES-8,50,5, APDES-2-50,5-rep and APDES-4-
50,5-rep) as shown in Figure 3.1(b). In addition, the peaks at 1261 and from 821 to 723
cm−1 attributed to Si-C stretching, Si-O-Si stretching and Si-C asymmetrical stretch-
ing, respectively display the existence of Silicon and self-polymerization between the
aminosilane groups [3]. Furthermore, there is band overlap between cellulose C-O-C
skeletal vibrations and signals associated with silicon-based linkages (Si-OH, Si-O-C,
Si-O-Si asymmetrical stretching). In addition, the band at 1446 cm−1 which is more
prominent in CNF-APDES-2-50,5-rep and CNF-APDES-4-50,5-rep was assigned to
CH2 bending of the silane propyl chain. Finally, the bands observed around 2500 to
2000 cm−1 are not assigned to existing bonds in the structure of the materials, but
rather to the presence of air affecting the measurements [89].
In Figure 3.1(a), the spectra of the FD aerogels, which were modified in different
conditions and with increased extensive washing cycles after reaction completion, are
shown. In these spectra there are no peaks present correlating to APDES absorbance
bands. The spectra of the samples CNF-APDES-6-50,5-W, CNF-APDES-6-25, CNF-
APDES-6-100,3, CNF-APDES-6-140,1,11 and CNF-APDES-6-140,1,4 are identical to
the one of the unmodified CNF. This result indicates that when thorough washing
was employed, regardless of the different reaction conditions, there were no amine
peaks detectable in the FTIR spectrum. In some samples where the temperature was
insuﬀicient, the formation of Si-O-C bonds between the silanes and CNF may not
have occurred (e.g., CNF-APDES-6-50,5-W, CNF-APDES-6-25). Even when thermal
treatment was applied, the presence of moisture and inadequate duration might have
reduced the number of Si-O-C bonds, which could have been subsequently hydrolyzed
back to silanols, Si-OH during washing. For CNF-APDES-2-50,5, washing cycles even
though limited, seem to have removed the low concentration of APDES. These results
imply that in CNF aerogels showing amine content, the aminosilanes are likely to be
physisorbed onto the pore walls of CNF through hydrogen bonding rather than being
covalently bonded.

Table 3.1. It can be observed that there is no trend that correlates the amine content
with the concentration of aminosilane that was added in the reaction media. This can

amine content likely because although a higher quantity of APDES was added in the
latter, the washing process was more thorough. Additionally, the higher concentration

modification was shown to impact the thermal degradation process of the CNF aerogel
and significantly enhance the thermal stability of the material. However, there was
no trend observed in agreement with EDS analysis results.

Summary and
Conclusion
The shaping and amine modification of nanocellulose (NC) as sorbents for Direct Air
Capture (DAC) was investigated, comparing freeze-drying (FD) and electrospinning
(ES) as a new technique to produce nanofiber structures.
For the preparation of
the nanofibrous sorbents by the FD method amine grafting of cellulose nanofibers
(CNF) was conducted under various conditions, including temperatures from 25 to
100oC, acidic and alkaline media, different aminosilane concentrations, and variations
in washing protocols. Limited washing combined with aminosilane treatment at 50oC
for 5 hours produced amine-modified NC aerogels. These aerogels exhibited silicon
contents of 6–8 wt.%, with nitrogen content ranging from 0.48 mmol N/g for CNF-
APDES-4-50,5 to 0.88 mmol N/g for CNF-APDES-2-50,5-rep, the latter attributed
to reduced washing.
Nevertheless, Fourier-Transform-Infrared (FTIR) analysis indicated that extensive
washing in water completely removed aminosilanes from the NC surface, regardless
of the reaction conditions employed for the previous amine grafting. Increasing the
media temperature from 50 to 100oC was ineffective in promoting significant Si–NC
bond formation, resulting either in limited bonding which was reversible by hydrolysis
during washing or complete lack of covalent attachment. There could be chemical
or physical bonding when the amines are incorporated to NC. However, removal of
amines due to washing indicates that strong chemical bonds are not achieved with
methods applied in this work. From literature, in similar studies, it is not clearly
described which type of amine attachement was achieved and this seemed diﬀicult
to be fully validated. This was a challenge encountered in this work.
For future
work, it is suggested that extended reaction times (10 hours) at temperatures above
85 oC as well as the use of different solvents such as tertiary butanol (TBA) be
investigated [40, 47, 48]. Regarding the microstructure development of the aerogels
displayed nanoporous structures with mesoporous characteristics and surface areas
between 14.47 and 22.94 m2/g.
As an alternative route, the shaping of CNF into nanofiber films via ES was inves-
tigated. Direct ES of CNF in DMF solvents did not yield fibers due to poor solubility,
inadequate elasticity, and the absence of jet elongation needed for fiber formation. To
overcome this challenge, the CNF were mixed with ”carriers polymers” such as PAN,

PVA, and PVP in DMF, water, and water/ethanol mixtures to enhance the forma-
tion of CNF-polymer composite nanofibers. PAN-CNF mixtures formed nanofibers
but exhibited agglomeration even at low CNF concentrations.
PVA-CNF showed
good dispersion but faced challenges with fiber formation due to slow water evap-
oration and agglomeration when combined with more volatile solvents. PVP-CNF
demonstrated the most promise, as it dispersed well in water and ethanol, forming
nanofiber structures. Further optimization may enable higher CNF incorporation and
the development of amine-functionalized fiber mats.
To address the challenges in ES and achieve suﬀicient amine addition, PAN electro-
spun fibers were impregnated with APDES. FTIR and EDS analyses confirmed amine
modification, but N2 adsorption measurements revealed very low BET surface areas
compared to freeze-dried aerogels. As a result, freeze-dried aminated aerogels were
prioritized for CO2 adsorption testing. Among these, CNF-APDES-4-50,5 showed a
steep CO2 uptake at 0.4 mbar, reaching 0.7 mmol/g, and a maximum uptake of 1.66
mmol/g at 430 mbar. CNF-APDES-4-50,5-rep also exhibited significant uptake at low
pressures relevant for DAC but underperformed compared to the other CNF-APDES
aerogel.
This study highlights the challenges of ES of CNFs due to the limited solubility
and compatibility of the CNF with conventional solvents. ES of CNF together with
polymers results in significantly low CNF loading which would allow very limited
amount of amines immobilized within the structure. Polymer structures modified
with aminosilanes displayed limited potential for DAC due to reduced surface areas
and amine loading. In contrast, CNF aerogels were superior supports in this study,
owing to their higher CO2 uptake capacity. Further optimization of their structure
and amine content through systematic investigation could substantially improve CO2
capture performance.
Nevertheless, comparison of micrustructure development between FD and ES out-
lined the potential of ES produced nanofiber mats over FD. The resulted fiber struc-
tures of PAN and PVP displayed finer fiber diameters and a more homogeneous
network presenting potential for air filter development with minimal pressure drop.
The direct synthesis of a large membrane is also a significant advantage to this end.
For future work, the direct ES of polymers containing high amount of amine groups
could prove significantly beneficial for DAC applications.
