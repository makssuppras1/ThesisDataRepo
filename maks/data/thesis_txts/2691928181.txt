Abstract
An epidemic can bring unprecedented challenges for public health, societies, economies, and edu-
cation. One of the most eﬀicient responses in mitigating disease spread is deciding which parts of
a population to immunize, particularly when resources or vaccinations are limited. Hence, this
study introduces a methodology where an immunization model is built upon a SEIR (Susceptible-
Exposed-Infected-Recovered) simulation model that evaluates various immunization strategies
employed on networks constructed from university data. The assessed strategies include known
immunization methods based on traditionally studied topological properties while also present-
ing novel network-specific heuristics and optimization methods.
The proposed strategies are considered for two types of networks, based on real-life data of course
attendance in a large university. Firstly, a student network where immunization amounts to
vaccinating said students or forcing them off school grounds by following every class online.
Secondly, a course network where immunization is possible due to the recent display of remote
teaching.
The network assessment in this study demonstrates how choosing the correct approach to im-
munization is essential. The results reveal that targeting a group of individuals significantly
reduces infections and the number of individuals in quarantine compared to vaccinating individ-
uals at random, regardless of the network and immunization coverage. Random immunization
requires substantially greater immunization coverage for achieving comparable results, i.e., far
more nodes need to be randomly vaccinated to attain the same performance as the proposed
methodology. Furthermore, it demonstrates that targeting university courses is a more robust
approach to reducing disease spread than immunizing individual students.
However, for each of these strategies, target nodes are determined based on only one centrality
measure at a time, even though the topology of the network often suggests that a single metric is
insuﬀicient to fully capture a node’s significance. Therefore, for further improvement, the study
introduces two genetic algorithms. The algorithms combine immunization targets considered
influential by other strategies, effectively trying various combinations of the other proposed
methods. As a result, the epidemic spreading becomes more restricted because the node se-
lection is diverse, detecting and immunizing a mixture of highly connected nodes, community
bridge nodes, nodes on shortest paths, and other nodes deemed promising by other measures.
The findings reveal that genetic algorithms manage to target nodes that remarkably decrease
the spread of an epidemic compared to the other immunization methods. This suggests eﬀicacy
in different real-life contexts, where resources or vaccinations are limited.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Epidemic diseases have posed a continual threat to humans throughout history. As early as 430
BC, the plague of Athens killed 25–35 percent of the city’s population. In the 1300s, the Black
Death ravaged medieval Europe and Asia, claiming more lives than any other known epidemic
up to that period [41]. During that time, the first recorded quarantine cases were established
where the Republic of Venice required ships to wait at anchor for 40 days before being allowed
to shore. Subsequently, the word quarantine comes from the Latin word for forty [102]. After
that, quarantine has been a widely used restriction intended to prevent the spread of infectious
diseases, even in modern times during the ongoing COVID-19 pandemic.
COVID-19 is wreaking havoc on societies around the world. Governments have made preventa-
tive measures to control the disease diffusion, notably nationwide lockdowns, extensive testing,
quarantine, and closure of social gatherings that bring forth opportunities for further spreading,
such as workplaces, schools, fitness centers, and other events. As an example, students around
the globe have faced weeks of school closures in the past 1,5 years, where teaching has stopped
or been moved online for a specific period as presented in Figure 1.1 [104]. Evidently, the pan-
demic has a significant impact on people’s lives as they adjust and adapt their social norms to
the current regulations at each time. The urge for life to return to ’normal’ is high. Thus, a
heightened understanding of the dynamics of infectious diseases is becoming crucial to minimize
the effect and lifetime of COVID-19 and any future pandemic.
Figure 1.1: Total duration of full or partial school closures worldwide during the ongoing
COVID-19 pandemic. Source: UNESCO global monitoring map of school closures
[104].
According to Newman, Barabási, and Watts, networks are widespread in modern civilization. In
their book ”The Structure and Dynamics of Networks,” they combine various research papers
from the fast-growing field of network theory, including novel insights on epidemic spreading in

social networks and methods for controlling them [69].
“Networks are everywhere. From the Internet to networks of friendship, disease transmission,
and even terrorism, the concept – and the reality – of networks has come to pervade modern
society.”
— Mark Newman, Albert-László Barabási, Duncan Watts [69]
The introduction of complex networks in modern society has opened new doors to elevate the
understanding of epidemics and assist intervention measures by analyzing the relations between
individuals in a social network, using information on their structure, and creating epidemiologi-
cal models to study disease behaviors [46].
The increased awareness of epidemics and their potential consequences due to the ongoing global
COVID-19 battle has triggered further interest in identifying key defense measures against
spreading. Simulation models have been designed that mimic the disease’s behavior to propose
effective preventative strategies. Nowadays, research on variations of the SEIR model is very
common [35, 68, 113], as the compartmental model captures the characteristics of COVID-19,
taking into account exposed individuals before becoming infectious, probability of infection, and
immunity of an individual. These recent works incorporate quarantine into the model as it has
been used as a primary resource to limit further transmission.
The most effective measure in preventing infectious diseases is vaccination. However, developing
a vaccine for a new disease can be a long process. Once ready, financing and production capacity
can limit a vaccine’s availability. The availability gives the need for a strategic approach when
distributing vaccine doses. By incorporating social network and epidemiological simulations,
various features can be utilized in an attempt to find the optimal vaccine strategy.
The pressure on governments and other decision-makers to swiftly respond to these unprece-
dented challenges brought by the COVID-19 crisis requires a deep understanding of the inter-
action between individuals in a specific environment and identifying the red flags that facilitate
disease transmission. In addition, strategies must be put into place that benefits the greater
good, making the epidemic spread less while trying to keep imposed restrictions to a minimum
to meet the demand of ’normal’ life. These elements make the design of targeted immunization
strategies exceedingly diﬀicult but a crucial preparation when faced with current and future
epidemics.
1.1
Problem introduction
Vaccination allows individuals to be protected while also preventing contamination from spread-
ing to their contacts. Because mass vaccination is impossible when vaccine doses are limited,
developing an effective immunization strategy is critical. Immunization procedures are tools
used for identifying influential spreaders in a network. Their primary purpose is to isolate and
immunize a small number of people from the entire network to prevent epidemics from spread-
ing. They accomplish it by relying on various network topology features, e.g., by focusing on
densely connected nodes or vertices that often appear on the shortest paths between a pair of
nodes. Furthermore, a well-known property of social networks is community structure. Recent
research has demonstrated that it has an impact on epidemic dynamics and that it should be
taken into account when developing tailored epidemic control methods [36, 38, 89].
2
==PAGE:23==
1.1 Problem introduction
In this paper, the goal is to design an immunization model utilizing these features when identify-
ing the most influential nodes in disease propagation and evaluate which of these measures have
the most significant impact on the spread of COVID-19. An immunization strategy does not
necessarily require vaccine doses. Immunizing a node essentially means removing it from the net-
work so that it cannot be infected. However, there is also the possibility of immunizing an edge.
Removing an edge from a network results in one less pathway for a disease to propagate through.
Schools are especially vulnerable to infectious disease transmission due to the high frequency of
close proximity interactions. However, during an epidemic, it is still vital to provide teaching
and operate despite diﬀicult times. This study, therefore, focuses on a school setting where it
encodes a university environment in two different networks. One network is the student network,
where nodes represent students and edges exist where two students attend a course together.
The other network is the course network, where nodes represent courses and edges are added
between the courses if at least one student attends both courses. Applying an immunization
strategy to these networks has different consequences. Immunizing a node in the student net-
work means either a student is given a vaccine or forced to isolate from the environment. On
the other hand, immunizing a node in the course network results in the whole course becoming
inactive in the university environment. Moving courses entirely online is a possibility, as the
COVID-19 pandemic has shown. If a course is immunized, it is equivalent to removing edges
in the student network. The methodology can be applied to a wide variety of activities with
similar characteristics, including, but not limited to, crew scheduling, shift planning in a factory,
and class scheduling in a fitness center.
After applying an immunization strategy, an epidemic outbreak is simulated on the univer-
sity networks.
The simulation follows the SEIR model, a compartmental model commonly
used for mimicking respiratory infections like COVID-19 [80].
The simulation, provided by
Niels-Christian Fink Bagger from his paper How to re-open during a pandemic: the case for
minimizing distinct contacts, additionally simulates contact-tracing and quarantine. In quaran-
tine, individuals have a probability of isolating if they have been infected or in contact with
an infected individual. Contact tracing entails the process of notifying recent close contacts of
an infected individual of their possible exposure, effectively sending them to quarantine as a
preventive measure.
The study addresses how it is possible to keep activities open during an epidemic by studying
the disease behaviors when contact patterns are changed according to different network features.
The hypothesis is that schemes that utilize information on the network can considerably lower
the vulnerability of a graph to infectious diseases such as COVID-19 over random immunization
schemes, i.e., when a random set of nodes is chosen for vaccination. In order to give the same
level of protection to the entire network as random immunization, these solutions require sig-
nificantly fewer nodes to be immunized. Similarly, it is believed that an approach that exploits
various network properties can outperform traditional strategies that solely focus on one fea-
ture, such as greedy methods that target nodes based on one network centrality measure. The
approach would not be bound to one measurement but utilize a combination of high-scoring
nodes according to various methods and thereby manage to change the graph structure to make
it more favorable to limit the spread of disease. Moreover, the study investigates the hypothesis
that immunizing activities instead of individuals is a more effective approach in reducing disease
spread. It includes allowing individuals at some point in time to physically partake in an activity
instead of being forced to participate in all activities remotely during an epidemic.
This promotes the idea that tailor-made solutions for the university based on an analysis of
its social networks give the best results. An immunization model is created to apply known
and customized immunization solutions to the networks, where, e.g., the fairness of students is

An immunization strategy attempts to find the optimal combination of nodes to minimize the
spread of an epidemic. It can be interpreted as the optimal combination that maximizes influ-
ence in a social network. However, finding the best group of nodes for vaccination that minimizes
epidemic spread is by no means an easy task. It would be necessary to test all possible com-
binations of individuals for finding the globally optimal solution, characterizing the problem as
NP-hard [47]. However, trying every possible combination of nodes is not considered viable.
Therefore, the opportunity arises for developing an optimization heuristic to find a suﬀiciently
good solution. This study introduces genetic algorithms for both the student network and the
course network in an attempt to specify the most influential candidates, which is vital to reduce
the risk of an outbreak when re-opening the school facilities in an epidemic.

The study presents an approach for solving the challenge of selecting a group of individuals to

suited for vaccination in a resource-constrained context to restrain the epidemic spread. It is
important to note that the proposed methodology can extend to various other applications. For

CHAPTER 2
Literature Review
Studies of epidemic processes in networks have become more and more prevalent in recent years.
Complex networks are common for representing real systems. How their features influence epi-
demic and disease spreading are of great interest [80].
Targeted immunization is a commonly used strategy for limiting disease spreading. Targeted
immunization or social distancing interventions aim to modify the network of susceptible indi-
viduals to limit the transmission of an outbreak [66]. The strategies rank nodes according to a
specific measure, indicating their importance, and immunize p nodes with the highest priority,
where p denotes the number of vaccination resources available [42, 63, 91, 92]. However, as
Borgatti pointed out, these strategies produce solutions far from optimal as nodes are greedily
chosen for immunization [13]. To address this drawback, researchers have proposed heuristics
and optimization approaches that utilize relations between disease outbreak and network topo-
logical properties [19, 91, 92, 106].
For evaluating the effectiveness of a proposed targeted immunization method, epidemiological
models are applied to predict the macroscopic behavior of the disease propagation by modeling
the disease spread through a population [24]. This chapter introduces exact methods and heuris-
tics frequently used for identifying critical nodes in epidemic spreading and the epidemiological
models used in experiments to assess their quality.
2.1
Exact methods
Several centrality measures have been proposed in various works to examine the influence of
nodes on a network [12, 32, 70]. Although centrality measurements were first used by Bavelas
in human communication [7, 8], they were quickly adopted in other fields, such as epidemiology
[12, 42, 53]. These measures have attracted much attention from researchers in that field when
designing targeted immunization strategies. Chapter 5 includes a detailed description of well-
known centrality measures commonly used for prioritizing nodes in targeted immunization.
Established methods choose specific nodes for immunization based on network structures [107].
Identifying the nodes that play a key role in maintaining connectivity and robustness of a net-
work is crucial to prevent or limit undesirable events, such as disease propagation, where the
critical nodes are either removed or protected. Many researchers have focused on identifying
so-called ’superspreaders’ with extensive experiments to capture critical nodes proactively. Su-
perspreader is an individual who produces large numbers of secondary cases, i.e., singly infects
a large number of people [60]. Even though Lipsitch et al. found the existence of superspreaders
to be rare and based on exception [57], Masuda et al. considered their inclusion in a network
model to be a key when proposing restrictions to prevent mass spreading [64].
Early researches of immunization strategies frequently classify superspreaders as the nodes with
the highest degree, e.g., according to Pastor-Satorras et al., the most successful immunization
strategies can mainly be based on the nodes’ connectivity hierarchy [81]. However, more recent
studies have challenged this statement by introducing the effectiveness of using various other
6
==PAGE:27==
2.2 Heuristics and optimization methods
metrics for predicting the most influential nodes in an epidemic outbreak. One such metric is
betweeness centrality, which can identify community bridges when a network has a community
structure. Salathé et al. pointed out that targeting highly connected individuals for immuniza-
tion might not be the most effective strategy if the network has a strong community structure.
In such cases, identifying nodes that act as community bridges for immunization results in fewer
infected cases than when nodes are selected based on degree centrality [89]. Further, Liu et al.
analyzed the community feature within networks and concluded that as the number of commu-
nities in a network increases, the infection rate decreases [59]. Chapter 6 provides more insights
into the community-based immunization strategies explored in this study.
The majority of previously mentioned studies disregard the fact that results might depend on an
epidemic’s phase. Therefore, some researchers have recently proposed new centrality measures
tailored to minimize epidemic growth by incorporating the disease dynamics. Piraveenan et al.
introduced percolation centrality that quantifies the importance of nodes based on both their
topological connectivity and their current percolation state. The percolation state represents
the node’s health condition [83]. Moreover, Shams and Khansari proposed average reachability
where the infection probability is incorporated to predict epidemic growth according to shortest
paths among nodes [93]. Further, Kamal et al. introduced spreader centrality that aims to cap-
ture superspreaders within a network by ranking nodes according to their out-links, recursively
prioritizing nodes that infect other nodes that turn out to infect many of their neighbors [45].
In conclusion, given the wide variability in performance over different studies, the centrality
measure that performs best in identifying superspreaders for different epidemic models and
networks has yet to be defined [82].
2.2
Heuristics and optimization methods
Taking advantage of the topological properties of a network combined with the epidemics has
induced the creation of immunization algorithms and heuristics based on optimization methods.
Some researchers have focused on disrupting graph connectivity, given that transmission can
only occur through connections. This gives rise to minimizing the giant connected component
of a network. Deng et al. introduced tabu search into the network disintegration problem. Their
proposed strategy, named Optimal Attack Strategy, uses tabu search to minimize the size of the
largest connected component after node removal. Their experiments show that the method out-
performs strategies based on the removal of nodes scoring high on either degree or betweenness
centrality [23]. Schneider et al. proposed an immunization strategy based on optimizing the
susceptible size where the objective is to schedule an immunization sequence. They define the
susceptible size as the sum of the sizes of the largest connected components in the network after
immunization. Their strategy sequentially tries random swaps in the immunization sequence
when minimizing the susceptible size, where the initial sequence is based on high-betweenness
immunization. Their strategy outperformed high-betweenness immunization and proved to be
highly eﬀicient for the SIR epidemiological model [92].
Further, Chen et al. introduced an equal graph partitioning strategy to transform networks into
many clusters of approximately equal size. Subsequently, since immunizing small clusters is not
necessary, the technique saves a significant quantity of vaccine doses [19]. Similarly, Ventresca
et al. describe how the design of immunization strategies that maximize the fragment of the
population into similarly sized components yields successful results as they may be more likely
to mitigate a pandemic [106]. Shams and Kanshari proposed a stochastic hill-climbing immu-
nization to optimize criteria that they consider as essential factors in network epidemic growth,

such as network density, the sum of square partition, and their previously mentioned centrality
measure average reachability [93].
Moreover, Saha et al. developed a strategy that aims to reduce the spectral radius of a network,
i.e., the largest eigenvalue of the adjacency matrix [87]. It aligns with the proposed approximated
epidemic threshold where the threshold is considered to be the inverse of the largest eigenvalue
of the network [17, 108], i.e., maximizing the threshold makes disease propagation more diﬀicult.
2.3
Epidemiological models
Epidemiological models are used as a tool to investigate the mechanisms by which diseases
spread, predict the development of an outbreak, and evaluate control strategies [22].
Disease models range from simple deterministic mathematical models to more complex stochas-
tic simulations. The approach depends on how well the epidemiology of a disease is understood,
the quality of data, and the study’s objectives. As stated by Garner and Hamilton, epidemio-
logical models are characterized based on how they handle variability, chance, and uncertainty
(deterministic or stochastic), time (continuous or discrete intervals), space (non-spatial or spa-
tial), and population structure (homogenous or heterogeneous mixing) [34].
Papers by Kermack and McKendrick [49, 50, 51] have had a significant impact on the forma-
tion of mathematical models for disease transmission and are still applicable in many epidemic
situations. The first of these papers introduced the susceptible-infected-recovered (SIR) model.
It laid out a foundation for modeling infections that result in complete immunity or death after
recovery. The population in these models is constant. That is, no births or deaths occur other
than from the disease.
The model is formulated using differential equations since the transition rates from one compart-
ment to another are stated mathematically as derivatives. In such models, the epidemic process
is assumed to be deterministic. That is, the behavior of a population is determined entirely by
its history and the rules describing the model [15].
Recently, agent-based models (ABMs) have been used in exchange for simpler compartmental
models [27]. For example, epidemiological ABMs have been used to inform public health inter-
ventions against the spread of SARS-CoV-2, the virus that causes COVID-19 [1]. Despite their
complexity and high processing requirements, epidemiological ABMs have been criticized for
simplifying and unrealistic assumptions [98]. However, when configuring ABMs correctly, they
can be valuable in guiding decisions concerning mitigation and suppression actions [65].

3.1
Introduction
This study emphasizes recognizing which immunization strategies limit the risk of disease spread-
ing within a university. However, the immunization model can also be applied to various other
activities in a society or even society itself, e.g., considering which stores, gym classes, train
stations, or workplaces need to close during an epidemic or which individuals are not allowed
to partake in these activities. It applies to environments that are particularly vulnerable to
infectious disease spread due to the high frequency of close interactions that most infectious dis-
ease diffusion depends on [40, 110]. The experiment is twofold, focusing on either immunizing
activities or individuals. The applicable social networks possess certain properties, which are
the following:
• The network includes a certain amount of activities where the participants arrive on the
scene according to a schedule.
• The activity occurs in an environment where individuals can come into close contact with
each other. This creates an opportunity for a disease to spread between the persons taking
part in the current activity or enabling further diffusion to other activities, given that some
of these individuals are associated with more than one activity.
• The activity types have in common that they can occur at different times over a time
period, resulting in the interaction between members on multiple occasions over time.
• Individuals are assigned to an activity according to their preference. However, an activity
can be closed down if it imposes a high risk of disease transmission, or specific individuals
can be banned from partaking in any activities if their presence is considered unsafe. This
means removing either activities or individuals from the network for the greater good.

scribes a graph based on DTU’s student relations. Section 3.2.2 provides an overview of a graph
with interconnections between courses at DTU.

attending both courses. The network is a weighted, undirected graph where the weights denote
how many students are shared between a pair of courses. Table 3.2 presents the graph structure

3.3
Adapting immunized networks to hypergraphs
After the identification and removal of certain nodes within the graphs, introduced in Sec-
tion 3.2.1 and Section 3.2.2, the graphs are transformed to hypergraphs. Berge initially intro-
duced hypergraphs in 1973 to enable more than just pairwise relationships between vertices
within a graph. Hypergraphs consist of hyperedges that connect one or more nodes [9]. Hy-
peredges allow this study to account for group interactions of any size, instead of, as in, for
example, [33, 105, 108], considering them as if they were a series of essentially unrelated pair-
wise interactions. Figure 3.3 shows the difference between a simple graph and a hypergraph.

The experiment takes inspiration from Bagger et al. [5] where a hypergraph is found to be a
better representation of reality when simulating the virus spread. First, transmission between
individuals participating in the same activity is only considered to happen during the activity.
Secondly, the activity takes place during specific time periods, e.g., daily, weekly, monthly.
Therefore, a time-space hypergraph is created where each hyperedge is only considered active
according to the activity’s schedule, e.g., a university course is only active in the time slot
given in a curriculum. Consequently, the disease spreads between vertices in a hyperedge at
a time when the hyperedge is active. Within each hyperedge, the health states of individuals
change dynamically with time. Therefore, the spread itself is dependent on the health states of
individuals in the hyperedge, see further discussion in Chapter 7.

CHAPTER 4
Immunization Model
The objective of this chapter is to build an immunizing model applicable in real situations. Here,
the focus is on the test case, DTU Fall Semester 2020 data. Nevertheless, the model is relevant
for various other real-life scenarios, such as those mentioned in Section 3.1. Initially, before
running epidemic simulations, the input network is run through an immunization model. The
model is used to identify which nodes in the network ought to be immunized. The goal is to
decrease the number of infections for a limited number of immunizations. Section 4.1 describes
how students are selected for immunization in the student graph from Section 3.2.1. Section 4.2
describes course selection for the course graph, previously mentioned in Section 3.2.2.
4.1
Student immunization
Immunizing students can relate to giving them a vaccine or have them follow every course on-
line, essentially removing them from the graph. Algorithm 1 describes the procedure where each
node is evaluated based on measure function. It is a stepwise procedure that constructs a set
of nodes by selecting nodes for immunization. The procedure is followed by node removal since
an immunized node cannot further transmit a disease. The model takes a pragmatic approach
where only a fraction of the student base is eliminated from the graph, e.g., due to vaccine
shortage or other limited resources.
Algorithm 1 presents the model given the following properties:
• G = (V, E): student graph G, where V are the set of students in the graph and E the
edges between them.
• p: percentage representing the immunization limit, i.e. the boundary of how many could
be considered immunized.
• computeMeasureFunction(G): measure function for calculating a metric for all nodes for
a given graph G, returning the node with the highest value.

4.2
Course immunization
Another alternative is to consider courses instead of students for immunization, meaning moving
courses online as mentioned in Section 1.2. Course immunization provides a new perspective to
the university environment. The procedure removes a course from the course network, which
compares to removing edges between students in the student network. When interpreted to the
immunization model, an important aspect to consider is imposing constraints on the model. The
course selection can achieve a suitable output by utilizing constraints. Namely, controlling the
number of courses moved online while also allowing for more interesting results. For instance,
requiring that all students have at least one course on campus eliminates the possibility of be-
ing entirely forced from campus. As a result, the model presumably maintains fairness by not
discriminating and singling out individuals prohibited from using the school facilities.
The initial idea of a fairness constraint is introduced to the course immunization model. However,
upon inspecting the data, roughly half of the entire course base has at least one student attending
only one course, making a large portion of the popular courses unviable for immunization. As
it could severely restrict the simulation results, the constraint instead applies only to students
enrolled in two or more courses. Therefore, the requirement ensures that all students who attend
at least two courses have at least one of those on campus. Algorithm 2 shows how this constraint
is implemented, given the following properties:
• S: student set
• SC: dictionary mapping students to their set of registered courses
• currentCourse: the current selected course by the model
• allOnlineCourses: set of courses that have already been moved online and current selected
course currentCourse

The course immunization model is then extended to account for active periods. A contact infec-
tion can only occur between two students attending the same course that is active in a period.
The courses that are more active increase the probability of a contact infection successfully
transmitting between students. Therefore, a design decision is made for the course immuniza-
tion model to scale a course’s rank according to how active it is over the total time period.

Immunization Strategies
This chapter describes the exact methods used as immunization strategies in this study. First,
Section 5.1 provides a general overview of how a node is targeted in a strategy, either by local

5.1
Immunization scheme
Different strategies that suggest important nodes to immunize have been proposed over the years
[61]. However, interest remains in finding or improving strategies as they may save human lives
and resources. When the goal is to reduce epidemic spreading by achieving uniform immuniza-
tion density, the most straightforward approach immunizes nodes at random [3]. Nonetheless,
researchers have focused on identifying vital nodes using different network features, hoping to
mitigate epidemic spreading by immunizing only a few nodes.
Lü et al. pointed out that identifying important nodes is not an easy task. It is impossible to
define a common index for these nodes as the criteria will vary. The goal might be to protect
an environment from an epidemic or conduct an online marketing strategy. Even if there is
a clear objective function, a strategy’s performance might vary a lot depending on the input
network or the parameters of the objective function [61]. Lü et al. also described how most
strategies are designed to identify a single important node, while real-world applications call for
a set of important nodes. Combining the two most influential nodes might be incorrect as their
influence in a network might overlap. Several algorithms designed for identifying individual
nodes perform poorly at identifying a set of nodes.
Figure 5.1 describes the methodology used to explore which identification strategies perform best
for large-scale immunization. The targeted nodes are chosen for local immunization and removed
according to a stochastic strategy initiated from randomly selected nodes in the graph. Global
immunization includes testing various deterministic strategies where the removal of targeted
nodes has proven to be eﬀicient. Deterministic strategies can apply adaptive recalculation to
manage the overlapping influence of clustered nodes. That is, a node of the highest importance
is chosen and removed from the network. Then, before selecting the next node, the influence is
measured again. Therefore, the impact of previously targeted nodes is no longer present in the
network.
Deterministic immunization strategies target nodes based on a centrality measure. These strate-
gies produce consistent results for a given network. The centrality measure indicates how a
node might propagate a disease. These strategies can effectively mitigate an epidemic but re-
quire knowledge of every node in the network, making them computationally heavy for large
networks. Cherifi et al. described the steps for applying a deterministic strategy the following
way[20]:
1. Select a centrality measure for the strategy.

Degree Centrality
Degree centrality is one of the most widespread immunization strategies [85]. For a given undi-

are more likely to transmit a disease to more nodes since they have more neighbors than others

5.2.1.2
Eigenvector centrality
Eigenvector centrality is formed on the same idea as degree centrality. More connections mean
higher importance [74]. Eigenvector centrality takes into account that not all connections are
equal. Therefore, a node’s eigenvector centrality relates to its neighbors’ centrality. The idea
is that connections to high-scoring neighbors are more significant than the same number of
connections to low-scoring neighbors [11]. Consequently, a high eigenvector centrality indicates
that a node is related to multiple other nodes, all of which have high scores.
The eigenvector centrality for node vi is the i-th element of the vector of centralities x, described
by the following equation:

5.2.1.3
K-shell decomposition
Degree centrality is entirely based on the number of neighbors a node has and states that nodes
of the same degree are of the same influence in the network. Kitsak et al. argued that there are
plausible scenarios where the most influential spreaders do not correspond to the most highly
connected nodes. Instead, they are better identified within the core of the network, identified
through k-shell decomposition [52].
When running a k-shell decomposition analysis for an undirected network G, the analysis starts
by defining the coreness ci of every isolated node vi as ci = 0. These nodes are removed from the
network before the k-core decomposition. In the first step of the decomposition, the nodes with
degree k = 1 are removed. As a result, the degree of the other nodes decreases. The analysis
continues removing the nodes whose residual degree is k ≤1, until every residual degree is k > 1.
The first step of the decomposition ends by defining the 1-shell as the set of nodes removed dur-
ing the step, and their coreness ks is equal to 1. The second step of the decomposition acts
the same, targeting the remaining nodes with residual degree k = 2, continuing until no nodes
are left whose residual degree is k ≤2. The removed nodes in the second step form the 2-shell
with coreness ks equal to 2. The decomposition performs steps of this process until no nodes
are left in the network. Finally, the coreness of node vi is the same as its corresponding k-shell.
A k-shell immunization strategy targets nodes with higher coreness first, as they are considered
to have more influence in the network.

CHAPTER 6
Community based immunization
This chapter aims to reveal the community structure in the DTU 2020 fall semester networks
as it can amplify the influence of a set of bridge nodes that maintain the connection in different
communities. As mentioned in Section 5.2, centrality measures pursue to identify key nodes
within the networks. However, these strategies are independent of the community structure.
Section 6.1 explains community structure and the community-based strategies that are relevant
for the study. Then, in Section 6.2 the community detection for the DTU networks is discussed,
presenting the identified partitions and how they differentiate from each other. Finally, Sec-
tion 6.3 and Section 6.4 give an introduction to the community strategies applied in the study,
stochastic and deterministic, respectively. The stochastic strategies do not require knowledge
of the complete network structure, and therefore, are applicable for large-scale networks such as
the student network. However, the deterministic ones use more information, increasing compu-
tational complexity, thus only considered for the course network.
6.1
Exploring a new perspective on the network
organization
In social networks, the similar choices and tastes of individuals often generate virtual clusters
or communities. In 1949, Luce and Perry [62] came up with the term cliques when analyzing
subgraphs in social networks by modeling groups of individuals who knew each other. Commu-
nity is a subgraph where the nodes within it share more internal connections than with the rest
of the network [71]. Some of these nodes can be more influential than others, differentiating
communities from cliques as a clique is a complete graph where all nodes within the subgraph
have an identical role. Identifying communities has become a common research area as it often
gives a clear idea of the network organization. Previously unknown properties are brought to the
surface, which can be beneficial for numerous applications, including the dynamics of epidemic
spreading processes across networks [20]. This gives rise to embedding community structure
when designing immunization strategies.
Figure 6.1 outlines the community-based immunization strategies used in this study, classified
according to the community structure model they apply. The feature that distinguishes overlap-
ping communities from non-overlapping communities is that the former recognizes the possibility
that a node can belong to multiple communities. This means that community partition is not
always clear cut which is further demonstrated in recent works by Jeub et al. [44], Leskovec et
al. [56], and Palla et al. [78].
As shown in Figure 6.1, immunization strategies considering non-overlapping structure can be
divided into global, local, and a combination of the two. The global strategies target bridge
nodes, i.e., nodes with outside connections towards foreign communities [20]. These strategies
highlight the boundary nodes in each community and consider them to play a significant role in
disease diffusion across the network. Local strategies solely require information on the commu-
nity level, excluding the global network structure. They tend to recognize nodes that are most

influential locally, in their own community [39]. Finally, the third type combines several aspects,
e.g., the main spreaders within the community, which are likely to spread a virus towards other
modules of the network [20].

6.2
Modularity-based community detection
“The aim of community detection in graphs is to identify the modules and, possibly, their hier-
archical organization, by only using the information encoded in the graph topology. ”
— Santo Fortunato [29]
Community detection in large-scale networks is often referred to as identifying a group of densely
interconnected nodes with sparse connections to other nodes within the graph [71]. The most
widespread used approach is optimizing modularity, see Equation 6.1, where the partitioning
is subjected to the assessment of cohesion and separation through the amount of intra- and
inter-community connections, respectively [75]. In other terms, modularity measures the rela-
tive density of a node’s internal community connections over its connections to nodes outside
the community. Amongst the various community detection methods, Lancichinetti et al., Or-
man et al., and Yang et al. found the Louvain algortihm, created by Blondel et al. [10], to be
eﬀicient in both synthetic and real-world networks [54, 76, 111]. Therefore, due to its popularity,
high-quality results, and low computational time, the Louvain method is chosen for detecting
community structures in this study.

6.3 Stochastic immunization strategies
6.3
Stochastic immunization strategies
According to Salathé and Jones [89], there are two major issues associated with the identification
of important nodes to affect virus diffusion on networks:
i) The graph structure is often unknown / data on network structure is lacking.
ii) Many graphs tend to be very large, making it diﬀicult to compute known, effective, deter-
ministic strategies such as centrality measures.
On such occasions, many stochastic immunization strategies are applicable as they address both
problems at once, e.g., the Community Bridge Finder (CBF) algorithm, discussed in more detail
in Section 6.3.1. These methods focus on the information available at the node level, thus propos-
ing target nodes without full knowledge of the graph structure. Consequently, these strategies
become more practical and computationally eﬀicient when compared to deterministic strategies
in large networks [20].
With this in mind, the stochastic strategies are considered to be relevant for identifying impor-
tant nodes within the student graph, introduced in Section 3.2.1, as the network is large, with
fifteen-fold more nodes than the course graph.
6.3.1
Community Bridge Finder (CBF)
Salathé and Jones [89] developed the CBF approach when mitigating disease spread in large
scale networks. In their work, they managed to outperform the widespread acquaintance immu-
nization strategy [18, 20, 21], still using the same properties, i.e., acting solely on locally available
network information. Cohen et al. acquaintance immunization method selects node v0 and a
random neighbor v1 of v0, and if v1 has been picked as an acquaintance n times it is vaccinated
[21]. Therefore it focuses on highly connected nodes similar to degree immunization, whereas
CBF aims to identify bridge nodes that connect two groups of densely connected nodes.
The stepwise procedure of the CBF algorithm is described in Figure 6.4 [88], and it works as
follows:
Step 1: Node vi=0 is chosen at random
Step 2: A random walk is performed from node vi=0 where nodes are visited based on the
condition that they have not been on the random path before.
Step 3: A check is made at each node vi≥2, to see if the node has more than one connection
to the random path. If only one connection is identified, node vi−1 is marked as a
potential bridge. In Figure 6.4, (a) and (b) show that v2 is connected to both v0 and
v1, and therefore, (c) the random walk continues to node v3. Since v3 has only one
connection to the random path (v3 to v2), v2 is considered as a potential bridge and
the algorithm moves on to next step.
Step 4: Node vi−1 is analysed further by selecting two random neighbors of node vi other than
vi−1. If those neighbors have no connection to the previously visited nodes, vi−1 is
identified as a bridge node and immunized. Otherwise, the random walk continues
from vi−1. In Figure 6.4, (d) node v3 is connected to a node that shares an edge with
a previously visited node, v0, and therefore, v2 is dismissed as a target which leads to
(e) a continued random walk to v4. Now v4 has no other connection to the random
path except for sharing an edge with v3, making v3 a potential target. (f) Two random

visited nodes. Consequently, v3 is marked as a bridge node and immunized.

6.3.2
Degree Community Bridge Finder (DCBF)
As mentioned in Section 6.2.2, the DTU Fall Semester 2020 networks both have a strong commu-
nity structure. Therefore, it is relevant to test Gong’s Degree Community Bridge Finder (DCBF)
algorithm since experiments have shown that it outperforms CBF when community strength is
high [38]. The DCBF algorithm is an extension of Salathé and Jones’ CBF strategy where during
the random walk, nodes are selected based on their degree, from high to low. Apart from that,
the procedure is the same as described in Section 6.3.1. However, to speed up computational
time and to avoid endless hunts for bridge nodes, two additional conditions are introduced to
the algorithm.
Check 1: A random path cannot include more than 10 visited nodes. If it exceeds 10 nodes, the
first node is removed from the path to make room for the new one (first-in-first-out).
Check 2: For every node, if it is visited more than k times (k = 2), it is immunized right away.

6.4 Deterministic immunization strategies
6.4
Deterministic immunization strategies
The majority of deterministic immunization strategies outperform stochastic ones as they utilize
the whole network structure [20].
However, as previously mentioned, they often have high
computational complexity, becoming problematic as the network input gets larger. Moreover,
community-based deterministic strategies often incorporate additional topological properties
regarding the communities that differentiate them from ’regular’ deterministic strategies. These
features include but are not limited to:
• The number of communities within the network
• Community size
• Density of inter-community connections
• Density of intra-community connections
Three factors weigh in when choosing the community based deterministic immunization strate-
gies to pursue: (i) the strength of the community structure (mixing coeﬀicient µ, Eq. 6.2), (ii)
performance in other recent experiments, and (iii) variety by focusing on the different network
topology requirements, as shown in Figure 6.1.
In Section 6.2.2 the approximate mixing parameter for the two graphs, student and course graph
is µ = 0.3, which in terms of community strength is somewhat close to a strong to medium
community structure [36, 39]. Although Rajeh et al. [84] found that Number of Neighboring
Communities (NNC) and Community-based Mediator (CbM) show inconsistency in their be-
havior from one network to another, i.e., they vary in performance based on the network input.
Ghalmane et al. [36] comprehensive experiments reveal that those strategies can outperform
other known strategies. They showed that for networks with community structures similar to
the student and course graph, NNC and CbM manage to capture better the most important
nodes to immunize, compared to other strategies, when minimizing disease spreading.
Moreover, to make up for the potential drawbacks of Louvain’s community detection algo-
rithm, described in Section 6.2.2, Influence Maximization based on Label Propagation Algo-
rithm (IM-LPA) is selected as it requires no prior information about the community structure
in the graphs. In addition, IM-LPA exploits the overlapping community structure features and
has near-linear time complexity. Hence it applies to large-scale networks, and therefore, applied
to both the course and the student network.

6.4.3

Algorithm (IM-LPA)
The IM-LPA, proposed by Zhao et al.
[114], is a method that does not require predefined
community structure by another approach and considers the potential that a node can belong

based methods in the study. The approach comprises two phases: (i) Seeding phase and (ii)
Label propagation phase.
Seeding phase
The goal of the seeding phase is to reveal the community structure in a network. The choice
of seed is therefore crucial, and for IM-LPA the nodes of interest are the ones with the highest
degree as, according to Zhao et al., the node with a higher degree tends to be the core of its
community. Thus, the stepwise procedure for a network G(V, E) ), where V is the set of the
nodes, and E is the set of the edges, is the following:
Step 1: Create an empty set of seed nodes Ω= ∅and a candidate node set C = V where
initially all nodes in the network are considered candidates.
Step 2: Calculate the degree kv of each node v in the network.
Step 3: Identify the node with the largest degree in the candidate set, vmax = argminv∈Ckv,
and add it to the seed set Ω= Ω∪{vmax}.
Step 4: Remove vmax from the candidate set along with its neighbors N(vmax),
C = C \ ({vmax} ∪N(vmax)) .
Step 5: Return to step 3 and repeat until candidate set is empty, C = ∅.
When C = ∅return the seed nodes Ω

The immunization model used in this study includes a course constraint, described in Algo-
rithm 2, where courses are restricted from being immunized if it entails forcing a student to
follow all of their courses online. IM-LPA only produces a limited set of nodes to be vaccinated,
i.e., only seed nodes, and therefore, leaves a large portion out of consideration. Consequently,
the immunization model fails to reach its desired immunization coverage as the set of seed
nodes is too small or includes infeasible nodes to immunize. In order to adapt the method to
the proposed immunization model, IM-LPA is re-run where the previously identified seed nodes
are removed from the network, as they are either already vaccinated or infeasible, and thereby
selects a new set of seed nodes to prioritize for immunization. A detailed implementation of the
algorithm is outlined in Appendix C.4.

CHAPTER 7
Simulation
This chapter describes how an epidemic is simulated in a network. Section 7.1 introduces variants
of epidemic spreading models. Next, the simulation model used for this project is described. The
simulation model was provided by Niels-Christian Fink Bagger. The model was created for the
paper Reducing disease spread through optimization: limiting mixture of the population is more
important than limiting group sizes, by Bagger et al., correspondingly the description provided
in this chapter is based on the paper [5]. Section 7.2 describes how the simulation models the
health state of individuals.
Section 7.3 outlines the process that the simulation model goes
through.
7.1
Epidemic spreading models
Compartmental models are used to simplify the modeling of an infectious disease. The disease is
modeled by assigning nodes in a population to various compartments, depending on the model.
The most commonly used epidemic models are the Susceptible-Infectious-Recovered (SIR) model
and the Susceptible-Infectious-Susceptible (SIS) model [55]. Other models exist, such as the
SEIR model, which is a variation of the SIR model that has been widely used for modeling
influenza-like diseases and respiratory infections [82], also in recent works when analyzing the
behavior of COVID-19 [35, 68, 113]. Therefore, the simulation model in this project uses the
SEIR model.
7.2
Health state of individuals
The health state of an individual during the simulation process is modeled using a Discrete Time
Markov Chain (DTMC) SEIR model. The SEIR model uses the following states [112].
S:
Susceptible
The individual is susceptible to infection.
E:
Exposed
The individual is exposed to the infection but cannot infect other individuals.
I:
Infectious
The individual is infectious and can transmit the infection to other individuals.
R:
Recovered
The individual is recovered or deceased from the infection.
Given the assumption that recovered individuals obtain permanent immunity,
no persons are transferred from the R state back to S, i.e. cannot become infected again.
An individual’s health state can move from a state to the next one, in the same order as the
labels S-E-I-R, except for R, which is an absorbing state in the Markov chain. The model is

Experimental setting
The objective of this chapter is to present the data and methods used in the empirical evaluation
of the various immunization strategies mentioned in Chapter 5 and Chapter 6. The chapter
introduces the dataset used for the case study in Section 8.1, the parameter configurations of
the simulation model in Section 8.2, and the various immunization coverages determining the

8.1
Datasets
In order to evaluate the performance of the various strategies under inspection and propose
methods to safely re-open an activity, such as a university, a DTU 2020 fall semester dataset
serves as the primary resource to create real-world student networks. It is important to note that
the majority of the immunization strategies applied are chosen according to their performance
in previous works on networks with similar topological properties as the DTU 2020 fall semester
graphs.
8.1.1
DTU fall semester 2020 networks
The main goal of the study is to evaluate which measures can be taken to safely re-open a univer-
sity during an epidemic. The 2020 fall semester course enrollment at the Technical University
of Denmark is used as a test case. The data includes the course registration of every DTU
student, giving insights into where students might interact within the university environment.
The dataset has its limitations as student relations on campus are only considered to take place
if students are in the same class, without estimating other possibilities of communication or
shared surfaces, e.g. in the library, restrooms, and canteen.
With a population of 9602 students and 652 courses, the size of course ∗student enrollment
equals 34.590, meaning each student signs up to, on average, 3 −4 courses. Over 40% of these
courses have more than 100 students signed up for the class [5].
During the 2020 fall semester at DTU, the course arrangement was changed due to the Covid-19
pandemic. For example, some courses kept their schedule with physical lectures, however, the
students were on a rotational schedule, i.e. for each class only a limited amount of students were
allowed in the classrooms whilst the rest participated online. Then, the following week, another
set of students was allowed to physically attend where the former physically present students
engaged online. Therefore, each individual student was at some point in time allowed on campus,
thus no one was forced to participate fully online. This inspired the study to explore whether
some courses or students induce a higher risk of disease spreading than others, and thereby
consider those for immunization by moving them entirely online or prioritize for vaccination,
assuming vaccine is available. Two separate networks are used, a course graph introduced in
Section 3.2.2, and a student graph described in Section 3.2.1.

8.4
Evaluation criteria
The Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) used to compare and evaluate the effect of the various
immunization strategies are measurements based on both the SEIR model simulation results
and certain graph structure properties. The simulation results estimate the impact of a disease
spread in terms of how many individuals were infected and how many had to go into quarantine.
However, graph structure KPIs are based on the assumption that certain network properties
make a graph less vulnerable to epidemic outbreaks.

The results from the epidemiological SEIR model are key when evaluating the performance of
an immunization strategy. The following four core aspects are of interest:
• Total Infections (TI)
• Contact Infections (CI)
• Spontaneous Infections (SI)
• Average Number of People in Quarantine per Time Step (QP)

Strategies that keep these measures to a minimum are considered to be most successful as they
manage to have the least amount of impact on individuals, particularly CI and QP since they
are the ones that are influenced by the immunization strategy.
The goal is to choose strategies that reduce the risk of disease propagation in a network. Thus CI
is an essential factor when testing the effectiveness of a proposed immunization approach. Spon-

CI denotes the infections that occur in the network, i.e. when an infectious node further trans-

SI since it is diﬀicult to control individuals’ actions outside the network environment. However,

outbreak in the network. Moreover, keeping a low number of individuals in preventative quar-

A disease in a network spreads between two neighboring nodes. Therefore, when the average

Network diameter
The network diameter denotes the longest distance amongst the shortest paths in the network.
If the diameter increases, disease transmission is assumed to become more diﬀicult within the
graph.
Epidemic threshold
The epidemic threshold condition is a relationship between virus properties and network struc-
ture in which if the criterion is met, a viral infection will die out over time. This motivates
raising the epidemic threshold to limit disease spreading in a network. However, various studies
have shown different approximations of threshold conditions based on the epidemiological model
used. Kephat et al. [48] proposed it as the inverse of the average connectivity of a network,
while Pastor et al. [79] suggested it was the expected connectivity divided by the sum of squared
degrees. Further, Eguiluz et al. [26] derived 1/(< degree > −1) to be an epidemic threshold
for real graphs.
Clearly, there is no apparent ’right’ solution when deriving an epidemic threshold. However,
studies have demonstrated that for ’flu like’ epidemic spreading models such as SIS and SIR,
disease propagation dies out more quickly if the spectral radius of the graph is below a particular
limit, resulting in an approximated epidemic threshold [17, 87, 108]. This justifies an approach
for limiting epidemic propagation by lowering the underlying network’s spectral radius by min-
imizing the largest absolute eigenvalue of the graph. Since this study uses the SEIR model for
simulating the disease spread, a evaluation will be conducted with inspiration from previous
works by Chakrabarti et al. [17]. They proposed the epidemic threshold τ to be the inverse of
the largest eigenvalue of its adjacency matrix λ1,A.

8.4 Evaluation criteria
Average contacts per individual
Contact stands for the number of distinct pairs of nodes associated in one hyperedge or more.
For the 2020 DTU fall semester networks, if two students are at least together in one course, they
are considered contacts. In order to decrease potential diffusion chains, the number of contacts
per individual must be kept to a minimum. The average contacts per individual are calculated
over all nodes in the subgraph after applying an immunization strategy to only account for the
remaining potential transmission paths for a disease to spread.
Average active contact periods per individual
The contact measure is averaged across all nodes in the remaining subgraph after immunization
to only account for the remaining potential transmission channels for a disease to propagate.
Therefore, the immunized students are excluded from consideration. It finds the average of how
often a student has an active contact connection, active period, with other students, i.e., how
often a student attends lectures with other students over the semester. Active periods resemble
how often a student attends a course on campus, and therefore, the more active the likelihood
increases of getting infected on school grounds and infecting other classmates.

restricts the choice of course removal, essentially holding back further improvement.
Given that degree and betweenness immunization are the most commonly evaluated immuniza-
tion strategies [42, 81, 89, 91], their performance compared to closeness, k-shell, and eigenvector
immunization could seem surprising. Betweenness targets nodes on the shortest transmission
chains, essentially breaking them via immunization. However, the course graph includes a high
amount of edges compared to the number of nodes, which leads to many nodes never being on
the shortest path between a distinct pair of vertices. Due to the lack of structural holes in the
network, a significant proportion of nodes receive a betweenness score of 0, ultimately making
it more challenging to identify nodes in a bridging position [16]. The degree strategy targets
the most connected nodes. However, the immunization model restricts the choice, considering
that the most popular courses are the ones that are most connected, and thus the immunization
coverage is reached fast. Furthermore, given the constraint that students are allowed to have one
course on campus, some of the largest courses are forced to be on campus. Another known limi-

tation is that degree does not consider the global structure of the network. As an example, even
though some nodes are highly connected, they might not be able to reach other nodes quickly
in the network, and therefore, are not the most influential nodes in transmitting a disease [12, 14].
The shortcoming mentioned with the degree strategy is captured and resolved with closeness
immunization. The intent behind the closeness method is to target nodes that have the poten-

no disconnected components, the closeness immunization performs well in targeting influential
nodes. Moreover, the eigenvector method seems to target a fair set of nodes as it essentially
removes nodes that before removal contributed most in decreasing the approximated epidemic
threshold, described in Section 8.4.2.
Although k-shell decomposition is not the most effective immunization strategy across all simu-
lations, it shows promising results and is the best strategy for p = 30%. Although the method
could seem related to degree immunization, Kitsak et al. [52] managed to distinguish the set of
targeted nodes by k-shell decomposition based on their location in the core of the network. In
this case, it outperforms both degree and betweenness centrality, and therefore, it is of interest
to explore the k-shell strategy further as it has room for improvement.

Section 6.3, and network-specific heuristics are developed when identifying the most influential
nodes in the graph. The evaluation criteria are based on the simulation results for all simulation
parameter configurations, described in Section 8.2. Additionally, the simulations are run for the
different levels of immunization coverage, p ∈{20%, 30%, 40%, 50%}, introduced in Section 8.3.
9.2.1
Student network specific heuristics
Three new immunization methods with low computational complexity are formulated for the
student network: active connections, neighboring weights, and most courses.
Active connections:
The student immunization methods have not accounted for how
often a student is active over the simulation period. The active
connections approach is similar to the neighboring weights by as-
signing weights to edges between students. In this strategy, the
edge weights represent the cumulative active courses a pair of stu-
dents attend in total. The importance of a node is then determined
by the sum of the neighboring edge weights. As edges can poten-
tially transmit a contact infection, the method tries to immunize
students that are the most active and have the most active neigh-
bors.
Neighboring weights:
In a weighted student network, the assigned weights reflect the
number of courses a pair of students are both taking. It is very
similar to the degree centrality measure, except it prioritizes nodes
based on the total weight of edges it is connected to, i.e., the sum
of edge weights associated with its neighbors. The higher the total
edge weight of a node, the more critical it is to immunize.

Most courses:
Students enrolled in the most number of courses are prioritized for im-
munization. The strategy is independent of how many modules per week
a class takes place; it solely focuses on how many different courses each
student is attending.

10.1
Introduction to the GA procedure
A genetic algorithm (GA) is a metaheuristic inspired by evolution. These algorithms mimic
the process of natural selection by modifying a population of individual solutions [97].
An
implementation of a GA starts by generating an initial population of usually random solutions,
called individuals or chromosomes. Their features are the following:
• Genes: Characteristics that compose an individual, e.g. a node that contributes to a
solution.
• Individuals or chromosomes: A set of genes or features which constitute a solution for
the problem to be solved by the genetic algorithm.
• Population: A set of individuals or solutions to be explored for the proposed problem.
At each generation, the population is subjected to four phases using the genetic algorithm:
• Evaluation: The fitness score of every individual is calculated. That is, the solutions
are evaluated.
Next, individuals are selected for reproduction, where higher evaluated
individuals have a higher probability of being selected than those with lower fitness scores.
• Selection: In each generation, fit individuals are selected as parents to create the next
generation. However, selecting only the best individuals would cause the search to proceed
in a single direction or get stuck in local minima. The selection must account for diversity
to improve the exploration of the search space. Several methods have been proposed for
selection, including Roulette Wheel Selection, Rank Selection, and Tournament Selection
[96].
67
==PAGE:88==
10.1 Introduction to the GA procedure
• Crossover: Subsequently, the selected individuals undergo crossover and mutation to
produce the next population. The crossover uses two selected individuals, called parents,
for recombination and mutation. The parents exchange information, known as genes, to
produce new individuals called children. The children are a recombination of their parent’s
genes. These new individuals have the possibility of obtaining a better fitness score.
• Mutation: The next operation, mutation, makes a random modification to the genes of
the new individuals. The mutation is applied to maintain diversity. The diversity prevents
premature convergence and avoids local minima. The mutation rate is generally kept very
low, as a high rate would cause the GA to behave similarly to a random search [77].
The GA used in this project applies elitist selection. Elitist selection carries the best individu-
als from generation G to the next generation G+1. When elitist selection is applied, the quality
of the best solution monotonically increases with each generation. Without elitist selection, the
best solution can be lost due to stochastic errors [6]. These steps are then repeated using a new
generation of individuals until reaching termination criteria. Commonly used criteria are a fixed
number of iterations, reaching a certain fitness score, or if there was no significant improvement
in a new generation [86]. This process is visualized in Figure 10.1.

Initial population
The initial population of the GA includes nodes that have a high score according to the best per-
forming immunization strategies. Active connections and degree centrality manage to produce
the best results and are therefore included. Neighboring weights and most courses are considered
promising as they both generate good results (see Section 9.2.2), and target a dissimilar set of
nodes (see Figure 9.8).
Nodes with a high degree are not necessarily most strategically located in the network. There-
fore, three additional strategies based on a node’s position are considered to introduce diversity
to the population; eigenvector, closeness centrality, and CbM. The first one is related to the epi-
demic threshold, introduced in Section 8.4.2 where the removal of nodes with a high eigenvector
centrality increases the epidemic threshold, assumed to make disease transmission more diﬀicult
in the network. It differentiates from degree selection as it attempts to capture the qualitative
aspect of the connections of a node by not only taking into account how many links a node
has (the node’s degree) but also the centrality of its neighbors [37]. The two latter ones show
promising results for the course graph but require too much computational time for large-scale
networks, like the student network. However, since for the GA, the centrality measures are
only computed once, without recalculations after each node removal, both CbM and closeness
centrality are applicable for the student graph.
To further demonstrate the GA’s potential in producing a more optimal set of nodes compared
to other strategies, Figure 10.2 depicts the correlation between the methods used to generate the
GA search space. By applying linear regression, the R2 is determined, which is the proportion
of variance (%) in one variable that can be explained by the other variable, i.e., how a change
in one centrality measure affects another centrality measure. According to Moore et al. [67], a
relationship with R2 < 0.5 is generally considered weak while 0.5 < R2 < 0.7 and R2 > 0.7 are
considered moderate and strong, respectively.
Most of the strategies seem to correlate with their counterparts except for the most courses
method. Although eigenvector and closeness centrality both focus on a node’s prominence in
a network, they do not seem to correspond as well as when compared to degree, CbM, and
neighboring weights.
The main difference is that closeness centrality ranks nodes high that
are in close proximity to other nodes in the network, enabling eﬀicient transmission across the
network, either directly or through shortest paths given their position. In contrast, eigenvector
centrality ranks the connections, marking connections to more influential, well-connected nodes
as more important, considering the node’s neighbors’ centrality, resulting in an influence directly
or indirectly through their links.
The purpose of Figure 10.2 is to demonstrate that the various centrality measures are associated
with different outcomes. The GA is designed to exploit these outcomes by combining the results
from the most promising centrality measures to identify a more optimal set of nodes.

Selection
Each generation consists of 100 individuals. The selection process includes elitist selection, where
the best 10 individuals from the current generation are directly moved to the next generation. It
ensures that no subsequent generation takes a step backward, i.e., good solutions found earlier
are not lost.
The remaining 90 individuals are generated by breeding pairs of individuals from the current
generation, chosen with a tournament selection followed with an additional check to ensure that
no parent mates with themself. When selecting the fittest individuals for mating, a tournament
selection is performed with a tournament size equal to 4. The reason for choosing a relatively
small tournament size is to ensure that weak candidates also have a chance of being selected as
parents since all potential nodes are already considered good potentials given their centrality
ranking. There is a possibility that these weak candidates could create better-performing chil-
dren when breeding with other parents. Given a random set of four individuals, one of them is
chosen based on the best fitness score from the current generation.

Employing a genetic algorithm on the course

Developing a genetic algorithm for the course network is more complex than for the student
network. The constraint in the course network causes the mating process to be more diﬀicult
as the student crossover function can easily produce invalid individuals for the course graph. In
addition, the number of selected nodes does not represent the immunization coverage. Instead,
it evaluates a node in the course network by the number of students registered per course. There-
fore, generating the initial population, crossover, and mutation operators need to be modified
for the course network while the fitness function and elitist selection are the same.
Initial population
The GA for the student network limits the search space by excluding low-ranking nodes based
on various measures. However, due to the different course sizes and the constraint, limiting the
search space for the course network could exclude nodes that are the only viable nodes to add to
an individual, which would impact the results. Therefore, the search space includes all courses
in the algorithm except for the initial population. The initial population is based on a portion
of the search space to guide the search in the direction of high-ranking nodes based on several
measures. The measures that the initial population is based on are the preexisting solutions
from the closeness, k-shell degree, eigenvector, NNC, CbM, and betweenness-based strategies as
well as the union of t highest ranking nodes from the respective measures. Algorithm 9 describes
the process of generating the initial generation for the course GA.

CHAPTER 11
Conclusion
The COVID-19 outbreak has brought unprecedented global challenges both for individuals and
society. It has demanded a rapid adaptation and response to minimize disease spreading be-
tween persons. Therefore, it is of high value and relevance to explore the effectiveness of actions
used to mitigate pandemic outbreaks, both due to the current crisis and becoming better pre-
pared for future epidemics. The objective is to demonstrate the application of immunization
strategies to mitigate the risk of contagion when re-opening activities during an epidemic where
individuals share resources over time that could be used remotely. The methodology can be
applied to propose a set of individuals or departments in a workplace to work from home, move
the teaching of specific university classes online, and so forth.
In this paper, an immunization model is formulated for a large Danish university, the Technical
University of Denmark. The immunization model evaluates proposed immunization strategies
using a SEIR simulation model where different viral parameters of the epidemic are tested, such
as infection probabilities and behavioral factors in terms of contact tracing and quarantining.
Two networks are used as input in the model for assessing the immunization strategies, a student
and a course network. Both networks encapsulate the university data, while immunization in
these networks translates to removing a student or a course from consideration in the epidemic
simulation, essentially forcing them off school grounds or providing them with a vaccine.
The study aims to investigate whether targeting nodes using intricate methods yields consider-
ably better results than vaccinating nodes at random. The results show how different aspects
of a network can be utilized when designing an immunization strategy that modifies a network
to withstand an epidemic outbreak. These aspects can focus on but are not limited to network
connectivity, community structure, or paths in the network. However, careful consideration of
the network is necessary to determine the appropriate immunization actions for each network.
Various known immunization strategies and proposed network-specific heuristics have been eval-
uated using the simulation model. The results differ considerably, even within the same method,
depending on immunization coverage and the viral parameters of the epidemic. The findings
obtained by applying the methodology show how the proposed targeted immunization methods
can drastically reduce contact infections and the number of individuals in quarantine compared
to a random immunization scheme. Random immunization requires substantially greater im-
munization coverage for achieving comparable results, i.e., far more nodes need to be randomly
vaccinated to attain the same performance as the proposed methodology. For example, for both
the student and the course network, most of the proposed strategies where only 30% of the
population is immunized outperform random immunization with a 50% immunization coverage.
Moreover, the most important aspect of the immunization process seems to involve accounting
for the number of active periods students share during the simulation. The concept is adapted
to immunization strategies for the course network while a heuristic is tailored to the student net-
work, the active connections strategy, which reduces contact infections up to 72,5% compared
to random immunization for the same amount of immunized nodes.

11 Conclusion
In addition, the study explores whether immunizing activities is more eﬀicient than immunizing
individuals. The outcome demonstrates that moving courses online is the preferred approach
rather than immunizing individual students. It yields better results concerning contact infec-
tions and the number of students in quarantine over time. The immunization of activities can,
on average, reduce the risk of contact infections by 20% in comparison to immunizing indi-
viduals. Furthermore, considering that course immunization accounts for allowing students to
participate in at least one course on campus, it is even more favorable to student selection, i.e.,
instead of forcing students to participate remotely throughout the entire semester. However,
for immunization coverage of p ≥50%, the university should preferably immunize students. It
is due to the fairness constraint which restricts the course selection and decreases effectiveness
with increased immunization coverage.
Nonetheless, the proposed network-specific heuristic, active connections strategy produces the
best results for student immunization, independent of the population immunized, i.e., immu-
nization coverage. As opposed to student immunization, the performance of course strategies
varies depending on immunization coverage, making it more diﬀicult to propose one unparalleled
method when immunizing courses. Future research could focus on creating an approach based
on both networks, essentially identifying more optimal results by proposing a combination of
students and classes to be immunized. For example, it could be beneficial to accommodate a
limited number of student vaccine doses with course immunization. Then some courses would
be moved online, and some students vaccinated.
Previous works have demonstrated that finding the optimal combination of nodes to immunize
is an NP-hard problem [47, 94]. In addition, the sensitivity analysis on the course constraint
indicates that the strategies produce suboptimal results. Therefore, two genetic algorithms are
proposed, one for each network. The genetic algorithm outperforms traditional techniques in
the literature, according to findings. This is a significant achievement because the evolutionary
algorithm goes through an enormous number of possible subsets of nodes rather than relying on
one centrality ranking to base its decision. As a result, the epidemic spreading becomes more
restricted because the node selection is diverse, detecting and immunizing a mixture of highly
connected nodes, community bridge nodes, nodes on shortest paths, and other nodes considered
of high influence by other measures. Thereby, it breaks up transmission chains both within a
cluster and across multiple parts of the network.
As mentioned, the proposed genetic algorithms find notable reductions in contact infections
but with high computational costs. The high computational complexity is the reason for the
lack of parameter tuning. Parameters were chosen intuitively according to their performance
on graphs in other studies with similar characteristics as the university networks. Therefore,
future research could focus on different combinations of the number of generations, population
sizes, mutation rates, selection processes, and crossover functions as well as expanding the algo-
rithm to cover different immunization coverages. In addition, exploring different combinations
of network measures and heuristics for widening or narrowing the search space is an interesting
aspect to investigate further as it directly affects the results. The genetic algorithm produces
interesting results in the initial stages but lacks intensification to proceed further. Therefore,
another idea would be to try to explore and compare different metaheuristics for the problem,
such as simulated annealing, or improve the genetic algorithm to complement simulated anneal-
ing, thus creating a hybrid algorithm to find better results.
The subgraphs resulting from each immunization method have been evaluated with various
criteria, either related to the SEIR model results or topological features of the networks. Ac-
cording to the graph structure evaluation criteria, the best performing strategies are not in line
with the successful strategies in minimizing contact infection. These discrepancies highlight the

randomness factor of epidemics that have to be considered. However, given that the random
immunization scheme performs worst when analyzing both the SEIR results and the structural
properties of the remaining subgraphs, it indicates a relationship between graph structure and
epidemic spreading. Thus, it concludes that random immunization is least successful in changing
the graph structure to reduce disease spreading. A more extensive analysis of which network
properties correlate with minimum contact infections would allow for more robust optimization
models. One could try to formulate a mathematical model associated with these network fea-
tures paired with optimization algorithms to determine optimal vaccine allocation.
It is important to emphasize the limitations of the case study data, given the fact that disease
transmission can occur not only through contact infection but also via contaminated shared
surfaces, such as in bathrooms, canteen, and library. Currently, the data captures potential
person-to-person infection solely if the pair of students are together in a classroom. Thus, al-
though the findings in this study can benefit the Technical University of Denmark to some
extent, it is clear that more data for future work would help to make more informed decisions.
Potentially by using wireless sensor network technology to capture the dynamics of close prox-
imity interactions of students during the day and use it to construct a social network based
on location data from their telephone devices [90]. Governments could also apply it to derive
public health recommendations, obtaining various social networks from community settings, e.g.,
workplaces, hospitals, and more. However, the findings support the notion that network-based
immunization strategies help to make insightful decisions regarding vaccination priority to mit-
igate epidemic outbreaks, particularly when target nodes are selected based on diverse network
topological features.
The proposed strategies and core immunization model built upon the SEIR simulation model
were adapted to DTU’s university environment. However, the developed methodology is not
solely bound to school structures. It could be extended to various other applications in society,
e.g., utilized for crew and shift scheduling, organizing seminars, or re-opening a theater. The high
reduction in contact infections and outperformance of random immunization schemes suggests
eﬀicacy in different real-life contexts, where the resources or vaccinations are limited.

APPENDIX C
Implementation details on
immunization methods
The following pseudocodes give a detailed description of k-shell decomposition and the deter-
ministic community-based strategies, showing the process of computing each measure for every
node in the network.
C.1
Ranking based on k-shell decomposition
Algorithm 12 K-shell decomposition
Input: Graph G(V, E)
Output:A map k-shell(node, value)
1: // Function to check if there are nodes left with degree d
2: function check(G(V, E), d)
3:
return ∃v : v ∈V ∧degree(v) ≤d
4: end function
5: // Function for finding a set of nodes with a particular degree

partition set C = {C1, C2, ..., Cn} where n is the amount of identified communities

1: // Initialize an empty internal density map
2: internal ←map: internal(node, value)
3: // Initialize an empty external density map
4: external ←map: external(node, value)
5: // Initialize an empty entropy map
6: entropy ←map: entropy(node, value)

11:

C.4 Influence Maximization based on Label Propagation Algorithm (IM-LPA)
C.4
Influence Maximization based on Label
Propagation Algorithm (IM-LPA)
The following pseudocode gives an overview of how the IM-LPA algorithm is computed and
adapted for the immunization model. The strategy includes two phases, the seeding phase in
Algorithm 15 followed by the label propagation phase in Algorithm 16.
Algorithm 15 IM-LPA computation, seeding phase
Input: Graph G(V, E), where V is the set of nodes and E set of edges.
Output: A set of seed nodes
1: function compute_seed_nodes(G)
2:
// Seeding phase
3:
seed_nodes ←∅
4:
// Copy of G, used by the seeding phase
5:
Gseeding ←copy(G)
6:
while |Gseeding| > 0 do
7:
max_deg ←max(degree(Gseeding))
8:
seed_nodes ←seed_nodes ∪max_deg
9:
neighbors ←Gseeding.neighbors(max_deg)
10:
// remove max degree and its neighbors from candidate set
11:
Gseeding \ {max_deg ∪neighbors}
12:
end while
13:
return seed_nodes
14: end function
