Abstract
The telecommunication industry faces significant challenges due to the global surge in data traffic.
With the growing demand for data, power consumption at data centers is on the rise, primarily
attributed to ohmic losses in electrical interconnections. A proposed solution involves replacing
these electrical interconnections with optical ones, which would result in a significant reduction in
power consumption.
To develop efficient optical interconnections, low-energy consuming and high-performance
light sources are required. Photonic crystal microlasers with quantum well gain materials are ideal
light sources for optoelectronic applications due to their design flexibility, compatibility with inte-
grated CMOS circuits, and overall high performance.
In this work, electrically-driven photonic crystal microlasers are characterized and evaluated
based on their performance and compared to numerical investigations. Low injection efficiencies
and parasitic background emissions due to leakage currents are critical factors that limit the laser’s
performance. Through multiple simulations, we identify the leakage paths around a quantum well
(QW) gain medium and explain the photonic crystal’s effect on the laser’s electrical properties. From
our findings, slight alterations to the design can significantly affect the device’s electrical charac-
teristics. Bringing the p-doped region closer to the QW leads to a lower threshold current, reduced
carrier leakage, and improved performance overall. Lastly, discrepancies between the experimental
IV curves and simulations indicate that the p-doped region extends further towards the QW than
initially thought, leading to a lower turn-on voltage and a different current contributions.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In today’s rapidly expanding world of modern technology, the demand for more reliable and efficient
electronic devices continues to grow exponentially [1]. Although the continuous miniaturization of
electronic components has enabled engineers to increase the speed and bandwidth of integrated
systems, the need for innovative solutions to overcome the limitations of traditional electrical inter-
connections has become an increasingly important research topic.
A significant barrier to enhancing the reliability and efficiency of integrated circuits lies in the
energy loss introduced by electrical interconnections. Integrated circuits contain billions of closely
packed transistors, which leads to highly dense and complex chips. As a result of this trend, the
limitations of electrical interconnections, namely the resistance, capacitance, and crosstalk, become
amplified, which poses a significant problem to the performance of the chips. As the demands for
higher data rates continue to increase, the electrical interconnections struggle to keep up, and is-
sues such as power consumption and heat generation are effectively inhibiting further progress [2].
A proposed solution is the integration of optical interconnections, where light is used to transmit
information on-chip. Optical interconnections have limited heat dissipation compared to electri-
cal interconnections, which would reduce power consumption considerably. However, to develop
efficient optical interconnections, low-powered light sources with large bandwidths must be devel-
oped. Semiconductor microlasers are miniature laser devices whose dimensions are in the order of
a few micrometers, which offer the advantages of low power consumption, high bandwidth, and
immunity to electromagnetic interference.
Semiconductor lasers also offer great flexibility in their integration with microelectronic sys-
tems due to their compatibility and ease of fabrication on top of silicon (Si). Thus, hybrid optical-
electrical circuits can be realized, where both the advantages of the microlasers for optical intercon-
nections and the transistors’ data storage ability are combined into one device.
However, numerous challenges must be addressed before microlasers become a mainstream
technology in chip-level interconnections. While, in theory, they are more efficient and reliable than
electrical interconnections, in practice, work still needs to be done to achieve this performance and
optimize their structure. Progress towards fabricating microlasers has been hampered by the need
for a more systematic approach towards scaling down the optical mode as the cavity dimensions
shrink and the high threshold current required to achieve lasing action [3].
Semiconductor photonic crystal lasers have the most extensive flexibility in on-chip integra-
tion. They offer extremely low power consumption, making them excellent candidates for transceivers
in hybrid electro-optical circuits. Nonetheless, further analysis is required to understand the under-
lying principles behind the low efficiency that these lasers have exhibited so far.
1.2
Photonic Crystal Lasers
There are three main components to a laser. Firstly, a gain medium is required to amplify a signal
by stimulated emission. Secondly, a photonic cavity is required, which reflects light through the
gain medium several times. Lastly, the gain medium must be pumped sufficiently, either optically

1.2.3
Electrical injection
To increase the gain in the active region, the injection of carriers is required to achieve stimulated
emission. Electrical pumping of carriers can be done by using a p-i-n structure, in which the active
medium is placed in the intrinsic region. Carrier injection can be performed either in a lateral or
vertical configuration, where each pumping scheme offers its advantages [7].
Vertical injection of carriers in a PhC structure was first shown by Imada et. al. [10], in which doped
regions and contacts were placed below and above a PhC cavity to make a vertical p-i-n structure.
However, a large active region was employed to make up for inefficient confinement, which resulted
in a very high lasing threshold. A similar current injection scheme was applied by Hong et. al[11],
in which the carriers could be injected more efficiently using a supporting pillar beneath the cavity.
However, p-doping absorption at the pillar caused increased optical losses, limiting the Q factor
of the PhC cavity. Additionally, high-speed modulation of such vertically emitted lasers will be
limited due to the capacitive effects of the large surface area and slight separation between the
doped regions.
Alternatively, lateral injection to PhC lasers can be done by doping the membrane on both
cavity sides, constituting a lateral p-i-n junction. Figure 1.4 illustrates the lateral injection scheme,
which was first reported by Long et al. in 2010 [12].

1.3
Scope of work
In this thesis, we study PhC semiconductor microlasers to understand their underlying principles
of operation and to further push them into being realized in integrated photonic circuits. Although
their optical properties are well documented in the literature, the electrical properties of such devices
remain unclear, and their design for optimal performance is still under investigation. The device we
will investigate throughout the thesis is shown in Figure 1.5.
The laser is comprised of an active material that is placed between n- and p-doped regions. The
p-i-n structure provides a lateral injection scheme for the carriers, while the trapezoidal shape of the
doped regions guides the carriers into the active material. A PhC nanocavity confines optical fields
with photon energy within the PhC bandgap. The active region is buried inside an InP membrane,
increasing carriers’ confinement in vertical and lateral directions, forming a buried heterostructure
(BH). The band gap of InP is larger than that of the BH; therefore, both carriers and photons are
confined in a wavelength-scale cavity [16].
The device consists of an InP/SiO2/Si structure, which enables direct integration with the cur-
rent technology of CMOS circuits. The BH is an InGaAsP/InAlGaAs QW that emits at a wavelength
around 1550 nm and operates at a low threshold current of approximately 10 𝜇A[14].
Previous work by Dimopoulos et al. [14] has shown that the injection efficiency of these
lasers is relatively low, estimated at around 1-10%. This has been attributed to leakage currents that
flow outside the BH due to the lateral injection scheme. Furthermore, InP emissions from the p–i
interface were detected at 950 nm, which further stresses the adverse effects of the leakage current on
the device’s performance. Attempts at electrically simulating the microlaser have produced results

Theoretical Background
This chapter presents the fundamental physics behind a semiconductor laser diode’s operation and
control. To understand the principles behind a semiconductor laser’s operation, we introduce essen-
tial concepts from semiconductor physics, followed by analytical investigations of the semiconduc-
tor equations and the pn-junction. Subsequently, carrier recombinations and laser rate equations
are considered. Lastly, we define the injection and internal quantum efficiencies of lasers.
2.1
Semiconductor Physics
Atoms in semiconductor materials have sufficient strong interactions that cannot be considered in-
dependent atoms. The strong interactions cause the atoms to arrange themselves into a lattice, where
the valence electrons belong to the entire collection of atoms. Solving the Schr¨odinger equation for
the electron energies, in terms of the electron wave-number k, reveals a splitting of the atomic en-
ergy levels and forming energy bands [4]. Each band contains several finely spaced discrete energy
levels that can be approximated as a continuum for bulk materials.
Semiconductors are characterized by a valence band, 𝐸𝑣, and a conduction band, 𝐸𝑐, separated by
an energy gap 𝐸𝑔. The conduction band is empty for semiconductors, while the valence band is
completely occupied. Figure 2.1a shows an example of the energy bands of gallium arsenide (GaAs)
plotted in reduced k space.

The semiconductor equations are a set of partial differential equations that describe the transport
of electrons and holes in semiconductors. The semiconductor equations are based on the principles
of charge conservation and the transport mechanisms of drift and diffusion, often referred to as the
drift-diffusion equations. The three crucial unknowns we solve are the electron-hole concentrations
𝑛and 𝑝, and the electric potential Φ. These equations are highly coupled non-linear4 differential
equations, for which analytical solutions are rare, except for very simple cases. The drift-diffusion
equations are often solved numerically using computational methods such as finite-difference, finite-
element, or finite-volume techniques. At the same time, their non-linear nature requires additional
advanced iterative solvers to find accurate solutions.

2.3
PN Junction
When a p-type and an n-type semiconductor are connected, they form a pn junction. The n-type
has a surplus of electrons, and the p-type has an abundance of holes. Initially, when they come
into contact, the electrons (holes) will flow toward the p-region (n-region) due to diffusion. The two
carriers will meet and recombine as they move across the boundary. Between the n- and p-regions,
a region will form where all the free carriers have been depleted, hence the name depletion region7.

to overcome this barrier is called the built-in po-

If diffusion were the only mechanism
present, then the depletion region would con-

2.3.1
Shockley’s Diode Equation
In this section, we provide a proof for Shockley’s equation, which describes the current flowing
through a diode. Semiconductor lasers behave similarly to diodes, and as a result, we use this equa-
tion throughout this thesis to describe the laser’s output current.
For our carrier transport problem, Poisson’s and the carrier continuity equations (2.17, 2.30,
2.31) have to be solved to obtain the carrier concentrations and the potential across the device, which
in turn are used to calculate the carrier currents 𝐉𝐧,𝐩. Under certain assumptions, these equations
can be solved analytically.
In a pn junction, we have an electric field inside the depletion region, creating a drift current
that opposes the diffusion current. It is convenient to assume that the electric field outside the
depletion region is zero. This is called the depletion region approximation since there is always some
minor electric field inside the bulk region, giving rise to drift currents. However, this approximation
allows us to obtain a solution that is often very close to the real solution9. This approximation allows
us to ignore the electric field terms in the drift-diffusion equations (2.29), which greatly simplifies
the problem.

values even after recombining with the minority carriers (𝑛𝑛≈𝑁𝐷and 𝑝𝑝≈𝑁𝐴). The minority

Electrical and Optical Investigation of
LEDs and Lasers
In this chapter, we investigate the laser device through simulations. Initially, we model an LED de-
vice that does not include the effects of stimulated emission, and afterward, we present the complete
laser simulation. This allows us to understand better the differences between LED and laser devices
and the impact of stimulated emission to the device’s electrical properties.
Simulating semiconductor lasers is generally challenging because it requires the program to
solve both the drift-diffusion and rate equations simultaneously, which is computationally demand-
ing. However, the steady-state condition allows us to substantially simplify the equations needed to
be solved, which allows us to simulate the laser efficiently.
4.1
Simulation Software
For this project, we use Comsol to simulate the laser. It features a semiconductor module that solves
the drift-diffusion equations using the finite-volume discretization scheme on complex geometries.
Other software, like Lumerical Charge, was also considered, but stimulated emission could not be
accurately implemented and was thus discarded.
Comsol employs an iterative method to solve the drift-diffusion equations. The algorithm be-
hind the iterative solver depends on the simultaneous solution of Φ, 𝑛, and 𝑝. First, we make an
initial guess for the electrostatic potential Φ0. Then, the carrier continuity equations are solved,
which gives a new approximate solution for the electron and hole concentrations. The new solu-
tions are inserted into Poisson’s equation to obtain a new potential field solution, and then the steps
are repeated. The iterations will stop depending on a convergence criterion [30], which dictates the
accuracy of the approximate solution.
The finite volume discretization scheme takes the drift-diffusion equations, which have a dif-
ferential form and transforms them into integral equations. Then, they are solved over small-volume
elements that comprise the entire structure. This discretization scheme is generally more compu-
tationally heavy than other methods, but compared to the finite-elements method, it is more accu-
rate[30]. The integral form inherently conserves the current density across the volume elements,
eliminating artificial currents that may produce divergent solutions.
In Figure 4.1, we illustrate our laser device. For the simulations, the applied voltage is intro-
duced into the structure through the terminal surfaces from the p- and n-regions, as seen in the
figure. The n- and p-doped regions are doped with 𝑁𝐴= 3 ⋅1018cm−3 and 𝑁𝐷= 1018cm−3 and a
background doping of InP of 𝑁𝑏𝑔= 1015cm−3. Furthermore, surface recombinations exist at every
air hole, so we can simulate the effects of trapping centers located at the photonic crystal and see
how they affect the laser’s performance. We assume equal surface recombination velocities 𝑣𝑠= 104
cm/s [38] for holes and electrons. Direct, SRH, and Auger recombinations are considered in the en-
tire device. In Chapter C of the appendix, we provide additional information about the methodology
behind the design of this geometry in Comsol. The two materials we use for the p-i-n structure and
the BH are InP and InGaAsP, which are direct bandgap semiconductors. The material parameters
are taken from the literature [39] and are shown in Tables 4.1 and 4.2.

In thermodynamic equilibrium, the Fermi energy is constant throughout the p-i-n structure.
This condition forces the energy bands to bend, and junctions are formed. Between the n and p
regions, the conduction and valence bands are tilted, due to the electric field created by the bound
charged at the junctions. When we apply a small voltage, the electrostatic potential is reduced and
the drift current at the intrinsic region becomes less than the diffusion current. As a result, carriers
start diffusing across the device the barrier and get captured by the BH. As the voltage continues
to increase, the energy difference between the conduction (valence) bands for the n- and p-regions
becomes smaller, and when the voltage becomes greater than the built-in voltage, current flows
freely through the p-i-n structure.
Looking at the energy levels, the bandgap decreases significantly near the middle point due
to the presence of the InGaAsP, which has a smaller 𝐸𝑔than InP. The potential well created by this
arrangement collects the electrons and holes as they flow across the structure.

conduction band of the well. The probability of finding electrons in the BH is very high, and the

Returning to Figure 4.6a, we can now see that the injection efficiency is initially high because
SRH recombinations are much larger than in the rest of the structure. Additionally, since SRH is
the most dominant recombination in the BH at low voltages, the IQE is relatively low. Gradually,
the spontaneous emission rate and background recombinations2 increase as the voltage increases.
At 𝑉= 0.65V, spontaneous emission surpasses the SRH recombinations inside the BH, and the IQE
exhibits a steep rise. At around 0.8V, the background recombinations become increasingly impor-
tant, and also, some drift-diffusion current starts flowing across the diode, which according to the
definition of the injection efficiency (2.73), should increase the denominator and therefore reduce it
even further. At the same time, the Auger recombinations inside the BH start competing with the
spontaneous emissions, and the IQE further drops. The drift-diffusion current is the most dominant
effect at voltages higher than the turn-on voltage. At this point, carriers traverse the structure and
become minority carriers, which are then collected by the terminals. The background spontaneous
emission is also significant at this range, and, together with the drift-diffusion current, they are re-
sponsible for any further reduction to the injection efficiency. Intriguingly, since additional carriers
are not injected into the BH, the IQE begins to saturate. The IQE reaches a value of just below 50%,
meaning only half of the carriers in the BH are emitted radiatively, while the Auger effect limits the
other half.

4.3.2
Electrical and Optical Simulations
To get a deeper understanding of the effect of stimulated recombinations on the device, we compare
the output of the laser to that of the LED from the previous section. These simulations allow for
a holistic assessment of the device’s functionality and facilitate the identification of key factors
influencing the laser’s efficiency, reliability, and overall performance.
Initially, we compare the IV curves and the injection efficiencies of the LED and laser devices.
The resulting curves are shown in Figure 4.7a. It is evident that, in terms of their electrical char-
acteristics, there are no significant distinctions between the two devices. Both the current output
and the injection efficiency are very similar to each other. The current densities flowing through the
LED and the laser are much alike, even though the laser has an additional stimulated recombination
term. The LED uses the injected carriers for SRH, Auger, and direct recombinations. In contrast,
when the laser reaches threshold, all the rates are clamped, but stimulated emission continues to
increase. Above threshold, any additional electron-hole pairs are used to produce photons.
The IV curve between the LED and the laser is slightly different. This is explained by the
fact that the dominating type of recombination rate above threshold voltage differs between the
two devices. Therefore, the ideality factor differs when lasing action happens, corresponding to a
slight shift in the IV curve. For the LED, the Auger recombination rate has a different ideality factor
corresponding to it than stimulated recombinations, which results in a slight shift to the IV curve.
Most of times however, this level of precision is experimentally difficult to obtain, if one also takes
into account the defects in the device.
The injection efficiency of the two devices appears to be approximately equal to each other.
The stimulated recombination rate has minimal effect on the device’s electrical properties. This was
expected since experimentally we could not observe a noticeable difference in the IV curves between
the two devices.

In the previous chapter, we analyzed the optical and electrical properties of a laser and a LED sim-
ulation. Subsequently, we compared the results from two simulations for the XY and YZ planes.
We concluded from the efficiencies and current density plots that the leakage was more significant
in the XY plane than in the YZ. Mostly the electrons in both simulations followed trajectories that
led them outside of the active medium area, decreased the injection efficiency, and gave rise to the
spontaneous emission near the p-i interface.
The structure in the XY plane is more complex than the YZ cut. Additionally, the XY plane
features air holes that act as recombination centers for the carriers not included in the YZ case. By
looking at the layout of the chip of the actual laser device and comparing it to our XY simulation, we
can see that we only simulated a tiny part of the actual laser. Undoubtedly, it features and simulates
the essential regions of the laser. However, we can raise some questions about the accuracy of such
a model when we look at what happens to the carriers near the structure’s perimeter.
In addition to the air holes, we surround our device with trap-assisted surface recombina-
tion. This simulates the effect of trapping centers that may have been created due to the impurities
formed during fabrication. Then, when we simulate the XY plane, the carriers that hit the struc-
ture’s perimeter are forced to flow tangentially to it and eventually recombine after some time. In
reality, however, these carriers would follow a different trajectory away from the chip because the
laser cavity extends much further than we have modeled. Additionally, carriers from the nearby
bulk intrinsic region should also be able to be affected by the drift-diffusion forces of our p-i-n junc-
tion, which would lead to an additional current term which we do not take into account. For those
reasons, we decided to model our XY plane simulation as accurately to the layout of the laser as
possible.
Often when we simulate structures in software, we try to include only the necessary parts of
the structure that adequately represent the problem. This is done so that the computational time and
space complexity of the problem is reduced, as many simulations can be exceedingly demanding,
especially non-linear iterative problems like in our case. Due to the intuitiveness of our problem,
we would like to simulate a geometry that is as close to the actual laser as possible so that we can
visualize all the possible carrier trajectories without sacrificing the simulation speed of our model.
Our elaborate XY-plane geometry is depicted in Figure 4.1. Here, we consider the entire length
of the cavity and the great size of the terminals, which we call anode and cathode since that is where
the probes are roughly placed in reality to drive the laser. The trenches in the chip layout exist
to prevent cross-talk among nearby lasers[6], but we include them here too. The computational
cost of this intricate design is similar to our original design. This is achieved using an adaptive
meshing scheme, where the bulk regions far from the central structure become increasingly coarser.
We can confidently use this method because the extended regions are far from the p-i-n structure.
Hence, the drift-diffusion equations are much simpler to implement, and the current conservation
condition is easily applied. Consequently, our new model enables the carriers to travel around the
structure through more paths, painting a more realistic picture of the carrier flow without being
constrained by a limited computational area. Furthermore, by using symmetry considerations, the
total computational time is halved. This is described in Appendix C. In the new simulation, the

6.2
Injection Efficiency-Carrier Leakage Discrepancy
In Chapter 4, we found that for an LED and a laser, the injection efficiencies were almost identical.
However, looking at carrier leakage from a Fermi-Dirac statistics point of view, the LED appeared
to have significant carriers leaking out of the BH, compared to the laser, due to its Fermi energy
lying outside of the potential well. As a result, our two observations appear to be contradicting each
other. It is not clear why this is the case, but we believe that the issue lies in the definitions for 𝜂𝑖𝑛𝑗
and 𝜂𝑙.
On one hand, the injection efficiency, by its definition, considers the entire volume 𝑉of the
structure in its derivation and therefore it is suitably used in simulations of any dimension. On the
other hand, the carrier leakage is calculated along a line segment drawn through the middle partion
of the structure. Since the energy bands vary across the 𝑥−𝑦space, a single line segment cannot
capture all the leaked carriers as a whole. Furthermore, once carriers are injected into the BH, they
are subjected to diffusion because they are not injected evenly from all directions. This effect is not
considered in this calculation.
A deeper understanding of the connection between the carrier leakage and the steady state
condition is required to answer this problem. Defining the carrier leakage through a loss rate rather
than a constant 𝜂𝑙should be more beneficial. Carriers leaking out from the BH must have a mecha-
nism that supplies them, otherwise, a steady state would never be achieved.
The LED draws a slightly higher current than the laser. We believe the additional current
drawn corresponds to the supply mechanism for this leakage rate. However, more thorough and
analytical research is required into this matter before we can provide a clear explanation.

was discovered early in the project, that the gain in some simulations would exceed the threshold

6.4
Limitations and Future Research
The simulated structures presented in Chapters 4 and 5 combined different 2D slices to investigate
the carrier transport across the laser. Early on in our project, we tried creating a 3D simulation
for the laser. However, we could not manage to obtain make the simulations converge. To fully
describe the efficiency of the devices, we believe that a 3D simulation of the device would lead to a
complete description of the operation of the microlasers. Although a 3D simulation would be more
computationally demanding, symmetry considerations have succeeded in this project and would
lower the simulation time for a full-scale 3D simulation.
Another issue with the simulations is the effects of quantum well materials and how to in-
clude them in drift-diffusion simulations. In this project, all materials have been considered as bulk,
although the vertical dimension of the BH is small enough to consider quantization effects. Hence, a
73
==PAGE:80==
CHAPTER 6. DISCUSSION
more accurate description of the carrier dynamics would be available if quantum confinement were
considered. On the other hand, the size of the BH in the lateral dimensions exceeds several hundred
nanometers, and thus it can be considered bulk.[8]
A way to correct for quantization in these simulations would be to solve Schr¨odinger’s equation
along with Poisson’s equation simultaneously[30]. However, this has not been attempted in this
project but could be attempted in future investigations. Other programs than COMSOL may also be
explored, such as Lumerical CHARGE, which has previously been used to simulate carrier transport
in similar devices[18]. Here, a multi-quantum well module may be used to calculate band diagrams,
spontaneous emission factors, and gain for semiconductor lasers [50].
Throughout this project, we have considered only the case where the device operated in a
steady-state condition. This has allowed us to simplify the problem enough, such that simulating
a laser was feasible in COMSOL. Previous work [17] has shown that the performance of the laser
when modulated is substantially less than the theoretically predicted one, and further research is
required. Thus, a simulation model that can solve both the drift-diffusion and the rate equations in
the time domain simultaneously would be beneficial by shedding light on the AC properties of the
device.
Still, from the steady-state analysis, we can identify elements that may play an essential role in
the laser’s RF characteristics not considered so far. In Section 5.1.1, we observed that a larger air hole
radii led to a higher resistance across the device. Extending our argument to the AC analysis, the
impedance of the laser will be affected by the air holes. As a result, the losses of the laser will increase
due to impedance mismatch, and the modulation bandwidth will be limited due to RC effects.
Furthermore, combining drift-diffusion simulations with an electromagnetic solver would al-
low for the integration of estimations for the confinement factor Γ and the cavity Q-factor. Addi-
tionally, this could lead to investigations on the effects of spatial hole burning, as the optical profile
would be available from such simulations.
The gain model used for the laser rate equations was based on logarithmic models, in which
the modal gain 𝑔0, transparency carrier density 𝑁𝑡𝑟, and a linearity parameter 𝑁𝑠were defined. How-
ever, the gain model may not accurately depict the gain in the BH. Including 𝑁+𝑃in the gain model
should consider the different spatial distributions of the carriers. There could be situations where
the hole concentration is much lower than the electron concentration in the BH, in which the gain
should be calculated for the limiting carrier density of the two. This could be implemented by using
min(𝑁, 𝑃) in the gain model, which could provide a better description of the gain.
Although the parameters are realistic, defining the gain using the separation of the quasi-Fermi lev-
els described in equation 1.4 could be ideal. The maximum gain, 𝑔𝑚𝑎𝑥, would be available though
various physical constants, the photon energy, the transition matrix element, and the reduced den-
sity of state [7]. Furthermore, the Fermi-level separation would be available to extract in Comsol and
thus the gain could be implemented in a feedback loop as we have used in this thesis. Such efforts
were not made in this project, but it may prove useful in further research.

simulations to characterize their performance and to analyze their unique properties.
Experimentally, we have conducted measurements for turn-on voltages, series resistances,
lasing wavelengths, and lasing thresholds on various microlasers. Additionally, we observed that
devices with a higher p-offset shifted the turn-on to higher voltages with less resistance.
We have investigated the microlaser numerically by solving the drift-diffusion equations. Fur-
thermore, stimulated recombination was included in the simulations using a steady-state consider-
ation of the photon density and a logarithmic gain model for the BH. Lateral carrier flow around the
BH was found to be the most prominent leakage path in the simulations.
Detailed investigations of the laser’s design were carried out. It was found that the size of air
holes in a PhC lattice impacted the electrical properties of the microlasers, where surface recom-
bination effects were of great significance. Decreasing the width of the doping profiles caused a
reduction in the electrical performance due to surface recombinations. However, this was accompa-
nied by improved optical performance, leading to generally lower thresholds. Additionally, it was
found that reducing the number of trapping sites at the boundaries of the air holes increased the
overall injection efficiency. Thus, the passivation of the microlasers is paramount for optimal laser
performance. By varying the distance of the p-doped region from the BH, we observed a substantial
change in the injection and internal quantum efficiencies.
Bringing the p-doped region closer to the BH further assisted the less mobile holes in recombining
in the QW. As a result, this increased the recombination rates in the BH and the injection efficiency.
We discovered an optimal distance at 𝑑𝑝= 0.8𝑎𝑦where the threshold current was minimal. The in-
creased recombination rates caused a change in the appearance of the shape of the IV curves, which
was also observed in the experiments.
An increase in the BH width was linked with a decrease in the threshold current. However, inter-
sections with airholes led to a drastic decrease in the performance of the microlasers.
Our simulations can further assist in optimizing the performance of future microlasers in
terms of their electrical characteristics. Further work includes the creation of a 3D simulation of
the structure, investigating various gain models, and including a time-dependent physics model for
modulation studies, among others. More accurate numerical simulations of the devices may lead
to microlasers with ultra-low threshold currents leading to reduced power consumption and larger
bandwidths.

Boundary Conditions
The solver’s convergence heavily depends on the choice of boundary conditions in the model. The
most important boundary conditions for our model are the following three:
1. Continuity/Heterocontinuity,
2. Trap-assisted surface recombination,
3. Insulation.
In Comsol adjacent regions automatically inherit the continuity (or hetero-continuity) bound-
ary condition. This enforces the continuous flow of the normal current ̂𝐧⋅(𝐉𝟐−𝐉𝟏) = 0 and displace-
ment field ̂𝐧⋅(𝐃𝟐−𝐃𝟏) = 0 across the boundary, as dictated by Maxwell’s equations for no surface
