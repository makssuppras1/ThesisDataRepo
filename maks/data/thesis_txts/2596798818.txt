Abstract
Water scarcity has become one of the most severe challenges in China and all over the world.
Beijing, as one of the most water scarcity city in the world, has a high requirement of water-
saving technologies. A decentralized water system, as a potential method of both water-saving and
environmentally friendly, could be potentially used in Beijing. Together with Grundfos, this thesis
introduces a new concept water system, ”Microgrids”, to build a decentralized urban water system
for the small-scale community in order to save water and improve the environmental performance
of the existing centralized system.
In this thesis, a Microgrids water system is built based on a concept community located in Beijing.
A Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is made to the centralized system and the Microgrids system to
compare the environmental impacts. The eco-efﬁciency of the two systems is evaluated, and the
sensitivity and uncertainty of the LCA study are discussed.
The Microgrids water system shows better environmental performance than the centralized system.
Microgrids decreases water resource depletion, have fewer impacts on eutrophication, ecosystem
toxicity, and human toxicity. The climate change impact is also reduced in the Microgrids water
system. Water recycling in the Microgrids system helps reduce the total water use of the whole
system, saving more than 80% of water. Clean energy used in the Microgrids system shows the
ability to the improvement of environmental performance. Hydropower energy supply has the
highest eco-efﬁciency, making it the most suggested energy type for the Microgrids system.
The results of the WWTP treatment process are considered reasonable in comparison with relevant
studies, while the MBR treatment has lower results than other research due to excluding facility
constructions in the system boundary. Uncertainties also remain for environmental savings from
the Microgrids, as the general centralized treating process was used for on-site treatment, the per-
formance is overestimated.

Introduction
Water is an essential element for human life and welfare, but with global population growth and
lifestyle changes, limited freshwater resources are now under enormous pressure for the world’s
increasing water demand (Hanjra and Qureshi, 2010; Jury and Vaux Jr, 2007). According to Hanjra
and Qureshi (2010), the current global water demand is three times that of the 1950s, but the fresh
water supply is gradually declining. With only 1/40 of the world’s average renewable freshwater
resources per capita, Beijing, the capital city of China, has long been facing the severe problems
of water scarcity (Ritchie and Roser, 2020). The concerns about sufﬁcient and reliable urban water
supply are regarded as one of the largest challenges for future development.
Currently, the global water supply market is dominated by the centralized water and sanitation
system since its appearance in the 19th century. It’s usually processed by large Waterworks before
distribution to users, and then collected and treated to the Wastewater Treatment Plant (WWTP)
before reclamation and discharged to the environment. Though considered as an efﬁcient end-of-
pipe method due to its uniform hygiene standard, a centralized system is also challenged for its
limited capacity of water and energy saving due to long-distance distribution and inefﬁciency of
reclamation (Dom`enech, 2011).
As an alternative solution, decentralized systems were designed with on-site drinking treatment or
wastewater treatment for reclamation near its supplying location. According to Peter-Varbanets
et al., technologies could be adopted for a single treatment of potable use or overall treatment for
entering a house, or for small-scale communities. Generally, membrane-based technologies, in-
cluding Microﬁltration (MF), Ultraﬁltration (UF), and Reverse Osmosis(RO), were mostly adopted
for small-scale communities.
Among the alternative technologies for wastewater treatment, the membrane-based method to re-
move nutrients and wastes in wastewater starts to be widely used (Stephenson et al., 2000; Vis-
vanathan et al., 2000). The membrane is often used to separate treated water with sludge to be a
replacement of the second sedimentation tank (Judd, 2010). Compared with the second sedimenta-
tion tank, the membrane bioreactor is usually much smaller (Melin et al., 2006), making it possible

to be used as an on-site wastewater treatment method for local prospective.
On-site drinking water treatment technology is also proved to be available for the small scale com-
munity, especially for grey-water treatment and reclaimed water (Friedler et al., 2005). The drink-
ing water treatment plant is usually small, making it possible to be built near or downstairs of a
community, if water resource is available (Ippolito et al., 2011; Friedler et al., 2005).
With the help of on-site water supply, treatment, and recycle, energy and chemical inputs in a de-
centralized system are always thought to be reduced (Lens et al., 2001). It is also proved that the
majority of water in the decentralized system could be recycled, and the system is believed to be
more efﬁcient than the centralized system in water utilization (Lens et al., 2001). Furthermore, a
decentralized system could be more available to use decentralized energy supply instead of cen-
tralized energy (Nair et al., 2014). It gives the possibility that clean energy can be more introduced
in a decentralized water system, as the decentralized energy supply, for example, wind and solar
energy, is usually cleaner than the centralized energy supply in China(Yuan et al., 2014).
Though certain beneﬁts can be generated from decentralized systems, it is still uncertain of the
speciﬁc gains and losses over the different designs of water systems, especially considering its
whole life cycle (Peter-Varbanets et al., 2009).
Interested in the environmental performances and future development of the decentralized water
system for small communities, the water section of Grundfos company has proposed the concept of
‘Microgrids’, which represents the decentralized urban water system for small-scale communities.
Many questions about the Microgrid community are remained to answer, including how we can
design a Microgrid water system to have the least environmental impact, what the cost is, and what
energy types shall we choose.
The objectives of this study can be summarized as:
• Make Life Cycle Assessment to a conventional centralized system and different designs of Mi-
crogrids, to understand the environmental performance of the water systems
• Understand the most contributed substances and process in the centralized system and Microgrid
system, to identify the Hotpoint of the systems

type should be used in the Microgrid.
• Evaluate the sensitivity uncertainty of Life Cycle Assessment

further studies should be done.

Case Study of Beijing
2.1
Water Scarcity and Water Resources
Located in the northern part of China, Beijing is the capital city with more than 21 million per-
manent residents (Beijing Municipal Bureau Statistics, 2019). Despite the rapid development and
urbanization, concerns about insufﬁcient freshwater resources of Beijing are increasing. The av-
erage annual precipitation of Beijing is around 585 mm, which is 59% of the world’s average, the
water resources per capita of Beijing is only 1/10 of the Chinese national value, and 1/40 of the
world’s average (Ritchie and Roser, 2020).
According to Beijing Water Resources Bulletin from 2002-2018 (detailed in A.4), Beijing originally
depended its water supply solely on ground and surface water before 2004. As lacking sufﬁcient
water resources, the dependence has lead to water depletion for years (Jiang, 2009). When con-
sidering reducing the pressure of the local water supply, wastewater treatment technologies were
imported for reclamation in 2004, and inter-basin transported water was introduced in 2007. After
the Middle Route of South-to-North Water Diversion Project was completed in 2014, Beijing de-
pended on more than half of its supply on reclaimed and transported water. As shown in Figure
1, in 2018, 41% of Beijing’s water was abstracted from groundwater, 31% from surface and inter-
provincial transported water, and the remaining 27% was reclaimed from the wastewater treatment.
Figure 1: Resources for Water supply in 2018. Though still mostly dependent on groundwater,
reclaimed and transported water now supplied more than half of Beijing’s water demand. The
overall trend from 2002-2018 was shown in Appendix A.4

Water Treatment Plants (WTPs)
There are 21 WTPs in Beijing. Among them, 12 are supplying the urban area and the rest for the
suburbs. The primary water resources of the urban area are surface water from Miyun Reservoir,
Huairou Reservoir, South-to-North Water Transfer, and groundwater from the emergency water
sources of Niulan Mountain, Pinggu, Huairou, Shunyi, and also from the western suburbs (Beijing
Water Supply Group Co., 2005).

Drinking water treatment plant
The conventional drinking water treatment process is presented in Figure 5. Five main processes
are outlined in the ﬁgure, which is: Coagulation, Sedimentation, Filtration, Adsorption, and Chlo-
rination. The processes are the common drinking water treatment process in Beijing (Can et al.,
2013).
The coagulation process used Aluminum Chloride and Ferric Trichloride to coagulate the sub-
stances in water abstracted from the environment. Then, the Sedimentation process sediment the
coagulators, and water is further ﬁltrated through Filtration. The adsorption process uses active
carbon to adsorb tiny materials and removable ion. After disinfected in the Chlorination process,
the water is distributed to the users.
In the Microgrid community, the drinking water treatment uses the same processes, however, it is
changed to on-site treatment. It means that the water abstraction is ﬁnished locally, which poten-
tially decreases the loss of water during transportation. Similarly, the distribution distance is also
reduced, meaning less water loss during the distribution.

4
Methodology
This study used Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) to evaluate environmental impact and used economic
evaluation tools to analyze the economic performances of a centralized and Microgrid water system.
The LCA is used to evaluate the environmental impacts of a product or a system for its whole
life cycle. The LCA is a quantitative method standardized by the International Organization for
Standardization (ISO). ISO 14040 and ISO 14044 have clearly deﬁned the process of an LCA,
which includes four main steps: Goal and Scope Deﬁnition, Life Cycle Inventory Analysis (LCI),
Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA), and Results Analysis (ISO, 2006b,a). LCA was known as
a holistic environmental assessment tool that appeared in the 1960s and further developed in the
1980s and 1990s. As a quantitative assessment, LCA provided with an evaluation of a life-cycle
perspective and covered a wide range of environmental impacts (Hauschild et al., 2018).
The results were conducted in Easetech 3.3.4, an assessment tool for LCA-models developed at
DTU (DTU, 2020). EASETECH a software that is used for LCA analysis, mainly on waste man-
agement (DTU, 2020). Other purposes, like water treatment, had also been introduced. This study
used Easetech to do the LCA on the urban water system. The reference ﬂow of the LCA study
was deﬁned as different material fractions in the main ﬂow, the water, in the system. Different
scenarios were built in EASETECH, to evaluate the difference between a centralized water system
and a Microgrids water system.

suitable for a case in China. The method gave characterize results and normalized results.
Characteristic results were used to assess the impact in different impact categories. Impacts of

6.1.5
Results Analysis
In general, the environmental impacts in all scenarios are mainly affected by electricity production,
centralized drinking treatment and water emissions.
From S1 Central to the Microgrids S2 and S3, the total electricity production of the water system
is reduced from 2372.6 kWh/d to 333.6 kWh/d and 215 kWh/d, respectively. Part of the reduction
is due to less amount of water for abstraction and distribution caused by reclamation, and the other
part is due to lower energy intensities of on-site water treatment and distribution. As the dominant
contribution to climate change, fossil depletion, terrestrial acidiﬁcation and ecotoxicity, human
toxicity, photochemical oxidant and particulate matter formation, the avoided of 86% of electricity
production is one of the main reasons that the S2 have achieved more than 80% saving of the
categories. Compared with S2, the impact of S3 is further optimized due to the power savings of
on-site treatment.
As for the centralized drinking treatment process, the impact of the process is proportional to its
inﬂuent. Since the freshwater abstraction has been reduced by 87% and 88% respectively in S2
and S, the amount of water inﬂow is reduced by the same percentage. Hence the savings from
Microgrids in the corresponding categories are predictable by the decrease in water abstraction.
While reductions due to water emissions are affected by the fate of different substances. Accord-
ing to the characterized results per substance view (detailed in the supplemented document, LCIA
per substance view.xlsx), Table 15 excerpts the three substances that have the highest impact on
eutrophication and ecotoxicity in S1. It can be seen that the discharge of phosphate and nitrate to
the water compartment are the main components that cause freshwater and marine eutrophication,
heavy metal components, such as Mn, Ag, Ni, Cu, Zn are the leading causes of freshwater and ma-
rine ecotoxicity and human toxicity. These components mainly exist in the wastewater or treated
wastewater, this is why emission to water has such a signiﬁcant contribution in these categories. It
is worth noting that the terrestrial ecotoxicity is mainly caused by the phosphorus during electric-
ity production and drinking treatment, so the wastewater emission does not show any signiﬁcant
impact on this Category. Compared to S1 Central, the Microgrids S2 and S3 have avoided 5%
of the sewage leakage and 87% of treated efﬂuent emissions due to wastewater reclamation and

Discussion
9.1
Microgrids vs Centralized System
The ﬁnal results indicate that the Microgrids with on-site treatment is more preferable than the
conventional centralized water system. If only on-site wastewater treatment (S2) is included, the
system can save 87% freshwater abstraction for the daily supplying of 1691.2 m3 water to the
community. While power consumption can be reduced by 83%. In terms of environmental savings,
the on-site reclamation reduces around 3 tons CO2-eq emissions for daily water supply, and avoids
more than 90% of impacts over eutrophication and toxicity. If the centralized drinking supply
is further changed to on-site treatment (S3), the system can save 1% more of water, 4% more
of electricity, and the GHG emissions for climate change can further be reduced by 0.13 tons of
CO2-eq.
As discussed in Section 6.1, the most contributed processes in the scenarios for climate change are
wastewater treatment, drinking treatment, and emissions from the wastewater treatment efﬂuent.
While the sludge handling process does show increasing importance in the Microgrids system.
As for the main contributors to the impact categories, it was found that the electricity generation
processes, centralized drinking treatment, and efﬂuent discharged to the water compartment are
the three main factors of the ﬁnal results. Discharged efﬂuent is the dominant contributor in ma-
rine, and freshwater eutrophication and ecotoxicity and has a 40% contribution to human toxicity.
At the same time, the rest categories are dominated by electricity and drinking treatment. Three
contributors are directly related to the water inﬂow. More water means proportional increases in
environmental impacts. However, it is worth noting that the water ﬂow of the drinking treatment is
correspondingly reduced due to the circulation of Microgrids. However, the amount of water enter-
ing the wastewater treatment remains the same in all scenarios. This is because the reclaimed water
and drinking water are mixed in indoor use and ﬂush in a ﬁxed amount of wastewater. Therefore,
the water saved by Microgrids proportionally reduces the environmental impact during drinking
treatment, while in wastewater treatment, the reduction in impact is due to the lower energy inten-
sity for distribution, sewerage collection, and treatment of on-site reclamation. In addition, as the
67 / 112
==PAGE:73==
Microgrids
1376 m3 of wastewater emission in S1 Central is reclaimed for non-potable uses in the Microgrids,
plus the higher water quality after MBR treatment (higher removal rate for solids, nitrogen, and
phosphorus), the environmental impact of emissions is also largely reduced.
In contrast, although Microgrids have added the cost of sludge transportation as lacking sludge
handle capability, it only has an important contribution to the ozone depletion and ionising radiation
in S2 and S3. As a relatively low impact score for these two categories, the sludge transportation
does not signiﬁcantly increase the impact of Microgrids. While compared between S2 and S3, the
impacts are further reduced in S3 due to water and energy savings from on-site drinking treatment.
9.2
Microgrids - Reclamation rate
In Microgrids, the reclamation rate of wastewater is the determining factor for the freshwater de-
mand of a water system. Lower reclamation indicates for more freshwater abstraction and treat-
ment. As discussed before, the increase of freshwater into the system will increase the environmen-
tal impact of drinking treatment. So when the reclamation rate in S2 and S3 is reduced from 99.8%
to 70%, the freshwater abstraction of the two scenarios are both increased by 2.4 times. The relative
change of impacts is increased among the range of 0.5-2.6. From the perspective of comparison
between S2 and S3, S3 is still more effective, as S3 has less increase of impacts.
While the relative increase for climate change is around 120%, which is lower than the increase
in water abstraction, as the reclamation rate mostly affects the impact of drinking treatment and a
small fraction from sewerage collection, most of the wastewater treatment process remains unin-
ﬂuenced. Hence the performance of the drinking treatment process is considered as the main factor
for evaluation if the reclamation rate is to be changed.
9.3
Microgrids - Sustainable Energy
After switching to clean energy, the impact of Microgrids has been further reduced. The climate
change saving can reach 20% in S2, while in S3, it can be reduced by 35%. The savings of the
three energy sources are very close. Hydropower performs relatively better than others, while solar
power has slightly higher GHG emissions.
68 / 112
==PAGE:74==
Microgrids
However, if other categories are to be considered, solar energy may not be comparable to hydro,
and wind energy as solar panel production increases the impacts of metal depletion, marine, and
terrestrial ecotoxicity. Furthermore, solar energy in Easetech external process is installed in the
open ground, which requires the installation of solar panels and the occupation of land. Neverthe-
less, if the solar equipment for small-scale water pumps can reduce such material consumption,
then the impacts can be avoided.
It has been shown that hydropower costs the least among all types of energies, including hard
coal. At the same time, hydro energy also decreases the environmental impact, making it the most
eco-efﬁcient choice of energy type for Microgrids. The wind energy is acceptable. Although ad-
ditional money is needed to produce wind power, big improvement of environmental performance
makes good eco-efﬁcient scores. However, solar energy shows low eco-efﬁciency with one of
ten wind power. Furthermore, considering the lower total energy costs and better environmental
performances, S3 is the better choice.
It would be easy to suggest that using hydropower as clean energy is the best choice of clean energy
type. However, the fact in Beijing is that the hydropower would be hard to get as hydropower plants
are usually far away. Thus, it can be concluded that wind power would be the best choice for the
community in Beijing, considering both power plant availability and eco-efﬁcient results.
The limitation of the eco-efﬁcient assessment is that, in reality, the energy type is a mix of different
types of energy. This study only considers one type of clean energy and one type of fossil fuel in
each scenario, while it does not include the fact of mixed-use. Fossil fuel, solar power, wind power,
and hydropower may be used together in a Microgrids water system, considering the stability of
power supply and availability of equipment. In this case, the maximum power output from solar
energy or wind energy could be limited, possibly causing different results.
The other limitation is that the eco-efﬁciency assessment does not consider the cost of on-site
treatment equipment. The change from centralized treatment to on-site treatment can increase the
total cost as a centralized system is with higher process-efﬁcient. More studies should be done to
include the eco-efﬁciency of changing equipment, to further understand the costs and beneﬁts of
the Microgrids water system.

9.4
Assumptions and Uncertainties
Along with the modeling in this study, assumptions and compromises have been made due to a
lack of sufﬁcient information and data. The overall simulation of the treating processes is based on
relevant LCA researches for centralized wastewater treatment and MBR treatment (Yoshida et al.,
2014; Cashman et al., 2018).
Parameter Uncertainties
According to the sensitivity check in Section 8, parameters of the
drinking treatment process for all scenarios, sludge transportation distance for the Microgrids sys-
tem, and transfer coefﬁcient for nitrogen and phosphorus as well as ferric chloride used for phos-
phorus removal during centralized wastewater treatment are identiﬁed as essential parameters that
are supposed to have essential inﬂuences over the ﬁnal results. Since the uncertainties of soda ash
for ﬂue gas treatment is effected by the sludge handling assumption, the analysis is included in the
modeling limitations.
The drinking treatment process is simulated by the well-established process in Easetech, includ-
ing energy and chemical consumption, as well as air emissions. The documented treat processes
are also an inconsistency with the situation of extensive waterworks in Beijing. Although the en-
ergy and material production it contains in the process may be different from Beijing as a general
drinking treatment process from surface water for Europe, the process is still considered as a good
simulation for centralized treatment. Uncertainties are mostly considered to be in the simulation
of S3, as the centralized drinking treatment is also used for on-site drinking treatment. As changes
only been made for water losses and energy intensity during distribution in S3, the compared result
between S2 and S3 is restricted for an explanation of environmental savings from energy and water-
saving by short distribution. Technological savings remains unknown. However, as expectations
that the centralized treatment process includes more chemical and energy consumption compared
to membrane-based technologies, the impact of S3 may have more signiﬁcant savings than S3
compared to the current results.
It’s also worth noting that the drinking treatment does not consider the inﬂuence of the composition
of the surface water, but considering the little impact from surface water emission, it is believed to
70 / 112
==PAGE:76==
Microgrids
cause little changes in the ﬁnal results.
As to check the reliability of the transfer coefﬁcient for N and P, the data was compared with the
average value estimated for WWTP in Beijing according to Pan et al. (2011), the assumed transfer
coefﬁcient is 8% higher for N, and 3% higher for P. As its high sensitivity to marine eutrophication,
the impact of the centralized system may be overestimated. In addition, the impact of emission to
water in the centralized system may be overestimated due to the simplistic assumption that the
treated water was directly discharged into the water body. While around 30% of Beijing’s water is
reclaimed for landscaping and irrigation (Zhu and Dou, 2018), its impact to marine eutrophication
is greatly overestimated due to the 90% of contribution by emissions to water compartment.
The transmission distance of sludge is an empirical assumption. Although the distance, as well as
ferric chloride, have high sensitivity in ozone depletion, as the impact score is rather small, ozone
depletion is not further discussed in the study.
Additionally, the Emission Factor (EF) for N2O during the wastewater treatment process is assumed
to be 0.48% of the nitrogen content based on Yoshida et al. (2014) for the speciﬁc WWTP in
Denmark. While according to the IPCC Guidelines for National Greenhouse Gas Inventories, the
EF for N2O from centralized aerobic treatment plants can be variable in the range of 0.016%-4.5%,
the average level is estimated to be 1.6% (Eggleston et al., 2006). Hence the greenhouse gas may
be under- or over-estimated. However, the contribution of air emission to climate change is not
signiﬁcant. Its insensitivity to the ﬁnal results shown in Section 8, the variance does not cause
many differences.

Except for the sensitivity and uncertainty detected for the parameters,
limitations also exist due to assumptions made for the selection of technologies and system bound-
aries.
Technical limitations: This section mainly discusses the technical limitations of Wastewater treat-
ment and sludge handling as their important contributions to the ﬁnal results.
Assumptions and data from literature research establish the process of the WWTP and MBR treat-
ment, so there may be errors for the technical simulation. As to check the results, the CO2-eq

emission of the WWTP system and MBR treatment are compared with relevant researches. In
the centralized system, around 32% of CO2-eq emissions come from the wastewater treatment
processes. According to Pan et al. (2011), a WWTP system with similar treating technology in
Guangzhou, China was estimated to emit 7.3 kg CO2-eq for removing of 1 kg of BOD in the
wastewater. Estimated in the same way for nitrogen and phosphorus concentration of WWTP in
this study, the BOD was estimated to be 150 mg/l in inﬂuent and 10mg/l in efﬂuent based on the
average value of Beijing’s WWTP (Sun et al., 2016). Hence around 1.73 tons of CO2-eq emission
is expected for the centralized wastewater treatment, while the actual result is 1.21 tons. The results
are under the same order of magnitude, but slightly smaller. The difference is partly due to various
processing methods of sludge in the two systems. The sludge in Pan et al. (2011) was used for
landﬁll, while in this study, it was assumed for incineration. Therefore, the result is considered as
reasonable for the centralized WWTP systems.
While for the aerobic MBR treatment process, the results of Cashman et al. (2018) have shown
that the CO2-eq emissions of the process is around 0.35 kg CO2-eq/m3 of treated wastewater if
supplying a community with 10,000 inhabitants. In this study, the MBR treatment has 0.05 kg
CO2-eq/m3 of treated wastewater for the Microgrids systems. The emission is only 1/7 of the study
of Cashman et al. (2018). By comparing the inventory, the results from Cashman et al. (2018) have
included the construction of facilities. Therefore the results show a larger impact.
However, the results of MBR treatment may be underestimated by the technical simulation in this
study. The EF of CO2 and N2O from C and N were excluded in this study as assumptions that
MBR treatment has no air emissions during treatment, while about 75% of nitrogen remains in
the residues, part of the content should be account for N2O emissions. Therefore, the impact of
MBR is underestimated. Its environmental savings over a centralized system may be smaller than
the ﬁnal results. In addition, as to simplify for modeling, it was assumed that there were no water
losses during the on-site treatment for the Microgrids system, which does not entirely conform to
reality. During the processes of distribution, treatment, and sewerage collection, there are losses
that will increase the environmental impact. Therefore, this assumption also underestimates the
environmental impact of Microgrids.

reclaimed for washing purpose in domestic use, the actual water quality after MBR treatment has
risks for non-qualiﬁed recycle. For better simulation, the real-time and long-term data of the water

As for sludge treatment, though the general process for anaerobic digestion, the dewatering pro-

CO2-eq/m3 in the centralized WWTP. While the advantages are restricted by the community scale
as well as the distribution pipes used in Microgrids, which can offset some of the savings.

Recommendations
Based on the LCA results, there are several recommendations that can be suggested.
• The Microgrids with both on-site drinking treatment and wastewater treatment are the most rec-
ommendable water systems among the 3 scenarios in this study. The system was supposed to save
88% of freshwater abstraction and 87% of electricity generation for the function of the system.
Though being overestimated for the environmental savings of the Microgrids, it is still recom-
mended that the substitution of centralized treatment is more sustainable in terms of energy and
material ﬂow.
• Increasing of reclamation rate in the Microgrids can avoid environmental impacts from the drink-
ing treatment processes, while the wastewater reclamation remains uninﬂuenced.
• Changing into the hydro, wind, or solar power can further reduce the impacts in Microgrids. The
application is more recommendable for hydropower as it is the most eco-efﬁciency energy. While
if lacking conditions for hydropower, wind energy is also recommended for substitution.
As recommendations for using the results of this study, applications should operate under restricted
conditions:
• The evaluation is mainly focused on the power generation and material ﬂow of the water system.
Hence the environmental performances are in terms of energy and material ﬂow, while conclu-
sions including any facility construction stages of the water system can not be drawn from this
study. Further researches shall be needed for more information.
• The on-site treatment in this study is aerobic MBR treatment, and the centralized wastewater
treatment is the general activated sludge process. As restricted by the speciﬁc technology, the
results shall be used carefully with variance technologies.

Conclusions
This study has used the LCA model as a decision support tool to evaluate the environmental per-
formances of two different designs of the Microgrids systems as alternatives to the conventional
centralized system when applying to the concept community of 10,000 inhabitants. One of the Mi-
crogrids was designed with on-site wastewater treatment for substitution of the centralized WWTP,
and another is designed with both on-site drinking and wastewater treatment. In addition to the
evaluation of substitution of the centralized treatment, a comparison of lower reclamation rate and
application of various clean energy in the Microgrids systems are carried out to investigate the
performances further. Moreover, the eco-efﬁciency evaluation of energy types in the Microgrids is
conducted based on the LCA results and the corresponding economic costs.
Overall, the results have indicated that the Microgrids are more sustainable compared with the
centralized system due to the superior capacity for environmental savings. As the environmental
impact ranges from Centralized system > Microgrids with on-site wastewater treatment > Mi-
crogrids with both on-site drinking and wastewater treatment, conclusions can be drawn that each
substitution of the centralized treatment can reduce part of the environmental impacts. The avoided
impacts are found to be caused by the reduction of power generation, freshwater abstraction, as
well as efﬂuent emission reduced due to reclamation. At the same time, the impact of greenhouse
gas emissions during the treatment process is not as signiﬁcant as the three factors. It has also been
identiﬁed that as the decreasing impact of on-site sanitation systems, the sludge treatment process
in Microgrids shows increasing contributions to Microgrids’ impacts.
In consistency with the above conclusion, the decreasing of reclamation rate indicates for the fresh-
water abstraction. The corresponding environmental impacts are also increased within the Micro-
grids system. It is also found that the results of the wastewater treatment process under the change
of reclamation rate remain uninﬂuenced. Hence attention can be mainly paid to the performances
of drinking treatment when considering the change of reclamation rate.
The hydropower has been proved to be the most eco-efﬁcient energy type for the Microgrids sys-
tem. It decreases the total cost of energy production and also decreases environmental impact. If
76 / 112
==PAGE:82==
Microgrids
hydropower is not available, wind power is also suggested. Solar power is the least eco-efﬁcient
choice.
However, limitations also exist from the selection of water systems and system boundaries. The
LCA results of MBR treatment are considered to be underestimated, and the results of centralized
WWTP performances may be overestimated since the construction stage of facilities is excluded.
The performances of the on-site drinking treatment are also overestimated as the use of a centralized
drinking treatment external process. At the same time, the advantages of the potential savings of
centralized treatment need further consideration. As focusing on the water and energy uses in the
water system, the results of this study are more qualiﬁed for the evaluation of energy and water
nexus impacts.
