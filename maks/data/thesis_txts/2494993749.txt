1 Abstract 
 
Rural areas of low-to-middle income countries are being increasingly exposed to the negative 
environmental consequences that the inadequate management of disposable diapers generates. 
This is due to the often flawed or entirely absent intervention of local governments to cope with 
the burden of solid waste management (SWM). Soiled disposable diapers are a complex waste to 
handle; although constituted by nearly 87% of organic matter, its high moisture content and the 
mixed composition of the remaining mass hampers straightforward valorization and recycling. In 
settings where SWM falls short, solid waste is generally burnt, buried or open dumped. These are 
all practices that inflict great stress on natural resources. On top of the deriving environmental 
implications, unsafely disposed diapers are also considered an indirect form of open defection and, 
as such, are responsible for the rapid spread of diseases affecting the indexes of diarrhea and infant 
malnutrition, already critical in many developing countries. Scientific research about management 
of disposable diapers in low-to-middle income countries is however scarce. Gathering information 
and quantitative data is of paramount importance in order to potentially develop management 
improvement plans. The present study aims at quantifying generation rates and identifying 
contamination paths for the “consume to disposal” fate of disposal diaper in rural areas of San 
Miguel Ixtahuacán, Guatemala. By means of one-to-one interviews and focus groups carried out 
in the communities of Maquivil and Chilive, the level of awareness about the investigated topic, 
current practices along with cultural and social factors influencing diapering habits is evaluated. 
This data is combined with results obtained from two waste assessments - at domiciliary level and 
primary collection point - to be able to delineate the mass flow diagram from use-to-grave of the 
weekly generation of disposable diapers. Given a total generation of 1003 kg per week, nearly 
67% is handled directly at home. Specifically, 27% of it is disposed of in the latrine, 22% is burnt 
while 18% is disposed of underground. Besides an acknowledged 7% of disposable diapers 
voluntarily discarded in ravines or other open spaces, the remaining share – thrown out in public 
trash bins or at the market waste collection point - ought to be considered open-dumped as well 
for it results exposed to animal carriers (e.g. street dogs and flies) and, once collected, will 
ultimately reach the official disposal site, being this an uncontrolled open area. An additional 
contamination source was identified in 24% of the sampled families, due to the poorly protected 
containers used to temporarily store soiled diapers prior to its final disposal. Variations in 
generation rates were found in line with the family’s income level while parameters such as 
education, the baby’s age and the day of the week proved no significant influence, once evaluated 
by means of statistical tests. Taking into account the high levels of exposure identified within the 
families’ homes as well as in public areas, recommendations for improvement are given for either 
scenario. Developing an informative campaign prior to the implementation of any technical 
solution is strongly suggested. Based on draft proposals developed at residential, community and 
municipal level presented during a closing meeting organized with the involved stakeholders, a 
unanimous consensus was reached on the most feasible technical solution. Small scale, rapidly 
implementable, low-cost solutions requiring limited organization and know-how were prioritized.

2 Introduction 
 
Solid Waste Management (SWM) is an essential element for a society to maintain sanitation. A 
deficient SWM poses a threat to local population and the surrounding environment (UN 2016). 
This is especially pronounced in low- and middle- income settings, where either waste collection 
systems are absent or management plans present major flaws (Kaza et al. 2018; Sindhuja et al. 
2018). A specific fraction of household waste that generates particular challenges is represented 
by sanitary products. According to a critical literature review conducted by Heather et. al in 2015, 
disposable diapers can make up between 1.62% and 14% of the general waste generated by a 
household in low- to middle- income countries. Geography, income, as well as local practices, are 
only a few of the factors that impact the quantity of diapers disposed of in a household’s solid 
waste. In the last decades, disposable diapers have started replacing traditional diapering methods 
in developing countries where the slowly increasing purchasing power of people has given them 
access to the market of cheap nappies (Gul et al. 2017). According to Espinosa-Valdemar et al. 
(2014), in 1997 used diapers were the 6% of urban waste generated in Mexico, while in 2014, the 
amount had increased up to 15% in some areas. 
World’s population is growing exponentially; according to UN’s Revision of World Population 
Prospects, total population was recorded at 7.6 billion people in 2017 and is expected to reach 8.6 
billion in 2030 (Kaza et al. 2018). Around the world, more than 134 million babies are born each 
year, at a rate of 255 births per minute or 4.3 births each second (Thaman and Eichenfield 2014). 
It is a common estimation that a baby needs between 4000 – 6000 diaper changes during the 
pre-potty phase, being this until 30 months of age (Philip 2017). These numbers combined are the 
cause of great amount of pollutants being released into the atmosphere, water and soil, causing a 
huge stress on natural resources. When managed properly, used disposable diapers are responsible 
for a great volume take up in landfills. Their mixed composition hinders straightforward 
valorization and recycling, while their high moisture content makes them unsuitable for 
incineration (Espinosa-Valdemar et al. 2014). In settings where disposal management or treatment 
systems fall short, environmental impacts can only increase and more severe threats to human 
health are generated. Soiled diapers either find their way, through collection trucks, to an 
uncontrolled landfill, which in most cases is simply an open dumping area, or are managed by the 
families themselves (Remigios 2014). The lack of awareness and information especially severe in 
low- to middle- income settings leads people to improvise disposal methods that generally have 
an adverse influence on health and sanitation. Most common practices include burning, burying 
or discarding it in ravines, uncultivated land or rivers (Kaza et al. 2018). 
 
Although concerning, waste management in low-to-middle income countries, let alone of 
disposable diapers specifically, has been put under the spotlight by scientific researchers fairly 
recently (Taboada-González et al. 2011). This calls for a great lack of data regarding generation 
rates and exposure pathways. The literature review performed by Heather et al. (2015) specifically 
reveals the deficiency of information: no data on yearly or daily amount of diapers used and 
disposed of in developing countries was found; no official documents or guidelines on safe 
disposal of children’s diapers at the household level were identified either and common practices 
used to dispose of diapers were also difficult to determine. While there were no identified studies 
that linked diaper disposal in solid waste directly to health outcomes, there have been various 
studies looking at microbial pathogens present in diapers disposed of in landfill, though they were 
all conducted in developed rather than developing countries (Heather et al. 2015). Baseline data 
==PAGE:13==
 
12 
necessary to compare similar settings and eventually develop technical solutions are currently very 
scarce. Information found in literature is mainly qualitative. It is of paramount importance to 
contribute to a better understanding of the magnitude of the problem and characterize the hotspots 
of exposure. 
 
San Miguel Ixtahuacán is an emblematic example of a low-income rural area where matters 
regarding disposable diapers are now under the spotlight of NGOs and organs of the local 
government. Since 2016, the Swiss non-governmental organization Helvetas has been operating 
in the area with the intent of, amongst other goals, improving the sanitary condition of its rural 
communities. By means of the SANTOLIC methodology developed by UNICEF, Helvetas is 
gradually eradicating open-air defecation by promoting the use of latrines (Helvetas, n.d.). 
Management of disposable diapers is tightly related to open air defecation as the uncontrolled 
disposal of these products poses its same environmental and sanitary challenges. The SANTOLIC 
methodology generally promotes pit latrines as recommendable for disposing of soiled diapers, for 
this is the easiest and cheapest way of confining them from human exposure. However, compared 
to the simple and primordial form of open defecation, disposable diapers have the aggravating 
circumstance of being a plastic-made product and, as such, they fall within the category of solid 
waste too. Being a sanitation program, SAHTOSO has limited knowledge to what the best 
practices of solid waste disposal are and therefore seeks for data and recommendations to better 
integrate the SANTOLIC methodology with a deeper understanding of disposable diapers’ 
management (UNICEF 2012). Identifying San Miguel Ixtahuacán as area of investigation for the 
present study was therefore considered appropriate.

3 Research Objectives 
 
3.1 General Objective 
 
The overall objective of this study is to quantify the generation rates, identify contamination paths 
and elaborate improvement recommendations for the “consume to disposal” fate of disposal 
diapers in rural areas of low-to-middle income countries. Besides a more general literature study 
of past and current challenges about management of disposable diapers and the technical state-of-
the-art, the present study is comprised of a field work carried out in the Municipality of San Miguel 
Ixtahuacán, in Guatemala. Here, since 2016, the Swiss NGO Helvetas has been executing the 
project SAHTOSO, aimed at further develop the hygiene and sanitation conditions of rural 
communities. 
 
3.2 Specific Objectives 
 
To achieve the primary objective, the following specific objectives had to be accomplished. 
 
- 
Analyze the employment and management of disposable diapers in family’s homes, by 
gathering information about the current management practices; 
- 
Understand factors influencing such practices and the level of awareness of the population 
about the investigated challenges; 
- 
Assess the amount of disposable diapers generated; 
- 
Map the fate of diapers from use-to-grave by means of a Material Flow Analysis; 
- 
Identify the exposure pathways of this waste material to identify human health and 
environmental risks associated with it; 
- 
Qualitatively propose potential scenarios for improved management of disposable diapers 
contextualized to the area of investigation; 
- 
Based on a series of qualitative criteria assessed by the local stakeholders, reach a general 
consensus for the most feasible solution, using the proposed scenarios as initiating point.

4 Background Information 
 
4.1 Disposable Diapers 
 
Before developing the research objectives, acknowledging background information about 
disposable diapers is necessary. Scientific literature was thoroughly researched and a brief 
exposition of the state-of-the-art and relevant facts of disposable diapers found is presented in the

4.1.1 History of Disposable Diapers 
 
It wasn’t until during World War II that Marian Donovan in 1946 in the United States and 
Pauliström in 1942 in Sweden designed the first disposable diapers (Krafchik 2016). Before then, 
primordial attempts of diapering included materials such as milkweed leaf, animal skins, packed 
grass and moss. In warmer climates, diapers were rarely used, instead mothers tried to anticipate 
their baby's bowel movements and cleaned up after them (Jesca and Junior 2015). In the 
mid-nineteenth century, industrialization and the manufacture of cotton fabrics introduced 
affordable cloth diapers on the market. The possibility of washing and reusing it created an 
extremely strong demand as it made diapers accessible to a wide public (Dyer 2005). The advent 
of disposable diapers in the 1940s offered an ideal solution for a more practical and hygienic 
management of infants’ urine and excreta, which allowed parents to save time and energy. In recent 
years, the continuously decreasing cost of disposable diapers, together with the increasing 
purchasing power of people globally has led to a strong expansion of the disposable diaper market 
in developing countries as well, replacing traditional and self-tailored diapering solutions (Gul et 
al. 2017). 
 
4.1.2 Worldwide Diapering Habits 
 
Premise 
In modern times, ‘cloth diaper’ is the expression used when referring to the modern reusable 
diapering solutions found on the market. It should be noted that, when searching in literature 
information about diapering habits worldwide, ‘cloth diapers’ is often the expression used for the 
non-commercialized diapers self-tailored by parents living in less developed countries who have 
no access to or can’t afford commercial diapers. For the majority of cases these come down to any 
simple piece of cloth suitable enough to be wrapped around a baby’s bum, thus varying from a 
wide range of materials depending on availability (e.g. wool, flax, cotton, or other fiber) (The 
Green Mama 2013). Given the higher chance of such practice occurring in developing countries, 
the expression ‘cloth diapers’ will be from now on used in this report to address this type of 
diapering only, unless otherwise specified. 
 
In 2014, Thaman and Eichenfield surveyed data worldwide documenting variations in diaper 
frequency as part of an international initiative on Global Infant Skin Care. Results show a 
prevalence of cloth diapering in middle eastern and southeast Asian countries compared to western 
and Latin-American countries, where disposables seem to have come a long way into the parent’s 
diapering habits. In fact, the use of disposable diapers has been recorded as the most common 
==PAGE:16==
 
15 
practice not only in western and more developed countries, where percentages of use range 
between 82-100% for toddlers within 18 months of age, (Dyer 2005; ERM 2008; Jesca and Junior 
2015; Srinivas and Dhar 2016; Remigios 2014), but in the majority of other countries as well. 
In many settings of the Pacific-Asian area, parents are likely to reject throwaway products for they 
are perceived as a waste of money and cotton is considered to have higher breathability properties 
(Thaman and Eichenfield 2014). In India and the Philippines, more than 80% of mothers report 
preferring cotton cloth underpants or nappies over disposable pants, especially for the daytime 
spent at home, while sometimes employ modern disposable diapers during the night and outings. 
(Srinivas and Dhar 2016) In China, toddlers wear kaidangku or "split pants" (trousers open at the 
crotch so that youngsters can urinate or defecate when they like) or, as (Krafchik 2016) states, until 
very recently, mothers of pre-ambulatory children wait to feel the peristaltic movement of the 
infant while breast feeding to anticipate excretion. Lately, the middle class seems to have 
increasingly adopted disposable diaper use, leaving behind the more traditional Chinese diapering 
methods. This seems to be the case for many other developing countries that according to Gul et 
al. (2017) are being targeted by the multinational companies as they see potentially big markets 
due to the high birth rates. Figure 1 shows an example of disposable diapers being sold in a street 
market of Guatemala. 
On the contrary, several findings have shown a general tendency of Latin-American countries 
towards the employment of disposable diapers. A research conducted by Gil et al. (2004) reveals 
higher percentages of disposable diapers in Mexico, Nicaragua and Peru, ranging between 
59%-100% for infants <24 months old. Habits in Guatemala were found published in an article 
reporting about a humanitarian intervention (The Green Mama 2013) during which diapering 
habits were assessed and fell almost exclusively under the category of cloth rags. 
Quantitative research conducted in poor resource urban settings in Zimbabwe revealed that 78% 
of women used disposable diapers (Jesca and Junior 2015), while during a qualitative study 
conducted in Nurpur Shahan, a peri-urban slum settlement in Pakistan, respondents said “They see 
friends and relatives using [disposable] diapers all the time‟ (Gul et al. 2017).

4.1.4 Common environmental implications of disposable diapers in low-to-middle income 
countries 
 
When it comes to rural areas of developing countries, low levels of education and the lack of access 
to a legitimate solid waste management system contribute to a highly contaminated environment 
(Kaza et al. 2018). Parents find themselves obliged to come up with alternative disposal solutions 
which generally have an adverse influence on health and sanitation. Shortages or absence of water 
supply systems often force parents to abandon traditional cloth diapering habits and convert to 
disposable methods (Remigios 2014). Environmental impacts associated to uncontrolled disposal 
of diapers strictly depend on the type of method being practiced; open incineration causes 
production of contaminants such as CO and chlorine compounds (Espinosa-Valdemar et al. 2014). 
For disposable diapers, consequences are aggravated due to the presence of flame retardants. Open 
dumping, besides generating an exposed source of diseases, contaminates soil and water courses 
by the slow release of polluting substances into the ground and through infiltration by rainwater, 
while uncontrolled landfilling generates leachate of polluting compounds that will inevitably 
contaminate soil and groundwater, especially given the high organic content of a soiled disposable 
diaper. The Environmental Agency estimates that, in terms of carbon footprint, a disposal diaper 
is responsible for 630 kg of greenhouse gas, which is equal to an average car driven 1800 miles 
(Rahat et al. 2014). A common scenario of mismanaged disposable diapers in rural areas of low-
to-middle income countries is portrayed in Figure 3.

4.1.5 Common health implications of disposable diapers in low-to-middle income countries 
 
Besides the environmental impacts of the waste fraction that disposable diapers account for, a 
relevant share of health implications is generated by the presence of human feces. When it comes 
to diapering in low-to-middle income countries, two main threats are posed to human health: 
contact dermatitis and epidemic diseases. Transmissive diseases are most commonly spread on 
account of water contamination, soil contamination, hand and food contamination or through 
animal carriers (Remigios 2014). The highly unhygienic conditions in which people are used to 
living in are a strong contributor to increasing the indexes of diarrhea and infant malnutrition, 
already critical in many developing countries (Rand et al. 2015). Besides the indirect health risks 
that the inappropriate disposal management of disposable diapers may cause, their improper 
employment can also be the source of contact dermatitis and urinary tract infections (UTI) (Gul et 
al. 2017). While clinical trials conducted in North America and Western Europe document a 
significant reduction in severity and frequency of diaper dermatitis with the use of disposable 
diapers instead of cloth (Srinivas and Dhar 2016), their higher absorbency capacity often misleads 
parents into adopting the habit of changing the baby very few times in a day (Thaman and 
Eichenfield 2014). Excessive wetness, friction, high pH, high enzymatic activity given by the 
presence of feces and urine are all factors that increase the chances of skin rushes and UTI and are 
more likely to occur when disposable diapers are not changed regularly enough. (Srinivas and 
Dhar 2016)

4.2 Description of the Area of Investigation (San Miguel Ixtahuacán) 
 
4.2.1 Geographic Data 
 
San Marcos is one of the 22 departments of Guatemala and, according to the national census of 
2018, it is home to around 1 million inhabitants (7% of the national population). Rural areas 
comprehend nearly 75% of its population (Govierno de Guatemala 2018). In 2014, 60% of the 
population was in poverty and 23% in extreme poverty (FAO 2016).  
San Miguel Ixtahuacán is one of the 30 municipalities that comprise the department of San Marcos. 
The origin of the word Ixtahuacán belongs to the Náhuatl, a Mesopotamian indigenous language, 
and it means “place with a wide view” (Wikipedia 2019). 
It is located in the northeast of the region at a latitude of 15° 16' 25'' N and a longitude of 91° 44' 
55'' W. It is elevated at an average of 2050 meters above the sea level (NatLong, n.d.). The urban 
area is located 64 km north of the homonymous departmental capital, San Marcos, and 314 km 
from Guatemala City. The total extension of the municipality is 184 km2. Geographical maps with 
political borders of Guatemala and the Department of San Marcos are shown in Figure 4 and 
Figure 5. The region has a mild temperature all year long fluctuating around 17oC. Seasons are 
mainly defined according to the precipitation level. The rainy season usually extends from May to 
October (Wikipedia 2019).

When setting the focus on diapering habits of rural population of San Miguel Ixtahuacán, the 
influence and consequences of the strongly paternalist culture are worth highlighting. Great respect 
to the mother-in-law should be given when it comes to making decisions about the family’s 
approach towards raising children. This transfer of decision power from the mothers to the 
mothers-in-law, who, for the same paternalistic reasons, have their son’s best interest at heart, 
breeds a generally accepted reasoning regarding the use of disposable diapers: the money earned 
by their hard-working son should not be wasted buying expensive disposable diapers. 
Daughters-in-law who express their preference towards the disposable product are portrayed as 
lazy to do a task - washing cloth diapers - that mothers of older generations spent their parenting 
life doing.

4.2.4 Sanitation Data 
 
No official plans of water supply and sanitation systems currently exist in the Municipal file 
records. The information here reported was drawn from the personal collection of data gathered 
during the field work by means of observation and interviews with the personnel from the 
Municipal Office of Water and Sanitation (OMAS) and the solid waste management team. 
 
4.2.4.1 Water and Sanitation 
In rural areas of San Miguel Ixtahuacán water is supplied free of charge by the municipality 
through a piping system connecting groundwater springs to people’s homes. Water shortages are 
common phenomena and can happen due to water scarcity at the source or to illegal connections 
that reduce water pressure. 
No drainage or sewage systems are implemented. Most widespread sanitary method is the use of 
simple pit latrines. These are generally 3 to 5-meter deep holes with no structural support or 
isolation from the surrounding ground soil. Protection from rain and privacy are guaranteed by a 
small hut, of which techniques of construction can vary from plastic bags supported with wooden 
sticks to mud bricks and steel laminae or cement walls. Once filled, used pits are covered and a 
new one is dug. 
 
4.2.4.2 Solid Waste 
The municipal solid waste management system of San Miguel Ixtahuacán started operating in 2015 
and it is administered by the OMAS. It serves the head town of San Miguel Ixtahuacán three times 
a week with a curbside pick-up system; no type of waste segregation is performed, neither at the 
source nor at the disposal site. On Sundays only, at dawn, the same collection truck drives to two 
rural communities (Maquivil and Chilive) to pick up the waste generated the previous day from 
the food market. In Chilive, the collection point in which market waste is gathered has been 
informally defined in a specific area of the community’s main square with no means of protection 
from neither rain nor pedestrians. Waste is piled up and left exposed to all kinds of animals (e.g. 
flies, dogs, chickens) - abundant given the rural environment - that ultimately become very 
effective vectors of contamination. A visual representation of Chilive’s collection point is given 
in Figure 7. In Maquivil, waste is gathered in a small hut, as shown in Figure 8, therefore screened 
from the rain and medium-sized animals. Flies though were found easily entering the structure 
through the multiple gaps between the wooden boards it is made of. Collected waste is ultimately 
unloaded at an uncontrolled official dumping site at the outskirts of the urban area of San Miguel 
Ixtahuacán, being this a ravine at the bottom of which flows a small river. A photographic record 
is shown in Figure 9 and Figure 10. 
Residents of the urban area who want to benefit from the collection service need to subscribe their 
home address and contribute through the payment of a monthly fee of Q.12.00 (equivalent of 
~US$1.80) to be paid personally in DAFIM (see Figure 6). Accumulated delays by debtor users 
are often complained by the municipal officers. The inefficient administration of payments and the 
failure to update the list of legitimate users causes the regular collection of household waste not 
accounted for. In Chilive and Maquivil, payment is due on a weekly basis and charge is taken by 
the local public organ. Should the fee not be paid, the collection truck will suspend the service for 
the following week. This inconsistency caused by missed payments, differently from the 
consequences witnessed in the urban setting, results in the accumulation of market waste – mainly 
organic, thus rapidly putrefiable – at the collection point. Additionally, it causes the inevitable 
==PAGE:25==

alteration of the collection schedule, whenever service is reactivated, for the truck will most likely 
have to travel to the community at least twice to be able to complete the collection. These types of 
complications are often the source of disorganization that ultimately lead to inefficient 
consumption of resources and scarce results.

4.2.4.3 SAHTOSO Project – SANTOLIC Methodology 
Since 2016, Swiss non-governmental organization Helvetas has been implementing the 
SAHTOSO project - Total and Sustainable Hygiene and Sanitation– in several areas of the country. 
The project aims at improving a community’s quality of life through overcoming sanitary 
challenges. Open defecation is still habitual for 10% of rural population of Guatemala according 
to (“Index Mundi” 2017). Under guidance and funding of UNICEF, the SANTOLIC methodology 
– Community-Led Total Sanitation – was implemented as the strategy to achieve the eradication 
of open defecation and promote a hygienic lifestyle. SANTOLIC is a methodological alternative 
to the conventional way of providing aid through subsidies or humanitarian funds. Instead, goals 
are met by generating commitment, empowerment and co-responsibility within the community 
towards the development of a self-implemented solution. Examples of sanitary improved solutions 
made possible by the SANTOLIC methodology in rural areas of the department of San Marcos are 
shown in Figure 11 and Figure 12. Result of this methodology is the certification of the community 
involved as “Free of open-air defecation” (FIDAL) thanks to a sustainable change of behavior 
(Helvetas, n.d.).

4.3 Criteria for Definition of Area of Investigation 
 
In order to define the area within the municipality of San Miguel Ixtahuacán in which carrying out 
the study would have been appropriate, the following listed requirements had to be fulfilled. 
Criteria was outlined so that conditions for data recovery were as favorable as possible. 
 
- 
Rural area; 
- 
Where environmental challenges related to disposable diapers have arisen; 
- 
Where some kind of solid waste management system is operating; 
- 
Where water supply system is available; 
- 
Where local authorities and population have proven to be collaborating; 
- 
Accessible by car for the majority of the extension. 
 
Chilive and Maquivil are rural areas belonging to the Municipality of San Miguel Ixtahuacán. 
Community members had often claimed to the local authorities the increasing number of 
disposable diapers found along the streets and in their own cultivated land. Both these communities 
are accessible by car for most of their extension, with the exception of a few houses that are only 
reachable through walkable paths. Both Maquivil and Chilive are supplied with potable water by 
the municipality and are served by the collection SWM system operated by the OMAS. According 
to Helveta’s and OMAS’ past experiences, both communities had proven to openly welcome the 
intervention of external institutions aiming at providing aid and know-how for development. 
Therefore, given the favorable conditions, Chilive and Maquivil were defined as the specific 
investigation areas for the present study. These two communities are meant to serve as proxy for 
other rural areas affected by the environmental consequences of the mismanagement of disposable 
diapers in middle-to-low income countries.  
 
4.3.1 Specifics about Chilive and Maquivil 
 
Maquivil and Chilive are two bordering communities of the municipal area of San Miguel 
Ixtahuacán. They are located at 9 km and 14 km respectively from the homonymous municipal 
head city, San Miguel Ixtahuacán. Maquivil accounts for a total population of 1925, while Chilive 
of 2163. Local administration is allocated to a mayor, its auxiliaries and the COCODE. As for the 
great majority of the municipality, both communities’ economies are mainly sustained by 
agricultural activities. Some others are dedicated to selling wood or as construction workers while 
a considerable share of families is sustained with remittance of funds from relatives emigrated to 
the United States. The social circumstances of these communities are well aligned with that of the 
municipality. Given the extremely poor conditions, education is often put in second place while 
priority is given to working to sustain the family, in case of a male child, or getting married and 
raising a family, in case of a female child. Each community is served by a public health center and 
a public school. The municipal waste collection truck drives to the communities on Sundays to 
take care of the market waste generated on the previous day. Although illegitimately, some 
residents take advantage of the market waste collection point to dispose of their household waste. 
Very few public trash bins are installed in the communities – primarily located in the main square – 
and consist of simple uncovered aluminum drums, therefore exposed to flies and, whenever placed 
in a vulnerable spot, to rainwater as well. The communities’ public waste bins are not officially

5.3 Quantitative Data Collection 
 
In addition to generation rates and a mass flow obtained by means of a waste audit, quantitative 
data is here also referred to information that, although of qualitative nature, allows making 
countable estimations about habits and general perceptions of the respondents about diapering, 
while correlating them to socio-economic factors.

diapers were inspected. Questions about the frequency of changes were included in the survey to 
provide a first approximation of the generation rate of soiled disposable diapers. This data was

disposed of.

5.5 Material Flow Analysis (MFA) 
 
The MFA of the waste fraction assessed was modeled along its generation-to-disposal phase using 
the drawing tool STAN V2.6.801. The faith of the total amount of soiled disposable diapers 
generated in the investigated area was sectioned into four steps: 
 
i. Total generation; 
ii. Source of generation; 
iii. Storage; 
iv. Final disposal. 
 
The MFA served to better identify the sources of contamination and estimate its magnitude based 
on the quantities of disposable diapers accumulated in each hazardous process. Sources and routs 
of contamination were identified thanks to the information retrieved from the questionnaires 
combined with close observation activities; specific quantities were calculated based on the daily 
mass generation rate obtained from the domiciliary waste assessment while distributions were 
derived from information gathered in the surveys. 
 
5.6 Statistics for Data Treatment 
 
Besides assessing data for each community individually, these were also pooled together to form 
a single sample, referred to as ‘Combined’, as to represent one general rural area. The unification 
of the samples also helped to have a wider dataset to run the tests on and hence acquire more 
statistically reliable results. 
Data treatment was performed using Excel V16.31. Outliers were excluded using the quartile 
method for these were intended as values falling outside of the 1.5-interquartile boundaries. 
Distribution similarities were proven comparing mean and median and performing t-tests with a 
5% significance level, while data correlation was carried out in the form of linear regression and 
assessed by comparison to the tabulated critical R.

6.1.3.2 Hand-washing Practice 
Contaminated hands are among the most immediate transmission form of diseases between 
individuals. Data discussed in this paragraph, rather than being considered quantitative of the 
amount of people actually adopting appropriate hygiene practices, should be considered qualitative 
of people’s awareness about them. During the survey the question was asked in the following form: 
“What do you do immediately after changing the baby’s diaper?” Hence, answers depend both on 
the respondent’s perception of the question and the level of honesty once the question’s purpose 
was indeed understood. Based on this preliminary remark, the great majority of caregivers 
interviewed were found aware of the importance of washing their hands after diapering before 
returning to their general occupations. However, those apparently more careless or unaware are 
still a 16%.

mismanagement of disposable diapers was asked. The totality of the sample turned out to be aware 
of the existence of negative consequences derived from the open disposal of soiled diapers. 
Opinions about the nature of such consequences and their originating source were furtherly 
investigated. Results are shown in Figures 27 and 28. A significant number of respondents (83%) 
revealed themselves aware of negative implications of the mis-disposal of soiled diapers. 
Awareness about environmental impacts is recognized almost as frequently as health risks. 
Similarly comparable is the share of people identifying the source of either kind of risk in the 
excreta and that allocating it to the polymeric materials instead, though the latter is prevalent. The

concern derives by the long draught that the region has been going through in the past year, heavily

6.1.5 Storage and Disposal Methods 
 
In order to identify all possible sources of contamination caused by the life of a disposable diaper 
in its use-to-grave phase, a deep understanding of diaper management prior to and for its disposal 
was considered necessary. Results about management of disposable diapers immediately after 
changing the baby are reported in Figure 29. Among the total number of mothers employing 
disposable diapers for their children, the majority (66%) disposes of them immediately after 
changing the baby. Final disposal is intended as a place or process after which the soiled diaper or 
its residues are no longer deliberately dislocated. The remaining mothers store soiled diapers in a 
temporary place before permanently disposing of them. Only nearly one third of the storage 
methods are to be considered safe, hence isolated from human exposure. The remaining kinds 
generate potential risks through either water contamination or transmission through animal 
carriers. Examples of unsafe storage are open baskets kept on the ground inside the house or bags 
left out in the rain or at reaching distance of flies and street dogs.

6.1.5.1.1 Collection truck 
Even though the municipal collection system is not meant to serve domiciles, while running the 
surveys some residents confessed that they illegitimately take their waste to the collection point 
used for the market residues before the truck arrives. According to Figure 30, 6% of them 
specifically do it to discard disposable diapers. These are ultimately collected as part of the market 
waste and are taken to the official disposal site. Knowing this, as part of the investigation about 
the routes of contamination of disposable diapers, respondents were asked to give an approximate 
indication of the time they usually bring the waste to the collection point. All of them stated the 
bags are generally delivered on Saturday in the late afternoon/night. Considering that the truck 
arrives at 4 a.m. of the morning after, it seems reasonable to think that the exposure of the waste 
to street dogs for such a considerable number of hours may be a strong contributor to the great 
amount of diapers spotted around the streets. Additionally, the unprotected state of the collection 
point from weather conditions represents a critical source of water and soil contamination. 
 
6.1.5.1.1.1 Awareness about collection truck and/or official disposal site  
The existing collection truck operating in the area of investigation is meant to exclusively serve 
the main square on Sunday mornings, collecting the waste produced only by the market stands on 
the previous day. Residents not owning a market stand were never officially informed about the 
collection system to limit the input of domiciliary waste illegitimately left at the collection point. 
In fact, 82% of the total sample was unaware of either the existence of a collection truck or its 
unloading site. This could indicate that people are probably not familiar with waste management 
systems in general. This should be considered with higher regard when contemplating the 
possibility of implementing a collection service in exchange of a contribution from the residents, 
for they will most likely be initially reluctant to pay and may challenge the outcomes of the 
management program.

significant difference was found between the distributions of each age-cluster. Age is from now 
on considered not statistically influential in the number of disposable diapers used per day.

6.2.1.1.2 Day 
T-tests showed no remarkable variation of diaper generation throughout the days during which the 
characterization was carried out (Sunday, Monday, and Tuesday). However, as reported in 
Table 13, the t-test run between Sunday and Tuesday resulted in a t-critical (one-tail) slightly 
greater than the t-statistical, which makes the interpretation unclear. Seeing no major difference 
between Sunday and Monday and considering that samples for the waste characterization only 
included families employing disposable diapers as sole diapering system, it seems reasonable to 
conclude that diaper generation is maintained nearly stable throughout the week. The null 
hypothesis is therefore accepted, suggesting no significant difference of generation between any 
day of the week. 
 
6.2.1.1.3 Income and education 
The influence of the family’s economic condition and the school level achieved by the caregiver’s 
on the number of disposable diapers used per day was tested and a moderate positive correlation 
was dectected in both cases. Results are shown in Appendix 5, Sheet 3. By means of a linear 
regression, the income level generated an R2=0.15, while the education level R2=0.095, proving a 
light higher frequency of diaper changes per day when increasing each independent variable. 
Correlation to the income level is 1.6 times stronger than the one to the level of education. This 
proves a common pattern according to which limited financial accessibility often forces families 
to prioritize food and other basic necessities over disposable diapers. Nonetheless, the correlation 
is not statistically significant when comparing the R-values to the corresponsing tabulated R-
critical. This may therefore indicate that some families consider disposable diapers as part of the 
essential goods for assuring basic life conditions. Or contrariwise, some higher-income level 
families, misconvice the adequate number of diapers to be used, which according to Thaman and 
Eichenfield (2014) should be 4 to 5 per day. 
Education is often an unreliable indicator, since this is tightly dependent on the quality and 
legittimacy of the academic institution. Even an advanced level of education does not necessarily 
translate into high awareness about the best and safest practices about baby diapering. Just like 
supposing the opposite situation would also possibly result in misleading conslusions.

6.3 Material Flow Analysis (MFA) 
 
The inadequate use and management of disposable diapers is causing a great amount of stress on 
the environment. Throughout the life cycle of a disposable diaper in its use-to-grave phase, the 
quality of soil, air and water is being continuously challenged with inputs of contamination coming 
from almost the totality of practices most commonly used in rural areas of low-to-middle income 
countries. 
Designing a mass flow diagram served to better understand the magnitude of the exposure caused 
by the diverse sources of contamination threatening population of Maquivil and Chilive. Total and 
specific quantities were calculated based on the daily mass generation reported in Table 12 and by 
considering the combined total number of families nursing babies less than 30 months old of 
Maquivil and Chilive, as reported in Table 2 and 3. The resulting mass flow is outlined in Figure 
36. The entire system has been clustered in four stages: 
 
- 
Generation: the total amount of diapers generated in the communities is displayed; 
- 
Generation source: the initial single flow is distributed according to whether disposable 
diapers are being part of the household waste or if they are disposed of in public areas; 
- 
Storage systems: disposable diapers disposed of at home are sometimes first stored in a 
temporary container, which can be safe or unsafe in terms of exposure; 
- 
Final disposal systems: all disposal methods practiced by the families of San Miguel 
Ixtahuacán are included in the system. 
 
In Figure 36, boxes are representative of processes or stages from and to which disposable diapers 
are generated and allocated. Blue frames were used to identify processes that, as they are currently 
practiced, represent sources of contamination. Dashed arrows are indicative for emissions related 
to such contamination; however, type and magnitude of emissions were not included in the 
objectives of the present study. System boundaries were defined accordingly, leaving emissions 
on the outside. The thickness of the arrows is proportional to the amount of diapers streaming from 
one process to the other.

6.3.1.1 Unsafe Storage Systems 
Temporarily storing soiled diapers prior to their final disposal adds a potential source of 
contamination to the life cycle of the disposable product. While surveying, the respondents were 
asked to show the place of storage and almost 70% of the cases used methods that were somehow 
hazardous to the household, the neighboring residents or the environment. The two unsafe methods 
most commonly adopted are an open basket kept on the ground inside of the house or a plastic bag 
hanging from the branch of a tree unprotected from the rain. A visual representation is given in 
Figure 37 and Figure 38. Keeping soiled diapers exposed inside the house creates a source of 
contamination primarily by means of flies and domestic animals reaching into the basket. 
Nonetheless, babies playing around the house, unaware of the hazards, were found coming in 
contact with the waste as well. On top of the contamination via animal vectors, the disposal of 
soiled diapers in plastic bags exposed to rainwater creates a contamination path through soil, 
surface and groundwater.
 
 
6.3.1.2 Dumping in the Latrine 
Employing the house latrine to dispose of used diapers is a fairly common practice for rural 
environments. Based on data collected in this study, an amount of 270 kg of diapers, corresponding 
to 27% of the total waste generated, is estimated to be disposed of in pit latrines weekly. According 
to answers obtained from the questionnaires, people who prefer other types of solutions mainly do 
it to avoid the quick volume take up caused by soiled diapers generated on a daily basis. Even 
though burying organic, and specifically fecal, matter underground in an uncontrolled way can be 
hazardous to soil and groundwater, the World’s Health Organization has included pit latrines as a 
safe and recommendable disposal site for human excreta, when financial resources are scarce 
(WHO 2019). Latrines become fully effective though only when well maintained, be this in terms

San Miguel Ixtahuacán. The most common burying method practiced in houses of Maquivil and

never witnessed, a possibility should be considered that the limited depth of these type of holes 
may not secure the actual permanence of diapers underground during the timespan necessary for 
decomposition. Only few cases were found employing deeper waste pits, which nonetheless were 
always left partially or fully uncovered, thus leaving waste exposed to rainwater and animal 
vectors. Evidence retrieved during observation is shown in Figure 41. Risks generated by this 
practice are therefore comparable to those of latrines.

7 Scenario Development 
 
By combining data collected during surveys and waste audits with the information retrieved from 
the observation activities and interviews with local institutions, the development of what should 
be considered draft proposals of potential solutions to disposable diapers’ management in rural 
areas of low-to-middle income countries was made possible. Scenarios are given at three levels: 
 
- 
Residential level 
- 
Communitarian level  
- 
Municipal level  
 
When assessing solutions at smaller scale level, economic considerations often led to prioritizing 
effectiveness over environmental performance. Bigger scale scenarios, and therefore wider 
financial availability, allowed the development of more environmentally oriented solutions. 
Specifics and technical information about each solution can be found on the factsheets in Appendix 
6. 
 
7.1 Proposal Solution at Residential Level: Latrines 
 
Simple pit latrines are an economical short-term solution to effectively prevent human waste from 
becoming a source of disease spread. Feces and urine are deposited through a hole inside a pit of 
significant depth, dug into the ground. For a latrine to properly function, it is necessary to keep it 
clean and protected from rainwater, besides maintaining the hole covered. Although burying waste 
is not environmentally sound, discarding disposable diapers in latrines ensures effective isolation 
of waste and excreta from human contact and animal vectors which could, otherwise, jeopardize 
the hygienic conditions of the environment.  
 
Given that the majority of inhabitants lives under the poverty line, encouraging the use of latrines 
as dumping pits also within those families that initially expressed an adverse opinion about it seems 
the most viable short-term solution. The low investment and operation costs, together with the 
simple technical know-how required, make them a very suitable solution for financially and 
educationally challenged rural societies. Additionally, they represent a method to which residents 
are already familiar with and therefore implementing them would not require a radical behavioral 
change. Using the house latrine for disposing of disposable diapers inevitably leads to a faster 
filling of the pit, which would entail closing it permanently and investing for a new one; this 
however is outbalanced by the savings obtained in terms of health expenses for diseases caused by 
a contaminated environment, especially to the more vulnerable members of the families, such as 
babies themselves. In the case of San Miguel Ixtahuacán, costs for building a 4-meter-deep latrine 
round up to approximately 120€. Given the generation volume rates obtained from the domiciliary 
waste assessment Table 12, a baby throughout his/her diapering age is responsible for the disposal

contribution was calculated taking into account 1.7 m3 of diaper waste for the first 2.5 years of life 
and an average adult production of excreta for the remaining years.

7.2 Proposal Solution at Communitarian Level 
 
The following developed scenarios are here presented as one subsequent to the other. However, 
the implementation of either solution independently can be contemplated as well. 
 
7.2.1 Neighborhood-based Waste Pits 
 
The main goal of a controlled waste pit is to provide families with a designated place to bury 
disposable diapers in order to avoid their exposure to community members. Besides the 
environmental and sanitary benefits, advantages of community-based solutions are reflected in the 
social development and potential employment generation that they entail. 
 
Rural communities of Guatemala are generally subclustered in smaller neighborhoods, in which 
the majority of roads are unpaved and often heavily uneven. This is a great challenge for 
implementing waste collection systems. A solution which could be developed at 
neighborhood-level, and sees residents take care of the transportation of their own waste, is 
therefore preferable. Following the same reasoning regarding financial and technical requirements 
as for the solution at residential level, employing waste pits seems another viable alternative. Each 
neighborhood would be in charge of a controlled waste pit, operating at specific times and on 
specific days during which it is monitored by an operator, whose salary would be taken care by 
the municipality. To limit dimensions of the pit, only disposable diapers should be introduced. To 
avoid other types of waste being illegitimately introduced outside working hours, the pit should be 
provided with a safe closing system. This will also ensure protection from rainwater and avoid 
exposure to flies. Once full capacity is reached, the pit is permanently closed with a thick layer of 
soil and a new one is dug. To avoid excessive nuisance, a pit allowing a maximum lifespan of 6 to 
12 months should be planned. Dimensions and capacity of the waste pits strictly depend on the 
number of infants residing in each neighborhood. Once this information is known, generation data 
reported in Table 12 can be used as reference. Coverage areas of each waste pit should be defined 
in order to maximize efficiency, allowing the allocation of adjacent neighborhoods to the same pit 
in case the population of babies in diapering age were too little, rather than rigidly allocating one 
pit per neighborhood.

7.2.1.1 Shredding and composting 
Once the self-managed neighborhood-based collection system is stabilized, the transition to a 
scenario involving a treatment system should be contemplated. Composting is a viable option for 
rural and financially limited settings. However, when it comes to disposable diapers, composting 
is only feasible if previously shredded and subsequently sieved to deprive the final product of the 
plastic residues. A more extensive analysis of the advantages and disadvantages of implementing 
composting of disposable diapers as treatment method is laid down in the following paragraph 
showcasing the proposal solution at municipal level. Anyhow, when implementing such method 
at smaller scales, the economic availability of the funding stakeholders represents a decisive 
variable. Investment costs for purchasing one shredder per neighborhood and the operational costs 
for collecting additional organic waste and operating the composting chambers would increase 
significantly. In any case, a solution demanding such low levels of technical knowledge and 
resources applied to such a small amount of matter to be treated would most likely guarantee 
promising results. This been said, a stronger focus to the cost-benefit analysis should be given. 
 
 
7.3 Proposal Solution at Municipal Level: Composting 
 
The advantage of planning a solution at municipal level is that the increased amount of waste to 
be handled allows the implementation of more environmentally sustainable treatment systems 
which would otherwise be less cost-effective. Composting is a microbiological process through 
which organic matter is transformed in stable, dark brown, soil-like material called compost. This 
process occurs as a result of microbial activity under aerobic conditions. Applied compost 
improves soil structure and increases the nutrients availability in the soil .  
 
Composting of disposable diapers has been proven successful by various lab and pilot-scale 
experiments (Espinosa-Valdemar et al. 2014; Colón et al. 2011). The high content of organic 
matter makes this type of waste suitable for biological degradation, as long as at the end of the 
maturation process, compost is sieved to deprive it of the plastic residues. While technically a very 
basic know-how is required, an inevitable pre-requisite for composting a waste load made of 
disposable diapers is for it to be previously shredded. It is important to take into account the 
investment costs for an industrial shredder when considering developing this solution. According 
to (Colón et al. 2011), an additional drawback detected during the experiment is the presence of 
high values of zinc in the end product (compost), which may compromise its commercial value. 
Because zinc is mainly derived from lotions and medical creams used for the baby’s bum, it should 
not be a problem for settings like the one investigated for such products are rarely used. 
Operational and investment costs of a composting facility are low per se. However, implementing 
this treatment method would necessarily imply developing a collection system, which would 
represent the biggest challenge. It requires a higher level of behavioral change as it implies efficient 
waste segregation and the collection of a monthly service fee from the household may be 
necessary. Additionally, composting disposable diapers would demand collecting the households’ 
organic fraction as well which may result in higher operational costs, though counterbalanced by 
the environmental benefits of the recycling process. Garden dry waste, suitable for composting 
processes as well, would be easily obtainable as agriculture is the main economic activity. 
Moreover, this characteristic outlines a fertile market for compost. Because excreta content of 
==PAGE:74==
 
73 
disposable diapers obtained in the present study is in line with that of diapers used in the 
composting trial carried out by (Colón et al. 2011), mixing organic household waste with a 3% 
weight percentage of disposable diapers for composting should serve as reference.  
 
7.4 Additional Scenarios 
 
Two other potentially implementable scenarios were taken into consideration. However, due to the 
high technical skills required by the former option and the excessively high costs of the latter soon 
detected caused for them to be discarded at their earlier stages of development, thus considerations 
and specific operational features are limited. Nonetheless, it was decided to include both scenarios 
in their rough descriptive form taking into account that they may still serve as an inspiration source 
for future readers interested in developing similar solutions specifically in settings where local 
conditions may vary from those of the investigated area of the present study. 
 
7.4.1 De Montfort Incinerator 
 
The De Montfort Incinerator was developed since 1996 by the homonymous university located in 
Leicester, England. De Montfort Incinerators are a relatively low-cost solution for treating a vast 
variety of hazardous waste, as recommended by international organizations and institutions such 
as WHO and UNICEF (Batterman 2004). When appropriately operated, they guarantee a minimum 
temperature of 800°C and a residence time exceeding 1 second, largely destroying the waste and 
helping to reduce the environmental burden of mismanaged accumulated waste volumes. For this 
and the advantageous possibility of building the technology with locally retrievable materials, the 
De Montfort Incinerator was initially considered to be a potentially viable alternative to the current 
hazardous management of waste witnessed in rural areas of San Miguel Ixtahuacán. These 
incinerators are, in fact, far preferable to waste burning in open field. However, according to past 
experiences, they were often found not performing optimally due to significant operation, 
maintenance and management issues (Batterman 2004). Incinerator emissions occur to air, water 
and soil. Much of the concern regarding incinerator relate to the discharge of dioxins and furans, 
highly toxic components affecting waste workers, the general public, and the environment 
(Batterman 2004). The excessively limited margin of error allowed by the technology to guarantee 
its efficiency determined the exclusion of the De Montfort Incinerator from the furtherly developed 
options. 
 
7.4.2 Third party involvement 
 
Given their content of human excreta, in Guatemala, soiled disposable diapers are officially 
classified as hazardous bio-infectious waste (MARN 2018). This is also the case for waste 
produced in medical centers and hospitals. Residues generated by the center for medical attention 
of San Miguel Ixtahuacán are collected by the private organization Ecotermo, which treats 
hazardous waste with safe incineration. The possibility of including disposable diapers collected 
from the surrounding rural communities to the existing service was assessed. Relying on a 
specialized third party excludes the burden of treating the waste locally and ensures adequate 
management. However, during preliminary confrontations with personnel of the Municipality and 
OMAS to discuss the viability of the proposal, disadvantages outweighed the advantages. The 
costs of a private company were determined excessive and the operation plan to be outlined to

efficiently collect and deliver the segregated waste from such a vast area was too 
logistically-demanding, according to the consulted local institutions. Therefore, the discussed 
option was ultimately discarded.

8 Specific Recommendations 
 
Specific recommendations to cope with the identified highest-priority challenges posed by the 
mismanagement of disposable diapers, currently generating environmental and sanitary hazards to 
the population of rural areas of San Miguel Ixtahuacán, are outlined in the following paragraphs. 
Interventions at social level as well as technical level, for indoor and outdoor management of 
disposable diapers, are suggested. Recommendations are drawn as a result of the combined 
assessment of data retrieved during surveys, one-to-one interviews with local institutions, 
observation activities as well as the waste audits. The most feasible solution for indoor 
management was obtained by subjecting the developed scenarios to an additional scrutiny and 
ultimate refinement along with the involved stakeholders at the closing meeting. 
 
8.1 Informative Campaign 
 
Developing an informative campaign prior to the implementation of any technical solution is 
strongly suggested. Because durable changes come from acknowledgment and not imposition, the 
support of a preliminary and parallel informative campaign is beneficial to reaching satisfactory 
results. None of the scenarios developed are, in this sense, considered to be self-sufficient. The 
informative campaign should be shaped with a bottom-up approach, to encourage community 
empowerment rather than promoting it as a governmental intervention. All community members 
should be targeted, though dedicating special focus to mothers and mothers-in-low, for these were 
proven to be the most active actors when it comes to the management of disposable diapers. 
Members of the health committee and school officials should be called to participate as well. 
Aspects to be included in the informative campaign are: 
 
- 
Advantages and disadvantages of both disposable and cloth diapers; 
- 
Environmental and health hazards generated by mismanagement of soiled disposable 
diapers, both at storage level and final disposal level. Burning of plastic products should 
especially discouraged; 
- 
Environmental and health benefits generated by an adequate management of soiled 
disposable diapers; 
- 
Raise awareness about solid waste management in general (e.g. by promoting public bins). 
 
Confrontations with members of the community and municipal organs, depicted a strong 
favoritism towards cloth diapering over disposables when analyzed in environmental and health 
terms. Risks deriving from the use of cloth diapers are often ignored and those deriving from 
disposable diapers magnified/inflated. This is common in insufficiently informed societies, like 
that of San Miguel Ixtahuacán, where authorities and members of the health committees tend to 
unconditionally recommend the use cloth diapers. This, however, should be discouraged until a 
proper environmental risk assessment is performed. A qualitative study of the consequences 
caused by the employment of cloth diapers in rural areas of San Miguel Ixtahuacán was included 
in the observation activities. A powerful path of contamination is generated due to the absence of 
drainage and sewage systems. Wastewater discarded from washing soiled diapers – usually done 
in a sink installed in the home yard - either stagnates in the immediate surroundings or seeps into 
the soil. Cloth diapers are also a great source of illnesses as their poorly absorbent nature and the 
lack of proper sterilization of the rags, for they are generally washed in cold water, generates an 
==PAGE:77==
 
76 
ideal environment for bacterial breeding. Of relevant importance is also the considerable use of 
water that washing cloth diapers entails, which becomes especially challenging in areas that 
undergo water scarcity. This and the frequent water shortages that inhabitants of rural San Miguel 
Ixtahuacán experience make the use of disposable diapers a reasonable solution for parents. 
The existence of hazards in the employment of either diapering method makes campaigning for 
the imposition of either option inadequate, until safety measures are implemented. Additionally, 
the number of inhabitants already employing disposable diapers (89% of the sample) would makes 
drifting the population’s opinion towards the exclusive use of cloth diapers unfeasible.  
Although weakly supported, the totality of the sample showed awareness about negative 
consequences derived from the open disposal of soiled diapers. This depicts favorable conditions 
for stimulating an environmental consciousness among the community. The investigation also 
proved that users play a relevant role within the faith of a disposable diaper. Therefore, 
implementing a technical solution without underestimating the importance of involving the 
community is crucial. Special regard should be spent on cultural aspects for they have a strong 
impact on people’s decisions and actions, also when it comes to diapering practices. The negative 
prejudice towards disposable diapers and the almost idolization of cloth diapers witnessed in the 
communities investigated is a strong limitation to any solution at community or municipal level as 
mothers who choose to use disposables for their babies manage and dispose of them feeling 
socially pressured. While other mothers go by employing only cloth diapers unconscious of the 
stress posed on water quality and consumption. Additionally, the unawareness about health and 
environmental risks of mismanaged waste has accustomed people to open-dumping and burning, 
practices made socially accepted and shared. In order to achieve high performances, the eradication 
of cultural limitations like these is recommended. 
Moreover, several indicators investigated in the present study, demonstrate a weak level of hygiene 
amongst residents of Maquivil and Chilive. Latrines were kept safely covered by the 22% of 
families visited during the survey phase, of which only 30% were also properly maintained clean. 
Storage of soiled diapers occurs in potentially hazardous places for 24% of the cases, while 16% 
of responses regarding hand-washing practices suggests that these are irregularly put in place. To 
support behavioral change towards a better management of disposable diapers, education for good 
hygiene practices is a high priority and should be strongly promoted as well. 
Including awareness promotion about general waste in the informative campaign should aim at 
raising a sense of responsibility into people’s perception for the waste they generate and by doing 
so gradually accustom them to segregation and collection systems which may eventually involve 
contributing with a service fee. 
 
8.2 Outdoor Management 
 
Taking into account that outdoor activities are among the motives that make disposable diapers 
more appealing than cloth diapering, according to 37% of mothers, and that 33% of them 
exclusively uses them for such occasions, coping with the mismanagement of disposable diapers 
in public areas is of relevant importance. Although in 76% of the cases, soiled diapers changed 
outdoors are temporarily stored in a plastic bag and ultimately managed at home, improving the 
service of public disposal bins seems opportune. The 5% of disposable diapers still open dumped 
when changed outdoors has a greater chance of becoming safely managed if a more adequate 
number of waste bins was installed. Daily generation’s variations, strongest on Saturdays and 
Sundays, according to the survey’s respondents, suggest that focus should be put on churches and 
==PAGE:78==
 
77 
market squares for these are the two main events that draw families out of their homes. In order to 
achieve maximum safety, containers should be provided with a secured lid. An increase in the 
number of bins installed would necessarily require an improvement in their management. 
Management should be taken care of locally by members of the community’s administration. A 
collection route and schedule should be defined. Collected waste should be unloaded at the market 
waste collection point for the municipal truck to handle it. In order for this management to be 
adequate, the condition of the market waste collection point needs to be improved as well. The 
great disparity of diapers witnessed in the piled market waste of Chilive to that of Maquivil during 
the one-time waste audit of the municipal truck, suggests that even the simple structure built in 
Maquivil to contain the market residues is effectively discouraging residents to take advantage of 
it and medium-sized animals to enter it. Nonetheless, the waste hut’s lack of maintenance grants 
access to a considerable number of flies through the fissured wooden boards, jeopardizing the 
population’s health. Improved waste huts should be maintained structurally sound and the entrance 
door preferably provided with a lock to allow opening only when necessary (e.g. on market day, 
for the collection truck to empty it). 
 
8.3 Indoor Management 
 
8.3.1 Assessment process and scenario elaboration 
 
During the closing meeting, stakeholders were invited to participate to a critical analysis of the 
previously proposed scenarios. Focus was put towards solutions rapidly implementable. A series 
of evaluating criteria was used to weight the weaknesses and strengths of each technical solution.  
The list of criteria ranked in the increasing order of priority defined by the stakeholders is 
presented. 
 
1. Investment costs; 
2. Environmental benefit; 
3. Operation costs; 
4. Sanitary benefit; 
5. Know-how required; 
6. Lifespan; 
7. Scale of applicability; 
8. Technical consultancy; 
9. Local availability of construction material. 
 
By contextualizing their feasibility, the least viable scenarios were discarded while the most 
promising ones were assessed and refined.  
 
Stakeholders considered implementing a solution at community or municipal level unadvisable. 
Costs for buying public land and the several complications in terms of sanitary and environmental 
legislation that operating it would entail may challenge the efficiency of the solution and delay its 
initiation. Scenarios at residential level were therefore preferred. These were also prioritized to 
minimize the level of logistic demanded which inevitably increases with the scale of the solution. 
Considering the existing latrines as an implementable solution for disposing of soiled diapers was 
discouraged. Many families of Maquivil and Chilive perceive building a new latrine as a very 
==PAGE:79==

costly responsibility. Thus, reducing its lifespan by introducing additional waste would most likely 
not be socially accepted. The elaboration of a solution which promotes the use of a designated 
destination exclusive to disposable diapers was therefore targeted. A collective consensus was 
ultimately reached and the agreed most feasible solution is here presented. This is to be considered 
a temporary solution, should the research evolve in an executive phase, while more definitive 
actions are planned and carried out at municipal level.

9 Obstacles and Limitations 
 
9.1 Obstacles 
 
No major obstacles were encountered during research. The plan suffered bearable delays that did 
not compromise the achievement of the research objectives. Most of these were caused by the 
almost total lack of available data locally (e.g. census of population, contact information). Cultural 
differences sometimes challenged the trust-building process with local authorities of lower 
institutional levels and the community members. Future researchers should consider involving 
personnel from local organizations although prior training may be necessary to make them well 
aware of the methodology and approach to the research’s objectives. 
 
9.2 Limitations 
 
The sample size was the main limitation to this research. More time availability would have given 
the chance to cover wider samples and allow more statistically significant results. 
Nonetheless, results obtained are considered to be fulfilling the research objectives. 
The research topic is a very socially sensitive one. Even so, the methodology was not shaped with 
a social-anthropological. The study did not count with laboratory analysis or life cycle assessments 
for environmental risk characterization.

10 Conclusion 
 
The gradual transition from traditional diapering methods to the employment of disposable diapers 
that populations of rural areas of low-to-middle income countries are going through is greatly 
threatening environmental safety and sanitary living conditions. Negative impacts are due to the 
flawed, and often financially challenged, solid waste management systems that fail to provide 
sustainable solutions to safely dispose of or treat and valorize the waste. 
Thanks to the case study carried out in rural areas of San Miguel Ixtahuacán, it was possible to 
identify the primary paths of contamination and express the magnitude of the risks disposable 
diapers pose to the environment and local population, based on generation rates. Stress relief, water 
shortages and outings were recorded as the mostly favoring factors to the employment of the 
disposable product, while financial inaccessibility is the main limiting aspect. Significantly 
perceivable are also the taboos revolving around the use of disposable diapers, caused by cultural 
factors combined with deficient education levels, which jeopardize their safe management. 
Given the limited financial availability expressed by the municipality, and to avoid logistically 
challenging systems, solutions at household level were prioritized. According to outcomes 
generated during a final workshop with the involved stakeholders, promoting the disposal of soiled 
diapers in waste pits at familiar level was defined as the most feasible short-term solution. 
Although environmental improvement is limited, implementing this low-cost and technically 
simple solution will decrease emissions to air and surface water courses while rapidly and 
significantly improving sanitary conditions for the local population. Because a great share of 
parents regularly favoring cloth diapers prefers employing disposables whenever leaving the house 
with their baby, improvement to the solid waste management in public areas is necessary as well, 
in order to effectively support the generated improvements at household level. Waste bins should 
be located in most attended areas while access to the waste market collection point should be 
regulated to avoid uncontrolled disposal. Additionally, a complementary informative campaign 
fostering hygienic practices while stimulating the exchange of information about risks and benefits 
of waste management should be promoted to trigger individual empowerment, and gradually 
eradicate taboos and cultural limitations. Awareness about the negative consequences of the 
mismanagement of disposable diapers, pivotal when seeking behavioral change, would gradually 
increase. 
Given the alarming environmental and sanitary conditions witnessed in which populations of 
settings comparable to that of the present case study are living in, further research is highly 
suggested. Available information regarding mismanagement of disposable diapers in rural areas 
of low-to-middle income countries is still very limited and quantitative data is most often related 
to western countries. Generation rates, statistical tests’ results and estimations drawn in the present 
study may serve as database for other researchers or institutions interested in improving 
management of disposable diapers in low-to-middle income rural areas. Future research should 
focus on characterizing and quantifying the environmental impacts caused by the mismanagement 
of disposable diapers explicitly in low-to-middle income countries. Financial and feasibility 
studies of the identified most potentially feasible solution, along with the other developed 
scenarios, should be carried out, for these were only presented in a draft form. Given the strong 
influence and the complex nature of taboos, specifically related to disposable diapers, furtherly 
investigating social and anthropological aspects is highly recommended to allow the 
implementation of a more effective improvement plan.

Appendix 2: Focus Group Methodology 
 
Focus groups did not have the objective of giving the participants any kind of indication on how to diaper or 
what the best and safest disposal practice is. It was not an informative meeting. 
Participants should not be helped or advised when asked to answer questions, for these are meant to help 
defining the investigated context as realistically as possible. 
 
The meeting evolved according to the following activities: 
 
- 
Introduce the research topic and objective. The research should be presented stressing impartiality 
towards either diapering method. It is rather crucial that mothers employing disposable diapers felt 
comfortable and by no means judged for their diapering choice.  
- 
Ask the participants what it is they wish the most for their children. Answers are to be reported on 
a poster. This activity is functional to create the link between health and misuse or mismanagement 
of disposable diapers.  
- 
By focusing on health, ask the participants to personally express the pros and cons of both types of 
diapering. Answers are to be enlisted on two posters, one for disposable, the other for cloth 
diapering. This activity has the main purpose of demonstrating to the participants that both types 
of diapers have advantages and disadvantages, especially that both can have health impacts 
according to their employment. It is generally meant to awake consciousness about beliefs and 
disbeliefs concerning diapering habits through sharing information among all.  
- 
Select two participants, one of each diaper method, willing to make a practical demonstration on 
how diapers are changed. The activity should be carried out pretending to be in their own homes, 
using representative items to simulate all the different areas of a house (kitchen, bedroom, 
bathroom/latrine, etc.). By doing so one should be able to detect whether mothers have the habit 
of washing their hands after diapering. Expectations are that if done so, it should also be naturally 
simulated during the demonstration.   
Ask the participants to expose all the different disposal methods for disposable diapers. This should stimulate 
the participants to argue about what the best practice should be by incorporating opinions and existing 
awareness. All useful information should be noted down.

FACTSHEET 1 - Latrine 
Description 
Simple pit latrines are an economical short-term solution to effectively 
prevent human waste from becoming a source of disease spread. 
Feces and urine are deposited through a hole inside a pit of significant 
depth, dug into the ground. For a latrine to properly function, it is 
necessary to keep it clean and protected from rainwater, besides 
maintaining the hole covered.   
Although burying waste is not environmentally sound, discarding 
disposable diapers in latrines ensures effective isolation of waste and 
excreta from human contact and animal vectors which could, 
otherwise, jeopardize the hygienic conditions of the environment. 
Process 
Only disposable diapers and human excreta should be introduced into the latrine. As soon as the 
latrine reaches saturation level, the hole should be permanently covered and a new latrine dug in 
a different area. 
Technical 
recommendations

2-8 meters deep, keeping the bottom at least 2 meters above aquifers;

• 
The pit should be dug at least 30 meters away from a water spring; 
• 
A protective structure to guarantee privacy and shelter from rain should be built; 
• 
A geological feasibility study may be required.

FACTSHEET 2 – Neighborhood Waste Pits 
Description 
The goal of a controlled waste pit is to provide families with a designated place to bury disposable 
diapers in order to avoid their exposure to community members. However, burying either organic 
or inorganic waste is not an environmentally sound solution, for it can contaminate soil and 
groundwater through the release of pollutants and leachate infiltration. 
Process 
It is recommended to keep the pits operative only in specific hours. During operation, the person 
in charge shall constantly monitor the pit to ensure proper disposal and limit it to disposable 
diapers only. At the end of every shift, a layer of dry soil should be added to limit bad odors and 
the pit should be kept safely locked until beginning the following shift; this should avoid the 
introduction of inadequate waste during night hours and deny access to animal carriers. As soon 
as the pit reaches saturation level, it should be permanently covered with a final layer of soil, 
making a mount in the center, and use a newly dug pit. 
Technical 
recommendations 
• 
3 to 8 meters deep, keeping the bottom at least 2 meters above aquifers; 
• 
Width of the hole should be calculated according to the number of babies within the 
coverage area and the expected lifespan of the pit; 
• 
The hole must be surrounded by a small berm and a ditch to prevent rainwater from 
flowing inwards. Ideally, apply a thick layer of clay at the bottom and to the pit’s walls to 
prevent leaching into the surrounding ground, as well as prevent water intrusion into 
the hole; 
• 
Shelter from rainwater should be built and a safe locking system provided to avoid out-
of-hours disposal and access of animal vectors. 
• 
A small hut for the person in charge to shelter him/herself during hours of operation 
should be built; 
• 
Geological feasibility study may be required. 
Location

Easy access on foot;

Flat surface;

At least 100 meters from residential areas and considerably far from school areas;

FACTSHEET 4 - Composting 
Description 
Microbiological process through which 
organic matter is transformed in stable, 
dark brown, soil-like material called 
compost. This process occurs as a result 
of microbial activity under aerobic 
conditions (in presence of oxygen). 
Compost improves soil structure and 
increases the nutrients availability in the 
soil.  
Process 
Disposable diapers, previously shredded, along with organic waste and additional garden waste is 
piled up in long heaps (windrows). These heaps need to be turned in order to maintain a given 
porosity level and oxygen content. Turning also ensures the degradation of all the material and 
mixes cooler and hotter portions of the pile. Depending on the moisture level of the material, a 
liquid product called leaching may be generated. This liquid can be recycled to increase moisture in 
the heap whenever necessary. However, it is necessary to integrate a drainage system since 
leaching can be highly toxic if discarded in the soil. At the end of the maturation period, the product 
must undergo a sieving process to separate plastic residues. 
Technical 
recommendations 
• 
Process duration: 3 to 5 months; 
• 
Surface area:  depends on the inflow of waste to be treated; 
• 
Infrastructure: construction of a sheltered area with concrete surface; 
• 
Screening device from street dogs and flies; 
• 
Leaching drainage system (ideally allowing liquid to recycle); 
• 
Volume waste reduction: 35-40%; 
• 
Thermometer and a pH-meter needed. 
Location 
• 
Easy access by car; 
• 
Possibly where access path is not uphill; 
• 
Flat surface; 
• 
At least 100 meters from residential areas.

FACTSHEET 5 - De Montfort Incinerator 
Description 
The De Montfort incinerator guarantees a 
minimum temperature of 800oC with a residence 
time of more than 1 second, largely destroying the 
waste and helping to reduce the environmental 
burden of mismanaged accumulated waste 
volumes. Many international organizations 
recommend De Montfort incinerators (WHO, 
UNICEF) as they are recognized as cheap but 
effective incinerators and can be built with locally 
available materials. If operated correctly, the 
incinerator is a good option for managing various 
types of hazardous waste. It is discouraged (and 
even prohibited in most countries) to put non-
hazardous plastic waste in the incinerator, as incomplete combustion could cause great 
levels of pollution. 
Process 
The incinerator comprises two combustion chambers, primary and secondary. 
Waste is heated, dried and melted in the primary combustion chamber, before burning 
in the main combustion chamber grille. Partially burned combustion gases and 
particulate matter are extracted from this primary area into the secondary chamber, 
where additional air induces secondary burning before the flue gases are evacuated 
into the atmosphere through the chimney. 
Technical 
recommendations 
 
• Area required: 5-8 m2 
• Infrastructure: Local materials 
• Lifespan: Min. 5 years 
• Fuel input: Demand depends on the calorific value of the waste 
• Temperature should be maintained above 600oC to allow a complete 
combustion process and ensure the elimination of pathogens, while reducing 
the risk of harmful gas emissions. 
• Because disposable diapers are a highly moisturized residue, an additional type 
of dry waste should be added (it is crucial to avoid the introduction of products 
made of PVC). 
Location 
• One incinerator per community/neighborhood 
• Flat surface 
• Smoke should not reach homes, public buildings or crops (min. 150 m away)
