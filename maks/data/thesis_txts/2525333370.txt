Abstract
Analysis of two-dimensional (2D) materials at the atomic-scale using High-Resolution Trans-
mission Electron Microscopy (HRTEM) is limited by imaging materials under different con-
ditions due to the presence of defects and the difficulty in being able to identify physical
structures of materials.
This research attempts to study HRTEM images using Convolutional Neural Networks
(CNN) with a human assisted approach. The two CNNs used in the experiments for perform-
ing atom-wise image segmentation, viz. the fusion architecture (Unet + Fully Convolutional
Neural Networks) and the Mixed-Scale Deep Convolutional Neural Network (MSD-CNN). We
have simulated datasets of monolayer MoS2 TEM images, with an additional graphene
substrate, are generated on the ﬂy with user-deﬁned microscope parameters using the
PYQSTEM software. Experimental HRTEM datasets were obtained on industrial-style sam-
ples with MoS2 deposited on a graphene substrate, which was imaged in low and high
noise conditions using the TEAM 0.5 microscope. The CNNs are trained and tested on a
focal series of three simulated TEM images.
The outcome of using trained CNNs to make predictions in experimental images shows
that it is possible to identify individual atomic species from 2D material HRTEM experimen-
tal datasets. Hyperparameter tests were performed on the fusion and MSD-CNN architec-
tures to tune the network conﬁguration. It is shown that tuned networks are capable of
classifying individual atoms and 2D nanomaterials with a higher accuracy from experimen-
tal HRTEM image focal series.

Introduction
Nowadays, modern oil and petroleum reﬁning methods used by industries are failing to
cope for the harmful emissions and are demanding for more clean sources of energy due
to strict environmental norms. Air pollution has been increasing over the past decades and
has had adverse effects on human health [1]. Combustion of fossil fuels emit sulphur diox-
ide which is harmful to the environment.
Catalysts, being one of the key cornerstones in industrial oil reﬁneries, are used to reduce
the impurity levels in oils by pre-treating them [2]. One of the early processes involving the
use of catalysts in treating oil is hydrodesulfurization (HDS) [3]. Catalysts, such as molyb-
denum disulphide (MoS2) are used in the hydrodesulfurization processes to remove nearly
2500 million tonnes of harmful sulfur annually from being released into the atmosphere [3].
The beneﬁt of using catalysts in treating fuel and other catalyst-based applications are yet
to be fully understood at the atomic-scale. Electron microscopes have opened doors to
shed more light on understanding material properties and their physical structures at the
atomic-scale regime to a great extent. Studying materials in the nano-scale plays a pivotal
role in discovering new 2D materials. Computational methods have made it possible to
have a 2D material Database consisting of 1500 new 2D materials with 30 different crystal
structures [4]. One of the limitations that researchers face when they need to conﬁrm phys-
ical structures or geometries of materials, is to have an accurate prediction of the materials
that are present in the experimental sample.
Machine learning has shown a growing interest in computer-aided diagnosis in pathol-
ogy, medical image segmentation and material science [5]. Transmission electron microscopy
images have a lot of information in them when used to study atomic structures. Interpret-
ing samples with defects or uneven structures can be difficult for the operator to tell apart
with the naked eye. A challenge faced by microscopists while predicting images is setting
up the right choice of parameters to obtain images with accurate information. Sometimes,
manual reconstruction of information can be time consuming in interpreting data. Trans-
mission electron microscopy images can be challenging to study when trying to distinguish
atomic species due to change in contrast over regions of the samples, making it hard to
compare the experimental and theoretical predictions.
Deep learning models can help in overcoming these challenges, when trained on sim-
ulated microscopic images. However, the problem underlying is learning the correct in-
formation that needs to be extracted from images to obtain more physical meaning from

2.2
Transmission Electron Microscope
A transmission electron microscope is unlike any other microscope which works on a simi-
lar principle as a light microscope but uses electrons for imaging. It was discovered in the
year 1931 by Ernst Ruska and Max Knoll [6]. It is used to investigate and characterise speci-
mens down to atomic columns with a local area ﬁeld of 100nm to a few micrometre ranges.
Optical microscopes are limited by the diffraction limit of light and its resolution factor
can be an obstruction in studying material at the sub-Angstrom level. Rayleighs criterion
(Equation 2.1) describes the theoretical resolution limit, which is the smallest resolvable
distance between two points. The wavelength λ of the illuminating source and the objec-
tive numerical aperture NA = n sin θ where n is the refractive index of the medium and
θ the aperture angle. From Equation 2.1, the resolution of a electron microscope can be
increased by using a electron source which has a shorter wavelength.

Principal setup of TEM

2.3.0.1
Aberrations
We expect the electrons are hitting the sample of interest to reproduce the complete im-
age (ideal TEM) of the sample, which does not happen in conventional TEM due to many

Artiﬁcial neural networks
Artiﬁcial Neural networks (ANN) are inspired by neurons in a human brain which are con-
nected by synapse to each other and are capable of communicating to the another neuron
via synapse using signals. Similar to that of neurons, artiﬁcial neural networks use nodes
which are connected by weights and are capable of making predictions given some inputs
on desired targets using mathematical models. Neural networks learn through three differ-
ent learning practices namely supervised, unsupervised and reinforcement learning. Today,
some of the most successful areas where they are being used are computer vision, speech
recognition, video game, healthcare, agriculture and robotics [16,17].

Some key characteristics of a CNN are:
• Spatial hierarchies of patterns: Every layer in a CNN is capable of learning different
features from the given input.
• Sparse weights: Performing a convolution with the input image uses a small portion
of weights from the kernel for computation, indicating only a small potion of neurons
are active. This reduces the memory requirements for computation [19].
• Parameter sharing: Refers to using the same set parameters over the input image for
more than one time compared to the neural net, which uses weights only once for
computing the output from a layer and a different set of weights for another layer.
•
Equivariant representations: Parameter sharing causes a layer to be equivariant to
translation implying that the input changes the same way as the output [21].

layer is a 3D volume of neuron activations. Each convnet has a Kernel which behaves as a
ﬁlter with same weights (weights) and a bias term.

4.2.1
MoS2 random
Firstly, the MoS2 random dataset is generated using an algorithm to create a square 2D
space (Figure 4.1 a) with randomly distributed seed points with a minimum separation
in terms of Euclidean distance (i.e. Poisson distribution). Secondly, it is followed by the
formation of Voronoi tessellates (Figure 4.1 b) with the seed points generation, and then
the vertices of the diagram are converted into atomic positions, disregarding the initial
positions, and then the Voronoi vertices are relaxed using Llyods algorithm (Figure 4.1 c).
Holes of varying sizes are randomly cropped in the structure, to match edges or various
shapes similar to experimental data.
4.2.2
MoS2 regular
MoS2 regular dataset (Figure 4.2) is built using the ASE mx2 module [11]. However, the
structure is modiﬁed from its ideal geometry by removing 20 percent of the atomic species
at random positions, moving random atomic species in the geometry by 0.3 nm away from
the ideal positions and cropping it in random shapes (refer Figure 4.2). Our motivation
towards the modiﬁcation of previous data is focused on training the CNNs with MoS2 regular
structures and then validate its performance from predictions made on experimental data.

5.5.1
Molybdenum disulphide (MoS2)
MoS2 belongs to a class of 2D materials classiﬁed as a Transition Metal Dichalcogenide
(TMD). A single layer of MoS2 has two sulfur atoms, one above and one below the molyb-
denum atom held by covalent bonds and MoS2 layers held by strong van der Waals forces.
Previous band-gap engineering studies have shown MoS2 to overcome the disadvantages
of graphene making it a promising material in 2D electronic application enabling thin tran-
sistors with high on-off ratio, intrinsic voltage gain and catalysis [37].

5.5.2
Graphene
Graphene is known as the wonder material, which consists of carbon atoms arranged in
a 2D honeycomb lattice structure with a bond length of 1.42Å. Graphene exists in two
forms known as zig-zag and the arm-chair conﬁguration. Graphene is a zero band gap ma-
terial which behaves like a semi-metal. Its importance in the industry today has increased
because of its exceptional optical, mechanical and electronic properties as well as its large-
scale production feasibility compared to other 2D materials. A few common application of
graphene today are touch screens, solar cells and coating [38,41].

5.9
Hyperparameter tuning
To improve the network performance we performed hyperparameter tuning tests as it was
not done by the authors [31]. Hyperparameter tuning plays a vital role in restructuring
the network architecture and improving its performance. We have done two rounds of
hyperparameter tuning for two different types of CNN. As the previous CNN conﬁguration
showed some limitations, we have tuned the kernel size, channels, layers and skip layers.
However, other network criteria such as the model optimizer, loss functions, learning rate
and regularization were already tested by the authors and kept the same.
Figure 5.6 depicts all the parameters that were considered in tuning the CNN architec-
ture. The outcome of our analysis showed that having skip connections played a critical role
in the networks architecture. Turning it off downsized the performance drastically, Similarly
using a large kernel proved beneﬁcial in capturing a large receptive ﬁeld and also the im-
proving the performance. The residual layers were kept the same and the input channels
were reduced to 16. Once we increased the residual layers and channels, we can see a big
decline in the test performance of the CNN.
All the CNNs with the conﬁguration K9-Ch16-l3-ST were trained using our generated
datasets and tested on provided experimental images. After the hyperparameter tuning
tests, we have seen some improvements in the neural networks ability to predict atomic
structures accurately. However, training these CNNs seemed to use around 12 million pa-
rameters. Therefore, we switched onto a newer architecture called the MSD-CNN. It uses
approximately 50,000 training parameters with 100 layers, 1 channel and a 3×3 kernel
size.
Figure 5.7 shows the parameters we have chosen while tuning the MSD-CNN. All of the
other parameters such as loss function and optimizers were kept the same. We have used
a dilation rate ranging from 1 to 10 for every ten layers until the user deﬁned range. All
MSD-CNN conﬁgurations trained on various datasets in the experimental section from this
point onwards use the ﬁnal conﬁguration shown in Figure 5.7.

Conclusion
In this thesis we provide a deeper insight for improving the performance of the CNN de-
veloped by Jakob Madsen et al. using new approaches. Prior to this study, the authors
have used gold nanoparticles, the graphene random dataset for training and testing the
model [31]. They have shown that it is possible to predict 2D materials such as graphene
and gold nanoparticles using the mentioned network architecture in the above mentioned
paper with 92% precision .
Whilst the previous study did not consider generating more regular atomic models for
preparing training sets, we had started off by investigating on the regular dataset and reg-
ular with graphene substrate datasets and ﬁnally mixing them both into one dataset. The
MoS2 data is more suited for the CNN to learn and make accurate predictions over other
datasets. The regular dataset used to train the CNN makes it hard for the network to be able
to distinguish atomic species present in the samples. This trend is seen to occur with all net-
works trained on this dataset. Our next step involved doing a series of hyperparameter tests
in search of a better architecture (K9-L3-Ch16-ST). Despite the large number of parame-
ters involved in training the K9-L3-Ch16-ST CNN, signiﬁcant improvements were observed
in predicting the MoS2 nanoparticles. The network is capable of detecting the atoms more
vividly in high and low noise data circumstances, whereas the architecture used in the orig-
inal paper ﬁnds it challenging to capture regions of the nanoparticle present in the image.
Some of our studies involved downsampling by a factor of four instead of two, changing the
input image focal series count from 3 to 5 to make improvements to the K9-L3-Ch16-ST
CNN performance. Our analysis showed that the network’s performance decreases dras-
tically on doing so. All the networks are trained with a monolayer MoS2 and are capable
of predicting MoS2 from experimental images even in the presence of other materials like
graphene and cobalt.
Doing these hyperparameter tests has shown that skip connections play an important
role in preserving features at different levels of the network. Another learning aspect which
we found very beneﬁcial in choosing a new neural network architectures was the kernel
size. Using a large kernel size of 12 × 12 worked well for all networks while training and test-
ing. However, we have chosen a kernel size of 9 × 9 for computational ease. Fourier ﬁltered
images are given to the network for making predictions to improve its performance. The
outcome of using Fourier ﬁltered images as an input to the network works well with only
networks trained on the random dataset. This technique proved to be beneﬁcial at the time
of identifying unknown regions present in the samples and was used to validate samples

Further improvements were not possible to be made to the K9-L3-Ch16-ST CNN. Based
on some of the learning outcomes from earlier attempts, we decide to target networks that
can use a larger kernel size and use more layers but lesser parameters for training. Our next
steps in improving the networks uses the MSD-CNN architecture. The MSD-CNN uses a
larger receptive ﬁeld due the dilation rate, and can preserve features on different scales.
This network shows great performance while training and testing compared to predicting
on experimental images. It was seen to be a more suitable choice for separately identify-
ing atomic species than the K3-L3-Ch32-ST architecture. Another new learning outcome
from this network was that we required only four channels and 100 layers for learning by
with setting the initial kernel size set to 3 × 3 with a dilation rate ranging from 1 to 10. This
network has shown to work well with high and low noise experimental TEM data.
Comparing to the K9-L3-Ch16-ST and K3-L3-Ch32-ST CNNs, the MSD-CNN can ﬁnd
nanoparticles from images more clearly and is capable of distinguishing the graphene ﬂake
underlying the sample. The MSD-CNN works well when trained with the mixed dataset than
others. Predicting the exact sequence of atomic species has been a major challenge from
Data 2. This restricts the ability of our network to make an exact match between original
experimental data used and the network’s prediction made on the experimental data. Our
ﬁndings show that the regular series dataset should be discarded as the network performs
poorly when trained on this dataset. Using drop out regularization for improvement in our
CNN is almost negligible and can be disregarded.
Our conclusion allows to conﬁrm that using the mixed-scale approach DCNN is a more
robust architecture which is easily trainable, capable of making more accurate predictions
and can distinguish atomic species in samples of MoS2 2D materials even in the presence
of noise. Therefore, making it our choice of network for 2D material analysis. The next
chapter discusses about the problems and some alternative approaches to improve the
CNN performance.

Further Outlook
The experiments performed using the trained CNNs in this thesis show that the results in-
dicate both improvements and some uncertainties to cross paths. The next few steps that
need to be taken in order to narrow down some of the uncertainties are, ﬁrstly, stabilizing
the dataset for training, secondly, modifying the network’s architecture and ﬁnally, making
the network more adaptable for all 2D materials analysis.
The CNNs trained using the four datasets prepared, show very different degrees of predic-
tion on the experimental data containing MoS2 with a large graphene ﬂake. We have been
able to conclude, that the regular dataset and the use of graphene substrate at the time
of training need to be removed as they degrade the performance of the network. The hy-
perparameter tuned CNN architecture performs well on the random dataset, whereas the
MSD-CNN is better adapted with training on the mix dataset. The methodology of generat-
ing datasets can be standardized by using a more optimal method to generate datasets like
the MoS2 random dataset generation. This can serve purposeful while training other CNNs
to predict all 2D materials with more ease. Creating TEM images from atomic models can
turn out to be laborious. This method can be replaced with a TEM database consisting
of labelled pre-trained microscopy images. One of the major problems that needs to be
addressed are the defects that are present in experimental samples. Training the CNN in
being able to distinguish defects or unknown materials from the sample, can reduce the
network’s confusion from regions that are not of interest.
A plethora of newly built neural networks are generated every day for commercial appli-
cations and it may be challenging to ﬁnd the right choice of network for predicting atoms
from TEM images. However, the MSD-CNN is the preferred choice over other networks that
were trained and tested, because it is very straight forward in its implementation and has
shown to be effective while predicting labels off our experimental images. One of the most
challenging tasks was knowing the exact positions of atomic species location and their
occurring sequence from experimental images. This makes it difficult to conﬁrm the pre-
dictions made by the neural network to real experimental data. However, a method has
shown a possibility to be able to reconstruct the exit wavefunction of TEM images using
deep learning methods, which can bridge the gap between predictions and original sam-
ple more precisely [43]. This research work can be of great beneﬁt to both academia and
the material science industry in the coming years.
