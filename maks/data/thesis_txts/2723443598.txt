Abstract 
 
Attempts have been made to develop a model for describing the gas-phase temperature 
development in fire compartments with interior CLT surfaces. The performed attempts are 
based on the experimental results from three different large-scale experiments with various 
CLT configurations. The enclosures of all large-scale experiments are reflecting ventilation-
controlled fire compartments in residential buildings. 
 
The attempts have shown that the traditional opening factor method is not reasonable to use 
as the available compartment types, containing non-combustible materials, are not able to 
reproduce temperature-time curves which match the results from the large-scale experiments. 
Attempts with modified opening factor formulas, which account for the exposed CLT area 
within a fire compartment, have neither been found useful. 
 
The energy balance equation has also been used to produce data points for the temperature 
time curve. However, the established temperature development has not been accurate. 
 
Lastly, a trial-and-error approach has been performed where self-developed compartment 
types have been established based on the experimental results. It has been found possible to 
establish compartment types that will reproduce a temperature development which matches 
the large-scale experiments. Attempts have been made to validate the trial-and-error 
approach. The validation is based on other large-scale experiments. The approach is not fully 
validated because of the differences between the actual fuel load densities of the two 
experiments. 
 
The final part of the thesis concerns the real-life “Arena Boliger” project where residential 
compartments with modern designs are built. Three compartment types are analysed using 
the trial-and-error approach. Using self-developed compartment types (various CLT 
configurations) the influence on the gas-phase temperature development is analysed. 
It is noticed that the self-developed compartment type from the validation section of the 
thesis is not providing expected temperature developments while the results from the 
remaining four self-developed compartment types are found reasonable.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Motivation 
Over the last years there has been an increased demand for using timber in the building 
industry even though timber has been a very popular building material for centuries. 
There are many possibilities of using timber in the building industry as the material provides 
a lot of advantages. Timber has great mechanical properties whereby it may be used for load-
bearing structures. Timber is also performing well as an insulating material and therefore 
timber may be used in external constructions, e.g. walls and roofs. From an aesthetic point of 
view the texture of timber and the many possible ways of shaping timber components are 
allowing architects to achieve unique interior and exterior building designs. Lastly, with an 
increased global focus on sustainability, it has been proved by life-cycle analysis that timber 
manufacturing is a lot more sustainable compared to other traditional building materials, e.g. 
concrete and steel. Hence, the society demands a higher use of timber to achieve more 
sustainable buildings as stated by Barber [1]. 
 
The desire of an increased use of timber within buildings has faced negative perceptions in 
terms of the fire safety design as many national building regulations restrict the use of 
exposed combustible materials in enclosures. Likewise, several building regulations restrict 
the use of combustible load-bearing structures for buildings with a certain use and height. 
Fire safety is therefore assessed as one of the main barriers when it comes to the additional 
use of timber. It is therefore necessary to revisit compartment fire behaviour to be able to 
understand fire dynamics when more timber is used and how this additional use affects the 
traditional way of designing buildings in terms of fire safety. 
 
Buildings in timber have traditionally been limited to a few stories, typically 3-4 stories. 
The desire of using timber for taller and more complex buildings has pushed towards new 
timber products. These timber products have been named Engineered Wood Products (EWP). 
The EWPs consist of timber components which are glued together using an adhesive. 
The timber products may be produced in many configurations using different types of wood 
and adhesives, respectively. The timber components may also have different thicknesses 
while the orientation of the components may be different when these are glued together. 
Glue-Laminated Timber (GLT), Laminated Veneer Lumber (LVL) and Cross-Laminated 
Timber (CLT) are currently the most common EWP products. The EWPs have made it 
possible to design high-rise buildings in timber as they provide improved properties 
compared to the traditional raw timber products. However, the introduction of EWPs has also 
led to the arrival of new phenomena, especially in terms of fire safety, which need to be 
addressed to avoid unsafe building designs. It is therefore important to understand these 
phenomena and the change in fire dynamics of buildings in timber to be able to minimize the 
risk of life safety and to minimize the risk of a building collapse. 
 
When designing a building in terms of fire safety, it is desired to predict the gas-phase 
temperature development within fire compartments. In 1970 Magnusson and Thelandersson 
[2] developed a method to calculate time graphs of temperature of combustion gases for 
characteristic types of enclosed spaces. The method was called “the opening factor method” 
==PAGE:10==
10 
 
and is still used up to this date. However, the characteristic types of enclosed spaces 
(compartments) are all based of non-combustible materials, e.g. concrete, bricks and steel 
sheetings. Thus, the opening factor method is challenged when CLT is used on the interior 
surfaces of a compartment, and it is hard to tell how the method will be affected when 
combustible interior linings are used. 
 
In the present thesis it is therefore analysed whether the traditional opening factor method is 
applicable when the exposed enclosing construction in a fire compartment consists of 
combustible materials (CLT). Using the traditional opening factor method attempts are made 
to predict temperature-time curves which match the test results of large-scale experiments. 
The results from the large-scale experiments have been reported by other people. Hence, 
experiments are not conducted during the thesis. Where the traditional opening factor method 
loses its applicability, it is attempted to develop an alternative method which accounts for the 
combustible interior linings whereby reasonable gas-phase temperature developments may be 
described. 
 
The primary focus of the thesis is the use of exposed CLT linings within ventilation-
controlled fire compartments. Thus, the found approach to describe the temperature 
developments is transferred to an actual building project in Ørestad, Copenhagen, where 
residential compartments are built. The building project is called “Arena Boliger” and 
concerns several new residential compartments in two buildings, L1 and L2. 
The L1 and L2 building contain 7 and 8 stories above ground level, respectively. 
The compartments have a modern design in terms of geometry, but they are not designed 
with exposed interior surfaces of timber. Instead, traditional interior surfaces are mainly used. 
Hence, the residential compartments are analysed because of their geometry while the 
configuration of exposed timber will be fictional to assess how the gas-phase temperature 
will change when CLT is placed within the compartments. The temperature development will 
be compared to traditional compartments with non-combustible interior linings to evaluate 
the influence of a given CLT configuration. The ISO 834 standard fire curve will also be 
shown along with the results throughout the thesis. 
 
At the end of the thesis it is assessed whether the found approach is reasonable to use and its 
limitations while comments are made on whether the ISO 834 standard fire curve is an 
appropriate fire curve for the classification of building components when fire compartments 
are built with combustible materials in its enclosing construction.

2 Literature Review 
2.1 Background of CLT 
Cross-Laminated Timber (CLT) is a mass timber product which in the late 1990s had its 
origin in central Europe [3]. CLT is formed from multiple layers of timber and is typically 
consisting of an uneven number of layers which are bonded together with an adhesive. 
In general three, five or seven layers are used. Layers may be individual timber boards or 
panels. Boards in the same layer are often finger jointed together using a structural adhesive. 
The layer thickness of boards ranges from 16-51 mm while the width of these ranges from 
60-240 mm. The layer thickness of panels is up to 508 mm while the typical width of these is 
0.6 m, 1.2 m, 2.4 m and 3.0 m. The length of panels can be up to 18 m [4]. 
 
Layers are bonded such that the orientation of the grain is perpendicular between each layer 
whereby a quasi-rigid connection is formed through the continuous adhesive bonding. 
The concept of forming CLT components using timber boards is shown in Figure 1. 
 
 
Figure 1: Example of a CLT component (Source: Fig. 3 in CLT Handbook [4]). 
 
Timber has great mechanical properties in the direction of the grain. The perpendicular 
connections of the layers will therefore result in a more homogeneous behaviour of the CLT 
components where the components perform well in two directions. For CLT walls, the outer 
layers are often oriented vertically while CLT floors and roofs, respectively, often have the 
outer layers in the major span direction. This explains the common choice of using an uneven 
number of layers for CLT components.

13 
 
2.2 Effects on the fire safety when using CLT 
The choice of using timber for the interior linings within an enclosing construction challenges 
the traditional approaches for the fire safety design of compartments. Many design methods 
are developed by assuming that the interior linings are non-combustible, e.g. gypsum 
plasterboards, bricks and concrete. The fire dynamics can change significantly when a 
combustible material like CLT is used for the interior linings which means that the traditional 
approaches may lose their reliability. The traditional approaches must therefore be evaluated 
and used with caution. 
 
From previous studies it has been reported that new phenomena arise when CLT is used 
within enclosures. Hence, additional design objectives need to be met to obtain an acceptable 
fire safety design. Some of the most common phenomena when using CLT are described 
below. 
2.2.1 Self-extinction 
Many national building regulations are restricting the use of combustible materials for the 
load-bearing construction in tall buildings while the amount of exposed timber within the 
building must be limited. These restrictions ensure, among other things, that the amount of 
additional fuel load from the components of the building is limited whereby the contribution 
to the fire of the combustible materials are minimized. 
 
When a traditional building experiences a fire and a complete burnout, the fire will be present 
until all fuel has been consumed. In a building with exposed timber it is uncertain whether the 
timber will be totally consumed during a burnout. If the timber is consumed problems may 
occur in terms of the fire compartmentation and the structural stability of the building. 
The fire safety design of a timber building must therefore focus on obtaining a fire which 
decays and self-extinguishes before any critical events occur. 
 
The ability of timber to self-extinguish has been known for many years. However, with the 
recent popularity of investigating tall timber buildings, studies have been made on how to 
obtain self-extinguishment and implement this design objective in the fire safety design of 
timber buildings. Currently there are different opinions about the definition of self-extinction 
and how to measure this. Self-extinguishment is in general divided into three categories: 
flame extinction (cease of flaming), smouldering extinction (cease of smouldering) and 
complete extinction. The extinction may be measured by analysing the burning rate of the 
fuel or by observing an increase in the generation of carbon monoxide. The magnitude of the 
incident radiant heat flux to an exposed timber surface and the airflow across the timber may 
also be analysed to determine whether extinction will occur [5]. Studies of the ranges of 
incident heat fluxes for which self-extinction can occur have also been performed by 
Emberley et al. [6]. 
 
Studies on self-extinguishment of timber have been performed by Rasbash, Drysdale and 
Deepak [7] where it has been found that timber is not able to generate enough heat itself to 
sustain continuous flaming. Thus, continuous flaming may only occur when the exposed 
timber surface is subjected to an external heat flux. 
 
 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
In a fire compartment external radiant heat fluxes may be found from the traditional fuel load 
or adjacent exposed timber surfaces. Investigations of the three stages of self-extinction have 
been reported by Crielaard [5] while Hadden et al. [8] and have reported experiments with 
compartments with various exposed CLT configurations to study whether self-
extinguishment can be obtained for a given design. Even though a lot of research has been 
made on self-extinguishment, the phenomenon is still not entirely covered. 
2.2.2 Thermal feedback 
A fire within a traditional fire compartment with non-combustible interior linings will 
typically lead to the highest gas-phase temperatures at ceiling level. This is a result of the 
oxygen and pyrolysis gases which are mixing uniformly within the compartment. The hot 
gases will move towards the ceiling due to buoyancy as cold air enters the lower part of the 
compartment through its vertical openings. The hot gases are typically leaving the 
compartment at the top of the openings and causing a relatively small external flame. 
 
The fire dynamics inside the compartment change when exposed CLT is used as interior 
linings. The fire dynamics depend on the specific CLT configuration. The CLT configuration 
is a term which covers the amount and location of exposed CLT within the compartment. 
In a scenario where a lot of CLT is present, the oxygen and pyrolysis gases are not expected 
to mix uniformly within the compartment. Compared to the traditional scenario, the 
temperatures at ceiling level will be lower because of the low amount of oxygen and the 
smoke layer will therefore descend even further towards the floor. These fire dynamics cause 
an increase in momentum inside the compartment which will result in larger external flaming 
outside of the openings. 
 
The fire dynamics of the two scenarios are illustrated in Figure 2. The traditional scenario is 
named Regime I while the scenario with exposed CLT is named Regime II-CLT. 
 
 
Figure 2: Fire dynamics when exposed CLT surfaces are present (Source: Fig. 12 in Gorska et al. [9]). 
 
Traditional fire safety designs set up stricter criteria for the interior linings at the ceilings than 
for the interior linings at the walls. An example of this can be seen in the Danish national 
building regulation (Bygningsreglementet) for residential buildings [10]. 
The stricter criterion is implemented as the highest temperatures are expected at ceiling level 
within the compartment. Thus, the burning rate at the ceiling, and the resulting heat release 
rate, will be larger than at the walls. The stricter criteria may then be justified. 
 
 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
When exposed CLT is used, the change in fire dynamics results in the highest temperatures 
and burning rates at the walls. Thus, the interior linings at the walls should possibly be met 
with a stricter criterion than the interior linings at the ceilings for this configuration. 
Experiments by Gorska et al. [9] have been performed which show gas-phase temperatures 
profiles along the height of compartments with exposed CLT. These profiles show that gas-
phase temperatures are increasing when the amount of exposed CLT is increased. 
 
The profiles also show that the highest temperatures are present at a lower height. The gas-
phase temperatures are higher inside the compartment when compared to the measured gas-
phase temperatures in front of the opening of the compartment. The gas-phase temperature 
development is analysed in this thesis for various CLT configurations. 
2.2.3 Delamination 
In general the strength of the adhesive bonding layer of CLT is larger than the strength of 
timber. Under ambient conditions, the failure of CLT will usually occur in the timber and not 
in the adhesive. When the temperature at the adhesive layer becomes higher than ambient 
temperatures, the adhesive strength is reduced. The reduced strength may result in layers 
which will detach. This failure mode is called delamination and it can occur in both local and 
larger areas. Delamination of larger areas can result in whole layers falling off. 
 
In compartment fires delamination may occur when an exposed timber surface chars or when 
the heat transfer through the timber reaches the adhesive layer. When delamination takes 
place, objects can fall off the CLT component and expose a fresh inner timber layer. 
The exposed timber layers may ignite immediately and hence increase the overall fuel load of 
the fire compartment. The continuous burning of the timber layers will result in a longer 
duration of the compartment fire and may prevent the desired decay phase from taking place. 
 
Delamination of CLT components in compartments is usually not accepted in a fire safety 
design. It is therefore essential for the fire safety design to prevent delamination from 
occurring. Delamination can be handled by encapsulation of the combustible surfaces using 
non-combustible materials, e.g. gypsum plasterboards. The encapsulation system must be 
designed such that the CLT components are not igniting while the temperature increase at the 
adhesive layer is limited when heat transfers through the enclosing construction. 
 
Alternatively, delamination may be handled by using exposed CLT components where the 
exposed outer layer is made thicker. The adhesive layer will then be located deeper into the 
CLT component. The outer CLT layer will have an insulating effect because of the thermal 
properties of timber. It is here important that the charring layer remains in place during the 
fire. If the fire safety design is performed properly, temperature increase at the adhesive layer 
will not be critical and lead to delamination while a more aesthetic design is also achieved. 
2.2.4 External burning 
The presence of exposed timber within a fire compartment will typically result in larger 
external flaming compared to a traditional compartment with non-combustible interior 
linings. The larger external flaming is also a phenomenon which needs to be investigated and 
handled carefully as the risk of vertical fire spread becomes larger when the flaming outside 
the compartment increases. 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
The external flaming typically occurs after flashover, where all combustible materials are 
ignited, and in ventilation-controlled compartments where the available oxygen is limited. 
Thus, the pyrolysis gases and the oxygen are not mixing uniformly because of the lack of 
oxygen within the compartment whereby a momentum is built from the larger velocity fields. 
The momentum forces the pyrolysis gases outside to mix up with the available oxygen and 
combust. External flaming is leading to a risk for external fire spread. Because of buoyancy, 
the flaming is moving more vertically than horizontally outside of the compartment whereby 
the risk of vertical fire spread is typically the most important to address. 
 
The large flames and high temperatures from the external flaming have an increased risk of 
spreading to the upper storeys of the building. The windows, the exterior materials of the 
façade and the load-bearing components, respectively, should therefore be chosen carefully to 
reduce the risk of ignition when these are subjected to heat from the external flames. The size 
and location of the openings in compartments must be investigated while the installation of 
an automatic sprinkler system may contribute to a reduced risk of vertical fire spread. 
 
From investigations by Law [11] it has been found that external flaming is governed by the 
burning rate of the fuel and the temperature of the flame. The burning rate of the fuel controls 
the duration of the fire and the size of the flame. The temperature of the flame controls the 
magnitude of the radiant heat flux. The fire safety design must therefore aim at implementing 
solutions which account for the increased risk of external vertical fire spread. 
2.2.5 Summary and research questions 
It is seen that several new phenomena arise when CLT is used in buildings. All presented 
phenomena must be addressed to obtain a satisfying fire safety design whereby the fire safety 
design of buildings with exposed CLT become complex. It is assessed that the fire dynamics 
within fire compartments are easier to understand if the gas-phase temperature development 
can be predicted. The thesis will therefore evaluate the applicability of the commonly used 
opening factor method and use this method (if possible) to predict reasonable temperature-
time curves for various CLT configurations. 
 
The remainder of the thesis will not address the phenomena self-extinguishment, 
delamination and external burning. It is therefore assumed that self-extinguishment will take 
place and delamination is not occurring while external burning is not analysed. The effects 
from thermal feedback within fire compartments are studied as this is a key element to 
determine whether the traditional opening factor method is applicable. 
 
Hence, the gas-phase temperature developments within enclosures will be observed for 
different CLT configurations. These are hold up against calculated temperature developments 
based on the traditional opening factor method with non-combustible interior linings.

3 Theory on Fire Dynamics in Fire Compartments 
Fires are difficult to describe as they consist of heat transfer processes and several chemical 
reactions. The interaction between these is complex to describe for a whole fire process. 
One of the key elements which must be considered when describing a fire is the fuel type 
which is combusting. The quantity and geometry of the combustible material must also be 
considered while the distribution of the fuel within a fire compartment may influence the 
behaviour of the fire. The fire is also affected by the quantity of supplied air while the 
geometry of the fire compartment impacts the fire dynamics. Finally, the enclosing 
construction type and the thermal properties of this must be known to describe the fire in the 
best way. 
 
It is assessed that fire compartments with normal room geometries lead to small internal 
temperature differences because of the turbulence during a fire. Thus, a uniform temperature 
distribution within these fire compartments can be assumed. In larger fire compartments, 
where the fuel load is not distributed uniformly, the temperatures cannot be assumed as 
uniform in the entire compartment. The same case applies to compartments with great 
distances to the nearest ventilation opening, e.g. compartments with great depths. 
 
To describe a fire analytically, two types of fires have been developed. The first type is 
named a ventilation-controlled fire (also referred to as Regime I) as the rate of combustion 
during the flame phase is governed by the ventilation in the fire compartment. The mean rate 
of combustion within a fire compartment may be described using Eq. (1): 
 
 
𝑅𝑚= 𝑘∙𝐴𝑜∙√𝐻𝑜 
 
(1) 
Here,  𝑅𝑚 [kg/h] is the mean rate of combustion during the flame phase, 𝑘 [kg/(h ∙m2.5)] is 
a constant depending on the chemical composition of the fuel, 𝐴𝑜 [m2] is the total area of 
openings in the fire compartment while 𝐻𝑜 [m] is the equivalent height of openings in the fire 
compartment. The term 𝐴𝑜∙√𝐻𝑜 is also called the ventilation factor. The above Eq. (1) is 
applicable when the ratio between the surface area of the fuel and the ventilation factor 
exceeds a certain limit. Thus, the 𝑅𝑚 term is not influenced significantly by the magnitude of 
the fuel load and the rate of combustion will instead be controlled by the air supply. 
 
The second type of a fire is named a fuel-controlled fire (also referred to as Regime II). 
For this type of fire, all air flowing into the fire compartment is not consumed in the 
combustion process. The 𝑅𝑚 term is instead governed by the properties of the combustible 
material, e.g. its quantity and geometry.

fires. The mean rate of combustion as function of the ventilation factor is shown for different 
fuel loads, 𝑞𝑔 [kg/m2]. The fuel loads are measured per unit floor area.

The 𝑘 term of wood is in the range 300-360 kg/(h ∙m2.5). From the results it is seen that

becomes larger. The specific value at which the transition occurs varies as it depends on the 
magnitude, geometry and distribution of the fuel load within the compartment. The transition 
occurs at a larger value of the ventilation factor if the fuel load is increased or if the fuel load 
is distributed across the floor area.

Fuel-controlled fires are typically hard to define because of the characteristics of the fuel. 
Using the fire engineering design method, described in Pettersson et al. [12], it can generally 
be assumed that a compartment fire is controlled by ventilation. Hence the mean rate of 
combustion becomes independent of the magnitude of the fuel load, its geometry and 
==PAGE:19==
19 
 
distribution, respectively, and solely depends on the opening factor. The assumption of 
ventilation-controlled compartment fires is conservative as the gas-phase temperature will be 
overestimated. According to Pettersson et al. [12], this overestimation is commonly found to 
be relatively small whereby the assumption is found reasonable. 
 
Temperature-time curves may be predicted using the opening factor method. Here it is 
assumed that the fuel type has characteristics which are equal to wood while the enclosing 
construction is non-combustible. Thus, a standard fire compartment is developed using 
average values for concrete, brick and lightweight concrete. The above assumptions are found 
applicable for many traditional buildings, e.g. residential buildings, where the dominant fuel 
type is assumed to have combustion characteristics equal to wood while the enclosing 
construction of many buildings is built in concrete or bricks. 
 
Magnusson and Thelandersson [2] have developed temperature-time curves by collecting 
experimental results from four test series, containing approximately 30 data sets, while a 
computer program was used to solve the following energy balance: 
 
 
𝑄̇ = 𝑞̇𝐿+ 𝑞̇𝑊+ 𝑞̇𝑅+ 𝑞̇𝐵 
 
(3) 
Here 𝑄̇  [kW] is the energy release rate due to combustion, 𝑞̇𝐿 [kW] is the rate of heat lost due 
to replacement of hot gases by cold, 𝑞̇𝑊 [kW] is the rate of heat lost to compartment 
boundaries, 𝑞̇𝑅 [kW] is the rate of heat lost by radiation through openings, and 𝑞̇𝐵 [kW] is the 
rate of heat storage in the gas volume. The last term of the energy balance, 𝑞̇𝐵, covers the 
change in mass when the gases heat up in the compartment. The term is smaller than 1 % of 
the total energy balance in many compartment fires and is therefore often neglected.

Magnusson and Thelandersson developed the temperature-time curves by assuming that 𝑄̇ is 
ventilation-controlled during the fully developed stage while experimental data was used to 
describe the growth and decay phase, respectively. A complete combustion within the 
enclosing construction and uniformly distributed temperatures within the compartment was 
assumed while only a single surface heat transfer coefficient for the interior linings was used. 
Lastly, a one-dimensional heat flow to and through the enclosing construction was used with 
the boundaries having infinite thicknesses. 
 
The developed temperature-time curves allow for a simple way of calculating gas-phase 
temperatures as function of time based on the opening factor of the fire compartment, the fuel 
load within the compartment and the type of enclosing construction. When these parameters 
are known it is possible to plot temperature-time curves by reading off temperature-time data 
sets using tables in the report by Magnusson and Thelandersson [2]. 
 
To account for an enclosing construction which is different from the standard fire 
compartment, both the opening factor and the fuel load must be converted into equivalent 
values of these. The conversion is done by multiplying both the opening factor and the fuel 
load by a 𝐾𝑓 factor. This factor depends on the type of enclosing construction and the actual 
opening factor, calculated as in Eq. (2). In Table 1 the developed enclosing constructions are 
shown along with the corresponding 𝐾𝑓 factors of these. 
 
Temperature-time curves may be found in the same way as for the standard fire compartment 
using values of the calculated equivalent opening factor and fuel load, respectively. 
It is noticed that all enclosing construction types consist of non-combustible materials with 
different thermal properties. Thus, none of the eight compartment types are accounting for 
burning interior linings and the resulting impact of these on the fire dynamics. 
 
Today the opening factor method is still popular and used for the fire safety design of 
buildings. However, the method is challenged when it comes to modern compartment types 
with exposed CLT as none of the current compartment types account for combustible 
materials. It is therefore necessary to check whether experimental results of large-scale fire 
compartments with exposed CLT can be reproduced using the opening factor method. 
Large-scale experiments with exposed CLT are presented in Section 4 while attempts are 
made in Section 5 to develop a gas-phase temperature model which matches the experimental 
results.

4 Large-Scale Experiments with Timber 
Compartments 
To check whether the traditional opening factor method is reliable when exposed CLT 
surfaces are placed within enclosing construction, the method must be held up against 
experimental results from large-scale experiments. Large-scale experiments are a necessity to 
obtain experimental results which account for the previously described phenomenon thermal 
feedback. 
 
In this section a total of 3 different large-scale experiments are described. The experiments 
are performed by Brandon et al. [13], Gorska [14] and Pope et al. [15]. The development of a 
model to describe gas-phase temperature is based on all 3 large-scale experiments. 
 
The large-scale experiments by Brandon et al. will be used for the attempts in Section 5 and 
for the in Section 7. The large-scale experiments by Gorska and the experiments by Pope et 
al. will be used for a validation in Section 6 and for the analysis of the Arena Boliger 
compartments in Section 7. Hence, it is decided to present all experiments in the following. 
4.1 Experiments by Brandon et al. 
Brandon et al. performed five large-scale experiments and documented these in a RISE report 
in 2021 [13]. The overall aim of the study was to assess possible changes to prescribed limits 
of interior timber surfaces in the US code. The products in the experiments were therefore in 
compliance with US product standards. 
 
The five large-scale experiments were named Test 1, 2, 3, 4 and 5, respectively. 
The experiments were performed in a fire compartment with similar dimensions. The internal

4.3 Experiments by Pope et al. 
Pope et al. conducted a series of large-scale experiments with ventilation-controlled fire 
compartments with exposed timber. The large-scale experiments were reported in 2021 [15]. 
The aim of the experiments was to study how the fire dynamics are influenced by the fuel and 
the geometry of the fire compartment. A total of 7 experiments were conducted for different 
CLT configurations. One experiment was conducted using a wood crib while the remaining 
six experiments used a kerosene pool fire. 
 
As this thesis focuses on residential buildings, the fuel nature of wood cribs is more 
reasonable to study compared to kerosene pool fires. Kerosene has a much higher 
combustibility ratio (ratio between ΔH𝑐 and ΔH𝑣𝑎𝑝) compared to timber whereas flashover 
will occur earlier. Although the experiments by Pope et al. showed temperature-time curves 
of both fuel types which were comparable, the fire dynamics were very different because of 
the combustibility ratio of kerosene. The kerosene has large stoichiometric air requirements 
which resulted in large velocity fields within the fire compartment due to the limited amount 
of oxygen. The large-scale experiments using kerosene showed that the burning regime was 
dominated by momentum-driven flows while the single large-scale experiment using a wood 
crib resulted in a burning regime which was dominated by buoyancy. Thus, the six large-
scale experiments by Pope et al. using kerosene pool fires as fuel are not presented in the 
following. 
 
The large-scale experiment using a wood crib is named “Test B” in the following to 
differentiate from the experiments by Brandon et al. (Test 1-5) [13] and the experiment by 
Gorska (Test A) [14]. 
 
The internal dimensions of the fire compartment were 3.15 m x 3.15 m x 2.70 m (W x L x H). 
The geometry was chosen to reflect residential-style building occupancies. The fire 
compartment was built with a single vertical opening of 0.85 m x 2.10 m. The ventilation 
opening was in the front wall of the compartment at floor level, corresponding to a common 
door. Hence, the compartment had no ventilation openings in the left, right and rear wall, 
respectively, nor in the ceiling. The geometry of the large-scale experiment is shown below in

experiment by Gorska [14]. As Radiata Pine is used in both experiments, it is expected that

The present thesis will use the test results of the median gas temperatures as function of time. 
The results will be used in Section 6 and in Section 7. 
4.4 Summary 
The presented large-scale experiments are found reasonable to use for the purpose of this 
thesis as the large-scale fire compartments contain exposed CLT with different 
configurations. The geometry of the enclosures is comparable with fire compartments in 
residential buildings while wood is used as fuel in the experiments. This fuel type has a 
burning behavior which matches residential fires. However, it is important to note that the 
geometry of the compartments is not identical while the fuel load density per unit floor area 
varies a lot. 
 
Brandon et al. [13] reported a fuel load density of 𝑄𝑓
′′ = 560 MJ/m2. As the magnitude of the 
fuel load density was found through a probabilistic study it is assessed being the most 
reasonable. Hence, the used fuel load density by Gorska [14] is 82 % smaller while the fuel 
load density by Pope et al. [15] is 45 % smaller. The fuel load density of the large-scale 
experiments by Gorska and Pope et al. might therefore not reflect a residential fire.

5 Development of a Model for Gas-Phase 
Temperatures in CLT Compartments 
In this section attempts are made to reproduce the gas-phase temperature developments in the 
large-scale experiments by Brandon et al. [13]. The attempts are based on the opening factor 
method where temperature-time curves may be calculated as described in Section 3 of this 
thesis. 
 
Five different attempts are made. Section 5.1 shows attempts based on the opening factor 
method and a standard fire compartment (type A). Section 5.2 shows attempts based on the 
opening factor method using compartment type G and H, respectively. In Section 5.3 
attempts are made with modified opening factor formulas. Section 5.4 attempts to reproduce 
the experimental results using the energy balance equation, see Eq. (3). Finally, a trial-and-
error approach is used in Section 5.5 to introduce new self-developed compartment types for 
specific CLT configurations. 
 
The simulation software “Argos” [16] has also been used during the thesis. The software 
contains a zone model which can predict smoke spread, gas-phase temperatures, radiation etc. 
However, the results of the software have not been found useful as the software has not been 
able to model the decay phase of compartment fires as desired. The software is also not able 
to account for thermal feedback from exposed CLT surfaces and the location of these within 
the fire compartment. The attempts with Argos are therefore not reported in this thesis.

temperatures in the compartment while adjacent exposed CLT surfaces lead to longer fully

approximately t = 180 min. The ISO 834 continues to increase and is not modelled with a 
decay phase. Hence, the ISO 834 does neither reflect the produced nor experimental 
temperature-time curves in the fire compartment. More comments are made on the use of an

not considered as a heat sink. The total heat loss through the enclosing construction is 
therefore decreased when the CLT area is increased. Thus, the gas-phase temperature within 
the fire compartment is expected to become higher.

5.4 Attempts with the energy balance equation 
The attempts in this section concerns the energy balance equation which was presented in 
Section 3 of this thesis. From the energy balance equation it is possible to determine gas-
phase temperatures at a specific time. Thus, temperature-time data points may be found 
whereafter a function can be established. It is noted that the energy balance equation is not 
able to determine data points during the decay phase of the fire. Hence, the temperature-time 
curve will only show gas-phase temperatures during flashover and during the fully developed 
phase. The energy balance equation is presented again below and is both described in

5.5 Attempts with a trial-and-error approach 
The attempts in the previous sections have shown temperature-time curves which are not 
matching the experiments at RISE as desired. Since all the compartment types by Magnusson 
and Thelandersson [2] have enclosing constructions which consist of non-combustible 
materials, it is in this section attempted to develop self-made compartment types which will 
fit the temperature-time curves better. 
 
The self-made compartment types are found using a trial-and-error approach. Based on the 
observations from the previous sections, attempts are made to determine the equivalent 
opening factor and equivalent fuel load density. Temperature-time curves are then produced 
and compared to the test result of the respective experiment at RISE. If a better fit is 
obtained, 𝐾𝑓 factors can be found as the ratio between the equivalent and actual values. 
Hence, new compartment types may be developed for each CLT configuration.

5.6 Summary 
The performed attempts in Section 5 have shown that is difficult to reproduce temperature 
developments which match the experimental results by Brandon et al. [13]. The combination 
of the current opening factor method and its available compartment types will not reproduce 
the desired temperature-time curves. The attempts of modifying the opening factor formula 
have not provided more accurate results whereby the invented formulas are not useful in this 
context. The same applies to the performed attempts using the energy balance equation. 
 
However, the trial-and-error approach in Section 5.5 has been able to reproduce temperature-
time curves which match the experimental results at RISE. It must be noted that this approach 
is based on large-scale experimental results with a given CLT configuration. Hence, the trial-
and-error approach might not work when transferred to other large-scale experiments. 
It is therefore attempted to validate the approach in the following Section 6.

6 Validation of Self-Developed Compartment Types 
In Section 5 it was concluded that the trial-and-error approach, see Section 5.5, led to 
temperature-time curves which are closest to the experimental results by Brandon et al. [13]. 
Before this method may be used on the Arena Boliger compartments, attempts are made to 
validate the approach. The validation is performed by using a self-developed compartment 
type with a given CLT configuration on another large-scale experiment with an identical CLT 
configuration. It is hereby possible to check whether the produced temperature-time curve 
matches the experimental result. 
 
The present thesis is based on large-scale experiments by Brandon et al. [13], Gorska [14] 
and Pope et al. [15]. The experiments have been described in Section 4. The self-developed 
compartment types with interior wooden surfaces are based on the four different CLT 
configurations (Test 1, Test 2, Test 3, and Test 5) by Brandon et al. 
 
Neither the large-scale experiment by Gorska (Test A) nor the large-scale experiment by 
Pope et al. (Test B) have a CLT configuration which matches the self-developed 
compartment types. However, the CLT configuration of Test A and B, respectively, is nearly 
the same. Thus, it is decided to develop a fifth compartment type using the experimental 
results by Gorska whereafter this self-developed compartment type is used to predict the 
temperature-time function of Test B. If this is possible, the trial-and-error approach may be 
validated. 
 
The temperature-time curves of Test A and Test B are shown in Appendix H. 
Data points for the temperature-time curves are found using the online web-based tool 
“WebPlotDigitizer” as data files containing the experimental results for the large-scale 
experiments are not available. The tool is not able to extract the exact data of the figures in 
the appendix. However, it is still found useful for its purpose. 
 
The data points found using the tool are shown in Appendix I. In the appendix, 12 mins are 
subtracted from the x-coordinates (time) of Test A while 3 mins are added to the x-
coordinates of Test B to obtain flashover at t = 0 min. It is seen that the duration of Test A 
becomes 48 mins while the duration of Test B becomes 33 mins. The durations are small 
compared to the experiments by Brandon et al. which lasted up to 120 mins after flashover. 
 
Table 13 shows calculated opening factors and fuel load densities for the two large-scale 
experiments. From the table it is noticed that the opening factors are almost equal while the 
fuel load densities approximately vary by a factor 3.

During the decay phase gas-phase temperature differences of up to 200 °C are seen which is 
mainly a result of the different durations of the fully developed phases. 
 
The temperature-time curve of compartment type T-A shows much higher temperatures than 
the other curves which is a result of the larger 𝐾𝑓 factors, especially the 𝐾𝑓,𝐹𝐿𝐷 factor. 
Thus, the gas-phase temperature becomes higher while the duration of the fully developed 
phase lasts longer. It is seen that the fully developed phase of T-A ends approximately 15 
mins after flashover while gas-phase temperatures are up to 1200 °C. The decay phase has 
higher temperatures which is expected because of the temperatures in the fully developed 
phase. The temperature-time curve of compartment type T-A is not supposed to lead to 
higher gas-phase temperatures than for compartment type T-2 as compartment type T-A is 
consisting of a CLT ceiling and single wall surface (left or right) with exposed CLT. 
 
The amount of CLT is therefore smaller than for compartment type T-2 while thermal 
feedback between two opposite located wall surfaces is neither taking place for T-A. 
The described differences between the compartment types should lead to lower temperatures 
in compartment type T-A compared to T-2 which is not the case in Figure 36. It has already 
been stated in this section that the 𝐾𝑓 factors of compartment type T-A could be problematic 
whereby the temperature-time curve of this compartment type should not be trusted as much 
as the temperature development from the other self-developed compartment types.

8 Discussion 
Several challenges have been met during the present thesis when developing a model which 
is able to predict gas-phase temperatures within a fire compartment. These are described in 
the following. 
 
Initially, a statement is made about the temperature profiles from the large-scale experiments 
as these may not be exact. The test instruments are expected to provide errors to some extent. 
These errors have already been explained in the reported large-scale experiments. 
Also, data points for the temperature profiles of the experiments have been found using the 
online web-based tool “WebPlotDigitizer” whereby the data point readings might be 
inaccurate. It is assessed that the errors regarding the test instruments and the web-based tool 
can be considered negligible. 
 
The attempts in Section 5.1 and in Section 5.2 show that the compartment types, developed 
by Magnusson and Thelandersson [2], are not useable as these are not accounting for exposed 
combustible materials and the thermal feedback between these. It is noteworthy that the 
method will not even produce a reasonable temperature-time curve for Test 1 (containing an 
exposed CLT ceiling). 
 
The attempts in Section 5.3 show that a modified formula for calculating the opening factor is 
not enough to reproduce temperature-time curves which fit the experimental results. The 
attempts indicate that the traditional compartment types must be analyzed further. 
 
In Section 5.4 it is seen that the gas-phase temperature development cannot be found simply 
from the energy balance equation as this equation is neither taking the exposed CLT area nor 
the location of the CLT within the fire compartment into account. 
Section 5.5 shows that it is possible to reproduce the temperature-time curves of the large-
scale experiments by introducing two 𝐾𝑓 factors (for the opening factor and fuel load density, 
respectively). However, the self-developed compartment types should be revisited. It is 
suggested that the two 𝐾𝑓 factors are based on more than a single large-scale experiment. 
 
The validation of the trial-and-error approach in Section 6 shows that the self-developed 
compartment type becomes inaccurate when the fuel load density per unit floor area is 
different. It is therefore suggested that the self-developed compartment types are based on a 
higher fuel load density so that the temperature-time curve becomes more conservative. 
 
The analysis of the Arena Boliger compartments is also illustrating the above. The two 𝐾𝑓 
factors of the self-developed compartment type T-A provide temperature developments 
which should be given less credibility. 
 
Throughout the thesis, the ISO 834 standard fire curve has been shown in figures. 
The standard fire curve is typically used for the classification of building components. 
Not surprisingly, the standard fire curve shows a temperature development which is 
completely different from the experimental results but also the produced temperature-time 
curves. The building components should ideally be classified by exposing these to a 
==PAGE:77==
77 
 
temperature development which is as realistic as possible. However, this is not the case when 
using a standard fire curve. This fire curve is a conservative exposure to building 
components. Thus, a building component is typically found acceptable if the component 
passes a classification test based on the ISO 834 fire curve. The figures of this thesis show 
that higher gas-phase temperatures are measured when a compartment with exposed CLT is 
experiencing a fire. It is therefore hard to tell whether the ISO 834 fire curve will be a 
conservative fire curve in these scenarios. If this is not the case, then the classification of 
building components might no longer be sufficient, and these should therefore be classified 
using another fire curve. This classification of building component should therefore always 
be a focus point when using CLT in buildings.

9 Conclusion 
This thesis has explored the possibility of developing a model to describe gas-phase 
temperatures within fire compartments with exposed CLT. Some of the performed attempts 
have provided poor results. However, these attempts have made it possible to identify the 
challenges when using the traditional opening factor method. Hence, a trial-and-error 
approach has been developed during the thesis. 
 
The approach has been found useful to some extent as the self-developed compartment types 
from this approach make it possible to reproduce large-scale experiments by Brandon et al. 
[13]. The approach has faced problems when a validation was performed. It is concluded that 
the almost identical CLT configuration of Test A and B, respectively, is not enough to 
validate the method as the fuel load densities of the large-scale experiments are very 
different. Thus, it is concluded that the trial-and-error approach should provide reasonable 
results if the fuel load density of the large-scale experiments was equal. 
 
In the analysis of the Arena Boliger compartments it was seen that reasonable temperature-
time curves were produced. However, the self-developed compartment type T-A did not 
provide trustworthy temperatures. It is assessed that this is because the self-developed 
compartment type is based on a large-scale experiment (Test A) where the fuel load density is 
too low. 
9.1 Future work 
It is suggested that the trial-and-error approach of this thesis is researched even further as the 
self-developed compartment types are very sensitive because of the small sample size. 
Magnusson and Thelandersson [2] used approximately 30 data sets when developing the 
well-known opening factor method. It is therefore suggested that each self-developed 
compartment type is also based on at least 30 data sets. 
 
A parametric study of the trial-and-error approach could also be performed even though it 
might be very time consuming and expensive as the experiments will have to be large-scale 
experiments to account for thermal feedback. 
 
Finally, it would be interesting to perform large-scale experiments of the Arena Boliger 
compartments and assess the credibility of the predicted temperature-time curves.
