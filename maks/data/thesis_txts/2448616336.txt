Summary (English)
Diagnosis of adenocarcinomas, a type of cancerous tumor that can occur in sev-
eral parts of the body, including prostate, breast or colon, requires obtaining
reliable morphological statistics. Determination of malignancy of adenocarcino-
mas involves analysing histopathological images, in order to accurately segment
glands. Traditionally, pathologist experts are carrying out the assessment, which
has many drawbacks. Automation of the process is necessary to increase the
reproducibility and reduce the workload.
The recent advances in deep learning have achieved promising results on many
biomedical image segmentation benchmarks. This thesis investigates the topic
of medical image segmentation with the use of multi-task deep learning. The
Mask R-CNN model, considered currently as one of the best architectures for
instance segmentation of nature images, is implemented and optimised for the
gland segmentation task. Considerations regarding data augmentation for this
model have been made.
The operation of Mask R-CNN is compared to the
SA-FCN model, the architecture designed especially for glands segmentation.
This thesis addresses the issue of post-processing, loss weighting and inﬂuence
of multi-task learning on the individual tasks’ performance.
The 2015 MICCAI Gland Challenge dataset is utilised to demonstrate the per-
formance of the proposed solutions. Our results show the signiﬁcance of the
Mask R-CNN model in medical image analysis domain.
The Mask R-CNN
model has promising results, providing instance segmentation results, while the
SA-FCN output requires post-processing actions to convert semantic segmen-
tation to instance segmentation.
Furthermore, the results indicate that the
SA-FCN performance strongly depends on the post-processing techniques. We
==PAGE:4==
ii
have succeeded in designing new post-processing method, which signiﬁcantly
improved the performance of the model. The evidence from conducted study
suggests that for multi-task learning, the loss weighting ratio matters only if
the tasks signiﬁcantly diﬀer from each other. Finally, our ﬁndings demonstrate
that the knowledge about contours strongly supports foreground/background
training in case of the SA-FCN model.
The solutions described in this thesis have a wide application in segmentation
of various types of medical images. They can be used to diagnose diseases, but
also very often used in pharmacological studies. Such research is carried out
by the "Nanoprobes" research group at the Department of Health Technology
at DTU. They are working on image segmentation of a bioﬁlm from a bacteria
called Pseudomonas aeruginosa. The goal of their research is to investigate how
eﬃcient it is to treat the bioﬁlm with antibiotic-loaded polymeric microdevices,
using a microscopic equipment called an oCelloscope from the company Biosense
Solutions. The automation of the segmentation process would signiﬁcantly im-
prove the quality and eﬀectiveness of their work.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Glandular morphology is used by pathologists to assess the malignancy of dif-
ferent adenocarcinomas, including prostate, breasts or colon. This process in-
volves analysing histological images in order to accurately segment the glands.
Obtaining reliable morphological statistics for quantitative diagnostics helps to
determine the aggressiveness of tumours. The assessment of glandular struc-
tures in biopsy samples is usually carried out by pathologist experts. However,
manual handling of these activities has many disadvantages, such as limited re-
producibility, time consumption or a great eﬀort from the pathologist. For these
reasons, the automation of segmentation methods is very necessary to increase
the accuracy and eﬀectiveness of the test, and above all, reduce the pathologists
workload.
To solve this challenging problem, in this thesis I would like to discuss the topic
of medical image segmentation with the use of multi-task deep learning.
In
particular, the attention is focused on two architectures: the SA-FCN and the
Mask RCNN. The SA-FCN is treated as a state-of-the-art model for the 2015
MICCAI Gland Challenge dataset. Characterised by a combination of two tasks:
foreground/background and contour detection, has been especially designed to
solve the problem of histopathological images segmentation.
The SA-FCN will be compared to the Mask R-CNN model, which is recognised
as one of the best architectures for the task of instance segmentation of natural

images. The Mask R-CNN also applies multi-task learning, where it combines
three diﬀerent tasks: classiﬁcation, ﬁnding bounding boxes and determining the
semantic masks for objects. One of the main assumptions of the project is to
verify whether the Mask R-CNN model, which was not speciﬁcally designed for
gland segmentation task, is able to eﬀectively deal with it. Furthermore, this
thesis deals with several other aspects related to both architectures, such as:
signiﬁcance of model optimisation, comparison of data augmentation methods,
relevance of post-processing methods, signiﬁcance of loss weighting in multi-task
learning or inﬂuence of multi-task learning on the individual tasks’ performance.
1.1
Related Work
We consider the related work in the following categories separately.
Firstly,
a brief overview of computer vision analysis methods for image segmentation
is presented. Secondly, we are introducing a few solutions for diﬀerent vision
analysis tasks, from a deep learning perspective.
Thirdly, the characteristic
aspects of gland segmentation on histology images are described, as well as
the latest solutions for them. Finally, the main assumptions behind multi-task
learning are presented.
1.1.1
Computer Vision Analysis
We associate any problem that has an image or camera input to encompass
problems with computer vision. Computer vision tasks include methods for ac-
quiring, processing, analysing and understanding digital images, and extraction
of high-dimensional data from the real world, in order to produce numerical or
symbolic information [Kle14].
Deep learning has revolutionised computer vision. Before that, image classiﬁca-
tion task was solved with traditional computer vision techniques. The ﬁrst step
was extracting features, which for instance could include: edge detection, corner
detection, object detection. The idea behind it is to collect a set of features,
which deﬁne particular class (eg. to recognise a cat, it would be suggested to
look for whiskers, ears, fur and eyes). The diﬃculty of this approach is deﬁning
a proper feature engineering for each particular task [Zbi18]. It becomes to be a
serious problem when the number of classes starts to grow. Moreover, it needs
to be decided which features are searched, since it would be too computationally
expensive to search for all of them. This kind of problems, that once seemed im-
probable to be solved, now are solved to the extent that machines are obtaining

1.1.2
Deep Learning in Computer Vision Analysis
Since 2012, a great development of deep learning methods in image analysis
applications can be observed. The main focus of researchers has been changing
throughout the years, from one vision analysis task to another (Figure 1.1).
At the beginning, neural networks were designed for classiﬁcation and localisa-
tion tasks, aiming to label image with one category and point out approximate
localisation of the object in the image.
One of the most relevant breakthroughs was introduced by Kaiming He et al.
in 2015 [HZRS16], by inventing Residual Networks (ResNets). Thanks to its
innovative shortcut connections, residual networks are easier to optimise and
can gain accuracy from considerably increased depth. Currently Squeeze-and-
Excitation Networks (SENet), developed by Jie Hu el al. [HSS17], can be de-
scribed as one of the best architectures for image classiﬁcation.
More demanding task than classiﬁcation, is pixel-wise labelling of the image,
also known as semantic segmentation. Majority of networks, which try to solve

this problem, base its structure on the approach of using "fully convolutional"
network trained end-to-end, pixel-to-pixel, introduced by Long et al. in late
2014 [LSD15].
The authors proposed using well-studied image classiﬁcation
network (eg. AlexNet) as encoder module of the network and adding a decoder
module with transpose convolutional layers to upsample the coarse feature maps
into a full-resolution labelled image. Liang-Chieh Chen et al. has introduced
a DeepLab architecture, which is considered as the most solid and outperforms
other architecture on almost every RGB image dataset, in semantic segmenta-
tion task [CPK+18]. Authors achieved great performance, among others thanks
to the implementation of atrous convolution and atrous spatial pyramid pooling
(ASPP) to robustly segment object at multiple scales.
In case of recent models solving semantic segmentation problem, the accuracy
of prediction is very often satisfactory, that is why the future works will have
to focus on real-time constrains, which might be ﬁnding a trade-oﬀbetween
accuracy and runtime [GGOEO+17].
Another type of computer vision task is object detection. As it was already
mentioned, an image classiﬁcation models classify whole images to one category.
When the image would contain multiple objects, the prediction will fail. In order
to solve this problem, object detection task has been deﬁned. The goal is to
identify multiple objects in a single image as well as a localisation of them.
One of the ﬁrst successful models in object detection was Region-based Con-
volutional Network (R-CNN). It harnessed a selective search method developed
by J.R.R. Uijlings and al. in 2012 [UVDSGS13], which is an alternative to ex-
haustive search method. It starts with initialising small regions in the image
and based on similarity metrics, it merges them with hierarchical grouping. The
output is a few region proposals, which are very probable to contain the object.
In 2014 R.Girshick et. al combined selective search methods and deep learning
to ﬁnd out objects in these regions. Each region is transformed to input vec-
tor for CNN and a multiple classiﬁers (SVM) produces probabilities of regions
belonging to each class [GDDM16].
In 2015 an improved version of R-CNN has been developed by R.Girshick, named
Fast R-CNN [Gir15]. The main objective was to reduce a time consumption,
which was caused by a high number of models necessary to analyse all the region
proposals. Instead of detecting the Regions of Interest (RoI) on original images,
as in the original R-CNN, R.Girshick decided to do it on feature maps, which
has signiﬁcantly accelerated the training process.
Since the selective search method was computationally expensive, S.Ren et. al
have replaced it with Region Proposal Network (RPN), and called the model

Among already described vision tasks, such as classiﬁcation, localisation, se-
mantic segmentation and object detection, an instance segmentation is the most
challenging, since it combines all of these tasks in one. Instance segmentation
can be deﬁned as the process of detecting and delimiting each distinct object
in the image. The problem solved by instance segmentation can be split into a
few parts. Firstly, a variable number of objects in the image needs to be found
and classiﬁed. The diﬃculty lies in the unknown number of objects within one
category. Secondly, image have to be labelled at the pixel level, as it is done in
case of semantic segmentation.
The example of instance segmentation as well as comparison to other described
vision tasks, is presented in Figure 1.1.
Many approaches to instance segmentation is based on segment proposals. Deep
Mask [PCD15], [PLCD16], [DHL+16] ﬁrst learn to propose segment candidates,
which afterwards are classiﬁed by Fast R-CNN. In such case, segmentation pre-
cedes recognition, which turns out to be slow and less accurate. On the other
hand, Mask R-CNN, which is considered as state-of-the-art in terms of instance
segmentation [HGDG17], and will be described in the next chapter, is based on
parallel prediction of masks and class labels, which is simple and more ﬂexible.
Another group of architectures [DRF16], [LQD+16] predict a set of position-
sensitive output channels fully convolutionally. At the same time, these channels
address object classes, boxes and masks, which allows the system to decrease
the operation time. However, these systems generate systematic error on over-
lapping instances and create false edges.
Articles [BU17], [AT17], [LJFU17] describe models, which are driven by the
success of semantic segmentation. They start with pixel-wise classiﬁcation and
then try to cut the pixels of the same class into diﬀerent instances. On the other
hand, the already mentioned Mask-RCNN architecture uses quite the opposite
process. First, the region of interest are found, so that semantic segmentation

1.1.4
Multi-task learning
The goal of multi-task learning (MTL) is to improve learning eﬃciency as well as
prediction accuracy by learning multiple objectives from a shared representation
[Car97]. By sharing representations between related tasks the model can be
enabled to generalise better on the original task [Rud17].
MTL can be viewed as one way for machines to imitate human learning activ-
ities, because people very often transfer knowledge from one task to another,
when these tasks are connected. A good example for that could be that fact
that squash and tennis skills can help improve each other [ZY18].
MTL is getting widespread in many machine learning applications, such as com-
puter vision [Gir15], speech recognition [DHK13] or natural language processing
[CW08]. An example of multi-task model is shown in Figure 1.4, which shows
uncertainty-based loss function weighting architecture developed by Kendall et
al. in 2017 [KGC18]. Generally, as soon as more than one loss function is opti-
mised, the multi-task learning is eﬀectively utilised (in contrast to a single-task
learning)[Rud17].
The two most commonly used ways to perform multi-task learning in deep neural
networks are [Car93]:
• Soft parameter sharing of hidden layers.
• Hard parameter sharing of hidden layers.
More common approach in MTL is hard parameter sharing. It is applied by

sharing the hidden layers between all tasks, while keeping several task speciﬁc
output layers (please look on Figure 1.5). Baxter [Bax97] has shown that this
method greatly reduces risk of overﬁtting, which can be logically explained. The
more task we are learning at the same time, the more the model needs to ﬁnd
a representation which captures all of the tasks and the less is the chance of
overﬁtting on the original task [Rud17].
On the other hand, the soft parameter sharing method requires each task to
have its own model with its parameters (please have a look on the right side of
Figure 1.5). The distance between the parameters of the model is regularised in
order to force the parameters to be similar [Rud17]. Authors of [DCBC15] are
using the L2 distance for regularisation, while authors of [YH16] use the trace
norm. In this project, hard parameter sharing is applied for all architectures.
One of the most common questions about Multi-task learning is: "Why does it
work?". There are a few reasons. Firstly, MTL helps with data sparsity problem.
Deep learning models, represented by normal networks with many hidden layers
and parameters needs plenty of data instances to learn accurate parameters. In
case of medical image analysis this requirement is hard to satisfy. That is where
multi-task learning can prove its usefulness by introducing so called implicit data
augmentation. MTL exploits useful information from other correlated tasks to
help reduce the data sparsity problem. Another advantage of MLT is the fact
that it can help a model to focus its attention on the more relevant features,
by modelling the relationship between the tasks. Moreover, MTL behaves as
regularizer by introducing inductive bias [Rud17].
Consequently, the risk of
overﬁtting is reduced, as well as its ability to ﬁt random noise.

ANN is a computational model inspired by the way biological neural networks

try to correct itself each time the object appears in diﬀerent localisation of the
image. One of the main reasons why MLPs are not the best for image processing
is the spatial information loss, where the image is ﬂattened into a MLP [Ste19].

the aforementioned problems. The unit connectivity pattern is inspired by the
visual cortex organisation, where units respond to stimuli in a restricted region
of space, also known as the receptive ﬁeld [Fum17]. Receptive ﬁelds are partially
overlapping and the entire visual ﬁeld is over-covered.

2.3.1
Architecture
The authors have designed the FCN, taking into account a number of require-
ments. Firstly, it had to be fast to train, which was a demand for the active
learning part. Secondly, it needs to have a good generality, in order to pro-
duce reasonable results when little training data is available [YZC+17]. There-
fore, authors applied batch normalisation [IS15] and residual network features
[HZRS16]. The bottleneck design has been utilised, which signiﬁcantly reduced
number of parameters. The structure of FCN components are shown on Figure

2.3 SA-FCN

The structure consists of encoder and two decoders,
one for the fore-
ground/background recognition and another edge detection of of glands. The
encoding part is strongly inspired by the DCAN structure [CQYH16]. It con-

at the end of each residual module [YZC+17]. Each convolutional layer is fol-

overﬁtting, drop-out layers are applied after 3rd, 4th and 5th "Intermediate

the size of feature maps is gradually increased, to ensure a smooth results
[YZC+17]. Both decoders consists of six such blocks, build out of transpose
convolution layers, each followed by batch normalisation and ReLU activation
function.
Feature maps from diﬀerent scales (from each block), are concatenated. 3x3
convolutional layer and 1x1 convolutional layer are applied to combine them
together. The softmax function is used in the ﬁnal layer of the network, to
provide the classiﬁcation scores.

• Random cropping of the image to a region of size 384 by 384 pixels.

3.1.1
Data Augmentation
Deep learning very often has to deal with the problem of limited size datasets,
as in the case of the 2015 MICCAI Gland Challenge dataset (85 training images
and 80 testing images). To achieve better generalisation in the model, a lot of
data and as much variation as possible in the data is needed [HGK18]. The
2015 MICCAI Gland Challenge dataset is not big enough to capture suﬃcient
variation, which creates a need to generate more data from the provided dataset.
For this purpose, the data augmentation turns out to be very helpful. The most
popular data augmentation techniques are [TN17]:
• Flip: ﬂipping images on horizontal or vertical axis.
• Rotation: rotating an image with certain degree.
• Cropping: cropping a section from given image randomly.
• Noise addition - e.g Gaussian noise.
• Colour jittering - random colour manipulation.
• Elastic distortion - e.g. pincushion transformation, barrel transformation.

In order to minimise the loss function, Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) is
used, as authors of Mask R-CNN propose. In order to limit the magnitude of the
gradient, gradient clipping norm equal to ﬁve is utilised, which in turn allows

In order to ﬁnd the most optimal values for the Mask R-CNN model, trained

to be explored. The parameters are:
• Learning rate.
• Momentum.
• Weight decay.
Learning rate and momentum values are directly connected with the SGD op-
timiser. Choosing a proper learning rate is crucial. Too small learning rate
leads to slow convergence, while too large can hinder convergence and cause the
loss function to ﬂuctuate around the minimum or even to diverge [Wal17]. The
weight decay is a regularisation parameter for L2 regularisation, which is used
to prevent overﬁtting. It is very important to carefully choose the regularisation
parameter. The L2 reduces overﬁtting, which in turn reduces the variance of
the model. However, it does this at the expense of adding bias to the estimate.
Increasing weight decay results in lower overﬁtting but at the same time greater
bias.

Mask R-CNN training on the COCO dataset as well as on the common practices
in training convolutional neural networks. Table 3.1 presents chosen ranges of

nearly no eﬀect if the learning rate is 10. This is due to the fact that the learning
rate multiplies the computed gradient in the update. Therefore, it is much more
natural to consider a range of learning rate multiplied or divided by some value,
than a range of learning rate added or subtracted by some value [Kar17]. The

In order to improve the performance of glands segmentation algorithm, it is a
common practice to utilise post-processing methods. Similarly, in the case of
this project a post-processing method, specially adjusted to the Mask R-CNN
characteristics, has been developed and is presented in the following section.
As it results from the operation of the Mask R-CNN, model’s output consists
of a multiple ﬁnal masks, one per object. When trying to merge all the layers
into one, an overlapping problem can occur for some pixels. This is equivalent
to the case where one pixel is assigned to more than one object. Taking into the
account our method of a ﬁnal model evaluation (calculation of F1 score, Dice
index and Hausdorﬀdistance), this is an unwanted behaviour. Consequently,
the ﬁrst step of the post-processing algorithm is to remove overlapping parts
of detected glands, based on their objectness score (object with a lower score
is deleted).
In other words, intersection from the masks with lower score is
removed. Thus, each pixel has exactly one label. This is represented graphically
in Figure 3.1.
The second step of the post-processing algorithm includes removing small ob-
jects and ﬁlling holes. Afterwards, a connected-component labelling algorithm,
with 8 connected neighborhood has been applied. As a consequence, predictions
of glands, which has been touching each other, were connected. Figure 3.1 shows
a ﬁnal output of the Mask R-CNN before and after post-processing. Examples
of overlapping cases and connecting smaller glands, which are in contact can be
observed.

3.4
Loss weighting in multi-task learning
Multi-task learning with multiple regression and classiﬁcation objectives turns
out to be very beneﬁcial for many deep learning applications. In case of many
systems, their performance strongly depends on the relative weighting between
each task’s loss [KGC18]. To better understand this, the loss weights can be
treated as values of the preference strength that we distribute into individual
tasks. During the training, in some cases the loss functions are going to dis-
agree. The gradients for individual losses may indicate two completely opposite
directions. In such case, the one that has a higher weight has a better chance
to push through its direction. Tuning these weights can be done in a diﬀerent
ways. The naive approach to combine losses is to simply perform a weights lin-
ear sum of the losses for each individual task. In order to ﬁnd optimal weights,
a Grid Search or Random Search methods (described in the section 2.4) could
==PAGE:60==
40
Methods
be used. Very often the model performance is extremely sensitive to the weight
selection. As a result, these weight hyper-parameters are expensive to tune.
More sophisticated approach has been proposed by Kendall et. al in 2018 in
the article [KGC18]. The authors have proposed a principled approach to loss
weighting multiple loss functions, by considering the homoscedastic uncertainty
of each task. It allowed them to simultaneously learn various quantities with
diﬀerent units or scales in both classiﬁcation and regression settings.
The goal of one of the planned experiments is to investigate the impact of
loss weighting on the performance of the SA-FCN architecture on the 2015
MICCAI Gland Challenge dataset. The original version of the system, described
in the article [YZC+17], assumes uniform weights for foreground/background
and contour losses. To the best of our knowledge, no results describing loss
weighting for this architecture were publicised.
Due to the time constrains, as well as the fact that the goal of the experi-
ment is not to ﬁnd the optimal weights for losses, but only to answer the ques-
tion whether loss weighting aﬀects the operation of the model, the Grid Search
method has been applied to explore the range of the variable α, describing the
relation between foreground/background and contour loss. The α describes how
much percent of the foreground/background loss is taken into account. On the
other hand, the contour loss weight is deﬁned as 1 −α. The ﬁnal loss of the
model including loss weighting is described by the equation 3.1.
L = αLf/b + (1 −α)Lc.
(3.1)
To properly select the α values for the experiment, so that as much as possible
can be learned and at the same time limit the computation and time costs, the
preliminary experiments have been conducted. The conclusion from them was
to use step 0.1 for the range between 0.2 and 0.8, while in ranges from 0 to
0.2 and from 0.8 to 1 sampling should be more dense, since it is more likely
to observe some changes in the model performance. For this reason, in these
ranges, the step is equal to 0.02. Figure 3.2 shows the sampling distribution of
the α variable in the range between 0 and 1.
Results of this experiments are presented and discussed in the section 4.5.

behaviour of the model, mainly in terms of transfer learning and training sce-

The conducted experiment does not allow to state whether the COCO or Im-

the training in relation to ResNet50 pre-trained model.

COCO), that in no way is related to glands, allows signiﬁcantly improve the
gland segmentation results. This mainly happens, due to the low level network
layers, which stores information about low level features of objects, such as
corners, edges etc.
The training scenario results indicates that for both ImageNet and COCO pre-
trained models, the "all" strategy works the best. Contrary to the expectations,
freezing the backbone of the Mask R-CNN model for the ResNet50 pre-trained
weights, did not help in acquiring lower validation loss. Loss curve for the "all"
scenario is always lower and more robust.
This implies to indicate that the

4.6

The Mask R-CNN is considered as the state-of-the-art model in terms of in-

As it was mentioned in the section 1.1.3, many data analysts consider gland

designed for natural images, will eﬀectively cope with another type of data,

Chapter 5
Conclusions
5.1
Summary and Discussions
In this thesis, the comparison between two multi-task deep learning models,
the SA-FCN and the Mask R-CNN is presented. The Mask R-CNN model has
been adjusted to conduct instance segmentation on the 2015 MICCAI Gland
Challenge dataset. Thanks to the model optimisation process using Bayesian
Optimisation strategy, the model proved to be eﬀective in its operation, and
its performance to be competitive with models speciﬁcally designed for seg-
mentation of histopathological images. Furthermore, the experimental results
conﬁrmed that the simpler data augmentation strategy (rotation, vertical and
horizontal ﬂipping) is better suited to the glands dataset. Enriching data by
Gaussian blur and brightness variation does not expand the dataset, since the
data does not have too much variation in terms of brightness or blurring. there
is not too much diﬀerence in terms of brightness or blurring images. Our ex-
periments validated previously published results, conﬁrming usefulness of data
augmentation in order to avoid overﬁtting.
Experiments conducted on the SA-FCN model, have led us to conclude that for
this system, the post-processing actions are of a great importance as it not only
transforms semantic segmentation prediction to instance segmentation, but also
hides the imperfections of the model (falsely identiﬁed small elements, holes or
==PAGE:98==
78
Conclusions
contour misalignment). Besides, we have devised, a post-processing methodol-
ogy, which have signiﬁcantly improved the model performance compared to the
DCAN or the original SA-FCN techniques.
This work further discusses the signiﬁcance of loss weighting in multi-task learn-
ing. In case of the SA-FCN architecture the results suggest that as long as the
ratio between losses is set to reasonable value, that does not exclude completely
one of the two tasks, there is not much diﬀerence in obtained results.
The
results from this study shows that in multi-task learning, taking into account
contour and region detection tasks for the gland dataset, if tasks align (need
similar kind of features) in a great extent, it does not matter too much what
the ratio between losses is. This ﬁnding does not conﬁrm the hypothesis posed
at the beginning of the project. Furthermore, this thesis is also highlighting the
importance of contour detection for the SA-FCN model. Contour prediction not
only plays a vital role for the overall system performance, but also the experi-
ment described in the section 4.7 indicates that the knowledge about contours,
coming from the contour decoder helps the foreground/background decoder to
put more attention to a particular regions of the images and ignore irrelevant
parts during the training. On the other hand, there is no visible assistance from
foreground/background side for the contour decoder. To sum up, the results for
the third hypothesis (from the section 1.2) are inconclusive, more experiments
are needed.
The two implemented models did not reach the current state-of-the-art on the
2015 MICCAI Gland Challenge dataset, which belongs to the SA-FCN archi-
tecture, publicised in [YZC+17]. Interestingly, despite all eﬀorts to reproduce
the SA-FCN model as accurately as possible, we have not managed to achieve
as high results as it was announced in 2017. Nevertheless, the results of the
experiments carried out on our SA-FCN and Mask R-CNN implementations
demonstrate that the Mask R-CNN is better at dealing with the task of seg-
mentation of histopathological images, which denied the hypothesis posed at
the beginning of this study. The analysis led us to the conclusion that detection
of bounding boxes by the Mask R-CNN and mask recognition only inside those
regions that have the highest probability of having an object, is very beneﬁ-
cial for the eﬀectiveness of the model. Besides, Mask R-CNN does not require
as many post-processing actions as the SA-FCN architecture. In contrast to
SA-FCN, the Mask R-CNN architecture itself solves the problem of instance
segmentation, which is its next advantage. As a consequence, the Mask R-CNN
model tends to outperform the SA-FCN model.
Work presented in this thesis has a wide range of applications. It can be success-
fully used for segmentation of medical images of various types. These images
can be used to diagnose diseases, like previously mentioned assessment of the
malignancy of diﬀerent adenocarcinomas, but also images used in a pharmaceu-

5.2
Future Work
This thesis work can open up many opportunities for the use of the Mask R-
CNN architecture in task of segmentation histopathological images.
In our
work, we only consider an original structure of the model. The next step in this
research could be merging a contour recognition task to the current multi-task
learning architecture of the Mask R-CNN. This idea is supported by the results
of contour detection by the SA-FCN model, which indicated a very positive eﬀect
of contour recognition on the instance segmentation of glands.
In this way,
heads of the Mask R-CNN would consist of four losses: class loss, bounding
box loss, mask loss and contour loss. Besides, the Mask R-CNN results are
promising and should be validated by other medical image datasets, for instance
2018 Data Science Bowl consisting of nucleus or Lymph Node Ultrasound Image
Segmentation Dataset [KMW+17].
From the experimental results of the SA-FCN, it is observed that the model in-
troduces strong misalignment of predicted gland edges in respect to the ground
truth, which in turn has direct inﬂuence on the classiﬁcation performance.
Therefore, future studies on that topic are suggested. One of the ideas could be
replacing max pooling layers with dilated convolution. Yu et al. in the [YK15]
article claims that proposing dilated convolution can empower the network with
a wider receptive ﬁeld without down-sampling. Less down-sampling means later
less up-sampling, which in turn may help reduce the misalignment eﬀect. This
type of change could improve the accuracy of prediction of both, the contour
and foreground/background detection for the SA-FCN.
The results of this study indicate that in the multi-task learning model, when
tasks need similar type of features, the distribution of loss weights does not
have a signiﬁcant impact on the ﬁnal performance. Future work should address
aspect of loss weighting in terms of the Mask R-CNN, which unlike the SA-
FCN, consists of more diverse types of tasks. Furthermore, it seems worthwhile
to analyse the suitability of the solution proposed by Kendall in the [KGC18]

uncertainty of each task.
