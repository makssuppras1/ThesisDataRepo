Abstract 
This master's thesis delves into the realm of corrosion detection in ship images through the 
implementation of three distinct deep learning architecture models: U-Net, DeepLabv3Plus, and 
SegNet. Through semantic segmentation, these models are tailored to identify corrosion areas 
within ship images, thus aiding in maintenance and safety assessments. The study involves training 
these models with a dataset containing ship images annotated with corrosion areas, enabling them 
to learn and predict the presence of corrosion accurately. Performance evaluation metrics are 
employed to measure the models' efficacy in identifying corrosion and determining its severity. 
Additionally, a comparative analysis is conducted to discern the most effective approach among 
the deep learning models for corrosion detection in ship images. This research contributes to the 
advancement of automated corrosion detection techniques in the maritime industry, offering 
insights into optimal model selection for enhanced preventive maintenance strategies.

to its potential to revolutionize maintenance practices in the maritime industry. The motivation

environmental risks. By employing deep learning techniques, such as convolutional neural 
networks (CNNs) [1], researchers aim to automate the detection and classification of corrosion in 
ship images with high accuracy and efficiency. This can lead to early detection of corrosion, 
enabling timely maintenance interventions and cost-effective repair strategies. For instance, by 
training a CNN model on a dataset of annotated ship images, researchers can develop a predictive 
tool capable of identifying different forms of corrosion, such as pitting or cracking, in real-time 
inspection scenarios. The integration of deep learning algorithms in corrosion detection not only 
streamlines the inspection process but also enhances the overall safety and reliability of maritime 
operations.

performance of the models on a wide range of datasets, the study aims to establish the effectiveness

2.4 Challenges and current trends in corrosion detection using deep 
learning models 
 
One of the persistent challenges in machine learning is the constraint posed by insufficient labeled 
datasets [2], which restricts the training process and subsequently impacts task performance. On 
the flip side, it is widely recognized that having access to more data leads to enhanced model 
performance. In the realm of general computer vision tasks, efforts have been directed towards 
overcoming data scarcity by adopting strategies such as utilizing smaller filters in deeper layers, 
experimenting with innovative combinations of Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) 
architectures, and fine-tuning hyperparameters to optimize model performance. These approaches 
aim to mitigate the effects of limited data availability and enhance the learning capabilities of 
computer vision models, ultimately improving their effectiveness in various visual recognition 
tasks. 
 
In image analysis, another critical challenge is data or class imbalance [2] within the training 
dataset. This imbalance occurs when the number of images representing different classes is 
uneven, potentially leading to skewed model performance if not addressed effectively. One way to 
tackle this issue is through data augmentation, a technique that generates additional training images 
by modifying existing data, particularly focusing on rare or underrepresented classes. While data 
augmentation can help alleviate the impact of data imbalances, there is a potential risk of 
overfitting—where the model learns noise in the augmented data rather than meaningful patterns—
necessitating careful consideration during the training process.

3 Methodology 
 
In this section, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) methods are discussed in more detail, based 
on their architectures. There isn’t a single CNN design that fits just one-use case; for instance, a 
CNN developed for corrosion segmentation can also effectively segment other structures with 
minimal or no adjustments. 
 
3.1 Data preparation 
Data preparation involves all the tasks carried out before the data is input into the network, 
including techniques like data augmentation or regularization. 
 
For our project, a diverse dataset of RGB ship images containing corrosion areas is collected. 
These are simulated images that encompass a wide range of corrosion patterns and sizes to ensure 
the model's robustness and generalization capabilities. Along with them, we have another dataset 
that consists of images showing delineated boundaries of corrosion patches pixel by pixel, which 
are assigned a distinct class label to corrosion regions. 
 
Given the limited availability of annotated ship images, data augmentation techniques such as 
rotation, flipping, scaling, and adding noise can be applied to artificially increase the dataset's size. 
This helps in enhancing the model's ability to generalize to different corrosion scenarios. Data 
augmentation is primarily used to expand the number of training samples and reduce the risk of 
overfitting. This approach is especially prevalent in computer vision tasks where (i) the CNN 
architectures are quite deep and (ii) acquiring large volumes of labeled training data is challenging 
[9]. Additionally, if the dataset isn't sufficiently large, the significant imbalance between majority 
and minority classes can result in biased classifiers. In such situations, data augmentation can help 
address these issues and enhance the model's overall performance. 
 
Before feeding the data into the model, it is essential to normalize the pixel values to a common 
scale (e.g., [0, 1]) and preprocess the images for better convergence during training. This may 
include resizing images to a uniform dimension or applying other transformations based on the 
dataset. 
 
Regularization [9] methods are commonly used in the field, such as L1 or L2 approaches which 
promote sparsity and small weight values, early stopping to prevent overfitting and aid in hyper-
parameter selection, batch normalization to standardize each batch, and dropout to prevent co-
adaptation among feature map units. Dropout is particularly favored due to its computational 
efficiency and ability to encourage independent contributions from each unit for accurate 
predictions. 
 
==PAGE:13==
 
The dataset needs to be split into training, validation, and test sets to evaluate the model's 
performance. We used 70% of the data for training, 15% for validation to tune hyperparameters, 
and the remaining portion for testing. 
 
The final step involves pairing each preprocessed image with its corresponding annotated mask, 
where the mask highlights the corrosion areas. These input-output pairs are then fed into the U-
Net model during training to learn the spatial relationships and features indicative of corrosion. 
 
By meticulously preparing the data specific to training the model with annotations of corrosion 
areas, we lay a solid foundation for building a robust and accurate corrosion detection system for 
ship images. The quality of data preparation directly influences the model's performance and its 
ability to generalize to unseen corrosion patterns, contributing to the advancement of maritime 
inspection technologies. 
 
3.2 Convolutional layer 
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) learn patterns in input images by using convolution and 
pooling operations to extract features. A convolution involves operating on two functions: one 
representing input values (e.g., pixel values) in an image, and the other being a filter or kernel 
represented as an array of numbers. By computing the dot product between these functions, an 
output is obtained (Figure 4).  
 
 
Figure 4: Convolution process 
 
The filter is then moved to the next position in the image based on the stride length, and this process 
is repeated across the entire image to generate a feature map. This map highlights where the filter 
is activated strongly, indicating the presence of features like lines, dots, or edges. In a CNN analysis 
of an image, low-level features such as lines and edges are initially detected by the filters, gradually 
progressing to higher-level in subsequent layers as the feature maps serve as inputs for the CNN's 
architecture [2]. The number of feature maps (Figure 5) generated by the convolution operation 
varies based on the quantity of filters applied. [10] 
 
==PAGE:14==
 
Figure 5: Traditional Pattern Recognition 
 
The depth, stride, and zero-padding are three hyperparameters that influence the size of the output 
volume of a convolutional layer.  
 
The depth (Figure 6) determines the number of neurons in the layer that are connected to the same 
region of the input volume. Each neuron learns to activate for different features in the input data. 
For example, in the first Convolutional Layer processing a raw image, neurons across the depth 
dimension may activate for different oriented edges or color patterns. 
 
 
Figure 6: Depth process 
 
The stride determines how filters are positioned across the width and height dimensions of the 
input data (Figure 7). With a stride of 1, filters move one pixel at a time, causing significant overlap 
between receptive fields and resulting in larger output volumes. On the other hand, a stride of 2 
means filters moves 2 pixels at a time, reducing overlap between receptive fields and leading to 
smaller spatial dimensions in the output volume. 
 
 
Figure 7: Stride process 
 
==PAGE:15==
The third hyperparameter involves zero-padding, which involves adding zeros around the border 
of the input volume to control the spatial size of the output volume. This is sometimes necessary 
to maintain the exact spatial dimensions of the input volume. For instance (Figure 8), if the input 
volume is 32x32x3 and we add zero padding to create a 36x36x3 volume, applying a convolution 
layer with 5x5x3 filters and a stride of 1 will result in a 32x32x3 output volume. 
 
 
Figure 8: Zero-padding process 
 
3.3 Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU) Layer 
Before pooling operations are applied, the output of each convolution operation in every layer is 
activated using activation functions. The RELU layer is an activation function that zeroes out 
negative input values, making calculations and training faster and simpler, while also preventing 
the vanishing gradient issue. Mathematically it is defined as: f(x) = max (0, x). 
 
3.4 Pooling Layer 
Pooling operations aim to simplify or decrease the spatial dimensions of information obtained from 
feature maps. In simple words, they are used to reduce the number of parameters to be calculated, 
as well as the size of the image (width and height, but not depth). There are three types of pooling: 
average pooling, L2-norm pooling, and the widely used max pooling (Figure 9), known for its 
speed and enhanced convergence, which is the one we apply in this project. Max pooling typically 
involves applying a filter (often 2x2 in size) with a matching stride length to the input volume, 
selecting the maximum value within each sub-region covered by the filter during convolution. 
 
 
Figure 9: Max pooling process

3.5 U-Net Architecture Design 
The U-Net architecture, proposed by Ronneberger et al. in 2015 [11], has gained significant 
popularity for its effectiveness in semantic segmentation tasks. It is designed for image 
segmentation, and it comprises convolution (contracting path = encoder) for feature extraction and 
deconvolution (expansive path = decoder) layers (Figure 10) for pixel-wise prediction.  
 
The contracting path follows a standard convolutional network structure and involves a repeated 
application of two convolutions with 3 × 3 kernels (without padding), each followed by ReLU 
activation and a 2x2 max pooling operation with a stride of two for downsampling. The number 
of feature channels doubles in each convolutional layer (downsampling step).  
 
In the expansive path, each step includes upsampling of the feature map, followed by a 2 × 2 
convolution that reduces the number of features by half, concatenation with the cropped feature 
map from the contracting path, and two 3x3 convolutions with ReLU activation. Cropping is done 
to match the dimensions of convolution and deconvolution results, addressing border pixel loss. 
The final layer uses a 1x1 convolution to map each 64-component feature vector to the desired 
number of classes. Overall, the network comprises 23 convolutional layers, which eventually 
produce a segmentation map, as an output, of the same size as the original input image. 
 
 
Fig. 10: U-net architecture (example for 32x32 pixels in the lowest resolution). Each blue box corresponds to a 
multi-channel feature map. The number of channels is denoted on top of the box. The x-y-size is provided at the 
lower left edge of the box. White boxes represent copied feature maps. The arrows denote the different operations. 
 
The utilization of the U-Net architecture for detecting corrosion in ship images showcases its 
potential to improve the efficiency and accuracy of maritime inspection processes. By leveraging 
the U-Net's robust semantic segmentation capabilities, researchers and industry practitioners can 
enhance the detection and monitoring of corrosion in ship structures, contributing to overall safety 
and maintenance efforts in the maritime sector.

3.6 Segnet Architecture Design 
One prominent architecture designed to address semantic segmentation challenges is SegNet. 
Developed by researchers at the University of Cambridge, SegNet [12] has gained popularity for 
its efficient utilization of deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) in pixel-wise labeling tasks 
with limited computational resources. Its architecture is characterized by its encoder-decoder 
structure, where the encoder network captures the hierarchical features of an input image, and its 
corresponding decoder network reconstructs the segmented output (Figure 11). This design allows 
for the efficient processing of high-resolution images while preserving spatial details crucial for 
accurate semantic segmentation. 
 
 
Figure 11: SegNet architecture 
 
The encoder network is comprised of 13 convolutional layers, mirroring the initial 13 
convolutional layers found in the VGG16 [13] network originally created for object classification. 
Each encoder conducts convolution using filters to generate feature maps. These maps are 
normalized and then passed through a rectified-linear function (ReLU). Subsequently, max pooling 
with a 2x2 window and stride 2 is done, leading to a 2x sub-sampling and providing wider spatial 
context for each pixel feature map. Though, repeated pooling enhances translation invariance for 
robust classification, as the layers go deeper, it diminishes spatial resolution in the feature maps, 
potentially erasing vital details like boundaries crucial for segmentation. To preserve boundary 
information before subsampling, only the max-pooling indices, i.e., the positions of maximum 
feature values in each pooling window, are stored for each encoder feature map. 
 
The decoder network in SegNet is designed symmetrically to the encoder network. It transforms 
low-resolution features into higher-resolution versions through convolutions and guided 
upsampling, utilizing stored max-pooling indices from the encoder. For example, a 2 × 2 low-
resolution feature map can be expanded into a 4 × 4 matrix filled with zeros, where the original 2 
× 2 map's values are placed back into the locations in the 4 × 4 matrix from which they were pooled 
in the encoder. This method (Figure 12) of reusing pooling indices helps restore spatial information 
and enhances boundary accuracy by creating sparse feature maps, which are then convolved with 
decoder filters to generate dense feature maps. Following this, batch normalization is applied. 
Notably, the first decoder, linked to the initial encoder, produces a multi-channel feature map 
despite the encoder input being RGB. Conversely, other decoders output feature maps mirroring 
the size and channels of their corresponding encoder inputs. The high-dimensional feature 
==PAGE:18==
representation from the final decoder output undergoes a multi-class soft-max classification to 
generate individual pixel class probabilities. The soft-max classifier yields a multi-channel image 
of class probabilities, with the final segmentation derived from the class with the highest 
probability for each pixel. In contrast to SegNet, which utilizes pooling indices, U-Net opts to 
transfer the complete feature map to the corresponding decoder layers. This approach, while 
requiring more memory, allows U-Net to concatenate these feature maps with the upsampled 
decoder outputs (achieved through deconvolution). SegNet leverages the pre-trained convolutional 
layer weights from the VGG network to enhance its performance. 
 
Convolution with trainable decoder filters 
 
Figure 12: An illustration of SegNet decoders. A, b, c, d correspond to values in a feature map. SegNet employs the 
max-pooling indices to increase the size of the feature map(s) without learning any new parameters. This enlarged 
feature map is then convolved with a set of trainable decoder filters. 
 
3.7 DeepLabv3+ Architecture Design 
The DeepLabv3+ series addresses the multi-scale challenges in image segmentation using a multi-
scale atrous convolution technique. The Atrous convolution [14], also known as dilated 
convolution, is an effective technique that enables explicit control over the resolution of features 
generated by deep convolutional neural networks. It allows for adjustments to the filter's field-of-
view (adjust the rate r), facilitating the capture of multi-scale information. This method extends 
the traditional convolution operation, providing greater flexibility in feature extraction. 
 
The DeepLabV3 Plus model primarily consists of a deep convolutional network featuring atrous 
convolution (Figure 13) to capture multi-scale contextual information without significantly 
increasing computational complexity. This design effectively enlarges the receptive field of each 
convolutional layer without increasing the parameter count or decreasing spatial dimensions 
(downsampling the input image), striking a balance between accuracy and efficiency. 
 
In semantic segmentation, adopting an output stride of 16 (or 8) enables more detailed feature 
extraction by eliminating the striding in the final one or two blocks and utilizing atrous convolution 
correspondingly. For instance, when setting the output stride to 8, atrous convolution rates of 2 
and 4 can be applied to the last two blocks, respectively. 
==PAGE:19==
 
 
 
Figure 13: Convolution (a) vs Atrous Convolution (b) 
 
DeepLabv3+ incorporates a powerful feature known as the atrous spatial pyramid pooling (ASPP) 
module (Figure 14). The ASPP module employs parallel atrous convolutions to process the input 
features of ship images, extracted with the use of a deep backbone network, in this case we use 
Resnet. It comprises a 1 × 1 convolution, three 3 × 3 atrous convolutions at various dilation rates 
and multiple fields of view, while also performing pooling to capture multi-scale context. Batch 
normalization layers ensure data normalization after each operation. The resulting feature maps 
from each branch are concatenated, followed by a 1 × 1 convolution for feature compression and 
integration. 
 
 
Figure 14: Atrous Spatial Pyramid Pooling (ASPP). To classify the center pixel (orange), ASPP exploits multi-scale 
features by employing multiple parallel filters with different rates. The effective Field-Of-Views are shown in 
different colors. 
 
 
Unlike DeepLabV3, DeepLabV3 Plus introduces a decoder that compresses low-level features to 
minimize their impact. It then upsamples the feature map processed by the ASPP module to match 
the low-level resolution, followed by another upsampling after a 3 × 3 convolution, gradually 
==PAGE:20==
restoring spatial information to better delineate the target boundaries in ship images for pixel-level 
predictions. The structure of the DeepLabV3 Plus model is shown in Figure 15.

3.8 Training protocols 
Hyperparameter tuning plays a critical role in optimizing a model’s performance. Parameters such 
as learning rate, batch size, and the number of epochs are systematically adjusted based on 
validation performance. A learning rate scheduler can be implemented to dynamically modify the 
learning rate during training, allowing for fine-tuning in the latter stages. The choice of batch size 
impacts memory consumption and convergence stability, with smaller batch sizes often fostering 
more generalization but requiring longer training times. 
 
The training procedure follows a supervised learning paradigm, where our models UNet, 
DeepLabv3+ and Segnet are trained on pairs of ship images and their corresponding segmentation 
masks, which distinguish corrosion in white and non-corrosion in black. The training dataset 
consists of 19,600 RGB images, each measuring 376x672 pixels. A key aspect of the training 
process is the selection of a suitable loss function, the binary cross-entropy loss 
(BCEWithLogitLoss) in our case, which measures the performance of a model whose output is a 
probability value between 0 and 1. A lower BCE indicates better performance. Moreover, we 
employed the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.001 to facilitate efficient convergence 
during training. The Adam optimizer's adaptive learning rate mechanism helped in adjusting the 
learning rates for individual parameters in the model, leading to faster convergence and improved 
performance. It is computationally efficient and requires little memory overhead, making it 
suitable for large datasets and high-dimensional spaces. Each epoch begins with a shuffle of the 
training set, and mini batches of 32 images are selected in sequence, ensuring that each image is 
included only once per epoch. The model that performs best on the validation dataset is chosen. 
 
==PAGE:21==
3.9 Evaluation metrics 
In evaluating semantic segmentation models, commonly used metrics include Intersection Over 
Union (IoU), also known as the Jaccard Index. Intersection over Union (IoU) quantifies the overlap 
between the predicted and ground truth corrosion areas, providing a measure of segmentation 
accuracy for each category separately, with mIoU giving the average performance across all 
categories. Higher mIoU values indicate better overall segmentation model performance. For each 
category i, TPi signifies the number of correctly predicted pixels for positive samples, FPi denotes 
the number of falsely predicted positive pixels, TNi stands for the number of correctly predicted 
negative pixels, FNi represents the number of falsely predicted negative pixels, and k represents 
the number of segmentation categories. 
 
 
 
Visualizing the model predictions alongside ground truth annotations can facilitate qualitative 
assessments of the corrosion detection results. Visual inspection of segmentation maps, severity 
predictions, and uncertainty estimates can provide valuable insights into the model's 
interpretability, highlighting areas of accurate detection and potential challenges in corrosion 
identification.

5 Discussion 
 
5.1 Findings 
Deep CNNs have significantly influenced a wide array of application areas, becoming the go-to 
solution for numerous challenges in computer vision, speech recognition, and natural language 
processing. In image analysis, researchers have capitalized on the computational benefits of 
working with small 2D and 3D patches instead of processing entire slices or volumes, allowing 
them to train deep CNNs for precise segmentation algorithms. This achievement has been widely 
embraced by the community, which previously relied heavily on shallow architectures. Today, 
most CNN architectures feature numerous layers, including additional normalization layers such 
as batch normalization. Moreover, these architectures are growing increasingly complex, 
integrating concepts from optimization and probabilistic models. 
 
While each model offers its own computational benefits and challenges, achieving strong 
generalization demands an architecture with optimized layers that incorporates the best 
hyperparameters and advanced training techniques. Ultimately, there is no one-size-fits-all 
architecture; ongoing research aims to develop models capable of effectively learning to represent 
the underlying input images without falling victim to substantial overfitting. 
 
5.2 Challenges 
We found that many proposed methods highlight the challenges associated with deep CNN models. 
The first issue is the computational demand: analyzing, manipulating, and processing each voxel 
in a volume requires substantial computational resources. This entails significant memory 
requirements for storing the extracted patches and a considerable amount of time for processing 
them. The deep learning software libraries that implement deep CNN layers typically utilize 
parallel or distributed frameworks, enabling researchers to train their models on multi-core 
architectures or GPUs. The second challenge in training CNN models for corrosion detection is 
data imbalance. Strategies such as two-phase training, careful patch selection, and specialized loss 
functions have been suggested to address this issue. 
 
Releasing source code to the public has become a common practice in the computer vision 
community, significantly accelerating research in the field. The deep learning libraries most 
frequently utilized for MRI segmentation include TensorFlow, Caffe, Keras, and PyTorch. 
 
5.3 Areas for future research 
Due to limited training data, low spatial resolution of images, and the requirement for quick 
prediction times, training significantly deep CNNs has been challenging. Developing faster 
convolution methods is essential for training these networks. While FFT algorithms and faster 
matrix multiplication techniques have enhanced CNN computation speed, there is still potential 
==PAGE:31==
for improvement in the training algorithms of deep CNNs. Upcoming algorithms are anticipated 
to not only boost the performance of deep CNNs but also to be highly optimized, minimizing or 
eliminating hyper-parameters, which often pose a significant challenge for users during tuning. 
 
Looking ahead, we aim to leverage the insights gained from our analysis of segmentation 
architectures to create more efficient models tailored for real-time applications. Additionally, we 
are keen on exploring methods to estimate the uncertainty of predictions made by deep 
segmentation architectures.
