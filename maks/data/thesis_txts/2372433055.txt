Abstract
The following thesis presents the analysis of the results of an experiment done by DHI
aiming to reproduce diﬀerent sea states. The main focus of the research is the study of
breaking waves on a monopile. The work is essentially divided into four sections; the
analysis of the tests, breaking waves identiﬁcation, slamming impulse analysis, and dy-
namic response of the pile caused by these waves. Additionally, some proposals for further
research are presented.
In the ﬁrst section, the analysis of the experiments present an evaluation on the perfor-
mance of the tests with respect to the targeted sea state characteristics and further on
an overview on how variations in the sea states aﬀect the wave’s surface elevation and force.
For the breaking waves identiﬁcation, in the second section, the development of three
methods and a validation criteria in order to detect their presence on a time series are
shown. These methods are thought based on theoretical background and empirical anal-
ysis from the tests results.
Once the slamming waves are identiﬁed, their eﬀects are studied. In the third section,
slamming impulse is calculated and it is searched for a relation between surface elevation,
force and impulse. Moreover, in the fourth section, the dynamic forces and moments are
shown with and without the slamming impulse in order to visualize how is the structural
impact of a breaking wave.
As oﬀshore wind energy is growing and it is foretold that it will grow more rapidly during
the next years, the analysis of DHI’s experiment and the results presented in this thesis
intend to be useful for further studies and research on wave slamming on monopiles and
its numerical reproduction.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Oﬀshore wind energy is an alternative source of energy production that has been growing
rapidly in the recent years. The ﬁrst oﬀshore wind farm was established in 1991 in Vindeby,
Denmark, but it was not until the 2000s that a signiﬁcant amount of capacity started to be
installed. According to the data published by the GWEC, shown in Figure 1.1, during the
last ﬁve years oﬀshore wind energy has increased a bit more than three times its installed
capacity and it has been forecast an important expansion for the incoming years.
Figure 1.1: Cumulative oﬀshore capacity 2011-2016 [6]
The European Wind Energy Association, EWEA, predicted three possible scenarios for
oﬀshore capacity installed in 2030; the high, the central and the low, with the central
being the most probable to occur, estimating that only in Europe the capacity installed
will be around 66, 000MW.
This would represent a growth of more than 700% in 15
years. Two other forecasts, IEA’s and EC’s, predicted 10% and 5% less installed capacity,
respectively, yet still upholding an imminent expansion of the oﬀshore wind energy.

==PAGE:22==
Chapter 1. Introduction
In 2013, more than 90% of the oﬀshore wind turbines installed were built on monopiles.
In countries like Germany, the UK, Denmark, and France, sea bed conditions are suitable
for this type of foundations. Thus, for the next years monopiles will remain dominant in
Europe [14]. This means that oﬀshore wind farms and monopile foundations are very well
positioned towards a huge market looming.
Oﬀshore wind farms present multiple advantages when compared to onshore. An oﬀshore
site presents higher and steadier wind speeds that result into higher eﬃciency. Also, soci-
ety in general prefer not to see wind turbines close to the cities and oﬀshore reduces this
so-called visual impact. Nonetheless, oﬀshore wind farms costs are higher than onshore.
1.1
Motivation
The high expectancies of oﬀshore wind farms and monopiles make this a desirable subject
of study. Reducing oﬀshore costs represents an engaging challenge. Cost diminution starts
with optimizing the design. In order to achieve this, data analysis of the wind-wave climate
loads is fundamental.
1.2
Aim
For this thesis the analysis of an experiment of multiple sea states reproduction, carried
out by DHI, is done. The aim is to develop an accurate method to identify slamming waves
in experimental data time series to present a detailed analysis on the eﬀects they produce
and to deliver valuable information and conclusions which may be used for numerical
validation and further research.

Chapter 3
DHI’s Experiments
DHI is a worldwide company dedicated to the research of water environments with more
than 50 years of experience [2]. They were in charge of carrying out the DeRisk - WP2
model tests which consisted in the reproduction of diﬀerent sea states, prepared for DTU
Vindenergi. The main objective was to generate a realistic data set to be used for further
numerical model validation.
Chapter 3 is pure description of DHI’s “DeRisk - WP2: Proposal for DeRisk 3D Physical
Model Tests” [3]. None of this Chapter’s authorship belongs to this thesis’ writer and it
is all referenced to the experiment’s paper. The tests performed by DHI are presented
hereafter.
3.1
Tests Setup
The tests were conducted in the shallow water basin at DHI Hørsholm, Denmark. The
basin is equipped with a multi-element wave generator capable of generating any realistic
2D or 3D wave spectrum. To minimize wave reﬂection, crushed stones with a slope of
1:4 were placed opposite to the wave generator and along the basin sides. Furthermore,
steel-wave absorbers were placed inside the basin in an M-like shape. The purpose of these
absorbers was to direct the wave energy reﬂected away from the wave generator and the
test area. The model tests were scaled with a factor of 1:50. The basin setup is shown in
Figure 3.1.
Moreover, the test setup was deﬁned. An arrangement of wave gauges, a drag column and
a monopile were placed in the test area as it is illustrated in Figure 3.2. Herein is listed a
more detailed description of the models and instrumentation used for the tests:
• Drag column model: A 1.8m (full scale) diameter aluminium cylinder simulating
wave loads on a jacket type structure. Nevertheless, as the primary purpose of this
paper is the wave slamming on a monopile, it is disregarded.
• Monopile model: A stiﬀ7m (full scale) diameter PVC cylinder.
• Surface elevation: Multiple wave gauges to measure the surface elevation.
• Global loads on monopile: Force transducers placed at the top of the monopile
and on the seabed capable of measuring Fx and Fy.

Chapter 6
Phase Angle Slamming Identiﬁca-
tion Method Proposal
In Chapter 5, the slamming identiﬁcation methods used for the experiment and analysis in
this thesis are described. However, a fourth slamming identiﬁcation method is proposed,
yet for multiple reasons was not totally developed. This Chapter describes the theoretical
background and an approach to develop it.
The following method is identiﬁed based on the surface elevation’s and force’s phase angles
and shifts. In order to get the phase angle of both time series, η and Fx, the following
MATLAB algorithm is applied:
X = hilbert(Xr)
X = Xr + iXi
ϕX = angle(X)
(6.1)
The Hilbert transform shifts the signal’s phase 90◦for positive frequencies and −90◦for
negative ones [10]. The MATLAB hilbert function performs the transform of the input
signal, Xr, which is a real vector. The output, X, yields the second expression in Equation
6.1, where Xi is the Hilbert transform of Xr. Subsequently the function angle of X yields
the phase angle, ϕX, of the signal. The functions are run for Xr = η and Xr = Fx.
Figure 6.1 is presenting three plots. In the upper plot it is showing three waves, η and
Fx. The ﬁrst wave is a slamming wave, while the others are not. In the middle plot it
is showing the phase angles for both signals and in third plot it is showing the diﬀerence
between the phase angles ϕη −ϕFx.
As it is seen in the upper plot, non-breaking waves’ force and surface elevation are out
of phase by approximately 90◦, while the peak of the slamming wave force gives an extra
impulse and almost matches both curve phases. In the middle plot, it is seen that when
this phenomena occurs, η and Fx phases are the closest to be equal and right afterwards,
the phases notably change their gradients. Finally, the lower plot reveals a peak approxi-
mating to zero. These pattern is present in every slamming wave.
Even though every slamming wave comes up with this behaviour, not all of them are as
clear as the example in Figure 6.1. Additionally, irregularity in the experiment waves and

Chapter 10
Conclusions
The analysis of DHI’s experiment led to valuable conclusions. The fact that the sea states
achieved were very close to the targeted values allowed an accurate comparison between
them and how the surface elevation and force behave. This gave a broad notion on how
diﬀerent parameters aﬀect the loads and eﬀects.
As this thesis main focus was slamming, the identiﬁcation methods were essential to do
a reliable study. The slamming identiﬁcation methods developed were good enough to
carry on with the analysis of slamming eﬀects. However, some ﬂaws were detected and
improvements were thought as the proposal to develop an additional method based on the
phase angles.
It was detected that slamming waves mostly come from the higher waves and the higher
forces, nevertheless middle range forces also came from slamming events. A relation be-
tween the force and the impulse revealed that lower forces represented lower impulses,
where these could be related to early and late slamming.
It was shown that slamming waves induced high dynamic responses. Although some of
them induced lower responses and it is assumed these events correspond to low force low
impulse slamming.
10.1
Further studies
• Development of slamming identiﬁcation method based on phase angles.
• Study the cases where slamming events produce low forces and low impulses as they
are assumed to be early and late breakers, yet it is not certain.
• Study the relation between the dynamic response, force and impulse of slamming
events.
• Validation and study of data analyzed for further numerical reproduction
