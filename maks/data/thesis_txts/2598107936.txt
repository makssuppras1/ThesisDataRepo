Abstract
The increasing efforts to tackle climate change and reduce CO2 emissions has con-
siderably changed the electricity sector. The decommissioning of traditional power plants,
based on coal, gas and nuclear energy, replaced by renewable energies, mainly wind and
solar power, has shifted the paradigm of electricity production, from few large electricity
production sites to scattered and numerous small production sites. However, mass-scale
production of renewable energies is crucial to accelerate the transition. Especially, off-
shore wind farms are expected to provide an important part of the power mix in the next
decades, mainly because land occupation and social acceptation are less problematic
for offshore wind power. As the distance from the shore increases, the interest of DC
connection to the onshore grids through HVDC links grows and mass-scale production
becomes possible.
However, the installation of large offshore wind farms connected to the onshore grid
through HVDC links still faces many technical challenges. The absence of traditional
synchronous generation on the offshore AC grid and the power electronic interfaced gen-
eration weaken the security of the power grid, as inertia and voltage support is lacking.
The concept of zero-inertia or low-inertia power system emerges, where the electronic
converters of the renewable energy units must control the voltage and the frequency of
the power grid. Such controls still need to be developed and tested.
This master thesis studies the voltage regulation of a low-inertia grid, the North Sea
Wind Power Hub, an artificial island collecting wind power and transferring it to different
countries through HVDC links, expected to be installed by 2030 in the North Sea. A sec-
ondary voltage controller is proposed to complement a local controller based on reactive
power droop. This voltage controller relies on a Model Predictive Control and is firstly
validated using steady-state simulations on Matpower. Then, dynamic simulations with
PowerFactory are studied to give further in-depth analysis of the voltage regulation of the
NSWPH.
Power system projects are often massive and field testing might not be possible for
economic and practical reasons. However, real-time testing is needed to validate the
operation of a control algorithm or of some hardware. RTDS provides such possibilities
with real-time simulations and hardware-in-the-loop possibilities. Real-time validation is
often the last step in a power system project. In complement to the secondary voltage
controller proposed, this master thesis aims at developing a NSWPH model on RTDS so
that control algorithms could later be tested in real time operation.
This master thesis draws the conclusion that the combination of a local controller and a
secondary voltage controller is particularly adapted to control the voltage of the NSWPH.
The secondary voltage controller developed relies on a MPC approach which is compu-
tationally efficient and straightforward to adapt to different power systems. The proposed
controller shows overall good performances in terms of voltage regulation, computational
speed and robustness to model inaccuracies and noisy measurements. The importance
of dynamic simulations to design a controller is also highlighted. Last, the first step of
the development of a NSWPH model on RTDS is validated and only few adjustments are
now needed to obtain the full model of the NSWPH, which will later be useful to study and
validate proposed controllers.

Introduction
1.1
Background
The fight against climate change has led to major changes in power grids and power
mix around the world in the past decades. The dramatic need to reduce CO2 emissions
emitted by the electricity sector has completely shifted the paradigm of electricity produc-
tion. Renewable energies are becoming increasingly important in the power mix of many
countries. Solar and wind energy represent the main new renewable energy installations.
Especially, wind energy is expected to progressively become the first source of electricity
production in many power mix, replacing gas and coal. In particular, offshore wind farms
are promising solutions to provide an important part of the energy mix as, on the contrary
to onshore wind farms, land occupation and social acceptation are less of a problem.
Some countries, such as Denmark, had more than 40 % of electricity produced from wind
power in 2015 and the Danish government aims that more than 50% of the Danish gross
energy consumption comes from renewable by 2030 [1]. As a whole, Europe is aiming at
27% of renewable energies in its power mix by 2030, with wind power as the main contri-
bution [2]. In other parts of the world, the trend is the same. For instance, Australia aimed
at having more than 23.5% of renewable energy production in 2020 and reached its goal
a year ahead of time [3]. Especially, the state of South Australia already experiences high
integration of wind power (more than 36% of the capacity installed in 2015) and the wind
production levels the consumption at low-demand time [4].
The wide integration of renewable energies across the world has therefore led to the
decommissioning of traditional power plants based on coal or gas, which operate with
synchronous generators. Historically, power grids have been build relying on those syn-
chronous generators which assure major role for the security and quality of the power
supply. Traditional power plants are participating in frequency regulation, by providing
ancillary services as well as with inertia support due to their rotor directly coupled to the
power grid. They are also able to provide voltage support through the absorption or injec-
tion of reactive power. The replacement of traditional power plants by renewable energies
(such as wind and solar) decoupled to the grid by power electronic converters therefore
reduce the system strength, which reflects the resilience of power system variables to dis-
turbances and the capability of a power system to recover a fault. The power electronic
converters interfacing renewable energy sources to the grid decouple the rotating part of
the generator (for wind power) to the grid, thus inertia support from wind turbines is not
possible. In addition, the rating of the power electronic converters is often limited to 1.1
pu which prevents high short-circuit current, decreasing the effectiveness of short-circuit
protection schemes. Moreover, historically, distributed generators such as wind farms
and solar panels were not providing any ancillary services such as voltage support or fre-
quency regulation. However, as they are becoming more and more present in the power
mix, a new paradigm of operating power grids needs to be adopted.
Such evolution of the power grid has given the concept of microgrid a lot of attention
in the research community. Microgrids are low-voltage electrical distribution networks,
heterogeneously composed of distributed generation, storage, load, and managed au-
tonomously from the larger primary network. Microgrids can connect to a larger power
system through a Point of Common Coupling (PCC), but are also able to “island” them-
selves and operate independently [5]. In those microgrids, wind and solar farm can and
must therefore provide ancillary services to assure a secure operation of the power net-
work, i.e. to regulate the frequency and the voltage within the desired range. The possibil-
ity of ancillary services from distributed generation unit (DGU) has long not been consid-

ered for the operation of power grids but it is now commonly accepted that they represent
an opportunity for Distribution System Operator (DSO) [6]. Using DGU to provide ancillary
services can postpone grid reinforcement investments as DGU can help, for instance, to
regulate voltage locally by absorbing or injecting reactive power. Many control algorithm
for voltage and frequency regulation of microgrids provided by DGU has been developed
in the past decades (see subsection 1.2).
Following the microgrid concept, the idea of low-inertia or zero-inertia power grids is
gaining momentum in the research community and is often presented as a possible solu-
tion to the wide integration of renewable energies in power grids. Zero-inertia power grids
are power systems with all source of electricity production interfaced to the grid through
a power converter [7]. In such grids, the power electronic converters therefore need to
operate as ”grid-forming” converter, i.e. to control the voltage and frequency of the power
grid. Their control algorithms need to be designed so that they can fully control the op-
eration of a power grid without any synchronous generators [8]. If such controls were
designed and successful, there will theoretically have no more limitation to the integration
of renewable energies in a power mix [9]. Grid-forming converters is therefore a promising
research field which draws the attention of TSO, the industry [10,11] and grid-forming con-
verters are even highlighted as a potential solution to secure operation of power converter
dominated power systems by ENTSO-e, the European Network of Transmission System
Operators for electricity which represents 43 TSO from 36 countries in Europe [12].
Zero or low-inertia power grids are particularly interesting for large offshore wind farms.
An example of such project is the North Sea Wind Power Hub (NSWPH) [13, 14], which
will be presented in section 2. An AC offshore power grid comprised of a very important
number of wind turbines would be linked to different onshore grids through High Voltage
Direct Current (HVDC) links [15]. Thus, the AC offshore grid would need to operate as
a zero or low-inertia power grid (depending on the presence or not of synchronous con-
densers on the offshore side), with the HVDC converters (and possibly the wind turbines
converters) regulating the voltage and the frequency within the desired range. Those con-
verters would also need to deal with the variability of the production of the wind turbines.
Those offshore energy hubs are often presented as a game changer in the wind energy
sector as the capacity installed would be very important, therefore enabling wind power
integration in the power mix to dramatically increase. In addition to the NSWPH project,
which is still in the investigation phase, the Danish government has already agreed to
install two offshore wind energy hubs for a total of 5 GW by 2030 [16].
To sum up, the increasing place of renewable energies in the power mix has triggered
the need for a change in the operation paradigm of power networks. DGUs (such as
wind and solar farms etc.) now need to provide voltage and frequency support so that the
decommissioning of traditional power plants resulting from the integration of renewable
energies does not diminish the security and quality of the power supply. Therefore, the
research community is really focusing on control algorithms from DGUs to regulate volt-
age and frequency to their desired values and that is in this field that this master thesis
lies.
1.2
State of the art
A high number of control algorithms for voltage and frequency regulation of power
grids has been added to the literature in the past decades. The main trend is to try to
design controls which are communication-free (i.e. each agent of the power grid does not
communicate with each others), model-free (i.e. they are not based on the modelling of
the power grid, whether it is a dynamic model or a static model) and decentralized (i.e.
where each agent computes its own control algorithm with its own computational capac-
2

ities). However, there is a compromise to attain between those characteristics and the
performances of the controller. This master thesis primarily focuses on voltage regulation
but some work on frequency regulation are still highlighted.
[17,18] propose respectively a quadratic droop control for voltage stability and reactive
power balancing of microgrids and a grid-forming control for converters based on match-
ing of synchronous machines behaviors. However, as numerous papers in the microgrid
control community, they heavily rely on analytical proof of convergence and stability, with-
out offering any simulation studies.
Feedback optimisation (FO) refers to voltage control where DGU are smart agents
that can adjust the amount of reactive power injected into the grid based on a feedback
law [19–22]. This feedback law can be based on measurements (voltage magnitude and
phase of the DGU own bus) and limited communication with close neighbors. [19] presents
a general feedback approach in details as well as how to implement FO in a distributed
approach. [20, 21] describe a FO for voltage control of distribution grids and validates
it experimentally with a comparison with droop control and optimal reactive power flow
control, firstly implementing in a centralized manner (hence with communication) and
then implemented in a distributed manner with limited communication (only with neigh-
bors DGUs). [22] investigates over the fundamental gap between performances of purely
local voltage regulation strategies and distributed strategies with communication between
agents. It does so by comparing static local feedback law (QV curves) and their proposed
FO based on a simple grid model (based on the admittance matrix) and limited commu-
nication with neighbors.
[23] proposes a distributed control scheme to mitigate overvoltage problem on distri-
bution networks. Its model-free control of photovoltaic units is based on logic, switching
from one mode of active and reactive power injection to another based on logic events
(depending on time and voltage levels). It requires very limited use of communication.
The advantage of a logic-based control is simplicity, being model-free and computed on
a distributed manner. However, logic-based controls generally achieve poorer perfor-
mances than more advanced controls.
[24] presents a distributed controller for secondary frequency and voltage control with
nearest neighbor communication needed and without the need of a power grid model. It
uses a Distributed Averaging Proportional Integral (DAPI) method, which regulates fre-
quency to its desired value and does a compromise between voltage regulation and reac-
tive power sharing. It is based on droop control to which is added a secondary control to
restore frequency and voltage value. Simulation results are thoroughly analysed in many
different cases. [25] complements this work by implementing and validating experimen-
tally a secondary frequency controller based on [24].
Novel voltage and frequency control algorithm based on data are being developed re-
cently thanks to the increasing computational power available. Those controls are called
data-driven controls and some are presented in [26–29]. [26] presents a Data Enabled
Predictive Control (DeePC) for grid-connected power converters, which mimics the MPC
but without the need of a model. It only needs input/output data measured to predict fu-
ture controls. However, DeePC method is expected not to be able to deal with high order
systems, hence a finite horizon output based MPC is introduced to replace DeePC for
large scale systems. The model of this method is derived in a recursive way and in an
online manner, which makes this method still interesting. The long computational time
of DeePC makes it difficult to implement in real time for high order systems. [27] focuses
on data-driven control for distribution grids, by using historical data and offline optimisa-
tion techniques to derive local controls that emulates optimal behavior without the need
of communication. This data driven method is different from the previous data-driven

one as to design the controller, a first step is to apply OPF methods offline with historical
data. [28] presents a co-optimised planning and operation of active distribution grids by
determining offline with an OPF the optimal QV characteristics of the DERs. An overview
of current practices for DGUs is also presented and simulation results and comparison
of their proposed controller are well detailed. The controller presented is an ”optimized”
purely local controller. [29] from the same author than [28] presents a method to derive
local controllers which mimic OPF control. With historical data, an offline OPF algorithm
is used to create a dataset of optimal controls. This dataset will then be used to create
Reactive Power and Active Power Curtailment curve for local controllers (which only need
the measure of the voltage and active power injected). Those local controllers are proved
to achieve near optimal controls, through simulation results. This concept of gathering
data and using them offline is particularly interesting when the topology of a grid does not
change too much over time.
On the contrary, many controls are based on models, i.e. how the quantities of inter-
est (voltage, frequency) will evolve through time depending on actions of decision vari-
ables (i.e. active and reactive power injection, tap ratio of transformers etc.). MPC con-
trols are often based on state-spaced model which is not really adapted to power grids
where such state-space models are often difficult to compute. However, in the past years,
MPC has gained interest again in the power grid community by adopting models which
are straightforward to compute such as in [30, 31]. [30] proposes a centralized and co-
ordinated Multi-Terminal DC grid supervisory control based on MPC. It relies on static
and sensitivity-based models through 2 current-power and voltage-power matrices. [31]
presents a combined local and centralized voltage control for distribution networks, tak-
ing advantages from both the local and centralized structure. The local control is based
on QV characteristics, and the centralized one is a multi-time step constrained optimisa-
tion based on MPC, which adjusts the QV characteristics of the local controllers. Active
power control is considered in case of severe conditions and the models for MPC are
based on current-power and voltage-power sensitivity matrices. [32] describes the formu-
lation and implementation of a MPC algorithm to regulate distribution network voltages in
the presence of high penetration of distributed generation. Impact of model inaccuracies
and measurement noise are analysed and the results show the robustness of the MPC al-
gorithm, which is a general characteristics of MPC thanks to their closed-loop nature. [33]
is an interesting complement to [31] and details the method to determine the sensitivity
matrices needed for the MPC applied in both papers.
It has to be noted here that many of the papers presented above apply to microgrids.
In the particular case of zero or low-inertia power grids, those methods would need to
be adapted and might not be as efficient as it is for microgrids when the generation mix
is more varied. In particular, stability issues can arise in zero or low-inertia power grids
whereas for microgrids, the PCC to an infinite grid or some DGUs using synchronous
generators can guarantee stability. This is why the concept of grid-forming converters
is gaining momentum in the research community. The grid-forming converters do not
only provide ancillary services by changing their active and reactive power setpoints, they
mainly ”form” the grid frequency and voltage as well as assuring stability at the same
time. Grid-forming converters and their advantages for power systems are thoroughly
addressed in [7–9, 12]. On the contrary, grid-following converters ”follow” the frequency
formed by the other components of the power grid (such as traditional synchronous gen-
erators, synchronous condensers etc.). In today’s power network, there are mainly grid-
following converters but as traditional power plants are being replaced by renewable en-
ergies, the need for grid-forming converters is increasingly important.
Many research projects work on those problematics, such as the MIGRATE project

1.3
Objectives and delimitation of the project
This master thesis, a part of the multiDC project [36], focuses on the voltage control
of the NSWPH, an offshore AC grid with low inertia. This offshore AC grid is connected
by HVDC links to onshore grids and the HVDC converters need to control the voltage of
the offshore AC grid. No voltage regulation is expected from the wind farms, i.e. they
operate with a power factor equal to 1 and try to maximise their active power output. This
work will use the NSWPH model on PowerFactory described in [15], whether it is the
one-island configuration or the three-island configuration. Voltage and frequency controls
are already implemented in the NSWPH PowerFactory model and offers satisfying per-
formances for the regulation of the frequency. Regarding the voltage control, the control
already implemented is successful at maintaining the voltage within the desired range but
a secondary controller is needed to bring the voltage back to its nominal value.
Therefore, a Model Predictive Control, adapted from the work from [31, 33], is pro-
posed to control the voltage of the NSWPH. It has been chosen to apply a MPC as the
topology of the NSWPH is fixed and that the model needed, which relies on sensitivity ma-
trices, is expected to be straightforward to obtain offline (and even online). Also, the MPC
is a centralized controller which requires communication between the centralized con-
troller and the HVDC offshore converters. However, as it will explained in more details in
section 2, the infrastructure of the NSWPH seems to be adapted to such a configuration,
without any major extra costs. No communication is required between the different HVDC
converters which is a true advantage especially in case of multi-vendor systems.
A model of the NSWPH on Matpower was created in order to validate the perfor-
mances of the MPC algorithm before implementing it to the NSWPH model on PowerFac-
tory. Thus, the MPC has firstly been validated using steady-state simulations with Mat-
power before going to dynamic simulations with PowerFactory. It is of major importance
to validate the results of a controller on dynamic models as instability can appear and
controller performances can only be guaranteed if tested on a dynamic model, especially
for low-inertia power grids with high penetration of power electronics. The implementation
of the MPC to PowerFactory has been realised through Matlab/PowerFactory coupling.
An other important aspect of this master thesis is the development of the NSWPH
model in the real-time software RTDS. This is needed for the multiDC project so that
control algorithms developed can be tested in real-time simulations and possibly with
Hardware-in-the-Loop (HIL). However, by lack of time, the developed MPC could not be
tested on RTDS. It is the controls described in [15] which are used in the RTDS model of
the NSWPH.
To sum up, considering the state of art and the objectives of the multiDC project, this
master thesis aims at providing the following contributions:
• A method to derive sensitivity matrices for power grids with the use of Matpower as
simulation tool.

a system architecture as presented in Fig. 2.2 enables to reduce interconnection costs (by

2.3
A secondary voltage controller for the NSWPH
As seen in Fig. 2.8, the HUB bus might experience voltage steady-state deviations.
Even if those deviations are minor and acceptable, it is of interest to develop a secondary
voltage controller able to bring back the HUB voltage to its nominal value. Indeed, in other
configurations, e.g. with longer wind farm cables, the AC offshore grid could experience
higher voltage deviations. Over time, those voltage deviations could induce losses (for
undervoltage situations) or wear and tear of the components (for overvoltage situations).
Considering the topology of the NSWPH, it has been decided to develop a centralized
14
Model predictive voltage control of low-inertia offshore transmission grids
==PAGE:26==
controller based on a Model Predictive Control (MPC). Only the HVDC offshore convert-
ers are considered to regulate the HUB voltage. No voltage regulation from the wind
farms is considered as they must operate in MPPT and at unity power factor. The cen-
tralized controller would communicate with the HVDC converters. The HVDC converters
would each send their active and reactive power measurements. The centralized con-
troller would also need to measure the voltage magnitude of the HUB bus. However, the
one-island configuration would gather all the HVDC converters and the centralized con-
troller on the artificial island, within the same infrastructure, reducing drastically the costs
for the centralized controller infrastructure, for the communications and the chances of
communication failures. That is why, in the NSWPH case, having a centralized controller
communicating with individual HVDC converter is not such a major drawback. Moreover,
the converters do not communicate with each other, which might be a real advantage in
case of a multi-vendor systems, where the converters could come from different suppliers,
making communication between them harder to realise. The model-based approach is
also quite straightforward and does not depend on a heavily complex state-space model.
As it will be described in more details in section 3, the MPC is based on sensitivity matri-
ces linking a change of active and reactive power at the HVDC converter to the change
of the HUB voltage magnitude (through ∂v
∂q and ∂v
∂p coefficients). Those sensitivity coeffi-
cients can easily be extracted from the Jacobian matrices, as described in section 3. To
compute the Jacobian matrices, the centralized controller will need to be able to compute
the load-flow solutions of the NSWPH. Therefore, the centralized controller would also
need a model of the NSWPH (which can easily be computed as the topology of the power
grid is fixed) and the wind power production data. Those data are believed to be easily
accessible as TSOs usually provide good forecast of the wind power production in order to
dispatch there production. Moreover, the closed-loop nature of the MPC offers significant
advantages for the controller in term of robustness to model inaccuracies, communication
failures, measurement errors etc. [31].
However, this secondary voltage controller must operate without disturbing too much
the onshore grid. The response of the centralized controller to a disturbance on the off-
shore grid (such as the loss of cable or a sudden change of wind power) must not generate
a disturbance on the onshore grid [14]. This will have to be taken into account when de-
signing this secondary voltage controller, typically by limiting the rate of change of the
active and reactive power of the HVDC converters.
More globally, the NSWPH will have to respect the grid code requirements. As the
NSWPH will be connected to 5 different countries, it might be complicated to define one
unique set of criteria that the NSWPH must respect. However, in the past years, ENTSO-
e has worked toward the harmonization of the European grid codes so that every TSO
of the European electricity market has a definite and similar set of rules to follow for the
operation of their power grid [43]. This will greatly help to achieve an integrated Euro-
pean electricity grid and to overcome the inherent difficulties of carrying out international
projects such as the NSWPH project. So far, the NSWPH power grid is quite in an un-
known territory regarding the grid code requirements it should follow and how such a
project can be achieved within the present policies, regulatory framework and European
energy market [44]. For instance, the rate of change of active power transmitted through
the HVDC links remains an open question, as frequent large changes in production and
flow in the HVDC links would make it more difficult to control the frequency of onshore
grids [45,46].

3.1
Problem formulation
The aim of the secondary controller is to bring back or maintain the voltage magni-
tude of the HUB within a certain range, under any circumstances. To do so, the offshore
HVDC converters can regulate their reactive power, and their active power if the desired
voltage range can not be attained with only reactive power control. Therefore, the control
algorithm must determine what are the optimal reactive and active power setpoints for the
HVDC converters which enables to bring back (or maintain) the HUB voltage within the
desired range, and this must be achieved at the lowest cost and, if possible, the control
actions must be equally shared between the HVDC converters. Thus, the secondary con-
troller is based on an optimisation problem, with its objective function being to lessen the
control costs of the HVDC converters and its main constraint, amongst others, being to
maintain the HUB voltage within the desired limits.

3.2
Sensitivity matrices
To implement the control algorithm, the sensitivity matrix SV Q needs to be derived.
The coefficients ∂v
∂q represent the small variation of the voltage with respect to the small
variation of the reactive powers. In this case, the variation of the HUB voltage magnitude
with respect to the variation of the reactive power of the 5 HVDC converters is needed. It
is important to note here that the closed-loop nature of the MPC can compensate for the
inaccuracies of the model, i.e. for this controller, the inaccuracies of the sensitivity matrix.
Thanks to this feature of the MPC, the sensitivity coefficient could be determined offline
or updated online less frequently than at every call to the MPC.
The sensitivity matrix can be obtained with a few different methods. First, it can be
obtained from the transposed inverse of the power flow Jacobian matrix [31–33]. It can
also be obtained by manually running a power flow calculation with one HVDC converter
reactive power slightly changed, recording the voltage variation and doing the ratio. Also,
the impedance matrix can be used to approximate the sensitivity coefficients [20, 21].
Last, [48] and [49] propose a method based on the resolution of an over-determined sys-
tem of linear equations constructed using only measurements of power injections and
Model predictive voltage control of low-inertia offshore transmission grids
17
==PAGE:29==
voltage magnitudes. Therefore, it does not rely on the knowledge of the admittance ma-
trix but on a rather complete monitoring infrastructure [48].
As the NSWPH model on PowerFactory contains very few buses and presents a fixed
topology which would certainly rarely evolve through a short time-frame, it is chosen to
use the first method which relies on the resolution of the power flow and the extraction of
the Jacobian matrix. As the complexity of the grid is small, the power flow might even be
solved online quickly enough to not diminish the performances of the controller. This will
be investigated later. Also, this method to derive the sensitivity coefficients is believed to
be the most accurate as it relies on the power flow model of the NSWPH. Moreover, even
if the actual NSWPH contains more buses that the NSWPH model on PowerFactory, it is
believed that a condensed model such as the PowerFactory one can be used to derive
the sensitivity coefficients without loosing too much precision.

4.1
Matlab/PowerFactory coupling
Now that the MPC ability to regulate the NSWPH HUB voltage within the desired lim-
its has been validated by studying steady-state situations, it is necessary to focus on
dynamics and time-domain simulations. For that, the NSWPH PowerFactory model will
be used. To implement the MPC developed in the last section on PowerFactory, it has
been chosen to couple PowerFactory with Matlab as the scripting capacity of PowerFac-
tory are not developed enough to implement directly the MPC in PowerFactory. Coupling
between PowerFactory and Matlab is getting more and more attention as more and more
complex control algorithms are being applied in power system controls, and those control
algorithms require scripting capacities, that Matlab offers. Coupling between Matlab and
PowerFactory enables to combine the advantages of both software: the dynamic and
time-domain simulations of power grid for PowerFactory, and the optimisation problem
formulation and solving for Matlab, or more generally its scripting capacities.
However, coupling two software is never a straightforward task to carry. The coupling
process between Matlab and PowerFactory is explained in PowerFactory user manual
(section 30.6.3) [52] and especially in [53], in chapter 13 and 18. PowerFactory and Mat-
lab could be coupled either by using Simulink or a Matlab script. In our case, the Matlab
script developed in last section is used. However, it is not possible for PowerFactory to
call Matlab every few seconds. It automatically calls Matlab at every time step of the
PowerFactory solver, that time step being extremely small for the purpose of dynamic
simulations. It means that at every time step, PowerFactory will call Matlab whereas the
MPC is only to run every few seconds. For instance, in the following, it is chosen to call
the MPC every 3 seconds. The time step of the PowerFactory simulation is 0.001 second
which means that 1 call to Matlab over 3000 ones is really needed. This dramatically
increases the computational time of the Matlab/PowerFactory coupling process.
A few other recommendations are important to follow and to point out for successful
Matlab/PowerFactory coupling. Both software must have the same bit architecture (i.e.
32 or 64 bits), the time step of both software must be equal (or the one of Matlab equals
to the one of PowerFactory divided by an integer) and initial state must be the same in
PowerFactory and Matlab. In particular, initialisation might be tricky as every variables
in PowerFactory and Matlab must be initialized accordingly. Debugging is also complex
as there is no error report. If there is a mistake in the Matlab code or in the coupling
process, PowerFactory will throw an error without much information to help. This is also
why proving the working operation of the MPC with Matpower was an useful first step.
Last, the Matlab files of the optimisation algorithm must be in the Matlab search path for
PowerFactory to be able to find them.
Firstly, PowerFactory must send to Matlab the HUB voltage measurement, the ac-
tive and reactive power of the HVDC converters and the active power output of the wind
farms. PowerFactory also sends the simulation time to Matlab, which runs the MPC every
3 seconds. It has been chosen, as a first step, to determine the sensitivity coefficients on-
line. Therefore, Matlab calls Matpower which runs load-flow with the active and reactive
power measurements from PowerFactory. Once again, the load-flow is re-run until the
synchronous condenser active power output is less than 1 MW, in order to compute the
sensitivity coefficients in a realistic steady-state of the power grid. Then, Matlab defines
the objective function, the constraints and the voltage evolution of the HUB thanks to the
sensitivity coefficients. Next, it solves the optimisation problem, with the reactive power
setpoints of the 5 HVDC converters as output. Finally, Matlab sends the newly calculated

Additional studies
First of all, EMT simulations have been tried to see if the results were changing. The
results were very similar despite a few very minor differences during the transient period
after the sudden wind output change. This comforts the idea that it is not relevant to
use EMT simulations for the study of the MPC performances, as it took 1h40 to simu-
late 16 seconds with EMT simulation, which is to be compared with the 11 minutes with
RMS simulation. Another method to couple Matlab and PowerFactory, which does not
call Matlab at every time step, would be helpful to run EMT simulation with an acceptable
computational time. This will be investigated in later work. For instance, other methods
are proposed in [55–57].
Then, the robustness of the MPC with respect to measurement noise and model in-
accuracies is tested. Noise is added the voltage measurements, as well as to the active
and reactive power measurements of the HVDC converters and the wind farms (which are
useful for the computing of the sensitivity coefficients). Following [32], a white Gaussian
noise restricted to ± 1% for the voltage and ± 1% of the maximum power output for active
and reactive power measurements is considered. This noise level is actually larger than
the accuracy specifications of available metering devices (in the order of ± 0.1%), so the
results given are conservative. This noise can be directly generated in PowerFactory, with
a noise generation block [52]. However, it would certainly have been more convenient to
generate the noise inside the Matlab environment but the Matlab/PowerFactory coupling,
which is quite sensitive, was not successful when function such as rand was used in Mat-
lab. It made PowerFactory solver crashing, with no apparent reasons. Therefore, the
noise measurement block from PowerFactory is used.
Fig. 4.11 shows the evolution of the HUB voltage, when a sudden decrease of wind
power production from 800 MW to 200 MW happens at 3.1 sec, with ideal measurements
and with noisy measurements. The voltage being regulated to close to 1 pu, even in
the case with noisy measurements, it proves that the secondary controller based on the
MPC is robust to noise. This has been tested in different situations, with different noise
levels. The main consequence of the noise is that the symmetrical role played by the 5
HVDC converters is lost. Indeed, the measurements being polluted by random noise, the
secondary voltage controller will certainly not receive 5 similar measurements from the
HVDC converters, even if they were actually having the same active and reactive power
flows. This will cause the secondary voltage controller to send different reactive power
setpoints to the 5 HVDC controllers which will further contribute to the situation being not
symmetrical. This is due to noise on active and reactive power measurements. In this
specific case, there is up to 20 MVar of difference between reactive power references
after a disturbance. Regarding noise on the HUB voltage measurement, it will generate
overshoot from the MPC as seen in Fig. 4.11. In that specific case, the voltage received
by the secondary voltage controller was 3.5% higher than the true value, therefore the re-
active power references were determined to counteract a higher voltage deviation that it
really was. Thus, this makes the voltage evolve to a smaller value than in the case without
noise. However, this is quickly corrected at the next call to the MPC. It has to be noticed
here that the noise is particular important in Fig. 4.11 for illustration purposes and that
normally, the impact of noise on the MPC operation would even be smaller, as the impact
is directly linked to the level of noise. The robustness of the controller to measurement
noise is then proven.

with respect to the reactive power of the HVDC converters. A computationally efficient
method based on the Jacobian matrix and load-flow resolution has been chosen and it
was shown that the sensitivity coefficients were quite constant over the wind power output

with reactive power support, and for frequency regulation with active power transmission.

farm is similar to a sudden major decrease of wind power output and it is not of interest to
study it on its own. In the MPC algorithm developed, the possibility of loosing a HVDC link
is added. If the active and reactive power measured by the centralized controller are both
equal to 0 or are unknown because of the HVDC loss, the MPC considers the HVDC link
as lost. This would clearly impact the resolution of the optimisation problem as if a HVDC
link is missing, the sensitivity coefficients would change (and the sensitivity coefficient of
that HVDC link must be set to 0). If a HVDC link is disconnected, it can not be reconnected

The impact of the MPC on the overall losses has also been investigated. As expected,
the MPC enables to reduce the losses when it corrects an undervoltage situation. The
losses being linked to the magnitude of the current, an undervoltage situation generates
higher losses as a higher current is needed to transfer the same amount of power. How-
ever, when the MPC corrects an overvoltage situation, there is no major differences on the
losses but the components of the power grid (transformers, cables etc.) are facing higher
stresses which generate wear and tear. To illustrate the impact of the MPC on losses,
the losses are calculated after a sudden increase of wind power from 160 MW to 640
MW. With the secondary voltage controller enabled, 3125 MW are transmitted to onshore
grids whereas it is only 3109 MW without the secondary voltage controller. This is due to
the undervoltage situation resulting from the sudden increase of wind power output and
which is not corrected if the secondary controller is not enabled. This difference is even
higher on the three-island configuration, introduced in next subsection, as the loading of
the inter-island cables, which transmits active power, generates more losses.
Last, the MPC algorithm developed seems robust to communication failures or delays.
If there is a communication failure, first of all, it is highly unlikely that it happens to the 5
HVDC converters at once. Therefore, the most likely situation in case of communication
failure would be to have only 4 HVDC converters working perfectly well, which would be
close to the situation of Fig. 4.14 which is satisfying. If the failure is at the centralized
controller, only the local QV droop would operate for the HVDC converters but as ex-
plained before, the local part of the controller would be able to stop the voltage deviation,
especially if KV = 0.12. This also highlights the advantages of combining both local and
centralized controllers as in [31].
Regarding computational time, the MPC algorithm needs around 0.5 to 1s to run, if the
sensitivity coefficient are determined online. This is achieved without trying to optimise
the computational time of the MPC. Moreover, a first-order delay is introduced in the con-
trol blocks after Qref (see Fig. 4.5). This first-order delay, with a time constant of 0.7s,
could account for the communication delays and computational time of the MPC algo-
rithm. Therefore, the effect of communication delays and computational time are actually
taken into account in the controller and, as the secondary voltage controller presents good
performances, it can be claimed that the MPC is robust to communication delays and that
the computational time would not be a problem in real time operation.

Three-island configuration
As a logical next step, the MPC developed has been applied to the three-island con-
figuration of the NSWPH. The three-island configuration can be observed in Fig. 4.16. It
basically consists of 3 islands similar to the one studied so far, which are connected to
each other by AC cables of 80 km [14]. Each island has 2 HVDC links to onshore grids,
3.3 GW of wind farm capacity and a 300 MVA synchronous condenser.
To apply the MPC to the three-island configuration, the Matpower model of the three-
island configuration has been created and the MPC algorithm adapted. Only reactive
power has been considered, which is consistent with the results of the one-island config-
uration. The Matpower model has been validated by checking by hand load-flow results
under different wind power production. Similarly, the sensitivity coefficients have been
extracted from the Jacobian matrix of the load-flow solution and checked by manually run-
ning load-flow on PowerFactory changing slightly the reactive power absorption from the
HVDC converters. The sensitivity coefficients have also been checked in non-symmetrical

situations when the 3 islands are not producing the same amount of active power. For the

The sensitivity coefficients obtained are negative, which is really surprising. This mean
that, with load-flow simulations, when the HVDC converters absorb more reactive power,
the HUB voltages tend to increase. This has been verified. This is certainly due to the
presence of the inter-island cables whose reactive power injection might change as well.
This has to be kept in mind as many control strategies presented in section 1.2 are based
on voltage evolution formula which assumes that the evolution of the voltage with respect
to reactive power follow a positive trend (the more reactive power injected, the higher the
voltage and vice versa). This also underlines another interesting aspect of the MPC: it
can be adapted to any type of power system as there is no preliminary assumptions on
the power grid. However, due to the presence of the local QV droop, a positive relation
between voltage and reactive power is still observed: to counteract an undervoltage situa-
tion, the reactive power absorbed by a HVDC converter will diminish and vice-versa. The
local QV droop is not taken into account when the sensitivity coefficients are calculated
with Matpower.

gency giving large voltage deviation with KV = 0.12 is the disconnection of an inter-island
cable. Indeed, those cables are 80 km long and therefore produce large amount of reac-

5.1
Introduction to RTDS
Power system projects, such as HVDC links, new DGU controls, AC lines, new pro-
tective schemes etc., need to be tested before being installed in the power grid. However,
it is extremely costly and unpractical to try new controls or new piece of hardware in the
real power grid. First, the quality of supply being crucial, it is not possible to interrupt the
delivery of electricity for a time frame in which the testing would be carried out. Moreover,
for projects requiring high investments such as HVDC links, AC lines, it is economically
not viable to create full-scale prototypes and test them. The developed control or piece of
hardware and its operation must be entirely validated before proceeding to the construc-
tion of the product. However, new controls or new installations must still be tested before
being implemented on the power grid.
That is why RTDS has developed real-time simulation of power systems. Real-time
simulation means that the simulation time of an event is equal to the real time it takes for
an event to occur. This is hugely powerful as many power grid software simulate events
slower than real time. This real-time capacity of RTDS enables to test control algorithms
or new pieces of hardware in the closest possible conditions to real power grid. Therefore,
the impact of communication delays or failures, computational time as well as the real per-
formances of the equipment under test can be thoroughly studied. Real-time simulation is
usually the last step before field testing, if field testing is doable, or before validation and
implementation in the power grid, if field testing is not doable [59]. The main advantage
of real-time operation is that it allows to connect physical devices under test in a closed
loop with the simulated environment. This is called hardware-in-the-loop (HIL) where the
entirety of a power grid is simulated except one component (a controller, a generator, a
solar panel etc.).
RTDS uses EMT simulations with a time step of 2 to 50 µs and is able to study phe-
nomena in the frequency range of 0 to 15 kHz. To be able to simulate power systems
in real time, RTDS is run on powerful hardware, a parallel processing platform based on
multicore processors. The resolution of the power system equation can be processed
on different processors by separating the power system model in different subsystems.
This is possible when two parts of a power system are separated by a transmission line
whose travelling wave time is equal or greater than the calculation time step of RTDS.
Therefore, the two parts will be mathematically isolated from each other as an event on
one subsystem at one time step will not affect the other subsystem before the following
time step. These specific portions of the network are said to be decoupled and may be
solved independently and hence in parallel on different processors [60].
To be able to solve power system equations in real-time, RTDS calculation process re-
lies on the Dommel algorithm. The Dommel algorithm uses trapezoidal rule of integration
to convert differential equations to algebraic equations. All power system components
are represented as equivalent current source and resistors [60]. An example is shown in
Fig. 5.1.
RTDS solves at every time step the equation:

done using LU decomposition. [I] is known from the previous time step, as the equivalent
current source and resistor model of Fig. 5.1 is used. The sparse nature of the conduc-

conductance matrix can be calculated once for the entire simulation period, for instance
for a fixed source. For switching elements which only have two states, such as a circuit

non-linear devices, the conductance matrix must be calculated at every time step.
Typical time steps for EMT simulation are in the order of 50 µs, which is not small
enough to model precisely high frequency phenomena. High-frequency switching cir-
cuits, typically power electronic converters, are therefore modelled within the small time
step environment, which uses several ”shortcuts” to reduce the time step down to 1.4 to
2.5 µs [60]. The challenge for high frequency switching devices is the resolution of the
inverse of the conductance matrix. Indeed, anytime the switching state of one compo-
nent of the devices changes, the conductance matrix changes as well. If there are 10
switching devices, there are 210 different conductance matrices to calculate. However,
currently, the inversion of a 66 node conductance matrix takes around 50 µs [61]. It can
be seen that achieving a time step of 1.4 to 2.5 µs is impossible if the conductance matrix
must be re-calculated every time a switching component changes state. Storing a high

number of conductance matrices is also not computationally efficient.
An alternative method to model switching devices is needed. First of all, a short-circuit
(the ON state of a switching device), usually modelled as a large conductance, and the
open circuit (the OFF state) usually modelled as a small conductance, will respectively be
modelled as an inductance and as a series connected resistor and capacitor, as shown
in Fig. 5.2. Following Dommel algorithm principle, the inductance and series connected
resistor and capacitor are modelled as a conductance in parallel with a current source. It
is now possible to integrate switching devices in the RTDS calculation process aforemen-
tioned. Then RTDS proposes the following ”shortcuts”: the parameters L, R and C of the
ON and OFF state of the switching device can be chosen so that gsc and goc are equal,
and it is the value of the current source which is modified from one state to another. If
both conductances are equal, then the conductance matrix of the devices simulated will
not chance when the state of a switch changes and there is no need to re-invert G at every
time step, but only to recalculate IHsc and IHoc. This approach permits huge computa-
tional savings and is the main reason why a small time step of 1.5 to 2.5 µs is possible,
even with switching devices.
Figure 5.2: Representation of a short-circuit (A) and open-circuit (B) in RTDS [61].
However, the different ”shortcuts” and assumptions taken by RTDS to be able to offer
real-time simulations present a few disadvantages. First of all, open and short circuits are
being represented using capacitors and inductances, which store a small amount of en-
ergy being discarded at every change of state, representing a small artificial energy loss.
For that reason, most switching devices can not be operated above 3 kHz [61]. Other
small time step models (called no-loss models as those switching losses are avoided) can
be switched at increased rates of up to 12 kHz but these switches have been modeled
using the traditional method and are mathematically decoupled from the small time step
conductance matrix, introducing additional parasite inductances and capacitors. Further-
more, the use of different processors to solve power system equations might necessitate
to introduce artificial transmission lines so that the power system can be decoupled and
separated into different subsystems. Last, the interfacing between small time step envi-
ronment and large time step environment also requires the introduction of a transmission
line or a transformer to decouple the small time step to the large time step calculations.
More globally, to be able to achieve real-time operation, some parasite inductances and
capacitors might need to be introduced and their impacts on the results must be studied
and kept negligible.

Within the MultiDC project, there is a need to obtain a model of the NSWPH on RTDS

As a first step in the design of the NSWPH model, one full branch from the wind farm
to the onshore grid is studied. Indeed, the NSWPH presented in Fig. 2.5 has 5 similar
branches, with only the wind farm cable length varying.
RSCAD is the simulation software in which a RTDS model is designed. A draft model
must first be created with the different components of the NSWPH and its control. Then,
a RUNTIME model is designed to carry out real-time testing with interactive possibilities
(such as changing wind farm production, control setpoints etc.) and measurement and
graph interfaces [60]. In Fig. 5.3, the draft model of one full branch is shown. For clarity
purposes, a single-phase representation of the NSWPH is presented but the model has
actually been created in 3-phase. The wind farm has been modelled as an aggregated PQ
injection block which follows its active and reactive power setpoints. This is similar than in
PowerFactory. Regarding the wind farm transformer, the wind farm cables and the syn-
chronous condenser with its transformer and AVR, this is pretty similar than in the NSWPH
PowerFactory model. The synchronous condenser is modelled as a synchronous gener-
ator with an input torque equal to 0. The AVR chosen from a wide choice [60] has been
expanded to include a PI block to regulate the synchronous condenser voltage to 1 pu.
For the wind farm cables, one general cable model is created and only the length of the
cable will be changed from one branch to an other. The onshore grid, considered as an
infinite grid, has been modelled using an ideal AC source. All the components of Fig. 5.3,
described above and which are not in the blue box, are modelled within the large time
step environment. A time step of 50 µs is chosen.
The HVDC links and converters were modelled within the small time step environment
as smaller time step than 50 µs is required to precisely catch high frequency phenomena
that the switching devices generate. The small time step model is within the blue box,
called a VSC bridge box, and is presented in Fig. 5.4. Only the offshore part is pre-
sented but the onshore part is similar. A small time step of 2 µs is chosen. To interface
the large time step (the onshore and offshore grid) and the small time step environment
(the HVDC link and converters), an interface transformer is needed [60]. The topology
of the NSWPH enables to choose the interface transformers as transformers originally
present in the PowerFactory model which is highly recommended [61]. Those interface
transformers mathematically separate the small time step environment and the large time
step environment by introducing an inductance (the one of the transformer) and a parasite
capacitor, calculated so that the travel time of the transformer,

than one large time step. Thanks to the inductance and parasite capacitor, an event oc-

For illustration purposes, the simulations are carried out on the one branch model

6.1
Limitations and future works
The secondary voltage controller developed in this master thesis was applied on the
low-inertia topology of the NSWPH. However, as the NSWPH was unstable without the
presence of the QV droop (i.e. the voltage part of the controller of Fig. 2.6), it was harder
to explicitly demonstrate the effectiveness of the MPC on which the secondary controller
relies. The proof of concept using Matpower enabled to do that but it was only with load-
flow simulations. For that reason, that would be interesting to apply the MPC algorithm
developed to another power grid model, where stability is insured by a PCC to the main
grid or other synchronous generators. That would enable to study the operation of the
MPC without a local QV droop, which means that the reactive power of the DGU under
test would closely follow the reactive power reference. The impact of the time between
two calls to the MPC could be analysed for instance. Globally, applying the developed
controller to another power grid, stable without local controllers, would enable to study
dynamically the MPC on its own.
As previously explained, the MPC relies on sensitivity coefficients linking the voltage
evolution to a change of reactive power. Those coefficients have been determined us-
ing the transposed inverse of the Jacobian matrix obtained with load-flow calculation.
However, other methods are available [20,21,48,49], such as the resolution of an over-
determined system of equations to obtain the coefficients or the use of the impedance
matrix, and that could be of interest to implement those other methods in the MPC. That
could be done in future works, even if the results given in Fig. 4.12 show that the MPC
is robust to model inaccuracies, which proves that the exact values of the sensitivity co-
efficients might not be needed for successful operation of the MPC. Still, the methods
could be compared in term of computational speed. Similarly, other methods to couple
Matlab and PowerFactory exist [55–57] and that would be interesting to implement them
and compare them with the one applied in this master thesis in terms of computational
speed.
Moreover, the developed secondary voltage controller has been studied in dynamic
simulations only in PowerFactory, where no switching is modelled. The high-frequency
phenomena generated in reality by switching devices could diminish the performances
of the controller and therefore that must be quantified. The additional losses due the
switching could also make the situation not symmetrical between the 5 HVDC links and
high-frequency phenomena could worsen the quality of the measurements. To study that,
a logical future work would be to apply the secondary voltage controller in the RTDS model
of the NSWPH through RTDS/Matlab coupling or, even better, through HIL testing of the
HVDC offshore controller, where the MPC developed would be implemented.
The NSWPH model developed in RTDS also needs further work and analysis. First
of all, the NSWPH model must be expanded to integrate 5 full branches and to do so, a
multi-rack simulation model is needed. Then, the complete design of the PI parameters
and the different RLC values (DC link capacitance, high-frequency filter, arm inductance)
might need to be readjusted to obtain optimal performances, even if the actual values
permit to obtain a satisfying model for the one and two-branch cases. The switching fre-
quency of the VSC has arbitrarily been set to 1 kHz (mainly to respect the inner current
control loop time constant), but this value might not be optimal. However, a change of
that value would certainly call for a change of many PI parameters. Globally, a complete

55
==PAGE:67==
analysis of the design of the HVDC converter control structure might need to be carried
out, either to further validate the actual values and settings or to change them to obtain
optimal and more satisfying performances.
However, the main limitation regarding the work carried out on RTDS is that it is com-
plicated to compare the PowerFactory original model and the RTDS model. Indeed, the
RTDS model contains many additional components (high-frequency filter, arm inductance,
DC link capacitance) as well as many parasite inductances and capacitors needed for
real-time simulation. Plus, switching is modelled in RTDS whereas it is not in PowerFac-
tory. For those reasons, comparing both simulation results is difficult. This makes it more
complex to validate the developed NSWPH model on RTDS, as there is not really a base
case to compare it with. Moreover, the influence of the parasite inductances and capaci-
tors introduced by RTDS for modelling purposes must be more thoroughly analysed. The
use of the rtds_vsc_PH3LEV2 component instead of the rtds_vsc_LEV2 component to
model the VSC also needs to be considered and that must done in future work to choose
which option is the most satisfying to model the NSWPH.
Last, the specificity of the low-inertia grid being to rely on a synchronous condenser
to stabilise the frequency by providing inertia support, that would be of interest to include
the synchronous condenser controller for HIL testing, or even the synchronous condenser
itself as HIL device under test. That would be more relevant for frequency regulation pur-
poses but it is believed that it is needed to further illustrate the difference between the
low-inertia and zero-inertia topology of the NSWPH, one of the main objective of the mul-
tiDC project [36]. The RTDS model of the three-island configuration and the zero-inertia
topology of the one-island configuration can also be created in RTDS, which is expected
to be relatively straightforward as many components and controls can be copied from the
RTDS model developed in this master thesis.
6.2
Conclusion
This master thesis summarises the work carried out at the Technical University of
Denmark (DTU) during 5 months, at the Center of Electric Power and Energy, under the
supervision of Spyros Chatzivasileiadis, an associate professor of DTU, its PhD student
Georgios Misyris and a research assistant, Matas Dijokas. The conduct of this master
thesis has been greatly impacted by the covid-19 pandemic but thanks to the support
from DTU, it has been possible to carry out the master thesis in the best possible condi-
tions.
During this master thesis, a secondary voltage controller for the NSWPH has been
developed to complement a local controller based on QV droop, in order to correct possi-
ble steady-state voltage deviations. Firstly, this secondary controller, based on MPC, has
been validated using load-flow calculations with the use of Matpower as simulation soft-
ware. Then, the operation of the secondary voltage controller has been thoroughly studied
through dynamic simulations carried out in PowerFactory, with Matlab/PowerFactory cou-
pling to implement the proposed controller. The dynamic simulations enabled to highlight
the importance of the local controller for the stability of the power grid, which confirms the
necessity of validating a controller with dynamic simulations. Indeed, using only load-flow
calculations does not permit to see any unstable behavior and it is therefore crucial to
carry out dynamic simulations. Moreover, regarding the NSWPH, it has been pointed out
in this master thesis that stability issues might easily arise as it is a low inertia grid, with
power electronic interfaced generation. This has to be kept in mind as many voltage and
frequency control algorithms proposed in the literature do not focus on stability as it is of-
ten assumed that the stability is assured by a PCC with an infinite grid or by other DGUs
in the power grid.

Another achievement of this master thesis was to create an operational model of one
full branch of the NSWPH on RTDS, a real-time power grid simulation software. This
one-branch model has been validated and then, the model has been expanded to 2 full
branches of the NSWPH. To obtain the complete NSWPH model, a multi-rack model con-
taining 5 full branches needs to be created but it is believed that the most difficult part has
been done by creating a working model of one full branch of the NSWPH. The NSWPH
model created in this master thesis is believed to come useful later to test control algorithm
in real-time simulations. Its validity has been proven using simple example and compari-
son with load-flow results from PowerFactory.
Globally, during this master thesis, the one-island and three-island NSWPH model on
Matpower have been created, the RTDS model of one and two branches of the one-island
NSWPH has been implemented even if further work is needed and a secondary voltage
controller for the NSWPH has been proposed and thoroughly analysed.
The proposed secondary voltage controller shows good performances in terms of volt-
age regulation, computational speed and robustness to noise measurements, model in-
accuracies and communication delays and failures. Moreover, it is a good complement
to the local controller, and the overall controller exhibits the advantages of both the local
and the secondary controller. The developed controller is a centralized controller needing
communication with the HVDC converters, but the infrastructure of the NSWPH does not
incur high additional costs for such a controller as the 5 HVDC converters are located on
the same artificial island for the one-island configuration.
Future works will include the test of the proposed controller with real-time simulations
on the NSWPH model developed in RTDS, with a possibility to carry out HIL testing for
the HVDC converter controller or the synchronous condenser controller.
