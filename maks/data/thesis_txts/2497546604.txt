Abstract
The understanding of shear strength of plain concrete is an important subject which
could prove valuable in the design of concrete structures.
A more accurate model to
describe the eﬀective strength of cracked concrete could reduce the requirement for shear
reinforcement. A better understanding could lead to safer and more economically viable
design solutions. The object of this project is to contribute to the knowledge of the shear
behaviour of cracked concrete.
In the existing literature on the topic there is a common understanding that the normal
stress applied have a direct impact on the ultimate shear strength of concrete. However,
the initial crack width and the diﬀerence between gravel and granite aggregates are param-
eters, which are not thoroughly examined in the present literature. Furthermore, it was
found in the literature review that no experimental setup existed, which eﬀectively could
keep a constant normal stress throughout the experiment. Applying a normal stress below
1 MPa during the push oﬀtesting, was also not found in the experiments investigated.
To investigate these properties, 10 push oﬀelements without shear reinforcement in the
crack plane were tested in a biaxial setup that was able to keep a constant normal stress
throughout the experiments. In the 10 push oﬀelements, two factors were varied; ag-
gregate type and normal stress applied. In addition to the push oﬀelements, concrete
cylinders were cast to examine the material properties and the softening behaviour of the
concrete.
The results obtained from the experimental testing of push oﬀelements showed no sig-
niﬁcant diﬀerence in shear strength of granite and gravel when normalised with the com-
pressive strength. The experimental setup design proved to be able to control the initial
crack width to a certain degree, as the average desired crack width was reached, but
measurements on the actual test setup showed some deviance in regards to an imbalance
in the system. The normal stress during shearing was controlled successfully throughout
all experiments.
The common expression for the eﬀectiveness factor ν with regards to shear in concrete
only takes in to account the compressive strength. In order to estimate the ultimate shear
strength of cracked concrete, a model was proposed that takes into account the softening
behaviour expressed by α, sizing eﬀect and crack width. The model was able to estimate
the ν-factor however, further research is needed to verify the proposed model.

Introduction
1.1
Research signiﬁcance
The shear strength of plain concrete is a topic that has not yet been fully understood.
In the present literature the eﬀective strength of cracked concrete in shear is typically
evaluated by an empirical formula, which only depends on the compressive strength. A
more extensive model based on a physical understanding could lead to safer and more
economical design solutions.
The previous research focusing on examining the shear strength of plain concrete in push
oﬀelements is fairly limited and mostly consists of studies by Hamadi [1], Regan [2],
Walraven [3], Mattock [4] and Akira [5]. Some general trends were noted when reviewing
these studies. Most of the experiments did not have a proper way of keeping a constant
normal stress σn during the shear part of the experiment. Furthermore, it was noted
that no push oﬀexperiments had been done with normal stress in the range 0-1 MPa. It
was additionally found that when examining the shear strength through push oﬀtesting,
the majority of the experiments varied on multiple parameters, which made it diﬃcult to
distinguish each parameter’s inﬂuence on the ultimate shear strength.
The diﬀerence in ultimate shear strength using diﬀerent aggregates have been somewhat
covered in the existing literature. The most common comparison of aggregates have been
between high- and low-density aggregates. No comparison have been found between gravel
and granite aggregates.
The plastic theory of concrete assumes concrete to be a rigid plastic material. However,
the solutions derived from this assumption does not align with the observed behaviour
of the material. In reality, cracked concrete in shear shows lower strength which is why
it is necessary to apply a reduction factor when evaluating the strength. This reduction
factor was introduced by Nielsen [6] and later named the eﬀectiveness factor in Nielsen
and Bræstrup [7]. It is usually given by an empirical formula that solely depends on the
compressive strength. However, it is found in literature that other parameters inﬂuence
the strength reduction of cracked concrete with regards to shear, e.g. initial crack width
[8]. It is the objective of this thesis to propose a more extensive model that can be used
to estimate the eﬀectiveness factor.

1.2. PROBLEM STATEMENT
1.2
Problem statement
The experimental part of this project will consist of 10 push oﬀexperiments and 30
cylinder compression experiments. The overall objectives of this thesis is to examine the
following:
• The diﬀerence in shear strength of concrete with granite aggregates and concrete
with gravel aggregates.
• The yield surface in a τ-σ plot especially for low values of normal stress below 1
MPa.
• The softening behaviour of concrete with regards to compression.
• The eﬀective shear strength of concrete with granite and gravel aggregates and
proposal of model for evaluating the eﬀectiveness factor ν.
• Development and subsequent evaluation of a new biaxial test setup that can be used
for push oﬀexperiments.

2.1.2
Experimental setup and testing procedure
In general, a push oﬀtest consists of two parts. One part is the cracking along the shear
plane to obtain an initial crack width, w0, while the second part is the actual shearing
of the specimen. Several diﬀerent setups have been used throughout the literature to be
able to control these parts of the experiments.
The most common way is to pre-crack the specimen along the shear plane by applying a
line load along the notched section [1, 9, 3], hereby controlling the crack width either by
internal reinforcement across the shear plane or external restraint. The external restrain-
ing bars were often attached to steel plates connected directly to the specimen by using
plugs inserted in the fresh concrete [9, 3]. To control the crack width in these experi-
ments the tension of the external restraint was adjusted to get the desired crack width.
A schematic representation of this is seen in Figure 2.2a.

2.1. PAST RESEARCH
Subsequent to the pre-cracking part of the experiment is the actual shear part of the
experiment. Here the element can either be placed vertically, where the shear force is
applied in the vertical direction [9] or horizontally [1]. The normal force is applied by
the external restraint, as shown in Figure 2.2b or by internal bonded reinforcement. This
method of testing the shear strength of push oﬀelements requires a bit of handling in
between the pre-cracking of the element and the actual shearing. To prevent this, a biaxial
setup may be used.
A thorough search in the relevant literature yielded no such test setup which have been
used for full size testing of push oﬀelements as seen in Figure 2.1. The biaxial setup
for testing the shear capacity of concrete on smaller specimens have been used by e.g.
Petersen [10] and Jacobsen [11]. The setup is shown in Figure 2.3:
Figure 2.3: Technical drawing of biaxial testing machine where (A) is the actuator, (P) is the
piston, (LC) is the load cell, (S) is the specimen, (C) is one of the four column, (B) is the linear
motion block, (R) is the rail and (W) is the web. [10]
By using a biaxial setup the handling in between the procedures may be reduced, as the
whole test may be conducted in one machine. For a biaxial test to be successful it is
important that the machine has a suﬃcient stiﬀness to be able to control the crack width.

2.2.1
Plastic theory for modiﬁed Mohr Coulomb materials
This section examines a theoretical model that may be used to describe shear in concrete.
The section is largely based on the work of Jensen [12] and Nielsen [13].
Concrete may be considered a rigid plastic material with Coulomb’s modiﬁed failure
hypothesis as its yield criterion. [12]. The yield criterion of a material is the combination
of stresses that results in yielding. This criterion may be expressed in terms of principle
stresses as:
f(σ1, σ2, σ3) = 0
(2.1)
In a 3-dimensional coordinate system with principle stresses as the axes (2.1) produces
the yield surface. If the normality condition is assumed, the strains that occurs at a
given stress ﬁeld may be determined to be normal to the yield surface.
However, at
any discontinuity along the yield surface the direction of the strain may take any value
between the direction on either side of the discontinuity.
In order to determine the carrying capacity of rigid plastic bodies, the extremum principles
are an important tool. The extremum principles consist of the upper bound theorem, the
lower bound theorem and the uniqueness theorem.
Upper Bound Theorem: The load found by solving the equations of work for an
arbitrary, geometrical admissible failure ﬁgure is equal to or greater than the yield load. A
kinematic admissible (commonly known as geometrical admissible) failure ﬁgure satisﬁes
the compatibility conditions as well as the geometrical boundary conditions.
Lower Bound Theorem: The load found from a safe and statically permissible stress
ﬁeld is smaller or equal to the yield load. A safe stress ﬁeld is inside the yield surface of
the material. A statically permissible stress ﬁeld satisﬁes the equilibrium conditions and
statical boundary conditions.
Uniqueness Theorem: If a corresponding load is found from both the upper bound
method and the lower bound method, this is the yield load of the body.

Eﬀective strength parameters
In past research it is generally understood that there are three factors, which have a great
deal of inﬂuence on the crack behaviour and strength of shear transfer across cracks. The
three factors are crack width, normal force across the crack plane and aggregate type [1].
In this section the three factors will be discussed and their relevance to the shear strength
will be examined.
The crack width is generally considered to be the most important variable inﬂuencing the
shear stress - shear displacement relation [3]. When focusing on the initial crack width,
w0, it is evident from Figure 2.6 that this has an inﬂuence on the ultimate shear strength.
This means that for a smaller crack width at the same normal stress one would expect
a higher ultimate shear stress. In Figure 2.10a the kinematics of a shear crack is shown,

During the ﬁrst 24 hours after casting, the push oﬀelement, cylinders and prism were
stored in the concrete lab. The push oﬀelement and the cylinders were cured horizontally.
A thin sheet of plastic was put on top of any free surface as shown in Figure 3.8a. The
temperature in the lab was kept at 20◦C ± 1◦C. After approximately 24 hours the push
oﬀelement and the concrete cylinders were demoulded. The cylinders and the push oﬀ
element were wetted and covered with paper towels and additional water was applied to
ensure that the samples were wet. The samples were put on a pallet and covered with
plastic as shown in Figure 3.8b. The pallet was stored in a climate room at the DTU
Structural Laboratory at 22-25◦C, with a relative humidity of 65-75%, documented in
Appendix C.

Test setup and procedure
In this chapter the experimental setup and procedure will be described. This includes
a description of the structural setup, measuring equipment, data acquisition and test
procedure.
4.1
Push oﬀexperiments
In the push oﬀexperiments the elements are ﬁrstly subjected to tension to induce a
controlled crack width then secondly compressed to a predeﬁned normal stress and lastly
sheared to failure. The machine used for this purpose was built at the DTU Structural
Lab previous to this project. It consists of a steel frame and two hydraulic load actuators.
Adjustments were made during this project to compensate for insuﬃcient stiﬀness of the
original setup.
4.1.1
Test setup
The original design of the biaxial test machine is shown in Figure 4.1.
The machine
consists of a vertical and a horizontal hydraulic actuator capable of inducing 1 MN and
1.2 MN respectively. The actuators are mounted on a steel frame made from structural
steel columns, UNP-proﬁles and multiple steel clamps. The steel frame is connected to
the high strength concrete ﬂoor. The vertical load actuator is pinned supported to the
two UNP proﬁles above and the element below it.
Figure 4.1: Push oﬀelement in experimental setup [15].
25
==PAGE:39==
4.1. PUSH OFF EXPERIMENTS
The concrete specimen is simply supported in both horizontal and vertical direction by
roller supports. The supports can be seen in Figure 4.2. When the element is mounted in
the machine, caution is taken to ensure that center lines of actuator pistons and supports
line up. This is done in order to avoid any moment in the element.
Figure 4.2: Roller supports and load actuator contact points.
After initial testing it was determined that the stiﬀness of the setup was not suﬃcient
during the crack opening. As the crack initiated in the specimen a sudden uncontrolled
displacement of the vertical actuator was observed which resulted in a crack width above
1 mm. A system of four Ø32 steel rods enveloping the specimen was designed. The rods
are mounted to the steel plate above the specimen by a transverse beam. The bottom of
the rods are ﬁxed by friction clamps that are welded to the underlying HEB proﬁle. The
steel rod system can be seen in Figure 4.4a and 4.3. The friction clamp can be seen in
Figure 4.4b. Additional pictures of the setup can be seen in Appendix N and technical
drawings in Appendix O.

The friction clamps consists of a two steel parts that ﬁts around the rods which can be

slippage of the rod at a certain force. Due to structural limitations of the machine, the
rods cannot be fully active until the desired crack width is reached, as the forces would

the results from the push oﬀtest and cylinder compression will be analysed. Secondly,

6.4. EVALUATION OF BIAXIAL TEST SETUP
6.4
Evaluation of biaxial test setup
This section will analyse the performance of the biaxial test setup with regards to the
crack opening. As no particular problems were experienced with the shearing of the push
oﬀelement, this will not be further examined.
6.4.1
Crack width
Figure 5.9a indicates that the maximum normal deformation was not 0.15 mm over the
entire crack plane but rather between 0 and 0.337 mm for G5.
Similar results may
be seen for each specimen in Appendix B. This indicates that the crack width measured
from the extensometers is not an accurate measure. The initial crack width measurements
presented in Table 5.1 should be considered an average crack width across the crack plane.
6.4.2
Rotation
From Figure 5.8 it is noted that in both cases the LVDT FV measures a compression until
a certain point after which it measures tension. A similar trend is seen for a majority
of the specimens. This compression is not expected since only tension is applied in this
part of the experiment. It could indicate that the element is being pulled unevenly and is
rotating about the z axis (where the z axis is deﬁned as perpendicular to the front side of
the specimen). This rotation could be explained by the vertical actuator piston not being
exactly vertical and centered above the center line of the element.
From ﬁgure 5.9 it is noted that the maximum deformation on the right side of the notch
are larger than the left side in both cases which also indicates rotation about the z axis.
A similar trend is seen for a majority of the specimens.
6.4.3
Slippage in friction clamps
From Figure 5.10a it can be seen that the force in the rods decreases after the ultimate
tensile stress has been reached in the concrete. This indicates that slippage starts in
the friction clamps at this point. This is the desired behaviour of the friction clamps.
However, at a normal deformation of 0.12 mm there is a sudden drop in force which could
be explained by a sudden slip in the friction clamps. As seen from the high rate data
acquisition from the Wavemaker software in Figure 5.10b this results in an irregular and
undesirable force deformation curve.

Conclusion

Diﬀerence in aggregates

7.2. FUTURE RESEARCH
Biaxial setup
• It was possible to perform the push oﬀexperiments with the biaxial test setup how-
ever, some possible improvements with regards to the crack opening were identiﬁed.
Improvements include more accurate measure of the crack width, elimination of
rotation and modiﬁed friction clamps as further described in Section 7.2.
• With regards to the shearing part of the push oﬀexperiments the test setup was a
success. The normal stress was controlled throughout the shearing.
7.2
Future Research
Due to the time constraint on this project several questions and challenges remains unan-
swered. Answering these questions and challenges could prove valuable to gain a deeper
understanding of the eﬀectiveness factor. Some of the most important areas to further
examine are presented below.
7.2.1
Tension tests
Determining the tensile softening behaviour for both aggregates, especially the crack
width where no stresses are transferred, would have further improved the model proposed
in Section 6.3, as this model used an estimated value as shown in Appendix R. Tensile
tests were performed, but diﬃculties with the applied glue and the testing procedure,
made it diﬃcult to achieve the aforementioned properties within the limited time scope.
It is proposed to use a two component glue. One that have proved successful in similar
tests performed at ETH in Zürich is the two component glue Sikadur -41 CF adhesive.
7.2.2
Verifying and optimizing proposed ν formula
More push oﬀtests would be required in order to more accurately determine the ex-
perimental reduction factors νE. Five points were used in the τ-σ plot however, it is
recommended that more measurements are made to evaluate how well the results follow
the yield surface derived from the plastic upper bound solutions.
In order to verify and optimise the proposed formula for ν more experiments would be
necessary with diﬀerent values for the parameters.
7.2.3
Biaxial test setup
As discussed in Section 6.4 there were three main issues with the biaxial setup.
First of all the crack width measured as an average of the two extensometers was possibly
not an accurate measure as indicated by the DIC measurements. In order to get more
accurate knowledge of the crack width it is the recommendation that four extensometers is
56
==PAGE:70==
7.2. FUTURE RESEARCH
used and that the individual measurements from each of the extensometers are recorded.
The four extensometers should be placed in each corner of the notch.
Secondly measurements from both the LVDTs and the DIC indicated that vertical actu-
ator piston was not exactly vertical and centered above the specimen. It is recommended
that the lower most part of the piston is accurately placed and then ﬁxed to the steel
structure. In this project the piston was placed by eye measure and only ﬁxed by its own
inertia. The piston could be ﬁxed by bolting it to transverse UNP-proﬁles after it has
been placed accurately.
Thirdly the force-deformation curves during crack opening showed that slippage in the
friction clamps resulted in an abnormal stress-deformation curve in the concrete. It is
recommended that another solution is found that does not allow rapid slippage.

Procedure for measuring moisture content in aggregates 
 
Use 
• 3 glass containers 
• Microwave 
• Glass bottle for water 
• Protective heat gloves 
• Safety googles 
• Weight 
 
1. Clean the three glass containers 
2. Weigh each of the glass containers and note in the 
receipt 
3. The three dried aggregates a placed in each of the glass 
containers  
4. The weight is noted 
5. Each of the glass containers with the aggregates is 
dried for 10 min (when taking the glass container out, 
remember to wear safety googles and protective heat 
gloves and remember to place a glass bottle with 150-
200 mL of water in the microwave) 
6. The sample is weighed, and the weight is noted. 
7. The sample is dried for additional 3 min before the 
weight is noted again and is written in the receipt.

Procedure for measuring density 
Container: min 150mm diameter and min 5L 
Find volume of container by filling it with water (density 998 kg/m3). Place a glass plate on top to be 
precise. 
 
1. Clean container 
2. Weigh before concrete 
3. Fill the container and compact with a rod. Hit the side of the container until bubbles no longer 
appear, at each layer (make 2 layers) 
4. Weigh the total weight of the container. 
 
 
Procedure for measuring the air content 
 
1. Clean the air container. 
2. Compact the concrete while filling it up. 
3. Cut the top of the concrete off and wipe of the edge thoroughly.  
4. Close main valve and open A and B. 
5. Spray water into one of the valves until it comes out of the other valve. 
6. Hit the container gently until bubbles appear.  
7. Close the air bleeder valve and pump air in the system. 
8. Close A and B valve and open main valve 
9. Read the value. 
10. Open A and B value and clean the container.  
 
 
Procedure for measuring the slump (according to DS/EN 12350-2) 
 
1. Wipe of slump cone and bottom plate with a dry cloth. 
2. Hold down the cone while concrete is being filled in.  
3. Fill 1/3 of the cone and compact the layer with 25 strokes with the compacting rod. Make 
sure to compact the entire layer, by tilting the compacting rod. Do not hit the bottom plate. 
Only compact the layer recently filled. By the last 1/3, make sure to heap the concrete layer 
before compacting. 
4. Wipe of the bottom plate and cone.  
5. Lift the concrete cone straight up in approximately 2-5 seconds. 
6. Measure the slump immediately afterwards. 
 
If you get a shear slump two times in a row, the 
concrete is not strong enough.

Procedure for concrete casting 
 
1. Weigh all the aggregates, additives and water (remember to adjust for moisture 
content) 
2. The mixer is moistened with a wet broom. 
3. Sand + the small size of stone is loaded in the loading container and afterwards 
poured into the mixer.  
4. Mix for 1,5 min.  
5. Water is poured directly into the concrete mixer 
6. Mix for 1 min 
7. A trowel is used to loosen big lumps of cement. Afterwards the mixing continues 
for 1 min. Any additional lumps of cement are loosened, and mixing continues for 1 
min. 
8. The additives are added directly to the mix. 
9. Mix for 1 min. 
10. The largest size of stone is loaded in the loading container and afterwards poured 
into the mixer. 
11. Mix for 5,5 min. 
12. Perfom a slump measurement.  
13. Mix for 1 min. 
14. When filling in formwork, fill up 1/3, vibrate at 50 Hz and continue until formwork is 
full.

Appendix Q
PID control
One important aspect of experiments is tuning of the PID controller. PID control is a
control loop mechanism that uses feedback to control a process.
During the crack opening the Instron 8800 controller uses PID to control the load actuator.
The PID controller regulates two servo valves connecting the load actuator to the external
hydraulic system. The controller uses the measured process variable PV and the desired
set point SP to calculate the error function e(t) as the diﬀerence between the two. During
the crack opening the measured process variable was the extensometer measurements.
PID control stands for proportional, integral, derivative control. PID is an algorithm
that runs a loop many times per second. During each loop the algorithm uses the error
function to calculate the output signal to the servo valves. The output signal is calculated
as:
