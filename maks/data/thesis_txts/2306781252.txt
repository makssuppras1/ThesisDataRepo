Abstract
The eﬀect of diﬀerent strain rates on strength and ductility parameters of steel have been
investigated. Tensile tests on steel test pieces were made with two strain rates. The total
amount of dogbone test pieces used to ﬁnd results are 32. The experiment included two
kinds of steel strengths, s235 and s355, and the steel was cut in both rolling directions.
For the four steel types the results showed that the yield strength and the ultimate tensile
strength is higher for higher strain rates. Furthermore the ductility parameters found in
these tests show that the elongation of the steel is higher for lower strain rate tests.
Post yield room temperature creep eﬀects on the material parameters were studied
as well. In half of the tensile tests the displacement was held constant for one hour at a
speciﬁed displacement after yield. This resulted in stress relaxation, which was used to
calculate an equivalent creep strain using the relationship between relaxation and creep
in hereditary integrals. The test results for the tensile strength did not show an eﬀect
from the stress relaxation. The ductility parameters were however inﬂuenced. The tests
with a dwell step gave higher values for the ductility parameters than the tests with the
same strain rate without a dwell step.
The possible eﬀect of strain rate on stress relaxation and creep was investigated as
well. It was found that loading the test piece with a higher strain rate, lead to a higher
degree of stress relaxation compared to the stress before keeping the deformation con-
stant for one hour.
The creep curves calculated from the stress relaxation were analyzed. All creep curves
ﬁt well with the logarithmic creep law, given by a · ln(t) + c, where t is time in seconds.
The parameter c was found to be dependent on the initial strain, which was expected
based on literature studies. A possible inﬂuence of the strain rate on this parameter was
seen in the results. The creep parameter a was found to have a possible inﬂuence by the
initial stress at the start of the dwell step, and the strain rate of the test. However, the
results were inconclusive due to a large scatter in the a values for the fast strain rate
tests. Thus, predicting the parameters of the creep law equation based on initial stress,
strain and strain rate was not possible based on these tests.
A detailed description of the tensile tests, including the results from each test can be
found in the test report included as a part of the present thesis.

Introduction
When material properties for steel are determined they are usually tested by procedures
speciﬁed in diﬀerent standards. DS/EN ISO 6892-1 [6] describes the procedure for uni-
axial tensile testing of steel. This procedure gives recommended values for the strain
rates, and methods used to determine material parameters. During a tensile test it is
recommended that diﬀerent strain rates are used to ﬁnd diﬀerent material parameters.
These diﬀerent strain rates in each test are referred to as ranges throughout this report.
In this project two strain rates were tested in a series of tensile tests. Furthermore, tests
were made with strain rates ten times faster and ten times slower than the recommended
values.
The objective of the tests is to investigate the eﬀect of strain rate on the diﬀerent
material parameters found from uniaxial tensile testing. It is known that yield strengths
could be inﬂuenced by the strain rate, and the purpose of this project is to see how much
the diﬀerent parameters are aﬀected. The parameters found from the tests are upper
and lower yield strength, ultimate tensile strength, and elongation properties.
In addition to this, stress relaxation and creep in room temperature is investigated.
Based on experiments in literature it is found that many metals have a signiﬁcant amount
of post yield creep at room temperature [2]. In [Freed et.al] post yield creep testing is
done by loading test pieces up to yield, and then measuring strain after 10 seconds and
after 1 hour. The strain was measured to see what percentage of the total strain at
certain times can be attributed to creep. Average steel was found to have 72 percent of
total strain occurring due to creep after one hour at yield stress level [2].
In a creep test the stress or load working on the test piece is held constant, and the
strain or deformation of the test piece is measured. In this project half of the tensile tests
had a dwell period of one hour in which the deformation was kept constant, and the stress
relaxation data was collected. The dwell step started after yielding of the test piece. The
tensile tests were deformation controlled, to get the last part of the stress-strain curve,
and to have a force controlled dwell step in an otherwise deformation controlled step was
found to be diﬃcult. The post yield stress relaxation was checked to see if it aﬀects the
strength and ductility parameters, or if they are only aﬀected by strain rate. The stress
relaxation was used to calculate creep strain, using hereditary integrals, to investigate

2.1.4
Yield strength
Yield strength is the engineering strength at which the material begins to undergo perma-
nent plastic deformation. If any load above the yield strength is applied to the material,
the geometry will not return the the original if the load is removed. In the stress-strain
curve the yield strength is found in the point where the curve stops being linear. If the
material is unloaded from a stress level higher than yield, the deloading curve will follow
a straight line parallel to the original slope of the curve. To determine this point, the
0.2% criterion is used. According to this criterion the yield strength is found where the
stress-strain curve deviates by a straight line by 0.2% , or 0.002 strain. The point is
found by drawing a line parallel to the elastic region of the curve, starting from 0.002
on the x-axis, and the point where it intersects with the stress-strain curve is where the
yield strength is obtained [16] this is illustrated in 2.1.
2.1.5
Ultimate tensile strength
The ultimate tensile strength UTS is the highest stress the material can be exposed
to before the onset of necking occurs.
Necking is a local deformation, and the ﬁrst
step towards fracture. This can be seen on the stress -strain curve as a drop in load and
engineering stress towards the end of the curve, because of local deformation. The tensile
strength is the highest measured stress (engineering), or load on the curve, i.e the point
right before the load drops [16].
2.1.6
Ductility
The ductility of a material is an important factor to consider, because it describes a
materials ability to deform without breaking. A common way to measure ductility is
to ﬁnd the percent elongation at failure is the elongation of the specimen after fracture.
The percent reduction of area can be used as well [16].
2.2
Strain rates
The yield stress of steel is directly dependent on strain rate. Usually the higher the
strain rate is, the higher the yield stress becomes up to the point of ultimate load [10].
This means that the yield stress must be qualiﬁed by a strain rate in order to have a
signiﬁcant meaning [10]. In several standards, such as DS/EN ISO 6892 [6] this issue is
addressed by having speciﬁed strain rates, depending on the method used.
The mechanical properties will change for higher strain rates. The yield stress and
ultimate strength will increase and the strain corresponding to these stresses, and the
strains at the beginning of the hardening range is either increasing or constant for an
increasing strain rate. The elastic modulus (initial slope) will however not be aﬀected
by the strain rate [15]. In literature it is found that tests have shown that steels with
lower yield strength are more sensitive to variations in strain rate than steels with higher

Machine stiﬀness
The machine stiﬀness must be accounted for in tensile testing, often the machine stiﬀness
is assumed to be much higher than the stiﬀness of the specimen, but this is not always
the case. If crosshead displacement is deﬁned as the relative displacement under zero

and the driving mechanisms engaged, will equal the deformation in the gauge length of
the specimen plus elastic deﬂections in components such as machine frame, load cell,
grips and specimen ends. Before the yield point, the gauge deformation is only a small
fraction of the crosshead displacement [5]. In literature it was found that there are large
diﬀerences in machine stiﬀness, even for identical tests.

should be calculated for each test to account for the specimen-machine interaction in

2.4.1
Creep behavior
At high temperatures deformation occurs in steel because heat changes the mechanical
properties of steel. Increased temperature can also make the deformation time depen-
dent, this means that the deformation will increase with time for a constant load instead
of remaining static. This phenomenon is high temperature creep. In room temperature,
creep in steel has been considered negligible. This is because the creep deformation is
small, and unlikely to lead to failure [9]. However signiﬁcant amounts of post yield creep
strain has been found in steel in room temperature [2].

Creep is mainly dependent on three variables; time, temperature and stress. There is
not a general accepted theory that describes creep behaviour, mostly there are empirical
equations from the results of creep tests [12]. The results of creep tests are often presented
as a strain-time graph, showing the increase in strain over a period of time. Figure 2.3
shows a typical creep curve, where the curve shows the strain over time, and the slope
is the strain rate. The creep process can be divided into three stages of creep; primary,
secondary and tertiary. The primary stage is the initial part of the curve in Figure 2.3,
here the strain increases rapidly, and the strain rate is decreasing. This creep behaviour
is also called transient creep. In the secondary stage the strain rate is almost constant,
and the strain is increasing less rapidly. This stage is the longest stage, and after most of
the lifetime has passed the creep rate increases rapidly until the material fractures. This
last stage is referred to as the tertiary creep stage [14]. The primary and secondary creep
stages are often described using empirical laws, such as the Garofalo equation(2.12) [14].

2.4.2
Creep testing in standards
The main focus when studying creep in this report is the time dependency of creep, and
the relationship between creep and stress relaxation. However, in the initial phase of the
project uniaxial creep testing in tension was studied. The following is an outlining of
uniaxial creep testing in tension in DS/EN ISO 204:2009.
Information in this standard is based on oﬀ-axis loading or bending on the creep
properties of diﬀerent materials. The standard also explains the uninterrupted and in-
terrupted creep tests and presents the diﬀerent metallic materials. In the uniaxial creep
testing some of the following parameters are included:
• Geometry: Reference length, original and ﬁnal reference length, original and ﬁnal
gauge length
• Initial stress, elongation, percentage elongation
• Creep: Percentage creep elongation, percentage elongation after creep rupture, per-
centage reduction of area after creep rupture, creep elongation time, creep rupture
time
The basic principle behind the test in the international standard is to preheat the
test to a certain temperature and straining the test piece by constant tensile force or
stress. This is done along the longitudinal axis for a certain amount of time. The reason
this is done is to achieve the following parameters:
• speciﬁed creep elongation
• values of permanent elongation at diﬀerent intervals
• creep rupture time
The test should be completed by applying a force along the axis of the test piece
while keeping bending and torsion to a minimum. The force should be applied without
shock and the machine should be protected from vibration and movement. The standard
recommends using an extensometer to measure the elongation of the test piece. By using
an extensometer it is possible to maintain the same accuracy without stopping the test.
In every lab experiment, it is recommended that the extensometer is calibrated regularly.
The standard recommends every 5th experiment.
Heating of the test specimen to a speciﬁc temperature is completed in this kind of
experiment. However, instead of measure the temperature on the surface of the specimen

Creep is a deformation caused by stress. And the rate of this deformation is caused by

when temperature is increased rapidly during a creep test it is possible to observe a in-

General Creep Equations from Literature
In literature on creep, many equations to predict creep behaviour are proposed.
In
experimental investigations of creep in copper and steel at room temperature, creep has
generally been found to be logarithmic [2], [13]. Room temperature post-yield creep in
diﬀerent metals have been found to ﬁt the general equation (2.14) from [2]
ε = ε0 + α + log(1 + t), t > 0
(2.14)
Where ε0 is the initial strain from the applied load, and α is a function of stress.
This equation is used to describe the creep behaviour if the strain vs- log-time curve is
linear. Experiments have shown that some types of steel have a linear strain vs. log-time
curve, and that the creep is roughly logarithmic [2]. From [13] metals are found to ﬁt
well with the logarithmic creep law given by equation (2.15).
ε = a · ln(t) + ε1
(2.15)

The tensile testing was done by applying a tensile force to a dog bone shaped test piece,

In DS/EN ISO 6892-1 there are two methods of testing, method A and B. Method
A is based on strain rate control. The method intends to minimize the variations of the
test rates during the moments when strain rate sensitive parameters are determined and

controlled.
In this project the tests were strain rate controlled (Method A) so that the part of
the stress-strain curve were the stress decreases is obtained.
Two diﬀerent methods of strain rate control is described in the standard. The ﬁrst
controls the strain rate itself, ˙eLe, that is based from data from the extensometer. The
second is the control of the estimated strain rate over the parallel length, ˙eLc. This is
achieved by controlling the crosshead separation rate at a velocity equal to the desired
strain rate multiplied by the parallel length. These strain rates should be almost equal if
the material has homogeneous deformation behavior and the force is nominally constant.

4.4Analysis of Creep Curves
4.4
Analysis of Creep Curves
The stress relaxation data during the dwell period was used to ﬁnd an equivalent increase
in strain, the equivalent creep in the test piece. This is calculated using hereditary in-
tegrals as explained in Section 2.4.4. The creep data was smoothed in matlab, with a
moving average span of 100 seconds, with 10 datapoints per seconds. The creep curves
corresponds well with the primary, or transient, creep explained in section 2.4.1.
The determination of the time at which the dwell step starts, i.e. where the deforma-
tion is kept constant was done by ﬁnding the data point where the deformation recorded
by the machine was constant. Due to some ﬂuctuation in the data, the start of the creep
was found approximately within the nearest second.
The ﬁrst part of this section compares the creep curves for each of the two steel types
in both rolling directions. Furthermore the logarithmic trendlines ﬁtted to the curves are
plotted. The last part of the section discusses the logarithmic creep curves in detail for
each test. In addition to this the parameters of the logarithmic creep law are investigated.
4.4.1
Comparison of Creep Plots
In plots 4.5a and 4.6a the calculated creep from the relaxation data is plotted for the
s355 steel in rolling direction x and y respectively. In each plot the two slow tests and
the two fast tests with creep is showed. The initial strain at the starting point of the
60 minutes of creep is higher for the tests with the slow strain rate. The slope of the
curve is similar for both strain rates. The initial part of the curve is growing fast, then
after a short period of time the curve becomes more horizontal. In plots 4.5b and 4.6b
the logarithmic approximation of the creep curves are plotted for the s355 steel in rolling
direction x and y respectively. The creep curves for this one hour dwell period, does not
exhibit a steady state, nor a sudden increase at the end of the curve. Therefore the creep
is considered to be transient, or primary creep, as explained in section 2.4.1.

Uncertainties and Errors
• Extensometer measurements: There were errors in the extensometer measurements
in some of the tests. When only one extensometer gave good measurements, only
this was used for the correction of the data. In the tests the extensometers did
not measure correctly up til fracture. This could be because they slipped due to
weakening of the rubber bands attaching the to the test piece, or because they were
stretched to far.
• Sampling rate: The sampling rate for all the tests was at 10 Hz. This was suﬃcient
for the slow tests, which had little ﬂuctuation. Some of the fast tests had some
ﬂuctuation which might have given inaccurate results in some tests. In addition to
this a higher sampling rate could have been used in the dwell step for the relaxation
tests, where the ﬂuctuation was signiﬁcant.
• Determining the start of the dwell step might have been inaccurate in some tests,
because the start was considered on a case by case basis, determined from the
deformation data and the force-time plot.
• Reduction of area: The width and thickness of the gauge of the test pieces were
measured before and after the test, this measurement reading was rounded to the
nearest 0.5 mm in some cases. This was because some tests were measured with a
ruler, and some with a digital caliper, depending on what was available in the lab.
• Processing of results: To ﬁnd the slope of the elastic part of the stress-strain curve,
the slope between two points were found to make an initial estimation.
These
points were selected on a case by case basis, but usually within an interval of 10 to
60 % of the yield strength.

Conclusion and Future Work
The main conclusion which can be drawn from this project is that strain rates can have
a signiﬁcant eﬀect on mechanical properties of steel. Generally the yield strength and
tensile strength will be higher if the load is applied to the steel with a high rate of strain.
The proof yield strength is more sensitive to the strain rate than the other strength
parameters in these tests, with a higher diﬀerence between the fast and slow test values.
The post yield dwell step is assumed not to aﬀect the yield strength. However, a clear
impact on the tensile strength from the stress relaxation was not seen.
The ductility of the steel however is higher when the strain rate is lower. This is shown
in the percentage elongation of the specimen. All ductility parameters had a diﬀerence
between the fast and the slow strain rate tests. The trend was that increased strain rate
gives decreased ductility. A one hour dwell step with constant deformation after yield
was shown to have an eﬀect on all the ductility parameters, except the yield point exten-
sion Ae. This ductility parameter is found between yield and workhardening of the test
piece, and is assumed not to be aﬀected by the dwell step, which occurs at higher strains.
In addition to evaluation the eﬀects of strain rate and creep on material properties of
steel obtained from tensile tests, the eﬀect of strain rate on stress relaxation was investi-
gated. Based on the data from the tensile tests, it was found that loading the test piece
with a fast strain rate lead to an increase in stress relaxation. This suggest that a high
strain rate leads to increased creep, given the relationship between creep and relaxation.
A creep test could be done to determine the eﬀect of strain rate on creep.
The investigation of creep curves calculated from stress relaxation data showed that
the post yield room temperature creep curves ﬁt well with the logarithmic creep law
a · ln(t) + c which is often found to ﬁt transient creep in several metals. The possible
stress and strain rate dependence of parameters a and c were considered. The constant
c was found to correspond well with the initial strain ε0, as expected. However, an in-
ﬂuence from the strain rate was seen as well. The creep parameter a was found to be
inﬂuenced by the initial stress before creep, s0, and the strain rate of the test. There was
however a large diﬀerence between the results of this value for the diﬀerent tests, and
especially the tests with a fast strain rate showed a big variation in the results for the
identical tests. More tests should be made to make conclusions about the possibilities of
estimating the parameters in the creep law, based on applied stress and strain rate, and
==PAGE:60==
a higher sampling rate should be used for fast tests, to eliminate ﬂuctuation in the data
results.
An eﬀect of strain rate on creep and relaxation is seen in these tensile tests. However
more testing should be done to verify this. From the slow strain rate tests it was seen
that the stress relaxation curves for the two test pieces in each test type was similar to
one another. Testing with higher sampling rates, and other strain rates could be done to
see if it is possible to predict stress relaxation and creep based on the strain rate of the
test.

Abstract
The eﬀect of diﬀerent strain rates on strength and ductility parameters of steel have been
investigated. Uniaxial tensile tests on steel test pieces were made with two strain rates.
The total amount of specimens tested was 39, however only 32 specimens were included
in the result and the comparison. This is because of diﬀerent errors in the calibration
in the beginning. The experiment included two kinds of steel strengths, s235 and s355,
and the test pieces were cut in both rolling directions to see if this had any aﬀect on
the properties. For the four steel types the results showed that the yield strength and
the ultimate tensile strength is higher for higher strain rates. Furthermore the ductility
parameters found in these tests show that the elongation of the steel is higher for lower
strain rate tests.
Possible eﬀects on the material parameters by post yield, room temperature creep
were studied as well. In half the tensile tests the displacement was held constant for
one hour at a speciﬁed displacement after yield. This resulted in a decrease in stress,
or stress relaxation, during this hour. The stress relaxation was used to calculate an
equivalent creep strain using the relationship between relaxation and creep in hereditary
integrals. The test results for the tensile strength did not show an eﬀect from the stress
relaxation. The ductility parameters were however inﬂuenced. The tests with a dwell
step gave higher values for the ductility parameters than the tests with the same strain
rate without a dwell step.
The creep curves calculated from the stress relaxation were analyzed in the main
report. All creep curves ﬁt well with the logarithmic creep law, given by a · ln(t) + c,
where t is time in seconds. The parameter c was found to be dependent on the initial
strain, which was expected based on literature studies.
However a possible inﬂuence
of the strain rate was seen in the results. The creep parameter a was found to have a
possible inﬂuenced by the initial stress at the start of the dwell step, and the strain rate
of the test. However the results were inconclusive due to a large scatter in the a values
for the fast strain rate tests. Thus predicting the parameters of the creep law equation
based on initial stress, strain and strain rate was not possible based on these tests.
This report is a test report showing detailed results for each tensile test made as a
part of the M.SC. Thesis Uniaxial Load Testing of Steel- Inﬂuence of Strain Rate and
Possible Creep and Relaxation. The processing and analysis of the results is found in
the main report.

Introduction
This report contains detailed results from all the tensile tests conducted in this project.
A number of uniaxial tensile tests were made. The tests were done with three diﬀerent
strain rates. The strain rates recommended by DS/EN ISO 6892-1 [1] were tested as a
trial test, and strain rates ten times faster and ten times slower were tested. The fast and
slow strain rate tests were used for the results. Half of the tests with the fast and slow
strain rates were done with a dwell step in order to check if stress relaxation aﬀects the
results of the tests. All the tests were done on steel dogbone specimens. The obtained
material parameters such as tensile strength, yield strengths and the diﬀerent elongation
data found for the test subjects are presented for each subject. Furthermore stress-strain
plots and illustrations are included in this report. The stress relaxation curves obtained
from the data in the dwell step is plotted. An equivalent creep has been calculated from
the stress relaxation data, using hereditary integrals. The creep curves for each test is
found in this report. Detailed explanations for the diﬀerent material parameters and how
to calculate the various results of tensile tests can be found in the main report.

6.1
Calibration of Extensometer
Figure 6.1: Mitutoy digital micrometer calibrator
The calibration of the extensometer is an important part of a tensile test.
If this is
done incorrect it is not possible to use the data from the test. In the ﬁrst 4-6 tests the
calibration of the extensomter was incorrect. This is reﬂected in the test result later in
the report. The calibration was done by using the Mitutoy digital micrometer calibrator
which is illustrated in Figure 6.1. The main procedure of the calibration is to compare
the values from the extensometer at diﬀerent lengths on the Mitotoy calibrator. The
==PAGE:96==
6.1Calibration of Extensometer
diﬀerent readings are inserted into an excel sheet made for this calibration process. The
basic principle of the calibration is that the strain at diﬀerent lengths are found from the
extensometer is plotted in excel. Another plot is made from expected strains found by
dividing the elongation of the extensometer by the measured length of the extensometer.
The slope of the expected strain curve is divided by the slope of the measured strain
curve, and the factor obtained is multiplied with the previous calibration factor until
the two factors are identical. This is illustrated in Figure 6.2. The calibration is done
correct if the two lines have the same slope.The calibrationfactor is inserted in the Station
Manager Program.
The description of the calibration procedure is as follows:
• Connect the extensometer to the Instron 8521 tensile test machine
• Attach the extensometer to the Mitutoy digital micrometer calibrator using rubber
bands. It is here important to ensure that the extensometer is completely attached
to the rail. A good way to conﬁrm this is to attach the extensometer and then
stretch it 5 mm.
If the extensometer do not "jump" during these 5 mm it is
correctly attached.
• Start the calibration at - 2.5mm and insert the values in the excel sheet for every
0.5mm distance all the way to 2.5mm. This gives 10 readings in total.
• For each calibration made during testing, the procedure 2-3 calibrations to obtain
a calibrationfactor with an acceptable precision.
• When the lines in the coordinate system is identical, insert the factor in the Station
Manager program on the computer.
• The extensometer is now calibrated

Conclusion
The following section gives a summary of the test results, and the main conclusions drawn
from the tensile tests. When evaluating all the result at the end it is possible to see a
connection when it comes to the strain rate and its eﬀect on the mechanical properties.
The yield strength and the tensile strength are higher when the load is applied with a
fast strain rate to the steel. The strength is also higher in rolling direction y than x for
both types of steel. This is true for both the fast and the slow strain rate tests. The
results show a tendency of the steel being more ductile when cut in rolling direction x.
This is seen in the percentage elongation after fracture, A, and the percentage reduction
of area, Z, where the values are higher in rolling direction x for both steel types. The
tests with a dwell step were found to have a higher ductility than the tests with the same
strain rate without a dwell step. No eﬀect from stress relaxation on the tensile strength
of the steel was found.

of the fast strain rate tests with a one hour dwell step is listed in Table 8.2. Table 8.3
lists the results of the slow strain rate tests, and the slow tests with a one hour dwell
step is listed in Table 8.4.
