Abstract
The Atmosphere-Space Interactions Monitor (ASIM) on the International Space Station
observes optical and X- and Gamma-ray emissions from thunderstorms. The project will
analyze UV impulsive emissions at 180 nm–250 nm that are measured at the unprecedent
high temporal accuracy of 10 µs.
The emissions are likely Elves, excited in the lower ionosphere by the electromagnetic
radiation from the lightning current. The project aims to confirm this assumption and
to understand how the pulse characteristics relate to those of the causative lightning.
ASIM data and data from lightning detection networks on the ground will be analyzed
with the help of models published in the literature.
Kongens Lyngby, June 13, 2019

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Earth is surrounded by an atmosphere. The atmosphere not only allows mankind to
survive, being composed of all the chemical elements necessary for our life (Oxygen,
Carbon, Hydrogen, Nitrogen, etc.), but it also is home to the Global Atmospheric Elec-
tric Circuit. Chemical species and electric currents interact with each other, causing
both temporal and spatial variations of the atmosphere on different scales: from mil-
liseconds to centuries, from local to global. These interactions shaped the atmosphere
as it is known today, and generate phenomena such as thunderstorms on a daily basis
[M. Füllekrug 2006a]. The following report will discuss about some specific atmospheric
phenomena through analysis of data from the Atmosphere-Space Interactions Monitor
(ASIM) instrument.
1.1
Background & Motivation
TLEs are spectacular yet mysterious phenomena that occur above our heads when a
thunderstorm is raging. Red, purple, and blue coloured spurts populate the Earth’s
atmosphere from the lowest latitudes up to the ionopause.
They come in different
shapes: as ellipsoids, rings, carrots, or even sparklers, and many more, all different from
each other. Most of them immediately follow a lightning and show up in its surroundings,
while some others can appear at any time and place.
TLEs are such a diverse collection of events that it seems very unlikely that they all
generate from the same phenomenon, i.e. a thunderstorm. However they do, more or
less. The atmospheric conditions generated during a thunderstorm can vary significantly
depending on some parameters: oceanic or land thunderstorm, positive or negative cloud-
to-ground discharges (CGs), local electric field, local electron density, and many more.
And a very small variation in the atmospheric conditions in the region surrounding the
thunderstorm can lead to extremely different events.
Such events clearly catch the scientific curiosity of physicists and engineers, but
this can justify only some side-studies, performed during the time left free from the
main research activities.
There is need for a more meaningful reason to make sure
that the proper resources of time and money are allocated to the topic of TLEs. A
thorough list of justifications to further continue with the TLEs study is offered by
[2006b]: TLEs may represent a hazard for satellites, rockets, and manned vehicles during
launch and eventual landing phases, their impact on the local mesospheric chemistry is
not negligible, and it may increase in the future. Global warming will lead to a greater

are military-related issues that make the study of these phenomena very important:

The very first recorded observation of these particular phenomena dates back to July

be considered as a new subject; it has raised a lot of questions, many of which are so far
unresolved.
1.2
Previous & ongoing studies
FORMOSAT-2, previously ROCSAT-2, was the first satellite launched with a payload
dedicated to the observation of Transient Luminous Event (TLE). The Imager of Sprites
and Upper Atmospheric Lightning (ISUAL) instrument provided the first multiwave-
length data of Sprites, Elves, and other TLEs. The main purpose of the FORMOSAT-2
mission, which lasted from May 2004 to August 2016, has been to investigate the rela-
tion between upper atmosphere ionization and optical emissions excitation observed in
TLEs. As announced by [Mende S. B. 2005], it turned out that Elves contain with no
doubt traces of ionization; further studies performed on ISUAL by [Liu N. 2006] indi-
cated the minimum value of electric field magnitude in order for these events to occur.
[Kuo C. L. 2012] were able to estimate the emission intensities from Elves, thanks to
ISUAL results, while [Offroy M. 2015] inferred the statistical shape of a lightning pulse,
and highlighted its temporal and amplitude features.
Following the promising results obtained through analysis of ISUAL data, similar
studies are now being performed on high temporal resolution data from the ASIM in-
strument. Better data will hopefully lead to a better understanding of the physics behind
the above mentioned phenomena.
Through the years, since the first TLE was observed, many efforts have also been
put in modelling the atmospheric conditions, lightning, and Electro-Magnetic Pulses
(EMPs) that leads to the creation of these phenomena. The early results on electric and
magnetic fields and EMP modelling published by [Cho M. 1998], [Rycroft M. J. 1998],
and again [Cho M. 2000], opened the way to more accurate and advanced representations
of the atmospheric dynamics leading to TLEs, such as the one presented by [Marshall
2012]. [Blaes P. R. 2016], [Marshall R. A. 2015], and [Liu N. 2017] works were more
focused on Elves, a specific kind of TLEs, whereas [Pérez-Invernòn F. J. 2018] model
attempts to clarify the chemical signature of some TLEs on the local atmosphere.

1.3
Project definition
The Atmosphere-Space Interactions Monitor (ASIM), launched in April 2018, is a new
climate observatory onboard the International Space Station (ISS). The ASIM observes
optical and X- and gamma-ray emissions from thunderstorms, paying particular atten-
tion to signatures from TLEs and Terrestrial Gamma-ray Flashs (TGFs). The UV emis-
sions from thunderstorms are likely Elves, which electromagnetic radiations induced by
lightning current have excited in the lower ionosphere. Elves, Sprites and other TLEs
have been subject of many studies, including those that were getting data from the
ISUAL instrument onboard the FORMOSAT-2 satellite [Liu N. 2006]. The data ac-
quired from the ISUAL instrument have helped the development of theoretical models
that aim to explain the ionization of the lower ionosphere due to EMPs induced by
lightning [Mende S. B. 2005], [Liu N. 2006], [M. Füllekrug 2006b], [Pérez-Invernòn
F. J. 2018].
This project takes advantage of the unprecedented high temporal accuracy of 10 µs
of the MMIA instrument of the ASIM observatory in order to analyze the measured UV
impulsive emissions in the range 180 nm–250 nm [Chanrion O. A. 2019]. The project
aims to confirm the assumption regarding Elves origin, and to understand how the pulse
characteristics relate to those of the causative lightning. In order to reach the project
objectives, data from ASIM will be analyzed together with data from lightning detection
networks of ground observatories. The analysis of data will be performed with help from

2.1
Earth’s atmosphere
The atmosphere of our planet consists of air, distributed over a thin sheet that covers all
Earth’s surface. This sheet extends up to ≈100 km altitude, where the virtual Karman
line defines the border between atmosphere and outer space, although the atmosphere
comprised of all its layers extends up to thousands of kilometers from the surface. Gen-
erally speaking, the term atmosphere refers only to the thin sheet made up by three
distinct layers: troposphere, stratosphere, and mesosphere; nevertheless, adding the last
two layers, thermosphere and exosphere, the atmosphere extends as far as halfway to
the Moon. In the following text, ”atmosphere” will only refer to the first 100 km above
Earth’s surface, terminating at the Karman line.
As it is shown in Figure 2.1, the
Earth’s atmosphere is visible from space, where it appears like a blue layer surrounding
the planet. The reason why the atmosphere is blue, and also why the sky looks blue, is
that the white light coming from the Sun is scattered in all directions by air molecules,
and the short wavelength of the blue light is affected more.
Since the Earth is a very massive object (≈6 × 1024 kg) when compared to the mass
of its atmosphere (≈5 × 1018 kg) [Liu 1999], the thin atmospheric sheet can be held close
to Earth thanks to the planet’s gravitational pull. For this reason, it has been possible
for life to develop on Earth. Breathable air is confined in a thin layer that spans from
the surface to approximately 10 km of altitude (although it becomes very difficult to
breath above 5 km due to air rarefaction), and the whole atmosphere shields the planet
from cosmic radiation and solar particles with the help of the Earth’s magnetosphere.
Moreover, the atmosphere distributes the heat received from the Sun, so that the day
is not too warm, and the surface does not freeze to extremely low temperatures on the
night side.
The atmosphere’s chemical composition varies mainly accordingly to altitude, but
differences can be observed also between geographic areas, having the two poles and
the equator as extremes. On average, the atmosphere is composed of 78% Nitrogen,

2.1.1
Atmospheric layers
As mentioned before, the Earth’s atmosphere can be divided into five macro-layers, which
from bottom to top are named troposphere, stratosphere, mesosphere, thermosphere,
and exosphere (see Figure 2.6).
The density of the atmosphere decreases gradually
from the troposphere to the mesopause, where it is no longer possible to distinguish
atmospheric molecules from the space particles due to gases dissipation.
From the
Karman line to the outermost part of exosphere, the atmosphere gradually fades and
mingles with the interplanetary space.
Figure 2.6: Atmospheric layers and temperature profile variations with altitude.
The main features of atmosphere change from layer to layer, and they can be sum-
marized as follows [Sharp 2017], [“Layers of Earth’s Atmosphere” 2015].
• Troposphere: extends up to 7 km–20 km depending on the geographic location,
and approximately 50% of the atmosphere is contained in this layer. It is fair to
assume that water vapour and dust are only present in the troposphere, considering
that it is the only layer in which clouds are observable, with the few exceptions
of Polar Stratospheric Clouds (PSCs) and Polar Mesospheric Clouds (PMCs). In
reality, 99% of the total atmospheric water vapour can be found in this layer.
Temperature, pressure, and density decrease with increasing altitude. Troposphere
is also home to most of the weather phenomena.
• Stratosphere: spans from the end of troposphere to approximately 50 km above
sea level, and air density drops as far as a thousands times lower than on Earth’s
surface.
The air in this layer is dry, and one of the most abundant chemical
components of stratosphere is Ozone. This molecule is the reason why Ultra Violet
(UV) solar radiations are not a major concern at the sea level, since Ozone absorbs
them while heating up the atmosphere. From the bottom to the top of stratosphere
the temperature increases, due to the still nature of air in this region, where
turbulences and updrafts are not present.
==PAGE:29==
2.1 Earth’s atmosphere
11
• Mesosphere: above the stratosphere and up to 85 km–90 km there is the meso-
sphere. Although the study of this layer has been challenging scientists due to a
lack of mean of transportation that can populate these altitudes, it is known that
meteors burn here. Going back to ”normality”, temperatures start to decrease
again with increasing altitudes, all the way up to the mesosphere outermost layer -
the mesopause - which is the place where the atmosphere temperature reaches the
lowest values (≈−90 ◦C). Air density and air pressure values are approximately
one millionth and one hundredth of the respective sea level values.
• Thermosphere: is situated between 90 km and 500 km–900 km above the Earth’s
surface. The extension of thermosphere varies strongly, depending on the amount
of solar energy received. It is in this layer that X-ray and most of UV radiations
from the Sun are absorbed, exciting the particles and warming them to extremely
hot temperatures in the range of hundreds or thousands of degrees. Despite the
high particles temperature, the density is so low that humans would feel the air
as very cold.
This layer hosts one of the most spectacular phenomena of the
night sky, the auroras: these occurs whenever charged space particles interacts
with atmospheric molecules, which are therefore excited and emit strong fluxes of
light. The thermosphere is the atmospheric layer in which many Low Earth Orbit
(LEO) satellites orbit, and therefore it is frequently referred to as space rather
than atmosphere.
• Exosphere: the last layer of the terrestrial atmosphere consists of very thin air,
mainly Helium and Hydrogen, which flows into the outer space at a constant pace.
The electricity naturally flows within all the layers in Earth’s atmosphere in what is
called the Global Electric Circuit (GEC). The GEC has various sources, among which
the solar particles interaction with the Earth’s magnetic field and the cloud-produced
electricity. The GEC is a system that includes all the Earth’s atmosphere electric gen-
erators (Cosmic rays, solar wind, thunderstorm clouds, etc), currents, and even TLEs.
This constant electricity flow in the atmosphere creates a potential drop of approxi-
mately 200 kV–500 kV between the Earth’s surface and the upper atmosphere. In very
simple words, in regions of fair weather there is a weak current (≈2 pA m−2) flowing
upward and above thunderstorms there is a stronger current (≈1 A) flowing in the same
direction [“Earth’s Global Electric Circuit” 2013].
The GEC current results from the movement of the atmospheric charged particles
under the influence of the electric field. The electric field is present everywhere there are
free charges and its presence induces a force on the charges. The reduced electric field
is defined as the electric field divided by the neutrals density: its unit of measurement
is the Townsend (Td).
2.1.2
Ionosphere
Conversely to the atmospheric layers reported in the previous section, the ionosphere is
not defined as a layer by the temperature trend within it, as much as by the degree of
==PAGE:30==
2.1 Earth’s atmosphere
12
ionization of atmospheric atoms and molecules. From the upper part of the mesosphere
(70 km–80 km) to the end of the thermosphere, the radiation coming from outside has
sufficiently high energy that it can separate the electrons in the outer shell from their
atoms, thus creating a region full of negative and positive ions. Since the ionosphere is
populated by charged particles, it will display peculiar characteristics compared to the
lower atmospheric layers.
External radiation cause dissociation of negative and positive ions and free electrons
in all atmospheric layers, and the ionosphere got its name since it is the most sensitive
region to this effect.
Indeed, radiation does not reach lower altitudes with enough
intensity to ionize the chemical species in the atmosphere, and at greater altitudes the
air density is too low for a statistically significant number of photon-matter interactions
to occur. Solar radiation and Cosmic rays have incredibly high energies, and are the two
main causes for the ionization of the Earth’s upper atmosphere. However, only the half
ionosphere on the day side of the globe can build up thanks to solar radiation; therefore,
the weaker Cosmic rays play a major role during nighttime, where no interference coming
from the Sun is present. The Sun is the main source of ionization energy during the day.
The solar corona that surrounds it, just like the atmosphere surrounds Earth, constantly
feeds the solar wind with hot plasma of highly energetic particles. Some ejecta can have
energies in the X-ray or Extreme UV bands of the electromagnetic spectrum, thus being
able to provide the energy for ionization to atmospheric atoms and molecules. On the
other hand, there are many more sources of X-ray and UV light in the Universe than
the Sun: Black Holes, Supernovae, Neutron Stars, to name few, which all are sources of
Cosmic rays. Although their emissions are more intense than solar radiation, the great
distance from Earth attenuates their effect on the ionosphere.

Ionospheric layers
The ionosphere can itself be split in three main layers, from the
lower D region to the upper F region, going through the E region. The reason that
justifies this division is to be found in the diversity of air composition and state of
gases, due to altitude and radiation intensity. Moreover, different causes initiate distinct
ionization processes, and result in different time and space distributions of ions. Since the
differentiation criteria for the three regions cannot be clearly defined, and they change
over time, the boundaries between the layers are not clear lines.
The ionization density (ions/m3) varies with altitude, being at its lowest in the D
region and increasing non homogeneously while moving towards the F region. As a
matter of fact, the density has two maxima situated respectively in the E (100 km) and
F (200 km–400 km) regions.
D region
The lowest region spans the altitudes between 60 km–70 km and 90 km,
coinciding with the upper part of mesosphere. The electron density in this region is
≈1 × 109 m−3, but it is very variable (see Table 2.1 below) and this region completely
disappears during the night.
E region
The middle region can be identified as the lower thermosphere, beginning
at 90 km and going up to 120 km–150 km. The electron density peaks in a local maximum
in this region with a value of 2 × 1011 m−3, which is still subject to great variations
depending on the intensity of the solar radiation hitting Earth.
F region
The uppermost layer has its lowest altitude around 150 km and its upper
boundary at approximately 500 km, and therefore it coincides with the medium-upper
part of the thermosphere. The electron density peaks again in this region, with the
highest maximum value of 3 × 1012 m−3. Under certain circumstances, this maximum
can further split in two local maxima.

Daytime
During the day, the Sunlight directly hits the Earth and the X-ray and
UV radiation from the Sun contribute noticeably to the ionization of the ionosphere,
in which four different regions can be observed (D, E, F1, F2). The D region exists
only during daytime, since the ionizing radiation have sufficient energy to penetrate the
atmosphere to lower altitudes.
From morning to evening, solar radiation constantly illuminates the Earth and its
atmosphere, thus supplying the energy that separates electrons from their atoms. The
ionization process occurs simultaneously with the recombination process, in which ions
and electrons collide and recombine into neutral atoms. The ionization rate is greater
than the recombination rate, and therefore the ionosphere builds up during the day.
Nighttime
As mentioned above, the only source of ionization during the night
are Cosmic rays, which are less intense than solar radiation; therefore, the ionization
rate decreases in the night hours, and the recombination process prevails. Since the ion
density drops and there are no sources energetic enough to ionize the lower atmosphere,
when the Sun sets below the horizon, the D region disappears, and the ionosphere
remains with only a weakened E region and the F region.
Seasonal changes
Alongside with daily variations, seasonal changes can be ob-
served in the ionosphere.
Over the time span of a year, the Earth exposes its two
hemispheres differently to the solar radiation due to the fact that Earth’s rotational axis
is tilted with respect to the orbital plane. During summer, the Northern hemisphere
receives a stronger flux of solar light, and vice versa during winter; for this reason, a
summer day ionosphere is much more populated by electrons and ions than a winter
night ionosphere.
Another factor that affect the aspect of the ionosphere seasonally is the variation in
chemical composition of the atmosphere; different species means different ionization and
recombination rates, leading to changes in the global and local ion densities.
Solar cycle variations
On a longer time scale, the Earth’s atmosphere is influ-
enced by the 11 years Solar cycle. The Sun fluctuates continuously between periods of
extremely intense activity, with strong ejections of mass and plasma and sudden X-ray
or UV bursts, and calm periods, during which the Sun presents a homogeneous surface
and the solar wind is essentially constant. These incredible variations result in a flux of
X-ray and UV solar radiation which is nearly ten times stronger during solar maxima
than during solar minima. Such great changes in the amount of energy hitting Earth
cause wide fluctuations in the ionization density of the upper atmosphere.
Throughout the Solar cycle many different events take place, and they are able to
modify the ionosphere for small time intervals from a few hours to some days. Some
of these phenomena are flares, Coronal Mass Ejections, and streams of particularly
intense and fast solar wind; in some cases, they can cause geomagnetic storms, temporary
ionospheric disruptions, and they can also influence radio communication.

Sporadic ionization
Other than the regular ionization process described above, in the
mid ionosphere region it is also possible to observe sporadic ionization. It is irregular and
often more intense than the normal ionization; areas affected by this kind of ionization
shows themselves as clouds.
Ions coming from external sources, especially from the Sun, are the cause for sporadic
ionization. They generate in Earth-facing solar regions where Hydrogen condensate in
Calcium, and arrive at the Earth around 30 hours later following the electromagnetic
and gravitational fields influence. The arrival of these particles is connected to E and F
region disturbances, otherwise known as ionospheric storms [Silleni 1949].
2.2
Thunderstorms
Thunderstorms are phenomena which are possible to observe in the lower atmosphere.
They are characterized by rain, lightning, and thunder. Nevertheless, not all the times
that rain pours down a thunderstorm is raging; in order for a thunderstorm to form,
three requirements must be met: presence of moisture, unstable air rising, and some
sort of lifting mechanism, which is usually provided by heat. Some thunderstorms may
come along with big hail, strong winds, or even tornadoes, and those are hence labelled
as severe thunderstorms [“What is a thunderstorm?” 2018].
Once all the three necessary elements for thunderstorms to develop are present, the
formation process can begin. The updraft is bent when it encounters the tropopause
and the cloud tops assume the peculiar anvil shape. The reason why the rising air is
deflected at the tropopause is that air molecules are pushed from a region of vertical flow
to the stratosphere, where the flow is parallel to the Earth’s surface. In some cases it
is possible to observe bulges above the cloud tops, which are due to particularly strong
convection that drives moisture in the tropopause.
A typical thunderstorm cloud is displayed in Figure 2.11. A fully-formed storm cloud
shows a structure shaped by strong convection. Close to the center of the thunderstorm
there is a fast updraft of warm moisture that spreads outward when it encounters the
tropopause, since it is warmer. As they move aside, the air droplets freeze into ice and
start falling, warming up and melting into rain. The rain transports the surrounding air
downwards, thus generating downdrafts all around the central updraft. The coexistence
of both up- and downdrafts results in heavy turbulence within the cloud, which further
leads to lightning [“The Anathomy of a Thunderstorm” 2012].
A thunderstorm is much more complex than a bunch of clouds bumping into each
other and generating lightning. In fact, in the region of atmosphere right above thun-
derstorm clouds the magnetic and electric field behave quite differently than in regions
of fair weather. In the same way, strong field lines expand towards the Earth’s surface,
downward from the storm clouds.
Thunderstorms act also as electrical dynamos. There is always an electric potential
difference of 150 kV–600 kV between the Earth’s surface and the ionosphere, presumably
maintained by the electrical activity of thunderstorms withing the GEC system described
==PAGE:39==
2.2 Thunderstorms
21
Figure 2.11: Cumulonimbus: the thunderstorm cloud with characteristic anvil shape.
before. CG lightning do not account for all the current necessary to feed the measured
voltage drop, even though they occur at a rate of 15 strikes per second all over the globe.
Macroscopically, the Earth’s GEC consists of two streams of current: one downwards
from the ionosphere to the surface, taking place in fair weather regions, one other flowing
upwards from the ground to the atmosphere thanks to the voltage pumping provided
by thunderstorms. Since CG lightning are not enough to close the GEC, there must be
currents driving upwards somewhere else. These currents were recorded on thunderstorm
cloud tops, and they most likely are a result of the increased charge at the ground below
a thunderstorm, due to a particularly strong electric field [Barry 2003].
But thunderstorms do not only generate effects in the lower atmosphere, they also
power electrical discharges at higher altitudes in the stratosphere and mesosphere, up to
the ionopause [Gaskill 2018]. These phenomena are TGFs and TLEs, the last of which
will be described in a following section.
2.2.1
Lightning
Lightning are the most evident expression of thunderstorms. Extremely bright lines
connecting clouds to the ground suddenly appear, illuminating the sky covered by grey
cumulonimbus. Even though CG lightning are very common, they represent only one
kind of strikes that can be generated during a thunderstorm. A second type of lightning
actually happens more often: the in-cloud lightning. They account for approximately
80% of all discharges in a thunderstorm, and propagate from a cloud to another. The
term total lightning refers to the combination of the two lightning types described before.
In reality, there are many different kind of lightning; the more relevant will be described
==PAGE:40==
2.2 Thunderstorms
22
later on in this section. In order to give an idea of how important this phenomenon is,
in the year 2017 about 100 million total lightning discharges were detected [“What is a
thunderstorm?” 2018], and on average 8.6 million lightning strokes hit the Earth every
day [Warrilow 2014].
Figure 2.12: Lightning during a thunderstorm.
Lightning are electrical discharges, whose formation process is still not completely
understood. What is known is that usually for lightning to generate, a thunderstorm
must form first; within storm clouds, both cold and warm air are present, one carrying
ice crystals and the other water droplets. When the two air particles meet, electrostatic
charges are made in the cloud. These charges then redistribute in the cloud, driven by
the electric potential, and cluster at the bottom when negative or at the top if positive.
In this way, a potential difference builds up, and when it reaches a breakdown value, a
flash of energy is freed and takes the name of leader stroke. The leader stroke then can
take different paths and become either a CG or a Compact Intracloud Discharge (CID),
both propagating in an unpredictable zigzag manner. The CG lightning is followed by a
return stroke, which heats the surrounding air making it expand very fast; this sudden
motion cause a sound wave that is perceived as thunder [“How Is Lightning Made?”
2007].
According to a study conducted by the Danish astronaut Andreas Mogensen during
his stay onboard the ISS, lightning are most likely to be generated from clouds that are
situated at an altitude of 17 km. Every lightning is a potential source for EMPs that
will reach the lower ionosphere altitudes, and excite the chemical species thus causing
==PAGE:41==
2.2 Thunderstorms
23
ionization and optical emissions. Lightning can therefore locally change the chemical
composition of the atmosphere and enhance the ionization density during nighttime
[“The chemistry of lightning” 2018], [“The Earth’s Ionosphere” 2016].
Back to the different kinds of lightning, the CG and in-cloud discharges, together
with cloud-to-cloud lightning, make up the total lightning. Another type of lightning
can be identified in the so-called air discharges. It follows a description of the three
types:
• cloud-to-ground discharge: a lightning strikes between the cloud and the ground,
and even though it follows a path clearly vertical, it does not form a straight
line and many streamers branch from the main channel. This kind of discharge
is initiated from an upward positive streamer that meets a downward negative
stepped leader; a conductive channel is created, followed by an electrical discharge
also known as return stroke. Normally, a lightning of this kind consists of several
strokes. The one described above is a negative CG lightning; also positive CG light-
ning exist, and they originate from a positive downward stepped leader meeting
a negative upward streamer. Positive strokes are brighter than negative lightning
and they can follow an horizontal path for several kilometers before bending to-
wards the Earth’s surface. Another distinctive feature of positive CG lightning is
the lack of streamers branching from the main channel. CG lightning is showed in
Figure 2.13.

2.2.2
Transient Luminous Events
A great description of Transient Luminous Events is provided in [“Lightning” 2017]:
Short-lived luminous electrical manifestations formed in the upper regions of
the atmosphere above large thunderstorms.
TLEs were theorized back in the 1920s, when C.T.R. Wilson hypothesized that thun-
derstorms can enhance the local electric field to values which are then able to accelerate
==PAGE:44==
2.2 Thunderstorms
26
electrons to velocities close to the speed of light. Furthermore, he predicted that electri-
cal discharges can happen up in the mesosphere. This theory was proved in 1993 when
NASA’s Compton Gamma Ray Observatory detected X-ray flashes above thunderstorms.
After the first TLE was photographed in 1989, several campaigns, both airborne and
ground-based, were launched to better observe the top of thunderstorms; satellites were
also launched into LEO in order to study high energetic emissions from the areas above
storm clouds.
The ISS is the ideal platform from which to observe these phenomena. First of all,
since it orbits in a LEO, it is the closest place as possible that any instrument can get to
the ionopause and upper atmosphere, where these events occur. Secondly, the ISS has a
satisfying coverage of the Earth’s surface, spanning the entire area between −50 ◦–50 ◦
latitude, that is where most of the thunderstorms and lightning take place. Moreover,
from the ISS it is possible to study TLEs within a great range of wavelengths, analysis
made impossible from the ground because of the atmospheric absorption; indeed, the
Earth’s atmosphere blocks all the Gamma, X-ray, UV, and most of the Infrared radiation
coming from space. TLEs have strong emissions in the UV band, and therefore it would
be pointless to set a ground observatory dedicated to them, knowing that a large part
of their emissions will remain undetected.
The ASIM mission is designed to detect all these kind of events, occurring at high
altitudes, and associated with high energetic optical emissions. Without the clouds that
absorb or scatter part of the optical signature of these phenomena in the way, it is pos-
sible to understand more deeply how TLEs manifest in the upper atmosphere, and how
they couple with the underlying lightning. Moreover, it will be assessed to which ex-
tent thunderstorms, lightning, and especially TLEs can affect the atmospheric chemical
composition, concentration of gases, and ionosphere features both locally and globally.
Since TLEs are very weak phenomena, they are observed by ASIM instrumentation only
during nighttime.
Upper atmospheric lightning, other name of TLEs, is a class of phenomena which
occur in the atmosphere right above storm clouds; events such as Sprites, Elves, Halos,
and Jets, are all included in this class. Figure 2.17 displays all the kinds of TLE pop-
ulating the atmosphere above a thunderstorm. A brief and simple description of some
events is provided by [Gaskill 2018]:
Sprites are flashes caused by electrical breakdown in the mesosphere. Blue
jets are lightning discharges reaching upwards through the stratosphere, and
Elves are concentric rings of emissions caused by an electromagnetic pulse
at the ionosphere’s bottom edge. Giants are large discharges that create an
electrical breakdown of the atmosphere from the top of thunderstorms to the
bottom ionosphere. Terrestrial gamma-ray flashes are a flash phenomenon
generated at the top of thunderstorms.
It is believed that TLEs, or at least some of them, are caused by runaway electron
discharges [2018]. Such discharges happen when the electric field within the cloud is
strong enough to accelerate many electrons to very high energies, even in the X- and

Gamma-Ray bands. The parent electrical discharges assume different shapes, emit in
many wavelengths, and last for hundreds of microseconds or milliseconds only [“The
chemistry of lightning” 2018]. The electrical discharges that lead to TLE production
are mainly generated in large thunderclouds, where the electric field reach higher values
than in smaller or ”normal” clouds [“Lightning” 2017].
A short description of three of the most common TLEs follows, as reported in [“Tran-
sient Luminous Events” 2011]:
• Sprites: they usually occur after a CG lightning, that alter the charge distribution
within a thunderstorm cloud by removing charges from its top.
These events
appear as structured light bursts, spanning between altitudes 40 km–80 km in the
vertical direction, right above the storm clouds from which they generate. They
can expand in width up to some tens of kilometers, and last from few to tens of
milliseconds. Usually, they appear as the upward prolongation of highly structured
blue streamers, which then turn into red tendril structures, and end in a single
diffuse red glow that resembles a halo, from which it gets its name.
• Elves: they are generally caused by a CG return stroke and they appear as reddish
rings situated at the boundary with the ionosphere (80 km–90 km). They expand
very fast with an apparent velocity greater than the speed of light from the center,
placed right above the CG stroke; the wider Elves reaches radii of approximately
200 km in less than 1 ms. These events are so fast that it is impossible to observe
them with the naked eye, and they were theorized and modelled before an instru-

Elves
Elves are the highest electrical discharges that happens in the atmosphere, developing
at altitudes around 90 km at the edge with ionosphere. Elves have a peculiar ring shape
that begins as a red glow in the atmosphere above the parent lightning and expands up
to 400 km in diameter with an apparent velocity which is faster than the speed of light
[“The chemistry of lightning” 2018]. Most Elves consist of two concentric rings, with
the inner glow corresponding to the return stroke of the parent lightning. Elves are the
most frequent kind of TLEs, and their peak brightness is strongly correlated with the
peak current of the parent lightning return stroke [Warrilow 2014].
Figure 2.18: Elve observed by Martin Popek on April 2nd, 2017.
Elves is an acronym that stands for Emission of Light and Very Low Frequency
perturbations due to Electromagnetic Pulse Sources. Indeed, CG discharges generate an
electromagnetic pulse that travels vertically, excites the upper atmosphere and expands
horizontally, thus initiating the optical emission which is known as Elve and that lasts
==PAGE:47==
2.2 Thunderstorms
29
less than one millisecond [“Lightning” 2017]. Elves are therefore not a direct consequence
of an electrical discharge, but an indirect effect due to the interaction between the EMP
and ionospheric particles [Pérez-Invernòn F. J. 2018]. What happens before it is possible
to detect an Elve-like emission is a discharge, most likely CG, which causes an EMP;
the EMP energizes and accelerates electrons, that then collide with and excite Nitrogen
molecules in the upper atmosphere. The excitation process of atmospheric molecules
produces light emissions with different brightnesses, shapes, and colors: in this case
the emission is a dim ring-shaped glow with peak visible brightness in the red band.
There is evidence of Elves generated from EMPs coming from other kind of discharges,
in particular CIDs and Energetic In-cloud Pulses (EIPs).
These EMPs induce Elve
doublets, the first coming from the direct pulse and the second from its reflection on the
Earth’s surface.

differently to the atmospheric absorption and their strengths decay in different ways.

2.5
GLD360 network
For long time there has been the need for a network capable of detecting lightning
all over the globe with high precision and exhaustively. GLD360 network solved this
problem, offering a database collecting CG discharges, their polarity, their peak current,
and a highly accurate location. GLD360 is the result of Stanford University and Vaisala
Inc. collaboration. This network has been based on, and later tested against Vaisala’s
National Lightning Detection Network (NLDN) in order to assess its efficiency.
==PAGE:53==
2.5 GLD360 network
35
The exceptional accuracy in lightning geolocation is due to the combination of data
from various ground sensors, which receive EMP signals reflected from the ionosphere,
after they have been generated by the electric discharge. The same signal can be detected
by more than one sensor, and thus the position of its origin can be triangulated, and
arrival times compared with the distances from sensors to the source in order to estimate
the lightning stroke timing. Peak currents and flash polarities are estimated through
models that take into account propagation and attenuation effects in the atmosphere.
GLD360 data can be used to generate world maps of CG lightning flashes distribution
and to predict where intense lightning will occur; hence, they are a useful tool to assess
in which regions there is a high chance of encountering those discharges that will most
likely generate TLEs [Lojou J.-Y. 2011], [Said R. 2016].

3.1
Data selection
Data from the MMIA instrument onboard the ASIM mission come along with a set
of information needed to identify, geolocate, and chronologically order them.
Data
generated by the photometers are composed of a series of measurements sampled with
a time resolution of 10 µs; Level 0 (L0) data are raw photon counts, while Level 1
(L1) data are counts converted to power density of the photon flux. ASIM data are
processed onboard in order to assign them a flag that represents the kind of observed
event: lightning or UV emission.
Since the aim of the project is to analyze the pulse characteristics of Elves to confirm
some hypothesis on Elve production theory and to couple them with the parent lightning,
a set of data matching specific characteristic was requested to the ASDC. All data that
were sampled since the beginning of the mission until the moment of the request, at
every latitude and longitude covered by the instrument FOV, with a minimum peak
amplitude in the PHOT2 band (180 nm–230 nm) of 50 counts (L0), had to be included
in the list. There is not a specific reason why the value 50 was chosen, other than being
the lowest round number of counts above which there is a high chance that the detected
photons are coming from a TLE.

100 counts”, ”100 to 150 counts”, ”150 to 200 counts”, and ”above 200 counts”. The

than normal, when flying through the flux of energetic particles that accompanies the
SAA. When the ASIM mission flyes through the SAA, all the instrumentation are shut
down for an entire orbit, thus the lack of events in that area and in some stripes before
and after it.

Elve production should now be more or less clear: (positive) CG discharges strike, an
EMP is emitted and travels up to the ionopause, around 90 km, where it heats electrons,
then, molecular Nitrogen and other chemical species are excited and start emitting 0.3 ms
after the CG stroke; after 1 ms the emission has assumed a ring shape that extends for
300 km in radius. During the time period between the first appearance of emission lines
and the setback to normal density and excitation states, the signal received by PHOT2
shows a rapid increase followed by a more gentle decrease after the peak. Figure 3.7

The photometer signals are noisy, especially the UV from PHOT2; thus, in order
to perform a better analysis, a filter can be applied to smooth the signals. Ideally, the
signal does not present any background noise, because a perfect instrument should be
able to clearly distinguish between signal and noise, thus setting the Signal-to-Noise
Ratio (SNR) to ∞. This would mean that in the data we receive, all samples have a
value of 0, either counts or power density of photon flux, with the only exception of
those measurements corresponding to the actual observed event. Hence, before applying
the filter, we should set the background noise to have a zero mean. In order to do so,
first we calculate the mean of the first set of measurements, from the first value to few
hundreds of samples before the peak; then, we remove the mean thus found from every
single measurement value. At this point, we are ready to use the filter; the filter chosen

The process of smoothing of a signal y = f(x) is generally
always the same, and it consists in replacing every sampled measurement y of the signal

measurements centered in the value to be replaced. The fit involves 2n+1 points, where

average, since they showed that the coefficients of the polynomial are the same for every

equation (3.1) can be customized to every case by applying different coefficients: this

Once the signal is smoothened, we can proceed with peak detection. Since for every
set of measurements there can be more than one or two events detected by all pho-
tometers, we only care about the main event, which is when the PHOT2 reaches its
highest peak. The Python function find_peaks allows us to define the desired features
of the peaks to detect: minimum height, minimum width, minimum distance between
consequent peaks, and minimum prominence. As mentioned right above, this analysis
involves only the main events of every set of measurements, and therefore it is useful to
define the minimum height around 80%-90% of the maximum signal value, so that minor
peaks will remain undetected. Moreover, the identification as peaks of fluctuations in
the signal due to the background noise can be avoided by setting minimum width and
distance; the chosen values are respectively 100 µs, and 400 µs, which take into account
both the Elve-like pulses modeled by [Pérez-Invernòn F. J. 2018] and [Kuo C. L. 2012],
and those observed with ISUAL instrumentation. For the same reason, a minimum value
for the prominence was also set: 1 × 10−9 for the signal detected by PHOT2, and twice

Finding the peaks is of utmost importance when analyzing a pulse shape. Indeed,
once we know where the peaks are, it is fairly easy to calculate the rise and decay time of
each pulse; or at least it is easy when the pulse presents both a monotonous increase and
drop. This is generally the case for PHOT2 pulses, while PHOT1 and PHOT3 usually
report a plateau before the peak, which indicates the lightning propagation before the
return stroke. Therefore, two slightly different methods have been used to estimate the
rise and fall time of PHOT2, and PHOT1 and PHOT3.
PHOT2
The signal is scanned from about 1000 bins before the peak to 5 bins after
it; at every step, the mean of 21 points centered in the current bin is calculated. As
soon as the value of the mean rise above the 10% of the signal’s maximum value, a
count starts increasing of one unit at each bin where the mean is above 10%, until
the mean of the 21-points signal stays below 90% of the peak value; at that point the
count stops, and the rise time is obtained in terms of number of bins, which can be
easily converted to µs knowing that one bin corresponds to 10 µs. A similar procedure
is followed to estimate the fall time of the pulse: starting 5 points before the peak, the
signal is scanned, the mean is calculated over 21 points, the count is initiated when the
mean value first measures below 90% and it stops when 10% of the peak value is reached.
The 10%-90% interval was chosen accordingly to the standards of Statistics, where
it is commonly used to define the rise time of a signal.
PHOT1 and PHOT3
The situation for PHOT1 and PHOT3 is more complicated,
due to many factors: first of all, the above-mentioned plateau due to lightning propaga-
tion, secondly, photometers sometimes detect signal coming from an intense lightning
activity region, which is all contained in the instrument FOV, thus the pulse can assume
many different and difficult-to-analyze shapes, as the one showed in Figure 3.11. Figure
3.12 shows the field of view of the instrument, the ISS trajectory and the ASIM position,
together with CHU1 data. For this reason, it is not possible to estimate the rise time
from the number of bins in between the range 10%-90%; indeed, the 10% threshold can
be situated too early with respect to the peak value. Therefore, after a few attempts,
the lower limit was set to 30% for PHOT1 and 50% for PHOT3. Of course it is now
not possible to estimate the rise time directly from the number of bins within the signal
30%-90% or 50%-90% values, and therefore we must find a suitable way to propagate
the lower limit down to 10%. An analysis of the simplest and most clear PHOT1 and
PHOT3 data showed that the rising side of the pulses can be approximated as a straight
line with angular coefficient m given by

3.3
Coupling with lightning
Since all Elves generate from a parent lightning, it is useful to match the events in the
data set with the discharges that induced them; for this purpose, we were given access
to Vaisala Lightning Exporter website and the Vaisala GLD360 database. In order to
retrieve lightning data, a query must be made, stating an area in which to search for
discharges, and a time interval. The geographical area can be defined as a rectangle by
minimum and maximum latitude and longitude, or by a circle of desired radius centered
in a point of known latitude and longitude. The time interval can be no longer than
24 hours and shorter than 1 minute when retrieving data from the website, but a query
with the Python code allows us to request lightning informations in a time interval as
long or short as we want.
In the first place, we decided to retrieve all the lighting data we could find in a
circular area of 300 km of radius around the point defined by the event’s latitude and
longitude, in a 1 minute time interval centered at the event’s time. Secondly, among
all lightning matching the query for one event, we looked for those occurring within a
safe time interval from 700 ms before to 400 ms after the event. Once this smaller list of
lightning suitable to have induced the selected Elve is compiled, we scan the lightning’s
peak currents; eventually, the discharge with highest current absolute value, and in case
of a tie the positive discharge, was identified as the most likely to have generated the
event.
If no lightning were detected within the 300 km radius circle for some specific events,
the query was repeated expanding the research area to a circle of 400 km radius, and all
the steps of the procedure described before were repeated. In this way, parent lightnings
were identified within a 300 km radius area for 1069 Elves, and 136 more events were
associated with a discharge by increasing the research area. A total of 259 events couldn’t
be associated to any lightning. For all events we also retrieved information about the
rise time of the current wave.
In the end, not considering the events not matched with any lightning, we counted
all the Elves that were generated by parent discharges whose peak current was below a
certain level. Thus, we could estimate the current value below which 10%, 50%, or 90%
of all events occurred.
Afterwards, a regression study has been made on the data set matching the parent
lightning peak current with the UVC peak amplitude. Only the events generated from a
negative CG discharge were take into consideration for this analysis, since they represent
the most statistically significant subset in our pool of data; moreover, it is better to
consider positive and negative discharges separately due to small but not negligible
differences in the physical processes that lead to Elve formation. A linear regression
performed on the peak amplitude as x vector and the peak current as y vector allows us
to find out the threshold (intercept) for Elve production. Linear regression consists in
finding the slope m and the intercept q of the equation of a straight line

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
We will now proceed with the discussion of results. The analyzed data cover a time span
of almost 10 months from April 2018 to February 2019. The 1469 events composing the
final set are not many, but as we saw in the previous chapter they provide interesting
results.
First, we comment on the PHOT2 pulses’ peak amplitude distribution (Figure 4.1).
The histogram obtained from L1 data show a clear peak at low values, meaning that the
frequency with which weak Elves are generated is greater than the rate of occurrence
of Elves with strong emission lines in the 180 nm–230 nm band. This is due to the fact
that Elve production depends on many factors, and the intensity of Elve emissions is
proportional to these factors; therefore, the less frequently some conditions are met, the
fewer the events linked to those conditions will be. As mentioned in chapter 2, these
factors are the local reduced electric field, the rise time of the parent lightning’s current,
the CMC, the degree of ionization of the lower ionosphere and its local composition.
Second, we presented a set of distributions showing for every subset the rise and
fall times of the pulses detected by each photometer (Figures 4.2 to 4.13 and Table
4.1). A feature which is immediately noticeable is the difference between rise time and
fall time time scale: the peaks of the rise time distributions are all around 0.20 ms,
with exceptional events whose rise times can be as long as 0.50 ms, while the fall time
distributions are remarkably shifted towards 1 ms, with peaks around 0.70 ms for PHOT2
pulses, and 1.3 ms for the other two photometer’s signals. The results matches those
presented in the literature, even though they can be improved by further analysis and
modifying the code for peak detection. Moreover, rise times shorter than fall times are
a good indication of the fact that the pulse could be approximated by a bi-exponential
function, as the derivative of the parent lightning’s current is.
The pulse shape of both lightning and Elve-like emissions depends on lightning cur-
rents and on the geometry. The word ”geometry” implies a lot of things in this case:
the altitude at which the lightning current started flowing, how deep in the cloud the
discharge happened, the composition of the cloud and its shape, the position of the
instrument with respect to the thunderstorm and the detected events, the local atmo-
spheric density profile, the time necessary for the EMP generated by the discharge to
reach the ionosphere and excite the chemical species’ electrons. All these elements, to-
gether with errors in peak detection, justify the outliers and the high variance in fall
time distributions.
The rise time and fall time alone do not provide us enough information, though;
their ratio adds a missing piece to the puzzle, giving an idea of the extension of the
total pulse. In Figure 4.14 we showed this ratio for every event in the four subsets, and
==PAGE:92==
5 Discussion
74
we can infer that the strongest pulses all present a similar shape, where the rise time is
about one third of the fall time. The weakest pulses are more varied, being their rise
time one third to one half of the fall times. This heterogeneous distribution of rise time
to fall time ratio for weak pulses may be due - again - to inaccuracies in peak detection,
but also to contamination of the emission band by other sources.
We will now make some considerations regarding the two subsets of data, one with
and one without CHUs information associated with them, as represented in Figure 4.19.
Theoretically, CHUs are not triggered if the lightning is out of their FOV, and therefore
those events with only photometer data should show pure Elves. If the Elve emission is
the only source of photons within the photometers FOVs, the PHOT3 should not return
any signal, since a typical Elve does not present emission lines at 777.4 nm, or if it does
they are very weak compared to, for example, the N21P emission lines. Therefore, if we
see a peak in PHOT3, as we do, we can deduce that there is still little contamination from
lightning. PHOT2 peak amplitude distribution does not suffer from the lack of lightning
in the photometer FOV, since its filter allows the detection of Elves (and other TLEs)
only. The situation with PHOT1 is a bit different, since it can detect signals from both
lightning and Elves. It is evident from the two distributions that the lightning emission
dominates, flattening the histogram which covers all peak amplitudes value; the peak at
the beginning may be due to very weak lightning events, which occur more often than
strong discharges. When lightning is not present, the peak amplitude distribution shows
a clear peak at low values, indicating the Elves contribution to the PHOT1 signal.
Moving to the time delays of PHOT1 and PHOT3 peaks with respect to PHOT2
signal, we start by looking at Figures 4.15 to 4.18, and at Table 4.2. The four subsets
do not exhibit remarkable differences in the time delay distributions; for most events,
the UV signal that produces the pulse in PHOT2 is the first to peak, then PHOT3 and
PHOT1 peak in this order. The reason why the instrument detects Elves first is that it
is closer to the altitude where they occur than to the low altitude clouds where lightning
are generated, and the light emitted from electrical discharges takes time to travel to the
ISS orbit. The time delay between PHOT1 and PHOT3 may provide information on the
type of discharge, being it a CID, an EIP, or a CG; in the case of a CID the blue emission
detected by PHOT1 could peak before the red light detected by PHOT3, nevertheless
blue light is also scattered more within the cloud, so we have to wait for results on the
cloud scattering side in order to tell more about this distinction. In Figures 4.20 and
4.21 we can observe the tendency of PHOT3 pulse to peak before PHOT1 pulse as the
event population below the graph diagonal. The population on the vertical axis turned
out to be a fictitious one, being composed of events for which PHOT3 saturated, and
therefore peak detection did not work properly, placing the pulse maximum value at a
time before it actually peaked. However, it is remarkable how all PHOT3 saturation
events coincide with a very short time delay of the pulse detected by PHOT1; this feature
may indicate that a particularly intense lightning occurred on top of the cloud, and its
high energy caused the saturation of the sensor. Indeed, when the time delay of the
pulse in PHOT3 is long, it means that the rise time has also been long; this indicates
a discharge that originated deep in the cloud, and therefore that suffered more from
scattering and absorption, resulting in a weaker photon flux at the instrument altitude.
==PAGE:93==

Figures 4.22 and 4.23 results shows that when lightning is not directly included in the
instrument FOV, PHOT1 peak generally comes before PHOT3 peak, because of the UV
contribution to the pulse detected by PHOT1. The rise time of PHOT3 will be indeed
longer, since the lightning source is located at great distance from the sensor.
The plot showing the relation between peak amplitude and rise time of the pulses
detected by PHOT2 (Figure 4.24) tells us that we observe strong UV events only when
the pulse peaks fast, while weak events display a wider rise time distribution. Since we
would expect that weak events display a longer rise time for symmetry, we can justify
the presence of weak events with fast rise times as events occurred very close to the
instrument.
Moving on to the coupling of photometer data with GLD360 network information,
we start by looking at the graphs displaying the retrieved parent lightning peak current
for every event, from the weakest to the strongest UV peak (Figures 4.25 and 4.26).
The amount of negative discharges is significantly greater than the amount of positive
discharges, following the global trend, and even though Elves are more likely to be
generated from positive discharges, the rate of Elve production from negative discharge
is still superior than the rate of Elve production from positive discharges. Moreover, we
observe a slight but clear increase in the average peak current from those that generated
weak events to those which produced the strongest UV peaks; this result is in line
with the models presented in the literature ([2018], [Kuo C. L. 2012], [Cho M. 1998]),
which estimated that an increase in the parent lightning peak current leads to a greater
brightness and photon flux density.
We could also estimate a range of peak current values within which the lightning
produced 80% of the total number of detected Elves (Figure 4.27): results show that,
disregarding the 10% of events coming from low peak current parent discharges and
the 10% of events generated from exceptionally high peak currents, the peak current of
Elves’ parent lightning is comprised between 32 kA and 225 kA. Nevertheless, there is a
significant amount of events associated with peak currents of values up to 400 kA and
down to 2 kA; this result may indicate that the current threshold for Elve production
is lower than previously thought. Indeed, the threshold was set to be equal to 88 kA
by [Blaes P. R. 2016], 80 kA by [Kuo C. L. 2007], and 60 kA by [Barrington-Leigh
C. P. 1999], but an exceptional event was reported by [Hobara Y. 2003], who indicated
a value of 30 kA as the peak current of an Elve’s parent lightning. In order to assess
whether our data are in reasonable accord with those values, despite the extremely
low peak current events, we fitted the negative discharges’ currents against PHOT2
pulses’ peak amplitudes, and thus determined our own threshold (see Figure 4.28). We
fitted both a polynomial of 2nd degree and a line to the data, and we extrapolated
the coefficients corresponding to the intercept with the vertical axis, which represent
the peak current threshold for Elve production.
From linear regression we found a
threshold of (−88.67 ± 11.08) kA, and a slightly different value from the polynomial fit,
i.e. (−83.03 ± 19.72) kA. Both values are in agreement with the mentioned literature
results.
We also want to comment on the relation that exists between the peak current of the
parent lightning and the associated Elve’s emission pulse rise time, which is exhibited
==PAGE:94==

in Figure 4.29. We notice that the plot follows the same trend as the one comparing the
Elve’s emission pulse rise time with the Elve’s pulse peak amplitude (Figure 4.24); thus,
since we just confirmed the correlation existing between PHOT2 pulses’ peak amplitude
and parent lightning’s peak currents, we can draw the same conclusions.
As last, we would like to comment on the attempt to fit the bi-exponential function
representing the derivative of the parent lightning current to our PHOT2 data (Figure
4.31). Adjusting the scaling and shifting parameters of the distribution, we assessed the
good behaviour of the fit for the rising edge, while it deviates from the declining phase.
A further analysis will provide better fitting parameters, especially if based upon more
accurate distribution parameters; indeed, while we used the lightning current rise time
provided by GLD360 network, the total duration of the stroke was estimated from our
fall time, which is still affected by peak detection errors when the signal is very noisy
and intense lightning activity is detected. Moreover, the distribution used for the fit
assumes that the current wave instantly generates emissions in the lower ionosphere,
which is not true, since it takes about 0.3 mm for the EMP to reach the higher altitudes
and excite the local chemical species. We can conclude stating that the Elve emission
is proportional to the derivative of the parent lightning current, unless factors to be
determined with more accuracy.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
This project analyzed UV impulsive emissions at 180 nm–250 nm, most likely Elves,
measured by the ASIM MMIA instrument with an incredibly high temporal accuracy
of 10 µs. The project aimed to confirm the assumption that the UV emissions detected
by PHOT2 are actually - in most cases - Elves, which are produced through lower
ionosphere excitation by EMPs radiating from the parent lightning currents. Moreover,
PHOT2 pulse characteristics were matched to those of the parent discharges in order to
find any correlation.
We got acknowledged with the data system. Data, which are labelled as TLE or as
pure lightning [Chanrion O. A. 2019], were downloaded from ASDC and their organi-
zational structure was analyzed. Then they have been plotted to get familiar with the
different characteristics that various types of events exhibit. We extracted the selected
data. The ASDC provided a list of events with specific UV signal amplitudes, including
some other significant information such as time and position of the event.
We identified a model that estimates the temporal emission from Elves. Data were
analyzed relative to an existing model, which aims to estimate the time delay for excita-
tion of ionospheric elements, developed by Francisco J. Gordillo-Vazquez and his team.
Data analysis allowed us to discriminate between two sources that generate signals: the
Elves rings after ionospheric excitation and the lightning signals coming from the clouds
after scattering.
We estimated the delay between different wavelength signals. The delay is due to
scattering in clouds of the direct lightning signal and other geometry factors. A com-
parison between data and the model provided an indication of how much delay comes
from cloud scattering. The UVC signature comes from the ionosphere if the lightning is
not in the FOV of the instrument (only Elves are detected); if the lightning is inside the
FOV, Elves can be subtracted from the signal, and the final product can be compared
with a theoretical idea of what the signal should look like.
We identified a subset of lightning data from the GLD360 network to be associated
with our set of events. The position and time provided alongside with the event-induced
signal allowed the identification of those lightning events that most likely induced the
detected Elves.
Data from ASIM onboard ISS and the GLD360 network were then
combined to obtain a better understanding of the analyzed phenomena.
Finally, we gave an overview of the main UV emissions’ pulse characteristics and how
to interpret lightning + Elves data.

6.1 Future challenges

6.1
Future challenges
As previously mentioned, further improvements on this analysis can be made. First of
all, the peak detection can be redefined, in order to tailor it to specific events through
learned pulse shape parameters. Secondly, the pool of analyzed data can be expanded
with new ASIM measurements to increase the statistical significance of the results. Then,
some efforts can be directed in the reconstruction of those events for which PHOT3, and
sometimes PHOT1 saturated, thus enlarging the data set and diminishing the risk of
fictitious findings. Last but not least, the curve fit of the data with the bi-exponential
distribution representing the derivative of the parent lightning discharge can be opti-
mized.
