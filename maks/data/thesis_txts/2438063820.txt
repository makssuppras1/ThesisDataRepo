Abstract: 
Fungi are organisms belonging to the Eukarya domain; a domain divided into 4 kingdoms: 
Plantae, Protista, Animalia, and Fungi. The aim of this thesis is to examine which influences 
these substrates has on the differentiation of different fungi. This is done by using 3-point 
inoculation, measure colony diameter, sporulation, then analyze the fungi via HPLC analysis in 
order to identify different secondary metabolites, then PCA in order to find which substrates 
function best for production of specific metabolites. 
10 different species fungi are used for this experiment, 7 Penicillium and 3 Aspergillus. 12 
different food products are chosen: banana, apple, blood orange, red onion, bell pepper, broccoli, 
portobello, rice, milk, cod, hazelnut and water. For each of the food products 2 types of media 
are created, one without Czapek agar (Cz), a chemically well-defined medium added, and one 
with. Each of the 10 fungi are inoculated on a plate of each of the 24 media. 
Pure Cz media did not make the fungi produce spores, but the different food products did, 
meaning they must contain different types of trace metals. The pure apple media stimulated very 
thin growing fungi and would not be able to be used alone in a media. In contrast both bell 
pepper and broccoli seemed to be good candidates for media, even without the added Cz. The 
food- and indoor fungi receive seemingly the most positive impact on the differentiation from 
either fruit- or vegetable media with Cz, or both. The Cz medium containing cod and milk have a 
tendency of being able to stimulate production of unique metabolites, while often banana or 
apple seems to generally stimulate the best condition for good simultaneous production of 
metabolites. The more newly discovered fungi do not seem to follow the same tendency and 
probably needs another type of media to show the same type of impact. The bell pepper medium 
with Cz did show potential for increased amount of phenylahistin in Aps. fructus. Meanwhile the 
medium containing both banana and Cz showed that both Pen. rubens and Pen. bialowiezense 
are able to produce n--p.

Introduction: 
Fungi are organisms belonging to the Eukarya domain; a domain divided from the bacteria and 
archaea domains regarding taxonomic ranks. In the Eukarya domain all organisms have 
organelles which are bound to membranes, and the domain itself can be divided into 4 kingdoms: 
Plantae, Protista, Animalia, and the one this thesis will focus on, Fungi. The fungal kingdom 
covers everything from mold and yeast to ordinary mushrooms, with an ever-expanding list of 
currently over 120.000 species. Due to the lack of hard structures in the fungi, it has been 
difficult to determine the period in which the fungi diverged from plants and animals for 
example via fossils. This is due to the high similarities between bacteria and fungi, when they 
become fossilized. [6] [9] 
While fungi, traditionally speaking, often has been lumped together with plants due to observed 
similarities, i.e. both being presumably because of vegetative growths, there are several 
differences between the two. For instance, fungi cannot perform photosynthesis as its cells lacks 
chloroplasts required for the process, and the cell walls is not composed of cellulose like that of a 
plant. The fungal cell wall consists of glucan and chitin; each molecule that individually can be 
found in other types of organism, but a combination of the two is unique to fungi. The fungi are 
heterotrophic organisms, like animals, which means it requires its energy from an organic carbon 
source. Unlike animals the fungi do not have a digestive system, and therefore secretes enzymes, 
like bacteria, so polysaccharides can be broken down to useful pieces, such as glucose. Glucose, 
alongside water and other nutrients, is absorbed through the hyphae; cells surrounded by a 
tubular cell wall creating long, filamentous structures, which can branch and are collectively 
known as mycelium. For most types of fungi this is their primary body of growth, but for other 
groups of fungi, like the Basidiomycota, the fruit bodies are most obvious visually. These types 
of fungi have developed root-like growths rhizomorphs or more commonly known as mycelial 
cords, through which they gather nutrients and water. [2] [4] [5] [29] 
Fungi reproduce through the usage of spores created by either rapid asexual reproduction, 
creating clones of the parent, or sexual reproduction, creating haploid spores, that follows either 
the heterothallic or homothallic mating system. Spores are ejected from the fungi and carried by 
either air or water, until it finds a viable sport for growth, and a mating spore in case of the 
haploid spore or dies. [16] [28]

Hypothesis and aim: 
This hypothesis takes basis in the theory of fungi being closely related to animals, rather than 
plants, based on experimentation of alignment of genes which showed the RNA sequences of 
fungi having closer alignment with animals than plants. 
Based on this assumption, it could be argued that food products which are considered healthy for 
animals, and by that extension humans, also would be good nutrient sources for fungi and 
stimulate a good fungal differentiation to some degree. [17] [38] 
For this thesis different food products, which are considered healthy for humans for one or 
another reason, have been picked and from them media were made. 
The aim of this thesis is to examine which influences these substrates have on the differentiation 
of different fungi. 
In order to determine the full extent of the substrates influences on the fungi, 2 types of media 
are made from the same food product, or basis material. One which is just the material, in order 
to see how it influences the fungi on its own. 
And another in which czapek, Cz, is additionally added to the media. Cz contains all the basic 
elements for fungal growth, such as 30g sugar per 1000g water as a carbon source, nitrate as 
nitrogen source, potassium chloride, a source of essential ions, iron- and magnesium sulfate, 
cation sources. [41] 
This is done for several reasons; a chase could be that the fungus would have a different 
differentiation profile depending on whether or not the basic ingredients of Cz are present, 
thereby implying that Cz could interfere with some metabolic processes. 
Another case could be made, that even if the fungi does not have any significant differentiation 
profile, while grown on the pure food-based media alone, maybe it just need to be ensured those 
basic building blocks in order to have energy to make metabolites from the base material of the 
media. 
Reference samples from fungal growth on pure water- and Cz media will be made to 
differentiate between insignificant and significant secondary metabolite products. Secondary 
metabolites will be found by using HPLC.

Basic ingredients for media: 
The numbers are found primarily using a database from DTU food. Often for a food product to 
be considered healthy for humans, they need to have a solid carbon source. This would be in the 
form of types of sugar and starch. Fructose and certain amount of starch is considered healthier 
than glucose, since the former takes a bit more energy to degrade. This gives the animal a feeling 
of being full longer, and the need to eat again less obvious. A fungus is a smaller organism, so 
this aspect of health might not be relevant. 
Proteins is an important source for amino acids, while a decent level of fat, with a balance 
between trans- and monounsaturated fat is needed to provide the body with the different kinds of 
necessary fatty acids. Fat can also be used as a carbon source but would not be considered 
healthy for the human body if used as a primary source. Other requirements is high amounts of 
vitamins, and minerals as these can affect the enzyme activity and production as well as other 
health factors of the body. A single food product does not fulfill all the requirements, which is 
the reason why humans need a balanced diet to be healthy. Below is an overview over the 
content of the different base materials used for creating media. The levels of nutrient of some of 
the food changes when presented to heat. In these cases, the entry of the boiled food product is 
used. 
The banana is highly consumed fruit, or berry if talking botanically, of the Musa genus. Without 
the peel a raw banana is approximately 75,3 % water and 22,6 % carbon, of which 92,5 % is 
available carbohydrates, of which 33,0 % glucose, 33,4 % fructose, and 20,6 % starch. Raw 
banana contains high amounts of B6 vitamin and potassium, and moderate amounts of vitamin C, 
manganese and fiber. Protein and fat are in insignificant amounts. [3] [27] [59] 
Red oranges, also known as blood oranges and under the scientific name Citrus sinensis, is a 
variant of the ordinary orange with an elevated number of anthocyanins present in the flesh of 
the fruit. The edible part of a raw orange is approximately 86,4 % water and 12,4 % carbon,  
79,6 % of the carbon is available carbohydrates, of which 21,8 % is fructose, 20,3 % glucose and 
36,7 % sucrose. They have high amounts of potassium, ß-carotene, and vitamin C, and decent 
amount of calcium, magnesium and phosphor. Protein and fat are in insignificant amounts. [1] [48]

Fungi: 
This section will provide with a short introduction to the fungi chosen for this specific thesis. 
Each will optimally provide info about where the fungus can be found and prefers to grow, its 
growth on standard media at 25oC, unless stated otherwise, as well as the chemical markers 
characteristic to that specific fungus. Some of these fungi is relatively newly discovered and 
information therefore might be scarce, and a closely related fungus will be chosen as a starting 
point on what to expect from the newly discovered fungus. 
Pen. expansum is a food- and indoor fungus. It prefers pomaceous- and stone fruits, especially 
apples, as well as nuts. It can be found in soil and air too. Regarding the more common media 
types, CYA (3,5-5,0 cm) simulates fast growth and exudate droplets, either hyaline or light 
yellow. YES (5,0-6,5 cm) also stimulates fast growth as well as heavy production of conidium. 
CREA gives good growth and stimulates the acid production, followed by the production of 
base. The fungus prefers low temperatures and have troubles growing at temperatures over 30 
oC. It is known to be tolerate towards low pH and water activities. It is identifiable by its 
chemical markers: Citrinin, chaetoglobosins A, B, C and more, communesins A and B, 
roquefortines C and D, expansolides A and B, and patulin, with patulin being the most important 
mycotoxin regarding this particular fungus. [36] 
Pen. italicum is a food fungus. It prefers citrus fruits as a base for its growth, creating rot in the

stimulates strong sporulation and rapid growth, while CREA creates poor growth and no acid 
production. Pen. italicum is psychrotolerant, meaning it can grow well at very low temperatures. 
It is common in arctic, temperate and subtropical regions. Some isolates are able to grow at 30 
oC, but none can grow at 37 oC. It is identifiable by its chemical markers: Abscisic acid, 
dehydrofulvic acid, deoxybrevianamide E, 2,5-dihydro-4-methoxy-2H-pyran-2-one,

Pen. rubens is highly similar to Pen. chrysogenum, which it mostly shares its morphological

Pen. arizonense is a relatively new discovered fungus and part of the Penicillium section 
Canescentia of which it seems to be most closely related to Penicillium yarmokense, based on 
selected DNA sequences, while looking purely protein allocation it would be A. oryzae. Pen. 
arizonense has been found to increasing amounts of proteins involved with carbohydrate 
metabolism compared to others in its section and would be expected to display heighted ability 
in degrading plant material and other complex carbon sources. Most fungi in this section are 
predominantly in forest litter and soil. The fungus has relatively good growth on CYA (2,8-3,5 
cm), MEA (1,1-2,9 cm), OAT (2-3,4 cm) and YES (2,8-4,2 cm) media, with moderate 
sporulation on the latter. It has poor growth at CREA media alongside no acid production. It 
appears that the fungus has a hard time growing at high temperatures, as no growth is present at 
37 oC. The known chemical markers for Pen. arizonense are austalides, pyripyropenes, 
thyptoquivalines, fumagillin, pseurotin A, xanthoepocin, and curvulinic acid. [15] 
Pen. crustosum is a food- and indoor fungus. It prefers to grow on nuts, cheese and sometimes 
pomaceous- and stone fruits. CYA (3,2-4,6 cm) and YES (4-5,6 cm) both stimulates fast growth, 
while the latter also creates very heavy production of conidium. CREA also gives good growth 
and stimulates the acid production, followed by the production of base. This fungus can grow 
well at very low temperatures, grows poorly at 30 oC, and not at all at 37 oC. It is common in 
arctic, temperate and subtropical regions. It is known to be tolerate towards low pH and water 
activities. Its chemical markers are: Cyclopenin, cyclopenol, cyclopeptin, dehydrocyclopeptin, 
penitrem A and others, thomitrens, roquefortines C, D and E, terrestric acid, viridicatic acid, 
viridicatin, and viridicatol. This fungus most important mycotoxin is penitrem A. [12] [36] 
Pen. antarcticum is a marine-derived fungus, which as the name suggest was originally 
discovered in the Windmill Island off Antarctica, and native to the biodome. The fungus is either 
part of the Canescentia- or the Furcatum section, depending on the parameters used. So far it 
has been extracted from the nests of the Antarctic petrels, Antarctic moss, soil, algal, and 
shellfish. Pen. antarcticum is seemingly closely related to Pen. paxilli and Pen. atrovenetum. 
The fungus has great growth on CYA (3,2-3,5 cm) media, as well as strong sporulation and 
occasionally produce small droplets of clear exudates. Good growth on MEA (3,7-3,9 cm) but 
has weak sporulation. The fungus is very resistant to low temperatures as it can grow moderately 
at 5 oC, while above 30 oC the growth slows down and at 37 oC there are no growth at all.

Identifying chemical markers: 
When samples are analyzed for organic compounds like toxins and secondary metabolites, both 
known and unknown, a method called LC-DAD-MS is most often used. This method consists of 
3 steps, LC an abbreviation for liquid chromatography, DAD short for diode array detector and 
MS, which is short for mass spectrometry. 
There exists different types of LC and the one used for this process is high pressure LC, HPLC. 
While the traditional type of LC is performed under atmospheric pressure,1,01325 bar, HPLC 
uses, as the name implies, high pressure around 400-600 bar of pressure. The increased pressure 
reduces the processing time as well as the amount of sample needed. 
The principle behind LC is to separate the different compounds in a sample by polarity, so it is 
possible to analyze them individually. This is done by pumping a solvent containing the sample, 
a mobile phase, through a column, the stationary phase. The narrower the column, the better 
separation of compounds. 2 types of solvents are used; A polar, water, and a nonpolar, like 
ethanol, as the polarity of the compounds determines which solvent it dissolve in. In the 
traditional method of HPLC the stationary phase is more polar than the liquid phase, being either 
cellulose or silica. The most polar compounds would then, through hydrogen bonding, bind more 
to the column than the solvent, making the least polar compounds move through the column into 
the detector first. As the solvent becomes more polar, some compounds will begin to bind 
stronger to the liquid phase, rather than the stationary. This can be done as either gradient or an 
isocratic elution. The isocratic elution is a fixed concentration and is simple to use, but also has a 
lower resolution, as 2 compounds with close similarity in polarity could be eluted together, also 
called coelution.

However the method used for the sample analysis for this thesis, is not the traditional type of 
HLPC, but a more modern approach known as reverse phase HPLC, RP-HPLC. This method 
reverses the polarities of the phases, making the liquid phase more polar than the stationary 
phase, and increases the reproducibility of results. This is done through derivatization of silica by 
attaching C-18 chains, which are long non-polar chains. 
RP works due to Van De Waals interactions, which are distance-dependent interactions between 
atoms or molecules. These interactions are weak and can easily be disrupted. The long C-chains 
creates a greater surface area, which allows for more interactions, and thereby more separation of 
compounds. RP does require higher pressure to be able to push the solvent through the solid 
phase and are therefore perfect to use in combination with HPLC. 
When getting UV-spectrums using for instance DAD, the 2 solvents need to be chosen outside 
the spectrum of absorption that the compounds normally lies within. The most used solvents for 
RP are water, and either acetonitrile or methanol. The setup for HLPC can be seen in figure 2. [33]

Determination of the health and fitness of a fungus: 
Fitness, in regard to biology, is a term used to describe the survivability of an allele, an 
individual or a group. When it comes to fungi the process of measuring and determining the 
fitness becomes a lot more complicated when compared to the same process with plants or 
animals. This is due to several factors. Where plants and animals have a clear distinction 
between individual organisms and follow one reproductive path, a filament fungus does not. It 
can be hard to limit down what an individual fungus is, as it does not limit itself to a specific area 
for growth. It also has more than one productive cycle, some has genetic mosaics, can self-
fertilize. This complicates the measurement of a precise fitness. An estimated fitness can be 
made by only using 1 relevant parameter. This, in most cases, would be mycelial growth rate, as 
it correlates with the mycelial surface area which has shown to be an important factor in 
predicting numbers of spores a fungus can produce. [35] 
While fitness determines the fungus ability to survive, it doesn’t really touch the health of the 
fungus although the same parameters can be used for the same thing. Health determines whether 
or not the fungus thrives in its place of growth or media. However, it is difficult to determine the 
health based on just one factor. For this thesis 3 factors are taken into consideration, when 
discussing if a fungus is healthy. These factors are based on basic fungal knowledge. They are 
growth, sporulation and production of secondary metabolites. Growth is a very good basic 
determinator whether or not a fungus is healthy, as it would not grow if the media didn’t at least 
partially support the inner workings of the fungus. But that is the pure basal needs, as a fungus 
can grow without sporulate, thereby not being able to spread, and without producing secondary 
metabolites. Pointing to the media supporting growth and not a healthy fungus. 
Under different circumstances a fungus can sporulate without massive growth. This is if the 
media or the environment is not a viable place for growth, the fungus will focus on sporulation in 
order to protect and be able to carry on its genes by creating offspring. A fungus commonly 
produces secondary metabolites under 2 types of situations: If the fungus is a healthy media, 
which supports the production of the metabolites, or if the fungus is growing in a hostile 
environment, which it as to fight against for one or the other reason. So, for a fungus to be 
consider in great health is must have great growth, sporulation and good production of secondary 
metabolites.

Creating agar plates with special media: 
When creating agar plates with special media, the following base recipe is used: 
• 200 g base material, like vegetables. 
• 500 ml water 
• 10 g agar 
• 17,5 g Cz (only in + Cz media)

the base material either very dry, like hazelnuts, or contain a lot of starch like rice. So, for those 
type of material 15 g of flour is created by dry blending the material until it has a flour-like 
consistency, then mixing it with 500 ml water, agar, and possibly Cz. Sometimes the opposite, 
the base material being too watery, can be true, like with milk. For the media to congeal more

Principal component analysis (PCA) 
To get an overview of the data principal component analysis (PCA) was used on the secondary 
metabolite data using the data program UNSCRAMBLER. The dataset used here can be found in 
the excel document named “Appendix VI”. This contains the samples, the metabolites created by 
the fungus, and the logA to the specific metabolite on a specific medium. Abbreviations for the 
metabolites are used. The full names, the abbreviations, and the type of compound for all of the 
metabolites found in all of the samples can be found in appendix VII. PCA is an analytic tool, in 
which a larger dataset with possible correlated variables, reducing dimensions in the variables, 
creating a set of uncorrelated factors.

The first principal component, PC1 is the linear combination of the uncorrelated factors with the 
biggest variance of all the linear combinations. PC1 can account or explain the largest parts of 
the data. The second principal component, PC2 is the linear combination of the uncorrelated 
factors with the biggest variance of the remaining linear combinations, while also being 
perpendicul to PC1. 
In this case the dataset is normalized in order for all of the variables to carry the same weight of 
influence. It is done by using this equation: 
(logA value – average) / standard deviation (1) 
In regard to these datasets PCA can show possible correlations between different metabolites and 
media, which can be observed using a biplot. If the medium point and the metabolite point can 
be placed in the same quadrant on the same line from the new origo, the medium can be 
considered good at producing that metabolite. The further out in quadrant, the more extreme 
medium points lay, the better it seemingly is in producing the metabolite. PCA also produces an 
influence plot, showing which datapoints are most extreme or different from the others in 
general. It also uses and shows a graph of the cross validation, which tells how well the dataset 
can be explained 100%. This is done by removing a datapoint and comparing the correlation to 
the previous calculated one. This cross validation will give a poor score if one or more datapoint 
differs a lot from the rest of the set. These graphs can be found for all for the different fungi in 
appendix VIII.

Unfortunately, sometimes compounds overlap, giving a false logA, the areal beneath the 
spectrum line. For those with significant overlap log to LU can be used to calculate the correct 
number for logA, although sometimes the peak will be so large that it exceeds the scale. Those 
compounds where logA is calculated will be marked with a *, while those unable to be will be 
marked with a +, indicating that it is not a pure UV spectrum and therefore not true logA. 
The numeration symbolizes numbers of variations found of significant value in that specific 
fungus, should the same compound be found in a different fungus, the numeration resets. Known 
already named variations will not have a space between the last letter and number, while 
unnamed variations will. Example named would be A1 and unnamed would be A 1.

Growth comparison: 
Before delving deeper into the comparison, a preface can be made for what it expected to be 
found regarding the growth. On the standard media, CYA, Czapek Yeast extract Agar, and YES, 
Yeast Extract Sucrose agar, or MEA, Malt Extract Agar. The fungi all have quite a span 
regarding what is considered normal growth after 7 days at 25 oC. It is expected that most of 
growth on the special media falls under this span, neither below or exceeding it. 
The media containing pure water and Cz are both excluded from this comparison, as all of them 
fall under the specific spans, having either close to no nutrition or just the bare minimum for the 
fungi to develop some significant amount of growth. 
For fungus 1, Pen. expansum, almost all of the growth on the special media falls in the growth 
span of the CYA standard medium of 3,5-5,0 cm, and does not exceed the growth span of the 
YES medium 5,0-6,5 cm. Taken into consideration that Pen. expansum is primarily a fruit and 
nut growing fungus, it is not surprising that banana, apples, oranges and hazelnut are the media 
with some of the best growths. But it was surprising to see that Pen. expansum, which has been 
descripted as preferring apples, actually shows larger growth on oranges. The 2 fruits are very 
similar nutrition-wise but does differ slightly. Apples contain 2 g/100g more sugar species than 
oranges, while the later has elevated levels of amino acids compared to the former. 
The amino acids that are especially elevated are leucin, lysin, valine, and arginine. The growth 
on pure cod medium could point towards the fungus being able to use amino acids as a carbon 
source. Another unexpected finding was the growth on broccoli, which the fungus seemingly 
grows better on than the fruit media and CYA, having a diameter of 4,4 and 5,3 cm, when grown 
without or with Cz respectively. The broccoli media has approximately half the amount of 
available carbohydrates compared to the orange media, but the levels of leucin, lysin, and valine 
are quadrupled, while the levels of arginine is almost doubled. This could point to amino acids 
being a vital part in determine whether or not Pen. expansum will experience great growth on a 
medium.

Metabolic abnormalities: 
In order to find metabolic abnormalities, the known chemical profile for the individual fungus is 
compared to the chemical profile of the sample. Known metabolites are then excluded and the 
rest examined. It is highly likely that the remaining metabolites fall into one of 3 categories: 
metabolites already that has already been found produced in the fungus but has yet to be included 
in its chemical profile, a precursor of an already known produced compound, or a metabolite 
never found produced by that specific fungus but is found in one or more samples in this row of 
experiments. The last type of metabolic abnormalities is not unable to be explained, but it is out 
of the scope of this thesis.

Conclusion: 
As expected different types of media effect the differentiation of fungi in different ways, and 
each fungus reacts in its own way to the media. Several of the fungi experienced better growth, 
sporulation and production of secondary metabolites, when the media was added Cz. 
This could point to there being certain key components, found in Cz, missing from the pure 
media, or that a certain level of either sugar, or other growth components, need to meet in order 
for the fungus to experience a larger positive effect, an optimal effect, on the differentiation. 
When only Cz was available in the medium, no spores was produced, in contrast to the pure food 
media. This point to the food containing certain trace metals, cobber and zinc, which are required 
for sporulation to take place. 
The pure apple media stimulated very thin mycelium growth, and would not be able to be used 
alone in a media. In contrast both bell pepper and broccoli seemed to be good candidates for 
media, as good mycelium growth, metabolic production and sporulation happened, even without

Perspective: 
For future perspective these results could be used to expand the chemical profile, not only for the 
chosen fungi, but also others, should the experiment be repeated with different fungi. It could 
help fill out missing chemical markers for newly discovered fungi, or those whom are yet to be 
researched in this fashion. And while not in the scope for this thesis, several unknown 
compounds were discovered, these unknown compounds could be identified as to expand the 
knowledge about secondary metabolic production in regard to fungi. Unknown compounds 
always a possibility for untapped potential. 
The bell pepper medium with Cz did show potential for increased amount of phenylahistin in 
Aps. fructus and might in the future be optimized for production of phenylahistin. Meanwhile 
the medium containing both banana and Cz showed that both Pen. rubens and Pen. 
bialowiezense are able to produce n--p, which was surprising. This could also be researched 
further, as to pin down exactly what causes this pathway to activate and bring light to a potential 
production of compounds, that can cure certain threats against the human health. 
It would be interesting to expand the experiment with different types of food products, some 
considered healthy and some considered unhealthy for human consumption in order to see their 
effect on the differentiation of the fungi, and to examine if the hypothesis of ‘if it is healthy for 
humans, it is also healthy for fungi’ holds true.
