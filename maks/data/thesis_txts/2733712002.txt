Abstract
This study investigates the potential of utilizing distributed units with consumption
uncertainty to provide flexibility in the FCR-D ancillary service market. Historically,
market barriers have been too extensive for these units to participate due to their uncer-
tainty; however, newly introduced market regulations seek to accommodate these hin-
drances. The primary focus of the study is developing an optimal bidding strategy for
charge boxes, particularly by exploiting the potential synergy effect of aggregating these
units. Two approximation methods, ALSO-X and CVaR, are used to adapt optimal de-
cisions under the presented uncertainty. These methods allow a tractable mathematical
relationship between the market regulations and the units’ uncertainty.
The study’s findings suggest that charge boxes can not participate individually under
the current market conditions. However, the synergy of aggregating with other units
with similar uncertainty challenges is shown to be a viable strategy. It is found that
aggregating the charge boxes reduces variability in terms of realized flexibility, which
increases predictability and adds more uniformity within the portfolio. This effect is
vital to accommodate the market constraints and thus enable higher bids. It is found
that ALSO-X significantly outperforms CVaR in enabling synergy for flexibility bidding.
Correspondingly, ALSO-X exploits the market to the limits of its conditions, in contrast
to CVaR, which has a more conservative approach. Notably, it is found that neither
method disobeys the regulations. In conclusion, the study determines that aggregating
charge boxes leads to significant synergy, which can be employed in the bidding strategy,
ultimately leading to an increase in the amount of flexibility that can be provided.

1.1
Background
The landscape of the electricity sector is currently going through vast transformations.
From the production perspective, renewables are replacing conventional power plants.
Meanwhile, various sectors are being electrified, which increases the overall demand. An
example is the transport sector, where there is a growing tendency for Electric vehicles
(EVs) to replace fossil fuel-driven vehicles. Such immense changes cause more uncertainty
regarding both the realized production and consumption, which eventually drives the
need for more flexibility in the energy system.
Traditionally, the needed flexibility has been delivered from the production side of the
Production-Consumption Equilibrium [1]. Here, a considerable share of the needed load
balancing has been provided by conventional electricity producers, such as gas turbines
and coal plants. These producers have been able to either increase or decrease their
power output according to the electricity grid’s immediate needs. However, these tech-
nologies are currently being phased out, meaning other dispatchable CO2-neutral tech-
nologies and practices must be incorporated into the grid2. In 2022, Denmark produced
34.42TWh, where 84% came from renewables, but only approximately 25% of the re-
newable production was dispatchable. This dispatchable electricity mainly comes from
imported biomass via Combined heat and power (CHP) plants, whose level of operation
is influenced by the need for heat, meaning their operation becomes seasonal3 [2]. In
the Appendix A.1, a complete overview of Denmark’s electricity production mix can be
found.
Denmark is currently very dependent on electricity imports in periods with little to
no wind and solar energy production and low production from the CHPs. Luckily for
Denmark, their neighboring countries, Sweden and Norway, have massive dispatchable
capacity shares that can produce renewable electricity4. However, the demand for their
dispatchable resources will likely increase as more European countries prioritize the cli-
mate crisis higher on the political agenda. Therefore, Denmark will presumably need
more self-controlled renewable dispatchables on both the production and consumption
sides.
Besides the loss of dispatchability when replacing conventional plants with wind tur-
bines and Photovoltaic (PV) panels, another derivative is a significant reduction of the

1.2
Problem definition and hypotheses
EV chargers are unique because they have a high power consumption compared to other
distributed units. The high power demand can challenge the grid when many EVs charge
simultaneously. On the other hand, the high consumption also presents a significant
opportunity to help the grid, as charging operations could be altered to respond to the
grid’s need, i.e., providing flexibility. Therefore, it is interesting to investigate whether
EVs, through Charging boxes (CBs) can assist the network by offering dispatchable
capacity and thereby partly compensate for the periodic stress they place on the grid.
Consequently, this study aims to quantify household CBs’ dispatchable potential by
analyzing their capability to provide flexibility in the Frequency Containment Reserve

analyzing the flexibility bid into the FCR-D market when aggregating the CBs in different
portfolio sizes. By comparing the results, the study seeks to determine whether a synergy
effect is found. The investigation will present and apply different optimization model
methods.
The study thus also looks into quantifying the abilities of different model
approaches to explore the portfolios’ flexibility potentials. Three research questions are
developed to guide this analysis.

Literature review
The development in the ancillary service market has recently gained more focus in
academia. Overall, it is found that the research can be divided into three categories.
1) Construction of the ancillary service market and new initiatives, 2) the main obsta-
cles participants face when bidding into the market, and 3) how market participants can
realize their potential earnings bidding into the market.
As this report investigates how the synergy effect mitigates potential obstacles while
enabling participants’ bidding potential to a higher degree, the second and third points
are the focus of this literature review.
2.1
Main obstacles
Over the years, studies have exploited the challenges the ancillary service market presents
for market participants. In a 2013 study i.e., with relatively obsolete market conditions,
two critical market barriers were identified for an aggregator with a portfolio of uncertain
consumption devices. Firstly, the paper states that these flexible consumption devices
will have limited energy reservoirs. For example, a heat pump’s operation will be bounded
by the temperature in a room. Secondly, the devices do not have a well-defined baseline,
i.e., the aggregator does not know what the consumption of the flexible devices is in the
future; this is if the devices are not primarily used for bidding into the ancillary service
market [6].
Arguably, the energy reservoir and the baseline uncertainty still need to be addressed
in today’s market. A 2022 study addresses the ecosystem for demand-side flexibility
in the Danish market [7]. It discusses the baseline issue mentioned in [6], stating the
importance for aggregators to forecast their baseline precisely. A precise forecast allows
more of the portfolio’s flexibility to be bid into the market, as much of the flexibility
otherwise would be wasted on tracking the baseline [7]. The current provision of the FCR-
D market allows the baseline to be calculated ex-post for units with baseline uncertainty
based on the realized measurement [8], which mitigates the challenges related to tracking
a baseline in real time. Nevertheless, the argument made in [7] still needs to be revised,
as an erroneous baseline forecast will result in an inaccurate estimation of available
flexibility. This challenge is highlighted by [9]. It showcases that flexibility uncertainty
leads to erroneous flexibility bids for flexible devices whose baseline can not be controlled.
The paper proposes aggregating assets to capture potential synergy amongst flexible
devices, thus allowing for more accurate baseline/flexibility forecasts. The paper scopes a
coalition of synthetically created flexible demands assets bidding in the manual Frequency
Restoration Reserve (mFFR) market. Overall, the paper illustrates a significant synergy
effect by creating a coalition of flexible demands rather than bidding them individually.
[10] identifies the potential of aggregating EVs to participate in the ancillary market.

A Swedish 2021 paper investigates the different revenue streams in the Nordic ancillary

3.1
The FCR-D market
FCR-D is one of the various ancillary services available in East Denmark. It is imple-
mented to handle frequency disturbances and is characterized by a fast response time
and a low activation rate. FCR-D is composed of two markets - upwards regulation
(FCR-D↑) and downwards regulation (FCR-D↓). FCR-D↑is activated in the frequency
ranges of 49.5-49.9Hz and FCR-D↓in the band between 50.1-50.5Hz (see Figure 3).
Entities activated are asked to supply more power or/and reduce consumption when
FCR-D↑is needed. In contrast, entities must decrease power generation or/and increase
consumption when FCR-D↓is activated. The FCR-D product is asymmetrical, meaning
entities participating in the market are allowed to submit unidentical bids in the two
regulation directions.

developed by TSOs in the Nordic area. The FCR-D provisions consist of both technical-

The FCR-D market is a capacity market, meaning participants are compensated for
their reserved capacity and not the activated energy. The compensation is determined
in an hourly auction with a marginal priced scheme settlement7 [13, 14]. The market
requires bids to be uniform within each respective hour, forcing participants to offer the
same flexibility for a full hour. The timeline of the market is illustrated in Figure 4.
It is cleared in two stages with an early and late gate-closure. The first stage, referred
to as ’D-1 early’, is cleared at 00.30 the day before the operation. The second action,
’D-1 late’, is cleared at 18.00 the day before the operation [15]8. As previously scoped,
this study will only consider The D-1 early market. It has been chosen, simply due to
accounting for the largest share of total procurement for the period of concern9 [12].

entity must respond according to the received activation signals. The activation signal

activation is shown in Figure 5 and is linear to the frequency’s deviation from the stan-

When assessing if an activation was delivered properly, the TSO will compare the actual
power delivery against a reference point - often referred to as the baseline. The baseline
is the power operation of the unit as if it was not activated. Hence, a deviation from
this baseline would resemble the frequency response. For fluctuating production and
consumption units, the baseline can be calculated ex-post. This structure is chosen as
the baseline for fluctuating units is uncertain, meaning it is challenging to estimate the
baseline correctly. As explained in the literature, if providing a predefined baseline were
imposed upon these units, it would likely force them to ’waste’ shares of their flexibility
to track a baseline [7]. The ex-post calculation hence allows for a more level playing
field between non- and fluctuations units [8]. However, as the baseline calculation is
performed ex-post, it could incentivize manipulation with the baseline as a participant
could choose to submit an untrue baseline, which has been fitted according to activation,
instead of actually delivering the activation power. To mitigate the potential manipula-
tion issue, market participants must pre-submit a consistent method for calculating the
baseline based on actual measurement to Energinet. An adequate method for calculat-
ing the baseline ex-post for all units included in the study is thus the measured power
consumption.

3.1.2
Prequalification
A set of requirements must be met for an entity to be enabled to participate in the market.
These requirements are tested in the prequalification phase, where numerous tests are
conducted. The requirements can be divided into an operation and bidding category.
The operation phase concerns the activation response, which, for example, could be
testing the portfolio’s response time. As stated in Section 1.3, units are assumed to
be prequalified regarding the operational requirements. Concerning the bidding scheme,
units classified as a fluctuating resource need their bidding scheme prequalification. Units
are classified as a fluctuating resource when the units’ baseline is subject to uncertainty.
Energinet has two qualification criteria for a bidding strategy to be prequalified. Firstly,
the entity should have its full bid capacity available with a frequency of at least 90%,
allowing for an entity to bid with the 10th quantile of its forecasted flexibility. The
frequency is measured across all available timesteps. Thus, the term ”frequency of bids”
is an average measure that is used in this report to put a percentual value on how often
a bid can be fully provided. For this study, the time step resolution is minute-to-minute,
meaning for at least 90% of all minutes, the flexibilities must be equal to or larger
than the bid. The risk should be accommodated for every bid, i.e., the aggregator is

The first

continuously available for a certain period of time.
For conventional units, validating the bids with these conditions is simple. The available
flexibility for a given time step must be equal to or greater than the bid; meanwhile,
the bid must be available for at least two hours. For LER units, the first two conditions
state that an entity must have at least 20% of their respective bid available in the other

and upwards regulation when decreasing the power consumption. As identified in the

Energy flexibility is the maximum downwards flexibility that can be delivered for twenty

maximum power the EV can continuously charge without the energy reservoir becom-

that case, it will not have any energy flexibility unless considered in a portfolio, where
another CB can provide energy flexibility when the CB is not connected. The explicit
mathematical formulation of calculating the flexibilities is described in Appendix B.3.

Objective (1a) maximizes the total hourly capacity bids across both the up and downward
regulation markets to a given day. The bids are indexed hourly as they must be uniform
over the hour’s duration. Please notice that no monetary properties are included in the
model, meaning the strategy does not ensure the decision is financially optimal. This also
implies that there are no distinctions between the two markets; thus, providing 1 unit of
upward regulation holds the same value as providing 1 unit of downward regulation
Constraint (1b) represents the mathematical formulations of Energinets’s bid require-
ments. Here, the three LER constraints presented in Section 3.3 can be seen represented
in a single JCC. A JCC is an extension of the simple hard constraint, which ensures that
a given restriction is always satisfied. The hard constraint can be relaxed by using an
individual chance constraint, which allows for a constraint only to be met with a certain
probability. JCC is extending this concept to asses multiple constraints concurrently.
I.e., a JCC requires the simultaneous satisfaction of multiple uncertain constraints with
a specified probability. The JCC, (1b), thus states that the bids generated under the
market conditions (Left-hand side (LHS)) must be valid at least 1−ϵ of the time (Right-
hand side (RHS)). To comply with the P10 rule, ϵ is set to 0.1, allowing the constraints
to be violated 10% of the time. This can also be phrased as ’the frequency of overbids’
must be lower than 10% comprehensively - hence, the probability of either of the three
LER bid requirements within the probability measure being violated must be equal to
or lower than 10%. Consequently, if just one of the bid constraints is violated, there is
no extra penalty for violating the other two.
The three LER requirements in the probability measure are mathematically formulated
as follows: The first constraint states that the upward flexibility available in a given
scenario (ω) must be greater than the upward bid plus 20% of the downward bid for
all minutes within a given hour. Likewise, the second and third constraints state that

convergence; denoting a lower value implies a higher certainty of convergence yet has an

CVaR
The second approach proposed is the Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR). CVaR was
developed and refined through academic research in the late 1990s and early 2000s and
is built upon the risk measure Value at Risk (VaR)26 [25]. Academia showcases that
CVaR can be used as an approximation method in various contexts, including constraint
approximations of CCPs like the General optimization Model (1).
Thus, the CVaR
approach serves as an approximated reformulation of the decomposed general Model
(5), which formulates the intractable JCC to tractable stochastic LP constraints. Like
ALSO-X, the reformulation includes representing the JCC in a finite space, meaning the
flexibilities are discrete values.
The CVaR method approximates the JCC by controlling how excessive the overbids
can become. Managing the magnitude of overbids is, thus, the initial purpose of the
approximation method.
Conceptually, CVaR controls the magnitude of overbids by
interpreting the input parameters as a distribution, communicating the expectation of
available flexibility.

4.2
Realization algorithm
The models’ decision-making is analyzed by testing the bids in an environment resembling
real life. Algorithm 2 acts as a means to do so, as it realizes the models’ generated bids
on a given sample day. All parameters denoted with an apostrophe (’) are values to be
realized, i.e., without uncertainty, whereas optimized decision variables with an asterisk
(*) refer to an outputted optimal value from the optimization model.
The algorithm receives a sample day, which contains the hourly reservation price and
penalty cost, the minute-to-minute maximum activation in each direction, and the flex-
ibilities of the portfolio of CBs. Subject to these conditions, the bids are realized. It
includes assessing how the bids and the unveiled flexibilities comply with the LER re-
quirements. For each minute, the flexibilities are measured against the bid, allowing
for testing if the full bid could be delivered. Moreover, the flexibilities available are
compared to the asked activation for each minute. This allows quantifying the missed
activation rate. For every hour, the monetary results are found. The maximum activa-
tion not met is penalized based on the given penalty price for that hour. Likewise, the
revenue is found by multiplying the hourly reservation prices with the bids.
The procedure overall allows for deriving the key performance metrics and measure-
ments used to ensure the bids comply with the market requirements. The key metrics
include the profit generated from the day, i.e., the overall earnings and penalty pay-
ments. Moreover, it contains the proportions of the available flexibilities that were bid
into the market, which are derived as the averages across all minutes. The frequency and
magnitude of overbids are also computed, as it allows for an assessment of the validity
of the bid.

4.3
Input data
The data foundation serves as a critical part of an overall methodology. This implies that
selecting fitting data is crucial to obtaining a good-performing model and valid model
output.
Concerning the model inputs, only the aggregated flexibilities of the portfolio are con-
sidered. Developing a method for generating data input to the optimization model thus
becomes a question of how to best represent the flexibilities. A better representation
of the available flexibilities results in more accurate bids. The representation should
foremost allow the model to make bids that comply with market regulations, meaning
the uncertainty and overall expectation should be communicated to the model. No ad-
vanced forecasting methods have been adopted. Instead, historical data are directly used
to generate the input data.
Creating the input data for the realization algorithm is less complex. It simply concerns
passing the correct raw data to the realization algorithm to test the bids in real-life
conditions. As data is received from multiple sources, the primary task is to ensure all
data is synced according to timestamps.
4.3.1
Model input
This section primarily concerns the model inputs to the stochastic models. The inputs
to the Oracle model are simply the flexibilities subject to the day of inspection. For
example, if the Oracle model is to develop bids for the 12th of February, it will receive the
corresponding flexibilities for that day only. The stochastic models ALSO-X and CVaR
also receive CB flexibilities; however, the flexibilities are subject to the day of inspection.
The generations of their inputs are composed of two steps. First, a representation of the
expected flexibilities is developed in the subsequent subsection. Secondly, as the models
are sample-based approaches, the expectation of the available flexibilities needs to be
communicated using a discrete representation. This is obtained using sampling methods
described in the sampling section.
Distribution
A distribution can be used to represent expectations of an event with a range of outcomes
and their associated probabilities. In representing the uncertainty regarding the available
flexibilities of a portfolio of CBs, a distribution is a tool used to inform the model of
the flexibilities probabilistically. Therefore, enabling an optimal decision is subject to
all possible outcomes and their associated probability. The distribution must represent
the portfolio flexibilities suﬀiciently, meaning the procedure of forming the distribution
is critical for the overall performance of the methodology framework.
When creating the distribution, the context of how it is utilized is essential. In this case,

Sampling method
Sampling from the above-described distribution communicates the probabilistic expec-
tation of available flexibility to the model. In this context, it is obtained by selecting a
number of representative samples, which are then used as inputs to the model. An intu-
itive approach would be to extract all samples included in the distribution. However, the
model’s computation time would increase, signifying an apparent trade-off between the
number of samples and the information delivered to the model. Therefore, choosing a
sampling method is a matter of selecting the suitable samples needed to resemble the dis-
tribution best. I.e., an optimal sampling method provides the most accurate information
with as few samples as possible.
Random sampling without replacement is applied, meaning each sample is drawn ran-
domly amongst non-already selected samples. The method removes the probability of
repeats because the distribution is created based on a finite set of distinct samples.
Hence, it ensures diversity amongst the selected samples, leading to a larger portion of
the distribution represented in the final set of samples. If there are enough samples, this
is preferable to sampling with replacement, as fewer samples will be needed to resemble
the distribution, thus lowering the computation time.
Choosing the number of samples needed to be drawn is a question of the model’s use
case, as academia explores different samples needed depending on the model type. As the
stochastic models (ALSO-X and CVaR) are approximations of the decomposed general
formulation holding the JCC, the number of samples has to be derived based on that
formulation (Model (5)). A violation of that JCC results in the exclusion penalty, mean-
ing the constraint must never be violated. Therefore, the model must produce decisions
that also ensure feasibility in OOS results. Following [26], an explicit minimum sample
size is needed to obtain feasible results in OOS tests. The number of samples needed
can be obtained using Formula (Q1). The formula is not usable for all problem classes.

Conceptually, various aspects need to be addressed when evaluating an overall method-

is a distinct evaluation procedure for the benchmark model (Oracle) and the stochastic

4.4.1
Stochastic evaluation
The relationship between the bids generated and the input to the realization algorithm is
critical when addressing the stochastic evaluation process. As mentioned, the realization
algorithm receives all the data input for a given sample day and the 24 generated bids
each associated with a given hour.
The stochastic models generating the bids only
consider the available flexibilities for the portfolio. I.e., the only common denominator
is the input CB flexibilities, implying the issuing of flexibilities used in the model and
the realization algorithm needs to be handled while assuring no data leakage.
Any bid must be created according to the expectations of the flexibilities for the day.
However, as earlier described, the flexibility parameters going in the model (F ↑, F ↓, F E)
are generated by randomly drawing Ωsamples from the distribution. I.e., neither the
assembling of the distribution nor the sampling considers which exact day the model
bids are to be used. Consequently, the bids are unconditional with the assessment day,
inferring that the model’s decision is universally useable and, therefore, applicable to all
days.
The model’s generated bids potentially hold some variability conditional on the random-
ness in the sampling process. It could motivate creating new bids for each day, allowing
for more robustness in the results30. However, the sampling process is built to ensure a
representative set of samples, implying that the bids are robust in themselves. Therefore,
bids are applied to multiple days, as it is preferred to limit the model execution count
as it can reduce the computation time of the simulations while preserving performance.
Repercussions are introduced to ensure that model bids can not be partially based on
the flexibilities of the day for which it creates bids. It is ensured by using a k-fold cross-
validation. An example of a simulation process where k-fold cross-validation is applied is
illustrated in Figure 17. Here, k is set to 3, meaning all historical data of the flexibilities
is divided randomly into three sets, each holding 121 days of data. Two of the folds are
used to assemble the distributions, meaning the distribution will be based on 242 days31.

6
Discussion
This section discusses this study’s findings concerning the initial objectives outlined in
the introduction. The research questions mainly concerned how the introduced market
regulations could leverage more fluctuating resources to participate in the FCR-D market.
Accordingly, the study aimed to explore the potential synergy of aggregating fluctuating
units. The investigation was built upon two consecutive hypotheses. These were that
aggregation would facilitate more uniformity and allow for higher predictability of the
flexibilities. Based on the findings, two overall themes for discussion are identified - the
synergy effect in the context of market regulation and how linear programming can be
applied to harvest the synergy optimally. The themes are discussed and contextualized
in the subsequent sections by providing recommendations from the perspectives of the
market operator and the participating entities.
6.1
Market regulations and synergy
The results show that the introduced regulations significantly enhance fluctuating re-
sources to participate in the FCR-D market. Had the P10 rule not been introduced,
implicitly causing a requirement of 100% certainty of deliverance, none of the portfolios
would have been able to participate in the market. The market provision still limits
fluctuating resources, as only a subset of portfolio flexibility can be bid. However, when
aggregating uncertain units (illustrated with CBs in this study), the bids increase signif-
icantly, showcasing the synergy effect.
Initially, two hypotheses showcased why synergy is being enabled when aggregating.
Firstly, the hypothesis stating that the predictability of flexibility increases by aggrega-
tion is demonstrated to be true. It can be quantified using the discrepancy between the
results obtained by the Oracle and Stochastic method frameworks. The difference can be
interpreted as the value between perfect predictability and the uncertain predictability
produced in stochastic methodology. I.e., as this discrepancy decreases as the portfolio
sizes become larger, it must refer to the stochastic methods leveraging more predictable
flexibilities. Addressing the second hypothesis, it is shown that the flexibility becomes
more uniform upon aggregation, which significantly influences the synergy. This can
be derived from the increased bids obtained by the Oracle method. Accordingly, the
only parameter that can increase the percentage of flexibility bid of the Oracle model is
the flexibility’s uniformity. Also illustrating the importance of adding uniformity to the
portfolio is the few portfolio increases that slightly reduced the percentage of flexibility
used from CB1 (see Figure 22). Consequently, aggregating CBs with exactly the same
charging pattern would not leverage any synergy. Nonetheless, the general observation
is that portfolios of CBs’ have enough heterogeneity to obtain a significant synergy by
aggregation.

Methods considerations
The study demonstrates how different method frameworks can leverage the synergy. The
Oracle methodology served as a theoretical benchmark and revealed a significant discrep-
ancy compared to the stochastic methodologies. Concerning the stochastic models, the
results indicate that ALSO-X obtains a higher profit as it can expose more flexibility to
the market.
The results align with the expectation, as ALSO-X directly controls the frequency of
overbid, which facilitates the model to utilize the full 10% overbid allowance. On the
contrary, CVaR indirectly controls the overbid frequency by constraining the absolute
magnitude of overbids.
Overall, this leads to CVaR making more conservative bids.
However, it is found that its conservatism does not directly translate to the relative
magnitude of overbids as CVaR generally missed a larger percentual subset of its bids
when overbidding. Seemingly, this contradicts the nature of the model approach. Here,
it is essential to note the difference between what CVaR regulates and what Energinet
defines as the magnitude of overbids. Again, CVaR controls the absolute sum of overbids,
whereas Energinet’s requirements concern the percentage of flexibility missing compared
to the submitted bids. I.e., CVaR’s control of overbids does not directly translate to
Energinet provisions. Consequently, for every overbid made, the percentage difference
between the bid and flexibility available will be larger for CVaR, even though the absolute
overbids have become significantly smaller for the worst scenarios.
These results are supported by existing evidence of CVaR producing conservative out-
comes, which lowers the overbid frequency, thus providing higher supply security com-
pared to ALSO-X. These results are obtained from 363 simulated days, which shows the
frequency of overbidding for the independent days varies greatly for ALSO-X. Hence, if
only a subset of days were audited, it could result in ALSO-X going beyond the 15%
threshold limit, which would exclude the entity. CVaR provides more certainty, as only
a few days go beyond the 15% threshold. On the other hand, ALSO-X leverages more
of the portfolio’s flexibility to be exposed to the market. Hence, there appears to be a
trade-off between the optimization models. Consequently, the final decision depends on
the perspective of the user. Presumably, Energinet believes that the P10 threshold at

7
Conclusion
This study has explored how market regulations facilitate fluctuating units to participate
in the ancillary service market by quantifying an optimal bidding strategy. In particular,
exploring the potential synergy when aggregating the uncertain units in bigger portfo-
lios served as a primary objective. The study sought to investigate the appliance of
mathematical approximation methods to relate these market conditions to the uncertain
flexibility of CBs with the aim of finding the optimal strategy for market participation
and enabling synergy. Overall, the objective was to thoroughly assess distributed units’
potential to provide flexibility to the FCR-D market.
A method framework was developed to facilitate the analysis. It included three cen-
tral components: Input data generation, Model development, and Realization.
The
framework allowed for simulating the behavior of various portfolios of CBs bidding their
flexibility into the FCR-D market.
Relating the simulation findings to the specific research questions allows conclusions
to be stated for the individual aspects of the objective.
On an individual level, the
market barriers are still too extensive for CBs to bid their flexibility. This is showcased
by CB1 individually not being able to bid any of its flexibility when operating alone.
Hence, a strategy to facilitate its flexibility in the FCR-D market is to collate with other
distributed units with uncertain baselines.
The aggregation has two derivatives, which were hypothesized. It will cause less vari-
ability in the distributions representing the expectations of available flexibility, meaning
the predictivity increases. Moreover, it increases the uniformity of portfolios’ flexibility,
which mitigates the challenges enforced by the LER requirements and provision of in-
variant bids within hours. The findings yield both hypotheses to hold. The decreased
discrepancy between the Stochastic and Oracle methods framework results emphasizes
the first hypothesis. The fact that the Oracle methodology improves upon aggregation
shows the significance of the second hypothesis.
Overall, aggregating CBs enables a significant synergy, leading to more of the portfo-
lios’ flexibilities being bid and increasing profits. For the stochastic methodologies, the
improvements from the synergy effect grow the most when portfolio sizes are increased
from around 50 to 100 CBs. Especially for these steps, the distributions begin to be-
come tighter while the uniformity increases significantly. Hereafter, a diminished rate of
return is obtained. However, there is no saturation point, though the results highlight
the importance of adding uniformity to the portfolio.
The approximation method ALSO-X was superior to CVaR in enabling the synergy
regarding the amount of flexibility bid. Concerning the overbids, the results showed that
ALSO-X exploited the P10 regulation entirely, as it consistently produced a frequency

supply-security. Although the methods are tailored to a portfolio of CBs, they can be
generalized to other distributed fluctuating units.
This study’s findings fill the identified gap in the literature, namely how the market
initiatives leverage fluctuating resources to participate in the market. Furthermore, the
study also highlights new areas to research. From Energinet’s perspective, reviewing
whether the LER requirements can be relaxed to some extent is critical, as they are
found to block immense flexibility from being bid. From the entities’ perspective, future
research should focus on modifying the bidding scheme to develop daily bids subject to
advanced probabilistic forecasting methods.

B.1
LER Operation
The new FCR-D market has introduced LER (limited energy reservoirs) units to mitigate
these endurance issues. Units are classified as LER if their energy reservoir is less than
or equal to the energy delivered by two hours of full activation[16].
To mitigate issues regarding the energy reservoir, there are, along with the implemented
bidding constraints, enforced operation actions that need to be taken if a unit is classified
as a LER. If LER units reach critical energy reservoir levels, action has to be taken to
bring the energy content back to an acceptable level. In general, the energy level can
categorized as critical when there is not enough capacity available in the reservoir to
deliver the aforementioned total capacity for 20 minutes. Depending on the state of grid
frequency, two states can be initiated;
• NEM: This operation is activated when the frequency is between 49.9 Hz and 50.1
Hz, i.e., when the units are not activated. It allows the units to deviate from the
The synergy effect of aggregating distributed units for FCR-D bidding optimization
75
==PAGE:88==
B
Problem description
baseline to restore the system’s energy level if the current energy level does not
allow full continuous activation for 20 minutes. Yet, if the frequency falls outside
the non-activated bond, the units once again must deliver the bid capacity to the
TSO.
• AEM: It’s an operation that can be activated when the frequency is in the band
for activation. Hence, it happens when the energy level is almost saturated due
to activation. AEM allows the units to slowly deactivate their frequency response,
which permits a slow out-phasing of the response rather than a sudden stop in
deliverance. AEM can be activated when the energy level only allows 5 minutes of
complete capacity deliverance[16].
The bidding constraint of 20% of the downwards bid must be reserved in the upwards
flexibility, is to ensure that NEM and AEM can be activated. Suppose the portfolio’s
SoC becomes close to a saturation point. In that case, NEM or AEM should be acti-
vated, which allows for the SoC to be re-established so the downward flexibility can be
delivered again. The upward flexibility used to restore the SoC can not be bid, as the
portfolio needs to deliver flexibility consistently. For example, if NEM is activated and
the frequency drops, the portfolio still needs to supply its upward flexibility.

D.1
Sample size
This section will argue why Formula (Q1) is the correct method for finding the number
of samples, which is equivalent to arguing that the Problem (5) belongs to the class of
problems stated below. Following [26], the class of problems belonging to Problem (Q2),
should use Formula (Q1) to find the minimum number of samples:
min{f(x) : x ∈X, Pr{G(x, ξ) ≤0} ≥1 −ϵ}
(Q2)
where X ⊂Rn, f : Rn →R, and G : Rn × Rd →RM.
The denotation should be
interpreted as a problem that minimizes a function f(x), where the arguments are the
decision variables. These decision variables are constrained over probabilistic criteria,
stating that some condition must hold with at least a probability of 1 −ϵ. Now, the
random variable ξ is an element in a subset denoted Rd which is d-dimensional space.
Hence, ξ ⊆Rd implies that the random variable ξ can take values in a d-dimensional real
space. f : Rn →R means that the objective function, f(x), takes values in the Rn space,
which is then mapped into real numbers. Finally, the expression G : Rn × Rd →RM
infer that for all specific constraint functions F, the production space of Rn × Rd maps
into the M dimensional space of real numbers.
Referring to the JCC program formulated in Model (5), the condition above is not
violated, and thus, the problem must belong to the class of problems in Problem (Q2).
Firstly, the random parameter F in Model (5) can only take values in real space. Also,
there is no multiplication of space in Model (5) as the random variables are not multiplied
with the decision variables, yet the space for the decision variables is within R. The same
holds for the expression concerning G.
