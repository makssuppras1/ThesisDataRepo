Abstract 
Introduction: Tissue engineering is a major field within regenerative medicine, oriented 
towards developing biological constructs for repairing or replacing pathologically 
afflicted tissue components. Biomaterials, cells and active molecules are principal 
constituents for these constructs and their selection is important towards the end-
product properties. Scaffold-based three-dimensional tissue models are widely explored, 
with hydrogels being a commonly used matrix material, especially those originating from 
natural polymers. Structural enhancement may derive from the inclusion of 
nanomaterials, such as nanoclays. The method these constructs are fabricated and 
handled asserts a crucial role in their features, requiring exploration through 
characterization techniques. Imaging is a frequently utilized field for studying tissue 
models. In particular, when imaging relies upon the sample’s inherent properties without 
altering its structure, it can be beneficial for longitudinal imaging. A modality that offers 
video-rate imaging with micrometer resolution and penetration of a few millimeters is 
Optical Coherence Tomography. Optical coherence tomography offers the potential for 
inner structure visualization and optical properties’ probing.  
Materials and Methods: The scope of this project is to study how optical properties are 
affected by degradation, cryopreservation and fixation of biological constructs aimed to 
be applied in vascular tissue regeneration. The samples examined were biofabricated 
using 1% gellan gum, 1% laponite and Wharton’s Jelly mesenchymal stem cells. The 
constructs are bulk cylinders and the time-points of interest include days 0, 7, 14, 21, 28 
and 35. Spectral domain optical coherence tomography with a central wavelength of 1310 
nm is utilized to visualize sample structure and morphology. Scattering coefficient 
alterations, as part of their optical properties, is simulated using a single scattering model. 
The samples are compared against their cell-free counterparts. 
Research findings & Analysis: For both sample types (cell-encapsulated and cell-free) a 
porous structure is observed, which is attributed to cryopreservation. Another notable 
difference regards the pore size and appearance. Specifically, for fixed samples in many 
instances, the pores appear smaller and their boundaries are well-defined, whereas this 
is not the case for non-fixed ones. A feature probably caused by fixation as it holds the 
material together. Sample surface also presents some irregularities with completely flat 
surface being observed in a few examples, something that might have resulted from 
freezing and thawing. Scattering coefficient values exhibit a significant degree of 
fluctuation and inconsistent trends in relation to the time-points and fixation conditions. 
This effect might have arisen because of the combined effect of cryopreservation, 
degradation and fixation.   
Conclusion:  Optical coherence tomography is instrumental in conveying the sample 
morphology with detail and satisfactory contrast. The probing of optical properties needs 
further investigation, ideally in a project that examines the outcome of each condition 
individually, before proceeding to evaluate their synergistic impact. It is strongly 
suggested to correlate the consequences observed qualitatively, with the samples’ 
mechanical properties using oscillatory mode shear rheometry, and assess their 
angiogenic capability through multiphoton microscopy.

1. Introduction 
Regenerative medicine (RM), and by extension tissue engineering (TE) constitute a 
multifaceted field. Both focus on ameliorating patients’ lives through research and clinical 
practice by seeking remedies for health issues that influence people’s daily lives. The main 
objective of TE, is the development of tissue components for the reinstatement or 
substitution of impaired or compromised tissues or whole organs. The functionality of 
such tissue constructs, depends on the biomaterials employed, as multiple elements have 
to be combined, these include cell line of interest, scaffold material and active molecules.  
A popular choice for tissue scaffolds is hydrogels, because of their biocompatibility and 
general resemblance to extracellular matrix (ECM), which creates a beneficial 
environment for cell growth and functionality. Natural polymers are typically explored 
for such applications, an example of which is gellan gum. The biofabrication techniques 
employed for biological constructs development have specific requirements in terms of 
structural integrity and mechanical properties, creating a need for matrix reinforcement 
of the hydrogels. This can be accomplished using biocompatible nanomaterials, a type of 
which are nanosilicates, and in particular clays, such as laponite. For the purpose of 
creating a tissue component, a cell line of interest needs to be incorporated. Commonly 
stem cells are a desired cell type owing to their potency and self-renewal. Mesenchymal 
stem cells are a promising prospect for tissue regeneration.  
Prior to clinical applications, these constructs need to be characterized in terms of their 
structure and properties, as well as investigate optimal fabrication conditions. 
Biophotonic imaging serves as a tool for characterization, as it includes a broad range of 
imaging modalities to choose from depending on the application and desired 
characteristics. Optical coherence tomography (OCT) is a cross-sectional and volumetric 
imaging method that offers axial and lateral resolution in the microscale with imaging 
depth reaching up to a few millimeters. OCT uses low energy optical waves to realize 
imaging, it is non-invasive and requires no external labeling, since it benefits from the 
sample’s inherent optical properties as a contrast mechanism.  
This project entails the efforts towards multimodal imaging with OCT and multiphoton 
microscopy of biological constructs, and their correlation with their mechanical 
properties. The biological constructs studied here, are aimed to be applied towards 
vascular regeneration. These were manufactured at the University of Crete, in 
collaboration with the Laboratory of Biomaterials in Tissue Engineering led by prof. Maria 
Chatzinikolaidou and were subsequently studied at Technical University of Denmark in 
the Biophotonic Imaging Laboratory, led by prof. Peter E. Andersen.  
In short, the constructs were fabricated using 1% gellan gum, as scaffold material, 
enhanced with 1% laponite and seeded with Wharton’s Jelly mesenchymal stem cells. Six 
time-points are examined, namely days 0, 7, 14, 21, 28 and 35. The constructs were 
cryopreserved to prevent contamination and biodegradation and were studied under two 
conditions, that of fixation and non-fixation. The fixed samples were aimed for 
multiphoton imaging, whereas the non-fixed samples for shear rheometry. Both 
conditions, are imaged with OCT, and their optical properties are modeled using a single 
scattering model for intensity decay by fitting a linear curve to the logarithm of the OCT 
signal acquired. Several challenges were encountered in all three techniques, and results 
were only yielded from OCT. Nonetheless, suggestions are included and experience 
gained is shared for future research relevant to what was realized during this project. 
The report layout involves the theoretical background for the construct materials in 2. 
Biological constructs, light-tissue interactions in 3. Light – Tissue Interactions and OCT in 
4. Optical Coherence Tomography. The experimental process is detailed in 5.

2. Biological constructs 
The biological constructs studied in my thesis, hereafter referred to as bioinks, were 
developed utilizing laponite (Lap), gellan gum (GG), and Wharton’s Jelly mesenchymal 
stem cells (WJ-MSCs). The control samples used included only Lap and GG and will be 
referred to as inks. In this chapter, I will be expanding on each bioink component, 
documenting their properties and their reported applications. 
 
2.1 Bioinks 
In recent years scientists have directed their efforts towards the development of 
biological constructs, including three dimensional tissue and organ models, which will be 
further employed in a plethora of biomedical applications, namely drug screening, tissue 
engineering and regenerative medicine. One notable example of such constructs, is 
bioinks, which are currently being explored with various biofabrication techniques [1]. 
Bioinks are cell formulations that may incorporate bioactive constituents and 
biomaterials, suitable for utilization in an automated biofabrication technology, as 
outlined by J. Groll et al. in 2019 [2]. The biomaterials employed for bioink fabrication 
must exhibit several key properties to ensure their efficacy towards their applications. 
Primarily, bioactivity is one of the main features of bioinks, since the constructs should 
provide a 3D environment that facilitates cell adhesion, proliferation and differentiation, 
along with other physiological cell activities [3] [4]. Non-toxicity, non-immunogenicity 
and biodegradability are paramount characteristics of the bioink constituents that further 
support the overall cell viability and functionality [1] [4] [5]. These formulations are 
designed to support the intricate processes of biofabrication [6]. Towards that aspect, 
physical properties of the constituents are essential. These include printability, high 
structural stability, processability and insolubility in culture media. Such characteristics 
are vital for the biological constructs’ fabrication, as these have to sustain their shape 
during and post processing for a dedicated period of time, as well as maintaining a 
protected 3D architecture for the cells to mature towards the desired tissue type [1] [4]. 
Biomaterial availability, cost-effectiveness and industrial scale-up potential need to be 
taken into consideration from a financial perspective when researching for suitable 
materials [1] [5]. 
Scaffold-based and scaffold-free bioinks constitute two main categories, under which 
most of the investigated formulations fall [1]. A suitable candidate belongs to the first 
category, namely hydrogels.  
Previous research has documented the usage of hydrogels in a variety of biomedical 
applications, including contact lenses, wound dressings and drug screening [1]. Owing to 
their physical properties, hydrogels represent a popular material choice for bioinks. Their 
mechanical properties, such as being elastomers and able to retain their shape enable 
manufacturing processes used in tissue engineering [7]. Another beneficial feature is 
hydrogel porosity, as it facilitates nutrients, oxygen and metabolic waste transportation 
within their structure, a fact that is important for cell viability, when these are embedded 
in their matrix [1] [7]. Their swelling ratio contributes to their biocompatibility, thereby 
enhancing their usage for cell encapsulation [1]. Additionally, one remarkable 
characteristic is the optimal three-dimensional environment that supports cell migration 
and physiological activity [1]. Hydrogels can be of natural or synthetic origin, depending 
on the type of the polymeric material used for the matrix [1].  
Natural polymers are more desirable over synthetic ones, as they mimic macromolecules 
that are already present in the human species, thus minimizing the risk of triggering an 
immunological response [7] [8] [9] [10]. Such macromolecules are a type of biopolymers 
called polysaccharides [11]. Polysaccharides and their derivatives have gained a lot of 
attention in recent years as biomaterials used for scaffold-based bioinks. Their popularity

2.1.1 Gellan Gum 
The repeating unit of gellan gum consists approximately of 60% glucose, 20% glucuronic 
acid and 20% rhamnose, along with two acyl groups, acetate and glyterate. GG is known 
for creating thermally reversible gels that present exceptional stability and significant gel 
strength. It comes in two forms depending on the presence of acyl. In the low acyl form, it 
produces brittle gels, whereas in that of high acyl, it creates transparent, soft, elastic and 
flexible gels [14] [15] [16]. The moderate processing conditions and the mechanical 
features GG demonstrates, are similar to soft tissues [17]. Owing to the soft nature of the 
produced gels with GG, the polymer often needs to be combined with other substances to 
obtain the application desired mechanical properties [12] [18]. It has been approved for 
usage as a food additive in many products, either acting as a stabilizer, thickener or gelling 
agent [16] [19].   
The presence of cations is crucial for achieving structural stability in gels [15] [16]. The 
type and concentration of cations significantly influence the gelation process in GG 
suspensions as it is noted in the literature review conducted by Oliveira J.T. et al. [15]. In 
addition to cations’ presence, their valency also bears a significant effect on the gelation 
properties. Fiorica G. et al. highlights that divalent cations improve the gelation process 
more effectively than monovalent ones [15] [16] [20]. The specific nature of the cations 
impacts the rheological properties of the GG suspensions in a similar manner, with K+ 
having a more pronounced effect than Na+, and Ca2+ having an enhanced effect than Mg2+ 
[15]. 
The processing versatility of GG serves as a further distinctive feature, as by modifying 
temperature and pH, specific and customized structures can be formed. This flexibility 
enables its application in various biomedical fields, including cell encapsulation [15]. Choi 
J.H. et al. reported that GG hydrogels are non-cytotoxic, as demonstrated in previous 
degradation studies. In their work, the suitability of gellan gum for TERM applications is

In general, nanoclays are nanoparticles of clay minerals, with at least one dimension

3.1 Absorption  
Absorption in biological tissues primarily occurs due to light interacting with water and 
other macromolecules, such as proteins and chromophores. This phenomenon starts with 
excitation, where an electron is transferred to an elevated state, from its ground one, upon 
the absorption of a photon with sufficient energy. Returning to its ground state, the 
electron follows either radiative or non-radiative relaxation. Non-radiative relaxation 
entails the return and the absorbed energy is subsequently dissipated with heat emission. 
Alternatively, radiative relaxation involves the emission of a photon with less energy than 
the one absorbed, a process that describes the general principle behind phosphorescence 
and fluorescence [42] [43] [45].

3.2 Scattering 
Scattering portrays the phenomenon of light being deflected from its original path, due to 
travelling through a medium with localized refractive index differences. Tissue is an 
example of a medium with the aforementioned optical variations. Photon energy 
exchange further classifies scattering into being an elastic or inelastic process, with the 
latter describing the existence of energy exchange and the former referring to the photon 
energy exchange being zero. Elastic scattering is further categorized according to the 
relation of the scatterers’ size and the incident wavelength. The two main theories that 
describe that relation are Rayleigh and Lorentz-Mie. Lorentz-Mie theory is regarded in 
biophotonic imaging, as it explains scattering, when the incident beam wavelength is 
comparable or smaller than the scatterers’ size [42] [43] [45] [46] [48].

4. Optical Coherence Tomography 
Optical coherence tomography (OCT) is an imaging modality that facilitates  tomographic 
and volumetric visualization of sample structure and morphology. The basis for OCT 
imaging is the probing of how light is backscattered from sample surface and internal 
structure. The wavelengths within which OCT operates belong to the VIS and NIR regions 
of the EM radiation spectrum, with NIR typically being utilized. Contrast in OCT stems 
from the inherent scattering properties of the samples, and as such, there is no 
requirement for external staining or use of contrast media, thereby providing the label-
free feature of OCT. In general, OCT does not induce any structural or chemical changes in 
the samples, thus maintaining them intact, rendering OCT a non-destructive technique. In 
Table 2, the general benefits OCT provides are included. Overall, the term “Optical 
Coherence Tomography”, summarizes the characteristics of the technique. Specifically, 
Optical refers to its use of light as a source of illumination, Coherence reflects its basis on 
the property of light for maintaining a constant phase difference, and Tomography 
highlights its feature for providing cross-sectional imaging [52] [53] [54] [55] [56] [57].

4.1 Working principle 
Low-coherence interferometry is the underlying technique for OCT, following the 
foundational principles of the Michelson interferometer. Imaging with OCT is 
accomplished through the measurement of optical path length delays in the sample. A 
graphic representation of its working principle is shown in Figure 5. Interferometry 
involves the following main steps:  
1. A light beam is emitted from a light source, and passes through a beam splitter. 
2. The beam is divided in two parts: 
 One part serves as the reference beam, which travels through the reference 
arm onto a reference mirror with a known path length, and 
 The other part is the sample beam, which travels along the sample arm and 
is directed on the sample.  
3. The two beam parts are directed back to the beam splitter post-interaction with 
their targets. Their interference signal is routed towards the detector.   
As stated in a previous literature review by Petridi I. “in low-coherent interferometry and 
by extension in OCT, interference can only occur, when the mismatch of the optical path 
lengths of both arms happens within the coherence length of the light source” [7] [60] 
[61] [62].

5. Experimental Procedure 
5.1 Sample preparation & Handling 
The biomaterials (inks) and the bioinks with encapsulated Wharton’s jelly mesenchymal 
stem cells (WJ-MSCs) [80] have been prepared in the Laboratory of Biomaterials in Tissue 
Engineering at the Department of Materials Science and Engineering at the University of 
Crete led by Prof. Maria Chatzinikolaidou. In brief, the preparation of Lap-GG hydrogels 
involves incorporating laponite powder (Laponite XLG, BYK Additives & Instruments, UK) 
dispersed in deionized water at concentration of 1% w/v. The laponite suspensions were 
subsequently stirred overnight at room temperature. Gellan gum (Gelzan, G1910, Sigma, 
UK) was included at a final concentration of 1% w/v and dissolved under thermal stirring 
at 50°C for 3 h, resulting in the formation of nanocomposite hydrogels (NC gels). Bulk 
cylindrical samples were then biofabricated via casting into 2x48 well-plates. The bioinks, 
consisting of the cell suspension and the NC gel, were loaded into a syringe, yielding a 
uniform bioink with a cell concentration of 10 × 106 cells/ml. A post-casting crosslinking 
step was applied using a 4% w/v KCl solution to enhance structural stability. 
In addition to the differences between control inks and bioinks, the samples were 
differentiated further with producing both fixed and non-fixed samples for each time-
point. The fixation process included two key steps: initially samples were rinsed three 
times in phosphate buffer saline (PBS), followed by immersion in freshly prepared 4% 
paraformaldehyde (PFA) solution for 45 minutes.  
Post sample formulation, the samples were kept in culture conditions for a total of 35 
days. In this study, six time-points are examined, namely days 0, 7, 14, 21, 28 and 35, to 
monitor possible changes in the bioinks every week. At each time-point, the respective 
sample was extracted from culture conditions, placed in a well-plate and stored in the 
freezer, to avoid potential contamination. Once 35 days had passed, the samples were 
shipped to Technical University of Denmark with an icepack and were subsequently 
stored at -20 ℃, before the experimental procedure started. Afterwards, they were 
removed from the freezer and maintained in the refrigerator at 4 ℃ to thaw and be 
preserved while the experimental part was conducted.  
Prior to imaging with OCT, samples were consistently handled with gloves and in a 
separate room from the imaging laboratory. Before commencing handling procedures, the 
workbench and tools were disinfected with 70% ethanol. To maintain sample hydration, 
PBS was added. During transportation from the sample handling room to the imaging 
room, the well-plates were always covered with their lid. The lid was removed only during 
the imaging sessions. Table 5 and Table 6 offer the outline of the sample compositions 
and characteristics.

towards the sample and post light-sample interaction, the back-scattered interference is 
routed to the spectrometer. The spectrometer analyses the interference signal and 
displays the preferred scan type – A-scan, B-scan or 3D scan – on the monitor [81]. 
 
Image reconstruction relies on proprietary software developed by Thorlabs, Inc. Once the 
spectrometer’s camera captures a spectrum, pre-processing is performed with the aim of 
acquiring the A-scan. Initially, error and background mitigation steps are implemented, 
followed by data filtering within the framework of apodization. The final step involves 
image formation via Fast Fourier Transformation (FFT) and computing the absolute value 
of the complex data, which further on yields depth information. Typically, data is stored 
in decibels (dB), calculated by taking the base-10 logarithm of data amplitude and 
applying a multiplication with a factor of 20. The following formula describes the relation 
between the intensity values and the amplitude of the OCT signal, based on the 
preliminary processing implemented by the system itself:  
 
𝐼= 20 log(|𝐴|) 
 
False or gray-scale color bar is applied for image presentation depending on the dB values 
of the data [82].  
 
Utilizing a wavelength centered at 1310 nm (NIR) enables adequate penetration depth, 
minimizes the risk of illumination light being absorbed by tissue components, and 
reduces losses due to scattering [81].

5.3 Imaging Protocol 
The formulation of the imaging protocol employed in OCT was informed on prior 
experience with similar samples [7]. Protocol definition included the establishment of the 
following parameters: image acquisition speed, pixel size, field of view and refractive 
index. 
Since the bioinks and the inks were previously found to be weak scattering samples, the 
acquisition speed that was selected, was at 28 kHz, also referred to as Video Rate mode. 
Considering that the samples mimic human tissue in composition and intended 
application, I decided to utilize a refractive index of 1.33, a value frequently encountered 
in comparable sample analyses, as it refers to the refractive index (RI) of water at 20 ℃ 
for visible light.  
Adhering to the Nyquist criterion and utilizing the enhanced resolution OCT offers, for the 
pixel size I opted for 2.50 μm in the lateral axes (x-y) [84] [85] [86]. The respective value 
for the depth axis (z-axis) is set by the system and remains fixed. In my case, this was at 
2.64 μm.  
For each sample, five images were acquired following a clockwise star-shape scheme, 
with the aim of retaining the ability to image most of the sample’s volume in a timely and 
efficient manner, while securing a competent amount of images for further statistical 
analysis. Each B-scan would commence from the central region of the sample progressing 
outward towards the periphery. A visual representation is depicted in Figure 9. 
 
 
Figure 9: A visualization of the imaging acquisition shape. The sample in the monitor image is a fixed bioink of 
D35.

samples are cylindrical with a fixed diameter of 6 mm, it was determined that the size of 
the lateral scanning direction (x-axis) should be 2.50 mm. The depth of the imaging was 
the maximum given by the system at 2.69 mm.  
The focus was placed at 1 mm in depth, while it was decoupled from the sample surface, 
which was mainly maintained at approximately 0.80 mm. Given that the sample surface

5.4.2 Conversion from digital to real dimensions 
Before proceeding further, it is necessary to convert the pixel values to millimeters. This 
achieves an appropriate representation of the real dimensions of the samples. It is 
important to note that this conversion also entails a rescaling factor, necessitated by the 
use of a refractive index of 1.33.  
5.4.3 Preprocessing: Maximum Intensity Projection 
Due to the nature of the samples, which exhibited enlarged pores, it was determined that 
a pre-treatment step was necessary, specifically involving Maximum Intensity Projection 
(MIP). Initially, MIP was intended to be applied to a lateral region of interest (ROI) that 
would have a size slightly larger than the pore size itself, to ensure that signal intensities 
were captured throughout the depth axis. However, this approach lead to cropping the 
image to nearly half its original size. Consequently, I decided to proceed with each B-scan 
in its entirety and discard the usage of lateral ROIs instead. The MIP applied to each B-
scan extracts the maximum intensity for each lateral position, generating a representative 
A-scan by aggregating the processed data from the B-scan.  
5.4.4 Corrections 
When implementing the SS model, there is a requirement to incorporate two correctional 
steps, originating from systemic effects. The first step is to account for the signal fall-off 
with depth propagation, a correction referred to as sensitivity roll-off. Essentially, this 
correction reflects the intensity attenuation the beam is subjected to as the depth 
increases, in other words it represents the non-constant character of the detection 
sensitivity for the imaging depth. It is a progressive loss in sensitivity present in every FD-
OCT system, which is caused by signal amplitude reduction and finite sampling [66] [87] 
[72]. In Zimmer M. thesis, it was determined that the scanning speed influences the 
sensitivity loss and that could be reflected on a parameter involved in modeling the 
sensitivity roll-off, namely the s parameter [66]. In A. Sensitivity Roll-off Characterization 
the experimental procedure followed for s parameter determination is described. 
The second correction is attributed to the confocal effect, which is generally termed as 
confocal point spread function (CPSF).  The CPSF describes the error introduced to the 
magnitude of the OCT signal because of the axial point spread function (PSF) [66] [7] [88]. 
The equation used for the CPSF correction was extracted from [66]. 
5.4.5 Filtering 
As Gong P. et al. highlighted, averaging or digital filtering is a fundamental step, with the 
aim of reducing speckle variations and noise control [46]. In my previous work, with 
similar samples investigation, I had opted for lateral averaging in a specific region of 
interest [7]. Here, since preprocessing included applying MIP on the entirety of each B-
scan, I decided to implement axial filtering instead. There are several filters that can be 
utilized for this purpose, which have been reviewed in the works of Sheeba T.M. and Raj 
S. A. A., Gopi T. and Bi G., Xu Z. Yi Y. and Xin S. X. and refer to filtering the entire OCT image, 
rather than a depth scan [89] [90] [91]. Prompted by these works, I determined which 
filter works best by testing three commonly used ones and referenced in the 
aforementioned publications, namely Gaussian, Mean and Median filters. To evaluate 
their performance and find the optimum kernel (window size), I examined kernel sizes 
starting from 3 towards 49, including only odd-sized ones and based their selection on 
their root-mean squared error (RMSE) variation, setting a threshold for RMSE difference 
==PAGE:42==
 
 
24 
 
between kernel sizes of 2%. Threshold selection was rooted on the empirical values 
suggested, which stated that commonly, threshold is set from 1% to 5% [92]. This step 
was to find, where the difference between the consecutive RMSEs stabilizes, thereby 
hinting on which kernel size is optimal for each filter examined. It was concluded that 
Median with kernel size of 35 was the best choice. Thus, I filtered axially all the MIP 
derived A-scans from each of the five B-scans of every sample with a median filter. The 
results from the filtering selection can be found in B. Filter Selection & Optimal kernel size 
determination. 
5.4.6 Axial Region of Interest 
The region of interest (ROI) to implement fitting on was informed based on the sample 
morphology, the focus set during imaging and the overall sample appearance in the OCT 
images. Since, the focus was set at 1 mm depth and the sample surface was a bit shallower 
ranging from 0.75 to 0.85 mm, the ROI should initiate after the focus, to avoid bias in the 
fitting process, as the highest intensity would come that point [66]. Hence, ROI start was 
set at 1.05 mm. The ROI length was determined at 0.53 mm, as decided by visually 
inspecting each B-scan, where I found that in this region, there was sample material to 
ensure that the intensity values would correspond to backscattering from sample’s inner 
structure. I chose to work with a stable ROI for each sample, the reason being that the 
samples were fabricated in a way such that, they would present similar dimensional 
features.  
5.4.7 Fitting 
Linear curve fitting was implemented in accordance to my previous work on the 
logarithm of the OCT signal and based upon the form in which the signal is stored from 
the manufacturer of the OCT system [7] [82]. The fitting process resulted in five scattering 
coefficient values per sample, which were then stored and statistical processing was 
performed. The statistical analysis included the calculation of the mean and standard 
deviation (STD) of the μs values for each sample.  
5.4.8 Optical properties extraction & Statistics 
The last step involved the visualization of the results by plotting both the μs values’ 
distribution and their means and STDs. For the former part boxplots were utilized, where 
for each one of the four sample types, a plot including the values computed per time-point 
examined was created. In a similar manner, their means and STDs were displayed.

7. Analysis & Discussion 
In the following chapter, I will be discussing the research findings. The scope of the thesis 
is to investigate if any alterations are introduced in the sample structures, because of 
sample degradation, preservation method and fixation.  
7.1 Structure and Morphology 
From the OCT images, it is evident that there are some morphological differences in 
relation to how similar samples had looked in a previous analysis [7]. Here, it can be seen 
that a porous structure has formulated, which is most likely attributed to the entrapped 
water molecules in the polymeric chains, which went through expansion during freezing 
and subsequent thawing. The porous structure constitutes a major challenge for the post 
processing, as it was experienced during the project realization. Firstly, a great 
percentage of the OCT image is occupied by noise instead of back-scattering of the 
material with few exceptions, namely fixed inks from D28 and D35 and fixed bioinks from 
D7. This is what led to the selection of the MIP as a means of preprocessing the images. 
However, with this process a lot of information is lost, that could be valuable. Further on, 
we can already observe from the B-scans in Figure 11 from D35 and in Figure 12 from D0, 
D14, D21 and D28, that the pores have created several large enough perpendicular to the 
beam surface. These appear as columns of reflections or high brightness areas, even at 
depths where this is not expected. This is also apparent with the MIP, where peaks are 
located in deeper areas, where no material could be otherwise observed.  
Another issue that was probably induced by cryopreservation is the uneven sample 
surface. It was expected that these samples would have flat surfaces, which would help in 
implementing the corrections mentioned in 5.4.4 Corrections and the optical properties 
extraction in general. However, this was not the case, as in many instances surfaces 
presented irregularities, with a specific inclination. I would assume that these were 
caused because of the samples being compressed and decompressed during sample 
storing.  
Another relative common differentiation between fixed and non-fixed samples is the 
general structural morphology. The first, appear to be more dense and the pore size is 
evidently smaller in some cases in comparison to their counterparts that had not 
undergone the fixation. An example could be seen in Figure 11 for D7, D14, D21 and D28, 
as well as in Figure 12 for D0, D7 and D28. This is most likely an expected outcome of the 
fixation process, which was realized through chemical cross-linking, thereby creating 
more centers that could be potential scattering surfaces as well as introducing more 
molecules into the material that could act as scatterers.

7.2 Optical Properties 
At a first glance, it is notable that optical properties’ extraction is a complex and 
challenging process. For the inks, in Figure 13 the scattering coefficients present 
significant variability as can be seen from the large boxplots yielded. Also, the values 
themselves are ranging over a broad area, with scattering values being negative and 
positive. This regards both fixed and non-fixed inks. A similar behavior can be observed 
for fixed and non-fixed bioinks shown in Figure 17. The difference here is that the fixed 
bioinks, exhibit a rather positive skewness and slightly narrower coefficient distribution 
than their counterparts. For non-fixed bioinks a considerable variation is again outlined. 
These distributions convey that the values observed are less consistent, rendering 
predictability of the samples’ optical properties a hard task. This situation might well 
reflect the sample inhomogeneity, that is also observed in the OCT scans. Extensive 
alterations between material and background, with no specific pattern could be the 
reason behind these fluctuations. In both cases, non-fixed samples show substantially 
wider alterations in their distributions, which can be credited to the fixation preserving 
the structural integrity of the samples. 
The linear fitting is inspired from a previous work [7] that had utilized the slope of the 
mean scattering coefficients to mathematically derive an increasing or decreasing trend. 
That reasoning is also employed here, with the aim of investigating if a trend does exist 
within such variation. Here, what we can observe is that while for inks the fixed ones show 
a subtle gradual increase, the situation for the respective bioinks is the opposite and vice 
versa for the non-fixed samples. One could assume that when degradation occurs, 
breaking down the sample entity into multiple pieces, the scattering coefficient is 
expected to rise, because of the created elevation in scatterer concentration. However, the 
porosity induced by cryopreservation might have inhibited this impact, as it has 
essentially kept the material within specific boundaries. This factor is a potential cause 
for such inconsistencies between the control samples and the cell-encapsulated NC gels.  
A clear overview of the scattering behavior of the samples might be also hindered because 
of sample processing. As it was stated earlier, MIP utilization reflects the maximum 
intensity detected versus depth, and for samples showing general inhomogeneity these 
values might not be representative, to have a conclusive remark and realistic influence on 
how the optical properties are fluctuating. Another parameter that should be considered 
is the definition of a stable axial ROI, which in various cases might have coincided with 
troughs in the amplitude of the OCT signal, leading to mean scattering coefficient 
variations within the same sample. In contradiction, it was also claimed earlier that these 
instabilities could represent sample inhomogeneity. All in all, the decision to have a stable 
ROI to extract the parameter of interest was influenced by sample morphology, 
appearance and the efforts towards an automated algorithm implementation.  
With respect to the plots that compare the mean scattering values for inks and bioinks 
under fixative and non-conditions, a further variability can be observed. In some cases, 
the values coincide or are ranging within the same magnitude, whereas in others a clear 
differentiation manifests. Once more, it remains inconclusive whether the PFA agent has 
modified the sample structure to such an extent, that this is captured in the optical 
properties’ probing, whilst such a result would be expected. It could be that sample-
storing conditions have implicated on what could otherwise be perceived and claimed in 
literature. In terms of values, a rise is remarked for the mean scattering coefficients of the 
bioinks, with respect to those of the inks, with the former fluctuating between roughly 0.5 
to 2.5 mm-1 and the latter ranging approximately from 0 to 2 mm-1. This fact could have 
arisen because of the cell encapsulation involved, if this is regarded from a more general 
perspective. If these values were found to be actual in a more thorough and rigorous 
==PAGE:52==
 
 
34 
 
project, and the optical depth expression mentioned in 4.3 Modelling optical properties 
with OCT was computed, then the multiple scattering models could be of use. 
On a last note, degradation of the polymeric matrix does induce structural alterations. 
Therefore creating variability in optical properties. However, one should take into 
account that the general trends in the scattering coefficients with them rising and falling, 
might indicate the simultaneous occurrence of tissue-layer formation occupying former 
polymer material, which is degrading.

8. Conclusion 
Sample handling and general treatment during fabrication are critical factors that do influence 
sample integrity, structure and consequently physical properties, as discussed in 7. Analysis & 
Discussion. Optical coherence tomography serves as a non-destructive tool to monitor the changes 
induced by cryopreservation and fixation of the samples, outlining high variability in the optical 
properties investigated. Matrix degradation also plays a part in how the optical properties are 
ranging. It is deduced that even though both methods were utilized for a different purpose, their 
combined effect on the study of the samples, is more complex, requiring further research to pinpoint 
the exact manner in which the constructs are affected. Fixation was aiming to pause time for the 
samples, so that their angiogenic potential could be examined, whereas cryopreservation was a 
means to interrupt degradation of the polymeric matrix and obstruct potential microbial 
contamination.  Utilization of other techniques is recommended for correlation of the structural 
alterations to other physical and biological properties, such as shear rheometry and multiphoton 
microscopy, namely light-sheet fluorescence microscopy, as was intended in the beginning of this 
project. All in all, OCT is a label-free tool that can offer longitudinal monitoring of sample morphology 
and physical properties’ variation with the sample treatment described here. Cryopreservation 
alters the samples’ structural integrity, as well as fixation, effects that are noted and visualized 
through OCT. Degradation induces further variations in the samples’ optical properties. The need 
for a systemic approach is strongly advised for, as the project remarks that optical properties and 
sample morphology variate in accordance to multifactorial events.

9. Challenges & Perspectives 
9.1 Overview 
I could confidently assert that no experimental project exists without encountering 
challenges. This is true in my experience as well. The original idea was to study these 
samples with OCT, two-photon microscopy (MPM), and shear rheometry. The aim 
included three aspects:  
 
Optical properties investigation through creation of two dimensional attenuation 
coefficient maps with OCT and optimization of the algorithm that was used 
previously in [7] 
 
Angiogenic potential exploration with the use of multi-photon microscopy, with potential 
comparison with single-photon fluorescence that is commonly employed. 
 
Mechanical properties characterization through shear rheometry to account for the 
viscoelastic character of hydrogels, which is typically implemented before the 
biofabrication. 
The outcome would involve correlating the results of each technique towards an overall conclusion 
regarding sample characterization. In this case, the porous network arising from cryopreservation, 
exhibited a less favorable contribution for the two-dimensional attenuation coefficient mapping. 
Hence, another approach was employed to track changes in optical properties and correlate those 
with what is observed in the OCT images.  
On the subject of shear rheometry, the sample size required did not match what was available. For 
the purpose of devising the experimental protocol, several parameters need to be tested, to yield the 
optimal combination, with the samples being destructed in the process. Another issue came forward 
when the dimensions of the samples were regarded. In brief, to implement shear rheometry, the 
sample diameter necessitates a greater size than the diameter of the plate performing the test. That 
is to ensure uniform distribution of stress and strain induced by the plate to the sample. Nonetheless, 
together with Tatiane Eufrásio-da-Silva, an experienced researcher and user of the shear rheometer 
DHR2 with hydrogels, we tried to perform rheometry on ink samples, that were acquired 
approximately a year earlier and were refrigerated since. For this purpose, we used an 8 mm plate, 
which according to Tatiane’s experience is commonly used for hydrogels. One had undergone an 
experiment of freezing and thawing to inspect if indeed the cryopreservation had induced the 
porosity in the structures. The frozen sample, presented many difficulties in handling, as discs of 
approximately 1 mm thickness had to be produced. The sample was extremely hard and could not 
be cut without traumatizing and risking the integrity of the sample with a scalpel. This was not the 
case for the refrigerated inks. Discs could be easily cut, and two types of experiments were tested on 
these, namely amplitude sweeps and frequency sweeps. The results are excluded from this project, 
because of the fact that the samples were not freshly prepared and had been preserved at 4℃ for 
over a year. The discs produced were manually cut, and the general process was not performed 
under strict experimental protocols. The aim was to investigate if there might be benefit from shear 
rheometry and provide an insight of how the parameters should be set. Apart from the issues 
outlined above, it is interesting to explore the mechanical differences between cryopreserved and 
refrigerated samples.   
Regarding multiphoton microscopy, we had decided to use the Gaussian Light Sheet Fluorescence 
Microscope (G-LSFM), to induce multiphoton excitation and subsequently measure the signal 
emitted. We used a fluorophore excited at 405 nm with single-photon conjugated with an antibody 
for PECAM-1, an angiogenesis marker commonly expressed in epithelial cells. The samples were 
prepared and stained for the fluorescence experiment as it is explained in F. Immunofluorescence 
(IF) staining protocol. Further, we performed sample mounting using FEP tubes and low-melting 
point agarose. Images of the mounting process can be found in G. Sample mounting for MPM. 
Afterwards, we tested several wavelengths, starting from 700 nm towards 1000 nm, to check which 
==PAGE:55==
 
37 
 
one excites the fluorophore best, imaging bioinks from D14, D21 and D28, time-points commonly 
tested for and where fluorescent signals are expected to have high intensities. In the beginning, we 
tried with a point-scanning MPM, with no result and then moved on to G-LSFM to inspect if there 
was any signal. Both microscopes yielded no signal. This could either be assigned to the staining 
process failing, structural changes induced by the sample mounting procedure or an incomplete 
staining protocol followed. Regarding the changes induced by sample mounting, I performed OCT 
on stained and mounted samples to visualize how the samples had changed. These results were not 
processed further though with the attenuation coefficient model employed to correlate what was 
visually observed. It could be a target for future research, to explore how sample mounting affects 
the samples.   
The aforementioned difficulties and experiences led to the following ideas for prospective research. 
9.2 Outlook 
9.2.1 Optical properties 
The original idea of tracking changes in the optical properties of the samples with respect to 
degradation occurring with time progression should be one of the first scopes of next projects. This 
will help identify sample homogeneity or inhomogeneity depending on the findings and will provide 
a better insight of the sample structure. Another viewpoint could be the investigation of MS model 
in use of cryopreserved samples or an optimized approach for SS model, probably with a structure 
detection algorithm and subsequent derivation of the OCT signal without the inclusion of noise. 
Then filtering could be applied and the rest of the steps implemented here could also be followed. 
As mentioned before, a comparison between fixed, non-fixed, stained and mounted samples could 
be implemented to conclude how these processes affect the samples in terms of optical properties, 
and further on with correlation with mechanical properties.  
9.2.2 Structure and morphology 
With the prospect of attaining further insight on the structural information and 
differences, one could employ scanning electron microscopy (SEM) or transmission 
electron microscopy (TEM), an approach commonly used to study similar samples, with 
the disadvantage of specific preparation protocols being required. One further idea, as it 
was also suggested for my project, is to measure and plot the distribution of the pores’ 
sizes observed, using ImageJ for instance. That would verify how the fixation has impacted 
the sample structure with relation to cryopreservation. 
Because of the multitude of parameters that were explored in this project, particularly 
degradation, fixation, cryopreservation, cell encapsulation it would be beneficial to 
conduct separate experiments to monitor their impact. This fact would enable the 
derivation of more definitive remarks.  
9.2.3 Angiogenic potential      
I would strongly advocate on the exploration of MPM for angiogenesis marker expression, 
especially in comparison with confocal microscopy (CM), as it is widely used in research 
for monitoring any functional activity and viability of encapsulated cells. The advantages 
of MPM over CM have already been documented in literature.  
9.2.4 Mechanical properties 
It is well established that specific engineering aspects should be provided for three-
dimensional engineering models. First and foremost, cells need specific physical signaling 
to develop to a functional tissue type and for usage in regenerative medicine, these models 
should emulate the environment provided physiologically. Shear rheometry has a vital 
benefit over compression testing, as it accounts for both the viscous and the elastic 
==PAGE:56==
 
 
38 
 
character of hydrogels, which is the scaffold material. Moreover, it might be of use to 
compare inks before and after biofabrication, to track any induced changes from the 
manufacturing method itself. For future research, it could also be interesting to evaluate 
any alterations on bioinks under thermal conditions. In particular this would entail to 
compare them for storing conditions (namely 4℃), room temperature and 37 ℃, as this 
is the normal temperature for human beings. A useful guide for shear rheometry testing 
has been documented in the work of Amorim P.A. et al. [93]. In this same work, it was 
determined that oscillatory mode tests are a finer choice for probing rheological 
properties. It should be noted here, that from our efforts, we concluded that amplitude 
sweeps might be the test researchers should aim for as a starting point.

A. Sensitivity Roll-off Characterization  
Sensitivity roll-off in OCT describes the progressive loss in sensitivity as a function of 
depth. This limitation is a systemic effect introduced from the finite number of pixels in 
the spectrometer, which is the detector in the case of SD-OCT [94].  
To retrieve the roll-off model for the OCT system, it is imperative to collect as much data 
as possible. One could use either samples to be studied or phantoms for the experiment. 
In this study, I used a parallelepiped phantom, which was also employed to set the focus 
of the imaging sessions.  
I started with placing the phantom on the sample stage. To ensure consistency with the 
imaging protocol, several parameters were kept the same. This includes, setting the 
acquisition speed at 28 kHz and the pixel size at 2.50 μm along the x-axis. The focus was 
adjusted to a depth of 1 mm, with the sample surface positioned at 0.40 mm. Imaging 
began at 0.40 mm depth. Subsequently, the reference arm was incrementally moved 
deeper in steps of 0.10 mm, covering the maximum imaging range the reference arm could 
accommodate. At each depth, a B-scan was captured, resulting in a total of 28 
measurements. The collection of multiple images ensured the facilitation and accuracy of 
the roll-off model retrieval, and the consequent extraction of the s parameter.   
The imaging parameters are summarized in Table 9, and in Table 10 the depths where 
each B-scan was captured are collectively described.

F. Immunofluorescence (IF) staining protocol  
The following protocol was developed with Susanne Primdahl, laboratory technician at 
DTU Health Tech, following the example of the protocol provided by the manufacturer of 
the fluorophore used [95]. The catalogue number of the fluorophore-antibody conjugate 
is 48-0319-42 and its listed as CD31 (PECAM-1) Monoclonal Antibody (WM-59 (WM59)), 
eFluor™ 450, eBioscience™ [96]. 
Permeabilization 
1. Add 400 µL of 0.1% Triton X-100 in 1X PBS and incubate the cells at room 
temperature for 15 min. 
2. Remove Triton-X-100 and wash the cells three times with 500 µL of 1X PBS. 
Blocking 
Add 500 µL of 2% BSA in 1X PBS and incubate the cells at room temperature for 60 min. 
(Alternately, the cells can be incubated overnight in 2% BSA or 1X PBS before proceeding 
to immunostaining.) 
Immunostaining 
1. Add the desired concentration of primary antibody diluted in 500 µL of 0.1% BSA to 
the cells and incubate for 3 hours at room temperature. 
2. Remove primary antibody solution and wash the cells three times with 500 µL of 1X 
PBS.

G. Sample mounting for MPM 
In brief, we started by creating the agarose gel, using low-melting point agarose powder 
to reach a concentration of 1% with DI water. We let the solution stir until it turned clear 
and then removed it from the heating plate of the magnetic stirrer to reach a temperature 
of less than 50℃. That is because gellan gum dissolves above 50 ℃. We cut the FEP tube 
in pieces of approximately five cm. Afterwards, a small amount of agarose gel was poured 
into a beaker and the sample was placed in the beaker with a spatula (Figure 33, A). Using 
a wide enough pipette to fit into the tube diameter, the sample was pulled inside, after a 
small portion of agarose was siphoned first (Figure 33, B). Immediately after the sample 
was inside the tube, it was turned horizontally and carefully placed in a black box to 
solidify and be protected as seen in Figure 34. The samples were immobilized with a 
pressure-sensitive adhesive, commercially known as Blu Tack. All samples were 
refrigerated afterwards, before imaging started the next day. The FEP tubes that were 
used, were purchased from Adtech, with part number FT7X7.6 [97].
