Abstract 
 
Frequency domain optical coherence tomography (FD-OCT) is a powerful tissue imaging technique for 
medical diagnostics. Current FD-OCT systems use expensive frequency doubled diode-pumped solid state 
lasers (DPSSs) as pump sources. In this thesis, a FD-OCT system is built where a novel tapered diode laser is 
used as the pump source for the Femtosource Scientific Titanium Sapphire laser driving the imaging. The 
measurement of signal-to-noise ratio from a single A-scan retrieved by the imaging modality is going to be 
presented.

Chapter 1 
 
Introduction 
 
Nowadays, medical imaging plays a major role in early detection, diagnosis and treatment of various 
human diseases. Optical imaging is currently growing at a fast rate and has become a promising tool for 
disease detection. In contrast to X-ray imaging techniques such as computed tomography, it uses non-
ionizing, visible and near-infrared light which has low energy and does not cause any tissue damage.  
Another advantage of optical imaging techniques is that they provide excellent soft-tissue contrast, fast 
data acquisition and low cost [1]. 
Spectral domain optical coherence tomography (SD-OCT) is an emerging optical imaging technique able to 
provide non-invasive, cross-sectional and three-dimensional (3D) volumetric imaging of biological tissue 
structure. In-vivo and in real time tissue imaging with high resolutions of 1-15nm is possible without 
sacrificing image sensitivity. The image depth is limited to 2-3mm in the majority of biological tissues 
because light is attenuated by scattering and absorption. Since the first in-vivo retinal imaging in 2002 [2], 
the technique is extensively used in ophthalmology as well as in other fields of medicine such as 
dermatology and cardiology. The substantial higher imaging speed and sensitivity that it can offer 
compared to time-domain OCT (TD-OCT) allow for 3D volumetric imaging over large segments of retina and 
cornea to detect ophthalmic diseases such as glaucoma and diabetic retinopathy [3]. Current SD-OCT 
systems capable of achieving SNR values up to 121dB [4] and imaging speeds of 6,700-310,000 A-scans per 
second have been demonstrated [5-7].     
Two of the main factors that make a SD-OCT system appealing are the low cost and high signal-to-noise 
ratio (SNR). Today, conventional frequency-doubled diode-pumped solid state lasers (DPSSs) used as pump 
sources in SD-OCT provide high beam quality and output power but they are highly costly. SNR plays an 
important role in a SD-OCT system as higher values of it are translated into higher image contrast and 
hence better image quality. One of the key factors that generates noise and reduces SNR is the random 
intensity noise of light sources used in SD-OCT systems [8]. Recently, the use of a frequency-doubled 
distributed Bragg reflected (DBR) tapered diode laser to pump a Ti:Sapphire laser for OCT imaging of the 
retina and skin has been demonstrated[9]. The high contrast of images obtained with the tapered diode 
laser combined with its low-cost showed great potential for their use in biomedical imaging.       
In this thesis, a SD-OCT system will be built in order to measure the SNR of A-scans obtained with a green 
frequency-doubled DBR tapered-diode laser as the pump source for the Ti:Sapphire laser driving the 
imaging. Chapter 2 gives a brief introduction to SD-OCT principles and system performance. In chapter 3, 
all the components used to build the imaging system are described comprehensively. The same chapter 
also explains the method used to acquire and process data. Chapter 4 includes A-scans from a glass cover 
slide as well as the measurement of SNR.

2.1 SD-OCT principles 
 
2.1.1 Low-coherence interferometry 
The operating principle of SD-OCT is similar to ultrasound imaging but the main difference is that in SD-OCT 
the detected signal is backscattered light from a sample instead of back-reflected sound. Another 
difference between these two imaging modalities is that due to the fact that the speed of light is much 
larger than the speed of sound, direct detection of OCT signal with electronics is not possible due to  
limited speed. Instead, a technique called low-coherence interferometry is applied for detection of SD-OCT 
signals. In order to obtain a more detailed image with higher resolution, ultra-short pulsed lasers with 
coherence lengths on the order of few     are commonly used. Below, a basic description of how SD-OCT 
works is given.

Broad-bandwith pulses emitted from a laser impinge on the beamsplitter which splits the incident light 
into two arms by reflecting a portion of it towards the reference arm and transmitting the remaining 
portion towards the sample arm. The light travelling in the reference arm is backreflected from a fixed 
mirror (reference mirror) and impinges on the beamsplitter, where one portion of it is transmitted towards 
the detector and the remaining portion is reflected towards the light source. The part of light that travels  
towards the sample is backreflected from it and impinges on the beamsplitter where a portion of it is 
transmitted towards the light source and the remaining part is reflected towards the detector. The two  
beams that travel towards the detector can interfere to each other if they overlap and the optical path 
length difference between the reference and sample arms is within the coherence length of the laser. The 
interference light is dispersed by a spectrometer into individual wavelengths which are then detected by 
an array of photodiodes (CCD or CMOS camera) to form an image of the sample.

2.2.2  Image depth 
In SD-OCT, absorption and scattering are the two basic mechanisms that attenuate the light as it 
propagates inside the human tissue and hence determine the image depth. Absorption and scattering are 
described by the absorption coefficient    and scattering coefficient    respectively which are both 
wavelength dependent. The greater the values of these two coefficients, the greater the attenuation of 
light inside biological tissue and the smaller the image depth that can be achieved. Optical absorption 
arises mainly from the presence of water, melanin, oxygenated and de-oxygenated hemoglobin inside 
tissue. Optical scattering is described as any deviation of light from a straight path and results from light 
interaction with biological structures. It is more intense for structures with a size similar to the wavelength 
of incident light and with a refractive index that differs from the one of the surrounding media. Two 
examples of biological structures that scatter light a lot are the cell nuclei and mitochondria [15].

3.1.1 Light source 
The light source for the SD-OCT system is a Femtosource Scientific Ti:Sapphire laser (s20)  manufactured by 
Femtolasers. It emits only one transverse mode (the       mode) with horizontal polarization. By mode-
locking the laser, ultra-short pulses can be generated with pulse duration below 20fs and spectral width 
above 40nm (at FWHM) centered at 800nm. The broad spectrum of the pulses allows for high axial 
resolution in the OCT system and the central wavelength of 800nm for optimum laser efficiency. The beam 
quality factor    is below 1.3 something that ensures that the light beam can be approximated with a 
Gaussian beam [20]. 
The scientific laser is pumped by a frequency doubled DBR-tapered diode laser (manufactured by Norlase) 
which combines good beam quality with smaller size, lower cost and superior electro-optical efficiency 
over a commercial diode pumped solid-state laser. The DBR-tapered diode laser outputs up to 8W of 
infrared light at 1032nm, which is then converted into green light at 516nm by employing single-pass 
second harmonic generation (SHG) in a bulk PPMgLN nonlinear crystal. The maximum output power of the 
green light coming out from the diode laser is 1.3W. The beam quality of the SHG beam depends on the 
output power of the laser with a minimum value of    equal to 1.1 at 1.3W. The tapered diode laser is a 
slightly modified version of the 1.5W frequency doubled tapered diode laser developed at Norlase [21] and 
both lasers have similar experimental set-up. 
 
3.1.2 Neutral density filters 
The system consists of two neutral density (ND) filters which reduce the intensity of all wavelengths of 
incident light equally. The first filter which is placed at the output of the laser is the NDC-50S-3M model 
manufactured by Thorlabs. It is a mounted step variable ND filter with a spectral range of 240-1200nm and 
an optical density range from 0.04 to 3. The optical density is specified  at 633nm.The second filter is the 
5215 model manufactured by Thorlabs. It is a dual ND filter wheel which contains twelve ND filters 
mounted in a single unit. The optical density varies from 0.08 to 3 in 0.1 increments. These filters reduce 
the amount of incident light on the CMOS camera of the spectrometer in order to avoid saturation of the 
camera. The use of filters also prevents any potential damage that can occur at the free-space-to-fiber 
interface when coupling pulsed light into the single mode fiber (SMF). This potential of damaging the fiber 
can be quite high without any attenuation of light, as the high peak power pulsed light needs to be focused 
down to a very small area for coupling, resulting in a very high intensity.   
 
3.1.3 Beamsplitter 
The beamsplitter is the Casix CBS0103. It provides a wavelength range from 650-900nm and the 
transmission-to-reflection ratio is 50/50 5% for un-polarized natural light. The beamsplitter is not polarizing, 
however the transmission and reflection depend on the polarization of incident light which can be modulated 
with a half-wave plate.

3.1.4 Sample arm 
The first component of the sample arm (as light travels from the beamsplitter to the sample stage) is the 
6220 galvanometer optical scanner by Cambridge technology. It can scan the beam along x and y axes 
allowing for 2D and 3D imaging of the sample. It consists of two identical mirrors 6M2205S-S and each of 
them is wired to the MicroMax Series 671 single axis board level for movement control. The power supply for 
the 671 board is   28V. The scanner has a settling time of 200  sec  for moving a 0.1  step. 
The next components are two plano-convex lenses with a focal distance of 80mm and a wavelength range 
from 600 to 1100nm. They are achromatic in order to reduce chromatic aberration which is basically the 
failure of a lense to focus different wavelengths on the same focal point. Chromatic aberration if not reduced 
can degrade the axial resolution of the image by limiting the actually effective bandwith of light [22]. The two 
lenses have been put in such a way that their convex surfaces look towards the x/y scanner and the objective 
respectively. As the lenses are not aspherical, this configuration has been chosen to achieve the minimum 
spherical aberration and hence the minimum blurriness in the image.  
A mirror is used after the two lenses in order to guide the light to the objective that focus the beam on the 
sample. The objective is the Newport M-20x with a magnification of 20x and focal length of 9mm. The 
numerical aperture is 0.40. Due to the fact that SD-OCT performs cross-sectional imaging, the relatively low 
numerical aperture of 0.40 has been chosen in order to achieve a sufficient depth of field for cross-sectional 
imaging. 
The last component of the sample arm is a z-axis movable stage on which the sample is put for imaging.

the reference arm in order to achieve interference between the two arms. Interference has been realized by

3.1.6 Detection arm 
As the backreflected beams from the reference mirror and the sample travel towards the beamsplitter, 
their electric fields combine together interferometrically in the detection arm. An objective is used to 
couple the interference pattern into a single mode fiber which sends the light to a spectrometer and CMOS 
camera for detection. 
The objective is the Newport M-10x with a magnification of 10x, focal length of 16.5mm and numerical 
aperture of 0.25. It is mounted on the Elliot gold MDE 122 xyz stage which provides 20nm of adjustment 
resolution with 2mm of travel in each of three axes. By adjusting the position of the objective with respect 
to the single mode fiber, coupling of light into the fiber is achieved.  
The single mode fiber is the Thorlabs 780HP with an operating wavelength from 780 to 970 nm and 
numerical aperture of 0.13. It transmits only the        mode with a maximum attenuation of less than 
3.5dB/km at 780nm. The mode field diameter (MFD) is 5.0   0.5   and denotes the size of the beam that 
propagates inside the single mode fibre. For a Gaussian power distribution, the MFD is the diameter where 
the power of light is reduced to 1/   from its peak value. The fiber is mounted, via a terminated fiber 
adapter, on a mirror mount with three adjusters. By rotating these adjusters, the angle of incidence at 
which the focused light from the objective hits the fiber can be modulated for optimum coupling efficiency. 
The most important component of the SD-OCT system, is the Bayspec OCTS-735-800-865 spectrometer 
with a wavelength range from 741 to 886nm. The spectral resolution is 0.14nm and describes the 
maximum number of spectral peaks (wavelengths) that the spectrometer can resolve across a spectrum. 
The single mode fibre is connected to it and the incident light is dispersed into its component wavelengths 
via a diffraction grating. Instead of a conventional reflection grating, the spectrometer consists of a volume 
phase transmission grating (VPG). This type of grating does not have any grooves and diffracts light by 
refractive index modulations within a thin layer of transmissive material (dichromatic gelatin) which is 
sandwiched between two glass substrates [23]. One of the advantages of VPGs over typical reflection 
gratings is that the amount of scattered light which adds noise to the photodetector, is significantly 
reduced. Scattered light is defined as the light leaving from the surface of a grating that does not obey the 
grating law, given by equation (3.1) [24] 
 
                                                                       sin   + sin   = m /d                                                                     (3.1) 
 
where    and    denote the angles between the diffracted and incident wave directions and the normal to 
the surface of the grating, m is an integer that denotes the order of diffraction,   is the wavelength and d is 
the period of the grating. This specific VPG from Bayspec also offers a diffraction efficiency up to 99% 
which is higher than the efficiency achieved by conventional gratings. The diffraction efficiency is defined 
as the ratio of the diffracted power of light to the incident power.

4.1 Laser characterization 
The scientific laser is pumped with 1.1W of green light from the tapered diode laser achieving an average 
output power of 110mW in CW operation. For mode-locked operation, the average output power of the

as the most stable mode inside the laser cavity is the continuous wave (CW) mode.  Mode-locking can be 
achieved by tuning the stability of the CW mode inside the cavity to make it a little unstable and allow the 
pulsed mode to become the most stable one.

Chapter 5 
 
Discussion 
 
An averaging coupling efficiency of 40% was achieved in the SD-OCT system which is close to the maximum 
theoretical value of 44.8%. The difference between these two values is due to the fact that the overlap 
integral equation used in numerical calculations does not take into account Fresnel reflections of light at 
the input and output interfaces of the fibre as well as propagation losses inside the fibre. Since the Fresnel 
reflection from an interface between air and glass is 3.5% at normal incidence, the total reflection losses 
from the input and output interfaces of the fibre are 7%. Hence, the theoretical maximum coupling 
efficiency including the Fresnel reflections is 41.7%. Propagation losses inside the fibre account for the 
remaining difference of 1.7% from the measured value of 40%.   
In terms of axial resolution, it can be seen that the experimental value of 13.6    differs from the 
theoretical one of 8.04  . This degradation in axial experienced in practice is depth-dependent and is 
more pronounced as the optical path length difference between the sample (dielectric mirror BB1-E03) 
and the reference mirror increases. This can be attributed to the fact that due to the limited size and 
number of pixels of CMOS camera, there is cross-talk between adjacent pixels as well as for signals 
measured away from the zero optical path length difference. This cross-talk, which basically means that 
the signal measured from a specific pixel is affected by the signal of neighboring pixels, can change the 
shape of the spectrum and degrade the axial resolution.     
The physical thickness of a glass cover slide was measured with the SD-OCT system and found to be 
0.138mm. This value is pretty close to the value of 0.15mm measured with the Mitutoyo caliper. This 
means that the signal processing made in Labview to obtain an A-scan as well as the calibration of depth 
axis were done correctly.  
Regarding the measurement of signal-to-noise ratio, the measured value of 16dB from the obtained A-scan 
is very low and differs a lot from the optimal value of 86.45dB for shot noise limited performance. This is 
mainly attributed to the fact that the SD-OCT signal was not averaged at all during the acquisition of all A-
scans with our system. The reason for that is that the scientific laser was not able to be pumped sufficiently 
due to significant instability in the output power of the tapered diode laser. Signal averaging is a technique 
that is commonly applied in SD-OCT systems in order to improve SNR. During this procedure, a large 
number of A-scans Is recorded repetitively by the spectrometer. The signals of each A-scan add together 
whereas the random noise averages out.By averaging N recorded A-scans, it can be proved mathematically 
that SNR can be increased approximately by a factor of    [32]. Hence, a number of 100 repetitive A-scans 
can increase the SNR of our system by a factor of 10. It can be seen that the larger the number of repetitive 
A-scans to average out, the greater the improvement of SNR. A-scans Another reason which is responsible 
for this low measured SNR is the fact that the DC and autocorrelation terms have not been reduced. In SD- 
==PAGE:33==
26 
 
 
 
OCT, only the cross-correlation terms are the desired signal whereas the DC and auto-correlation terms 
give rise to image artifacts and can decrease the SNR of the system. These two unwanted terms can be 
reduced significantly by placing a phase modulator in the reference arm to introduce a phase shift of   
between the reference and sample arms. By subtracting the spectral interferogram with a phase shift of   
from the spectral interferogram with zero phase, the amplitude of the signal can be increased by a factor 
of two and therefore improve the SNR. This technique of the reduction of DC and auto-correlation terms is 
known as dual balanced detection [33]. Finally, the signal-to-noise ratio can be also increased by increasing 
the integration time of the CMOS camera to reduce shot noise but this would be at the expense of imaging 
speed. It should be noted that all the above methods of SNR improvement concern the case of our system  
using the same components.             
 
 
5.1 Conclusion 
The development of a SD-OCT system using a femtosecond Ti:Sapphire laser to drive the imaging and a 
frequency-doubled DBR tapered diode laser as the pump source was demonstrated. A measurement of the 
physical thickness of a BK7 glass cover slide was done and showed that the system operates properly.  
However, the measured signal-to-noise ratio from an obtained A-scan was too low in comparison with the 
optimal SNR value under shot-noise limited performance. This can be attributed to the fact that signal 
averaging of multiple A-scans was not performed due to significant power instability experienced from the 
tapered diode laser.       
 
 
5.2 Outlook 
First of all, the output power of the tapered diode laser needs to be stabilized in order to perform signal 
averaging of a large number of repetitive A-scans and hence improve the signal-to-noise ratio. The tapered 
diode laser can be easily replaced by a conventional diode-pumped solid state laser to pump the scientific 
Ti:Sapphire source. By this way, a fair comparison of the SNR achieved with the two different pump 
sources is possible. This would allow us to determine if the green laser from Norlase is less noisy than a 
conventional pump source and able to provide higher quality imaging in SD-OCT.
