Abstract
Over the last years, great advances in the design of natural gas markets are taking place
with the aim of creating a marketplace that will attract more players and stimulate
investments. Special focus is placed on the establishment of a gas balancing model to
improve the overall efficiency of the natural gas market.
This thesis focuses on the natural gas balancing market in Denmark. A complementary
approach of the problem is achieved by investigating both the shipper’s and Transmission
System Operator’s perspective. The first part introduces a profit maximization problem
from the shipper’s perspective.
The balancing market and the impact of imbalance
costs on shipper’s behavior are examined. Also, the model is validated and the effect of
parameters, such as demand and prices, is investigated through different test cases.
The second part of the thesis studies the interaction between the electricity and
natural gas balancing market. The intermittency of renewable energy sources is taken
into account. A cost minimization problem is solved in this part from Energinet.dk’s
perspective. By including both balancing markets in one optimization problem, the need
for designing more efficient natural gas balancing market is highlighted. This is due to
the intermittency of renewable energy sources which introduce an increased demand and
variability for gas.
Next, two possible improvements for the natural gas balancing market are considered.
Both fixed and proportional penalization factors achieve the desired results, keeping the
system closer to balance and reducing costs for Energinet.dk. Those hourly penalization
factors are implemented each hour that shipper’s imbalance surpasses certain volume
limits. At the same time, shipper’s profits are reduced due to extra penalization and
change of his behavior induced by this penalization. Therefore, an optimal penalization
is found taking into account the balancing costs and shipper’s profits.
In the last part of this thesis, the impact of different parameters on Energinet.dk’s
balancing costs is further studied. Introducing a small penalization factor reduces overall
costs for Energinet.dk. Also, increasing the penalization limit increases gas balancing
costs in general, whereas electricity balancing costs are reduced.
By ‘relaxing’ the
penalization limit for certain hours of the day, the electricity balancing costs are reduced.
The asymmetric green zone definition used by Energinet.dk creates the right incentives
for shippers to either balance their portfolio or create an imbalance to the opposite
direction than where the System Commercial Balance landed at the end of the previous
gas day. Finally, an hourly green zone definition is examined. Despite this hourly green
zone definition is currently not applicable in the Danish natural gas balancing system,
it has a positive impact on both natural gas balancing costs and shipper’s profits, while
at the same time ensures natural gas delivery for the electricity balancing side.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
General context
In 1979 the Danish parliament (the Folketing) agreed on the introduction of natural
gas in Denmark [1]. The construction of the natural gas transmission and distribution
system started then and the natural gas fields in the North sea went on stream in 1984.
The natural gas is transported ashore through two submarine high pressure pipelines,
which connect the fields in the North sea to a gas treatment plant in Nybro. The natural
gas is prepared there for transportation in the transmission system (removal of liquids
and sulphur from the natural gas). The customers are connected to the distribution
grid, which consists of the main and secondary distribution grid.
Natural gas, from now on referred to as gas, holds a significant share in the global
energy mix. Gas share is expected to grow slightly, although share of other fossil fuels
is expected to decrease [2]. In the meantime, a significant increase has been noticed
in the gas share of the generation mix for power production.
More specifically, the
gas share in EU has increased by 119 % between 1990 and 2014, at an average rate of
3 % per year [3]. Compared to other fossil fuels, the lower greenhouse gases and local
pollutants make it much more attractive [4]. Economic reasons, such as investment costs
of a combined-cycle gas turbine (CCGT) and government policies being unfavorable
to nuclear power, have a significant impact in gas popularity [5], [6].
For all these
reasons, gas is considered to be the most adequate fuel used for the transition from
massive usage of fossil fuels to the renewable energy era. Conventional recoverable gas
resources are equal to more than 60 years of current consumption, whereas there are
also unconventional resources expected to cover demand for a plentiful amount of years
[7]. Moreover, power-to-gas (P2G) technology is becoming increasingly popular in recent
years, allowing for large-scale energy storage and facilitating the integration of renewable
energy sources [8]. Gas is also used for heating purposes. In Denmark, CHP units which
use gas as a primary fuel accounted to 7.1 % of the heat supply in 2014. Also, gas units
that produce district heating alone account to 11.6 % of the heat supply [9]. The heat
supply by primary fuel can be seen in Appendix A.
In the past, gas supply was controlled by a single company (Maersk Oil incorporated
in Denmark), who had both exploration and production rights, making thus the balancing
mechanism reliable. After the liberalization of the gas market, the gas market changed
dramatically. The number of actors increased, each one with their role and objectives.
Together with the liberalization of the gas market, a new online trading market, Gaspoint
Nordic, is created. Now, prices for gas are determined through Gaspoint Nordic based
on supply and demand.

2
1 Introduction
Gas balancing is essential in order to keep the gas system within safe limits. Also, it
is important from a well-functioning market perspective. In Denmark, the new balancing
model was introduced on the 1st of October 2014 [10] and changes towards more market
oriented balancing settlements were introduced later, as well as future proposals both
by Energinet.dk and shippers [11]. At the time of writing this thesis, the gas balancing
model is stable, without recent changes. But the examination on further improvements
in the balancing model is an ongoing process.
The large scale integration of renewable energy sources, e.g., wind and solar power,
has introduced new challenges in power system operation due to their intermittent and
partially predictable generation.
The increased efficiency and fast power regulation
provided by gas-fired power plants constitutes them as an ideal choice to facilitate
the integration of renewables.
For these reasons, a tighter coupling between these
two systems is expected highlighting also the need for designing efficient gas balancing
markets as the gas demand increases.
Imbalances between supply and demand may create network disruptions if the gas
pressures move close to the critical limits. Hence, maintaining the necessary balance in
the network is a key factor for the secure operation of the system. However, compared
to the electricity system, the need for instant balancing is lower due to the linepack
flexibility that is available by the system infrastructure [12].
Under the traditional operating scheme, system operator was the only entity responsible
for balancing the system and the various charges were set to finance the balancing actions.
However, this gas balancing model design has changed during the recent years towards
a more market-oriented mechanism which aims to provide cost reflective charges of the
balancing actions that incentivize market participants to respond to system imbalances.
The aim of the transmission system operator is to optimally decide the physical green
zone so as to be robust against the worst case disruption. Moreover, the size of the
commercial green zone as well as its shape have an impact on the efficiency of the
market design and needs to be optimally decided in order to provide the appropriate
signals to market participants.

Aims and objectives
This thesis focuses on the gas balancing market. The gas balancing market in Denmark
is modeled.
Two different optimization problems are implemented.
First, a profit

Second, a cost

are proposed and further investigated.

in order to simulate the gas balancing system in this thesis. The balancing regime in
Denmark is described extensively in [10]. This business model along with information
provided by Energinet.dk is used to understand and simulate the balancing market in
Denmark. Shipper’s behavior, but also the effect of different parameters on shipper’s

1.3
Contributions
This thesis focuses on the balancing regime existing in Denmark at the time of writing.
A deep analysis on the gas balancing market design is done, recreating an overview of the
market and examining two different optimization problems. First, a profit maximization
problem from the shipper’s perspective is implemented. Shipper’s behavior is the focus
in this part. Also, the impact of different parameters on shipper’s behavior and overall
system’s imbalance is investigated.
Last, two different penalization factors for the
shipper’s imbalance are implemented. These hourly penalization factors are implemented
when shipper’s imbalance exceeds a limit. Their effect on shipper’s decision making is
further examined, providing practical insight for the TSO regarding shipper’s behavior
but also possible ways to diminish the gas balancing costs.
In addition, the interaction with the electricity market is considered through a cost
minimization problem from the TSO’s perspective. Power plants which use gas as a fuel
are becoming more and more popular. That creates additional challenges to the design
of the gas balancing market, due to increased and unexpected demand of gas.
The
imbalances introduced by stochastic power producers are balanced with gas fired power
plants and other thermal power plants. Then, part of these imbalances are passed in the
gas balancing system by the gas fired power plants. Two different propositions are further
investigated through two different wind scenarios. The result of a penalization factor
and stricter penalization limits are depicted in both the gas system and the electricity
balancing side. Last, a thorough sensitivity analysis is carried out providing further
insight in which factors affect the TSO’s total balancing costs from both markets.

2.2
Actors in the gas system
After the liberalization of the Danish gas system, each part of the supply chain was
divided, creating four main groups of actors [17]:
• Owners of the physical infrastructure, fe TSO
• Commercial users of the physical infrastructure, fe shippers
• Consumers
• Storage Owners, fe Energinet.dk
Energinet.dk is the gas Transmission System Operator in Denmark and owns the
transmission system. Energinet.dk is responsible for both long and short term network
reliability. Long term Energinet.dk’s obligation is to invest in the pipeline system in
order to meet present and future gas demand. Also, Energinet.dk is responsible for
monitoring the linepack limit (network flexibility) during the gas day, keeping pressure
in safe operational limits. In order to do that, Energinet.dk trades in both day ahead
and within day markets, when the physical and commercial system balance respectively
is outside the green zone. Shippers are commercial users of the physical infrastructure,
who buy transmission rights in the transportation system. They buy gas either from
long-term contracts with gas suppliers, domestic or foreign, or from the market and
sell gas to either distribution companies or big gas consumers [18],[19]. There are also
two storage facilities in Denmark, one in Stenlille on Sealand and one in Lille Torup in
Northern Jutland, owned and operated by Energinet.dk and DONG respectively. Storage
is used mostly by shippers in order to maintain contractual balance at the end-of-day.
Also, Energinet.dk may use storage in order to keep linepack flexibility in safe levels.
Last, consumers are divided into two subgroups: daily metered consumers, who are

2.3
Zones in Denmark
The calculation of the commercial green zone is based on the physical constraints of
the system and a forecast for the expected flow for the following gas day [21].
In
order to derive the commercial green zone each day, a calculation of the physical
green zone is executed first. The upper physical red limit is the maximum pressure
at supply points. It is also the maximum linepack that the system can withstand. The
maximum pressure for the Danish gas system is at 78 barg. The lower physical red
limit is the minimum linepack, which means the minimum pressure at consumption
points (whichever minimum reached first-in the exit points or exit zone) [22].
The
minimum pressure for the gas system in Denmark is 44 barg. The upper yellow limit is
only affected by input parameters, such as consumption forecast and nominations from
shippers. The lower yellow limit is the least linepack required in order to withstand a
‘worst-case’ scenario. The difference between the upper and lower yellow limit is the
flexibility available for shippers-the physical green zone. In order to derive the yellow
zone, Energinet.dk collects the accepted nominations for the coming gas day and makes
a forecast of the expected offtake in the Danish exit zone.
The yellow zone for the
coming gas day then is the linepack needed in order to operate the system based on that
information (nominations and offtake). Having determined the physical limits needed in
order to operate the system, the commercial green zone is derived by using a percentage
of the physical linepack. For long time, 1/2 of the physical green zone was used as a
commercial green zone. Figure 2.2 demonstrates the limits for the green zone calculation.
Lately, the commercial green zone is 3/4 of the physical green zone.

2.3.1
Green zone definition
2.3.1.1
Symmetric green zone definition
In the past, Energinet.dk calculated the green zone each day, symmetric around zero.
The green zone represents the market flexibility for the given gas day. For this calculation,
Energinet.dk taked into account the physical limits of the system but also approved
nominations of shippers and a forecast of the offtake of the Danish exit zone. Possible
imbalances carried on from the previous days were not taken into account. Thus, an
assumption that the system was more or less balanced on the previous gas day was done
each day. That wasn’t always true though. Especially, after 1st October 2014, where
the new Danish gas balancing model entered into force. Figure 2.3 shows the imbalance
of the system before and after 1st October.
Figure 2.3: System imbalances before and after 1st October 2014.
As it can be seen, imbalances after 1st October 2014 were entirely outside the
green zone, creating physical problems to the system. In order to address this problem,
Energinet.dk reduced the symmetric green zone to 1/3 of the original size on the 25th
of November 2014 [11]. This considered to be a short term solution in order to reduce
accumulated imbalance of the system. As it can be observed in Figure 2.4, this reduction
had a positive result reducing overall system imbalances.
Figure 2.4: Impact of change in the green zone on the 25th of November.

Nevertheless, it was considered a short-term solution, followed by an asymmetric
green zone definition, which was created in order to address the problem of gas shortage
in the market, while taking into account the imbalances of the previous day.
2.3.1.2
Asymmetric green zone definition
Energinet.dk introduced an asymmetric commercial green zone definition on 1st of
October 2015. Each gas day, the imbalance of the previous day is considered, formulating
the green zone in a way that favours an imbalance opposite to the previous end-of-day
imbalance. Figure 2.5 demonstrates the principle of the asymmetric green zone definition,
where the end-of-day imbalance on the previous gas day affects the green zone during
the next gas day.
Figure 2.5: Principle of asymmetric green zone definition.
This green zone definition has introduced a stricter limit to the system, ‘forcing’
the shippers to either balance their portfolio or drive their imbalance to the opposite
direction.
Until today, the asymmetric green zone definition works in favour of the
market and has been proved to be an effective measure for keeping the system in balance.
Expecting the gas system to grow day by day and players who participate in the market
to increase, an alternative green zone definition may be suitable. A dynamic definition of
the green zone could be more efficient, especially as shippers participating in the market
and liquidity increase. An hourly defined green zone is implemented in the Netherlands,
which can be an inspiration for a future green zone definition in Denmark.
2.4
System commercial balance
Each gas day, the position of the physical balance is estimated once per day by Energinet.dk.
Based on the physical conditions of the system, a green zone is predefined. If the position
of the physical balance reaches the yellow physical zone, then Energinet.dk will trade in
order to bring the physical balance back into the green zone. This trade is mostly done
in the day-ahead market. Figure 2.6 depicts exactly that.
Except for the physical balance which is determined based on the physical green zone
definition, Energinet.dk calculates a System Commercial Balance (SCB), which uses the
commercial green zone definition. SCB is the balance of the system at the end-of-day.

CHAPTER 3
Shipper’s problem
Chapter 3 introduces a profit maximization problem from the shipper’s perspective. At
first, the problem consists only from one shipper, meaning that the shipper in question
also constitutes the whole gas system. The mathematical formulation is described, as
well as some test cases in order to validate the model but also to investigate the impact
of different parameters in shipper’s behavior. Finally, the shipper is separated from the
system. The system is here introduced as a parameter with a set of constraints, which
changes the way the E(SCB) is calculated.
3.1
Mathematical formulation
Shipper’s objective is profit maximization. His objective function, which is demonstrated
by Equation (3.1), consists of revenues minus acquisition costs and imbalance costs forced
by the TSO. The main focus of this thesis is the balancing market, therefore possible
flexibility costs, like storage costs are considered sunk costs and are not taken into
account. Furthermore, conservation of energy must hold for all time units (here it’s one
hour), as it is shown in Equation (3.2). That means that the shipper’s portfolio must
balance itself, therefore the supply rate of the shipper into the gas pipeline minus the
demand rate of the shipper that is withdrawn plus any ex-ante flexibility must equal
the imbalance of the shipper at all hours. Demand is considered exogenous (Equation
(3.3)), a parameter and not a variable here.
The supply rate is limited each hour between the contractual minimum and maximum,
as expressed by Equation (3.4). It should be noted that negative flexibility refers to
injection of gas in the pipeline, bounded by a contractual F min and positive flexibility
refers to withdrawal of gas from the pipeline, bounded by a contractual F max, as defined
in Equation (3.5). The available stored flexibility is also limited between 0 and the
booked storage capacity of the shipper, as Equation (3.6) shows.
The difference of
the stored flexibility between two consecutive time slots (hours) is the rate of ex-ante
flexibility for the shipper at every hour. This relation is depicted in Equation (3.7).
The model is implemented with a zero flexibility and stored flexibility in the first hour.
The difference between the accumulated imbalances for two consecutive hours equals the
shipper’s reliance in ex-post flexibility, as Equation (3.8) depicts, where the final value
of the accumulated imbalance each day refers to the end-of-day position of the shipper.
Last, the ex-post imbalance costs are implemented using six different terms, based on

CHAPTER 4
Connection with the
electricity system
Denmark serves as a unique opportunity to study the integration of gas and electricity
systems. One of the arguments for that is because Energinet.dk is the TSO for both gas
and electricity system. That makes any interaction and sharing of information easier
between the two systems. This chapter focuses on the electricity system from the TSO’s
perspective. The formulation of this optimization problem takes into account shipper’s
behavior, which is still optimized for maximum earnings.
That is done in order to
combine the balancing stage (real-time) of both markets into one optimization problem.
The scope of this formulation is to investigate the impact of shipper’s behavior in both
markets.
A player (the shipper in this case) can participate in both markets. In this chapter,
the shipper uses the network flexibility of the gas system in order to provide a gas
fired power plant with the necessary gas to participate in the electricity balancing
market. Thus, investigating how profitable can be for a player to exploit gas system’s
flexibility in order to compete in the electricity balancing market can give insight in
the probable imbalances of the overall gas system. By examining this case including
shipper’s behavior, useful results can be obtained regarding network flexibility, but also
possible solutions in order to mitigate imbalances in the gas system, reducing overall
costs for the TSO.
In this chapter, an assumption that gas and electricity balancing markets are cleared
simultaneously is made. In reality, the two balancing markets are cleared each hour of
the day but independently from each other. This assumption is done in order to include
the shipper’s behavior in the problem, as well as to examine the effect of imbalance costs
on shipper’s behavior.

CHAPTER 5
Balancing model upgrade
This chapter introduces two possible improvements for the gas balancing model, a fixed
and a proportional penalization factor. Both penalization factors apply to shipper’s
hourly imbalance. The effect of both possible improvements is tested in the gas balancing
model. Also, the impact of the proportional penalization factor as well as penalization
limits on the electricity balancing system is examined for both wind scenatios presented
in the previous chapter.
5.1
Hourly penalization
As it can be seen from all the test cases and also from how penalization in Denmark
is implemented, the shipper is only obliged to be as close to zero imbalance as possible
only at the end-of-day. This can be explained by the fact that each shipper pays or
receives a payment with an unfavorable price only for the amount of gas that he is
unbalanced at the end-of-day. Therefore, there is no incentive for a shipper to balance
his portfolio throughout the day. This could possibly create huge imbalances in the
system. The imbalance could be amplified by possible escalation in the gas demand due
to the intermittency of the renewable energy sources.
In this chapter, an hourly penalization factor is implemented. This is done as a
precaution in order to control the hourly imbalance of the shipper throughout the gas
day. This hourly penalization factor is introduced for shipper’s imbalances that overcome
a certain volume limit, either in the positive or negative side. This limit, r+ or r−,
should not be confused with the limit of the green commercial zone (lim+ or lim−).
The limit for the green commercial zone is applied when Energinet.dk calculates the
E(SCB), indicating whether Energinet.dk needs to intervene in the market. The limit
for the penalization factor, r+ or r−, indicates when a penalization factor should apply
to shipper’s imbalance.

CHAPTER 7
Discussion and conclusion
The Danish gas balancing market is reproduced in this thesis. A MILP problem that
models systematically the existing balancing market rules is implemented for that purpose.
Next, an extension to that problem is created including the electricity balancing market.
Shipper’s profit maximization is a mixed integer linear problem consisting of revenues
minus acquisition costs and imbalance costs. Shipper’s imbalance is greatly affected by
prices in the within-day market, the green zone definition as well as other parameters,
like demand and flow rate. The effect of the imbalance costs on shipper’s behavior is
investigated.
From the different test cases implemented, the shipper chooses to pay
the imbalance settlement if the price of buying gas during is lower than the neutral
price minus the adjustment. Furthermore, when the shipper has to pay or receive a
marginal price, then his decision making drives him to balance his portfolio at the
end-of-day. Therefore, the shipper takes into account his relative end-of-day position
to where the E(SCB) landed outside the green zone in order to pay or receive the
imbalance settlement price at the end-of-day.
Attention is given to the green zone
definition.
A symmetric green zone definition is used throughout the thesis, only
because one gas day is taken into account. But an asymmetric green zone definition
is tested, which results in an opposite direction of the imbalance of shippers than the
imbalance of the system in the previous gas day. Until now, an asymmetric green zone
definition is used by Energinet.dk with successful results regarding system’s balance.
An asymmetric green zone definition is effective mostly because the within day market
lacks liquidity but also because the shippers are few in Denmark. As the gas market
grows and the number of shippers increases, possible improvements may be required in
order to keep the system in balance. In that concept, two different hourly penalization
factors are simulated, where an optimized value for each penalization factor is found
in order to keep the E(SCB) as close to zero as possible. Those two improvements are
introduced as a fixed and a proportional penalization factor, which are implemented
every hour that shipper’s imbalance surpasses a certain volume limit.
Both achieve
similar results.
As penalization increases for the shippers, system balance costs are
reduced. Therefore, both penalization factors could be implemented in order to keep
the system in balance in the future, without affecting significantly shippers’ profits. The
proportional penalization factor though is considered to be the best choice in this thesis.
The imbalance costs that shippers pay when their imbalance surpasses certain limits
should be proportional to their imbalance. Those possible improvements are necessary
if the electricity balancing market is taken into account, since the demand and the
demand variability for gas increases more.
Extending the shipper’s problem, the connection with the electricity balancing market
==PAGE:92==
74
7 Discussion and conclusion
is considered taking into account the partly predictable renewable energy sources in the
electricity market. Now the problem becomes a cost minimization problem from the
TSO’s perspective. Useful results are found for the TSO taking into perspective both
balancing markets and also players who participate in both markets.
The shippers
take advantage of the flexibility given from the gas network in order to cover the
demand. By penalizing shipper’s imbalance every hour of the gas day, this flexibility
is reduced. However, because of the increase in demand and the participation of more
and more shippers in the gas balancing market, new tools have to be found in order to
keep the system in balance. Implementing a proportional penalization factor and also
investigating further the penalization limit, an optimized solution can be found, where
overall costs (gas and electricity balancing costs) for the TSO can be minimized, with a
small impact to shipper’s profits. It is key not to restrict shippers too much in trying to
reduce gas balancing costs, because the gas demand for the electricity side is not covered,
resulting in higher balancing costs for the electricity side. Balancing costs for the TSO
are greatly affected by the imbalance of the rest of the system, introduced as a parameter
throughout the thesis. Furthermore, an hourly penalization limit is simulated, which is
more ‘relaxed’ during the hours when gas demand is increased and more strict during
the rest of the gas day, something which has positive results in electricity balancing costs
for the TSO and also increases profits for the shipper due to increased flexibility during
those hours of high demand. On the other hand, by increasing the penalization limits
in shippers’ imbalance, gas balancing costs also increase for Energinet.dk.
Regarding the green zone definition, an asymmetric green zone definition is simulated,
giving different results based on where the E(SCB) landed at the end of the previous gas
day. When the SCB landed on a negative imbalance at the previous gas day, shipper’s
profits are increased but TSO’s balancing costs increased as well. When the SCB landed
on a positive system imbalance at the end of the previous gas day, profits for the shipper
are decreased, whereas overall costs for the system reduced as well. To fully conclude
about the asymmetric green zone definition, more gas days should be simulated in order
to observe the impact on the system’s imbalance. But in general, by implementing an
asymmetric green zone definition, Energinet.dk pushes system’s imbalance towards the
other direction from where the SCB landed on the previous gas day. Finally, an hourly
green zone definition is considered. The optimized hourly definition for the green zone
provides better results regarding both shipper’s profits and overall costs for the TSO
in both balancing systems. It is however hard to implement that in the current gas
balancing system.
In conclusion, this thesis provides a gas balancing model recreating the entire gas
balancing market and its effects on shipper’s behavior. A deep analysis in system’s costs
is carried out including the electricity balancing side. The work in this thesis could be
expanded in order to better serve the TSO, but could also be used from shippers in
order to provide an optimized market behavior for profit maximization. Some test cases
could be expanded in a long-term horizon (some months or a year) in order to provide
further insight as to when the imbalance of the system lies outside the green zone and
which penalization should be implemented for better deal with the long-term imbalances.
Moreover, the interaction of the behavior of more than one shipper in the balancing
==PAGE:93==
7 Discussion and conclusion
75
model could be studied. Also, the gas day ahead market could be included in the model,
rendering thus the forecast of the E(SCB) more accurate. By including the day ahead
market and nominations from shippers for the next gas day, a proportional penalization
factor which penalizes more the shippers when the Expected System Commercial Balance
reaches close to green zone limits could be simulated. In this way, the shippers can
change their nominations two hours prior to real time, avoiding thus extra penalization
and also help the system to stay inside the green zone. Further work could be done for
the easier implementation of the hourly green zone definition, which seems to serve both
the TSO and the shippers. The hourly green zone definition cannot be implemented in
the current Danish system due to several reasons, like the hour that Energinet.dk trades
in the within day. Energinet.dk should be ready to intervene in the within day market
every hour of the gas day. Another reason is that in order to use an optimized hourly
green zone definition, a ‘perfect’ forecast of the gas demand in both systems should
be taken into account, which is only possible to do closer to real time. This however
contradicts the transparency of the gas balancing system.
More specifically, in this
case, the TSO will publish the green zone definition long after shipper’s renominations.
But shippers will give their renominations to Energinet.dk based on another definition
of the green zone, which will then change, thus not being transparent and ‘fair’ to
market players. A solution to that problem could come from the gas balancing market
in the Netherlands, where the hourly green zone limits are published before the start
of the gas day, giving thus the possibility to shippers to change their renominations. It
is nonetheless an interesting and possible improvement to consider in the Danish gas
balancing model.
