Chapter 1
Abstract
This study examines pose estimation for optical navigation of satellites operating in
close proximity. To this end, algorithms applicable to determine pose have been tested
against conditions seen in satellite navigation, and applied in experimental setups rep-
resentative of mission conditions. Physical tests with an optical stimulator have been
conducted to establish expected performance of the overall system, and used to build
image-based simulations of the upcoming PROBA-3 mission.
Experimental setups and tools were tested to evaluate accuracy and robustness, to ver-
ify their applicability to evaluate performance of pose estimation algorithms. Strategies
to determine accuracy & robustness of setups were conducted with varying degrees of
success, as camera calibration was challenging, but with successfully demonstrated con-
cepts of satellite approach, for both physical and simulated tests.
The RPnP-algorithm was determined to be the best suited to conduct navigation,
as it achieved high accuracy, low execution speed and decent robustness for tests of
algorithm merits, and was used in conjunction with an emulation of the Vision Based
Sensor (VBS) of optical stimulator. The emulated VBS, which performed the basic
tasks of the real system, was adequate to evaluate performance of pose estimation for
tests, and was proven to be robust towards outliers detected during image processing.
Position and attitude of the target during tests were estimated by the emulated VBS
for a simulated approach of partner-satellite, with overall accuracy meeting require-
ments established for PROBA-3 and former missions. The constructed simulator and
emulator are suited for future concept studies, but should be expanded with regards to
the simulator’s ability to model noise-ﬂoor and mire-position.

Chapter 3
Introduction
He is the best sailor who can steer within
fewest points of the wind, and exact a motive
power out of the greatest obstacles.
Walter Scott
The impact of satellites has increased in recent years, with tomorrows ever growing
demands increasing in rapid pace with the possibilities presented by yesterdays achieve-
ments. Satellite technology is desired by various scientiﬁc communities and industry,
due to the structured revisits and coverage it provides. With smaller satellites, the
number of applications for satellites operating in groups have expanded over the years,
as their capabilities have increased as well. These missions are intrinsically desirable,
as the use of smaller satellites over larger monolithic satellites, are often cheaper to
develop, deploy and spread the overall risk of the mission. This is especially true for
homogeneous satellite formations. This lends itself to a number of mission applications.
Larger scientiﬁc missions often entails the study of the environment, viewed passively as
light at diﬀerent wavelengths. This requires a telescope for suﬃcient photon-collection
and magniﬁcation, which pushes the design towards a long focal length, increasing the
overall size of the telescope, often resulting in the optics being displaced along a boom
extending from the primary bus. The large risks involved in space-missions generally
favors conservative designs to ensure reliability and overall survivability of missions,
which can justify building the detector and optics as separate spacecrafts, discarding
the need for large structural supports.
Outside of scientiﬁc applications, such formation ﬂying satellite operations can en-
tail maneuvers conducted in close proximity, such as when performing rendezvous or
docking. During rendezvous, one satellite approaches another in a controlled manner
allowing for operations such as station keeping with little separation between craft in
order to form an artiﬁcial telescope or allow for inspection of spacecraft.
7
==PAGE:9==
Rendezvous can transition into docking, if this separation is closed, establishing phys-
ical contact between mechanical interfaces of involved spacecraft. Docking allows for
servicing or salvaging of spacecraft - a discipline which is instrumental to build space
stations from multiple assemblies and sustain manned missions, exempliﬁed with sep-
aration and docking of the Apollo lunar module, along with access to and resupplying
the International Space Station. All these are also technologies to be improved upon
to facilitate NASAs plans for the next phases of exploration outside of Earth-orbit and
in particular for missions to Mars, providing amble future prospects for the technology
[38].
Figure 3.1: Left: Illustration of NuSTAR x-ray telescope.
Right: Illustration of
PROBA-3s Occulter and Coronograph spacecraft.
Close range formation ﬂying is not without its challenges, as compared to similar mono-
lithic missions: Operations in close proximity requires much more frequent updates of
relative position using thrusters to avoid collisions, with adjustments to orientation us-
ing reaction wheels. However, the relatively smaller size of involved craft, often means
less fuel & power capacity, necessitating that desired relative positions and attitudes be
achieved with strategies to ensure the least set of maneuvers are used, with small fuel
expenditure.
This hinges on accurate determination of relative position and attitude of involved
spacecrafts, with solutions requiring frequent updates. Satellites are said to co-operate
if they both contribute to keeping the formation, whether actively or passively, which
aids position-keeping. This can be achieved by having one of the spacecraft observe a
set of known features mounted on the partner spacecraft with a camera. The action of
relating the observed appearance of the features, with the known appearance and/or
dimensions of the features to obtain the relative position and attitude between observer
and observee (pattern), is known as pose estimation. The most prominent methodology
to perform this, is using a point-based pattern and solving the Perspective-n-Problem
(PnP). Depending on the size and shape of the pattern, the problem may not have an
analytical solution, leading to a host of diﬀerent algorithms and approaches to solve
the problem. They all aim to provide accurate, robust and timely solutions, but this is
seldom achieved for all encountered situations, and may lead to trade-oﬀs in algorithm-
selection.
8
==PAGE:10==
While the PnP problem is rooted in the ﬁelds of photogrammetry and computer vi-
sion, the mission environment for space application introduces some challenges to pose
estimation, outside of the inherent limits to PnP-algorithms, in part brought on by
the smaller amount of information in the environment and conditions of the system
operated in space. In this study PnP-algorithms are deployed in conditions similar to
that of the outlined PROBA-3 mission, in order to perform pose estimation for the
challenging phase of close range formation ﬂight: rendezvous maneuvering.

Chapter 4
Project Formulation
The primary goal of this study is to compare performance of algorithms that perform
pose estimation and to do so, in an environment representative of that in which optical
navigation would be deployed for satellite formation ﬂying. The PROBA-3 mission is
used as a basis for this, with focus on the functionalities of the Vision Based Sensor
(VBS), that is set to determine relative pose between involved spacecraft for said mis-
sion.
Physical experiments with an optical stimulator have been carried out to this end.
The stimulator consisted of an engineering model of DTU Space Instrumentation Groups
in-house micro Advanced Stellar Compass (µASC ). The VBS is implemented as part of
µASC , but has been emulated in MATLAB for this project, in order to relate imagery
captured with the µASC with PnP algorithms included in this study. The emulator
mimics the basic procedures of the true VBS, but does not replicate them.
To evaluate how the µASC may perform when used for true missions, a selection of
tests were based oﬀof the planned maneuvers of the PROBA-3 Rendezvous Exper-
iment (RVX), which were conducted with both physical equipment and simulations.
Data captured from physical experiments were combined in a simulation-package to
conduct tests for a large range of target-poses, while retaining realism, by empirical
modeling of the relevant parameters from physical tests.
Study of accuracy and robustness of the experimental setups were conducted in con-
junction with tests. Accuracy was evaluated on the basis of available tools, physical
references in the test environment and evaluation of built tools. The robustness-study
evaluated overall VBS emulator performance and included tests for worst case scenar-
ios, based on viewing illuminated target mockups, which included false features for pose
estimation.
10
==PAGE:12==
The above systems and setups of this study, are evaluated according to the following
questions:
• How accurate are the considered algorithms? How fast do algorithms provide
solutions?
• How robust are the algorithms and the overall VBS? How well do they handle
pose ambiguities, excessive/lack of features and other faults?
• How well does the simulator recreate the real tests?
• How far can we extend the accuracy of experimental setup? Can it be done with
modest means?
• Finally, can the fully emulated system complete the mission? Assuming the em-
ulator replaced the planned VBS on the PROBA-3 Chaser-craft, would the emu-
lator be able to provide pose estimations adequate to meet demands outlined for
the VBS of PROBA-3?

4.1
Project Limitations
The photon-budget for RVX or other tests are not formulated in this study, as the
imagery produced during testing were not captured with settings directly related to
the pulse-time and synchronization of the µASC and LEDs of the Target Plate, as
they are to be used for PROBA-3.
The considered distances of the mockup-model
and exposure-times of the µASC did allow for the LEDs to be viewed in all test. Full
characterization of the utilized LEDs has also not been carried out. Instead the study
assumes the LEDs have near-uniform radiation-cones, similar to conditions for formerly
built PRISMA-mockups [5].
PnP-algorithm-codes has courteously been made available by Chi Xu, co-creator of
RPnP [26], which is lent for this study and used as is. The source code is located on
the webpage, [54]. A number of PnP-algorithms were prototyped, working from papers
but were not ﬁnished prior to conducting tests and disregarded as RPnP was found
to be a suitable candidate, instead focusing on construction of the emulator, simulator
and other tools for experiments. Integration of algorithms with µASC have not been
carried out. All codes have been implemented/included and tested on MATLAB ver-
sions 2016a and 2017a.
Information about µASC , PROBA-3 and its planned maneuvers have been obtained
from public and conﬁdential sources, and reported where aspects were necessary for the
study. In some cases with the latter sources or scarce/limited information, assumptions
have been made, instead either relying on model performance or former missions.
Two Camera Head Units (CHUs) are available with the µASC -system, allowing targets
to be resolved with focal lengths of 20 and 100 mm, but only the former has been used
for physical tests in this study. Focus is kept on the 20 mm camera for physical tests,
as nearly all resolvable abstractions of the target under test, can be obtained during
physical testing. The author is well-aware that the system installed on PROBA-3 is to
make use of both CHUs, and change between use of the two, and indeed that a large
portion of the dynamic range of which the VBS is to operate, uses the 100 mm CHU.
It was therefore necessary to simulate said camera-capture for rendezvous-simulations,
relying on the camera-intrinsics presented in Section 11.1.2.
This project was carried out over extended time, with physical test setups taken down
over the course of the project, making some of the reporting for physical tests more
sparse than originally intended, and the study shifting focus towards means to simulate
test conditions. As this book spans a larger number of topics, the presented tests are
divided into smaller studies, using a large number of ﬁgure and section references, to
keep the narrative intact for each topic.

Nomenclature & Formalia
• Vectors are denoted with bar on top, e.g. A, matrices in both bold and with bar
on top, e.g. ¯A.
• Intensity is only used to described the 256 levels of both the grayscale 8-bit
images captured during experiments, and simulated imagery, written explicitly as
I ∈[0, 255]. Pixels is used both as a reference to elements of the image plane,
and as a unit of displacement along either camera axes and written px when used
as such.
• Vision Based Sensor (VBS) refers to the extended functionality of the µASC .
• Target Plate designate the LED-board used for physical test, while Target Pattern
refers to the projection of the Target Plate. Target may refer to either of the two
depending on the context.
• Code build and used throughout the project has been attached to a zip-folder and
uploaded to DTU Inside. A read-me is part of the folder, which provides a small
overview of codes and programs.
• Coordinates for placement of Target in the camera’s frame of reference has been
used interchangeably , where this is applicable.
• Video-material referenced in the study and a compilation of videos from physi-
cal tests and simulations carried out under this study, have been collected and
uploaded to a Youtube-account associated with this project. URL to visit account:
https://www.youtube.com/channel/UC6skRjEvR_fsI7tKfEpVtTg?view_as=subscriber.

Chapter 6
Report Structure
The Theory chapter describes aspects the environment, pose estimation and aspects
from former missions to form a basis for the interest of this study. Design & Method-
ology of Investigation present the tools and environment available for tests, describes
the challenges to be tested for and presents the full list of experiments conducted in
the study. The investigation is divided into four parts, to handle the formulated prob-

Chapter 8
Theory
In this section, theory relevant to the mission-parameters of PROBA-3s VBS-based
ﬂight are presented, providing an overview of formation ﬂying, relative navigation,
considerations and means to facilitate optical navigation and the perspective-n-problem,
in relation to the perspective projection as a model for camera systems. This leads
into a description of the Vision Based Sensor (VBS) and PROBA-3 and its associated
optical-navigation experiments.
8.1
Formation Flying and Relative Navigation
Satellite Formation Flight (FF) entails a group of satellites ﬂying in the same orbit
with relative small separation of craft, usually in the order of 250-5,000 m. This is a
novel prospect of space ﬂight, as it allows for smaller satellites to replace singular ad-
vanced satellites, e.g. for satellite-borne-radar, where several smaller craft can make up
a collection of phased arrays, eﬀectively projecting a much larger synthetic aperture as
a consequence of their separation, increasing spatial resolution of captured imagery [31].
Formation ﬂight does however involve stringent position-keeping of all involved craft,
not just to achieve science objectives that require accurate baselines to combine the
crafts individual observations, but also for remaining in control of the spacecrafts. The
discipline also requires a larger degree of automation than singular ﬂights, as active
collision avoidance becomes a requirement, where the crafts may automatically per-
form maneuvers to avert potential collisions, as it requires fast reaction times that are
not possible with a ground station in the loop for such actions. Without automation,
an error in position e.g. from overshooting the burn-duration of the thruster of an
actuator-system, may otherwise result in gross misalignment of involved craft or in
the worst case, cause a collision, before ground control can intervene. To avoid such
incidences, position corrections also have to occur more frequently This stricter require-
17
==PAGE:19==
ment also calls for much more frequent position-corrections, increasing the demand for
thrust-expenditure relative to singular ﬂights, which further emphasizes the need for
precise navigation for optimal fuel usage, as this in turn dictates the overall lifetime of
the mission [19].
The Guidance, Navigation and Control (GNC) subsystem of a satellite fulﬁlls this
task, encompassing sensors to extract position of its companion satellite and relate this
to its own heading and if working automatically, to respond to the companions position
accordingly, controlling the on-board actuator-system. The considered Vision Based
Sensor system makes up the ﬁrst link in this chain, that relays the Target position &
attitude to the on-board computer.
Missions may solely entail maintaining a stable separation of spacecraft to achieve mis-
sion objectives, but more than often, involves relative movement of spacecrafts. This
expands the formations overall capabilities, enabling resupply or servicing of Target.
This closer proximity demands rendezvous of involved spacecraft - with the goal of
docking the Chaser with Target or achieve extreme proximity.
The overall mission
phases are relevant for both formation ﬂight and rendezvous, with the latter forming
the basis for VBS-experiments with PROBA-3. The close range phase of the mission,
where VBS becomes applicable is presented Section 8.1.2.
8.1.1
Mission Phases
Spacecraft rendezvous is subdivided into diﬀerent mission phases, depending on the
separation of involved craft. Diﬀerent nomenclatures are utilized in literature; for this
book the phases involving distances which deploy VBS will lend from [5], and elaborated
in Section 8.5.3, with the following rendezvous mission phases lending the terminology
from [19], ordered chronologically: Launch, Phasing, Far Range Rendezvous, Close
Range Rendezvous and Mating. The description assumes a formation of two spacecrafts,
with one craft mounted with sensors to obtain position (the Chaser), moving relative
to the companion craft (the Target), with the Target remaining passive for maneuvers
beyond mission phases of phasing. Details refer back to Figure 8.1.
Launch: Mission launches may occur separately or with satellites launched in docked
stages. Due to the large fuel required to change orbital parameters, missions using
optical guidance systems, have thus far been launched and deployed in docked conﬁgu-
ration, meaning operations conducted can be isolated to small changes best described
by a local frame of reference. However, spacecrafts/modules may be launched within
diﬀerent launch windows, such as is the case with the continued rendezvous conducted
between the ISS and supply-craft.
This requires that the Chaser has to reduce its
phase-angle relative to the Target. This part of the mission is known as Phasing, with
adjustments often carried out at perigee to change the impulse of the spacecraft, ∆v.
For both Launch and Phasing, positions are often monitored with Earth centered and

8.1.2
Relative Navigation
During phasing, absolute position of the craft is reported in one or more Earth-centered
frames of reference, helping ground control keep track of the spacecrafts orbit. As it
will be presented for the available sensors, greater precision is achieved with sensors
that report the position of the Target, relative to the Chaser outﬁtted with the sensor
in question. To this end, we introduce a coordinate system to encompass the relevant
parameters.
To describe the relative position a series of reference frames are necessary: the camera
on the Chaser observes a pattern on the Target, which allows the on-board VBS to
determine the pose of the Chaser relative to the Target. The extracted pose becomes
the coordinates of the known pattern-frame (referred to as the world-frame from liter-
ature), relative to the camera-frame. These two respective frames become the Chaser
and Target frames respectively, as seen in the top of Figure 8.2, with origins displaced
from the two frames related to the sensor-frames; camera- and world-frame. This extra
abstraction is introduced, as GNC works relative to the center of mass (CoM), since
this provides the principle axis of rotation. The actuator-systems are designed to cause
movement around the CoM, to control cross-coupled rotations to as large extension as
possible, that would otherwise result in tumbling of the spacecraft, due to inertia bias
buildup. But as the considered PROBA-3 spacecraft do not employ strategies such as
a hybrid or pure spinning of the main-structure, cross-coupled rotations will develop,
making pure rotations around any of the principal axes uncommon [21, p. 64-71]. As
the target models used for test do no make up any properly deﬁned craft, we concern
ourselves solely with reporting the world frame relative to the camera frame.
The available amount of information regarding the relative Target position, depends
on the resolvability of the Target. The simplest reported position is the Line-Of-Sight
(LOS), pinning the center of the Target, in the image-plane, relative to the optical axis
of the camera. This can be reported in angular coordinates of elevation & azimuth
(El,Az), as illustrated in lower left of Figure 8.2. To avoid confusion with position,
which commonly refers to 3D Cartesian coordinates, this will be referred to as LOS or
LOS-position.
Range to target can be obtained as the overall translation, tWC, of the world frame rel-
ative to the camera-frame, which constitutes the three translational degrees-of-freedom
(3DoF) reported relative to the camera: (XC, YC, ZC). The attitude or orientation, of
the Target is described as the rotations around the three principal axes of the Target
frame, with ZT-axis normal to the face of the front-panel of the Target. While roll, yaw
and pitch often refer to the action of movement of aircraft, it is used here to denote
the angle along each principal axis of the Target, relative to the attitude of the Target
when facing the Chaser at a completely normal angles, which will be referred to as the
standard pose of the Target, as this is desired attitude for rendezvous and docking.

8.2
Equipment for Navigation and Sensor Input
8.2.1
Sensors for navigation
Satellites utilize Global Positioning System (GPS) to obtain absolute position. Improve-
ment consists of utilizing diﬀerential and relative GPS, further improving the overall
accuracy of the systems by combining the received GPS signals from receivers of both
Target and Chaser, to ﬁlter out noise via diﬀerencing of signals, and increase overall
accuracy. Such systems are commonly limited to position errors of circa 10 m, without
providing pose and hence are mainly utilized during phasing [13, Ch. 5].
Sensors for relative navigation are commonly divided between Radio Frequency (RF)-
and optically based metrologies, respectively working in wavelength intervals of 105 to
10−3 m and from 10−3 m and below. The former consists of systems either interfacing
with GPS, comparing signals of receivers onboard both Target and Chaser or Time-of-
Flight (ToF) measurements carried out by an on-board system. While RF can operate
in various conditions and obtain decent accuracy, they also introduce larger mass and
power requirements, relative to optical missions. Unless multiple antenna are deployed,
only LOS and range of target can be determined. Measurements may furthermore be
disturbed by multipathing of radiation incident on Target-craft, resulting in multiple
received transmissions only nanoseconds apart that increase the noise-ﬂoor of transmis-
sion, aﬀecting the overall accuracy of the system, as subsequent signals are occluded [36].
Optically based metrology consists of cameras and laser rangeﬁnders working within the
infra-red and visible part of the electromagnetic spectrum. Their working principle is
either passive detection of radiation or active ranging (ToF-measurement), applicable to
cameras and LIDAR respectively. Metrologies for shorter wavelengths are ill-suited for
these applications, as eﬃciency and resolution of such systems are not mature enough
to characterize structures [36]. As with the choice of any spacecraft subsystem, the
primary constraints of sensor choice are size, mass and power. This is counterweighted
by the need for redundancy and robustness of the overall system. LIDAR can provide
stable 3D-point references from ToF-measurements at upwards of 10 km, but with ﬂex-
ibility of cameras to be designed to a large dynamic range, spanning LOS of Targets in
excess of 500 km and 6DoF down to a few meters separation, means that such systems
can also handle various mission objectives, making it a very versatile tool. Combining
cameras with diﬀerent magniﬁcation allows the overall system to span the aforemen-
tioned operation window, which is part of the design philosophy of the µASC presented
in Section 8.5.

8.2.2
Features for cooperative rendezvous
Spacecrafts come in many shapes and sizes but are predominantly built according to
conservative designs, which often results in box-shaped satellites, with few distinctive
features outside of solar panel(s) & antenna(s). VBS relies on optical recognition of
stable features of the object it searches for, which is not guaranteed for all operation
modes for space application. This can be solved by extracting low-level geometry such
as edges, lines and curves of the apparent Target, but it can more often result in am-
biguous solutions for few geometric features [11]. This can be a quite a challenging, as
it relies solely on the sensor of the Chaser to resolve pose of Target. This ﬁeld is known
as non-cooperative tracking [36].
To improve conditions to utilize VBS, known points or features-of-interest are added to
the spacecraft, known as ﬁducials. As the Target is outﬁtted with ﬁducials, it provides
information to facilitate pose estimation and hence the problem to be solved is referred
to as cooperative tracking. Fiducials for pose estimation are divided between types of
passive and active conﬁguration: active ﬁducials typically involve optical emission, such
as the use of LEDs or lasers reﬂected by corner reﬂectors, whereas passive ﬁducials rely
solely on ambient lighting and distinctive patterns painted on the craft.

budgets of the mission. However the dynamic range for usage is severely lower as it
relies on external ambient or active lighting. It is better suited for very close proximity
such as during docking, which has been successfully carried out with NextSat. Exam-

Circumventing problems of illumination by providing the chaser with its own light-
source can be done as has been demonstrated with the Orbital Express mission using
corner-reﬂectors. However the radiometric link budget, e.g. how much power is used to
transmit a signal viable for processing, is eﬀected by the two-way travel path of the light
from transmission, reﬂection and receival. Alternately the Target can be outﬁtted with
local light-sources which has been use for the Engineering Test Satellite-7, PRISMA
and planned for PROBA-3, as it cuts the signals travel path in half, and eﬀectively
reduces power requirements by a factor proportional to the inverse distance. LEDs
are often selected as lightsource, due to their larger illumination cones and reliability
relative to lasers. The approach does however require a powered Target, as opposed
to a Target using retro-reﬂectors, where the reﬂectors can still aid cooperative track-
ing in case the Target is derelict, if they retroﬁtted in advance for potential salvaging.
The Target-illuminating setup is further improved by synchronizing transmission and
shutter-time of the camera on board the Chaser, providing an improved radiometric
link budget.

8.2.3
Interpreting and Processing Imagery for Optical Navi-
gation
With ﬁducials present in the camera frame, information viable for pose estimation is to
be determined. Low-level image processing operators such as binary threshold forms the
ﬁrst step, dividing the image into foreground and background. However other features
may be present in the camera frame, which is can be falsely interpreted as ﬁducials if no
countermeasures are in place. Systems can be designed to help alleviate this task, such
as by capturing two images for scenes with and without ﬁducials present, to segment
via diﬀerencing the images or synchronizing the camera-shutter with LED-ﬂashes, as
used for the Orbital Express mission & PRISMA respectively.
Figure 8.4: Imagery captured by Video Guidance Sensor (VGS) of the Orbital Express
mission for 10 m separation, rendezvous and docking. Left: NextSat viewed in low
contrast. Middle: NextSat illuminated by sunlight. Right: NextSat viewed with the
Earth in the background. Images courtesy of DARPA, image source [52].
After culling features which are not related to the ﬁducials, information can be extracted
via a number of diﬀerent approaches. Feature-based processing searches imagery for
distinctive features present, relating them to a model of the sought object via template
matching. This is commonly implemented via Normalized Cross Correlation (NCC),
scanning the entire image for a match between the image and a template, selected from
a host of images [13, chapter 7] However this technique is computationally heavy, as it
relies on repeated window-operations carried out across the entire image. Other feature
extraction techniques such as the Scale Invariant Feature Transform (SIFT) also have
diﬃculty in providing unique feature-matches, as spacecraft often have several axes of
symmetry, providing ambiguous results, with multiple facets being perceived as being
identical [43].
An alternative approach is to obtain the LOS-position of ﬁducials. Each ﬁducial is
isolated as a separate foreground-region (blob) via a binary threshold. Each separated
region is then labeled for later identiﬁcation of the regions. Within each region, the
26
==PAGE:28==
associated pixels make up a structure for which the centroid can be computed. The
centroid for each blob is reported as the LOS-position of a given ﬁducial. Relative to
the other schemes, this setup is far less computationally expensive.
For a focused camera, the size of an LED-ﬁducial in the frame does not take up
much more than an area of one pixel, making centroid-determination imprecise. Using
diﬀraction-limited cameras, defocussing of optics introduces blur in the imaged scene,
which smears the light incident from the ﬁducial across a larger area, which improves
accuracy of centroid determination, but limits the range for which the camera can be
operated, as blending of the blur discs occur at closer distance to Target, relative to
not using a diﬀraction-limited system. It is therefore often used for closer proximity,
where demands to accuracy are also higher.
The camera system is also aﬀected by various internal sources of noise such as shot
noise stemming from occurrences of charges in the system, quantization as the images
are limited to 8-bit and by thermal gradients. These all contribute to the overall noise-
ﬂoor of the system when performing optical navigation. In this book, the noise-ﬂoor is
described with the mean and standard deviation of the noise, µI and σI respectively.
The noise-ﬂoor should overall have low variability. However, its impact on centroid
estimation is more severely aﬀected at ranges further away, as the relative intensity
added to blobs for estimation becomes greater, causing greater shifts of the centroid
within the blob. The bias and spread of estimated centroid due to noise-ﬂoor is denoted
by µpx and σpx respectively.
Operational range of the mission is also dictated by both the radiation intensity of
mires inverse-square-distance and the received power from undesired light entering the
image chip of the camera, referred to as ambient light. Such sources include stars, the
sun and celestial bodies, the latter of which is seen in the right-hand side of Figure 8.4.
Another source for ambient light include solar light-reﬂectance oﬀof the companion
spacecraft. Spacecraft are covered in Multi-Layer-Insulation (MLI), with mylar mak-
ing up the top layer, in order to reﬂect solar light, while transporting heat generated
within the spacecraft bus outwards, to ensure thermal stability of the vessel. Mylar
may reﬂect upwards of 98-100% of incident light. When strewn across a spacecraft,
the blanket is curled, which ensures less conductance within MLI, which also results
in less light being reﬂected oﬀof the spacecraft towards other craft, but the eﬀect can
still be quite signiﬁcant. From investigating related literature and inspecting ﬁlm-reels
captured from cameras used for close-range formation ﬂight 2, it became apparent that
ﬂares and veiling glares, presented some of the greatest challenges to robustness of VBS.
Throughout this book, image or mylar glares refers to light reﬂected oﬀof the Target,
observed by the camera of the Chaser. This phenomena is taken into consideration for
design of experiments.

8.3
Perspective projection & pose estimation
Here we shall consider the case of determining the relative orientation in close proximity
for the 6DOF consisting of euclidean three dimensional translation, and the attitude
described by pitch, yaw and roll of the Target, relative to the Chaser. These are to
be determined relative to a common reference. Due to the measurement frames used,
these are related to the camera and world frames outlined in Section 8.1.2, as we simply
consider the system without considering the vehicle-frames.
The methodology follows the disciplines of photogrammetry. It is instructive to consider
the pinhole camera model that uses the concept of world space and projected space:
objects viewed in the world space are projected onto the image plane of the camera,
passing through the center of projection. The ﬁducials of interest, the world points, are
directly linked to the image points projected onto the image plane, as light rays follow
paths of similar triangles; the corresponding world and image points and center of per-
spective are collinear with the image points under the ideal pinhole camera-model. The
full perspective projection is elaborated in the following. This is visualized in Figure
8.5. Throughout this study, u- and v-axis will always refer back to the image coordinate
system.

8.3.3
The Perspective-n-Point Problem
The Perspective-n-Point (PnP) problem is illustrated referring back to Figure 8.5. We
seek to derive a pose for the world points P with known relative placement, and relate
30
==PAGE:32==
it to the camera-coordinate system. First considering the three world points located
in isolated corners of the Target Pattern, these three-dimensional world points project
a triangle onto the image-plane, following the dashed light rays, observed as image
points, Q. Without knowledge of the depth along ZC, the projected triangle can only
be constrained along the rays, and hence for a given image, this provides four diﬀerent
valid solutions or perspectives from which the world points can be viewed from. This
ambiguity in choice of perspective gives rise to the PnP problem. By increasing the
number of known points in view, the potential solutions can be further constrained,
seen as the added fourth point makes for a unique solution, in the case of a co-planar-
point conﬁguration.
The original problem of determining the center of perspective from perspective-n-points
was originally posed by Fischer & Bolles, outlaying analytical solutions to cases of 3
and 4 points, introducing the Random Sample Consensus (RANSAC)-technique to sort
potential outliers amongst observed image points, and otherwise iterate through possi-
ble permutations until the misﬁt error between sampled points and model pattern was
suﬃciently minimized [20]. RANSAC is often employed to help sort viable image-points
to establish correspondence with model-points.
Algorithms used in the ﬁeld can be divided into two broad categories to solve the PnP-
problem: non-iterative algorithms (semi-analytical solutions) and iterative algorithms.
Since analytical solutions to PnP are highly dependent on n, non-iterative algorithms
often employ methods to build a system of equations, solving it with Singular Value
Decomposition (SVD) to condition the system of equations. Such methods have poorer
performance set against noise as they do not reﬁne pose. Iterative algorithms seek to
minimize a cost function, by parameterizing Equation 8.1 and formulating a suitable
strategy to minimize residuals of model ﬁtting.
Current trends make a trade-oﬀbetween the execution speed of analytical solutions
and the higher precision of iterative methodology, which can be combined into hybrid
solutions. The real and synthetic scenarios constructed for experiments in the inves-
tigated literature often perform pose estimation for computer vision, with objects of
interest in close proximity, several image-model-point correspondences and for targets
with three dimensional constellation of points, which eases pose estimation. The fol-
lowing provides descriptions of considered candidates to solve PnP.

8.3.4.1
Algorithm Selection
Sharma & D’Amico recently surveyed state-of-the-art, testing EPnP, POSIT and Newton-
Raphson (similar to Gauss-Newton), using arbitrary target patterns with non-coplanar
points [43]. While Newton-Raphson provided the most accurate results, both POSIT &
EPnP were much faster to execute, with POSIT being more robust to special situations
than EPnP, such as extreme proximity between Chaser and Target, where the initial
assumption of orthographic scaling works well.
Since the Target Pattern will be of coplanar point conﬁguration, POSIT is not an
option. P4P was not ﬁnalized for this study and is therefore reserved for future work.
Hence, the performance of GN, LHM, EPnP and RPnP are investigated, which gives
two sets of iterative and non-iterative algorithms to evaluate. GN is set to use pose esti-
mated from RPnP as initial guess, as the supplied EPnP already uses a Gauss-Newton
based optimization to select the best null-space for solutions found via SVD.
8.3.5
Pose Ambiguity
Determining change in rotation of a planar surface is more diﬃcult when the surface
is viewed head- or edge-on. The problem was ﬁrst outlined by Oberkampf et al [35],
providing proof that such cases would give rise to at least two indistinguishable so-
lutions, for baselines that experience little apparent movement in the camera-frame,
making the phenomena harder to solve for at greater distance to target. If algorithms
adequately handle pose-ambiguities, success-rates should be signiﬁcantly higher than
50%, and hence average rotation error should be below half that of the interval between
the ambiguous cases. E.g. if the ambiguity is encountered for a true pose of yaw = 15◦,
algorithms without attention to the problem should change between rotation errors of
0◦and30◦(mistaking true pose for yaw = −15◦), resulting in a mean-error of 15◦. This
problem can be remedied when adding a non-coplanar-point, to the set of ﬁducials, as
such points have a perceived larger degree of movement in the frame. The full set of
considerations are shown in Figure 8.7. Ensuring algorithms can handle such ambigui-
ties regardless of point conﬁguration is investigated for PnP-performance comparisons
in this study.

individual points are based oﬀof euclidean distance between them, selecting correspon-
dence on the basis of obtaining the smallest error across all point-correspondences. This
is still dependent on angular relationship, but this should be quite robust for the outer
frame as the subtended angle is 90◦.
8.4.1
The N-point-problem
The correspondence problem is directly solvable with any of the formerly outlined meth-
ods above, for the case of the total number of detected points, exactly equating the num-
ber of mires, NDetected = NMires, assuming that only the mires are in view. However,
cases with NDetected = NMires + NOutlier are encountered, as the number of resolvable
mires is also a function of range to Target, and the fact that outliers might be present.
This gives rise to two problems to be handled prior to solving the correspondence prob-
lem (referred here to here as n-point-situations):
NDetected > NMires: This case may occur for outliers brought on by image glares.
These points naturally have to be sorted from the true mires in order to solve the
correspondence-problem, but depending on their placement, they may be mistaken for
true mires, causing gross errors for pose estimation.
NDetected < NMires: Fewer points may be encountered at sharp orientations, where
mires may be partially occluded, in case mires have been turned oﬀ, or more com-
monly, for range modes were the full pattern is not resolvable. For such cases, the
correspondence solver must correctly identify the situation and adjust the model pat-
tern used for ﬁtting to establish proper correspondence of points. This is referred to as
changing range modes, which is elaborated on in Section 8.5.1.

8.5.2
VBS Modules
For a given range and mode, the basic operation of the VBS can be described by the
three modules: Image Processing, Mire Identiﬁcation and Pose Estimation.
Image Processing: This module analyzes the images captured with the CHU and deter-
mines the centroids of objects-of-interest or blobs in the imagery. For this procedure, it
may be instructive to consider one of the ﬁrst optical relative navigation systems, The
Advanced Video Guidance Sensor (AVGS), with outlined diagram in Figure 8.12.
Mire Identiﬁcation: Each of the computed centroids needs to be checked against the
Target Pattern, to determine if the individual centroid is associated with any individual
mire, solving the correspondence and n-point problem, relating to techniques of Section
8.4.
Pose Estimation: With an established correspondence between mires located in the
input image and their position on the Target Pattern, the pose may be determined by
solving the Perspective-n-Problem, using one of the algorithms presented in Section
8.3.3.
8.5.3
Modes for VBS
While the VBS system functions as one coherent unit, it is presented here using two
mode-distinctions, to highlight two of the fundamental challenges to cooperative track-
ing, to be solved by the emulator. Both modes operate the primary VBS-modules:
Target Mode: Performing and switching between acquisition and tracking of Target, if
it is correctly identiﬁed. Tracking mode is used in order to ease the computational load
of extracting pose.
Operational Mode: Switches between the range-interval to Target. Target Mode is run
on all diﬀerent operations modes, but the utility of the VBS modules change according
to the amount of available position and attitude information.

8.5.3.2
Acquisition & Tracking of Target
When VBS is ﬁrst initialized, it has no prior information about the Target, and has to
search its surroundings to acquire the Target. This usually entails having the Chaser
steadily reorienting itself in a spiral-pattern, until acquisition of Target is achieved,
in a manner similar to that outlined for NASAs Ku-band-radar system for Shuttle-
rendezvous [33], with the span of the spiral depending on the range to Target; the
range would have to provided a priori otherwise the system is to default search out to
the maximum-range of VBS. Acquisition requires a lot of information-gathering: the
range mode has to be established to dictate how further processing is performed, the
mires have to be found in the scene, and identiﬁed correctly before the centroids can
be used as input for the pose estimation module.
Once acquisition has been completed, it is preferable to reuse this information for the
consecutive frames, e.g. perform tracking of Target within small search regions of the

formerly detected mires, since the Target can only move a small distance in between
frames for continuous tracking. If the Target is lost during tracking, the VBS must
revert back to search for and acquire the Target. This gives rise to the two distinctive
acquisition and tracking modes, which both Orbital Express and PRISMA have made
use of, and PROBA-3 is planned to do so as well [24]. The procedure for Target modes

8.6
PROBA-3
ESAs PROBA-3 mission consists of two micro-satellites with the primary objective of
increasing technology readiness of formation ﬂight, building upon the former successes
of the ESA-supported missions TanDEM-X and PRISMA. The combined metrologies of
the satellites consist of a scientiﬁc payload: The pair make up a coronagraph to study
the Sun, with one satellite carrying the telescope, the Coronagraph Space-Craft (CSC),
while the other acts a pylon to block out the umbra of the Sun for observations of
the corona, the Occulter Space-Craft (OSC). The satellites share near-identical highly
elliptic orbits with perigee and apogee of 600 km and 60,530 km respectively, resulting
in an orbital period of 19.7 hours, with around 6 hours of the orbit being viable for
testing VBS-based navigation, during portions of the orbit occurring close to aphelion.
This diminishes inﬂuence of gravitational perturbations of position and attitude, with
the time constraint being set by the thermal tolerance of the craft [7].
With the large time margin for operating VBS and the demand for VBS to be op-
erated autonomously, the demands to maneuvers are as a consequence very conserva-
tive, using very small adjustments of ∆v, with frequent updates and relying largely on
forced-motion of the orbit throughout maneuvers, as drift-errors of maneuvers of just
1 mm/s may in the worst case otherwise propagate to cause upwards of 21.6 km sepa-
ration between craft in the time-span of one orbit. The remainder of the orbit-time is
spent in sun-pointing conﬁguration, which will charge the batteries via the solar panels
oriented normal to the sun-pointing-angle [9].

discrepancy between ground truth and measurements are determined from residuals,
which are reported for absolute values in this book unless stated otherwise1. Precision

without a ground truth, such as the reported imprecision of estimated centroids.

precise, use small increments of angle or distance (high resolution) and have a large op-

9.2
Accuracy & Robustness of Experimental Setups
Each test presented herein requires a well-established model accuracy, which is best
achieved with stable physical references. With the added extremely high precision of
the LTS, the pneumatic table provides the best model-reference of this study, but does
only allow for displacing the Target up to 4.2 m, with little space in the room for
camera-mounting outside the table. In terms of increasing model accuracy, the ideal
mount for the Target Plate would involve a gimbal with ball-bearings providing full an-
gular freedom, and with all rotational axis coinciding at the same origin, such as might
be available. Aside from being a costly solution, with examples priced at 7,000-11,000
USD for high precision of 1.56◦·10−4 and accuracy of 0.16◦[45], the reported accura-
cies from the larger rotation stage and exempliﬁed quadrants should be adequate to
evaluate pose estimation. Work conducted in the frame of the pneumatic table should
be reported with sub-cm and sub-degree precision. This shifts the focus back to the
main-drawback of the setup in Optolab: Lack of operational range.
Distances within Optolab are too close for the CHU-Long to be utilized. The adja-
cent room is connected to Optolab with and opening for a ventilation shaft, allowing
for potential targets to be viewed up to 25 m away, but with relative movement still
restricted by the limited freedom of movement of the LTS. For creating rendezvous-
scenarios, the translational range of 0.3 m of the LTS provides a small window for
ranges, requiring the Target to either be moved between setups, or to scale the Target
down.
The adjacent laboratory, Robot Navigation Laboratory, houses a robot-arm-mounted
camera, but challenges present themselves as the environment and scene all have to
be scaled down accordingly. This includes using a camera with smaller focal length,
diﬀerent intrinsic camera parameters, noise-ﬂoor among other aspects.
The greatest limitation is to compensate for the downscaling of the mires, as the expe-
rienced blur and intensity will be very diﬀerent from that of a true-dimensions-model,
which in turn will aﬀect how well the VBS emulator will represent the real system,
as opposed to the Target Plate in use, which is mounted with LEDs representative of
those to be used for PROBA-3. This is not to say the robot-arm setup could not have
been used for the study, exploring a high degree of pose variations with high ﬁdelity,
but focus was kept using the same µASC throughout physical testing.

9.2.3
Ensuring Model Robustness
Aside from ensuring performance of the VBS emulator can handle the various range
modes, encountered, establish correspondence and reject outlier points, robustness is
studied for worst-case scenario conditions for establishing correspondence of mires.
The outlined discussion regarding captured imagery from missions using close range
optical navigation in Section 8.2.3, led to the creation of tests that would represent
the expected imagery, with worst-case-scenarios in mind, where facets, crevasses and
ridges of deformed mylar, reﬂect light in such a manner, that it would be mistaken for
true mires as the glares may occur in very close proximity to them. For PROBA-3s
Target-pointing conﬁguration, the critical angle spanned between craft and direction to
the Sun is 30◦in order to ensure light reﬂected oﬀof the Target, is directed away from

the Chaser.
However, glares are some of the most challenging hazards to system robustness if en-
countered, and good doctrine to ensure robustness of the system calls for safeguarding
against such a possible occurrence. With a system designed to handle glares, it may be
extended to outliers located further away from the Target in the camera plane, making
the overall system more robust towards outliers.
9.2.3.1
Modelling the Glares from Mylar-frame
Preliminary tests were conducted to determine viability of the setup. A dedicated source
to simulate the spectrum of the Sun was required to get a representative dataset. This
was not available and the development of such was outside the scope of the project.
Instead a single LED (with similar characteristics of the mires) was placed 80 cm from
the Target, facing the center of the plate acting as a solar simulator. Light incident
from the sun would be near-parallel in the real situation, but given the relative distance
of the LED, some rays may hit the edges at angles upwards of 22◦. Due to this, the
majority of light reﬂected will be located around the center of the Target Plate, than
at the edges. Furthermore the perceived reﬂections will be heavily altered with smaller
incidence angler at greater distance for the approach-tests.
Unsurprisingly, the reﬂected light only show up as dark smudges, barely distinguishable
from the background, if both the solar simulator and the mires are viewed by the CHU.
For these setups, each pose was recorded with the two light-sources separately, ensuring
to set shutter time and oﬀset/gain to enhance visibility of both sources optimally and
later superimpose them, as exempliﬁed in Figure 9.7. In the example it is quite notice-

9.3.1
Fault Tree Analysis
To provide an overview of the requirements to calibration and validation of the VBS
system, Fault Tree Analysis is conducted to provide an overview of how possible errors
may propagate through the system. This methodology has been utilized in many ﬁelds
of engineering to ensure high reliability and safety margins in particular for aerospace.
The outlined analysis adopts terminology from [3] [37]; a full description also calls for
calculating the probabilities of the diﬀerent events occurring, but this is not attempted
here, instead emphasizing a qualitative description of the type of errors experienced for
VBS. Both the description of the approach to the analysis and a detailing of the basic
events follow here, that are both related to the fault tree presented in Figure 9.9.
The ﬁrst step is to identify the problem of interest, deﬁned as the top-event, which
in this case is deﬁned as Failure to obtain acceptable pose estimation, as this is the
primary function of the VBS. Next the types of diﬀerent outcomes that may lead to
the top event are outlined. These consists of diﬀerent basic events, which may cause
the outcome on their own or may require other basic events to take place. The former
requires the addition of an OR-gate while the latter requires an AND-gate, leading up
to the outcome - in this case all identiﬁed events may cause failures on their own, so
only OR-gates are utilized. The case of exceeding maximum or minimum baseline of
the Target Pattern, which is deﬁned as an undeveloped event, as the outlined VBS sys-
tem does not attempt pose estimation in this case, and instead simply reports LOS of
Target, if available. These operation modes constitute extreme proximity or far range
which are not encountered, but simply outlined here. An overview of outcomes (in bold,
underlined, italic) and basic events (in italic) that may result in error are listed here:
Poor Accuracy
This includes cases where measurements are systematically biased.
Flexing/bending: Physical distortion of the Target, may result in displacement of ﬁdu-
cials, not accounted for by the model of ﬁducial location in the world coordinate system.
This will fool pose estimation to ﬁt a model not representative of the true model, and
propagate an error throughout detection. This scenario is highly unlikely and not con-
64
==PAGE:66==
sidered in the study.
Pose Ambiguity: As presented in Section 8.3.5, this case may provide opposite ori-
entation along the axis of rotation in question.
The severity depends on range to
Target and relative oriention.
Model Errors: Model errors consists of uncorrected bias of measurements of the mire
locations, relative to the Target, e.g. determining the displacement of the axis of rota-
tion during tests. Parallax for the mires is encountered as the lens of the mires do not
coincide precisely with the lightsource, which needs to be taken into account for the
overall accuracy.
Poor initial pose-guess: This error is speciﬁc to iterative algorithms for pose estimation
relying on initialization, such as Gauss-Newton or LHM, that may not converge to a
probable solution without an initialization suﬃcively close to the real pose. Only LHM
is globally convergent and should therefore avoid this problem.
Poor Precision
Centroid-determination may be aﬀected by image noise, or the outlined blending of
mires, where the model precision is much poorer than the established Target-pattern.
Image noise: For the process of determining centroids of detected blobs, the addi-
tion of noise may displace the centroid, by shifting the relative weighting of pixels used
to determine the centroid and/or including pixels that are otherwise unrelated to mires,
as the noise may make them exceed the threshold set for centroid estimation.
Mires blending together: This is best outlined with considering the eﬀect on the docking
pattern: at greater distance these mires will form one bright light perceived as a more
intense mire than the remainder of mires. Since the inter-mire distances of the docking
pattern are not identical, this position will not directly correspond to the mire in the
center of the pattern, and so the model-pattern used in this case, has to rely on the
centroid of the entire docking-pattern, which propagates the uncertainties associated
with each mire-position, resulting in lower accuracy.
Wrongful Correspondence
Certain situations and phenomena may cause the VBS to identify mires incorrectly,
leading to gross errors in pose estimation.
Outlier Points: The presence of image glares may cause additional centroids to be
detected during image processing, which may in turn be mistaken for mires. Further-
more, the additional centroids to be handled may not necessarily cause error, but can
be taxing on the µASC, without proper rejection of such events.

==PAGE:67==
Few mires: At more extreme angles, it is possible that parts of the pattern is un-
able to be resolved or occlusion of the Target, where a portion of the spacecraft has
parts shadowing the ﬁducials, such as an orientation of a solar-panel or boom. The
primary occurrence will be from the blending of mires for diﬀerent range modes, which
requires that the reference Target Pattern is updated accordingly.

9.4
Design of Study
The environment to conduct experiments in, allows for tests to be performed for various
poses, with the operational range extended with the inclusion of tests in the hallway.
However such tests are time consuming, requiring calibration and setup both prior to
and in between tests, resulting in a limited number of experiments to be conducted.
Simply projecting the Target Pattern in a synthetic camera frame according to Equa-
tion 8.7 from deﬁned set of poses and maneuvers, allows for a large degree of freedom
in setups, with much larger number of measurements, which are easily repeatable, with
less time to set up. It is possible to restrict the study solely to using projected points
to test the PnP-algorithms. This approach does however neglect the system in which
the algorithms are implemented: it does not replicate the encountered noise that aﬀect
centroid-placement, the blending of mires at greater distances, aside from encountered
outliers and handling hiccups with the system among others. Although some of these as-
pects can be modeled in the projected space, utilizing an engineering model for physical
tests provides a much better foundation for testing, as it encompasses the aforemen-
tioned aspects.
It is therefore desirable to combine the positive attributes of both point-projection and
physical tests, combining high data-frequency and low setup time with realistic system
performance. From preliminary tests conducted in Optolab, it became apparent that
imagery from tests could be simulated with the available tools. Building upon point-
projection for placement and using physical tests to model mires and system noise,
synthetic imagery could be constructed. This is presented as the CAMera LABoratory
(CAMLAB) program. The mylar-glare study was also used to include the generation
of synthetic mylar glares for the simulator.
To capture realistic system performance, the µASC ’s VBS-system needed to be em-
ulated for the tests in the study. The emulator was to perform the basic tasks of the
VBS system, as it needs to be able to handle the correspondence problem as well as
potential outliers. The VBS modes have to be included, in part to account for diﬀerent
operation modes encountered for tests over greater ranges.

• Calibration of µASC and built tools

Image Formats
The µASC can deliver three valid outputs for the VBS emulator, in the form of diﬀer-
ently formatted images captured by the system:
Raw data is saved in 8-bit format, with pixel-intensities ranging 0 to 255. These images
are most desirable for the emulator as it retains all the information the true VBS would
use prior to processing, but are not used for all tests as the transmission-time is 40-44

JPG formatting is made available for the images. This compresses the images into
equally spaced blocks with adjusted mean-noise, reducing the overall ﬁlesize, which in

compression is lossy, increasing overall noise of the image. Especially the former may

seen by the CHU at the correct position, before starting tests.
Region Of Interest (ROI) format, selects isolated blobs present in the captured raw
image, and saves a surrounding ROI, corresponding to an image patch. The data is
still in raw format, but the overall ﬁlesize is severely reduced, resulting in transmission
times of circa 0.05 sec pr. image, making it ideally suited for the long-range approaches.
The downside is the instability of the format as mires tend to be divided into multiple
blobs at random occurrences and being striped. The high data-rate does however com-
pensate for these drawbacks in many instances.
Due to the outlined considerations, raw images were solely used for the setup-tests in

Evaluating Results
The ground truth for hallway-tests does not have as strict an accuracy as the point-
projected/simulated tests, due to the lack of directly measured parameters (XC, YC)

have constant biases throughout tests, in order to derive ground truth accuracy as a
mean-value of residuals, whereas we use the standard deviation (3σ to retain 99% of
the residuals) of residuals to describe the overall accuracy of pose estimation. Only the
utilized range-ﬁnder may add spread to residuals, but its precision is documented to be
2.5 mm and should therefore be adequate to evaluate pose estimation.

10.4
The VBS Emulator
The VBS emulator is contained within the function SC pose estimation.m, which takes
input in form of Target Pattern, input-data (projected points or images) and choice of
PnP-algorithm to solve pose estimation. Output consists the 6DoFs from pose esti-
mation, and the LOS-position of the Target and centroids detected from the image
processing module. The structure and functionality of the emulator is elaborated in
the following:
Measurement Input & Intrinsic Calibration
Measurement Input fetches the coordinates of the chosen Target Plate conﬁguration,
all based oﬀof true measurements of the Target Plate. Intrinsic Calibration fetches the
intrinsic camera matrix. It is set to idealized values per default for usage of both CHU-
Short and -Long, due to the challenges faced during camera calibration as highlighted
in Section 11.1.2.
Image Processing
Input images are binarized via Otsu’s method, which determines the optimal threshold-
value assuming that the background and foreground-pixels form two separable intensity-
distributions. Otsu’s method computes the threshold-value which retains an equal vari-
ance of both selected distributions [34][p. 77-81]. To avoid performing blob-detection
on random noise, the threshold is ﬁxed at a minimum of intensity of Imin = 17, cor-
responding to Imin ≈µI + σI of the noise-distribution of the CHU, modeled in the
Stability Test (Section 11.1.4). If projected points are provided as input, image pro-
cessing is skipped.
The binary image is then labeled according to 4-way-connectivity, making each isolated
region detected as a potential mire. Each candidate is then checked against require-
ments of circular appearance, computing the ratio between the measured circumference
of the blob in question, and that which is expected from a measured radius. Regions
with blobs that do not fulﬁll this requirement are set to background. The labeled image
with blobs is then used for centroid estimation.
Centroid Estimator
Small regions around the individual blobs are used for computing the centroid, on
the basis of summing all pixels along the vertical and horizontal axes and dividing by
the total number of pixels. At closer range where the mires will appear larger, the
centroid-determination should perform with greater precision.
Correspondence Module
In case of successful outlier removal or a scenario solely with the 8 mires visible, cor-
respondence of detected mires and the Target Pattern may be established. The Corre-
spondence Solver was implemented with inspiration from Procrustes Analysis [48]. The

10.4.1
Cluster-Fit
Cluster-Fit mainly has to solve the correspondence problem and perform correct range
mode transition. As part of the robustness study, it is also build to to solve the n-point
problem for outliers. The implementation relies on determining the overall Target Pat-
tern in three stages: ﬁrst determining the outer 3-point frame, second the docking
pattern and lastly, the entire Target Pattern to ensure the two previous stages obtained
viable solutions. The procedure is detailed in the following, following the annotated
situations of Figure 10.12.
(A): A set of detected points with outliers are input to Cluster-Fit. (B): The pro-
vided query-points Q are divided between groupings of inner- and outer-points, QInner
and QOuter. The grouping is done according to Otsu’s method, grouping their relative
distance to the common centroid of Q.
The outer 3-point frame of point-ID numbers 1, 7 & 8 of the Target Pattern are deter-
mined, by going through all possible 3-point permutations of QOuter used in conjunction
with a fourth point constructed from the centroid of the docking pattern, which helps
ensure the correct point-set for QOuter is selected. The correspondence module is called
for each set of QOuter, selecting the best from the minimal distance between identiﬁed
points. The initial rotation determined from the correspondence module is propagated
through the next steps to ease computational load.
With the outer frame established, the docking pattern is determined from the remain-
der of points. (C): Prior to determining the docking pattern, the fourth point of the
outer frame is constructed by extending vectors along parallel baselines formed by the
3-point-frame. This is called the anchor point, QAnchor, as it makes up part of the pat-
tern parsed for (situation (D)) the next call to the correspondence module, along with
point QOuter that makes up the corner furthest away. The docking pattern is parsed
for three diﬀerent conﬁgurations, corresponding to the (8/7/4)-point transition of the
Target. As it is easier to establish good correspondence between slave and master with
fewer points, selection of the most suitable conﬁguration is based of empirically deter-
mined penalties, choosing the 8-point conﬁguration if viable, 7-point as second priority
and at minimum the 4-point conﬁguration if available.
(E) A ﬁnal call to the correspondence module, using all points selected from previous
steps, to ensure the full pattern can be established, otherwise discarding the solution.

The procedure of Cluster-Fit is summarized in the following:
1. Divide the query-points, Q, into an outer and inner clustering of points, Qouter
and Qinner.
2. Establish a ﬁt for the outer-points (3-point-frame).
3. Construct a point extending from the outer-points (anchor-point).
4. Depending on nQuery, step 4) goes through the outlined (8/7/4)-points.
5. Establish a ﬁt for the inner-points ((8/7/4)-point-ﬁt).
6. The outer- and inner-points are collected for a ﬁnal run through the correspon-
dence module.
This approach still uses permutations, but with the division between groupings, the
number of computations is severely cut down, relative to solving for Q in one go, and
ensures relatively robust range mode transitions, correspondence solutions and outlier
rejection as presented in Section 11.2.

to the closest edges of the Target Plate, corresponding to describing the checkerboard
distance from each plate-edge, 2) inter-mire distances were measured, and used as a
checkup with the distances computed from the ﬁrst set of measurements. The reported
misﬁts between the two are depicted in Figure 11.1, with RMSE = 0.84 mm.
The majority of misﬁts are reasonably close to the expected imprecision, with the
exception of larger inter-mire distances, in part stemming for the necessity of using
measuring tape in these instances. If the Target plate is projected at a distance of

Checkerboard was used with MATLABs calibration program and Python’s OpenCV-

tersections between black and white squares, in checkerboard-patterns. The method
determines the intrinsic camera matrix as well as the distortion parameters described
in Section 8.3. The method has been an industry standard since its release, being the
default calibration method for both considered platforms. The suggested number of
images to be used is between 10 and 20 images, while well over 350 images were used
in total, but still resulted in large discrepancies in focal length as documented for the
results modeled in OpenCV in Figure 11.2.
All regular usages of the calibration methods included strong distortion parameters,

11.4.9
Remarks regarding algorithm choice
RPnP outperforms EPnP and LHM in the vast majority of tests, especially EPnP as
the utilized version is ill-suited to solve pose for coplanar targets. LHM does improve
upon pose for greater levels of image noise and is more resilient to outlier-displacement,
but does not resolve pose ambiguities and has trouble converging to a well-reﬁned pose
for the coplanar target. The latter is surprising, since LHM is supposed to be globally
convergent, meaning that it should not get stuck in local minima.
Gauss Newton does improve upon pose with RPnP for some cases and has relatively
low execution times, but improvements are not consistent or signiﬁcant and it did not
negate image noise, which would have been the main reason for including it, as this
was one of the tests where RPnP had poorer performance.
Despite the one-sided comparisons of algorithms, it stands clear from the conducted
tests that RPnP is well-suited to carry out pose estimation for the outlined mission,
as it obtains small errors for rotation and translation tests, is not susceptible to pose
ambiguity, and has descent performance for the other tests. It is therefore used for both
hallway-tests and rendezvous simulations in the study.

11.7
Simulations of the Rendezvous Experiment
The Target is simulated for an approach with distances of 10 to 166 m, with each frame
separated by 1 m displacement. For CHU-Short, the minimum distance is set down to
1 m, to determine the lower limit for viable pose estimation during the approach, to
establish the operational limit. Yaw is varied linearly from -20◦to 20◦throughout the
approach, corresponding to the PROBA-3 requirement of maximum separation angle
of ±20◦relative to the optical axis of the Chaser, and a rotation rate of around 0.25◦/s.
With strong variation of Target attitude in the test and a an overestimated noise-ﬂoor
as documented in Section 11.3.3, the following test should be considered a worst-case
estimate of obtainable accuracy for the VBS system.
The approach is simulated separately for ideal camera intrinsics of CHU-Short & -
Long, and then compared to ascertain the obtainable accuracy for a full VBS system
that successfully changes between input from the two cameras during RVX. Two sepa-
rate tests are reported in conjunction with the study: The ﬁrst test consist of a series

measure to ensure correspondence in the considered environment.
As a proof of concept, the strategy is superior to using RANSAC as it uses fewer per-
mutations and while it can handle cases with multiple outliers, it is not recommended
to deploy the scheme if a large pool of points are detected. Rather, Cluster-Fit can
be used as a fail-safe for a handful of outliers, but the Chaser-craft should revert to
non-cooperative tracking for greater number of outliers.

Chapter 12
Discussion
Intrinsic camera calibration was attempted with Zhang’s method, but was not success-
fully achieved, instead reverting to using ideal camera matrix and intrinsics for the
remainder of conducted tests. Parallax was examined and shown to be negligible for
overall model accuracy. A large bias of translation of Target was observed for Fine
Rotation & Translation test, which was attributed to wrong documentation of relative
CHU-position at the time of tests. Positional accuracy of the Target from ground truth,
could not be reduced below cm-regi. It is evident that the range of tests conducted in
Optolab should have been evaluated concurrently at the time of carrying out the tests,
to work out the inaccuracy.
Since hallway tests were carried out at ranges further away than that of test in Optolab,
requirements to accuracy could be relaxed. It was demonstrated that pose could be
determined relatively close to established ground truths for test and that pose estima-
tion could obtain accuracies close to expectation for approach tests, but also that the
setup was not suﬃcient for evaluating this performance directly. It was evident that
the LR rangeﬁnder did not provide a stable ground truth, in part inferred from the
regular performance of RPnP and OCR during tests and evidenced by the number of
gross outliers amongst the rangeﬁnder measurements. Alternative means to measure
range to Target or a higher tier rangeﬁnder should be used if the concept was to be
expanded upon. Still, the overall conduct of the test was according to plan and allowed
for synthetic imagery to be constructed with high ﬁdelity.
For tests of algorithms, RPnP proofed to be the only valid candidate among the con-
sidered candidates. The reason for RPnPs undisputed success is not conclusive, but
points to a simpler implementation using smaller-point-sets. The addition of Gauss
Newton for use with RPnP did not provide results to justify its use. Despite the fact
that it could be shown to improve upon results for some test-cases, unexplained gross
errors were encountered. The implementation of the method might be at fault, but it
was based strictly oﬀof the formulation of [30], and a scheme based oﬀof the Gauss
158
==PAGE:160==
Newton method was successfully used for mire-modeling.
It would have been prudent to include other algorithms, rather than relying on a single
source for algorithms, aside from the Gauss Newton implementation, especially using
P4P, due to its success with the PRISMA-mission. The addition of a non-coplanar
point to the Target Plate did improve upon results, which makes a case for studying
various conﬁgurations of ﬁducial patterns.
The emulator successfully handled the basic functionalities outlined for VBS in Sec-
tions 8.5.2 & 8.5.3. The correspondence module, was robust to large principal rotations
and large noise levels, while it has more limited performance when handling cross-varied
angles. Due to the usefulness of the module for a large variety of other tested situations,
it is considered that the approach could be expanded upon, to better account for 3D
rotation of the frame angle, when determining overall baselines.
Robustness was investigated on the basis of the presence of image glares brought on by
light reﬂected oﬀof the mylar draped around the Target. The module to simultane-
ously solve for correspondence, regardless of range mode, and perform outlier rejection,
called Cluster-Fit was tested against this phenomena and proofed feasible for various
test-conditions. However, from simulated glare tests, the concentration of mires proofed
diﬃcult for the module to solve, as the query-centroid was grouped much closer to the
cluster of glares, than the centroid of the Target Pattern. Alternate methods to divide
or cluster query-points could be sought in extension of this concept, to make the mod-
ule more robust towards such occurrences. Fusion of co-operative and non-co-operative
tracking in this case could be investigated in the future, as handover should occur in
cases with great illumination of Target. It is also noted that encountered glares are
entirely dependent on the texture of the mylar, which may vary greatly, as exempliﬁed
in Appendix Section 15.9.
The Rendezvous Experiment was successfully simulated in CAMLAB, and using the
build VBS emulator and RPnP optical navigation for the mission was conducted. Ac-
curacy of navigational parameters met requirements to PROBA-3 and Orbital Express
for the majority of the ranges considered. Similarities of navigational parameters were
also observed between the simulated approach and hallway approach. The reported
accuracies were on par with the limitations set by the simulator, and hence might be
underestimated relative to true deployment of the system.
Pose ambiguity was observed at extended ranges for both CHU-Short and -Long stem-
ming in part from larger inﬂuence of noise level at greater range to Target, and the
range mode used. While the primary parameters that were diﬀerent relative to the pose
ambiguity test with point projection, where RPnP experienced no such issues.

Chapter 13
Conclusion
In this study, the utility of pose estimation for spaceborne optical navigation was inves-
tigated. Algorithms to conduct pose estimation were presented and compared. Tests
of pose estimation were conducted for both physical and simulated tests, evaluating
accuracy and robustness in conjunction. The VBS emulated included a novel concept
to conduct simultaneous correspondence solution and outlier rejection, and proofed to
be suﬃciently robust for tests.
Accuracy of the experimental setup was not well-established for the test environment of
Optolab. The bias encountered from tests was isolated to an undocumented translation
of CHU-Short. The concepts presented for the hallway-tests were partially successful
in establishing a procedure to simulate satellite rendezvous, but had a limited ground
truth for evaluation of pose estimation. A simulator was built on the basis of data
from hallway-tests, to evaluate the pose estimation with VBS using the conditions of
PROBA-3’s Rendezvous Experiment. It was shown that synthetically generated im-
agery captured realistic aspects of the hallway-test. Accuracy of mire placement and
modeled noise ﬂoor set upper limits to obtainable accuracy for simulations, the latter
of which was overestimated relative to physical tests.
RPnP proofed to be the most accurate and robust of the considered algorithms for
pose estimation. The selection was one-sided as none of the other algorithms consid-
ered were able to properly handle the coplanar Target Pattern. RPnP was included
for use in the VBS emulator constructed for this study, and successfully achieved mis-
sion requirements of the Rendezvous Experiment. Pose ambiguities were much more
pronounced for the simulated tests, which were attributed to the noise-ﬂoor of the sim-
ulator.
With improvements to accuracy of mire-placement, the full system to simulate and
evaluate pose estimation developed, may be extended to diﬀerent mission parameters
allowing for proof of concept studies for future missions to be carried out.

Chapter 14
Future Work
A continued study should encompass one or more of the following tasks. Model ac-
curacy was not well-established throughout physical tests, and should therefore be of
greater focus. To achieve camera calibration, alternate methods could be included such
as Tsai’s method, which uses a 3D calibration pattern which should provided more
stable results [49].
A µDPU-side implementation of the formulated VBS emulator or direct integration
algorithms with the existing VBS system should be performed, to ascertain if the VBS
system can provide the required solutions at 1 Hz. Non-cooperative tracking is viable
extension of the work presented herein, and used for handover from co-operative track-
ing, in cases where ambient noise becomes a large issue by the identifying locality of
image glares.
The algorithms presented do not present an exhaustive list of those available for tests.
Vector-based methodology has been used in former cooperative missions, and may be
viable to investigate. Alternative methodology to establish point correspondence and
outlier rejection could be investigated. While SoftPOSIT was neglected for its rela-
tively long execution time of the study, it has not been tested for the same conditions
as Cluster-Fit. Applying ﬁltering of imagery prior to may also reduce the task of outlier
rejection, making RANSAC viable (examples of ﬁltering provided in Appendix Section
15.7). Hallway-tests can be repeated for future tests, but should include means of better
control the movement of the cart under test (possibly motorized) and better equipment
to measure range. The Robot Navigation Laboratory was not considered in this study,
but could included as a test environment used for comparisons with simulated ren-
dezvous and approach tests.
To improve the accuracy of mire-placement for the simulator, it is considered that
interpolation of local regions prior to performing phase-shift and subsequent resizing
of the region might improve upon results. The large σpx documented for the simula-
tor should be further investigated. Furthermore, radiometry should be included in the
simulator if it is to be used for other operation modes.

15.7
Investigated means for Glare Segmentation
In considerations regarding counteracting glares as outliers during measurements, means
to ease acquisition of mires, namely to aid segmentation by collecting a series of im-
ages, on the basis of the 4 Hz solution speed of µDPU versus the required 1 Hz for the
PROBA-mission, using low-level techniques. Due to dynamic changes in lighting Otsu
threshold is applied for blob-segmentation. The resulting approaches are outlined in
Figure 15.8. The principle behind was inspired from [5], although the ﬁltering behind
this procedure is vastly diﬀerent from the one applied in the citation. As can be made
out from the ﬁgure, the variance best retains the mires throughout the image series,
allowing for regions-of-interest to be selected in each image, by tracking the centroids.
It is observed that most glares de-correlate across frames when computing the variance
across the image-stack, reducing the eﬀective number of outliers to solve for.
