Abstract
This thesis project marks a significant milestone in the field of electrochemical biosens┬¡
ing by introducing a novel integration of additive manufacturing and microneedle┬¡based
sensing. The project employs an innovative approach of developing a 3D┬¡printed, py┬¡
rolytic carbon┬¡based microneedle biosensor, specifically designed for TNF┬¡╬▒ detection, a
medically relevant pro┬¡inflammatory cytokine.
The undertaken work describes the method of functionalizing the microneedle biosensor
with gold nanoparticles and capture antibodies, realized through a rigorous optimization
process. Key steps in this optimization process included the establishment of an optimal
gold nanoparticle deposition protocol and the determination of suitable parameters for
electrochemical sensing via differential pulse voltammetry.
The robustness of the biosensor was validated through a series of characterizations, us┬¡
ing electrochemical impedance spectroscopy, cyclic voltammetry and scanning electron
microscopy. The biosensor demonstrated exceptional linearity over a range of 0.1 to 100
ng/mL, and a theoretical limit of detection as low as 28 pg/mL.
This master thesis highlights the potential of additive manufacturing in enhancing biosen┬¡
sor technology and underscores the need for ongoing innovation in the rapidly progressing
fields of medical diagnostics and materials science. The exploration of 3D printing, mi┬¡
croneedle technology, and pyrolytic carbon application in this work signifies an important
contribution towards the development of point┬¡of┬¡care diagnostic solutions. The insights
generated form a solid foundation for future investigations in these converging scientific
areas, emphasizing the value of further advancement in this field.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This chapter introduces the topic of the critical role of diagnostic tools in healthcare,
the fundamental principles of biosensors, and the unique advantages of electrochemical
biosensors, followed by an introduction to microneedles and how they are used as state┬¡
of┬¡the┬¡art biosensing tools. In the end, the limitations of these systems are discussed and
how this thesis work aims to address them.
1.1
Motivation
Importance of diagnostic tools for healthcare
In the rapidly evolving landscape of healthcare, diagnostic tools play a piv┬¡
otal role in ensuring optimal patient outcomes. These tools, ranging from
simple physical examination techniques to complex laboratory tests and
imaging technologies, are the cornerstone of modern medicine. They en┬¡
able healthcare professionals to accurately identify diseases, monitor pa┬¡
tient progress, and evaluate the effectiveness of treatments.
Early and accurate diagnosis is a critical first step in the management of any
health condition. It allows for timely intervention, which can significantly
improve prognosis and reduce the risk of complications. For instance, early
detection of conditions like cancer or cardiovascular disease can dramati┬¡
cally increase survival rates and improve quality of life. These and other
chronic diseases contribute to 74% of all deaths globally, which accounts
for 41 million people per year as accounted per the World Health Organi┬¡
zation [1].
Traditional laboratory tests, while effective, often involve invasive proce┬¡
dures, require significant time for results, and can be costly. They typically
necessitate the extraction of a substantial volume of biological samples,
such as blood, which can cause discomfort to the patient. Moreover, these
tests often require specialized laboratory equipment and trained personnel
to conduct the tests and interpret the results, which can limit their accessi┬¡
bility, particularly in resource┬¡limited settings.
Furthermore, many of these tests do not provide real┬¡time or near┬¡real┬¡time
monitoring of health conditions, which is increasingly important for the
management of chronic diseases. The need for repeated testing to monitor
disease progression or treatment effectiveness can also increase the burden
on patients and healthcare systems.

1.2
Introduction to biosensors

Biosensors are analytical devices that combine a biological recognition
element with a physicochemical transducer to detect specific biological
molecules, structures, or processes (Figure 1.1). They are designed to con┬¡
vert a biological response into a quantifiable and processable signal.
The biological recognition element, or bioreceptor, is a biomolecule that in┬¡
teracts specifically with the target analyte. Common types of bioreceptors
used in biosensors include enzymes, antibodies, nucleic acids, and cells [4].
The choice of bioreceptor depends on the specific analyte to be detected and
the desired selectivity and sensitivity of the biosensor.
The transducer is the component of the biosensor that converts the biologi┬¡
cal response into a measurable signal. There are several types of transduc┬¡

ers, including optical, electrochemical, piezoelectric, and thermal transduc┬¡
ers [5]. The choice of transducer depends on the nature of the biological
response and the requirements of the detection task.
Biosensors can be made from a variety of materials, including carbon, met┬¡
als, semiconductors, polymers, and biological materials [5]. The choice of
material depends on the requirements of the biosensor, such as its sensitiv┬¡
ity, selectivity, response time, and stability. Recent advances in materials
science have led to the development of new materials with improved prop┬¡
erties for biosensor applications, such as graphene [6], silicon nanowires
[7] and gold nanoparticles (AuNP) [8].
There are several sensing methods used in biosensors, including direct de┬¡
tection, where the presence or concentration of the analyte is measured di┬¡
rectly, and indirect detection, where the analyte is detected through its ef┬¡
fect on a secondary molecule or process [9]. The choice of sensing method
depends on the nature of the analyte and the requirements of the detection
task.
Depending on the design of the biosensor and the transducer used one de┬¡
termines the detection strategy which will be used for the signal generation.
One category, namely electrochemical biosensors have been recognized for
their numerous advantages in various fields, particularly in healthcare and
environmental monitoring. The next section will explore their versatility
and advantages.
1.3
Electrochemical biosensors
What are they and how do they work?
Electrochemical biosensors are a subset of biosensors that rely on the elec┬¡
trical properties of biological materials to detect and quantify analytes. They
are known for their high sensitivity, which allows for the detection of minute
quantities of analytes, and their wide linear range, which enables them to
measure a broad spectrum of analyte concentrations spanning multiple or┬¡
ders of magnitude [10].
There are several types of such sensors, including potentiometric, ampero┬¡
metric, and conductometric biosensors [9]. Potentiometric biosensors mea┬¡
sure the potential difference between two electrodes, amperometric biosen┬¡
sors measure the current produced by a chemical reaction, and conducto┬¡
metric biosensors measure the change in electrical conductivity of the ana┬¡

The mechanisms of detection used by electrochemical biosensors are di┬¡

under in vitro conditions to provide precise and rapid results. This allows
for real┬¡time and near┬¡real┬¡time monitoring applications, making electro┬¡

1.4
Microneedle Biosensing Strategies
Microneedles represent a significant advancement in the field of biosens┬¡
ing, offering a minimally invasive alternative to traditional hypodermic nee┬¡
dles. These tiny, needle┬¡like structures, typically ranging from hundreds of
micrometres to a few millimetres in length, are designed to penetrate the
outermost layers of the skin, known as the stratum corneum and epidermis,
to access the underlying interstitial fluid in the dermal layer of the skin.
This allows for the extraction of biological fluids or the delivery of thera┬¡
peutic agents directly into the body [16], without causing significant pain
or discomfort typically associated with hypodermic needles.

Research Objectives
1. Design and Fabrication: Traditional methods for fabricating micronee┬¡

2.2.3
Conversion to pyrolytic carbon
Following the 3D printing procedure the MNs were transferred to a ceramic
pyrolysis furnace by arranging them on an iron mesh on top of a ceramic
boat, wherein the presence of nitrogen atmosphere and absence of oxygen,
a step┬¡wise heat protocol was executed, starting from ambient room temper┬¡
ature. The thermal protocol initiates with an increase of the temperature to
350 ┬░C at a rate of 5 ┬░C per minute. This temperature is maintained for two
hours, then itÔÇÖs incrementally raised to 375 ┬░C at a rate of 1 ┬░C per minute,

2.4
Discussion
The first objective of this research project was to leverage the versatility
of 3D printing for MN fabrication. The results presented in this chapter
demonstrate that this objective was successfully achieved. The use of Au┬¡
todesk Fusion 360 for the design of the microneedles allowed for the cre┬¡
ation of a complex structure with a cylindrical base, a hexagonal prism, and
a hexagonal pyramid at the top, all of which were designed and 3D printed
with precision. The inner lattice structure, which was included to provide
stability, was also successfully incorporated into the design (Figure 2.5).
The 3D printing process, using BMF S140, was able to accurately replicate
the digital design into a physical structure. The use of projection Micro
Stereolithographic additive manufacturing allowed for the creation of the
microneedles layer by layer, with 20 ┬Ám resolution in Z┬¡axis and down to
10 ┬Ám resolution in the X and Y axes. This method of additive manufac┬¡
turing proved to be efficient and precise, as evidenced by the retention of
the fine details of the inner lattice structure following the 3D printing pro┬¡
cess when the samples were observed with SEM after pyrolysis as shown
in figure 2.7 .
The second part of the fabrication process involved the conversion of the
3D printed structures to pyrolytic carbon, a robust and biocompatible ma┬¡
terial, with extensive use for biomedical applications [25][26]. The use
of a ceramic pyrolysis furnace under a nitrogen atmosphere allowed for
the successful conversion of the microneedles to pyrolytic carbon with the
resulting structures being black in colour and shrunken in size due to the
evaporation of all non┬¡carbon materials and aromatization of carbon atoms,
indicating a successful pyrolysis process. This process of a 3D printing
resin to carbon conversion has been thoroughly characterized by Rezaei et.
al. and the results of this study are consistent in regards to the shrinkage
observed in the MN structure [24].
The electrochemical characterization via CV revealed the good conductiv┬¡
ity of the carbon electrode and confirmed that the custom 3D printed setup
works well and can indeed be used to integrate the small pyrolysed carbon
MN in a 3┬¡electrode system with a counter and reference electrodes. Fur┬¡
thermore, the ÔêåE values of 140 mV (┬▒20 mV) provide good evidence for
electron transfer from the redox probe through the MN electrode, which is
comparable to other studies [27]. One limitation of the present setup would
be its inability to measure the surface area in a meaningful way. Currently,
