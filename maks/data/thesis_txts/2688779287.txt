Abstract 
The aim of this thesis was to identify segments of potential and existing Electric Vehicles (EV) users with the 
intention to derive conclusions about ways to increase the adoption of EV.  
The data used in this analysis originate from a large survey used to evaluate an EU funded project called the 
GREAT project. The goal of the GREAT project was to improve the infrastructure for EV users by increasing 
the number of EV chargers across Denmark and Sweden. The survey collected sociodemographic characteristics 
such as age, income and household size. Attitudinal questions regarding the perception of EVs from different 
angles, including lifestyle, political, social and environmental beliefs were collected as well. The sample of the 
survey consist of BEV users and non-users, making it suitable for the aim of this thesis.  When investigating the 
distribution of the attitudinal variables, it is possible to convert the attitudinal variables into factors using a 
principal component analysis (PCA). The resulting factors were used as part of the segmentation. 
Segments were identified by using cluster analysis. As cluster analysis can be conducted using different 
techniques, the literature was reviewed in order to determine the cluster analysis technique most appropriate for 
the project. From the literature study and data available, a latent class cluster analysis (LCCA) was chosen as the 
best suited method. The analysis was further developed by including a choice model investigating the market 
shares of the identified clusters, making the analysis a latent class choice analysis. 
From the analysis, it was possible to estimate 3 models, representing 2, 3 and 4 clusters. These models were 
evaluated based on their statistical performance based on Akaike Information Criterion (AIC), and Bayesian 
Information Criteria (BIC) to determine which model has the best fit for the data. Results of AIC and BIC tests 
showed that the model with 3 cluster had the best fit, but missing and contradictory parameter estimations made 
the model consisting of 2 clusters the preferred model for further evaluation.  
For this model market shares were calculated, which divided the two clusters into BEV- and non BEV users.  It 
was possible to identify the segment representing active BEV users as more likely to be above 36 years of age 
and having an income of above 50.000 kr. These characteristics are consistent with the findings in the existing 
literature on BEV users of BEV users. For the attitudinal factors, it was possible to identify that functional 
barriers related to issues regarding charging were not perceived as barriers towards EV adoption for active BEV 
users, but uncertainty towards political support of EVs was a statement that active BEV users agreed upon. This 
indicates that missing incentives and political support could make it less likely for existing BEV users to buy an 
EV again. 
==PAGE:5==
4 
 
The cluster which represents potential EV adopters, is characterised as having an income of less than 30.000 kr. 
monthly and being in the age group 18 – 35, and having a 85% probability of driving an conventional car. 
Participants belonging to this group agreed with the functional barriers towards driving a BEV, and that driving a 
BEV does not fit with their lifestyle. This indicates that improvements made towards functional barriers, and 
matching driving a BEV with the lifestyle of the identified segment group can contribute to increased EV 
adoption, if potential users are made aware of these improvements.

1 Introduction 
Over the last decade the consequences of a modern lifestyle with the globally increasing production is seen    
as a large threat to the future. Increased emissions from various sectors results in severe environmental 
consequences caused by global warming. Therefore, the need of promoting a more sustainable lifestyle is 
essential for the future. The transportation sector is no different, as this sector alone is responsible for almost a 
quarter of greenhouse gases in Europe (Union), and is the main cause of increased air pollution in cities, which 
can have serious consequences related to health problems.     
1.1 Problem statement 
Increasing the share of electric vehicles (EV) is a desirable goal in order to promote sustainability and reduce car 
emissions. The GREAT project has improved conditions for EV’s, in forms of infrastructural actions by 
increasing the number of EV chargers across Denmark and Sweden. In addition, a collection of a large online 
survey was conducted, providing various information regarding participants preferences between EV and 
conventional vehicles (CV).  
This project concerns finding different attitudinal, behavioural and various socio-demographic characteristics 
between car owners of electric and conventional vehicles based on the survey from the GREAT project. This is 
done with the use of cluster analysis, in which user segments based on various attitudes, preferences, and 
behaviours of the participants are identified. 
The identification of relevant user segments will be used to find new ways to promote and increase the share of 
EV’s, contributing to reduce emissions and promoting sustainability in the transport sector.  
1.1.1 Learning objectives 
To answer the problem statement the following learning objectives needs to be fulfilled. 
• 
Perform a literature study within market segmentation and cluster analysis and identify advantages and 
disadvantages from “traditional statistical analysis” upon integrating it into the transport sector. 
• 
Perform cluster analysis to identify different user segments. 
• 
Discuss the results of the cluster analysis. 
• 
Identify evaluation criteria and factors from the results of possible clusters found. 
• 
Discuss the adaption of using the methodology of market segments in the transportation sector. 
• 
Provide recommendations based on the results from the cluster analysis.

2. Background 
2.1. EV adoption  
One of the effective ways of promoting a more sustainable transportation is the reduction of internal combustion 
engine vehicles (ICEV) for private car users. A method of achieving this goal is to make people more likely to 
adopt electric vehicles (EV). 
Increasing sustainability in the transportation sector is an evident goal and increasing the share of EVs is a great 
way to accommodate this. The adoption of EV’s has however been a struggle in the last decade. People have 
been used to driving ICVs for many years, and the adopting to a new type of car has proven to be a challenge. 
This section will describe the challenges within EV adoption and provide insights into are the most challenging 
barriers in the Scandinavian region. Insights in factors influencing EV adoption will also be presented. 
2.2 Factors influencing EV adoption 
2.2.1 Socio-demographic factors 
The use of socio demographic or socio economic factors are appropriate in order to identify the characteristics of 
a sample. This is commonly used when describing the characteristics of any market or investigation in order to 
identify how different costumer groups react. This investigation has been made for EV adoption, where (Plötz, 
Schneider, Globisch, & Dütschke, 2014) found that part time workers who are male, value new technology and 
have environmental concerns, are the most likely be part of the first movers to adopt to EV. Additionally, 
(Hidrue, Parsons, Kempton, & Gardner, 2011) found that by using stated preferences in market segmentation, 
well-educated and middle-aged small families are more likely to choose EV as their next vehicle purchase. 
Additional relevant sociodemographic factors will be presented in section 3.2.2.1 and 3.2.2.2 (Differences in 
countries and Differences in car ownership).  
2.2.2 Attitudinal factors 
(Moons & Pelsmacker, 2012) argues that emotions and behaviour influence the consumer intention to adopt 
EVs. Additionally, (Schuitema, Anable, Skippon, & Kinnear, 2013) argues that self-image, the symbolic 
meaning of having an EV contributes to EV adoption, as well as the consistency between EV capabilities, and 
lifestyle requirements can contribute to increased EV adoption (Axsen, TyreeHageman, & Lentz, 2012).  
When using attitudinal variables in segmentation analysis, having a theoretical background ensures a more 
systematic consideration of relevant variables to consider. The most commonly theoretical framework used in 
this context is the Theory of Planned Behaviour (Ajzen, 2019). The theory considers attitudes, subjective norm 
and perceived behavioural control as main predictors of intention and intention as the main predictor of 
behaviour. 
==PAGE:18==
17 
 
Subjective norm is associated with normative beliefs, in which the perceived behaviour of people important to 
you reflects on yourself. Perceived behavioural control refers to the perception of how well the respondent 
values their ability to perform a specific behaviour. When presenting actual barriers towards EV adoption 
(section 2.3), these are considered indirectly when measuring for perceived behaviour control, as the same 
barriers can be perceived differently by different people. 
 Ajzen states that the combination of subjective norm and perceived behavioural control are present when 
indicating a specific attitude and explains the intention of the respondent. The intention stated by the respondent 
towards an attitude is also influenced by the actual behavioural control of the respondent. Figure 8 shows a 
graphical explanation of Theory of Planned Behaviour. 
 
 
Figure 8 - Theory of Planned Behaviour diagram (Ajzen, 2019) 
 
2.3 Barriers towards EV adoption 
2.3.1 EV policies 
The political incentives can have a large influence on the adoption of EVs. An example of this was the Danish 
government in 2015 decided to gradually remove the reduction of the registration fee in Denmark 
(Skatteministeriet, 2015). It is common knowledge that Denmark has a very high registration fee, which could 
have an influence when Danes are considering their next car purchase. From 2015 to 2016, the share of EVs 
==PAGE:19==
18 
 
dropped from having sold 4605 in 2015 to only 1369 in 2016 (FDM, 2017). This significant reduction of EV 
purchase shows the magnitude of the effect of policy incentives can have on the adoption of EV’s. 
A different showcase of the influence of policy incentive on EV adoption, can be illustrated by the ambitious 
goal of reducing greenhouse gases from domestic transport by at least 70 percent in 2030 in Sweden (Ministry of 
the Enviorment, 2019). This goal is to be realized by a high economical investment, and the introduction of low 
emission zones, with the goal of improving mobility for electric vehicles. These zones have been introduced as 
of January 2020 (Ministry of Enviorment, Government makes announcement on low emission zones, 2018), 
with continuously increasing restrictions over the next years. As this is a relatively new incentive, the effects are 
not yet evident, but it is a showcase of how the government involvement can affect EV adoption. 
2.3.2 Charging 
The introduction of EVs as the sustainable alternative to traditional ICV constitute a large challenge concerning 
the ability to use the existing infrastructure to charge your vehicle. (Giansoldati, Monte, & Scorrano, 2020) 
found that insufficient number of charging stations was the highest ranked (out of 20) barriers for EV adoption. 
Driving a vehicle with a smaller driving range than the average ICV combined with limited number of charging 
stations (compared to ICVs) constitutes one of the largest barriers for EV adoption, underlying the importance of 
incentives as the GREAT project.  
Ranked 5th and 6th of the survey investigation of EV adoption carried out by (Giansoldati, Monte, & Scorrano, 
2020), issues related to charging were also evident. Ranked 5th, charging problems in the absence of a garage 
causes issues for potential EV adopters located in societies with a high population density, which usually results 
in having limited opportunities of charging an EV. The act of charging your EV is seen as potential barrier 
towards EV adoption.  
A typical electric car with a 60kWh battery has a charging time of just under 8 hours from empty to full (POINT, 
2021), forcing the driver to include the charging of your vehicle as a part of planning activities, which would not 
be a requirement when driving an ICV. It is important to point out that with the constant improvement of the 
electric batteries and infrastructure related to EV charging, the negative effects of these issues will decrease over 
time.      
2.3.3 Purchase price 
Related to the issue concerning policy incentives towards EVs (especially for Denmark), the purchase price of 
EVs can also be an issue concerning EV adoption. This issue varies across countries, as a result of the different 
incentives present for each country (Lévay, Drossinos, & Thiel, 2017).

2.3.4 Other barriers related to EV adoption 
Other issues that are included as potential barriers to EV adoption are the risk of loss of resale value of EVs, the 
risk of increase in electricity prices and the risk of battery degradation. These issues are valued to be of less 
significant barriers compared to other barriers presented in this section. 
2.4 Market segmentation in the transport sector 
Market segmentation is a well-known method, used in various sectors, as a great tool to identify various 
segmentation groups in terms of distributing demand. Evidence suggests that market segmentation dates all the 
way back to the Bronze Age, in which traders segmented trade routes according to geographical circuits (Alberti, 
2016).  
Traditionally, market segmentation has a different set of bases, representing different aspects of identifying 
segmentation groups. These bases include demographic, geographic, psychographic, and behavioural variables.  
Market segmentation has been increasingly used in the transport sector to promote more sustainable transport 
modes. Over time, more complex segmentations have been developed within the transport sector. (Haustein & 
Hunecke, 2013) presents a more complex segmentation approach, which differs from the traditional bases. This 
approach identifies the following variables to identify segmentation groups: Travel behaviour, spatial variables, 
socio-demographic variables, and attitudinal variables.  
2.4.1 Travel behaviour 
Market segmentation approached by travel behaviour, groups consumers based on actual traveling behaviour. 
This could be trip frequency, trip length, what purpose their traveling is related to, and mode choice. An example 
of a segmentation analysis based on traveling behaviour is shown by (Prillwitz & Barr, 2011). Their analysis was 
based on daily travel behaviour, in which they used cluster analysis to find four different clusters: Persistent car 
users, frequent car users, constrained public transportation users, and consistent green travellers. Additionally, 
they found that these segmentation groups were especially related to age, income, and political views.  
2.4.2 Spatial variables: 
Spatial variables explain the geographic settings of consumers. Therefore, a segmentation analysis based on a 
spatial approach concerns the location of consumers. This could be an analysis based on whether the residents 
are living in an urban, suburban, or rural area.  
2.4.3 Socio-demographic variables 
The use of socio-demographic variables in segmentation analysis are variables such as age, income and gender. 
These variables represent different life stages/lifestyles, which can be useful to explain traveling patterns and 
==PAGE:21==
20 
 
mode choices. Examples of this is showed by (Ryley, 2006), in which cluster analysis was used to identify 
different lifestyle patterns as “Students”, “High Earner with Children” and “Retired in a couple”). Riley was able 
to identify different traveling patterns for each cluster.  
2.4.4 Attitudinal variables 
Segmentation analysis in the transport sector based on attitudinal variables often relates to the mobility styles 
(Haustein & Hunecke, 2013). Identifying various mobility types can be based on different variables, related to 
what context the segmentation analysis tries to explain/identify. Examples of this could be a combination with 
the other variables introduced (Travel behaviour, spatial and socio-demographic variables). An example 
presented by (Haustein & Hunecke, 2013) is looking into a specific population, for example older people, in 
which variables as age, accessibility and size of their social network combined with attitudinal variables is seen 
as important factors in analysing mobility behaviour for this particular population group.

3. Method 
3.1 Cluster Analysis 
(Hair, Anderson, & Tatham, 1998) defines cluster analysis as: “Cluster analysis seeks to separate data elements 
into groups or clusters in a way that both the homogeneity of elements within the cluster and the heterogeneity 
between clusters are maximized”. The application of cluster analysis is widely used in many different fields, 
with various engineering studies including pattern recognition, machine learning and electric engineering. Other 
relevant fields include computer science, medical science, earth science, social science and economics (Everitt, 
Landau, & Leese, 2001). As the range of the appliance of cluster analysis is large, different variants of cluster 
analysis has been developed through the years. The first type of cluster analysis originated back to 1932, used in 
anthropology by Driver and Kroeber, which was used to identify different culture relationships (Driver & 
Kroeber, 1932)  
3.1.1 Segmentation approaches 
Traditionally, there are two types of segmentation approaches, represented by (Haustein & Hunecke, 2013): 
priori segmentation, and post hoc segmentation. 
3.1.1.1 A priori segmentation 
When performing an a priori segmentation, the segment profiles are well-defined, in which the segmentation 
groups are based on pre-defined variables/groups which makes it easy to clearly assign each respondent to a 
segmentation group. Examples of this would be choosing pre-defined variables such as age, income or 
demographical variables (urban, surbuban, rural) and classify respondents according to these variables (Haustein 
& Hunecke, 2013).  
3.1.1.2 Post hoc segmentation 
Post hoc segmentation are in contrary to a priori segmentation, based on the result of an analysis in which 
respondents are grouped automatically based on the data, applying specific algorithms instead of defining groups 
beforehand. These groupings are traditionally based on results from cluster analysis. In this project, the 
segmentation analysis will be based on the results from a post hoc segmentation. 
3.1.2 Cluster analysis techniques 
The most common technique of cluster analysis is based on heuristics that try to maximize the similarity 
between in-cluster elements and the dissimilarity between inter-cluster elements (Fraley & Raftery, 2002). This 
is known as a similarity-based clustering. (Depaire, Wets, & Vanhoof, 2007) defines similarity-based clustering 
as where a specific distance function for elements containing continuous features and similarity measures for 
elements with qualitative features.  
==PAGE:23==
22 
 
Within similarity-based clustering technique, the most common approach is known as the partitional approach 
(K-means).  
3.1.2.1 K-means clustering  
Within K-means cluster, the methodology concerns choosing a known amount of centroids based on the number 
of clusters (k= number of clusters). From these centroids, which are randomly chosen points, the mean for each 
datapoint to each centroid is measured, in which each datapoint is appointed the centroid which have the smallest 
mean. This interaction is then repeated a number of times, with the goal of minimizing the variance for each 
interaction. The final clustering is based on the interaction with the smallest variance. The advantage of this 
clustering method is that it is a relatively simple approach, and the result is also easy to interpret, as each 
datapoint is given a clear cluster group to belong to.  (Fraley & Raftery, 2002) argues that the weakness lies 
within the statistical properties of this clustering method. This is more evident when the methodology of 
probability-based methods is presented. 
3.1.2.2 Probability-based clustering 
Another known clustering technique is the probability model-based clustering. Unlike heuristic-based clustering, 
the statistical properties are better known. This clustering technique also has different approaches, where the 
most known are latent class clustering. Within probability model-based clustering, each cluster has an individual 
probability distribution from the data of which each element is generated from. Knowing the probability 
distribution, reduces the problem of finding clusters to a parameter estimation problem. (Depaire, Wets, & 
Vanhoof, 2007). The mathematical formulation of probability model-based clustering is quoted by (Depaire,

In doing this approach you are assigning a cluster based on the probability between each clusters. This means 
that some clusters can overlap. This is one of the disadvantages compared to the heuristic-based clustering. 
3.1.3 Choosing the right clustering approach 
Choosing the correct clustering technique is based on the evaluation on how the dataset fits the advantages and 
disadvantages that are associated with each clustering technique. K-means is based on an algorithm, in which 
probability based-clustering is based on statistical models where the goal is trying to optimize the fit for the data 
==PAGE:24==
23 
 
points. As explained earlier, this means that we are trying to estimate appropriate segments based on parameters 
(means, variance and covariance), where k-means uses an algorithm based on the minimization of variance 
between each interaction of the algorithm. The goal of this project is defining segments based on a sample of 
roughly 3000 respondents and stated preferences from 45 variables. It is chosen that due to the complexity of the 
model and the combination of attitudinal questions and socio demographics, knowing that the statistical 
properties are better known within the probability-based clustering, makes this the preferred approach to use in 
this project. It is easier to validate results from a probability-based clustering method, with the parameter 
estimation enabling this, and this is easiest obtained with the latent class clustering approach (LCC).

3.4 Variable reduction (Principal component analysis) 
Upon having selected various attitudinal variables explaining the psychological aspects of EV adoption, a total 
of 23 ordinal variables have been included. When working with these variables, having the variables included as 
single item representing the participants attitudes towards EV adoption is not good practice. This can be argued 
by the uncertainty that lies within using stated preference questionnaires as data input, as participants can have 
multiple reasons behind their answers. An example could be a showcase of how they wish to be perceived, and 
therefore not giving an accurate response to the questions, despite the anonymous forum. To account for this, 
reducing the number of variables based on their similarity in responses can be applied. Tools like factor analysis, 
and principal component analysis (PCA) are appropriate for this, where a PCA in this project is used to reduce 
the number of variables. 
As stated, PCA is a method for finding low-dimensional representations of a data set that retain as much of the 
original variation as possible (Boehmke & Greenwell, 2020). The identification of appropriate reduction of 
variables are initially done by investigating the correlation between variables, and making appropriate groupings 
based on the correlation. This approach also helps investigating for multicollinearity in the dataset. The 
identification of the appropriate eigenvalues (the result of the feature covariance matrixes for all variables) is 
used in order to identify the number of principal components. The Eigenvalue criterion is a method to determine 
the appropriate number of principal components, stating that the number of principal components is equal to the 
number of eigenvalues above one, illustrated on figure 21: 
 
               Figure 21 - Graphical illustration of the Eigenvalue criterion

3.5 Model framework 
As presented in section 3.1.3 (Choosing the right clustering approach), the probability based cluster approach 
was argued as the best fit for this project. As this project aims to find segment groups for a specific mode choice, 
the latent class cluster model was further developed into a latent class choice model (LCCM). This gives the 
possibility of estimating the market shares regarding the probability of participants choice towards mode choice 
within each cluster. This means that the model consists of a class membership model, consisting of the 
categories from the attitudinal factors and socio-demographic variables, and a choice model consisting of a 
choice variable, denoting whether they drive a BEV, ICV or both.  
The model framework is displayed on figure 22. Based on the framework, the latent classes are based on the 
variables represented by the class membership model, in which the probability of individual i belonging to class 
s is calculated. Upon estimating these probabilities, it is possible to determine the utility function for individual i 
belonging to class s. The specification of the utility functions are presented in section 3.6 (Model specification). 
With the utility functions denoting the probability of belonging to class s, the probability of individual i choosing 
mode v can then be calculated. The utility used in this analysis is presented in section 3.6 (Model specification). 
The model framework displayed on figure 22 is inspired by (Thorhauge, Vij, & Cherchi, 2020).

5. Discussion 
Upon discussing the results, evaluating the parameters across the choice- and membership model is important to 
further make interpretations on the results. As presented in the results section regarding the cluster analysis 
consisting of 2 classes, the characteristics of the sociodemographic variables and attitudinal factors for class 1 
fitted with the existing literature for BEV users, as class group 1 had a 97.1% probability of driving a BEV (or 
both). Presented in section 4.1 (Model with 2 cluster groups), it was shown that the negative attitudes towards 
EV adoption and sociodemographic variables for class 2 fitted with the probability of 86% for driving an ICV. 
Based on these arguments, it is evident that the parameter estimations from the class membership and choice 
model are corresponding.  
Estimating appropriate segment groups, based on the data from the GREAT project, has been challenging. There 
are different reasons as to why this has been the case. One of the strengths of using a latent class cluster analysis 
is being able to identify latent variables and distinguishing appropriate segments. However, in order to make 
appropriate segments of such a large dataset at this, different types of variables, both in regards of 
socioeconomic, but also spatial variables, traveling habits and attitudinal variables are very relevant when doing 
market segmentation on transportation projects as this. This was one of the great challenges, as the models were 
very sensitive, which made the inclusion of additional variables difficult. In the final model, only age, income 
and household size (which was not significant) were included to describe the segment groups from a 
socioeconomic view. Additional variables as gender, education, and spatial variables indicating whether they 
lived in a rural/urban or suburban area, were included in the models, but were either insignificant or resulted in 
very high standard errors for some parameters. This made it very challenging in selecting which variables were 
to be included, and which were not, as the model became very sensitive when too many variables were included. 
This is especially seen in the models with 3 and 4 clusters.  
To provide more generic variables on each topic, several indicators on the same topics were included and then 
joined in a PCA analysis. However, a significant amount of the factors identified in the PCA analysis had a low 
variance. Therefore, it was necessary to account for this by categorizing the factors, with respect to keeping the 
variance as high as possible.  
As a result of the sensitive models, it was a necessity to reduce the number of parameters to reasonable results 
for the models, as an addition to the need of reducing the model in order to calculate market shares. For the 
models with three and four clusters, this was a difficult task, with the result of a large number of parameters 
taken out, even though the result of the LR test did not allow for it. However, because of the high sensitivity of 
the models, this was a necessity for the model for four cluster groups.  
==PAGE:56==
55 
 
Reasons to why the models are so sensitive, could be justified by the complexity of the models, and how they are 
programmed. In this project, Python Biogeme has been the preferred programming tool to set up the models as 
this is a very flexible tool for advanced modelling. However, the complexity of the model was a challenge for 
correctly implementing the different type of variables, with the treatment of the attitudinal variables presented in 
section 3.6 (Model specification) being an example of. 
Another consideration is whether another clustering method would have changed the outcome. Using a more 
heuristic based cluster method as K-means clustering, could have given a different outcome as this method is 
more algorithm based than the probabilistic approach, which could have provided a simpler clustering, but with 
better results, especially for the clusters of 3 or 4 classes. The downside of using this approach would have been 
evaluating the statistical properties of the model, as these are lesser known with K-means clustering. The use of 
K-means clustering would also not have had the possibility of including a choice model to determine market 
shares for the different mode choices.  
Evaluating on the data from the GREAT project, the share of respondents were primarily men. As presented in 
section 3.2.2.2 (Differences in car ownership), the characteristics of BEV users were likely to be male. However, 
as 90% of the BEV users were found to be male in this thesis, this indicates that males could be overrepresented. 
This could be related to how the sample was recruited, and which BEV users registered on E.ON which was the 
primary recruitment source for BEV users.  
The 2 segmentation groups obtained by the model show large differences between BEV users- and non-users. 
This is evident by the differences by both the sociodemographic- and attitudinal variables. However, having a 
solution with only two groups makes it difficult to differentiate between the differences within BEV users, and 
non-BEV users. If there had been 3 or 4 cluster groups segments within these cluster groups would have been 
identified that could be targeted to increase EV adoption.

6. Recommendation and conclusion 
6.1 Recommendations for increasing EV adoption 
In the latent class cluster analysis, it was possible to estimate two cluster groups. These two groups differentiated 
in socio-demographic and attitudinal characteristics. As the cluster analysis was further developed into a latent 
class choice model, it was possible to estimate the market share in terms of modal choice. With clear differences, 
as class 1 represented active BEV users, and class 2 non-active BEV users, the two segmentation groups were 
categorized as Existing EV adopters, and Potential EV adopters.  
The sample of Existing EV adopters had the sociodemographic characteristics of having a high probability of 
being above 36 years old and have an income of above 50.000kr monthly. These characteristics fits the existing 
findings on EV users and helps validating the general characteristics on EV users. In terms of attitudes, active 
BEV users from this sample do not agree that the functional barriers related to the challenges regarding charging 
(long traveling distance, keeping your BEV charged and the need for careful planning when driving an BEV). 
This indicates that the issues regarding charging, which is identified to be one of the largest barriers towards EV 
adoption, is improving. It was also found that Existing EV adopters agree with the statement towards personal 
norm regarding the consideration of the environmental benefits driving a BEV provides compared to driving an 
ICV. Ultimately, the sample of Existing EV adopters strongly agreed on the uncertainty that is related to the 
political support towards EVs. This strongly indicates that despite being an active BEV user, the need for 
political incentives is evident towards EV adoption.  
Participants represented as Potential EV adopters are likely to be between the age of 18 – 35, and have a 
monthly income of less than 30.000 kr. From the choice model used in the latent class choice analysis, these are 
regarded as non-BEV users. Regarding the attitudinal statement towards EV adoption, Potential EV adopters 
disagreed that the lifestyle regarding driving an EV is suitable for them. This indicates that having a specific 
lifestyle is not compatible with driving an EV. This could be further analysed with regards of what kind of 
lifestyle participants in the age of 18 – 35 with an income lower than 30.000kr. have, and what incentives could 
be offered towards this group in order to increase EV adoption. Examples of this could be political incentives 
that favours people having an income of below 30.000kr, where a higher tax reduction for smaller EV classes 
could be a solution.  
Another significant attitudinal factor is regarding the social norm for the participant. Potential EV adopters have 
a low social norm regarding driving a BEV. This means that people who do not have a BEV are also surrounded 
by important others who do not own an BEV and thus do not perceive social support or pressure for BEV 
adoption. 
==PAGE:58==
57 
 
In contrary with the findings on functional barriers for Existing EV adopters, the sample representing Potential 
EV adopters agrees that issues related to charging are evident. This indicates that the issues represented by 
functional barriers can be regarded as a barrier towards EV adoption. However, as the technology towards 
improving car batteries, and projects like the GREAT project are conducted, these issues can be of lesser impact 
in the future. However, as this is still regarded as a barrier towards EV adoption, it is recommended that this 
issue will still be focused on. Both in terms of improving the conditions related to charging, but also in 
informing the segment of non-BEV users of the continuous improvements that are being made towards this 
issue.  
6.2 Conclusion of this project 
It is evident that the results regarding the analysis in this project are not satisfactory. The results from the latent 
class choice model did not successfully identify more than two segments. With a higher number of segments and 
more distinct profiles, it would be possible to develop intervention strategies more specifically tailored to 
different segments with low EV adoption. As discussed, there were multiple factors regarding the challenges of 
identifying larger cluster groups. However, it was possible to identify segments based on the 2-class cluster 
analysis, and successfully identify cluster groups into Existing EV adopters and Potential EV adopters. It was 
possible describe these groups with limited socio-demographic variables, which matches previous literature 
towards identifying BEV-users. For the two cluster groups, it was possible to identify factors that influenced EV 
adoption, both positively and negatively.
