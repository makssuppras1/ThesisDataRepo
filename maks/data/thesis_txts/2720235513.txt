Abstract
Presence of neoepitopes on tumor cells does not necessarily have to lead to immuneôÙ
mediated cell rejectionëƒ it depends on the clonal fraction (i.e. fraction of cells bearing
each antigen) and the antigen itself. Hence, tumors often can scape the immune system
and coexist with TôÙcells that are the cells responsible to recognize somatically mutated
peptides exhibited by cancer cells on major histocompatibility complex class I (MHCôÙI). It
is of importance to identify which exhibited neoepitopes are immunogenic in order to be
included in immuneôÙoncology therapies. Even though, this is currently mainly conducted
thanks to mass spectrometry (MS), this technique has its drawbacks and new methods
to identify peptides capable of binding MHCôÙI molecules are being explored.
The aforementioned is Evaxion Biotech A/S case, which uses an artificial intelligence (AI)
platform for discovery and design of patientôÙspecific neoepitopes used to derive immuneôÙ
oncology therapies. Evaxion Biotech A/Sãs AI algorithms are trained by MS, yet, there is a
need of additional strategies to corroborate neoepitope candidates to be used in vaccine
compositions. One interesting assay for neoepitope corroboration is to identify peptides
that bind MHCôÙI molecules in vitro, thus, being one step closer to elect those neoepitopes
with higher probability of being immunogenic.
This master thesis project consists on developing an in vitro assay for the discovery of
MHCôÙI ligands using flow cytometry. A plasmid comprising an endoplasmic reticulum (ER)
signal peptide associated with an already processed neoepitope sequence is going to
be transfected to RMAôÙS cell line, which is a murine cell line deficient for the transporter
associated with antigen processing (TAP) protein. TAP protein is required for endogenous
antigen processing, and thus, presentation of MHCôÙI molecules since they are unstable
without a peptide binding to their groove. Presence of MHCôÙI molecules displaying the
specific neoepitope on a RMAôÙS cell surface would mean that the neoepitope is more
likely recognizable by the immune system and could have potential to be used in immuneôÙ
oncology therapies. This technique would allow to, perform a highôÙthroughput screening
of neoepitopes in one go, and create a MHCôÙI ligand library.

Introduction
Cytotoxic T cells (CTLs) primarily function to eliminate virusôÙinfected and malignant cells,
which they detect by specifically recognizing short peptides (i.e. epitopes) bound by maôÙ
jor histocompatibility complex class I (MHCôÙI) molecules on the surface of the target cell.
Regardless of the vast diversity of T cell receptor (TCR) repertoires and large number of
peptides within a specific antigen, CTLs responses between individuals sharing class I
alleles are predictably directed against one or few common epitopes. For instance, mice
studies have shown that when testing a pool of epitope candidates, C57BL/6 mice are
dominated by reactivity against one specific epitope, while in BALB/c mice, the main reôÙ
sponse is directed against a different epitope [1, 2]. Numerous mechanisms stress this
phenomenon of immunodominance, including TCR availability, antigen processing and
density, peptideôÙMHCôÙI binding affinity, and competition between T cell specificities [3].
Although the study of specific CTLs responses is feasible thanks to the restrictions on diôÙ
versity imposed by immunodominance, identifying epitopes is still a difficult and resourceôÙ
exhaustive task.
Fundamentally, epitopes are corroborated by demonstrating CTLs effector activity, such
as interferon (IFN)ôÙö° production or target killing, after incubation with the peptide and the
suitable class I restriction element. However, testing all peptide candidates within an
antigen is usually not possible, because of the costs of peptide synthesis, and the low
frequency of specific memory T cells, which restricts the number of peptides that can be
evaluated in a single assay.
The affinity data of the epitopes for MHCôÙI molecules is quantitatively the most important
parameter, generating a smaller list of likely candidates. Evaxion Biotech A/Sãs approach
to generate peptideôÙMHCôÙI binding data is through training their in silico algorithms with
data from MS. Yet, such predictions typically have a high rate of false positive binders,
thus, requires experimental confirmation. One of the simplest, most costôÙeffective methôÙ
ods of testing peptide binding to MHCôÙI molecules is a peptideôÙinduced MHCôÙI stabilizaôÙ
tion assay, which uses TAPôÙdeficient cell lines. In the absence of efficient TAPôÙmediated
transport of cytosolic peptides into the ER, assembled MHCôÙI molecules are unstable and
unable to advance to the cell surface. Yet, when these cell lines are transfected with a
plasmid encoding a signal peptide to enter the ER linked to a peptide capable of binding
MHCôÙI molecules, peptideôÙMHCôÙI complexes (p/MHCôÙI) are stabilized on the cell surface
and easily detected by flow cytometry with an antiôÙclass I antibody.
Here, the RMAôÙS cell line, which is TAPôÙdeficient, is being used to test pools of murine epiôÙ
topes (publicly available) to develop a valuable immunologic tool for identifying MHCôÙI ligôÙ
ands. This methodology should contribute to further generate an assay for human leukoôÙ
cyte antigen (HLA)ôÙligand discovery and defining epitopeôÙspecific CD8+T cell responses
in humans.

Aims
Nowadays the most widely used strategies to identify peptides that bind MHCôÙI are in silôÙ
ico peptide prediction and MSôÙbased immunopeptidomics. However, in silico predictions
have the disadvantage of depending on accuracy of prediction algorithms (section 3.6).

3.1
Antigen Presenting Cells (APCs)
The majority of nucleated cells in the body express MHCôÙI molecules, and thus can
present intracellular antigens. Therefore, in general, the term antigen presenting cell
(APC) refers to cells with the capacity to capture, process and present antigens by major
histocompatibility complex class II (MHCôÙII) molecules [4]. There are two types of APCs:
nonprofessional APCs (nonôÙpAPCs) and professional APCs (pAPCs).
nonôÙpAPCs can present, albeit at low levels, MHCôÙII molecules, and under certain condiôÙ
tions, such as inflammation, they increase the expression of MHCôÙII molecules. However,
they do not present antigens to naive T lymphocytes [4].
pAPCs present MHCôÙII molecules and coestimulatory signals, which allow the activation
of naive T lymphocytes. pAPCs includes dendritic cells (DCs), macrophages and B lymôÙ
phocytes. DCs are the primary pAPCs since they present all the machinery to generate
neoantigens [4]. Additionally, an important feature of DCs is the ability of crossôÙpresenting,
thus, having the ãabilityã to present antigens by MHCôÙI (see section 3.3.7).
3.2
T lymphocytes (T cells)
T cells recognize through their receptors (TCR) processed portions of antigen, generally
peptides, bound to major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules [5].
Each T cell has around 30 thousand identical antigen receptors on its surface, every reôÙ
ceptor consisting of two different polypeptide chains, named the T cell receptor öÝ (TCRöÝ)
and öý (TCRöý) chains. Each chain of the öÝ:öý heterodimer is composed of two immunoglobôÙ
ulin (Ig) domains, and the two chains are linked by a disulfide bond, resembling the strucôÙ
ture of an Ig Fab fragment (figure 3.1). A minority of T cells have a different receptor
designed by ö° and öÇ chains. ö°:öÇ T cell receptors have different antigen recognition propôÙ
erties, and their functions are still being elucidated. Although both types of T cell receptors
share similarities with the membraneôÙbound Ig they only have one antigenôÙbinding site,
while a B cell receptor has two, and are never secreted, while Igs can be secreted as
antibodies. In addition, even if T cell receptor share the three hypervariable regions with
Ig, it has a fourth hypervariability region (HV4) in both of its chains [5].
In contrast to B cell receptors which recognize directly an intact antigen, T cell recepôÙ
tors recognize short, continuous amino acid sequences. These peptide sequences are

MHC restriction phenomenon describes the fact that antigen recognition by CD8+ T cells
depends on the presence of specific MHCôÙI molecules on the APCs. This phenomenon

genotype ãrestrictsã the antigen specificity of the T cells. Hence, MHC restriction is a

The method begins by homogenizing and lysing tumor material, followed by immunoôÙ

amounts of starting tumor material is required to identify candidate epitopes [17, 15].
A further consideration is that MS relies on efficient ionization and fragmentation of the
peptides. Hence, the successful identification of the peptideãs sequence will depend on its
amino acid composition and the biochemical characteristics of such amino acids, which
will determine their capacity to be ionized and efficiently fragmented [17]. For instance,
MS has big issues with proteins that contain cysteines in their sequence since these
residues form disfulfide bonds between them. Hence, sample preparation involves the
artificial reduction of these disulfide bonds because if preserved in their native state they
would complicate subsequent enzymatic hydrolysis and interpretation of results. To avoid
the reôÙformation of the disulfide bonds, cysteine residues are protected by special groups,
usually by alkylation [18].

Peptide processingôÙpredictors and MHCôÙI binding

As seen in section 3.5, various methods have been developed to determine which pepôÙ

binations the binding affinity is measured, introducing this data in in silico algorithms to
develop highly accurate MHCôÙI binding predictors. The affinity data of the epitopes for
MHCôÙI molecules is quantitatively the most important parameter, since it generates a
smaller list of likely candidates [19]. Yet, data regarding further steps of the processing
of the precursor protein into MHCôÙI ligands, such as the processing by the proteasome,
other proteases and the TAP transporter, could be implemented to have more accurate
predictions of the p/MHCôÙI complexes [7].
Regarding peptide processingôÙpredictors, there are several studies that are trying to mimic
proteasome and immunoproteasome cleavage, taking into consideration and combining
both proteasome and immunoproteasome specificities since there is no evidence that
MHCôÙI ligands are made only by one of them. These studies suggest that flanking regions,
especially long flanking regions (up to 9 amino acids), play an important role in determining
the cleavage site. However, there is the possibility that for instance numerous NôÙtermini
of MHC ligands are generated by nonôÙproteasomal pathways and CôÙtermini may be postôÙ
trimmed by exopeptidases. TripeptidylôÙpeptidase II (TPPII) seems to be involved in the
generation of MHCôÙI ligandsã CôÙtermini [22]. In addition, hydrophobicity and sideôÙchain
properties related to the protein secondary structure can discriminate cleavage sites, the
majority of discriminating properties being in P1 residue, although positions P2, P1ã and
P2ã are also important [23]. Other studies attempt to predict TAP transport efficiency of
epitopes, Peters B et al. 2003, exploited the fact that the terminal residues of a peptide
determine most of its binding ability, specifically the CôÙterminus and the three outmost NôÙ
terminal residues [24]. However, TAPôÙindependent processing pathways also play a part
to the peptide repertoire, and these cannot be predicted with currently available transport
prediction tools [17].
By combining proteasomal cleavage predictions with those for TAP transport, a pool of
peptides available for MHC binding in the ER can be predicted. Hence, allowing to screen
protein sequences for potential MHCôÙI ligands independently of the type of MHC allele,
such screening can be used to obtain a shortlist of candidates [24]. Thereafter, MHCôÙI
binding predictors come into play.
Algorithms able of predicting peptide binding to MHCôÙI molecules are the most widely
used for in silico prioritization of neoantigens. These tools are commonly trained with
large datasets of experimentally defined MHCôÙI ligands and peptides eluted from MHCôÙI
molecules using MSôÙbased immunopeptidomics [17]. Peptide MHCôÙI binding prediction
takes into account the binding properties of peptides to MHCôÙI molecules (section 3.3.2
and 3.3.4), in regards to the anchor residues and the amino acids flanking them [17].
Despite advances in prediction algorithms, the tools that are currently available cannot
accurately identify which of the presented peptides will be immunogenic, being one of
the main limiting steps in neoepitopes screening [17]. Although the prediction of MHCôÙI
binding is a good predictor of immunogenicity, mounting evidence indicates that the bias
of in silico prediction toward strong binders (<500 nM) may cause immunogenic peptides
with lowôÙbinding affinity to be overlooked [17]. Therefore, several parameters have been
suggested to improve the prediction of immunogenicity of peptides. For example, pepôÙ
tide size (i.e., large), amino acid composition (i.e., aromatic residues) and differences in

3.7
How antigens become immunogens?
Although there are high accuracy predictions of which p/MHCôÙI complexes are formed
upon infection (seen in section 3.6), what distinguishes antigens from immunogens is
still inconclusive. Diverse factors could be involved [7]. Firstly, the quantity of presented
p/MHCôÙI complexes plays a role in immune targeting, this can be affected by peptideôÙ
MHCôÙI binding affinity and stability, the abundance of the precursor protein, and the effiôÙ
ciency of MHCôÙI ligand processing. Secondly, a p/MHCôÙI complex has to be recognized by
T cells (i.e. it has to be immunogenic). Thirdly, p/MHCôÙI complexes that derive from proôÙ
teins that are expressed early in infection are more probable to elicit a response. Fourthly,
even if an immunogen is presented under the right conditions, a response may be impeded
by regulatory processes if a (nonself) p/MHCôÙI complex is too similar to a self p/MHCôÙI
complex. It has been estimated that around oneôÙthird of the nonself p/MHCôÙI complexes is
too similar to self p/MHCôÙI. Lastly, due to competitive exclusion phenomenon, an immune
response may be outcompeted by other T cell responses because of limited survival facôÙ
tors. Hence, plenty of events eventually determines which antigens become immunogens
[7].
Calis et al. 2013, compared several immunogenic and nonôÙimmunogenic p/MHCôÙI for
immunogenicity. On one hand, they compared the amino acid frequencies in both sets
and showed that T cells have a preference for certain amino acids, especially aromatic and
large residues [7]. On the other hand, they analyzed the importance of different positions
of the presented peptide with respect to immunogenicity, as aforementioned in section
3.3.2, the middle positions (4 to 6) showed to be the most important. The data generated
from their results was introduced in a model to test if this could extrapolate immunogenicity
of new p/MHCôÙI. Validating their observations in crossôÙvalidations and in two data sets
they showed that immunogenicity is to some extend predictable [7]. Furthermore, by
using their prediction model they investigate the possibility of adaptation of the immune
system to recognize pathogenôÙderived peptides, and revealed that a preference for these
peptides exists [7]. Hence, suggesting that similarity of epitopes to microbial antigens
contributes to peptide immunogenicity [7, 25].
Although they found a different pattern of scores for amino acids preferences between HôÙ2
and HLA p/MHCôÙI complexes, HôÙ2 restricted p/MHCôÙI complexes have also an association
with immunogenicity when peptides present both aromatic and large amino acids. The
difference can be attributable to the altered peptide binding preferences of HôÙ2 molecules,
that present short 8mer peptides and use more and different auxiliary anchor positions
than HLAôÙI molecules. Likewise, the immunogenicity of peptides may be different when
presented on different HLA molecules [7].

Conclusions
It can be concluded that lipofectamine method of transfection worked subôÙoptimally when
transfecting RMAôÙS cells with gWizôÙGFP plasmid. After all the troubleshooting experiôÙ
ments we concluded that RMAôÙS cell line is quite resistant to being transfected with lipoôÙ
fectamine because of being a suspension cell line [38]. In addition, the transfection gets
more cumbersome when using large plasmids (ã7kb), like the plasmids control, since
uptake of the nucleic acid by the cells happens when the nuclear envelope breaksôÙdown
[41]. Therefore, we could not see transfection of RMAôÙS cells with control plasmids. ConôÙ
trary, HEK293 cell line could be transfected with both gWizôÙGFP and control plasmids,
verifying in this way that control plasmids work in mammalian cells since there was exôÙ
pression of GFP. Hence, they were not solely the problem for not having transfection in
RMAôÙS cell line.
Transfection of RMAôÙS cell line using nucleofection was successful and efficient for pmaxGFP
plasmid and control plasmids. In addition, there was experience acquisition on which proôÙ
gram and solution combination gives the best results in regards to transfection efficiency
and viability for RMAôÙS cell line when using 4DôÙNucleofector device. Although DNôÙ100
program and SE solution seems to be the best combination regarding both RMAôÙS cell
viability and transfection efficiency, further research needs to be done to optimize transôÙ
fection of RMAôÙS cell line.
Having efficient transfection of RMAôÙS cells allowed to verify the assay proofôÙofôÙconcept.
RMAôÙS cells transfected with a plasmid encoding a neoepitope that binds to MHCôÙI molecules,
such as SIINFEKL which has been previously described in literature [14, 5] are presenting
p/MHCôÙI complexes on the cell surface that can be easily detected with a PE Mouse AntiôÙ
Mouse HôÙ2kb antibody. On the contrary, RMAôÙS cells transfected with a plasmid encoding
a neoepitope that does not bind to MHCôÙI molecules, such as FEKIILSN, also described in
literature [14, 5] are not presenting p/MHCôÙI complexes on the cell surface. Additionally,
there was verification of the possibility of sorting RMAôÙS cells presenting p/MHCôÙI comôÙ
plexes from RMAôÙS cells not presenting p/MHCôÙI complexes thanks to gating on positive
and negative PE fluorescence. Nevertheless, validation of the sorting by realôÙtime PCR
was not successful because the used SIINFEKL primer showed to be not efficient enough
in samples with low cell concentration.
In regards to plasmid mutagenesis, siteôÙdirected mutagenesis method had a 71,4% effiôÙ
ciency. The calculated efficiency only takes into consideration the 14 sequenced colonies,
thus being far from representing reality. However, at present this cloning strategy method
works efficiently enough for making plasmid libraries to be used in a highôÙthroughput
screening assay.
Being able to distinguish neoepitopes candidates that bind MHCôÙI molecules from those
that do not is a powerful implement for the development of immunotherapies that could
overcome or relieve tumourôÙinduced immunosuppression. Considering that cancer immuôÙ
noediting can imply loss of tumor antigen expression [55], eventually escaping detection
by MS.

Future Perspectives
Having proved the assay, a future ambition is to optimize it further. On one hand, more
assays could be done to optimize nucleofection, for example testing again the four best
resulting combinations (SE solution with DNôÙ100, DSôÙ137, ENôÙ150 and EWôÙ113 program)
would elucidate the best program and solution combination in terms of transfection effiôÙ
ciency and viability of RMAôÙS cell line. On the other hand, it has been observed that when
performing minipreps to purify pools of plasmids after E. Coli transformation, the DNA
plasmids concentration ranges from 15 to 90 ng/ôçL. This concentration is subôÙoptimal for
subsequently transfection in RMAôÙS cells since usually 1ôÙ5ôçL of a plasmid aliquot at conôÙ
centration 1mg/mL is used. Improvements in E. Coli transformation and increasing DNA
plasmid recovery could be done using electroporation, finding which E. Coli strain is more
optimal for our DNA plasmids production, and purify the plasmids pool with a maxiprep kit
(gives a yield up to 1mg [56]) instead of a miniprep kit. Finally, if the assay would demand
higher efficiency of plasmid mutagenesis, Gibson assembly [57] technique could be an
alternative.
A next step of the project would be to stain RMAôÙS cells with an antiôÙmouse H2ôÙdb anôÙ
tibody. This would allow to discover MHCôÙI ligands from the 196 pool binding H2ôÙdb
molecules since RMAôÙS cell line has HôÙ2b haplotype, which includes H2ôÙkb and H2ôÙdb
molecules.
The assay final steps, which could not be achieved in this thesis project, are doing next
generation sequencing to RMAôÙS cells after being sorted by FACS in order to uncover
which MHCôÙI ligand are they bearing. Lastly, a library with the uncovered MHCôÙI ligands
would be constructed.
Finally, the assay described in this project could be also applied on T2 cell line, which
is a mammalian TAP deficient cell line. This would alternatively allow the discovery of
human MHCôÙI ligands.

A.4
PCR optimization
A first PCR reaction was done with a combination of standard PCR parameters [59] and
the recommended cycling protocol for siteôÙdirected mutagenesis with Platinum SuperFi II
DNA Polymerase [60].
Results showed bands on the PCR product expected size, 7000 bp, yet, the gel also
showed bands at 650 bp level, which were discussed to correspond to an offôÙtarget priming
of the primers (figure A.30).
Figure A.30: PCR reaction for 6 ligands pool with standard parameters. A) ParamôÙ
eters, B) gel electrophoresis and C) columns of the gel electrophoresis corresponding to
the ligands. See ladder on figure A.34.
To eliminate the offôÙtarget priming, annealing temperature was raised to 64ãÎC to enable
primers to have more binding specificity to the complementary sequence of DNA [61].
Increase of the annealing temperature gave almost no offôÙtarget priming but it gave low
amounts of PCR product (figure A.31). Therefore, extension time was increased to 175s
