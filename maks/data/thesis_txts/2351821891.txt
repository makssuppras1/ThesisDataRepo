1 Abstract 
Powder bed fusion is an additive manufacturing process where powder particles are 
selectively fused into 3D parts using a high energy source. The process was developed in the 
mid-eighties and has slowly evolved, and are today used in production within aerospace, 
dental, medico etc. A lot of research has already been conducted to improve the powder bed, 
and energy source. There is however still a lot of research to made, e.g. within scan strategies  
and materials. The scope of this project was to design and construct a powder handling 
platform for a powder bed fusion system, with accessible parameters, in order to create the 
framework for future research. This thesis covers the design and construction of such 
platform. Powder characteristics relevant to the process, has been identified and found for 
three different powders. A method for direct optical comparison, and a method to determine 
apparent powder layer density are presented. The platform was built and initial 
experimentation of powder deposition have been conducted, which showed that the platform 
was capable of depositing thin and uniform powder layers with a polymer powder, with a 
53% layer density of the apparent powder density. The platform can with minor 
improvements make the foundation for further experimental research with powder bed fusion 
of metal powders. 
2 Abstrakt 
Powder bed fusion er en additive fremstillingsmetode hvor et fint pulver bliver selektiv 
sammensmeltet til emner i 3D ved hjælp af en kraftfuld energi kilde. Processen blev udviklet 
i midt firserne og har siden udviklet sig til i dag, hvor den bliver brugt i industrien inden 
for luftfart, dental, medico etc. Der er allerede foretaget en del forskning på forbedring af 
processen indenfor pulverhåndtering, og energi kilde. Der er dog stadig rum for forskning 
indenfor bl.a. scan-strategier og materialer. Hovedformålet med dette projekt har været 
design og udvikling af en pulverhåndterings platform til et powder bed fusion system, med 
tilgængelige parametre, hvormed at danne rammerne for videre forskning. Denne afhandling 
dækker design overvejelserne og konstruktionen af omtalte platform. Samt pulver 
karakteristika relevante for processen, fundet for tre slags pulver. En metode for direkte 
optisk sammenligning af pulverlag, og en metoder til at bestemme egentlig lag densitet. 
Platformen er fremstillet, og begyndende eksperimentelt arbejde er udført. Hvormed det er 
påvist at platformen kan udlægge tynde og ensformige pulverlag med et polymer pulver, 
hvor laget har opnået 53% af tilsyneladende pulver densitet. Platformen kan yderemere, med 
mindre forbedringer danne grundlaget for yderligere eksperimentelt arbejde indenfor powder 
bed fusion af metal pulver.

6 Introduction 
The Additive Manufacturing (AM), process of Powder Bed Fusion (PBF), is selective 
fusion of regions on a bed of powdered material, typically either plastics or metals, 
using one or more thermal sources.  
This dissertation is about the development and construction of a powder handling 
platform, as part of a PBF system intended for research purposes at the Department 
of Mechanical Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark, (DTU). 
Commercially known AM processes, that utilizes powder bed fusion, are Selective 
Laser Sintering (SLS), acquired by 3D Systems Corporation (2001)[1], Direct Metal 
Laser Sintering (DMLS), from EOS – Electro Optical Systems (1994)[2], and 
Selective Laser Melting (SLM), from MTT Technologies[3] (1999), now SLM 
Solutions[4]. SLS was the first commercialized PBF process, originally intended for 
AM of plastic parts, developed by Dr. Carl Deckard and Dr. Joe Beaman, at the 
University of Texas at Austin (1986)[5]. While PBF of metals were developed in the 
middle of the nineties, by the Fraunhofer Institute for Laser Technology, 
Germany[6]. Their success with melting metal was primarily due to the use of lasers 
with a wavelength better tuned to the absorptivity of metal powders such as an 
Ytterbium laser, which were a key factor for the fusion of metal. They named the 
process Selective Laser Powder Remelting (SLPR), which have made the foundation 
of all commercially available SLM systems of today. 
A similar AM technology for metals is the method of Directed Energy Deposition, 
which utilizes a focused energy beam to fuse materials by melting, which are being 
deposited as opposed to the stationary powder bed. Typically the deposited material 
comes in wire or powder form. Compared to PBF, Directed Energy Deposition 
generally produces parts with a less defined surface, but has the benefit of being 
generally faster and scalable up to several meters.  
The terminology within PBF varies among competing manufactures, where sintering 
and melting are commonly used in the arrangement of words to describe the PBF 
process. However, usually SLS refers to the processing of plastic materials, and SLM 
refer to processing of metals. Whereas PBF is the standardized general term, 
referring to all processes involving selective fusion of regions on a bed of powdered 
material[7]. The use of sintering is a misleading historical term, since most AM 
processes using laser sintering involves both full and partial melting of the 
material[8]. 
==PAGE:10==
10 
 
The SLS/SLM process of metals can be described as selective fusion of a thin powder 
layer, typically 20-100μm thick. Using laser scanning, typically with galvanometric 
mirror drives, to trace out the layer in the X-Y plane. The laser source is one of high 
power, commonly 200W and upwards to several kW, Ytterbium fiber lasers, Nd: 
YAG and CO2 lasers are used. However due to the poor absorptivity for metal 
powders of the CO2 laser and higher cost of the Nd: YAG laser, almost all PBF 
machines processing metals use fiber lasers[9]. The more powerful the laser, combined 
with a high-grade fast scanning system, the faster the build rate is achievable. The 
laser beam is focused using an F-Theta lens or a vario-optic system (less common). 
After the fusion of the subsequent cross-sectional layer of the part, the build plate 
on top of which the powder bed lies, moves down according to one layer in the 
vertical direction. A new layer of powder is distributed across the build area, using 
a re-coater system, for the SLS process this is done by a counter-rotating leveling 
roll (inherent to the SLS process by 3D Systems). The powder bed is shielded by an 
inert gas, either argon or nitrogen, in order to prevent oxidization. This is critical to 
the process, and some manufactures use vacuum to purge the process chamber, which 
afterwards is filled with inert gas. Some use a laminar flow over the powder bed, to 
control oxidization and weld spatter. Unlike the Electron Beam Melting (EBM), 
process, which uses a high vacuum, and has only little (Helium) or no atmosphere. 
The powder is typically preheated to temperatures ranging 80-200℃, either by 
having a resistive heating element embedded in the powder bed, by having an 
infrared source above the powder layer, or a combination of both. Whereas the EBM 
process utilizes the electron beam to pre-heat the powder. Innate to PBF processes 
involving metals, is the practice of welding the 3D part being built onto the build 
platform, in order to prevent warping of the part[10]. This is due to the high thermal 
conductivity of metals, which during the process will create large temperature 
gradients, resulting in residual stresses. This effect can also be reduced or even 
eliminated by the use of sophisticated scan strategies, research within this area is 
one of the future scopes for this PBF platform [11]. The preheating procedure can 
ease this effect[12], but not eliminate the need to bond the part to the build plate. 
This introduces another step to the PBF process of metals. Unlike PBF processes 
with materials such as plastics where parts can “float” in the build chamber, and a 
larger build volume is achievable. The PBF process in general reduces the design 
flexibility for parts, due to the nature of the process. It is not possible to make hollow 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
parts, without having residual loose powder within the cavities. Overhangs cannot, 
by rule of thumb exceed 45°, without having rigid support structures, which 
afterwards requires machining to be removed.

1. The SLS/SLM process provides a standard description of all PBF processes, which 
share the same key characteristics, with exceptions on heat sources, atmospheric 
conditions, laser scan patterns and details of the material deposition.

6.1 Motivation 
The industrial Additive Manufacturing, AM, machines utilizing the process of 
powder bed fusion are typically being developed and built to reach the requirements 
of a high-end, production facility. Typical use of this technology in the industry, is 
found within aerospace, dental, medical, jewelry, mold design and other industries 
that need to produce parts that could not otherwise have been made using traditional 
manufacturing methods, or for low volume production of complex geometries. To 
meet this customer segment, and to reduce part costs by reducing fabrication time. 
The commercial industrial machines use high power lasers and high-end laser scanner 
systems. Which makes the technology a relatively cost and time effective 
manufacturing technology. Commercial machines are intended for production and 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
meet the requirements for fabrication of advanced parts, they are becoming more 
and more automated with fever human interaction needed. Nevertheless they are 
closed systems with very few variable parameters. Current commercial machines are 
merely being sold with varying sizes of build volume, number of lasers, scan speed, 
precision and power, as the only variables. Some do allow for the use of, and 
experimentation with third party powders. For research purposes, such machine does 
not meet the requirements, since all parameters should be accessible. Furthermore, 
the need for fast and uncomplicated fabrication of parts are less important compared 
to the commercial machines. 
The motivation for this project was sparked from the desire of developing a machine 
platform designed for laboratory testing, where all parameters such as energy source, 
machine control, deposition mechanism and materials, are interchangeable. The 
process technology of powder bed fusion, offers unique fabrication methods for metals 
which is not met by other AM technologies, regarding surface quality, mechanical 
properties and design freedom. To conduct and verify research within the influence 
of scan pattern strategy, local microstructure manipulation during fabrication, and 
thus optimization of local material properties, an open PBF platform is essential. 
The future of PBF related research can be leading contributor to a giant leap within 
manufacturing of metal parts with varying geometrical and material properties. E.g. 
bearing surfaces, areas with vibrational damping, and undoubtedly, topology 
optimization.  
Therefore, the scope of this project should be to develop and manufacture a Powder 
Bed Fusion test platform making the basis for investigation and verification of the 
technology. This report covers the authors work with the development, 
manufacturing and verification of a powder handling system for described PBF 
platform. The work carried out on the development and construction of the part 
regarding the energy source and control of the PBF system will be covered by the 
master project; Development, Construction and Process Parameter Investigation of 
a Laser Driven Additive Manufacturing Rig (SLM/SLS) by Sebastian Aagaard 
Andersen.

6.3 Overview of Powder Bed Fusion 
As described in the introductory, section 6, the technology of PBF is diversified with 
many developer dependent varying mechanisms. However, the process can be 
differentiated by the details in the mechanisms behind sintering method, energy 
source, material deposition process, and machine configuration. Some of the most 
important details will be discussed in the following section.

6.3.1 Binding Methods 
Sintering 
Sintering is a process for bonding of particles and thus forming a solid (porous) 
structure, by heating without melting (alternatively in combination with, or by 
pressure). There is three distinct sintering mechanisms: diffusion (Solid-State 
Sintering), chemically induced sintering and Liquid Phase Sintering. 
Solid-state sintering occurs when a powdered material is heated. Adjacent powder 
particles will start forming and bond during a diffusion mechanism, see Illustration 
3. After bonding, the bonded bulk material will experience a change in global 
properties, such as strength, density, ductility, and thermal/electrical conductivity. 
As a result of the process the bulk material shrinks. 
 
Illustration 3 - Solid State Sintering, 1) Adjacent powder particles, 2) Solid-
state material transport, with necks forming and a pore in the middle, material 
volume decreases. 
From Illustration 3, it can be seen that once the particles diffuse, the center distance 
decreases. A second sintering mechanism known as surface diffusion, occur when the 
powdered material is heated, atoms will be released from the surface of the particles. 
The surfaces fuse due to vapor-phase transport, and the center distances stay the 
same. This mechanism is utilized in liquid-phase sintering. If a mix of different metal 
particles are used, neighboring particles will form an alloy in the liquid phase. If one 
component of the mix has a lower melting point, that component will melt before 
the other and surround it. This is used to create very strong and dense parts, e.g. 
tungsten-carbide tools[13].  
Chemically induced sintering is as the name implies, a mechanism that uses 
thermally activated chemical reactions between powders to induce sintering. This is 
primarily used for ceramic materials, and are of less importance to AM.  
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
During laser sintering, a combination of Solid-State Sintering (different diffusion 
mechanisms), and Liquid Phase Sintering will occur. Sintering processes are complex 
by nature, and a complete study of the process would breach the frame of this project. 
It is however, necessary to know that certain controlling parameters exist, such as 
material composition, grain size, bed temperature and laser energy density. These 
are crucial when controlling the fusion of the powdered materials, and essential to 
the properties of the final part. 
 
Full Melting 
The characteristics of metal alloys such as conductivity, and a distinct melting point, 
makes them a suited material for full melting in powder bed fusion. The rapid melting 
and solidification enabled by the PBF process, allows for parts with tailored 
properties, distinct from those obtained from traditionally processed parts. The 
controlling parameters for the process will be configurable on the platform. The 
configuration of the high power laser, will allow for various process variations, such 
as beam positioning, power and frequency. Thus allowing for unveiling the full 
potential of the PBF process. [3] 
The engineering alloys which are available as gas atomized powders, being utilized 
for AM are plentiful. The most common being; austenitic stainless steels, tool steels, 
aluminum alloys, cobalt alloys, low-alloy steels, nickel alloys, titanium alloys and 
precious metals. 
PBF manufactured metal parts can experience various process scenarios due to the 
nature of the process of powder bed fusion, e.g. very high cooling rates are achievable 
due to the high conductivity of the powder bed. This facilitates the exploitation of 
localized material properties, obtained from refined microstructures. Formation of 
non-equilibrium phases, and supersaturated solutions. Second-phase particles such 
as inclusions and carbides, or extremely fine, refined microstructures, are side effects 
of the build process. If being controlled, these can be extremely beneficial for the 
manufacture of parts with advanced material properties. One example of application 
could be a part with bearing surfaces, which could be 3D printed using one laser 
strategy utilizing rapid full melting for maximum strength, whereas the rest could 
be printed using another strategy utilizing sintering, yielding a porous bulk for 
damping properties. For PBF with plastics, the approach is a bit different due to 
their different characteristics. Nylon being the most popular powder, and belonging 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
to the semi crystalline polymer group, has a distinct melting point. To produce high 
strength parts, full melting is preferred. However, due to the poor conductivity of 
plastics, the heat will be concentrated around the fused area, thus resulting in part 
growth. Therefore, the parameters chosen for the process, is often a compromise 
between strength and precision. Where the fusion are tuned to happen at the 
threshold between full melting and liquid phase sintering. 
 
Illustration 4 - Illustration of full melting zone and surrounded sintering zone 
Due to the nature of these binding processes, whether the technology is referred to 
as sintering or melting, often both of them are present. As opposed to non-AM 
(traditional) sintering processes. The PBF platform, disregarded the commercial 
designation, would be capable of utilizing both sintering and melting. Since these 
processes mostly depends on the particle composition and energy source. Hence SLS 
or SLM as a general term being a historical inaccuracy.  
Furthermore, in order for the process to be as economically competitive as possible, 
the fusion of the powder should happen as quickly as possible in order to increase 
the build rate. This is achieved by a fast moving energy source, with a high energy 
flux, and fusion by melting. As fusion by sintering alone, typically takes much longer 
time. Hence, the reason for most AM processes, not to use sintering as the primary 
mechanism of fusion. Nonetheless, sintering still plays secondary roles in the PBF 
process [14].

6.3.2 Energy Sources and Control 
The first SLS machines developed for plastic powders utilized CO2 lasers as energy 
source, to sinter or melt the powder. The Fraunhofer Institute for Laser Technology 
developed and refined the technique, enabling selective laser melting of metals, by 
replacing the laser to one with a wavelength suiting the absorptivity of metal 
powders.  
To begin with an Nd: YAG laser was used, which now have been replaced by fiber 
lasers, by practically all commercial SLM systems of today. Compared to Nd: YAG 
lasers, fiber lasers outperforms them on virtually any aspect with regards to AM, 
such as; energy efficiency, beam quality, price and maintenance[15]. Despite this, the 
energy source is still considered one of the more costly components of the PBF 
platform. Nevertheless, many commercial machines of today, are beginning to utilize 
more than one laser in order to increase build rates. Each laser having their own 
scanner system, guiding the laser beam through its own F-Theta lens, independently 
fusing one square of the respective cross section. As a result the build rate is also 
increased, with a magnitude similar to the number of lasers. An example to this is 
the quad laser system, EOS M 400-4, which is a twin of the 1kW system EOS M 
400, with a relative large build area of 400x400x400mm. Except that the quad laser 
system utilizes 4-400w lasers, increasing the build rate of up to 4 times, giving a 
build rate of 100cm3/h[16]. 
The laser beam is controlled by a scanning system, which works by controlled 
deflection of the laser beam with two mirrors, one for each direction of the build 
plane. The positioning of the mirrors is done by a galvanometer. Which works as an 
actuator, also known from analogue instruments used to measure electrical current. 
The actuation happens when an electric current is running through a coil placed in 
a magnetic field, or vice versa. Therefore two types of galvanometers exist, which is 
referred to as a moving magnet or moving coil type. The ones used for scanning 
galvanometer mirror systems are generally of the moving magnet type. Whereas the 
galvanometer mechanisms found in hard disk drives, is of the moving coil type. The 
low weight of the actuator mechanism (magnet and mirror), makes the positioning 
of the mirror ultra-fast. Due to the nature of the galvanometer, the precision of the 
positioning is determined by the ability of the controller to tune the output voltage. 
The accuracy can be controlled by using a closed-loop system, which utilizes an 
optical encoder, to monitor the angular positioning. Typically the mechanical range 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
of the scanning galvanometer is ±12,5°. The angular resolution achievable of a small 
beam diameter scanning galvo mirror system from Thorlabs is 0.0008° (15µrad) [17], 
where a high resolution stepper motor will achieve 0,36° (1000 step per revolution). 
6.3.3 Material Deposition and Handling 
The precision, surface quality and thus quality of parts produced by a PBF process 
is highly controlled the by the characteristics of the powder, especially shape and 
size distribution. Many of the aspects of powder handling will be controlled by the 
aspects of handling ultra-fine powders. Therefore the material deposition can be 
specified by the features needed to deal with such powder. Generally the smaller the 
particles, the deposition mechanism is capable of handling, while dealing with the 
side effects, the better the PBF platform will be. The perfect powder deposition 
mechanism, will be able to deposit, layers with a thickness and uniformity down to 
a size ultimately allowed by the powder characteristics. 
 
Figure 1 - Size comparison of different objects. 
From Figure 1 a size comparison of different objects is presented in order to illustrate 
the scale of the powders used for PBF. 
To put things into perspective, gas atomized metal powder particles used for AM 
resembles something between ash and the thickness of a human hair. For most 
powders, the distribution follows a Gaussian distribution, with 50% of the particles 
being comparable to the size of icing sugar. 
==PAGE:19==
19 
 
 
Figure 2 - Spoon full of MS1 powder from EOS 
When particle size decrease the flowability of the bulk powder, starts to be influenced 
more by the friction between particles, and electrostatic forces. Imagine having a 
spoon full of granulated sugar, and one full of icing sugar, that are being poured into 
a cup. The one with granulated sugar will start emptying at a shallow angle, and the 
sugar will flow at a steady pace. Compared to the one with icing sugar where the 
sugar will tend to flow in large clumps, unless the spoon is shaken or tapped to the 
brim of the cup. Even though the particle shape of the two sugars probably are 
similar. This illustrates the need for an energy input, in this example a tap on the 
spoon, to make the fine powder flow. With respect to the effectiveness of the recoater, 
an energy input in the form of a mechanism to make the powder flow is needed. 
Being a counter rotating roll, a vibrating blade, or something different entirely. 
The motion of the recoater will inevitably make some of the fine particles airborne, 
which will make them migrate from the powder bed, onto surfaces in the process 
chamber. Therefore surfaces sensitive to the particles, needs to be shielded. Especially 
any optics will be sensitive to particles, which might deflect the laser beam or create 
a spot for energy to be absorbed. Together with moving parts, where particles will 
create excessive wear or dramatically increase friction.  
Furthermore, when particles begin to be small enough for the surface-area-to-volume 
ratio to reach a point where the material becomes more reactive due to the increased 
surface energy. As a result the powder bed must be shielded from oxygen, to avoid 
reactive processes, and the powder handling should therefore not generate sparks 
which could in the presence of oxygen initiate an explosion. For more reactive metals 
such as aluminum, the process is complicated even further, since the powder must 
be shielded from oxygen at all steps of the process.

When the layer thicknesses start to approach the magnitude of the diameter of the 
largest particles, rather high forces will be exerted onto the recoater mechanism. 
Naturally there will be a limit to the minimum layer thickness, dictated by the 
particle size distribution. PBF systems often operate with layer thicknesses, 
comparable to the particle size. The smaller the gap between the build plate, or the 
fused part, and the recoater, the bigger the contact pressure. Together with the 
motion of the deposition, the powder becomes abrasive to the parts. 
Ultimately the finer the powder being used is, the finer and more detailed the parts 
become, but all the aspects identified for powder handling, will exaggerate the 
requirements for the powder handling mechanism.

9.1 Concepts for a Powder Handling Platform 
There are various ways in which a powder handling system can deposit powder 
layers. Many different systems have been developed, and the absence of a single 
general system, is caused by manufactures simply avoiding one another’s patents, 
such as the counter-rotating roller of 3D Systems for the recoating mechanism. 
Regardless the details, any powder handling system for PBF all share the same 
principal design. 
9.1.1 Considerations 
The structural design of a powder layer deposition mechanism consists of a horizontal 
feed that moves a powder deposition mechanism over a build plate. The build plate 
moves, between each powder deposition, the height of one layer vertically 
downwards. Thus ensuring a continuous powder layer by layer deposition, 
characterizing the 3D printing manufacturing process. 
The horizontal feed moving the powder distribution mechanism can either move 
linearly, or in a rotating motion as seen on Figure 5. The entire system containing 
feeder and powder deposition, is referred to as the recoater. The linear feeds is 
typically isolated from the powder, either by placement along the plane, 
perpendicular to the build plane, or below (underside). The powder deposition 
mechanism, can be made by a simple well defined linear shape, such as a doctor 
blade, known from rotogravure printing, or by a rotating roll. The counter rotating 
roll is protected by a patent owned by 3D systems and are utilized by their ProX 
machines[23], whereas the blade is utilized by most other manufactures such as EOS. 
These two being the most used approaches, other less popular methods have been 
developed. Such as the use of a flexible wiper to flatten the deposited layer, developed 
by Trumpf[24]. Also post grinding of the fused layer have been developed by 
Fraunhofer Institute[25]. This adds an additional process step, and inevitably 
complicates the design.  
==PAGE:26==
26 
 
 
Figure 3 - Process chamber of a Trumpf TruPrint 1000, showing linear feeds perpendicular to the plane, a 
blade for recoater and circular powder feed and build chamber. FabMeta lMag. (2016, June 7). TruPrint 1000 
3D Metal Printing. 
The use of a rigid blade or roller, involves fairly high forces, exerted on the recoating 
mechanism that requires a sturdy mechanical design. On the other hand, a rigid 
recoater are capable of producing, very precise layers, which is probably why the 
blade or roller system are the most common. 
 
Figure 4 – Process chamber of an EOS EOSINT M 270, showing linear feeds perpendicular 
to the plane and a blade for recoater. GPI Prototype & Manufacturing Services. (2013, 
January 15). Direct Metal Laser Sintering (DMLS) Overview. 
The deposition mechanism transports the powder from the reservoir in a scraping 
action, distributing it in a thin layer across the build plate, and delivers the rest, to 
the powder overflow. While the blade or roll maintains a constant height through 
the motion, the height of the layers will be dictated by the vertical position of the 
build plate. The precision and repeatability of the layer deposition will therefore be 
==PAGE:27==
27 
 
determined by the precision of both the horizontal movement of the recoater, and 
by the vertical movement of the build plate.  
During the powder deposition, a vibrator can be used to improve the flow ability of 
the powder, fluidizing it, this typically requires ultrasonic frequencies. Both blade or 
roll mechanisms can be combined with a powder extrusion mechanism, known as a 
hopper, forming a sophisticated recoater, consisting of a powder delivery and 
depositing mechanism. Alternatively, the powder is delivered from a reservoir 
(powder feed) next to the build chamber, as illustrated on Illustration 1, where the 
powder is delivered from below as opposed to the hopper which delivers it from 
above.

Some DMLS systems from EOS, uses a hopper and doctor blade recoater system. 
While 3D Systems use the counter rotating roller adapted from their SLS systems. 
The AM systems from Realizer, utilizes a more unconventional approach to the 
recoater mechanism, by distributing the powder with a rotating motion, as seen on 
wiper systems. The recoater system and process chamber of a Realizer SLM 100 is

Preliminary experiments were carried out in order to examine the solution space for 
the recoater mechanism. A blade based recoater was made by using a linear piece of 
steel with a spacer at each end. The blade were dragged by hand through several 
materials, including kitchen flour, fine glass beads and a Nylon/polystyrene powder 
used for SHS. These simple experiments showed that it was possible to create smooth 
layers, by scraping material from a pile with a steady linear motion. With a uniform 
layer height defined by the thickness of the spacer, refer to Figure 6.

Experiments using a rotating roll was similarly carried out. A rod was dragged 
through a pile of powder while being rotated by hand.  It was demonstrated that by 
counter rotating the roll relative to the forward motion, the best deposition were 
obtained. By rotating the roll the same way relative to the forward motion, the

powder did not flow in front of the roller, but rather packed beneath it. This resulted 
in non-uniform layers and much higher forces were felt during the motion of 
deposition. 
Based on these experimental observations, two concepts for a recoater mechanism 
was chosen for use in the platform. Namely a rigid blade and a counter rotating roll. 
9.1.2 Powder Deposition 
From these simple experiments it was shown that by moving a blade through a pile 
of powder, in a linear motion over a flat surface, a thin layer of powder could be 
deposited. Hereby sprung the idea for a simple design, which would in-cooperate, 
recoater system and material reservoir in one. By having the recoater, relocate a pile 
of powder, from one end of a plane (powder bed) to the other, several powder related 
obstacles would be eliminated. This would require an amount of powder equal to, or 
greater than that required to fill the build chamber to the depth of the part, adding 
the amount needed to distribute the last layer. Thus always having a surplus of 
powder during deposition. Besides being simple, this would eliminate the need for a 
powder reservoir and respective delivery system, together with the powder overflow 
and storage. These can be complicated to design, since several effects must be 
considered when handling ultra-fine powder. This approach will require a linear 
horizontal motion, to move the recoater over the powder bed. Together with a blade 
or roller capable of vertical movement, in order to be able to pass the powder pile 
after deposition. However, the powder feed would be eliminated and only one 
vertically moving plate would be needed, to position the build plate, thus 
determining the layer height. 
==PAGE:30==
30 
 
 
Illustration 5 - Recoater mechanism illustrated with a counter rotating roll moving the powder pile to one 
side while distributing a thin layer 
 
The linear motion will be guided by two linear guides, with appertaining linear 
bearings and housings. The housings supporting the recoater mechanism, will be 
actuated by a spindle and nut, coupled to a stepper motor. The recoater will consist 
of a rotary system, actuated by another stepper motor. By placing a blade or a roller 
on two arms, mounted on an axle actuated by the stepper motor. The axle can be 
rotated, allowing the recoater to be lifted at each end of the deposition, avoiding the 
pile of powder. Furthermore, the rotary can act as a tool magazine and will enable 
simultaneously use of multiple tools. By placing them on separate arms, placed on 
the circumference of the axle. As an example, a rotating roller, a blade, an infrared 
heating element and a sensor device, could be placed on separate arms on the axle.  
==PAGE:31==
31 
 
 
Illustration 6 - Rotary system, with different tools relevant to the recoating process 
 
A deposition process could then consist of several overpasses each with a different 
function. Firstly an overpass with the blade facing downwards distributing the first 
layer of powder, then a second overpass with the infrared heating element facing 
downwards, pre-heating the powder. Another overpass with the roller rotating, while 
being moved at a steady pace, through the powder, depositing a fine layer in its 
wake. As a last step, one last overpass could be made, with the sensor device facing 
down, either examining the deposited powder, or the areas which had been fused by 
the laser. Between each overpass, the rotary axle would turn 90°, switching the places 
of each tool. 
9.1.3 Vertical Stage 
Piston 
The vertical stage consists of a guide piston sliding in a cylinder, a trapezoidal 
spindle, and stepper motor with mounting. The actuation of the spindle are made 
through a lead screw nut driven by a timing belt. The height of the layers will be 
determined by the movement of the build plate. This requires a precise and 
repeatable movement in the vertical direction, since this will be a highly controlling 
factor for the outcome of the print process. The horizontal position of the build plate 
is also an important factor, unlike the recoater, which has to maintain a steady 
height, but can have variations parallel to the powder bed. If the vertical movement 
of the build plate, fail to follow a straight line, the layers of the part will not align, 
and this will reduce dimensional accuracy and surface quality of the part. To ensure 
a linear vertical movement, the build plate will be mounted on top of a piston. The 
==PAGE:32==
32 
 
piston will be contained by a cylinder with a close running fit between the piston 
and cylinder wall. A piston seal will act as a guide and prevent escaping particles, 
ensuring a perfect linear motion. Furthermore the seal will hermetically seal the 
piston-cylinder connection. The piston, cylinder and piston-ring should be abrasion 
resistant during the temperatures at which pre-heating of the powder will occur. 
Furthermore, the piston ring should keep the powder from escaping the build 
chamber through the piston-cylinder clearance. Since the piston is running in the 
cylinder acting as a linear guide, the function of the vertical movement is sensitive 
to particles escaping the seal. The design depends on a very tight seal between the 
piston-cylinder clearance and the build volume, in order to keep powder particles 
packing in the clearance, causing high friction, ultimately blocking the vertical 
motion. 
Build Plate 
Due to inhomogeneous cooling of the part layers, internal thermal induced stress in 
the part will be exerted on to the build plate. Therefore the build plate itself should 
be rigid and able to withstand the warping of the part, which will be welded on to 
the build plate during fusion of the first layer. Furthermore, the build plate should 
be made from a material with the same composition as the powder being processed, 
or at least from a compatible material. 
9.1.4 Powder Recycling 
When pre-heating the powder bed, solid-state sintering of local adjacent particles 
will occur, thus forming agglomerates. Average particle size will increase, thus 
distorting the particle size distribution, changing powder deposition, and melting 
characteristics of the powder. If very large clusters are formed, recoating may even 
be impossible, due to the relation between particle size and minimum layer height. 
A positive side effect of the pre-heating induced solid state sintering of the powder 
bed, is an increase of powder bed bulk strength, which can ease stress induced 
warping of the part. As a result of these effects, once the powder have been used, it 
cannot be used again without being processed, to insure a repeatable and consistent 
build properties. Typically by utilizing either via a vibrating sieve based sifting 
device or an air classifier system. When using plastic powders, solid state sintering 
and changing material properties, is more pronounced, especially for Nylon which is 
widely used, also changes its molecular weight. Therefore when recycling plastic 
powders, new powder have to be mixed with the recycled. 
==PAGE:33==
33 
 
Several commercial machines offers modular additions to their PBF platform, which 
offers handling and processing of used material in preparation for recycling. The 
powder supply unit, “PSX”, of the “SLM 500” from SLM Solutions, uses a vibration 
sieve to process used metal powder. This process takes place under the inert gas 
atmosphere, to shield the material. This, together with other important features, 
makes the top of the line machine; SLM 500, highly automated. Making it fit for a 
production environment, suited for batch production. Several other manufactures 
offers similar additions to their top-drawer systems. Which have helped moving PBF 
from prototyping, into production, by reducing material cost and overall 
manufacturing time.  
9.1.5 Protective Atmosphere 
Because of the reactive nature of the powder, shielding it from oxygen during 
processing is imperative to a positive outcome. This is done by a protective 
atmosphere, consisting of an inert gas such as nitrogen or argon. Presence of oxygen 
within the process chamber, will result in surface oxides to form and create slag while 
the powder is exposed to the laser beam. The oxides alters the absorptivity of the 
laser, and acts as insulation for inter particle heat exchange. Thus changing the 
powder bed characteristics, and reducing the liquefied powders ability to flow and 
wet adjacent powder particles, as a result strong bonding cease to form[26].  
To ensure a purging of oxygen from the process chamber, a vacuum can be used. It 
is however important to replace the vacuum by a protective atmosphere, since for 
most metals the vaporization point moves below melting point, under low pressures. 
Which means that during fusion of the layer, the powder will vaporize before melting. 
To prevent vaporization, the process chamber should be kept under a partial 
pressure[27]. 
Commercial systems has sophisticated atmosphere control system, and use either 
argon or nitrogen from an external source or from gas cylinders. The process chamber 
contains very little oxygen, 3D Systems claims that their ProX DMP 320 are capable 
of creating an atmosphere with an oxygen concentration as little as 25ppm[28]. 
Compared to oxygen concentration in air with 21%≈210.000ppm.  
Since nitrogen is abundant in our atmosphere, it is possible to extract high purity 
nitrogen, using a nitrogen generator. Several types of nitrogen generators exist, but 
generally the process is energy-efficient and cost effective[29]. Top of the line systems 
from EOS, all come with a nitrogen generator integrated[30].

10  Manufacturing Process of the Platform 
The recoating process together with the selective fusion of the respective cross 
sectional areas, consists of several steps. The recoater should be calibrated to be level 
with the build plate, to ensure a controlled deposition of the first layer, before the 
print process can begin. When the recoater and build plate are parallel, the powder 
is loaded into the powder handling chamber, through the access holes. The powder 
should be arranged in a pile in front of the recoater, or on top of the build plate. The 
access holes are sealed, using hatches with O-ring sealing. Then the air is evacuated 
from the process chamber, using a vacuum pump. When a significant vacuum is 
obtained, an inert gas will be led into the process chamber flushing it. These two 
steps might be repeated several times, ensuring a purge of oxygen trapped in the 
powder, and any oxygen that might cling to surfaces. This process can be aided by 
an oxygen sensor located in the exhaust flow, to monitor the presence of oxygen in 
the chamber. When a suitable atmosphere have been established, the print process 
can begin. The build plate is lowered by the height of the initial layer. The recoater 
moves through the pile of powder, depositing a thin compacted layer in its wake. 
When the build plate has been covered, the selective fusion of the respective cross 
sectional areas begin. The recoater hereafter changes place with the powder pile, in 
order to position itself to the initial position, ready to deposit another layer. The 
build plate is lowered by the height of a layer, and the recoater deposits another 
layer on top of the fused regions and the remaining powder. The last steps are 
repeated until all the layers are deposited and the final height of the 3D part is 
reached. The recoating process is illustrated on Illustration 7.

12.1 Powder Deposition and Compacting 
The powder layer density of the powder bed is generally considered an important 
factor for the outcome of the print process. The higher the layer density, the denser 
and stronger the parts will be. The part density will be influenced by the compacting 
of the layer, since voids between the particles can induce pores.  Porosities is 
generally undesirable from a part perspective, since they will have a negative 
influence on tensile strength and fatigue properties during cyclic loads. Since a 
porosity resembles a crack which can start propagate under certain conditions and 
lead to immature failure. For critical load bearing parts, hot isostatic pressing as a 
post treatment of the part can be used to increase the density, and thus improve the 
properties. Even so, the density of parts produced by PBF of metals, can reach rather 
high values >99% relative density compared to bulk material[39]. The nature of the 
PBF process allows for melting of tiny amounts of material at a time. Therefore for 
metal alloys segregation of alloying elements, during the solidification, happens on a 
much smaller scale compared to traditional casting. This leads to more uniform 
composition and microstructure of the part, which is advantageous. 
==PAGE:41==
41 
 
Due to the flow characteristics of a fine powder, to ensure a maximum powder layer 
density, the powder needs energy exerted from the recoater mechanism, to compact 
the powder ensuring a density closer to the tapped density rather than the poured 
(apparent) density. There is a natural boundary for the maximum compacting of a 
powder, which is determined by the particle size distribution. The theoretically 
highest compacting can be found by examination of the particle geometry. If the 
powder consist of perfect spheres, a theoretical value for the highest compactness 
could be found. The presence of smaller spheres, to fill the voids between the bigger 
spheres, will determine the compactness. The finer the particles present, the denser 
the powder layer. 
 
 
Illustration 9 – Illustrative representation of face 
centered cubic compact packing, with perfect even 
spheres. 
For a hypothetic monomodal, monodisperse particle distribution, the particles will 
with a sufficient energy input, assume the highest possible density when packing in 
a face centered cubic orientation. Due to the uniform size and shape, voids will 
inevitably be present between the particles. As illustrated on Illustration 9. This 
packed density will represent a relative density of about 74%[32] compared to bulk 
density. 
To increase this density, an addition of smaller particles, changing the dispersity, 
will result in voids being occupied by particles with an adequate size. A figure for 
the maximum density is the Anderson’s equation: 𝑊= 𝐴∙𝐷𝑞  
Where W is the cumulative weight fraction (actual frequency), and D is the class 
diameter. A and q are empirical factors. The best density can be obtained for values 
of q between 0,5 and 0,67, which corresponds to a large part of smaller particles[33]. 
==PAGE:42==
42 
 
This density is a theoretical value, and would require perfect conditions. Therefore 
given the correct particle size distribution, the powder layer density will be 
determined by the ability of the deposition mechanism to deliver the energy input 
needed for a maximum compacting. 
An effective approach to the highest powder layer density would be to create a 
similar sized (monomodal) bulk powder, and then determine the actual void size to 
be filled, as illustrated on Illustration 9, with smaller particles through multimodal 
granulometry. [41] 
To achieve the highest degree of compacting allowed by the specific size distribution, 
the parameters associated with the powder deposition must be carefully adjusted 
accordingly, with regards to applied compacting force by the recoater onto the 
powder bed. These parameters can be characterized by; the dynamics of the recoater, 
being vibrational, rotational and linear speed and the recoater surface contact 
properties (the surface roughness of a roller should be finer than the smallest particle, 
to avoid particles sticking). Lastly the process parameter - layer height, will also be 
controlling for the compacting. 
The mechanisms of powder deposition and powders, such as spread ability and flow 
characteristics are complex and nonlinear, most studies are therefore based on trial 
and error. Therefore for every powder and powder handling system, experiments 
must be carried out to understand the mechanism behind the recoating.  
The platform can provide the framework needed for verification, of mathematical 
models and simulations, providing insight to the deposition.  
12.1.1 Rotary 
The rotary will represent a tool magazine, with room for four pairs of arms mounted 
on the circumference of its axle. The rotary setup consists of two aluminum blocks, 
which sits on top of the linear bearing housings, linked via a robust cross member. 
The blocks houses plain bearings, supporting a shaft, perpendicular to the direction 
of motion. One block also supports a Nema 17 stepper motor that actuates the rotary 
axle. The other block supports the leadscrew nut, which provides the actuation 
necessary for motion and layer deposition. The bearings are acquired from Igus, from 
their Iglidur-X polymer range. They are made from a high performance polymer, 
with good wear resistance through the entire temperature range, going up to 250℃. 
They can withstand a pressure load of up to 150MPa. The center geometry of the 
shaft is square, to ensure a geometric lock of the rotary arms.

12.1.2 Recoater Blade 
The blade recoater, is basically a well-defined strip of metal, used to scrape the 
powder across the surface of the powder bed. The blade is designed with slots in each 
end for mounting and levelling. The cross section of the blade has a straight side and 
an angle of 45°. This allows for deposition with an angle of attack perpendicular to, 
or 45° to the powder. The sharp edge of the blade has a geometric straightness 
tolerance of 0,005mm, allowing for a worst case scenario of 5% variation of a 100µm 
thick layer. The material used is EN 1.4307 / AISI 304L austenitic stainless steel, 
providing necessary toughness against abrasion, and corrosion resistance. 
Furthermore the non-magnetic properties, is crucial when processing powders that 
are magnetic. 
The deposition motion of the blade alone will not assist the flow of the powder 
particles, which as a result will not ad compacting of the particle layer. The blade is 
a very simple recoater mechanism, the scraping action on the powder can however 
generate increased shear forces exerted on the previously fused layers. This can be 
destructive when fabricating intricate structures such as lattice structures and parts 
with fine details. 
 
Figure 12 - Recoater blade with particle agglomeration after several depositions 
In order to increase the compacting of the powder, thus increasing powder layer 
density, and to minimize the shear forces exerted on fused parts, an energy input can 
be utilized. This input could be in the form of a shaker, generating a frequency that 
would excite the blade. The vibrations of the blade would migrate through to the 
powder, which with the correct frequency would fluidize the powder, improving the 
flow characteristics. 
==PAGE:45==
45 
 
To achieve this excitation of the blade, two approaches were constructed. One with 
a piezo ceramic speaker mounted to the blade, and another with a small DC electric 
motor fitted with an unbalance to its shaft. A piezo ceramic element with a sufficient 
size and output were chosen, capable of reaching 20kHz coupled to a function 
generator and signal amplifier. The piezo element and corresponding amplifier, is 
from Sonitron, capable of a sound effect of 93dB, with a frequency range of 300-
20kHz. The signal was generated by a tone generator software running on a PC, and 
transmitted through the aux port.

the steel provides necessary toughness against abrasion, and corrosion resistance.

Adding the sixteenth-step mode, microstepping of the stepper motor driver, reduces 
the possible incremental movement even further, potentially increasing the precision 
sixteenth times. The additional accuracy of the microstepping mode is however 
controversial. The resolution of 1,8° on the stepper motor, is a mechanical factor, 
limited by the “number” of electromagnets. Whereas the microstepping divides the 
signal of a single full step into sixteen steps, by withholding a current on the 
“previous” electromagnet, while switching between steps. Intuitively, while the 
stepper motor is positioned on one of these steps between the two electromagnets, 
the holding torque will be dramatically reduced. This is also the case in practice, the 
phenomena can be described as magnetic backlash. Therefore, while under load, it is 
not fair to assume that the micro stepping will achieve an accuracy of more than 
that of the mechanical resolution, which would lead to an accuracy of ±0,9°. 
Ultimately, during load, it is uncertain where at any of the sixteen micro steps, the 
position of the stepper motor will be[36]. Besides the minimal addition of accuracy, 
microstepping does carry some advantages such as: reduced mechanical noise, less 
jerky actuation and fewer resonance problems. 
The mechanical step angle accuracy of the stepper motor is ±5%[37] equal to 0,18° 
and to 0,1 step. This gives an accuracy of ±0,375µm. This also means that since the 
stepper motor accuracy is 10% of a step, microstepping beyond 1/10, would not yield 
additional accuracy, but merely “empty resolution”.

12.2.1 Piston seal 
The piston to cylinder clearance are sealed using two different approaches, one seal 
is a rigid piston ring made from an endurable high performance polymer, while the 
other is an X-ring made from a softer silicone rubber. This seal is very crucial to the 
positioning of the piston and build plate. Since the seal needs to keep powder 
particles inside the build volume, and prevent a buildup of particles between the 
piston-cylinder clearance that would cause high friction and ultimately blocking the 
piston. 
The piston ring is acquired from Igus, and are made from their Iglidur-Z polymer 
range. This polymer offers high wear, low friction, and temperature resistance up to 
250℃ [38]. The manufacturer claims higher wear resistance than PTFE-strips. A 
downside to the piston ring is the lower sealing compared to the X-ring, which might 
cause evacuation of protective atmosphere. 
==PAGE:53==
53 
 
The X-ring is acquired from M-Seals, and are made from the fluoropolymer FPM, 
which offers good mechanical properties and high resistance to temperature up to 
200℃. The symmetrical four lobe geometry of the X-ring offers unique properties 
compared to O-rings, and are designed for dynamic applications[39]. The X shaped 
cross section enables the seal to withstand stresses from any direction, and reduces 
twisting during load. The X-ring provides a better seal compared to the piston ring, 
but introduces a higher frictional load. Furthermore, the geometry allows for 
lubrication to create a depot between the sealing lips. The radial force should 
therefore be a compromise between not having trapped particles, and a low enough 
frictional force for actuation with the stepper motor.

the stepper motor. Since the torque delivered by the stepper motor is limited, there

and cylinder. This force will be very sensitive to variations of this dimension. To 
reduce the sensitivity, the tolerance is adjusted during assembly. The adjustment is 
made using different thicknesses of shims until the functional performance is as 
intended (proper friction between seal and cylinder wall). The two types of seals, 
and a shim is seen on the left on Figure 20.

12.3 Frame 
The frame that consists of the machine plane, two sides, and a top plate are all made 
from EN AC 5083, cast and plane-milled 10mm aluminum plates. This material is 
chosen due to its specific characteristics. It is lightweight and will help make the 
platform portable when it has to be lifted from the experimental setup and carried 
to e.g. a fume hood for external handling of the powder. Aluminum has a high 
thermal conductivity, which helps conduct heat away from the pre-heated cylinder, 
outwards to the side plates, where it will mostly be lead away from natural 
convection.  
The plates are delivered from the supplier as plane milled, with a well-defined 
geometry. Therefore only little machining is required to fabricate the parts needed. 
The plates are cast, which results in uniform characteristics when it comes to 
expansion from applied heat, compared to e.g. extruded and rolled elements. 
The machine plane is delivered with the outside geometry. The final geometry, is 
achieved through machining with a vertical mill. This covers a mounting feature in 
the middle, for the vertical stage made up by the cylinder and piston, and threaded

13  Test Methods 
In the following sections, thoughts regarding experimental procedures and the theory 
behind will be discussed. Firstly the PBF process dependent parameters will be 
determined. Before any experiments can be initiated, the vertical movement of the 
piston is verified, in order to determine the ability of the platform to accurately 
position the piston according to layer thicknesses. Afterwards the powders intended 
for experiments are examined, in order to determine characteristics and to verify 
whether they are suited for thin layer deposition and handling. Three powders were 
examined, one plastic polymer and two metal powders. Where particle size 
distribution have been determined together with flow properties and composition of 
the metal alloys. These analysis where carried out on powders intended for different 
purposes, in order to distinguish characteristics suited for PBF of metals. The plastic 
polymer is intended for selective heat sintering, whereas one metal powder is 
presumed to be intended for conventional sintering, and the last metal powder is 
intended for powder bed fusion. Hereafter, experiments were carried out in order to 
verify the platforms ability to deposit uniform layers. The layers were studied 
regarding uniformity by optical examination and by direct measurement of apparent 
layer density. Two different approaches to verify and compare layer densities have 
been established. After the layer density have been estimated, the layer deposition 
is studied in order to establish any possible dependability between layer density and 
process parameters such as feeder speed, recoater (blade or roll) and energy input. 
The optimum parameters regarding maximum layer density are establish. Lastly a 
full factorial experiment is suggested, in order to establish a broader perspective 
towards dependency of parameters. 
13.1 Process Parameters 
Adequate process parameters of PBF is imperative to the outcome of the process, 
which is parts that meet the expectations of the user. Most of the parameters are 
interdependent, but can be categorized by the hardware. Generally parameters are 
related to the powder bed platform or the laser system. The parameters for the laser 
system are related to the energy source and the scanner system. There are two 
general energy parameters; laser power and duty cycle. The power is measured in 
watts, whereas the duty cycle can be two things, either pulsed or continuous wave, 
==PAGE:59==
59 
 
some lasers are limited to one operation. A continuous wave operation yields a 
constant laser beam, with a net effect to that of the power. A pulsed operation, yields 
bursts of laser, and the power will be a result of the cycle and the effect of the laser. 
The parameters associated to the scanner system and F-theta lens are, spot size, scan 
speed, and overall scanning strategy such as patterns and overlap. 
The parameters associated with the powder handling are; powder characteristics, 
such as size distribution, absorptivity, and particle shape. The deposition parameters; 
such as feeder speed, recoater type, and layer thickness. The powder layer density 
will be dependent on mostly all of these parameters. Pre-heating temperature, 
powder absorptivity, and energy density will also be interdependent, since an 
elevated temperature, will lower the energy density needed for melting of the powder. 
The absorptivity will depend on the powder, but also on the particle shape and the 
layer density. Pre-heating and energy density are balanced to provide the needed 
compromise of part characteristics, such as dimensional accuracy, surface quality, 
built rate and mechanical properties. 
Generally high energy density and high pre-heating temperature will yield dense 
parts, but will increase solid state sintering and introduce part growth, and lower 
surface quality. Whereas lower energy density and pre heating temperature will result 
in better dimensional accuracy, and surface quality. With a greater chance of 
delamination and less dense parts. The energy density is determined by laser power, 
spot size and scan speed. 
To provide the best repeatability of the process, monitoring of the process should be 
made. Thermal monitoring of the powder bed, using an infrared sensor, to insure a 
uniform powder bed temperature. Together with an optical monitoring of scan 
patterns, will help control part quality. Solution proposals are found in section 15.1.1. 
The powder layer density are governed by the particle size distribution, and recoater 
mechanism. Powder layer densities of commercial systems and powder typically reach 
60% of bulk density[3]. The parameters associated with the recoating process that 
might influence the powder layer density are presented below (13.1.1).

13.1.1 Variables of Powder Deposition 
The input variables that affect the output of the powder deposition, are determined 
through experimental analysis of the process. The inputs variables are listed below, 
and the output is powder layer density and uniformity.  
 Material (powder) characteristics 
 Material amount (and initial position) 
 Material temperature 
 Feeder speed 
 Blade/Roller height, relative powder or machine plane 
 Roller dimensions 
 Angle of attack 
 No. of overpasses 
 Strategy (Same height of blade during deposition, alternating height, or 
deposition direction) 
The powder characteristics are determined by the powder, and are not variable but 
can change during processing. The pre-heating of the powder and laser energy input 
can change the material characteristics. Especially for plastic powders, where 
absorptivity changes during processing. The particle size distribution, can also 
change due to solid state sintering, or from mechanical stresses induced by the layer 
deposition. Material temperature will depend on both pre-heating and the input from 
the energy source.  
The pre-heating, feeder speed, recoater adjustments, number of overpasses, and 
strategy of the deposition are all variables which will need to be addressed through 
experimentation. 
The parameters highlighted above was examined through experiments carried out 
with a stationary rigid blade recoater. The rest of the parameters should be addressed 
preferably through a full factorial experiment, which could reveal interdependency.

13.3 Initial Experiments 
In order to verify the platforms ability to deposit uniform layers with a reasonable 
apparent powder layer density, the first experimental tests were carried out using a 
plastic powder suited for SHS. A polyamide-polystyrene blend from Blueprinter 
BPM-1, was chosen, with an average particle size of 45μm (after examination using 
an optical microscope the average was found to be 41,6μm). Utilizing a plastic 
polymer powder for the initial tests, before testing the platform with metals, is far 
more practical compared to processing of fine metal powders. Since impracticalities 
as having a protective atmosphere, heated build chamber and safety precautions, are 
unpresented when dealing with plastic powders. However, before the initial tests, the 
BPM-1 powder is examined and evaluated as to whether it can be compared to the 
characteristics of known metal powders used for PBF. 
13.4 Powder Characteristics 
Previous studies show that particle size distribution and particle shape of a fine 
powder, influence the layer density of powder beds. The powder bed layer density 
and powder composition can be directly correlated to final part density, and thus 
the mechanical properties and surface quality [41][42][43][44]. These studies 
concluded that particle shape decide how well a powder will pack, and thus result in 
a higher bulk density. Spherical particles tend to arrange themselves more efficiently. 
When the spherical particles pack in a close arrangement, voids will inevitably 
remain between the bodies. In order to fill these voids, a certain amount of fine 
particles should be present. In theory, every remaining void could be filled with even 
finer particles. However, there is a natural limit to the obtainability of such particles, 
not to mention handling issues.  
Therefore, following guidelines for size distribution of powder particles have been 
established. In order to achieve high-density parts assuming optimized laser 
parameters. The ratio between coarse and fine particles should be over 1:10, with a 
composition of about 30% fine particles. A powder layer density increase of up to 
15% can be expected [36]. Based on these findings which were carried out for 
optimizing the density of thin powder layers for SLS. [42] Stated following guideline 
criteria for size distributions of metallic powders with regard to optimum surface 
quality and mechanical strength:

D50 denotes a size where the 50% fraction of the powder particles lies within, this 
size should be 10 times bigger than the 10% finest particles, denoted D10, (5), giving 
a 1:10 ratio. A sufficient amount of fine particles should be present, in order to fill 
voids between bigger particles (6). Lastly the layer thickness should be thicker than 
the size where 90% particles are within (7). This thickness will only be achieved from 
the first deposition, since the effective layer thickness will increase after the initial 
layers, and converge towards a larger thickness, due to lower layer density and thus 
shrinkage after melting. 
These results are based on volumes many times larger than that of the particles. 
This is however not the case for PBF, where layer heights often is within the 
magnitude of particle size (tlayer<100 μm). Therefore another phenomena known as 
the wall effect is present, which greatly affect the powder layer density. The study 
estimates over 40% voids for layers below 100μm [36]. Since particle size is 
comparable to the layer thickness, additional voids will be present between the 
particles and the build platform. The wall effect is illustrated on Figure 27.

The wall effect of the first layer will be reduced when having a build plate with an 
adequate surface roughness, comparable to that of the void size.  
Conclusive to these studies regarding optimization of powder bed layer density, are 
the importance of both the presence of fine particles, and overall particle size 
distribution on the powder bed layer density. The higher the layer density is, the 
higher the final part density and mechanical strength becomes. Fine particles and an 
absence of coarse particles are beneficial to surface quality and mechanical strength. 
Conversely, an absence of coarse particles increases part brittleness. While the 
==PAGE:64==

presence of coarse particles will improve the ductility, while the ultimate tensile 
strength decreases [37][42]. 
Due to the importance of adequate powder characteristics, the powders used for 
experimentation was analyzed regarding particle size distribution and flow 
characteristics. The initial experiments were carried out using the polyamide-
polystyrene powder, BPM-1 from BluePrinter, which was already on hand. To assess 
whether this powder has comparable properties to that of metallic powders, and 
could produce useful experimental results that could be translated to the use of 
metallic powders. This powder was examined first.  
To determine the particle size distribution, an optical microscope with a digital 
camera mounted, was used to capture images at 100x magnification. The images 
captured were analyzed using the Metrology software SPIP to determine particle 
sizes. The distribution were based on selective manual measurements, recorded and 
analyzed with Excel. Only particles which could be isolated as a single particle were 
measured. Care was taken in order to find particles of different sizes, and thirty 
samples were taken in three different regions, in order to achieve a true distribution. 
This method was a tedious process but proved very practical, and gave a good first 
hand estimate of the particle size distribution. The method is however not very 
accurate, since only a fraction of particles are measure, and the fact that larger 
particles might shadow and hide miner particles. Furthermore the results can be 
biased due to the measurements being done manually by a person. 
The inbuilt function in SPIP to determine particle size distributions were not fit for 
the captured images, since it failed to distinguish particles from one another, and 
would measure adjacent particles as one. The manual method was therefore more 
robust since every particle was addressed. 
Images used for estimation of particle size distribution, for the metal powders were 
capture using a scanning electron microscope. This also enabled for determination of 
composition by Energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy.

13.5 Verification of Deposited Powder Layer Density 
Quantifying the density of the deposited powder layer is rather challenging. There 
are numerous ways to indirectly determine the layer density, one of which is by 
examining the final part. A previous study addresses the lack of a common standard 
for part density measurement techniques for additive manufactured metallic 
parts[47]. This study investigates three principles for measurement of part density 
by: Archimedes method, microscopic analysis of cross sections and X-ray scanning. 
Conclusively they suggest using the Archimedes method for a practical and economic 
way to produce reliable density measurements of 3D parts.  
The powder layer density can be correlated to the final part density, there is however 
no similar methods of determining the layer density directly without fusion of the 
powder. The PBF process is optimized through a trial and error approach, where the 
final parts are examined. This approach is however very time consuming due to the 
fact that a full part has to be fabricated together with the time consuming handling 
issues regarding fine metal powders.  
In order to quickly compare the deposited powder layer, with the previously 
deposited, with regards to layer density. In order to optimize deposition parameters. 
A way to determine the density of the deposited layer directly is needed.  
Therefore, two approaches to determine the relative layer density directly have been 
designed. One method utilizing a camera, and image analysis to compare contrast 
ratio of captured images of white powder deposited on a black build plate. Another 
where the recoater is forced to only deposit powder within a known volume, where 
the mass is measured afterwards. The first method is a fast and practical tool to 
determine relative changes of deposited powder layer density, whereas the latter can 
be used to determine apparent layer density.  All of the initial experiments were 
carried out with a stationary blade for deposition, with all but one parameter kept 
constant. 
 
==PAGE:67==

13.5.1 Optical verification of powder layer density with regards to feeder speed 
In order to evaluate the deposition of powder layers, an experimental setup where 
powder layer density can be quantified is required. Initial deposition of layers, showed 
that with layers below 300µm, the powder layer appeared inhomogeneous, with 
clusters of powder and patches without. The patches, or islands within a sea of 
powder, made the build plate visible.  
  
 
Figure 29 - Image of black build plate with deposited layer 
of BPM-1 with a thickness of 100µm 
It was believed that the relative powder layer density could be estimated by 
processing of a picture taken of the build plate. The polyamide-polystyrene powder 
used for initial tests appear white in color. Therefore, in order to create optimum 
contrast, the build plate was spray painted matte black. Refer to Figure 29 for the 
painted build plate with a thin deposited layer of BPM-1.  
An experimental setup with a digital camera and controlled light conditions were 
prepared, to produce data which could be used to compare powder layer densities, 
of the first layer. The ambient light will make the powder stand out on the dark 
background (build plate), using MatLab to transform the image into greyscale, and 
afterwards compare dark areas to white on the captured images through the contrast 
ratio, each deposited layer can be compared quantitatively. The image is loaded into 
a matrix with a size corresponding to the pixels of the image. Each pixel is 
represented as an 8-bit value of image contrast, where 0 is pitch black and 255 is 
==PAGE:68==
68 
 
white. A mean value of all the contrast values in the matrix is found, to generate a 
single figure illustrating the density of the powder layer. The MatLab script is found 
in the appendices. 
While the method of optically comparing layer densities from the contrast ratio of 
an image, was very practical for comparing layer densities relative to each other. The 
method is very sensitive to variations of the recoater. A small variation in recoater 
height, will yield a relatively high variation in contrast due to the change layer 
thicknesses. Therefore in order to be able to compare different recoater mechanisms, 
care must be taken when calibrating the recoater height relative to the build plate. 
Furthermore the method is very sensitive to having constant ambient light 
conditions, since a change in ambient light between the captured images would yield 
the same result as an increased powder layer density.  
Another attribute of this method, is the possibility of easy automatization, since the 
data is acquired from a stationary camera. 
 
To optimize the deposition feeder speed for maximum layer density, the following 
parameters where used for experimentation. Since the experiments are carried out 
using a plastic powder, the material temperature is kept at the room temperature, 
to avoid sintering of the powder particles. The target of the experiment was to 
determine the optimum range in Y axis feeder speed during layer depositing. All 
other parameters were kept constant, in order to isolate effects of varying feeder 
speed. The motion of the blade was varied from 100-2000mm/min, with increments 
of 100mm/min, yielding 20 samples. Three identical test runs were carried out in 
order to increase statistical significance. The feeder speed was varied in a random 
order to eliminate the sources of extraneous variation, thus giving a clear view 
whether feeder speed have any effect on the powder layer density or not.

Figure 42 - Cumulative size distribution of the three powders plotted together 
From the distribution plot of the three powders together, on Figure 42, it can be

by gas or water atomization.

bed density, since it has many same sized particles to create a bulk, and smaller 
particles to fill voids. Where the M2 powder has too many small particles which 
would increase inter-particle friction. Furthermore, the MS1 powder has the best 
flowability according to the Hausner ratio.

14.3.5 Angle of repose 
The angle of repose was compared for the three powders, in order to verify the flow 
characteristics described by the Hausner ratio. The angle of repose is the angle at 
which the slope of a powder pile will arrange itself, when poured onto a surface. At 
this angle the powder is on the verge of flowing. The better the flowability of a 
powder, the shallower the angle. On Figure 43 below, the angle of repose is illustrated 
for the three powders. It can be seen that MS1 on the far right has the best 
flowability, which corresponds to the Hausner ratios found. The BPM-1 and M2 
powder share the identical less desirable flow characteristics.

14.5 Deposition with EOS MS1 powder 
The deposition with the Nylon/polystyrene powder BPM-1 was uncomplicated, and 
a lot of data was recorded. It was however also possible to deposit thin and uniform 
layers with the metal powder MS1 from EOS. There was however complications 
involving the movement of the piston and build plate. The piston ring seemed to be 
unable to prevent leaking particles. As a result particles packed between the piston 
and cylinder, seizing the movement of the piston after a short while of operation. 
Both piston ring and X-ring seemed to be unable to prevent leakage of particles 
totally. Therefore in order to be able to continue experiments, with the metal 
powders, the design of the vertical stage has to be optimized. A proposal for reducing 
the piston and cylinder sensitivity to the particles are presented in section 15.1.1. 
 
Nonetheless, it was shown that the platform could deposit thin and uniform layers 
with MS1 powder from EOS. As illustrated below on the images on Figure 48, where 
a layer above 100µm and below 100µm have been deposited. The thinner layer reveals 
the build plate below, suggesting the use of optical validation of the powder layer, 
at thicknesses below 100µm.

15  Discussion 
The domain of PBF systems have moved from the research stadia in the nineties, to 
actual fabrication, to where it is today, where we see a rise in automation of the 
process and actual production line processes. With the development of cost effective 
high power lasers, and material research within powders for AM. Bigger and faster 
PBF systems are starting to dominate the market. Machines are becoming more and 
more automated and modularized, as they move from small scale fabrication into 
bigger assembly lines. 
The natural potential of AM is evidently being acknowledged by most industries. 
Especially the AM of metals hold great potential within industrial manufacturing. 
For now the technology has mostly been limited to specialized production. As the 
technology matures, it will move from high end applications, to more general 
fabrication. This will lead to applications that will almost only be limited by the 
imagination of the engineers. 
One example is the fuel nozzle of the Boeing LEAP-1B jet engine, where each engine 
is equipped with 19 3D printed fuel nozzles. The nozzles are being manufactured by 
a PBF system, and they are claimed to be five times more durable than traditional 
nozzles. The production steps of the fuel nozzle was dramatically reduced, due to the 
simpler design achieved by AM. The welds and brazes was reduced from 25 to 5, 
which led to reduction in production cost[50]. 
The tendency within PBF seems to go two ways, where established manufacturers 
develop faster and more automated machines intended for production, like the quad 
laser system of EOS [16]. Where others develop more open, more affordable machines. 
Aurora Labs claim to provide a multi mode 3D metal printer, for the price of 
40.000$[51].  
On the process side especially within PBF there is quite the potential to develop 
systems capable of producing novel material properties, enabling entirely new 
applications by utilizing the control over the selective fusion of the powdered layers. 
Future research of optimized scan strategies and energy control, might reveal the full 
potential of the process. The question whether local microstructure manipulation can 
be utilized to create parts with inhomogeneous properties, compared to the wanted 
homogeneity of traditional casting, can be answered through future experimental 
work. 
==PAGE:93==
93 
 
There seems to be a tendency within the AM of metals, for a beginning rectification, 
based on the desire of more open platforms, being called out from the market of PBF 
systems. One initiate from General Electric and Lawrence Livermore National 
Laboratory, aim to develop open-source algorithms. To answer the lack of 
standardization within the process of PBF. 
These initiatives together with research similar to[11][52],will help mature the process 
of powder bed fusion as an industrial scale process. 
15.1 Future work 
15.1.1 Platform improvements 
The experimental work with the platform have revealed some areas in which the 
platform can be optimized. These areas including solution proposals are presented 
below, with the most important alterations first: 
A more robust solution for the vertical stage would be achieved by significantly 
increasing the piston-cylinder clearance. Thus reducing the sensitivity to escaping 
particles. The linear motion would be controlled by adding separate linear guides 
either below or parallel to the cylinder, controlling the linear motion. The only 
contact between piston and cylinder would be through the seal. This would however 
increase the height of the platform significantly, and thus conflict with the total 
height limitation of 300m as specified in section 8.

16  Conclusion 
The motivation and scope of this project, to develop a machine platform designed 
and constructed for laboratory testing. Where all parameters such as energy source, 
machine control, deposition mechanism and materials, are changeable, have been 
accomplished.  
Through the design process, experimental work, thorough analysis of process 
requirements, and from investigation of existing commercially available systems. 
Crucial specifications for a powder handling system have been presented. Including 
machine elements such as bearings and automation components, insensitive to the 
process temperature and particles.  
An open design have been developed, with easy access to and changeability of 
components, electronics and use of software. 
The machine has been built and final improvements for the platform to make a solid 
foundation for further experimentation and research within the area of AM of metals, 
have been suggested. Including an optimized design for a more robust design of the 
vertical stage. 
The methodical work behind characterization of powder deposition and material 
characteristics, together with results of three different fine powders have been 
presented, and process relevant parameters have been pinpointed and addressed.  
Two techniques for direct determination of powder layer density have been proposed 
and investigated through experiments.  
 
Through the experimental work following conclusions can be made: 
The BPM-1 polymer powder share some similarities with the M2 metal powder, such 
as flowability. The polymer powder does however not share similar shape, nor 
particle distribution as any of the metal powders. A higher powder layer density is 
therefore expected with the metal powders. 
Movement of the vertical stage have been shown to be precise, with a deviation from 
target of less than specified, and below 1% for most layers. 
The deposition mechanism have shown ability to deposit thin and uniform layers of 
a polymer powder, with a high degree of repeatability and no dependency on 
deposition velocity or direction. Despite the just passable flow characteristics of the 
powder, mean powder layer densities of up to 53% of the apparent (poured) bulk 
==PAGE:97==
97 
 
density was achieved. By means of deposition with a simple scraping action 
performed by a blade, without further vibrational excitation. 
The platform has shown ability to produce same results with metallic powders and 
can with minor alterations reach the same degree of repeatability. 
The X-ring proved to be sensitive to operation with the BPM-1 powder, due to 
powder compacting in the depot intended for lubrication. The piston ring proved 
very efficient for operation with BPM-1 powder. Both solutions were unfit for metal 
powders, and the design should be optimized by suggested improvements. 
 
The author believes that the experiences gained, and documentation produced will 
act as inspiration for future experimental research and improvements on the powder 
handling platform. That the developed and built platform will act as framework for 
future research, contributing to the field of additive manufacturing by PBF. That 
research within PBF can be leading contributor to a giant leap within manufacturing 
of metal parts with varying geometrical and material properties. E.g. bearing 
surfaces, areas with vibrational damping, and undoubtedly, topology optimization.  
There is undoubtedly room for developments within the PBF process, especially 
within machine integrated monitoring systems to verify associated models related to 
powder bed density, the thermal field, and residual stresses. Future research will help 
streamline the PBF process, and hopefully major players will cooperate in order to 
achieve a higher degree of coherence and standardization. Together with larger 
insight into process parameters, will lead to future improvements of machines, and 
thereby to parts with better, entirely new, and higher consistency of material 
properties.
