Abstract
In this thesis, mechanical face seals are studied. This is done by building a One-Dimensional
Axisymmetric Two-Phase Transient Thermo-Hydrodynamic Model of the seal. To model the
different physical domains, multiple areas of physics and engineering are used.
This includes an Equation of Motion to describe the transient seal opening, a Finite Volume
implementation including Momentum Conservation (in the form of Navier-Stokes equations),
Mass Conservation, and Energy Conservation. The Finite Element Method is used to model the
Transient Heat Conduction in the seal rings, which has a dominating effect on seal performance.
Finally, a Continuous Homogeneous Two-Phase flow model is used to describe the trans-
ition between phases. This includes a Thermodynamic Saturation model based on the IAPWS-
97 standard.
These models combined, yield a system of Non-Linear Differential-Algebraic Equations
(DAE), which is solved numerically. The MATLAB function ode15s is used to solve the DAE-
system.
Multiple simulations are run, both to validate the model (done at steady-state), and to study
the transient behaviour of mechanical face seals. The primary focus is on the qualitative de-
scription of seal behaviour, and less on quantitative parameter study.
It is found that the transient thermal effects in the seal have a widely different timescale
than the flow dynamics. This combined with limited step size, caused by numerical stiffness,
mean a poor time efficiency of the model.
A final simulation is run to investigate the qualitative behaviour of Puffing. However, nu-
merical precision problems caused the simulation to stop prematurely. It is thus difficult to
confirm that it is Puffing, although the behaviour indicated it.

Chapter 1. Introduction
1
Introduction
Pumps are fundamental components behind almost every modern mechanical technology. They
are used in water supplies, central and district heating, cooling systems and power generation.
Understanding pumps is thus critical to improving and optimising all these areas. The primary
dynamics, both fluid and mechanical, are well understood, thoroughly tested and modelled.
These dynamics include the fluid flow and the rotational stability of the primary components,
which are the impeller, shaft, and bearings. An area that is less understood, tested, and mod-
elled, is the seals responsible for limiting leakage. Experience suggests that as many as 50%
of pump failures are caused by the seal[4]. A better understanding of this area may provide
critical knowledge of pump design, which may increase the quality of the products.
A subcategory of seals is the mechanical face seal, which is studied in this project. These
seals share a lot of the characteristics of hydrodynamic thrust-bearings studied in Tribology
(Lubrication Theory), and may thus borrow research from that area. However, some of the
before-mentioned applications may require the pump to operate with fluid above its evaporation
point. This is especially true for cryogenic and heat pumps, though also relevant for water
pumps in certain situations. This means that at some point in the seal the fluid will evaporate
due to flashing (evaporation caused by pressure drop). This phenomenon is not frequent in fluid
bearings, where oil is the primary fluid and evaporation is thus rarely studied in Tribology.
Studying pump seals is inherently more difficult than hydrodynamic thrust bearings due to
the smaller film thickness. This is due to different primary objectives between bearings and
seals. The main objective of a fluid bearing is to carry as much load as possible and wear as
little as possible. This is often done by increasing the film height at the cost of a higher leakage
rate. While this often increases fluid pumping power, the power of the machinery being carried
often dominates the power usage.
In pump seals, the primary objective is to minimise leakage. This is done by minimising
the fluid film height. The film in a pump seal often is in the order of 1 µm [7], while fluid
bearings can have film heights of tens or hundreds of micrometres. As will later be seen, the
flow rate is proportional to the film height to the power of 3. A tenfold increase in film height
thus increases the leakage rate by a thousand. There does exist a lower boundary for the film
height. Especially particles in the pumped fluid are of concern. If the pumped fluid has particles
greater than the film height, then increased wear should be expected[4].
The lower film thickness in seals has little to no effect on which models are used compared
to Tribology. This is because it often does not change the underlying assumptions. The smaller
height does however greatly increase the difficulty of measurements in experiments, where
especially measuring the film height is difficult. This often makes the film height unknown or
very uncertain. It will later be shown that not only the leakage but also the heat transfer and
heat generation are very dependent on film height. Comparisons thus become very uncertain if
the film height is just a bit uncertain.

1.1
Description
The fundamental geometry of mechanical face seals is presented in Figure 1.1. The fluid film
thickness is exaggerated in order to visualise every component. The pumping fluid is kept on
the outside of the rings and is leaking inwards (at least in this case). This primary flow direction
is assumed throughout this thesis. The top ring is locked in rotation and axial location. The
bottom ring rotates with the pump shaft and is free to move axially, independently of the pump
shaft. This means that the bottom ring has 1 DOF (Degrees Of Freedom) and that its position
and velocity are described from Newtonian force balance.
In essence, one would like to keep the clearance fixed at a minimum opening in order to
reduce leakage. This is not easy when the ring is free to move. However, the alternative
would be to design the clearance into the machining of the shaft seal. This introduces other,
much more severe, problems. Machining to a tolerance below 1 µm is simply not possible.
Even if machining could be done at an adequate precision, the next problem becomes thermal
expansion, which easily exceeds 1µm in a pump shaft. And finally, the shaft is supported at
the other end by some kind of bearing (perhaps a hydrodynamic bearing). The axial location
of this bearing will be too uncertain or even variable. Thus, statically machining this tolerance
is not possible, and dynamic force balance is necessary.
The primary force acting on the lower disc is the hydrostatic pressure caused by the dif-
ference in pressure between the atmosphere and the pumped fluid. A spring is often added to
increase the closing force.
In this thesis a model is built, that includes fluid dynamics, solid heat transfer and phase
change. This makes it possible to study the interaction between the different components in the
seal. The phase change and the compressibility of the steam phase mean that the flow becomes
non-linear. Thus in order to solve the flow, finite volume is used.

Chapter 1. Introduction
1.1. Description
A homogeneous two-phase model is used to describe the fluid by assuming that the fluid at
any state can be described as a mass average of the two saturated phases (complete liquid and
complete vapour).
The solid heat conduction in the seal rings is solved using the finite element method. The
thermal interaction between the fluid and the seal rings is modelled by the use of an analytical
expression for the heat flux. Because of the thin film, heat transport is dominant in mechanical
face seals. This is therefore a critical part of the model, even in situations where only small
variations in temperature are indicated.
The following general assumptions are made when building the model:
• It is assumed that the low fluid film height allows the use of the homogenous two-phase
model.
• In accordance with the assumptions used by [2], thermal expansion and solid deformation
are neglected. Including this would require the implementation of these effects which is
outside the scope of this thesis. By the same argument is axis symmetry also assumed.
• In contrast to [2], the seal is not assumed in steady-state
• More assumptions are made later about the flow.

Chapter 2. Mathematical Model
2
Mathematical Model
The model will describe the multiple physical domains illustrated in Figure 2.1. In the two
rings, heat conduction is modelled by Finite-Element (FE). The fluid film is modelled using
Finite-Volume (FV). And finally, the fluid film height is modelled using standard dynamics. In
the interfaces, different methods will be used as boundary conditions. This can best be com-
pared to a thermo-hydrodynamic (THD) fluid bearing model often used in Tribology. The big
difference between this model and classical THD models is the addition of saturation functions
to describe the thermodynamic state of the fluid as it flows through the seal.
Figure 2.1: Illustration of the domain being modelled [2]
2.1
Equation of Motion
Investigating the dynamic opening of the seal is the primary motivation for this project, and this
means freeing one of the seal discs to allow for transient fluid film height. The height of the
fluid film is defined from a 2nd-Order differential equation just like for a mass-spring system.
The difference between an ordinary Equation of Motion (EQM) for a mass-spring system
and the equation of motion for this seal, is the complexity, including nonlinearity. While the
EQM in itself looks linear, only depending on a linear summation of pressure, the pressure is a
result of a complex and nonlinear fluid flow.
An analogy to this is the classical mass-spring system (illustrated in Figure 2.2). Only in
this case, the force acting on the mass does not stem from a linear spring but instead a very
non-linear relation between film height and pressure profile.

2.2
Fluid Dynamics
Fluid Dynamic is used to model the fluid flow between the seal rings. This is done from the
fundamental conservation equations used in fluid dynamics, Conservation of Mass, Conser-
vation of Momentum and Conservation of Energy. In Tribology theory, a series of equations
known as Reynold’s equations, are often used. These are derived from the mass conservation
equation and the momentum conservation equation. Varying sets of assumptions are used in
order to simplify and often linearise the problems.
In this case, many of the assumptions used to derive most versions of the Reynold’s equa-
tion, are invalid. It is therefore necessary to derive a new governing equation for the fluid flow

in the seal. Even though the result might resemble a Reynold’s equation, no such version was
found elsewhere in the literature as a Reynold’s equation. It is thus to avoid confusion not
called a Reynold’s equation, but instead a special case of the Navier-Stokes equations.
The Navier-Stokes equations are used as the basis for the derivation of the governing equa-
tion. While the Navier-Stokes equations in principle completely describe fluid flow, they are
not trivial nor computational efficient to solve. Instead, simplifications are made due to as-
sumptions based on physical argumentation. The assumptions are as follows,
1. Very small film thickness: In order to keep leakage as low as possible, pump seals are
designed to operate at very small film heights.
2. No Axial Velocity: Because the height is small, there is no room for the fluid to accelerate
and decelerate in the vertical direction. The lower seal ring is of cause able to move
axially and does thus impose an axial velocity in the fluid. This velocity is however
assumed to be very small, due to the small movements expected from the seal ring.
3. The fluid properties and pressure are constant across the film height: Since the height
is small any significant variations in fluid properties would create large gradients. It is
assumed that these gradients would not be thermodynamically stable, and thus disperse
quickly. The only exception to this is the temperature. In the definition of the heat
flux described later, a temperature gradient is needed. In all aspects of fluid flow, the
temperature is however assumed constant across the height as with the other parameters.
4. The fluid flow is assumed to be axisymmetric: The only obvious cause of variation in the
tangential direction is turbulence which is not expected due to the very small height. The
presence of tangentially varying seal geometry makes this assumption invalid. The seal
geometry must thus be axisymmetric.

bottom ring rotates with ω. The rotational velocity of the fluid in the pump is not of concern in
this model and it is thus unknown. Three cases (with different assumptions of the pumped fluid
flow) are presented to illustrate possible scenarios.
Case number one: The impeller rotates the fluid so it rotates with a speed close to ω. In this
case, the velocity difference is ≈0 between the fluid and the bottom ring and ≈ω between
the upper ring and the fluid. The bottom ring is thus isolated and the top ring has a convection
proportional to ω.
Case number two: The fluid is not set in rotation and is in rest. In this case the velocity
difference is ≈0 between the upper ring and the fluid, and ≈ω between the bottom ring and
the fluid. The convection is just flipped compared to case one.
Case number three: The fluid is rotating at some speed in between 0 and ω. This means
that both rings have convection to the fluid. The sum of this convection must however equal
the convection provided to one of the rings in the previous cases.
Gani uses case three and assumes that the velocity difference is evenly distributed. He does
however not split ω to each ring but instead says that both rings have a velocity difference of ω.
This does not seem reasonable.
Case three is probably the most reasonable, but does impose a new variable, that not only
makes comparison harder but also makes the model less general. Letting this parameter be
fixed to 0.5 makes the problem symmetric and no use would then come from including both
rings. Therefore it is decided to go with case 2 which is just the mirror image of case 1 and
adds some asymmetry to the model.

2.6
Parameter Interpolation
The continuity equation, energy equation and the heat flux equation, require the knowledge of
a parameter (pressure, density, temperature, etc.) at a point not described by a node. In these
cases, interpolation is used. Interpolation is used as a "best guess".
While in most cases [2] is followed when performing interpolation, some extra consider-
ation is needed due to the removal of the steady-state assumption. The argumentation for the
entire interpolation is therefore repeated.
Evaluating the radius and film height at the CV faces is trivial since these are geometrically
defined by the discretisation and the pump geometry. The density, enthalpy and pressure gradi-
ent are however not prescribed at the CV faces. The pressure and density are only defined at the
CV centers. An interpolation is therefore needed. Many methods are used in CFD, but only 1st
order schemes are considered here. The choice is between Central Difference Scheme (CDS)
and Upwind Difference Scheme (UDS). The primary consideration is whether the parameter
more resembles the upwind parameter value or an average of the two nearest parameter values.
Pressure becomes convective near the speed of sound, but is very diffusive (disperses
without the need for a flow) at low speeds. This means that the pressure is influenced from
all sides independent of flow direction. CDS is thus the appropriate choice for pressure.
Enthalpy diffuses slowly, and convection becomes dominant at even low velocities. This
means that enthalpy will follow the flow. Thus the enthalpy, at a face, will more resemble the
enthalpy upstream than downstream. UDS is therefore chosen for enthalpy.
Density and viscosity are not interpolated but instead evaluated in the faces, using the in-
terpolated pressures and enthalpies.

3
Numerical Implementation
The governing equations were derived (or used from [2]) in the previous section. The equations
are now implemented in a MATLAB program, like the one described in [2]. The program is
not a modification of Gani’s program. Instead a new program was written from the ground up.
This was not a necessity but was beneficial in multiple ways.
Firstly, having to program all the small features again, meant a greater insight into the code
and faster debugging. The previous program by Gani was big and had features irrelevant for
this thesis. Debuging in such a big program would be inefficient.
Secondly, the primary solvers in the program by Gani and the program presented here are
fundamentally different and the systems of equations are fundamentally different. The previ-
ous program searched for a steady-state, using a Newton-Raphson like method. This meant
that the program worked implicitly with every variable. In the program presented here, a tran-
sient model is solved. This means that an ODE is used instead of an purely algebraic solver
like Newton-Raphson. Most fluid parameters are simply explicit functions of the previous time
step. This means that many of the variables solved for in the code by Gani can be removed
from the system of equations (heat flux and fluid temperature). The disadvantage of the tran-
sient simulation is however the great number of steps required. But rebuilding the code meant
focusing on computational efficiency, at the cost of flexibility and perhaps usability. Examples
of this are, that only the height is "remeshed" at each iteration (while the radial meshing is kept
constant) and that the FEM mesh is kept constant.
Where possible, and beneficial, some core functions from the previous program are used,
fx. the FE heat model. It is however modified in order to include transient terms, and not
require remeshing at every timestep.
The entire code will not be described, but the system being solved will be presented, along
with a flow diagram of the process. Finally, a short guide to the program is made, enabling
others to quickly use the code.
3.1
DAE system
The four individual equations are gathered into a system of Differential-Algebraic Equations
(DAE) in a manner consistent with MATLAB’s built-in DAE-solver, ode15s. MATLAB’s in-
ternal solver was chosen over a bespoke Euler-type ODE solver, for two reasons.
Firstly, MATLAB’s solver has built-in error control. This means that MATLAB can find fx.
precision problems, like the one described in subsection 3.3.2 Normalisation. This meant that
the results can be trusted more, without having to use a considerable amount of time studying
different causes of precision loss. Having an estimate of the error allows the solvers to adjust
the time step based on the error. This means that computational resources can be saved where
precision is unnecessarily high.

3.2. Code Structure
3.2
Code Structure
As previously mentioned, the program is structured around the built-in ODE solver included in
MATLAB. This means that the focus could be placed on the dynamic modelling of a mechan-
ical face seal. It also means that the program in essence can be condensed to one command and
one function, ode15s and odefun. odefun is a function that takes the state (described by {y})
and returns the derivatives ∂{y}
∂t and the error in mass conservation. ode15s evaluate odefun at
every timestep, and integrates or updates the solution based on the return values from odefun.
A flowchart of the program is included to illustrate the general structure of the code. It is
shown in Figure 3.1.
The code can be split into three parts; Initialising, Solving and Post Processing. This
matches the structure of other simulation software like Ansys and COMSOL. The advantages
of this are the readability of the code and flexibility. It is fx. possible to run initialising and
solving on one computer and then plot and animate on another. This is especially useful when
working on headless computers like DTU’s HPC.
3.2.1
Initialising
In initialising everything is prepared for solving. This includes creating initial conditions, set-
ting solver options, and precomputing as much as possible. Because of the iterative nature of
ODE solving, reducing the computational work during solving, greatly reduces computation
time. Thus, everything that is constant (considering time and state) is calculated and saved.
This includes interpolation functions, FEM-matrices, and mesh-parameters like CV heights
and positions. Objects are used to organise these precomputed variables. Objects are chosen in
order to increase readability and debugging of the code. Four objects are used;
• Fluid Object: Contains fluid properties including precomputed interpolation functions.
Precomputing the interpolation means that the table only needs to be read once.
• Mesh Object: Contains mesh geometry like node positions and CV sizes. It also defines
the system vectors used for the ODE solver.
• Boundary Object: Contains the boundary conditions like eastern and western pressure,
enthalpy and temperature. It can also include time-varying boundary conditions. This
allows the pumping fluid to change temperature (and thus enthalpy) or the rotational
velocity to increase or decrease.
• FE Object: Contains the large system matrices used to evaluate the heat conduction.
Computing these is slow and since they are constant (at least as long as thermal deform-
ation is neglected) they can be computed once and saved.
Finally, the odefun is defined. This merely consists of parameterising the odefun with the
four objects previously defined.
3.2.2
Solving
As stated previously, solving primarily contains the command to start the chosen ODE solver
(ode15s). Besides this, a few lines are used to save useful information, like time usage and the
date/time of the simulation. The ODE solver returns a structure containing, time steps, the state
at those time steps, and solver options. These options allow for an extension of the simulation
later.

3.3
Numerical Considerations
Although MATLAB’s built-in ODE solver handles many of the numerical considerations re-
garding solving, it does not eliminate every problem. In principle, everything within odefun is
out of reach of the algorithms, and problems caused in odefun must be addressed.
3.3.1
Memory
MATLAB is excellent at managing memory allocation and code optimisation, but it does have
limitations. As stated earlier, it is chosen to precalculate the FE matrices and save them instead
of calculating them at each step. MATLAB can not be expected to do much optimisation in this
case, and it must thus be considered whether this is the right approach.
One should normally be careful when saving a large amount of data. Because of memory
bottlenecks, saving a variable for use later can sometimes be slower than simply calculating the
value again. This is especially a problem if the amount of data being saved exceeds the cache
available in the CPU. This is because compared to CPU speed, RAM Latency is slow. Many
calculations could be done in the time it takes the RAM to send the data required. However,
due to the use of MATLAB’s sparse matrices and the sparsity of the FE-matrices (which are
the significantly largest), the matrices will have a size in the order of 100KB (of cause mesh
dependent) which is within the limits of even L1 Cache on modern desktop CPUs. It can
however not be ruled out that doing element-wise multiplication of the system matrix might be
faster. But this has not been implemented nor tested.
3.3.2
Normalisation
As shown earlier, a fundamental element in the analysis of mechanical face seals is the large
difference in scale between dimensions. The extremes are the pressure at an order of 106 and the
film height at an order of 10−6. This was useful when simplifying the Navier-Stokes equations
earlier, but it is a problem numerically. The large difference in scales makes the system ’Stiff’.
Stiff systems have a system matrix (in this case the Jacobian) which varies greatly in or-
der of magnitude. Because computer precision is relative to the order of the value, this can
greatly reduce the precision of mathematical operations. Stiffness is often expressed by the
condition number (κ), where a high number means a stiff system. The condition number tells
how much precision is lost. A condition number of 10k means that up to k digits of preci-
sion are lost when solving the system[1, p 406]. A system with a high condition number is
called ill-conditioned. The precise condition number changes from state to state, and is thus
not known. As an example, the condition number of a simple matrix with the same orders of
magnitude as in this problem is calculated in Equation (3.4). It is seen that 12 of the 16 digits

3.3.3
Jacobian
ode15s need a Jacobian in order to better predict next time step. It will by default approximate
the Jacobian by finite difference. The use of linear interpolation to approximate the saturation
functions makes analytical derivatives frivolous. This is because the interpolated values will
have a derivative equal to a finite difference approximation. The saturation values affect vis-
cosity, density and temperature. A large number of entries in the Jacobian will thus be finite
difference approximations even with an "analytical where possible’ approach. Finite difference
is therefore found adequate in regards to precision.
Evaluating some derivatives analytically is also not trivial. As mentioned in section 2.5
Fluid Properties, λ is only C0 continuous regarding both pressure and enthalpy, which means
the derivative is discontinuous. As a result, everything dependent on quality will have discon-
tinuous derivatives. This includes density and viscosity.
It was considered to use 2D curve fitting to remove this discontinuity in quality, but using
finite difference made it unnecessary.
The biggest problem when using finite difference is however often not precision but com-
putational time. In the default behaviour, the algorithm searches for the influence of every
variable in every equation. With just a few variables, this becomes very computational de-
manding. However, ode15s allows specifying the pattern (or sparsity) of the Jacobian. By
telling the algorithm where it should expect zero values, it can skip those elements of the mat-
rix. It is found that the Jacobian is pretty sparse. Especially the FE heat model requires many
nodes and thus many variables but it is also very sparse with elements only present in an 8-wide
diagonal band. The Jacobian pattern is shown in Figure 3.2. It is clear that it is very sparse
which is what enables the use of finite difference.
Some intermediate tests showed that there is no significant time difference between the
analytical and the finite difference method as long as the pattern of the Jacobian is supplied.

3.4.1
Condition File
An example is provided in Code 3.1. The primary parameters to change here are the initial
and final temperatures, angular velocity, taper angle, and initial height (when locking height
in the options file, this height becomes the constant height). These parameters are mostly self
describing, but it should however be noted that the pump geometry (ri and ro) should not be
changed without also changing the files defining seal geometry.
Only one method for transient operational conditions is implemented. This is a step in
temperature, going from initial temperature to final temperature at the specified time. Due to
numerical problems, this step could not be instantaneous and it is therefore done with a slope,
over a timespan equaling 10% of the step-time.
The taper angle is not well known and is, therefore, a guess. The geometry described
in Code 3.1 results in a height difference of 2 µm across the seal. This is thus a significant
difference with a film height of 1-10 µm while becoming insignificant at film heights larger
than 20 µm.
The balance ratio is one of the trickiest things to get right. It is an geometric property and
should thus be defined for the pump being studied. If for some reason the balance ratio is not
known (fx. if simulating to find an appropriate ratio) then a guess must be made. When having
a too high balance ratio compared to the taper angle, then the seal converges toward a very small
film height. It will later be shown that this has an dramatic effect on the computation time. If
however the balance ratio is too low the seal will almost immediately pop open. The range
within these two cases can be very small. 0.85-0.90 is fx. an appropriate range for two-phase
flow for the seal studied in this thesis.

As stated previously, the Option file provides options for the program independent on the prob-

scaling parameters both influence speed and precision, and what is fast might not be precise,
even though it could be. The MATLAB solvers will in most cases detect the low precision and
throw an error.

Main Script
The program consists of a collection of functions and objects. The main script collects these
functions in the correct order. It is therefore the main script that is run in order to perform a
simulation. Most of the simulation and problem varying parameters have been moved to one
of the two previously mentioned files. The main script does however still contain few lines of
code that are problem or simulation dependent (like the simulation timespan). The main script
is not described here in detail. The reader is instead refered to the source files.

The program is tested in several steps. Before anything else, the extended model along with the
code is validated. This is primarily done against Gani’s Program [2], which has already been
validated against other models. Since the model is almost identical to Gani’s in steady-state,
the discrepancy should be small. In order to directly compare the two models, compressibility
and height variation are turned off. Afterwards, compressibility is turned on again, and the
assumption of incompressible flow by Gani is tested.
After validation, the extended capabilities of the model are tested. This is done by running
multiple simulations. The first simulation is a transient convergence of film thickness in one
phase. This is then extended to two-phase. A simulation is also run that simulates the transient
response of the seal, including ring temperature, in a case where the pumping fluid changes
temperature. Finally, a simulation is run which includes what is assumed to be puffing.
While the work done by Lau et al. [3] and Brunetiere et al. [5] have a very strong quant-
itative focus with fx. parameter studies, this thesis will have a more qualitative focus on the
behaviour of mechanical face seals. Quantitative data will however still be presented and com-
pared to other work.
4.1
Model Validation
In order for the model to be trusted, it must be validated. This is done by comparing it to
other literature. While some experiments have been conducted they are hard to find, and uncer-
tainties make replicating the experiment difficult. Instead, the code is validated against other
mathematical models.
As stated previously, Gani’s program assumes steady-state and incompressibility. It can
thus only be compared in steady-state, and with compressibility turned off (depending on the
influence of compressibility).

same arguments as is made about this model’s discrepancy. It has been seen that 210 °C is too

4.1.2
Including Compressibility
To test the isolated influence of including spatial compressibility which is excluded by both [2]
and [5], the same simulation is run but with compressibility included. By using the steady-state
found without compressibility, this simulation can be run much faster, since the initial guess
state is much closer to the final state. This is because compressibility only slightly changes the
flow.
There are two primary parameters of interest. Compressibility only influences the energy
equation in the form of work. This has one primary effect on the system; the extra work will
result in higher fluid energy in this case measured in enthalpy. Enthalpy will affect the evap-
oration and density functions which in turn affect the pressure. Enthalpy and pressure are thus
the parameters of interest.

The most basic use of this model is to simulate the transient convergence of fluid film
parameters and seal ring temperature. This response is dependent on the initial conditions and
the operating conditions. Combinations of these conditions are of cause infinite. In this section,
two scenarios are chosen to demonstrate the abilities of the model.
The first simulation will be with only one phase (water at ≈90 °C), while the second will
be with two phases (water and steam at ≈207 °C). The general conditions that are used for
both simulations are mostly as given in Table 4.1 though with a changed taper angle, reduced
mesh refinement and an added mass (listed in Table 4.2). While the mass could be found from
the geometry of the seal and the material density, this is not used. The geometry of the seal was
not given much consideration and the mass would thus be very random. Secondly, the mass
does not just depend on the mass of the seal ring but might also depend on the mass of a spring
and other components. Instead, a fixed mass was chosen that is easier to replicate.
It should be noted, that the boundary condition is returned to the one presented earlier in
this thesis. Thus, the upper ring is isolated on all sides except the lower side which is in contact
with the fluid film, and has a flux boundary. The lower ring has the same flux boundary on the
top face in contact with the film, while the eastern face (in contact with the pumped fluid) has
a convection boundary.
Along with these operational conditions, some initial state must be supplied. One way to do
this is to use a previously found-steady state. However, in an effort to simulate startup operation
the initial conditions are set to match a situation, where the seal has been closed up until the
simulation start. The initial conditions are set so the seal rings and fluid film have homogenous
temperature fields equal to the pumping fluid. A pressure field is found that satisfies continuity.
Since the seal has been closed up until the start of the simulation the pumped fluid temperature
has diffused into the entire seal. Ideally, the initial height would be 0, but several equations are
singular at zero height and very stiff at values under 1 µm. The initial value is therefore set at
1 µm which in neither case is the steady state.

4.3. Film Height’s Influence on Computation Time
To do this, the balance ratio is increased with 5 percent point to 0.9 (see Table 4.5). This
seems like a small change, but has a large effect on the film height. The result are shown in
Figure 4.9 and Figure 4.10.
In Figure 4.9a it is seen that the film height no longer oscillates, but takes one overshoot,
and then immediately dampens to a steady height. It is also seen that the film height is greatly
reduced by an order of magnitude. Both regarding the overshoot and the steady-state. And
although a steady-state height of 5 µm is perhaps a bit high, it is certainly plausible. The
leakage rate plotted in Figure 4.9b shows a steady leakage rate of 10 kg/h which is certainly
higher than for one-phase, but it should be remembered that the fluid is almost completely gas
phase when exiting and thus will be less noticeable. According to Nau [6], this is a rather high
leakage rate, but still within the range of his observations. It is therefore deemed plausible for
a seal of this size.
It is also seen in Figure 4.9a, that the temperature variation in the seal ring surfaces is
approximately 10 °C lower than on the previous two-phase case. This indicates that the heat
transfer to the fluid film from the seal walls is much greater, which is also expected for a lower
film height.
The temperature field shown in Figure 4.10 resembles that seen in Figure 4.8, but with a
significantly higher minimum temperature.
4.3
Film Height’s Influence on Computation Time
It is discovered that there is a clear correlation between the film height and the computational
efficiency. When taking steps at low heights (≈1 µm) the step size is much smaller than those
taken at larger film heights (≈10 µm). As a result computation time per simulation time is
much higher at low film heights. To quantify this, simulations are run with the height locked to
different values. The simulations are run in a precalculated steady-state, to eliminate the effect
of transient changes.
The results are shown in Figure 4.11. Although it is a small sample size a log-log relation-
ship between film height and time seems to exist. This indicates that if film height is reduced
to 1/10 then the computational time increases tenfold.
Time efficiency is the biggest limitation of the program. When it takes 1000 seconds to
simulate a second, some simulations become very cumbersome. This is especially true for
simulations that include significant changes in temperature since thermal effects are often rel-
atively slow. An example of this is seen in section 4.4 Transient Response to Fluid Temperature
Change.
Finally, it should be noted that these numbers are from a simulation that is in steady-state
from start to finish. This usually means that the step size can increase to the maximum allowed,
since there is no change from time step to time step. This is however not the case here. It is
hypothesised that this has something to do with the stiffness of the system and that although
the system was normalised to decrease stiffness, this might not have been enough.

4.4
Transient Response to Fluid Temperature Change
In order to highlight the full potential of the transient model, a simulation is run with varying
pumping fluid temperature. While this is just another simulation case used to showcase the
model’s abilities, it enables the user of the program to simulate advanced startup behaviour and
the effects of a time-varying operation.
The real challenge of this simulation turned out to be computation time. As described
in section 4.3 Film Height’s Influence on Computation Time the computational time is very
dependent on the film height. A simple way to speed up simulations is therefore to increase the
film height by even a small amount. However, in order to test the full capabilities of the model,
the height should be free to move. The height can therefore not be prescribed but must be a
result of the forces acting on it.
As long as the flow does not change fundamentally, these forces could be adjusted to ap-
propriate values allowing for a time-efficient height. However, in this case, the flow changes
fundamentally from one-phase to two-phase. A result of this shift is that the pressure profile
shifts dramatically, and thus also the opening force.
Another challenge is that it is impossible to run a simulation with one-phase and parallel
seal rings. This is because the pressure distribution becomes independent of film height and
thus becomes constant. This implies that for any valid closing force, the opening would con-
verge towards completely closed. And since low film heights are very slow, a convergence
toward completely close was not possible to compute.
This all means that finding the right force balance (in the form of the balance ratio and taper
angle) is a compromise between the film height in one phase and the film height in two phases.

differentiate temperature at any snapshot. The snapshots of the pressure profile do however
add some insight. It is seen in the first snap, that the pressure profile is very much like the one
shown in the one-phase simulation. Then as the seal heats up (snap 2-4) the pressure profile
lifts to the left. This is caused by beginning evaporation on the westernmost face. Until snap 4
the film height is relatively low. Then at snap 5, the seal is much more open and it is seen on
the quality curve, that evaporation moves inward. The quality is however still small. Finally,
in snap 6 the film height is very large and heat transfer is thus much less significant. The result
is that evaporation begins far into the seal, but only evaporates to a small degree. Instead, the
fluid cools to approximately 160 °C. This resembles adiabatic evaporation, whereas previous
evaporation more resembled isothermal evaporation.
Just as in subsection 4.2.3 Two-Phase the final leakage rate is so high that the model as-
sumptions break. This simulation must therefore also be used with caution. The most inter-
esting is however also the dynamics before the seal reaches these film heights. Especially the
thermal response is interesting because it illustrates the large difference in time scale between
the flow dynamics, the film height dynamics and the thermal dynamics. The time scale of
height dynamics is easiest seen in Figure 4.7a where it takes around 0.05s for the height to
converge. In contrast, it takes the thermal solution 30s to converge as seen in Figure 4.12a. The
thermal solution is thus 600 times slower to respond than the height.
It should be noted here that the thermal convergence is affected by the film height and that
the height and heat generally can not be studied independently as it was just done. The thermal
response is in most cases dominated by the heat flux from the fluid film. And if the film height
is small and the leakage is low, then only a small amount of thermal energy is available through
the fluid film. Thus as the film height increases, the thermal time scale will reduce.

While the objective of this thesis originally was to model puffing effects, the focus quickly
moved to general transient behaviour. Puffing turned out to be fundamentally much more diffi-
cult to solve than other transient problems.
Though oscillations have been seen in multiple places previously, these are not considered
puffing. Instead, these are seen as oscillating convergence to a steady-state.
Puffing on the other hand is a continuing periodic constant oscillation. It is seen in pumps
that operate with fluid above the boiling point at atmospheric pressure. It is often seen as peri-
odic puffs of steam and water from the pump seal. This oscillation is also a steady-state but in
the same way as the steady-state known from basic vibration theory. In vibration theory, it is
used to describe a steady response to harmonic excitation. In order to call a behaviour puffing,
it thus requires oscillation at constant amplitude and frequency.
One Puffing simulation is run in a limited form. Because of numerical precision problems,
the simulation stopped after a couple of "Puffs". The results are therefore as good as they can
be for the current state of the program.

The simulation is presented in Figure 4.14. The same presentation is used as in section 4.2.
In Figure 4.14a it is seen that the film height takes a while to build up (around 0.8s) after which
it has one big "Puff". After this, the seal begins to open with an almost constant amplitude (at
least when compared to the previous simulations with oscillation). In the same figure, it is seen
that the seal ring surface temperature also responds to these changes. This was not necessarily
expected since the thermal effects in general have a longer time scale.
In Figure 4.14b it is seen that the boiling point also moves with the same frequency. In the
final two oscillations, a very interesting complex behaviour of the boiling point is seen. When
starting from a boiling point valley, it is seen that the boiling point slowly increases. Then
approximately halfway to the peak the slope steepens. It then plateaus for approximately a
quarter of the period after which is immediately drops to the valley and starts over.
It is also seen that the boiling point seems to have a slight phase-shift compared to the film
height (seen compared to leakage rate).
The above described is exactly what is expected from a puffing seal. In essence, every time
the seal closes, energy in the form of heat is generated, which then puffs the seal open again.
This energy generation by heat should end up balancing the energy dissipation that is seen in
fx. the two-phase simulations in subsection 4.2.3. The oscillation should thus keep opening in
a constant periodic fashion.
A small change has been made to Figure 4.14c. Instead of plotting the initial and final
state, a height peak and valley are chosen and then plotted. Since the steady-state in focus
is an oscillating steady-state, this was more representative. Starting with the quality. At the
peak, it is seen that the quality change occurs over approximately 20% of the seal and does not
evaporate completely. In contrast, in the valley, the fluid evaporates over a very short space
(≈1%) and completely evaporates before the exit.
This results in very different pressure profiles. Where the peak pressure profile has a very
smooth transition to vapour, the valley has a very sharp shift. It is also seen that the valley has
a greater integral value which is what causes the seal to open again.
Finally, Figure 4.15 shows 6 snapshots during one period. It is seen that at the peak (Snap 1)
the film has a wide evaporation span. Then as the film collapses (Snap 2) the westernmost CV
evaporates completely, which raises the entire pressure profile. This causes downward deaccel-
eration. Then in Snap 3-5 it is seen that the seal rings and fluid heat up. At the same time, the
boiling point moves inward (towards east/towards the inlet). The suction caused by the upward
velocity is probably also responsible for the increase in evaporation. There is still an excess
of opening force and the seal now accelerates upwards. Finally at the last step before the peak
(Snap 6) the evaporation begins to widen again and the point with complete evaporation moves
outwards.
It is seen that the temperature in the westernmost CVs and seal nodes vary between 175 °C
and 200 °C. This is a very high variance when considering that this occurs approximately 50
times in a second. This is a clear indication of how extreme the conditions are inside the seal.
It is so high a temperature variation that thermal expansion could have a significant influence.

The previous section highlighted the different ways this model and program can be used to give
greater insight into the physics of mechanical face seals. In this section, focus will be on the
unsolved problems and shortcomings of the code.
At the end of this section possible solutions and a prioritised list of lacking features will be
described.
5.1
Problems
The most limiting problem in the model in the current form is the computational efficiency. As
described in section 4.3 Film Height’s Influence on Computation Time, it may take in excess
of 1000 seconds to simulate 1 second. It was found that this for some reason depends on the
film height.
To better understand this time inefficiency, which is pretty consistent, a test is conducted.
The density of the seal ring material is reduced by a factor of 1000. The idea is that the step size
can stay the same while the simulation time span is reduced. This however results in the step
size reducing exactly by a factor of 1000. The problem thus seems to be located in equations
including the seal ring density. The density is only present in the solid heat conduction equa-
tion. This strongly indicate that the computational time is limited by the solid heat conduction
equation.
Some insight is found by studying the heat flux compared to the heat conduction equa-
tion. The equations are simplified to only include terms including the seal temperature and the
constant coefficients are collected into C1 and C2.

5.2
Missing Features
A lot of useful features or capabilities of the model could not be included in the time span of
this thesis. Some of this are relatively easy to program but could make a big difference.
As an example, it was not possible to directly compare with the results found in [5], since it
studies geometry not valid in the current form of the program. A more general implementation
of seal ring geometry would allow for implementing the often complex geometry of seal rings
found in production pump seals. The program built by Gani [2] has the same shortcoming.
Including this would require a rework of the FE model and the boundary between fluid and seal
rings. This could be done together with the redesign of heat flux.
Surface variation is often used in seals [4]. Thus including surface variation could allow for
studying a greater range of pump seals. This would however require a redesign of the FV and
FE mesh.
Non-uniform mesh could allow for better mesh control. This is especially useful if the
computational efficiency is not improved. This would allow for more nodes in the west where
the pressure gradients often are greater.
As seen in some of the simulations presented in the previous section, the assumption of
viscous flow is not always valid. The reason why this assumption is used in [2] and [5] is
that as long as the height is small enough, inertia is neglectable. However, when studying
puffing and "popping open" it would be beneficial to not be limited in height by the model. The
simulation in subsection 4.2.3 Two-Phase could have been much more informative if the final
state could be trusted. Since the inertia terms simply become insignificant at low heights, the
model would only be more precise, with a wider valid height span.
5.3
Combining Models
As mentioned earlier, this program was built from the ground up instead of building on top of
the program built by [2]. This allowed for faster implementation because compatibility was not
a concern. It would however be more user-friendly, efficient, and certainly more polished if the
programs were combined. This program should take one set of inputs (seal geometry, operating
conditions and program options) and then solve the problem either as a steady-state problem or
a transient problem, depending on user input.
Besides the immediate simplification for the user, this would also allow the user to find
a steady-state using the model built by Gani and use this as initial conditions for a transient
simulation. Finding steady-state is certainly not computationally efficient using the model built
in this thesis.

Chapter 6. Conclusion
6
Conclusion
In this thesis, mechanical face seals are studied, with a special focus on transient effects in a
two-phase operation. To do this a transient thermo-hydrodynamic model was built, including a
homogenous phase mixture model.
The model is an extension of the work done by Gani [2], which studied steady-state. The
equations used by Gani were either derived again with transient terms or were extended to
include transient terms.
The Navier-Stokes equation and Mass conservation were used together with a finite volume
scheme to model the fluid dynamics. The heat conduction in the seal rings was modelled using
the finite element method.
The model included differential and algebraic equations which were combined into a system
of DAE (Differential-Algebraic Equations). MATLAB’s ode15s was used to solve the DAE-
system.
It was found that the system was so ill-conditioned that solving was impossible. The vari-
ables and equations were normalised to decrease the condition number and make the system
less stiff. It was however found that the heat flux between the fluid and seal rings caused a stiff-
ness that could not be solved by normalisation. The stiffness was however only a significant
problem at film heights around and below 1 µm. The stiffness limited the maximum step size
and made the program very time inefficient at low film heights. It is proposed to rethink the
boundary condition between the fluid and seal rings in order to remove this stiffness.
The model was validated in steady-state against Gani’s model [2] along with the model
proposed by Lau et al. [3]. It was found that the transient model built in this thesis was in good
agreement with Lau. while in reasonable agreement with Gani. This was deemed odd since
the model was only slightly modified from the one built by Gani. It was then tested whether
compressibility had any influence. This was done because both Gani and Lau. neglected this,
while it could not be neglected according to the analysis of scale conducted in this thesis. It
was found that compressibility had little to no real effect on the flow, although some big dis-
crepancies were seen between the two cases.
After validation, the transient capabilities of the model were tested. This was done by a
combination of one-phase and two-phase simulations. In these simulations, the seal was al-
lowed to transiently converge towards a steady state. Here it was seen that the seal had a
response similar to a 1. order differential system when in one-phase. In two-phase, the model
showed oscillations and overshoots characteristics of a 2. order differential system. It was
found that a small increase in balance ratio had a dramatic influence on both steady-state film
height and the transient convergence toward this steady state.
Next, a simulation with transient operating conditions was run. The pump was started with
Page 56 of 58
==PAGE:67==
Chapter 6. Conclusion
a fluid temperature of 90 °C (ie. in one phase). After 1s the pumped fluid temperature was
raised to 207 °C. The transient response showed how the thermal effects acted much slower
than the fluid flow and the seal opening. It was seen that as the seal reached 207 °C the seal
popped open almost instantaneously.
Finally, a simulation was run with operating conditions chosen to induce Puffing. It was
seen that the seal slowly heated up, after which it popped open as in the previous simulation.
This time the seal closed again, heated up and then popped open again. This was thus very
much like the behaviour expected from Puffing. However, numerical problems (perhaps the
heat flux stiffness) caused the simulation to stop after a few puffs. This made it difficult to
conclude anything about the continuing behaviour.
During the work on this model, program and thesis several shortcomings and problems in
the program were found. These could not be rectified within the scope of this project but are
left as further work. The model has certainly proved its capabilities compared to other models
found in the literature surrounding mechanical face seals.
