Abstract 
 
Osteomyelitis is defined as bone infections caused mainly by the Gram-positive bacterium 
Staphylococcus aureus and characterized by a progressive loss of bone tissue due to severe, 
local inflammatory response. Implant-associated osteomyelitis (IAO) is one of the most 
common and costly complications of reconstructive orthopaedic surgery. Up to date, there is 
neither prophylaxis nor treatment 100% effective. MicroRNAs (miRNAs) are small, non-coding 
RNA that function as post-transcriptional regulators. Due to their high stability in biofluids as 
well as their role in several biological processes, miRNAs are considered to be potential 
candidates to use as non-invasive disease biomarkers.  
 
In this study, circulating miRNAs isolated from sera were studied in a porcine model for local, 
implant-associated osteomyelitis. Samples from pigs exposed to low (102 CFU) and high (104 
CFU) bacteria as well as to different implants - stainless steel and Zn with titanium oxide 
coating implants – were used. Serum, inflammatory- and bone- related miRNAs were selected 
based on literature research as well as in silico analysis by target prediction algorithms of 
mRNA involved in inflammatory responses. High-throughtput qPCR (Biomark ® platform) was 
used for miRNA expression analysis due to its high sensitivity. 
 
From a total of 67 different miRNAs; hsa-miR-197, hsa-miR-210 and hsa-miR-141-5p were 
significantly regulated in response to either bacteria dose or different coating of the implant. All 
three miRNAs target genes involved in bone homeostasis. Therefore, significant regulation after 
bacterial infection suggests a role of these miRNAs as fine-tuning regulators of bone 
homeostasis during S. aureus infection. Technical variation represents a challenge during 
miRNA profiling studies and every step should be carefully standardized.

Aim 
 
The aim of the master thesis consisted of assessing the microRNA transcriptional signature of 
implant-associated osteomyelitis in a porcine model. The hypothesis was to find circulating, 
cell-free microRNAs in serum that could be used as biomarkers for an earlier diagnostic of 
implant-associated osteomyelitis, thus leading to better prognosis and earlier treatment of the 
disease. Potential candidates including osteomyelitis- or/and inflammatory-related microRNAs 
selected based on previous literature were studied. In addition, in silico analysis of miRNA 
targets by miRNA target prediction algorithms (e.g.: MiRWalk) was performed for several 
central mRNA transcripts involved in host response to osteomyelitis. High-throughput RT-
qPCR was used as the gold standard technique to assess miRNA expression levels.

1. Background 
 
1. 1 Osteomyelitis 
Osteomyelitis is defined as bone infections caused essentially by Gram-positive bacteria of 
Staphylococcus species – mainly Staphylococcus aureus - and characterized by a progressive loss 
of bone tissue due to severe, local inflammatory response as well as an increased resorption of 
the bone. Resorption consists of breaking down the bone and releasing the minerals and 
calcium from the bone to the blood during bone remodelling 1–3. Haematogenous seeding, 
spread of infection from contiguous area and trauma with skin perforation concomitant with 
contamination are several forms of osteomyelitis (Figure 1). However, infections associated 
with fracture fixation devices and prosthesis, so-called implant-associated osteomyelitis (IAO), 
appear to be the most common and costly complication of reconstructive orthopaedic surgery 
in adults 4,5. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
It is considered that approximately 4.3% of 2.6 million orthopaedic implant surgeries 
performed annually in the United States will become infected 6,7. If other types of implants are 
considered such as cardiovascular or indwelling implants, the annual infection rate is even 
higher and can rise to 1 million of implant-related infections per year in the United States. 
Alongside this is the fact that most of bone implants – mainly hip and knee joint replacements – 
are performed on 65-years-old people and older. Taking into account that worldwide life 
expectancy as well as advances in medical technology are increasing; it is not surprising that all 
together will lead to larger demand of implants and thus an increase of implant-associated 
infections. Nowadays, there is neither prophylaxis nor treatment 100% effective against 
implant-related infections; hence the urge of improvements in new technologies as well as 
therapies 4.

1.1.1 Bone homeostasis 
Healthy bone is constantly remodelled by the action of two main cell populations: bone-
forming osteoblasts (in blue) and bone-resorbing osteoclasts (in red, Figure 2) 8. Osteoblasts 
are responsible for synthesizing and mineralizing an organic matrix around themselves, leading 
to formation of the bone extracellular matrix (BEM), where osteoblasts are then integrated as 
osteocytes. Osteoclasts are large multinuclear cells that secrete H+ protons as well as proteases 
in order to balance the synthesis of the BEM by the process called resorption 1. 
 
 
 
 
In normal bones (Figure 3), osteoclast maturation is driven by osteoblasts by their production 
of osteoclast-promoting growth factors, such as macrophage colony stimulating factor (M-CSF) 
and receptor activator of NFκ-B ligand (RANK-L) that interact with monocyte/macrophage 
precursors to drive their differentiation into osteoclasts by fusion of monocytic precursors. 
Osteoclastogenesis is simultaneously limited by osteoblast production of osteoprotegerin 
(OPG), a soluble receptor that acts as osteoclast-limiting RANK-L antagonist 9.

Therefore, the coordinated function of both osteoblasts and osteoclasts is tightly regulated by 
balanced production of RANK-L and OPG, leading to a permanent physiological homeostasis. 
However, pathological conditions such as cancers and infections have shown to alter this 
important balance, favouring osteoclast activity and thus bone degradation 1,9. 
 
1.1.2 Implant-associated osteomyelitis (IAO) 
 
1.1.2.1 Staphylococcus aureus pathology in IAO 
The gram-positive bacterium Staphylococcus aureus has been found to be the most commonly 
cultured microorganism from patients with bone infections and it is thus considered the most 
prevalent pathogen in IAO 4. S.aureus-osteoblast interaction plays a key role in the evolution 
and persistence of bone infections and its success is due to its ability to interact with 
osteoblasts at extracellular and intracellular level, following its internalization (Figure 4, I). 
Extracellularly, S.aureus is able to adhere to BEM through the expression of virulence factors 
such as toxins and adhesins that function as receptors for bone matrix components (e.g. 
fibronectin, collagen, among others)(Figure 4, I.A) 1. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Then, internalization and intracellular survival in osteoblasts are two following and/or other 
mechanisms S. aureus use to escape from the immune system (Figure 4, I.B & I.C), leading 
ultimately to S. aureus resistance to antibiotic treatments (Figure 4, II) 1,5. However, this has 
mainly been described in vitro. In addition, formation of biofilm – a thin layer of 
microorganisms that adheres to organic or inorganic surfaces by production of extracellular 
matrix - around implanted devices is also known to be involved in S. aureus osteomyelitis and is 
considered a primary cause of antibiotic resistance 4.

During S. aureus bone infection, infected osteoblasts increase their production of RANK-L 
whereas OPG production is decreased, leading to an increased osteoclastogenesis and hence 
loss of bone (Figure 5). This imbalance of RANK-L and OPG is provoked by the expression of 
virulence factors from S. aureus biofilm usually formed on prosthetic materials as well as 
molecules expressed by the host in response to S .aureus infection - toll-like receptor (TLR) 
stimulating pro-inflammatory cytokines such as TNF- and IL-1 1,3. 
 
 
Although S. aureus infection is known to increase bone resorption indirectly by its interaction 
with osteoblasts, the direct influence of S. aureus on osteoclasts is still unclear. Gallet et. al. 
2014 demonstrated that S. aureus is internalized also into both precursors and mature 
osteoclasts (Figure 6). However, while internalization into mature osteoclast induces fusion 
and enhances bone resorption capacities; internalization into precursors inhibits 
osteoclastogenesis. In contrast, internalization into precursors promotes their differentiation

1.1.2.2 Host response to S. aureus infection 
It is well known that staphylococcal infection leads to local recruitment and activation of 
immune cells, driven by osteoblasts secretion of inflammatory factors such as cytokines, 
chemokines and growth factors, provoking an inflammatory response 3. More specifically, 
osteoblast production of mainly IL-6, G-CSF and GM-CSF, and IL-8 and CXCL2 leads to the 
recruitment and activation of macrophages; favour leukocyte maturation and chemoattract 
neutrophils, respectively (Figure 7). Moreover, infected osteoblasts also increase the release of 
IL-12 and CXCL10 as well as the ability to express CD40 and MHC II, all implicated in Th1 
lymphocyte response. Thus, infected osteoblasts are considered to take part in the innate as 
well as adaptive immune response against S. aureus infection 1. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
1.1.2.3 State-of-the-art treatments 
The main clinical objectives regarding IAO treatment consist of first preventing the initiation of 
infection, to cure the infection once IAO is developed and prevent its recurrence afterwards. 
Generally, when an implant-related infection is diagnosed, the first and most common 
treatment used to overcome the infection is a 6-week course of systemic antibiotics 10 . 
However, cases of IAO caused by antibiotic resistant S. aureus strains have increasingly 
emerged and is of a major concern since long-term antibiotic treatments no longer work for 
patients with such S.aureus strains (e.g. Methicillin-resistant S. aureus - MRSA)4,5. In those cases, 
there is a need of surgical removal that will depend on the patient’s condition, type of device

Due to biofilm formation often found in IAO, few local antibiotic delivery systems have been 
used as potential treatments in order to avoid systemic antibiotics that cannot reach the local 
infection due to poor permeation of the infected area 4. Regarding prophylaxis of the disease, 
several attempts have been made to find an ideal biofilm-resistant material for bone implants 
such as antibiotic-coated implants 4 or biocompatible materials. Silver has been used as a 
strong antibacterial agent 11. However, silver may have a cytotoxic side effect and act as Trojan 
horses, damaging intracellular functions and leading to apoptosis 12.  
 
1.1.2.4 Coating implants with Zn 
Zinc (Zn) is a trace element known to participate in several biological activities such as DNA 
synthesis and nucleic acid metabolism. It is also well known for its excellent antibacterial 
qualities, as it seems to bind and disrupt bacterial cell membranes (Figure 8) 11,13. Recently, the 
incorporation of Zn in bone implants as biomaterial has gained special attention. This is not 
surprising since Zn has been shown to stimulate osteoblast formation and mineralization 14. All 
these features make Zn an excellent candidate, combined with other materials such as titanium 
oxide (TiO2)-based biomaterials, as biomaterial to use in bone implants. Although several in 
vitro studies have demonstrated the high biocompatibility and long-term in vivo stability of 
titanium oxide, there are no studies assessing the combination of Zn and titanium oxide as 
coating agents to improve bone implant responses in an IAO animal model 14. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
1.2 Pig as animal model 
In general, there is a vast range of animal models when considering musculoskeletal infections, 
including implant-related osteomyelitis. Depending on the anatomic location, cause of infection 
and fixation system used; different animal models are required in order to mirror those 
features found in humans. For example canine and porcine femora have shown similar ash and 
collagen phase proportions to humans, but canine bone have significantly higher fracture 
toughness, a measure of the energy required to propagate an existing microfracture in bones 4.

Furthermore, different species vary in the immune response to bacterial infections and this is 
of a major importance during infection trials 4. Humans and pigs, in contrast to rodents, have 
high percentages of neutrophils in the peripheral blood and mice do not have a direct homolog 
of IL-8, a major chemoattractant for neutrophils 15. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
While larger animals are more suitable to tolerate surgical interventions and their bone 
architecture is commonly similar to humans, smaller animals such as rodents can be housed 
and fed more easily (Figure 9). In addition, larger numbers of small animals can be included in 
different treatment groups, leading to more statistically significance at an acceptable cost 4. The 
above circumstances elucidate the current lack of standardization of animal models for bone-
associated infection studies and the fact that there is no a perfect animal model suitable for any 
type of studies. Significant variation of the ideal animal model for implant-related osteomyelitis 
studies exist in the literature, mainly due to the different bone sizes of different species. 
Usually, rodents and rabbits have been used to model implant-related complications, but some 
implants cannot be scaled down for testing clinical relevance to humans, being hence not 
suitable for implant-related infection studies 16.  
 
Several animal models – from rodents to pigs - have been characterized for haematogenous 
osteomyelitis. However, IAO animal models have been only described in rodents. In addition, 
most of the animal models characterized for osteomyelitis studies have used materials such as 
polymethylmethacrylate bone cement or bone wax in order to develop a chronic infection, as 
this is observed in humans 17. However, there is no pig characterized for IAO as animal model. 
As IAO is an infectious disease, the immunome of the animal is a main feature to consider when 
selecting the right animal species 15,16. Dawson et al. 2011 demonstrated that most of the 
immune proteins from pigs share structural and functional similarities with their equivalents in 
humans. More specifically, the conservation of homology and structural motifs of more than 
1000 orthologs from pigs, mice and humans showed that overall mean similarity to human 
proteins was significantly higher for pigs compared to mice 15, 16.

1.3 MicroRNAs as biomarkers 
MicroRNAs (miRNAs) are endogenous small, non-coding RNA molecules of 20-22 nucleotides 
length that function as gene expression regulators at post-transcriptional level. These recently 
discovered class of RNA molecules are regulated and transcribed like protein coding genes. It is 
thought that about 60% of human genes are regulated by miRNAs 18. Once transcribed in the 
nucleus, miRNA transcripts are processed by Drosha and Dicer enzymes (RNases) and the 
mature miRNA is then associated with the RNA-induced silencing complex (RISC) (Figure 11)

Gene expression regulation by miRNAs is performed by mainly two different mechanisms: (I) 
perfect complementarity and (II) partial complementarity of the miRNA and the targeted 
mRNA (Figure 11). Generally, while perfect complementarity results in degradation of the 
mRNA, partial complementarity leads to destabilisation and translational inhibition 19. While 
cleavage and degradation of mRNA is mainly observed in plants, destabilisation and inhibition 
of translation by partial complementarity is mostly seen in mammals 20. The complementarity 
sequence is most commonly between the 3’ untranslated regions (UTR) of the mRNA and the 
seed sequence of the 5’ end of the miRNA (Figure 12), defined as a conserved, short region 
situated at positions 2nd to 7th of the miRNA (Figure 12) 21. Due to the fact that the seed 
sequence is the responsible for the complementarity and thus the regulatory function of the 
miRNA; one miRNA can target hundreds of mRNA species and one mRNA transcript can have 
multiple binding sites for different miRNAs 22. Moreover, as regulation of mRNA and/or protein 
levels can proceed faster than the DNA transcription process in the nucleus, miRNAs mediate a 
rapid adjustment of gene expression in response to environmental changes, which is defined as 
fine-tuning regulation of gene expression. These properties predict miRNAs as powerful 
molecular tools that operate by controlling several gene regulatory networks simultaneously 8. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 11. Biosynthesis of miRNA and its funtions. MiRNAs are transcribed from the DNA to a primary miRNA 
(pri-miRNA), which typically consists of 1 kilobase (kb) length. Then Drosha enzyme cleaves the haipirin at
approximately 11 bp away from the basal junction between single stranded and double stranded RNA, leading to 
precursor miRNA (pre-miRNA). Subsequently, pre-miRNA is exported to the cytoplasm by exportin-5. There, pre-
miRNA is cleaved by Dicer close to the terminal loop, liberating a RNA duplex. The mature miRNA is then loaded 
into the RISC complex and the complementary strand is discarded 19. RISC-miRNA complexes, together with other
proteins (e.g. Ago2), are known to destabilize and shorten mRNA poly(A) tails, leading thus to mRNA translation 
inhibition or degradation 19.

1.3.1 Role of miRNAs 
Many miRNAs have been described to be involved in several biological processes - e.g. cell 
cycle, apoptosis and survival - as well as development of specific tissues such as skin, muscle 
and bone. In fact, miRNAs contribute to osteogenesis by regulating every step from embryonic 
bone development to maintenance of adult bone tissue 8. Likewise, specific miRNAs appear to 
play a critical role in different diseases, including cancer and cardiovascular diseases, among 
others 23. Therefore, special interest exists in using small non-coding RNAs for cancer research 
and drug development. MiRNAs are also known to be present in biofluids such as serum, 
plasma and urine, and numerous studies demonstrated that secreted miRNAs could be 
implicated or a result of pathological conditions 23. In fact, it has been shown that each cancer 
tissue might have specific miRNA signature 24. In addition, the high stability of miRNAs, 
attributed to their encapsulation in protein complexes or microvesicles, such as argonaute2 
(Ago2), high density lipoprotein (HDL) and exosomes, respectively, makes miRNAs potential 
candidates for use as non-invasive biomarkers for disease diagnosis and prognosis (Figure 13)

1.3.2 MiRNA detection – high-throughput qPCR 
There are several methods of detection of miRNAs, including Microarrays, Next Generation 
Sequencing (NGS) and quantitative real time PCR (RT-qPCR) 26. Nevertheless, the most 
sensitive platform that enables detection of low amount, cell-free miRNAs is the RT-qPCR and 
therefore it is still considered the gold standard in miRNA quantification (Figure 14). 
Furthermore, RT-qPCR is used to validate results from NGS or microarrays 22. NGS is used for 
discovery of new miRNAs concurrent with profiling studies but have some drawbacks such as it 
is less sensitive, more expensive - sample wise compared to high throughput qPCR - and larger 
sample volumes are needed 27. 
 
 
1.3.2.1 Technical aspects regarding miRNA profiling 
The high sensitivity of qPCR makes it a perfect method to use for miRNA profiling in biofluids 
since miRNAs are known to be present in very low amounts in body biofluids. However, special 
precautions need to be taken regarding technical aspects because there is a lack of consensus in 
isolation procedures, optimal quantification platform, quality control of samples or 
normalization methods (Figure 15) 23. First, handling bias can occur during RNA extraction, 
leading to contamination from cell associated miRNA ,e.g. by haemolysis - rupture of 
erythrocytes and the release of their contents into the serum – that will influence the miRNA 
signature of the sample 22,28. Even sample collection methods can influence the expression of 
several miRNAs. Second, it is still unknown whether plasma or serum represents a trustworthy 
sample material for miRNA profiling. Yamada et al., (2014) showed a lack of correlation 
between miRNA levels in serum and plasma 29. Moreover, the inter-individual variability needs 
to be taken into account, as it is unclear whether conditions such as age, gender or lifestyle 
could affect miRNA profiles 29. Therefore, all pre-profiling factors that could influence miRNA 
levels in sample material need to be carefully taken into account and reported according to 
Minimum Information for Publication of Quantitative Real-Time PCR Experimets (MIQE) 
guidelines 23,30.

When performing miRNA-profiling studies, validation is always required. However, due to the

overcome with the occurrence of high-throughput qPCR microfluidic based platforms (e.g.

(Figure 16). The vast possibilities to introduce technical variation as described above might be 
the reasons why several studies identifying miRNA biomarkers for the same disease have 
shown variable results 31. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
1.3.3 MiRNAs and bone diseases 
Regarding bone diseases, for prediction of disease progression or monitoring treatment 
success, bone turnover markers such as serum procollagen type I N propeptide (s-P1NP) or 
serum C-terminal telopeptide of type I collagen (s-CTX) can be used, as they reflect the 
coordination of bone formation and bone resorption. However, up to date, these specific 
markers still specific limitations such as a lack of normative reference population databases, 
inadequate standardization of quality control or sample handling. Therefore, novel and 
different biomarkers are needed to give a better understanding of bone diseases 32.  
 
Several studies have shown that bone physiology as well as the development and progression 
of certain bone diseases (e.g.: osteoporosis) are linked to specific changes in circulating 
miRNAs. However, a lot of work is still needed in order to be able to use circulating miRNAs as 
established and robust diagnostic tools for bone diseases in a clinical manner 32.

Data analysis and normalization 
Row data was analysed by using GenEx® 6.0 software. Row Cq values were efficiency corrected 
by a default value of 1.1, as all efficiencies were too high for most miRNAs. Mean values of cDNA 
replicates were used after replicates with Cq > 1.5 were removed. Moreover, miRNAs with 
more than 20% of missing values were eliminate. 
 
To remove technical variation, normalization of raw data was performed by global mean and 
reference miRNAs. Due to too many missing values, normalization by reference miRNAs was 
preferred. Reference miRNAs were selected by GenEx®. Both normalization methods gave 
comparable results and thus reliable results in both normalization methods were used for 
further analysis (data not shown). Relative quantities of Cq from samples after treatment were 
calculated compared to the same pigs before treatment, whose mean was scaled to 1. Data is 
presented in relative quantities (RQ). Log 2 transformation was performed for statistical 
analysis. Paired t-tests were performed to assess statistical significance. Changes in miRNA 
expression levels were considered statistically and biologically significant if p-values < 0.05 and 
the relative gene expression difference was > 1.5-fold – up or down (see Appendix D).

3.2 Pre-profiling factors influencing miRNAs in serum 
 
3.2.1 Different sample collection methods can affect miRNA profiles 
Peripheral blood is used to extract serum and plasma for miRNA expression profiling studies, in 
order to identify potential biomarkers for specific diseases. The main difference between 
plasma and serum is the presence and absence, respectively, of fibrinogen and clotting factors 
(Figure 26)40. However, precautions need to be taken as miRNA expression differences might 
be a result of technical variation. This include platelets, containing a vast range of miRNAs that 
can be released to the serum during coagulation, together with miRNAs from red blood cells if 
haemolysis is present 40,41.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Different tubes are used during blood collection. While serum is normally collected in tubes 
with clotting agents, allowing thus coagulation and separation of serum; tubes containing 
anticoagulants such as citrate, heparin or ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA) are used for 
plasma collection. However, it is known that citrate and heparin act as inhibitors of the 
enzymes used during qPCR. Therefore, if plasma wants to be extracted for miRNA profiling 
studies, EDTA collection tubes should be preferably used. 
 
In this study, BD Vacutainer ® Clot Activator Tubes were used for serum collection (Figure 27). 
However, coagulation during serum preparation can lead to release of miRNAs from blood cells

3.2.2 RNA quality control 
3.2.2.1 Assessment of RNA yield and purity 
Although body fluids such as plasma and serum represent suitable sample materials to use as 
sources of potential minimum-invasive biomarkers, miRNA profiling in serum represents a 
challenge due to several technical aspects. First, serum is known to be a cell-free fluid that 
contains very low amounts of RNA. This implies that standard methods for RNA purity and 
quality assessment such as OD or Bioanalyzer are unsuitable for serum samples. In this study, 
NanoDrop measurements showed that RNA concentrations were variable between the samples, 
ranging from 12.39 ng/μl the lowest to 111.55 ng/μl the highest (Figure 28). These results are 
not reliable measurements of miRNA concentration. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Absorbance at 230 and 280 nm is performed to measure the presence of contaminating agents 
(e.g: phenol) and proteins, respectively. Typical A260/280 and A260/230 ratios of 2.0 and 2.0-2.2 
indicate pure RNA, respectively 42. However, in this study all RNA samples displayed both 
A260/280 and A260/230 ratios below 2. Nevertheless, the very low concentration of RNA in serum 
samples makes spectrophotometer measurements not reliable.

leading to low A260/230 ratios. In addition, MS2 was added during RNA isolation. MS2 is a RNA

3.2.3 Pilot study 
The pilot study was performed with porcine serum samples isolated from a different group of 
pigs. Even though the sample material used for the pilot study was not the same as for the 
master thesis study, both were serum samples from pigs and therefore the results were 
believed to be comparable. 
 
3.2.3.1 RNA concentration for cDNA synthesis 
Four different concentrations of RNA samples were tested: undiluted RNA; 1/5 dilution, 1/10 
dilution and 1/100 dilution. Based on the results, undiluted RNA displayed higher Cq values 
compared to diluted RNA samples. This is because undiluted samples lead to inhibition of the 
cDNA synthesis due to presence of inhibitors from the extraction method/procedure such as 
phenol; therefore RNA samples need to be diluted to perform an optimal cDNA synthesis. From 
the three dilutions assessed, the best results were obtained when 1/10 dilution was used 
(Figure 31). Accordingly, 1/10 RNA dilutions were used for cDNA synthesis in the present 
master thesis.

However, although the same parameters were used for the master thesis, the results were not 
similar. In general, all samples were below or at the end of the dynamic range when compared 
to the pilot study, meaning that the initial miRNA input was too low to be able to be analysed. 
MiRNA concentration is known to be really low in many body fluids including serum 43. 
 
Several studies have demonstrated the difference of RNA yield and quality depending on the 
extraction method used 23,41. Nevertheless, if the same RNA extraction kit is used for all 
samples, comparison between them should be valid. However, while the same kit - MiRNeasy 
Mini Kit (Qiagen) – was used for RNA isolation from both pilot study and master thesis samples, 
the results were very different, reflecting a great amount of technical variation. In order to 
increase the dynamic range in the present study, higher amounts of initial RNA are needed. 
Therefore, an alternative approach could be to use double amount of serum in the same 
extraction procedure, meaning that 500 μl of serum instead of 250 could be added in the same 
extraction column. This would result in double concentrated RNA sample and hence higher 
amounts of miRNAs. Again, several dilutions of the original RNA samples should be tested to 
assess the best dilution factor and avoid inhibition in further steps such as cDNA synthesis. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
On the other hand, as miRNAs seem to be associated with proteins, encapsulated in exosomes 
or extracellular vesicles; other approaches could focus on isolation of exosomes or extracellular 
vesicles (EV) (Figure 32) 25. As EV arise from internal cells of progenitor cells, isolation of 
tissue-specific EV could be isolated by targeting tissue-specific surface markers, leading thus to 
isolation of tissue-specific miRNAs 25. However, as those EV would be highly diluted once 
released into the blood stream, local sampling could be an alternative to even get higher 
concentrations of desired miRNAs. Still, this is a relatively invasive method.

4. Conclusion 
 
MiRNA expression analysis of porcine sera revealed four miRNAs, hsa-miR-210, hsa-
miR-197-3p, hsa-miR-141-5p and hsa-miR-20a-5p, to be significantly regulated in response to 
either bacteria dose or different coating of implant. Hsa-miR-197, hsa-miR-210 and hsa-miR-
141-5p were significantly upregulated in response to regular implant with saline, high bacteria 
dose with regular implant and Zn-implant, respectively. All three miRNAs are known to have 
gene targets encoding proteins involved in bone homeostasis. Therefore, significant regulation 
after bacterial infection suggests a role of these miRNAs as fine-tuning regulators of bone 
homeostasis during S. aureus infection. 
 
It must be considered, however, that miRNAs are fine-tuners of gene expression, 
meaning that they have a modest effect on their target mRNAs. A part from that, the 
differentially expression of the four significant miRNAs need to be validated with supplemental 
studies in order to confirm whether the regulation of these miRNAs would be a response to 
local S. aureus infection in the bone or cause of a different unknown response. Moreover, the 
fact that one miRNA can target several mRNAs adds an extra layer of complexity and thus 
functional studies of miRNA:mRNA interactions both experimentally and in silico would give 
more insights in the role of miRNA regulation during osteomyelitis. Likewise, to further study 
the host response during IAO, mRNA coding for immune regulated genes should be analysed 
both in situ and in circulation. 
 
Histological sections of bones revealed that high bacteria dose was required to develop 
an infection in pigs. In addition, the tendency of pigs with Zn coated implants to display lower 
inflammatory response might indicate the benefits of Zn as antibacterial agent. For further 
studies, bigger implants could be considered in order to increase the amount of Zn and 
therefore test for significant response. 
 
On the other hand, technical variation is known to represent a challenge during miRNA 
profiling studies, especially when using plasma or serum samples. Therefore, every step from 
sample collection to platform used should be carefully standardized and the same protocols 
must be applied if two sample sets need to be compared.
