Abstract
As a fundamental medical procedure, transfusion of donor blood suffers from important draw-
backs that reveal the need for developing artificial alternatives. In the search for synthetic RBC
substitutes, hemoglobin (Hb)-based oxygen carriers (HBOCs) of different nature have been pro-
posed to ensure oxygen delivery, long circulation time, stability and safety that cannot be achieved
with free Hb. Herein, we explore the in situ encapsulation of Hb within nanoparticles (NPs) based
on zeolitic imidazolate framework (ZIF)-8. We report a facile and rapid synthesis method in aque-
ous solutions and mild reaction conditions which prevents the damage of Hb during the reaction
and allows it to retain its O2 carrying functionality. We provide an in-depth characterisation of
the particles prepared with different concentrations of Hb and PEG, including ζ-potential, FTIR,
XRD, SEM and CD analysis, among others. Through the incorporation of PEG at high concentra-
tions, we successfully prepare NPs with sodalite ZIF-8 crystal structure, demonstrating its ability
to promote crystal growth in aqueous solution and room temperature. Furthermore, we prove
that PEG enhances the encapsulation efficiencies of Hb in ZIF-8 to values in the 80-100% range.
The encapsulation method did not affect the secondary structure and oxygen release functionality
of Hb in NPs prepared with 20 and 40 mg mL−1. Moreover, we report crystalline Hb-loaded PEG-
ZIF-8 particles in the presence of low Hb concentration. We propose multiple hypotheses on the
roles of Hb and PEG within the system based on the experimental observations. In summary, this
study demonstrates the simplicity,flexibility and efficacy of a novel ZIF-8-based HBOC synthesis
reaction, and provides the guidelines for adjusting the Hb and PEG concentrations to enhance the
desired properties of the HBOC.

Introduction
Blood transfusions are a common yet essential procedure in many clinical contexts, including
treatment of acute trauma, blood disorders such as hemophilia or anemia, and severe loss of blood
components[1]. Although the use of donor blood transfusion is widespread, there are important
challenges and risks associated with this process, even under the safest conditions. Moreover,
blood from donors must be screened for the presence of infectious agents, such as HIV or hepati-
tis. Moreover, it is crucial to ensure blood type matching by screening for compatible blood group
antigens. The delays required for these screening procedures, which are key to ensure patient
safety, may in turn be fatal in emergency situations where time is a critical factor. The short shelf
life of blood, corresponding to 42 days at 4 ºC, contributes to the difficulty of having large blood
supplies that are readily available for emergencies affecting many individuals. Furthermore, red
blood cells (RBCs) suffer some changes during their storage (the so-called "storage lesion"), affect-
ing their stability and leading to adverse transfusion outcomes[2]. Paramount to these limitations
is the fact the world faces frequent blood supply shortages, with a decreasing amount of donations
from eligible blood donors and a growing demand due to an aging population [3]. All in all, it is
evident that there is an urgent and persistent need for developing RBC substitutes that are stable,
universally compatible and possess long circulation time and shelf life, which ultimately ensures
safety and availability[4, 5].
Figure 1: (a) Representation of the Hb tetramer with its four subunits represented in different
colours. Each of the subunits contains a heme group, represented in (b) with its detailed chemical
structure. This group contains an iron atom (shown in red), which is responsible for Hb’s O2
carrying ability. Note that the schematic of the heme group also includes two of the histidine (His)
radicals in Hb (distal and proximal, in bright red).
Hemoglobin (Hb) is the protein present inside RBCs that is responsible for their ability to transport
oxygen. Oxygen and carbon dioxide bind reversibly to Hb and are transported from high to low
concentration areas. Specifically, Hb is composed of four subunits: two α and two β polypeptide
chains, each accommodating an iron-containing heme group (see figure 1) that has the ability of
binding one O2 molecule with high affinity [6]. Upon binding of O2, Hb changes conformation
from deoxyHb to oxyHb, adjusting the affinity for O2 according to the environmental conditions
[6]. Even though Hb is responsible for O2 transport across various tissues in the body, it must
be encapsulated to serve as a long circulating RBC substitute. This is because free Hb is quickly
cleared out of the body, with severe side effects like renal toxicity and vasoconstriction. Addition-

ally, free Hb is unstable and takes the form of methemoglobin (metHb), which is not capable of
binding O2 and induces oxidative stress [4, 7, 8, 9]. Considerable efforts have been devoted to the
fabrication of Hb-based oxygen carriers (HBOCs), in which Hb is encapsulated in a supporting
matrix. Although the interest in HBOCs took off in the 1980s, answering to the fear of transfus-
ing HIV contaminated blood, currently, no product is clinically approved for human applications
[10]. Some of the main challenges faced by the first generation of HBOCs include the oxidation
into metHb (implying loss of function) and short circulation times in comparison to RBCs. This
mainly arises from the interaction of the HBOCs with cells, leading to the removal of the RBC
substitutes [4].
Recently, novel materials known as metal organic frameworks (MOFs) have been used as nanocar-
riers in a wide range of applications [11]. These are crystalline, high surface area porous materials
that result from a coordination of metal ions and organic linkers, leading to a one-, two- or three-
dimensional network that can adopt diverse structural topologies with tunable size [12]. There-
fore, MOFs have been suggested as a potential HBOC matrix [13]. A particular widely studied
kind of MOFs, zeolitic imidazolate framework-8 (ZIF-8), appears to be an attractive candidate
thanks to its reported low cytotoxicity, high porosity allowing oxygen diffusion, as well as pro-
tective and high stability qualities to prolong Hb’s biological properties [14, 15]. Moreover, this
system has been synthesized successfully in the presence of proteins under mild biocompatible
conditions that do not damage the protein’s structure, through a simple one-pot method based
on a self assembly reaction called biomineralization [16, 17, 18]. This has shown to achieve high
Hb encapsulation, as well as high yield efficiency after optimization in preliminary results in the
group. Therefore, there is a strong interest to keep investigating this promising candidate.
Figure 2: Molecular representation of ZIF-8 with SOD structure generated using Mercury [19].
The Zn ions are shown as tetrahedra. Nitrogen and carbon atoms are shown in blue and grey,
respectively. Hydrogen atoms are not shown for simplicity.
ZIF owe their name to their zeolite-like topology resulting from the bridging of tetrahedral metal
ions (transition metals) through imidazolate linkers [20]. In particular, ZIF-8 is composed by zinc
ions (Zn2+) that coordinate with the imidazole ring in 2-methylimidazole. Its crystal structure

belongs to the I¯43m space group, with unit cell dimension a = 17 Å at room temperature [21].
Moreover, ZIF-8 is characterised by a sodalite (SOD) topology (figure 2) with calculated1 pore di-
ameter and aperture of dp = 11.6 Å and da = 3.4 Å, respectively, and surface area SBET = 1630
m2 g−1 according to the Brunauer-Emmett-Teller (BET) model [22, 20]. Thus far, a great number
of synthesis strategies have been proposed to obtain ZIF-8, including hydrothermal, solvother-
mal, sonochemical and microwave-assisted methods with different solvents, temperatures and
synthesis times [12]. Relatively pure ZIF-8 products have been obtained under harsh synthesis
conditions and/or involving organic solvents [20, 23]. Recently, there has been an increased inter-
est in developing synthesis methods for ZIF-8 in aqueous solution as a green, biocompatible and
cheaper alternative. Moreover, the use of water as a solvent would circumvent issues observed
with organic solvents with large molecular size like dimethylformamide (DMF), which has been
observed to remain trapped inside ZIF-8’s pores [24]. Diverse approaches have been reported in
the literature by involving different pH-adjusting additives and modifying the linker:Zn molar
ratio [25]. However, the obtaining of ZIF-8 crystals in an aqueous system has proven to be chal-
lenging, and the presence of by-products and amorphous phases has been observed under many
conditions [26].
In this project, we have investigated the development of a novel HBOC constructed with ZIF-8
nanoparticles (NPs). To do so, the synthesis of ZIF-8 in aqueous solution has been explored in
the presence of the biocompatible polymer polyethylene glycol (PEG). Moreover, different con-
centrations of Hb have been successfully incorporated into ZIF-8 NPs in the absence and presence
of PEG. The different formulations developed have been thoroughly characterised through tech-
niques including Fourier-transform infra-red (FTIR) spectroscopy, scanning electron microscopy
(SEM), X-ray diffraction (XRD). This has allowed to investigate the roles of Hb and PEG in the
particle formation. Furthermore, we have evaluated the secondary structure of the encapsulated
Hb by circular dichroism (CD), as well as the oxygen carrying functionality of the HBOC system
proposed. Herein, we exploit the tunability of this ZIF-8-based system by defining the synthesis
conditions under which different properties are enhanced in the HBOCs, including Hb loading,
functionality or crystallinity.

2.2
Particle fabrication methods
(a)
Synthesis of empty ZIF-8-based particles
Synthesis of ZIF-8MQ.
A control sample of Hb- and PEG-free ZIF-8 particles were prepared in
MQ at room temperature, with a Zn2+:2-MIM molar ratio of 1:4 based on previous research[28, 29].
This choice also stems from the optimization of Hb encapsulation with varying Zn2+:2-MIM mo-
lar ratios, performed before the start of this thesis project.
A solution of Zn(NO3)2 · 6H2O (1 mL, 20 mM in MQ) was added to an 8 mL glass vial containing
2-MIM (1 mL, 80 mM in MQ), and the mixture was incubated for 10 min under stirring at 800 rpm.
The resulting suspension (2 mL) was transferred to a 2 mL Eppendorf tube and centrifuged (12300
g, 10 min) to collect the obtained particles, which were then washed three times with MQ. Samples
were stored at 4 ◦C or freeze dried in a ScanVac CoolSafe 55-4 Pro system (Labogene, Denmark),
if required for characterisation.
Reaction time studies were conducted to explore the changes in reaction yield, morphology and
crystallinity. For that, incubation of the reagents was prolonged for 1h, 8h and 24h, keeping the
rest of the protocol unchanged.
Synthesis of PEG-ZIF-8.
Particles were also synthesized in the presence of different concentra-
tions of PEG (10, 50, 100 and 200 mg mL−1; denoted as PEG10,50,100,200-ZIF-8, respectively). Simi-
larly to the process described above, MQ, 2-MIM (40 µL, 2 M in MQ) and an aqueous solution of
PEG (33, 167, 333 or 667 µL respectively, from a 600 mg mL−1 solution in MQ) were combined in
a glass vial to have a volume of 1 mL. To this, 1 mL of aqueous Zn(NO3)2 · 6H2O (20 mM) was
added under stirring at 800 rpm. The mixture was incubated for 10 min and subsequently washed
as explained above. It is worth noting that due to the high viscosity of PEG, the 2-MIM-PEG aque-
ous solution was also stirred before the addition of Zn(NO3)2 · 6H2O to ensure homogeneity.
Reaction time studies were also performed on PEG200-ZIF-8, with same aforementioned incuba-
tion times of 1, 8 and 24 h.
Synthesis of standard ZIF-8MetOH.
For reference, ZIF-8 NPs were prepared in MetOH, based
on standard protocols often described in literature[30, 18]. In short, two separate solutions of
Zn(NO3)2 · 6H2O (20 mM) and 2-MIM (80 mM) were prepared in MetOH. In a glass vial, 1 mL of
each solution were combined. The mixture was stirred for 5 min at 800 rpm and then incubated for
24 h at room temperature. The formed particles were spun down and washed 3 times in MetOH
(12300 g, 10 min). The solvent was then removed before freeze drying.

Attenuated total reflectance (ATR)-FTIR spectra were recorded using a Shimadzu IRAffinity-1S

(b)
CD Spectroscopy
Data acquisition.
Far-UV CD spectra were acquired using a JASCO J-815 spectropolarimeter
(JASCO, Essex, UK). Freshly prepared Hb@ZIF-8 and PEG-Hb@ZIF-8 NPs (less than 48 hours old)
were dispersed in MQ at an estimated Hb concentration of 0.08 mg mL−1. The same procedure
was followed for free Hb, in order to obtain a reference for protein folding. The diluted samples
were measured at room temperature in a 0.5 mm path length cell. The acquisition range was
190-260 nm, with a step size of 1 nm. Eight spectra were acquired for each sample. Five blank
(MQ) measurements were performed before each sample. The cuvette was cleaned with a 2%
aqueous solution of HELMANEX II in between samples. It is worth noting that spectra from
empty ZIF-8MQ and PEG-ZIG-8 were also measured to confirm that there was no absorbance from
the particles themselves.
Data processing.
The software Spectra Analysis - Spectra Manager Version 2 from JASCO was used
to post-process the CD data. For each sample, the eight spectra acquired were averaged. The cor-
responding five blank measurements were also averaged, and subsequently subtracted from the
sample’s spectrum. The CD data, quanitfied in ellipticity (mdeg) units, were converted to mean
residue ellipticity (Θ, deg cm2 dmol−1) units. This was done in order to correct for concentra-
tion differences and ease the comparison across samples. These two units are related through the
expression [33]:

Functionality studies
(a)
Oxygen releasing properties
The oxygen releasing ability of encapsulated Hb was quantified and compared to that of free Hb.
The release of O2 from OxyHb was forced by the addition of K3[Fe(CN)6], in a reaction where the
ferrous ion (Fe2+) in Hb is oxidized into the ferric state (Fe3+), which cannot bind oxygen. In other
words, K3[Fe(CN)6] reacts with Hb turning it into metHb [39]. The consequent increase of O2 in
solution was measured with a needle-type oxygen microsensor (PreSens, Regensburg, DE).
Data acquisition.
Fresh Hb1,10,20,40@ZIF-8 and PEG200-Hb@ZIF-8 (low, medium, high and very
high Hb concentration) were prepared less than 8 hours before measurement. The Hb content
of the resulting samples was quantified via UV-vis absorption at 280 nm. Samples and free Hb
were diluted in MQ to 4 and 6 mg mL−1. In a glass vial, 600 µL of sample suspension were

Results and Discussion
3.1
Fabrication and characterisation of empty ZIF-8 particles
(a)
Fabrication of ZIF-8MQ and PEG-ZIF-8
The preparation of standard ZIF-8 is usually carried out in organic solvents, being dimethylfor-
mamide (DMF) [40, 20] and MetOH [30, 24, 41, 42, 43] the most commonly used ones. In order
to keep away from toxic, polluting and expensive solvents and to avoid damaging Hb during the
synthesis process, it is desirable to fabricate ZIF-8 in aqueous and mild conditions (without high
temperatures or harsh ultrasonication). Different groups have reported the successful synthesis
of ZIF-8 in water under different conditions. Notably, Pan et al. were the first to obtain ZIF-8
produced in MQ and at room temperature by mixing aqueous solutions of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O and
2-MIM at a 2-MIM:Zn+2 molar ratio of 70:1 and higher, with an incubation time of 5 min [44].
Liang et al. used zinc acetate (Zn(CH3CO2)2)) in their synthesis protocol and tested Zn+2:2-MIM
molar ratios of 1:1, 2, 4, 8 and 16, incubating the mixture for 24 h under static or stirred conditions
[45]. These and other reports have shown that many parameters play a critical role in the for-
mation of the ZIF-8 crystals, which are formed by the self-assembly of the organic linker 2-MIM
and Zn2+ through coordination bonds. Among others, the zinc source (e.g. Zn(NO3)2, ZnSO4
or Zn(CH3CO2)2), the Zn2+:2-MIM molar ratio, the reaction time and the water content (i.e. the
Zn2+:2-MIM:H2O molar ratio) have been reported to strongly affect the morphology and crystal
structure of the resulting ZIF-8 [25, 26].
In this project, we decided to use Zn(NO3)2 as the Zn salt and to keep a constant Zn2+:2-MIM:H2O
molar ratio of 1:4:5552. The reason for this is that prior investigations in the group demonstrated
this ratio to be optimal in terms of Hb encapsulation2. Moreover, in general, it is desirable to
keep the 2-MIM content low due to its toxicity and relatively high cost. We also explored the
incorporation of PEG into the formulation, as this polymer has been reported to improve the bio-
compatibility and stability of different MOFs, including ZIF-8, in protocols with different levels
of complexity and time requirements [46, 47, 48]. Here, we adopted a straightforward synthesis

in different ways for different applications [52, 15]. When designing a RBC substitute is the aim,
having an arrangement of atoms generates a defined structure that can be used, for example,
to co-encapsulate Hb and different active agents displaying functionalities of the biological RBCs.
However, the crystallization process of ZIF-8 is complex and dependant on many parameters and,
thus, it might be affected by the protocol modifications implemented in this project. Therefore, we
assessed the crystalline structure of the different ZIF-8 particles using XRD.

Proposed hypothesis on the role of PEG
In the herein presented results, we have found that in the presence of PEG, ZIF-8 NPs with SOD-
like structure are obtained, while in the absence of it we obtain some unidentified structure under
the same synthesis conditions. Herein, we propose a preliminary hypothesis on the effect that
PEG might have in the formation of ZIF-8 in MQ at room temperature. We propose two possible
roles that PEG might be taking, considering our observations and the phenomena reported in the
literature under different linker, water content, and Zn2+ source [25, 26].
It has been reported in literature that the nucleation rate is critical in the crystal growth, especially
in low Zn2+:2-MIM molar ratio. A rapid nucleation phase will lead to a depletion in precursor
concentration, which in turn will hinder three-dimensional crystal growth, which might explain
the flake-like morphology observed for ZIF-8MQ. [26, 18]. In order for the coordination reaction be-

Fabrication and characterisation of Hb-loaded ZIF-8
The loading of biomacromolecules has been a sought after application of ZIF-8 in the past years.
Successful incorporation of different proteins has been reported in the literature [18, 17, 16], with
Hb being among them [18, 68, 69]. In preliminary research in our group, we also achieved encap-

as well as on the physicochemical properties of the Hb-loaded ZIF-8 particles prepared through
different synthesis methods.

Initial characterisation

the addition of Zn(NO3)2, maintaining its bright red colour. This change in opacity is a clear in-
dication of particle formation and was surprisingly instantaneous as opposed to the slow paced
change in opacity of ZIF-8MQ and PEG-ZIF-8. The formation of a pellet after centrifuging, which
was preserved during the full washing process, further supported the idea that particles had
formed. Thus, we explored the incorporation of different amounts of Hb. Moreover, given the
observed properties of PEG, we also investigated its effect on the NP assembly in the presence

Effect of Hb concentration
Figure 14a shows the average size of particles prepared with Hb
concentrations of 1, 10, 20 and 40 mg mL−1.
We observe that the average diameter roughly ranges between 150-200 nm, with considerable
batch to batch variation reflected by the large error bar in samples Hb1@ZIF-8 and Hb10@ZIF-8.
However, significant differences in size are not observed (p=0.1962) as Hb concentration increases.
This is a desirable particle size range in order to enhance circulation time, being large enough
to avoid clearance and small enough to minimize phagocytosis [71]. Figure 14b presents the ζ-
potential, for which we can see a notable shift from a positive (21.3 ± 2.4 mV) to a negative value
(-5.3 ± 0.5 mV) as soon as Hb is introduced in the synthesis process. This indicates a successful
loading of Hb, which has an isoelectric point pI∼6.8 and thus is negatively charged at the basic
pH conditions present during particle synthesis [13, 26]. As Hb concentration is increased, the
ζ-potential does not undergo significant changes (p=0.3533). In terms of yield (figure 14c), we
see that as soon as Hb is added to the reaction, the synthesis becomes more efficient, reaching
a yield of 52.7±1.5% for Hb20@ZIF-8 in just 10 min. However, with further addition of Hb, the
system becomes saturated and can no longer incorporate more Hb, showing a drop in the yield.
Indeed, sample Hb40@ZIF-8 showed a remarkably red supernatant, which is also reflected by
the lower EE as presented in figure 14d. Meanwhile, samples Hb1−20@ZIF-8 offer a similarly
high EE, with increasing Hb content as represented in purple in figure 14d. It is worth noting
that sample Hb1@ZIF-8 shows an average EE above 100%. This might be a result of inaccuracy
in the concentration determination. Furthermore, when we compare between yield and EE, we
observe that although almost all Hb is being incorporated, the yield remains below 55%. This
seems to reveal that part of the ZIF-8 precursors (i.e., Zn(NO3)2·6H2O and 2-MIM) might not to
be incorporated to the particles within the 10 min reaction. We expect the weight from the nitrate
and the water molecules to be excluded. However, this should not account for a 50% loss in yield.
Therefore, it could be that the reaction time does not allow for full crystal growth, or that Hb (in
particular the imidazole ring in the His residues) and 2-MIM are competing for the coordination
with Zn2+. Further discussion is presented in section (e).

Chemical structure: FTIR spectroscopy
The Hb-loaded NPs were characterised by FTIR spectroscopy in order to analyse the chemical
structure present in the particles. Figure 17 shows the results obtained for the Hb@ZIF-8 particles
prepared with increasing concentration of Hb, together with the spectra measured for free Hb and
ZIF-8MQ. As a protein, the spectrum of Hb is governed by the backbone vibrations of the peptide
bond, which corresponds to an amide group. The absorption bands that stem from the vibrational
modes of the amide group appear in different regions of the spectrum, and are commonly referred
to as amide A, B, I-VII. The principal bands are: amide I (1600-1800 cm−1), which mainly originates
in the C O stretching vibrations, with some contribution from C N stretching. It is the most
intense and depends strongly on the protein’s secondary structure; amide II (1470-1570 cm−1),
which mainly arises from the in-plane N H bending vibrational modes and from C N and C C
stretching vibrations to a lesser extent; amide III (1250-1350 cm−1), which is a highly complex band
affected by factors like the side chains and H-bonds of the protein; amide A (3300-3500 cm−1),
which is due to the N H stretching vibration [72, 73, 74]. Furthermore, Hb displays the band at
∼2950 cm−1 associated to aliphatic C H stretching, which is also present in ZIF-8’s spectrum
[75].

Crystalline structure post-encapsulation
In order to explore if these chemical changes were accompanied by changes in the crystal structure,
we analysed the different Hb-loaded ZIF-8 particle formulations by XRD. Figure 21a shows the
XRD patterns obtained for samples Hb@ZIF-8 prepared with increasing Hb concentration. Firstly,
upon the addition of 1 mg mL−1 Hb, the pattern displays peaks in the same positions as ZIF-8MQ
(main reflection at 2θ=10.9◦), which do not match the pattern of SOD-ZIF-8 as discussed in section
(c). We also notice the contribution of an amorphous phase in the form of a ’hump’, especially
pronounced at the lower angles. As we increase the concentration of Hb, this hump becomes
more prominent, while the peaks rapidly lose intensity. At a concentration of 20 mg mL−1, the
diffractogram does not present any reflections, which indicates that the samples are amorphous.
This is also reflected by the crystallinity quantification shown in figure 21b, which decreases with
the concentration of Hb and is practically zero at 20 mg mL−1 8. Therefore, these observations
suggest that Hb disrupts the structure in which Zn2+ and 2-MIM would otherwise arrange at the
Zn2+:2-MIM:H2O molar ratio of 1:4:5552. Consequently, higher Hb content in the particles seems
to result in a higher degree of amorphicity. Similar observations have been reported with other
proteins and encapsulation systems, where the carrier lost its crystallinity after the protein was
incorporated [77, 78].

Hypothesis on the role of Hb and PEG
As demonstrated in the previous sections, important differences are observed in the synthesis pro-
cess and in the products obtained when performing the reaction in the absence or presence of Hb.
One of the first observations was the accelerated change in sample opacity, indicating the forma-
tion of a suspension of particles, and the increase in yield. Thus, the presence of Hb is indubitably
playing a role in the particle formation. As pointed out in figure 18, Hb contains a considerable
number of His residues, many of them located at the surface of the molecule. His contains an
imidazole ring similar to that present in 2-methylimidazole, with which Zn2+ coordinate to form
ZIF-8. This indicates that an interaction between Zn2+ and superficial His might occur. The ap-
parently smaller particle size compared to the particles without Hb observed in the morphology
analysis, combined with the higher yield and faster reaction indicate that the presence of Hb might
be increasing the nucleation rate.
The interaction between Hb and the ZIF-8 precursors might be occurring via different mecha-
nisms. As suggested by Liang et al., the incorporation of biomolecules into ZIF-8 might take place
similarly to the growth mechanisms found in naturally occurring biomineralization, where pep-
tides tend to concentrate inorganic cations. In in this case, the authors suggest that intermolecular
affinity with 2-MIM’s imidazole ring by H-bonding and hydrophobic interactions also increases
the local concentration of 2-MIM, thus promoting nucleation [18]. On the other hand, Lyu et al.
suggest that the coordination between Zn2+ and the amide bonds in the protein might be the main
contributor to the successful encapsulation in ZIF-8 during crystal formation [42]. We propose that
there might be some direct interaction between Zn2+ and Hb’s superficial His, given the high EE
achieved and the disruption in the crystalline structure. This interaction might possibly lead to
a competition between Hb and 2-MIM, as high Hb concentrations result in Hb dominating the
interaction with Zn. In a preliminary experiment aimed at testing said hypothesis, we modified
the synthesis protocols of Hb40@ZIF-8 and PEG200-Hb40@ZIF-8 by omitting the addition of 2-MIM
(replacing it for MQ). Figure 26 shows the products of these reactions (repeated twice with the
same outcome). We observe clear signs that Hb and Zn have reacted. In the absence of PEG, we
observe the formation of a gel-like substance with an increased viscosity with respect to the aque-
ous solutions. This might be due to the formation of a network of Zn2+ and Hb without any kind
of nucleation process. Surprisingly however, in the presence of 200 mg mL−1 a pellet is formed
after centrifuging (figure 26, tube b). We speculate that PEG might be playing a role as a capping
agent, or perhaps again acting as a nucleation modulating agent as suggested in the hypothesis on
the role of PEG (section (e)), possibly promoting the deprotonation of the imidazole rings in Hb-
and perhaps leading to stronger interactions that cause the formation of clusters. We have nev-
ertheless not found a good enough explanation for these observations yet. Still, it is nonethless
worth noting that throughout the washing process, there was continuous loss of Hb due to parti-
cle destruction, indicated by the reddish appearance of the supernatant and the reduction in pellet
size (figure 26, tube c). This observation might indicate that the interaction between Zn2+ and Hb
is weaker than that with 2-MIM. Further characterisation of the products from this reaction might
be necessary to provide better understanding of this interaction.

3.3
Protein characterisation
One of the most essential factors that determine the potential success of a HBOC candidate is the
preservation of Hb’s functionality, namely it’s ability to transport oxygen. As with all proteins,
functionality depends strongly on the preservation of the secondary structure. Furthermore, for
Hb, the oxidation state of the Fe cation in the heme group is crucial, as Fe2+ has the ability to bind
O2, while Fe3+ present in metHb does not [13]. Therefore, the generation of metHb during the syn-
thesis method has to be avoided. Moreover, the binding process of O2 to Hb is cooperative. This
means that when the first O2 molecule binds to one of the four available Fe2+ sites, Hb undergoes
a conformational change that increases the binding affinity for the next three O2 molecules [6]. It
is therefore important to determine the oxygen dissociation curve9 of encapsulated Hb to assess
whether its O2 binding and releasing kinetics have been affected.
Due to the project’s time limitations, herein we focus on analysing Hb’s secondary structure and
O2-binding capacity after its encapsulation in ZIF-8 through different formulations developed (i.e.
Hbx@ZIF-8 and PEG200-Hbx@ZIF-8, respectively, with x = 1, 10, 20 and 40 mg mL−1). These
properties were then compared to free Hb for reference.
(a)
Protein folding: CD
The preservation of Hb’s secondary structure upon encapsulation was assessed by CD spectroscopy.
Freshly prepared10 NP suspensions and a free Hb solution were diluted to an approximate Hb con-
centration of 0.08 mg mL−1. CD spectra were then acquired at standard temperature and pressure
conditions. It is worth noting that samples Hb1@ZIF-8 and PEG200-Hb1@ZIF-8 prepared in 10 mL
were also measured (Hb1@ZIF-810mL and PEG200-Hb1@ZIF-810mL, respectively, shown in purple
in figure 27). These two samples were used in the functionality studies (section (b)), and their CD
spectra were acquired to be able to correlate the observed functionality with the protein folding.
The semi-raw11 CD spectra, shown in figure 27a, reveals a substantial difference between the
formulations prepared with low (1 mg mL−1) Hb concentration and the rest of samples, including
the free Hb reference. In contrast, Hb encapsulated in NPs prepared with medium (10 mg mL−1)
to very high (40 mg mL −1) Hb concentration present similarly shaped spectra with different
amplitudes. Nonetheless, these differences in amplitude could be a consequence of discrepancies
in the Hb concentration of the final measured dilutions. However, the radically different spectra
shape observed for the low Hb content NPs indicates that the folding of the protein has been
compromised. This is in agreement with the results obtained from the FTIR analysis presented
in figures 17 and 20, where we observed a slightly altered shape in the amide I and II bands.
Following the hypothesis presented in section (e), the His found in Hb might be highly integrated
in the ZIF-8 structure by coordination of the imidazole ring and Zn+2, especially in the absence of
PEG. It might follow that said interaction disturbs the folding of Hb.

Functionality studies
To perform an initial assessment of the HBOC functionality, the oxygen binding capacity of Hb
encapsulated through the different formulations was measured and compared to that of free Hb.
Suspensions of freshly prepared particles and the free Hb stock solution were diluted to concen-
trations of 4 and 6 mg mL−1. Due to the inherently low concentration of Hb1@ZIF-8 and PEG200-
Hb1@ZIF-8, we prepared these particles in 10 mL (Hb1@ZIF-810mL and PEG200-Hb1@ZIF-810mL,
respectively) and resuspended them in a final volume of 2 mL to increase the concentration. After
preparing the dilutions, their Hb content was double checked through absorbance at 280 nm and
any necessary corrections were applied to ensure that all concentrations were equivalent. This was
specially important to allow the reliable comparison between samples and with the reference, i.e.,
to ensure that any reduction in O2 release was due to a lower protein functionality and not due to a
lower Hb concentration. Subsequently, the release of O2 bound to Hb was forced by adding a 10%
K3[Fe(CN)6] solution, while monitoring the concentration of dissolved O2 in solution. Thus, the
release of all bound O2 could be quantified through the sudden increase in dissolved O2 detected.
Figure 29 shows the results from the functionality studies, where we can see that the oxygen
binding capacity is superior for the high Hb content particles. This is in agreement with what we
expected from the results of the protein folding analysis (section (a)). The samples prepared with 1
and 10 mg mL−1 Hb, both with and without PEG, show significantly different O2 binding capacity
compared to free Hb. In particular, for Hb1@ZIF-810mL and PEG200-Hb1@ZIF-810mL, the concentra-
tion of bound O2 relative to that of free Hb is ∼63% and ∼74%13, respectively. Given the notable
level of misfolding noticed in figure 27, this could indicate that the binding site is not gravely af-
fected. However, characterisation of the binding kinetics might reveal other consequences of this
misfolding. Moreover, the results of the experiment do not reveal a statistically significant differ-
ence between the formulations with and without PEG, indicating that the polymer is not hindering
the binding of O2. Lastly, samples with high and very high content, both with and without PEG,
show no statistically significant differences with free Hb in terms of amount of bound O2. All
in all, we can conclude that Hb encapsulated in Hb20,40@ZIF-8 and PEG200-Hb20,40@ZIF-8 shows
promising retention of its functionality, further revealing their potential as HBOCs. However, once
again, this does not imply that the binding kinetics are unaffected, which should be evaluated in
further experiments.

Conclusion
This study presents a novel ZIF-8-based HBOC which can be obtained in a rapid and highly effi-
cient synthesis process in aqueous solution, with remarkable potential for upscalibility. Further-
more, we report the synthesis of ZIF-8 in aqueous solution under mild reaction conditions and
at a low 2-MIM:Zn+2 ratio. The incorporation of PEG allowed the production of homogeneous
crystalline SOD-ZIF-8 NPs through a simple one-pot synthesis method. Conversely, this protocol
fails to produce such a crystalline structure in the absence of PEG. To the best of our knowledge,
this PEG-induced crystallinity change has not been reported previously.
Through comprehensive characterisation of the different particle formulations, it was observed
that PEG can not only improve sample crystallinity and homogeneity, but also promote the encap-
sulation of higher concentrations of Hb. Nonetheless, our results indicate that there is a trade-off
between increased crystal structure and high Hb content, which in turn has been linked to the
54
==PAGE:58==
Technical University of Denmark
preservation of Hb’s secondary structure and functionality. Thus, we found that when adding a
higher concentration of Hb, the protein preserves its O2-carrying ability and the NPs are amor-
phous, while at low Hb concentration in the presence of PEG, EE and functionality are reduced
albeit preserving their crystalline structure. We have therefore set up some guidelines on how to
approach the HBOC synthesis depending on the desired properties. For example if one desires to
employ a ZIF-8 network for co-encapsulation of other active compounds, one should reduce the
Hb concentration. Conversely, if maximal O2 carrying capacity is prioritized, then it is best to opt
for a high concentration of Hb. Other variables that could not be explored in this project, such as
O2 binding kinetics or protein stability, should be assessed to further determine the pros and cons
of the different strategies. Ultimately, it is plausible that we could produce HBOCs with specific
properties of interest in the context of artificial RBC development.
In addition to this, we have showcased the difference between the use of PEG and PVP in our
protocol, demonstrating the superior properties that PEG grants in terms of EE and preservation
of SOD-like crystal structure. Furthermore, based on the different observations collected in this
work, we have proposed some initial hypotheses on the roles possibly played by Hb and PEG
within the ZIF-8 system. We suggest that Hb might compete with 2-MIM due to the imidazole
ring present in the His residues of this protein. Furthermore, we speculate that PEG might mod-
ulate the crystallization process by slowing down the first nucleation stages and thus promoting
crystal growth.
Pending analyses that would bring further value to these studies include the examination of par-
ticle size from the SEM images, combined with crystallite size analysis based on the peak broad-
ening in the XRD patterns. Furthermore, the determination of the porosity and surface area by
nitrogen adsorption and BET analysis is also of interest. This could bring further insight on the
distribution of PEG and Hb within the sample. Moreover, it would be interesting to study the
preservation of Hb@ZIF-8 and PEG-Hb@ZIF-8’s functionality over time under different storage
conditions (e.g. refrigeration, room temperature and 37 ◦C) and compare it to the degradation
time of Hb in its free form. In terms of future perspectives, work can be directed towards devel-
oping surface coatings for these NPs in order to enhance their stability in buffer solutions, which
currently is one of the main challenges to conduct in vitro and in vivo preclinical tests with ZIF-
8-based HBOCs [79]. Finally, the SOD crystalline structure herein reported could be exploited to
develop a multifunctional system with potential as an artificial RBC through the co-encapsulation
of Hb and other functional entities (e.g., nanozymes that simulate the antioxidant system in RBCs).
All in all, we believe that the fundamental findings described in this work can provide a promising
start-point for the understanding and development of highly functional ZIF-8-based HBOCs.
