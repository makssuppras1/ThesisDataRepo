Abstract
Magnetic levitation is a fascinating phenomenon with profound technological implications
and has been the subject of our investigation. Using well­designed experiments and sim­
ulations, we explore levitation dynamics through theoretical principles based on the mag­
netic bound state (MBS). The frequency dependence, off­center displacements, and the
vertically oriented magnet were systematically investigated to reveal nuanced interactions
in the levitation system. Simulation analyses generally agreed with experimental uncer­
tainties during the second experiment and suggested an optimum displacement of around
10 mm for long levitation durations. Implications of our findings extend to transformative
applications in transportation, material handling, sensors, and actuators. The conclusion
synthesizes experimental, simulated, and theoretical insights and maps a course for future
investigations. Magnetic levitation now appears as a scientific curiosity and an exciting
field for innovation and exploration with enormous potential in transportation, manufactur­
ing, and beyond.

1
Introduction
Magnetic levitation has always been a challenge to static levitation with just permanent
magnets [1], going back to Samuel Earnshaw’s principles in 1842. Earnshaw’s theo­
rem suggested that no permanent magnet could levitate another without external force or
diamagnetic materials. But recent advances challenge this long­held view and reveal a
surprising truth: A fast­rotating magnet may levitate a permanent magnet.
This novel magnetic levitation approach utilizes the dynamic interaction of magnetic fields
because one magnet rotates rapidly near another [2]. It differs from the usual static setups,
and although promising, scientific publications on this topic are currently low; Only two
have emerged so far [2][3].
This master’s thesis project will systematically investigate fast­rotating magnet­induced
magnetic levitation. Using a setup to levitate another magnet with a high­speed rotating
magnet, the study explores new phenomena and critical parameters that affect levitation,
such as the size and position of the rotating magnet. Refer to figure 1.1 for a visualization
of the phenomenon.
This project is motivated by the experiment and data analysis of fast­rotating magnet­
induced magnetic levitation. Exploring the factors affecting this unconventional levitation
technique will hopefully challenge assumed values and reveal new perspectives that might
transform the understanding of magnetic levitation and its applications.
The lack of scientific literature on this topic calls for a systematic investigation to fill knowl­
edge gaps and explore applications of this intriguing phenomenon. In this context, the
research poses a simple question:
• How does the size and position of the rotating magnet specifically influence magnetic
levitation?
Answering this question provides new technologies for many different applications in mag­
netic interactions.

Theory
2.1
Earnshaw’s Theorem
Earnshaw’s theorem is a result of classical electromagnetism [1]. It states that it is im­
possible to achieve stable levitation using solely static electric charges. This was proven
for electrostatic fields initially but also applies to magnetic fields, as will be shown subse­
quently. Stable levitation is explained by having an object suspended against gravity in a
controlled environment. The theorem originates from Gauss’ law, which tells us that the
divergence of any possible electric force field is zero in free space, ∇· F = 0. This can
be explained using the idea that the force (F(r)) is derived from the potential (U(r)) and
follows the Laplace equation:
∇· F(r) = ∇· (−∇U(r)) = −∇2U(r) = 0
(2.1)
Earnshaw’s theorem applies to a magnetic levitation system since both the magnetic and
gravitational forces can be written in this mathematical form. This even involves flexible
or conducting bodies as long as they are not diamagnetic. In simpler terms, if ∇·F = 0, it
means there are no ”points” in space where the force is originating or converging, and this
absence of a source implies that there is no force available to levitate an object against
gravity.
2.1.1
Magnetic Forces
The expression of magnetic forces in the form of ∇·F = 0 is a consequence of the mathe­
matical properties of magnetic fields and their relationship to the magnetic potential. The
critical idea is that magnetic monopoles, as sinks or sources of magnetic fields, haven’t
been observed in nature. Unlike electric fields, where electric charges (monopoles) can
be sources or sinks, magnetic fields are permanently closed loops with no isolated mag­
netic charge.
In classical electromagnetism, the magnetic field (B) is related to the magnetic vector
potential (A) through the equation:
B = ∇× A
(2.2)
Taking the divergence of both sides:
∇· B = ∇· (∇× A)
(2.3)
Utilizing the vector calculus identity ∇· (∇× V) = 0 for any vector field V, the expression
simplifies:
∇· B = 0
(2.4)
So, without explicitly introducing a scalar potential as in (2.1), we have shown that the
divergence of the magnetic field (B) is zero, consistent with Gauss’s law for magnetism.
This result indicates the absence of magnetic monopoles and the closed­loop nature of
magnetic field lines and is compatible with Earnshaw’s theorem.

2.1.2
Gravitational Forces
The expression of gravitational fields in the form of ∇· F = 0 is a consequence of the
mathematical properties of gravitational fields and their relationship to the gravitational
potential.
In classical physics, the gravitational field (g) is related to the gravitational potential (Φ)
through the equation:
g = −∇Φ
(2.5)
This relationship implies that the gravitational field is conservative, meaning it can be
derived from a scalar potential (Φ). Taking the divergence of the gravitational field:
∇· g = −∇2Φ
(2.6)
In general relativity, the gravitational field is described by the metric tensor and the cur­
vature of spacetime. The Poisson equation, which governs the gravitational potential, is
given by:
∇2Φ = 4πGρ
(2.7)
where G is the gravitational constant and ρ is the mass density. In free space where mass
density is zero, this equation simplifies to ∇2Φ = 0, indicating that the gravitational field
divergence is zero.
This result aligns with the understanding that gravitational fields are conservative, and in
the absence of mass density, there are no ”sources” or ”sinks” of the gravitational field in
free space. The Poisson equation further solidifies this understanding, showing that the
gravitational potential is zero in regions without mass, supporting Earnshaw’s theorem
and providing insights into the nature of gravitational interactions.
2.1.3
Exceptions
Although Earnshaw’s theorem states that stable levitation is impossible with static electric
or magnetic fields, some inventive methods and phenomena circumvent this classical re­
striction. Here, we discuss exceptions to Earnshaw’s theorem and show how levitation is
possible by unconventional means. These exceptions include quantum effects at micro­
scopic scales and engineering feats like electromagnetic suspension and diamagnetism.
Each exception tests the classical bounds of Earnshaw’s theorem and provides unique
insights into the interactions of forces, fields, and materials.
Quantum Effects
Whereas Earnshaw’s theorem applies in classical physics, quantum mechanics provides
a more nuanced perspective. In quantum mechanics, all objects are technically levitated
a microscopic distance above their resting surface. This is due to the electromagnetic in­
termolecular forces between the molecules [4]. Although this is true, the small distances
are not considered levitation in classical physics, and Earnshaw’s theorem is based only
on classical physics. Quantum­induced levitation effects occur at molecular distances
where traditional macroscopic levitation is inconvenient. Earnshaw’s theorem, formulated
in classical physics, does not take phenomena on such small scales. These intrinsically
small distances distinguish quantum levitation from the macroscopic scenarios supported
by Earnshaw’s theorem and demand a more profound understanding from both the clas­
sical and quantum perspectives.

Electromagnetic Suspension
If you have a system that can detect the position of a levitated body and use this to con­
trol the strength of an electromagnet, it is easy to levitate the body. Such a system must
strengthen the electromagnet when the object moves away and weaken the electromag­
net when the object moves close. This can even be done with permanent magnets that
are physically moved around. This method is called electromagnetic suspension and is
typically used in maglev trains or other levitating objects [5]. Expectedly, this also cir­
cumvents Earnshaw’s theorem because the electric charges, or in this case, magnetic
dipoles, are not static.
Diamagnetism
Superconductors or other diamagnetic materials that magnetize in the opposite direction
of the applied field can also levitate. It has become more common to see high­temperature
superconductors levitated this way.
Superconductors are perfectly diamagnetic when
cooled below their critical temperature, meaning they undergo the Meißner–Ochsenfeld
effect [6]. Other diamagnetic materials can also be levitated if the field is strong enough
(1­20 Tesla). Big water droplets or small bodies like frogs have been levitated this way [7].
Earnshaw’s theorem does not apply to diamagnets since they behave like ”anti­magnets.”
They align anti­parallel to that of the field. In contrast, the magnetic moments in the lev­
itating body following Earnshaw’s theorem will always try to align parallel to the field as
paramagnets and ferromagnets do.
Electromagnetic Induction
An oscillating magnetic field will induce an alternating current in a conductor and thus
generate a levitating force. This is due to Faraday’s law of electromagnetic induction.
The oscillating field will make a conducting material create an opposing magnetic field
due to the induced current, so it levitates [8]. This makes a conducting material behave
the same way as a superconductor, circumventing Earnshaw’s theorem.
Rotation
One of the few ways to levitate a magnet using only permanent magnets is by using rota­
tion, more precisely, spin­stabilized magnetic levitation. The ’Levitron’ is a magnetic toy
that uses this concept [9]. The toy developed by Roy Harrison in 1983 is a spinning mag­
netic top that can levitate on top of a fixed permanent magnet using gyroscopic effects
(see Figure 2.1). However, this only works when the rate of precession and spatial com­
ponent are within their respective stable regime, which is relatively narrow. This effect is
close to the one investigated in this paper but, as expected, disagrees with Earnshaw’s
theorem since the levitating magnet is not static.
2.1.4
Conclusion
As expected, the almost 200­year­old theorem by Samuel Earnshaw is still relevant and
accurate today. Despite these difficulties, the continued interest in magnets and their
levitating properties is unwavering for the foreseeable future.

2.2.1
Previous Work
There are currently only two papers on this topic, and they share agreements, disagree­
ments, and uncertainties on the theory. Although this thesis concentrates on experimental
investigation, some theoretical uncertainties remain. However, the following briefly sum­
marizes existing theories.
Hamdi Ucar introduces two terms in his paper [3] to characterize the phenomenon’s be­
havior: Polarity­free magnetic repulsion (PFR) and magnetic bound state (MBS). PFR
occurs due to dynamic magnetic interactions, which produce a net force on a body due to
its interaction with a cyclic magnetic field with a gradient. The generated force is stated
to be in the direction of the weak field. In other words, the body is pushed away from the
stronger part of the magnetic field and towards the weaker part.
The PFR effect is reported to exist even if the body has a specific magnetic orientation or if
there is an angle between the magnetic field vector and its gradient vector. This indicates
that neither the body’s magnetic orientation nor the orientation of the magnetic field about
its gradient influences the occurrence of PFR. If the cyclic magnetic field belongs to a
dipole, the body is repelled by this dipole.
Hamdi Ucar reported that the repulsive force decreases rapidly and is inversely propor­
tional to the seventh to eighth power of the distance between the interacting dipoles.
MBS is a particular case of trapping a body in a stable equilibrium, achieved through cyclic
magnetic interaction between two entities. A cyclic magnetic field and a free body are
required for MBS to occur, with the cyclic field typically obtained by rotating a magnet. In
this scenario, at least one of the magnetic moments of the entities must be dipolar, while
the other can be quadrupolar. MBS distinguishes itself from specific classical binding
scenarios by not requiring the interacting magnetic field to have a local minimum.

3
Experiments
This chapter covers the main point of this thesis: The experimental investigation. The
general idea behind the experiments will be covered, and a short introduction to the ex­
perimental setup will be given. The system is sensitive, and data variations are expected
due to inherent uncertainties. For example, changes in the initial position of the floating
magnet, small changes in motor speed, or even changes in the environment may cause
the variability seen. We will analyze these variations and gain meaningful insights into
the levitation phenomenon. This is expected as a consequence of uncertainty in the ini­
tial conditions of the setup. Significant variations will be briefly covered in explanations.
3.1
Experimental Setup
The experimental setup consists of a high­speed spindle motor (Vevor JST­JGF­F65A)
that can rotate up to 400 Hz. Fastened to the motor is a magnet in a 3D­printed plastic
case. The rotating magnet varies in placement, amount, and orientation but always con­
sists of at least one 10 mm cubic nickel­plated neodymium magnet. Further explanation
will follow in the relevant chapters. Beneath the rotating magnet, another magnet will be
placed. This is the central part of the system, the so­called floating magnet. The float­
ing magnet used in this project is a 10 mm spherical chrome­plated neodymium magnet,
which is the same throughout all experiments. As we explored in the theoretical section
(2.2.1), the Magnetic Bound State (MBS) plays a crucial role in our experiments. In MBS,
the interaction between the rotating and floating magnets results in a stable equilibrium,
preventing the floating magnet from falling under certain conditions. When starting the
experiment, an aluminum plate is placed underneath to ensure stability and make the ini­
tial conditions as close to each other as possible. A GoPro camera is placed beside the
experimental setup to document the experiments in detail. See figure 3.1 for a portrayal of
the practical arrangement. The experimental setup aims to make environmental variables
such as temperature and humidity irrelevant during the experiment period.
3.1.1
Procedure
With this in mind, the procedure is as follows:
1. Select a specific 3D­printed case from the set of configurations, each designed with
unique magnet orientations and placements, and attach it to the motor. For instance,
consider a setup where the rotating magnet has a vertical alignment or explore con­
figurations with magnets strategically displaced from the center for varying effects.
2. Turn on the motor and set it to the desired frequency.
3. Place the aluminum plate at a fixed distance beneath the spinning magnet.
4. Start filming with the GoPro.
5. Place the floating magnet between the rotating magnet and the aluminum plate. You
can physically sense when the magnet enters the Magnetic Bound State (MBS).
6. Carefully remove the aluminum plate from the system and avoid creating perturba­
tions as best as possible.
7. Wait for the magnet to fall or escape the MBS in other ways.
8. Stop filming.

magnet could levitate better.

as in the first experiment, tiny metal plates were added next to the magnet for weight sta­

3.5
Fourth Experiment: Vertical Magnet
The fourth experiment examined how adding a vertically oriented magnet over the central
rotating magnet would affect the system. The rotating magnet at the center, or 0 dis­
placement, had another magnet added on top, pointing upward. Note that the spinning
magnet was oriented horizontally while spinning. This vertical magnet was theorized to
help levitation by adding a magnetic field opposing gravity. The vertical magnet was then
moved upwards in stages 0, 2, 4, 6, 8, and 10 mm.
The experimental results showed exciting dynamics in the levitation phenomenon with
added vertical magnets. In contrast to the second experiment, where there was no ap­
parent optimal displacement for maximum levitation time, the fourth experiment showed
sharper frequency versus time peaks. The peaks in the second experiment were broader
and more even. See figure 3.13 for the results and figure 3.14 for the standard error.
Additionally, the levitation times varied widely for the same frequency and displacement,
introducing significant standard errors, especially for specific frequencies. This variability
indicates a sensitive response to the rotating versus added vertical magnets.
One notable observation was at 4 mm vertical displacement and 73 Hz, where levitation
continued for over 24 hours before manual intervention to stop the experiment. This result
implies the possibility of extended levitation duration almost to ”infinite.”

The experimental setup comprised a high­speed spindle motor with a 3D­printed plastic
case containing a rotating magnet and a spherical chrome­plated neodymium magnet
called a floating magnet. The system sensitivity to uncertainties such as initial magnet
position change, motor speed, and environmental condition was acknowledged. Strict
experimental procedures were applied to eliminate these variations, including a plate of
aluminum for stability and a GoPro camera for documentation.
3.6.2
First Experiment
The first experiment tested the validity of the levitation phenomenon under different rotat­
ing magnet frequencies. The displaced rotating magnet showed variable levitation times
at five fixed frequencies. Results confirmed the frequency dependence hypothesis and
showed a direct correlation between rotation frequency and levitation times. This valida­
tion provided the basis for subsequent investigations.

Second Experiment
In the second experiment, off­center displacements of the rotating magnet were inves­

Although uncertainties remain at larger displacement scales, the results shed light on the
complex interaction of magnetic field adjustments, frequency, and levitation duration.
3.6.4
Third Experiment
The third experiment investigated the effects of different heights between the rotating mag­
net and the aluminum plate. Systematic change of height difference revealed non­linear
dependency resembling an inverse function curve. The relationship of height difference
with levitation time implies an optimization potential to realize longer durations. This un­
expected curve adds complexity to the system and requires exploration.
3.6.5
Fourth Experiment
The fourth experiment placed a vertically oriented magnet over the central rotating mag­
net. Unlike the second experiment, this configuration showed sharper frequency versus
time peaks. Levitation times were highly variable, including an observed extended levita­
tion duration at 4 mm vertical displacement and 73 Hz. This unexpected result indicates
the possibility of long levitation, which shows the system’s sensitivity to the orientation of
the rotating and added vertical magnets.

Discussion
In discussing our magnetic levitation experiments, we must also acknowledge the com­
plexity of the observed phenomenon. This experimental adventure has revealed the dy­
namics of levitation and prompted us to investigate the underlying principles governing
this extraordinary phenomenon.
From frequency dependence to off­center displacements and the vertically oriented mag­
net, each experiment contributed significantly to understanding magnetic levitation dy­
namics.
With this holistic view, we now proceed to simulations to complement our experimental
results and decipher the role of the magnetic field in the levitation interplay. Hopefully,
This journey will refine our understanding and illuminate possible applications and future
research directions.
4.1
Simulation
A simulation further understood the second experiment, that is, the analysis of the z­
component of the magnetic field regarding different displacements of the rotating magnet.
The simulation peaked at about 10 mm displacement, an appropriate value for augmented
interaction between the rotating and floating magnets. Refer to figure 4.1 for a graphical
view. The displacement is on the horizontal axis, and the magnetic fields’ z­component
is on the vertical axis. The dots represent a dipole, and the diamonds represent a cubic
magnet.

These simulation results match the experimental uncertainties observed at larger dis­
placements in the second experiment. Converging simulation and experimental results
point to a sweet spot at around 10 mm where the magnets interact maximally and thus
may achieve longer levitation durations.
Future experiments could explore displacement values around the chosen optimal point
from these combined insights. This iterative approach attempts to unravel additional as­
pects of the levitation phenomenon and potentially apply it to magnetic levitation research.
4.2
Implications of Findings
The experimental and simulated results reveal many aspects of the magnetic levitation
dynamic. The frequency dependence observed in the first experiment provides a ba­
sic understanding that the rotational speed of the magnet affects levitation times signif­
icantly. The second experiment involving off­center displacements indicates an optimal
point around 10 mm where maximum interaction between the rotating and floating mag­
nets occurs.
The third experiment’s inquiry into height differences introduces a non­linear dependency.
This finding suggests possibilities for optimization, possibly resulting in longer levitation
durations. The fourth experiment using a vertically oriented magnet shows various un­
expected responses, including extended levitation. This sensitivity to magnet orientation
emphasizes the nuanced nature of levitation.
4.3
Applications
Understanding magnetic levitation’s driving factors has broad applications in many tech­
nologies. An immediate application is transportation, especially in developing more effi­
cient and stable maglev (magnetic levitation) systems. Maglev trains, for instance, could
use optimized levitation configurations for speed and energy efficiency.
Beyond transportation, magnetic levitation may be used for material handling, where con­
tactless manipulation of objects in manufacturing processes may reduce wear and tear.
Besides, the sensitivity of the levitation system to different configurations suggests appli­
cations in sensors and actuators where precise levitation control can be utilized for various
purposes.
4.4
Future Research Directions
The experimental journey into magnetic levitation opens up new research possibilities.
Some directions might be explored:
1. Optimization Studies: Further experiments to systematically explore displacement
values around the identified optimal point (e.g., 10 mm) to confirm the trends.
2. Advanced Simulation Models: Create more advanced simulation models that take
additional parameters like magnetic field gradients, damping effects, and material
properties into account. This might help in understanding the system.
3. Extensive Levitation Studies: Investigate the conditions that cause extended levita­
tion (see fourth experiment). Understanding the factors driving prolonged levitation
may allow applications requiring sustained levitation.
4. Control Mechanisms: Explore control strategies like feedback and automation to
keep stable levitation in various conditions. For practical applications where envi­
ronmental factors change, this is important.

Conclusion
We have been exploring magnetic levitation to discover its mysteries. Our exploration
provided a complete picture of levitation dynamics, from the theoretical underpinnings to
a series of carefully designed experiments and insightful simulations.
The introduction introduced the role of magnetic levitation in technology advancements
and hinted at its future complexity. The theoretical underpinning of our experiments was
the Magnetic Bound State (MBS). With this framework, we started experiments with dif­
ferent aspects of the levitation dynamic.
The first experiment, which dealt with frequency dependence, showed that the rotational
speed of the magnet is fundamentally related to levitation times. This discovery set the
foundation for later experiments. Off­center displacements, height differences, and the
vertically oriented magnet exposed other details of the levitation system ­ each experiment
added another layer to the magnetic levitation dynamic web.
The simulation provided complementary insights to and extensions of our experimental
observations. The convergence of simulation and experimentation showed a critical point
at about 10 mm displacement where interaction between magnets reaches an optimal
state. This knowledge fuels future experiments and promises longer levitation durations.
The implications of our findings resonate across various applications. From enabling
more efficient maglev systems in transportation to optimizing material handling processes
through contactless manipulation, magnetic levitation has endless possibilities in multiple
applications. The sensitivity to different system configurations also provides opportunities
in sensors and actuators for precision and control in various technological domains.
We conclude this chapter on magnetic levitation exploration and outline a direction for
future investigations. Optimization studies around the identified sweet spot, advanced
simulation models, and long levitation studies are part of the roadmap. Control mecha­
nisms, material explorations, and quantitative analyses require further inquiry and a better
understanding of the underpinning physics.
In summary, our combined effort in experimentation, simulation, and discussion lays a
strong foundation for the future of magnetic levitation research. The present results con­
stitute a significant step toward elucidating the levitation dynamics complex with implica­
tions far beyond our understanding. Magnetic levitation emerges not just as a scientific
curiosity but also as an exciting field for innovation and exploration with tremendous po­
tential for transport, production, and beyond.

Project Plan
1
Background
Static magnetic levitation is not physically possible, as was proved by Samuel Earn-
shaw showed in 1842, i.e. a configuration of permanent magnets cannot levitate another
permanent magnet. Magnetic levitation is only possible if there is either a control system to
continuously adjust the position of the levitating magnet by controlling the magnetic field,
or if a diamagnetic material is used.
However, it has very recently been shown that it is possible to levitate a permanent
magnet using another fast-rotating magnet. In this novel magnetic levitation technique, a
magnet can be levitated by spinning another magnet near it at a high speed, several thou-
sand RPMs. However, this knowledge is still very new, with only two scientific publications
on the topic.
The idea in this project is to explore the phenomena from an experimental approach.
We have a setup with high-speed rotating magnets that can levitate a magnet. This master
project will explore how the size and position of the rotating magnet influence the levitation
phenomena as well as exploring the influence of the shape of the levitated magnet etc..
2
Objectives
• Have an experimental setup with reliable results.
• Continuously develop the experimental setup.
• Investigate the frequency dependence on levitating a magnet.
• Displace the rotating magnet from center and observe the results.
• Add a vertically orientated magnet to the rotating magnet to increase the vertical field.
• Induce eddy current by replacing parts of the setup with aluminium.
• Replace the rotating magnet with a quadrupole to reduce the length of the magnetic
field lines.
• Angle the rotating magnet to increase the vertical field.
• Characterize the forces in each setup.
• Simulate the experiments using COMSOL.
