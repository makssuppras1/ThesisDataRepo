Abstract
A rate based CO2 capture column model for piperazine promoted potas-
sium carbonate solvents has been successfully developed, tested and anal-
ysed. The underlying model is previously developed at DTU - Department
of Chemical and Biochemical Engineering. The model parameters are esti-
mated through correlations based on available literature. This concerns the
following physical-chemical properties of the system: density, viscosity, sur-
face tension, diﬀusion coeﬃcients and kinetic reaction rate constants. The
model is implemented and validated against experimental pilot data avail-
able in literature. The validation plots show scattered, moderate deviations
but no systematic oﬀset. Uncertainty analysis of the correlated parameters
shows that the calculations are not very sensitive to the estimated viscos-
ity and surface tension. However, the calculations are largely eﬀected by the
ionic strength exponential factor, and the value of this contributes with much
uncertainty.
Sensitivity analysis is performed in order to analyse how the system is
aﬀected by changes in various operational conditions. The absorber and des-
orber are analysed separately. The analysis involves L/G ratio, temperature,
lean and rich loadings, column height, reboiler duty and diﬀerent solvent
compositions. Overall, the results of the sensitivity analyses conﬁrms the
expected trends. Among the simulated solvents, the 6.4m K+/1.6m PZ sol-
vent shows the lowest absorption rates. This solvent also shows the highest
speciﬁc reboiler duty.

Purpose
Reducing the emission of CO2 from fossil fuel ﬁred power plants is one of
the major challenges of this century. Post-combustion CO2 capture can be
considered to be an easy method to implement since it can be applied to
existing power production facilities without any changes to the core of the
power producing facility. Solvent based CO2 absorption has been investi-
gated for many years. However, it is a challenge to ﬁnd a solvent with a
fast absorption rate, a high capacity and a low energy requirement that can
signiﬁcantly and eﬃciently reduce CO2 outlet from full scale power produc-
tion. Aqueous mixtures of K2CO3 and piperazine has the potential to fulﬁl
these requirements of satisfactory and sustainable operation. However, there
is a risk of precipitation in the K2CO3/piperazine system that could possibly
lead to equipment failure and reduced eﬃciency. Thus, an accurate model
of the absorption and desorption process taking precipitation into account is
desired.
The aim of this project is to develop and implement a rigorous rate-
based model which accurately describes the behaviour of the system for CO2
absorption/desorption. In order for the model to be able to describe the
behavior of the system performing CO2 absorption and desorption, correla-
tions for various physical and chemical properties such as density, viscosity,
surface tension, diﬀusivity and kinetics are to be created and implemented.
A thermodynamic model developed at the DTU Department of Chemical
Engineering is used for equilibrium calculations. To prove the validity of
the model, experimental pilot plant data from literature should be found
and used as comparison. Sensitivity analysis simulations of the absorber and
desorber at varying operation conditions and conﬁgurations are to be con-
ducted. Finally, the obtained results are discussed and compared to relevant
experimental and simulation studies.

Background
In recent years, more and more eﬀort has been put into reducing the ever
increasing carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions from the burning of fossil fuels.
Many scientists agree that the high CO2 concentration in the atmosphere
contributes to the increasing global temperature. In addition, it is predicted
that the global temperature will continue to increase unless the rate of CO2
emission is signiﬁcantly reduced. However, it is well known from the public
debate that the resulting temperature increment as well as the severity of
the related side eﬀects is a hot topic for discussion.
There are mainly three diﬀerent techniques for removal of CO2 result-
ing from power production: Oxy-fuel combustion, pre-combustion and post-
combustion. Oxy-fuel-combustion is removing of CO2 from ﬂue gasses re-
sulting from combustion of fuels with pure oxygen as combustion air. Pre-
combustion involves gasiﬁcation of the fuel and creating a synthetic gas con-
sisting of H2 and CO2. The gasses are then separated before combustion
and the hydrogen is used as fuel. The concept of post-combustion CO2 cap-
ture covers the removal of CO2 from a ﬂue gas resulting from conventional
combustion. Both pre-combustion and oxy-fuel-combustion have the advan-
tage that the separation of CO2 is favored by a high partial pressure of CO2
compared to post-combustion ﬂue gasses. Post-combustion is, however, eas-
ier to implement since the equipment can be directly added (retroﬁtted) to
existing power production facilities. In principle, post-combustion capture
techniques could be integrated with other sources of CO2 emission, e.g. pri-
vate cars or furnaces. Since power plants are one of the main contributors of
carbon dioxide emission to the atmosphere, it is one of the structures where
the highest potential of capturing a large amount of CO2 with relatively few,
large carbon capture units are.
Solvent-based CO2 absorption has been applied to various industrial ap-
plications for several years. Diﬀerent substances and blends have been in-
vestigated and also mixtures of piperazine (PZ) and potassium carbonate
(K2CO3) have been in the spotlight. Potassium carbonate is expected to
have a low heat of absorption resulting in low regeneration energy require-
ments, whereas piperazine is expected to contribute to fast reaction rates
and high capacity (Cullinane and Rochelle, 2004).

4.1
The developed model
A steady state, rate based model for CO2 absorption and desorption using
monoethanolamine (MEA) and piperazine was developed at the DTU De-
partment of Chemical and Biochemical Engineering. It is closest related to
the type (4) model of Figure 2. It was originally proposed by Gabrielsen
et al. (2006) and Gabrielsen (2007). It has since been used and explained by
Faramarzi (2010) and Sønderby et al. (2013).
The model consist of a system of 8 coupled diﬀerential equations which
together describe the mass and energy balances. The system of equations
are valid under the following assumptions (Gabrielsen, 2007):
1. The reaction is fast enough to take place in the liquid ﬁlm, and the
bulk of the liquid is in equilibrium.
2. Liquid side heat transfer resistance i small compared to the gas phase,
thus the liquid interface temperature is the same as the liquid bulk
temperature.

3. The liquid side mass transfer resistance for the volatile solvent i negli-
gible.
4. The interfacial area is the same for heat and mass transfer.
5. Axial dispersion is not accounted for.
6. The absorption column is considered adiabatic.
7. Both the liquid phase and the gas phase are formally treated as ideal
mixtures.

Properties of K+/PZ mixtures
The important physical-chemical properties of the K+/PZ/H2O system is
estimated, since reasonable values of these properties at various conditions
will be crucial to the model performance. The estimated properties are den-
sity, viscosity, surface tension, diﬀusivity of CO2 and rate constants of the
reactions with CO2. Furthermore the diﬀusivity of piperazine is estimated.
Ideally, the correlations should be based on all available experimental data of
the solution or self made experimental measurements. But the data sources
are very scarce and this project does not leave time or resources to produce
experimental data in laboratory. In fact, experimental data only existed for
density and viscosity. Therefore some correlations are based on other related
systems, providing only a ”best guess” for K+/PZ mixtures. As a result,
these correlations cannot be validated against data on properties of the mix-
ture. A table showing the properties, data sources and estimation methods
is provided, see Table 3.
Experimental data should ideally be covering relevant temperatures and
concentrations with a reasonable number of data points. It means that the

inﬂuence of variations in both temperature, concentration of K+ and con-
centration of PZ should be possible to detect. However, this high number
of variables often leads to a high number of experimental measurements to
cover all variable ranges properly. The number of measurements at a ﬁxed
set of variables is thus often limited. Moreover, the many variables lead to
more complex correlation expressions, as well as more possible interaction
eﬀects can occur. Also the applied temperature range is often limited, since
both experimental data and correlations from literature are often based on
experiments at room temperature or moderately elevated temperatures. As
a result the correlations might be rather inaccurate at ”extreme” conditions
(high temperatures, concentrations etc.), and there is no simple way to check
whether it is actually the case. That is even though the model shows an ex-
cellent ﬁt at experimentally covered conditions. It is therefore important to
study (e.g. graphically), the behavior of the model and assess whether its
behavior is physically reasonable and expected at these conditions. If not,
another type of expression should be considered or eventual errors in data
and models should be uncovered.

6.3

case with absorption processes where a high surface area is enhancing the
mass transfer between liquid and gas phases.

7
Implementation
The physical properties correlations made in the previous section is imple-
mented in the DTU CAPCO2 software. The model has previously been used
for simulating solvent solutions of piperazine alone and MEA. Therefore the
model code has to be modiﬁed in order to deal with the an extra compound,
K2CO3. Since the kinetic rate constants are depending on the ionic strength
of the solution it also has to be set up to calculate the sum of all ions in the
solution. The model can be utilized from developed user interfaces in both
Excel and Matlab. There is also a possibility to implement the model in the
process simulation tool Aspen Plus.
As input to the simulations, a number of data variables are required.
These are explained in Table 7. Furthermore, the type of packing needs to be
speciﬁed by characteristic parameters like speciﬁc surface area, corrugation
angle, void fraction and surface enhancement factor used for mass transfer
correlations. For absorption the inlet gas stream is the ﬂue gas, and the inlet
liquid stream is the lean solvent from absorber. For desorption the inlet gas
stream is the vaporized solvent from the reboiler, and the inlet liquid stream
is the rich stream from the absorber. The vapour amount and composition
from the reboiler is automatically calculated. Principally, the reﬂux water
from the condenser should be considered as part of the inlet liquid. In this
case it is neglected though, due to its small amount compared to the solvent
inlet.
The software creates various results from simulations: outlet ﬂow rates
and compositions, outlet temperatures and reboiler duty are calculated. Ad-
ditionally, various performance data e.g. temperature and composition along
the column is provided. All simulations assume no pressure drop and no heat
loss from fully insulated columns.

very robust to moderate variations in ﬂue gas CO2 content.

The overall optimal
lean loading is a balance of lean solvent capacity, liquid ﬂow rate and energy
requirements for the desorber.
The capture rate as function of lean loading is seen in Figure 38 (right
axis).
It can be observed that up until a lean loading of around 0.2 the
capture rate only decreases little with increasing loading. At lean loadings
above 0.25 the slope of the curve is much steeper, and the capture rate
decreases rapidly at higher loadings. The rich loading (left axis) shows a
break in the curve at the same position. These breaks in the curves between
two linear parts could indicate that there are two absorption ”regimes” for
high and low lean loading and a transition state around the soft break. The
explanation is likely to be found in the fact that the column height is kept
constant through the analyses. Starting from a high lean loading the capture
rate rapidly improves when decreasing the lean loading. In this regime not
all of the packing height is used since the performance is limited by high
lean loading. Moving leftwards, above a certain point all of the absorber
heigh is eﬀectively utilised for absorption. In this regime the column does
not contain the necessary packing required for the extra mass transfer that
can potentially take place at lower lean loadings. Thus, only limited capture
rate improvements can be achieved when decreasing the lean loading unless
the absorber height is increased simultaneously. In other words, at diﬀerent
conditions the performance can be either limited by driving force or by the
packing height.

Rich loading
Increasing the rich loading naturally requires a higher reboiler heat duty to
reach a certain lean loading, since more CO2 has to be released. However,
since more CO2 is released the speciﬁc reboiler duty can get lower for higher
rich loadings. Figure 42 conﬁrms this trend.

the left at a lean loading close to 0. Instead they seem not to go much further
down than a lean loading of 0.16. This could indicate that the performance
is limited by the column height and that it would require a higher column to
reach lower lean loadings with an acceptable speciﬁc reboiler duty. Otherwise
it is extremely hard to strip the remaining CO2.

ulations is 0.16. The simulated desorber size and operation characteristics
require disproportionately much energy to reach this value, so it is not an
optimal lean loading for the total system.

Discussion
The validation and implementation part of the model has been the most chal-
lenging part of this project. Debugging and parameter adjustments are time
consuming tasks but important in order to ensure that the model predictions
are as close to measured values as possible. The validation of the developed
model has shown reasonable results. For the absorber, there is some scatter-
ing in the validation plots, but no systematic oﬀset are visible. The highly
deviating mass balance and analytic uncertainties of the pilot plant data is
very likely the cause to the scattering. The desorber validation shows even
higher scattering, but the extreme sensitivity to the reboiler temperature
and the reported foaming are very likely contributing to this. No systematic
oﬀset is visible for the desorber.
The model had diﬃculties converging for the desorber runs at vacuum
conditions and 6.4m K+/1.6m PZ solvent. It would strengthen the model
validation to have run the desorber at these conditions as well. Furthermore
it would have been preferable to validate against data consisting of even
more diﬀerent solvent compositions, pressures and packings. In this way the
model could be proven applicable to a broader range of system conﬁgurations.
However, this experimental data is not available in literature.
Since the
model performs reasonable at both absorber and desorber conditions, it is
fair to conclude that the model is suitable at the entire temperature range
of operational interest.
The parameter uncertainty analysis showed a remarkable sensitivity to
the ionic strength exponential factor of the kinetics. The results of the model
are highly dependent on this factor. This parameter can also be adjusted in
order to give more accurate predictions. Further analysis of this parameter
and how the ionic strength is aﬀecting the kinetics are obvious areas of focus
for future work.
Overall, the sensitivity analysis conﬁrms the expected trends and be-
haviour of the system at varying conditions. It is shown how the performance
can be limited by both driving force and column height.

Conclusion
The validation plots show some scattering around the line of perfect ﬁt and
moderate deviations.
CO2 mass balance calculations show that the pilot
plant mass balance is far from closing, indicating that concentration or ﬂow
measurements are not very accurate. Furthermore, foaming was observed
in the pilot desorber. These sources of error are likely to contribute to the
deviations seen in the validation section.
Uncertainty analysis of the correlated parameters shows that the cal-
culations are not very sensitive to the estimated viscosity and surface ten-
sion. However, the calculations are largely eﬀected by kinetics via the ionic
strength exponential factor. Since this factor is a roughly estimated value it
is subject to a lot of uncertainty. The ionic strength eﬀect should be further
analysed.
The sensitivity analysis involves L/G ratio, temperature, lean and rich
loadings, column height, reboiler duty and diﬀerent solvent compositions.
The desorber operation and performance is found to be extremely sensitive
to the reboiler temperature. Generally, the results of the sensitivity analy-
ses conﬁrms the expected trends. Among the simulated solvents, the 6.4m
K+/1.6m PZ solvent shows the lowest absorption rate.
This solvent also
shows the highest speciﬁc reboiler duty. Both results correspond to results
obtained from pilot plant operations by Chen (2007).
