Abstract
The content of this project revolves around a structural analysis on human immunodeﬁciency
virus (HIV) protease with focus on mutations and their contribution to drug resistance. HIV
protease is a vital part of HIV and eﬀective inhibition of the protease can stop the virus.
The current treatment approach utilises competitive inhibition which leads to mutations in
the protease causing resistance to treatment. This project is mainly focused on explaining the
impact of E34V and F53Y mutations in HIV protease through molecular dynamics simulations.
It was found that the E34V-F53Y variant showed higher ﬂuctuation in the protein. The variant
showed an increased preference to opening the ﬂap region, hence suggesting drug resistance.
The opening of the ﬂap region from changes in backbone interactions can help explain the
mechanism of drug resistance in the studied HIV protease variant. Additionally, a hydrogen
bond analysis indicated that variants had a slightly higher hydrogen bond count, suggesting
an increased recognition of inhibitor in mutated structures. Finally, a substrate groove theory
presented by an Laco (2015) was tested using molecular dynamics simulations. The ﬁndings
did not ﬁnd conclusive evidence to conﬁrm the theory.
The outcome of this project can hopefully incentivise further analysis in uncommon HIV pro-
tease mutations. The ﬁndings in this project show that despite the mutations being uncommon,
they have a signiﬁcant impact on the dynamics of HIV protease.

Introduction
Human immunodeﬁciency virus (HIV) is a global problem aﬀecting more than 37.9 million
people worldwide [1]. HIV is under continuous research as no cure is currently known. Data
from the World Health Organization (WHO) reported that 770 000 people died from HIV
related causes in 2018. HIV mortality is declining, but at a slower pace than hoped [1]. The
HIV epidemic is most prevalent in the African region where about 3.9% of the population is HIV
positive [2]. Continents such as Europe, North and South America, and Asia have a prevalence
of about 0.3 to 0.4%. On a yearly basis, 1.7 million people are infected with HIV.
Treatment of HIV involves the use of highly active antiretroviral therapy (HAART) [3]. The
strategy is a competitive inhibitor strategy, targeting key proteins in the viral cell. These key
proteins are a part of the viral life cycle. Proper treatment causes the virus to stop spreading in
the infected individual, but will not kill the virus. These proteins include reverse transcriptase,
integrase and HIV protease. Inhibitors have been designed to target these enzymes with a high
aﬃnity to combat the respective substrates. The project focuses on HIV protease.
HIV protease is involved in the maturation of HIV. Segments of the virus are contained in
precursor proteins. These precursor proteins are processed by HIV protease to achieve their
active form. HIV protease is therefore a prime drug target as inhibition of HIV protease would
stop the maturation of the virus and leave it inactivated. Treatment of HIV protease is not
an easy task due to HIV having a high mutation rate. Under the pressure of inhibitor drugs,
mutations might occur in the protease structure which alters the binding of the inhibitor and
decreases its aﬃnity. HIV protease can thereby develop drug resistance and new treatment
plans would be needed for the patient. Drug resistant mutations are still being researched.
The work of this project will cover the structural analysis of HIV protease. The insight in
HIV protease research is presented in the following sections. Sections include the mechanism of
HIV maturation and its infection of new cells. For HIV protease, its structure is described as
well as research on drug resistant mutations and their impact on HIV protease. Mutations most
important to drug resistance are found in the HIV Stanford Database [32, 33]. The current
understanding of the cause of drug resistant mutations is presented as well.
The research in this project includes a hydrogen bond analysis for bound inhibitors saquinavir
(SQV) and darunavir (DRV) in crystal structures from the Protein Data Bank. Analysis also

Human Immunodeﬁciency Virus
HIV is a spherical particle with glycoproteins expressed on the surface. HIV infects T-helper
(CD4) cells which impacts the immune system of the infected. The genome of the virus is
about 10kb and contains all the required information to build the particle. The majority of
structural components are found in the group speciﬁc antigen (Gag) and Gag-Pol polyproteins,
see ﬁgure 1. Pol refers to the protein containing the reverse transcriptase. Gag and Gag-Pol are
native to retroviruses [8]. Gag-Pol contains the viral enzyme protease (HIV protease), reverse
transcriptase and integrase [9]. Gag and Gag-Pol are expressed in a 20:1 ratio. The mechanism
behind the ratio of these polyproteins is not fully understood [10]. It is proposed that the 20:1
ratio is essential for dimer stabilisation of HIV’s RNA and therefore essential for replication [11,
12]. The transcription of Gag-Pol is initiated by a -1 frame shift during transcription by reverse
transcriptase [9, 13]. The frame shift occur due to a slippery site where the nucleobase thymine
is repeated in the genome [9, 13]. HIV also has a third polyprotein, env, which contains the
glycoproteins, gp41 and gp120 [14].
A
The Building Blocks of HIV
An overview of the building blocks of HIV can be seen in ﬁgure 1A. At the N-terminal of Gag
and Gag-Pol precursor proteins is the matrix (MA) domain. The matrix domain binds the Gag
precursor protein to the plasma membrane of the virus by a myristic acid interacting with the
phospholipids in the membrane [8, 15]. The virus particle can form in the absense of the matrix,
but will not form a mature particle [16]. The following domain, known as the central domain, is
the capsid (CA). Once released from the precursor protein, the capsid mediates protein-protein
interactions and is essential for viral maturation as it forms the conical shell at the core of the
virus. Inside the capsid, the viral genome is stored. The viral genome is further encapsulated
by the nucleocapsid (NC) protein during maturation [10, 17]. The capsid and nucleocapsid
domains are separated by the spacing protein (SP1). Next to the nucleocapsid domain is the
other spacer protein (SP2) followed by p6 at the C-terminal. SP1 and SP2 have a function
during assembly of the virus particle as they regulate conformational changes [10, 17]. SP1 has
been found to inhibit the formation of the mature capsid as the lattice is unproductive with SP1
bound to the capsid [10]. SP2 is less understood, but is thought to play a role in coordinating
and maturing the nucleocapsid [10]. p6 recruits the endosomal sorting complex required for

particle is released ESCRT catalyses ﬁssion of virus particle and new host cell membranes [15,

Virus Maturation
Maturation is a quintessential part of the virus replication cycle. When the virus is mature,
it is infectious. The formation and maturation of the virus particle is an ongoing research [18,
19, 20]. The Gag and Gag-Pol precursor polyproteins are expressed through translation of the
RNA genome and the processing of them by HIV protease matures the virus. Before processing,
Gag proteins complexes at the cell membrane during budding and mediates the formation of
Gag-Pol complexes [18]. ESCRT initiates the budding of the new virus particle. Post budding,
the maturation process begins [17].
A new non-infectious virus particle is shown in ﬁgure 1B(i). HIV protease is responsible
of maturing the virus through cleavage of Gag and Gag-Pol polyproteins [19, 20]. How HIV

protease is activated is not completely understood [19, 20]. The current theory is that two
protease monomers from two Gag-Pol polyproteins complexes and HIV protease dimerises.
The protease is activated by the ﬂap region of the protease opening up and allowing cleavage
of the protease’s N-terminal and C-terminal, see next section for HIV protease structure [19].
Processing of the precursor proteins happens at diﬀerent rates as cleavage sites are all diﬀerent
in sequence. The order of cleavage varies from study to study as they have been tested in vitro
[21]. The diﬀerence in rates has been measured to be up to 400 fold [22, 23]. A proposed
sequence of site cleavage is summarised in ﬁgure 1.
The mature virus particle enter the host cell through interactions between receptors on the
host cell surface and the HIV surface proteins, gp41 and gp120.
gp120 binds to the CD4
receptor on the CD4+ T-helper cell, triggering the binding of the co-receptors, α-chemokine
receptor and β-chemokine receptor [24]. A change in gp41 conformation leads to the fusion of
the cell membranes [24]. The N-terminus of gp41 is highly hydrophobic and can distort the
T-cell membrane. The content of the virus particle can now enter the cytoplasm of the host
cell. Studies on the capsid have shown that the viral content is protected by the capsid, post-
entry [25]. Protection of the viral cell content by the capsid remains until about transcription
where the viral components are uncoated [26]. The capsid has been observed near, but not
complexed with, the integrase protein during integration of the viral DNA into the host cell
DNA [26].
A trait of the retrovirus is that it can eﬀectively produce a viral DNA strand
during early infection [24]. The generated DNA strand is integrated by the integrase protein
into the host cell chromosome [24]. The mechanism of integrase has been studied in vitro [24,
27]. Two nucleotides are removed from the double DNA strand, exposing a hydroxyl group
on each strand. The integrase protein catalyses the reaction to combine the viral DNA with
the host DNA. Enzymes of the host cell repair the integration intermediate and the host cell is
successfully infected [27].

HIV Protease Structure
HIV protease belongs to the aspartic protease class. This class is rarely found in living systems
due to the need of an acidic medium [14]. They are present in the human body to control diges-
tion of proteins in the stomach and lysosomes [14]. The catalytic region of aspartic proteases
commonly consists of a two triads consisting of Asp, Thr/Ser and Gly, one in each chain [10].
Cross interactions between the three involved residues form a rigid hydrogen bond network.
This triad is known as the ﬁreman’s grip [28]. One aspartic acid acts as a base, the second as
an acid. A tetra coordinated water molecule plays a critical role in hydrolysis of the peptide
substrates [10], see ﬁgure 2.
Figure 2: Schematic of catalytic mechanism in aspartic proteases. In HIV protease, the water molecule
is tetra coordinated with Asp25 and Asp25’ as well as the backbone of Ile50’ and Ile50.
Figure
reproduced from [14].
Aspartic proteases contain a ﬂap region. The ﬂap is conformationally ﬂexible in all the
aspartic proteases. Unlike the pepsin-like proteases, which are monomeric and possess only
a single ﬂap, the active HIV protease is a homodimer employing two ﬂap regions, one from
each monomer. The function of the ﬂap is to take an active role in binding a substrate or an
inhibitor and exclude water from the catalytic site [29]. It needs to move substantially in order
to allow access to the active site. The comparison of crystal structures of the WT apoprotein
against inhibitor complexes has shown that the tips of the ﬂaps moved as much as 7 ˚A [29, 30].
It has been estimated that the ﬂap tips must move about 15 ˚A apart in order to allow ligand

Due to its mutable nature, consensus sequences have been deﬁned for HIV
protease. Multiple subtypes of HIV protease are known [2]. The sequence used in this project
is consensus B, seen in ﬁgure 3. The catalytic function of HIV protease involves two aspartic
acids on position 25 and 25’. HIV protease is active within a wide pH range. Conﬁrmed in both
in vivo and in vitro, HIV protease is able to cleave its substrates at pH 2-7 [34]. The dimer
structure is quintessential to HIV protease activity as the catalytic mechanism results from the
interplay between the two triads, one from each monomer. The region of highest stability is the
dimerisation domain at the meeting of the C- and N-terminal from each monomer [35]. The
ﬁreman’s grip is essential for dimerisation of HIV protease and the stability of the dimer [36].
Thr26 from one chain forms hydrogen bonds to Thr26 and Lys24 in the other chain, holding
the monomers together [36]. These interactions are mirrored from the other chain as well. The
least stable region is the ﬂap region. HIV protease is diﬀerent to other proteases as it has no
disulﬁde bonds present in its structure [14].

ﬂap elbow (34-42), fulcrum (11-22), cantilever (59-75), interface (1-5, 95-99), and active site
(23-30) [37]. The ﬂap region is considered responsible for opening up for substrate and inhibitor

happens on a µs scale [42].
The sequences cleaved by HIV protease ﬁt inside a deﬁned pocket, see ﬁgure 5 [43]. The
pocket holds a strand of the peptide substrate consisting of 7-8 amino acids. These amino
acids have a deﬁned nomenclature common to other proteases [14]. Substrate positions are
deﬁned from P4 to P4’. Between position P1 and P1’ is where the peptide is cleaved. Each
substrate position fall into subsites found along the binding pocket, labelled S4 to S4’ [44].
S1 and S1’ contain hydrophobic residues as well as the catalytic aspartates. S2 and S2’ are
also hydrophobic subsites, but can contain both hydrophobic and hydrophilic residues [44, 45].
S3 and S3’ can contain any type of residue. Approved inhibitors bind inside the S2’ to S2
subsites [44, 46]. S4 and S4’ have no general type of interaction [21]. Ultimately, the preferable
interactions in the subsites are deﬁned by the substrate bound [21].

HIV Protease Mutations
HIV protease is a protein which undergoes many mutations due to the lack of proofreading of
the genome by reverse transcriptase. As a result, many random mutations might occur in the
structure. The genome is subject to about 1 nucleotide mutation per replication cycle [47]. It
has been estimated that about 1010 new variants of the virus are produced every day [47]. These
small variations of the virus are coined as quasispecies [24, 48]. Every mutation in the virus
impacts the ﬁtness. Fitness is used to describe the overall function of the virus. If the virus
mutates and becomes drug resistant it has to compromise on functionality most of the time,
hence becoming less ﬁt [47, 49]. The virus can restore the loss in ﬁtness with other mutations,
but generally the potency of the virus decreases with mutations [47]. Drug resistance is deﬁned
as the inhibitor not being able to stop the function of HIV protease. Despite having a high
mutation rate, the number of nucleotide changes in the genome during replication is limited
[50]. Most of the time, only one nucleotide is mutated, but multiple nucleotide mutations are
needed in order for amino acid changes in the expressed proteins. Therefore, the evolution of
HIV-1 protease is relatively conservative [50].

Mutations of HIV protease are classed into primary secondary and tertiary mutations. 46
positions in HIV protease have been reported to mutate [5, 51]. Mutations in HIV protease
on positions with direct contact to the substrate are considered primary mutations. Primary
mutations generally decrease the viral ﬁtness and/or potency of the protease [32, 33]. Positions
subject to secondary mutations are not in direct contact with the bound inhibitor or substrate.
Secondary mutations, also known as accessory mutations, can compensate for the lost potency
[10].
The order of mutations is not trivial.
In general secondary mutations occur before
primary mutations [52, 53]. Additionally, tertiary mutations in the endogenous substrate can
compensate for lost viral ﬁtness [10, 54]. Both secondary and tertiary mutations aims to enhance
substrate aﬃnity or turn-over rates [10]. Tertiary mutations are also called co-evolution and
occurs when a given substrate loses a key interaction due to a drug resistant mutation. An
example is the cleavage of the NC-SP1 site in Gag which lose a key interaction due to V82A
mutation, a common cross resistant mutation [54, 55]. A cross resistant mutation is a mutation
which has an impact on multiple inhibitors [51].
Combined eﬀorts have mapped out the signiﬁcant mutations in HIV protease, presented
in the Stanford HIV database [32, 33]. The complete understanding of HIV protease and its
mutations is an ongoing project. Shafer et al. released an article on guidelines for proper HIV
surveillance [56]. Main points are that mutations should have an impact on drug resistance and
be caused by the use of therapy [56]. Knowledge on mutations is needed when a treatment-naive
is infected. Mutations are most likely observed in a treatment-naive patient, but the question
is whether these mutations are drug resistant or not. Random mutations, occurring in more
than 1% of the population without drug pressure, are identiﬁed as polymorphic mutations. It is
argued, that due to the many drug resistant positions in HIV protease, the use of a 1% cut-oﬀ
might inﬂate the estimates on drug resistance [56]. Therefore, a 0.5% cut-oﬀis proposed for
all subtype B HIV strains. In a treatment-naive patient, mutations on key positions might be
present due to transmitted resistance or by natural occurrence. Instances where a treatment-
naive person is infected with drug resistant HIV from another treatment-naive person are rare
[47, 56].
Mutations of HIV protease complicates treatment as cross resistance can be achieved through
only a few mutations on key positions [47, 51]. The positions classiﬁed as major mutations in
HIV protease from the Stanford HIV database are shown in table 1 [32, 33, 51, 57].

Protease Inhibitors
Highly active antiretroviral therapy (HAART) is the most eﬀective treatment of HIV, where
protease inhibitors play an important role [58]. Treatment of AIDS involves multiple drugs
to inhibit target proteins in the virus. Other than HIV protease, inhibitors are administered
to target reverse transcriptase and integrase [58, 59].
The administration of HIV protease
inhibitors is a health concern to the patient who experience side eﬀects from the life long
treatment [58].
Common side eﬀects include insulin-resistance and cardiovascular diseases
[58].

the inhibitors are designed to mimic the intermediate of the peptide bond synthesis [58]. Each
inhibitor, except tipranavir, has a secondary hydroxyl group which interacts with the aspartates
in the catalytic region.
Old generation inhibitors such as indinavir, saquinavir, nelﬁnavir and ritonavir are utilizing
a hydrophobic eﬀect to bind to the protein, meaning they are more susceptible to primary
mutations on hydrophobic residues in the binding site [60]. Newer generation protease inhibitors
such as amprenavir, liponavir, tipranavir and darunavir can retain aﬃnity despite multiple
mutations [60]. Treatment with tipranavir is free of cross resistance and is recommended for

B
Treatment Targeting HIV Protease
Currently, once someone is infected with HIV, the condition is not curable. Only available
treatment involves HAART treatment. This means that infected individuals are subject to
life-long treatment [3]. The drug load is high as the recommended treatment plan involves at
least two, but preferably three diﬀerent drugs [3]. Drugs are classiﬁed depending on which
protein they target. Nucleoside reverse transcriptase and non-nucleoside reverse transcriptase
inhibitors target reverse transcriptase. Drugs targeting reverse transcriptase are generally used
together with another class of drugs like integrase strand transfer or HIV protease inhibitors.
Plans involving integrase strand transfer inhibitor as a third agent is more common compared to
protease inhibitors [3]. Treatment of HIV is highly individualised as suppression of viral repli-
cation, immune recovery and avoiding drug-drug eﬀects are all therapeutic goals [3]. Immune
recovery is evaluated by recovering CD4 cells. Treatment failure is used to describe instances
where HIV is treated but the concentration of healthy CD4 cells fail to increase signiﬁcantly
[75]. Patients subject to treatment failure have to get a new treatment plan as soon as possible
[75, 76]. Treatment failure is linked to mutations in the target protein. In that case, the virus
particles can start spreading again. If such an event occurs in a patient, mutations can start
accumulating, potentially leading to cross resistance [76]. In a study by Kantor et al. [57], it
was found that 80% of patients continuing a failing therapy accumulated reverse transcriptase
or protease mutations.
B.1
Adherence and Links to Drug Resistant Mutations
Drug resistant mutations in a patient under proper treatment for HIV rarely occur [77]. Riton-
avir is a unique inhibitor as it is administrated together with other HIV protease inhibitors. The
function of Ritonavir made the treatment of HIV protease more eﬃcient [78]. Ritonavir boosts
the eﬃciency of other inhibitors by inhibiting the cytochrome P-450 CYP3A4, slowing down
the metabolism of HIV protease inhibitors in the liver [78]. It has been found that patients with
a boosted protease inhibitor rarely develop drug resistance [47, 79]. It is theorised that muta-
tions occur in treated patients due to drug adherence [47]. Adherence is term used to describe
how much of the time the patient is taking their prescribed medicine. Studies have shown that
correlation between adherence and drug resistant mutations is not a linear relationship [79,
80, 81, 82]. Low adherence is not associated with development of drug resistance, as low drug
pressure on HIV does not push for a mutations to occur [83]. Intermediate to high adherence

==PAGE:25==

July 17, 2020
has shown to cause the most drug resistant mutations. In a study by Sethi et al., it was found
that 70 to 89% adherence had a high chance of drug resistant mutations [82]. Reasons being
that with an intermediate adherence there are times with suﬃcient drug pressure on the virus,
but also times with too low drug pressure. As a result, the virus will naturally express drug
resistant variants by natural selection [53, 81]. A study found that very high adherence levels
(79-100%) promoted about one drug resistant mutations during a 6 month study [84].
It is worth noting, that when these adherence studies are carried out, the collection of
data is dependant on how patients document their treatment program [85]. Therefore, saying
which level of adherence is the best is a complicated task. An adherence level of about 95% is
recommended for long term HIV treatment to keep virus replication and maturation in check
[85].
C
Substrate Recognition and Speciﬁcity
In order to understand mutations and develop potent HIV protease inhibitors, an understanding
of the endogenous substrates is needed. HIV protease binds its substrates through substrate
recognition. In studies by Prabu-Jeyabalan et al., they investigated crystal structures bound
with peptides resembling the cleaving regions in Gag and Gag-Pol [86, 87]. They found through
the crystal structures that the substrates for HIV protease share a static common volume, a
consensus volume, see ﬁgure 9(left).
Conserved hydrogen bond networks explain how HIV
protease recognises its substrate and forms the consensus volume [87]. The sequence of the
substrates are very diﬀerent and therefore the substrate envelope theory has been widely cited,
as it can help explain how substrates are recognised by HIV protease. The envelope theory has
also shown to still be valid during co-evolution of the substrate due to drug pressure [54]. The
research surrounding the substrate envelope makes it the best current theory to explain the
cause of mutations in HIV protease and its substrates [54, 88].
Another study on the substrate envelope by the same group was carried out with MD
simulations [86]. In this study, a dynamic volume was deﬁned, see ﬁgure 9(right). The dynamic
volume is considered a better representation of the envelope as it gives a probability distribution.
The aim is to use the consensus volume to develop drug candidates towards HIV protease where
the volume can be used as a constraint in drug design [86, 89]. The substrate envelope theory
has been used to develop DRV, which is considered the most potent protease inhibitor currently
[61].

of isolates, a group of drug resistant variants with only one mutation in the binding site was
found [91]. This incentivise the substrate envelope theory as mutations in this group decrease
inhibitor binding, but preserves substrate recognition by not changing the binding envelope
[91].

C.1
The Substrate Groove Hypothesis
Studies on HIV protease selectivity and substrate binding have been limited to 8 and 10mer
peptide substrates to ﬁt inside the established P4 to P4’ recognition sequence [92]. There has
been a discrepancy in terms of ordering the cleavage sites between in silico and in vitro [21]. in
silico studies used 8mer WT regions from the Gag polyprotein. It was found that the MA/CA
region is cleaved faster than SP1/NC, despite the cleavage time being found to be 10 fold
faster in vitro [92]. Using a longer peptide chain of 24 amino acids yielded results which agrees
with experimental data. This has led to the theory of substrate recognition outside of the
binding pocket. This theory is known as the substrate groove hypothesis. It states that 24mer

A
Crystallisation Principles and Theory
Crystallisation of macromolecules, including proteins is a ﬁeld that has no comprehensive the-
ory. The approach to protein crystallisation is largely empirical [94]. When starting crystalli-
sation experiments, one approach is to investigate previous crystallisation attempts of a given
protein to design successful conditions for crystal growth. Conditions for crystal growth include
choice of precipitants, pH and temperature. Usually, two conditions are optimised in a two di-
mensional grid. Most crystal structures are obtained a room temperature [95]. Optimisation
of conditions are therefore usually focused on type and concentration of precipitant as well as
the pH.
Optimisation of conditions usually starts with a screening with broad ranges of precipitant
concentrations and pH values as well as types of precipitants. If no data on the target protein is
available, a screening can be used to test a variety of conditions [96]. If a given set of conditions
provides a good lead, it can undergo further optimisation. Say conditions of a screening were
initially deﬁned from 20 to 80% precipitant and pH 4 to 6. A good lead was obtained at 40%
precipitant and pH 5, hence the next screening could be expanded around this lead with a
precipitation concentration of e.g. 35 to 45% and pH 4.5 to 5.5. Next lead can be optimised
further to obtain the best crystal.
The cause of protein crystal formation is an established theory. The most common methods
for crystallisation is vapour diﬀusion through either a sitting or hanging drop [94]. Hanging
drops were generally used during this project. The principle of a hanging drop is to create a
closed system. The drop is placed on a piece of glass which hangs over a well containing a
reservoir solution containing the precipitants and buﬀers chosen. The drop consists of a mix
of protein and reservoir solution. Assuming a similar volume of protein and reservoir is used,
the concentration of the mother liquid is essentially halved in the drop. As a result of the
diﬀerence in chemical potential between the drop and reservoir solution, the vapour in the
drop will evaporate and the concentration of precipitant and protein both increase over time,
see ﬁgure 10 [94]. The critical part of crystallisation is to approach the ”Labile” phase slowly
enough for nuclei to form, but not too many as that will cause only smaller crystals to appear
once the labile phase is reached. By staying in the metastable region for longer, bigger nuclei

July 17, 2020
can form so that once the labile phase is reached, bigger crystals can form.

Structural Analysis of HIV Protease Crystal Struc-
tures
Following the crystallisation experiments, a structural analysis of HIV protease was carried
out.
The analysis included a hydrogen bond and RMSD analysis.
The structural analysis
aimed to shed light on the impact of speciﬁc mutations speciﬁed in the Stanford HIV database
[32, 33]. Structures of interest contain either SQV or DRV as ligands. For each ligand, the
crystal structure of WT HIV protease with the ligand was aligned with the crystal structure of
HIV protease resistant variant with ligand to investigate the impact of a given mutation. WT
crystal structures used for analyses are 1T3R for DRV and 3OXC for SQV.
Diﬀerent approaches were considered for the structural analysis.
One approach was to
use the PDBe PISA server [102] to estimate a binding free energy for the ligand in WT and
variant structures. For DRV, the WT crystal structure 1T3R was considered together with
the highly mutated ”T4S, L10V, I13A, K14R, K20I, A22V, L33I, E35D, M36I, N37D, R41K,
K43S, G48A, I54V, I66F, H69K, T74S, V82A, I84V, L89I, L90M, and T91S” (PDBID: 3T3C
[103]) and ”I13V, G16E, V32I, L33F, K45I, M46I, L76V, V82F, I84V” (PDBID: 6OPX [63]).
The calculated binding free energy was found to be the -6.5kcal/mol in the WT and mutated
crystal structures. As a result, this method of analysis was discarded as no distinct changes
were made in the binding free energy. Visual inspection of the structures in PyMOL yielded
no signiﬁcant ﬁndings. It was found that the tertiary structure, except loops, of HIV protease
appeared to be conserved across all structures with diﬀerent levels of mutations.
An approach using contacts [102] in the program suite CCP4i [104] was used as an alternative
method. Using a cutoﬀof 4 ˚A, all O-O, O-N and N-N hydrogen bonds were noted for each
variant. For each hydrogen bond, the length, residues and atoms involved in the binding were
recorded for analysis.
The expected result would be to see a diﬀerence in either count of
hydrogen bonds or the average length of hydrogen bonds between the inhibitor and protease
variants. Logically, more mutations would mean that the binding of the inhibitor would weaken.
In this analysis the catalytic water molecule known to bind at I50 in the ﬂap region has not been
considered for hydrogen bonds [70]. The analysis was also not taking non-polar interactions
such as vdW into consideration. Changes in vdW energies are usually computed together with
a MD simulations [70, 72, 73].

RMSD analysis was carried out for the analysed crystal structures.
The RMSD data was
generated using PyMOL [112]. RMSD of the backbone atoms for every residue was calculated
in relation to the respective WT crystal structure. A signiﬁcant change was deﬁned to changes
above 1 ˚A as seen in another study [103]. No signiﬁcant changes were observed in regions with
deﬁned secondary structure, aside from a few examples. From the data in ﬁgure 15, 6OPZ is
the structure with the highest increase in Ki. From ﬁgure 17, a signiﬁcant change, just above
1 ˚A, in RMSD can be seen for residue 44 and 69. The observed RMSD for these residues does
not suggest a signiﬁcant change in the tertiary structure as both residues are found at the start
of a β sheet.

Changes in the tertiary were observed in the loop regions such as the fulcrum (15-16) and the
ﬂap elbow (35-41). Changes in these regions seem to be unrelated to the number of mutations
as seen in ﬁgure 18. The four RMSD plots shown in ﬁgure 18 contain a low, intermediate
and high amount of mutations and represent the most interesting ﬁndings in the data. 2HS1

Molecular Dynamics Simulation Studies of HIV Pro-
tease
MD simulations use classical mechanics to simulate the movement of molecules. For this project
the software AMBER17 was used for MD simulations [113].
MD simulations use a given
interpretation of Newton’s 2nd law to determine the velocity and resulting position of the
molecules [114].
These interpretations are condensed in a rule set called a force ﬁeld.
In
the force ﬁeld it is determined how a given alteration is scored energy wise. Force ﬁelds are
ever evolving from experimental data that is used to update parameters and scoring functions.
Force ﬁelds used in this project are OPLS3 [115] used for docking with Glide and ﬀ14SB
from AMBER17 [116]. Force ﬁelds and MD theory is not covered in this project as it was
previously covered in a previous bachelors project. The bachelor project has been handed in a
supplementary material. The material relevant to this project can be found on pages 24 to 39.
Setup of the in silico models were carried out using Schr¨odinger’s interface software, Maestro
and AMBER using LEaP [116, 117].
WT HIV protease and the E34V-F53Y variant complexed with SQV was investigated using
MD simulations. Position 34 and 53 have both been identiﬁed in studies as being important
for substrate binding as seen in table 3.
Additionally, the theory of a substrate groove on HIV protease was tested. By building the
24mer substrate described in Laco’s study [92], it could shed light on the possibility of the
legitimacy of the theory.
A
Preparation of in silico Models
WT HIV protease structure (PDBID: 6DIF [118]) was selected from the Protein Data Bank.
The structure was loaded into Schr¨odinger’s interface, Maestro. Using the ”Protein Prepara-
tion” wizard [119], the structure was preprocessed. All het atoms and water molecules were
removed except the co-crystallised ligand. For this project, any catalytic waters in the binding
pocket are not considered.
To prepare for docking, the grid generation tool from Glide was used [120, 121]. Using the
position of the co-crystallised ligand, the grid deﬁnes an area where the new ligand can be
placed inside the crystal structure. The molecules were prepared for docking using LigPrep

[122]. LigPrep uses OPLS3 force ﬁeld to relax the molecule before docking.
Docking of SQV was carried out using Glide standard precision. The docking with the best

The E34V-F53Y variant was prepared in VMD [123] using the ”Mutate Residue” tool. VMD

Changes on these positions as a result of the mutations should therefore not cause signiﬁcant

can be found in supplementary section 16.

24mer Substrate Model Setup
Docking of the 24mer was carried out. Due to limitations and concerns for precision, a 24mer
cannot be docked to the crystal structure using Glide. Glide uses a rotamer approach which is
limited to 300 atoms [120, 121]. Therefore, the method was to dock a small fragment, building
on to that fragment and then let the 24mer ﬁnd its binding conformation using molecular
dynamics. For docking, a small 4 residue peptide was prepared based on the MA/CA sequence
from Laco’s paper [92]. By building a smaller peptide, the docking should place the cleavage site
inside the catalytic region. The small peptide was built using the ”Build” tool in Maestro. The
sequence of the docked fragment was Asn-Tyr-Pro-Iso. The peptide was prepared for docking

docking was successful.

MD simulations were carried out using AMBER17. The simulations included a minimisa-

minimisation the structure was relaxed. The system was then heated from 10 to 300K during
the heating step. Density and volume of the system were adjusted during equilibration. The
structures were then simulated using constant volume (NVT) proﬁle in a production run. The
time of production runs varied from 10 to 210ns.
Production runs of 10ns were set up to
produce 1000frames for analysis. Simulations were generally repeated up to ﬁve times. Each
production run was started from a unique seed, meaning each system might converge diﬀerently.

Discussion
In order to gain a structural insight into the eﬀects of drug resistant mutations of HIV protease,
crystal structures with DRV and SQV bound to HIV protease and resistant variant hereof were
analysed. Looking at the interactions from HIV protease to SQV and DRV, it is not easy
to pinpoint causes and eﬀects. Despite big diﬀerences in sequences with up to 22 mutations,
RMSD analysis revealed that the tertiary structure of HIV protease remains conserved in areas
with a deﬁned secondary structure. Changes in mutated side chains could not be calculated
using PyMOL due to the diﬀerence in atoms.
Hydrogen bond analyses did not provide a consistent result to explain drug resistance. The
high energy involved in hydrogen bonds could help explain a change in binding aﬃnity. Data
for HIV protease with SQV suggests that more and shorter hydrogen bonds leads to worse
inhibition. Hypothetically, a weaker binding through longer and fewer hydrogen bonds are
expected to hamper inhibition. However, the estimated hydrogen bond energy for SQV suggests
that more energy is involved between the ligand and protein. The data for DRV is not consistent
and shows no correlations outside of the structures from the Schiﬀer researc group [70, 73].
From the hydrogen bond analysis, most of the structures have a more favourable energy than
the WT, but structures with many mutations such as 3TTP and 6OPV have worse hydrogen
bond energy. Assuming that the observation of variants having more favourable hydrogen bond
energy is valid, what are the implications? It could mean that HIV protease has an increased
recognition mechanism through a change in hydrogen bonds. Like previously mentioned, the
hydrogen bond analysis is a crude model and the sample sizes are relatively small. In order to
test these structure properly, MD simulations would be needed to observe the impact of the
mutations.
MD simulations were conducted to investigate the uncommon mutations E34V and F53Y
and the hypothesis of a substrate groove as well. E34V and F53Y had little direct impact on
the bound inhibitor SQV, but both showed altered interactions with the backbone. Together
with the RMSF plot, it became clear that despite subtle changes in the protein, the backbone
became more ﬂexible. Longer MD simulations also revealed that the ﬂap region opened up
signiﬁcantly, suggesting a mechanism of drug resistance in the variant. The exact reason for
drug resistance caused by these mutations is not completely clear. The interaction between
position 34 and 82 is similar to the 90 to 25 interaction as seen in, ﬁgure 8. Both position
52
==PAGE:60==
Benjamin Clausen
July 17, 2020
34 and 90 interact with the backbone of an important residue in the binding pocket. The
L90M mutation was shown to alter the electron density of Asp25 [46, 71]. The same principle
could apply to V82 and V34 despite it being a non-polar bond as the side chain hydrogens
of V34 interacts with the backbone of V82. CH··O and CH··N hydrogen bonds are not fully
understood, but commonly observed in protein structures [130]. The interaction between 34
and 82 did not result in an increase in RMSF for their respective regions. From ﬁgure 27, it
is observed that the RMSF of 30s and 80s regions are non-consistent across the two chains.
Therefore, the impact of a mutation of position 34 could lead to a chain of events that leads to
instability in the dimerisation domain. The dimerisation domain has consistently higher RMSF
for the variant.
F53Y seemed to have an impact on the ﬂap region movement.
After 10ns all variants
showed a slight opening of the B chain ﬂap tip, see ﬁgure 24. These ﬁndings explain the change
in count with the ﬂap region. The ﬂap from the A chain rotates slightly and moves away
from the inhibitor. Longer MD simulations suggested the ﬂap region is more likely to open
with E34V and F53Y present in the structure. From the two long MD simulations it became
clear that the change in the ﬂap region was pushing SQV out of the binding pocket. It is
unlikely to observe SQV leaving the binding pocket completely during MD simulations due to
the lack of a competing molecule and the unfavourable local maximum the system has to go
through to eject the inhibitor into solution. More 200ns MD simulations would be needed to
say that this ﬂap movement is a consistent observation. The ﬁnding in this project suggest
that drug resistance from E34V-F53Y is linked to a system with higher ﬂuctuation and changes
in backbone interactions, leading to changes in the ﬂap region which facilitates ejection of the
bound inhibitor.
When it comes to drug resistance and mechanism of resistance, it is hard to imagine that
only one mechanism exists. Some mutations distort the dimerisation domain, some change
vdW interactions to ligand and others change their backbone interactions.
The mutations
investigated in this project had little to no impact on inhibitor interaction. The analysis of
crystal structures had multiple structures with mutations in the binding pocket, changing the
hydrogen bond network slightly. If that change means a better binding of the inhibitor, it goes
against common sense. However, together with the observations in MD simulations, it could
mean that a change in hydrogen bonds are needed to encourage ﬂap opening and transport of
the inhibitor out of the binding pocket. Energy analysis from MD trajectories does not back up

==PAGE:61==
Benjamin Clausen
July 17, 2020
this hypothesis, but mutations introduced also had little to no impact on the inhibitor binding.
It seems appealing to believe that a conformational change is needed to encourage removal
of inhibitor from the binding pocket which involves a change in hydrogen bonds and higher
ﬂexibility. As stated, there is probably not just one mechanism of resistance and the ﬁndings
in this project are not easy to rationalise in a common hypothesis.
The substrate groove was tested through MD simulations set up to mimic the starting
structures from Laco [92] as well as free ligand structures. Free ligand MD simulations showed
no consistent preference. The MD simulations were run for 30ns and there was no indication
that running the simulations for longer would change the outcome as the structures converged
to their positions shown in ﬁgure 30. Trying to ﬁnd an indication of the substrate groove from
these initial conditions was ambitious. The conditions do not reﬂect the in vivo conditions with
the polyprotein involved. Using a diﬀerent starting structure with the 24mer forced into the
substrate groove gave an indication of a substrate groove, but does not conﬁrm the hypothesis.
Further work with mutated residues in the substrate groove would be needed to ﬁnd arguments
for the theory.
The most convincing result is that the 24mer interacts with a few positions which are found
in the Stanford HIV Database classiﬁed as accessory mutations. MD simulations with changes
to these positions would be needed to give a better conclusion to this theory. It is also possible
that multiple peptide sequences from Gag and Gag-pol would be needed for this analysis. As
previously mentioned, the cleaving sequences are all diﬀerent and it is possible that the cleavages
sites have diﬀerent conformations. Some of these conformations possibly include the use of a
substrate groove for stabilisation while some others might not. Regardless, a proper analysis
would require modelling of the neighbouring proteins in the polyprotein to gain a relatively
realistic representation of the events involving cleaving of the polyproteins. With the work
presented in this project, the theory cannot be properly judged. What speaks for the theory
is the fact that the 24mer substrate is in contact with almost every mutable position found in
the Stanford HIV Database.

Conclusion
HIV protease is still under research to understand its mechanism of action and how to properly
treat it. Current treatment plans involve a heavy use of medication targeting multiple pro-
teins. Treatment plans require that the patient have a high adherence to avoid drug resistant
mutations. The impact of individual mutations and their impact on drug resistance are an on-
going research. Multiple ﬁndings help understand the cause of drug resistant mutations. One
suggestion is the substrate envelope theory which suggests that the current design of inhibitors
are fundamentally ﬂawed. It has been found that inhibitors that protrude the deﬁned binding
volume promote mutations in HIV protease by natural selection. A new generation of protease
inhibitors are needed to prevent the occurrence of drug resistant mutations. Additionally, a
hypothesis expanded on the substrate envelope theory to include a substrate groove on the
surface of the protease. The theory aims to explain substrate recognition and secondary muta-
tions occurring at positions with direct interactions to the substrate. So far the theory has not
received any conﬁrmation from other publications.
An analysis on HIV protease crystal structures has been carried out. These analyses orig-
inally intended to revolve around crystals structures of HIV protease and the E34V-F53Y
variant. Using a MES buﬀer and NaCl precipitant gave the best results. Crystallisation work
was underway but interrupted by the breakout of COVID-19. No HIV protease protein crystals
were obtained during the screenings.
The focus was changed to in silico studies and a Protein Data Bank analysis. Hydrogen
bonds in crystal structures from the Protein Data Bank were analysed.
Together with an
RMSD analysis, it became clear that the tertiary structure of HIV protease is conserved despite
mutations. Slight changes were observed in hydrogen bonds with an inconsistent suggestion
that structures of higher resistance have more hydrogen bonds and a stronger inhibitor binding.
Molecular dynamics simulations revolved around HIV protease wild-type and the E34V-
F53Y variant in an attempt to explain the observed drug resistance from these two mutations.
Results suggested that drug resistance comes from changes in backbone interactions. Conserved
backbone motifs between position 34 and 82 were observed, which lead to changes in the ﬂap
region. Longer molecular dynamics simulations showed an open conformation in the ﬂap region.
Together with the observation of higher ﬂuctuation in the mutated system, it is suggested that
55
==PAGE:63==

July 17, 2020
the impact of E34V leads to instability in the interface domain. F53Y causes new interactions
in the ﬂap region which encourages ﬂap opening. The ﬁndings suggests that the mechanism
of drug resistance from the E34V-F53Y variant towards saquinavir is due to decreased dimer
stability and higher preference for ﬂap opening.
Additionally, the substrate groove hypothesis was investigated. A more in-depth energy
analysis would be needed to better quantify the binding aﬃnity of the simulated conformations.
What speaks in favour of the hypothesis is the fact that the substrate interacts with almost
every mutation listed in the Stanford HIV Database.
A
Future Perspective
The ﬁndings of this project showed a surprisingly apparent impact of mutations in HIV protease.
The conserved motif observed on position 34 should encourage a similar analysis for more
commonly known mutations. Results also underlines the importance of secondary mutations
in HIV protease and their contribution to drug resistance. It would be interesting to carry
out a similar analysis for the structures presented in section 7 to gain further insight into the
dynamics of drug resistance in HIV protease.
