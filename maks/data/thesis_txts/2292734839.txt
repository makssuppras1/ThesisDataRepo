Abstract
The present master thesis investigates the use of ASR systems in combination with missing data for the
evaluation of segregation systems. It has been shown that non-ideal binary mask outputs of segregation
systems have a detrimental effect on speech intelligibility. The non-ideal binary masks contain errors and
error structure that causes the deterioration of human performance. Nevertheless, the technical metrics that
have been already developed so far do not capture the trends of behavioral listening tests. Therefore, the
need for a metric arises that could encapsulate both an objective and perceptual aspect of speech segregation
evaluation. Towards that direction, an ASR system, modiﬁed with a missing data classiﬁer is evaluated on its
behavior in the presence of errors and structure when binary masks are utilised.
The ﬁrst chapter of this document offers an introduction to the challenges of the topic as well as the
goal of the project. Furthermore, in the next chapter, a brief review of the theory surrounding the tools that
were used is presented, such as binary masks, Hidden Markov Models, automatic speech recognition and
missing data. The third chapter presents the experimental setup for training the ASR system, while in the
fourth chapter the experiments that were conducted are described. Additionally, the results are illustrated in
the same chapter and the key observations are presented. The last chapter offers a discussion regarding the
results and especially the observed discrepancies between the performance of the missing data ASR system
examined and speech intelligibility tests, when errors and structure are present, as well as recommendations
on future improvements and research.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The topic of understanding and mimicking the abilities of human vocal communication, in the presence
of noise is one of the long-lived subjects of machine hearing systems. The pronounced capability of the
human auditory system to separate relevant spoken information from background noise has been often the
subject of analysis and the performance of algorithms which attempt to replicate aspects of human perception
have substantially improved over the last decade [2]. Nevertheless, more effort is necessary in order to
approach the accuracy of human hearing, given the still current performance gap between computational
systems and human [3]. Numerous technical systems that attempt segregation of target and interfering
signals have been developed, and there have also been different approaches to the problem of segregation,
such as computational auditory scene analysis (CASA) and blind source separation.
The human hearing system has an exceptional performance in challenging tasks where speech has to be
segregated from interfering noise. It can deal with partial information - e.g. parts of a word, or ﬂuctuations
in the interfering signal e.g. modulation of noise which allows for listening in the dips to achieve these
results. The ability of analysing complex scenes with different types of noise, like multiple talkers, stationary
noise etc. is called auditory scene analysis (ASA). The attempt to mimic and replicate aspects of human
hearing through machine systems has been termed as computational auditory scene analysis (CASA, [2]).
Computational speech segregation in particular - as part of CASA, can be performed in a number of different
ways as mentioned.
A computational segregation system analyses the time-frequency (T-F) representation of noisy speech
and classiﬁes each T-F unit as either target-dominated or noise-dominated. The output is a binary matrix,
referred to as binary mask, with every unit assigned to a binary digit, denoting the presence of speech (target),
or noise (interference). The assignment takes place through a decision process, where there is an estimation
for speech and noise information for each unit. The most common decision scheme is comparing the local
signal-to-noise ratio with a predeﬁned global SNR criterion (a threshold value that shows above which level
a unit can be considered as speech-dominated). When a priori information on speech and noise is available
(for example the noise and speech signal are provided as separate signals - before they have been mixed into
one), the binary mask can be accurately calculated, hence there is no estimation, rather a speciﬁc calculation
of the metrics needed (e.g. local SNR values for the T-F units). In the aforementioned case, the mask is
calculated ideally, otherwise termed as an ideal binary mask. The estimation of local SNR, when a priori
information is not available, is a matter of approach for the estimation process.
In the context of speech segregation using a kind of spectro-temporal representation of the input signal,
the work by May and Dau [4] can be considered as an example. It presents a way to segregate speech from
1
==PAGE:17==
noise, utilising auditory-related features (Amplitude Modulation Spectrogram). The features are obtained by
applying a modulation ﬁlterbank to the inner hair cell representation of the signal (frequency weighted) -
obtaining the AMS features, then a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) classiﬁer is trained with the features
combined with the a priori information on the SNR in individual time-frequency (T-F) units. Observing the
process involved, independent of their speciﬁcations, the output of such a segregation system is produced by
feature extraction (AMS features) a method for training a model (GMM classiﬁcation) that can act as an
estimator of SNR values in each T-F unit of any given signal and a decision stage where the ﬁnal output
is calculated based on a criterion for the dominance of noise and speech. Namely, the trained estimator, is
statistically calculating the energy differences(SNR) of the T-F units which then are subject to a threshold
function that determines the outcome. For example, the calculation of one of this T-F units’ SNR as 3 dB
with a local criterion of 0 dB would produce a speech-dominated unit. The output of such a system is a
binary-valued 2-D matrix, know as a binary mask, which in this case is estimated through the described
system.
As brieﬂy mentioned before, despite the progress of CASA in general and speech segregation in particular,
a signiﬁcant performance gap is still a matter of research [5]. The task of computational segregation is highly
complicated, as the auditory system performs in a vastly greater level than current state-of-the-art solutions,
yielding results one or two orders of magnitude higher than computational methods - depending on the noise
types, as is stated in [3,6]. The accuracy of a computational system to successfully segregate speech from
noise has to be quantiﬁed through an evaluation. As the segregation is applied bearing in mind that it should
reﬂect the human performance of identiﬁcation of speech in noise, the obvious evaluation method is that of
listening tests using human subjects. This requires the reconstruction of the output of the segregation system
in such a way that it will provide a reproducible audio signal, rather than a collection of time-frequency
units. To evaluate the performance of segregation systems, listening tests can be conducted where the task of
the subjects is to recognise the phonemes/words/sentences that are provided as reconstructed speech, which
in turn yields an intelligibility score (word recognition score, speech reception threshold - SRT etc.) The
main drawback of conducting such test, is their time-consuming and resource-consuming nature. Several
alternatives have been proposed in the interest of minimising the need for listening experiments.
Current and past literature reports the use of several technical metrics or measures for the evaluation
of segregation. Such measures are Segmental signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) [7], Perceptual Evaluation of
Speech Quality (PESQ, [8]), log-likelihood ratio and more. Segmental SNR calculates the signal-to-noise
ratio for two signals by averaging blocks of samples for the two signals. Nevertheless this comparison
is only a time-domain process, and it does not account for the signiﬁcance of frequency bands and their
respective SNR values, which can prove important for speech intelligibility, since even though the SNR
might be low in a certain part of the mixture but it does not necessarily show that a human will not be able to
comprehend speech. As an example, very high noise levels in high frequencies (more than 4 kHz) might
be irrelevant for intelligibility, nevertheless the metric will consider such a signal as unintelligible. As far
as PESQ is concerned, the speech quality is evaluated with a target on telephone networks (mobile, ﬁxed
etc.) and their respective errors. These errors are quantisation errors speciﬁc to such networks. As stated
in [8], listener quality is not part of the assessment that PESQ can apply. Even though the original aim of
the aforementioned metrics differed for each of them, they still remain relevant as means of quantifying
performance for speech segregation in rough numbers. One of the most recent algorithms for intelligibility
prediction of time-frequency weighted noisy speech, where also the T-F units have been binary-weighted (in
the form of speech and noise-dominated units) is the short-time objective intelligibility (STOI, [9]) measure,
one that can be applied to binary masks. Alongside STOI is the HIT-FA (HIT rate minus False Alarm
rate, [10]) measure, which is often used to measure the accuracy of segregation systems. The latter metric
2
==PAGE:18==
compares the output of the segregation system (the estimated binary mask) to the ideal. One is the estimated
binary mask as output of an segregation estimator, such as the one described in the previous paragraph,
and the other is the ideal values of such a mask, which are computed when having a priori information, as
previously described. It then cross-references the ideal values with the estimated values. The HIT-FA rate is
calculated by the numerical difference between the correctly estimated speech-dominated units (hits) and the
misclassiﬁed noise-dominated units (false alarm).
Nevertheless these measures, even though they have been widely tested for their performance and
correlation to intelligibility [9,11], can sometimes produce discrepancies between the metric’s values and
the results of testing with human subjects, for example using the speech reception threshold (SRT).
More speciﬁcally, the HIT-FA is a popular metric used in literature as a means of measuring the
segregation performance and it has been shown to highly correlate with recognition scores of human
subjects [11]. Nevertheless, the study conducted by Kressner and Rozell [1] has shown that aspects of the
segregation output can be crucial to human intelligibility, ones that are not at all captured by such a metric.
More concretely, the presence of classiﬁcation errors in the output of a segregation system (binary mask
errors) can be detrimental to intelligibility which is what the HIT-FA rate evaluates. Nevertheless, their
distribution can affect signiﬁcantly human intelligibility. The latter does not imply necessarily a higher
error rate, merely a certain organisation/clustering of errors. Consequently, the metric will still evaluate an
output as equally performing, not taking into consideration these error formations. The motivation for this
research project arises from the limitations of the mentioned evaluation schemes, where the initial drive is to
eventually come up with a metric that would combine the technical and perceptual aspects of intelligibility,
thus rendering the listening tests unnecessary.
Moreover, a segregation estimator like the one described in the previous paragraphs (from the work of
May & Dau) is replaced by a graphical model, that artiﬁcially introduces errors to ideal binary masks in
a controlled manner. As the work by Kressner and Rozell focuses on examining human speech reception
thresholds based on speciﬁc error rates, it is important to be able to control the error statistics for the
segregation in order to have the same base for comparison. That would be impractical when using a real
estimator where a few or more parameters have to be ﬁne-tuned in order to produce the desired error
distributions and statistics.
The goal of this thesis is to investigate to which extend automatic speech recognition (ASR) can be used
to reﬂect the intelligibility of segregation systems for speech based on a valid connection to an objective
measure, through the recognition scores provided by ASR. In essence, such a system is one that provides an
estimation on how a signal is separated into speech and noise. Segregation systems for the purpose of this
thesis are considered to act on noisy speech to separate the clean signal from the interferer signal. Validity
depends on the correspondence of the estimated performance of such a system to human performance. An
extra step would be necessary towards evaluating human performance through listening tests, that of speech
reconstruction. The output of a segregation system is merely a set of values in a feature space, a mask that
denotes which part of the signal is likely to be speech or noise. Ultimately, the evaluation of a segregation
system lies in its ability to describe this particular human ability.
The current concept of a new evaluation system is centered around automatic speech recognition as a
means of evaluating intelligibility without the need for listening tests. Moreover, the main inspiration for the
building blocks of the evaluation system has been the work of Cooke et. al. [3], which suggests the usage of
missing data for the recognition task using binary masks. The reasoning behind using the aforementioned
masks and missing data, is ﬁrstly the output of segregation systems (binary, representing speech and noise in
3
==PAGE:19==
a time-frequency map). Additionally, the missing data approach suggests that only parts of the information
on a feature space are reliable for recognition calculations, which in turn improves the performance of a
decoder.
The attempt on assembling an evaluation method for segregation systems is founded on automatic speech
recognition. However the ultimate goal is oriented towards speech enhancement through intelligibility
prediction, rather than recognition-based where the end purpose is to maximise word score performance.

CHAPTER 2
Theory
In this chapter, a review of all the theoretical background that was used for this thesis is presented. The
main guide for recreating the procedures of automatic speech recognition using missing data, has been, as
mentioned before, the work by Cooke et al. [3], where the main back-end for ASR was the HTK software,
which will be addressed later. The missing data classiﬁcation essentially modiﬁes a conventional ASR
system so that the output of a segregation system (binary mask) can inform the ASR classiﬁer about reliable
(present) and unreliable (missing) T-F units. The present or missing state is deﬁned by a criterion as it will
be explained in this chapter, which essentially shows if a T-F unit is speech or noise-dominated.
2.1
Binary Masks
The source of motivation of this project is the need for an evaluation metric which is sensitive to
perceptually-relevant errors in segregation systems, as measured in behavioral listening tests. In order to
comprehend the different building blocks of such an evaluation metric, it is important to examine the basis of
work on segregation systems. For this project, the latter decomposes the input into individual time-frequency
units and the segregation stage identiﬁes time-frequency units as speech-dominated or noise-dominated.
Such a system processes the input of a mixture of a speech signal (target) with noise (interference). The
output is the segregated speech and noise in the form of binary masks (2-D matrices with binary information,
which denotes the dominance of speech or noise). A generic segregation system is shown in Figure 2.1.

In order to further relate the binary mask to the segregation concept the output of the segregation system
is introduced as a binary mask of time-frequency (T-F) units, which represents the gain for speech. A T-F
unit with a value of 1, is a T-F unit representing speech, while a 0 valued unit indicates an interference unit.
Regarding the origins of such a map, it has to be stressed that there are different methods of calculation,
although the key aspect of it is that, spectrotemporal processing has to be involved as well as a criterion for
the decision on the binary problem of ’noise or speech’. To add to that, in a realistic setup, the calculation
of such a mask would be only an estimation, as opposed to controlled experiments where there is prior
information on noise characteristics or even the exact signal. The implications of an estimated mask are
discussed in section 2.1.4.
The separation of target and interference signal is decided utilising the information (e.g. SNR) on both
of them. Nevertheless, in real-world applications the information is not always readily available, thus an
estimation of the properties of such signals is used instead. A decision process makes use of them and
produces a mask of the useful signal with clear indication of the interferer in the same mask. However,
this estimation can always contain errors, a note that will be discussed later on regarding the meaningful
evaluation of segregation. A further understanding of such a (basic) system can be obtained by observing its
output. The presentation of the binary mask is chosen as a relevant tool that has been used throughout the
project and does not imply that it is the only form of segregation results.
Every square in the binary mask produced, as seen in Fig. 2.1, has an assigned value of 0 or 1. The
result comes from a comparison of the SNR value of individual time-frequency units, the local SNR, with a
criterion that has been set as the threshold between speech and noise. A more detailed explanation based on
the nature of the binary mask is provided in this chapter. For the sake of simplicity, the local criterion for
assignment is considered to be 0 dB in all of the cases explained.
2.1.1
Unity masks
For the purpose of explaining binary masks it is important to, ﬁrst, examine the baseline case of the
output, with regards to binary masking. More simply posed, a feature space consisting of a value set (a value
for a speciﬁc sample and attribute for each element of the space) that although they may have an intrinsic
ﬁltering or weighting process such as a ﬁlterbank decomposition in a speciﬁc scale (Bark, ERB etc.), when it
comes to their ﬁnal representation as speech or noise dominated units they have the same weighting, namely
speech-dominated. This type is called a unity mask because it reﬂects the presence of only 1-valued weights.
Essentially, a unity mask denotes that no segregation has been performed whatsoever, which is a useful
baseline for comparison with other processing techniques. In a given space of n dimensions, a vector x can
represent all the observations of a given mixture. For every element of the vector, there is a corresponding
weight, which in the unity case, takes the value 1. That is translated into that all the elements of x are present.
Consequently, they are all treated as part of the target signal, indicating that the latter is dominant in those
elements.

(between speech and noise) and the local criterion based on a priori information on noise, in Fig. 2.2. The
latter shows the SNR values between speech and noise only for a small fragment of the auditory spectrogram.

Even though both cases of errors, seen in Fig. 2.6 denote the erroneous binary value assignment to a T-F
unit, the consequences of those two distinct errors are not the same when intelligibility is concerned. These
types of errors, also referred to as type I and type II errors respectively, in literature [10], have different
effects on intelligibility. The research by Li and Loizou showed that
• false positives have a very detrimental effect on intelligibility even for rates as low as 20%, while
• false negatives yield high intelligibility scores even for rates of 60%.
One important aspect of the errors as analysed in [10] is their underlying structure. For both cases, they are
uniformly distributed across the binary mask. The second and most crucial assumption in this experiment is
that the error types do not coexist.
9
==PAGE:25==
The work by Kressner and Rozell [1] has addressed this by developing a graphical model for the
introduction of errors as well as by conducting listener experiments to validate the results by Li and Loizou.
More importantly, the model has been designed to produce structured errors whose distributions depend on
a parameter which controls the likelihood of an error unit appearing next to another one. A combination
of structure as well as the presence of both types in a binary mask have been both tested with regard to
intelligibility in listening tests. The results of the experiments corroborate the idea that structure inﬂuences
intelligibility, concretely showing a deterioration with increased structure. The latter conclusion contradicts
the results by [10], showing that structure affects intelligibility when the clustering of errors increases.
Additionally the researchers stress that the two different types of errors can be equally detrimental to
intelligibility when introduced in structure.
It can be deducted that the investigation of ASR performance using missing data in conjunction with
binary mask estimations, with the aim of revealing the similarities of it with intelligibility, or the limitations
of such a system, should ultimately incorporate a structure investigation. In the context of this project, the
estimations of binary masks have instead been replaced by the graphical model as described in [1] where an
artiﬁcial non-ideal binary mask is produced, where the input can be an ideal binary mask as well as an error
rate (for each type of error separately) and a structure coefﬁcient. Thus, the error rate and structure can be
controlled and predeﬁned in order to examine their inﬂuence.
2.2
Automatic speech recognition with Hidden Markov Models
Hidden Markov Models have been widely used for three decades for speech recognition tasks. The use
of ﬁnite-state machines to represent the formation of utterances is the central notion of HMMs. An example
of an HMM can be seen in Figure 2.7 [12].
Figure 2.7: Example of Hidden Markov Model consisting of 6 states. Taken from [12].
The observation sequence (utterance) O, consists of smaller parts, o1, o2, ..., o6, which are used to
calculate the probability vectors a and b. The former is the probability of transitioning to the pointed stated
(i.e. a1,2 is the probability of transition from state 1 to state 2), while the latter is the probability of an
observation subset (e.g. b2(o1) is the probability of observation 1 in state 2). It has to be stressed at this
10
==PAGE:26==
point that the elements of the utterance are not words in the context of this project. Since the training is
taking place in word level, each word is an utterance, O. The breakdown of each word happens as a result of
the number of states speciﬁed for the HMM training algorithm.
The distribution of probabilities for each state of the model is simply assumed to be adequately described
by a Gaussian distribution, and depending on the application, the states can have a common distribution or
separate (by deﬁning different mean and variance values for each state for example). Alternatively, each
state can be described through a set of Gaussian mixtures. The Gaussian mixtures can have a full covariance
matrix or a diagonal. In ASR applications the diagonal is preferred as it is computationally less complex and
resource-consuming [13].
HMM recognition requires two phases, training of HMMs using a number of utterances and testing on a
set of utterances. It is recommended to separate the two sets in order to avoid testing on features used for
training.
The training procedure of HMM-based recognition involves
• feature extraction, i.e. a set of vectors/attributes in a relevant format,
• initialisation of a prototype HMM,
• Baum-Welch reestimation of the parameters of each HMM (one for each word/phoneme).
The testing procedure involves a Viterbi routine to calculate the most likely path for an utterance
compared to the available HMMs. The maximum likelihood employed by the Viterbi decoder yields the
most probable HMM, which in turn, points the most probable word/phoneme.
2.3
Missing data automatic speech recognition
The work by Cooke et al. [3], among others, suggests a creative utilisation of missing/unreliable acoustic
data in order to obtain a better result in the calculation of expectation for speech recognition. The missing
data approach has been already formulated from the 1970’s by Rubin and has been the subject of analysis and
improvement over the years [13]. In the latter, a fundamental difference in the previous approaches of noisy
information is described, where instead of improving or changing a model for the better depiction of data,
the distorted information is treated as missing. A simplistic example of the concept is the correspondence
with visual occlusion of objects by other objects. Nevertheless, the visual masking of objects does not share
the same properties as speech masking, in the sense that audio signals are additive.
In this approach, two different methods of unreliable data utilisation are proposed, data imputation or
marginalisation. The former, estimates the values of missing data based on conditionality applications that
may arise from the dataset coming from certain hypotheses for the dataset. The latter marginalises the
missing data, in the sense of calculation of marginal probability, effectively ruling out the missing data
distribution by integration. In the context of this thesis, marginalisation has been chosen as the best practice,
as it has been proved to be superior, due to the fact that "it takes into account the distribution of the missing
points", instead of counting on just a single estimate, as in data imputation [3]. Another advantage of this
method for speech recognition as stated in the last mentioned paper, is the independence of the system to
noise-speciﬁc training. More concretely, the aim of this method is to obtain good performance regardless of
the type of noise that is present in the observations. This necessity of an independent system originates in
11
==PAGE:27==
the high variance noises that can occur in real world applications which in turn produce poor performance
due to the unpredictability of them, thus rendering the noise-speciﬁc solutions unsuited for recognition. As a
countermeasure, the training takes place only on clean data, supporting the generality of the results, as well
as reinforcing the robustness of the system to unexpected noise proﬁles and/or statistics.
The marginalisation technique as described by Cooke et al, is divided in two types of marginalisation,
full and bounded. Full marginalisation considers all unreliable data as missing. On the other hand, bounded
marginalisation considers an interval of unreliable data, which if known, can be marginalised. The rest of
this section repeats the mathematical formulation of bounded marginalisation for missing data, while the
reader is pointed to [3] for further insights on the method.

CHAPTER 3
Experimental Setup
In this chapter, the setup for obtaining word recognition scores of ASR in combination with binary
masks is described. The setup consists of training of the HMMs in HTK and testing using a MATLAB
Viterbi algorithm [14]. HTK training was selected because of the robustness of the software and MATLAB
testing has been chosen because the use of binary masking for recognition For HTK, custom settings have
been applied, while for MATLAB ready-made functions have been utilised where minor changes have been
applied to better represent the calculations.
3.1
System description and overview
A brief description of the building blocks of the system that is utilised to produce the performance results
is presented in this section. In accordance with ASR systems, a ﬁrst level of distinction of building blocks
is the training and testing phases, as it has already been discussed. The former is described in this chapter,
while the latter is presented in Chapter 4, as part of the evaluation process.
Training - as well as testing - requires feature extraction, so that the raw audio signals are transformed
into a meaningful data structure. The typical ASR feature space is the coefﬁcients of the cepstral domain,
although for the purpose of missing data classiﬁcation, this type of features does not provide a resolution
high enough for binary mask applications. After the features have been accordingly evaluated, a HMM
parameter estimation and reestimation algorithm produces the models. This is a complex procedure as far as
parameter calibration is concerned, as there are many aspects that have to be decided. Nevertheless, during
this project a baseline of parameters from [3] has been considered as a starting point.
Testing is the procedure where the trained models are used to classify a set of signals, which is separate
from the training set. A clear perspective of testing can be considered by ﬁrstly introducing an abstract
structure where a test signal is processed by the feature extraction block, then is passed on to a decoding
algorithm. The latter has two primary inputs, the models and the feature space. The Viterbi algorithm is
evaluating the most prominent of the given models. A schematic overview of both training and testing is
shown in Figure 3.1.

Experiments and results
4.1
Overview and assumptions
This chapter presents the various experiments that were conducted throughout the investigation for
performance of the ASR system, along with their results. With regards to the HMM training, the same
conﬁguration was used throughout the following experiments and only if noted otherwise, the setup was
altered. For example, in the experiment for the effect of the local criterion (LC) on performance, the values
were varied to show the difference in results that this parameter can cause.
A total of ﬁve experiments were designed in order to test the assumption of using a missing data ASR
system in the context of intelligibility prediction. The ﬁrst experiment involved testing the validity of the
missing data ASR system for three different setups, a standard MFCC-oriented recognition system with a
unity mask, a baseline ﬁlterbank energy system with a unity mask (no missing data option was deﬁned) and
a ﬁlterbank energy system with missing data, utilising a priori information, i.e. the ideal binary mask.
The second experiment handles an investigation regarding the optimal value of the local criterion for
missing data classiﬁcation within a binary mask. For the purpose of this experiment, only the ideal binary
mask with missing data system was employed and tested on both noise types.
The third experiment concerns the inﬂuence of isolated errors and structure on the binary mask in low
(global) SNR values with respect to speech and noise. In order to produce errors on binary masks of the test
ﬁles, the graphical model described in [1] was employed, where the binary mask can be imputed with errors
at a speciﬁc error rate (for both error types) as well as a structure coefﬁcient, using missing data as described
in Chapter 2. The isolation refers to imputation of the two different error types in a mutually exclusive
fashion, as to examine the validity of the ASR system and its alignment with literature, especially [10] and
the deterioration of performance when one of the error types is solely present. Additionally, the structure
coefﬁcient was increased in order to examine the performance trend for two different structure conditions
and both noise types were tested.
The fourth experiment, concerning the inﬂuence of combined errors and structure, in contrast to the
previous, is designed to show the performance trend when both error types are present in the recognition
process. In the same fashion as before the structure coefﬁcient, noise types are altered as well and the SNR
range that is examined in the isolated error experiment is identical. The direction towards this experiments
originates mainly from the work by Kressner and Rozell [1] were the listening tests with human subjects
showed the effect of structure when combined error types are present in a binary mask, which differs greatly
than the isolated error case, especially with regard to the amount of inﬂuence of combined error types.

==PAGE:35==
Finally, the ﬁfth and ﬁnal experiment that approaches the imputation of errors by introducing frame
skipping instead of structure, serves as a cross-validation and further insight into the results obtained in the
combined error evaluation. Concretely, the performance shift when structure is more intense in binary masks,
is examined by creating structure manually, through skipping frames in the binary mask, hence concentrating
the errors in certain time frames.
The setup that was used has been already shown in table 3.1, and it was common for all testing, with
respect to the features used in each evaluation experiment.
4.1.1
Dataset and vocabulary
For the purpose of the experiments, the TIDigits isolated digits database has been employed. This
database consists of 95 speakers for training, 113 speakers for testing, all of them pronouncing one of the
eleven digits, 1-9, ’oh’ (for zero) and ’zero’. For each speaker, there are two versions of the same digit,
hence 22 ﬁles per speaker. Each ﬁle trained is a single, isolated word (no silence or noise before or after the
utterance is present). A total of 2072 train ﬁles were used (missing 18 ﬁles from the last male speaker) for
both FBE and MFCC feature training, 220 test ﬁles were included for the ﬁrst two experiments, and 1100
ﬁles for the rest of them.
4.1.2
Evaluation procedure
In order to perform evaluation, the training described in Chapter 3 was performed. As discussed before,
the testing phase of the system is performed toward the performance evaluation of a certain set of Hidden
Markov Models that have been previously trained. The ASR system has been set up to train models in
clean condition, effectively pointing into the direction of noise-independent recognition. Nevertheless, a
realistic evaluation of the capabilities requires that different noise conditions are examined. Two aspects are
of interest with regard to noise,
• type of noise, and
• the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR).
The next step is feature extraction, which takes place in the same manner as in training, as presented
in section 3.2. The SNR adjustment process handles the mixing of clean speech and selected noise into
a mixture with a speciﬁed SNR value. The resulting feature space has to be utilised and accordingly
manipulated, depending on the type of features. The next section attends to said manipulation in detail, while
the overview of signal ﬂow in a generalised form is presented in Figure 4.1.

Feature processing method
MFCC features decorrelate the feature space across frequency channels by applying a discrete cosine
transform. Thus, no segregation between speech and noise components is possible. They are based on the
entire feature space, and thus, cannot be used to calculate local SNR values. Each MFCC feature unit can
contain information not only about its speciﬁc frequency bin, but additional information that comes from
the weighting in the decorrelation process of the ﬁlterbank (using discrete cosine transform). For example,
the DC component of an MFCC feature space is a result of calculation including all of the frequency range,
hence a classiﬁcation as missing or present would highly inﬂuence the outcome of the recognition process.
The missing data approach is not applicable in this case as the MFCC features spectrally smear the content
in a local level, which results in reduced capacity of representing the local energies that are essential for the
decision of binary masking processing.
On the contrary, FBE features, are precisely of interest because of the relative accuracy in determining
the local SNR values of a feature space, thus deeming relevant the calculation of a binary mask. Considering
bounded marginalisation, the output of the binary mask calculation/estimation, is split into present (binary 1)
and missing (binary 0) data, thus the decoding algorithm calculates the expectation based on the formulation
presented in section 2.3. In addition to that, the original unprocessed ﬁlterbank energies can also be used
for recognition in their entirety, where the decoder accepts all of the feature space as present data. It is
imperative to stress that even though the unity or missing data approach is not directly related to feature
extraction, in the context of this project, missing data processing is only relevant for ﬁlterbank energies. A
summary of the combinations of processing and feature types is presented in Figure 4.2.

4.1.3
Types of noise
The types of noise used throughout the experiments were provided by the International Collegium of
Rehabilitative Audiology (ICRA), more speciﬁcally
• Unmodulated random gaussian noise,speech-shaped, male-weighted (normal effort)
- marked as ICRA 01 - and
• Modulated 6-person babble, speech-shaped (normal effort) - marked as ICRA 07.
The process of the selection of noise was to randomly obtain a section of the ﬁle, in order to avoid using the
same sample for the different test ﬁles. The two noises speciﬁed, were selected because of their contradicting
characteristics. The ﬁrst, is stationary speech-shaped noise, while the second is highly non-stationary,
speech-shaped and speech-modulated, which can server as a testing ground for the ASR system and its
ability to mimic human perception under those different circumstances of noise.
4.2
ASR and noise
In order to calculate the baseline performance of MFCC and FBE features, as well as the performance of
IBM processing with missing data, three sub-experiments were conducted,
• FBE with a unity mask,
• FBE with missing data using IBM (ideal binary mask) and
• MFCC with cepstral mean-variance normalisation.
The ﬁrst sub-experiment is used as a no-segregation system in order to compare it with the other two, were
segregation is applied. The SNR was set to vary from -5 to 20 dB with steps of 5 dB, as well as a clean
condition were the SNR was set to inﬁnity. Additionally, the experiments were completed for both noise
types to validate the performance of the ASR system with respect to the effect of the two noise types on
human intelligibility. The expectation for the performance curves is ﬁrstly that the FBE recognition using
the ideal binary mask would have to be superior than the other two. Moreover, the FBE recogniton with a
unity mask should perform poorly, at least in low signal-to-noise ratios. For the MFCC recognition, a robust
performance is expected due to the mean-variance normalisation, despite the lack of delta and double-delta
(acceleration) features. Nonetheless, MFCC recognition is expected to be above FBE recognition with a
unity mask because of the high correlation of the FB energies, which effectively provides HMMs that are
trained on similar feature vectors.
As far as noise types are concerned, the desired behaviour of the recognition, regardless of the feature
types, is that the multitalker babble noise will have less of a masking effect than the unmodulated noise,
due to the ability of the auditory system to perform better in situations of temporally ﬂuctuating noise. The
motivation for this experiment is a similar comparison made by Cooke et al. [3, p. 279], with two types of
noise and similar feature spaces.
The local criterion (LC) used in the aforementioned FBE experiments was set to 0 dB, as a default, as
it was not crucial for the comparison of the inﬂuence of noise and feature type, due to the vast differences
between the types of feature spaces that were known beforehand.

In the aforementioned illustration, ﬁrstly it can be observed that regardless of the noise type, there is a
clear performance difference between the three methods that have been employed. A comparison between
the ﬁlterbank with unity mask (FB_UN), where the features are highly correlated and the MFCC with
cepstral mean and variance normalisation [17] (MFCC) shows that indeed the latter is a more robust method
with regard to speech recognition, as literature already suggests and that indeed the FBE’s perform quite
poorly. However, the ﬁlterbank energy features that are grouped using the ideal binary mask are performing
better at all SNR values than the other two, with a great difference in score. This happens due to the a priori
information provided through the mask, which is not a real word scenario, although it is highly promising
for the missing data approach.
The second key comparison that these results yield, is the performance shift between the two noise types.
The performance is lower for stationary noise, as represented by the male, speech-shaped noise (ICRA
01) as opposed to non-stationary, speech-modulated, speech-shaped noise as represented by the 6-person
babble (ICRA 07). This is in line with literature and ﬁndings regarding masking release in the presence
of modulation, also known as ’listening in the dips’. It can especially be noticed for the MFCC curve,
especially for the SNR value of -5 dB, where there is a difference by 10 percentage points in the recognition
performance. For the FBE_UN curve, it is also distinct for the lowest SNR values of -5, 0 and 5 dB. For
higher SNR values the differences are smaller and sometimes the modulated noise seems to perform worse.
Nevertheless, these differences are observed when the performance is higher than 85-90% which is not an
area of critical information loss when speech is considered.

conducted. More speciﬁcally, a subset of the test set, like before, of 20 ﬁles per digit is tested for recognition
scores. The local criterion in each autonomous recognition task varies from -12 dB to 12 dB with a step of
3 dB. The global SNR range is from -5 dB to 20 dB with a step of 5 dB, plus the clean condition (Inﬁnite

4.4
Inﬂuence of isolated errors and structure
In the interest of reproducing the results from [1,10], an experiment where the inﬂuence of false positives
(type I) and false negatives (type II) on the IBM is performed. For that matter, the graphical model developed
by A. Kressner for producing binary masks with a pre-deﬁned percentage of errors has been employed, as
described before in section 2.1.4.
The two types are introduced exclusively, meaning that only one of the types is introduced at a certain
error rate for several SNR values, in order to evaluate the behaviour of the system in a similar manner
as [1, 10]. The error rate values for false positives and false negatives indicate the percentage of errors
with respect to the true positive or true negative values respectively. The structure coefﬁcient is also varied
between the values 1 and 2. A coefﬁcient of 1 denotes an unstructured condition, where the errors are
distributed throughout the binary mask in a uniform, random fashion. On the other hand, a coefﬁcient of 2,
as described in [1], forces all T-F units to be twice as likely in the same state as neighbouring units, rather
than a different state, thus employing structure, or otherwise called, clustering.

In the interest of a more speciﬁc commenting on the results, the isolated errors, i.e. presence of only one
of the two types of errors, are presented separately from the results of combined error scoring. The plots
presented in this section concern only the ICRA 01 noise type because of the similarity of the trends within
the two noise types.
As it has already been described in this section, the experiment targets at recreating the experiments
by [1,10]. The IBM is infused with errors of one of the two types, exclusively. Additionally, two different
scenarios for structure are employed for both types of errors. Figure 4.5 is a presentation of the scoring
values for each combination of error type and structure. Due to the similar trends observed by the two
different noise types, the illustrated results for all of the remaining experiments on errors and structure are
calculated as the mean of the two separate noise type results.
The literature and experiments conducted by Li and Loizou [10] suggest a stronger impact of the alpha
errors (False Positives), while the impact of beta (False Negative) errors is distinctively lower. In the case of
Figure 4.6, the effect of beta errors is even unnoticeable for the case of uniform error distribution, i.e. γ = 1,
especially for SNR 0 and 5 dB.
For the case of alpha errors, the performance is expected to drop steeper for lower SNR values. On
the other hand, the expectation of deterioration of performance for beta errors is not as steep and not as
detrimental, because the beta error rates are calculated as part of the True Positives turning into False
Negatives process. The True positives of a binary mask that is tuned with the parameters mentioned before,
are prone to be a lot less in count, than True Negatives, especially for low SNR values. Consequently, the
expectation of the results for deterioration, only taking into account the aspect of what the error rate means
for the alteration of the binary mask, is a lot smaller.

4.5
Inﬂuence of combined errors and structure
In the interest of examining the accordance of the ASR system’s performance in comparison to the
results of [1], an evaluation of simultaneous false positives and negatives has been conducted. In this scheme,
the error rates for both types are introduced in the same binary mask, as well as the structure coefﬁcient. For
this experiment the same values as in Table 4.2 have been used, excluding the error rate of 0, in order to
avoid having only one error type in the binary mask.
This particular evaluation is essential for the determination of the capabilities of the current system.
Kressner and Rozell have shown that the coexistence of both error types can be detrimental to intelligibility,
which is a more realistic condition than the error types occurring in an exclusive manner. Moreover, the
introduction of structure is also detrimental to intelligibility and more pronounced when both types of errors
are occurring. The ideal ASR system for the evaluation of intelligibility of segregation systems should reﬂect
this deterioration in performance, on the grounds that the aim is to recreate an ASR that is reﬂecting human
performance, and not to increase the performance of the ASR itself as a recognition tool.
This second version of the experiment focuses on only the combined error introduction to the ideal
binary mask. A three-dimensional representation of the scores (correct word %) is shown in Figure 4.6. The
ﬁgure is divided in 6 sub-ﬁgures for each combination of the chosen SNR values and structure coefﬁcient, γ.
In the case of this experiment, the difference compared to the previous one is that the two error types are
present simultaneously (i.e. all of the simulations contain a certain non-zero percentage of each error type).
The effect described in section 4.4, where the performance increases when structure is introduced (γ = 2), is
observed in this case as well for all SNR values. In a similar manner, the performance is almost doubled for
SNR = -5 dB, between the two different structure coefﬁcient values, and the difference appears to be of a
lesser magnitude when increasing the SNR, which clearly does not reﬂect the results measured by Kressner
and Rozell [1].
Nevertheless, for an individual simulation set, for example an SNR of 0 dB and γ = 2, the behaviour of
an error type, while the other is constant, is similar to the isolated error experiment. More concretely, the
steep deterioration with the increase of the alpha error rate is observed when focusing on a certain value
of the beta error, and vise versa, where the deterioration with beta error rate is smoother. It should lastly
be noted that the behaviour of the ASR system with the increase/decrease of the SNR value is evolving as
expected, in the sense that lower SNR values result in overall lower recognition scores.
The inspiration for these plots comes from the work of Kressner and Rozell [1], where a ﬁgure of the
intelligibility scores from listening tests with human subjects for different structure coefﬁcients and error
rates is presented. A thorough analysis of the correspondence of results with the work in [1] is provided in
Chapter 5.

4.6
Inﬂuence of structure and errors - approach with concentration of errors
A secondary experiment that is used to validate the previous, as well as to gain some insight on the
results of it, is that of concentrating the errors in certain time frames, in order to produce frames with errors
and frames that are error-free with a 100% certainty. This was achieved by introducing frame skipping when
imputing the errors to the ideal binary mask. The motivation that drives this, is the fact that the behaviour of
the system in the previous experiment is not in line with the intelligibility tests in the presence of structured
errors. For that matter, this approach has been introduced, as it will solidify the further discussion points for
why this mismatch is observed.
The aim is to present and clarify the behaviour of the ASR system in the presence of structure, by
isolating the errors in certain frames, which could also be considered as a form of structure. In this context,
the structure is created by concentrating the introduced errors in certain frame intervals. An equivalent of the
uniform, unstructured error distribution is now the placement of errors randomly in a time frame, for each
frame.
When frame skipping is introduced the error distribution has to change. Frame skipping denotes that
errors will only be introduced every n frames. If n is equal to 1, then every frame will contain errors. If n
is 2, the errors will appear every 2nd frame etc. This more simplistic way of clustering the errors in single
frames could be another important proof of the reasoning behind the behaviour of such a system when
structure is introduced. The hypothesis for creating this experiment is that a missing data ASR system as
the one tested in this project is actually aided by the presence of error-free time frames in the process of
decoding, where a decision between the provided Hidden Markov Models is made. If this holds, it can be
used as evidence of the limitation of such a system, towards modifying such a system to better reﬂect the
results of behavioral listening tests. Figure 4.7 depicts an example of the frame skipping approach. It should
be noted that skipping frames does not decrease the error rate. On the contrary, the error rate is kept constant
while the errors increase within the selected frames.

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
The main subject of this project, has been the investigation of an alternative metric or tool that could
fuse the two elements of different approaches to quantifying intelligibility. Objective, purely mathematical
metrics on one hand and the undeniable reliability of proper listening tests. Throughout the process of this
effort towards eventually creating an system that could be properly evaluated as to its suitability for the
intended use, intermediate results and conclusions have also been extracted.
5.1
Inﬂuence of errors and structure
The initial intention of the project revolved around examining the suitability of a missing data ASR
system for the evaluation of segregation where the use of recognition scores as a speech recognition index
would correlate with the perceptual abilities of the human auditory system.
The ﬁrst experiment, regarding noise, served as a proof that the recognition system, being independent
of noise type training, manages to behave in a manner similar to human intelligibility and more speciﬁcally
to the ability of ’listening in the dips’ as it is often called in literature. The modulated noise allows for better
recognition even in the case of a unity mask, where all the T-F units are treated as speech. The Viterbi
decoding is more successful at identifying the correct word model as more units in the feature space (due to
the dips in noise) are comparable to the trained hidden Markov models, which however does not indicate an
improvement towards correlating to human listening tests since there has not been measured and illustrated
through a performance curve. Additionally, the ideal binary mask recognition score shows that the missing
data ASR out performs the MFCC features and yields results over 90% even for low SNR values. Even
though the case of an ideal binary mask does not apply to real conditions, where the interferer signal is not
known, it shows the potential performance that could be obtained with an ideal segregation system, which
has already been shown in literature. In the context of the project it serves as a veriﬁcation that the missing
data incorporation to ASR is working as expected and that the improvement range is quite large.
The subject of errors and structure in the binary masks produced by segregation using estimators, has
been divided in two parts, that also highlight the importance of how errors occur on binary masks. The
experiment on isolated, meaning mutually exclusive, type I (alpha) and type II (beta) errors was in line with
the overall trend that was presented by [10], which clearly demonstrates the signiﬁcant impact of type I
errors to the performance of a binary mask and the contrasting low impact of beta errors. This difference
in performance is justiﬁed by the effect of each type of errors on the feature space. The beta errors can
be interpreted as misclassiﬁcation of speech units as noise units. The loss of certain speech-dominated
33
==PAGE:49==
Figure 5.1: Performance results for human listeners when presented with restructured speech from non-ideal
binary masks, unstructured (γ = 1) and with structure (γ = 2). Taken from [1].
units of information in the process of log probability calculation slightly affects the computational result in
combination to the fact that no actual noise is introduced (in the form of noise-dominated units).
On the other hand, alpha errors, which essentially introduce noise, by falsely presenting noise-dominated
units as speech units, can be detrimental even in small rates, as it was shown. The decoder now wrongfully
recognises and uses T-F units as speech, which is unlikely to lead to a succesful recognition of the word
contain in the original noisy mixture due to the data being used - in contrast to beta errors - as part of
the target-dominated units in the binary mask. Furthermore, the rates of type I and type II errors, are
translated to vastly different absolute numbers of erroneous units. This can be understood by examining
one of the examples of binary masks, where the speech units are clearly less than the noise units. As far as
structure is concerned, the change from a uniform distribution of errors to a structured distribution even for
isolated errors, already shows the trend of the performance which seems to improve when the errors create
clusters. This clearly contrasts the ﬁndings by [1] which suggest that structure tends to be detrimental for
intelligibility. The results from that study can be seen in Fig. 5.1, where structured errors (γ = 2) deteriorate
the performance of listeners.
The results obtained in [1] showed that subjects have difﬁculties in understanding words when the errors
in the segregation system are structured, which, from the results of this project, proves to be an advantage
for the ASR system. The human auditory system shows that there is difﬁculty in understanding words even
when parts of the speech are error-free, which declares the weakness of the missing data ASR system to
capture this property. A speculation regarding this behavior, is related to the training of the HMM models
with clean speech. Consequently, these models are prone to be sensitive to mismatches between the training
(clean speech) and testing (noisy speech) conditions. In the case where the missing data block handles an
ideal binary mask, this mismatch is reduced because only the speech-dominated T-F units are exploited as
part of the speech in the feature in the decoding task. However, if the binary mask contains errors, they will
be used as part of the speech-dominated units as well, which will drastically reduce the effectiveness, since
34
==PAGE:50==
the trained models will be compaired against units that have been marked as speech-dominated even though
they are not.
A random distribution of errors will affect many frames, especially in the case of alpha (false positive)
errors because the speech-dominated units of an ideal binary mask tend to be a small part of the mask.
However, in the case when the errors are structured, the missing data ASR system has more opportunities,
as seen in the experiment with error concentration, to correctly classify a speciﬁc frame, since it is an
error-free frame. This increases the log probability of the correct HMM model being the dominant one
in the recognition task, having an advantage over the others and that causes the increase in performance
between different structures even with the same error rates. The experiment with combined errors veriﬁes
the observation of the previous isolated error test. All the previous observations regarding the rate of
deterioration depending on the error type are still visible, and the inﬂuence of structure on recognition is
equally pronounced. The combination of results using the graphical model and the frame skipping approach
allows for a safe conclusion regarding the system’s behavior regarding structure. Error-free time frames
enforce the decoding process yielding a difference in results up to 20% in favour of the structured errors.
A fundamental difference in the comparison of literature results and the results of speech recognition
in this project should be taken into consideration. In the speech enhancement ﬁeld, in order for segregated
signals using binary masks to be evaluated by human subjects, an additional step that conducts speech
reconstruction has to be made. It could be part of further research as to how the reconstruction could
potentially affect the ﬁnal form of the signals that are presented to subjects.
A distinction between the word recognition score as a measure of strength for the recognition system
and its usefulness in speech enhancement is necessary. The inital goal of producing correlated results with
regard to speech intelligibility and speech enhancement has not been met due to nature of the system at
hand. Nevertheless, a potentially meaningful conclusion could be drawn, bearing in mind the ASR-driven
approach, where the clustering of errors aids to a better perfromance.
5.2
Noise type considerations
It has been clearly stated that as with the work in [3], an important aspect of building an intelligibility
evaluation system is to provide a ﬂexible solution that is noise-independent. Indeed, the system that was
presented is trained only on clean speech signals, thus yielding certain conclusions regarding the behaviour
of the ASR system, when tested with noisy speech. As the results in section 4.2 show and as a reminder
from the observations made, a behaviour trend similar to human speech is observed when testing the system
with modulated and unmodulated noise. The beneﬁt of ’listening in the dips’ for human, is also traceable in
the recognition.
5.3
Limitations of the system
It is important at this point to underline certain aspects of the system that could indicate limitations in
the assessment of the results and additionally these remarks could be used in the direction of improving
the present project’s setup in order to reach the goal of a metric that could potentially replace or limit the
human listening tests for speech intelligibility. Firstly, a closed set of words (11 digits) was selected to target
a simple training task, while a real word recognition system would have to be able to incorporate a big
35
==PAGE:51==
corpus of words as to tackle challenges that extend to more than digits. To add to that, the training utterances
were single, non-continuous digits as well as the test utterances. This is deviating from everyday normal
speech where there are connections between words, pauses, and other elements of speech. Moreover, the
Hidden Markov Models were trained using word-level recognition, so that each model represented one word,
having a total of 11 models. Namely, there was no breakdown of the words into phonemes or triphones
as it is usually practised for speech recognition. The reason for word-level recognition is the adopting of
the simplest system as well as the focus on relating speech recognition using missing data for intelligibility
evaluation, rather than trying to optimise the recognition task in itself.
5.4
Conclusion
The perceptual studies by Kressner and Rozell [1] have shown that structured errors in the output of
segregation systems can be detrimental to speech intelligibility. Nevertheless, the experimental results in this
thesis show that the opposite effect holds for a missing data ASR system. Additionally, in connection with the
HIT-FA metric, considering the results in performance for two different structure coefﬁcients while changing
the clustering, yields that such a metric is not a clear indication of intelligibility, since the same error rate in
a binary mask can have completely different performance scores, depending on how the errors are structured
in the mask. This conclusion could lead in future investigations on the performance results for ASR as well
as segregation systems that incorporate a form of clustering. An ASR-oriented approach will beneﬁt from
the effects of clustering and structure in errors since the performance will improve. Nevertheless, from
the speech enhancement point of view, clustering in combination with error-based metrics can potentially
produce misleading result. An example of this aspect is a system is described in [4]. The latter, applies a
window function to the T-F units before applying the binary mask classiﬁcation in order to achieve higher
HIT-FA rates. That essentially introduces a form of clustering (by "hiding" errors in the form of "averaged"
units), which can produce a possible discrepancy between behavioral listening test results and a metric like
HIT-FA, which has been designed to reﬂect speech intelligibility, as the clustering will improve HIT-FA -
hence appearing as an improvement in speech intelligibility through the metric - while at the same time
structured errors actually correlate with deterioration of speech intelligibility.
