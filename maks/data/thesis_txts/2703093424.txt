Abstract
Till this day industries and research facilities still lack the necessary technology to
continuously monitor high glucose concentrations in bioprocesses in on­line manners.
In this study, an electrochemical biosensor, with a custom made diffusion limiting
membrane has been characterized through several conducted experiments with the pur­
pose of evaluating its performance and furthermore determine its suitability for commercial
use as an on­line monitoring instrument. When exposed to compounds, such as ethanol
and lactic acid, typically found in yeast fermentation processes, the measurements re­
vealed that these compounds had an interfering effect to some degree, although not very
large. Furthermore results suggested that smaller cell volumes in a flow­through design
yielded higher signal strengths and in the given cell size at 500 µL, the optimal flow rate,
which gave the most readable measurements, was found to be around 3.2 mL
min. With the
custom made membrane applied manually on each tested biosensor, variations in fabri­
cation quality and membrane thickness were present. This meant that the dynamic range
differed for each individual sensor, where some sensors showed linearity up to around 50
mM glucose while others were capable of measuring above 100 mM.
Overall the sensor performed quite well and although not found very implementable
for industrial scale bioprocesses, where very high glucose concentrations are typically
found, the biosensor is fairly suitable for lab­scale test with lower glucose concentrations.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Fermentation uses living organisms to produce a wide range of products. To achieve
and maintain industrial/commercial competitiveness, it is vital to guarantee high product
yield and consistent quality. Hence, in order to increase process yields, it is crucial to mon­
itor and maintain, as well as to optimize and control fermentations efficiently. To this end,
it is paramount to be able to routinely and automatically control fermentations at their op­
timal point. Precise control of glucose addition can be the key factor to prevent substrate
inhibition (avoiding Crabtree effect [1]) and prevent overestimation of substrates needed
in certatin processes and thereby reduce the ammount of applied nutrutients. If success­
ful, this potentially leads to significant operating cost reductions [2], [3], [4]. However,
bioprocesses are complex, and thus have certain features that bring continuous chal­
lenges for the monitoring and control tasks, such as: (1) the batch and fed­batch nature
of many commercial fermentation processes; (2) multi­phase system; (3) nonlinearity; (4)
high correlation and interactions between process variables: and, (5) the non­stationary
nature of the process [2], [5], [6].
Real­time monitoring gives constant information about what happens in the bioprocess,
which can be vital for early problem detection, to verify that the process is evolving as
expected, and when possible, supports taking proactive control decisions [2], [6], [7]. In
practice, it aims to reduce tasks in the production and cultivation process, especially de­
creasing the number of manual tasks, and thus reduce error propagation. This is in line
with process analytical technology (PAT) practices which strive for continuous improve­
ment of the bio­manufacturing industries [8].
1.2
Bioprocess monitoring
To take and implement efficient control actions, it is vital to gather real­time process
data, though seldom available, on chemical (e.g., pH and metabolites), physical (e.g.,
pressure, viscosity, and temperature), and biological variables (e.g., biomass and product
and byproducts concentration, substrates, nutrients [2], [6]). The quantification of the bio­
logical variables is an active research field. For example, the ability to measure cell density
and viability and substrates in the extracellular medium and products such as enzymes,
proteins, and metabolites is paramount to rapidly assess the fermentation performance
and its progress with time. Availability of on­line data allows one to act promptly and tweak
the controllable variables. Examples of sensing technologies under continuous research
for the monitoring of, for example, substrates and products include, in­situ biosensors, mi­
croscopy, spectrophotometric techniques (e.g., Fourier­transform infrared spectroscopy
(FTIR), Raman, Near­infrared spectroscopy (NIRS)), particle characterization, and opti­
cal methods, among others. However, the established methods to track these variables
are still based on off­line sensing methods such as High­performance liquid chromatogra­
phy (HPLC) . Per the PAT guidelines, and process variables can be achieved by applying
different strategies, such as in­line, on­line, at­line, and off­line monitoring. For in­line
or in­situ monitoring, the sensor probes are in direct contact with the broth; pH and dis­
solved oxygen are examples of standard in­line measurements. In on­line measurement,
the sample is diverted from the bioreactor with a by­pass, and it may or may not return to
the bioreactor. This sample is automatically analyzed, which brings benefits such as easy
sterilization and quick and straightforward access to the sample. When using at­line sens­
ing technology, the samples are collected periodically, either manually or automatically,

and then analyzed close to the reactor. Thus, however small, there are still time delays
between sampling and analysis. Hence, in this case, the data can be used for monitoring
but often not for control purposes [2]. Similarly, off­line sensors cannot be used as data
source for dynamic process control. The samples are collected and subsequently ana­
lyzed in the laboratory, leading to long time delays before the analysis result is available.
Due to the characteristics mentioned above, off­line monitoring methods should not be
regarded as true PAT technologies, except if there are no alternatives. Nevertheless, off­
line and at­line measurements are often used in validation of other methods, to verify the
accuracy of the in­line and/or on­line sensor measurements [9]. In conclusion, on­line and
in­line sensors are the best option under the PAT principles since they are the techniques
that enable real­time monitoring and process control.
1.3
Biosensors
As a subgroup of chemical sensors, biosensors have a biological recognition element,
which transmits a specific recognition signal of the target analyte in intimate contact with a
chemical or physical transducer, which produces a quantifiable and detectable electrical
signal. This electrical signal can then be sent as an input to a control system or directed
to a digital readout which is used for bioprocess monitoring and control. Biomolecules
such as enzymes, antibodies, other binding elements such as nucleic acids, living cells,
or their fragments can be used as biological detection elements in the biosensors. A redox
reaction, altered ion levels, heat emission, a fluorescent or luminescent signal, or a shift in
mass associated with a surface could all be detected by the transducer (see Figure 1.1).
Figure 1.1: Schematic of a biosensor in general.
Figure 1.1 depicts the general design of a biosensor. In the case of a glucose biosen­
sor, the analyte would be glucose, the bioreceptor would be an immobilized layer of
enzyme and an electroactive substance would be the result of the interaction of these.
Biosensors potentially offer some advantages over conventional analytical techniques
such as chromatography (e.g., HPLC and GC­MS) and enzyme­linked immunosorbent
assay (ELISA) since they can provide real­time analysis, rapid accessibility, and auto­
mated process monitoring [10]. The use of the biological recognition elements is the
strength and weakness of biosensors for bioprocess monitoring. The biological compo­
nents of the biosensor are crucial because they provide the response specificity based on
their evolved ability to recognize the substrates with high affinity (especially proteins) [11].
However, the immobilized biomaterials are relatively incompatible with standard steriliza­
tion procedures, including sterilizing agents and high temperatures. The interaction be­

tween process operating conditions and the biosensors can decrease their performance
over time, reducing the lifetime of the sensors and requiring frequent replacement. For this
reason, it is convenient to regularly check on the biosensor’s health [10]. Biosensors have
been applied, in­line and on­line, to monitor bioprocesses. When used for in­line appli­
cations, the probes contain semi­permeable membrane separators that allow placing the
biosensor inside the reactors. This technology has been demonstrated for enzyme­based
biosensors [12], [13]. On­line applications have been applied using either tangential flow
microfiltration units or flow injection analysis (FIA). In FIA, the samples are taken auto­
matically and injected into a flow of a carrier stream of buffer solution which is transported
through the detector, hence ensuring continuous monitoring of the bioprocess [14].
The amperometric method is the most conventional type of transduction in the biosen­
sors field. Clark and Lyons [15] developed the first biosensors based on glucose oxidase,
and they are the most common type of biosensors since they have been continuously
used in blood glucose monitoring in diabetic patients. The amperometric biosensors de­
termine the current produced between the working and reference electrodes (such as
Ag/AgCl) while the potential is kept at a constant voltage by using a potentiostat. The
target analyte can be reduced or oxidized on the surface of the enzyme­immobilized
electrode and generate a detectable current [11]. The working electrode’s potential is
held at a constant value for the oxidation or reduction of the specific analyte while min­
imizing the reactions of other potential interfering compounds (that may be present in a
fermentation broth). Arrays of oxidases can be used to identify several analytes simulta­
neously since they all function in the same way and produce an electrical signal that is
different and substrate­specific [12], [14]. Amperometric enzyme­coated glucose sensors,
with flow­through design measuring on­line, can potentially be the solution to faster mea­
surements. The biosensor applies the principle of first generation glucose biosensors: A
thin layer of the enzyme glucose oxidase (GOx) comprising the enzyme­bound cofactor
FAD/FADH2 is entrapped into a hydrogel membrane and placed on top of an H2O2
sensitive electrode. Glucose is enzymatically oxidized to gluconic acid, generating H2O2.
The amount of H2O2 produced is proportional to the glucose concentration present. The
redox reactions are summarized in Equations 1.1 to 1.3:
GOx(FAD) + Glucose −→GOx(FADH2) + Gluconicacid
(1.1)
GOx(FADH2) + O2 −→GOx(FAD) + H2O2
(1.2)
H2O2 −→O2 + 2H+ + 2e−
(1.3)
This reaction relies on oxygen as an electron carrier and oxygen may not be avail­
able in quantities large enough. Second generation design of glucose sensors therefore
use so­called mediators to solve the oxygen­deficit problem. The mediator is a synthetic
electron acceptor which function is to move electrons from the GOx to the electrode. The
reactions for this type of sensor­design therefore vary from first generation and looks as
shown in Equations 1.4 to 1.6:
GOx(ox) + Glucose −→GOx(red) + Gluconicacid
(1.4)
GOx(red) + 2M(ox) −→2M(red) + 2H+
(1.5)
2M(red) −→2M(ox) + 2e−
(1.6)
Where 2M(red) and 2M(ox) are the reduced and oxidized form of the mediator, respec­
tively. When the reduced form comes in contact with the electrode it is re­oxidized back
to its oxidized form and then ready to repeat the cycle. The process is visualized in Figure

In the second generation glucose biosensors, oxygen was substituted by the artificial

The enzymatic convertion from glucose to gluconic acid still requires oxygen, the mediator

1.4
Sensor design
For industrial purposes the biosensor not only needs to be able to give precise and
accurate measurements. It also needs to be able to withstand different fermentation envi­
ronments for long­lasting and reliable product­life. Some main issues include the potential
damage from cells in fermentation tanks. Present cells can potentially block the sensor
and render it useless for measurements. The sensor also needs to be capable of han­
dling varying pH­fluctuations as these may occur during fermentation processes, although
typically not very large. The general design of a modern biosensor chip can be seen in
Figure 1.4.

From the figure, it is seen that only the tip (top part of the sensor) is the part of the
chip that facilitates the reactions for measurements. Reference­, counter­ and working
electrodes may not necessarily be placed in that order and design. The working electrode
is the electrode at which the reaction(s) in interest takes place, the counter electrode is
there to complete the circuit by providing the current required for the working electrode
while the reference electrode is, as the name implies, used as a reference by maintaining
a constant and stable potential. The electrodes can be made from various materials,
such as gold and silver, but for the experiments in this study, carbon nanotubes (CNTs)
is the chosen material as a carbon based electrode for glucose sensing. As a carbon
nanomaterial it is inert and resistant to oxidation and will not be part of the redox reactions
taking place. The basic composition of the sensor is shown in Figure 1.5

The biosensor can be divided into four overall parts. With high affinity and selectivity
towards glucose, glucose particles will travel through the top layer of the coated electrode
and make contact with the GOx. This can be seen as the mass transfer part. The redox
reactions, as explained previously then takes place. The reduced form of GOx comes
makes contact and reacts with the oxidized form of mediator, which then becomes re­
duced. The reduced form of the mediator, then gets oxidized again after being in contact
with the electrode, where it ”donates” its electrons, which in return generates a current

1.5
Problem statement
The aim of this thesis is to charactarize an electrochemical biosensor for glucose mon­
itoring, with a custom made membrane, and its suitability for commercial use in mainly
yeast fermentation processes. For this purpose, various experiments will be conducted
in order to evaluate the dynamic range, optimal settings in terms of flow rate, flow times,
cell volumes and the potential interference effects of (bi­)products typically found in yeast
fermentation.

2
Theory
2.1
Amperometry
In electrochemical sensoring, a surface is functionalized in order to make it specific
towards an analyte of interest. In this study glucose is the analyte of interest, thus GOx
is attached to the surface of an electrode. Amperometry is a relatively simple and cheap
technique, as the instruments required are quite affordable [18]. The previously men­
tioned three­electrode biosensor system consists of a working electrode, reference elec­
trode and a counter electrode. The working electrode is maintained at a desired potential
relative to the reference electrode (which will be a Ag/AgCl­electrode). The instrument
able to do this is called a potentiostat. An illustration representing the circuit of a three­
electrode system connected to a potentiostat can be seen in figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Circuit of a potentiostat connected to a three­electrode system [19].
A desired potential (UWE/RE) is put into the instrument and the control amplifier (CA)
maintains the potential between the reference­ and working electrode by either draining
or removing current through the counter electrode. In the case where hydrogen perioxide
­ a product from the enzymatic reaction ­ is oxidized (as shown in equation 1.3), current
is provided through the counter electrode to compensate for the change.
2.2

3.1
Biosensor fabrication
For this project, a custom made electrode membrane was specifically designed. The
fabrication process is will be described here.
Three­electrode screen­printed configuration with working, counter and reference elec­
trodes was chosen for the design of the amperometric biosensor. In comparison to finger­
shaped electrodes, the three electrodes were positioned in concentric circles, ensuring a
uniform distribution of the electric field lines between them and making the arrangement
independent of the direction of flow in stirred fluids. Because fluids produce round droplets
on surfaces, the circular shape was also well suited for functionalization by hand distribut­
ing an enzyme solution. As shown in Figure 3.1 the working electrode was placed in the
centre, surrounded by the reference and counter electrodes. Thus, the reference elec­
trode was close to the working electrode, and the counter electrode had the largest area,
assuring the smallest possible potential drop when delivering or draining current.
Figure 3.1: Three­electrode setup on ceramics with screen­printed electrodes (WE: car­
bon, CE: carbon, RE:Ag/AgCl) [26].
The CNT based working electrode was designed with a diameter of 4 mm, while the
carbon counter and Ag/AgCl reference electrodes had a width of 1 mm. The electrodes
were spaced 1 mm apart. A procedure based on enzyme crosslinking with bovine serum
albumin (BSA), a protein derived from bovine plasma, was employed to functionalize the
sensor. This immobilization approach is less complicated than more elaborate protocols
like covalent or bioaffinity bonding, and it delivers comparable long­term stability of up
to ten weeks [27]. Crosslinking is defined as the process of joining two biomolecules by
using an intermediary molecule, in this case glutaraldehyde. Glutaraldehyde is a homob­
ifunctional crosslinker that has an aldehyde group on both ends and can attach to amino
groups on the surface of biomolecules covalently [28]. By crosslinking the enzyme with
BSA, a matrix is produced that preserves the enzyme’s natural form. Figure 3.2 shows a
schematic representation of the crosslinked matrix.

==PAGE:22==
Figure 3.2: Crosslinked film of enzyme, bovine serum albumin (BSA) and glutaraldehyde
on a surface [18].
1% Nafion and Tetrathiafulvalene (TTF) (50mM) in ethanol was prepared and then
drop­casted onto the CNT­based working electrode. TTF being the mediator, is an organic
compound, which has shown to be an excellent mediator [29]. In 500 µL of 10 mM phos­
phate buffered saline (PBS), 10 mg glucose oxidase (activity 100 ­ 250 U/mg) and 20 mg
bovine serum albumin were dissolved. Following thorough mixing, 25 µL of 8% glutaralde­
hyde (v/v) was added and the solution was thoroughly mixed once more (all chemicals
obtained from Sigma Aldrich). When utilized with diffusion­limiting membranes, these val­
ues provided the greatest sensor signal per applied mass of enzymes and demonstrated
reliable sensor performance. Other formulations yielded a small number of functioning
biosensors per fabricated batch or had a narrow linear range. A microliter pipette was
used to dispense 2 µL onto the carbon working electrode, which was then allowed to dry
in the air. Poly(dimethylsiloxane) (PDMS) or polyurethane (PU) were used to prepare
the diffusion­limiting membrane on the surface of working electrode. PDMS membranes
were made by mixing the base polymer and curing agent in a 20:1 (w/w) ratio and then
degassing the mixture. When partially cured in water, this ratio was found to generate per­
meable membranes. Lower base ratios resulted in non­permeable membranes, whereas
higher ratios proved to be too viscous to be processed consistently. Using a spin­coater
(WS650MZ, Laurell, USA), the PDMS was spin­coated onto the working electrode for 30
seconds at 3000 or 6000 rpm with a 500 rpm/s2 acceleration.
PU pellets were dissolved in a mixture of tetrahydrofuran (THF) and dimethylformamide
(DMF) to make polyurethane membranes. A series of membranes were prepared in order
to assess the impact of various fabrication factors on the linear range. A mixture of THF
and DMF was used to make PU solutions with concentrations of 4%, 6%, 8%, and 10%
(w/w). In the used solvents, higher polymer concentrations were found to be indissoluble.
The THF­DMF ratio was varied to 9:1, 7:3, 5:5, 3:7, and 1:9, yielding five series of four
concentrations each. Even after many days, PU was discovered to be indissoluble in the
mixture with the ratio THF:DMF 1:9, hence this series was not used in subsequent studies.
On the enzyme layer, 2 µL of the PU solution was applied and dried under air. A scan­
ning electron microscope was used to evaluate the surface morphology and thickness
of the manufactured membranes (S­4700, Hitachi, Japan). PBS was used to condition
electrodes for 12 hours before amperometric measurements. PBS was used to create
glucose solutions. MultiEmStat3 potentiostat were used to do the measurements at room
temperature (PalmSens, The Netherlands).

5
Discussion
After various experiments with the glucose sensor, it seems like a promising tech­
nology for on­line glucose monitoring, with the applied membrane­coating, but it is not
completely flawless and ready to enter a commercial market on a big industrial scale. It
is rather more suited for small lab­scale experiments with low glucose concentrations, as
it is right now.
First of all, the sensor did not exhibit consistent current measurements during exper­
iments. One calibration curve may read a given concentration of glucose at a certain
current­value, but when repeating the experiment with another sensor, the same given
concentration of glucose may give a substantially different current­measurement. Mean­
ing that one­fits­all current­to­concentration conversion cannot be applied to a set of sen­
sors, as they would all need a calibration curve before application. This is arguably not
a huge disadvantage, but it makes it less of a plug­and­play product. The reason for this
variation can be of many. Firstly it could be due to production variations, e.g. different
thickness of the applied membrane.
Another aspect to take from the experiments is the interference studies. Compounds,
such as ethanol and succinic acid had nearly no effect of the measurements, while com­
pounds like lactic acid and sodium pyruvate seemingly had a noticeable effect, but it can­
not be said for sure, as these were also those with the highest variations in measure­
ments, as seen in Figure 4.3 in Section 4.3. In general, the sensor handles the potential
interference products quite well. In the off­line fermentation measurements (as drop test
measurements), the sensor was also quite capable of measuring samples from an actual
fermentation process with good precision, meaning that it also works when the sample
being measured does not only consist of buffer solution and glucose.
In the flow­through cell, the sensor showed higher linearity when comparing to drop­
test measurements. This can be explained by higher dissolved oxygen levels. Aspects
to consider when looking at the flow­through design is definitely the rate of flow and the
size of the cell, according to the results from the experiment. The correct flow rate is
crucial for good and readable data. It is desired to have the flow­through measurement
working at the lowest possible flow rate, as the leads to lower sample volumes needed.
Furthermore cell size did also seem to have importance in signal strengths. It seems
somewhat mysterious why half a cell volume yields double signal strength, apart from
probably increased oxygen levels. The right cell volume is a balance between a long
enough retention time to obtain a readable measurement and short enough retention time
to replenish the cell with oxygen. This retention time obviously also depends on the flow
rate.
The last experiment conducted (the fermentation simulation) is a very contradicting.
Results from previous experiments indicated that the sensor would be able to handle the
experiment quite well, but looking at the results, that was not the case. The sensor may
somehow have been damaged in between calibration and the experiment. The liquid in
the cell may have dissolved some parts of the membrane­coating. Signal strength was
weakened substantially and linearity was seems to have decreased as well. Furthermore,
when looking at the graph displaying the results from the experiment (Figure 4.11, Section
4.8), other obscure things happened during the experiment. From hour 5 to hour 6, there
is a sudden change in measurement values, implying that the change of rate, which the
buffer solution (used for dilution) was added, had an effect on the measurement. What the
connection exactly is, is difficult to tell. What makes is even more obscure, is that similar,
but smaller, changes also occur throughout the experiment, but these happen outside

reasons for why the experiment turned out the way it did, is that vibrations on the surface of

was noted throughout some of the previous experiments, when sometimes accidentally
hitting the table, on which the instruments were placed, caused intense fluctuations of the
measurements.

Future work
For further developing and testing of the sensor, a series of experiments could be
conducted to prepare it for potential commercial implementation.
First of all, the sensor was never tested in a real fermentation process. This would
certainly be part of future experiments, to truly test the sensor in the environment it is
designed for.
Developing further on a diffusion­limiting membrane, maybe a completely different
type is one approach to increase linearity and reach higher glucose measurements. An­
other way, which actually does not involve further development of the sensor it self, but
rather the measurement technique, would be to implement an intermediate step between
sampling and measuring, where the sample volume is diluted. This obviously introduces
some difficulties, as you would need a separate container with a suitable liquid for dilution
and furthermore, it also introduces a small delay in the measuring process. Increasing
the concentration of the mediator (TTF) could also potentially benefit the dynamic range.
Another very interesting thing to further investigate is the cell volume. The results
from the relevant experiments, showed a very big difference in signal strength. It could
potentially lead to promising discoveries. It would be interesting to test the correlation and
further optimize the cell volume.
Furthermore, the fermentation simulation experiment (or a similar experiment that ap­
plies the sensor for a longer period of time) would need to be investigated further, to
test if the sensor can withstand measurements over even longer periods, e.g. 48 or 72
hours and determine its product­life, i.e. for how long it can ”survive” while giving precise
measurements.

Conclusion
The glucose sensor’s performance has been characterized and evaluated through
various experiments. The exact concentration interval, in which the sensor was able to
detect glucose linearly, was not found, as the individual sensors used, performed differ­
ently. However, it seemed to be up to around 100 mM and possibly more, when measuring
in a flow­through cell. Conditions related to implementability in an on­line connection to a
bioreactor, were investigated. Compounds often found in yeast fermentation processes,
such as lactic acid and ethanol were examined for their potential interfering effects. Most
of the compounds examined did not seem to have a huge impact on the measurements.
Off­line yeast fermentation measurements confirmed that the sensor was able to measure
in conditions with other compounds than glucose and buffer solution. Trace metals, found
in the more complex de Bont buffer, did appear to have a small effect as well.
Furthermore volumetric flow and cell volumes were optimized to yield most readable
measurements. It turned out that a flow rate at around 3.2 mL
min gave the beast readings
and that lower cell volumes favored measurement readability as well.
With the optimized settings, a fermentation simulation experiment was performed. It
essentially mimicked the glucose concentration profile found in typical yeast fermentation.
The results from the experiment did not turn out as expected. It is suspected that some
undetermined error between the calibration and experiment start has caused the results
to turn out this way. Despite the results from this experiment, the sensor turned out to
exhibit very good performance in a real fermentation experiment, where the biosensor
was connected for on­line measurement.
To truly tell its suitability and implementability for commercial use, a series of experi­
ments have been suggested as part of further research.
