Abstract
For almost decades now the quantum computation field has been looking for scal-
able and tunable devices to hold quantum information (qubits). One of the many
possibilities for doing so is an approach using location qubits, relying on stacking
crystal phase quantum dots along nanowires. This thesis focuses on a trial-and-
error iterative process aiming at growing such nanowires, with a special focus in
the end on the quantum dots that can appear in the shell of those nanowires. It
will show that there are suitable properties for those wires, what the next steps are
towards achieving location qubits, and how the quantum dots in the shell can act
as single photon sources which could also be helpful down the road.

Introduction
Whether through simulations on classical computers or actual quantum systems,
quantum computation has been around for decades now. Its funding principle
is based on the superposition property of quantum systems, which allows for a
system to be in a superposition of states: whereas classical computers store the
information in bits (either 0 or 1), quantum bits (qubits) allow for an infinite
number of states which could describe them:
|Ψ⟩= α |0⟩+β |1⟩
This proves to be an interesting property especially for complex calculations
as quantum implementations of algorithms (such as the Grover algorithm [5] for
instance) allow for the evaluation of multiple solutions at the same time. This
can result in an increased ability to solve certain classes of problems (i.e. NP-
problems [8]).
However, to perform such calculations we must have a way to store the informa-
tion, hence the interest of the qubits. They come in many forms such as spin-based
qubits, time-of-arrival qubits or even what is the focus of our research activity, lo-
cation qubits. [7]
These location qubits are based on a couple of neighbouring quantum dots.
More specifically, we will consider crystal-phase quantum dots, which are quan-
tum dots formed by the alternation of crystal phases along a nanowire. In short,
the nanowire is a pillar-like structure, in our case with a height of the order of a
couple of micrometers, in which the charges are confined by an alternation of the
crystal phases along the nanowire. As a consequence of such a confinement, the
allowed levels of energy are discretized, which results in spectral emission with
narrow spectral width. Put in context with the development of quantum internet
and the need for scalability [13], a platform for quantum dots would need to be
[7] :
• tunable in order to build a quantum dot designed for one’s needs;
• possessing a photonic interface to communicate with other devices easily;
• "scalable to multiple qubits";
• at the nanoscale so that one can create them in bulk.
5
==PAGE:6==
In that spirit, this project aims at creating nanowires that would fulfill such
requirements in order to be able to propose a platform in which the qubit would
have the following functioning (see Figure 1). In said figure, the electron (blue
dot) can be in the conduction band at the left or at the right of the alternation of
crystal phases, representing respectively the |0⟩& |1⟩states.
Figure 1: Design of a location qubit [7]
Finally, even if the growth of a nanowire with multiple quantum dots was
achieved, it remains tgat one would need to characterize its optical properties
such as, among others, its photoluminescence (PL) spectrum of emission, the
excitation lifetime and its ability to act as a single-photon source (i.e. its auto-
correlation function and especially g2(τ = 0)). This project mostly considered
Gallium-Arsenide (GaAs) nanowires in the scope of its study because it results in
III-V semiconductors, known for their electron mobility and direct bandgap. [15]
As clean (or narrow) emission in the expected range for Wurtzite and Zincblende
was never achieved completely or in a satisfactory manner, it was decided in the
end to focus on the characterization of quantum dots which can appear in the
Aluminium-Gallium-Arsenide (AlGaAs) shell of GaAs nanowires, and if possi-
ble the influence of the physical properties of the shell for instance its thickness,
on the aforementioned optical properties.

the quantum dot states, provided that the laser emits in the Wurtzite-Zincblende
(WZ-ZB) GaAs bandwidth (780-860nm). It must however be noted that those

AlGaAs/GaAs Quantum Dots
AlGaAs/GaAs quantum dots (QDs) can come from various causes, from alloy
fluctuations [4] to band misalignments and crystal defects. [stark_shifts] In the
scope of this project, the focus is mostly on Au-catalyzed and Self-catalyzed Al-
GaAs nanowires grown through molecular beam epitaxy (MBE). From the pre-

wavelength values can suffer a red or blue shift depending on the physical charac-
teristics of nanowires, especially the width of both the core and the shell.
It has also already been shown that the impact of a shell is still very important
as it increases tremendously the density of quantum dots [3] as well as making
them have a high optical gain. [14] We must also note that the bandwidth for
certain crystal phases such as pure WZ or ZB has been studied before, giving us

2
Crystal-Phase Nanowires
2.1
On the growth of our nanowires
The nanowires under study within the scope of this project were all grown by
Center for Nanoscience and Nanotechnology (C2N) under the supervision of Dr.
Federico Panciera and PhD student Nam Hong using Molecular Beam Epitaxy.
[9] [1]
Molecular Beam Epitaxy (MBE) is a technique used to grow thin films of
crystalline materials with high precision. The process involves depositing atoms
or molecules onto a substrate in a vacuum chamber. The substrate is heated to
a high temperature. The atoms or molecules then condense onto the substrate,
forming a thin film. The objective is to create a controlled environment in which
atoms or molecules can be deposited onto a substrate with high precision. A vac-
uum chamber is needed to create a low-pressure environment in order to prevent
the deposition of impurities onto the substrate.
The growth process in MBE involves the deposition of atoms or molecules
onto a heated substrate. The atoms or molecules are directed towards the sub-
strate in a beam, which is created using a molecular beam source, which usually
consists of a crucible containing the material to be deposited, and a heater to va-
porize said material. The thickness of the film can be controlled by adjusting the
rate of deposition and the temperature of the substrate. In our case it means that
we can create nanowires from the bottom-up layer by layer.
So as to increase the precision of the growth, the Vapor-Liquid-Solid (VLS)
method is used. Its interest is in the use of a catalyst, which would either be gold or
Gallium in the case of the GaAs nanowires of the study, which is used to promote
the growth of the nanowire. The process involves the use of a vapor, which is used
to transport the material to be deposited onto the substrate. The vapor is typically
a gas, such as hydrogen or nitrogen. The substrate is typically made of a crys-
talline material, which provides a template for the growth of the nanowire. This
catalyst is used because the materials used for the production of the microfilm (for
instance a GaAs layer) are under the form of a vapor and it is difficult to sublime
them. The VLS technique comes in at that point. Its funding principle is that by
forming a liquid droplet, the material to be deposited will be dissolved into the
liquid droplet, forming a supersaturated solution which then solidifies, forming a
9
==PAGE:10==
nanowire. The need for the sublimation of the nanowire-constituve compounds
has been circumvented. The shape of the nanowire can be controlled by adjust-
ing the shape of the catalyst droplet as well. After the nanowire has been grown,
Aluminium can be heated and introduced into the vacuum chamber in order to
create the shell that will wrap the nanowire. The composition of the shell (i.e. its
Aluminium and Gallium percentages) is tuned by adjusting the temperature of the
crucibles, which results in the evolution of the Ga and Al fluxes.
It still needs to be figured out how to select the crystal phase of the layer grow-
ing in order to produce in the end location qubits, which require an alternation of
crystal phases between Wurtzite and Zincblende in this case. The following model
[9] was used for the growth and it was at the heart of the project to give feedback
on the nanowires its application resulted in. Further more, it is according to this
model possible to tune the width of the nanowire by adjusting the contact angle
and surface energy difference (i.e. growth regime).

2.2
Methodology
The wires studied throughout this project all point towards one objective : creating
a location qubit with a by-design embedded photonic interface. A qubit photonic
10
==PAGE:11==
interface is a system that allows for the transfer of quantum information between
a qubit and a photon. In this case, the ideal nanowire with a crystal phase distribu-
tion allowing for it to be used a location qubit would act as both the holder of the
information (qubit) and the photonic interface as nanowires emit photons.As such,
it is crucial to characterize several of the optical properties of the sample to work
towards that goal, and the main features observed in the scope of this research are
:
• Photoluminescence measurements;
• Lifetime measurements;
• Autocorrelation function g2(τ) measurements.
Photo-luminescence measurements can be used to study the optical properties
of quantum dots, such as their bandgap energy and the width of their emission
peaks. The latter can provide information about the distribution of defects within
a quantum dot or nanowire while the other will inform on the composition of
the nanowire: one would be able to determine based on a PL spectra whether a
nanowire is emitting photons with an energy associated with Wurtzite or ZincB-
lence.
This would be done by exciting a nanowire with an incoming laser, which
in the case of this project would be a 532nm Continuous Wave Laser or 400nm
pulsed laser of tunable pulse frequency. The incoming photons excite the nanowire,
which in turn emits back photons as it goes back to its normal state. Those photons
are sent to a spectrometer which can identify the spectral features of the nanowire
such as the energy of the photons, the width of the peaks and the intensity (i.e. the
number of Counts per second).
In the context of a qubit photonic interface, the lifetime of a quantum dot is
particularly important as it determines the time during which the quantum dot will
retain the information before emitting a photon. Lifetime measurements can be
used to optimize the design of qubit photonic interfaces for applications such as
quantum computation and quantum internet. This technique is useful for studying
the efficiency of light-emitting materials and can be used to optimize the design
of light-emitting devices. In this project, it is done by sending 400nm pulses at a
rate of 40MHz and capturing back the time-resolved photo-luminescence spectra
11
==PAGE:12==
thanks to a SPAD (Single Photon Avalanche Diode) or APD (Avalanche Photodi-
ode), which differ mainly by their number of dark counts per second. By analyzing
the decay of the emission over time, the lifetime of the material can be determined.
Autocorrelation measurements are also important in the context of a qubit
photonic interface. In particular, it is of utmost interest that the quantum dots
forming the location qubit act as single photon sources, which emit one photon
at a time as the emission of several photons could lead to errors such as exciting
another quantum dots or readout errors. In particular, this is important for appli-
cations that could be imagined down the road for location qubits such as quantum
cryptography, where the security of the system relies on the ability to generate and
detect single photons. By characterizing the ability of the sample to send exactly
one photon, it becomes therefore possible to optimize the design of the qubit pho-
tonic interface that is a nanowire and the quantum dots it is holding.This would be
done in the setup of this project by splitting the emitted light into two paths which
are recombined afterwards at detectors using a time-tagger solution. By analyzing
the correlation between the two paths, it is possible to determine if the sample is
acting as a single photon source. For example if the sample emits one photon at a
time, the autocorrelation function will show a peak at zero time delay.
In summary, qubit photonic interfaces are important systems for the transfer of
quantum information between a qubit and a photon. Photoluminescence measure-
ments can be used to study the optical properties of quantum dots, while lifetime
measurements and autocorrelation measurements are particularly important in the
context of a qubit photonic interface. By using these techniques, researchers can
optimize the design of qubit photonic interfaces for applications such as quan-
tum computation and quantum internet, as well as for the development of new
technologies for applications such as quantum cryptography.

Results
This project is based on a collaboration between C2N and Nika Akopian’s team,
both parties fulfilling complementary roles in such a process. As such, the former
is dedicated to mastering to grow nanowires using MBE to control the crystal
phase during the growth so that the aim of creating nanowires suitable for location
qubits is made possible whereas the DTU team is focused on characterising the
samples sent by C2N in order to give them feedback on the optical properties of
the grown nanowires. It thus created an iterative process based on a back-and-forth
between the labs which was at the heart of this project.

Discussion
While for various reasons it was not possible in the end to get more insights
into the possible correlation between the physical properties of the AlGaAs shell
aroung GaAs nanowires, it was still shown that they can act as quantum dots in
some cases. This would call for a more comprehensive statistical study with a
larger scope and increased number of samples in order to confirm that this be-
haviour is, if not deterministic, appearing with a high probability.
Focusing only on quantum dots, other properties of the shell can be considered
for their impact on the ability to produce quantum dots within the structure. A
good example of that is the percentage of Aluminium in a AlxGa1−xAs shell :
Could Aluminium richer shells have an impact of the lifetime or bandwidth of
the quantum dots that can appear within the shell ? One could also thin about
changing the very structure of the shell. Design with double layers of shell have
already been put forward in the literature. [15] Perhaps some set of parameters
could lead to quantum dots with lifetimes in the order of tens of nanoseconds but
it is only through fumbling around and iterating on acquired knowledge that such
a nanowire and its shell be produced.
Going back to the larger scope of the research project and getting out of the
focus on the quantum dots in the shell, it is very interesting to note that the tech-
nology for the selection of the crystal phase to grow has now been developed and
is working, or at least has some promising results. A way to go forward in the
direction of location qubits applicable to larger quantum computation schemes
could be to indeed try and grow nanowires with crystal phase alternation in order
to actually experiment how they behave under the excitation from a light source.

5
Summary
The goal of this project being the growth and characterization of a nanowire with
an actual crystal phase configuration suitable for a location qubit application, this
is still an ongoing project. However there are several findings that were discov-
ered or confirmed over the course of this research journey. These could be called
success criteria or welcomed prerequisites in order to grow the best nanowires
possible for our research.
First and foremost, we can put forward the presence of a shell is very impor-
tant. Not only it increases the intensity of the photoluminescence of nanowires,
but it also does not damages substantially the cleanness of the emission (i.e. it
does not increase the width of the peaks that much). The increase in intensity is
crucial for us as well, as it allows for easier and more precise lifetime and auto-
correlation measurements as the ratio signal-to-noise improves with the number
of counts per second. This also can compensate the losses of photons due to the
beamsplitter needed for the creation of a second optical path in an autocorrelation
measurement.
Next, it can be said that the elimination of the Gallium droplet on top of the
nanowire is not that essential as far as we are concerned. Its presence does not
have that much of an impact of the energy bandgap of the nanowire. It still must
be shown that it does not affect the lifetime of future quantum dots in the nanowire
or the ability of the QDs to act as single photon sources.
Finally, it has been shown that the AlGaAs shell of GaAs nanowires, no mat-
ter its thickness, can behave as a quantum dot. While we do not have the exact
cause of this, the hypotheses in the literature seem plausible in our case and the
observation of quantum-dot-like behavior rejoin. We even pushed forward to see
that they are able to hold information for a couple of nanoseconds and could be a
very good photonic interface (high intensity of photoluminescence and can act as
a single photon source).

Experimental setup
Layout of the setup
The setup is as follows :
• The sample is cooled down in a CryoVac to a temperature of 6K (can go
down to 4K with an extra thermal shield) and a laser beam is focused on it
from the top by an objective.
• In the optical path between the laser there are two beamsplitters, one leading
to a white light source and a CCD camera to observe in real time what is
going on while the other is to redirect part of the laser beam to a power meter
as well as the photoluminescence from the sample towards the spectrometer.
• The spectrometer diffracts the incoming photoluminescence with a tunable
grating before either sending it to its camera or in the other outgoing port
where the incoming light is coupled into a fiber plugged into an APD for
lifetime and autocorrelation measurements.

Spectrometer and photon detectors
The spectrometers we use are Princeton
Instruments Acton SpectraPro SP-2750 spectrometers with three different gratings
(150gr/mm, 1200gr/mm and 1800gr/mm). In front of each of them there is a lens
to focus the beam at the slit. After being diffracted, the light can either be sent to
the camera which has an efficiency of around 95% in the wavelength range that
interests us [10] or single photon detectors.
The detectors are of two kinds :
• one Photon Micro Devices PDM APD with a 20% efficiency and a 50ps
time resolution (suitable for lifetime measurements);
• two Excelitas SPCM-AQRH-FC APDs with efficiencies around 45% and a
350ps time resolution (better for autocorrelation measurements). [12]
