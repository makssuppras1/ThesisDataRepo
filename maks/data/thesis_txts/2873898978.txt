Abstract
Floating offshore wind unlocks deep­water wind resources but places stringent demands
on station keeping. This thesis investigates shared mooring systems for floating offshore
wind turbines using a spar­buoy platform and progressively larger arrays (1×1, 1×2, 2×2).
A hybrid chain–polyester–chain concept with short suspended lines is designed via an
iterative screening loop. Numerical simulations representative of an operational sea state
and a storm sea state are used to assess global response, offsets, and mooring ten­
sions. The study provides comparative trends and practical guidance for early­phase
array screening. The results indicate that directional reinforcement aligned with the pre­
vailing weather reduces unnecessary material while improving station keeping, transverse
shared lines effectively restrain lateral motion but can increase roll–yaw coupling if yaw
stiffness is insufficient, and short lines with polyester segments reduce suspended weight
and seabed footprint without exceeding design tensions in the tested cases. Detailed
accidental and fatigue verifications, and site­specific optimisation, are left for future work.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation and background
Climate change and the the increasing demand for energy require an immediate change
in energy resources. One promising solution is floating offshore turbines, but it presents
several challenges that need to be resolved.
1.1.1
Climate change and the role of energy sources
One of the most pressing issues of our time is climate change, a phenomenon closely
linked to human activity, particularly the intensive use of fossil fuels and coal since the In­
dustrial Revolution. The changes observed in the global climate system are now evident:
extreme events such as exceptional heat waves, intense rainfall, uncontrolled fires, dev­
astating floods, and increasingly violent storms are occurring with increasing frequency
[1]. Such events, once rare, are becoming common and indicate a clear alteration in the
climate. Underlying this process is the extensive use of non­renewable energy sources,
particularly coal, oil, and natural gas. Although these fuels are still widely used due to their
accessibility and existing infrastructure, they are one of the main causes of greenhouse
gas emissions that are responsible for global warming [2]. The current global energy sys­
tem remains heavily dependent on these sources to meet the growing demand for energy,
both in industrial sectors and in the daily life of modern societies.
An additional stressor is global population growth, which is expected to lead to an increase
in energy demand in the coming decades [2]. If this growing demand continues to be met
through the reliance on fossil fuels, it will exacerbate air pollution and accelerate climate
change, leading to increasingly severe and harder­to­mitigate consequences.
Beyond its direct environmental impacts, climate change is also expected to generate
indirect consequences, such as intensified conflicts over the management and access to
essential natural resources, including water and food[1]. Addressing these challenges is
essential and requires a fundamental rethinking of energy production and consumption
systems, focusing on a sustainable and resilient model.
Renewable sources, such as solar, wind, hydroelectric, and geothermal energy, repre­
sent one of the main alternatives for decarbonizing the energy system and reducing de­
pendence on fossil fuels [3]. The transition to a cleaner and more sustainable energy
system is no longer an optional choice, it is an urgent necessity in order to contain the
effects of climate change and hereby ensure a more stable future for coming generations.
Renewable energy sources are those that naturally regenerate within a relatively short
time frame and, unlike fossil fuels, are not depleted through use. A comparison between
these two categories clearly highlights the limitations of nonrenewable sources: for ex­
ample, a single liter of petrol is derived from crude oil that required millions of years to
form. Once consumed, it irretrievable and generates polluting emissions that contribute
to global warming. Renewable sources, on the contrary, harness recurring natural phe­
nomena such as sunlight, wind, or wave motion. For example, wind energy does not
require millennia to regenerate; instead, it is continuously available, as illustrated by Den­
mark, where wind energy has become a central component of the national energy mix.
The installation of wind turbines allows the kinetic energy of the wind to be converted into
electricity with a significantly lower environmental impact than that associated with fossil
fuels.

However, it is important to consider the entire life cycle of renewable technologies, in­
cluding the processes of production, transport, and installation of the necessary infras­
tructure. These also depend in part on non­renewable resources and involve a certain
level of emissions. However, the overall environmental balance of renewable sources is
largely favorable compared to conventional sources [1]. In conclusion, addressing the
climate crisis and the pursuit of net zero emissions require a strong emphasis on the de­
velopment and deployment of renewable energy sources. Wind energy, in particular, is
one of the most promising solutions for significantly reducing greenhouse gas emissions
and promoting a transition to a sustainable and resilient energy system [2].
1.1.2
Wind energy for energy transition
When discussing energy transition and climate goals, it is paramount to refer to the Paris
Agreement, which establishes the need to limit the global temperature rise to below 2°C
above pre­industrial levels, preferably aiming for the more ambitious limit of 1,5°C [4]. To
continue this trajectory, at the 28th United Nations Climate Change Conference (COP28),
130 countries committed to tripling the global renewable energy production capacity by
2030 [4]. According to the latest estimates, achieving these targets will require at least
2 TW of installed wind capacity by 2030, and around 8 TW by 2050 [2]. In this context,
offshore wind energy is one of the most promising and strategic technologies, with a po­
tential contribution estimated at 380 GW by 2030 [5]. However, in order to achieve this
expansion, it will be essential to develop projects in deep waters. Currently, most offshore
wind turbines are installed on bottom­fixed foundations, suitable for water depths of less
than 60m [5]. Considering that approximately 80% of global offshore wind potential is
located at depths greater than 60m, it will be necessary to resort to floating offshore wind
technology, i.e. floating wind turbines, which allow the use of energy resources located
in areas previously inaccessible with conventional technologies to be exploited [6]. Off­
shore wind power is particularly well suited to address the challenges of energy security
due to its high capacity factor and lack of competition for land use [2]. Furthermore, it
compensates for the growing instability of sources such as hydroelectric and thermoelec­
tric power, which are subject to the effects of climate change such as droughts and heat
waves [1]. Recent instances in Brazil and Europe show how extreme weather events
have already put pressure on traditional energy systems.
From an economic standpoint, offshore wind energy has become increasingly competi­
tive. Despite the global increase in raw material costs and geopolitical turmoil, the lev­
elised cost of energy (LCOE) of offshore wind has fallen significantly in the last decade
[3], resulting in lower costs than gas­fired generation in many countries. In China, LCOE
is particularly low due to economies of scale, cost stability, and local supply chains [3].
The global trend shows that offshore wind costs will continue to fall in the coming years.
This technology also plays a crucial role in the promotion of industrial development and
decarbonization. Offshore wind powers energy­intensive sectors such as steel and refin­
ing and is key to the production of green hydrogen and alternative fuels. Furthermore, its
proximity to ports encourages the creation of marine energy hubs, with positive effects on
economic competitiveness and job creation [7].
In addition, offshore wind energy contributes to the construction of a sustainable Blue
Economy, due to strategic marine planning practices and the participation of local stake­
holders. In Europe, each gigawatt installed generates billions of euros in added value,
and the wind sector is expected to account for almost 1% of the GDP of EU by 2030 [7].
Public­private partnerships have been instrumental in making offshore wind a mature and
competitive technology and will be even more crucial in addressing new challenges in
emerging markets. Initiatives such as the Global Offshore Wind Alliance (GOWA) and the

1.1.3
Floating Offshore Wind Turbines vs Fixed­Bottom Turbines
Despite the increasing urgency of the energy transition and the promising potential of off­
shore wind, the deployment of floating offshore wind farms remains extremely limited. As
of 2024, only a handful of commercial­scale floating wind farms are operational worldwide.
This stands in stark contrast to fixed­bottom offshore wind, which represents the vast ma­
jority of the 75.2 GW of global installed offshore capacity at the end of 2023 [5]. The reason
for this discrepancy is a combination of technological immaturity, economic challenges,
and regulatory uncertainty. Fixed­bottom offshore wind turbines, anchored directly to the
seabed via monopiles, jackets, or gravity­based foundations, have benefited from over
two decades of industrial­scale deployment, particularly in Europe and China. These sys­
tems are technically mature, well­understood, and benefit from optimized supply chains
and standardized installation practices. In economic terms, fixed­bottom projects have
reached what is referred to in the industry as a “bankable business case”: the levelized
cost of energy (LCOE) has dropped significantly in recent years, making them attractive
to investors and governments alike. By contrast, floating offshore wind turbines repre­
sent a younger and more complex technology. They are designed to operate at water
depths greater than 60m, where fixed­bottom solutions become technically and economi­
cally unfeasible. Around 80% of global offshore wind potential lies in such deep waters [6],
making floating wind farms essential for scaling up offshore renewable energy sources.
However, the technology is still in its early commercial stages. Most floating turbines rely
on moored platforms, such as spar­buoys, semi­submersibles, or tension­leg platforms,
which introduce a host of engineering and logistical challenges.
One of the primary barriers is cost. The LCOE for floating wind energy remains signifi­
cantly higher than that of fixed­bottom installations, mainly due to the lack of economies
of scale, limited industrial experience, and the complexity of anchoring, dynamic cabling,
and maintenance operations at sea [8]. According to BloombergNEF, current floating wind
projects often have LCOEs exceeding $150/MWh, compared to $95–114/MWh for fixed­
bottom turbines in mature markets like Germany and the UK [9]. Until costs decrease
and more demonstration projects prove technical reliability, floating wind farms remain a
higher­risk investment for most developers.
In addition to cost, regulatory and permit frameworks for floating wind farms are often un­
derdeveloped or non­existent, especially in emerging markets. Infrastructure challenges,
such as the need for deep­water ports, specialized vessels, and floating­specific installa­
tion equipment, also slow the deployment of floating wind farms. Moreover, uncertainty
in long­term maintenance and grid integration strategies adds further hesitation among
stakeholders. Nonetheless, the outlook for floating wind is evolving. Technological inno­
vation, public­private partnerships, and targeted policy support are beginning to shift the
balance. As the demand for renewable energy grows and suitable shallow­water sites
become scarce, floating offshore wind farms are expected to play a pivotal role in un­
locking vast untapped resources [6]. Countries like Norway, Japan, and South Korea are
actively investing in pilot projects and long­term strategies to de­risk the technology and
build scalable floating wind capacity [2].

Litterature review
2.1
Environmental impact
Assuming that floating offshore wind technology reaches full maturity, it is essential to an­
ticipate and assess its environmental consequences. Past experience has demonstrated
the importance of assessing ecological impacts before scaling new marine technologies.
If the negative effects on marine ecosystems exceed acceptable thresholds, or if the ben­
efits are outweighed by long­term damage, then the feasibility of FLOW, despite its tech­
nical promise, could be compromised.
2.1.1
Positive environmental impacts of FLOW
Floating offshore wind turbines (FLOW) introduce physical structures into marine environ­
ments that can create new habitats and alter ecosystem dynamics. The substructures,
floating platforms, mooring lines and anchoring systems provide surfaces for coloniza­
tion and egg deposition in otherwise unsuitable areas, supporting species from benthic
to pelagic zones and increasing the complexity of the regional ecosystem [10, 11, 12, 13,
14, 15].
FLOW structures can function as artificial reefs and fish aggregation devices, attracting
species through shelter, resource availability, predator avoidance, and the provision of
spawning or nursery habitat [16, 17, 15, 13, 18]. Structural complexity and material com­
position influence colonisation and species diversity, with intricate designs, such as “fish
hotels” or added rock layers, supporting richer assemblages [19, 20, 21, 22]. Bio­fueling
communities and local pelagic prey can further attract higher predators, including ma­
rine mammals [12, 23, 24]. Observed reef effects can extend hundreds of meters from
structures [12], though their intensity is context­dependent.
By disrupting stratification and increasing vertical mixing, especially across the thermo­
cline, FLOW structures can enhance nutrient availability, boosting phytoplankton produc­
tion, and support greater zooplankton and fish biomass [25, 26, 27]. These productivity­
driven effects may indirectly benefit higher trophic levels, but their magnitude varies with
site­specific factors such as water depth, natural stratification, turbine size, and array den­
sity [28]. Temporal variability in primary productivity means that aggregations of fish and
predators may be seasonal or episodic rather than permanent [23].
Many FLOW sites establish exclusion zones that restrict commercial fishing and vessel
traffic, lowering disturbance, collision risk, and bycatch for sensitive species [29, 30, 31,
32, 33]. Such zones can act as de­facto marine protected areas [34], potentially enhanc­
ing fish stocks and creating spillover effects [35, 36]. However, these benefits depend
on enforcement, species­specific responses, and the balance between reduced fishing
inside the zone and increased effort around its periphery, which can lead to unintended
bycatch [37, 38].
Attraction to FLOW structures does not guarantee positive outcomes. Ecological traps
occur when species respond to cues, such as apparent food availability or shelter, that
historically signalled habitat quality, but that now increase mortality risk [39, 40]. For
example, large predators may be drawn to turbine areas for prey but face noise pollution,
entanglement hazards, or electromagnetic field exposure [41, 42]. Climate change could
exacerbate such risks by altering species distributions and increasing physiological stress
[43].

Since its commissioning in 2017, Hywind Scotland has demonstrated several of these
mechanisms. Substructures have been colonized by diverse macrofaunal and algal com­
munities, with mooring lines supporting more taxa than painted surfaces [44]. Surveys,
eDNA sampling, and fish catch data indicate attraction of pelagic and demersal fish, in­
cluding Atlantic cod, whiting, and mackerel [44, 45, 46], which in turn may attract seabirds
and marine mammals [47, 48, 49]. However, evidence for sustained biomass increases
or spillover effects remains inconclusive [46], and predator responses appear variable,
with some avoidance behavior observed in harbour porpoises [50].
2.1.2
Negative environmental impacts of FLOW
While floating offshore wind turbines present significant ecological and climate mitigation
opportunities, they also introduce several potential negative impacts that may affect ma­
rine mammals and ecosystem dynamics [51, 52]. These can include both behavioural
responses, such as displacement, avoidance, altered diving patterns and physiological
risks such as hearing loss or even mortality. As deployment of FLOW expands, under­
standing and mitigating these impacts becomes critical for sustainable development.
Underwater noise is generated throughout the lifecycle of offshore wind farms, from con­
struction and operation to maintenance and decommissioning. This noise can alter marine
mammal behavior by causing displacement, communication interference, or increased
stress levels [53, 54]. In more severe cases, high­intensity noise, especially during pil­
ing activities, can result in physical harm or hearing loss [55]. While mitigation efforts at
fixed­bottom wind farms have focused mainly on construction noise [56], FLOW presents
a different acoustic profile. The floating design alters the frequency and intensity of emit­
ted sounds, particularly with the increase in turbine size [57]. Additionally, sensitivity to
noise varies across species, depending on their auditory range [58]. This variability com­
plicates impact assessments and necessitates tailored noise modeling for different marine
contexts.
Floating wind farms transmit electricity via dynamic cables suspended in the water col­
umn which emit electromagnetic fields, in short EMFs. These fields consist of both electric
and magnetic components and may interfere with marine species that rely on the Earth’s
geomagnetic field for navigation or prey detection [59, 60]. Although impacts on marine
mammals are still largely unknown, elasmobranchs and some fish species have shown
behavioural responses to EMFs [61]. The suspended nature of FLOW cables increases
the likelihood of interaction with pelagic species, as exposure occurs within the biolog­
ically active water column [62]. In contrast, fixed­bottom systems often bury cables in
the seabed, reducing this risk. The magnitude of EMF impact depends on cable type,
layout, voltage, and local conditions [63]. Despite indications that marine mammals may
experience minimal EMF interference, further empirical studies are necessary to confirm
long­term effects.
Entanglement in fishing gear, mooring lines, and marine debris represents a global threat
to marine fauna, often leading to severe injuries or mortality [64, 65, 66]. Within floating
offshore wind sites, three types of entanglement risks have been identified: primary (di­
rect interaction with infrastructure), secondary (debris such as ghost fishing gear becom­
ing attached to moorings and subsequently trapping organisms), and tertiary (organisms
already entangled becoming further ensnared on the structure) [67]. Although primary en­
tanglement risk is considered low, given that FLOW mooring systems typically use chain
or synthetic rope with diameters between 120 and 200 mm [68, 69], the risks of secondary
and tertiary entanglement may be more significant. These risks are influenced by multi­
ple factors, including the amount and type of marine debris, the visibility of mooring lines,

animal behavior near turbines, mooring line movement, array layout, mooring design, and
biofueling accumulation [70].
Mooring design plays a crucial role, with taut systems considered to pose lower risk than
catenary configurations. Additionally, FLOW sites located near active fishing grounds
are more susceptible to entanglement due to the presence of gear and debris. Over
time, biofueling can increase snagging potential, further elevating secondary and tertiary
entanglement risks. While no confirmed cases have been documented, this is likely due to
the infrequent nature of such events and the difficulty of conducting systematic monitoring.
Developers may mitigate risks by incorporating regular subsea inspections using remotely
operated vehicles or similar tools.
2.1.3
Reflection on the environmental impact
Floating offshore wind technology holds promise as a key contributor to the global en­
ergy transition, offering potential ecological benefits such as artificial reef formation, in­
creased primary productivity, and reduced anthropogenic pressure through vessel exclu­
sion zones. However, evidence remains inconclusive regarding long­term biomass en­
hancement and population­level effects on marine fauna. Risks such as noise pollution,
entanglement and ecological traps require careful consideration, particularly as the scale
and density of FLOW installations grow. Given the early stage of deployment and the
small number of operational sites, continued hypothesis­driven research and long­term
ecological monitoring are essential. Addressing current knowledge gaps, especially con­
cerning mobile predators, cumulative impacts, and electromagnetic field exposure, will
be critical for ensuring the sustainable and ecologically responsible expansion of floating
offshore wind in the coming decades.
2.2
Mooring Lines and Shared Mooring System
In this section, the theoretical framework that has supported the development of the design
is presented. Each key component of the floating system is systematically reviewed,
highlighting the fundamental principles. Before introducing the concept of shared mooring,
this chapter focuses on the core technologies and structural elements that influence its
behavior, with the aim of building a solid theoretical foundation for the discussion that
follows.
2.2.1
Types of Floats
A floating wind turbine consists primarily of three main components: the floater, the tower,
and the turbine. The floater is responsible for providing buoyancy to the entire structure
and must therefore account for the weight and dynamic loads of the tower, nacelle, and
rotor. This section focuses exclusively on the floater, presenting the advantages and
disadvantages of the main floating technologies currently in use (see Figure 2.1 for the
types of floaters).

==PAGE:16==
Figure 2.1: Types of floater[71]
The tension leg platform (TLP), is a structure anchored to the seabed through vertical
tendons that operate exclusively under tension. The stability of the system is ensured by
the constant pre­tension in these tendons, which significantly limits the vertical motions
of the platform [72]. This configuration provides a highly stable foundation, even under
harsh environmental conditions, making it particularly suitable for large scale wind tur­
bines. One of the main advantages of the TLP is its limited vertical displacement, which
allows for more stable and continuous turbine operation. In addition, the TLP features
a compact horizontal footprint, making it well­suited for areas with high maritime traffic.
However, its implementation also presents certain challenges. It requires seabeds with
good bearing capacity to ensure the effectiveness of the tendons and involves high instal­
lation costs, especially due to the pre­tensioning operations [73]. Furthermore, compared
to other types of floaters, the TLP is less flexible in terms of water depth applicability, and
is generally used in intermediate depths ranging from 80 to 200 meters.
The semi­submersible, is a structure composed of multiple vertical columns intercon­
nected by submerged pontoons. These columns are partially submerged and stabilized by
ballast. The floater remains buoyant through Archimedes’ principle and the distribution of
submerged volume, while its stability is primarily achieved by the wide spacing between
the columns and the placement of ballast at the bottom in order to lower the center of
gravity [72]. The main advantage of the semi­submersible is its adaptability to various
water depths, making it suitable for a wide range of offshore locations. Compared to spar
platforms, it has a lower draught, simplifying transportation and installation. Moreover,
its modular construction and the possibility of onshore assembly facilitate manufacturing
and transportation [73]. However, the semi­submersible also has some drawbacks. It
requires an extensive mooring system, which can occupy a large area on the seabed.
Additionally, its dynamic behavior tends to be more sensitive to environmental conditions,
particularly to long period waves. Its relatively high mass and structural complexity can
additionally lead to an increase in construction and maintenance costs.
The barge is a type of floater characterized by a wide and shallow floating platform, gen­
erally with a rectangular shape. Unlike other solutions, the barge relies mainly on stability
provided by its width and the distribution of buoyant volume near the water surface [72].
This design is relatively simple and can be realized using conventional shipbuilding tech­
8

==PAGE:17==
niques, making it one of the most cost effective options in terms of manufacturing. One
of the main advantages of the barge is its structural simplicity, which translates into lower
production costs and easier transportation and assembly [73]. Additionally, being a flat
structure, it can be built in existing shipyards using standard infrastructure. However,
the barge is also the most sensitive to weather and sea conditions among the mentioned
floater types. Its proximity to the water surface makes it more exposed to wave action,
resulting in greater vertical and horizontal motions. This can negatively affect turbine per­
formance and increase wear on components. Furthermore, the mooring system required
to ensure station keeping can be complex and bulky, especially in areas with significant
wave activity. Overall, the barge is a suitable solution for sites with moderate marine con­
ditions and relatively shallow waters, where simplicity and low costs are prioritized over
maximum operational stability.
The spar buoy, which is the type of floater adopted both in this thesis and in the ESO­
MOOR project. The spar­buoy consists of a long and slender cylindrical structure, with a
significant portion of its volume located below the sea surface. Stability is ensured through
a combination of buoyancy in the upper section and heavy ballast in the lower section,
which lowers the center of gravity and provides an effective righting moment against envi­
ronmental loads [72]. This type of floater offers excellent stability performance, particularly
with respect to roll and pitch motions. Due to its streamlined geometry and very low center
of gravity, the spar is minimally affected by surface waves and is especially suitable for
locations with regular and deep wave conditions [73]. However, the spar buoy requires
deep waters, typically greater than 100 meters, for effective installation. Its length also
poses significant logistical challenges during construction, transport, and offshore deploy­
ment, often requiring horizontal floating solutions or specialized lifting systems. Despite
these complexities, the spar buoy remains one of the most promising solutions for float­
ing offshore wind, particularly for long term deployment in high energy areas with deep
waters, such as Norway.
2.2.2
Types of Mooring Lines
The next aspect considered is the mooring lines, which are the systems responsible for
maintaining the position of the floating wind turbines. By focusing specifically on the moor­
ing line systems, several types can be identified, primarily classified according to their ge­
ometric configuration and the level of pre­tension applied (see Figure 2.2 for an overview
of all the components)..

The catenary mooring is named after its catenary shape. In this configuration, the mooring
lines are typically composed of heavy chains or steel cables, and their stability is derived
primarily from the weight of the line rather than from pre­tension [74]. The catenary lines
are anchored to the seabed and extend toward the platform in a curved profile, allowing
horizontal loads to be absorbed gradually. This mooring system is widely adopted due to

9
==PAGE:18==
its structural simplicity and ease of installation, and it is particularly suitable for relatively
deep waters where horizontal seabed space is not a constraint [75]. However, it also
presents some drawbacks: it requires a large footprint on the seabed to allow proper
line deployment, and the platform’s response to environmental loads tends to be less
stiff, resulting in larger displacements compared to other configurations. Moreover, wave­
induced dynamics can cause significant oscillations in the lines, especially in rougher sea
states.
The semi­taut mooring is an intermediate configuration between the catenary system and
the fully tensioned taut system. In this case, the mooring lines retain an initially curved
shape similar to the catenary but with increased pre­tension, making them stiffer and re­
ducing their overall length [76]. The system combines the self­weight of the lines with
a certain degree of tension, resulting in a compromise between flexibility and stability.
The main advantage of the semi­taut mooring is its improved dynamic response com­
pared to the catenary system, reducing horizontal displacements and enhancing overall
platform stability, especially under variable loading conditions. Furthermore, it requires
less seabed area than a pure catenary system, making it more suitable for marine envi­
ronments with spatial constraints or existing infrastructure [77]. On the other hand, the
semi­taut configuration involves greater design and construction complexity, as it requires
careful balance between weight, tension, and line inclination. Additionally, the forces
transmitted to the anchors are generally higher than in catenary systems, which may in­
crease structural requirements and the cost of anchoring components.
The taut mooring, is a mooring system characterized by highly pre­tensioned lines ar­
ranged at a steep angle between the floating platform and the seabed. Unlike catenary
systems, the taut mooring system’s stability and the capacity to resist environmental loads
derive almost entirely from the tension in the lines rather than from their weight [78]. This
configuration significantly reduces the horizontal footprint of the mooring lines, making
it particularly advantageous in areas with limited marine space or high maritime traffic.
Moreover, the increased rigidity of the system results in a more restrained platform re­
sponse to both lateral and vertical displacements, improving positional accuracy and
operational efficiency, especially for sensitive systems such as energy production plat­
forms. On the downside, taut mooring systems require high performance materials, such
as synthetic fibers or high­strength cables, to withstand elevated tensile loads. This leads
to higher costs during both the design and installation phases. Additionally, the forces
transmitted to the anchors are significantly higher, which can increase the complexity and
cost of the anchoring system on the seabed.
To summarize, the choice between catenary, semi­taut, and taut mooring configurations
depends strongly on site­specific environmental conditions, seabed depth, stability objec­
tives, and budget constraints.
2.2.3
Composition of Mooring Lines
Mooring lines for floating offshore wind turbines can be constructed using different ma­
terials, each with specific mechanical properties, costs, and installation constraints. The
three main configurations are all­chain, all­synthetic fiber, and hybrid designs.
All­chain lines are commonly used in traditional catenary systems, typically composed of
heavy steel chain or wire rope. Their restoring force derives mainly from the line’s weight
and the geometric curvature between the floating platform and the seabed. A portion of
the line often rests on the seabed, and the restoring force increases non­linearly as the
platform drifts and the line lifts off the bottom. These systems can be analyzed using
catenary equations under simplified assumptions such as a horizontal seabed, negligible
10
Integrated Analysis of Floating Offshore Wind Farms with Shared Mooring Systems
==PAGE:19==
bending stiffness, and limited elasticity, conditions that are acceptable for steel chains
with small curvature.
All­synthetic fiber lines, often made of polyester or high modulus polyethylene, are in­
creasingly used in deep­water and taut mooring configurations, where restoring forces
are generated mainly by line stretch rather than geometry. Their low weight, high com­
pliance, and ability to absorb dynamic loads reduce overall line length, seabed footprint,
and vertical loads on the platform. However, their stiffness is highly non­linear and de­
pends on load history, material aging, and environmental conditions. Challenges include
hysteresis and heat build­up from cyclic loading, UV degradation, fatigue at termination
points, susceptibility to cutting and abrasion, marine biofueling, and long­term creep (e.g.,
about 1.5% elongation over 20 years for polyester).
Hybrid lines combine the strengths of steel and synthetic materials. A common configu­
ration places steel chain near the seabed for abrasion resistance and anchoring stability,
synthetic fiber rope in the suspended midsection for compliance and weight reduction,
and a short chain segment near the platform to protect the rope from UV exposure and
mechanical wear. This chain–polyester–chain arrangement offers a balance of strength,
flexibility, and environmental protection, but requires careful design of segment transitions
and connector integrity to ensure mechanical compatibility and durability.
2.2.4
Types of Anchors
This section presents the anchors, which are essential components responsible for main­
taining the position of the entire floating system. As with mooring lines, the failure of a
single anchor can have severe consequences, not only for the directly connected turbine
but also for the others within the wind farm, due to the risk of collisions or structural im­
balance in the interconnected system. There are different types of anchors, which are
selected based on seabed conditions, expected loads, and the configuration of the moor­
ing system. The following four types af anchors are relevant.
The suction anchor is a solution widely used in deep­water environments. It consists of a
large hollow cylindrical structure positioned vertically on the seabed and embedded into
the sediment by creating an internal vacuum. Once installed, it provides substantial pull­
out resistance, making it particularly suitable for taut or semi­taut mooring systems where
vertical load components are expected [79]. The main advantages of suction anchors in­
clude high load capacity, the possibility of recovery and reuse, and compatibility with soft
or sandy seabeds [80]. However, their effectiveness is highly dependent on site­specific
geotechnical conditions, and installation requires specialized equipment for vacuum gen­
eration and penetration control. Additionally, they are not suitable for rocky or very hard
seabeds where proper embedment is not achievable.
The drag embedded anchors, which are dragged into the seabed during installation un­
til they reach a depth sufficient to ensure the required holding capacity. Unlike suction
anchors, which are embedded vertically, drag anchors are installed horizontally and pen­
etrate the sediment due to the resistance encountered during the dragging process [81].
Once the target depth is reached, the mooring line is tensioned, and the anchor operates
primarily under horizontal load. Drag embedded anchors are widely used in catenary
systems, where the mooring lines do not apply significant vertical forces [82]. Their main
advantages include structural simplicity, low manufacturing costs, and high holding ca­
pacity in cohesive or sandy sediments. Additionally, their installation is relatively quick
and does not require complex infrastructure. However, they also present some limita­
tions. The final position of the anchor on the seabed is not easily predictable, which may
complicate the alignment of the mooring lines. Furthermore, they are not suitable for hard
Integrated Analysis of Floating Offshore Wind Farms with Shared Mooring Systems
11
==PAGE:20==
or rocky seabeds and cannot be used in taut or semi­taut systems where vertical load
components are present, as they do not provide adequate uplift resistance.
The pile driven anchors, which consist of steel or reinforced concrete piles driven vertically
or at an angle into the seabed using hydraulic jacks or mechanical hammers. This type of
anchor is particularly suitable for hard or cohesive seabeds where other systems, such as
suction or drag anchors, would be ineffective or difficult to install [83]. Pile driven anchors
offer high holding capacity in both horizontal and vertical directions, making them ideal
for taut and semi­taut systems where mooring lines apply multidirectional forces. Another
advantage is their ability to provide well­defined and predictable mechanical behavior,
which is essential in applications requiring high structural reliability. On the downside,
these anchors involve complex and costly installation procedures, requiring specialized
support vessels and heavy equipment, and they can generate significant acoustic and
environmental impact during the driving phase. Additionally, once installed, they are not
easily retrievable or reusable [84].
The gravity anchors, which relies solely on its own weight to ensure holding capacity
and ensuring it does not actively penetrate the seabed. These anchors are made of
massive concrete or steel structures that rest directly on the seafloor and resist applied
loads through friction and the inertia generated by their mass [84]. Gravity anchors are
mainly used in rocky or very hard seabeds where the installation of other anchor types
would be impractical. They are relatively simple to design and install, and do not require
complex anchoring equipment. Moreover, they are an effective solution for temporary or
demonstration projects where retrievability without leaving embedded elements is desir­
able. However, their main drawback is the large mass required to ensure stability, which
leads to high transportation and deployment costs, along with a larger seabed footprint.
Additionally, they are generally less suitable for deep­water applications where the nec­
essary weight would be excessive compared to the performance gained [85].
Ultimately, there is no universally optimal solution; the most appropriate anchor type must
be selected through a trade­off between mechanical performance, cost, installation fea­
sibility, and site characteristics.
In this thesis, the selection and detailed design of the anchoring system are not addressed,
as they fall outside the scope of the study. The primary objective of this work is to inves­
tigate the dynamic response of floating wind turbines equipped with shared mooring lines
under various loading conditions.

2.2.6
How to Design a Mooring Line or A Shared Mooring Line
The mooring system provides over 80% of the total damping contribution [86], which is
critical for efficient electricity production, and represents approximately 37,5% of the total
cost [92]. For this reason, it is essential to design both individual and shared mooring
lines as effectively as possible.
The design of a mooring line or shared mooring line typically follows an iterative process,
where metocean forcing and hydrodynamic response are translated into a configuration
that ensures strength, integrity, and durability with transparent safety margins. The design
process begins with an assessment of environmental conditions such as waves, wind
and currents, and the execution of the hydrodynamic calculations necessary to evaluate
platform movements and loads on the mooring lines. Based on these results, a preliminary
mooring layout is developed and verified against three main criteria. The first one is the
ultimate limit state (ULS), in which the system must withstand extreme loads, usually
associated with rare but severe environmental conditions. The design is then checked and
verified to ensure that line stresses and platform displacements remain within acceptable
limits. If the design does not pass this verification, the mooring configuration must be
resized and the process repeated. If the first step is passed, then the designer can test the
design against the accidental limit state (ALS) which are accidental conditions, such as the
failure of one or more mooring lines. The system must still ensure structural integrity and
operational stability. If it does not comply, a redesign is required. After passing the ALS
check, the mooring system is evaluated for fatigue limit state (FLS) expected during its
service life. This involves calculating the cumulative damage resulting from cyclic loading.
If the fatigue life of any of the lines is insufficient, the system is redesigned. The design
cycle continues iteratively until all three limit state checks are satisfied. At that point,
the mooring configuration is considered acceptable and the process ends. This method
ensures a robust, safe and optimized design that meets both short­term strength and
long­term durability requirements.
Given the scope and extent of this thesis, a streamlined screening loop was applied: the
individual and shared mooring lines were first designed and a static equilibrium and off­
set check was run; if it failed, the process returned to re­sizing, otherwise the operational
dataset was tested and motions and tensions were verified to remain within operability
thresholds; failure here also triggered re­sizing. Finally, the extreme conditions were ap­
plied to approximate the ULS envelope and, if the configuration passed, the design was
accepted for this phase, whereas any failure sent the process back to the initial sizing.
This way of working accelerates convergence and is acceptable for comparative studies
or early­phase selection, but it deliberately postpones ALS and fatigue verification and

therefore must not be treated as a final certificate of fitness. Its results are contingent on
the fidelity of the static model, the representativeness of the working­weather set, and the
conservatism embedded in the extreme­case definition.

3.1
Design of Shared Mooring System
This section presents the design process for a shared mooring system, combining nu­
merical simulations with practical engineering considerations. The discussion maintains
a dual perspective between computational modeling and real­world application, outlining
the key parameters, design choices, and lessons learned regarding both successful and
unsuccessful approaches.
3.1.1
Design Approach and Lessons Learned from Simulations
This section focuses on the iterative simulation­based design process that shaped the
proposed shared mooring configurations. The simulations were not considered abstract
exercises, but rather surrogates for real­world design, continuously linking numerical re­
sults with engineering judgment. Practical aspects such as installation feasibility, seabed
spatial constraints, environmental loads, and material limitations were integrated into the
evaluation. Through this approach, several insights emerged that informed the final sys­
tem configuration.
Each configuration tested, ranging from a single floating wind turbine to larger arrays, re­
vealed specific challenges and trade­offs. Particular attention was given to the interaction
between mooring line pretension and turbine offset, the influence of mooring line weight
and layout on dynamic response, and the implications of line length and stiffness on envi­
ronmental impact and structural loads. Another important factor was the simulation time
which proved to be unexpectedly high but understandable given the number of points and
bodies considered in the system. Compliance with the DNV standard of three hours of
simulation was attempted but could not always be achieved due to time constraints and
limited computational memory.
This section also highlights a proposed iterative loop that could be applied in real­life de­
sign scenarios; starting from environmental data and geotechnical conditions, progress­
ing through initial layout assumptions, simulation feedback, and design revisions. The
integration of technical knowledge with simulation outputs is essential to ensure that a
mooring design is not only numerically stable, but also practically viable.
3.1.2
Array Configurations and Structural Implications
Several array configurations of floating offshore wind turbines were investigated to explore
how mooring system performance changes with increasing structural complexity. The
progression began with a single­unit configuration (1x1), followed by linear expansions
(2x1 and 3x1), and later extended to two­row arrays (2x2). These configurations allowed
for a systematic analysis of both individual turbine behavior and the interactions within an
array under shared mooring conditions.
Initial simulations employed symmetric mooring layouts under the assumption that sym­
metry would simplify the analysis and evenly distribute forces. This proved inadequate,
as real offshore environments are dominated by unidirectional loads such as wind and
waves, typically aligned with prevailing weather conditions. Consequently, an effective
mooring system must be reinforced along the principal loading axis (0° in this study),
while remaining compliant in other directions to enable controlled movement. This direc­
tional approach improves both structural reliability and cost­efficiency. Another parameter
to be evaluated in this scenario is the angle between the two mooring lines, as it plays a
18
Integrated Analysis of Floating Offshore Wind Farms with Shared Mooring Systems
==PAGE:27==
fundamental role in determining the components of the forces acting on the mooring lines.
In general, wider angles are preferred over narrow ones.
In addition to environmental directionality, seabed spatial constraints play a major role in
anchor placement. Subsea infrastructure such as pipelines, cables, or previous installa­
tions may limit the feasible positions for anchors. Furthermore, geotechnical conditions
must be carefully evaluated, as anchoring is not possible in regions where soil strength is
insufficient or where there are natural hazards, such as gas pockets. A practical design
process should therefore begin with a thorough assessment of the geotechnical profile
and available seabed area, followed by evaluation of environmental loads and layout op­
timization.
This section indicates that as the array size increases, the importance of rigorous struc­
tural planning and consideration of environmental conditions becomes more critical. Al­
though symmetric configurations offer computational simplicity, they fail to adequately
capture the complexity of offshore environments. Consequently, future design approaches
should incorporate asymmetry and directionality from the outset, ensuring that the sys­
tem is aligned with prevailing environmental loads. The most critical aspect concerns
the spacing between turbines in the crosswind and downwind directions, primarily due to
the wake effect. Turbine efficiency is strongly influenced by this spacing, which is why
conventional layouts typically adopt distances of at least ten rotor diameters downwind
and five diameters crosswind. However, such requirements greatly extend the length of
shared mooring lines, potentially making the concept less attractive compared to a sin­
gle floating turbine with three short mooring lines. To render shared mooring systems
competitive, turbines would need to be arranged at much shorter intervals, for example
three rotor diameters crosswind and five downwind. This raises a fundamental question
regarding the actual feasibility and value of pursuing such a design strategy.
3.1.3
Mooring Line Design and Environmental Considerations
The selection of mooring line length, material composition, and layout plays a critical role
in the overall performance, feasibility, and environmental impact of a floating offshore wind
turbine array. In this study, the mooring system design focused on short mooring lines,
both to reduce the suspended weight supported by the floater and to limit the seabed
contact area, thereby minimizing ecological disturbance. Longer mooring lines, while po­
tentially more compliant, introduce challenges in terms of added weight and the likelihood
of resting on the seabed. Such configurations increase the vertical load on the float­
ing structure and may negatively affect marine ecosystems. To mitigate this, one viable
solution is the implementation of a lazy wave configuration, which introduces buoyancy
modules along the mooring line to lift it off the seabed and create a wave­shaped profile.
This layout not only reduces the vertical force transferred to the floater but also contributes
additional restoring force, improving station­keeping performance.
For this project, a chain–polyester–chain configuration was adopted. Polyester ropes are
significantly lighter and more compliant than steel chains, making them especially suitable
for deeper water applications. The chain segments are used at both ends: the lower sec­
tion ensures adequate seabed abrasion resistance and anchoring capacity, while the up­
per section provides wear resistance near the floater interface. This hybrid setup achieves
a favorable balance between strength, flexibility, and environmental compatibility.
An additional consideration in mooring line design is the level of pretension applied. In
this work, minimal pretension was selected to limit the static offset of the floating turbines
while preserving the system’s ability to absorb dynamic loads. According to DNV guide­
lines, the optimal mooring system design must balance compliance, to prevent excessive
Integrated Analysis of Floating Offshore Wind Farms with Shared Mooring Systems
19
==PAGE:28==
loads on the floater, with stiffness, to avoid large displacements that could endanger ris­
ers or reduce power production efficiency. A low pretension value is often adopted as
a conservative design choice, acknowledging that extreme environmental conditions in­
herently induce high dynamic tensions. Pretension is typically defined to ensure that the
natural periods in surge, sway, and yaw remain well separated from the dominant wave
and wind excitation periods, along with those in heave, roll, and pitch. An increase in
pretension enhances the stiffness in surge, sway, and yaw, thereby reducing the associ­
ated natural periods, which are generally required to exceed 100­200 seconds to prevent
resonance. Conversely, lowering pretension reduces this stiffness, resulting in larger hor­
izontal offsets. Consequently, pretension is set as a compromise; sufficiently high to shift
the natural periods beyond critical excitation zones, yet limited to avoid excessive platform
displacements.
Overall, this section emphasizes the need to integrate structural design, material selec­
tion, and environmental responsibility into the mooring system architecture. The decision
to use short, suspended mooring lines with buoyancy elements and composite materi­
als reflects a strategy aimed at optimizing both engineering performance and ecological
footprint.
3.1.4
Mooring Materials and Mechanical Properties
The selection of mooring line materials significantly influences the structural performance,
fatigue life, and long­term reliability of a floating offshore wind turbine system. This sec­
tion focuses on the rationale behind choosing specific chain types, material grades, and
synthetic ropes, with additional considerations for connectors and design standards.
Chain Types and Grades
Chains remain a core component of mooring systems, especially for shallow and inter­
mediate depths. Two main types are used in offshore applications: stud­link and studless
chains. While stud­link chains have historically been preferred for their handling stability
and resistance to kinking, studless chains are increasingly adopted in permanent mooring
systems due to their lower weight and improved fatigue performance.
In this study, studless chains were selected, as they offer approximately 10% lower weight
per unit strength compared to stud­link chains. Furthermore, the absence of welded studs
eliminates common failure points such as cracks and loose fittings. Studless chains are
also easier to manufacture and inspect, which improves reliability over the system’s life­
time.
Chain strength is typically categorized by grade, with R3 and R4 being common in off­
shore applications. Although R4 chains provide higher tensile strength, they are often
unnecessary when the chain lies on the seabed in static conditions, leading to overde­
sign and cost inefficiencies. For this reason, grade R3 chains were deemed sufficient for
the present mooring layout. A key parameter in chain design is the diameter d, which
determines the minimum breaking load or MBL; typical offshore values range between 70
mm and 200 mm, depending on depth and loading conditions.
Polyester Rope Properties
Synthetic fiber ropes, particularly those made of polyester, have become standard in deep
water mooring systems. Polyester ropes offer high compliance, low weight, and excellent
fatigue characteristics under axial loading. Their lower stiffness compared to steel chains
enables better energy absorption, reducing peak loads on the floater. One notable ad­
vantage of polyester ropes is their continuous manufacturing process, which minimizes
the likelihood of individual defects. In contrast, steel chains are assembled link by link,
and a flaw in a single link can compromise the integrity of the entire line.

Over time, ropes exhibit both construction stretch and creep, which can lead to permanent
elongation. This necessitates adjustments to mooring line lengths during installation or
maintenance. Additionally, polyester ropes require careful handling to avoid abrasion and
should be kept away from the seabed and the fairlead to prevent damage.
Despite these considerations, the high performance to weight ratio of polyester ropes
allows for fewer mooring lines to achieve the same restoring force as a traditional chain­
wire­chain system, which can result in substantial cost savings and simplification of the
layout.
Connectors
All mooring systems rely on robust connectors to join components such as chains, polyester
segments, and anchors. These connectors must ensure load transfer without introducing
stress concentrations or fatigue­prone geometries. Although connector design was not
explicitly modeled in this study, it is crucial to note that industry­standard practices must
be followed. For detailed specifications on connectors, shackles, and end terminations,
the reader is referred to DNV Standard OS­E301 Position Mooring, which outlines the
requirements for component classification, testing, and integration into offshore mooring
systems.
Connectors are also very important for another phenomenon involving floating wind tur­
bines which is the roll­yaw instability. This phenomenon is due to the coupling terms
of the anti­symmetric aerodynamic stiffness in roll and yaw caused in turn by the thrust
force. In mooring configurations with a single line from the anchor to the floater, floating
wind turbines may be more prone to roll­yaw lock, an aerodynamic instability character­
ized by unwanted coupling between roll and yaw motions. This behavior is caused by the
presence of anti­symmetric transverse stiffness terms (K46 and K64) generated by the
rotor thrust, which can lead to coupled and amplified oscillations [93]. This phenomenon
was observed in the initial designs of the mooring lines, which is why it was decided to
the adoption of two bridles in a 1×1 configuration helps to increase control over the yaw
degree of freedom and reduce unwanted coupling with roll. By providing an additional
attachment point, the second bridle increases the lateral and torsional stiffness of the
mooring system, thereby altering the hydrodynamic and aerodynamic restoring charac­
teristics in yaw and reducing the magnitude of the antisymmetric coupling terms. This
decision led to an increase in the overall stability of the system under variable operating
conditions.
During the simulations, an unexpected phenomenon was observed. In the operational
phase of the turbine, after the transient response, a constant rotation around the z­axis,
yaw motion, appeared. Upon further investigation, this phenomenon can be attributed to
the conservation of angular momentum and the torque generated by the rotor, which is
transferred to the floating platform. This behavior, commonly referred to as platform yaw
drift, has already been reported in the literature for several floating offshore wind turbine
configurations. Numerical studies on the NREL 5 MW rotor mounted on the DeepCwind
OC4 platform have shown a stable yaw drift of approximately 39◦at a wind speed of 16 m/s
[94], while additional analyses have revealed standard deviations of about 6◦in spar­type
platforms equipped with dual rotors [95]. The main cause is related to the vertical moment
induced by the rotor, which acts on the yaw degree of freedom of the platform in the ab­
sence of sufficient restoring stiffness in the mooring system, as in the case of single­point
mooring. This phenomenon does not occur in bottom­fixed turbines, where the structural
foundation absorbs the torque generated by the rotor. Several mitigation strategies have

can counteract the induced moments and reduce yaw drift [94, 96]. More recently, inte­

of the coupling between rotor, platform, and control systems, highlighting yaw motion as
an intrinsic challenge for the operational stability of FOWTs [97].

In this case, there is an offset because the origin of the coordinate system does not co­
incide with the actual position of the origins of the turbines. Specifically, the origin of the
turbine layout is shifted by 1200 m with respect to the global coordinate system, and this
displacement must be taken into account when analysing the array geometry and the
resulting mooring line configuration. In the shared mooring configuration, the dynamic
response of the two floating wind turbines shows a clear lack of synchronization, de­
spite being subject to identical environmental conditions and having the same structural
properties. One would expect symmetrical and synchronized behavior precisely because
both structures have exactly the same characteristics.This behavior can be explained by
the emergence of coupled modes in the multi body system. When two platforms are
connected through a common mooring line, the system no longer behaves as two inde­
pendent single floaters, but develops two normal modes: an in­phase mode, in which
both floaters move together, and a counter­phase mode, in which their motions are out
of phase. The relative contribution of these modes depends on the excitation frequency
and the nonlinear restoring behavior of the shared mooring element. As a consequence,
even if the external loading is uniform, the time series of the two platforms may appear
shifted or oscillating with different offsets. This effect is consistent with previous studies
on shared­mooring floating wind farms, which demonstrated the presence of “comb­like”
frequency responses in the line tensions due to interference between the coupled modes
[98]. [99] Lozon & Hall (2023) also highlighted that shared moorings can reduce global
motions and anchor loads, but at the cost of introducing asymmetries in line tensions and
mean offsets between platforms. Therefore, the non­synchronization observed in Fig­
ure 4.15 is not a numerical artifact, but a physical manifestation of the coupled dynamics
inherent to shared mooring systems. From a design perspective, this underlines the need
to assess both global stability and local load imbalances, as the most highly stressed lines
may experience significantly different fatigue loads compared to conventional single­unit
mooring layouts.

5
Discussion
5.1
Interpretation of Results
The results of this thesis demonstrate that shared mooring systems influence the global
response of floating offshore wind arrays in ways that differ markedly from single­turbine
baselines. This behavior highlights that farms cannot be treated as a simple sum of iso­
lated units due to coupled mode. Directional reinforcement along the main weather axis
improves station keeping while reducing material requirements in weak­loading directions.
Transverse shared lines provide lateral stability, but if yaw stiffness is insufficient, they
tend to intensify roll­yaw coupling, confirming phenomena reported in literature. Short
hybrid chain­polyester­chain lines reduce suspended weight and seabed footprint without
exceeding design tensions, offering a pragmatic balance between strength, compliance,
and environmental compatibility. These insights are best understood when compared
with findings from earlier studies, as discussed in the following section.
5.2
Comparison With Literature
The observed trends are broadly consistent with prior academic and industrial findings.
Similar to works of Arun Ingersoll, this analysis indicates that dynamic coupling becomes
significant in multi­unit arrays and that yaw stability is a critical design driver. The yaw drift
and roll­yaw coupling observed in the simulations mirror behaviors reported for spar­type
platforms in other studies, reinforcing the importance of including aerohydro­servo inter­
actions in array design. At the same time, the findings challenge the assumption, common
in early conceptual works, that symmetric mooring patterns are acceptable simplifications.
In practice, unidirectional forcing dominates offshore conditions, and asymmetry should
be introduced from the outset. Compared to DNV guidelines, the simplified iterative loop
employed here proved useful for comparative screening, but it cannot substitute for the
full ULS, ALS, and FLS verification required for certification. These comparisons provide
a foundation for assessing the broader design and operational implications.
5.3
Practical Implications
From a design standpoint, several practical insights emerge. First, symmetric layouts,
although computationally convenient, underestimate the structural demands imposed by
prevailing weather conditions. Second, short hybrid mooring lines are particularly attrac­
tive, as they achieve station keeping with reduced seabed footprint, lower suspended
weight, and potentially reduced cost, making the technology more competitive. Third, tur­
bine spacing is decisive: conventional wake­aware distances inflate shared­line lengths
to the point where the concept loses competitiveness. To make shared moorings viable,
the arrays would need tighter spacing, which introduces trade­offs with energy yield and
maintenance access. These considerations illustrate that shared mooring systems may
not be universally advantageous but could offer value in specific contexts, such as deep­
water sites where material savings offset increased complexity.
5.4
Limitations of The Study
The scope of this thesis necessarily involved simplifications. Currents were neglected,
power cables were neglated, and seabed geotechnics were not explicitly represented.
The iterative loop postponed ALS and fatigue verification, instead focusing on operational

tailed sea states. Energy yield and wake interactions were also not jointly considered with

5.5
Critical Reflection
Overall, this thesis has shown that shared mooring systems are technically feasible at
screening level and can deliver material savings, but their competitiveness strongly de­
pends on site conditions and array spacing. Under the conditions tested, they did not
outperform conventional three line systems. Nevertheless, the design levers identified
here directional layouts, yaw­aware stiffness, hybrid lines, and tighter co­design of moor­
ings with control and cabling remain promising avenues. In deeper waters and under
specific environmental constraints, shared moorings may yet prove to be a credible solu­
tion for reducing cost and footprint in floating offshore wind farms. Ultimately, the findings
underscore that while shared moorings are not a universal solution, they represent an
innovative design pathway whose success will depend on integrated, site specific engi­
neering approaches.
5.6
Future Work
One possible direction for future work is to perform a systematic parameter sensitivity
analysis of mooring system design variables for a given floating wind turbine. Starting
from a baseline configuration (1×1, 1×2, 2×1 etc), each parameter, such as number
of mooring lines, line length, composition, segment lengths, chain grade and diameter,
polyester diameter, anchor location, fairlead position, and bridle usage, can be varied in­
dividually while holding the others constant. For each variation, key performance metrics
such as pretension, maximum line tension, and the turbine’s six degree of freedom re­
sponse under identical loading conditions should be evaluated. This approach can help
quantify the influence of each design choice and guide optimization strategies in future
projects. Building on the understanding of how each design variable affects the mooring
system performance, a possible future development would be the creation of an auto­
mated mooring design optimization tool. The input parameters would include feasible an­
chor locations based on seabed characteristics, probabilistic metocean conditions for the
site to identify dominant load directions, and floater geometry. The software would sys­
tematically evaluate all possible mooring configurations, assessing performance metrics
such as pretension, maximum tension, six­degree­of­freedom motion response, and fa­
tigue life. Using multi­objective optimization algorithms, the tool would identify the optimal
trade­off between structural performance and total cost, thereby enabling more efficient
and cost­effective design solutions.

6
Conclusion
This thesis has investigated the feasibility and performance of shared mooring systems for
floating offshore wind arrays. Starting from the motivation of reducing material usage and
seabed footprint, a comparative analysis was conducted between conventional mooring
layouts and various shared configurations.
The results showed that array level coupling effects emerge even at modest scales; dy­
namic interaction amplifies low frequency responses. Directional reinforcement along the
prevailing weather axis improved global station keeping, whereas insufficient yaw stiff­
ness led to enhanced roll­yaw coupling. Short hybrid chain­polyester­chain mooring lines
were found to provide a practical compromise, reducing suspended weight and footprint
without exceeding design tensions. However, turbine spacing proved critical; at conven­
tional wake aware distances, shared lines became excessively long, eroding potential
advantages.
The findings suggest that shared mooring systems are technically feasible and can yield
material and environmental benefits, but their competitiveness is highly site dependent.
While they did not outperform conventional three line systems under the tested conditions,
the study identified promising design levers directional layouts, yaw aware stiffness, hybrid
lines, and integrated co­design with cabling and control that merit further exploration.
In conclusion, shared moorings should not be seen as a universal replacement, but rather
as an innovative option whose success will depend on careful site selection, tighter array
spacing, and holistic engineering approaches. The work presented here contributes to
a more nuanced understanding of the opportunities and challenges of shared mooring
systems, laying the foundation for future research and design developments in floating
offshore wind.
