Abstract
This research aims to investigate how Futurice collaborates to provide customer­facing solutions through
its innovation ecosystem. It aims to identify the actors, activities, artifacts, and relations that are im­
portant for improving the firm’s innovative performance, and to combine this ecosystem with future
thinking methodologies and tools to anticipate future scenarios and develop a resilient business strategy.
This study is novel in that it combines innovation ecosystems with futures thinking methodologies and
tools to anticipate the future, and in mapping and relating Futurice’s innovation ecosystem to its business
strategy. The research question for this study is: ”How can an innovation ecosystem and futures thinking
methodologies and tools to anticipate the future help Futurice GmbH Germany to create a resilient busi­
ness strategy?” To address this question, the research combines the ecosystem pie model by Talmar et al.
with the Lean Futures Creation Handbook of Futurice and Voros et al. foresight process framework. The
aim is to create an adaptable process that can be applied by other researchers, and to generate insights and
recommendations for improving Futurice’s innovation ecosystem and business strategy. Effective inno­
vation ecosystems must be able to adapt and evolve in response to changes in the market, technological
advancements, and shifts in customer needs and preferences. By using a future­oriented mindset and ap­
propriate tools and methodologies, organizations can better navigate the complexity and unpredictability
of innovation and position themselves for long­term success.

Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
The motivation for this research derived from three main areas: i) as an industrial engineer and passionate
strategist, the author has engaged with business and innovation strategy throughout his academic and
professional career, and as such, wanted to develop an empirically based understanding of this area; ii) the
limited academic research in the area of foresight in ecosystems means that there is a limited theoretical
base, particularly in innovation ecosystems and futures thinking methodologies and tools to anticipate
future scenarios, related to the focus of this research; and iii) the desire to collaborate with a company
to apply first­hand knowledge and to develop an understanding of this manner in which the findings are
transformed into action items that aim to improve the companies business and innovation strategy.
These motivations are set against a backdrop of increasing demands placed upon digital innovation and
engineering companies to respond to the challenges of the 21st century. This research has identified that
the application of innovation ecosystem mapping and futures thinking methodologies and tools is grow­
ing. Businesses are shoring up their core business, pursuing known opportunity spaces, conserving cash,
and minimizing risk as the political landscape is shifting and becoming more unpredictable; technology
is influencing everyday life, environmental pressures are reaching alarming levels, and societal tensions
are rising almost in every part of the world. Recent developments such as the COVID­19 outbreak and
the invasion of Ukraine have increased uncertainty in the market and, therefore, the behavior of many
companies. In response, many companies are adapting their core to meet shifting customer needs, iden­
tifying and quickly addressing new opportunity areas created by the changing landscape, reevaluating
the innovation initiative portfolio and ensuring resources are allocated appropriately, and building the
foundation for post­crisis growth to remain competitive in the recovery period. Innovation ecosystem
and futures thinking have emerged as promising approaches to tackle challenging situations by reducing
inevitable disruption caused by a volatile, uncertain, complex, and ambiguous world.
1.2
Problem Statement and Objectives
To tackle current challenges with the innovation ecosystems approach ­ thinking about the future becomes
indispensable to create resilient and long­lasting ecosystems that create expertise in new areas, help to
diversify the economy, and allow businesses to meet their customers where ever they are or heading.
Thus the problem statement of this research focuses on combining innovation ecosystems with futures
thinking methodologies and tools to anticipate future scenarios. The problem statement of this thesis is:
”How can an innovation ecosystem and futures thinking methodologies and tools to anticipate future
scenarios help Futurice GmbH Germany to create a resilient business strategy?”
To close the gap between innovation ecosystem building and business strategy, action items will be cre­
ated to support strategic planning at Futurice to build a resilient business strategy that guides decisions,
meets the business objective, boosts innovation, delivers value, and builds competitive advantage. The
1
==PAGE:14==
central aim of this research is to find a way to combine innovation ecosystems with futures thinking
methodologies and tools to anticipate future scenarios. Accordingly, the objective is to find method­
ologies and tools that can be combined to create a holistic process that provides one constant stream of
information that allows the processing of innovation ecosystem data with futures thinking methodologies
and tools that can be, in due course, related to business strategy.
An underlying objective of this paper is to report back to the innovation ecosystem and foresight practi­
tioner community on a specific instance of practical innovation ecosystem and foresight implementation
to align business strategy within an organization. Also, the objective is to improve Futurice innovation
practices by applying such a holistic process firsthand. Accordingly, it improves Futurices competences
to enhance customer value creation through collaboration and to improve innovation strategy as an emer­
gent strategy that helps to adapt to Futurice’s overall business strategy.
1.3
Delimitation
This research was conducted solely for Futurice GmbH Germany. Therefore, when referencing Futurice,
only its strategy, innovation ecosystem, work practices, or anything else related to Futurice GmbH Ger­
many is considered. Additionally, only three of the purpose circles of the Futurice organization are
examined. This is because the three examined purpose circles directly contribute to Futurice’s innovative
performance, while the other two circles support and steer the company (see section 2.2). Other limi­
tations include the relatively small sample size for the conducted design workshop, which is due to the
length of the workshops (30 minutes) and the availability of Futurice’s employees. As a result, only one
representative from each purpose circle competence was interviewed.
1.4
Structure
This thesis is structured into seven chapters. Illustrating the background and objective of the research
within this Introduction (Chapter 1), the thesis starts by exposing the demand for the application of ecosys­
tems and foresight work at Futurice. The Futurice GmbH Company Introduction (Chapter 2) continues
by giving a generic company overview while providing a comprehensive overview of the organizational
structure, business strategy, mission, and vision. Afterward, the Literature Review (Chapter 3) goes on by
comprehensively surveying relevant publications and revealing the research gap that led to the research
goal. In the chapter, the thesis establishes the necessary business and innovation strategy, ecosystems,
and futures thinking terms and scientific knowledge around each topic. The thesis displays current prac­
tices, gives guidance on applying the presented framework, methodologies, and tools, and eventually
presents the limitations of each topic, as well as the scholarly critiques and problems arising. Next, the
research questions are presented in the Methodology (Chapter 4). The chapter also describes the research
process in detail, highlighting the amendment of frameworks and methodologies presented in the third
chapter. Part of this chapter reports on the data collection and analysis for the Ecosystem Startegy Map,
the Ecosystem Pie Model, and the consequent data collection process with Voros et al. Foresight Process
Framework. The Processing the EPM Data and Applying Futures Thinking with Voros et al. Foresight
Process Framework (Chapter 5) does the evaluation and data analysis of the ESM and EPM. After, the
EPM data is processed with Voros et al. foresight process framework. Within this framework, futures
thinking methodologies and tools of the LFC are applied. This step forms the foresight phase, in which
2
==PAGE:15==
alternative outcomes and scenarios are created and, in turn, are analyzed with a light analysis. At the
end of this chapter, recommendations and action items for Futurice’s business strategy are presented.
These can be applied on an organizational but also project level to improve the innovation capabilities of
Futurice’s innovation ecosystem. The Discussion (Chapter 6) consists of a critical reflection on the lim­
itation of the study, its aimed contribution, and its positioning within the ecosystem and futures thinking
research. The last chapter of the thesis, the Conclusion (Chapter 7), captures concluding thoughts on the
process and insights gained from the research.

Introduction
2.1
Company Overview
Futurice is a digital innovation and engineering company established in 2000 in Finland. Currently, Fu­
turice’s team comprises over 800 professionals in offices in, Helsinki, London, Berlin, Munich, Stuttgart,
Freiburg, Stockholm, and Tampere. Despite the number of offices, the headcount is still highest in
Helsinki, comprising approximately 600 professionals. In Germany, the headcount lies at approximately
150 people. Futurice’s core business is developing and designing digital services and products while help­
ing its clients to become future capable by transforming company culture, co­creating digital strategies,
and turning technology into business opportunities.
Futurice combines strategy, design, and software engineering to enable it to accompany the innovation
process of a product or service from ideation to successful market launch. Its core expertise, combined
with a business mindset, allows it to offer a unique human­centered co­creation process that establishes
creative practices within the company. As an outcome­focused digital innovation company, Futurice
inspires others to innovate and transmits its ways of working to clients, introducing them to a culture that
naturally embraces innovation. Additionally, the company’s Finnish work culture makes its employees
approachable and easy to collaborate with.
Futurice helps clients to put data to work, rethink customer experiences, or design entirely new business
models while trying to unleash a culture of innovation. The company works across industries – from
mobility to energy, retail to finance, health and media to construction, and much more. [1, 2]
2.2
Organizational Structure of Futurice GmbH Germany
According to Král and Králová, an organization structure is a combination of its processes, strategy, peo­
ple, technology, culture, and environment [3]. Furthermore, an organization’s structure is also compiled
through information sharing and decision­making processes [4, 5]. Futurice’s organizational structure is
purpose­driven, as suggested in the Holacracy [6]. Holacracy is characterized by an equal distribution
of power among self­organizing purpose circles rather than the top­down authority in a typical hierar­
chical corporate culture model. Holacracy aims to provide a flat management structure that distributes
authority. Within this structure, each purpose is represented by one circle while each purpose circle com­
prises sub­circles containing several competences (see figure 2.1). All­purpose circles link to each other.
The linkage is symbolized by the grey area around the individual purpose circles. Additionally, each
purpose circle can comprise initiatives. These initiatives are either circle driven or work as cross­circle
initiatives. In the latter case, the initiative contains people from other purpose circles and competences.
In the purposes circles, individual information sharing and decision­making take place. They develop
and implement processes independently of the core business while adhering to the company policy and
4
==PAGE:17==
strategy. Steering operates across the purpose circles, comes together in a self­organized manner, and is
thus integrated into the strategy process.
Since this study aims to investigate the innovation capabilities of Futurice’s individual purpose areas
and competences, not all circles are considered equally here. Accordingly, the client­facing circles that
deliver customer value through innovation will be considered. The ones excluded are operational and
greyed out in 2.1. The relevant purpose circles for this study are: Offering and Learnings, Skill Stack,
and Client Development.
Figure 2.1: The organizational structure of Futurice GmbH Germany based on internal documentation
by Markus Leidinger and the author [7]
Within this study, steering is not considered because innovation ecosystems look at the evolving set of
actors, activities, and artifacts that are important for the innovative performance of an actor or a population
[8]. At Futurice, steering is responsible for communicating work and decisions to the company based on
its purpose circles. Even though the purpose circle steering is maneuvering the company, innovative
performance is created on an individual purpose circle level. Accordingly, steering is not part of this
study. The competences of the respective purpose circles are described in Appendix A.

2.3
Business Strategy, Mission and Vision of Futurice GmbH Germany
Futurice is active in different countries. As the markets differ in a variety of ways, the business strategy
is created in a decentralized fashion according to the individual market needs. The following chapter
focuses on the German business strategy.
2.3.1
Futurice Germany’s Business Strategy
At the beginning of 2022, steering defined a new business strategy. This business strategy for Futurice
GmbH entails vital drivers and enablers as a strategic framework for navigating its company direction
in the market. Drivers are used to work ”in” the company, while the enablers are used to work ”on” the

3.1
Business and Innovation Strategy
The following sub­chapter will present the relevant literature from the business and innovation strategy
field. It highlights sources reflecting the interplay between business and innovation. Moreover, it reveals
its purpose, meaning, and definitions as its specific application in an entrepreneurial context is displayed.
3.1.1
General Introduction to Business Strategy
Whenever a business enterprise is founded, it implicitly or expressly uses a business model that explains
the design or architecture of the value generation or delivery of a product or service and captures the
processes used [11]. A business model’s core outlines how an organization provides value to consumers,
tempts them to pay for that value, and turns those payments into profit. Thus, a business model represents
management’s theories and makes assumptions about what consumers want, how they want it, and how
the business should be set up to best supply those demands, be compensated for doing so, and turn a
profit [12]. Yet, according to David Teece (2010), an organizational economist and professor, creating
a profitable business model is insufficient in and of itself to guarantee a competitive advantage. [12]
Rather, to protect such a competitive advantage, business strategy comes into play. David Campbell,
George Stonehouse, and Bill Houston (2002) describe business strategy as ”a continuous process that is
constantly reassessing strategic objectives of the always changing environment a company is operating
in” [13]. Additionally, the purpose of business strategy is to make a business continuously fit into and
for its environment to achieve the goal of the business [13]. In turn, as two professors from Bratislava
economics university state, ”a business strategy helps to describe and explain how, where and for what
purpose and goal a business model will be used” [14]. Thus, business strategy works in two ways: it
reflects and helps to develop the state in which an endeavor operates.
3.1.1.1
Resilience in Business Strategy
Considering the working title of this thesis and keeping the above in mind, it is crucial to define the term
resilience and see how it relates to business strategy overall. According to the journal ’Annual Review of
Ecology and Systematics’ from 1973, the term ”resilience” originated in the 1970s from Crawford Stan­
ley Holling’s research in the ecology field. Holling, an ecologist whose academic research addressed the
9
==PAGE:22==
interdependence and co­evolution of human economies and natural ecosystems, defined resilience as a
”measure of the persistence of systems and of their ability to absorb change and disturbance and still main­
tain the same relationships between populations or state variables” [15]. In a business context, a resilient
business strategy can quickly adapt to disruptions while maintaining continuous business operations and
protecting employees, assets, and overall brand reputation [16].
3.1.2
General Introduction to Innovation Strategy
There are various definitions within the two separate domains of innovation and strategy. Both domains
need to be defined to understand and establish a general definition. First, strategy in a business context has
many different meanings. Coming out of a long tradition of military thinking on the subject, Ulysses S.
Grant, a general in the U.S. Civil War and politician who served as the 17th president of the United States
from 1869 to 1877, viewed strategy as ”the deployment of one’s resources in a manner which is most
likely to defeat the enemy” [17, 18]. Alfred Chandler, a former Harvard professor, coined the classical
definition of strategy in 1962. According to him, strategy in a business context is ”the determination of the
basic long­term goals of an enterprise, and the adoption of courses of action and the allocation of resources
necessary for carrying out these goals” [11]. Chandler perceives strategy as the overarching goal while
adopting courses of action defines the ways and means to achieve the goal. This is in keeping with the
Oxford English Dictionary (OED), which states that strategy is ”a plan of action or policy designed to
achieve a major or overall aim” [19]. Even though strategy has been studied for years by business leaders
and business theorists, there is no definitive answer about what strategy is. However, all definitions have
in common that strategy is perceived as an enabler to define a way to achieve a future state.
To understand innovation strategy as a whole, the term innovation needs to be examined. The concept
of innovation has become more complex over time, and despite the vast body of literature available, it is
difficult to provide a comprehensive definition of the term. Garcia, from Michigan State University, high­
lights that several authors have tried to detain the essentials of innovation and establish shared typologies
of innovation [20]. However, as of yet, the term innovation is becoming a buzzword as it is perceived as
a significant achievement to re­evaluate firms’ products to develop a culture of innovation [21]. A cul­
ture of innovation generally focuses on longevity in an environment of a fast­moving market. An early
definition of innovation, from an economic perspective, was proposed by Joseph Schumpeter (1934), an
Austrian­born political economist. He defined it as ”a new combination of productive resources”. Ac­
cordingly, Peter Drucker (1998), a pioneer in modern management theory, claims that ”innovation, and
the ability to measure the performance thereof, is the one competence needed for the future” [22, 23].
The 21st Century Working Group defines innovation as ”a driver to transform insight and technology into
novel products, processes and services that create new value for stakeholders, drive economic growth and
improve standards of living” [18, 24]. The terms new and novel are used in several other definitions [25,
21]. In summary, innovation can be understood as a process that is designed to create a product or service
solution that delivers significant new customer value.
A review of the existing literature lacks a useful definition, structure, and use of the term business strategy
itself. When reviewing web­based references, it becomes evident that there is a variety of attempts that
try to define a business innovation strategy. However, most differ according to the author’s context and
environment. Even when reviewing innovation strategy practices in companies, only a few document
10
==PAGE:23==
their innovation strategy separately, while most include it as part of their overall business strategy. This
has been shown by Tendayi Viki, Dan Toma, and Esther Gons (2019), the authors of the book ’The
Corporate Startup’ who state that an innovation strategy is a source of an emergent strategy that helps to
adapt to its overall business strategy [26]. Yet, as the terms strategy and innovation have been defined
above, ”innovation strategy” can be delineated.
It may help an overall business strategy achieve its long­term goals through innovation and supports the
overall strategy. Thus, innovation strategy is based on a company’s specific business strategy and strategic
goals. This is supported by Roger Martin (2019), former dean of the Rotman School of Management at
the University of Toronto and author, along with A.G Lafley, of the book ’Playing to Win: How Strategy
Really Works’. He states that strategy is a set of interrelated and powerful choices that positions the
organization to win” [27]. As a consequence, innovation strategy can be defined as a ”cumulative and
sustainable manner, about the type of innovation that best match corporate objectives” [28]. As of today,

Ecosystems
The following sub­chapter will present relevant literature from the field of ecosystems. It presents and

3.2.1
General Introduction to Ecosystems in Management
Over the last few years, there has been a surge of interest in ecosystems as a new way to depict a company’s
environment. Innovation ecosystem literature has become popular within the field of business and strat­
egy [31]. As the Boston Consulting Group‘s (BCG) Henderson Institute states, the term ”ecosystem” has
firmly established itself in the dictionary of management buzzwords, occurring 13 times more frequently
in annual reports than it did a decade ago [32]. The term is entering the vocabulary of technology firms,
and other established sectors from mobility, travel, health, housing, financial services, and manufactur­
ing [33, 34, 35]. Julian Kawohl and Denis Krechting (2022), authors of the book ’Ecosystemize Your
Business’, support this and think that ecosystems ultimately fulfill human needs in all areas of life [36].
According to Adam Hayes (2021), an economic sociologist and assistant professor at Hebrew University
in Jerusalem, ”a business ecosystem is the network of organizations—including suppliers, distributors,
customers, competitors, government agencies, and so on—involved in the delivery of a specific product
or service through both competition and cooperation.” [37]
Not only companies from established sectors benefit from ecosystems; incumbent firms also use ecosystem­
based business models to create value [38]. This was accelerated by the COVID­19 outbreak in 2020 as
the significance of digital interactions increased and will likely further hasten the development of busi­
ness models based on digital­ecosystems [39]. The ecosystem business models hold up a lot of potential
for additional revenue. According to a McKinsey report, the integrated network economy of ecosystems
could represent a global revenue pool of $ 60 trillion in 2025 [39]. The network economy is the expansion
of a firm’s network and portfolio. McKinsey underlines its statement by referring to seven out of the six
world’s top companies, measured by market capitalization, are ecosystem companies [35].
Beyond its popular business press and increase in search frequency, ecosystems have been eagerly adopted
in business strategy. Teece (2018) states in his reference work ’The Palgrave Encyclopedia of Strategic
Management’ that the concept of ecosystems might now be used in industries to substitute for perfor­
mance analysis [40]. Eamonn Kelly (2022), Chief Marketing Officer for Strategy & Operations at De­
loitte, says that some view the rise of ecosystems as an opportunity to create a powerful new competitive
advantage [34]. At the same time, growing interest in it and its application are driven by necessity be­
cause business environments are evolving more rapidly, requiring a quick acquisition and coordination of
diverse and novel capabilities [33, 40]. This is due to increasing competition, change, and globalization
in corporation environments requiring them to seek more efficient models to organize and innovate [40].

3.2.1.1
Definitions of Ecosystems in Management
In management, the concept of an ecosystem provides a metaphor for loosely coupled, dynamically co­
evolving associations of firms that compete and cooperate interactively to achieve complementarity and
innovation, which result in mutually shared value creation [41]. Based on an extensive literature re­
view incorporating 25 years of research, Marcel Bogers from Eindhoven University, Sims from Babson
College, and West from Keck Graduate Institute (2019) have proposed to define an ecosystem as ”an
interdependent network of self­interested actors jointly creating value” [42]. This definition emphasizes
the structure of the independent activities that underlay a valuable contribution to create a central goal of
joint value creation for the firm and ordinary customers. As stated by Bogers, Sims, and West (2019),
this definition focuses on four components: interdependence, network, self­interested actors, and creat­
ing value [42]. Notably, these components allow for explaining ecosystem success. In contrast, their
activities explain the goals of the ecosystem members, the network of relations between them, and the
interdependence and goals of these members. Understanding the ecosystem activities while connecting
them to the different components of an ecosystem creates an overall better understanding of the ecosys­
tem.
Still, there is no widely accepted definition of an ecosystem. This master thesis will use a general and
simple definition from Ron Adner (2016), professor of Strategy and Entrepreneurship at the Tuck School
of Business at Dartmouth College and founder of the Strategy Insight Group, that puts special emphasis
on its biological metaphor of ecosystems: ”The ecosystem is defined by the alignment structure of the
multilateral set of partners that need to interact in order for a focal value proposition to materialize [43]”.
This definition helps to clarify all parts of the ecosystem concept. It needs to be outlined in more detail
to understand the definition better. First, Adner uses the phrase ”alignment structure”. This wording
concentrates on all the ecosystem actors’ defined positions and activity flows. It highlights the actor’s
incentives and motives to act a certain way. Second, he declares ecosystems to be inherently multilateral
because it involves the participation of more than two [44]. Third, the term ”set of partners” in Adner’s
definition means that an ecosystem is not open­end as membership is defined by using the expression
”being a set”. Additionally, in this context, the word ”defined” means that the participating actors in
the system have a joint value­creation effort as a general goal. Fourth, the clause ”for a focal value
proposition to materialize” means that an ecosystem is about realizing and developing the activities of
the value proposition. Here the focus lies on the value proposition and the promised benefits. However,
the word ”materialize” weakens the extent of divergence as an ecosystem can sustain and still bring about
the promised value. [43]
Teece (2018) supports this definition by Adner (2016) while adding that ecosystems represent the en­
vironment that the firm must monitor and react to, which affects its dynamic capabilities and thus its
chance to build sustainable competitive advantage [45]. Others state that the notion of ecosystems is a
constant interplay of inter­linkages between multiple organizations in which relationships are constantly
co­evolving through actions and interactions of involved actors [46]. Here, individual performance is
tied to the overall performance of the ecosystem.

3.2.2
Different Types of Ecosystems
Despite the different perceptions of ecosystems and their interplay, three different types of ecosystems
can be identified: (i) business ecosystems, (ii) innovation ecosystems, and (iii) platform ecosystems [33].
In a range of situations, scholars have established a collection of definitions and concepts that use the
term ”ecosystem” with various labels and, occasionally, various meanings and purposes. On the one
hand, these different literature streams may be related to the concept’s applicability and flexibility. On
the other hand, these three streams are all different conceptualizations that might lead to contradictory and,
in some cases, competing concepts. For instance, Harald Overholm (2015) from Cambridge University
used the business ecosystem as a synonym for the innovation ecosystem. By contrast, others suggest that
all three concept streams of ecosystems are different and that it is vital to pay individual attention to them
[47, 48, 49]. The following section will outline each literature stream individually to provide an overview
of all three ecosystem streams.
The first stream, business ecosystems, focuses on firms as actors in a community of interaction [45]. This
is in line with James F. Moore (1993), a business strategist, who agrees by saying that a business ecosys­
tem is ”an economic community supported by a foundation of interacting organizations and individuals
– the organisms of the business world” [50]. Also, Teece’s (2007) definition of an ecosystem supports
the idea of a business ecosystem as the environment a firm is operating in must be monitored and reacted
to as this affects its dynamic capabilities and thus its ability to build a sustainable competitive advantage
[33, 45]. To achieve a competitive advantage, business ecosystems pay attention to the interaction of the
different actors impacting the enterprise, its customers, and suppliers to define strategic direction over the
other members [41]. Business ecosystems can be derived into two distinct types (see figure 3.2). These
types apply to all ecosystems and are relevant to this research.
Figure 3.2: Two basic types of business ecosystem based on BCG Henderson Institute [51]

First, solution ecosystems have a core firm coordinating the complementors’ offering that brings together
multiple contributors to produce a good or service. Throughout the creation of a new solution, suppliers to
the core firm or important complements can be part of the ecosystem as they are interdependent, and their
innovation activities must be coordinated with other players [51]. Second, transaction ecosystems are
defined by a central platform that connects independent producers of goods or services with independent
consumers in a two­sided market. This platform is most often a digital platform. Within a transaction
ecosystem, network effects help to attract new customers, often resulting in more suppliers delivering
more value to the ecosystem platform. This approach is connected to the third stream of ecosystems,
outlined in this literature review [36, 51].
The second stream, innovation ecosystems, looks at how firms collaborate to provide ”customer­facing
solutions” [52]. Granstrand and Holgersson (2020) define an innovation ecosystem as ”the evolving set of
actors, activities, and artifacts, and the institutions and relations, including complementary and substitute
relations, that are important for the innovative performance of an actor or a population of actors” [53].
Innovation ecosystems thus foster growth and capture the value created solely through the interaction
of an innovation ecosystem’s actors, activities, and artifacts. Observing and changing the interactions
and inter­linkages between these three pillars can bring the potential for optimization [49]. According
to MassChallenge (2022), a global network for innovators, innovation ecosystem include universities,
government, corporations, startup accelerators, venture capitalists, private investors, foundations, en­
trepreneurs, mentors, and the media. Each contributes significantly to innovation ecosystems by trans­
forming new ideas into reality through access, and financial investment [54]. To better understand the
concept of an innovation ecosystem, the science learning hub Pokapū Akoranga Pūtaiao in New Zealand
(2014) established one of the most prominent examples of an innovation ecosystem using a food bowl
example. It shows how the food bowl supports food companies to develop new product ideas, scale­up
production, and create new market opportunities while highlighting different components that make up
the ecosystem [55]. The example helps to better understand innovation ecosystems and how to look at
them in practice.

The third stream, platform ecosystems, consider how actors organize themselves around a platform. The
term platform can be interpreted as the common components used across a product family whose func­

3.2.3
Trends behind the Evolution of Innovation Ecosystems
There are various trends behind innovation ecosystems as innovation has become increasingly complex
in recent years. This complexity, in turn, has led to an increase in Research and Development (R&D)
spending, believing that higher spending would result in high returns [26, 56]. For instance, innovation
labs have flourished in a wide variety of places for several years. The intention and strategy behind an
innovation lab are to create a protected space for generating and implementing new, innovative ideas that
would otherwise not receive the attention and time needed to mature and grow in a company’s day­to­day
business. In innovation labs, ideas can be developed and implemented free of hierarchies, bureaucracy,
and process regulations as they are fully separated from the ordinary environment within a company.
Still, innovation labs often fail because companies need to build management processes around them
[57]. This is because innovators work on whatever they want to while neglecting the company’s cur­
rent ecosystem position and thereby forgetting to discuss possible strategic decisions while setting up
an appropriate frame of ambition for the company and identifying the ecosystem forces at disposal or
available to the company’s competition [36, 26]. Viki, Toma, and Gons (2019) acknowledge that only
a few products from corporate innovation labs will have validated business models or find themselves
aligned with a particular company’s strategic vision. As a result, companies have learned to put more
effective processes in place to manage innovation as a way to realize a specific value proposition. This
process is managed under the guise of innovation ecosystems. Allowing a network configuration of a
number of actors that together make a complex value proposition possible [58]. Innovation ecosystems
relate to existing networks of companies and entities and co­evolve capabilities around a shared set of
technologies. An ever­evolving world provides mechanisms to leverage technology, achieve excellence
in research and business competence, and compete efficiently against other companies. In due course,
innovation ecosystems originate from rising social and environmental challenges [37].

3.2.4.1
The ESM Roles
Ecosystems and their participants generally operate more efficiently than standalone companies because
of ”the power of the many”. Companies need to understand their current position in the marketplace
17
==PAGE:30==
to realize this advantage and fathom future options to participate in and build ecosystems. Therefore,
the ecosystem players‘ roles within the ecosystem need to be defined to create the ESM. These roles
define the position within the ESM (see section 3.2.4). According to Kawohl and Krechting (2022), the
following key roles define the position of a player within an ESM. [36]
Orchestrators run a business­to­customer (B2C) platform and interact directly with the end users. They
manage the customer interface and bundle products and services from various realizers (or sometimes
even other orchestrators). Becoming an orchestrator requires building and managing a platform, en­
abling collaborations, and possessing technical know­how to interpret the obtained data to provide novel,
innovative offerings (either by themselves or through partners). One can find orchestrators in all life
areas and not only in digital environments. Every retailer with a physical store that sells products and
services is an orchestrator. As stated by Kawohl and Krechting (2022), examples of orchestrators are
Airbnb, Uber, Amazon Alexa, Aldi a discounter, and a farmer’s market. [36]
The realizers are the providers of products, content, and services for the end user. They can directly
sell their products to end­user or indirectly distribute them through orchestrators. Realizers are active
participants in an ecosystem and have specialized knowledge and domain expertise to ensure that their
services and products meet customer needs and complement each other. Realizers are threatened by
Enablers that want to move closer to the final customer. Keeping in line with Kawohl and Krechting
(2022), examples of realizers are Mercedes, Harvard University, Hilton Hotels, Kellogs, and Universal
Pictures. [36]
Enablers act as supporters of other enablers, realizers, or orchestrators. They are business­to­business
(B2B) players who do not provide products or services to the end user. Enablers possess special knowl­
edge and develop high­quality goods and services for other businesses. According to Kawohl and Krecht­
ing (2022), most companies are Enablers, as they are the foundational backbone of the economy. With
their B2B focus, Enablers have the disadvantage of not having direct contact with final customers. They
often suffer from a strong, inward­directed production and technology focus, forcing them to innovate
constantly. With domain expertise below average, they risk low bargaining power and the commodifica­
tion of its core business. Enablers support businesses to be more successful. In compliance with Kawohl
and Krechting (2022), examples of enablers are Shell, IBM, Bosch, Boeing, McKinsey, and Siemens
Financial Services. [36]

Although one would expect to easily find charts and graphic material to show the flow of the innovation
ecosystem, there is not a lot available. The few that exist intend to equip managers with just some basic
idea of the system. Basically, there are three relevant models of innovation ecosystem modeling. Two
use existing qualitative methodologies like System Thinking and the Value Network Analysis from Verne
Allee’s [60, 61]. The third uses a new qualitative application approach called the Ecosystem Pie Model
(EPM). The EPM was invented as a toolkit by Madis Talmar et al. (2018), an assistant professor of
innovation and entrepreneurship at TU Eindhoven, to enable the modeling of innovation ecosystems.
The following section will outline the three approaches, their advantages, and their disadvantages.

To begin with, the key principle of Systems Thinking is that everything is connected [62]. Demonstrating
a company’s interconnections is supposed to find new ways to improve efficiency and resource economy.
However, mapping all interconnections quickly becomes increasingly complex, and it is difficult to keep
an eye on the whole, especially when the system keeps growing. The process of gathering information
and representing it meaningfully becomes rather complicated. Furthermore, defining a system’s bound­
ary is onerous and depends on limiting the problem space, which delineates the research’s scope and
boundaries. Elements within the boundary are part of the system; those outside the boundary comprise
the environment or context. This makes Systems Thinking unsuitable for this study because looking
at a set of interacting elements is inadequate to explain how the innovation ecosystem can increase the
chances of successful innovation activities. Element properties and functions are much more relevant as
these transform input into innovation outputs. Also, systems change quite frequently as interactions and
relationships evolve as elements of the environment change. [63, 64]
The Value Network Analysis (VNT) from Verne Allee, an American business consultant, and writer,
displays a network of relationships that generates economic value and other benefits through complex
dynamic exchanges between two or more individuals, groups, or organizations [61]. The VNT looks at
tangible and intangible exchanges as part of the value networks. Tangible value exchanges involve con­
ventional exchanges of goods, services, or revenue, including all transactions that involve contracts or
invoices. The system mandates these transactions and is necessary for successful product or service de­
livery. Intangible value exchanges include two primary subcategories: knowledge and benefits. Strategic
information, planning knowledge, process knowledge, technical know­how, collaborative design, policy
formulation, and other factors are examples of intangible knowledge exchanges that occur inside the sys­
tem and support the main product and service value chain creation [61]. The VNT specialty is to unearth
the interplay of tangible and intangible value exchange in value networks. The disadvantage of the VNT
is that it focuses too much on micro details while the broader strategic perspective easily gets lost.
The final model presented here was created by Talmar et al. in 2018. They explicitly designed a qualita­
tive research strategy tool for innovation ecosystem mapping called the EPM [58]. The EPM results from
basic concepts and methodologies of business and strategy literature and has been repeatedly adopted,
modified, and further developed. Over time, the EPM has turned from being a mere metaphor into a
persuasive definition of a concept [31]. It includes a graphical tool and a modeling methodology that
makes it possible to map, design, and analyze innovation ecosystems both for managerial and scholarly
purposes [64]. The EPM consists of actors, a value proposition, and user segments. To do the analysis,
its building blocks resources, activities, value addition, value capture, and risk are determined in detail.
Talmar et al. EPM (2018) will henceforth receive the main focus because it was explicitly designed to
build a visual representation of an innovation ecosystem. It convenes with the scope of this study to
develop an ecosystem that captures innovation practices that, in turn, can be framed into action items
to improve business strategy and, accordingly, innovation practices. Yet, some parts of the Systems
Thinking and the VNT will be adopted (see section 4.5.1). This is relevant as it provides a sufficient basis
to investigate all parts of an innovation ecosystem while simultaneously demonstrating them visually and
clearly. The following figure 3.5 demonstrates the EPM. To better understand its different components
and building blocks, please refer to the upcoming section 3.2.6.

3.2.6
The Main Components of the EPM
The real­world performance of an innovation ecosystem emerges from the interplay of features held by
individual actors. How they interact in creating and capturing value can be mapped. Mapping, designing,
and analyzing ecosystems involves simultaneous consideration of features at both scales: the ecosystem
21
==PAGE:34==
and each contributing actor. [64] The three main components of the EPM are first, actors, second, an
overarching value proposition, which is referred to as the ecosystem’s value proposition (EVP); and third,
user segments (see figure 3.5). To better understand the EPM model, the following section will explain
its main components.
Actors
In innovation ecosystems, the organizations, institutions, communities, and individuals are the main ac­
tors engaging with value creation and capture in any given ecosystem [58]. Actors can be conceived as
a construct representing the legally independent but economically interdependent entities that engage in
specific productive activities within the modular architecture of the ecosystem [58, 33]. Individual actors’
productive contributions do not need to be equal. Each actor in the EPM is represented by a portion of the
”ecological pie” to symbolize the separation (boundaries) of the parties involved. The actors are shown
clockwise, starting with the one who is the most distant from the end user. These actors in the pie form
a value chain in which each new actor employs the good(s) or service(s) of the preceding one as input
and creates output that, of course, includes some aspect of the former actor’s added value. According to
Talmar et al. (2020), several value chains operate side by side in most ecosystems and may even com­
plement each other. They are all demonstrating internal but also external dependencies. In such cases,
there is no specific rule for how they position themselves against each other within the EPM. To keep the
model less complex, some actors may be pooled into a single sector based on similarity. To further reduce
complexity, a user can choose to exclude specific actors without jeopardizing the analytical power. [64]
Ecosystem’s Value Proposition
Within the structuralist approach, an ecosystem is characterized by a system­level goal in the form of a
coherent customer­oriented solution. The ecosystem’s value proposition (EVP) represents an overarching
offering by the supply­side agents in the ecosystem corresponding to the assumed need and desire of the
end user [58]. It can be considered as the integrated output of the whole ecosystem. The EVP is the focal
point of the EPM to signify that it is being carried out by all actors represented therein, either directly or
indirectly. It is crucial to comprehend how the end user interprets the EVP. This implies that the ‘job’ an
EVP does for the user may be different than what the actor thinks it is. [64]
User Segments
User segments specify who the target audience of the EVP is [58]. They are different from users. Talmar
et al. (2018) differentiate between users and consumers and state that users conduct an explicit transaction
in the form of a purchase, while in most occasions, the users are indeed expected to pay for a product or
service while not in all instances do user segments need to buy the value offering [64]. In the EPM, user
segments constitute a separate sector. Talmar et al. (2018) justify this statement by providing an example
of Google’s search engine. They outline that ”the value offering of the search is available to users for free,
but its existence enables Google to receive revenue elsewhere from a set of actors other than the users of
the search engine (i.e., selling advertising space to businesses)” [64]. These sectors are positioned last in
the clockwise sequence of the ecosystem pie, representing the user’s positioning as the ultimate target of
the entire ecosystem’s networked value creation and delivery logic. There can be more than one sector if
22
==PAGE:35==
there are several distinct segments. There are three reasons why users are an independent component in
the innovation ecosystem pie. First, in many ecosystems, consumers have some degree of control over
the actors who contribute to providing value in a given instance of use or consumption. Second, value
in ecosystems is often co­created as users are involved in the product or service development process
and provide frequent feedback on the development circle. Third, users can provide value that can be
translated, such as usage data, that some ecosystem actors can utilize to create more value either in or
outside the ecosystem. [64]
3.2.7
Building Blocks of the EPM
Several building blocks on an actor level provide an understanding of each actor’s value proposition,
how each contributes to the ecosystem, and how reliable one may expect the other contributions to be.
These building blocks increase the analytical power for understanding the ecosystem and its real­world
implications. The building blocks of the EPM are analyzing the main components of the EPM (see
section 3.2.6). Looking at each building block provides significant information about the other actors
in the ecosystem. The focus is on the resources used for activities that create and capture value. The
following section will highlight each building block while describing what it includes and what to look
for when gathering information on the specific building block. [64]
Resources
According to Edith Penrose, an American­British economist (1996), resources are the basis of firm­
specific value creation [65]. Besides conventional resources like capital, personnel, and technology,
Penrose (2000) considers the relationship between the various kinds of resources within a firm works and
the development of skills, ideas, experience, and knowledge [66]. The first building block is illustrated
as the outer circle of the EPM (see figure 3.5). Resources describe the most important assets that form the
basis for the value creation of a particular actor. Here tangible and intangible assets are captured, which
include capabilities, organizational processes, firm attributes, information, and knowledge available to the
actor for performing value­creating activities. Resources do not need to be owned by one single actor but
can be shared with other actors in the ecosystem. According to Jonathan Sapsed (2001), demonstrating all
available resources to an actor is to show links between the existing knowledge resources and strategies
of the firm [67]. Looking at a company’s resources helps to identify knowledge gaps that generally point
to a certain strategy misalignment. For precision, paying attention to the type of resources in connection
to the EVP helps to ensure that only relevant resources are being mapped. [64, 58]
Activities
The second circle of the EPM represents activities (see figure 3.5). According to Talmar et al. (2020),
activities are the mechanisms by which an actor generates its productive contribution to the ecosystem
[64]. Specifically, activities encompass the utensils that the actor uses to add value to the ecosystem.
Different activities across the ecosystem combine with activities of the other actors [43]. Each actor’s
activities within the pie provides valuable information on the different activities across the ecosystem and
their reciprocal relations [58].
23
==PAGE:36==
Value Addition
Value addition is the outcome of complementary offerings in the ecosystem provided by the activities of
each actor. Consequently, each actor has a contribution that accomplishes the EVP. All products and ser­
vices contributing to the EVP are mapped within the building block value addition. Having an overview
of all additions helps to understand if each value chain in the ecosystem has all necessary elements in
place and to stress test if all value additions in combination are providing value to the EVP. [64]
Value Capture
In exchange for subjecting resources and activities that result in value addition to accomplish a particular
EVP, actors are interested in receiving a gain of some kind. This gain can be understood as value capture.
It presents how, what kind, and how much value a particular actor creates through the ecosystem. This
building block provides accurate information on who creates and owns how much in terms of financial
gains, growth, increase in reputation, better efficiency or additional resources like an increase in knowl­
edge [68]. This chart represents the mechanism by which an actor gains returns. According to Talmar
et al. (2018), the more actors customize their resources, activities, and value addition in regard to the
ecosystem, the higher the demand for value capture is expected to be. [64]
Risk
According to Adner (2016), all the significant value additions necessary to complete the EVP must be
fulfilled from the perspective of the entire ecosystem. This condition requires that the actors are willing
to contribute positively to the ecosystem as a whole by delivering their individual value additions [43].
This condition should not be assumed to exist automatically as problems might hinder an actor from
providing the assumed technological solution as it may be too difficult to accomplish in scope, cost, or
time [69]. Beyond, another risk factor is the potential sources of unwillingness. a) A low effort caused
by disinterest, most likely caused by a marginal impact dependence of that actor on the ecosystem as a
whole. b) Additional spending is needed on resources that can only be used for one specific ecosystem
and cannot be redeployed elsewhere. And c) a misalignment between the actors and the ecosystem’s
strategic interest in the value addition. Apart from the willingness and the ability evaluation, the risk is a
compound assessment of the actor’s likelihood to contribute to the value addition that helps accomplish
the EVP. Within the visualization of the EPM, the risks are demonstrated in colors. It applies the traffic
color scheme using green for no risk, yellow for medium, and red for high risk. [64]
3.2.7.1
Dependence
Innovation ecosystems include a diverse set of actors that all have diverse profiles. While completing
the EVP may be of the utmost importance for some of these actors, for others, it may just represent
a minor portion of their overall activity [43]. The degree to which an actor’s success correlates with
the ecosystem indicates the actor’s ”dependency” on the ecosystem. The innovation ecosystem has three
”dependency” levels: L ­ low dependence, M ­ medium, and H ­ high dependence. These are mapped next
to the individual building blocks of the ecosystem (see figure 3.5). The dependency level demonstrates
an actor’s dependence on whether or not the particular EVP is accomplished. [58]

3.2.8
Challenges and Issues of Innovation Ecosystems
Since the approach to innovation ecosystems is relatively new, and only a few have experienced what
it means to define and build an innovation ecosystem, literature on challenges and issues is still limited.
Nonetheless, through interaction industries are in the infant stages of their innovation ecosystem develop­
ment, some challenges and issues of the innovation ecosystem theory can be derived. According to some
of the literature cited above, the implementation of an EPM would appear to be of an almost initiative
nature, whereas in practice, its implementation is anything but intuitive for various reasons.
First, the ecosystem concept is challenging and lacks a clear and coherent self­explanatory understanding
of what it entails, as the Joint Research Centre of the European Commission (2017) has found out [70].
In their latest report on challenges in fostering the European innovation ecosystem, it says that the terms
and conditions of such an innovation ecosystem need to be designed for a swift roll­out to enable a
set of instruments that help to establish an innovation ecosystem in an approachable way. According
to the Joint Research Centre, this could be further developed by creating a collective for experiences
and good practices in the design of funding such instruments. [70] Consequently, this lack of coherent
understanding of its substance has led to considerable heterogeneity in how the ecosystem approach has
been applied. Second, organizations often struggle to align their innovation ecosystem with different
fields of scientific knowledge or for the immediately practical demand for real­time data gathering [60].
Beyond the scientific challenges, identifying the managerial objectives of the ecosystem approach is
difficult, especially given the varying and diverse meanings ascribed to the approach by various actors
and interests. This is perceived by the study of Regah Yaghmaie and Wim Vanhaverbeke (2019), who
state that identifying the innovation ecosystem orchestrated goals is complex as communication among
ecosystem actors might encounter organizational communication problems caused by technology, people,
processes, or the structure of the firm [71].
Third, there is no singular, definitive articulation of the concept of ecosystems. This literature outlined
different types of ecosystems, while having different commonalities, nonetheless vary in terms of mean­
ing and even in purpose. Some formulations are occasionally used interchangeably, yet they address
specific meanings in some contexts. Particularly, ”ecosystem approach to management” is frequently
used to express that humans interact with ecosystems in one context; in another, it refers to ecosystems
themselves that need to be controlled. [60]
Objective setting in an innovation ecosystem is crucial for innovation ecosystem planning. To that end,
even if there is some terminological ambiguity, the ecosystem approach is generally rather clear­cut and
cohesive. Even though it is primarily a top­down paradigm, there is a lot of flexibility for adjusting to
specific needs. In fact, individual and situational applications are the main drivers for issues and chal­
lenges arising from the approach. Getting a grasp of how to apply it and bring it into the organizational
context is perceived as very challenging. [60] After reviewing the three existing approaches to define and
build innovation ecosystems and incorporating them into an organizational context, it becomes evident
that all practices are mostly qualitatively driven. Qualitative data are descriptive and conceptual findings
collected through questionnaires, interviews, or observation [ualitativedata]. Yet, quantitative data can­
not be collected by using one of the models. Including quantitative data that can be counted or measured
in numerical values could be of utmost importance to support analysis and decision­making after an inno­
25
==PAGE:38==
vation ecosystem has been defined and built. This literature review has shown that actors rarely concern
themselves with defining and building the visual representation of an innovation ecosystem. For this to
happen, it would require the provision of accurate real­time data in order to solve some of the challenges
discussed above. The three approaches presented contain certain restraints and produce challenges. Yet,
Systems Thinking is highly flexible, which, in turn, makes a specific definition of the boundaries of re­
search a challenge. However, its flexibility in its boundaries of research is a great advantage in adapting
to the given situation. It allows the inclusion or exclusion of research perceived as relevant or irrelevant.
While this is true for the innovation ecosystem approach this is different for the others.
The VNT and the EPM approaches operate within relatively rigid parameters. This is especially true
for the EPM, as all building blocks depend upon each other and thus allow for limited flexibility only.
Also, the EPM collects various data streams through its building blocks without classification. For this
reason, data understanding and analysis are superior within the VNT approach than the EPM, which
solely maps different types of tangible and intangible resources. The building block ’activities’ does not
distinguish between tangible and intangible ’activities’ even though this might bring valuable insight into
value­creating activities. This is specifically important when analyzing the EPM after it has been built.
Section 4.4 will explain why combining all three methodologies will help to close this literature gap while
adapting the EPM for this research.
3.3
Foresight and Futures Thinking
This sub­chapter will present research publications from the field of future studies. It discusses its origin
and trends while looking at measures undertaken to cope with the increasing technological and social
change. Beyond, different approaches to anticipating the future are laid out. Particular focus will be put
on a foresight process framework (FPF) to showcase how to apply futures thinking methodologies and
tools can be applied. Within this thesis, a broad review of the key concepts of futures thinking will help to
develop an understanding of the field. Ultimately, challenges and issues of the FPF and futures thinking
methodologies and tools will be presented. The breadth and depth of the literature is vast, which is why
the following review of the subject has focused on the context of this research.
The future has received much attention amongst literature and scholars for centuries. The term ’foresight’
was the original term that was first used by Herbert G. Wells in 1932 [72]. Later on, it was referred to
as ’Futurology’ and defined as the ”study of the future” [73]. The term ’Futurology’ was coined by the
German lawyer and political scientist Ossip K. Flechtheim in the mid­1940s. Flechtheim proposed it as a
new research topic that would include a new approach to probability theory [74]. More recently, various
terms have been established for futurology, such as future studies, future research, futurism, or futures
thinking.
According to the journal ’Trends in Cognitive Science’ (2001), futures thinking originates from human
cognition in which thinking about the future is an integral component [75]. The idea is that the human
brain often involves two distinct systems: episodic and semantic memory. Episodic memory usually
includes details of an event, the context in which the event took place, and emotions associated with
the event [76]. Semantic memory is a category of long­term memory that demands the recollection of
ideas, concepts, and facts commonly regarded as general knowledge [77]. Futures thinking, specifically,
26
==PAGE:39==
derives from episodic memory because it projects the self into the future to pre­experience an event
[75]. To a greater or lesser extent, predicting the future is an inescapable part of human destiny, and our
provision for the future always involves some element of prediction. In the last forty years, this episodic
futures thinking has been applied to imagine or simulate experiences that might occur in one’s personal
future. This imagination and simulation has now been added to a business and industry context. Within an
organizational context, futures thinking empowers businesses to anticipate future scenarios and intends to
make it future­proof [78, 79, 80]. A standard definition of futures thinking is ”a creative and exploratory
process that uses divergent thinking, seeking many possible answers and acknowledging uncertainty”
[81]. In this definition, divergent thinking can be referred to as a thought process or a method used to
generate creative ideas by exploring many possible solutions [82].
Within an organizational context, futures thinking helps to approach challenging situations by reducing
inevitable disruption caused by a volatile, uncertain, complex, and ambiguous (VUCA) world. Although
the point of futures thinking is not to predict certain future scenarios, it will create awareness of the change
to come. It helps to tackle VUCA situations in today’s decision­making processes [83]. Futures thinking
provides organizations guidance in prioritization and preparation for concrete future worlds while pay­
ing attention to risks and opportunities that may arise when such challenges are being tackled. Futures
thinking helps organizations fathom future scenarios while simultaneously saving them from potentially
incorrect assumptions solely based on current beliefs. No matter if the future will be different and uncer­
tain, an organization is well advised to prepare for it through reflection on what may change and what
may stay the same. [79, 84] The term futures thinking uses the plural form of ”future” because there are
many possible futures, not just one.
3.3.1
Trends behind the Evolution of Futures Thinking
Business thrives on stability. Yet, any sense of stability is a fleeting occurrence over a short period of
time at best. In fact, stability never happens [85]. Just recently, inflation has hit its highest level since
the early 1980s. Many technology stocks have plunged dramatically from the beginning of this year; the
pandemic has pressed into its third year, deepening the supply chain crises while Russia wages war on
Ukraine. Combined, these events and others appear to accelerate the future in general [80]. According
to McKinsey’s ’Technology Trends Outlook 2022’ report, technology continues to be a primary catalyst
for change in the world. Trends like climate change, web3, cloud and edge computing, machine learning,
the metaverse, and applied artificial intelligence (AI) are only major examples of vast trends that are
amending the ways companies innovate and compete with each other [86].
Due to increased volatility, trends, and an ever­changing business environment, companies must maintain
their customer base within the currently volatile economic environment [80]. Anticipating and actively
shaping the future by developing images and ideas on tomorrow’s world helps companies to stay resilient.
Thus futures thinking bridges the gap between the present and the future so that companies may begin
linking future work with today’s business. Futures thinking supplements strategic planning, which is
intended to provide solutions that harness volatility and unpredictability to certain degrees. It helps to
create a resilient business strategy and an actionable road map by providing guidance for today’s decision­
making. Imagining alternative futures supports a company in understanding where it is at the moment
compared to the possible future worlds. [79] As mentioned, the goal of futures thinking is not to predict
27
==PAGE:40==
the future but to understand the potential of long­term impacts of today’s phenomena. There are vari­
ous ways of conceptualizing the future with different approaches. Section 3.3.3 will highlight the most
relevant for this study.
3.3.2
Applying a Foresight Process Framework
This section provides an overview of a generic FPF. The three­phase framework was proposed by Averil
Horton, from Brunel University London, in 1999. It was further developed based upon prior independent
work by a Canadian academic and author on business and management, Henry Mintzberg, and a writer in
the field of futures studies, Richard A. Slaughter [87, 88]. It is one of the most comprehensive approaches
to strategic foresight and turned out to be one of the most recognized FPFs in literature [89]. This is due
to its characteristics as it emerged as a very adaptive and iterative framework that can be applied to
various use cases and scenarios. According to the United Nations Development Programme (2018), this
framework is capable of designing customized foresight methodologies. However, it can be applied as
a diagnostic tool to evaluate existing foresight initiatives [89]. Based on its characteristics, the FPF can
guide the structure and design of holistic and diverse futures thinking processes. It transforms information
streams into input and uses as well as directs this information in a futures thinking process. It structures
and executes futures thinking methodologies and tools by translating the findings into specific outputs,
which can be translated and transformed into strategy. The strength of the FPF ­ its general approach that
contributes structure and provides room for adaptation simultaneously has inspired a number of authors
to work with it [88].
Joseph Voros (2003), foresight educator, researcher, and consultant, and his colleagues from Australia
made one of the most prominent and recent adaptions by dividing Horton’s phase three into two distinct
elements: first, the outputs of the foresight process and second, the actions taken resulting from the out­
puts. In their adoption, these actions are called strategy as they form the consequent strategy development
and strategic planning process of the foresight process. The following figure 3.6 demonstrates Voros and
colleagues’ latest adaption of the four­phase foresight process.
Figure 3.6: The four­phase FPF based on Voros et al. adaption [88]

Voros et al. (2003) intentionally separated the outputs of the foresight work from strategy as outputs, in
turn, provide input for considering decisions and implementing actions that address traditional strategy
work. In essence, it separates the foresight output from the strategy that becomes implemented due
to the foresight work that preceded it and thus allows for an independent look at the former success.
Additionally, the framework shows how foresight feeds into strategy while extending the framework
from a three­phases to a more sophisticated and better­calibrated four­phase structure. The framework
recognizes four interdependent phases: Inputs, Foresight, Outputs, and Strategy. [88]
For the purpose of this study, only Voros et al. (2003) FPF will be considered further because it alone
allows for a systemic integration of existing methodologies and practices into a workable structured FPF.
Voros FPF is especially appealing as it not only processes predetermined types of data but also implements
data from a variety of even unpremeditated data. Therefore, the FPF allows designing an individual but
highly customized futures thinking process that can be modified to the respective situation and input type.
It so clearly shows how outputs can be transformed into strategy. The four phases of Voros et al. (2003)
FPF will be explained to better understand the structuring process of a generic and adaptive FPF.
3.3.2.1
Inputs Phase
The Input Phase (see the first white phase of figure 3.6) is the first phase of the FPF. This phase covers
the phases of framing, gathering information, analyzing, sharing and collaborating, and disseminating
high­quality information. Many methods, techniques, and tools exist to collect information. According
to Slaughter (1996), it is where, for example, in workshop formats, the group is asked a set of critical
questions designed to open the thinking about the near future [90]. These workshop formats provide an
open environment and can have any format. The primary characteristic of this workshop format is the
collection of an enormous amount of overlapping, and often contradictory data [91].
3.3.2.2
Foresight Phase
The Foresight Phase (see the second blue phase of figure 3.6) comprises three broad steps following an
interdependent sequence. The first step is analysis. Voros et al. (2003) consider it a preliminary stage for
more in­depth work. The goal is to create order from a vast variety of data generated by the Input Phase.
Common approaches are, for instance, a trend analysis, cross­impact metrics, or an analytical technique
like a thematic analysis to map issues. Second, comes the interpretation probing into the structure of
patterns and themes. Voros et al. (2003) state that the result of such an interpretation effort will open
up the realm for critical future studies where analysis through in­depth futures thinking approaches can
be conducted. The interpretation phase is, in fact, instrumental for identifying key divers for a futures
thinking initiative. The final third concerns what Voros et al. (2003) coined as purposefully looking
forward to creating future perspectives. The use of methodologies and tools can somewhat vary based
on the purpose and scope of a given project which may, in due course, determine if potential futures are
considered possible, plausible, probable, or preferable. [88, 92]
3.3.2.3
Output Phase
Within the Output Phase (see the third white phase of figure 3.6), there are tangible and intangible outputs.
Tangible outputs are the ones that form the action range of options generated by the work. According to
Voros et al. (2003), intangible outputs include the changes in thinking engendered by the whole process
29
==PAGE:42==
and are considered riskier than tangible ones. Creating acceptance for intangible outputs can be difficult
to achieve up to the point of rejection. At the same time, however, the authors regard it as the more
useful output as it alters the mechanism of strategy development, i.e., the perceptions of those involved in
strategizing. The overall focus of the Output Phase is to understand the insights and the simulation gained
to support the decision­making process and set the direction for the Strategy Phase. This phase answers
the question of ’what might we need to do’. It aims to expand people’s perspectives and impressions
about the perceived options and serves as the basis to form strategic options to be fed into the fourth and
last phase, the Strategy Phase. [88, 92]
3.3.2.4
Strategy Phase
The Strategy Phase (see the fourth rose­colored phase of figure 3.6) is the final part of this framework
that builds the relationship between foresight and strategy processes and planning. Suitably, Voros et al.
(2003) state that foresight is used as an element of strategic thinking as it opens and expands the range
of perception of the strategic options available. It explores options and thus influences strategy in the
making. Within strategic planning, options can be implemented as action items. Strategic thinking does
not, however, replace strategic planning. Rather, the Output Phase supports the decision­making process
and provides guidance. In contrast, the Strategy Phase defines action items based on the development
undertaken in the Output Phase. [88]
Since the FPF is an iterative process, all results need to be constantly fed back into the loop of the overall
foresight process framework. According to Voros et al. (2003), there are feedback loops after each phase
and step. Feedback loops ensure that the processed information is monitored and improved. [88, 92]

3.3.4
Challenges and Issues of Futures Thinking
Knowing how to start and apply futures thinking might be one of the biggest obstacles a company faces
when looking into future work. Principle Designer at Futurice, Annina Antinranta (2022), states that
foresight leads to innovation as futures thinking can help an organization to highlight areas where the
company can grow [99]. Only a few authors describe a holistic approach to applying futures thinking
as part of a foresight process, and identifying where to grow becomes increasingly challenging. Most
reviews and literature streams suggest the individual application of methodologies and tools that can be
chained together, thereby setting up a generic framework on how to run futures thinking as a process rather
than an individual activity is only described by a few literature streams. This makes future work with
its methodologies and tools accessible for individual applications but does not bring clarity about their
interactions and complements to create a holistic process. Also, applying a FPF and futures thinking
methodologies takes time and effort. This is because the LFC’s futures thinking methodologies and
tools do not provide a guideline as to which methodologies and tools, in the form of canvases, can be
strung together. This makes applying the LFC challenging and overwhelming as the user needs to get
an overview of all methodologies and tools provided. Applying these methodologies and tools in a FPF
could lead to absolute bewilderment as the Input Phase of the FPF determines the data collection and,
therefore, restricts the data use and collection of the LFC considerably.
Applying and understanding the FPF and its different futures thinking methodologies is one of the biggest
challenges. Companies frequently claim to be focused on innovation and growth but fail to put the tools
and procedures in place that support their employees in becoming successful innovators [99]. Most com­
panies have never invested in the skills and practices required to lead like futurists and now experience
a capability gap [100]. Some companies apply futures thinking methodologies and tools but need to pay
more attention to the practice within a FPF. Yet, such a framework is needed to translate the outputs
of futures thinking methodologies and tools into valuable action items that, in turn, can be anchored in
strategy.
Another challenge to applying a FPF and futures thinking methodologies and tools is ”near­termism”.
Justin Lokitz, Lisa Kay Solomon, and Patrick van der Pijl (2016), the authors of the book ’Design A Better
Business’, think that organizations are wired to protect what is known and create incentives for short­term
35
==PAGE:48==
measurable results only [101]. Organizations measure near­term results like deadline­driven projects,
quarterly earnings, and annual goals. They are easy to measure and visible. Conversely, investing in
the future requires time to discover areas of hidden value. Solomon (2019) suggests overcoming this
near­term bias by creating a specific allocation of free time or a reward structure for exploration [100].
Another issue for the application of futures thinking is company politics. A company’s people network
across functions and geographies to accomplish shared goals. Generally, management is focused on
protecting their people, budgets, and accounts. The latter is not conducive to a functioning future­
thinking culture. When an organization purposefully transcends information, ideas, and experiments
across boundaries, departments, and geographies, they start to think about the future holistically and
expansively. Solomon (2019) claims that senior leadership may aid in this endeavor by establishing
incentives for collaboration and experimentation based on discovery and shared learning opportunities.
Making the wall of a shared future where news items, trends, and unexpected wild cards are posted is one
possibility. [100, 101]

Chapter Summary
In sum, this study utilized a systematic literature review approach that combines bibliographic coupling
and content analysis methods to draw on perspectives from three different areas. A sequence for a con­
secutive understanding of the general literature on business and innovation strategy served as a basis.
After that, the concept of ecosystems, Voros et al. FPF and futures thinking methodologies and tools was

Innovation ecosystems have become a core element in some firms’ growth strategies in various industries.
While past reviews revealed the conceptual and theoretical connections between innovation ecosystems
and other related concepts, there remains a lack of comprehensive appreciation of future and strategy
work within innovation ecosystem studies. This may be the case because analyzing companies based on
an ecosystem is still a relatively recent development. Also, as stated in the business and innovation strat­

Having a company’s business and innovation strategy aligned can promise innovation ecosystem collab­

Knowing how to relate to the existing strategy while assessing the current ecosystem is one of the crit­
ical challenges where most companies need help. Ecosystems are not about building and integrating an
entirely new concept. It is about understanding current practices and contributions that create customer
value related to its business model, business operation, and innovation strategy. Promoting an ecosystem
is only possible by referencing and considering a company’s strategy.

ecosystem approach often needs to be customized, even though there has been a great effort to create
a generic approach to map the innovation ecosystem by Talmar et al. [64]. Nevertheless, using and

study introduced the EPM as a tool to create a visual representation of Futurice’s innovation ecosystem.
In accordance, its components and building blocks are highlighted in detail.

Methodology
The methodology applied in this study is based on qualitative empirical research. It uses an abductive
approach that adjusts the approach based on the research process and information gathered. It tests the
theory while adapting it to the Futurice use case. Accordingly, the fourth chapter describes the generic
research question and its associated sub­questions. The following explanations of the research design
show how the data is obtained and how the research questions are answered according to different research
methods. To better understand the research design and its methods, an adoption and amendment of the
innovation ecosystem will be presented to show how the method has been adapted for this specific use
case. The data collection highlights the details of the empirical data collection process and showcases a
design workshop to map the innovation ecosystem at Futurice. The chapter ends with a description of
the data analysis.
4.1
Research Questions
Research frequently starts with a quite general question. However, a wide range of interest needs to be
narrowed to a tighter focus, moving from a general research area to specific research questions [102].
Alan Bryman (2016), professor of organizational and social research, writes in his book ’Social Research
Methods’ that this movement acknowledges that the research will not answer questions and research will
not answer questions ad infinitum but for practical reasons. It will stay within the boundaries of the
original question or interest because the abductive approach necessitates a narrowing focus to ensure that
the research questions stay related to a coherent set of issues. The more clear and concise the questions
of interest are formulated in the beginning, the better and quicker the results will be [103]
The literature review provided a theoretical understanding of three different literature streams a) on busi­
ness and innovation strategy, b) approaches to applying innovation ecosystems, and c) futures thinking
methodologies and tools to anticipate future scenarios. The theoretical base informed and enabled the
development of one generic research question:
RQ: How can an innovation ecosystem and futures thinking methodologies and tools to anticipate
future scenarios help Futurice GmbH Germany to create a resilient business strategy?
To answer the generic research question, it has been divided into sub­questions. These sub­questions are
focused on the individual parts of the generic research. These sub­questions are not too narrow, capable
of being researched through data collection, based on and related to prior study or theory, but with the
potential to make an original contribution. Accordingly, six sub­questions have been developed.
RSQ1: How can the innovation ecosystem at Futurice GmbH Germany be mapped?
RSQ2: How to combine business innovation and strategy with innovation ecosystem mapping and
approaches to anticipate future scenarios?

RSQ3: Based on its innovation ecosystem, what adjustments can Futurice GmbH Germany make to
improve its innovation capabilities?
RSQ4: What foresight process framework can be applied to anticipate future scenarios by using the

RSQ5: What reasonable action items can be derived from the output of the futures thinking work to

4.5.1
Adapting the EPM
To close the literature gap, the next part will solve some of the challenges of EPM tools and issues
identified (see section 3.2.8). It aims to combine all positive aspects of Systems Thinking and the VNT
principle into the EPM. Adapting the EPM will create a comprehensive research model for applying the
innovation ecosystem at Futurice.
As this study is interested in fostering a resilient business strategy, the research must be equally focused
and constantly reflective of the original issue. This is why only parts of the Systems Thinking approach
can be applied to this study, as Systems Thinking is, by definition, flexible to a point where it is in danger
of losing its original focus. On the other hand, Systems Thinking provides a degree of flexibility in setting
parameters that are useful for the investigation of the problem space. Adapting this flexibility to the EPM
can be valuable to ensure that more relevant company­specific information is mapped. This is especially
poignant in a multi­actor system when additional ecosystem­level building blocks help to map additional
value streams.
Regarding Futurice, adding a building block called ’Impact’ provides additional value to see where the
company is investing most. ’Impact’ is a key result (OKR) at Futurice. It is measured to achieve the
organization’s objectives. Accordingly, the OKR ’Impact’ measures the revenue percentage for each
competence of the purpose circle ’Offering and Learning’. Metrics on how much percentage of the rev­
enue generated through expertise fields or themes is available. Using ’Impact’ as an additional building
block shows which actor of the circle accures the most value in terms of revenue. This addition to the
adaption of the building block for the EPM can only occur for the purpose circle ’Offering and Learning’
but not for the other two purpose circles. This is because the ’Offering and Learning’ circle competences
are anchored in Futurice’s business strategy and consistently measured, while the other purpose circles
are not measured at all (see section 2.3.1.1). Putting it all together, ’Impact’ is an addition to the building
block ’Value Capture’. It quantifies the captured value by providing business metrics.
The VNTs focus on tangible and intangible value exchange is particularly interesting and important to
investigate how an increase in innovative capabilities at Futurice might be possible. This way, it will be
possible to analyze, evaluate and improve Futurice’s capability to convert tangible and intangible assets

==PAGE:54==
into another form of negotiable value, i.e., innovation efficiency [61]. Along the way, it becomes clear
which of the VNTs intangible resources could be made tangible or, conversely, which resources could be
cut out altogether. Mapping tangible and intangible innovation activities of the EPM, the second building
block, might additionally provide an understanding of which activities may lead to the implementation of
innovations. For instance: one employee coaches another, which enables the latter to generate revenue

4.5.3
Data Analysis of the EPM
In order to systematically evaluate the collected material, i.e., the statements made within the design
workshop, qualitative content analysis is applied. This is a method by the sociologist Philipp Mayring
(2017), which evaluates text­based data and was developed for analyzing large amounts of data [109].
Written materials are key for this kind of analysis which is why the sticky notes written during the EPM
mapping were evaluated. Accordingly, the EPM is defined as the material to be processed. The eval­
uation process aims to filter certain material aspects and assess them under specific criteria. Arthur J.
Cropley (2008) from Hamburg University thinks that the qualitative analysis serves to gain a general
understanding and knowledge of the content [110]. Accordingly, the EPMs data is systematically pro­
cessed and evaluated. Professor Fatemeh Rabiee (2004), from Cambridge University, describes the data
processing and evaluation process in three steps. [111]
First, evaluation criteria is formed. Here, five different EPM criteria suggested by Talmar et al. (2018)
[64] and three additional criteria created by the author are used to investigate the design workshop data
(see table 4.2). Second, these criteria are employed to create an evaluation guideline for the data analysis.
Using pre­defined criteria for analysis helps to create a mutual understanding of the data while providing
a structured and valid approach for understanding relationships and dependencies between the various
actors and parts contributing to Futurice’s innovation ecosystem. Ideas, challenges, problems, or de­
pendencies based on the criteria collected can be identified and transformed into descriptive statements.
These statements summarize data from the EPM and bring formerly singular data points into context.
Third, the most essential statements are categorized and listed based on the criteria. This will be done for
each EPM individually.

4.6.1
Data Collection through Voros et al. Foresight Framework and the LFC
Since the data generated with the EPM forms the Inputs Phase, the generated data directly enters the
Foresight Phase of Voros et al. FPF. The analysis, the first step of the Foresight Phase, uses thematic
analysis to analyze the collected data. Thematic analysis is a method for capturing patterns and themes
across qualitative data sets [112]. It synthesizes qualitative research findings and analyzes ideas. The
patterns and themes are identified based on their recurrence and the tabulated data similarities and dif­
ferences. Thematic analysis has the advantage of being flexible as it does not follow specific guidelines
to process the data [105]. According to Mark Saunders, Philip Lewis, and Adrian Thornhill (2007), the
authors of the book ’Research Methods for Business Students’, the thematic analysis does not belong
to any particular research philosophy. However, it provides generally accepted insight into activities of
various kinds [106].
Categorized and tabulated data sets are then released to the interpretation step of the Foresight Phase.
Here, the deeper structure and insights of the patterns and themes become visible and can be singled out
for a possible futures thinking initiative. These statements are categorized according to their purpose
circles. Also, the patterns can address all purpose circles at once and, therefore, the entire organization
or reference the respective purpose circles.
After leaving the interpretation step of the Foresight Phase, the LFC futures thinking methodologies and
tools become applied. This forms the prospection step of the Foresight Phase. For this, a combination
of several canvases in quasi­random sequence from past Futurice projects and workshops was used. Ad­
ditionally, canvases that fit the purpose of this study have been selected. The following selection of LFC
canvases is used to execute the futures thinking work based on the data generated in the design workshop
in this executive order.

5.2.1
EPM Data Analysis of the ’Offering and Learnings’ Purpose Circle
The ’Offering and Learnings’ purpose circle at Futurice comprises competences like the expertise fields
and theme clusters. Both have been described in section 2.3.1.1. They are part of Futurice’s business
strategy and are also called strategic drivers that intend to take Futurice’s business into the future.
The following results are identified through analysis of the tabulated data of the ’Offering and Learnings’
EPM (see Appendix E). In summary, the EPM shows that the actors connect to the EVP. This becomes
visible when looking at the value addition of the individual actors as they contribute to the EPMs seg­
ments. Here, the actors aim to understand and solve client problems by identifying new ideas that are
transformed into new or improved products, services, or processes. There is a shared concern about
improving clients’ lot to pursue new partnerships and other business relations. Additionally, there is a
willingness to adopt and improve the contribution towards a holistic understanding that encompasses a
customer journey from start to finish. In this regard, the focus will lie on helping clients not only with
one particular problem in hand but with various problems.
Some participants in this study found the variety of problems overwhelming and confusing. This is due
to Futurice’s complex set of business strategy drivers (see section 2.3.1). At times, they were unsure
about the required knowledge, skills, or experience to comply with the business strategy drivers. This
created silos within the ’Offering and Learnings’ purpose circle as the individual actors stood their ground
rather than engage with the others. This, in turn, led to a point where resources were not shared within
the individual expertise fields or theme clusters. Desirable, of course, would be the opposite: resources
should be shared within the expertise fields or theme clusters to better cooperate to reduce complexity
and ultimately save money and time.
On the contrary, resources are shared between the theme clusters and the expertise fields. This occurs
when current offers or initiatives for clients’ expertise fields are combined with themes. But as it turned
out during the workshops, it was rather the engagement with an actor’s external network that was fre­
quently listed under shared resources. This means that the person responsible for either an expertise field
or theme cluster reaches out to his or her personal external network to strengthen business connections,
access new information, accelerate the development of new ideas and, finally, make recommendations.
Accordingly, the data shows that the success of a proposition to a client is highly dependent on external
52
==PAGE:65==
relationships and less so on the collaboration of players inside the field of expertise or the theme clusters
but ­ in a rather traditional fashion ­ on the skills of the person putting it up. This state of affairs creates
a dependency in which the success of certain companies depends again, in a rather traditional fashion,
more on individuals and less on certain company strategy. Despite the combination of the expertise fields
and the themes, some actors do not perceive a unique value proposition (USP) of the offering. Also, as
stated with the EPM, some actors claimed that clients do not understand what Futurice is selling, and this
is for multiple reasons.
First, the ’Offering and Learnings’ purpose circle lacks interaction with the two other purpose circles.
Having little to no support from the Marketing and the New Business (NewBiz) competences within
Futurice which, in turn, are part of the ’Client Development’ purpose circle, was pointed out repeatedly
by three of the four actors of the ’Offering and Learnings’ purpose circle. Also, at Futurice, it was
regarded as being crucial to have the support of the Marketing and NewBiz competence because these
keep a close ear to market developments and can thus play a crucial role in shaping a proposal for a given
client. NewBiz can be especially instrumental in this regard. They identify and understand what expertise
fields and theme clusters are needed to better address client problems while being able to forecast project
work at Futurice, which, in turn, helps to better forecast business operations. Then there is also little to
no interaction with the ’Skill Stack’ section, which tends to create confusion about Futurice’s business
strategy and a degree of dissatisfaction with the process itself. In fact, not involving the ’Skill Stack’ in
the ’Offering and Learnings’ purpose circle tends to decouple strategy from the people executing Futurice
client work.
Second, ideally, within the ’Offering and Learnings’ purpose circle, there should be a greater reliance
on the theme clusters and expertise field competences that are meant to define Futurice’s USP. Actors
of the EPM described that they would try their best to combine theme clusters with expertise fields to
find solutions which could be developed into client offers. Yet, as mentioned above, it is primarily the
expertise fields that make for Futurice’s USP. In the latest proposals offered to clients, Futurice adapted
themes for certain expertise, a state of affairs that can actually be traced in the revenues: 95% of the 2022
revenues of Futurice came from expertise fields whereas merely 43% of the revenue recurs to the theme
clusters. Strong ties between Futurice themes and the expertise fields would support the development of
both.
Third, in 2022, both expertise fields were treated as individual fields, although they are no standalone
offerings. This was stated by two of the four actors of the ’Offering and Learnings’ purpose circle.
Combining both expertise fields creates a uniform offering (analysis of the processes (IDO) and the en­
vironment (ISE)). Purposefully combined, they might form a USP that could foster long­lasting client
relationships.
Fourth, there is no actual collaboration between theme clusters but strengthening the relationship between
them appears to be a desirable goal. This would require knowledge­sharing sessions to improve an un­
derstanding of how Futurice resonates with the market. Furthermore, increasing collaboration between
the Futurice offices in their different markets might be beneficial.

==PAGE:66==
This was stated by the theme cluster experts, who think that they could benefit from such a knowledge
exchange as this step could improve the company’s business prospects.
Fifth, as identified with the EPM, budgeting for expertise fields is at risk. This is because expertise fields
are still very much initiative driven and operate between or after business hours. There is no dedicated
time and space for developing these initiatives and, accordingly, no dedicated budget. Nevertheless,
Futurice uses the metric ’Impact’ to measure the expertise fields’ success. ”Why measure something that
the company is not invested in?” was mentioned by one of the ’Offering and Learnings’ purpose circle
actors. Allocating a budget for both expertise fields is necessary when listing them as drivers in Futurice’s
business strategy. Not budgeting the expertise fields reduces Futurice’s innovation capability as the fields
are instrumental in solving client problems and creating value for the customers. If the expertise fields
were to stumble, Futurice’s competitiveness could decline because of reduced revenues which in 2022
made up 95%. Finally, similar to what has been said above, it could make sense to develop long­term
goals for the expertise fields for a more precise and easy to grasp USP.
The revenue model of the ’Offering and Learnings’ competences differs from competence to competence.
This is because each theme cluster or expertise field uses different methods to generate revenue. While
some theme clusters or expertise fields generate revenue by finding new clients, others try to sell more to
existing clients. Sometimes a kick­off project offers sales promotions to boost the volume of sales in the
long run, and sometimes there is only one quick win in the pipeline. And while some endeavors help to
generate new products or services, others focus on improving existing ones. As mentioned above, many
Futurice’s business activities depend on its employees using their own networks to close a deal which, in
the end, pays into the revenue of Futurice. For a stronger focus and the development of its competences,
it might be worthwhile looking into creating a unified revenue model for the ’Learnings and Offerings’
purpose circle.
The risk within the ’Offering and Learnings’ purpose circle is that there are, as of 2022, only a few experts
with the necessary knowledge of the theme clusters at Futurice, and these do not have access to sufficient
resources to foster innovation. According to one participant, fostering innovation requires people with an
innovation mindset, i.e., people who are perceived as creative and who have command of time and money
to develop their thoughts. Such a setting seems to be a preliminary step to becoming an orchestrator in
a given field and would immediately support Futurice’s claim to develop innovation in its clusters, or as
stated by Kawohl and Krechting, area of life [36]. Within the EPM, only the theme cluster health and
the expertise field IDO have been marked to carry medium risk (yellow color code within the EPM).
Both have a significantly lower share of the overall revenue of Futurice even though they are central to
Futurice’s business strategy. Yet, as it turned out from the workshops, all competences at Futurice are
more or less siloed and will not cross­pollinate each other but instead continue their operation within their
boundaries.
The intangible activity data reflect that Futruice highly depends on its employee’s individual profes­
sional networks. The same is valid for knowledge building and its curation. For one thing, there are no
guidelines on training or specific learning. Yet, one actor in the ’Offering and Learnings’ purpose circle
mentioned that curating awareness internally through sharing sessions is something Futurice is incredibly
54
==PAGE:67==
good at. At the same time, initiatives are piling up, and people no longer know where to participate. The
result is that some employees at Futurice feel overwhelmed while others use the initiatives to learn as
much as possible. Pulling knowledge out of initiatives is vital for Futurice professionals to learn about
other topics and to connect with each other. These initiatives are very intangible for client work. The
same goes for workshops with other purpose circles, as some actors of the ’Offering and Learnings’ pur­
pose circle are conducting learning sessions and workshops with other purpose circles. But there is no
clear structure to this, and many of these feedback activities happen under the radar.
Within this purpose circle, the OKR ’Impact’ is measured. In 2022, the theme cluster health covers 9%
of the revenue generated while mobility covers 34%. The remaining 57% is generated elsewhere (see
figure 5.1). When looking at the ’Impact’ of the expertise field, it becomes evident that IDO is covering
23% of the generated revenue while ISE is responsible for 72%. The remaining 5% is covered by other
expertise and experiments. This quantitative data shows that the expertise fields take a significant part
of Futurice’s revenue. At the same time, the defined theme clusters cover only 43% of the total revenue,
even though they are key drivers in Futurice’s business strategy and define where the company wants to
make business.
Actors in the purpose circle wanted to closer align Futurice’s business strategy in Germany with current
developments in the market for inspiration. Although this seems to come naturally, it poses a potential
problem: clients would ­ and should ­ expect that Futurice declared USP to purposefully foster innovation
to strengthen their clients’ future position in the market, which would derive from a unique concept devel­
oped at Futurice to be applied where needed. This ought to be the central source of inspiration. At best,

Discussion
This chapter delves into the results’ meaning, importance, and relevance. It explains and evaluates the
findings, shows how they relate to the literature review and develops arguments toward the conclusion
in the next chapter.
All three literature streams presented in chapter 3 helped shape business strategy action items to make
Futurice’s business strategy more resilient. Accordingly, Talmar et al. EPM captured current turbulence
to perform a value creation analysis that streamlines existing processes or establishes a new business,
management, or client­related processes. Using the EPM data to conduct futures thinking work helped
to identify opportunities that different futures might bring. Emphasis was placed on using futures think­
ing methodologies and tools to anticipate future scenarios while leading through the previous identified
present turbulence. Moreover, laying out these opportunities in scenarios helped to anticipate future
scenarios and to draw strategy action items. By combining innovation ecosystem mapping with futures
thinking techniques, Futurice could be enabled to create a more resilient business strategy.
According to the EPM tool, Futurice’s current business strategy is limited, focusing on two specific theme
clusters: health and mobility. However, as shown in Figure 5.1, Kawohl’s ESM indicates that Futurice
is involved in six areas of life. This means that the current business strategy ignores four of these areas,
which account for 57% of Futurice’s revenue in 2022, as shown in the ’Offering and Learnings’ purpose
circle EPM (see Appendix E). Operating four of the six areas of life without a clear vision means that the
business is vulnerable to changes in the market and is not being developed further, leading to a decline in
revenue and reduced investment in new business. This is because two of the six areas of life do not have
any strategy in place that might be able to guide the areas through turbulences. Additionally, the fact that
most of Futurice’s employees are not directly involved in the new business and its strategy means they
are disconnected from the company’s overall direction.
Additionally, as stated in section 3.1.1, the purpose of a business strategy is to ensure that a company is
continuously aligned with and able to thrive in its environment to achieve its business goals. However,
after conducting this research, it is uncertain whether Futurice’s new business strategy is well­suited to
the company’s environment and operations for a few reasons.
First, a strategy is a way to overcome a challenge or obstacle, but if the challenge is not clearly defined,
it is difficult or impossible to evaluate the effectiveness of the strategy. Furthermore, without the ability
to assess the quality of the strategy, the organization cannot improve upon or reject a poor strategy. In
2022, the expertise fields at Futurice attempted to understand and define their clients’ challenges to better
solve their problems, but there is little investment in understanding the market and clients. As a result, the
expertise fields are not addressing a specific, defined challenge but rather trying to invent one to generate
business. Utilizing resources such as the strategy & culture competence of the ’Skill Stack’ purpose circle
could help address this investment gap, as it has the skills and tools to define such challenges and identify
market needs.
77
==PAGE:90==
Second, every business operation should have a clearly defined set of objectives that guide it in a specific
direction and prevent it from drifting off course. However, this is difficult with Futurice’s current organi­
zational model, in which the purpose circles are isolated (see Figure 2.1). Holacratic organizations, like
Futurice, have many roles organized into circles, all of which contribute to the organization’s purpose.
While this structure provides employees with a lot of flexibility and autonomy, without proper training,
support, and communication, it is likely to fail. As a result, each purpose circle operates independently,
and achieving corporate alignment is nearly impossible. There is no long­term strategy or objectives
within the purpose circles. Defining objectives for each purpose circle is necessary to get their support
for the business strategy. To address this, it is essential to create a unified vision tied to Futurice’s busi­
ness strategy to allow for strategic planning in which long­term goals can be defined. This would shift
the focus from needing to accomplish everything today to balancing long­term goals with day­to­day
business tasks while aligning them towards a larger purpose.
Third, it is clear that Futurice does not have the necessary resources to fully commit to its business strat­
egy. This is particularly true in terms of employees, equipment, and capital, as most people are not closely
connected to the business strategy. Only a few employees with specific expertise are able to work on the
business strategy. This is partly due to the isolation of the business strategy drivers within the ’Offering
and Learnings’ purpose circle and the current client work being done by the ’Skill Stack’ purpose circle
and partly due to a lack of communication, training, and involvement in the business strategy. As men­
tioned in section 2.3.1, the ’Client Development’ purpose circle is also included in Futurice’s business
strategy drivers. During this investigation, it became apparent that there is no connection or close collab­
oration between the strategy business drivers in the ’Client Development’ and ’Offering and Learnings’
purpose circles. As a result, employees do not have the knowledge, equipment, or experience to engage
with the business strategy drivers. According to Workday Peakon Employee Voice data at Futurice from
2022, this leads to stress and frustration among coworkers at Futurice. Workday Peakon collects and
analyzes employee feedback in real­time to improve the business. Ultimately, Futurice is not investing
equally in all business strategy drivers, leading to a lack of strategic development in the expertise fields
and theme clusters. To address this, it is necessary to invest equally in the expertise fields, field experts,
and consultants with the right skills to solve the defined challenges and enable development. One poten­
tial solution could be to merge the expertise fields into a single, generic product offering, which could
reduce investment costs even though it may increase complexity. This is because a uniform, generic
product offering could address multiple theme clusters at the same time, ideally all six areas of life that
Futurice is engaged in. This would allow for minimal hiring of theme cluster experts.
Fourth, an unexpected finding was that Futurice struggles to keep up with innovation on both corporate
and project scales. Innovation involves new ideas, and sharing knowledge and ideas among different
teams is crucial for fostering an innovative spirit. However, a siloed culture is a major barrier to business
innovation. After all, a company that cannot cooperate within itself cannot effectively share new ideas
overall. The EPM findings support this (see Appendix E and section 5.2). If each purpose circle operates
independently, they will focus on chasing their own OKRs and maintaining high performance to impress
the board rather than contributing potentially divisive, novel, or risky ideas. One of the biggest challenges
to innovation and creativity within an organization is isolation. An ecosystem ensures that every purpose
78
==PAGE:91==
circle and its competences have a role to play so that each one contributes to driving innovation forward
to develop new ideas and solutions that have the potential to make a significant impact on the world.
Finally, Futurice is impacted by many different factors, but all can be traced back to its business strategy
and leadership, which influence the entire organization. As identified in this research, a decision must
be made. Both expertise fields and theme clusters represent significant transformations for the company,
and each would require new skills and work practices. While some of these have already been acquired,
others still need to be developed. It is not feasible to simultaneously undertake two separate, deep trans­
formations of a company’s core. To better align Futurice’s business strategy, the company should aim for
a compromised strategy that incorporates multiple interests and concepts. Following the 22 action items
for Futurice’s business strategy encourages this approach. It is time to combine the new business with
the old one, supporting each other and striving to remain competitive, even though Futurice has already
begun to experience the consequences of misalignment in its business strategy.
6.1
Limitations of the Study
The study’s limitations refer to the characteristics of the methodology that impacted and influenced the
application or interpretation of the results. Since the focus of this study was to create a resilient business
strategy based on innovation ecosystem mapping and futures thinking methodologies and tools to antic­
ipate future scenarios, it can be concluded that using both approaches supported the development of a
resilient strategy. This resilient business strategy can be pursued by following the 22 action items that
support strategic planning and help Futurice recover from current challenges.
One limitation of this study is its demographic boundaries, as the research was only conducted for Fu­
turice. In hindsight, evaluating Futurice’s innovation ecosystem using other indicators first would have
revealed that there is no natural innovation ecosystem at Futurice. Previous research could have helped
narrow the scope of this study. As a result, the innovation ecosystem mapping would not have resulted in
a performance analysis, but rather a value creation analysis. This research only partially captured value
rather than enabling value creation. Therefore, it could be debated whether the application of an innova­
tion ecosystem was justified or whether mapping a business ecosystem would have been more appropriate
since it specifically addresses value capture [115].
Another limitation is the scope of the working title, which may have been too broad and difficult to fully
cover within this study. Focusing on a single aspect of Futurice’s business strategy could have helped to
create more specific action items with clear priorities, owners, descriptions, and dependencies.
Choosing the LFC’s futures thinking methodologies and tools is complex and challenging to reference
without causing confusion. The reader may not intuitively understand why the researcher chose certain
LFC canvases over others. Additionally, applying the approaches to anticipate future scenarios within an
FPF may add to the confusion, as there is no guidance on when to use the canvases. This creates another
limitation in terms of traceability. Both the LFC and FPF have different stages and steps with different
sequences, making it difficult to follow them one after the other without prior knowledge. Therefore,
expressing and presenting these clearly and understandably was another limitation of this research.
79
==PAGE:92==
Using more generic futures thinking methodologies and tools that do not require prior knowledge to
anticipate future scenarios could have reduced the complexity of this research.
No similar research was available at the time this research was conducted. It would have been helpful
to reference existing academic work that combines innovation ecosystems and futures thinking method­
ologies and tools that anticipate future scenarios to create business and innovation strategy action items.
This would have put this work into context and allowed for acknowledgment of the work of others while
developing a less complex and more broadly applicable approach.
The EPM allows mapping, designing, and analyzing innovation ecosystems based on its building blocks.
However, these building blocks capture real­time data with a time constraint and only capture data on
the current situation. This may lead to bias in the results of a study due to Futurice’s current situation
and distort the findings. However, since the EPM’s purpose is to map, design, and analyze an innovation
ecosystem, situational challenges must be considered to draw meaningful conclusions reflecting current
business operations.
Another limitation of this study is the lack of traceability in the implementation of the strategy action
items at Futurice. If changes are made, and some strategy action items are implemented, it is difficult to
understand where the changes have been made and how the rest of the processes have been affected. A
defined change management process could create more viability and traceability in implementing strategy
action items at Futurice.
6.2
Discussion of Methodology
In this thesis, the ESM was used to evaluate the current business operation of Futurice. The model was
used to assess the status quo of Futurice’s innovation capabilities and better understand the current and
desired positioning. Its ease of use and integration made it intuitive, as its two dimensions are easy to
understand. It helped develop and steer the action items, as it clearly and distinctly represents Futurice’s
desired state.
The other methodologies used in this research formed a comprehensive approach that related to Futurice’s
business strategy, investigated its current innovation ecosystem, and used the generated data to form sce­
narios that anticipated the future. The LFC served as the basis for applying approaches to anticipate future
scenarios. The LFC practices are widely accepted and, therefore, well understood, used, and known by
many of Futurice’s employees. It was also a good test of the LFC toolkit as this research provided a new
case and application environment. Using different LFC canvases, such as the alternative outcomes can­
vas, scenario outlines canvas, and light analysis canvas, influenced the strategy action items and allowed
for the identification of both opportunities and threats in the scenarios.
The EPM was adopted (see section 4.5.1) for innovation ecosystem mapping in this study, but it failed
to collect qualitative data for the ’Skill Stack’ and ’Client Development’ purpose circles. However, this
was not due to the EPM tool itself but rather because Futurice did not provide the necessary data. Despite
this, it was still possible to collect quantitative data for the ’Offering and Learnings’ purpose circle, which
was used extensively in the analysis to support the findings. The risk levels identified with the EPM tool
tended to be ignored because they were only color­coded, with no corresponding justification of the risks
80
==PAGE:93==
recorded. This is a problem specific to the tool and could be addressed by having a larger area for notes
in addition to the color coding, which could then provide more information about the risks of different
actors. Knowing how to respond to risks in an uncertain environment and understanding an organization’s
response to risks can provide valuable insights into a company’s innovation capabilities.
Overall, the EPM is a valuable tool for visualizing an innovation ecosystem but lacks structure for evalu­
ation and analysis. This leaves users to their own devices, which can have advantages and disadvantages.
The data captured may not be considered enough due to various eventualities. Additionally, the abstract
terms used in the EPM, such as building blocks, actors, dependencies, and other abbreviations, may be
confusing to those who do not have an advanced understanding of the tool. Analyzing the EPM based
on an organizational structure may also cause confusion, as the data analysis may not appear clear and
comprehensive.
A model that could transform inputs into outputs and be adaptable to various situations to transform fore­
sight data into strategy action items was desired. Accordingly, this study used Voros et al. FPF, which has
been successful in previous studies due to its adaptability and flexibility. This framework helped stream­
line the process of using data generated by the innovation ecosystem, mapping EPM, and processing it
through the different phases and stages of the framework. The FPF was also used to guide and structure
the research approach (see section 4.1). One limitation of the FPF is its Input Phase. According to the
framework, foresight­related data is used to go through each phase. However, this study used innovation
ecosystem­related data focused on Futurice’s innovation capability. Using foresight­related data in the
Input Phase would have allowed this research to generate long­term indications about the future in the
Output Phase. This study data only provides a snapshot of the current state of affairs and does not offer
any insights into what the future might hold. This means that it is missing a temporal horizon.
The LFC canvases applied in this research are from module two (Alternative Outcomes Canvas and
Scenario Outline Canvas) and module four (Light Analysis Canvas) of the double diamond (see figure
3.7). The data for the LFC process was derived from the identified patterns and themes of the EPMs, so
this study omitted the first module of the double diamond. This means that this research may have missed
exploring opportunities and areas that initially seemed irrelevant. Additionally, existing assumptions
about core problems and solution ideas were not eliminated and directly processed into the second module
of the double diamond, which could have biased the research from the start. Omitting the third module of
the double diamond also carries the risk of narrowing down the solution too quickly, as the flow of data
is minimized. This can lead to the temptation of jumping straight to a solution instead of fully exploring
the data before evaluating and testing potential solutions for strategy roadmaps. Another limitation of
the LFC is that using the canvases can be overwhelming, especially when working with a large amount
of data. This can make it difficult to condense the information, leading to a high output of data and little
influence on how to transfer and process it. For example, using the three canvases in this study resulted
in 22 strategic action items. It is not possible to condense the data from the start to obtain clear outputs,
and the success of the LFC canvases depends heavily on the data used as input.
The methodology presented in this research can be applied to other organizations, combining methodolo­
gies and tools to create a holistic process that provides a constant stream of information for processing
81
==PAGE:94==
innovation ecosystem data with future­thinking methodologies and tools that can be related to business
strategy. However, it should be noted that this stream uses the LFC canvases to anticipate future sce­
narios. Suppose another company does not want to use, is unfamiliar with, or finds the LFC canvases
confusing. In that case, they may need to find alternative future­thinking methodologies and tools that
can be incorporated into Voros et al. FPF. In the latter case, the methodology used in this research may
not be immediately applicable to others.
6.3
Discussion of Results
The results of this study are transparent, but they are not replicable because they are based on qualitative
data. This means that verification of the results can vary across researchers, and it is difficult to investigate
causality or establish statistical representativeness and replicability. Additionally, the data generated
depends on the workshop participant’s and the facilitators’ note­taking. It is important to recognize that
the results generated with the EPM and its EVP do not demonstrate where value is created but rather
uncover key stakeholders and their activities. As a result, the strategic action items focus on breaking
down silos to improve cross­organizational activities, which in turn improve value creation and Futurice’s
innovation capability. The results do not focus on the innovation activities at Futurice directly but rather
on how to create a foundation for them.
An alternative explanation for the results might be that Futurice’s holacratic organization has some lim­
itations and challenges. For example, a holacratic organization may not be suitable for a company that
relies on the work of others and waits for their results. Additionally, as Futurice has grown in size, it may
be too big to be self­organized and managed effectively. The lack of clear leadership can have a negative
impact on the company’s revenue and profit goals. Without leadership, employees may feel uncertain,
and some may not be able to reach a consensus on decisions, leading to disorganization and ineffective­
ness. This can also result in a lack of future vision, as was noted in the all­purpose circles investigated
with the EPM. Also, holacracy does not provide formal ownership, so employees may be unwilling to
take responsibility for failures or lack of results. Finally, maintaining a holacratic organization can be
challenging, especially in terms of hiring self­managers and independent thinkers who fit the holacratic
mindset. It may be possible that Futurice has a few employees who do not fit this profile.
It is important to clarify how to incorporate the action items into Futurice’s strategic planning, as the
results of this study do not provide guidance on how and when to do so. The action items are also presented
in a vague sequence that does not specify which ones should be prioritized or done simultaneously to
achieve certain goals. Therefore, a detailed project plan is needed to specify when and how to start each
action item and bring it to fruition. The project plan should also consider the level of change, cost, and
time involved for each action item, as these can vary significantly.
Future research could focus on three areas: i) creating a change management process to implement the
action items at Futurice and understanding the real­world implications and impacts of doing so, ii) ana­
lyzing the effect of the action items on Futurice’s innovation ecosystem, and iii) examining what steering
Germany can do to prevent similar turbulences in the future. Such future research could provide more
insight into the effectiveness of the methodology and the actual application of the identified action items.
It would then assess Futurice’s situation after the implementation and potentially provide guidance on
82
==PAGE:95==
what to do next. Specifically, looking at steering could provide valuable insight into maintaining Fu­
turice’s innovation ecosystem. There may also be indications that it would make sense to use this study’s
methodology after some time again to perform a value­creation analysis and further enhance Futurice’s
innovation ecosystem.
According to the working title of this study, applying innovation ecosystem mapping and futures thinking
methodologies and tools is not sufficient to foster a resilient business strategy at Futurice. Some of the
action items generated through the used methodology (see section 4) cannot be quickly adapted without
causing disruptions while maintaining continuous business operations and safeguarding people, assets,
and Futurice’s overall brand equity.
Finally, the scope of this research was overwhelming in many ways due to Futurice’s current organiza­
tional situation. If the scope had been better defined, it would have been possible to delve deeper into a
specific topic rather than considering many topics at once. This is especially true for the term ”business
strategy” in the working title of this study. The research would have provided a clearer guideline if only
certain aspects of the business strategy had been considered.
6.4
Contribution of Thesis
The contributions of this study lie in synthesizing and integrating various literature streams, including
their frameworks, methodologies, and tools, to create a continuous flow that brings them together. These
contributions can be classified into several dimensions. First, this study increases knowledge by conduct­
ing extensive and innovative research on Futurice’s innovation ecosystem, expanding the EPM tool by
adopting the building blocks of the EPM to allow for tangible and intangible activity mapping and quanti­
tative data collection. Second, this study expands the LFC toolkit of Futurice by integrating it into Voros
et al. FPF, creating a holistic and guided process that provides a structure for data processing and the
application of the LFC. One of the main contributions is that the FPF helps translate the canvas data into
business strategy, enabling the data to be used to illuminate unexpected implications of the present and
empower individuals and organizations to actively design desirable future scenarios. The novelty of this
contribution is that it provides a process that enables innovation ecosystem mapping and then processes
this data with future­thinking methodologies and tools to anticipate future scenarios. Third, this study
contributes to the understanding of a relatively new tool in business by conducting an open­ended study
that reports and analyzes Futurice’s innovation ecosystem, expanding Futurice’s knowledge beyond what
was already known.

7
Conclusion
This thesis provides an overview of twenty­two action items that form the basis for creating a resilient
business strategy at Futurice. The overall objective of this thesis was to combine innovation ecosystems
with futures thinking while mapping Futurice’s current innovation ecosystem practices to eventually iden­
tify its innovation performance. For this purpose, design workshops were conducted to map the innova­
tion ecosystem at Futurice. The shortcomings of these innovation ecosystem mappings are highlighted
in section 5.2. The business strategy action items derived from the innovation ecosystem mapping and
futures thinking methodologies and tools to anticipate future scenarios can be taken from table 5.3.
To conclude, it is possible to combine innovation ecosystems with futures thinking methodologies and
tools to anticipate future scenarios. Combining both approaches and directly applying them at Futurice
resulted in various findings. In conclusion, Futurice does not have a functioning innovation ecosystem.
Understanding relationships and dependencies between various actors and purpose circles that contribute
to creating innovation is challenging. Findings are that there are few to no dependencies between actors of
the same purpose circles. In addition, a shared vision and individual goals of the individual purpose circles
are not defined. Yet, a narrow understanding of entire value streams and supply chains at Futurice were
impossible to map. Consequently, the innovation ecosystem mapping resulted in a performing analysis
rather than a value creation analysis. The chain of methodologies and tools captured value and did not
reveal how the respective value was created. Within this study, it was impossible to outline a resilient
business strategy based on Futurice’s innovation ecosystem and futures thinking methodologies and tools
to anticipate future scenarios. This is because the business strategy action items focus on stabilizing and
improving current business operation instead of making the business strategy resilient, which makes it
impossible to absorb change and disturbance while maintaining the same relationships between purpose
circles and other variables. Actually, Futurice’s business strategy creates silos within and outside the
purpose circles on the one hand. On the other, however, the business strategy action items improve
Futurice’s innovation capability while enabling a renewed culture of innovation with a shared vision and
a continuously developing skill set that is committed and acknowledged by leadership.
If Futurice wants to embrace an innovation ecosystem, it will need to improve its business strategy.
Currently, Futurice’s business strategy is not consistent in setting clear goals and, defining key variables,
determining roles, responsibilities, and relationships along all purpose circles; it does not delegate work
nor executes a plan that is monitored equally to receive continuous and gradient support, and finally, it
does not take any corrective actions but tries to combine business strategy drivers randomly. Futurice
could follow a blue ocean strategy to differentiate its products and increase competitiveness in a largely
uncontested market space. This approach would involve low cost efforts to make competition irrelevant
and position the company as an orchestrator in targeted areas, aligning with its business strategy. The
innovation ecosystem mapping confirmed these findings. The key findings of the innovation ecosystem
mapping and futures thinking methodologies and tools to anticipate future scenarios are discussed in the
following passages.

Futurice currently claims to engage in six areas of life but ignores four of them. A lack of coherence in
Futurice’s business strategy while simultaneously conducting business with clients has led to a lack of
objectives, so that the purpose circles do not have a unified vision. Disorientation in one purpose circle
leads to disorientation in the others, as all of them are linked. Circumventing this issue, purpose circles
have become siloed rather than being connected to each other. This has led to a lack of dependencies
and workflows required to improve business operations. While resources are not adequately allocated
or available, the organizational structure turns hazy, and employees work in a rather free­floating atmo­
sphere. Thus the clarification of job duties and development becomes fuzzy. Additionally, there are
problems in creating a coherent communication flow between purpose circles; employees start strug­
gling to identify their purpose, and creating a fluid plan altogether is impossible due to Futurice’s current
business strategy or, rather, the lack of one.
Technologically­driven companies like Futurice need to be ever more innovative and pioneering than
before to lead, grow, compete and endure. Innovation ecosystems can support this by creating an en­
vironment where innovation is encouraged through culture rather than being institutionalized through a
process. Building a functioning innovation ecosystem at Futurice that is aligned with its business ob­
jectives and overall organizational purpose could create and then enable an environment where ideas
flourish throughout the organization, in every purpose circle, and at every level, and thus could provide
opportunities for change. Cross­organizational networks could help Futurice spark communication across
the siloed purpose circles and encourage and inspire new ideas with the right cultural mindsets in place.
Such mindsets would enhance diversity at Futurice, leading to organizational learning and sharing across
purpose circles. It would promote holistic thinking in which business strategy is anchored in the day­to­
day operation of Futurice employees. An anchored business strategy allows for continuous measurement
that, in turn, adopts the business strategy and guides it to better future performance. As Peter Drucker
famously said, ”the best way to predict the future is to create it” [116].
If Futurice were to improve its innovation ecosystem, it would directly benefit its projects. As projects op­
erate within the ecosystem of Futurice and other organizations, the company engages in broader markets
made up of competing organizations, which in their own right are also a kind of ecosystem. To dominate
a market with an innovation ecosystem, a company needs a product or service USP that is recognized for
something specific and not as a variety of somethings. Aligning and coordinating project teams accord­
ing to a company’s business strategy and innovation ecosystem reduces project failures and helps control
their own destiny. Accordingly, projects would constantly evolve with the ecosystem and support the
development of the company in due course. Therefore, applying the strategic action items suggested in
this research on a project level is similarly important to boost the development of an ecosystem overall.
The action items for creating a resilient business strategy at Futurice include: enhancing collaboration
among all purpose circle competences; breaking down silos through regular competence­sharing meet­
ings; creating dependencies in all purpose circles to improve communication, productivity, and employee
engagement; involving employees in shaping the business strategy to create a shared vision and mission;
providing mandatory training to shift mindsets and behaviors; making personalized training and cross­
purpose circle work mandatory; creating long­term goals and plans for each business strategy driver and
a unified vision for all purpose circles; aligning marketing with the business strategy; creating a recurring
85
==PAGE:98==
revenue model and streamlined proposal process; supporting equal growth and development in all exper­
tise fields and theme clusters; involving the strategy & culture competence in the ’Client Development’
purpose circle; providing career development plans to improve skill development and alignment with
Futurice’s business strategy; establish shared accountabilities that can be measured with OKRs or KPIs;
merge expertise fields to establish a shared message and USP; implement agile ways of working and an
innovation process; support external learning opportunities and sharing of work and knowledge; create
a culture of trust and open communication; and use the company’s ecosystem for client acquisition and
provide benefits for closing new deals.
Summing up, there is a lot of work to be done at Futurice before a functioning innovation ecosystem
can foster growth and capture the value created solely through the interaction of an innovation ecosys­
tem’s actors, activities, and artifacts. This will resolve the current lack of innovation by promoting an
innovation culture, overcoming the fear of change, creating ownership of end­to­end processes, and en­
ables adequate benchmarking. To get there, Futurice could follow the twenty­two strategy action items
to improve current business operations and generally enable innovation ecosystem building at Futurice.
Then, the company starts to build the minimum requirements for building an ecosystem that allows for
value creation. Also, the company should focus on the individual purpose areas first while constantly
improving all the necessities mentioned above to make them run. After following the strategy action
items and aligning Futurice’s business strategy, Futurice is ready to embrace an innovation ecosystem
that transforms ideas into reality.
Even though there might be some immediate disadvantages, Futurice could develop new products and
services and become more agile by tearing down silos and optimizing internal processes. The purpose
circle ’Operation’ might make for helpful support in building and running the company. Using their
expertise to work ”in” and ”on” the company will be highly relevant when following the twenty­two
strategy action items. It could boost Futurice’s innovation capability and foster a culture of awareness.
To start from a small scale and to better grasp innovation for the business strategy drivers, Futurice could
temporarily engage with an innovation lab. Afterward, a transition into an innovation ecosystem seems
possible. Such a transition would further remove the internal and external innovation boundaries of
Futurice.
The results of this thesis are being used at Futurice as a guide for the introduction of an innovation
ecosystem but also for operational and strategic alignment. The results draw the company’s attention to
the innovation ecosystem practices that emerge from its business strategy and drivers. An outlook for the
company would be to procure the knowledge necessary to carry out such an implementation. Therefore,
the purpose circles might want to conduct another more in­depth analysis of the situation than was possible
for this study and query the explicit knowledge of the employees. This could create a clear plan of
action. In conclusion, it appears that Futurice is experiencing many of the same challenges that other
companies of similar focus and size are facing, including limited resources, rapid growth, competition,
market changes, and management difficulties. To overcome these challenges, it is important for Futurice
to be proactive and strategic in their planning, and to be prepared to adapt their business strategy as needed
in order to stay on track and achieve their goals.

Appendix A ­ The Purpose Circle Competences
Competence Name
Description
Go­to­Market
Is in dialogue with the market and reacts to market needs and trends.
Communicate value proposition and offering to potential clients and ac­
quire new business.
Delivery excellence
A support function reflects, evaluates, defines, or redefines the way of
working and practices in close exchange with the teams. Developing
key accounts by managing the client relationship, finding and winning
new and exciting projects within the client.
Competences
Represent the three competences available at Futurice (Design, Tech,
Strategy & Culture)
Expertise Fields
Supporting sales and business development efforts with expert partici­
pation and sales material, supporting the definition of skill development
and talent acquisition needs, working on theme­relevant projects, and
participating in sales conversations
Theme Cluster
Build new strategic business initiatives (projects/accounts) in the theme
industry. Enlarge internal Futurice expertise in the industry domain.
Strengthen Futurice’s strategic positioning in the market vis­à­vis indus­
try leaders.
Initiatives
Anything that is not part of a billable project but still integral to Futurice
competences.
Description of Futurice’s purpose circle competences

EPM Data Analysis of the ’Offering and Learnings’ Purpose Circle
Criteria
Data Statements of the EPM
CRIT1
• Solving and understanding client problems
• Adopting and improving offerings according to feedback
• Funnel creation for the client (helping out from start to finish)
• Data on industry and client problems
• New partnerships and relations
• Better market, processes, and customer understanding
CRIT2
• Intangible domain knowledge
• People with skills needed to do the job and cases
• External relationship to experts and organizations
• No resources shared within theme clusters
• No resources shared within expertise fields
• Only resource sharing between theme clusters and expertise fields ac­
cording to offering and project
CRIT3
• Organizational development
• Theme clusters should include expertise fields ­ and shape them accord­
ing to the theme (as of now, flow is wrong: goes from expertise field and
is adapted for the theme)
• Strengthen collaboration between theme clusters (e.g., knowledge shar­
ing)
• There is a dependence between the expertise fields
• ISE & IDO are no standalone offerings as they are both required to create
a uniform offering (analysis of the processes (IDO) and the environment
(ISE))
• Activities in both theme clusters are trying to create a brand from them­
selves separately
• Expertise fields are much more involved in driving Futurice revenue than
the theme clusters (95% expertise field, 45% theme clusters)
==PAGE:123==
CRIT4
• Revenue model differs across theme clusters and expertise fields ­ as
they generate money in different ways. Some theme clusters or exper­
tise fields are generating revenue by finding new clients while other sell
more to existing clients. Sometimes, a kick­off project is offering sales
promotions to boost the volume of sales. Additionally, some are gener­
ating new products or service lines while other improve existing ones.
Within the ’Learning and Offering’ purpose circle is no generic revenue
model.
CRIT5
• Offering USP is not perceived and clear to actors of the ’Offering and
Learnings’ circle
• Arbitrary combination of expertise fields and theme clusters to form
client offers
• Not the right people with the right mindset and skills to foster innovation
• View experts with respective theme cluster knowledge that do not pos­
sess enough resources to foster innovation within the theme cluster
• Lack of theme cluster health and expertise field IDO in revenue share
• Budgeting for expertise fields is at risk even though the impact is mea­
sured. Why measure something that is not invested in?
• Risk level shows that theme clusters are siloed from expertise fields
• Across purpose circles: Theme cluster collaboration with marketing is
lacking
• Knowledge transfer from Finland to Germany is lacking (learning from
Finland can already be very beneficial)
• Across purpose circles: ’Skill Stack’ purpose circle is not involved in
themes and expertise fields
• Across purpose circles: ’Client Development’ purpose circle is not in­
volved in themes and expertise fields
==PAGE:124==
CRIT6
• Making connections to market
• Establishing Futurice as a brand in the themes
• Knowledge building and curating
• BizDev connection to external players
• Creating awareness internally through sharing sessions
• Regular workshops with ’Skill Stack’ purpose circle
• Establishing Futurice as a brand ­ recognized in both expertise fields and
theme clusters
• Initiative work at Futurice (as an enabler to connect across competence
+ learning opportunity)
CRIT7
• For all theme clusters and expertise fields, ’Impact’ is measured (Mobil­
ity: 34%, Health: 9%, other theme clusters: 57%, IDO: 23% and ISE:
72% and other expertise fields: 5%)
CRIT8
• The purpose circle ’Offering and Learnings’ should follow the following
structure to align with strategy: Market ­> Theme Cluster ­> Expertise
Field ­> Competence
• Strategy is aligned with the ’Offering and Learnings’ purpose circle as
all parts of the circles are involved in the strategy
• No set long­term goals for either expertise fields or theme clusters that
make them develop ­ and if goals set, they are revenue based
• Purpose circle building and development is not solely clear from Fu­
turice’s business strategy

EPM Data Analysis of the ’Skill Stack’ Purpose Circle
Criteria
Data Statements of the EPM
CRIT1
• Empowerment to make data­based decisions
• Value addition to customers
• New partnerships and relations
• Better market, processes, and customer understanding
CRIT2
• Design and process understanding
• The Futurice way of working
• Client engagement and relationship skills
• Historical data from projects and deliverables
CRIT3
• Insights generation and synthesis of knowledge
• Design & Business: making informed decisions based on a common idea
• Collaboration of design and tech
• Dependence: Strategy & culture as they are the driving force to solve
problems and find out market needs. Design aims to do the research on
the identified needs while tech is implementing the solutions (a close
collaboration of all skill stacks required)
• There should be a dependence for all three skill stacks as Futurice is
operating in a complex environment were skills of the others need to be
combined to succeed
CRIT4
• Revenue model of the ’Skill Stack’ is a transaction­based model. Here,
revenue is generated by directly selling an product or service to a cos­
tumer.
Transactional pricing models charge customers based on the
amount of work done in a specified time.
==PAGE:126==
CRIT5
• Time and innovation knowledge available
• Market is very competitive and tech people stand at risk of leaving the
company due to the salary level
• Collaboration is not clear between the three actors ­ there is a high de­
pendency between design and tech
• Lack of strategy & culture alignment to strategy (skill, people, lack in
research capability)
• Knowledge is siloed, and when somebody leaves the company, the
knowledge is gone
• As of now, many people of the respective competences do not align with
strategy nor have any strategy understanding ­ accordingly steering the
company with a lot of employees that do not know or understand the
strategy is tough
• There is no measurement of the impact of competences and people, em­
ployees are going and doing however they like (also makes career­level
evaluation tough)
• Across purpose circle: New­Biz / Account / Marketing ­ no alignment to
skill stack (what they are selling, and how they collaborate on the project)
• Across purpose circle: no relation to ’Offering and Learnings’ purpose
circle
CRIT6
• Getting people on the same page and empowering them to make deci­
sions
• Collaboration of tech and design (feedback on feasibility)
• Knowledge sharing of experiences
CRIT7
• ’Impact’ is not measured for the purpose circle ’Skill Stack’. Accord­
ingly, no understanding of involvement in revenue and incentive to im­
prove
==PAGE:127==
CRIT8
• No relation to business strategy. As of now, skill stack is responsible
for business execution even though strategy & culture has the capability
to do research on market needs and fill the theme clusters and expertise
fields with the knowledge that could be passed down to design and tech
to improve innovation capability overall
• Possibility to create connection through regular and mandatory initia­
tives and learning sessions

EPM Data Analysis of the ’Client Development’ Purpose Circle
Criteria
Data Statements of the EPM
CRIT1
• Capture of new market segments
• Project acquisition in new domains
• Testing innovation with clients
• New partnerships and relations
• Better market, processes, and customer understanding
CRIT2
• People with different backgrounds and networks
• Available marketing content: talks, blog posts, press articles, papers,
thought leader talk about a specific topic (no alignment to Futurice’s
business strategy 2022)
• Innovation hypothesis testing
• Innovation topics coming from outside (clients ­ trends)
• Experience in client acquisition
• Knowledge from the market
CRIT3
• Offering development
• Sharing of client feedback and discussion
CRIT4
• Revenue model differs across the ’Client Development’ purpose circle as
all three competences generate revenue the same way. In an ideal world,
all three actors would generate revenue differently while complementing
their revenue models. Marketing should rather focus on an advertising
revenue model or web sales revenue model, newbiz should focus on a
transaction revenue model and accounts on a retail revenue model.
==PAGE:129==
CRIT5
• No boundary spanning activity from marketing to the other two actors
(overall very little exchange of activities in this purpose circle)
• Not having the right people that do not understand the market
• A mixture of farmers and hunters is required (Futurice has more farmers
and fewer hunters)
• No benefit when closing new deals (no intrinsic motivation to gain new
clients)
• All three actors are siloed and do not collaborate. Marketing ­> NewBiz
/ Marketing ­> Account / NewBiz ­> Account
• Across purpose circles: No expertise field and marketing collaboration
• Across purpose circles: No involvement of ’Skill Stack’ purpose circle
(blind selling without knowing if expertise is available)
CRIT6
• Sharing and talking about a topic the FutuWay
• Personal interest of a person
• Discussions about personal experiences
CRIT7
• ’Impact’ is not measured for the purpose circle ’Client Development’.
Accordingly, no understanding of involvement in revenue and incentive
to improve. Also, the actor’s involvement in the selling activity is not
clear
CRIT8
• Is partly engaged with Futurice Germany business strategy
• Marketing is perceived as an enabler for business goals ­ business strat­
egy defines their activities, and all others depend on them.
• If marketing is not aligned, newbiz cannot succeed in getting new clients
within the theme clusters, and account management is also struggling to
do strategy­based after­sales
