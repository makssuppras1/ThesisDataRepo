Abstract
As wind energy’s share in electricity markets grows, price volatility increasingly affects
the financial viability of wind power projects. Traditional optimization strategies focus
on maximizing Annual Energy Production (AEP) and minimizing the Levelized Cost of
Energy (LCOE), but these metrics do not account for market price fluctuations. This
thesis explores a market-driven wind turbine selection approach using alternative met-
rics: Annual Energy Value (AEV), and Cost of Valued Energy (COVE), which assess
financial performance based on when energy is produced rather than total output.
This study evaluates how turbines with varying specific powers (SP)—the ratio of rated
power to rotor-swept area—perform in terms of COVE and Income in the Danish elec-
tricity market. Results show that lower-SP turbines, which generate more power at
lower wind speeds, achieve better financial outcomes despite their higher costs. These
findings highlight the importance of integrating electricity market dynamics into wind tur-
bine design to enhance financial resilience and cost efficiency.

Introduction
The transition to renewable energy is reshaping electricity markets worldwide, with wind
energy playing an increasingly significant role. In Denmark, where wind power already
supplies more than half of the country’s electricity [1], market dynamics are shifting
as the sector has moved away from government-backed subsidies and now relies on
competitive, market-based mechanisms [2]. While wind energy has traditionally been
optimized to maximize Annual Energy Production (AEP) and minimize the Levelized
Cost of Energy (LCOE), these conventional metrics do not fully capture the financial
realities of a volatile electricity market [3].
As wind penetration increases, the self-cannibalization effect, where high wind energy
production leads to lower electricity prices, has become more pronounced [4]. This cre-
ates economic challenges for wind farms, as revenue becomes increasingly dependent
on when, rather than only how much, energy is produced.
This project evaluates a new approach to wind turbine selection by integrating elec-
tricity market dynamics into the optimization process. Specifically, turbine designs are
assessed using alternative metrics proposed by Bechmann et al. (2024) [5]: Annual
Energy Value (AEV), Value Factor (VF), and Cost of Valued Energy (COVE). AEV rep-
resents the total energy produced by a turbine, weighted by the relative value of the
electricity based on market prices. VF represents the ratio between AEV and AEP,
measuring how well a turbine’s energy production aligns with high-value electricity price
periods. COVE quantifies cost efficiency by measuring the cost per unit of valued en-
ergy, defined as the ratio of cost to AEV, rather than treating all energy production
equally. These metrics offer a more comprehensive understanding of a turbine’s finan-
cial performance and could lead to a more effective optimization strategy.
The thesis aims to explore how the optimum turbine design, evaluated in terms
of highest Income and lowest Cost of Valued Energy, is affected by incorporating
Danish electricity market conditions. Turbine design is defined in terms of Specific
Power (SP), the ratio of a turbine’s rated power to its rotor-swept area. The impact
of Danish market volatility is analyzed for both offshore and onshore wind conditions
and turbine models, with an assessment of current trends and potential future devel-
opments. A focus is placed on determining whether low-specific-power turbines, which
generate more energy at lower wind speeds, align better with market pricing trends
than high-specific-power turbines designed for maximum output in high winds.
The thesis is structured as follows: The Background outlines the historical role of Danish
financial incentives in shaping wind turbine design, explains the effect of renewables
on the electricity market, and discusses the shift toward market-driven performance
metrics. The Methodology outlines a model framework consisting of three interlinked
models—the Annual Energy Value Model, Cost Model, and Revenue Model—which
enable the calculation of Income and COVE as functions of SP. This section details
the mathematical foundation and the implementation of each model. The Results sec-
tion presents and analyses findings on optimal turbine designs in terms of minimal
COVE and maximum Income under varying conditions and explores the sensitivity of

directions for future analysis.

2
Background
This chapter explores key trends and developments in the Danish wind energy sec-
tor. It examines how policy changes have amplified price volatility and increased wind
developers’ reliance on market value dynamics, driving the need for new evaluation
metrics such as COVE.
First, the chapter explores the historical relationship between Danish renewable energy
financing policies and wind turbine design, highlighting how financial incentives have
driven the industry toward larger turbines with higher energy outputs. Next, it discusses
the impact of increased renewable energy production on electricity prices, explaining
how the expansion of wind power contributes to price volatility and declining market
revenues for wind energy producers. Lastly, the chapter addresses the emergence of
new performance metrics that aim to align wind turbine design with electricity market
fluctuations. This shift is driven by the sector’s transition away from government-backed
financial support and toward competitive, market-based mechanisms, requiring new
strategies to enhance financial resilience in a volatile pricing environment.
2.1
Historical Trends in Danish Wind Turbine Design
as a Result of Capitalisation Incentives
Over the past few decades, wind energy in Denmark has evolved from a niche energy
source to a central component of the country’s electricity system. Financial mecha-
nisms have played a crucial role in shaping both wind turbine technology and project
development strategies.
In the early years, the Danish wind sector was driven by
government-backed subsidies and fixed Feed-in Tariffs (FITs), which provided stable
revenue streams and encouraged investment [6]. This policy environment supported
small-scale, locally owned cooperatives, allowing communities to invest in wind power.
Consequently, early turbine designs were smaller in scale, well-suited for local owner-
ship and financing [2].
The liberalization of the electricity market in the late 1990s and early 2000s, driven by
EU directives, introduced competitive pricing mechanisms that fundamentally reshaped
the sector [7]. The transition from fixed subsidies to a market-plus-premium model
shifted financial incentives, attracting larger commercial developers and enabling the
deployment of larger turbines with increased rotor diameters [2]. During this period,
turbine design was largely driven by the need to maximize AEP to improve efficiency.
As wind power became a key element of Denmark’s energy transition strategy, there
was also a growing emphasis on offshore wind development, where larger projects
could take advantage of economies of scale.
In 2018, Denmark implemented an auction model, that aligned with EU competition
regulations. Under this system, wind developers bid for financial support based on the
lowest price per unit of energy produced [8]. This approach reinforced the industry’s
focus on cost reductions and the development of multi-megawatt turbines designed for
a broader range of wind conditions. As financial models became increasingly market-
driven, LCOE along with AEP became a key metric for assessing project viability, further

is the ratio of the total levelized cost to the total energy production over the turbine’s

2.2
Decarbonization and its Effect on Wind Energy in
the Electricity Market
The global push for decarbonization, reinforced by agreements such as the Paris Agree-
ment, has accelerated the transition toward renewable energy. Many countries, includ-
ing Denmark, have set ambitious net-zero emissions targets, with Denmark aiming for
a fossil fuel-free electricity sector by 2030 [9]. As of 2023, wind energy accounts for
over 50% of Denmark’s electricity generation, positioning the country as a global leader
in wind power integration.
The acceleration of renewables has not only increased the share of wind energy in the
grid but has also amplified challenges related to its integration. The fluctuating nature
of wind energy, caused by inconsistent wind conditions, leads to imbalances between
electricity generation and grid demand. [10]. While this variability was historically less
problematic due to government support, the transition toward market-based mecha-
nisms for energy financing is exposing new financial vulnerabilities for wind energy
developers [11].
Figure 2.1 illustrates the effect of increasing renewable capacity on the electricity mar-
ket. In competitive electricity markets, energy producers submit bids for the next day’s
electricity supply. The merit order effect ranks energy sources by their marginal produc-
tion costs, the cost of producing an additional unit of energy. The market operator then
determines the market clearing spot price, which is the price at which supply equals
demand. Under these conditions, renewable sources displace more expensive fossil
fuel generation due to their near-zero fuel costs. However, this effect leads to self-
cannibalization, a situation where an oversupply of wind-generated electricity drives
down market prices, reducing financial returns for wind energy producers [4]. The ex-
pansion of renewables, while crucial for decarbonization, has therefore introduced new
financial risks, as wind energy revenue becomes increasingly dependent on market
fluctuations rather than reliance on government financing schemes.

This price volatility has direct implications for wind turbine design. Historically, turbine
technology was optimized to maximize AEP, favoring high SP turbines that produce
peak output during strong winds. However, these high wind periods often coincide
with lower electricity prices due to oversupply, reducing financial returns. As a result,
there is a growing interest to move towards low SP turbine designs, which prioritize
greater energy capture at lower wind speeds when market prices tend to be higher [4].
A paper by Kannan and Jeyaraj (2022) [13] concludes that low SP wind turbines can
significantly reduce the LCOE especially for large-scale wind energy production. This
design approach could not only improve revenue stability for developers but also align
wind energy production more effectively with real-time electricity demand.
2.3
Moving Beyond Traditional Performance Metrics
The dependence on market dynamics and volatile prices not only affects discourse on
optimal turbine design but also how this optimization is evaluated. A growing body of
research advocates for a shift away from traditional performance metrics such as AEP
and LCOE. As highlighted earlier LCOE has been a standard benchmark for evalu-
ating the cost-effectiveness of renewable energy projects. However, this metric does
not account for the temporal value of energy production, particularly in market-based
electricity systems [3].
To address these limitations, Bechmann et al. (2024) [5] propose the use of AEV and
VF to evaluate turbine designs more comprehensively. AEV measures the total energy
produced, weighted by the relative value of electricity based on hourly market prices.
The VF is defined as the ratio between AEV and AEP, providing a comparative metric
of energy output. A higher VF indicates that a turbine produces energy during more
profitable hours, reflecting better alignment with market conditions.
The Cost of Valued Energy has been proposed as an alternative to the commonly used
LCOE metric [3]. COVE reflects the monetized energy value, like AEV, and includes
the cost of energy production, like LCOE, thereby providing a more realistic measure
of profitability.
These metrics represent a paradigm shift in wind turbine optimization, moving beyond
energy output maximization to focus on value creation. By integrating market value
dynamics into the design process, developers can potentially identify turbine types that
deliver higher economic value under evolving energy market conditions.
Having established the context behind the emergence of new evaluation metrics, the
next chapter, Methodology, introduces the models developed in this project to assess
COVE and Income for different turbine designs, defined by specific power, in alignment
with the thesis aim.

3.2
The Cost Model
Wind turbine design is evaluated in this study based on SP. To assess the economic
implications of different SP configurations, the Cost Model is applied. While larger

model integrates Capital Expenditures (CAPEX) and Operational Expenditures (OPEX)
to compute the Annual Cost, which is one of the two components required to determine
both COVE and Income.

Revenue Model
The Revenue Model is needed to determine the Income generated by wind turbines

3.4
Implementation of the AEV Model
As outlined in the previous section, the AEV model consists of three key components:
the wind speed distribution represented by the Weibull model, the turbine power output
curve, and the energy value curve. This section details the implementation of these
components and specifies the parameters used in the analysis.
To account for varying wind conditions, four wind speed distributions were created:
high and moderate wind conditions for both onshore and offshore environments. These
distributions allow an evaluation of turbine performance across different wind regimes.
For the power output model, two turbine scenarios were developed: one for offshore

3.4.4
Limitations of the AEV Model
The AEV Model has several limitations that should be acknowledged and could be
addressed to improve the accuracy and applicability of the results.
This study evaluates turbines individually, assuming they operate in an open-flow en-
vironment without wake effects from nearby turbines. However, in real wind farms,
wake turbulence reduces wind speeds for downstream turbines, affecting overall en-
ergy output and power curves. Additionally, the model assumes a uniform wind speed
distribution across all turbines, regardless of their SP. In reality, turbines with different
SP values have different hub heights, influencing their exposure to wind conditions. Re-
search suggests that annual average wind speeds can increase by 1.0–1.5 m/s when
moving from 80 m to 160 m [17], which is not reflected in this model.
The power output model also relies on several simplifications. It assumes a constant
power coefficient (Cp), whereas in reality, Cp varies with wind speed, tip-speed ratio
(TSR), and blade pitch adjustments, particularly near rated wind speeds. Modern tur-
bines optimize rotational speed and blade angles to maximize efficiency, a behavior
not captured by a fixed Cp. Additionally, the model assumes sharp transitions at cut-in
and cut-out speeds, neglecting inertia effects and partial power generation. Another
simplification is the use of a constant air density for both onshore and offshore sites,
despite air density varying with altitude, temperature, and pressure.
The energy value curve model introduces further simplifications that affect the accuracy
of the AEV calculations. It assumes a linear relationship between energy value and
wind speed, ignoring the observed high value of high wind speeds. Additionally, in the
future scenario, the α value is selected without a clear methodology or precise timeline.
While historical trends suggest a decline in energy value sensitivity to wind speed, grid-
scale energy storage, market regulation changes, and evolving electricity demand could
disrupt this trend, making the assumption uncertain.
Moreover, the model assumes that all generated electricity is fully utilized. However, in
practice, wind farms face curtailment due to grid congestion, negative market prices,

18
==PAGE:25==
or transmission constraints, none of which are accounted for in this analysis. This
omission may lead to an overestimation of actual energy production and economic
performance.
Additionally, the analysis only explores turbines with a single constant rated power con-
figuration, 5 MW for onshore and 12 MW for offshore models. While this simplifies com-
parisons across SP cases, it does not account for real world variability in rated power
across different turbine designs. Many modern turbine models also offer flexible rated
power options to adapt to specific wind conditions and market needs.
Finally, extreme SP cases, where rotor diameters and swept areas are large, may be
unrealistic due to structural and practical challenges. Turbines with very large rotors
face higher material stresses and increased manufacturing and logistical constraints,
potentially requiring advanced and costly engineering solutions. Furthermore, the anal-
ysis does not consider dynamic loads and fatigue effects, which significantly impact
turbine reliability and lifespan. Beyond structural feasibility, low-SP turbines may also
present social and environmental challenges. Their larger rotor diameters increase
visibility, potentially reducing public acceptance. Additionally, they may produce more
noise and require more space, raising concerns about land use, wildlife impact, and
environmental sustainability.

3.5
Implementation of the Cost Model
To develop a realistic cost model, separate onshore and offshore models were created
to reflect the distinct cost structures of these projects.Offshore turbines require more
complex foundations, grid connections, and maintenance logistics, leading to higher
capital and operational costs compared to onshore turbines. The cost model evaluates
these differences by expressing cost as a function of SP, allowing for an analysis of how
turbine design influences total expenditures.
For CAPEX estimation, the DTU Offshore Wind Farm Cost Model was used as a base-
line. This model, originally designed for offshore projects, was modified to develop a
corresponding onshore cost model, primarily through adjustments to Balance of Plant
(BoP) costs, which are significantly lower for onshore installations.
To quantify total project costs, OPEX was estimated as a percentage of CAPEX, cap-
turing the ongoing maintenance and operational expenses of wind turbines over their
lifetime. Since OPEX has exhibited a declining trend over the years, a future OPEX
scenario was also introduced to reflect potential future cost reductions. A benchmark
analysis was also conducted to compare model outputs with industry-reported CAPEX
and OPEX values, ensuring alignment with real world cost trends.
3.5.1
CAPEX
To estimate CAPEX for turbines with varying SPs the DTU Offshore Wind Farm Cost
Model [18] was used. This Excel-based model was originally developed in 2019 and
has since undergone some updates. It is integrated into the TOPFARM Python pack-
age [18], a comprehensive tool recognized for its capabilities in economic optimiza-
tion of wind farms. The DTU model was designed to evaluate offshore wind projects,
therefore, modifications were implemented to arrive at an onshore cost model as well.
A torque adjustment was also introduced to account for increased drivetrain loads at
lower specific power values.
DTU Cost Model
In the DTU Cost Model, four main input parameters define the turbine’s critical de-
sign characteristics: the rated power, rotor diameter, rotor speed, and tower height.
From these inputs, essential turbine performance metrics are determined, including
rotor area, rotor torque, tip speed, and turbine weight. These parameters serve as
the foundation for all subsequent cost calculations. After processing these inputs, the
model calculates and outputs the specific CAPEX cost of the turbine, providing the cost
per unit of rated power for the given design configuration in EUR/W.
The CAPEX estimation for a wind turbine is divided into two primary components: the
sales price and the balance of plant (BOP). The first parameter of the sales price is the
Bill of Materials (BOM) cost, which categorizes the costs associated with major turbine
components:
• Blades
• Hub: Includes the hub structure, pitch bearings, pitch actuator system, hub com-
puter, secondary equipment, and spinner
• Nacelle: Covers the main shaft, main bearings, gearbox, coupling and brake
system, generator, cooling, power converter, controller, bedplate, yaw system,
Price volatility’s effect on wind turbine design
20
==PAGE:27==
canopy, and secondary equipment
• Tower: Includes the tower structure and internals, power cables, main trans-
former, and secondary equipment.
Each of these cost components is linked to an equation that calculates the price based
on the component weight, which is determined by the input parameters.
Beyond the BOM, additional cost factors are incorporated to estimate the total produc-
tion cost. For the final sales price, further costs such as warranty accruals, financing,
and transport are added. A fixed profit margin of 8% is also applied to the overall cost
to account for the manufacturer’s return on investment.
The Balance of Plant (BOP) cost includes all expenses related to site preparation, tur-
bine foundations, and the infrastructure required for turbine installation. For offshore
turbines, monopile foundations were assumed at a water depth of 30 meters. Addi-
tionally, BoP covers all costs associated with connecting the wind farm to the electrical
grid. In the DTU model, these costs include the costs of inter-array cables (connect-
ing turbines within the wind farm) and export cables (transmitting power to the onshore
grid), the cost of burying these cables beneath the seabed for protection, and the cost
of substations and other grid connection components.
Onshore Model
To adapt the DTU offshore model for onshore scenarios, the Balance of Plant costs
were adjusted, as BoP is one of the most significant contributors to the high cost of off-
shore wind projects. BoP costs differ significantly between onshore and offshore instal-
lations due to differences in installation logistics, foundation and electrical infrastructure
complexity. According to the European Technology and Innovation Platform on Wind
Energy, BoP costs can account for up to 50% of the total CAPEX cost of an offshore
wind farm ([19]). In contrast, for onshore turbines the turbine cost is the largest contrib-
utor to CAPEX . The International Renewable Energy Agency estimates that BoP costs
for onshore projects typically account for only around 15-25% of total CAPEX costs [20]
. Overall, offshore CAPEX is significantly higher then onshore CAPEX. For the onshore
model, the BoP costs were reduced by 70%, bringing the total BoP share to 25–30%
of the overall CAPEX.

3.5.4
Limitations of the Cost Model
While the cost model developed in this study provides a structured framework for eval-
uating the economic implications of varying SP in wind turbine design, several assump-

==PAGE:32==
tions and limitations should be acknowledged.
One key limitation is that decommissioning costs are not included in the CAPEX calcu-
lations. The eventual removal and disposal of wind turbines can represent a significant
cost, particularly for offshore projects, where foundation dismantling and subsea ca-
ble retrieval contribute to additional expenses. Excluding these costs may lead to an
underestimation of expenditures.
Additionally, OPEX is assumed to remain constant over the turbine’s operational life-
time. In reality, OPEX can vary significantly over time. As the turbine ages, mainte-
nance requirements typically increase, and certain components may need to be re-
paired or replaced.
The model also does not account for macro-economic factors that influence CAPEX
trends. Historical data has shown that the cost of raw materials, supply chain dis-
ruptions (e.g., COVID-19-related bottlenecks), and inflation significantly impact turbine
costs. For instance, the average CAPEX for new onshore wind capacity was €1.3 mil-
lion per MW in 2021, the lowest on record, but rose to €1.4 million per MW in 2022 due
to inflation and rising material costs [22]. These external economic conditions introduce
variability that is not explicitly modeled in this study.
Another simplification is the exclusion of discounting from the COVE equation and cost
model. Discounting, which adjusts future costs and revenues to present values, would
require knowledge of project-specific discount rates such as WACC (Weighted Average
Cost of Capital). These rates vary depending on factors such as region, developer, and
market conditions, making their inclusion highly case-dependent.
Regarding data selection, the current scenario values were chosen based on the most
recent available data. However, these sources do not necessarily follow a single, co-
hesive methodology, leading to potential inconsistencies. Additionally, the current con-
ditions are not all tied to the same base year or adjusted for real vs. nominal price
differences, which may introduce further discrepancies. Similarly, the future OPEX
scenario is not tied to a specific year, as the available sources do not indicate when the
projected cost reductions are expected to materialize.
Lastly, the benchmarking analysis was relatively simple and relied on a limited number
of data points. While it provides some validation for the modeled CAPEX values, further
data would be needed to increase confidence in the model’s accuracy. Additionally, no
onshore OPEX data was available for benchmarking, making it difficult to assess the
accuracy of the model’s operational cost assumptions in that segment.

3.6.2
Limitations of the Revenue Model
The model assumes that both the mean electricity price and the AEV remain constant
throughout the turbine’s lifetime. However, in reality, electricity prices are subject to
fluctuations driven by market conditions, policy changes, fuel costs, and renewable
energy penetration. Similarly, the energy value of wind power may shift over time as
grid integration improves and supply-demand dynamics evolve.
Additionally, the income calculation is based solely on electricity spot market prices,
excluding other potential revenue streams such as payments for ancillary services, ca-
pacity market participation, or green certificate revenues. The model also does not
account for the effects of inflation or discounting, effectively assuming a constant mon-
etary value over time. In practice, financial factors such as loan repayments, interest
rates, and capital financing costs play a crucial role in determining the overall prof-
itability of wind energy projects. By omitting these economic considerations, the model
simplifies the analysis but does not fully capture the financial complexities of wind power
earnings.

5
Conclusion
The purpose of this thesis was to evaluate a new approach to wind turbine selection by
incorporating electricity market dynamics into the optimization process. The research
question examined how SP influences the economic performance of turbines when as-
sessed using Income and COVE under Danish market conditions. The study explored
whether low-SP turbines, which perform better at lower wind speeds, offer a more cost-
effective and resilient alternative to high-SP turbines, which are optimized for peak wind
power capture.
The thesis followed a structured approach to investigate how wind turbine design in-
teracts with market dynamics. First, a literature review was conducted to examine the
historical evolution of the Danish wind sector and identify the most relevant economic
metrics for turbine selection in a market-driven energy landscape. Based on this review,
a modeling framework was established, integrating three interlinked models: the AEV
Model, the Cost Model, and the Revenue Model. These models enabled the evaluation
of two key design metrics, COVE and Income, by capturing the relationship between
turbine performance, market conditions, and cost structures. The models were imple-
mented, calibrated, and validated using the best available data to ensure accuracy and
reliability. With a focus on Denmark, the study defined realistic scenarios based on rep-
resentative wind conditions and market characteristics. A systematic analysis was then
conducted to assess how COVE and Income vary with SP, highlighting the trade-offs
associated with different turbine configurations. To further explore economic viability, a
sensitivity analysis was performed to quantify the impact of key input parameters under
current market conditions. Finally, projected future parameter changes were applied to
assess how different turbine designs might adapt to evolving market conditions.
The analysis first evaluated wind turbine performance under a baseline scenario where
energy production was valued uniformly across all wind speeds, meaning the alpha
parameter was set to zero. As expected, lower SP turbines generated more energy
due to their larger rotors, leading to higher AEV values. Offshore turbines benefited
from stronger and more consistent winds, yielding greater energy outputs than onshore
turbines. However, lower SP turbines incurred higher capital costs, making the optimal
COVE occur at relatively low SP values offshore, while onshore turbines minimized
COVE at higher SP values due to lower overall CAPEX.
In terms of Income, revenue maximization aligned with energy maximization, leading
to the highest Income at the lowest SP (200 W/m²) for both offshore and onshore tur-
bines. These results confirmed that under uniform energy valuation, low SP turbines
offered a financial advantage by maximizing energy output, particularly in high-wind
environments.
Introducing wind market dynamics by setting the alpha parameter to -0.077 led to notice-
able shifts in turbine performance. AEV decreased across all configurations, reflecting
the reduced economic value of energy produced during high-wind periods. Offshore
turbines saw a narrowing gap in AEV between moderate and high wind sites, while

COVE values increased compared to the baseline due to lower AEV, but onshore tur-
bines exhibited a more pronounced U-shaped trend, with optimal SP shifting lower.
This suggested that turbines with larger rotors were better suited to market conditions
where high wind speeds held less value. Offshore turbines continued to benefit from
decreasing SP, reinforcing the cost advantage of low SP designs.
Income declined due to lower AEV, with offshore turbines experiencing a steeper drop.
However, the optimal SP remained at the lowest SP of 200 W/m² for both offshore
and onshore models, reaffirming that turbines prioritizing energy production generate
the highest revenue despite price fluctuations. The gap between optimal turbines for
Income and COVE persisted, underscoring the trade-off between maximizing revenue
and minimizing costs.
The sensitivity analysis under current conditions showed that while CAPEX and OPEX
directly impact costs, wind resource characteristics—particularly the A scale factor—
play a dominant role in shaping AEV and, consequently, COVE. The alpha parameter
had the weakest effect on COVE, reinforcing that energy valuation dynamics are sec-
ondary to resource availability and cost structure. For Income, electricity price was
the most influential factor, with even small fluctuations significantly affecting profitabil-
ity. The A scale factor followed, influencing both energy production and valuable wind
speeds, particularly for high-SP turbines. Offshore turbines exhibited greater sensitiv-
ity across all parameters compared to onshore models, reflecting their higher AEV and
exposure to price volatility.
Applying future projections reinforced these trends while highlighting key risks and op-
portunities. While a reduction in OPEX significantly improved cost-effectiveness across
all turbine types, the impact of a more negative alpha was weaker, leading to only
a moderate increase in costs. This suggests that while declining energy value trends
could erode economic performance, improvements in OPEX efficiency could largely off-
set this effect. Income remained highly sensitive to electricity prices, with lower prices
drastically cutting profitability and even resulting in negative Income for some offshore
turbines.
Looking ahead, this study underscores the increasing role of market dynamics in wind
turbine optimization. The findings suggest that as renewable energy penetration grows,
lower-SP turbines may become more advantageous. However, the analysis is con-
strained by several simplifications, including assumptions about electricity pricing, cost
structures, and energy valuation. Real-world complexities—such as grid constraints
and policy changes—could further influence optimal turbine design. Moreover, while
Denmark serves as a relevant case study, these results may not directly apply to mar-
kets with different wind resources and regulatory frameworks.
Despite these limitations, this thesis demonstrates the value of incorporating market dy-
namics into turbine selection. The results highlight the need to move beyond traditional
performance metrics focused solely on energy production, as they may overlook key
economic trade-offs. By integrating market-driven valuation, this approach provides a
more comprehensive basis for turbine design and deployment. As wind energy con-
