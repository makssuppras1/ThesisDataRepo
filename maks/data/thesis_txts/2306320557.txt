Master Thesis
Abstract
The aim of this thesis is to investigate digital transformations within companies
of the sector of energy and utilities and to propose a management tool - which is
called a roadmap - whose purpose is to guide executives or digital project leaders
all along the implementation of digital changes by helping them to deﬁne a con-
crete digital strategy.
In the ﬁrst part, research in academic papers, consultancies’ white papers and pro-
fessional from major energy organisations show that the whole sector is facing a
growing demand for energy supply and an increasingly rigid legislative framework.
In this context, digital transformations appear to be a growth driver that could
sustain the increase in demand while complying with the regulation system. Even
though some companies have already started their digital transformation journey,
most of them did not since digital transformation entails massive changes in cor-
porate key operations. The thesis would provide a strategic roadmap in order to
help organisations to trigger the change.
Two key components of digital transformations: digital and leadership capabilities
are then identiﬁed. These two elements are detailed in order to capture what is
essential in implementation of digital transformation and what is mandatory to
consider.
From this analysis, recommendations can be drawn up and feed the
strategic roadmap.
Eventually, the roadmap is developed and validated in order to ensure its quality,
eﬀectiveness and usability.
This thesis hopes to oﬀer all digital project managers an opportunity to get ad-
vised and helped when deﬁning digital strategy and leading corporate challenges
and thus, promote digital as a key asset to capitalize on. This thesis is a small
contribution to fostering implementation of digital change wihtin the energy and
utilities sector.
Keywords:
Strategic roadmap, digital strategy, digital transformation, digital
technology, digitalisation of energy industry and utilities, digital capability, leader-
ship capability.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
In Europe and in many other countries, since the early 2000s, protection of the
planet and reduction of carbon footprint through smart and conscious energy de-
livery and consumption started to be at the core of the debate in the sector of
energy and utilities.
These preoccupations are still valid today.
However, the
sector looks for further developments through Digitalisation [1].
1.1
Origin of the Idea
While the energy infrastructure that underpinned much of the economic and social
development of the 20th Century continues to perform its original functions well,
there is widespread consensus that it must be fundamentally transformed in order
to meet the needs of the 21st Century.
Most of the energy and utility companies have already adopted new renewable and
sustainable policies in order to develop their activities considering their impacts
on the planet [1]. As an example, last December, during COP21 that took place
in Paris, EDF’s CEO1, Jean-Bernard Levy stated: "EDF has become the European
leader in renewable energy".
However, leading companies have to keep ahead of competitors and ﬁnd new
sources of revenue; the actors of the energy sector have to transform and inno-
vate in order to survive and thrive.
Luis Calleja, Associate Director at Oresys2 observes that 8 people out of 10 use the
Internet on daily basis in developed countries and the penetration rate3 of smart-
phones is increasing and reaches 40% by now [2]. The advent of digital technology
is a tidal wave and companies have to adapt by expanding their oﬀer, changing
their business model as well as transforming their inner working.
Accordingly, digitalization is seen as the new issue aiming at developing business
models and processes [3]. Digitalization is the increase in use of digital or com-
puter technology by an organisation [4]. This process aims at fostering the use of
connected devices and, at the same time, the collection and use of digital informa-
tion at diﬀerent levels within an organisation. For those companies, the issue is
therefore to proﬁt from digital technology and transform their business strategy.

INTRODUCTION
Digital technology allows to gather and process a large amount of data. This en-
ables energy and utility companies to turn their business towards a data-driven
business. Philippe Monloubou, ErDF’s CEO1, conﬁrmed this view when taking
oﬃce in 2014: "Tomorrow, ErDF will become a data management company. It’s
a revolution!"[1].
Companies of the sector are therefore determined to take the digital turn and
deeply change their corporate strategies. Nevertheless, implementation of digital
transformation is not an easy task since it has to be well embedded and it entails
changes all along the company value chain.
1.2
Problem statement
Energy and utility companies are becoming conscious that digital transformation
is a successful source of innovation for them.
However, the implementation of
such digital solutions has to be deﬁned with caution. That is the reason why, the
purpose of this research thesis is to investigate the way to lead digital transforma-
tions and identify the factors of its success. The research problem is raised and
formulated:
How to eﬀectively introduce digital transformations in the energy sector?
The current situation of the sector is assessed and information about existing
digital solutions and their beneﬁts are investigated. From this analysis, the na-
ture of digital transformations which could be worthwhile for energy companies is
drafted. In addition, parameters to consider when leading and implementing such
digital changes are deﬁned and examined. The essential criteria for a successful
implementation plan are also pinpointed.
1.3
A case from the energy sector
It is to be noticed that the research is carried out based on representative compa-
nies of the sector. Data is collected from diﬀerent energy companies, Veolia UK,
GrDF, ErDF and Schneider Electric in order to have a broader view of what is
needed by the sector in terms of digital implementation. Veolia UK is a local
agency of a massive transnational organisation. Veolia UK helps cities and in-
dustries to manage, optimise and make the most of their resources - in particular
energy [5]. ErDF and GrDF manages the public electricity and gaz respectively
distribution network for continental France. The companies are implementing new

RESEARCH METHOD
2
Research Method
As stated in the introduction, this thesis deals with the implementation of digital
transformations in the energy sector and in utilities. In order to study these digital
transformations, diﬀerent sources of data were needed.
First of all, data from litterature helped to scope the problem and to get an
overview of the energy and utilities sector and its related topical issues. By look-
ing into the perspectives of development of the sector, it came into light the ex-
pected achievements of digital transformations. Scientiﬁc articles were also used
to understand the technical background inherent to digital transformations. In-
deed, researches in data science are currently being led and can be used to deﬁne
corporate strategy in data management. Furthermore, scientiﬁc papers provided
meaningful surveys that were used to catch the trend of the sector as well as the
best practices of digital technology implementation. Finally, papers proposing a
method to produce guidelines, roadmaps and other implementation plans were
helpful to designing an eﬀective roadmap for executives leading digital transfor-
mations.
With the view to render the data collection more consistent, data from companies
was gathered. Two kinds of companies can be distinguished. Obviously, energy
companies and utilities were approached in order to back up the data collected
from litterature. Investigations were carried out in energy companies and utilities
aiming at identifying the way organisations deal with digital issues in their core
operations. Current digital projects were observed and future perspectives and
objectives were discussed. Indeed, interviews were performed with executives who
wanted to contribute to this thesis. This was the opportunity for them to share
what challenges they are facing on daily basis and what their vision of digital
transformation is. Interviews let room for digital leaders to speak their mind so
it was the opportunity to collect data from people living the transformation from
the inside in. The second type of companies which contributed to the data col-
lection are consultancies. These companies are often mandated to conduct digital
transformations and projects within companies. Consultants and project leaders
brought an expert assessment of the sector regarding digital developments.
Table 13 in appendix 2 provides an overview of these interviews. This information
serves to identify the burning issues in digital challenge. This was used to review
the perception of digital transformations. Consultant provided also methods and
insights about what would be ideally done when carrying digital transformations.
Moreover, a consultancy accepted to collaborate and develop the roadmap. This
collaboration gives the roadmap its ﬁnal form. The strategic management tool is

CURRENT SITUATION ANALYSIS
3
Current Situation Analysis
This section presents what the energy market is and the current digital transfor-
mations in this sector as well as their beneﬁts. Indeed, in order to propose an
eﬀective implementation plan, one has to know the speciﬁties of the sector and
its related market. Having this overall understanding of the sector leads to better
conceive how digital technology can improve the existing processes and what the
related issues are all along the value chain. Designing a strategic roadmap for the
energy sector also means to investigate what is going on in other sectors that have
already taken the digital turn, observe and learn from the best practices.
3.1
General Analysis of the Sector
The sector of energy can be divided into three diﬀerent groups of activities. The
ﬁrst one is the transformation of raw material into fuel and energy, the second is
the delivery of power and the last one is related to customer operation. These
activities compose the energy value chain. In the sector, the whole value chain
undergoes radical changes since the 1970s [6].
3.1.1
The energy and utilities market
First oﬀ, it is to be noticed that the consumption of energy has signiﬁcantly
evolved. Figure 2 shows that the type of fuel used in the ﬁnal consumption has
changed. Indeed, fossil fuel consumption tends to reduce in favour of more sus-
tainable form of energy such as electricity. This reveals a change in the energy
market which is developing in a more sustainable way [6].

3.1.2
Regulation of the sector
Another speciﬁcity of the sector is that energy companies and utilities are subjected
to global and local regulations. The latter guide energy activities by providing a
legal framework.
Since 1997 and the adoption of Kyoto Protocol, most of the countries committed to
minimise their carbon footprint by reducing greenhouse-gas emissions [10]. Hence,
more complex regulation has been set at the local level.

5.3
Core Operation
Another key aspect of the digital transformation is: core operations transforma-
tion. Core operations is the part of a company’s business that is central to its
overall strategy and generates the bulk of its revenues [20]. As a consequence, the
issue of digital transformation is to improve operations and core processes in order
to create a competitive advantage.
Core operations is a valuable source of competitive advantage. Competitors can

tained [30]. An operational advantage is diﬃcult to copy and this is the reason
why digitally transformed operations is a real possibility to consolidate its meth-

indicators, front line workers can be armed with up-to-date data and therefore
solve problems more quickly, technology can accurately detects defects in produc-

as observed by George Westerman, Didier Bonnet and Andrews McAfee [30], they

free from outdated assumptions due to older technology.

Building the Roadmap: Leadership Capability
In section 4, the starting point of digital transformations in the energy industry
has been studied. Energy companies and utilities are placed in the down-right side
of the digital mastery matrix [18] meaning that they are lagging behind in terms
of digital capabilities but they rely on a strong governance.
In chapter 5, the what of digital transformation - digital capabilities - as transfor-
mation opportunities have been investigated and will feed the strategic roadmap.
Now, the how of driving transformation is to be discussed considering the speciﬁ-
ties of the energy industry. As identiﬁed by George Westerman, Didier Bonnet and
Andrews McAfee [34], the vast majority of companies does not face the problem
of getting people started but the issue to get people moving in the same direction.
Both challenges are the domain of leadership. The point of building digital capa-
bilities is to ensure to turn digital capabilities investments into concrete business
advantage.
Energy companies and utilities do not start from scratch and in the following
paragraphs, it is assumed that digital capabilities are strong enough to support
wide-scale project such as digital transformation. This chapter will describe the
three components of leadership capability. Digital vision ensures that people across
the company are aligned and therefore move in the same direction. This fosters
the implementation of digital transformation. Another key aspect is the fact that
people must feel engaged in the transformation. Finally, strenghtening governance
and focusing investment enable to steer the transformation in the right direction.
6.1
Creating a Compelling Digital Vision
Digital vision is the cornerstone of successful digital transformation. The objective
of creating a digital vision is to align stakeholders from senior executives to workers.
6.1.1
A Top-Down Approach
A research1 conducted by George Westerman, Didier Bonnet and Andrews McAfee
[35] shows that, among the companies asked, none have conducted its digital trans-
formation from a bottom-up approach. Indeed, successful digital transformation
starts at the top of the company. Only executives and seniro managers can create
a compelling vision of the future and communicate it throughout the organization.

6.2
Engaging People Across the Company
Digital transformation is not a local change in the company but contrarywise, it is
a huge challenge for the whole organisation. Creating a compelling digital vision
is mandatory to get started in the digital journey. Once executives are consitent
when deﬁning the strategic vision and aligned, the vision is communicated to top
and middle managers. However, this is not suﬃcient to lead digital transforma-
tion. In the energy industry, digital change is not a step by step process but a
transformative one meaning that it will happen only if people are engaged.
6.2.1
Engaging the Organisation
The point of this leadership capability is therefore to ensure the engagement of
many. Engagement in this paragraph means that people across the company are
energized to make the vision a reality. The process to reach this last objective

Governing Digital Transformation
Even though the digital vision has been created sucessfully and people across
the company feel commited in the transformation, it remain complex to steer
the organization eﬀort in a single direction. Energy companies and utilities are
know to be, in majority, massive companies. These last can be full of entropy
and by deﬁnition move in the direction of less order. Indeed, even if employees
and managers are engaged in the transformation, they might move in their own
directions. Moreover, those who did not adhere to the digital vision can continue
to ignore it and thereby slow the transformation. Another issue is that some units
involved in the transformation could move at diﬀerent pace. Moving too fast could
be risky and therefore a source of threat for the entire transformation. There is
obviously a need to orchestrate the diﬀrent units and stakeholders working on
the transformation. This orchestration also avoids to waste resources and lead

6.3.1
How to Build Digital Governance
Governing products, services or distribution network for example is not something
new for energy companies and utilities. However, digital transformation entails
the creation of a new type of governance.
First of all, the company has to capitalize on its best practices. It is therefore
recommended to investigate internally and ﬁnd a governance model that works
eﬀectively.
The point of governance is to build up a structure composed of a leader and some
senior managers and capable to establish priorities and ensure the good coordi-
nation among the teams working on the transformation. There is also a need to
determine what should be discussed at the governance level and should be debated
at a lower level. So far, the description digital governance is not diﬀerent than a
classic governance. So what is the diﬀerence?
The diﬀerence consists of governing digital technology as well as the data inher-
ent to any digital transformation. The structure of digital governance must allow
to stay abreast of the fast changing digital technology market. New digital tech-
nology can foster innovation and emphasize company’s performance but only if
applied in the right places with the right investment. Furthermore, it has been
shown that data is at the center of digital transformation and this data needs to be
governed. Digital governance ensures the quality and the consistency of data that
is used within the company. In addition, transparency of data is a key aspect of
competing in the digital age. Eventually, new demands for digital capabilities and
new risks from digital activities have made digital governance essential in energy
companies and utilities.
The mechanisms of digital governance are to be investigated and companies should
identify which mechanism best ﬁts to the corporate digital vision.

7.5.1
Discussing Time Dimension
First of all, the circular layout appeared to be a bit disturbing for users wanting to
deﬁne transformation strategy. Even though digital transformation in the energy
industry is not a step-by-step process some strategic actions are to be made at
the onset of the transformation and some others later. A circular model does not
encompass time dimension or planning. Thus usability of the roadmap is reduced
because users can feel a bit lost when using the roadmap. Strategic items are iden-
tiﬁed but their projection in a schedule or on a time line is not facilitated which
reduces eﬀectiveness of the roadmap. Even though the roadmap includes priority
management, it does not deﬁne the time allocated to a speciﬁc task, it does not
inform precisely when to consider strategic items.
Aiming at adressing this shortcoming, a complementary module should be added
to the roadmap. The idea that was suggested by Mathieu Mounier, is to com-
bine the roadmap with a steering tool. The role of the roadmap is to identify the
strategic items and describe the transformation components. The complementary
role of the steering tool is to place the strategic items on a time line according to
their description. Companies or consultancies already propose this kind of man-
agement product. Steering tools are mostly based on Gantt charts1. Jean-Louis
Salomon, CEO of Quimeo2, was interviewed to better understand the issues related
to scheduling and planning in transformation project. "Our clients are companies
or consultancies leading digital projects in every sector of activity. Project leaders
need a smart and intuitive steering tool to manage project’s development. Planning
key tasks, assessing key relationships, overview and project control are the crucial
issues solved by our turnkey solution".
The turnkey solution that Jean-Louis Salomon refers to is articulated around three
stages: deﬁning project strategy, implementing a tailored steering tool and sus-
taining the steering system. This shows how the roadmap and a steering tool
can be combined in order to manage planning related issues in digital transfor-
mation. This is the action that would be investigated in order to develop the
roadmap further and thus increase its usability and eﬀectiveness. Collaboration
with Quimeo for instance would generate an "all inclusive" solution adressing the

7.5.2
Discussing Application Scope
As stated above in the thesis, the roadmap has been developed in order to meet
the requirements of digital transformation in the energy industry. the roadmap
is therefore applicable in this sector of activity. This is ensured by the validation
chapter that veriﬁed this criteria. However, can this scope be extended to another
sector?
The roadmap is composed of strategic items classiﬁed in four groups: Objectives,
Business Units, Leadership capability and Digital capability. Ojectives come from
an in depth analysis of topical issues of the energy sector. As a result, they are
speciﬁc to energy issues and developments. The three other categories are related
to Digitalisation. Business Units are assessed in terms of existing capabilities, re-
sources and level of digital mastery. Leadership and digital capabilities elements
describe the action levers to activate in order to trigger the transformation and
lead the project to meet the objectives.
Consequently, one can imagine that such a roadmap can be extended to other
sectors of activity dealing with digital transformation. The objectives are too spe-
ciﬁc to be immediately extended but should be completely redeﬁned. In addition,
the digital mastery background must be reviewed in case of an application in an-
other industry. However, the roadmap demonstrates, once again, its ﬂexibility and
adaptability. One can start from this paper and use the same approach to develop
a strategic roadmap that can ﬁt to any other industry.

Conclusion
Across the world, scientists agree that energy production and delivery will be the
issues of the Century. An unstoppable growing demand drives the energy market
and forces energy companies and utilities to address this need. At same time,
eco-responsability increases in people’s minds which obligates the entire energy
industry to reduce its carbon footprint and greenhouse-gas emissions, to include
renewable energy in its core processes and to emphasize energy eﬃciency across its
operation value chain. These issues are protected by regulatory frameworks and
energy companies have to ﬁnd new ways to meet these objectives.
Transforming energy companies by implementing digital technology generates ben-
eﬁts and will enable companies of the sector to address some of their issues. Digital
transformation entails wide changes across companies. Such wide projects are de-
cided at the executive level of organisations.
The research that has been carried out in this thesis aimed at producing a strategic
management tool that helps transformation leaders to deﬁne their digital strategy
with caution by considering every crucial aspect. The strategic roadmap would
therefore ensure an eﬀective implementation of digital transformation.
Research was performed to collect background data from the energy industry. Dig-
ital technology as well as digital transformation caracteristics were investigated.
By working in close collaboration with Oresys Consulting, the study gave birth to
the strategic roadmap. This is the ﬁnal product of a phased research combined
with expert support from the industry.
The strategy roadmap is still a theoretical management tool since it has not been
applied. However, the concrete dimension of the tool allows to think that it can
be proposed to professionals operating in the energy industry. Consultancies can
act as a relay and pursue the development of this strategic roadmap in order to
make it used by companies leading digital transformations.
