Abstract
In this work, three main systems of methanol production are investigated. the three
cases differ in their way of producing synthesis gas (syngas) prior to methanol production.
In the first case, referred to as “PtX”, biogas originating from a biogas plant is first cleaned
and upgraded by removing the CO2, with the biomethane being injected into the gas grid.
The CO2 obtained is then used as a carbon source together with hydrogen from electrolysis
to synthesize methanol. In the second system, the same type of biogas undergoes steam
reforming to create syngas, which is then mixed with hydrogen from electrolysis.
This
system is referred to as “Mixed”. The last system, the "Bio”, consists of the same type of
biogas, mixed with additional methane from the grid and steam reformed to syngas.
The systems are simulated using the Aspen Plus Process Simulator to obtain the mass
and energy balances, with operational conditions chosen based on average literature data and
sensitivity analyses. All systems are scaled for the production of 130,000 tons of methanol
per year. A technological analysis shows that the PtX system exhibits the highest carbon
efficiency, with the methane content of biogas considered as a by-product, but it also shows
the lowest total efficiency due to high power consumption from a large electrolyzer.
Furthermore, a cost assessment of the systems is conducted in this report, divided
into a private economic and a socio-economic approach, which includes additionally the
CO2 avoided emissions of each system as part of their benefit to society. This analysis is
conducted in phases, adding gradually levels of complexity, in a variable prices environment.
More specifically, the impact of the varying electricity, CH4 and CO2 prices on the levelized
cost of methanol (LCoM) and the socioeconomic benefit for a period of 20 years is examined,
visually highlighting which system is most viable.
Starting with the private economics, the investigation reveals that the PtX system’s
optimal capacity factor, is 60% full load. This factor is maintained for PtX, while Mixed
and Bio system consistently operate at 100% full load. The results indicate that PtX is the
most profitable with methane prices above 1,150 EUR/ton when using solar PV and onshore
wind. When operating at lower capacity factors CAPEX increases, but power prices decrease.
The study establishes trends for electricity price reductions starting from 2025 for operations
in lower capacity factors, assuming consistent price behaviors. It is found that going up in
capacity factors, there becomes an area for which the electricity prices increase overcomes the
CAPEX decrease and it affects negatively the PtX system, and an area for which the power
prices are low enough that the CAPEX decrease becomes pronounced and it has a positive
impact in the PtX profitability. In the socio-economic approach, including the CO2 avoided
emissions, for CO2 values of 70 EUR/ton and above, PtX is the most socio-economically
advantageous for a combination of solar, onshore, and offshore wind technologies.
Lithuania, Spain, and Sweden are analyzed as potential locations, with their starting
electricity prices influencing their optimal capacity factors, which are found at 40% full load
for Lithuania, 70% for Spain and 80% for Sweden. Denmark’s electricity and methane prices
might indicate that it is potentially economically viable to repurpose and integrate methane
into its industrial processes, while Sweden is intriguingly found to be the best candidate for
PtX socioeconomically, mostly because of its high methane price.

Introduction and Objective
2.1
Energy transition
The consequences of using fossil fuels these days have been extensively documented,
alongside the advantages of renewable energy sources [1]. The accelerating greenhouse gas
emissions have already disrupted the equilibrium of the natural carbon cycle [2]. Climate
change and global warming effects due to anthropogenic CO2 in the atmosphere have become
a concern, and as a result ambitious treaties have been signed globally, such as the Kyoto
Protocol and the Paris Agreement of 2015, aiming to restrict global warming to 1.5 °C above
pre-industrial levels. The Intergovernmental Panel of Climate Change (IPCC) states that for
that goal to be reached, carbon neutrality by 2050 is required, meaning net zero emissions
of CO2 [3]. Under this context, in 2019 European Commission established the "European
Green Deal", for making Europe carbon neutral by 2050 [4]. Today, 194 countries have
joined the Paris Agreement, with Denmark, Germany, France, Canada, UK and more to
have pledged to achieve carbon neutrality by 2050, and Iceland and Sweden by 2040 and
2045 respectively [5].
For carbon neutrality, the deployment of carbon-neutral fuels, meaning carbon fuels
that do not increase the CO2 in the atmosphere as they are produced from natural CO2
sources, and therefore there is a zero net emission gain in the atmosphere, is important [3].
However, alongside the use of these fuels, there is a critical need for electrification across
sectors to reduce emissions further. The additional introduction and use of renewable energy
sources (RES) for the development of sustainable energy systems are crucial [6]. However,
renewable sources, such as wind and solar, are characterized by fluctuations and dependency
on weather conditions. To tackle this problem, energy storage solutions are required to be
developed on a big scale, in order to decarbonize sectors that are difficult to electrify, mainly
maritime and air transportation [6].
Direct electrification, for instance, battery-based electric cars, is the most optimal and
cost-effective way to use electricity from renewable sources. Other technologies should be
prioritized when direct electrification is not possible or it is very expensive. These types of
sectors would be aviation, shipping, and certain industrial sectors.
2.2
Pollution control
As explained earlier, it is widely known that climate change poses a significant risk to the
environment, therefore policies to reduce emissions are of high importance. Environmental
pollution is an example of an economic externality, meaning a cost caused by an economic
actor that is not suffered directly by that same actor. The associated costs that greenhouse
gas emissions (GHG) cause to society are not paid by the entities that emit them, and so they
are not reflected in the market prices. Market-based policies have the purpose of creating
systems where firms have to pay the societal cost of their pollution [7]. They aim to reduce
GHG emissions as, theoretically, they can achieve the environmental objectives at lower
costs, providing also financial incentives to develop lower-emitting technologies, leaving it to
the market to decide which technologies will thrive [7].
1
==PAGE:15==
N.Anastasia Gkountou
The most basic forms of market-based policies are the taxes, the subsidies, and the
cap-and-trade programs. By introducing a tax, the polluting entity endures an extra cost
based on the amount of pollution emitted, giving an incentive to reduce its pollution. Sub-
sidy programs provide assistance from the government for particular types of low-emitting
technologies, primarily used to incentivize the deployment of renewable energy sources. In
the cap-and-trade program, instead of setting a price on each unit of pollution, the total
quantity of pollution (cap) that will be allowed is determined by the regulatory authority.
Then companies trade certificates, in other words, they buy and sell emission allowances,
according to their needs. The cap is met by the requirement that companies own enough
allowances to cover their emissions, which also creates the demand for the allowances. A
company will reduce its emissions if it is less costly than buying allowances, and if it reduces
the emissions lower than the requirements, its excess allowances can be sold in an open
market. The scarcity of allowances is putting a price on GHG emissions, which provides the
economic incentive necessary for companies to reduce their emissions [7].
2.2.1
Emissions trading system- ETS
The European Union Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) is the largest cap-and-trade
system with which EU dedicates itself to the reduction of CO2 emissions. EU ETS has been
developed in phases, since 2005. It covers around 11,000 power stations and manufacturing
plants within the 28 member states, additionally Iceland, Liechtenstein and Norway. It also
covers aviation activities and it regulates around 45% of the total GHG emissions in EU [8].
According to this system, a limit on overall emissions from installations is put and gets
reduced every year. Within this limit, companies are able to buy and sell emission allowances
according to their needs. Each allowance gives the holder the right to emit one ton of CO2.
The allowances have to be surrendered, and big fines are imposed to rule breakers [8].
2.2.2
Socio-economic value of CO2-Carbon pricing
By capping the GHG emissions, EU ETS creates an incentive for companies to invest
in more efficient and less carbon-intensive technologies. The system is setting the supply
and the market determines the demand. The market price for allowances, known as "carbon
price", creates a bigger incentive as it increases, meaning that if the CO2 emissions permit
becomes high enough, it may be cost-efficient for some industrial installations to implement
environmentally friendly technologies or switch to renewable fuels instead of paying the
allowances [9], [8].
ETS is an important step in developing in international carbon market. National and
regional systems have already been implemented in China, Canada, New Zealand, US, and
Switzerland. If the ETS systems are linked through an international carbon market, global
cooperation on climate change will be enhanced, and the carbon price will get stabilized [8].

2.3
Renewable energy integration
With an established price in CO2 emissions, the enhancement of renewable energy inte-
gration would be the next step. The engagement of different players in the energy transition
is essential. For power generation from different sources, there are typical capacity factor
values for a set time interval and a set input (light, water, wind). The capacity factor of
a power plant measures how much power it actually produces, and is given by the actual
amount of energy the plant produced over a period of time divided by the amount of energy
the plant could, in theory, have produced if it was run continuously over this period of time
in its full capacity. [11]. In the case of renewable energy generators, the capacity factor,
how continuously a plant runs over time, is affected not only by maintenance but also by
weather fluctuations, how much the sun shines and the wind blows. Figure 2 illustrates the
trends of the global weighted average (GWA) capacity factors and levelized costs of electric-
ity (LCoEs) for solar PV, onshore, and offshore wind power over the last years. The LCOE
depicts the average cost that a power plant owner can expect to pay for each unit of energy
the plant will generate over its lifetime.
The GWA capacity factor for onshore wind power has seen substantial improvements.
In 2010, the global weighted average capacity factor was 27%, while by 2022 it had increased
to 37%. This increase is largely attributed to advancements in turbine technology, including
larger diameters and higher hub heights, which allow for more energy capture. Additionally,
better site selection and optimized layouts to minimize wake losses have contributed to this
growth. The variability in such capacity factors is influenced by differing wind resources,
3
==PAGE:17==
N.Anastasia Gkountou
site configurations, and technological applications. Furthermore, the LCoE for onshore wind
power has significantly decreased, with a decline of 69% from 2010 to 2022, enhancing its
competitiveness as a renewable energy source. Key factors driving this reduction include
technological improvements in turbines, reductions in operation and maintenance (O&M)
costs through digital technologies, supply chain competitiveness, and perhaps economies of
scale [12].
The GWA capacity factor for utility-scale solar PV has also shown improvement, in-
creasing from 13.8% in 2010 to 16.9% in 2022. This improvement is driven by deploying solar
PV systems in sunnier locations with higher radiation levels and reducing system losses. Re-
garding LCoE, solar PV has experienced one of the most dramatic declines among renewable
energy technologies. From 2010 to 2022, the global weighted average LCoE of utility-scale
PV plants fell by 89%. This decline is attributed to several factors, including reductions in
total installed costs and O&M costs, and improvements in capacity factors. Technological
advances across the entire value chain, including the expansion of larger polysilicon factories
and innovations in production processes, have been pivotal [12].
Offshore wind technology has been advanced as well. The GWA capacity factor for newly
commissioned offshore wind farms has grown from 38% in 2010 to 42% in 2022. This growth
is primarily due to developments in turbine technology, enhancing electricity generation.
Additionally, improved analytics and experience in identifying optimal sites have optimized
the output. The LCoE for offshore wind has also seen a substantial decrease with the LCoE
to have fallen by 59% from 2010 to 2022 [12].
LAZARD’s report for June 2024 estimates the levelized cost of energy for all technolo-
gies, including for solar PV utility at 29-92 $/MWh, for onshore wind at 27-73 $/MWh and
offshore at 74-139 $/MWh [13].

2.4
Power-to-X
Even if enough renewable energy systems were installed to power all sectors in the
world, that would not be possible as not all sectors can be electrified, shipping and aviation
for instance. In this case, the electrons need to be converted to a molecule, which is more
stabilized and is excellent at carrying and releasing energy efficiently.
Power-to-X is a general term for the technologies that are based on using electricity to
produce a fuel (electrofuel) via the production of hydrogen. This hydrogen could be directly
used as a fuel itself for transportation or industrial purposes, or it can be further converted
into other fuels and chemicals [14].
The role of intermediate hydrogen production is to
provide a balance between power supply and fuel production, and provide flexibility in the
system due to the fluctuating nature of renewables, as well as the desire for operation while
electricity prices are low and sell when they are high. Under the Power-to-X concept, excess
electricity is taken into advantage by converting it into other fuels via a chemical plant [15].
The seasonal imbalances that need to be addressed are characterized by time span and
power capacities that the batteries or compressed air storage cannot handle cost-effectively.
Hydro-pumped storage would be a better option than these, however, it is characterized by
geographical restrictions. Furthermore, PtX provides the opportunity to supply renewable
energy to areas that do not have solar and wind potential, by converting it to hydrogen or
other fuels, that their transportation would have less energy losses than if electricity was
transported [15]. However, the PtX technologies are energy-intensive and costly, mainly due
to electrolysis, hence they require incentives, subsidies, or CO2 displacement regulations to
make it more attractive to industries [14].
2.4.1
Power-to-X in Denmark
Together with the "Climate agreement for energy and industry" in 2020, Danish gov-
ernment agreed to prepare a strategy for "Power-to-X" (PtX) and Carbon Capture and Uti-
lization (CCU) projects, by launching initiatives and proposals for their promotion. They
proposed the insrallation of 4-6 GW of electrolysis capacity by 2030, supporting the po-
tential in the PtX area and reaching international climate goals by reducing Denmark’s
footprint. Additionally, PtX tenders have been proposed with big investments to support
the production of hydrogen and other PtX products [14].
A spin-out from Aalborg University in Denmark, REintegrate, developed a system for
e-methanol production using the Power-to-X concept, by combining H2 and CO2, under the
"Power2Met" project, in 2019. By 2021 a pilot plant demonstration was able to produce
300,000 L of green methanol.
The Danish renewable energy company European Energy
acquired REintegrate the same year as part of their new PtX division [16]. Today, in 2024,
the world’s first commercial PtX plant has been built in Kassø, located west of Aabenraa in
Southern Denmark. For the hydrogen production, a 52 MW electrolyzer plant using PEM
(proton exchange membrane) electrolysis is utilized. One of the bigger end users of the e-
methanol will be the shipping company A.P. Moller - Maersk. The project is expected to be
completed by the end of 2024 and produce around 32,000 tons of e-methanol [17].

2.5
Carbon capture, storage, and utilization
Carbon capture and storage (CCS) systems are reported as the main option for CO2
emissions reduction for many carbon-heavy industries, since they are already a big part of
the global economy. However in reality the lifetime of the CCS is limited because of their
energy-intensive nature and the CO2 tax system that gives the emitters penalties. Although
CCS systems have not been financially so attractive, the utilization of the captured CO2
(CCU) can give a new perspective to the picture [1].
Sourcing and securing a consistent supply of CO2 for the production of green fuels can be
a challenge. Air and biomass are reported as possible renewable sources for CO2. Biomass
sources mainly refer to municipal and industrial waste and agriculture residues, whereby
biogenic CO2 is produced through various technologies as a byproduct [1]. For instance,
carbon can be captured in the form of CO2 from flue gas streams of incineration or biogas
plants. When the carbon is of biogenic origin, it is considered CO2 neutral and it can be
used to produce PtX fuels, which in turn will be considered climate neutral. CO2 can be
directly captured from the atmosphere as well (Direct Air Capture), but this technology is
not so cost-effective, since there are only about 400 ppm of CO2 in the atmosphere. However,
it is under optimization efforts and is expected to play an important role in the future due
to the abundance of air in the atmosphere [14]. Regulations such as the Delegated Act on
the EU’s Renewable Energy Directive (RED) set the criteria on when CO2 deriving from
fossils can be utilized in PtX, thus the final products can be eligible for Renewable Fuels
of Non-Biological Origin (RFNBO) certifications [18]. Therefore the preference now lies on
using biogenic CO2, for instance originating from biomass or biogas.
2.6
Biogas and its production
Biogas is a viable and sustainable energy resource, produced by cheap feedstocks, pri-
marily waste, and has many applications in power generation, heating, transportation, and
6
==PAGE:20==
N.Anastasia Gkountou
raw material for further production of chemicals and biofuels [19]. It is regarded as CO2
neutral, since the amount of CO2 emitted when it is combusted is the same as the one used
for the organic matter to be formed, and has been stored in living organisms as part of the
short-term carbon cycle. [20].
Different biomasses like pig and cattle manure, organic food waste, agricultural crop
wastes, and wastewater sludge can be substrates for the anaerobic digestion process, a bi-
ological process in which microbes degrade the organic matter and produce gases through
their various metabolic pathways. Biogas consists of around 50-70% of CH4, 30-50% of CO2,
and some small percentages of hydrogen sulfide and ammonia, among others, depending on
the type of substrate and process used for its production [19]. The calorific value of biogas
depends on its CH4 content. The type of organic materials (substrate) that is used influ-
ences the amount of CH4 that is produced. More specifically, substrates with increased fatty
acids produce biogas higher in CH4 content, whereas substrates rich in carbohydrates lead
to biogas with higher CO2 content [20].
2.6.1
Biogas in Denmark
Biogas has a long tradition in Denmark. Biogas production in Denmark is mostly based
on manure, and organic waste, which includes source-separated household food waste and
sludge from wastewater treatment. It is used in combined heat and power (CHP) plants or
upgraded to remove the CO2 content, moisture, and impurities and distributed in the natural
gas pipelines [20]. It is mainly produced at two types of facilities, agricultural plants and
wastewater treatment plants. The first typically uses manure and organic waste is added to
increase the yield. The digestate is used as fertilizer. The latter uses sludge from wastewater
treatment and may include additional biomass. The digestate is used either as fertilizer or
it is incinerated, depending on its polluting content [21].
More specifically, there are centralized and farm biogas plants. The centralized ones
digest between 50 and 600 m3 of manure per day, corresponding between 18,000 and 220,000
tonnes annually. The farm ones digest between 5 and 50 m3 of manure corresponding between
2,000 and 18,000 tonnes annually. The centralized plants use continuous stirred tank reactors
(CSTR) and in 2020 contributed 60.4% of the total biogas production [22]. By 2020 there
were 163 biogas plants, 50% of which use agricultural products, 31% sewage residues, 3%
industrial products, and 16% landfill gas. Specifically, in 2020, biogas from 33 plants was
delivered to the gas network, making Denmark the one with the highest share of biogas in
gas consumption amongst European countries [23].

2.7
Green Methanol
Methanol is a colorless, neutral, and polar liquid in ambient conditions, having a boiling
point of 64.6 °C and a freezing point of -97.6 °C. It is a simple oxygenated hydrocarbon that is
amongst the most widely traded chemicals [26], as it constitutes a material for the production
of several chemical products.
Methanol produced from natural gas, coal, and oil, is called "black", and it is usually
produced via reforming and gasification.
"Green" methanol is produced from renewable
sources with zero net CO2 emissions, with hydrogen from electrolysis and CO2 from biogas,
biomass or the atmosphere. "Grey" methanol is produced from waste and byproducts that
are partly renewable [1]. Its wide range of feedstock sources, together with its low emissions
of nitrogen and sulfur oxides and particulate matter when combusted, due to it being a
single carbon molecule, make it a popular candidate for reducing the fossil carbon footprint,
especially in transportation [26].
Methanol can be used in various sectors and has several applications. Traditionally it is
used to produce paints, silicones, and pharmaceuticals and plays a role in the wood industry
as well [26].
It is widely used in the chemical industry, providing the raw material for
the production of many chemical substances, such as formaldehyde, dimethyl ether (DME),
various fuels, gasoline, and olefins which are important for further petrochemical processes
[1]. In the past years, its use in the energy sector as an energy carrier has been gaining
interest, reaching 40% of its total production in 2020, with an estimation in demand of
around 100 million metric tons per year [1]. Due to its high latent heat, no carbon-to-carbon
bonds, high octane rating, its non-toxicity, and its not explosive nature makes it perform
comparatively better than conventional fuels in internal combustion engines. China first
developed a policy to use methanol as transportation fuel produced by coal gasification,
proving methanol a viable fuel and having vehicles running on pure methanol or methanol
blends today. Methanol can be also used in fuel cells, either directly or via methanol steam
reforming into a hydrogen-rich gas mixture [1].
As already mentioned, electrofuels can play an important role in decarbonizing the
shipping sector. In 2018, the International Maritime Organization (IMO) implemented a
climate strategy including an ambition of 50% reduction in GHG emissions by 2050 in
shipping. According to the IMO, all ships need to use fuel that meets new requirements
regarding SOx emissions limits, a fact that increases the interest in the use of methanol as
a fuel in maritime shipping, together with Bio-Liquid Natural Gas (bio-LNG). A.P. Moller-
Maersk has made a relevant study, which concluded that green alcohols are fully mixable
in the vessel’s bunker tanks [1, 27]. Additionally, it is easy to be stored and transported,
being liquid at standard conditions, not harmful in case of leakage, and the infrastructure is
already in place. A.P. Moller - Maersk, embracing the green transition, has ordered 18 large
methanol-enabled vessels, with the first one to have already been launched, and has ensured

sufficient green methanol to fuel its methanol-enabled vessel fleet [28].
In regards to methanol pricing, it varies significantly depending on the choice of tech-
nology, the feedstock, production capacity, operating conditions and by-products revenues.
For e-methanol, its cost depends mostly on the hydrogen production cost, which is closely re-
lated to the electricity prices, and the CO2 cost which depends on its origin, carbon capture
method, purification and compression before its utilization for methanol production [29].
The cost of e-methanol is reported by various works and publications to fall within a broad
range of approximately 800-1600$/ton. Regarding the cost of bio-methanol, which refers
to methanol produced using biomass and biogas as feedstock, it is reported to range from
320-770$/ton [29–35].
In general, e-methanol is currently more expensive than the one derived from fossil, and
hence its market price, which is around 350-450 EUR/ton [Figure 45 Appendix]. However,
the International Energy Agency states that in the long term, e-methanol cost is expected
to decrease, becoming competitive with the cost of the one with fossil origin. This can be
attributed to the electricity price reductions due to the expected wider renewable energy
sources deployment, which accounts normally for 40-70% of the total production cost, but
also due to the technological improvements in the electrolyzers, the development of advanced
catalysts for the CO2 hydrogenation and perhaps due to economy of scale effects. Addition-
ally, methanol’s growing demand, together with increasing environmental restrictions, may
allow a higher price in the future market. Simultaneously, fossil-derived methanol’s price is
also expected to increase due to CO2 emission credits cost. These combined effects could
make renewable methanol competitive in the international market [31].
2.8
Objective
The purpose of the present work is to investigate three different routes of biogas to
methanol production, varying the upstream processes of obtaining the syngas. The systems
primarily differ in whether the methane content of biogas is utilized together with the CO2
or it is separated and injected into the gas grid, and whether hydrogen from electrolysis is
included or additional methane from the grid is added.
These systems are simulated in Aspen Plus and compared in terms of technological
aspects (energy and carbon losses and efficiencies), but also in terms of business case attrac-
tiveness through their levelized cost of methanol (LCoM). This comparison is additionally
performed under a socio-economic framework, taking into consideration the current social,
economic, and environmental status that formulates the market nowadays in central Euro-
pean countries.
Price-changing environments are incorporated into the cost assessment, in order to give
a holistic overview of the effects of varying prices and visualize the trade-offs of the various
parameters influencing the outcome. The main objective is to facilitate informed decision-
making for future renewable energy integration and green methanol production across various
regional contexts.

Analysis of the processes
3.1
Hydrogen and its production
Hydrogen constitutes one of the most promising and clean fuels, with high energy den-
sity (142 MJ/kg) and many attractive properties as an energy carrier. It is mainly used
in industrial applications, for instance, petroleum refining, chemical industries, fuel cells,
and supply for manufacturing chemicals such as ammonia, methanol, pharmaceuticals, and
hydrochloric acid [36,37]. Over the years hydrogen has been produced from both renewable
and non-renewable resources, such as steam reforming of methane, coal gasification, biomass,
biological processes, and water electrolysis, with the most common, until recently, being the
steam reforming of methane. However, the sustainability issues and environmental impacts
from the use of fossil fuels have turned the attention to the use of eco-friendly sources, hence
water electrolysis with the use of renewable energy is currently the dominant process [36,38].
It has been stated that green hydrogen can assist in mitigating the most challenging 1/3
of the global GHG emissions by 2050, because of its potential to be used as an energy storage
system for excess power and as zero-carbon raw material for the chemical fuel industry [6].
It offers ground to decarbonize the most difficult sectors, such as steel, cement, chemicals,
shipping, and aviation [15].
However, hydrogen exhibits challenges, such as its difficult
storage and transport conditions [6].
The production of hydrogen can be classified into main categories, such as black (coal-
based production), brown hydrogen (lignite-based production), grey (from natural gas via
steam reforming), blue (CO2 emissions from grey hydrogen production are captured and
deposited underground) and green (renewable energy based production via electrolysis) [14].
3.1.1
Steam reforming
Steam reforming is the conventional process used to produce the majority of hydrogen
in the world (80-85% of the H2 produced) [39]. There exist several types of reformers, the
conventional steam reformer (externally fired tubular), the autothermal (where fired oxygen
is added in the steam reformer), and the partial oxidation (like autothermal but with no
catalyst) [40]. As an initial step, CH4 (or biogas) has to be desulfurized. Then, the reforming
happens, where steam reacts with CH4 at high temperatures, forming carbon monoxide and
hydrogen mixture gas. The produced CO can be further utilized to react with steam and be
converted to CO2 and more H2 in a water gas shift reactor [37].
Hydrogen production from steam methane reforming is one of the largest endothermic
processes, accounting for around 3% of global CO2 emissions [41]. Normally the heat needed
is supplied to the reaction by the combustion of a mix of natural gas with off-gases from the
process, which produces big amounts of CO2 emissions. Moreover, an industrial reformer
consists typically of an array of more than 100 long tubular reactors inside a big furnace with
gas burners. This configuration, except of the very large size, carries limitations regarding
the thermal conductivity and steep temperature gradients in the catalyst. Recently, a fully
electrical reformer based on direct resistive (ohmic) heating based on a FeCrAl-alloy tube
was developed in the laboratory of DTU Physics at the Technical University of Denmark in
11

N.Anastasia Gkountou
collaboration with Haldor Topsoe A/S. The electric heat source removes the need for fired
heat, significantly reducing the emissions and volume, and improving the thermal limitations,
enabling a more environmentally friendly hydrogen production [41].

3.1.2
Electrolysis
Hydrogen can be electrochemically produced from water (or steam) electrolysis, whereby
water is split into oxygen and hydrogen:
H2O(l or g) + electricity →H2(g) + 1

(1)
The reaction happens on two electrodes separated by an electrolyte. The oxygen evolu-
tion half-cell reaction happens on the anode side, whereas the hydrogen evolution half-cell
reaction on the cathode side [1].
Although very high-purity hydrogen is produced via electrolysis and the direct carbon
emissions are zero, it exhibits high energy consumption. For that reason, the scientific com-
munity is working towards the development of alternative technologies and ways for energy
reduction. Currently, the most common technologies are the proton exchange membrane
electrolyzers (PEMEC), the alkaline electrolyzers (AEC), and the solid oxide electrolyzers
(SOEC) [36].
AEC is the most commercially mature and well-established technology. It uses liquid
electrolytes and operates at around 30-90 °C temperature, and presents some advantages in
structure and cost. Diaphragm and porous nickel-based materials are usually used, and it
needs low investment and operational cost. However, it usually exhibits low operating current
density, and limitations in working at higher pressures [42]. On the contrary, SOEC presents
the highest efficiency because high operating temperature favors the reduction of its electrical
12
==PAGE:26==
N.Anastasia Gkountou
demand due to the favorable thermodynamics. Usually, the electrolyte material is yttria-
stabilized zirconia. SOEC operates in higher temperatures (500-850 °C) and pressures and
thus it uses steam as feed. However, high operating conditions cause the faster deterioration
of the catalytic performance and correspond to better materials and increased cost, hence it
requires further development [42].
PEM commercial electrolyzers operate normally at 30 bar and 20-80 °C using solid acid-
based polymer membranes and pure liquid water. PEM stands out for its higher gas purity
(99.9%), which AEC and SOEC can achieve with extra purification processes, and its higher
hydrogen production rate. However, due to the metals used in the cathode and anode, the
cost is also relatively high, which makes it a more expensive technology than the alkaline
[1, 36, 38]. PEM electrolysis has great advantages, for instance, a simple compact design,
high current density, high efficiency, it can operate in lower temperatures and the proton
exchange membranes present small thickness and low gas permeability [36]. In addition, it is
reported that PEM electrolysis offers a quicker reaction to renewable energy fluctuations [37].
They present higher flexibility and coupling with dynamic power systems and they operate in
lower cell voltages and higher current densities. The power consumption in a megawatt-scale
electrolysis plant ranges between 50-60 kWh/kgH2 [31].

3.2
Biogas to fuels
In many countries, biogas is being upgraded to compressed bioCH4 (CBG) (200 bars),
a more sustainable version of compressed natural gas (CNG), used in cars and buses. In
the last years, liquefied bioCH4 (LBG) (-167 °C) for usage in heavier transports in place of
liquefied natural gas, has also gained some interest [39,44].
Biogas can be used as a raw material for the production of other fuels. After cleaning,
purification, and reforming to syngas, it can lead to the production of jet fuels, diesel and
gasoline (Fischer Tropsch synthesis), methanol, hydrogen, dimethyl ether etc. [19]. Syngas,
or synthesis gas, is a mixed gas containing primarily H2, CO and CO2.
Besides biogas
reforming, it can be produced from natural gas, coal, and biomass with reaction with steam
or oxygen, for example, biomass gasification. It is a valuable raw material as it can be an
intermediate for many gas-to-liquid processes.
3.2.1
Biogas cleaning and upgrading
Besides CH4 and CO2 that biogas primarily consists of, it also can contain small amounts
of N2 (0-3%) originating from the air saturated in the influent of the reactor, vapor water
(5-10% or more in thermophilic temperatures from medium evaporation), O2 (0-1%) from
leakages, hydrogen sulfide H2S (0-10,000 ppmv) produced by sulfate reduction from some
types of bacteria, ammonia NH3 from the hydrolysis of proteins, hydrocarbons (0-200 mg/m3)
or siloxanes (0-40 mg/m3) [45].
Cleaning of biogas is normally the first treatment of biogas and it usually refers to the
processes where sulfur substances and impurities are removed [45]. From all impurities, H2S is
most necessary to be removed due to its toxic and corrosive nature. Various physicochemical
methods have been developed for desulfurization, including chemical oxidation, scrubbing,
biological treatment, precipitation etc. One of the most widely known methods is adsorption,
where natural or synthetic zeolites, activated carbons, or metal oxides are used as adsorbents,
in low temperatures and pressures [46]. Biological air filtration is also a known method,
widely adopted and industrial tested, using biofilters, biotrickling filters or bioscrubbers [47].
The Danish company Biogasclean is a leading company that utilizes biological desulfurization
using the Quick Sludge Removal system, leaving biogas with less than 50-250 ppm of sulfur
and therefore enabling the avoidance of corrosion in the equipment and reducing vastly the
toxicity of the gas [48].
The second treatment is called upgrading and refers to the removal of CO2 in order
to obtain clean bioCH4. The upgraded biogas or "bioCH4" can be stored in the natural
gas infrastructure, as long as it meets certain quality conditions. The specifications for its
composition depend on national regulations, but in most countries, >95% CH4 content is
required [45]. Some common processes to remove the CO2 are pressure swing adsorption
(PSA), water scrubbing, chemical amine absorption, cryogenic distillation and membrane
permeation [39]. One of the most widely implemented is the chemical absorption using amine
solutions of amine scrubbing. One of its advantages is that H2S can be almost completely
absorbed in the amine scrubber [45].
Ammongas, the company acquired by the Danish
company European Energy, is using the absorber-stripper system with amine to upgrade the

3.3
Methanol Synthesis
Synthesis gas (syngas) is typically subjected to a catalytic conversion process to produce
methanol. The typical operation temperature range is between 220 and 280 °C, since above
200 °C is necessary to obtain acceptable reaction rates and temperatures above 300 °C are
affecting the catalysts [31, 50]. Typical catalysts used are Cu/Zn/Al-based catalysts [31].
Regarding the pressure, a typical range for the methanol synthesis is 50-100 bar [31, 50].
Thermodynamically, due to the exothermic nature of the reaction, methanol synthesis is
favored by low temperatures and high pressures.
Compared to the conventional process where CO is the main carbon source, the CO2
hydrogenation process generates less heat but it is still highly exothermic [51], therefore a
challenge is the removal of this heat and the cooling of the system or its heat integration
in the system [39]. Another challenge it presents is that water is formed as a by-product,
having negative effects on the catalyst [52]. Also, CO2 to methanol reaction is kinetically
limited to 15-25% conversion [51]. Therefore, to increase the conversion, it is necessary to
recirculate unreacted syngas back to the reactor to shift the balance towards the methanol
production [39].
Many reactors have been commercially developed, with differences in the reaction heat
handling, pressure drop and cost. They can be divided in two types in terms of the heat
removal method, adiabatic and isothermal (internal cooling) reactors.
Typically, for an
adiabatic reactor with multiple catalyst beds, gas quenching is normally used to lower the
temperature of the gas after each bed. The isothermal reactors typically integrate plates or
tubes for heat removal, for example, a shell-tube structure with boiling water in the shell
and the catalyst loaded in the tube. Such configuration provides higher efficiency in the heat
transfer process but exhibits increased capital costs [52].

Biogas upgrading and cleaning
Biogas is assumed to emanate from a green origin, specifically produced by anaerobic
digestion in biogas facilities using manure and other types of waste, for instance, food waste.
The composition of biogas is presumed to be 60% CH4 and 40 % CO2, as the average typical.
It is assumed that the biogas is desulfurized and cleaned a priori.
The upgrading system mainly consists of an absorber and a stripper. In the absorber,
the biogas enters from the bottom of the tank (at 1-2 bars), while the amine solution flows in
a counter-current way from the top. During this process, CO2 gets bonded to the solvent by
an exothermic reaction, and afterward, the solvent is headed to the stripping column to be
regenerated. The stripper operates at 1.5-3 bars and contains a boiler for heating at 120-160
°C. The purpose of heating is to break the bonds formed in the absorber stage in order to
regenerate the solvent, but also to create a vapor stream acting as a stripping fluid. In the
end, the stream that contains the CO2 is cooled in a condenser, where the condensate returns
to the stripper and the CO2 gets released. The final CH4 content using this technology can
reach >99% purity and the CH4 loss can be lower than 0.1% [45]. In Figure 10 a typical
configuration of such system is depicted.

The choice of temperature and steam/carbon (S/C) ratio affects the equilibrium. Ac-
cording to relevant literature, the H2 fraction stabilizes nearly at 850-1,000 °C while CH4
decreases until zero. Below 600 °C, CO2 increases indicating a preference for the water gas

the reactant side) favors the H2 production (on the product side). However, for a S/C ratio
above 3 the gain becomes less substantial due to excessive water consumption [55].

on the effect of the ratio on the CH4 content after the reformer and the CH4 present in the
stream entering the methanol synthesis reactor. The reason is that CH4 acts as an inert in
the methanol synthesis loop, adding up to the total flow making the reactor volume bigger,
but also increasing the recirculation flow, and increasing the possibility of prohibiting the
actual reactants from being fully recovered, and sent back to the reactor. In the sensitivity
analysis, the effect of temperature is also studied [Figure 11].

should be chosen so the conversion of CH4 is adequate but not too much water stays in the
system, as it influences the equilibrium of the other reactions in the next steps of the process.

converted in the reformer, and so less CH4 content reaches the methanol synthesis reactor.

recirculation stream to be returned to the steam reformer, because of the high concentration
in CH4 in the Mixed and Bio system, instead of the methanol reactor, and therefore the
reactor’s size would be reduced, but with a possible cost of less methanol production.

4.6
Methanol purification
Continuing from the methanol synthesis loop, the purification of methanol is required.
For this part, distillation columns are normally used to perform the separation between
the different compounds with respect to their different boiling points.
When modeling,
two fractional distillation columns are used (RadFrac). In the first column (stabilizer), the
purpose is to separate the CO2 left and remove all the possible remaining gases after the loop
from the liquid components, which is a mixture of water and methanol, otherwise referred
as raw methanol. A partial vapor condenser at a pressure of 1 bar and a kettle as a reboiler
are utilized. The gases, as the lighter components, are obtained on the top of the column,
while raw methanol is as liquid at the bottom. A design spec is constructed to calculate the
bottom rate in the column so that the condenser operates at 35°C, in order for cooling water
to be a possible utility. The reflux ratio is established at 1 due to the minimal distillate rate.
The binary liquid mixture afterward is headed to the second distillation column, where
water, as the heaviest compound, sits at the bottom, whereas pure methanol (99.9% mole
fraction) is obtained on the top of the column at conditions of 1 bar and 64°C with a
total condenser. These conditions are valid for the methanol to be stored as a liquid in
atmospheric conditions so no further processing is necessary. To ensure a good separation,
a design specification is created to calculate the distillate rate so that almost no methanol
(0.01%) is found in the bottom rate, and a specification for calculating the reflux ratio so
that maximum 0.1% of water is found on the top stream.

5.1
Energy flows
All chemical reactions include energy, which is used to break/create bonds or is stored
to form new products, depending on whether the reaction is endothermic or exothermic.
According to the law of conservation of energy, energy only changes forms and does not
disappear, as is for example the change from chemical energy to heat.
The chemical energy flows of the systems are represented using Sankey diagrams in
Figure 13 For the systems to be comparable, all flows are normalized by dividing with the
energy output of the system, meaning the energy of the methanol product, considering it to
be 100 MW, or else, 100%. The chemical energy flows have been calculated under the low
heating value basis. In the diagrams, the losses of energy are depicted during the stages of
the process where either chemical reactions are happening, so energy is absorbed or released
in the form of heat, or where stream flows with calorific value are removed or added from
and to the system.
In the PtX system, the electricity consumption of the electrolyzer constitutes the en-
ergy input in the process. Some of this energy gets realised as waste heat during electrolysis
(around 22% of the electrical consumption), and some is lost due to water evaporation
(around 13% of the electrical consumption), which is basically the latent heat of vapor-
ization of water. The rest gets converted to hydrogen, which then gets mixed with CO2.
Approximately 14% of the energy of the final methanol product is converted to heat in the
methanol synthesis stage, due to the exothermic nature of the methanol synthesis reaction,
while 2.3% of the chemical energy is lost from the purge stream, mostly in the form of H2.
During the distillation stage, the 1.7% losses of the energy of the final methanol product
are attributed to the separation of the remaining gases in the stabilizing column, mostly in
the form of methanol (CH3OH) as CO2 has no calorific value. It is important to mention
that even though the energy in biogas, hence bioCH4 is not used for methanol production,
bioCH4 is not waste, but a by-product, and so this amount of energy is not considered lost.
In the Mixed system, the energy input is the biogas, and more specifically its bioCH4
content, together with the electricity. Similarly, as before, only some of the electricity is
converted to hydrogen, as some will be lost in form of heat and water evaporation (around
22% and 13% of the power input). Steam reforming is an endothermic process, therefore
more energy in the form of electricity is added to the system. The amount of energy from
syngas entering the methanol synthesis loop is higher than the PtX, due to the presence
of additional CO created in the reforming stage. The loss in the form of heat during the
methanol synthesis is depicted as 18.4% of the methanol product, which appears higher than
the one of the PtX system, due to the presence of CO, the conversion of which to CH3OH
releases more heat. The energy loss in purge is around 6%, mostly attributed to H2, but
the difference with PtX is related to the presence of CH4, which did not react during steam
reforming and acts as inert in methanol synthesis. Finally, a 4% is lost in the distillation from
the stabilizing column, mostly attributed to CH3OH and CH4 (being again the difference
from PtX), since CO2 has no heating value.

7.1
Private economic approach
The private economic approach is in reality an investment analysis, which determines the
feasibility of a project from the side of a private investor. The cash flows that are normally
accounted for are the capital expenditures, the operating costs, and the revenues from the
sales of the products. For accuracy reasons, inflation rates, tax rates and discount rates are
included by the economists in such analysis. For simplicity reasons and ease of understanding,
in the current study inflation and tax rates are not included, and the considered cash flows
include the operational costs from biogas and electricity and the revenues/costs from CH4
with a discount rate of 6% and 20 years of evaluation period starting from 2025.
The private economic is separated into three phases. In phase I, the analysis focuses on
the energy needs of the systems, ignoring the capital expenditures of the plants. Full load
operation for all systems and steady electricity prices per year are assumed. In phase II, the
capital costs are also taken into account, and all systems are still operating at full load and
with steady electricity prices per year. In Phase III, after the influence of different capacity
factors for the PtX system is investigated, the PtX system operates at its optimum capacity
factor with applied electricity price reductions per year and adjusted capital expenditures.
7.2
Socio-economic approach
Socio-economic analyses are normally undertaken in order to identify costs and benefits
for society as a whole, and create a picture for decision-making. However, they do not follow
a specific methodology, and are difficult to standardise. The focus of this analysis is on how
the different projects are embedded in a societal context and related to social welfare.
Assuming that markets work efficiently, then the market prices reflect the social cost of
goods and services. Some of the related costs or benefits fall on third parties, the so-called
externalities, as for example the environmental impacts. For the purpose of this study, it
is assumed that the major societal flows are the CO2eq emissions displacement from the
production and use of methanol and bioCH4, and the utilization of gas from the grid, which
currently is a mixture of bioCH4 and natural gas as explained in subsubsection 2.6.2. The
societal impact of CO2eq emissions can be reflected and monetized through its market value
via the Emissions Trading System (ETS) for each country subsubsection 2.2.1. Similarly,
efficient electricity markets are assumed and that the market prices for electricity, CH4 and
methanol reflect the real costs to society. Additionally, the variable mix during the next years
of bioCH4 and natural gas is taken into consideration. Capital costs are also relevant. The
discount rate is set again at 6% for the same evaluation period as in the private economics.

8.5
Influence of different capacity factors
Having seen the results of Phases I and II, one might argue that the comparison is not
entirely fair, as Power-to-X systems typically have the ability to not operate at full load due
to the fluctuating nature of renewable energy sources, such as wind and solar. Additionally,
these systems are usually optimized through the electrolyzer to operate at lower electricity
prices and thus at lower costs.
Therefore, it is necessary to investigate the influence of
different capacity factors on the cash flows.
In this analysis, to maintain a steady final methanol production across all systems at
130,000 tons/year, varying the capacity factors for the Power-to-X system means scaling
the plant’s capacity accordingly. For example, a plant operating for half the annual hours
would need to be scaled to a double capacity to deliver the same amount of methanol yearly.
Consequently, if the number of operating hours changes, the capital cost of the plant also
changes. This approach is preferable in this case, instead of varying the methanol amount
produced, as in this report the subject is the comparison of the systems and therefore the
normalization has been done regarding a steady methanol output.

For operation in higher capacity factors, the slope of the line between the Bio and
PtX system would shift in favor of the Bio system, due to the lower relative reductions, and
therefore the increase in electricity prices for the PtX. This effect continues up to a "tipping"
point, below which the 2025 electricity price becomes sufficiently low that the CAPEX
decrease becomes pronounced and outweighs the increase in electricity prices. Hence, below
that point, the slope would shift in favor of the PtX system. Looking at it the other way
around, moving to lower capacity factors would mirror the effects in the opposite direction,
where above the “tipping” point the slope of the line between the Bio and PtX system would
increase in favor of the PtX system, due to the higher relative reductions in electricity prices,
however, below the point the 2025 electricity price becomes sufficiently low that the CAPEX
increase overcomes the decrease in electricity prices, hence making PtX less profitable.
To visualize better the concept one can look at Figure 31, where the comparison of
PtX operating at a capacity factor of 0.5 versus 0.6 can be seen. With purple dot lines
the equality lines for capacity factor 0.5 are depicted, while with white lines the case of
capacity factor 0.6 is shown.
A horizontal line separates the interfaces of PtX and Bio
system, and PtX and Mixed system, respectively. Looking at the “zoomed-in” part of the
graph and the intersection of PtX and Bio system, the “tipping point" is marked, meaning
the point where the electricity prices and the CAPEX change have an equal effect on the
LCoM, corresponding to a specific 2025 electricity price (around 85 EUR/MWh in this case).
Above the “tipping” point, the equality line has been moved against the PtX system with
the increase of the capacity factor, where the area of change is represented by the number
1. Below the point, the area of change is represented with the number 2.
In other words, there are areas in which lower capacity factors influence positively PtX
and higher capacity factors have a negative impact on its profitability. At this point, the
correlation with the analysis behind finding the optimal capacity factor, where the combi-
nation of the relative reductions and the CAPEX results to the best possible solution with
minimum levelized cost of methanol, becomes evident. For lower capacity factors, as CAPEX
increases, the "tipping point" would move upwards, as there is going to be a need for higher
starting electricity prices to compensate for the change. Hence, moving away from the op-
timal point to lower capacity factors, it would need higher and higher electricity prices for
PtX to become profitable, a system that would not be optimized.
The slope between the Mixed and PtX system would basically be formulated following
the same reasoning, but influenced by the CAPEX and the electricity needs deference be-
tween the Mixed with the PtX system instead. In the present case, the interface would be

The displacement achieved in all systems is considered as revenue.

Conclusions
This report comprehensively investigates the feasibility and efficiency of three methanol
production systems: "PtX", "Mixed", and "Bio". Each system’s approach to producing
syngas prior to methanol synthesis significantly influences their efficiency, cost, and adapt-
ability.
The PtX system, which utilizes CO2 from biogas upgrading and hydrogen from elec-
trolysis, achieves the highest carbon efficiency at 93%, considering the methane content of
biogas as a by-product. However, the PtX system also incurs the highest power consumption,
leading to the lowest total efficiency among the three systems due to the large electrolyzer
required. Simulations using Aspen Plus and subsequent Sankey diagrams reveal that the
dominant energy losses are from the electrolyzer (waste heat and evaporation loss), but also
the heat from the exothermic methanol reactor, while the carbon losses appear from the
purge and the gases separation in the stabilizer column.
The cost assessment, divided into private economic and socio-economic perspectives,
highlights the varied impacts of fluctuating electricity, methane, and CO2 prices on the
levelized cost of methanol (LCoM) and the socioeconomic benefit over a 20-year period.
CAPEX calculation shows that the Bio system’s CAPEX is near 75% less, and the Mixed
system’s CAPEX is above 50% less, than the one of PtX, respectively. The private economic
approach depicted that normally, the PtX system occupies the area with low electricity prices
and high methane prices, the Bio is found at the opposite side, and the Mixed system is
found somewhere in between. PtX system is independent of the CH4 price, and the CAPEX
contribution to LCoM is smaller compared to the rest of the operational flows.
As the next layer of complexity, the benefit of the PtX system to adapt to fluctuations in
electricity prices by operating in lower capacity factors has been taken into consideration by
estimating its optimal capacity factor, assuming that it remains steady throughout the years.
The CAPEX for each capacity factor is estimated using scaling effects, and the reductions in
electricity prices are taken into account. Observing the trends, it does not make sense to run
in high capacity factors, where the CAPEX contribution to the cost is low but the electricity
prices go exponentially high. On the other hand, in low capacity factors, electricity prices are
reduced but the CAPEX increase is extreme. The optimal capacity factor is found to be at
60% full load, although it is observed that variable intermediate operational strategies give
remarkably similar results due to the market dynamics. The optimal factor is utilized for the
PtX, while Mixed and Bio always operate at full load. To be able to implement reductions
in the electricity prices range that is examined, the relative reductions to the average price
of 2025 of the available set of prices are calculated. That way a trend is established, that
could be implemented in other environments as well, hypothesizing that the price trends
follow the same behavior.
The results show two equality lines, one between the PtX and the Mixed and one
between the PtX and the Bio, with the LCoM ranging between 0 and 1,300 EUR/ton. PtX
appears always profitable for methane prices above 1,150 EUR/ton for a combination of
solar PV and onshore wind, and when offshore is included methane price needs to be above
1,450 EUR/ton. Additionally, investigating the moving of the equality lines when changing
67
==PAGE:81==
N.Anastasia Gkountou
capacity factors for the PtX system, it is observed that there is a tipping point, above which
when increasing the factor results in the shift of the equality line against the PtX, due to
the lower relative reductions in electricity prices, however below it the line shifts in favor
of the PtX due to the CAPEX decrease. The tipping point constitutes the equality of the
influence of the electricity prices increase and the CAPEX decrease to the LCoM of the
PtX. In the socio-economic approach, which includes the CO2 avoided emissions, PtX is the
most socio-economically advantageous for a combination of solar, onshore, and offshore wind
technologies for CO2 values of 70 EUR/ton and above.
Regional analyses for Lithuania, Spain, and Sweden indicate significant differences in
optimal capacity factors based on their local 2025 average power prices. Lithuania’s high
power price leads to a lower capacity factor of 0.4, Spain’s intermediate price to 0.7, and
Sweden’s low price to 0.8. Denmark presents electricity prices not as low as Sweden but
not as high as Lithuania, and the prices of methane are low enough, hence it might be cost-
effective to upcycle it and incorporate it into industrial processes. Remarkably, Sweden’s
socioeconomic analysis showed the PtX system dominant throughout the whole price range
examined, a fact that is mostly attributed to the high methane price considered for Sweden.
These findings contribute to a deeper understanding of the economic and environmental
implications of methanol production systems, supporting strategic decision-making regarding
the feasibility, and techno and socio economics for green methanol production implemented
in various regional contexts.

Limitations and future perspectives
Process simulators are essential tools for replicating the behavior of chemical and electri-
cal systems, however, they go together with significant assumptions and limitations. Aspen
Process Simulator is utilized to derive the mass and energy balances, employing equations
of state and thermodynamic engineering packages. These packages are crucial and merit
further investigation to determine which provides the most accurate approach. However,
this report does not include investigation into specific thermodynamic property models such
as Soave-Redlich-Kwong (SRK) or Non-Random Two Liquid method (NRTL). Additionally,
the pressures and temperatures applied in various units are based on literature review and
may not reflect the most optimized choices.
Regarding the CAPEX calculation, it is primarily focused on the major components
utilizing average values sourced from diverse literature. For unmodeled units like biogas
cleaning and upgrading, and units that are not commercial yet, such as the electrical re-
former, average capital and operational costs of entire units are adopted from sources to
bridge gaps, under the assumption of uniform configurations. Furthermore, the CAPEX
scaling effects used are obtained from average literature, especially assuming a linear price
scaling for the electrolyzers, which is most likely not corresponding to reality as technological
developments are expected in the future. It is important to recognize that this method in-
troduces potential uncertainties and inaccuracies in costing. If one obtains better knowledge
and understanding, one could incorporate more realistic prices, equipment costs and scaling
effects.
Moving on to the cost assessment, significant uncertainties accompany the prediction of
electricity prices, even if they are produced by Energinet. Also, the need for forecasted data
on methane, CO2 prices and the biomethane share in the grid status for the evaluation period
and for all the different countries consist of important limitations of this study. Therefore,
one with accessibility to more accurate and precise sets of data could improve these aspects.
Lastly, one of the known issues of the private economic and socioeconomic approaches is
determining the optimal capacity factor for the PtX system by assuming it is unchangeable
throughout the evaluation period, whilst if power prices exhibit a fluctuating trend, it would
make sense to adapt the capacity factors over the years. One way to advance the modeling
already presented is to incorporate the fact that PtX system can be run dynamically on a
yearly basis, hence if one wants to tackle high power prices one would run in low capacity
factors and when the market stumbles into low prices one could run in high capacity factors.
This concept, due to practical limitations of time could not be accommodated in this report,
but it could be a good place to start for future continuation of this work.

Appendix B-Supplementary analyses
12.7
Heat integration
Heat integration is a method for heat recovery. It is based on the combination of hot
streams with cold streams to mutually satisfy their heating and cooling requirements. In
this study heat integration is performed using Aspen Plus Energy Analyser. The computed
composite curves can be seen in Figure 58.
Pinch analysis is performed by the program prior to the heat integration, where the
minimum temperature difference, ∆Tmin, has to be set. In a heat exchanger, heat is trans-
ferred from the hot stream to the cold stream through the surface. Theoretically, the closer
the temperature of both streams, the more efficient the heat transfer between the streams.
In reality, equipment and environment limitations exist, that make a ∆Tmin = 0°C unreal-
istic. Therefore, ∆Tmin is set to be equal to 10°C, which is common practice. Overall, the
pinch analysis helps identify the minimum cold and hot utility required. Thus, the rest of
the heating and cooling demand of the streams can be achieved through a heat exchanger
network designed for maximum energy recovery.
Composite curves are T/H diagrams (temperature/heat diagrams) used to visualize cold
and hot streams and potential heat transfer between them. The process data is represented as
a function of heat load (product of specific enthalpy and mass flow rate) against temperature.
These data are combined for all the streams in the plant, one for all hot streams (releasing
heat) and one for all cold streams (requiring heat). Therefore, the red line represents the
streams that need cooling, and the blue line the streams that need heating. The point of
closest approach between the hot and cold composite curves is the "pinch point".
(a)
82
==PAGE:96==
N.Anastasia Gkountou

(c)

Bio.
The result of the analysis shows that all the scenarios do not exhibit a pinch point, but
the so-called "threshold point", meaning that there is demand for only heating or cooling for
this choice of ∆Tmin. In these cases, only cooling is required, equal to 23.13 MW for the PtX,
65.63 MW for the mixed, and 37 MW for the Bio system. For the PtX, the main cooling
needs emanate from the reactor, the heat exchanger after the reactor, and the condenser of

To calculate the avoided CO2 emissions, or else the emission savings, the CO2 emissions
from the methanol and bioCH4 production have to be estimated and get subtracted from
the total emissions of the corresponding amount of fossil fuel and natural gas:
Avoided emissions from CH3OH :
CO2av.CH3OH = CO2fossilfuel −CO2CH3OH
(27)
Avoided emissions from bioCH4:
CO2av.bioCH4 = CO2naturalgas −CO2bioCH4
(28)
where:
CO2av.CH3OH the emission savings attributed to methanol in tons/y
CO2av.bioCH4 the emission savings attributed to biomethane in tons/y
CO2fossilfuel the emissions from the fossil fuel that would be used instead of methanol in
tons/y
CO2naturalgas the emissions from the natural gas that would be put in the gas grid in tons/y
CO2CH3OH the emissions from methanol production in tons/y
CO2bioCH4 the emissions from biomethane production in tons/y
According to relevant theory [79], the emissions of a fuel include:
E = eec + epp + etd + eu −eccs
(29)
where:
E the emissions of the fuel in interest in tons/y
eec the emissions from the extraction of raw materials in tons/y
epp the emissions from production and processing in tons/y
etd emissions from transport and distribution in tons/y
eccs emissions from the use in tons/y
eccs emissions savings from carbon capture and storage (sequestration) in tons/y
For the purposes of this analysis, as the focus does not lay on the performance of a
full life cycle assessment, and since detailed data are not available for the full life cycle, the
emissions are roughly estimated by excluding the contributions of the construction of the
plant and the transport and distribution of the fuel, and mainly focusing on the input flows
of the processes. Sequestration is also not relevant for the cases under investigation.
The relevant assumptions considered for the emission estimation are the following:
