Abstract
The worlds final energy consumption during 2016 was to 51 % heating and cooling and
only 9.8 % of that was supplied from renewable sources. The cumulative collector area
within large-scale solar thermal has gone from 500 000 to 2 300 000 m2 from 2010 to 2019
worldwide with Denmark showing, by far, the greatest development and market maturity.
For solar district heating a concept of combining flat plate collectors and parabolic trough
collectors in series has been validated in Taars, Denmark. The concept, here referred to
as Hybrid, let both collectors operate in the temperature range where they excel, the flat
plate collector in the lower and the parabolic trough in the higher. In Taars, the concept
enabled a 13 % increase in annual flat plate collector output due to lowered temperature.
Provided solar thermal continue to increase in plant and market size, even small relative
performance increase of the plants will have large absolute value.
This study has further increased the knowledge about Hybrid plants and how they can
enhance the performance of solar thermal with the learning outcomes from Taars as a
starting point. It aimed to generalise the results with one goal being to develop a generic
TRNSYS model for simulation and design of Hybrid plants. The other goal was to bench-
mark the Absolicon T160 parabolic trough collector against flat plate collectors and provide
relevant technology comparisons.
The study display parametric studies of the two collector types giving general insight into
how seasonal profiles and annual output are affected by collector tilt, azimuth and opera-
tion temperature. Within the thesis, three quasi-dynamic TRNSYS models have been de-
veloped. (1) A Separate collectors model used to simultaneously generate insights about
the dynamic behaviour, performance and control of the two collector types by simulating
collector performance with measured 1 min-resolution solar radiation data from the Tech-
nical University of Denmark. (2) A Hybrid model connecting single- and double-glazed
flat plate collectors in series with a parabolic trough collector to study control strategies
and compare performance and behaviour of the proposed Hybrid concept in relation to
reference flat plate collector and parabolic trough collector systems. Finally, (3) a generic
model combining the Hybrid model with a stratified tank and load matching for use when
assessing specific customer cases. The model takes measured load and return tempera-
ture profiles of a district heating grid as inputs and gives the user the possibility to evaluate
different system design (area ratios, storage size, collector orientations etc.) against the
requirements of the particular customer case. The model is prepared for using local stan-
dard climate data from all over the world.
The findings are illustrated graphically by seasonal overviews and sample days that il-
lustrate and compare the behaviour of different solar collector systems. Among the key
findings one may note that for the studied climate and location (Copenhagen, Denmark):
• The parabolic trough collector oriented with east/west azimuth gave the highest win-
ter output, almost three times the flat plate collector and the south azimuth parabolic
trough. The annual output was 12 % lower for the east/west than the south azimuth
parabolic trough collector.
• Below 70 ◦C operating temperature the double-glazed flat plate collector excelled,
above it the north/south azimuth parabolic trough did.
• For heating from 50 to 95 ◦C, the reference south azimuth parabolic trough collector
had 19 % better annual output than the reference flat plate collectors but only 0.4 %
better than a proposed Hybrid with 30 % flat plates.
iii
==PAGE:6==
• Boosting a flat plate collector field by a resulting collector area of 30 % south az-
imuth parabolic troughs covered 60 % of the annual output gap between flat plate
and parabolic trough collectors. Correspondingly, 50 % covered 84 % of the gap
indicating clear system benefits in combining the two.
• Both collector types need direct radiation to produce high temperatures. In overcast
conditions, none produced temperatures above 75 ◦C even on a summer day with
18 ◦C ambient temperature.
• The reference flat plate collector system showed higher peak outputs needing about
30 % more pipe and pumping capacity than the other two.
• The proposed control strategy delivered over 90 % of the annual solar energy within
10 ◦C from the set point for all three systems, flat plate collectors, parabolic trough
collectors and the Hybrid.
The developed TRNSYS models can be used for feasibility studies and design of new
Hybrid plants by Absolicon and its industrial partners. The detailed simulations of stor-
age utilisation and load matching are crucial for deliberate decisions on technology and
investments.

Introduction
Global attention towards climate protection is a strong market driver for all renewable
energy systems. All sectors need to reduce their emissions and as apparent in the latest
Global Status Report from REN21 [1], the demand for heating and cooling is a great
share that is still far from being renewable. Figure 1.1 shows that 51 % of the worlds final
energy usage is heating and cooling and only 9.5 % of that share comes from renewable
sources. Electrical energy is only 17 % of the final energy consumption, out of which 26
% is renewable. The competition for biomass is growing as more sectors intend on using
it to reach its climate targets. This is expected to challenge the extensive dependency
of biomass that is present in many district heating systems [2]. In Sweden, attention has
already been put on occasions where lack of biomass has led to increased emissions in
the district heating sector [3]. It is a global challenge to decarbonise heating demand using
as little biomass as possible so that biomass can be used for biofuels to cover transport
sector demand and replace plastics. At the same time, adding electricity demand by
electrification of thermal energy will not by itself make it renewable since not even the
existing electricity demand is renewable. As the worlds heat usage changes towards
sustainable energy sources, solar heat can play an important role by reducing biomass
dependency during the summer months so that more biomass is available for use during
wintertime and for other applications.
Figure 1.1: The worlds final energy usage 2016 divided by sectors. Shares of renewable
energy are also shown. Source: REN21 Renewables 2019 Global Status Report [1]
So far, the most extensive usage of solar thermal in large scale has been in district heating.
Denmark is the global leader of the solar district heating (SDH) market [4] and it now has
an installed capacity of 1.1 GWth and a collector area of almost 1.6 million m2 divided on
a total of 120 systems according to the Danish consultancy PlanEnergi [5], see fig. 1.2.
From the figure one can tell there is a trend towards large scale systems since installed
aperture area grows faster than number of plants. In 2018, Denmark alone accounted
for 54 % of the added collector area worldwide excluding parabolic troughs [6]. Outside
Europe, China was dominating with about 84 % of the area addition in large-scale systems
[6].

Figure 1.2: Market development of solar district heating in Denmark 2009-2019. Installed
collector aperture area and number of plants in operating and upcoming plants. Source:

The majority of the danish plants consist of mass produced large flat plate collectors (FPC)
[4] and their operation temperature ranges from about 40 ◦C to 95 ◦C [7]. Above 70-95 ◦C
the efficiency of flat plate collectors drop quickly whereas concentrating collectors main-
tain its efficiency even at higher temperature.
Absolicon has developed the only concentrating solar collector that is certified with Solar
Keymark [1]. It is a small parabolic trough collector (PTC) called T160, which deliver heat
up to 160 ◦C. Traditionally, concentrating solar technology has been more expensive than
FPC but thanks to highly automatised production were the collector is built and tested in
the factory, as opposed on site as other concentrators, the Absolicon PTC technology can
be delivered at costs that are similar to those of FPC, according to Absolicon.
Concentrating collectors have the possibility to defocus if, at a certain time, the potential
output from the collectors is greater than the demand and there is no storage, or the
storage is full. As opposed to FPC fields, where the operator need to plan and cool down
the storage if there is more sunny days in a row than the plant was designed for, PTC fields
give the flexibility of shutting off the plant by stopping the solar tracking [4]. FPC cannot be
turned off without the system boiling and having to be refilled. Defocus possibility reduce
planning effort for plant management and allow for smaller storage choices in design
phase (less margin for unusually sunny periods needed). For a plant in operation its
storage can be utilised more efficiently in a system with PTC since the storage is allowed
to get closer to full. In an industry setting were the load side need to be matched with very
high accuracy, the flexibility of PTC systems is invaluable since parts of the field can then
operate independently to match the load.
For applications that need heating from below to above 60 ◦C one can connect FPC and
PTC in series to allow for higher system efficiency. This concept has been validated in dis-
trict heating in Taars, Denmark, where a hybrid collector field of 5960 m2 FPC and 4039 m2
PTC is in operation since 2015 [7]. It is based on the idea of increasing performance by
2

letting different collector technologies work in the temperature range where they excel:
FPC collectors in the lower temperature span and PTC in the higher span. Research
on the Taars field show that due to lower temperatures in FPC field, the FPC produced
60 kW h m−2 more, an increase with 13 % and totalling 510 kW h m−2, than plants with
only FPC [7]. The concept of combining FPC and PTC will in this work be referred to as
FPC/PTC Hybrid or simply Hybrid.
Solar district heating is an application where the temperatures are in favour of the Hybrid
concept. Average Swedish district heating temperatures are 86/47 ◦C (Supply/Return)
[8], in Denmark grid temperatures are lower but to utilise storages all the way up to the
boiling point, higher temperatures are still needed.
The optimal collector area ratio (FPC/PTC) in a Hybrid plant is highly affected by the in-
vestment costs of the collector types and their inter-mutual quality. It is also very project
specific and affected by parameters such as local climate, demanded temperatures, land
cost, demand profile and target fraction of the demand to be covered by solar. The cho-
sen area ratio affect the control of the Hybrid and the temperature rise ratio between the
collector types, which is varying with the season and within a day. Design and operation
of different collector types in the same system requires insight into the characteristics of
the solar collectors and how they interact for efficient usage of the solar energy at every
time.
1.1
Scope, goals & aim
The scope of this project was to build a generic TRNSYS model for investigating further
how Hybrid plants can enhance the system energy production of SDH plants. The model
consist of the current state-of-the-art configuration with single- and double-glazed FPC
collectors in series and in addition there is a possibility to run PTC in the end of the series.
Since the performance relation between the collector types vary greatly within days and
throughout the year, the model is designed with possibilities to run only parts of the field
and bypass others. For example, PTC operates much earlier in the mornings so then the
FPC loop can be bypassed. The TRNSYS model can be used to help answer questions
such as: What is the optimal area ratio between PTC and FPC in a Hybrid plant for a
specific district heating grid? It can also be used to get an overview and comparison of
FPC and PTC collectors. The present TRNSYS model, the steps towards it and the results
from it also aims to build general knowledge and methods connected to hybrid systems
that can be utilised to build SDH plants with better performance.
The goal of this study was two-folded: (1) to develop a generic TRNSYS model for
FPC/PTC Hybrid systems and (2) give a benchmark of Absolicon PTC technology against
state-of-the-art FPC and display relevant technology comparisons.
This project aims to further increase the knowledge about Hybrid fields in the solar industry
with the learning outcomes from Taars, Denmark, as a starting point. It aims to promote
industrial research collaboration that can help suppliers of FPC and PTC to build solar
thermal plants with increased system efficiency at reduced cost. It aims to generalise the
results from Taars and build methods for simulation and optimisation connected to hybrid
systems. Methods that can be utilised directly by Absolicon and its industrial partners. It
will focus on existing commercial technologies for flat plate collectors and Absolicon T160
PTC. Evacuated tube collectors will not be studied, and neither will other PTC models.

2.2.1
Flat plate collectors
In large scale solar thermal fields for district heating, the vast majority of the collectors are
flat plate collectors (FPC) [25] which have seen great increase in efficiency and reduction
of costs over the last years [26]. They typically consist of an absorber plate of aluminium
behind which welded copper tube manifolds carry a propylene glycol based water solution
that is heated by the solar radiation [26]. Covering the collector is an anti-reflective treated
glass and it is insulated in the back and on the sides and mounted in an aluminium frame
[25]. An illustration of a FPC composition for the Sunrain collector FPC1200D is displayed
in fig. 2.7.
Chapter 2. Background
9
==PAGE:28==
Figure 2.7: Example of flat plate collector components composition. Source: Sunrain [27]
The flow distribution of the collector vary between brands and can be divided into two
main groups, harp manifolds (fig. 2.7) and meanders. In large scale collectors, having
manifolds reduce pressure drop by several parallel absorbers [26]. The harp manifolds
can further be divided into horizontal and vertical manifolds with different configurations of
the inlet and outlet of the collector. For district heating purposes, the collectors are typically
larger, 10-15 m2, compared to the ones used in small scale solar heating applications to
speed up installations [26]. To minimise heat loss in the collector, both double glazing and
an anti-convection barrier has been used [28], [29]. Double-glazed collectors on average
have about 20 % higher yield at a mean operating temperature of 75 ◦C [28]. The extra
glazing however reduce the optical efficiency of the collector. The current state-of-the-art
SDH when return temperature is 40-60 ◦C is therefore to combine the two in series such
that the single-glazed collector operates at a relatively lower temperature and the double-
glazed at a higher, giving the best of two worlds. With very high or low return temperature
(outside 40-60 ◦C), one would only go for double-glazed (high) or single-glazed (low) [30].
Since flat plates are mounted in a fix position, they heat up as soon as there is sun. This
creates a requirement on the system to always be able to cool the collectors to prevent
boiling. In low solar fraction SDH there is always available return flow in the grid to keep
the collector temperature down. When the solar fraction gets higher, so that the solar plant
produces more energy than the load at a given time, energy can be stored in hot water
storages. However, if the storages get full, the operators need to cool down the storage
at night by using the solar collectors as radiators and spill energy, so that there is room
for more energy the next day. This is a very important feature in the warm periods when
district heating consumption is low. With the more efficient collectors of today, 25-30 % of
a day’s production can be cooled away at night [19], earlier that number was higher.
2.2.2
Concentrating collectors
In recent years, several benefits of using concentrators in SDH has been identified [31]
and large parabolic trough plants have been installed for district heating and large build-
ings in Denmark, China and Mexico [1]. In Denmark, which is known for low district heat-
ing temperatures in general [8], typical supply temperatures are still 85-95 ◦C [31]. Since
solar collectors deliver to the grid via heat exchanger, an additional 3-4 K are needed.

In systems with hot water storages, the needed storage volume is reduced if the storage
has a higher maximum temperature (larger temperature difference gives larger storage
capacity per volume). Flat plate collectors has an efficiency curve that drops fast in this
temperature range whereas concentrating collectors maintain their efficiency better [31].
As displayed in fig. 2.8 there are several types of concentrating technology. They can be
divided into line-focus concentrators and point-focus concentrators where the parabolic

solar power towers focus on a point. An additional type of double-axis tracking point
focus collector is developed by Danish Heliac and uses a flat lens that focus the light on
a receiver behind the lens [32]. In 2018 parabolic troughs dominated the Concentrated
Solar Power (CSP) market being 70 % of the total capacity addition [1].

be analysed here. The Absolicon T160 consist of a parabolic silver reflector that focus

The tracking increase the utilisation of the solar energy by tracking the sun from sunrise to

the demand is low and the storage is full. There is no need for the night cooling operation
used for FPC and the managing effort for planning the operation can hence decrease.
Overproduction is therefore less of an issue than for FPC.
2.2.3
FPC/PTC Hybrids
In Taars, Denmark, an Energy Technology Development and Demonstration Program
(EUDP) project was realised to demonstrate the how 5960 m2 FPC and 4039 m2 PTC
can be combined in series in order to increase system performance and lower costs of
solar district heating when delivering relatively high temperatures [34]. The system layout
is illustrated in fig. 2.10 where one can note that the FPC has their own glycol stream and
is transferring its heat through a heat exchanger whereas the PTC is heating the boiler and
tank water directly [7]. The PTC were assumed more expensive but much more efficient
at high temperatures, the FPC on the other hand was cheaper and more efficient at lower
temperatures [35], [36]. So by letting different collector technologies work in the tempera-
ture range where they excel, the system performance would increase. The resulting lower
temperatures in the FPC increased the FPC heat output by 60 kW h m−2, an increase with
13 % and totalling 510 kW h m−2, compared to a plant with only FPC [7]. However, the flat
plate part of the field was oversized compared to the demand resulting in lower annual
performance due to PTC having to defocus during summer [34], [36]. One conclusion is
the importance of optimisation of collector area of the different collector types and size
of the storage based on the heat demand during the planning and design phase of such
hybrid fields [36]. It was also concluded that the integration of PTC increased the plant
flexibility due to being easy to put out of focus and that thermal performance increased,
maintaining a relatively high and constant outlet temperature [37].

2.3

2.4
Solar theory
On average, 1367 W m−2 is reaching outside of the atmosphere of Earth from the sun.
This is called the Solar Constant and is the energy per unit time received on a unit area of
surface held perpendicular to the direction of propagation of the radiaton [42]. The Solar
Constant sets the boundary for how much solar energy can be absorbed at Earth if there
would be no atmosphere. While going through the atmosphere the radiation is reduced
by two significant phenomena, it is scattered by air molecules, water and dust, and it is
partially absorbed by O3, H2O and CO2 [42]. The solar energy reaching the surface of
the earth at a given time depends on the clearness of the sky. The portion of the radiation
reaching earth that has not been scattered by the atmosphere is referred to as direct solar
radiation, or beam radiation. The portion that has been scattered and changed direction
is referred to as diffuse radiation.
When referring to the position of the sun and a solar collector surface a set of commonly
used angles is adopted from [42] and displayed in fig. 2.12. The definitions of the symbols
used for the angles can be found in the Nomenclature list.

Identified main effects on solar engineering of the movement of the sun (directions refer-
ring to a Northern Hemisphere location without considering effects on locations above the
polar circle) are that:
• Surfaces located north of the equator receives more radiation in wintertime if they
are tilted towards south, this due to decreased angular loss.
• Surfaces located at the equator should not be tilted towards the south but may track
the sun from East to West.
• Optimal surface tilt of a south facing collector depend on the angular distribution
of direct radiation on solar altitude and azimuth efficiently combined into the profile
angle as defined in [42], see fig. 2.16 and eq. (2.3). The optimal tilt is higher at
higher latitudes.
• The surface azimuth angle affects the received radiation of a fixed tilted surface by
shifting it towards mornings (due East) and evenings (due West).

3
Methods
This thesis has aimed to build a TRNSYS model for the FPC/PTC Hybrid concept that
is generic enough to be used for industrial feasibility studies and design considerations.
The model is implemented such that different Hybrid configurations can be tested for
project specific weather data and load characteristics (heat rate and temperatures) and
also bench-marked against reference FPC and PTC systems. In the process of develop-
ing the final TRNSYS model, each modelling step (see fig. 3.1) was used to give insights
into the characteristics of first the different collector types and later the Hybrid concept in
relation to reference FPC and PTC systems. The process started with parametric studies
of the individual collector types utilising existing TRNSED models. It then continued with
building new TRNSYS models, first simulating the separate collector behaviours (fig. 3.1a)
at high resolution with 1 min-resolution climate data from the climate station at the Depart-
ment of Civil Engineering, Technical University of Denmark (DTU) [44]. Then the Hybrid
concept (fig. 3.1b) was introduced to the model and its control strategies were examined
on the same high resolution. The process ended with a Hybrid model with load matching
and thermal energy storage (TES) (figs. 3.1b and 3.1c) to be used with 1 hour-resolution
climate data accessible for locations all around the world from e.g. the commercial soft-
ware Meteonorm [45]. Throughout the modelling, it was investigated how flow control
based on radiation and the QDT solar equation (eq. (2.1)) can fulfil the objective to de-
liver a constant set supply temperature. The methods behind the modelling steps and
connected assumptions and delimitations are described in the following sections.

3.1
FPC/PTC parametric studies
To get an overview of the sensitivity of different parameters for FPC and PTC and to give
a basis for design optimisation, parametric studies on operation temperatures, azimuth
angles, tilt and row separation of FPC were conducted. Those studies were done with a
previously developed TRNSED model that does not consider the dynamic term of the QDT
solar collector equation in eq. (2.1), and that is therefore fast for big parametric studies.
This is also a simplification chosen in ScenoCalc [46] used within Solar Keymark. The
TRNSED tool was developed and validated for the Absolicon T160 PTC at (DTU) during
2017 [39] and the simulation of FPC is performed analogously but with a fixed collector
surface instead of a tracking one. The climate data used was a normal year for Sjælsmark
(approximately 12 km from DTU, Copenhagen) based on measured data during 2001-
2010 and exported from Meteonorm [45]. The climate data had 1-hour resolution. The
results of the parametric studies were expected to depend heavily on the location, both
in terms of climate and latitude, so to delimit the scope and align the parametric studies
with the results from the detailed modelling with weather data from DTU, the above choice
was made.
A range of tilts for the FPC was simulated to clarify what choice of tilt that gave the highest
annual output and how the output varied with other choices. This was also studied month-
wise to understand how tilt affected the distribution of energy on different seasons. The
same was done for azimuth angles of both collector types (FPC and PTC). Both tilt and
azimuth angle studies helped to get an understanding of how one can vary the seasonal
profile of the collectors to match a particular customer load profile better. By simulat-
ing the annual heat output at different mean operation temperatures, an indication of the
temperature intersection above which PTC outperforms was achieved. The intersection
is dependent on configuration assumptions but the results still gives an idea on how the
annual solar energy utilisation of the collector types vary with temperature. The configura-
tions where chosen such that both collectors had the configuration giving the best annual
output.
The solar collectors chosen for the parametric studies were the GREENoneTEC double-
glazed GK 3133 FPC and the Absolicon T160 PTC, with collector parameters and configu-
rations presented in table 3.1, unless otherwise stated. The TRNSED model implemented
the ASHRAE (American Society of Heating, Refrigeration, and Air Conditioning) incidence
modifier as opposed to the tabulated modifier mentioned in connection to eq. (2.1). The
ASHRAE incidence modifier is a fitted equation where Kb(θI) is approximated with [42]

loop behind a heat exchanger, as opposed to in Taars where the FPC are behind a heat

3.3
Modelling steps
This section aim to give a brief summary of the TRNSYS modelling conducted within
the thesis work. Details on system components and control strategies for solar collector
system and district heating plant follow in separate sections.
Throughout the modelling GREENoneTEC single- and double-glazed GK 3133 S/GK
3133 was used to represent FPC and the Absolicon T160 was used as PTC. The collector
parameters and configurations used, unless otherwise stated, are presented in table 3.1.
The single-glazed FPC is referred to as FPC1 and the double-glazed as FPC2, as the
direction of flow is first through the single-glazed and then through the double-glazed.
Based on the row distance per collector height ratio of the Danish SDH plants in Bræd-
strup, 2.4 [29], Høje-Taastrup, 2.4 [25] and Taars, 2.5 [31] a ratio of 2.4 was chosen for
both FPC and PTC in this study. The reason for choosing the same ratio was to have
approximately the same land footprint per aperture area of the two collector types. In
other words, for both collectors the aperture area was about 42 % of the footprint. Ap-
proximately since the collector distance in the other direction might differ slightly between
the collectors. When optimising a hybrid field, the optimal ratio is likely to differ between
the collector types due to different sensitivity to the sparsity. The collector tilt, β, of the
FPC was chosen as to give highest annual output. That was also the case for the azimuth
angle, γ, of both collector types.
For all modelling steps the collector performance is simulated with eq. (2.1), hence includ-
ing the dynamic behaviour of the solar collectors during heat-up and cool-down so that
the control strategy could be evaluated in detail.

Hybrid model
The second step of TRNSYS modelling, displayed in fig. 3.1b, was to couple the collector

3.6
Delimitations
This section aim to give the reader an overview of the main assumptions and delimitations
connected to this study. Some of them are mentioned also in the sections concerning the
topic where the assumptions or delimitations were made.
Technology
As already mentioned, to delimit the scope of this study, only one set of collector models
were considered. The two FPC, both from a big player on the market, and the Absolicon
T160 PTC since the study was done on behalf of Absolicon. One could argue that other
FPC models or evacuated tube collectors and other PTC would improve the comparisons.
Such comparisons are completely possible within the tool and this work can be seen as
one example of of to apply the tool for comparisons.
Modelling
The following main delimitations are connected to implemented models:
• Negligible influence of wind speed and sky temperature in collector output is as-
sumed in the choice of the simplified QDT solar collector equation. For neither of
the collectors studied these effects were characterised with significance and pre-
sented in the Solar Keymark certificates.
• No array shading of diffuse radiation is implemented for PTC.
• If present, the flow rate dependency of the collector heat outputs are assumed to be
covered by the QDT coefficients.
• No collector degradation, soiling and/or life time is considered within the model.
• No pipes are included in the model and hence no pipe dynamics and/or heat losses.
The decision to not include pipes was done to make the model as generic and with
as few input parameters as possible.
• The boiler is assumed ideal in the sense that it can start/stop immediately and that
its heat rate can be controlled exactly. The boiler is to be considered as auxiliary, to
represent everything that is not covered by the solar thermal plant.
• All pumps are assumed to be able to operate within the full range from no flow to its
maximum capacity with exact control.
• In the current implementation, the TES can only be used by solar collectors and
not the boiler. So the model cannot be used to analyse if and/or how the TES can
minimise start/stop of the boiler or be used for peak shaving wintertime.
• No heat loss is implemented in the tank model, making the model error increase with
storage time. The storage of this study is considered being a buffer storage and is
not to be used for seasonal storage. For simulation of such storages adequate
implementation of losses are suggested.
• Although real PTC fields can defocus partially, the model is currently delimited to
defocusing the complete field.
• Apart from defocus of PTC there is currently no way to turn off the system or to cool
down the tank so it needs the choice of the FPC area to be adequate compared to
the load size.
38
Chapter 3. Methods

• Pump power consumption is not considered even though the difference in pressure
drop between different collector types could potentially generate interesting com-
parisons and affect engineering choices.
Configurations
In comparisons and parametric studies, one must delimit the number of configurations
tested. Below is a list of the most significant delimitations.
• Only one location and its climate is simulated and used to illustrate the models
results and the Hybrid concept. The generic approach with the final model read-
ing 1 hour-resolution climate data allow for location specific assessments in future
projects all over the world.
• Only one temperature setting illustrated. Comments on how other choices would

• East/west azimuth of the PTC is not considered in the detailed analysis. It would

Findings
This chapter present the results of this thesis project along with analysis of the same. The
concept of a presumed solar engineer is used frequently to exemplify how different de-
sign choices regarding collector types and their configuration affect real world engineering
tasks. The following sections present the results and analysis divided by the models that
produced the results. All area specific results presented refer to collector gross area.
Among other things, the results from the parametric studies show that east/west oriented
PTC achieve highest winter output and that below 70 ◦C, the FPC gives the highest annual
output and above it PTC does.
The Separate collectors model results present in-depth analysis of the dynamic behaviour
of the collectors and statistics shows more operation hours for PTC but more operation
days for FPC.
The findings from the Hybrid model show highest annual output from the reference PTC
plant, with a total of 450 kW h m−2. A hybrid field consisting of 70 % PTC and 30 % FPC
was shown to produce 448 kW h m−2 whereas the reference FPC produced 377 kW h m−2.
The FPC was shown to need about 30 % more pump and piping capacity compared to
PTC and the Hybrid, leading to lower material costs for the latter two. Boosting an FPC
field with an area corresponding to 30 % PTC, the FPC production improved by 11 %.
With 70 % PTC, the FPC output was improved by 23 %.
Finally, the section on the Hybrid model with tank and load matching demonstrate the
results from connecting a sample hybrid plant to a district heating grid. Graphical inter-
pretations demonstrate the system interactions on different time-scale and system effects
of the TES are discussed. The findings exemplify how the developed model can be used
for design of solar thermal fields all over the world.

QDT equation decomposition
Going further into the details of the collector type characteristics one may look at the
how the terms in the QDT equation eq. (2.1) behave. One clear April day (fig. 4.11), one
overcast August day (fig. 4.12) and one shifting weather September day (fig. 4.13) serves
as examples. The FPC is displayed in the upper subplot and the PTC in the lower. In
addition to the terms of the QDT equation the energy balances of the collectors (sum of
all terms) are shown with a thicker blue line and the heat losses at set point are displayed
with dotted lines so that one can easily track what the heat loss would be at set point and
whether the collector is operating at set point or not.
Looking first on the clear April day in fig. 4.11, one notice that the daily collector output
curves are very different for the two collector types. The FPC, being oriented with a tilt and
facing south, has a bell shaped curve with its highest output at noon. The PTC however,
being horizontal and tracking the sun’s movement from east to west has a flatter curve
with a slight dip at noon when the incidence angle and hence also the angular losses
is at their highest. Comparing the operating times, the PTC operates much longer due
to its tracking, resulting in greater area under the curve, i.e. energy output, for the day
as a whole. Before 09:00 and after 15:00, the PTC gives roughly quadruple and double
the energy, respectively. At noon however, the FPC gave 650 W m−2 and the PTC only
350 W m−2. Comparing the heat losses, the PTC had total heat losses below 100 W m−2
when operating at set point whereas the FPC had a total of almost 200 W m−2. Due to its
lower thermal mass, the PTC was heated up faster, this is noted by comparing the heat
loss terms with the heat losses when the collector has reached its set point.

4.3
Hybrid model
This section display results from modelling the proposed Hybrid system with single- and
double-glazed FPC along with PTC in a series that starts with the single-glazed FPC and
ends with the PTC. In the model, the temperature on the load side of the heat exchanger
is controlled, and simulations are performed in 1 min-resolution.
Installing a FPC/PTC Hybrid, the solar engineer will be posed with the problem of deciding
what area ratio one should put between the two types. This and previous sections can
be guiding in how to achieve the system characteristic wanted. Total annual output may
not be the only parameter to optimise. As noted in the parametric studies of the individ-
ual collectors, sacrificing annual output for better characteristics might give other values.
More of the FPC gives, intuitively, more of its behaviour. In addition to the characteris-
tics wanted, the solar engineer would have to consider collector and storage economics
when choosing the ratio, weighing increased performance against connected costs and
or savings. Costs is however not discussed in this study except from noting that Absolicon
claims similar PTC pricing as FPC.
In the results presented in this section a PTC share of 70 % was simulated, giving an an-

for the simulated case and a 30 % share 421 kW h m−2. The reference FPC and PTC
systems gave 377 and 450 kW h m−2 respectively. For this case the PTC performed 19
% better than the FPC. While achieving the best annual output from having only PTC, one
may note that by introducing only 30 % PTC to the reference FPC, the resulting Hybrid
filled 60 % of the performance gap between the FPC and PTC. Correspondingly, 84 % of
the gap is filled by introducing 50 % PTC. This conclusion may be very interesting for ex-
isting FPC fields where, by introducing PTC as a temperature booster, the existing field’s
performance can be increased a lot from introducing relatively little PTC. Assuming there
is room for an extension, that is. The annual FPC output with 30 % PTC was 418 kW h m−2

the defocusing possibility reduce the planning effort of the manager. Both over production
measures reduce the total annual output of the plant and again, the storage cost has to be
weighed against what margin the solar engineer wants for being able to store abnormally

5
Conclusion
Three generic TRNSYS models have been developed: (1) The Separate collectors model,
used for simultaneous high resolution simulation of dynamic collector behaviour; (2) The
Hybrid model, for evaluating the control and performance of a proposed concept with
single- and double-glazed FPC in a series with PTC; (3) The Hybrid model with tank and
load matching, for simulating solar plant and boiler interaction with a district heating grid
and a TES.
The findings from the models have built knowledge connected to hybrid systems and
bench-marked the Absolicon PTC technology against state-of-the-art FPC. A range of
graphical and numerical technology comparisons on different time-scales from 1 min-
resolution to monthly, seasonal and annual sums, have been presented, all in a Danish
climate. The proposed FPC/PTC Hybrid concept have been compared with reference
FPC and PTC. Parametric studies on the individual collector types included tilt, azimuth
and operation temperature and provide several guidelines into design configuration im-
pact.
Among the concrete findings one may note especially that:
• For the studied temperatures and climate, the PTC gave 450 kW h m−2 annually.
The proposed Hybrid concept with 70 % PTC and 30 % FPC was shown to produce
448 kW h m−2 whereas the reference FPC produced 377 kW h m−2. Different tech-
nologies should be used in the temperature range where they excel, by boosting
the temperature of the FPC with PTC, the FPC produce more thanks to the result-
ing lower operation temperature. With 30 % PTC, that increase was 11 % and with
70 % PTC it was 23 %.
• FPC harvest diffuse radiation better than PTC but both collector types have been
shown to need DNI to reach set point temperature. Not even on a summer day with
18 ◦C ambient temperature did the FPC reach above 75 ◦C in overcast conditions.
• If, at all times, the harvested energy has a matching load (usually low solar fractions),
the highest annual output will give the lowest energy price. As soon as one introduce
a miss match between the supply and demand (usually higher solar fractions) it is
no longer obvious that highest annual output is the optimum. The value of a high
annual output will have to be balanced against the cost and heat losses of a storage
that can make use of it. East/west azimuth PTC showed the greatest potential in
flattening the seasonal output profile, the Hybrid concept showed the flattest daily
curve. In a load matching consideration, also the seasonal variation in what fuels
are replaced and the connected costs, emissions, policies and laws etc. need to be
considered.
• The proposed control strategy delivered high temperature quality with over 90 %
of the annual energy output delivered within 10 ◦C from set point temperature for
all three systems, FPC, PTC and the Hybrid. Stable outlet temperatures were dis-
played also in rapidly shifting weather conditions.

The thesis is considered to have fulfilled its goals to: (1) to develop a generic TRNSYS
model for FPC/PTC Hybrid systems and (2) give a benchmark of Absolicon PTC technol-
ogy against state-of-the-art FPC and display relevant technology comparisons.
Future use of the TRNSYS models can be internally at Absolicon and/or with its industrial
partners to evaluate customer cases and to help guide both technology and investment
decisions. Hopefully, it will promote industrial research collaboration, helping suppliers of
FPC and PTC to build solar thermal plants with increased system performance at reduced
cost.

Chapter 5. Conclusion
==PAGE:89==
6
Future work
The presented models simulate a stepwise bigger energy system with high level of detail
and the findings present extensive analysis of the energy considerations connected to the
system design. In the industry however, everything has a price and the resulting energy
cost is often what matters the most. The presented analysis give some guidelines as
to what costs are to be balanced and the obvious extension of the modelling would be to
include also economic aspects of the plants. A layer of optimisation could be programmed
to automatically identify the most feasible solution for a given case given the related costs.
Another clear future step is to validate the Hybrid model against a physical materialisation
of the concept. To have the model validated and run as a digital twin of a real installation
would further strengthen the credibility of the model, the Hybrid concept and the operation
of the installed plant.
When further extending the energy models and the comparisons presented this work,
the list of delimitations in Methods can be used as a background. Among the possible
extensions, the ones considered most interesting are:
• Simulations of the Hybrid concept in other climates, with neighbouring temperature
levels and with other load profiles to map how the concept perform under different
circumstances.
• Analysis of east/west azimuth PTC within the Hybrid model with tank and load
matching to study the impact of a better seasonal profile on high solar fraction plants.
• Implementation of heat loss simulations within the tank model such that solar energy
delivered when needed gets greater value than energy that has to be stored.
• An added separate TES for seasonal storage to simulate higher solar fraction grids.
• Implementation of partial defocus of the PTC such that when the storage is full,
only enough collector area to get the heat rate down to the load is turning away. A
stepwise defocus that account for the hydraulic possibilities and limitations within
the field is then recommended.
• Dynamic control considerations either by PID or by moving average of dynamic term
in QDT equation to further improve the temperature quality of the control, especially
for FPC and in case pipes and pipe dynamics are added.
Future modelling and implementations of this Hybrid concept should consider finding more
accurate programming of the split temperatures to make sure it more closely matches the
real temperatures also at low flow rates. The use of the Hybrid model with tank and load
matching should include investigations into the identified supply and demand miss match
in mornings and afternoons.
In addition to the above, studies of the storage capacity of a district heating grid and the
possibility to intentionally supply more than demanded and hence raise the district heating
return temperature would be interesting and could be modelled. Utilising the capacity of
the grid as a buffer might decrease to tank storage volume needed. Such considerations
would have to carefully consider temperature cycling wear on the grid.
