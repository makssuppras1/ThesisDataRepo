Abstract
In urban hydrology it is common practice to investigate the performance of
urban drainage systems by applying uniformly distributed rainfall. Since rain-
fall is the key component governing the hydrological responses in an urban
drainage system, the dynamics caused by the spatial variability of rain re-
mains uncaptured. The following study investigates expected hydrodynamic
responses to distributed rainfall. The performance of the Damhus model in
greater Copenhagen was studied in MIKE Urban, and historical rainfall meas-
urements from 13 different rain gauges were employed. The objective of the
examination was to provide a suggestion on how to choose rainfall events ex-
pected to yield overﬂows in a catchment of signiﬁcant size. A selection of weirs
expecting to overﬂow into the river from an event with a return period of 5
years was used for the evaluation. A total of 44 block rain simulations with
3 different durations of varying depths were performed. Responses recorded
in the southern part of the catchment were applied as benchmark of when to
expect overﬂows throughout the system, and a critical point was chosen for
the intensity in which a 30 % of the southern weirs started to overﬂow. Cri-
teria were deﬁned at 5.8, 1.9 and 0.7 µm/s for rain durations of respectively
20 minutes, 1 hour and 3 hours. A continuous simulation with spatially dis-
tributed historical data showed that overﬂows were expected to begin at an
average of 0.3 µm/s for a 6 hour duration. Based on the 4 deﬁned intensity cri-
teria and a requirement of minimum separation of 24 hours dry period, rainfall
events were determined by applying the RamLTS software for the year of 2015.
The selection of events were subsequently compared to to results from a con-
tinuous network simulation performed with the same historical measurements
as input. Results showed that 96 % of the events covered the expected over-
ﬂows. Areal weights were assigned to the individual rain stations measuring
intensities above the criteria for each event. A tendency of signiﬁcant over-
ﬂows were observed when the sum of areal weights were above 52 %. It was
concluded that all the deﬁned events exceeded the 6 hour criterion whilst the
other criteria were too moderate. The systems critical point which was set to
30 % of the southern overﬂows is thus considered to be overestimated. It is
recommended to look into the intensities which cause the ﬁrst overﬂow.

Dansk Sammenfatning
I urban hydrologi er det almen praksis at undersøge aﬂøbssystemers kapacitet
ved brug af jævnt distribueret nedbør for hele oplandet. Da nedbøren er en
styrende faktor for oplandets hydrologiske respons, går dynamikken som føl-
ger den rumlige variation i nedbøren tabt. I denne rapport undersøges effek-
ten af distribueret regn på Damhusåens opland på baggrund af simuleringer i
MIKE URBAN med historiske nedbørsdata fra 13 regnmålere, hvor de enkelte
deloplande tilknyttes den nærmeste regnmåler. Undersøgelsen forsøger at ud-
vikle en metode til udvælgelse af regnhændelser som forventes at forårsage
overløb fra aﬂøbssystemer i større oplande. De overløbsbygværker der under-
søges er udvalgt på baggrund overløb til Damhusåen ved en femårshændelse
i nuværende klima.
Der er udført beregninger med 44 kasseregn med tre
varigheder af varierende dybder. Overløb i den sydlige del af oplandet blev
anvendt som indikator for systemets kapacitet og systemets kritiske punkt
vurderes at være når der forekommer aﬂastninger ved 30 % af bygværkerne.
Intensitetskriterier for at opnå det kritiske punkt for 20 minutters, en times og
tre timers varigheder blev deﬁneret som henholdsvis 5.8, 1.9 og 0.7 µm/s. En
kontinuerlig simulering med historiske regndata fra de anvendte regnmålere
viste, at der forekommer overløb ved en 6 timers gennemsnitlig intensitet på
0,3 µm/s. Baseret på disse ﬁre deﬁnerede intensitetskriterier samt et krav på
minimum 24 timers tøjrvejr mellem hændelser blev regnhændelser for 2015
udvalgt ved brug af programmet RamLTS. De udvalgte hændelser blev sam-
menlignet med overløbshændelser fra den kontinuerlige simulering for samme
år og viste en 96 % sammenhæng. For hver hændelse udregnes det samlede
areal af deloplande tilhørende regmålere der opfylder mindst et af de deﬁnerede
kriterier. Når dette areal overstiger 52 % af det samlede oplandsareal observeres
der betydelige overløb fra systemet. Undersøgelsen viste at det primært var
seks timers kriteriet der gav anledning til aﬂastninger mens de andre kriterier
var sat for højt. Systemets kritiske punkt sat til overløb fra 30 % af de sydlige
overløbsbygværker, vurderes at være overestimeret. Det anbefales at fokusere
på den intensitet der resulterer i det første overløb.

1
Introduction
In urban hydrology, rainfall is a key component governing the major hydro-
logical processes taking place in an urban drainage system. Precipitation pat-
terns are anticipated to modify in the future and an increase in both magnitude
and frequency is expected as a consequence of climate change [Arnbjerg-Nielsen,
2012]. Drainage systems in urban catchments typically have a very fast re-
sponse time, as urban areas predominantly tend to be covered with imper-
meable surfaces. With few natural retention areas, runoff is produced as a
response to rainfall, and more or less directly introduced to the drainage sys-
tem. Urbanisation is only predicted to increase, a factor which will contribute
to larger fractions of impervious areas. Most existing drainage systems are not
designed to handle increased volumes of water and surcharges are thus expec-
ted to occur more frequently in the future.
Rainfall input to runoff simulations therefore play a crucial role when at-
tempting to predict hydrological responses of an urban drainage system. When
analysing such responses, it is common practice to apply rainfall statistics that
are based on point measurements i.e from rain gauges. Runoff calculations
are consequently computed with the assumption that the rain is uniformly dis-
tributed over an entire catchment, and thus do not capture the rainfalls mo-
tion or structure. The lack of spatial variability of the rainfall data as simu-
lation input may lead to signiﬁcant errors, [Niemczynowicz, 1988] [FAURES,
1995], particularly when considering the rapid response time to the runoff pro-
cesses. The signiﬁcance of the temporal and spatial rainfall distribution on
hydrodynamic urban drainage response has been extensively studied ([Obled,
1994], [Bruni, 2015], [Arnaud, 2002], & [Smith, 2004]). Multiple studies sug-
gest that the spatial-temporal variability indeed does have high impacts on the
hydrological response, the topic however still remains poorly understood and
requires more research [Segond et al., 2007].
The following paper will explore the topic by testing rainfall data on an
urban drainage model from Greater Copenhagen. The catchment of study is
connected to a river which runs into the harbour - a popular spot for bathing.
When Combined Sewer Overﬂows (CSO) enter the river, polluted waters are
lead to the harbour and bathing facilities are consequently closed down. CSO
return periods are usually calculated based on rainfall statistics from point
measurements, and thus only quantify the CSO occurring from rainfall input
of an entire catchment. With the spatial rainfall variability being ignored, the
model predictions are bound to be uncertain. Design criteria set by the author-
ities typically include a limit for CSO’s return period, a limit which in reality
may be exceeded.

1.1
Aim and objectives
The thesis aims to perform and analysis of the responses an urban drainage
model has to spatially distributed precipitation. By testing a series of different
rainfall, the project seeks out to develop a method for predicting the character-
istics of rainfall input that will put constraint on certain interest points within
an urban drainage model. The ﬁndings of the investigation may ultimately
serve as helping a tool for efﬁciently gaining relevant information on hydro-
dynamical responses in drainage models of considerable magnitude. Analysis
will be performed by applying MIKE Urban as the urban drainage software
and the study will incorporate the following examinations:
• Identiﬁcation of points of interest within the model. The ﬁrst step in the thesis
will be to identify the locations where information from model simula-
tion are relevant. For this study, it will be the points in which CSO’s
are expected to occur into the Harrestrup River. The procedure includes
a model run with a CDS rain series with return period of 5 years. The
identiﬁed points will serve as a base for result interpretation throughout
the project.
• Testing the model with Block rain. The model will be run with a series of
block rain with varying intensities and durations. The objective of the
analysis is to gain knowledge on how the catchment responds to various
inputs. A model of such signiﬁcant size incorporates a complex drainage
system that does not respond in a uniform manner. Simulations com-
puted with different block rain scenarios will serve as a ﬁrst indicator on
how the model performs, and point out the areas/points of particular
interest.
• Network simulation with historical rainfall input. A computation of the net-
work model for a continuous year (2013) will be carried out with spa-
tially distributed rainfall as input. Results from the simulation will serve
as base data that may be used for validation and moreover allows for an
interpretation on how the system reacts as a whole to distributed rainfall.
• A combination of ﬁndings from the points above will facilitate the choice
of setting up criteria for the rainfall events which, as input to the drain-
age model, are expected to yield overﬂows at the points of interest. The
deﬁned thresholds may ultimately serve as criteria for doing Long Term
Simulation (LTS) for this speciﬁc model.
• Validation of criteria choice. A selection of events based on the deﬁned cri-
teria will be performed on the spatially distributed rain for 2014 and 2015
and will be compared to results obtained from continuous simulations

2
Case study and data sets
2.1
Study area
The area studied is a part of a larger river network system that goes under the
name of Harrestrup Å. The catchment is shared by 9 municipalities and covers
roughly 7 ha with 30 km of streams, see Figure 1. Harrestrup Å has historic-
ally served as a drinking water supply for Copenhagen but was transformed
into a waste water channel leading to the city’s harbour [Rambøll, 2013]. Up
until recently, the system has been severely deteriorating as consequence of
the frequent sewer overﬂows to the river. New regulations have already been
implemented [City, 2008] thereby signiﬁcantly reducing the overﬂows. The
river system is however still very vulnerable considering the anticipated cli-
mate change which is likely to contribute to an increased frequency of CSO’s.
There is an underlying political will for Harrestrup Å to return to its natural
state so that the river can add both recreational value to the city and also that
the water body can live up to an environmentally sustainable condition. A re-
established state of the system will furthermore ameliorate the bathing water
quality by the harbour, where the municipality of Copenhagen wishes to estab-
lish a beach. The restoration project is ongoing and is carried out by HOFOR.
The project includes the implementation of climate adaptation measures such
as big storm water tunnels and is expected to ﬁnish in 2018.
For the purpose of this paper, the model for the Damhus catchment was
studied. The catchment is a part of the Harrestrup Å system and includes,
from North to South, the rivers Kagså, Harrestrup Å and Damhus Å as illus-
trated in Figure 1. The area of the catchment is roughly 45 km2 , has 230.000
inhabitants and is shared between 6 municipalities. The catchments drainage
networks leads its sewer to the Damhus Waste water treatment plant, which
has the capacity to rinse 10.000 m3 /hour.
2.2
Hydraulic model
A hydrodynamic urban drainage model for the Damhus catchment was provided
by HOFOR. The model was originally created in MIKE Urban by Rambøll as a
part of Intelligent Waste Water Management (Intelligent Spildevandshåndter-
ing) . The MIKE Urban program is used to describe two independent model
domains. The ﬁrst domain consists of a rainfall-runoff model which, with an
input of rainfall data can simulate the expected runoff that ultimately enters the
sewer system. The model outcome from the rainfall-runoff simulation serves
as a load to the network model, a model which simulates the ﬂow in the pipe
system.

2.3
Rainfall data
2.3.1
CDS & Block rain
Sewer systems in Denmark are commonly dimensioned using Chicago Design
Storms (CDS) as rain input. A CDS rain is a synthetic precipitation pattern
based on historical data, which gives a statistical approximation of the return
period of different rain intensives over a given duration [Ingildsen, 2016]. A
provided spreadsheet was applied for the purpose of producing the CDS rains
relevant in this investigation [Arnbjerg, 2016]. The choice of rain duration was
based on the time of concentration, tc - the time it takes for water to ﬂow from
the point furthest away from the outlet locality. Figure 3 illustrates a CDS rain
with a return period of 5 years and a duration of 240 minutes. Simple block
rains with a constant intensity at different duration are also applied in this
research.

Methods
3.1
Hydrodynamic model in MIKE Urban
3.1.1
Rainfall-Runoff simulation
The various rain data was implemented in MIKE Urban as the input for rainfall-
runoff simulation. Time series were created for for both CDS and Block rains
with a time step interval of 1 minute. All created rain series were added an-
other 12 hours of no rain in order for the network simulation to simulate the
pipe ﬂows occurring after a precipitation event. The MIKE Urban times series
application has a built in function for importing .kmd ﬁles from the historical
data series. All 13 rain gauge stations were implemented in the MIKE Urban
model with their respective X-Y coordinates. For each individual catchment in
the model, MIKE Urban uses the EULER method to deﬁne which rain gauge
station that will serve as a boundary condition in the runoff simulation. The
distribution is illustrated in Figure 4 in different shades of blue.
It should be noted that MIKE Urban only allows simulation where all rain
input, deﬁned in the boundary condition, have data available. This means
that the simulation can only start once rain is recorded on all stations simul-
taneously, and there may therefore consequently be a period where signiﬁcant
events are not included. An alternative would be to simply alter the time series
so that they would include time steps with 0 precipitation before the ﬁrst re-
cording. The same thing is valid for the simulation stop. For the purpose
of this investigation it was considered that the missed events at the start and
end of the year were insigniﬁcant, and the max time was set in the simulation
window in MIKE Urban. Run-off simulations were created for each of the indi-
vidual rain events studied. Run-off parameters applied in the simulations are
depicted in Figure 6.

3.1.2
Network simulation
Multiple preliminary test runs of the network model were computed in order
to check for warnings and/or errors. The water balance resulting from the
network simulation was furthermore assessed before running the main sim-

3.2
CSO selection
This study will focus only on the overﬂows potentially polluting the harbours
bathing water. Since the model consists of 268 weirs, it is crucial to narrow
down a selection of interest points in order to get a comprehensive overlook
of simulation results. A weir selection was determined by evaluating the per-
formance of the drainage model using a synthetic rain series as a rainfall input.
In extreme events with high return periods, overﬂows and ﬂooding are expec-
ted to occur, as the design criteria for combined sewer systems in Denmark
requires that water only must reach the terrain every 10 year . A CDS rain-
series with a 5 year return period was therefore chosen for the determination
of the weir selection. No climate factor was applied for the CDS generation. A
rain duration of 4 hours was chosen, ensuring that the time of concentration,
tc, was covered.
The Damhus model was run with the chosen CDS rain. Overﬂows were
registered in 214 out of the systems 268 weirs. All overﬂows were evaluated
and it was made certain that no water left the system. A selection of weirs with
overﬂows to the river was determined and implemented into the MOUSE Res-
ult Manager application in MIKE Urban. When running the MOUSE simula-
tion, the weir selection is used by the MIKE Urban application to save a results
ﬁle in which only the weir discharges are included, thus signiﬁcantly reducing
the size of the results ﬁle. The time step for results was set to 1 minute in the
MOUSE result manager.
For a more comprehensive overlook, the weirs were grouped into three
zones and given new names, namely North (N1-N22), Middle (M1-M12) and
South (S1-S24). The weir selection did not include weirs that were connected to
the South-eastern part of the system. This decision was made based on the fact
that the water from this area partly originates from another catchment of sig-
niﬁcant size. A complete picture of all overﬂows to the river would ultimately
require simulations from an entire extra catchment, a task which within the
frame of this project would not be feasible. Although the underlying interests
of the investigation is to predict the pollution of bathing waters, it was con-

sidered that the results upstream from the point of entry of the south eastern
part was sufﬁcient to support the analysis.
3.3
LTS
The performance of urban drainage systems is often evaluated by applying
historical rain data as input applicable to the area of study. With real data as
input, it is possible to carry out evaluations of expected CSOs, ﬂooding and an-
nual water balances. Long term simulations are a powerful way to determine
such statistics as it only carries out simulations at chosen time intervals where
relevant precipitation events take place. Since drainage systems are designed
to handle dry weather ﬂows and, additional precipitation of certain magnitude
for combined systems, there are time intervals that would be irrelevant for CSO
and surcharge analysis. With LTS, the simulation is thus carried out for a dis-
continuous series of rain events that depict a continuous historical rainfall time
series.
3.3.1
LTS criteria
The scope of this project is to determine which speciﬁc inputs will lead to over-
ﬂow in the Harrestrup River. By targeting these speciﬁc events alone in a his-
torical rainfall time series, simulations are only computed for the events that
are assumed relevant. In MIKE Urban, LTS simulations are computed based
on a “job list”, a text ﬁle which includes the start and stop time for MOUSE
simulations. The job list is created based on user-speciﬁed criteria and sim-
ulations are only carried out in deﬁned intervals. MIKE Urban has a built in
application for creating job lists. Based on the system loading, the user can spe-
cify thresholds for inﬂow in the model at deﬁned locations and with speciﬁc
duration. The tool is very useful, but quickly becomes a complex matter when
working with the dynamics of a big model.
A different approach was taken for this study; instead of looking into the
dynamics within the system, LTS criteria were based directly on the rainfall
used as input to the model simulation. The scope was to try to analyse rain-
fall time series, and deduct what effects that events with different shape and
magnitude would have on the hydrodynamic responses in the the drainage
system.
Two tools were applied to process rainfall data, the ﬁrst being RamLTS, a
program developed by and for Rambøll. The second program Keny is a soft-
ware developed for HOFOR. Both programs can, using multiple kmd rain ﬁles,
produce job lists which are required for LTS simulations in MIKE Urban. A
short description of the two follows.
15
==PAGE:36==
RamLTS creates job lists based on user-speciﬁed criteria constituting of max-
imum rain depth and maximum rain intensities at rain duration ranging from
20 minutes to 6 hours. A screen shot of the software presenting the possible in-
puts are included in Figure 6. Multiple criteria may be chosen simultaneously;
if an event fulﬁls one or more of the deﬁned criteria, the program generates a
job that incorporates the simulation start- and stop-time corresponding to the
rain event. Events are furthermore deﬁned by a minimum dry period interval
between events, which for the purpose of this study was set to 24 hours. Lastly,
the program adds a duration to the end of events, which in this investigation
was set to 30 minutes.
Figure 7: Screen shot of the program RamLTS. As seen, the program uses a kmd
ﬁle as input. The criteria for choosing events are depicted in the bottom. The
events are furthermore deﬁned by the dry-weather criteria and a dry-weather
period is added to the rain event, hence prolonging the job.
Keny works more or less in the same way, but cannot like RamLTS tar-
get speciﬁc events with multiple user-deﬁned intensity/duration relations. In-
stead, the user may deﬁne the minimum intensity that the event must include.
The program furthermore has the advantage that it makes a statistical over-
view of the intensity/duration relations which may be useful if the user needs
to do a closer investigation of the events, i.e examine IDF curves. For this re-
view, RamLTS was applied for the job list creation whereas Keny was used for
support and validation.
A ﬁrst approach to understanding the dynamics of the system was to let
the model run with different rainfall inputs. Block rains with a duration of 20
minutes, 1 hour and 3 hours were run with average rain depths varying from 3

3.3.3
Results management
Simulation Results. Once the MOUSE runoff and subsequent network simu-
lations are computed, the user speciﬁed weir discharges are retrieved. The
results were opened in MIKE View and saved as text ﬁles which subsequently
were imported into Excel. With roughly 500,000 time steps for the each simula-
tion, data sets were shortened by removing times steps with a overall discharge
sum equal to zero. Overﬂow events were subsequently deﬁned by summarising
the discharge for each consecutive time step and furthermore allowing a min-
imum of 24 hours between discharge stop and start of a new overﬂow event.
Job list. Job lists from each individual rain gauge station were combined
using the program RamLTS.The job lists were subsequently combined to one
job list which would for each year cover the events that fulﬁlled the deﬁned
criteria.
Validation. The overﬂow events were successively compared with the job
lists. Time intervals where jobs did not cover the overﬂow events or where jobs
had no expected overﬂows were investigated. An evaluation of the deﬁned
LTS criteria was ﬁnally made.

4
Results and Discussion
The following section presents and discusses the results obtained throughout
the investigation. The analysis is supported by multiple ﬁgures and tables in-
cluded in the Appendices and are numbered alphabetically accordingly, i.e Fig-
ure A.1 and B.1 are found in Appendix A and B respectively. Speciﬁc weirs are
referred to throughout the analysis and illustrations of their locations may be
found in Appendix A.
4.1
Runoff and network simulation - File structure
Keeping a good ﬁle structure is of vital importance when doing multiple runs
in MIKE Urban. Run-off ﬁles or Hotstart ﬁles applied in the network simula-
tions can only be used by one network simulation at the time. It is therefore
necessary to copy runoff/hot start ﬁles so that simultaneous network simula-
tions can be run. Sometimes it is beneﬁcial to copy whole directories in events
where simulations with minor changes need to be run simultaneously. It is
however important when doing this to make sure that the network simula-
tions apply the correct runoff ﬁle. Problems were experienced when copying
directories; it was discovered that even if the option for overwriting runoff ﬁles
in the MIKE Urban software was chosen, the action was not always performed.
The MOUSE simulation was computed and a summary report was produced,
but the runoff ﬁle was not overwritten. This was an inconsistent problem and
the solution was to ensure that a new runoff ﬁle was indeed created.
4.2
Water balance
Water balances can be checked by investigating a summary ﬁle which MIKE
Urban creates for each completed simulation. For the runoff simulation it is a
good idea to look through the accumulated runoff for the different catchments,
in order to ensure that the correct runoff volume will be utilized for the net-
work simulation.
Summary ﬁles from the network simulations give an overview of the water
balance in the system. When the network simulation was run with a rain-
fall runoff input from a 5-year return period CDS rain with a duration of 240
minutes, the summary report showed that a signiﬁcant amount was generated
during the simulation. In order to compute the hydrodynamic processes of the
drainage system, MIKE Urban needs water in the pipe system. The model will

4.8
Rain input and spatial distribution
It was only discovered after having processed all results that the runoff from
2014 was incorrect. The mistake was due to an incorrect method of saving the
time series for each of the rain gauge stations in the MIKE Urban software. In
the summary ﬁle from the rainfall-runoff simulation it was noticed how the
accumulated rain depth for each of the rain gauge stations were incorrect. The
runoff simulation was run again with the correct rain-input. Figure 27 illus-
trates the difference in the accumulated rain depths between the correct and
incorrect rain-input. The accumulated value was hence underestimated for
multiple rain gauges.
The bigger problem lies in the fact that the rain was no longer distributed
which make the results incomparable to the results from 2013 to 2015. Figure
28 illustrates the applied distribution of rain. With only rain gauge number
5697 covering major parts of the catchment area, it was considered that the
dynamics in the drainage system caused by the spatial distribution of rain was
completely lost. Due to the projects time frame it was not possible to perform
a new network simulation and the results from 2014 where thus disregarded.

Suggested procedure
Figure 29 sums up the procedure suggested for setting criteria of LTS simula-
tions for catechumens of signiﬁcant size and . The method includes 5 major
steps and are described as follows.
1. Identify points of interest. In order to get an overview of the dynamics in
the system it is a good idea to narrow down a selection of the points of
interest. This will facilitate the data processing. For this study, weirs
with overﬂows were based on a network simulation with a rain event
with return period of ﬁve years.
2. Quality assurance of rainfall data. Validation of rainfall data applied in sim-
ulation is of great importance. A standard procedure should be to com-
pute the accumulated rainfall for each rain station and compare this with
the accumulated rainfall produced by the simulation.
3. Test the model response with Block rain. The model should be run for block
rains with 3 durations; 20 minutes, 1 hours and 6 hours. Rain depths
should start on the lower side, i.e 5 mm and be altered up or down until
the critical point is reached where the ﬁrst overﬂows appear. The rain
depth is obviously catchment-dependant, and a trial and error approach
must be carried out. For this study it would have been sufﬁcient with
roughly 3-4 simulations for each rain duration. The criteria for the dif-
ferent duration are deﬁned by the rain depth which produces the ﬁrst
overﬂow.
4. Simulation with distributed rain. The model needs to be run with historical
data, as the block rain intensity will not capture the dynamics from the
distributed rainfall. An LTS simulation can be set up with criteria that are
less conservative than the intensities found in the clock rain simulations.
In this way it is ensure that all overﬂow events will be simulated.
5. Determining the ﬁnal intensity criteria for choice of events. The LTS results
should be used to look into the situations in which overﬂows just start to
occur at the critical points of the system. An average of the intensities for
these events should be computed and ﬁnal criteria for the LTS setup may
be deﬁned.

Further investigations
A spacial distribution of rainfall applied to a catchment of a signiﬁcant size
is very complex. This study can be considered as merely a starting point in
understanding the implications of distributed rainfall. The complexity has its
nature in multiple aspects, a couple of which are listed as follow:
• Critical response time of catchment. A big catchment is bound to have sub-
catchment areas that respond differently to rain events. This can be for
various reasons, systems can be built differently according to which mu-
nicipality they belong to, or there can be certain geographical or physical
constraints that dictate how a system is constructed. These aspects con-
tribute to the complexity of a drainage system of signiﬁcant size, and
uniform response patterns to rain events are not to be expected through-
out the structure. It might therefore be necessary to look at different parts
a system as individual sub catchments and try to deﬁne what criteria that
locally put constraints on the drainage system.
• Contributing Area versus the Boundary condition Area In this study, an areal
weight was applied to the different rain gauges once registered intensities
exceeded the deﬁned criteria. In reality there were some of the areas
that completely or partly did not contribute to the overﬂows examined
in this project. It could be interesting to try to have a more precise areal
weight deﬁnition. This would entail a determination of what boundary
condition that would apply as catchment load for each individual sub-
catchment.
• Combination of intensities and their expected outcome. This investigation has
primarily focused on the point where the ﬁrst overﬂow takes place. It
could be interesting to quantify criteria combinations which would pre-
dict something about the magnitude of the overﬂow.
By looking into the points mentioned above one could ultimately combine the
gained knowledge so that speciﬁc criteria for event selection would apply for
speciﬁc areas.

7
Conclusion
In this these, a method has been developed on how to identify rainfall charac-
teristics that are expected to yield overﬂows in a catchment of signiﬁcant size.
The approach has attempted to analyse the implication of using distributed
rainfall.
Deﬁning event criteria for expected overﬂows
By testing the urban drainage
model with block rain scenarios of different intensities and durations, the hy-
drodynamic responses were analysed, and a set of rainfall characteristics were
deﬁned as criteria for events which were expected to produce overﬂows. An
additional analysis was performed with a network simulation using spatially
distributed historical rain as input, and rainfall averages were performed for
the events were overﬂow occurred. Events from the historical data were sub-
sequently chosen by allowing at least 24 hour of dry weather to separate events
and furthermore fulﬁlling at least one of the deﬁned intensity-duration criteria.
The criteria based selection of precipitation events were compared to expec-
ted overﬂows obtained from network simulations with the same historical data
as input. It was concluded that criteria based events covered 96 % of expected
overﬂows. The events that were not captured were due to (1) a shortcoming in
the software RamLTS which was applied for event determination; (2) an event
which had a 6 hour intensity marginally lower than the determined criteria
and (3) a rainfall event which had a signiﬁcant intensity and a short duration.
Areal weights, corresponding to the rain gauge boundary conditions ap-
plied in the rainfall runoff model, were assigned to the rain gauges that ex-
ceeded the deﬁned criteria for each event. When the sum of these were above
52 %, signiﬁcant overﬂows were expected. Some of the events with a small
added areal weight still presented signiﬁcant overﬂow volumes. It was dis-
covered that these events depicted short rainfall events with signiﬁcant in-
tensities, and it was thereby concluded that the criteria based on block rain
analysis were too conservative. A block rain simulation with 6 hour duration
showed that the ﬁrst overﬂow in the system was expected at 0.37 µm/s, a value
above the deﬁned spatially distributed average from the historical data of 0.30
µm/s. The results emphasise the complexity of spatial data and an average
over longer time is difﬁcult to capture.
A total of 44 block rain simulations were carried out for the purpose of this
investigation. This turned out to be highly unnecessary for the evaluation pro-
cess, and it is recommended to rather perform block rain simulations with low
intensities until the ﬁrst overﬂows begin to appear. It is suggested to simulate
average block rain runs for 3 durations; 20 minutes, 1 hour and 6 hours and
it is expected that 3-4 simulations for each duration would be sufﬁcient, all in
51
==PAGE:72==
all mounting up to a total of 9-12 simulations. Results should followingly be
used as a measure to determine intensities which are even more moderate. The
criteria can subsequently be applied on the spatially distributed data and a LTS
simulation should then be carried out. The critical point should be examined
for the minor overﬂows taking place and average intensities of the rain lead-
ing up to these events should be computed. This can serve as the ﬁnal criteria
which is needed for setting up the LTS simulations.
Working with spatially distributed rainfall data
Although the investigation
originally intended to analyse data from 3 separate years, two of them had to
be discarded in the process as they did not represent results with the correct
rainfall data as input to the rainfall runoff model. This was discovered long
into the process of data analysis and emphasises the importance of making sure
the rainfall data is correct. For the year 2013 it was discovered that the built in
application in the MIKE Urban software for importing .kmd rain ﬁles did not
proceed as expected. Overﬂow results from 2013 could thus not be compared
to the rainfall events exceeding the deﬁned criteria. Rainfall data for 2014 was
incorrect as the .dfs0 ﬁles created in MIKE Urban for many of the rain gauges
were overwritten by time series from one single rain gauge. The dynamics
caused by spatially distributed rainfall was thus completely lost. In order not
to loose valuable time it is therefore crucial to make sure that the right rainfall
data is applied. A standard procedure should therefore include a calculation of
the accumulated rainfall for each rain station which enables a comparison with
the accumulated rainfall depicted in the summary ﬁle produced by the rainfall
runoff simulation.
