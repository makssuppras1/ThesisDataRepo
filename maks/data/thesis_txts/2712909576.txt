Abstract
Renewable energy has grown rapidly in recent decades, and this trend is expected to
continue. As technological advances and innovations are making solar and wind power
more efficient and affordable, the lack of accurate power output forecasting of renewable
sources points to a disadvantage in contrast to fossil fuels. Thus, in order for our society to
fully transition to renewable energy, we need to minimize the uncertainty that surrounds
renewable energy sources. Therefore, new tools and methods have to be constantly
developed to improve forecasting accuracy, which is the major factor for establishment
and maintenance of smart energy grid. While manual forecasting for multiple renewable
energy plants is challenging and time­demanding task, it also appears to be prone to
errors. The object of this study is to build a forecasting long short­term memory (LSTM)
model to predict the power output for the upcoming days. Furthermore, once the model
is trained, the aim is to be able to adapt to multiple power plants by additionally online
training of the model with each additional power plant data. The predicted data is also
streamed to cloud in real time, along with other useful information. The complete pipeline
of data collection, forecasting and cloud data streaming is hosted on a Raspberry Pi,
which makes the whole system easy to reproduce and scale up.

Introduction
As technological advances are rapidly changing our world, it is additionally increasing
our modern society hunger for energy. Therefore, with the energy demand continuously
rising, we need more efficient, sustainable and reliable energy sources. While on the one
hand, fossil fuels were a major factor to industrial revolution, on the other hand, they had a
significant impact on environmental pollution and geopolitical conflicts. Renewable energy
sources can make countries more independent on energy, which can provide long term
stability. This is the reason for the rising trend of funds towards researches on renewable
energy sources, and projects over the last decade. In recent years, taking advantage of
machine learning (ML), deep learning (DL) and artificial intelligence (AI) algorithms, the
renewable energy sector has improving its control, monitoring, forecasting and overall
efficiency.
This thesis aims to in developing two separated DL forecasting models. Primarily focused
on photovoltaic (PV) power plants and secondary on wind power plants. Both models will
have identical neural network architecture, but will be trained on different features, such as
historical power generated data, from multiple power plants (which are located in Born­
holm, Denmark) and historical weather data. The goal is to forecast power output for
the next 24­48 hours of PV and wind power plants based on past weather and gener­
ated power observations and future weather predictions. In building such a model we are
choose to take advantage of Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN), and more specifically
of Long Short Term Memory (LSTM) which is an RNN neural network, that includes a
memory cell. Inspired by a similar research Deng [1], where an LSTM model is used for
forecasting visibility based on historical weather features, this implementation tries to take
advantage of the memory that LSTM provides which is missing from other implementa­
tions such as (RNN or a simple Multiple Linear Regression). Since we are trying to solve a
time­series forecasting problem where the input dimension is historical weather features
and power generated of the past 24­48 hours LSTM seems to be an efficient approach,
[2].
Moreover, the whole pipeline application is designed to be hosted on a Raspberry Pi,
providing us with portability, low cost and energy efficiency, as well as the ease of scaling
up, since the Raspberry Pi offers the capability to develop and reproduce the system in
quick matter of time. In addition, the data collected and processed from all the Raspberry
Pi’s networks that will be placed across multiple power plants, will be streamed to a google
cloud service, where power plant performance can be monitored both individually and
collectively, giving us a thorough overview of the power plant performance and condition.
Additionally, as more data is being collected, it enables us to evaluate and also retrain the
existing model or develop novel ones depending on the metrics and application needs.
Thus, the forecasting accuracy can be continuously improved since we are gathering more
data.

2.1
Deep Neural Networks
Deep neural networks is part of a broader family of machine learning, which consists for
an artificial neural network with more than 2 layers. A neural network is set of connected
units where each connection has a mutable weight attached to it [6]. They have been
used to solve a wide variety of problems, in a more probabilistic and stochastic way, al­
though the solution may not be the optimal or always right, we can built models that gives
us close to best answers, that are acceptable on the specific constrains of each problem.
It is based on the way our brain works, and tries to simulate it, Figure 2.2c (at least par­
tially on specified tasks), by learning from a vast amount of data and the corresponding
feedback [7]. There are multiple types of neural networks based on the architecture of
the nodes and how the information is directed and processed in each node, such as feed
forward, convolutional and recurrent neural networks being amongst the most popular
ones. Also based on the training data labeling, we divide deep neural networks learning
method into three main categories, supervised, unsupervised and reinforcement learning
[8]. In reinforcement learning the machine is interacting with its environment and trained
based on the positive or negative feedback which is gained after each or a sequence of
actions. In supervised learning we provide the model with labeled dataset, whereas in
unsupervised one the model tries to learn by processing raw data and extracting features
and patterns on their own, and lastly, a subcategory of the last two called semi­supervised
we provide some labeled data but also give the machine some freedom dimension in or­
der to explore different patterns that we are not aware. Although the first general working
learning for deep learning algorithm was published as early as 1967 [8], deep learning
became popular in 2012, where it was made shown that we can get a significant per­
formance improvement by adding multiple layers and neurons [9]. This, of course, was
made possible due to advances in computing power and taking advantage of GPU power
by running the computations in parallel hence decreasing the learning time.

Gradient Descent
Neural network models require a training period where a large amount of data is passed
through the networks nodes, and on each repetition the weights of these connections are
adjusted in order to minimize a loss function, which for example can be described as the
offset of predicted value and the actual value |Ypredicted −Yactual| [6]. Here, we have to
mention that this may not always be the case, and the loss function has to be defined
and adjusted specifically for the problem that we are trying to solve, but the goal remains
the same, which is minimizing the loss function. In every scenario we have to adjust
the weights, through backward or forward propagation, etc. This point is where gradient
descent algorithm provides us the with the formula to update weights. It is an algorithm
used to find the local minimum/maximum of a given function, in our case minimizing the
loss function e.g. Figure 2.3.

and supervised learning, thus, they are widely used in time­series forecasting. What

particular time instance, the unfolding, represents visually how the previous output is fed
as an input on every next consecutive step of calculation. Thus, RNNs models obtain the
ability to remember past events, resembling some kind of artificial memory. One of the

2.2.1
Vanishing Gradient Descent
Although RNNs are capable or storing past input information that will help the model pro­
duce more accurate prediction data, this ability causes them to suffer from the vanishing
gradient descent. This problem arises when training RNNs with gradient based algo­
rithms, as shown in the previous sections, and backward propagation [13]. Because the
current error signal has to flow ”back in time” over the feedback connections to past inputs
[14], this causes the gradient descent, in some cases, to be extremely small and being
unable to change the weights value consequently the learning slows down significantly
and in worst case scenario can stop the learning process of the model [15]. To overcome
this obstacle alternative architectures where build, giving rise to Long Short­Term Memory
(LSTM) [16].
2.3
Long Short­Term Memory
LSTM which is arguably, by the time of writing this project, the most successful gated
RNN, with applications in plenty of domains and real life applications (e.g. Time series
prediction, speech recognition, text auto complete etc.). This plethora of application is
possible due to the intelligent selection and use of the gates in an LSTM unit, which also
overcomes the vanishing descent problem. A unit purpose is to process input data and
through training decide what information to remember and discard the rest. If we take
a look inside of an LSTM unit, as seen in Figure 2.5, we can distinguish the individual
components, such as the multiplication, addition, sigmoid and tanh. the combination of
these components forms the four main groups, i) Cell state, ii) Forget Gate, iii) Input Gate
and iv) Output Gate [17], [18].

Cell State
Cell state, is where the main information of the LSTM unit is stored, as we can see it can
only have linear changes, those are multiplication and additions, in order to decide the
rate at which the cell will forget the information, and what new information will be added to
the current timestamp of the cell, which will be passed on to the next timestamp. Those
updates performed on cell information depends on the inputs from the other gates.
Forget Gate
In the LSTM cell the first operation is to decide whether we should keep or discard the
information of the previous timestamp. It gathers information from the current input Xt and
the previous hidden state ht−1 which are then passed through a sigmoid function. This
operation will produce a value between 0 and 1, which will be later used by the cell for the
pointwise multiplication [17]. Thus, 0 will result in completely forgetting the information
from previous timestamp and 1 will keep it intact:

Bayesian Optimization
In contrast to grid search which is expensive and blind process (it search the whole space),
Bayesian optimization evaluates past hyperparameters changes in order to approach the
hyperspace global minima faster. As a result, we can imagine a hyperparameter as a
different dimension, Bayesian will almost always move in the direction with the greatest
slope (loss function rate of change). Based on [31], [29], [32] and [33], the optimization
process is explained on the Flowchart 5.3.
Figure 5.3: Bayesian optimization: Flowchart of the algorithm provides us with the main
idea on how the algorithm searches and moves on each iteration, in its goal to find the
global minima on given finite steps.

Summary
To sum up, the results showed our implementation strengths and weaknesses and it is im­
portant to acknowledge those in order for future improvements. Some key points gained
for the fittest PV power model was the increase in error when tested in real time scenario,
the factors that contributed to this can be the difference of weather data input in the model
training phase, and the forecasted weather data as obtained from OpenWeather. More
tests and comparisons are required to be performed in order to evaluate the quality of the
weather forecasting data and the actual conditions that take place in Bornholm. For the
wind power forecasting model there was a lack of real time data, so we were unable to
evaluate on real scenario application.
Moreover, a major difference can be seen between the PV and wind power models in
MSE. In an attempt to try and explain this behavior in our implementation, firstly, PV power
forecasting is only performed for half of the day since at night the value is always zero
which decreases the error. Furthermore, we can note that the correlation between wind
power and wind speed is lower in the historical data used to train the models than it is
for PV power and solar radiation, and we can see from autocorrelation plots of solar,
temperature, and PV power that they are stationary, whereas this is not the case for wind
power and speed, as Greuner [34] points out.

Discussion
In this thesis project, we implemented a deep learning algorithm focused on solar fore­
casting (and wind power) hosted on Raspberry Pi and monitored from a cloud platform.
In this chapter, further discussion about the results, conclusions, findings, and viable im­
provements are included.
6.1
Results Interpretation
The results of our implementation showed a major difference between solar and wind
power forecasting, with the former having a greater performance, although the wind power
was not tested in real time data conditions due to some difficulties in obtaining real time
data from powerlab.dk. This can also be seen in the way the Bayesian optimization func­
tion is tuning both models. It is observed that since wind power forecasting it only had one
correlated feature and autocorrelation, the tuning wants to approach a linear regression
model. Despite the complacent PV power forecasting model on the testing dataset, we
saw a measurable increase in MSE when tested on a real scenario. Since, in the training
phase of the model, the weather data does not carry any error, and if they do, the model
is adjusted for those errors. However, when it comes to the actual performance, the use
of OpenWeather API plays a significant role in the forecasting errors.
6.2
Future Work
We understand that a linear regression model, should perform quite adequate since the
number of used features is overly small and they share high correlation with the target.
However, the advantages of using a more complex model lies to the fact that we take
advantage of memory in an LSTM model, gaining information from the past and using
the autocorrelation property that appears some weather features as we saw in Chapter
3. Moreover, we can retrain the model with expanded number of features, ideally we can
take advantage of all correlated features that OpenWeather API provides us, given that we
have enough data to train the models. Such features can be humidity, visibility, pressure
(wind direction for wind power model). On the other hand, some errors are included
on data provided by OpenWeather which are translated to a decrease in the forecasting
models performance. Hence, it points out to the need of getting past (is a benefit for
high autocorrelation that is observed in our data) and future data from a different source.
Online training can also be introduced, as times passes and more data are collected, the
model can be updated and even can adapt to future possible long term conditions such
as additional PV panels installations, improved efficiency etc.
In the need of obtaining more accurate past weather data, the Raspberry Pi implemen­
tation gives us the opportunity to install each Rasbperry Pi at its dedicated power plant,
which will improve data collection and forecasting data. It only requires a Raspberry Pi,
access to the internet connection and the setup can be easily copied multiple times. All
the available data collecting will be view and monitored from a single point on a Google
sheet or any other cloud platform.
