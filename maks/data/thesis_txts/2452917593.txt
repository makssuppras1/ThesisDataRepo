Abstract 
Flooding in cities is predicted to become an even bigger problem in the future due to increased 
rainfall and continued urbanization. In recent years urban drainage systems using more nature-
based solutions, here referred to as Sustainable Urban Drainage Systems (SuDS), have evolved 
in different parts of the world. These systems are often applied in series, referred to as treatment 
trains, as they can improve the water quality and the lifetime of the whole drainage system. 
However, the treatment trains are not dimensioned as one whole. Instead each system that 
makes up the train is dimensioned individually, which can lead to discrepancies. 
 
The aim of this project was to identify and compare the processes behind the dimensioning of 
SuDS in terms of water quantity in different parts of the world and if possible, evaluate these 
methods through SWMM simulations as a basis for dimensioning SuDS in series. Six 
dimensioning methods from four countries were included in the evaluation, one from both 
Denmark and the UK, and two each from different locations in the USA and Australia. In 
addition, the 3PA (Three Points Approach) was included in the analysis to compare the results 
to a more simplified and less detailed method.  
 
The results indicate that even though the methods used to dimension SuDS in these different 
countries are very similar, there are minor differences between them which can result in large 
volume differences. The main differences detected in this project lie in different runoff 
calculations, infiltration area and duration of infiltration. Comparison of the results from the 
3PA method and the other methods for infiltration systems and pervious pavement resulted in 
values of the same order of magnitude, indicating that that 3PA could be used to dimension 
SuDS but needs further evaluation.  
 
The results of this project provide a summary and comparison of different dimensioning 
methods that can be used as a basis for creating a method to dimension SuDS in series

1 Introduction 
1.1 Background 
We live in a world that is constantly changing, forcing us to adjust our way of living. Due to 
global warming, rain events are for example expected to become more severe in the future. 
Combined with increased urbanization, this future rainfall will produce surface runoff that the 
current sewage systems are not dimensioned for, and can result in sewage overflows and 
flooding in cities (Fletcher, Andrieu, & Hamel, 2013). To better manage and reduce this 
flooding, sustainable drainage systems (SuDS), also known as best management practices 
(BMP), low impact development (LID), water sensitive urban design (WSUD) (Fletcher et al., 
2015) and sponge cities (Chan et al., 2018; Nguyen et al., 2018), can be implemented in urban 
areas (Goncalves et al., 2018; Haghighatafshar et al., 2018). All these different terms represent 
stormwater controls which are more nature-based than the traditional pipe-based sewer 
systems. They can be used to retain, slow down, treat, transport and reduce surface runoff 
locally through infiltration, evapotranspiration and retention and can improve water quantity, 
quality, amenity and biodiversity in urban areas.  
 
The problems which arise due to increase in rainfall intensities depend on local conditions and 
can be in terms of water quantity, water quality or both. Due to this dependency on local 
conditions, measures have evolved differently in different parts of the world which explains 
the different terminology provided above. Fletcher et al. (2015) provides a summary of the 
different terminology used around the world, explaining the history and evolution of each 
method. All these terms have however emerged from the same need for local treatment of water 
through unconventional stormwater management. In this paper, the term SuDS will be used to 
refer to these types of practices.  
 
Countries around the world have already been forced to adjust to a changing climate and 
Denmark is no exception where among other things, rainfall intensities are expected to increase 
causing excess pressure on the drainage systems. The Copenhagen Climate Adaptation Plan, 
which was published in 2011, includes SuDS as part of possible solutions that can be 
implemented to cope with this increased rainfall (City of Copenhagen, 2011). Following major 
flooding due to cloudbursts in the city of Copenhagen in 2010 and 2011, there was increased 
interest in stormwater control which resulted in an improved stormwater plan for the city (City 
of Copenhagen, 2012). For the implementation of SuDS on a large scale like this, it is necessary 
to use multiple systems. They can interact with each other and together they are more effective 
for managing urban runoff. 
 
The hydraulic design criteria for SuDS are in general based on individual systems, even though 
they often occur in series. This can affect the actual demand and required size of the systems 
both in terms of water quality and quantity. These series, were a sequence of two or more 
systems is used, are referred to as management trains or treatment trains (Bastien, Arthur, 
Wallis, & Scholz, 2010; Fletcher et al., 2015). Treatment trains are now often preferred over 
individual systems as they can improve the treatment performance of the whole system as well 
as decrease the required volume of the system (Bastien et al., 2010; Lashford, Charlesworth, 
Warwick, & Blackett, 2014). Understanding the role of treatment trains in terms of water 
quantity is important for countries like Denmark to be able to effectively include them in their 
stormwater plans.

Because of the development of multiple terms, SuDS can be dimensioned differently in 
different parts of the world. In order to be able to dimension SuDS in series, it is important to 
understand how these systems are dimensioned around the world to see if a global system can 
be created that can dimension SuDS in series. The aim of this project is therefore to investigate 
how SuDS are dimensioned in different countries in terms of water quantity and evaluate the 
methods through detailed hydraulic modelling software using SWMM (Storm Water 
Management Model).  
 
1.2 Methodology and learning objectives 
The methodology of the report can be split into four main parts: 
 
 Identify and understand which parameters are used to currently dimension individual 
SuDS and compare dimensioning practices in different places around the world.  
 Based on a hypothetical case study, dimension the SuDS based on the dimensioning 
practices and compare the results. 
 Evaluate the different methods by simulating the SuDS using SWMM and historical 
rainfall data. 
 Discuss the effects treatment trains can have on the dimensioning of individual SuDS 
 
The first step is to research, through literature review, how individual SuDS are dimensioned 
and identify the relevant parameters needed. This includes looking at and comparing guidelines 
for SuDS in different countries around the world. The literature search is restricted to countries 
where SuDS have been implemented and local design manuals or guidelines have been 
published in either English or Danish. Then the SuDS are dimensioned for a given hypothetical 
case study and the results are compared. The efficiency of each dimensioning criteria is tested 
by running a long-term simulation in SWMM, a detailed hydrological model created by the 
EPA (Environmental Protection Agency). Finally, the effects treatment trains can have on the 
dimensioning are discussed. 
 
The following are the learning objectives of the thesis: 
 
 Learn to plan and work on a large scientific project. 
 Understand the hydrological processes behind hydrological models and understand 
how these models can be used for design of sustainable urban drainage systems. 
 Through literature review, search for relevant scientific information and critically 
review the information to use and build on in further work. 
 Compare different dimensioning methods, run simulations and evaluate the 
performance of the methods. 
 Set up and write a technical report with a clear and concise text.

The term sustainable urban drainage systems (SUDS) originates in the UK and describes a 
collection of technologies and techniques which can be used to handle stormwater and runoff 
in a more sustainable way than other, more traditional pipe-based solutions. The word urban is 
sometimes excluded from the term, resulting in sustainable drainage systems (SuDS), although 
it essentially means the same thing. The term SuDS became the terminology used in legislation 
in the UK and will be the term used here to refer to these unconventional nature based practices 
(Fletcher et al., 2015). 
 
Variety of SuDS exist, such as green roofs, swales, detention basins, infiltration systems and 
rain gardens, to mention a few. Some are implemented underground while others are placed on 
the surface, and they come in different shapes and sizes, based on need and availability of space 
(Fletcher et al., 2015). Dimensioning of SuDS is in general based on several factors such as 
rainfall, size of the drainage area, runoff rates/factors, infiltration rates and soil properties 
(Department of planninng and Local Government, 2009; SEMCOG, 2008; Woods-Ballard et 
al., 2017), depending on the type and aim of each system.

2.2 Literature search 
Literature search for scientific papers was performed using the search engine Web of Science. 
The search was conducted using the terminology from Table 2-1 and search phrases such as: 
urban drainage, stormwater, treatment train and management train. In addition, a literature 
search on the internet for reliable manuals from each of those countries from Table 2-1 was 
carried out. As both the USA and Australia are quite large countries and can encompass 
different climate and geology, the search was not only limited to one manual, but manuals from 
different parts of these countries to see if there could be differences in dimensioning methods 
within the same country.  
 
2.3 Modelling of SuDS 
When considering whether or not to implement SuDS in urban areas there are many things that 
need to be taken into consideration. Lerer, Arnbjerg-Nielsen, & Mikkelsen (2015) provide a 
good overview of available decision support tools used for the consideration and 
implementation of SuDS. In addition, several modelling programs have been created which 
allow for modelling of SuDS, either in terms of water quantity, quality or both. Elliott & 
Trowsdale (2007) reviewed ten existing models which allow for the modelling of SuDS 
including programs such as MUSIC, MIKE URBAN and SWMM all of which can be used to 
model water quantity.  
 
MUSIC, which stands for Model for Urban Stormwater Improvement Conseptualisation, is an 
Australian software from eWater that was first developed in 2001. MUSIC was specifically 
created to model SuDS, but the focus is more on the treatment efficiency of the systems rather 
than water quantity reduction (EWater, n.d.). MIKE URBAN is one of many software 
packages, developed by the Danish Hydrological Institute (DHI), that can be used to simulate 
challenges in the water environment. MIKE URBAN is an urban water modelling software 
which includes all water networks in urban areas. This includes modelling of drinking water, 
storm water and wastewater as well as the evaluation of SuDS in terms of water quantity (DHI, 
n.d.; Lerer et al., 2015). SWMM (Storm Water Management Model) is an open source software 
from the United States Environmental Protection Agency (EPA). SWMM is a dynamic rainfall-
runoff simulation model that was first developed in 1971 and can be used to simulate 
hydrologic, hydraulic and water quality for single events as well as time series (Rossman, 
2016). A recent upgrade of SWMM included a LID (Low Impact Development) control which 
allows for the simulation of the reduction in runoff mass load for eight different types of SuDS. 
SWMM has a very user-friendly interphase and is simple in use which is why it was chosen 
for this project.  
 
2.3.1 SWMM 
SWMM is a physically based, discrete-time simulation model that uses the principles of 
conservation of mass, energy and momentum. Each subcatchment is considered to be a 
nonlinear reservoir with inflow, from rainfall and runoff from upstream subcatchments, and 
outflow, which can be a combination of infiltration, evaporation and surface runoff. Figure 2-1 
shows a conceptual view of the processes included in modelling surface runoff. Runoff starts 
to form when the depth in the “reservoir” exceeds the depression storage depth (ds) and is then 
calculated using Manning’s equation. The user can choose between five different infiltration 
methods in SWMM: Horton’s method, Modified Horton method, Green-Ampt method, 
Modified Green-Ampt method and the Curve Number method.

There are two ways to implement SuDS in SWMM. The first method is to place the SuDS 
within an existing catchment where it occupies a part of the catchment. The second one is to 
create a new catchment that is fully occupied by the SuDS. The first approach allows for more 
than one system to be placed in a single catchment, so they act in parallel, while the second 
approach makes it possible to create treatment trains (L. A. Rossman & Huber, 2016).  
 
2.4 Rainfall data 
The calculation of runoff from catchments is either based on a single event or multiple events 
(time series). According to Butler, Digman, Makropoulos, & Davies (2018) there are three 
types of single events: synthetic design storms, historical single events and critical input 
hyetographs (superstorm) and two types of time series: historical time series or synthetic time 
series. 
 
==PAGE:12==
6 
 
According to Butler et al. (2018), intensity-duration-frequency (IDF) curves are the most 
common tool for dimensioning of urban drainage systems. IDF curves give the relationship 
between rainfall intensity (mm/h) and the storm duration (h) for a given return period. An IDF 
curve is often used to create a block rainfall with a constant intensity over the storm duration 
which is then used in runoff calculations. This is done in the Rational Method for example 
where rainfall is assumed to have the same constant intensity over the duration of the rain 
event. This storm is not very realistic, but simple and easy to use.  
 
Time series can either be historical or synthetic (Butler et al., 2018). Historical time series are 
as the name implies measurements of historical rainfall over a long period of time at a certain 
location. They are often used in model simulations as they can be expected to include the 
critical conditions for the location being studied, and therefore giving more reliable results than 
design storms. However, the disadvantage of using historical rain series is that the data sets can 
be very large, resulting in a long simulation times and additional cost. This can be avoided by 
using synthetic time series which are statistically based and can include a small number of 
synthetic storms. Synthetic storms can also be statistically based continuous rainfall time series 
which are similar to historical data. The historical rain series datasets are also not perfect. They 
are very site specific so they can only be applied to a limited area and they are often missing 
parts of the time series.

3.1.5 The Three Point Approach (3PA) 
The Three Point Approach (3PA) was first introduced in 2008 as a tool to guide the decision 
making of urban flood risk management (Fratini, Geldof, Kluck, & Mikkelsen, 2012). The 3PA 
provides stakeholders with a tool that encourages communication and discussion between the 
different participants. It consists of 3 domains: domain of technical optimization, domain of 
urban resilience and spatial planning and domain of day-to-day values. This tool was then 
further modified and extended by Sørup, Lerer, Arnbjerg-Nielsen, Mikkelsen, & Rygaard 
(2016) to a quantitative version of the previous method. The domains were renamed to 
quantitatively distinguish three rainfall domains: (A) the everyday domain referring to rain 
events with return period of 0.2 years, (B) the design domain focusing on rainfall with a 10-
year return period and (C) the extreme domain with rainfall corresponding to a 100-year 
rainfall. They analyzed historical rain series from Denmark and estimated the rainfall depth for 
each of the three domains, see Figure 3-2.  
 
 
Figure 3-2: Depth of rainfall corresponding to return period (Sørup et al., 2016) 
 
The idea behind this was to quantify a rainfall depth corresponding to the return period of each 
domain. This figure can however also be used to estimate rainfall depths for other return 
periods. The 3PA can therefore be used as an alternative to the method to described in Chapter 
3.1.4, to dimension SuDS using only the expected depth of the rain corresponding to a return 
period and the catchment area. This is a very simple method that only considers the total water 
volume, excluding all processes of SuDS such as infiltration and evaporation. The depth of the 
rainfall corresponding to a certain return period can simply be read from the graph and 
multiplied by the catchment area to estimate the required volume of the SuDS.

3.2 Definitions of SuDS 
The functions of SuDS can be divided in several categories depending on the main processes 
of the system. In this project the research and evaluation will be carried out for the following 
categories:  
 Infiltration systems 
 Pervious pavement  
 Bioretention systems 
 Conveyance systems 
 Detention systems  
 Rainwater harvesting  
 Green roofs 
 
SuDS come in various types and sizes and are therefore not always defined in the same manner. 
The definition for each system is therefore provided below for clarification and to ensure that 
the results within each system are comparable.  
 
3.2.1 Infiltration systems 
Infiltration systems are usually rectangular systems 
that have been filled with porous material that 
allows for storage of water and infiltration into the 
subsoil (see Figure 3-3). Dimensioning of these 
systems depends therefore on the infiltration rate of 
the soil underneath the system and the storage 
volume in the storage layer. Infiltration systems 
usually take up a small percentage of the 
contributing area or around 2-3% (Department of 
Water and Swan River Trust, 2007). In this project 
infiltration is considered constant throughout the 
duration of the rain event if nothing else is specified.  
 
3.2.2 Pervious pavement 
Pervious pavement is very similar to infiltration 
systems in terms of processes. It consists of a 
permeable surface layer over a porous material that 
allows for storage and infiltration (see Figure 3-4). 
Pervious pavement can be applied to areas where 
pavement is required but infiltration to the soil is still 
desired, such as parking lots. The surface layer can 
either consist of a porous pavement that allows 
water to infiltrate through, or permeable pavement, 
that in itself is impervious but is constructed with 
areas that allow for infiltration to the subsoil 
(Woods-Ballard et al., 2017).

3.2.3 Bioretention systems 
Bioretention systems are mainly used to 
improve water quality by filtering water that 
percolates 
through 
the 
system. 
The 
dimensioning of these systems is therefore 
usually based on obtaining required treatment 
efficiency rather than water quantity. They 
consist of a surface storage and a filter medium 
above a drainage layer. Bioretention systems 
can allow for infiltration but they can also be 
constructed with impermeable geomembrane 
liner if infiltration is not desired. They can also provide storage in the drainage layer. Therefore, 
the dimensioning of bioretention systems is based on infiltration rates, drainage rates and 
storage volumes (see Figure 3-5). The advantage of these systems is that they are usually small 
and flexible in shape so they can be installed in a variety of places. The issue with bioretention 
systems is however that they are relatively expansive compared to many of the other systems 
and are therefore best located at, or close to, the source of water and work best for treating flow 
from small catchments (Department of Water and Swan River Trust, 2007; Woods-Ballard et 
al., 2017). They are also often placed upstream of other systems, such as infiltration or 
detention systems, to extend the lifetime of the downstream systems, by removing sediments. 
Bioretention systems are mostly designed to manage and treat small and frequent rain events 
(Woods-Ballard et al., 2017).  
 
3.2.4 Conveyance systems 
Conveyance systems differ from all the above-
mentioned systems as they are mainly used, 
and therefore dimensioned, for conveyance. 
Swales are the most common type of these 
systems. 
They 
are 
usually 
trapezoidal, 
vegetated, open channels (see Figure 3-6), and 
the dimensioning focuses 
on providing 
appropriate cross-sectional area to transfer the 
water with a certain maximum velocity. Swales 
can therefore be used in between all of the other systems to transfer water from one system to 
another. As swales are usually vegetated, significant infiltration can occur along the 
conveyance path, but this is generally not included in the dimensioning of these systems.

Detention systems are large open depressions 
with controlled outflow that allow water to be 
temporarily stored during large rain events. 
These systems often have a multifunctional 
purpose, e.g. a football field or a playing ground, 
that one would not think of as being part of a 
drainage system. Due to this multifunctional 
purpose it is important that the spaces can be 
fully drained, so that the space can regain its usual function after the rain event (SEMCOG,

3.2.6 Green roofs and rainwater harvesting 
Green roofs and rainwater harvesting are always 
placed first in a treatment train due to their 
elevation above ground. Green roofs store water 
in the soil placed on top of the roof where the 
available storage volume depends on the 
volume and porosity of this soil (see Figure

reduction of rainwater tanks is therefore simply based on the volume of the rainwater tank and 
the water usage (see Figure 3-9). Both of these systems have relatively small storage compared 
to the other systems and do therefore not have a huge impact on the dimensioning of other 
systems further down in the treatment train during large rain events. They can however 
significantly reduce runoff from small rain events, and 
therefore annual runoff, if empty at the right time 
(Berndtsson, 2010; Locatelli, Mark, Mikkelsen, et al., 
2014). However, one issue with including green roofs and 
rainwater tanks in treatment trains is that it is very uncertain 
if the full storage is actually available when needed. The 
emptying of these systems can be difficult to predict since 
it can for example depend on weather conditions 
(Berndtsson, 2010) and water usage prior to the rainfall.

Even though studies have shown that the use of treatment trains can improve the performance

 Green roof – infiltration/bioretention/detention 
 Rainwater harvesting – infiltration/ bioretention/detention 
 Bioretention – infiltration 
 Bioretention – detention 
 Infiltration – detention

3.3 Model application 
3.3.1 Parameter sensitivity 
A detailed hydrological model can be a very valuable tool to verify dimensions of SuDS. It is 
however important to keep in mind that a model is only a tool and can never fully describe 
reality. The phrase “garbage in, garbage out” which describes the requirement of a reliable 
input data is very descriptive for any modelling program, as the results from a model always 
depend highly on the input parameters. In addition, minor changes of some parameters can 
have more effects on the results than others, called sensitive parameters. It is therefore 
important to first identify the most sensitive parameters and then properly define them in order 
to get reliable results. This is often carried out using calibration, where parameters are adjusted 
to match the output of measured data. The sensitivity of parameters in SWMM has been the 
core of several studies, both were the focus is on the catchment parameters as well as the LID 
specific parameters.  
 
According to Zaghloul (1983), the catchment imperviousness is the most sensitive parameter 
where higher percentage of imperviousness causes increased runoff volumes and peak flows. 
This is in accordance with the results of Barco, Kenneth, Wong, Stenstrom, & Asce (2008) and 
Palermo, Zischg, Sitzenfrei, Rauch, & Piro (2018). The second most sensitive parameter 
according to Zaghloul (1983) was the width of the overland flow which effects the peak flow 
and runoff volumes. This is supported by Li, Wang, Xiong, & Chen (2014) who looked at the 
sensitivity of 12 parameters and how they affect the runoff, peak discharge and the time to the 
peak. Additionally, Li et al. (2014) found that the catchment area and slope were sensitive. The 
impervious depression storage has also been identified sensitive in several studies (Barco et 
al., 2008; Krebs, Kokkonen, Valtanen, Koivusalo, & Setälä, 2013; Palermo et al., 2018). 
Additionally, Li et al. (2014) concluded that Manning’s N is a sensitive parameter which is in 
accordance with Krebs et al. (2013) and Rosa, Clausen, & Dietz (2015), as well as minimum 
Horton’s infiltration rate. Finally, studies which have specifically focused on the sensitivity of 
parameters of the LID control in SWMM suggest that the soil hydraulic conductivity is one of 
the most sensitive parameters (Palermo et al., 2018; Rosa et al., 2015). Another sensitive LID 
control parameter is, according to Rosa et al. (2015), the initial soil moisture deficit. 
 
Based on the above summary, the following parameters need to be considered when setting up 
a simulation in SWMM: catchment area, imperviousness, width and slope, impervious 
depression storage, Manning’s N, minimum Horton’s infiltration rate, hydraulic conductivity, 
initial soil moisture deficit and vegetated volume.  
 
Some of these parameters are not relevant to the study performed here as the parameters are 
simply not applied in the SWMM model. One of these parameters is minimum Horton’s rate 
which indicates the minimum rate of infiltration on the Hortons curve in Horton’s infiltration 
method. Green-Ampt Method is however used in this project. Another example is the initial 
soil moisture deficit, which indicates how much water is present in a system before the start of 
a rain event. It is however only important for the simulation of a single rain event but not for a 
long-term simulation as it will only affect the volume of the first event of the rain series. The 
value selected in the SWMM simulations in this project will therefore not affect the results of 
a long-term simulation. 
 
Several of these parameters are not considered sensitive for this project as the values in the 
SWMM simulation are the same as used in the dimensioning of the systems. These parameters 
include the size of the catchment, the fraction of the impervious area and the hydraulic 
==PAGE:23==
17 
 
conductivity. The size of the catchment area directly affects the rainfall volume and therefore 
the runoff volume. For a hypothetical case as is used in this project, the selected value should 
not be an issue as long as the same area is used in the dimensioning of the SuDS and the 
SWMM simulation. The same applies to the fraction of the impervious area. This parameter is 
important as paved areas produces more runoff than pervious areas. The soil hydraulic 
conductivity represents water movement through media and therefore affects the infiltration 
volume. For all these parameters, the same value is used when dimensioning the system as in 
the SWMM simulation.  
 
The parameters that do not fall under the two categories above require more consideration. 
Impervious depression storage represents initial losses due to depressions/holes in the 
impervious area that stores water until it evaporates. High values will therefore result in lower 
volumes of runoff and vice versa for low values. Manning’s N represents the roughness of a 
surface which affects the runoff from a catchment and depends on the type of the surface. 
Finally, the width and slope of a catchment can affect the characteristics of the runoff as 
mentioned before

3.3.4 Data input 
In addition to the parameters needed to dimension the systems manually, SWMM requires the 
input of several more parameters. Based on the literature review of parameter sensitivity 
conducted and explained in Chapter 3.3.1, several catchment parameters need to be specifically 
considered or tested. These parameters are catchment width and slope, depression storage, and 
Manning’s N. These parameters would generally be selected based on measurements from an 
actual catchment, but as the study area in this project is hypothetical, that is not possible and 
realistic ranges must be tested or selected based on previously calibrated values.  
 
Two of these parameters are usually site specific and the sensitivity is therefore tested in 
SWMM. Firstly, the sensitivity of the catchment width is tested assuming realistic range of 50 
to 100 m. Simulations using a return period of 0.5 years resulted in few additional overflows 
when 100 m are used compared to 50 m, indicating that the width is not significantly important 
for small areas, and the width of 100 m is selected. Secondly, the sensitivity of the depression 
storage is tested using the typical range of impervious areas in Denmark of 0.5-1 mm (Winther 
et al., 2011). Simulations with both 0.5 mm and 1 mm were therefore tested. In terms of 
overflows, no significant difference could be detected, and the value of the depression storage 
is set to 0.5 mm.  
 
According to the literature provided in Chapter 3.3.1, the catchment slope can be sensitive, 
especially in large areas. Slope is however generally low in Denmark and the catchment is 
small, so the value of the slope is not expected to have significant effects on the results and the 
area is given a slope of 0.5%. Typical value for Manning’s N for overland flow on asphalt or 
concrete is around 0.01 and for grass between 0.15-0.41 depending on the type of grass (L. 
Rossman, 2015). As the catchment is assumed to consist of only impervious pavement, the 
value of Manning’s N for pervious areas will not affect the results as it is not applied in the 
simulation. Manning’s N for impervious areas must however be selected and the default value 
of 0.01 is applied which is in accordance with calibrated values from Qin, Li, & Fu (2013),

3.3.5 Application of SWMM  
The LID controls in SWMM are relatively new and the program, as any other program, comes 
with its flaws and limitations. In this chapter the problems/limitations faced over the course of 
this project are explained and discussed.  
 
Void ratio vs porosity 
Porosity is often used in the world of urban drainage to indicate the available pore space of a 
material. It is the fraction of the volume of voids over the total volume of the material, and 
therefore has the available range of 0 to 1. In the storage layer, SWMM however uses void

Another point that needs to be considered is the drainage from the detention system. If not

Calculation of return periods of overflows from SuDS 
As the LID controls in SWMM are not specifically designed to dimension SuDS but rather to 
investigate the total runoff reduction caused by the systems, it can be difficult to obtain the 
required results, which for this project are the overflows from the systems. In some cases, like 
for infiltration systems, it is possible to simply look at the flows in the link draining the 
catchment area, and everything that cannot be stored in the system or infiltrated, is overflow. 
To obtain the overflows from systems with drainage it is important to direct the drainage to a 
different node than the runoff from the catchment so that the flow draining from the catchment 
represents overflows from the system as described above. Swales are however more difficult 
as they are used for conveyance and the simulation results, which mainly focus on the volume 
reduction, do not distinguish between runoff transported by the swale and excess water that the 
swale cannot convey and would therefore result in overflows. It is therefore not possible to 
evaluate the dimensioning of SuDS in SWMM with the current version. 
 
When calculating the number of overflows in SWMM the user has to define what an event is.  
An event is defined based on the minimum time that needs to pass between two events. Properly 
defining an event is especially important for systems dimensioned for lower return periods. 
Larger rainfall events are usually shorter and more intense and one constant overflow is likely 
to occur while smaller rainfall events are longer and can cause several overflows over a short 
period. If the time between events is defined too short, these will be counted as several 
overflows. The time of an event is taken into account in SWMM by selecting a separation time 
(hours) when the number of overflows is calculated.

included in the catchment, the size of the SuDS can affect the runoff volume from the 
catchment. The treatment train method, where the SuDS fill up a whole catchment, ensures 
however the same amount of runoff, no matter the size of the system. The latter method, using 
the treatment train approach, is therefore applied in this project to make sure that the runoff

4.1.2 Discussion 
The sizing of infiltration systems, according to the methods provided in Table 4-1, depends on 
the storage- and infiltration volumes. Even though at first glance these methods may look 
different, if rearranged they are in fact dimensioned in a very similar way, just with minor 
modifications between methods. These minor modifications can however change the results 
considerably. 
 
The first difference is the area of the system where infiltration takes place. In the UK infiltration 
occurs only through the base whereas in Australia the infiltration is both through the base and 
the sides. The LAR-dimensionering regneark, used to dimension systems in Denmark, allows 
the user to choose between infiltration occurring only through the sides (traditionally used) or 
through the bottom as well. In Michigan, surface storage is included in the dimensioning, which 
will be ignored here based on the previous definitions of the systems in Chapter 3.2.1. If that 
is done and the equation rearranged, then it is dimensioned the same way as it is in the UK. 
SWMM only accounts for infiltration which occurs through the bottom, so it is to be expected 
that systems that include more than that will seem under dimensioned when simulated in 
SWMM.  
 
The dimensioning methods for South Carolina focus on finding the maximum depth of water 
that can be infiltrated, then calculating the depth of the surface ponding and the drainage layer 
and finally calculating the required surface area for a given event. If the depth (dwater=dgravel) is 
however set constant as for the other methods, the required surface area can be calculated as in 
the UK and Michigan (assuming surface ponding is excluded). The only difference is then the 
safety factor of 2, for the infiltration rate. This factor is applied when using field verified 
infiltration rates to account for possible compression of the soil during construction. This factor 
can therefore also be excluded here because the infiltration rate is only an assumption and not 
field verified.  
 
A similar trend can be seen for the Australian equation where a ‘point’ soil hydraulic 
conductivity moderation factor is included. The method assumes that the hydraulic 
conductivity is measured on site and the factor is included to take into account that the soil is 
heterogenous. If the infiltration is not based on detailed measurements, as in the hypothetical 
case used here, this moderation factor can be ignored.  
 
One of the main differences between the methods in the UK, USA and Australia is the time 
used to calculate the infiltration volume. The time to fill the infiltration facility (typically 2 
hours) is used in South Carolina while the storm duration is used in the UK. In Michigan the 
infiltration period during the storm when the bed is receiving runoff and capable of infiltrating 
at design rate is used (typically 6-12 hours), and in Australia the storm duration plus the time 
of concentration is used. For such a small area, the time of concentration is however negligible 
and therefore excluded in the calculations. 
 
Another factor that can cause differences between the systems is the rainfall duration time. In 
Australia, storm duration from six minutes to 72 hours should be calculated and the one that 
gives the largest volume is used. The IDF curves used here do however only cover durations 
up to 48 hours so the durations up to 72 hours cannot be checked. In the UK they same method 
is used except that storm durations from 15 minutes to 48 hours are checked. The CN method, 
which is used to dimension SuDS in the USA according to the two manuals, uses a 24-hour 
rainfall with the return period of 2 years to dimension the systems. In order to compare the 
==PAGE:34==
28 
 
different dimensioning methods for different return periods, the systems have here been tested 
for different return periods, still using only the rainfall with the duration of 24 hours.  
 
The runoff coefficient can also cause differences in the runoff volumes from the catchment. In 
Australia, a specific method is used to calculate the runoff coefficient based on the rainfall 
intensity of a rainfall with a return period of 10 years and the return period of the rainfall that 
the systems should be dimensioned for, see Equation 11. This results in much lower values 
than in the UK for example, were a runoff coefficient between 0.8-1 can be assumed for 
impervious areas (the average of 0.9 is applied here).  
 
The Rational Method is used to estimate runoff from sites in the UK as explained in chapter 
3.1.1. The equation for infiltration systems seems however to exclude the runoff coefficient. 
This means that all the water that lands on the catchment is expected to end up in the infiltration 
system, which is not likely to happen in reality due to small depressions on the surface, 
therefore resulting in larger runoff volumes.

results. Looking at the Danish results, with and without infiltration through the bottom, shows 
that the method that includes infiltration through the bottom is just a little under dimensioned, 
while the one which assumes infiltration occurs only through the sides is over dimensioned. 
SWMM only accounts for infiltration through the bottom which could be one of the reasons 
why some of the methods seem to be under dimensioned. The calculations assume infiltration 
through the sides which is not included in the SWMM simulation, resulting in lower infiltration 
volume and therefore more frequent overflows. This raises the question of whether SWMM is 
actually the correct tool to estimate the efficiency of those systems which assume infiltration 
either through the sides and bottom, or only through the sides instead of only through the 
bottom.  
 
The surface area calculated by the 3PA and the simulated return periods are in the same order 
of magnitude as the other more detailed methods. The 3PA over dimensions the storage layer 
for all return periods, but still falls within the boundaries of the other methods. This could be 
due to the fact that the 3PA does not take into account any processes such as infiltration which 
reduce the amount of water the system is required to contain. This means that with a larger area 
the infiltration volume increases, and the losses not considered by the 3PA increase as well.

4.2.2 Discussion 
The methods for dimensioning pervious pavement provided in Table 4-3 focus on the storage 
volume in the sub-base and the infiltration volume. The method in Western Australia is 
however different and focuses only on the required surface area of the pavement needed to 
allow all the rainfall to infiltrate. The manual from the UK does not provide a specific equation 
to use to dimension pervious pavements but mentions that it should be done using standard 
methods which include infiltration, attenuation and flow controls.  It can therefore be assumed 
that they are referring to similar methods as are used for dimensioning infiltration systems. The 
manual also states that it is important to make sure that the infiltration rate through the 
pavement is sufficient but that it is in general acceptable and the focus is therefore rather on 
the storage volume (Woods-Ballard et al., 2017). In Denmark, the only difference between 
infiltration systems and pervious pavement is the calculation of the afløbstal. The system is 
also dimensioned for the required depth and not the length. 
 
The two equations from the USA are the same if rearranged for the same value. The only 
difference is that the infiltration rate in the equation from South Carolina is divided by two as 
a factor of safety. Like with the infiltration system, this can be excluded in the calculations here 
as this applies to field verified rates in order to account for compression of the soil during 
construction. Although the equations are the same, the duration of infiltration is different. In 
South Carolina the time of infiltration is only 2 hours whereas it is assumed to be 6 hours in 
Michigan (same value as used for infiltration systems).  
 
The idea behind both infiltration systems and pervious pavement is to store and infiltrate water 
to the subsoil. By comparing Table 4-1 and Table 4-3 it becomes evident that these systems 
are not always dimensioned in the same way. The main difference is that infiltration in pervious 
pavements occurs only through the bottom of the system, whereas for infiltration systems, the 
infiltration can occur either only through the bottom, only through the sides or through both 
simultaneously.

4.3.2 Discussion 
Bioretention systems are mainly used to treat pollution in runoff and are therefore usually 
dimensioned based on treatment efficiency rather than water quantity. Bioretention systems are 
also often included partially or fully in other systems, such as a bioretention swale and a 
bioretention basin, or considered to be a combination of one or more systems. This is the case 
in Denmark where a bioretention basin is considered to be a combination of rain garden and an 
infiltration/drainage system. The LAR-dimensionering Regneark does therefore only calculate 
the volume for a rain garden, that is only the required volume of the top soil layer without the 
storage layer underneath. This combination of systems and focus on water quality over quantity 
can explain the difference in the dimensioning methods in Table 4-6. In South Carolina the 
focus is only on the total volume reduction. In the UK and Michigan, the required surface area 
is first calculated, followed by the total storage in the whole system, including surface ponding 
and/or storage volume in soil layers. A similar equation is used in Australia except that it has 
been rearranged to calculate the maximum infiltration rate and it does not consider the total 
storage volume of the system.  
 
In the UK and Michigan, the same equation is used to calculate the required surface area. The 
volume used to calculate the required area is however not the same for the two methods. In the 
UK the volume from the 1-year rainfall event is used, but the required storage volume is used 
in Michigan. This detail can produce large differences in terms of the required storage area and 
therefore in the depth of the gravel layer. This difference is due to the fact that in the UK the 
system is dimensioned to provide good pollutant removal for events that occur on average once 
every year or more often, but in Michigan it is dimensioned for volume reduction. The total 
volume reduction is also calculated differently as surface ponding is not included in the 
equation for UK and the storage layer is a combination of two layers, the filter media and the 
gravel layer.  
 
The two equations from the USA show that there can indeed be difference between how SuDS 
are dimensioned in the same country, where one method first dimensions the required surface 
area and the storage volume is calculated differently. The equation provided to calculate the 
total storage volume in Michigan includes only one layer, the underdrain layer. The description 
of bioretention systems in the manual does however include more than one layer so it can be 
assumed that the underdrain layer represents the filter- and drainage layers. Drainage from the 
systems is also usually not included in the equations, except in Michigan. There the required 
surface area is calculated with or without drainage, where the system without drainage has 
infiltration instead. In South Carolina where the surface area is not specifically calculated first, 
the total storage volume is a combination of the volume in the two soil layers and the surface 
ponding. This equation is the only method that excludes infiltration which is included in all of 
the other methods.  
 
To simulate bioretention systems in SWMM the user is required to use the surface- and soil 
layers, with the storage- and drainage layers optional. The calculation methods presented in 
Table 4-6 for bioretention basins are very different which makes it difficult to compare the 
results. Two methods (UK and Michigan) calculate the required surface area first and then the 
total volume reduction. Another method only calculates the total volume reduction (South 
Carolina). The fourth focuses on the maximum infiltration rate (Australia). And the last does 
not include a gravel layer and can only calculates the depth of the filter layer in a rain garden 
(Denmark). It is therefore not considered to be useful to dimension and compare these systems 
as too many assumptions would have to be made for each individual method.

4.4.2 Discussion 
Table 4-7 shows that swales are dimensioned with Manning’s equation in all the manuals, with 
focus on the required flow and velocity, excluding possible infiltration. The only difference 
between the two equations is the unit conversion factor of 1.49 which is added when the flow 
is calculated in English units. Even though the same equation is used to dimension swales, 
there can be a difference in how the peak runoff is calculated which can result in different 
dimensions. Dimensioning of swales is not included in the Danish LAR-dimensionering 
regneark but Manning’s equation is generally used to calculate flow on surfaces, so it can be 
assumed that the same method is used in Denmark (Winther et al., 2011).  
 
According to the manuals swales are usually dimensioned to convey a specific rain event or 
two, but not for different return periods, depending on the location and conditions as is usually 
the case for the other systems. The guidelines also include the general range of the bottom 
width and depth. In the UK swales are usually designed to convey the 30-year rainfall event. 
Bottom width is generally 0.5-2 m and the maximum depth is around 400-600 mm. Flow 
velocities should preferably be kept below 1 m/s for extreme events to prevent erosion (Woods-
Ballard et al., 2017). In Australia minor and major floods are used to dimension swales, usually 
the 5 and 100 year ARI in Melbourne (Melbourne Water, 2005) and the 6 month and 5 year 
ARI in Western Australia (Department of Water and Swan River Trust, 2007). In Michigan, 
swales are dimensioned using the peak discharge from the 10-year storm event. The average 
depth of the swale should be 12 inch (30 cm) and the bottom width 2-8 feet (60-240 cm) 
(SEMCOG, 2008). In South Carolina swales are designed to convey the peak flow from the 2- 
and 10-year storms at non-erosive velocities (which is generally less than 6 feet/s = 1.8 m/s). 
==PAGE:46==
40 
 
Bottom width should be 4-8 feet (120-140 cm) and the depth should be kept below 4 inches 
(Ellis et al., 2014). 
 
Swales are one of the systems included in the LID controls in SWMM and require only the use 
of the surface layer. They are however, as mentioned before, not able to evaluate the 
dimensioning of swales as the program does not distinguish between runoff conveyed by the 
swale and additional runoff/overflow. The focus of all the SuDS simulated in SWMM is on the 
reduction in runoff flow volume. The same applies to swales even though infiltration is not 
included in any of the dimensioning methods in Table 4-7 and an infiltration rate is not 
specifically defined in the LID control for swales. This means that SWMM requires the value 
of the surface area of the swale, not only the cross-sectional area which is what the 
dimensioning methods focus on. However, the length is usually known when swales are being 
dimensioned in reality, as the swale is used to convey water from one point to another, so when 
the cross-sectional area has been calculated using Manning’s equation, the surface area can be 
calculated.

4.5.2 Discussion 
Denmark is the only country that provides guidelines on how to dimension detention basins. 
There they are dimensioned using a block rain, as for all the other systems, and the afløbstal

the LAR-dimensionering regneark. The other manuals do not include equations for how to 
dimension detention systems even though they all cover the concept of the system 
 
Detention basins differ from the other methods that have been discussed here as they only hold 
on to excess water until it can be managed by other systems, without really using the ideas 
behind SuDS such as infiltration. They have also been included as a part of the pipe-based 
urban drainage system. This can be seen in the equation presented for Denmark in Table 4-8 
where the dry weather flow is included in the equation which is only relevant for pipe-based 
systems but not for SuDS.  
 
Detention basins are not one of the eight systems that can be simulated using the LID controls 
in SWMM, but they are a part of the more traditional pipe-based drainage system in SWMM 
where they are referred to as storage units. These storage units can only be added outside the 
catchment as a node connected to the pipe system, whereas the LID controls can be applied 
either within the catchment or as a separate catchment. It is however possible to simulate 
detention basins using the LID controls using the rain barrels control as detention basins as 
described in Chapter 3.3.5. The Storage unit does however provide more settings to describe 
the outflow from the basin, for example evaporation and infiltration (L. Rossman, 2015).

4.6.2 Discussion 
Rainwater harvesting systems are usually not dimensioned specifically for runoff reduction but 
rather based on the demand and the volume that is available for capture. They are usually rather 
small compared to many of the other SuDS and therefore not effectively applied to avoid 
flooding during large events even though they can reduce the amount of runoff on annual scale. 
In the USA and Australia, the dimensioning depends on the average annual rainfall and the 
required demand of water from the tank. Even though costs are not included in the 
dimensioning equations, it is mentioned in the manuals that it is important to take costs into 
consideration when rainwater tanks are designed, as they can increase substantially with larger 
tanks. 
 
Rainwater tanks are not included in the LAR-dimensionering regneark from Denmark. This 
could be because there have neither been environmental nor economic reasons to implement 
rainwater harvesting on a large scale in Denmark where groundwater is of sufficient quantity 
and quality  (Mikkelsen, Adeler, Albrechtsen, & Henze, 1999). In other parts of the world, for 
example in Australia, conditions are very different. Due to hot and dry climate and limited 
fresh water, rainwater tanks are commonly used and are even the main source of drinking water 
in some cases (EnHealth Council, 2004). 
 
4.6.3 Evaluation with SWMM 
Rainwater tanks can be simulated in SWMM and only require the use of the storage layer and 
the drain layer. In order to evaluate the dimensioning of rainwater tanks the drainage from the 
tanks must be sent to a different node than the outflow from the catchment, as described in 
Chapter 3.3.5. Rainwater tanks are often used in treatment trains as they can help reduce the 
needed size of the systems further along in the treatment train. This was tested in SWMM by 
creating a treatment train consisting of a rainwater tank followed by the infiltration systems 
dimensioned and evaluated in Chapter 4.1.3. Two sizes of rainwater tanks, 1 m3 and 3 m3, were 
tested with constant outflow of 0.0001 m3/s. The comparison of the treatment trains and the 
single systems can be seen in Table 4-10. Figure 4-3 to Figure 4-7 show the results for each 
method, one figure per dimensioned return period. The results show that including the 
rainwater tank increases the simulated return period. There is however one exception from this, 
that is for the 5-year return period in South Carolina, where the system without the tanks has a 
higher return period than the systems including the tanks. The difference seems large, in terms 
of return periods, but is caused by only one additional event for the system without a tank. The 
reason for this could be changes in the flow to the infiltration systems when the tank is added, 
causing a small buildup of water which then results in the additional event.

4.7.2 Discussion 
In South Carolina (USA), green roofs are dimensioned based on the available storage volume 
in the media and drainage layer, as can be seen in Table 4-11. The other manuals cover green 
roofs in terms of materials, construction etc. but do not include any information on how to 
dimension them in terms of water quantity. One of the reasons for this could be that volume 
reduction is generally not considered to be one of the most important functions of green roofs 
even though they have been proven to be able to substantially reduce annual runoff (Locatelli, 
Mark, Steen Mikkelsen, et al., 2014). Another is that green roofs are often added to already 
consisting houses, that come with limitations in terms of weight and space. The runoff 
dynamics from green roofs are also dependent on several parameters such as vegetation, slope, 
season, weather, age of the green roof etc. (Berndtsson, 2010). The interactions of all those 
parameters and related processes, make it difficult to provide an equation that is applicable for 
all scenarios.  
 
Green roofs are considered a variation of a bioretention cell in SWMM, with a soil layer at the 
top and a drainage mat underneath instead of the gravel aggregate in the storage layer (L. A. 
Rossman & Huber, 2016). Even though the processes of green roofs can be difficult to simulate, 
Cipolla, Maglionico, & Stojkov (2016) compared results of experimental green roof 
monitoring with simulations in SWMM and concluded that SWMM is capable of assessing the 
performance of green roofs. Green roofs are always, if included, first in treatment trains 
because of their elevation and can only catch water that falls on the roof. They should therefore 
be implemented as a part of the catchment and not as an individual catchment like was used to 
simulate the other systems in this project.

5 Discussion  
5.1 Dimensioning criteria around the world 
Literature review searching for comparisons of different dimensioning criteria did not provide 
any results, suggesting that a comparison like the one presented in this project has not been 
carried out before. Software and several tools are however available for use when considering 
the implementation of SuDS. Lerer et al. (2015) provides a review of tools which can be used 
in the decision-making process of implementing SuDS. This overview does not only include 
tools which focus on quantification but also tools that can be used to spatially analyze SuDS 
and tools that can help with choosing between different options. Another tool is B£ST (Benefits 
of SuDS tool), created by CIRIA which was released in 2015 and updated in 2019 (Digman, 
Horton, Ashley, & Gill, 2019). This tool can be used to asses and compare benefits of SuDS in 
terms of environmental, social and financial aspects.  
 
The results presented in chapter 4 indicate that SuDS are indeed dimensioned differently in 
different parts of the world. The manuals do not always provide dimensioning methods for all 
the systems investigated in this project. Infiltration systems, pervious pavement, bioretention 
basins and conveyance systems were for example well presented in most of the manuals, and 
equations were provided for dimensioning in terms of water quantity. Detention systems, 
rainwater harvesting and green roofs were however not as well presented, even though most of 
the manuals covered the concepts. The focus for these systems was more on how to construct 
them, appropriate materials and maintenance requirements. It could be that the dimensioning 
of these systems depends more on other published documents for these particular systems or 
that they are usually not dimensioned for water quantity.  
 
The selection of the method used to calculate the runoff from a catchment can affect the size 
of a system. Few of the manuals provided more than one method to calculate runoff. In that 
case, the recommended method was used to calculate the runoff. In the manuals gathered for 
this project, there is sometimes an inconsistency between how the runoff is calculated in 
general and how it is then calculated when the SuDS are dimensioned. Where the methods for 
calculating runoff are explained in both the UK and Australia, the intensity of the rain is 
selected from the IDF curve based on the time of concentration of the catchment, whereas in 
the equations for the individual SuDS the rain duration is used. This inconsistency could be 
due to the fact that dimensioning of runoff for the traditional pipe-based drainage systems is 
based on the time of concentration whereas the storm duration is more commonly used for 
SuDS. It is also possible that this was simply overlooked when writing the chapters on the 
general methods to calculate runoff and that the previously used method was copied from other 
older material, referring to the traditional way of doing so.  
 
Comparison of the methods provided in the manuals to the 3PA resulted in dimensions and 
return periods in the same order of magnitude as for the more complicated methods. This 
indicates that simple methods, such as the 3PA, could be used to dimension SuDS either as a 
preliminary dimensioning tool or as a dimensioning method. The results for the infiltration 
systems and pervious pavement are however interesting. The 3PA over dimensions all of the 
infiltration systems but under dimensions most of the pervious pavement. The difference 
between these two methods lies in the surface area as the infiltration systems are dimensioned 
for the length whereas the pervious pavement is dimensioned for the depth. This means that 
the infiltration volume in the simulation for pervious pavement is always the same but increases 
==PAGE:54==
48 
 
for the infiltration systems with increasing return period. Changes in the predefined surface 
area of the pervious pavement can therefore affect the infiltration volume. The same applies to 
the infiltration system, where a deeper system will result in a lower surface area and therefore 
lower infiltration volume and vice versa.   
 
It is evident that the dimensioning of SuDS is a complicated process and many things have to 
be taken into consideration.  For most of the systems, additional criteria must also be met when 
they are dimensioned, such as half emptying time, treatment efficiency, minimum required 
depth or surface area etc. Often these requirements also differ between countries, which can 
affect the dimensions of the SuDS.  
 
5.2 Simplifications and model limitations 
Nature is unpredictable and it is impossible to precisely predict what will happen in the future. 
The problem at hand is therefore to find the right balance between the level of details required 
and the time it takes to produce them. The calculation methods provided in the manuals are 
only a simplification of the processes happening in reality. Computer programs are also not 
perfect and often require more input data and therefore additional assumptions.  
 
Elliott & Trowsdale (2007) provide a comparison of 10 different models that can be used to 
simulate SuDS. SWMM was included in the comparison but the research was conducted before 
the LID control was added to the software. SWMM has been used to simulate SuDS in several 
recent studies taking advantage of the LID control (Chui, Liu, & Zhan, 2016; Goncalves et al., 
2018; Krebs et al., 2013) and the sensitivity of parameters has been investigated in several 
studies (Barco et al., 2008; Li et al., 2014) including the LID control parameters (Krebs et al., 
2013; Rosa et al., 2015). These results indicate that with properly selected parameters, SWMM 
is capable of simulating SuDS. Comparison between the different simulation models has also 
been tested. Schiolborg & Lindbladh (2016) simulated bioretention performance in SWMM 
and MIKE URBAN and concluded that the results were similar. 
 
SWMM was selected for this project as it is an open source, easy to use and has a specific LID 
control that allows for simulation of SuDS, both parallel and in series. The LID controls in 
SWMM are not specifically created to validate and calculate the return periods of individual 
SuDS, but rather to calculate the difference in total inflow and outflow from the system. The 
program can however be used to evaluate the efficiency of SuDS in terms of overflows from 
systems that utilize infiltration, such as infiltration systems, bioretention systems and pervious 
pavement, as the overflows from the catchment represent overflows from the system itself. For 
systems which utilize drainage the program allows the user to choose whether the drain is 
connected to the same node as the outflow from the catchment or not. By selecting a different 
node, the overflow from the catchment will therefore represent overflows from the system. 
However, this makes it impossible to test the efficiency of swales in terms of conveyance as 
the program does only focus on the total volumes and does not distinguish between runoff 
conveyed by the swale and excess water that the swale cannot convey and overflows.  
 
SWMM only allows a porosity in the storage layer of less than 0.5, because void ratio is used 
there instead of porosity. Converting between void ratio and porosity is not an issue, but this 
inconsistency that void ratio is used only for the storage layer, while porosity is used in other 
layers can cause confusion. First of all, this can cause confusion if the parameter is not given a 
proper consideration and used as porosity. Secondly, the allowed range of values can be an 
issue as geocellular storage systems and engineered soils can have porosities up to 0.95 
==PAGE:55==
49 
 
(Woods-Ballard et al., 2017) which means that the software is not capable of simulating this 
type of media. A first-time user is therefore likely to mistake the void ratio for porosity as it 
has the same allowed range as porosity, even though the real value scale is from 0 to infinity. 
 
Based on the limitations described above, it is evident that SWMM is not the perfect software 
to evaluate the dimensioning of SuDS. However, the user-friendly interphase, which does not 
require professional modelling skills, and the fact that it is an open source does counterbalance 
many of the disadvantages. While using a more complicated software, requires defining 
additional parameters which can be both time consuming and increases the chances of 
mistakes.  
 
5.3 Current state and development of SuDS in series 
The idea of SuDS in series is widely discussed in the field of urban drainage, both in literature 
and in guidelines for construction and dimensioning of SuDS. The discussion is however 
mostly related to the improvements in water quality and the extended lifetime of systems. What 
is missing is the discussion of treatment trains in terms of water quantity, which can be just as, 
if not more, relevant for places where flooding is the main issue more so than water quality. 
Just from considering a simple treatment train consisting of a swale and an infiltration system, 
it is evident that the order of the train affects the sizing of the two individual systems. An 
example of this is a swale placed before an infiltration system which needs to be much larger 
than if it is placed after the infiltration system, because the infiltration system is capable of 
infiltrating and holding back part of the water, whereas the main focus of the swale is simply 
to convey the peak flow.  
 
Literature search for treatment trains, both in scientific literature as well as online, did not result 
in many hits, indicating that even though the use of those is often recommended, not many 
articles have been published on the matter. However, the search showed that tools that can be 
of an assistance when working with SuDS in series do exist. One of those tools is the 
stormwater treatment train assessment tool (STTAT), which is a scoring system for SuDS in 
treatment trains focusing on water quality (Jefferies, Duffy, Berwick, McLean, & Hemingway, 
2009). Other tools are more site specific such as the LID Compliance Calculator spreadsheet 
tool for South Carolina. This tool can be used to dimension SuDS in treatment trains in South 
Carolina, but only in terms of water quality (Ellis et al., 2014).  
 
The effects of using SuDS in series compared to individual systems have been studied in 
several papers. Kachchu Mohamed, Lucke, & Boogaard (2014) investigated the effect of using 
swales and permeable pavement in a treatment train. They concluded that using a short swale 
before permeable pavement can significantly increase the lifetime and effectiveness of the 
pavement. Brown, Asce, Line, Asce, & Hunt (2012) compared the efficiency of a single 
bioretention basin with a treatment train consisting of pervious pavement and bioretention and 
found that the treatment train both improved treatment and reduced runoff. The idea of SuDS 
in series is relatively new and it is evident that much is still unknown at this stage.  
 
5.4 Further work 
The current project focused on the dimensioning methods in four countries in three continents. 
Expanding the research even further by including more dimensioning criteria such as 
guidelines from Germany and China would give a better overview of the different methods 
used around the world. These could not be included here because manuals in English from 
==PAGE:56==
50 
 
those countries could not be found. The sponge city concept in China is relatively new 
compared to the other methods as it was only introduced in 2014 (Randall, Sun, Zhang, & 
Jensen, 2019). It is a very ambitious plan including a total of 30 cities around China to be pilot 
cities with challenging targets, such as aiming for capturing 80-85% of the annual rainfall in 
Beijing. It would therefore be particularly interesting to include how SuDS are dimensioned 
there. In Germany SuDS have mostly been implemented to improve water quality (Fletcher et 
al., 2015) but also to reduce flooding in urban areas (Dierkes et al., 2015; Nickel et al., 2014). 
Germany is also well known for its research on the runoff reduction from different types of 
green roofs with several papers published in German (Mentens, Raes, & Hermy, 2006).  
 
SuDS in series can be incorrectly dimensioned if only using the dimensioning methods for 
individual systems and adding them up. Overflows from one system can affect the 
dimensioning of systems further down in the treatment train. Delays can also be introduced 
causing changes in the inflow to a system. When a proper overview of the different 
dimensioning criteria has been established, figuring out how SuDS can be dimensioned when 
they occur in series is the next step. This requires a more detailed analysis of the different 
methods to see which parameters are most important for the dimensioning of each system. One 
of the questions that needs to be answered is whether it is possible to create one method for 
dimensioning of SuDS in series or if it is not possible because of crucial differences in the 
dimensioning methods of each system.

6 Conclusion  
Dimensioning of SuDS has evolved throughout the years in different parts of the world. These 
methods have been influenced by local conditions resulting in different dimensioning methods. 
In this project the dimensioning methods in four different countries were investigated and 
compared, to identify the differences and similarities. These four countries are Denmark, 
Australia, UK and USA. The project was focused on these countries in particular because SuDS 
have been, and are currently being, implemented there and manuals in English (or Danish) are 
available online. Two manuals from Australia (Melbourne and Western Australia) and two 
from the USA (Michigan and South Carolina) were included to investigate whether there could 
be different dimensioning criteria in the same country due to difference in climate. Where 
possible, the dimensioning methods were finally compared and evaluated through long term 
simulations using SWMM and a hypothetical case study.  
 
The results indicate that the differences in the results of the dimensioning methods are mainly 
twofold. Firstly, in terms of how the runoff from a catchment is calculated, and secondly, in 
terms of the dimensioning criteria. In the six manuals studied in this project, three different 
methods were used to calculate runoff. The Rational Method is used in the UK and Australia, 
the runoff curve number (CN) method in the USA and in Denmark runoff is calculated using a 
method which is based on historical block rain and an afløbstal. 
 
Infiltration systems are dimensioned in a similar way in the four countries, based on storage 
volume and infiltration volume. The differences between the methods lie in the area where 
infiltration is assumed to occur and the time of active infiltration. Based on simulations in 
SWMM there is a large difference between how accurately these systems are dimensioned. 
SWMM assumes infiltration through the bottom, while some of the methods are calculated 
assuming infiltration only through the sides, or both through the sides and the bottom. For those 
systems the use of SWMM to evaluate the efficiency can be questioned as the software does 
not work on the same assumptions. Even though very similar in processes, infiltration systems 
and pervious pavement are not dimensioned in the same way. Pervious Pavement is 
dimensioned based on the available storage volume and the total volume reduction due to 
infiltration, except in Australia where the focus is only on the required surface area. 
Comparison of the results of the SWMM simulations shows that the difference between the 
methods is small and the simulated return period is close to the dimensioned return period for 
the lowest return periods but under dimensioned for the larger return periods. 
 
Bioretention systems are more complex than many of the other systems as they are built up of 
several layers and can allow for infiltration and drainage. Bioretention is often included in other 
systems where additional treatment is required, such as bioretention swale or basin, which 
increases the diversity of the dimensioning methods. The dimensioning methods were too 
different for it to be sensible to dimension and evaluate in SWMM. Some of the methods first 
focus on the required surface area for either the total volume reduction or the volume from a 
1-year rainfall event followed by the calculation of the required storage volume. One method 
only focuses on the required storage volume and another considers bioretention a combination 
of a rain garden and an infiltration system and the last method focuses on the maximum 
infiltration rate.  
 
Conveyance systems differ from all the other systems as they are mainly used for conveyance 
instead of storage and retention. Conveyance systems are always dimensioned in the same way 
in the UK, Australia and the USA, using Manning’s equation. The only difference between the 
==PAGE:58==
52 
 
countries would therefore be in terms of the peak runoff volumes. SWMM is not capable of 
evaluating the dimensions of swales as it only focuses on the rainfall-runoff process 
disregarding the difference between conveyance by the swale and overflow. Infiltration is also 
included in the SWMM simulation even though it is not applied in the dimensioning criteria 
presented in the manuals.  
 
The concept of detention systems was covered in the manuals but none of them included 
dimensioning methods except for the Danish guidelines. This could be because detention 
systems do not really rely on the ideas behind SuDS, such as infiltration and evaporation, and 
are often included in the more traditional pipe-based systems. Green roofs were covered in all 
the manuals, but dimensioning criteria were only included in the manual from South Carolina 
where the total volume reduction is calculated based on the storage in two different layers. This 
could be due to the fact that green roofs are not specifically focused on water reduction even 
though they have been shown to be able to reduce annual runoff substantially. The same applies 
for rainwater harvesting, as it is not really efficient in terms of flood protection as the tanks are 
usually small and only effect the annual runoff. Dimensioning criteria were however included 
in the manuals from the UK, USA and Australia and were all based on the annual runoff volume 
and water demand.  
 
SWMM was selected for this project because of its user-friendly interphase and the feature 
which is specifically created for simulations of SuDS. The LID control in SWMM is easy to 
use and can be useful for estimating the efficiency of different dimensioning criteria of SuDS 
in terms of overflows. The software is however not specifically designed for this purpose but 
can be adapted to do so for all of the systems considered in this project except for swales where 
it does not distinguish between flow conveyed by the swale and additional overflow.  
 
The 3PA, a very simple method which can be used to dimension SuDS based on rainfall depth 
for a given return period and excluding all SuDS processes, was included in the evaluation to 
compare to the more complicated methods. The results show that the simulated return period 
was in the same order of magnitude as the other results, indicating that a simple model like this 
could be used to dimension SuDS. The differences between the results from infiltration system 
and pervious pavement do however indicate that the area of infiltration is an important factor 
which needs to be examined further to evaluate whether this method can be used for 
dimensioning of SuDS.  
 
The comparison and evaluation carried out in this project shows that there are indeed 
differences in how SuDS are dimensioned around the world. By further expanding and building 
on this research, a better overview of the different dimensioning methods can be obtained, 
providing a foundation for creating a method for the dimensioning of SuDS in series.
