ABSTRACT
In the ongoing electriﬁcation of the transport sector, the Vehicle-to-Grid (V2G) integration
is an opportunity to beneﬁt the electricity grid, nevertheless, concerns about the battery
degradation from grid services represent an obstacle. As part of the ongoing project "Across
Continents Electric Vehicle Services (ACES)", this thesis analyses the battery degradation
when the vehicle is subjected to driving and to V2G services such as frequency control.
The initial battery model provided from [1] is developed for a 40 kWh battery pack
composed by NMC 56.3 Ah cells. The model receives as input the power, to and from the
battery, and the outside temperature, and it gives as output: current, voltage, temperature
of the battery, calendar aging and cycling loss. The model is based on a one-day simulation
which is then repeated for all days/years of the investigation.
The main goal of this thesis was to take the previously developed battery model and
to develop a V2G charger with the controller, which given the frequency determined
the power the battery had to provide or consume. In this way, there was no need for
providing the power into the model, since the power was directly generated in the model
while also considering the user needs. The charger was developed with certain threshold
limits regarding frequency inputs, while also taking the bias of ±2kW into account for
additional support. After the charger was modelled, a simulation of 1 day was performed
to analyze the degradation process for one day as well as to validate the charger for further
use. Furthermore, simulations of 1, 5, 10, and 20 years followed to investigate three
diﬀerent scenarios for the degradation process: driving only, frequency only, and driving
plus frequency.
Afterward, the battery model was extended to characterize a 62 kWh battery model, by
maintaining the same cell type, however, the electrical and thermal behaviour was adjusted.
The same type and length of simulations were performed on this model as well (driving
only, frequency only, and driving plus PFC), and its results were compared to the previous
model.
The degradation between the same scenarios in both battery models did not diﬀerentiate
to a high extend. It was observed that calendar loss accounted for most of the losses for
both battery models. It was also found that storing a battery at a higher SOC level over a
longer period of time leads to a higher calendar degradation. Cycling loss occurred more
for the 40 kWh battery since it had a lower capacity compared to the 62 kWh model.
Finally, it was found when providing frequency regulation along with driving, an EV owner
has a possibility to earn more than 1000 € per year for both battery models, even when
accounting costs of the battery degradation.

INTRODUCTION
1.1
Background and motivation
Increasing the concentration of greenhouse gases aﬀecting global climate change requires
urgent actions to develop a cleaner environment which lately has become a global trend.
A growing number of governments around the globe committed to respecting the Paris
Agreement from 2016, whose main goal is to reduce greenhouse gas emissions. As renewable
energy is a highly inﬂuential factor for reaching the following target, power plants driven by
renewable sources as wind, solar, hydro, and others are scheduled to overtake conventional
power plants driven by fossil fuels. Denmark has set a goal to be fully operational by
renewable energy by 2050 [10]. Following the agreement from 2018, Denmark has also set
a ban on sales of diesel and petrol automobiles from 2030, and plug-in hybrid cars from
2035 [11]. With the ongoing decisions, electric vehicles (EVs) shall replace fuel and hybrid
driven vehicles in the future setting a fully electriﬁed (ground) transportation. The main
advantage of EVs is the production of zero-emissions when operating using electric power
obtained from renewable sources. Since there is more than one billion registered vehicles
today [12], this number can only exponentially grow in the future which created a solid
foundation for a global interest and investment in the EV industry.
The integration of EVs in present and the future market should not present a problem
when thinking about their performance characteristics. Unlike in the past when petrol
and diesel-powered vehicles fully dominated the market, EVs have drastically improved
over the years making their electric motors very eﬃcient combined with the instantaneous
torque. Electric motors could be made to operate with an eﬃciency of more than 90%,
compared to less than 30% in conventional vehicles [12]. Slowly but steadily they can
surpass the conventional fuel-driven vehicles which would establish not only clean but also
a powerful way of transportation.
EVs are powered solely by batteries as their main source of the energy. Batteries are
characterized by diﬀerent characteristics as energy density, temperature range, weight, life
cycle, and cost among many others. There are many types of batteries which could be
used to power EVs, but three types of chemistry as lithium-ion, lead-acid and nickel-metal
hydride have dominated the market for the past few decades. Lithium-ion batteries are
gaining popularity among manufacturers since they are cheaper and able to provide high
speciﬁc energy.
EVs could have another purpose rather than only being used to limit the greenhouse gas
level. They could be also used to provide V2G service support in the power grid, which
would be achieved through their batteries. EV batteries could be charged from the electric
power grid at various times and various places using regular or fast chargers. The charging
process is mostly performed overnight at users’ homes. When connected to the grid, energy

and vice versa stabilizing production. This means that the system operator could use EVs

1.2
Thesis objectives
The thesis will deal with battery-charger modeling as well as battery degradation assessment
for diﬀerent charging proﬁles, including the use of the battery for frequency regulation
services. Battery degradation from the grid service point of view represents an obstacle
since the degradation of the batteries in EVs will limit the support to the grid. From user’s
point of view, battery degradation is related to the cost of the battery itself which would
need to be replaced as the battery lifetime would be shortened.
The main objective is to model the battery-charger conﬁguration in order to
develop degradation analysis and derive the economic value of V2G services.
The learning objectives given the challenge can be summarized as follows:
• Which are the optimal conditions to use the battery in order to limit the
degradation processes? It is important to take into account the battery type and
its characteristics, which would be in-depth analyzed in order to fulﬁll the following
objective. Performing the following studies, optimal conditions could be found which
are necessary in order to gain a better perspective of the battery and limit the
degradation process.
• How can an EV and chargers be modelled in order to simulate driving
and driving plus V2G services? Considering the present charging patterns of
EVs and chargers, it is necessary to model an optimized charger that would be used
for necessary regulations of the EV charging and frequency regulations. When the
model is completed, diﬀerent scenarios will be used as sole driving and driving plus
frequency regulation. The following scenarios will be compared in order to determine
the share of the total degradation that the V2G services belong to.
• What barriers does the Danish regulations/taxes/tariﬀs create for the
battery degradation cost and how the proﬁt from the grid service
provision could be maximized? As the battery capacity would be shortened with
the degradation process, EVs would be more frequently charged and V2G services
would be limited to a certain extent because of the lower usable battery capacity.
Limited services towards power grid would aﬀect the system operator, and the cost
for battery replacement would aﬀect the user. Therefore, it is crucial to perform
an economic analysis where the tradeoﬀbetween battery degradation cost and the
proﬁt from the grid service provision will be investigated.

With a high output in the wind industry, Denmark is slowly phasing out fossil fuel plants,
which as a consequence has increased demand for additional ancillary services, such as
frequency control. The main task of frequency control is to ensure that the grid frequency
stays within an acceptable range of nominal frequency. The mismatch between electricity
production and its consumption can cause an imbalance in the power system, which would
reﬂect on the frequency deviation from its nominal level. Such violation in severe cases
can cause a blackout. Therefore, the frequency can be viewed as a representative measure
of the system’s power balance.
Danish power system is part of European Network of Transmission System Operators for
Electricity, ENTSO-E, which operates on a nominal frequency of 50 Hz as all countries in
Europe. As for the Nordic power region, nominal operation is maintained at 50 Hz with

acceptable deviations of ±0.1 Hz [15].
Ancillary services are done through transmission or distribution system operators (TSOs
and DSOs). TSOs are responsible for balancing the overall power system, and they are
operating in the region of high voltage (HV) grids. On the other hand, DSOs are operating
in low and medium voltage grids (LV and MV), and their task is to keep the voltage in
nodes within limits, deploy line loading protection systems, and they are responsible for
the quality of the supply.
Since Denmark is divided into two operational energy regions, East Denmark or DK1, and
West Denmark or DK2, the supply of diﬀerent ancillary services varies. As obtained from
Energinet [16], ancillary services to be delivered in DK1 are as follows:
• Primary reserve, FCR
• aFRR supply ability
• Secondary reserve, aFRR
• Manual reserves, mFRR
• Properties required to maintain power system stability.
On the other hand, ancillary services to be delivered in DK2 as obtained from Energinet
[16] are:
• Frequency-controlled disturbance reserve, FCR-D
• Frequency-controlled normal operation reserve, FCR-N
• aFRR supply ability
• Manual reserves, mFRR
• Properties required to maintain power system stability.
Due to high share of renewable resources, and an ongoing trend of decrease of conventional
power plants as shown on the previous ﬁgures, battery energy systems have the possibility
to provide primary frequency control regulation (PFC), which is an ancillary service that
is used to maintain grid frequency in an interval of around 50 Hz [16]. Due to their fast
response time, batteries can be used to cover the requirements of larger generating units
which are operated at slower ramp rates [17].

Battery technology is rapidly improving from the last decade onwards due to an increased
share of applications relying on battery storage from household electronic appliances to
EV batteries. In the long-term future, with the ongoing battery improvements, EVs might
not be regarded only as loads but rather as dissipated energy storage used to support the
power grid.
EVs are one of the possible solutions which could be used to utilize demands from ancillary
services, namely primary frequency control, since EVs are plugged into the post most of

power in both directions. Due to their ability to smoothly adjust consuming and producing

2.3
Battery fundamentals
A battery can be described as an electrochemical device used to convert electrical energy
into potential chemical energy during charging, and convert chemical energy into electrical
energy during discharging [4]. A battery consists of one or more electrochemical cells
stacked together. A cell is the smallest packaged form of a battery, which consists of two
electrodes (positive and negative) submerged into electrolyte as shown in Fig. 2.7. Negative
electrode is charged with electrons, while positive electrode has a deﬁcit of electrons. When
both electrodes are connected (usually via wire), electrons will start to ﬂow from the
negative electrode to the positive electrode. This reaction represents the ﬂow of the current,
and the energy of the moving electrons can be harnessed to perform certain operations,
e.g., powering an EV. As the number of electrons ﬂowing from the negative to the positive
side is decreasing over time, the voltage of the battery is decreasing as well. If there is a
similar amount of electrons on the positive, and the negative side over a longer period of
time, the battery is considered ’dead’, and it is no longer suitable for use.
Figure 2.7: Battery cell representation [4]
For EV applications three main types of rechargeable batteries are used: lead-acid, lithium-
ion, and nickel-based batteries.
Lead-acid is a mature type of battery mostly used for automotive starting and lighting. This
battery type possesses sulfuric acid in an open container which is used to produce electricity.
Due to its maturity, it is cheap to produce, but it can be hazardous if overcharged [12].
Nickel-based batteries have lighter metal compared to lead-acid, which is equipped with
good electrochemical properties desirable for battery applications. There are four diﬀerent
8
==PAGE:27==
2. Theoretical background
types of nickel-based batteries: nickel-iron, nickel-zinc, nickel-cadmium, and nickel-metal-
hydride [4].
Last but not least, lithium-ion batteries, or commonly referred as Li-ion, came into
commercial use in the 1990s. Unlike other battery types they are less likely to lose their
charge when not being used, despite having a very high energy density. This type of
battery is commonly used in electronic devices such as mobile phones, laptops, etc. When
being subject to overcharge or overdischarge, potential failures might occur. Swelling
of the cell can happen due to overcharge of the cell, while overdischarge can lead to a
short-circuit of the cell, building up to the excessive heat which in severe cases can cause
ﬁre [12]. Lithium-ion is regarded as the most suitable battery type to power future EVs.

3.4
Degradation dynamic
Rechargeable lithium-ion battery is often taken as a battery of choice for wide variety of
applications, ranging from consumer electronics to EVs. It is relatively safe while oﬀering
high energy and power density. Despite improvements in recent years for EV applications,
this battery technology remains expensive [9].
As the battery stays one of the most expensive parts of an EV [43], [44],[45] the main goal
of this thesis is to investigate its degradation process over time.
The aging of lithium-ion cells happens due to the degradation process of the individual
cells, which accounts electrical and chemical changes over time. Chemical degradation
mechanisms among many include release of gaseous side products, oxidation, and reduction
processes [46]. Besides chemical changes, internal resistance of a battery and its cell
capacity change over time as well, as well as mechanical properties such as structural
changes and changes in volume. The aging process can be divided into cycle aging and
calendar aging. Cycle aging is given by the number of charge and discharge cycles, while
calendar aging is a function of time. Due to battery’s dependence on temperature and SOC,
Ref. [47] states calendar aging as the main source of battery degradation. Calendar aging
is dependant on the SOC and the battery temperature, while cycle aging is dependant on
battery temperature and current input/output. Contrary to calendar degradation, cycle
degradation is non existent when the battery is in idle due to no current ﬂow. On Ref.
[48], it is found that battery degradation happens to be twice as much from calendar aging
if the battery is stored fully charged, rather than charged to a SOC lower than 60 %.
Therefore, in order to maximize lifetime of lithium-ion battery, it is important not to store
it at high SOC levels. Ref. [49] states when storing battery for long-term storage, the SOC
level should be lower than 50% in order to maximize battery life.
In this thesis both battery models, 40 kWh and 62 kWh, have been modelled using the
same semi-empirical Wang model for the battery degradation [50] [34], which is brieﬂy
presented below.

4.1.3
Driving Proﬁle
From Ref. [3], [1], [54] and [55], it was found that the average Danish driving distance
is 45 km per day. In this regard, the driving pattern used in the thesis considers a 45
km distance, 22.5 km driven in the morning from 08:00 to 08:39, and the other 22.5 km
in the afternoon from 16:30 to 17:09. The driving proﬁle used in the thesis is measured
for 24 hours with a sample rate of 1 second. When considering an average consumption
of 5 km/kWh, 45 km drive corresponds to an average daily driving consumption of 8.7
kWh. Due to measurements limitation of the driving data, the same driving proﬁle was
assumed to be the same for each day of the simulations. Variations in driving consumption
would yield diﬀerent results, e.g., if the consumption would be less, EV would require less
charging power, and vice-versa.

4. Technical results
Driving plus PFC
Using the charger for the simulation process given on Fig. 3.2, after adjusting the droop
characteristics, the vehicle is supplied with the full designated charging capacity of the
V2G charger. Limitation of one day in seconds (86400) is applied to facilitate observations
of the scenarios, and due to the length of driving data being 86400 seconds. Furthermore,
switch control block is applied to stop PFC at 06:00 in the morning, after which period
an EV is used for charging up to 08:00, where another switch is applied to stop charging.
Observing real driving data, the vehicle is used for driving for 39 minutes (08:00 - 08:39)
from home to work, after which it is parked to 16:30 in the afternoon. Data measurements
during the time when an EV is parked are removed from the driving dataset since the
battery power is zero. Frequency data was also removed for the time between 06:00 and
17:00 since PFC is not used in this period. For this reason, only ﬁrst and last second of
the parked period data, and frequency period data are taken. Consequently, Simulink was
able to keep the same values (zero) for the whole duration of the parked period, hence
tremendously reducing simulation time.
Afterward, the vehicle is used for the same trip length and duration to return back home.
Frequency regulation is assumed to start immediately after the vehicle returns home, at
17:09, and it is performed until 06:00 next day. In order to allocate 13 hours of PFC, the
driving proﬁle has been adjusted where the trip return home would start 9 minutes earlier
than scheduled, at 16:21. Therefore, frequency regulation is done for a total of 13 hours
between 17:00 and 06:00.
The charging of an EV was decided to be performed in the morning rather than in the
afternoon due to PFC. If more power would be needed to be provided for the PFC overnight,
such case could result in a more discharged battery in the morning when a user would
need an EV. In order to allocate both PFC and driving needs, there is a window of 2 hours
which could be used to utilize necessary charging.
The battery power during 24 hours of driving plus PFC simulation can be seen in the
bottom subplot of Fig. 4.3.
Driving (only)
The driving (only) proﬁle follows almost the same guidelines as described for driving plus
PFC scenario. PFC is omitted in this scenario, and there are added changes in the charging
proﬁle. The charging process starts the same at 06:00 in the morning, and it lasts 52
minutes. Since an EV used 8.7 kWh in a previous day for driving, using the 10 kW charger
would require 52 minutes to fully charge the vehicle. After reaching a certain SOC level,
charging is slowed down until it stops. The driving proﬁle starts and ends at the same
time as in the previous scenario, where the only diﬀerence is no PFC after an EV reaches
home. It is noteworthy to mention that when an EV is used for driving, short term high
power is present when accelerating the vehicle as well as regenerative breaking for slowing
down or to stop the vehicle. The following process results in a reverse power ﬂow which
can be seen in the middle subplot of Fig. 4.3.

Comparing outside temperature to battery temperature used for driving plus PFC
regulation in duration of 1 year as seen on Fig. 4.5, it can be concluded that battery follows
temperature pattern deﬁned by its surroundings. Due to the geographical region of the

mild temperatures during summer, and temperatures during winter which rarely go below

climates.

plus PFC regulation was -8.3◦C, which was measured on 6 January 2017 at 06:00. On
the other hand, the lowest outside temperature was measured on the same day at 09:00,
and it was -10.3◦C. Due to EV being parked in the following period, Joule losses aﬀecting
the battery from PFC provision did not account much due to a limited amount of energy
supplied from the battery, and/or due to not very much needed PFC in that speciﬁc period.
As consequence, the temperature of the battery reduced proportionally to the outside
temperature. As soon as the EV started charging at 06:00, which followed by driving
trip to work, battery temperature gradually increased, thus it deviated from the outside
temperature which was lowest in that period. This explains how Joule losses account for a
temperature increase of the battery when exposed to charging and driving events.

5
ECONOMIC EVALUATION
In this chapter, the economic analysis of an EV frequency provision will be investigated
when provided by 40 kWh and 62 kWh Nissan Leaf models. First and foremost, the price
diﬀerence between the last 5 years followed by the diﬀerence of an average hourly price for
each year will be presented and unfold. Moreover, revenues, costs, and losses when EVs
are subjected to the PFC provision will be calculated and elaborated. Last but not least,
an estimation of the battery degradation costs will conclude this chapter.
5.1
Price and power
Since Denmark is divided into two market areas of Western Denmark (DK1) and Eastern
Denmark (DK2), all further investigations will be considered for the DK2 region.
The electricity prices used in the thesis are historic spot marked prices of the last 5 years
(01/01/2015 - 01/01/2020) obtained from Energinet [58]. The reason behind the exclusion
of the ﬁrst half of 2020 was due to a fact that the year is still in progress, and in order to
avoid any errors when comparing average hourly prices.
There are two types of frequency containment reserves in DK2, FCR-N, and FCR-D, which
are used for normal operation and disturbances, respectively. Frequency containment
reserve for normal operation (FCR-N) is assumed and used for the rest of the economic
analysis.
Due to two diﬀerent payments of FCR-N, Euro, and Danish krone, it was decided upon
using Euro for a better overview of the whole EU market when comparing diﬀerent prices
and services of EVs and its components.
Fig. 5.1 compares the diﬀerence between FCR-N prices for each of the last 5 years. The
lowest range of prices can be observed in 2015 due to a higher share of renewables [59] [60].
Ref. [59] states that Danish wind power production for that year exceeded the total Danish
electricity consumption for 409 hours. Also, 2015 was the year with a higher precipitation
concentration in the Nordic region, thus hydropower plants were used to provide more
reserves than usual, which caused such price decrease [3].
On the other hand, the highest prices could be seen for 2018. The reason behind such price
increase throughout 2018 was due to limited precipitation, which was absent for most of
the year. This accounted for a limited reserve provision from hydropower plants and more
relying on energy imports from neighboring countries of Norway, Sweden, and Germany.
Net imports of electricity in 2018 in Denmark were 18.8 PJ [14], hence an increase in
prices. Fig. 5.1 shows the highest price occurred on 8th November 2018, when the price
was 261.35 €/kWh.

CONCLUSION
The aim of this work was to investigate how diﬀerent simulation scenarios account on the
degradation process of the battery. Furthermore, it was necessary to analyze how would
the cost of battery degradation reﬂect between driving only and driving plus frequency
regulation scenario. Moreover, it was necessary to analyze if it is feasible to provide
frequency control both in terms of battery degradation, and in terms of proﬁt.
The ﬁrst step in the thesis was to analyze the given battery model, and to optimize it
for further use. The initial model received power as an input, while the modelled V2G
charger in this thesis used real frequency data in duration of 1 year with sample rate of 1
measurement per second. As a result of so so many data inputs, it was necessary to have
SOC control to avoid running out of energy. This control was implemented in Simulink
to make the EV being able to operate for a year, which was later repeated for number of
desired years in simulations. This way frequency data was used to determined the power
which battery had to provide or consume. Unlike in the previous model, there was no
need for providing the power into the model, since the power was directly generated in
the model while also considering the user needs. Using frequency data of 1 year led to
more precise results, which accounted for diﬀerent number of cycles, and more precise
degradation results, when compared to, e.g., 1 day of frequency measurements.
After modelling the V2G charger, the simulation was performed for a day to test if
the charger works properly. Afterwards, simulations up to 20 years followed for three
diﬀerent scenarios: driving only, frequency regulation only, and driving plus frequency
regulation (PFC). Regarding the results, the emphasis was placed on the ﬁrst 5 years of
the degradation process due to higher level of trust in the results. This has been done
since battery chemistry could change drastically after certain time, hence approximately
calculated results after this period can be much diﬀerent from the real results. Moreover,
it was found that storing the battery with a high SOC for longer period of time, calendar
degradation would aﬀect battery degradation more than usual. As for the results, calendar
degradation occurred the most for the driving only scenario since the vehicle was parked
most of the time (almost 8 hours during the day, and 13 hours during the night). In both
cases, SOC level was high when EV was parked (89.12 % after ﬁrst trip, and 78.25 % after
second trip), which as consequence per battery storing deﬁnition over longer period of time
accounted for the highest calendar loss in all scenarios. On the other hand, cycling loss
occurred the most for driving plus PFC scenarios since in this scenario, battery was used
the most. for both driving and PFC regulation which drained it more times than in other
scenarios. Overall degradation was the highest for combined driving plus PFC scenario,
and it accounted for 9.51 % after 5 years of the simulation.
Furthermore, the previous battery was extended to characterize a 62 kWh model, by
maintaining the same cell type. However, electrical and thermal behaviour were adjusted

according to given references, and through assumptions. The same type of simulations
were applied to the following battery and the diﬀerence between degradation of the 40
kWh and this battery was found to be similar for each scenario. Cycling degradation

Calendar degradation accounted for most losses in all scenarios as for the previous model,

investigate if it is feasible to perform frequency regulation along with driving of the EV for
both battery models. Following analysis was necessary to perform since by having a lower
capacity of the battery, V2G services would be limited, thus it was aimed to analyse how
would the following reﬂect over the years.
Domestic and industrial prices of DK2 from second half of 2019 were used along with

respectively, using industrial prices, even when costs of the degradation were included.

FUTURE WORK
More sophisticated data
Work conducted in this thesis relayed on a limited amount of data. Comparing simulation
results using diﬀerent frequency duration measurements would generate diﬀerent results.
More detailed data might not diﬀer to a high extent, but using data with a greater amount
of measurements would yield more precise and realistic results. Limitation of data also
applied to driving and outside temperature measurements, which are limited to 24 hours
and 1 year, respectively. In order to mitigate real-world scenarios, data measurements
should correspond to the duration of the simulation.
Charging proﬁle
Charging proﬁle conducted in this work was performed each day at predeﬁned periods for
all simulated scenarios using the same V2G charger. For a more complex, or a possible
practical scenario, diﬀerent charging proﬁles should be assumed. In practice, a driving
pattern could diﬀer with time which in some situations would require charging of an EV
being performed at charging stations using chargers with diﬀerent power rates. If following
is done on a frequent basis, battery’s chemistry could be aﬀected which might accelerate
the degradation process
Accurate predictions of battery life
Due to limitations in literature and oﬃcially published information for both investigated
batteries, assumptions were made in order to perform the modeling process. If oﬃcial
battery characteristics are to be provided for research purposes under a non-disclosure
agreement, a more comprehensive view of the degradation process could be obtained.
Consequently, this would lead to a more accurate economical perspective from the V2G
provision.
