Summary (English)
The goal of this thesis is to develop a fully automatic algorithm for generating
a short-axis tranformation of transversal 3D CT scans. With a short-axis image
stack, Medviso AB’s software Segment can be used for automated left-ventricle
segmentation.
Manually transforming the transversal image stacks are time
consuming. In addition it is prone to bias, injected by the doctor doing the
manual annotation. An automated model would remove the bias, and allow
for oﬄine batch processing of large quantities of images, such as the Swedish
CArdioPulmonary bioImage Study (SCAPIS) data set which contains CT scans
of more than 30,000 patients.
The proposed solution consist of three independent convolutional neural net-
works (CNN), and is split into two sequential phases.
In phase 1, a binary
classiﬁcation network is used to determine a suitable 2D slice from the 3D im-
age stack. A suitable slice should clearly show the left-ventricle, such that three
landmarks can be annotated. A diﬀerent CNN is used to predict the location of
3 landmarks on the suitable 2D slice. Two points are annotated on the mitral-
valve plane, and one at the apex of the left ventricle. From these points a general
orientation of the left ventricle is computed in the (x, y) plane. Using this orien-
tation an orthogonal 2D image is computed using Segment’s algorithms. Lastly,
a third CNN is used to predict the location of 3 landmarks on the orthogonal
image. With all 6 points, a 3D orientation of the heart can be calculated. The
tranversal slices can then be transformed to follow this orientation, and the
transformed slices are generated using interpolation.
The proposed ﬁnal solution is implemented as a plugin to Segment. The solution
perform on par with human annotation, when compared to the inter-observer
variability between manual annotations by two independent doctors. The auto-
matically transformed image stacks were also used for left-ventricle segmentation
(using the implemented algorithm in Segment). The proposed model performed
equally well in terms of End-Diastolic Volume (EDV) and Left Ventricular Mass

(LVM) when compared with the inter-observer variability on the same metrics.
In conclusion the proposed solution in this project allows for oﬄine processing
of large quantities of 3D CT scans, such as the SCAPIS data set.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This chapter provides an introduction to the project by ﬁrst describing the
problem at hand, and the motivation behind this project. This is followed by
the goal of this project, and the proposed method for achieving this goal. The
section concludes with a look at related work within the ﬁeld medical image
annotation.
1.1
Description and motivation
This project is based on the Swedish CArdioPulmonary bioImage Study (SCAPIS)
[1]. SCAPIS is a unique, cross-sectional study carried out in Sweden, involving
30,000 patients between 50 −64 years of age. The goal is to develop a rich
data set that includes medical imaging and various physiological tests. The vi-
sion of SCAPIS is to be able to identify, diagnose, and treat, people who are
prone to or aﬀected by diseases such as Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease
(COPD), stroke, cardiac arrest/heart attack and other diseases aﬀecting the
cardiovascular system.
At the time of writing, the manual pre-processing of the data set has begun. The
data in this project consist of a subset of the SCAPIS study with approximately
500 CT scans that has been analysed by a medical professional, using a total of
6 points to generate a short-axis view (SAX) of the heart. Having the short-axis
view of the heart facilitates better and easier left-ventricle (LV) segmentation
and quantiﬁcation (volume and mass of LV), and is valuable in the diagnosing
process, as well as for future research.
==PAGE:14==
2
Introduction
There are two main motivators for automating the analysis of the CT scans.
The ﬁrst is to reduce the time it takes a medical expert to do the analysis.
A complete manual annotation and segmentation of the LV takes even an ex-
perienced physician 10 −20 minutes. This process requires the physician to
manual annotate landmarks around the LV in multiple 2D slices. This method
is accurate, but also prone to human error and bias, and also time consuming.
Instead, software like Segment CT allows for semi-automatic annotation and
segmentation of the LV. Here the physician has to do 10 −20 mouse clicks,
and the process takes around 30 −60 seconds, depending on how familiar the
physician is with the tools and the data. For large data sets like SCAPIS it is
highly impractical to process the entire data set, even with the semi-automatic
system. The entire data set would take 375 hours (assuming an average of 45
seconds per case) to analyse using the semi-automatic method. This amounts to
over two months of full time employment, assuming a physician only performs
this task (which again is infeasible and expensive).
The second motivator is to remove the variability between diﬀerent physicians’
analysis. While the annotated landmarks used in this project are located near
anatomical structures, the position of these points are subjective as there are no
strict consensus as to where they should be annotated exactly. Therefore, two
physicians will annotate the landmarks diﬀerently causing a subjective bias in
the analysis. While the variation between the two annotations might not heavily
impact the general outcome of the analysis, it is desirable to remove this type
of bias to ensure a more objective annotation.
Automating the process of generating the short-axis view would mean that the
entire data set could be processed oﬄine, without the need for medical personnel
to inspect each of the 30, 000 CT scans. In addition to reducing the processing
time, if this method proves successful it could be applied to other pre-processing
tasks of the SCAPIS data set, as well as automatic CT analysis in a clinical
setting.
1.2
Goal of the project
The goal of this project is to develop a model for fully automated annotation
of the mitral-valve plane and apex in the left ventricle (LV) of the heart in 3D
CT scans, for the creation of short-axis image stacks. While this is doable using
medical image software such as Segment CT, automating this process remains
a challenge.

1.2.1
Proposed method
The proposed method is to split the problem into two phases. For the ﬁrst phase
a convolutional neural network is used to identify 3 landmarks on a single 2D
slice from the transversal 3D CT stack (stack refers to the full 3D CT image
that consist of multiple 2D slices). These landmarks are used in combination
with algorithms developed by Medviso AB to create an orthogonal 2D image.
The second phase then proceeds to use another neural network to identify 3
landmarks on the orthogonal image.
Using the 6 landmarks an orientation
of the heart can be calculated, and the image stack can be transformed and
interpolated to traverse along this orientation rather than the initial transversal
orientation. A graphic explanation of the process is shown in Figure 1.1.

1.3
Related work
While this project focuses on the very speciﬁc task of calculating the orienta-
tion of the LV in the heart, automatic landmark detection in images has been
researched extensively.
J. Guo et al. developed a fully automatic model for registration of dense 3D
facial images, using 17 automatically annotated landmarks [2]. The model uses
a Principal Component Analysis (PCA) approach for annotating the landmarks,
and achieved accurate annotations of the landmarks with an average root-mean-
squared-error (RMSE) of 1.7 mm. J. Zhang et al. presented a two-stage task-
oriented deep learning method for detecting large scale anatomical landmarks
in 3D images, using limited training data [3]. The ﬁrst stage consist of a con-
volutional neural network (CNN) that was trained on image patches from the
training set. The purpose of the ﬁrst stage is to determine the image patch’s
displacement to multiple landmarks. The second network builds upon the ﬁrst
network by using the weights of the fully connected layers in the ﬁrst stage, as
weights for convolutional layers with kernels of size 1 × 1 × 1. This allows the
second network to take a full image as input and output the predicted location
of the landmarks.
The model outperformed several previous state-of-the-art
methods at the time of writing.
A. Suzani et al. presented an automatic model for localization, labelling and
segmentation of vertebrae in MR images [4]. Intensity-based features from each
voxel is used for training a feed-forward neural network to predict the relative
distances of the possible labels with regards to that voxel. This is done for all
voxels in the image, and aggregation technique is used predict the location of
the diﬀerent vertebrae. The model had a mean annotation error of 3.0 mm, and
correctly identiﬁed 100% of the vertebral bodies.
C. Payer et al. proposed various CNN’s to automatically locate landmarks in
X-ray images of hands [5]. The network were trained on X-ray images and the
ground truth were heatmaps with Gaussians at the position of the landmarks.
The U-net presented achieved state-of-the-art performances with a mean local-
ization error of 0.87 ± 1.05 mm.
Q. Tao et al. used an U-Net architecture to automatically fully quantify the LV
in cine MR images (images steady in spatial location, but covering a full cardiac

Deep Learning Theory
Deep Learning is a term that has gained so much traction and popularity in
recent years, that anyone with the slightest interest in technology and IT have
heard of it. This section introduces the theoretical foundation that lays ground
for the large more complex deep neural networks. First, a brief introduction
to Artiﬁcial Neural Network (ANN) is given. It is followed by an explanation
of deep learning and the various structures that makes up today’s complex
ANN. The concept of error back-propagation is described and motivated by
an example. At last various optimizers used for training ANN’s are explained,
along with a few pitfalls in regards to optimizing such a network.
2.1
What is Artiﬁcial Neural Networks
The term Artiﬁcial Neural Networks stems from biology and describes the idea
of a model that mimics the neural network in the human brain. Connected layers
of neurons that pass signals forward and backwards to imitate the process of
learning. These layers introduce a type of hierarchy that allows the model to
learn by its exposure and adaption to the data it is fed.
This architecture
makes it possible for computers to learn concepts that are not guarded by a
clear system of equations. As an example the task of identifying objects is an
easy task for the human brain. Through the exposure to various objects in our
life, humans learn to diﬀerentiate between a cat and dog, even though the higher
levels of such a structure is similar (four legs, head, tail, fur, etc.). This task
is immediately much harder for a computer to learn, and was one of the ﬁrst

remains very subjective.

a kernel of size 3 × 3 × 3, zero-padding and stride size 1 this can be achieved
by using 3 · 3 · 3 = 27 weights. Using a fully connected layer it would require
(32 · 32 · 3) · (32 · 32) = 3145728 ≈3.1 × 106 weights. Parameter sharing also
means that the convolutional layer is equivariant. Equivariance means that if
the input changes, the output changes equally, and f is equivariant to a function
g if

2.1.5
Pooling layers
Pooling layers are used in CNNs to reduce the dimensions of the input for
subsequent layers. Passing the entire image through every layer would slow down
the training process. Instead, a pooling operation is used, in which a kernel is
used to evaluate a sub-region of the image and only output one value. In this
sense it is similar to the convolutional kernel, though there are no weights to
be learned. The most popular pooling operation is max pooling that selects the
most dominant feature in the region, which is useful for object detection/image
classiﬁcation. Average pooling uses the average value of the region. To reduce
the dimensions of the input a stride of 2 is typically used (with zero padding).
The dimensions of the input is then halved for each max pooling layer.
2.1.6
Residual networks
Residual networks was introduced in 2015 [14] in an attempt to answer whether
learning better networks was as simple as adding more layers. [15] and [16] both
observed issues with deeper networks where the training error became saturated
before beginning to degrade as depth of the network increased. This is referred
to as the problem of degradation, and this is why blindly adding more layers to
a network is not guaranteed to improve performance. [14] proved this with a
==PAGE:29==
2.1 What is Artiﬁcial Neural Networks
17
genius approach, where two networks are trained, a shallow and a deep. The
ﬁrst part of the deep network is a copy of the shallow network, and the added
layers are merely identity mappings (input is equal to output). The worst case
scenario for the deep network should be that it perform as well as the shallower
network. However, experiments showed that the training error of the deeper
network was higher than for the shallow network. The current solvers are not
able to ﬁnd solutions for the deeper network that perform on par with the
shallower network.
To address the degradation problem [14] implemented a network with residual
learning blocks. Let H(x) denote the underlying mapping of a stack of non-
linear layers in a network. Let F(x) be the mapping of the stack, but instead
of directly optimizing for the underlying mapping H(x), it is mapped to the
residual, F(x) := H(x)−x. Rearranging this gives F(x)+x, and the hypothesis
is that it is easier to optimize this residual mapping than the original.
Figure 2.7: Building block for residual learning4.
This mapping can be implemented by using skip connections, as shown in ﬁgure
2.7. The input to a stack of layers is fed to both the stack and an identity
mapping.
After the stack, the output of the stacked layers and the original
input is summed. These extra connection adds no additional parameters to be
trained.
By introducing these skip connections, the previous problem with a shallow and
deep network (consisting of the shallow and identical mapping layers), should
be more easily optimized. If an identity mapping is the optimal solution for a
layer, the skipped connection allows the optimizers to quickly drive the weights
of the stacked layers to zero. [14] showed that using these skip connections with
networks as deep as 1000 layers consistently decreased the training error, thus
solving the issue of degradation (the test error was not better for the very deep
networks as they overﬁtted to the training data).

In most real life applications of ANN, a regularization term is included in the
loss function. Similar to other machine learning models the regularization term
helps reduce over-ﬁtting and increase how well the model generalizes. It can be
thought of as a bias-variance trade oﬀ, where bias is the expected deviation from
the true value of the function, and variance is the deviation from the expected
value of a function that any sampling of data is likely to cause5. The MSE can
be formulated as (for the proof see appendix 8.1)

huge bias but zero variance. Likewise if we over-ﬁtted a high degree polynomial
to the data one would achieve low bias but high variance, as the model would
vary greatly depending on the sampled data used. It is this trade-oﬀbetween
bias and variance that a regularization can improve to bring an overall decrease
to the MSE.

2.2.3
Batch learning
In the above example a single input was processed, the error was back prop-
agated, and the weights were updated. This means that the gradient descend
might take a step in a sub-optimal direction, in comparison to the overall gradi-
ent of the entire data set (if the data point is an outlier). This type of training
is called Stochastic learning, due to the random element of processing each
observation alone. While it might seem bad to have this stochastic element it
can actually be beneﬁcial. If the solution space is highly non-convex with lots
of local minima and saddle points, the stochastic training allows the model to
escape these local minima due to the noisy gradients. The downside is that it
typically requires the training to loop through the data set a large number of
times.
==PAGE:40==
28
Deep Learning Theory
A diﬀerent method is to process the entire data set before updating the weights,
which is called Batch learning. The change to each weight is calculated for every
observation in the training set, accumulated and then updated. This way a single
observation will not send the gradient in a sub-optimal direction. However this
method is more likely to be stuck in local minima, which is troublesome for
complex non-convex problems. Similarly batch training is not eﬃcient, as the
entire data set has to be fed through the network in order to do a single weight
update.
The default option is to use Mini Batch learning. The data set is sub sampled
into smaller batches which are then processed like the full batch method. This
introduces a level of noise that can help escape local minima, while remaining
more eﬃcient than regular batch learning. It is especially notable in the early
stages of training, where the gradients of the diﬀerent batches will tend to point
in the same direction (as the weights are initialized randomly). Thus computing
an update on 1/100’th part of the training data is more eﬃcient than computing
it on the entire data set.
[17] showed that mini-batch sizes of 2 < m < 32 was preferred for the CIFAR-10
data set, and 16 < m < 64 on the CIFAR-100 data set. While this research
suggest that using mini batches in that range is superior, a thorough mathe-
matical understanding of the eﬀect of mini batch size is still to be discovered,
and like the majority of the theory in this ﬁeld, the general consensus is based
on observed performance and what has worked previously.

Batch normalization
To understand why normalization of inputs is beneﬁcial, consider the simple case
where all input variables are positive. The weights for a given node is updated
with a value that is proportional to the error at that node and the input vector.
However if the input is all of the same sign, all of the weights either increase or
decrease together. This produces a zig-zag path towards the optimal weights,
which is slower and less eﬃcient than a more direct path. An intuitive example
of this is shown in ﬁgure 2.10.

The problem can also be described by a term called internal covariate shift.
If the distribution of the input to a layer li changes throughout the training
process (as the weights in previous layers are updated, the distribution of the
output is typically shifted) the alterations made to the weights in li are not
guaranteed to reduce the loss function for this layer. Thus reducing the internal
covariate shift would be a way to obtain quicker learning. [18] achieved matching
performance on the ImageNet classiﬁcation task with only 7% of the previously
required training steps, by using batch normalization.
It should however be noted that the exact reason why batch normalization work
is not yet fully understood. [19] shows that it might not be stabilizing the input
distributions of the various layers, that makes batch normalization such a great
tool. Rather it suggests that it makes the optimization landscape signiﬁcantly
smoother. By having a smoother optimization landscape the gradients are more
predictive and larger learning rates can be used, which in turn speeds up the
learning process.

By ignoring a fraction of nodes from a given layer during a training iteration,
the idea of a "diﬀerent" model is introduced. This forces the layer to learn more

2.3.5
Pitfalls of neural network optimization
Non-convex optimization is in itself a complex problem that is not fully solved,
and might never be. Other Machine Learning models typically attempt to only
optimize convex problems by carefully constructing the object function and
constraint such that it is a convex problem.
Unfortunately this is typically
infeasible for training neural networks, and while the object function for a neural
network might be convex, the domain on which this function is evaluated very
rarely is.
A problem that is prevalent in both convex and non-convex optimization is ill-
conditioning of the Hessian matrix H8. Ill-conditioned matrices has a large
ratio between the smallest and largest eigenvalue. This can be problematic for
ﬁrst-order methods as it typically forces a zig-zag path, rather than a more
direct path (similar to the example in ﬁgure 2.10). Second-order methods are
typically not heavily impacted by this unless H is very ill-conditioned.
For non-convex problems local minima are also a potential hazard. Local min-
ima is guaranteed to exist in neural networks as these models suﬀers from the
models identiﬁablity problem [9]. If a model can rule out all but one set of
8Ch. 8.2 in [9]
==PAGE:50==
38
Deep Learning Theory
parameters, given a suﬃciently large data set, the model is said to be identi-
ﬁable. In a neural network one could swap the input weights for unit i with
the weights for unit j and do the same for the output weights. The result is a
slightly diﬀerent set of parameters, yet the result of the network would be the
same. This issue of non-identiﬁability causes neural networks to have a large
number of local minima, however as all these local minima results in the same
value in the object function they do not constitute a problem. Local minima
are only problematic if their cost function value is high (much higher than the
global minima), and thus typically the issue becomes ﬁnding a local minima
that has a suﬃciently small cost value.
Saddle points are another type of critical points that can cause problems for
an optimizer. [23] argues that saddle points are much more prevalent in high
dimensions than in low dimensions. The reasoning is that for a local minima
the Hessian will have only positive eigenvalues, for a maxima only negative
eigenvalues, and for a saddle point the eigenvalues will be both positive and
negative. Thus for high dimensions it becomes exponentially unlikely that all
eigenvalues have the same sign, thus making saddle points far more prevalent
in high dimensions than local minima.
Another issue of gradient descent optimization methods is the issue of vanishing
gradients.
A lot of the traditional activation functions that were ﬁrst used
with neural networks suﬀered from this issue. The hyperbolic tangent function
as example has derivatives in the range (0, 1), which in deep networks will be
multiplied several times as the error back propagates through the network. This
led to vanishing gradients, meaning that the early layers trained very slowly.
This is also one of the reasons that ReLU has become a popular choice for
activation, as it’s derivative is always 0 or 1. On the other hand this function
suﬀers from the issue of dead neurons, as a 0 gradient would result in no update
to the given weight. Adaptions to ReLU such as Leaky ReLU and Parametric
ReLU can handle this issue by altering the function slightly so the derivative is
positive yet very small on the left hand side of the function.
Similarly to vanishing gradients, exploding gradients constitutes a problem for
gradient descent methods. For deep networks the solution space will more often
than not contain steep regions where gradients will be very large.
A single
gradient descent step could throw the parameters far oﬀits trajectory and erode
the optimization results reached up to that point. As mentioned earlier various
method can be used to reduce the impact of this issue such as adaptive learning
rates. A similar technique called gradient clipping reduces a step if it exceeds
a certain limit. Exploding gradients can be spotted by examining the validation
error during training. If the loss function ﬂuctuates rapidly between iterations it
could suggest that there are issues with large gradients, and additional measures
for reducing this might be necessary.

DICOM format
DICOM is an abbreviation of Digital Imaging and Communications in Medicine
and is the international standard format for storing, processing, and transfer-
ing medical image ﬁles. DICOM is a complex format, and ﬁles can contain a
lot of information such as patient ID, study ID, scanner information, settings,
time and date, modality, location, medical information (treatment information,
medication, anatomical measurements, etc.) and more. It was developed by
American College of Radiology (ACR) and National Electrical Manufacturers
Association (NEMA) [24] who formed a standard committee in 1983 to help
radiologists, physicians and the equipment vendors work towards a more ben-
eﬁcial standard. At that time it was hard for anyone but the manufacturer to
print and decode the images generated by the systems. In 1985 the committee
released ACR-NEMA 300 which was the predecessor to DICOM. In 1993 the
ﬁrst version of DICOM was released, and since it has gradually expanded to
cover all varieties of medical images and has become the standard format for
manufacturers as well.

these methods are advantageous. Researchers have tried numerous variations of
network structures, depths, activation functions, optimizers etc., and the ones
that tend to generalize the best to other problems/areas become the default
choice. Thus deciding on a structure prior to having tested diﬀerent ones is
almost guaranteed to produce a sub-optimal network.
For this project a strategy of trial-and-error was applied to identify a suitable
network structure.
As described in Section 1.3 ANN has been used and re-
searched extensively within the medical ﬁeld, and inspiration was found in the
related works. This section begins with describing the inter-oberserver variabil-
ity that serves as a benchmark for evaluating the performance of the networks.
An explanation of the additional preprocessing steps used prior to training the
networks are given, before various experiments with diﬀerent network structures
are explained. This include networks of varying depth and width, transfer learn-
ing using well-documented architectures, and a custom loss function. Lastly
experiments with augmented training data is explained. The networks for the
two phases of the problem is presented sequentially. The performance of the
diﬀerent networks are shown in Section 5.

4.1
Inter-observer variability
To better evaluate the performance of the networks it is vital to have benchmark
measurements. Inter-observer variability describes the variation between obser-
vations done by diﬀerent observers, and is an important aspect of all medical re-
search. If two doctors measure/observe very diﬀerently on the same object/case
the inter-observer variability is large, and if their measurements/observations
are very similar it is low. To test the inter-observer variability of annotating the
3 points in CT images, 12 observations has been annotated by two independent
medical professionals. By measuring the diﬀerence between the coordinates of
each annotation on the same observation, the inter-observer variability can be
estimated, and used as a benchmark for the networks. The Root-Mean-Squared-
Error (RMSE) is used as the error metric, see Section 5.

4.2.1
Additional preprocessing
The ﬁrst step of preprocessing was to generate additional points on a straight
line between the two points on the mitral-valve plane, and from the apex point
along the orthogonal line segment. The idea was to force the network to better
learn the importance of the line spanned between the two points, and the line
connecting it to the apex point, rather than solely the location of each point.
Simple linear regression could then be used to ﬁnd the optimal line through the
predicted points of the network. Generating these points is easily done using
trigonometry as the points spanning the lines are given. The implementation is
shown in appendix B. The data set with generated points then has 54 ground
truth values ((x, y) values of each of the 27 points), and without the generated
points there are 6 ground truth values. In addition to generating new points
the training data was also normalized to enhance the anatomical structures
in the images. After normalization the borders/edges in the image are much
clearer. The normalization was done by subtracting the mean and dividing by
the standard deviation. The image was then multiplied by 255 and converted

In an attempt to better the performance of the networks, additional transversal

4.2.7
Custom regression layer
The ﬁrst 2 landmarks correspond to the points that deﬁne the mitral-valve plane,
that is the plane separating the left atrium and the left ventricle. Accurate
annotation of this plane is important as the algorithms used for transforming
the image stack (in Segment) depends on it to correctly transform the stack
and segment the left ventricle.
The metric used for training and evaluating
the networks are Root-Mean-Squared-Error and is a measure of how far each
point is from its ground true value.
This means that two predictions could
have equal RMSE, even though one of the predictions would lead to a better
transformation due to the orientation of the line spanned by the two points. A
visual representation of the problem is shown in Figure 4.9.
==PAGE:69==
4.2 First phase
57
Figure 4.9: While the two predictions (red and blue) have the same RMSE,
the angular diﬀerence between the lines spanned and the ground
truth are very diﬀerent. Ideally the predicted line is parallel to
the ground truth line, and thus while both predictions have equal
RMSE the red points are actually a better prediction than the
blue.
To try and reduce this angular displacement a custom regression layer was cre-
ated. The idea was to use two weights, w1 and w2, in the objective function. w1
is a weight for the regular RMSE term, and w2 is a weight for a term describing
the angular displacement between the ground truth and the prediction. The
objective function is then deﬁned as
L(θ) = w1 · RMSE(θ) + w2 · γ
where θ is the current set of parameters for the network and γ is the angular
displacement between the vectors spanned between the ground truth points and
the predicted points. The angle between two 3D vectors (v1, v2) can be derived
as
θ = atan2 (||v1 × v2||, v1 · v2)
where θ is the angle in radians, || ·|| is the Euclidean norm, · is the dot product,
× is the cross product, and atan2 is the Four-quadrant inverse tangent [28]. The

layer for Matlab, the derivatives with regards to the x and y coordinates of each

4.2.7.1
Augmented training data
One key aspect of this project is the size of the data set. 350 images is a small
data sample to train a network, even though the variation between the images
are small compared to data sets like ImageNet [27]. One way to increase the size
of the data set is by using augmentation methods. By rotating and scaling the
images slightly one could generate additional images. Note that while this does
increase the size of the data set, it is not guaranteed to have a large impact on the
accuracy. A lot of the variation within the images are preserved through rotation
and scaling, and thus these new images are not equally impactful compared to
having additional real training data.
To test if using augmented training data improve the networks, a Matlab func-
tion for rotating, scaling and translating the images was created. It randomly ro-
tates the image ±maxRot°, applies a scaling factor in the range [1−maxScale, 1+
maxScale], and translates the image ±maxTrans pixels in both the x and y di-
rection. The implementation is shown in appendix E.

6.4
Predicting a suitable slice
One way to try and improve the model is to ensure that a suitable slice is used
for predicting the points in phase 1. A suitable slice should be similar to the
slices that the network (in phase 1) was trained on, and as such stem from the
same theoretical distribution. In the perfect world, the only variance between
the images should be the variance caused by the diﬀering anatomical structures,
and not due to the slices being selected for annotation.
To try and predict a suitable slice, a new data set was constructed. For a single
3D stack the ground truth slice (used for annotation) ±15 slices on each side,
was labelled Good, and the rest was labelled as Bad. Every other slice of the 201
middle slices was then stored for training a network to do binary classiﬁcation.
This labelling can be described with the following equations
Good = {T ± 2 · i : 0 ≤i ≤7}
Bad = {T ± 2 · i : 8 ≤i ≤50}
where T is the ground truth slice number, and Good/Bad is the set of slices
labelled Good and Bad respectively. As an example imagine a 3D image stack
used slice 230 for the manual annotation. The classes Good and Bad then consists
of the slices
Good = {i : i is even , 216 ≤i ≤244}
Bad = {i : i is even , 130 ≤i ≤214 and 246 ≤i ≤330}.
Using this formula a data set of 47571 slices was constructed. It was split into
a training, validation and hold-out test sets with splits of
Training = 40, 000
(84.1%)
Validation = 2, 571
(5.4%)
Test = 5, 000
(10.5%).
Note that the training set is skewed towards the Bad class, as class Good only
constitutes ∼15% of the slices. Generally this is far from ideal as the network
is taught the bias that exists in the training data. There are ways to deal with
this by either augmenting the training data, down-sampling the Bad class or use
a custom loss function that penalizes selecting the Bad class. Fortunately the
way this network is used in the solution provides excellent results, so improving
the network further would not aﬀect the outcome of the solution greatly.
A network with the ResNet50 architecture was trained for doing the binary
classiﬁcation of slices. The images was resized to dimensions of 128 × 128, a

The last iteration of the proposed model (trained on the combined data set) per-

segmentation algorithm can then be used, which takes ∼7 seconds. This means
that the proposed solution reduces the time it takes to process a single case by
∼62% (45 seconds for the previous semi-automatic method), and it the process
does not require manual annotation. In addition, the subjective bias from the
doctors is eradicated.

7.4
Reﬂection
As previously mentioned, the work presented in this report is very much the
result of a trial-and-error methodology. This is due to the fact that neural net-
works in general have low explainability. It is often hard to determine exactly
why a certain architecture performs well on any given problem. In hindsight,
transfer learning could have been examined earlier in the project instead of
testing various homemade networks.
This might have allowed for additional
network structures to be tested such as InceptionNet or GoogleNet. This brings
up the question of when is a model good enough?
Because the neural net-
works have such low explainability it is hard to reason why a certain change
would greatly inﬂuence a network’s performance. Using an exhaustive hyper
parameter search method is also infeasible as there inﬁnite number of network
conﬁgurations. Similarly, the experiments with augmented training data could
be extended to train the networks on an even larger data set. In this project it
has been a constant balance between improving the model, limiting the training
time and the time-constraint of the project (as is the case with all research).

Chapter 8
Conclusion and future work
SCAPIS is a Swedish data set containing information of more than 30,000 pa-
tients, including 3D CT images. Speciﬁcally for research in cardiovascular dis-
eases, the heart and the left ventricle plays a key role. Medviso AB has devel-
oped an image software that allows for manual annotation of landmarks on the
left ventricle, short-axis transformation and LV segmentation, which yield key
anatomical measures such as End-Diastolic Volume and Left-Ventricle-Mass.
Manually annotating landmarks is a costly and time consuming procedure, that
also introduces bias from the doctors.
This project proposes an automatic model for annotating and transforming 3D
CT scans. It is build as a plug-in to Segment, and allows for single ﬁle and
batch processing. The proposed solution utilizes 3 independent convolutional
neural networks, all based on the ResNet50 architecture. The automatic model
performs on par with the manual transformations, when measured on the inter-
observer variability on metrics like EDV and LVM. The last iteration of the
proposed model outperformed the human inter-observer variability signiﬁcantly,
but is also likely to be positively inﬂated due to conﬂicting training and test
sets. An additional beneﬁt to the automated model is eradicating the subjective
bias from the doctors, leading to the inter-observer variability.
8.1
Future work
There are two primary directions in which future research can build upon this
project and solution. The ﬁrst is to try and improve the accuracy of the networks
==PAGE:102==
90
Conclusion and future work
used in the proposed solution. The work presented in this project is the result
of a trial-and-error approach, and while extensive experiments were carried out,
there are additional experiments that could be tested in order to improve the
networks used.
This mainly revolves around varying the architecture of the
networks and testing architectures that has proven successful for other image
related tasks. Another way of improving the networks would be to acquire ad-
ditional training data of the same format as used in this project, and preferably
with annotations from various doctors, to ensure a better generalization of the
networks.
The other approach would be to rethink the problem and attack it from a dif-

segmentation of the left ventricle. Doing this would require a substantial amount
of preprocessing, as the data required for training such a network is very diﬀerent
to the data used in this network.
