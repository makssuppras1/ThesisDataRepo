ABSTRACT
The research on improving the accuracy of renewable energy forecasting has become one
of the key activities in electricity markets due to the increase of penetration into the
grid of this technologies in the past few years. However, a big challenge appears when
addressing this problem due to the high variability of renewable energies. This thesis focuses
on the use of distributed learning as a way of taking advantage of the spatio-temporal
dependencies of the time series of proximal power plants through Autorregressive models
with Exogenous input (AR-X). The necessity of online algorithms, capable of including
the latest information and updating the estimators, is studied through the implementation
of an online algorithm based on the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM)
and a novel online approach, based on Coordinate Descent (CD).
The tracking abilities and performance of the algorithms have been checked through a
toy data set, based on the creation of a synthetic autorregressive model of second order
and two real data sets. On one hand, the functioning of the algorithms on real data set
is proven not to have an advantage over traditional models with only local information,
while on the other hand, the tests carried out with the solar data set denote a signiﬁcant
improvement regarding those.

INTRODUCTION
The following chapter is divided in three parts: the context of the thesis, the main objectives
that have been addressed in this piece of work, and the structure of the present document,
where a short description of the main parts is presented.
1.1
Context
The rising of renewable energy in electricity markets due to their low cost and a growing
environmental awareness and desire for a more sustainable sustainable society, has become
one of the main concerns of electricity suppliers. However, these technologies come with
the trade-oﬀof high variability, which makes it very diﬃcult to produce accurate forecasts.
Improving the forecasts would translate into an increase into the producer’s revenue, which
makes the potential resolution of this problem very attractive. Consequently, this interest
in approaching the forecasting problem by reducing variability has been addressed more
and more often in the recent years.
The challenge of reducing the uncertainty term that comes with renewable power plants
also concerns system operators, as an improvement in forecasting accuracy would lead in
a better management of the power grid, allowing better allocation of reserves intended
for system security, handling the scheduling of conventional generators in the electricity
market and enhancing the integration of renewable energy in the power grid [2]. The
variability of the so-called stochastic generators is typically studied through short term
forecasting. Short term prediction can be divided into two approaches, the physical and
the statistical. Nevertheless usually both models are used together for better performance.
Additionally, forecasts can be classiﬁed depending on their time horizon, which indicates
the number of time stamps ahead the prediction is issued, and this can go from minutes
to hours and even days. The interest of this thesis is focused on short term (less than
12 hours) and very short term (less than an hour), where statistical models have been
proved to perform better than physical models [3]. The models suitable for short time
prediction are time series based models. Recent investigations have shown that forecasts
can be improved by taking advantage of the spatio-temporal dependencies between time
series of proximal power plants. This holds the assumption that plants subjected to similar
weather conditions can have a direct relation among their time series.
By inspecting the corresponding literature, it has been seen that this type of short
term forecasting problems are typically addressed through statistical models based
on Autorregression (AR) models such as Autorregressive Moving Average (ARMA) or
Autorregressive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA). These methods try to ﬁnd the
relationship between the input variables, generally past data from one or more generators
that can be extended to power plants or group of power plants, and the output variables that

involve the predictions for the desired lead time and generator or power plant. Furthermore,
these methods are very powerful and convenient when dealing with high dimensional data
sets because the goal of taking advantage of spatial relationships between time series is to
be able to combine a large amount of power plants.
Nonetheless, the increase in forecasting accuracy by the integration of multiple time series
in high dimensional data sets comes with a drawback: the raise in the computational
costs of the model parameters. Therefore, it is needed to develop recursive approaches
that are able to adapt the model parameters in each step based on a small batch of past
data. The forecasting problem presented as an aggregation of agents can be solved in a
centralized and more traditional way or with the novel approach of distributed learning.
The implementation of several agents in the same model is done through the introduction
of distributed optimization problems. This allows to split the forecasting problem in small
scale problems that can be easily solved by the individual agents. The distributed learning
approach is confronted in diﬀerent ways in the literature with two predominant methods:
those based on the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) and those derived
from gradient descent methods.
Additionally, the algorithms used in the resolution of the problem can be divided in
two types: the ones using batch learning and the ones based on online updates. Batch
algorithms are based on the stationary property of a time series, and the calculation of the
coeﬃcient estimators of the model is done only once with a ﬁxed batch of data. Then, they
are used in the model as constants to predict the data in the next steps. This approach
allows models to be computationally cheap and with low amount of communication steps.
However, due to the high variability of renewable generators, especially in wind energy, the
time series cannot be considered stationary, and thus these models perform poorly in the
long term. Here is when the introduction of online algorithms comes useful, because the
idea is that the model parameters are updated whenever new data is available to introduce
in the model. The aim is to make the updates recursive so as to reduce as much as possible
the computational costs of the model and the amount of data that needs to be used in
each iteration.
However, another problem arises when taking into account the integration of diﬀerent
agents in the power system. Due to the high competitiveness in markets, the owner of
a power plant is generally against sharing its data with the rest of the agents. This is
because it would decrease its advantage in the market if the rest of the participants could
improve their forecasts thanks to the free sharing of data. Therefore a new constraint has
to be added to the forecasting problem with the introduction of privacy protocols.
In the particular example of the system operator, as aforementioned, it would be
advantageous to improve its predictions to upgrade the grid management. Therefore,
knowing the individual predictions and potentially useful past data of all the contracted
agents that take part in that particular power system would improve it. However, the
contracted agents would not want to share their data with the rest of the power plant
contractors. Thus it is necessary to implement a method that secures the data sharing
between the speciﬁc agent and the central one (system operator) by the introduction of a
privacy term. Then again, this comes with another variability term produced by the error
introduced by the privacy protocol.

Finally, in order to reduce the computational costs, the models are required to be as sparse
as possible, and this is added through regularization, such as Least Absolute Shrinkage
and Selection Operator (LASSO) penalization or truncation algorithms. The degree of
sparsity is intended to be increased to reduce the data size from which the algorithms are
expected to produce forecasts.
1.2
Objectives
This thesis focuses on the review of the current literature on eﬃcient distributed learning
methods used in the forecasting of renewable energy and the understanding of the
application of distributed optimization in solving forecasting problems.
Afterwards it includes the selection of two appropriate methods, one based on the ADMM,
that rely on the development of the method by the author of [4], and the other based
in gradient descent, with a novel implementation based on the working principle of the
coordinate descent algorithm, and the following implementation in the programming
language Python as an attempt to have a further understanding of their structures.
Finally, the last objective will come with the application, discussion and analysis of these
selected methods on a wind data set composed of 22 wind farms placed in Southeast
Australia, and a solar data, that consists of 40 photovoltaic plants based in San Francisco,
California, in order to explore the possible advantages and drawbacks of using these kind
of models on wind and solar time series.
The aim will be to have models that fulﬁll the requirements of an online set up, as well as
the possibility of being decentralized for the ultimate application of distributed learning.
1.3
Outline
Following the present introduction, the second chapter consists in a formal presentation
of the main deﬁnitions of the basic and necessary concepts to understand this piece of
work. This chapter will cover the explanation of AR models and the key concepts of the
application of distributed learning in renewable technologies.
Then, chapter 3 deals with the introduction of distributed batch learning and the following
implementation of a model based on the alternating direction method of multipliers.
It includes the development of an interpretation of the algorithm published in [5] and
description of the steps needed for the tuning of the hyperparameters, ending with a
short description and illustration of the main drawbacks of this kind of approaches, and a
transition to online algorithms.
In chapter 4, distributed online learning is introduced through the study and posterior
implementation of an online algorithm, based again on the ADMM that serves as an
upgrade to online learning of the previous batch algorithm. Then, a novel approach is
presented based on the gradient descent method of coordinate descent. This part concludes
with the description of the tuning of the hyperparameters needed in these cases, a brief
study on the complexity of the algorithms and their computational costs.

DISTRIBUTED LEARNING
This chapter serves as an introduction to the main topics of the present thesis, as well as
deals with the key concepts that will be treated throughout the document. It starts with a
general overview in AR models, which are the ones that have been used during this thesis,
followed by the deﬁnition of distributed learning and its implementation in the forecasting
of renewable energy.
In terms of notation, for the whole report scalars are denoted as non bold x, bold lower
case letters are used to denote vectors x and bold upper case letters are used to denote
matrices X.
In the recent years, many studies regarding the improvement of power forecasting in
renewable energy have focused in the spatial and temporal relationships between power
plants, such as [5] and [6]. The vast majority of theses studies have been done in the area of
wind energy as it has had the biggest and fastest approval in the market like [2]. However,
there are also some studies in the ﬁeld of solar energy, such as [7] and [8]. In very short
term forecasting, which is the issue of study during this thesis, statistical methods with
time series are better than numerical models, for they are generally more precise than the
statistical ones. However, they come with the disadvantage of being very time consuming
[3]. Therefore, for very short term forecasts of renewable energy, statistical time series
models are used in the form of AR.
2.1

words can be deﬁned as an observed realization of an stochastic process [9]. The AR model
belongs to the type of linear models, and the general linear model is deﬁned as 2.1, where

2.2
Centralized learning
The learning of the estimators of a model can be done through two diﬀerent set ups. The
ﬁrst scenario is called centralized learning, and it corresponds to the simplest way of solving
the optimization problem for learning the parameters. In this conﬁguration, only one agent
or server in the system holds the necessary information to perform the learning process.
For a better understanding of the problem, let us consider an electricity market composed
of m power plants that produce energy from renewable sources. In this market, a central
node is represented by an agent S, which owns a wind farm or several wind farms (the
same example could also serve for an owner of solar power plants). The central agent S
has made an agreement with mS wind farms, willing to share their data that are gathered
in a portfolio denoted as ΩS = {s1, s2, ..., sj}. Thus, the portfolio ΩS is a subset of the set
of m wind farms that belongs to the market. In this scenario, the contracted agents do
not participate directly in the learning of the model, and they only act as data providers,
whereas the central agent can choose the model it desires for the ﬁtting of the estimators.
However, this process is very unlikely to happen in reality, as the privacy of the hired
agents would be non existent, and the central agent is able to retrieve better results at the
expense of the other agents. This is not a realistic set up unless the contracted agents can
get something out of the transaction. A visual description of this scenario is presented
in ﬁgure 2.1. In each time stamp of the learning process, the agents that belong to the
portfolio ΩS send their individual predictions to the central agent S. Then, the central
agent assembles the partial predictions from the contracted agents of the portfolio with its
own, in order to solve the global optimization problem that will allow the improvement of
its forecasts.
Nevertheless, this set up does not ensure the privacy of the data of the contracted agents
in the network, as well as no tangible beneﬁts other than drawbacks, as the other nodes
will suﬀer losses when the central agent improves its forecasts, for it will have a substantial
competitive advantage in the market. A possible solution is proposed when the portfolio of
contracted agents have their power plants in diﬀerent markets. This would lead to positive
revenues for the central agent, with the extra beneﬁt of not drawing down its contracted
agents. However, this solution implies that the central agent and its hired parts are not in

2.3
Decentralized learning
The second set up is called decentralized learning, and it can be understood as an
enhancement of the centralized set up, as it is based on the decomposition of the
optimization problem proposed by the central agent.
In this case, the nodes in the
network do not work only as data providers, but they are also involved in the learning
process. Let us consider the same electricity market of the previous example, where a
central agent S is the node interested in learning the parameters of its model by using
external information, given by a set of contracted power plant owners. Nonetheless, in this
scheme, each of the nodes can estimate their model parameters and then share the partial
predictions with the central node. This new layout opens up the possibility of dividing
the initial optimization problem into small and simpler subproblems to be solved by each
of the contracted agents. Afterwards, all the individual predictions or solutions to each
particular problem are joined together by the central agent, who is in charge of updating
the model parameters and send them back to the particular agents that take part in the
learning process. Therefore, in this instance all the parties involved in the operation get
something out of it, as the updates of the learning parameters that are shared by the
central agent are used by the rest of the agents included in the set of the agreement for
the improvement of their personal forecasts.
In addition to that, for both centralized and decentralized frameworks, adding an
intermediate agent that is trusted by both the central and the rest of the nodes that
conﬁgures the network, appears as a good initial solution to introducing privacy in the
process. This extra intermediary is called a fusion center, and it is in charge of gathering
all the data bi-directionally, as it would put together all the information coming from the
contracted agents to be sent to the central one, and on the other hand, send back to the
set of agents in the learning agreement the updated parameters from the central agent, in
order to be able to continue the process. This intermediate ﬁgure can be seen as a wind
or solar forecast provider, and its function consists of ﬁtting the model parameters and
sending them back to the respective agents. In this case, also in a centralized scenario,
the contracted agents would beneﬁt from the agreement. However, this would lead to an
obvious increase in the communication steps of the learning process.
Therefore, the main diﬀerences between centralized and decentralized learning are that in
the centralized set up the central agent can freely choose the model to be used to ﬁt the
parameters, while when decentralizing, the model is chosen by all the agents involved in the
learning agreement. Additionally, the central agent solves one big optimization problem in
the centralized scenario, whereas in a decentralized situation, each agent solves a small
subproblem, which are gathered together by the central agents so as to puzzle out the big
optimization problem. This last improvement of decentralized scenarios is what makes
them well suited when working with high dimensional data sets, which is the case when

of using AR models with exogenous inputs is to take advantage of the spatio-temporal

learning parameters from large data sets so as to perfectionate the forecasts, as the larger
the data set, the more possibilities of having similar time series that improve the ﬁnal

either with the central or with the intermediate parties. Therefore, the decentralization of
the optimization problem and the introduction of distributed learning represents a huge
beneﬁt for the introduction of privacy into the network.
Furthermore, as mentioned in the previous section, the optimization problem that the

case, the learning of the parameters would lead to an iterative process instead of a direct
solution done in one step, increasing the time spent in the task of ﬁnding the optimal
solution, but increasing the performance of the estimators.
Another fact introduced by a decentralized set up is the split either vertical or horizontal
of the data set [13]. A horizontal partition of the data set means a division by records,
so in this case the agents or data owners perceive the same features. For example, given
a time series of 3000 time stamps with three features, each of them representing one lag
of the series, it would be split by records if each agent owns 1000 time stamps of the
three features (the ﬁrst agent from 1 to 1000 and so on). Nevertheless, in the case of
decentralized algorithms for time series of wind or solar power, each agent holds one or
a few features, going from the initial to the last time stamp values. As an example, the
same electricity market with m wind farms is taken. The central agent has the data for
a wind farm, and its time series would represent one feature of the data set that would
be its portfolio (several features if it includes one or more lags). Each of the agents that
take part in the learning agreement would own one or more features, all diﬀerent from
each other. Therefore, this type of data set split is denominated vertically split or split by
features.

3
DISTRIBUTED BATCH LEARNING
In this chapter, an introduction to batch learning in renewable energies is presented,
through the detailed explanation of the ADMM as a decentralize learning method for high
dimensional wind or solar power. The advantages and disadvantages of this model and the
techniques used for the tuning of the necessary hyperparameters for the algorithm are also
described thoroughly.
Most of the algorithms available in the literature for performing distributed batch learning
treat the data set as horizontally split.
For instance, in [14], the author proposes a
distributed version of the Stochastic Variance Reduced Gradient (SVRG), adding privacy
for the classiﬁcation of images. The data set is clearly divided by records, as each node
holds information for all the features that represent a certain image. However, as explained
before this is not possible when applying them to wind or solar time series. Therefore,
the aim is to solve a learning problem, which is usually an optimization problem that can
be divided into small subproblems distributed among all the data owners. This allows to
reduce the data sharing among the agents, as well as the computational costs of solving an
optimization problem of high dimensional data sets. The optimization problem includes a
convex loss function, generally in the form of the square of the forecast error. Moreover,
when dealing with large data sets, a regularization term is added as well. Some algorithms
propose the use of the ADMM to perform distributed learning, such as [13], that introduce
a model based on ADMM and VAR models with a regularized loss function with LASSO.
However, it has been decided to develop the ADMM-based algorithm developed in [5] due
to its robust structure and proper development of a distributed approach.
3.1
Batch learning with ADMM
The author of [5] proposes an algorithm motivated by the alternating direction method of
multipliers for performing distributed learning. It allows the vertical splitting of the data
set, distributing the optimization problem among the agents that belong to the portfolio,
adds regularization, and partially preserves the privacy of the parties that conﬁgures the
learning agreement. This section will make an attempt at describing and studying the
algorithm as an example of distributed batch learning, and it will include a discussion of
its advantages and disadvantages on its application for the forecasting of renewable energy.
First of all, the term batch learning refers to the fact that the estimators are calculated
though an iterative process by using a part or block of the dataset, generally denoted as
training set. In batch learning, the estimators of the model are calculated once, and then
used assuming that its value is constant for the rest of the data set and without updating
the variables. This leads to several problems that will be presented and discussed in the
following section.

3.2
Hyperparameter tuning
Another important part in the distributed learning process is the need for choosing the
appropriate inputs of the algorithm. Firstly, it is essential to diﬀerentiate between the
terms parameter and hyperparameter. The former refers to the values that are obtained
during the training phase of the algorithm, while the latter is selected before the training
and chosen through a strategy in order to have the best possible values to result in a good
performance. Hyperparameters are generally chosen out of a range of numbers, where the
limits are drawn by the entity that has good knowledge of the algorithm. These span of
values can be either continuous, if the hyperparameter can adopt any value within the
selected range, or discrete, if it can only take certain values.
16
==PAGE:29==
3. Distributed Batch Learning
In the case of distributed batch learning with ADMM by using the algorithm described
in section 3.1, there are three hyperparameters to be tuned before the training part is
reached. These are the penalization parameter λ, the variable in charge of the step size
ρ, and lastly, the number of lags L. There are some strategies that can be followed so as
to ﬁnd the best performing values. The most commonly used strategy is cross validation.
This method consists of dividing the data set in training and test data. Then the ﬁrst part
is again split in train and validation subsets, so the tuning of the algorithm is done by
training the model with a selected set of hyperparameters and computing scores on the
validation data portion. Finally, the scores will give the information needed to see the best
performing hyperparameters and tune them accordingly.
Inside the cross validation process there are several types, but the one that is used for
training and model selection of time series is the rolling cross validation. The rolling
cross validation method diﬀers from the traditional k-fold cross validation, where the data
sample is divided in k sets and the score is evaluated in each of those sets when training
on the rest of the data. Nevertheless, this method is not valid when using time series, as
the values are order temporarily.
The rolling cross validation is illustrated in 3.2, where the blue points refer to the training
set and the red points denote to the validation set. Therefore, the term ”rolling” is
understood such that the validation subset in each fold becomes part of the training set
in the next fold, increasing the size of the training set in each data clutch, and rolling or
advancing the validation part while keeping its size constant.
Figure 3.2: Rolling cross validation diagram. Figure obtained from [1]
Subsequently, the performance evaluation score is computed in each of the folds or divisions
on the test set, and then averaged over all of them. After carrying out the same procedure
over the whole range of hyperparameters, these scores are compared between the sets of
hyperparameters and the optimum values chosen. Sometimes, the optimum values are
considered suboptimal when the tuning process has been simpliﬁed following some criteria.
This criteria is usually to choose the set of hyperparameters that lower the forecast error.
However, for the algorithm presented in this thesis, this may not always be the predominant
factor, as it is also required to have an acceptable level of sparsity in the estimators set. A
balance must be found between a low forecasting error and a high degree of sparsity.

with these numbers in order to obtain the estimators that will be used to make predictions.
In batch learning, the estimators are learnt in the training set, and then they are kept
constant during the whole forecasting process, which leads to some problems.
3.3
Drawbacks of batch learning
Once the hyperparameters are selected and the model trained to ﬁnd the best possible
estimators, these are used to make predictions for the next time steps. However, using the
same constant estimators for the rest of the forecasting operation is making the assumption
that the given time series is stationary [4]. For a time series to be stationary the mean
value must always be the same and there cannot be noticeable trends or seasonality [19].
This hypothesis is not true when forecasting renewable energy, for instance in the case of
wind power, where the production is subjected to the high variability of the wind, and
therefore the estimator values may change over time. The same happens in solar power
production, the similar patters of the sun over a year might lead to some confusion, but it
is again subjected to high variability due to weather conditions, such as cloud cover or
precipitations.
In order to illustrate the stationary hypothesis and how it aﬀects power forecasting
estimators, an example has been created. A synthetic data set is created for training the
batch ADMM algorithm (deeper understanding on how analysis on synthetic data sets are

4.2
Online Distributed Coordinate descent
After checking a large variety of algorithms from the gradient descent family, such as [22],
[23], [24] and [25], most of them proposing stochastic versions of the original gradient
descent with horizontal splitting of the data set, it was decided to try to adapt the well
known coordinate descent algorithm in a distributed way, in an attempt to solve the
online optimization problem. The desire of using this algorithm came from [26], were the
authors address the problem of l1 and l2 regularization using coordinate descent. However,
the inspiration for using this particular algorithm came from [3]. The authors use the
Coordinate Descent algorithm combined with VAR models in the prediction of wind power
using high dimensional data sets. The aim is to use the simplicity and easy implementation
of the algorithm to develop an online distributed version, suitable for working with large
data sets and with acceptable tracking abilities, so as to respond well to renewable energy
production variability.
The coordinate descent algorithm consists of ﬁnding the minimum along each of the
coordinate directions, one a at a time, while keeping the others ﬁxed. This way, the problem
becomes simpler, as the aim is to solve a univariate problem in a loop. Furthermore, the
development of an online version of the algorithm is a non trivial task, as the calculations
are a summation of all the previous ones, and this can be transformed into an addition of
the past computations plus the new one.
As aforementioned, the main goal of distributed learning is the division of a main task
into smaller ones that are easier to solve and the global minimum can be reached by the
association of the previous. This algorithm has two main requirements [27]:
1. the loss function must be convex and diﬀerentiable.
2. if the function can be decomposed in convex functions, the non-smooth functions
must be separable.

the author of [4] points out that it is important to be careful with this statement, as
decreasing its value too much does not lead to better forecasts, on the contrary, it can
origin high instability, in particular during stationary periods.

The testing of the ODCD approach is done through the creation of a synthetic data set,
in order to compare it with the OADMM approach that has been developed as well. A
meticulous study on the resulting estimators will be carried out to check the tracking
abilities of the novel approach of the coordinate descent, before testing it on real world
data. Additionally, a sensitivity analysis on the key hyperparameters of the algorithm is
also included to discover to which degree the changes in their values can potentially aﬀect
the estimators and the other variables. The simulation study on synthetic data has been
done in the framework of Monte-Carlo simulations [29], where each run represents one
realization of the time series in order to demonstrate robustness and avoid overﬁtting.
The analysis is done by creating a centered synthetic time series with two lags and two
parameter matrices. The development of a time series that is centered is done by subtracting
the mean value for each time series involved in the operation to each individual time stamp.
This procedure allows the assumption of a zero value of the intercept term β0, reducing
the number of estimators to be ﬁtted. In the distributed learning operation, three agents
take part in the agreement and the one that is the central agent will be speciﬁed in each
study set up.
Subsequently, the time series is deﬁned in the form of a VAR model where the response
vector yt ∈RT,3 whereby T is the total length of the considered data set.
In these
simulation studies the number of steps has been set to 30000 time stamps. Each time
stamp is calculated as a linear regression of the explanatory variables of the agents
yt−k ∈RT,3 times the parameter matrices βt−k ∈R3,3 plus white noise, where k denotes
the lead time. Moreover the design matrices yt−1 and yt−2 represent the lagged time series
of all the 3 agents. They are conﬁgured as close as possible to reality by following a normal
distribution with mean µ = 0.5 and a standard deviation σ = 0.2. This values are chosen
to keep the power values within possible ranges and to avoid them to diverge to inﬁnity.
Lastly some noise is added as ϵt that follows a Gaussian distribution with µ = 0 and
σ = 0.1. All the simulations represent one-step ahead predictions. Given measurements up
to time t −1 the one step prediction for time t is given by equation 5.1.

factor deﬁnes the number of past time stamps or data points that the algorithm takes into
account for minimizing the loss function. During the simulation studies with synthetic
data of the former section and the simulations with real data, the value of the forgetting
factor has been kept constant. Increasing or decreasing its value aﬀects the accuracy of
the forecasts during the distributed learning agreement.
The architecture of the three scenarios is quite similar to that presented in the previous

correlation factor between the ﬁrst lag of agent 1 and the current value of the central agent
(agent 1 as well, thus the high β parameter). On the other hand, the hyperparameter λ is
set to 0.01, so that the shrink of the parameters given by this penalty parameter does not
inﬂuence the study.

SIMULATIONS ON REAL DATA SETS
During chapters 4 and 5, the OADMM and the new online approach via coordinate descent,
the ODCD, algorithms were presented and their tracking abilities checked through several
simulation studies. Considering the satisfactory results of the analysis with synthetic data,
the next step is the application of the online algorithms over a real data set. In the present
chapter, a performance analysis is done for the two algorithms with two diﬀerent data sets.
The ﬁrst data set is constituted of several wind farms in Australia, whilst the latter is
based on photovoltaic power plants in San Francisco. Both the OADMM and the ODCD
are compared against an AR model when only the one agent using its own data is involved,
that will serve as benchmark model.
The performance analysis of the algorithms is carried out by calculating statistical scores
and its improvement over persistance forecasts. Each of the cases is divided in four parts:
presentation of the data set, data preprocessing, hyperparameter tuning and performance
analysis. Lastly, the estimator coeﬃcients are checked during a period of time stamps.
In all the cases, the simulations are considered done in a controlled environment, and
therefore, no privacy has been added to the ODCD approach.
6.1
Case study procedure
The evaluation of the algorithms has been done in four steps. In the wind data set, as
well as in the solar case, the section starts with a brief overview of the study case and a
description of the data set. Then, a subsection is included where the data preprocessing
steps followed before working with the algorithms are described. For the last two parts of
the evaluation of the algorithms, the data set has been split in two parts. The ﬁrst one
corresponds to the third section of the procedure in order to to evaluate the algorithms,
and consists of the tuning of the hyperparameters of each model. In the case of the ODCD,
these values are the forgetting factor µ, penalization parameter λ and number of lags L.
On the other hand the OADMM needs the tuning of the previous hyperparameters plus
the additional ρ parameter that controls the step size. In these studies, and similarly in the
ones carried out over the synthetic data set, the forgetting factor has been considered to be
static to reduce the number of hyperparameters to be tuned, making the cross validation
process a bit easier.
Furthermore, a benchmark approach has also been taken into acoount to compare the
performance of both algorithms. This has been done through an AR model where only one
agent is involved in the learning process. This agent predicts its estimators using only local
information given by the data it can obtain from its power plant or set of power plants.
This process does not follow an online approach but a batch one, thus the agent selects
the hyperparameter and then calculates the coeﬃcients of its model. Finally, it generates

Test case on wind data set
The wind data set consists of 22 wind farms located in South East Australia. It includes
power values of the wind farms for the years 2012 and 2013, with a resolution of 5 minutes,
where the production of each wind farm at time t corresponds to the sum of all the
wind turbines included in that wind farm at time t. This data set was published by the
Australian Electricity Market Operator (AEMO) [30], and it is now available at [31]. Some
data processing has been performed, as each of the wind farm power production values
have been normalized by their nominal power so the values lies within a range from zero
to one.

It has been decided to reduce the resolution of the data set, from 5 minutes to 30 minutes,
due to the fact that it is easier to work with less time stamps, and it has been assumed that,
even though the resolution is way lower, the obtained results will still be acceptable and

6.2.1
Data preprocessing
Before starting the learning process the time series of all agents have been centered so
as to avoid ﬁtting the intercept term. The centering has been done by subtracting the
mean of the selected time series and adding it again after the learning process is ﬁnished.
Additionally, the data has been split in two subsets. As aforementioned the ﬁrst subset is
used for the hyperparameter optimization and for that the ﬁrst 20000 data points of the
data set will be the training set. Afterwards, the statistical scores are computed on the
next 10000 time stamps. Therefore this second part of the data set corresponds to the test
set.
6.2.2
Hyperparameter tuning of the algorithms
Once the data set is prepared for its use in the learning process, the tuning of the
hyperparameters can start. Before explaining the strategy followed in each algorithm,
there are several common assumptions that aﬀect both algorithms in the same way. First
of all, the hyperparameters selected for the OADMM and the ODCD have been assumed
to be the same for all agents. This is due to the large time it would require tuning each of
the wind farm hyperparameters, especially in the OADMM, where it has been experienced
a higher computational time. Therefore, it has been decided to apply this condition for
both algorithms, even though it is known that this may aﬀect the overall performance of
the process.
Conversely, this assumption has not been applied to the autoregressive model of a unique
agent in which the optimal regularization parameter λ has been chosen and applied for
each agent. The model selection of the AR has been done by using the function LassoCV
from the library scikit learn in Python. This function is inside the set of linear models
and can be customized. The selected parameters were cv = 5, so it performs a ﬁve folds
cross validation, and fit_intercept = False, on the basis of the previous assumption and
subsequent data centering that the intercept value is zero. Finally the best hyperparameter

is chosen after the cross validation operation and the model is ﬁt. Another assumption
would be that in both approaches the optimal hyperparameters have been found for
one-step ahead predictions. This could result in a problem due to bad forecasting in the
performance evaluation, when checking the error in the estimations for predictions several
steps ahead. The rolling cross validation procedure has been chosen for the tuning of
the hyperparameters. This process has been explained already in chapter 3.2, but each
algorithm follows a strategy to select the optimal values. The model selection has been
done by comparing the RMSE score in several sets of hyperparameters and by checking
the sparsity of the estimators.
Online Distributed Coordinate Descent
In the case of the ODCD three hyperparameters need to be optimized: the forgetting factor
µ, regularization strength λ and the number of lags. A strategy based on grid search has
been adopted. Firstly, the number of lags was set to L = 2. Then a range of λ and µ values
were selected. The results were given in a network of values as the RMSE and the degree of
sparsity (%) in the estimators. If the RMSE value only happened to increase or decrease,
the range of values, given for the forgetting factor or the regularization parameter, were
redeﬁned until a possible minimum was found. The same procedure was repeated for L =
3 and L = 4, and it transpired that the lowest RMSE values were found when the lag was
set to L = 3. The cross validation results are obtained by averaging the resulting RMSE
and sparsity values of repeating the process, with each agent being the central agent once.
This means that the distributed learning process is performed in parallel 22 times. In each
of these simulation scenarions, one of the wind farms is chosen as the central agent and
the rest as the wind farms inside said agent’s agreement, in order to perform distributed
learning in an attempt to copy reality, as the wind farms in a grid will be at the same time
central agents and contracted.
Moreover, the rolling cross validation has been slightly adapted to the tuning case, in order
to decrease the time of computing. The process followed has been to give the algorithm a
warm start at time stamp 5000, so the burn-in period is not included in the cross validation.
Then the next 15000 data points have been divided in 3 sets of 5000, where the data has
been trained for the ﬁrst 4000 and the RMSE and sparsity values have been tested for the
last 1000. Finally, the RMSE and sparsity values have been averaged. This method can be
denominated as a pseudo three fold cross validation. It is important to mention that the
RMSE values were not compared with persistence, but only with the computed RMSE
through the formula given by 3.15. Finally, the average values of the RMSE and sparsity
obtained for the best reﬁned grid search when the number of lags is set to 3. Therefore,

this situation having a great improvement in the RMSE but quite a small one in the MAE

leading to higher values of RMSE. However, the predictions are fairly accurate and this
can be seen to compensate for such higher RMSE values. Thus, when equal weight is given
to all errors in the MAE calculation, an improved performance may be observed.

whilst it is reduced greatly in the second and third steps ahead. Here the eﬀect of optimizing
the hyperparameter as the average values has either no impact on the results or it enhances

performance.

CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORKS
This last chapter covers the conclusion of the present case study and a list of possible
future works that can be done as a continuation of this thesis.
7.1
Conclusion
The main objectives of this thesis were understanding the use of distributed learning in the
forecasting of renewable energy, and implementing two algorithms, one from the literature
reviewed and a novel approach to another one based on the literature as well, so as to test
them with real data sets. This is done to take advantage of the spatio-temporal correlations
among the time series of nearby power plants.
The study of a batch distributed learning led to poor results in the forecasting of renewable
energy. A simple simulation study could show that, as the estimators are learn only once,
the resulting values appear to be an average of the recommended parameters for the
selected training set. Therefore, the principal disadvantage is the impossibility of following
the changes in power production, especially in wind power data sets, where phenomena,
such as variations in the wind direction or speed due to meteorological conditions, can
lead to the unusefulness of the calculated estimators. Thus, it was noted that the need of
algorithms that allowed updating the estimators was relevant.
The most popular solution applied on batch learning to improve the forecast accuracy is
to train the estimators after a certain number of time stamps, including the new available
data. However, the computational burden of this process would be quite large, as the
set of iterations needed to obtain the estimators would have to be repeated every time
a recalculation was needed, which is impractical. Also, if a major change in the power
production occurs right after the new estimators are deﬁned, the whole period of time until
the next calculation would result in higher deviations from the real value, which would
lead to low forecasting quality.
Therefore, the introduction and study of online learning was done in an attempt to ﬁnd
robust algorithms that allow the recalculation of the estimators whenever new data points
were available. In a ﬁrst stage, the aim was to compare two algorithms that were based on
diﬀerent approaches, and thus, one was based on the ADMM, the OADMM and the other
on gradient descent methods, the ODCD. The addition of new available information in the
algorithm increases the size of the input data as the time passes. This problem is addressed
in the literature by the introduction of a forgetting factor, in order to consider only useful
information in the estimators updates. The main motivation in the development of the
ODCD was to construct an online algorithm with the same properties of the OADMM
but with a complexity reduction. It was noted that the implementation of an algorithm
via coordinate descent enable the reduction of the number of hyperparameters, hence
60
==PAGE:73==
7. Conclusion and Future Works
decreasing the tuning diﬃculties of the ODCD and with apparent similar properties.
Nevertheless, it was noted that this algorithm came with the disadvantage of sending a
large amount of messages, as the production of the partial predictions of each member
that takes part in the learning agreement had to be done sequentially, increasing the
communications of the network.
The testing of the algorithm implementations through a synthetic data set showed that
the approach based on coordinate descent has basically the same tracking abilities as the
ODCD, with minor changes in the speed of reaching the true values during the burn-in
period. This led to the conclusion that the ODCD approach could perform well with
simplier computations and less hyperparameters. However, during this phase, several
assumptions were made, such as the suboptimal hyperparameter tuning.
Lastly, the test of the algorithms on real data sets created new points of discussion. In the
case of the application on the wind data set it was noted that, even though the algorithms
can estimate the parameters quite well, the correlation among the time series of the wind
farms were very low and therefore no signiﬁcant improvement over a traditional batch AR
model without exogenous information was achieved. Additionally, some limitations of the
ODCD appear here, as its performance is worse than the ODCD, since it is not capable of
obtaining an adequate sparsity degree. On the other hand, the tests carried out over a
solar data set were more promising, as now the improvement of both distributed algorithms
over persistence was over 30%. In this case, the sparsity level obtained in the ODCD was
greater, and this led to not so diﬀerent results when compared to the OADMM. The great
enhancement on the solar data set results means that the study of distributed learning
approaches on solar energy production could become quite important in the future.
To sum up, it has been noted that the use of distributed learning in the forecasting of
renewable energy can become one of the most important strategies to follow in the future
of power grids and smart grids. However, it is important to remark the necessity of ﬁnding
more accurate models that can actually surpass the performance of traditional AR models
and capture the spatio-temporal correlations between similar time series.
7.2
Future works
The continuation of research projects on distributed learning applied to renewable energy
needs to be an important focus of the industry in the future. The studies introduced in
the present thesis allow several lines of investigation.
First of all, on chapter 4, an attempt was made in the acceleration of the ODCD approach
by reducing the number of messages sent in the network with the parallel update of the
estimators on all the agents. This led to acceptable results with a lower number of agents
in the grid, but the increase in uncertainty of the parameters that came when more agents
were added into the network made this approach not recommended for working with high
dimensional data sets. An interesting research part could come from trying to ﬁnd an
improvement of the algorithms that would come from the reduction of messages shared in
the grid.
Additionally, this work can also make the implementation of privacy on the algorithm easier.
61
==PAGE:74==
7. Conclusion and Future Works
The development of an algorithm that protects the messages sent is key in distributed
approaches, as when working with other companies they might not be willing to share
their data. Therefore, the importance of privacy in the development of algorithms is vital
for the future introduction in the grid. In the case of the OADMM approach, the privacy
term is already included. However, in the available studies, the encryption matrices are
kept constant during the whole learning process. After a certain period, this fact can lead
to privacy breaks, and therefore, further studies on variable encryption matrices without
increasing greatly the communication steps and maintaining the same good properties are
an important task to be followed.
The tuning of the hyperparameters of the present thesis has been done by averaging the
optimum values of all the agents conforming the learning process. It would be interesting
to develop a method to perform the individual hyperparmeter tuning for each agent.
Furthermore, the explanatory variables included in the simulations are the lagged time
series of the power values. This could be extended by using other variables, such as wind
direction or speed.
Lastly, other possible and more ambitious extensions would be to make a transition from
producing deterministic forecasts to probabilistic, or the introduction of regime-switching
approaches, like the ones proposed in [11] and [35].
