Abstract
A European goal in the heating and cooling sector is to expand the district heating and
cooling network share in the thermal energy market to 50% by 2050. A promising energy­
efficient and local waste heat source in urban areas are data centers. The potential eco­
nomic and environmental benefits of using surplus heat in the district heating network
are especially advantageous in Nordic countries like Denmark. They have a beneficial
cold climate, an efficient electrical grid with cheap electricity, a large share of renewables
in their electricity portfolio and an extensive and developed district heating sector with
high penetration of renewable sources due to the use of biomass. A significant factor for
economically viable waste heat recovery is policy. Taxation abstentions and other pol­
icy schemes can remove barriers of entry into the district heating market and displace
market shares with new players, such as data centers. Because of this, it is essential to
design policy schemes in such a way that they form a future district heating market that is
desirable. To this end, current EU policy schemes targeting waste heat recovery are in­
vestigated and the economic and environmental benefits of integrating data center waste
heat in the district heating market were analyzed. A set of scenarios incorporating district
heating market portfolios based on electric, fossil fuel and biomass thermal energy pro­
duction were modeled separately. The objective of this master thesis is to study the effects
occurring due to the entry of data center surplus heat usage in the various district heating
market scenarios under a one­time payment and a monetary payment per unit produced
support scheme. The descriptive district heating models are developed to include the EU’s
regulatory, climate and market conditions. The two support schemes were compared and
evaluated concerning the effects on the district heating system. Conclusions were drawn
regarding the support measures on the economic and environmental outlook for integrat­
ing data center surplus heat in the district heating market in each generated scenario.
The study concluded that the data center integration in the biomass portfolio had a total
system cost reduction of 0.89 M€ and 0.59 M€ for the one­time payment and monetary
payment per unit produced support schemes, respectively. Data center integration in the
electric and fossil portfolios lead to an increase in the cost of the system. In the electric
scenario, a cost increase of 9.8 M€ and 5.09 M€ was determined under the one­time pay­
ment and monetary payment per unit produced support schemes, respectively. The fossil
scenario showcased a system cost increase of 5.32 M€ and 4.73 M€ for the one­time
payment and monetary payment per unit produced support schemes, respectively. It was
determined that significant emission reductions due to data center waste heat integration
were achievable in all portfolios under the two policy schemes considered, except for the
one­time payment aided biomass portfolio scenario and the monetary payment per unit
produced electric scenario.

Introduction
A primary policy goal of the European Union is to reduce carbon emissions by 80% by
2050 compared to the 1990 carbon emission level [1]. To achieve this goal, major restruc­
turing in the energy sector is required. A fundamental aspect that needs restructuring is
the heating sector, which accounts for about 50% of the final energy consumption globally
[2]. An economical solution approach is district heating, a piping network connecting heat
sources and consumers utilizing water vapor [3], [4]. Expanding the district heating sec­
tor in Europe will increase system efficiencies and centralize the emissions of the heating
sector, in addition to aiding the entry of surplus heat from industries, such as data centers
and power plants [5].
The current European strategy is to increase the thermal energy share supplied by
district heating networks from the current 13% up to 50% by 2050 [6]. A source with great
potential is waste heat produced in urban areas, including data centers [6]. The data
center sector has been rapidly growing over the past years and is expected to continue to
do so. The European data center industry has a waste heat recovery potential of roughly
56 TWh [7] and it is estimated to account for 1.1­1.5% of the global energy consumption
[8]. Considering the magnitude of this power demand and the expectation that it is going
to increase 15­20% annually [8], [9], it is vital to investigate data centers as a thermal
energy source, offsetting their carbon footprint and increasing their importance in society
[8], [4].
The high demand for heat in predominantly northern European countries and the well­
established district heating network has a high potential for data center waste heat in­
tegration [10]. The temperature requirements in the district heating network are striving
towards lower temperatures, thereby increasing the future potential of data center low­
quality waste heat integration [10]. The increasing demand for data processing has been
corresponding with a trend in consolidation of data centers into larger entities [11]. This
corresponds to a larger and more reliable heat source from data centers as their size
increases [11].
Of the European population, approximately 73% live in urban areas, indicating that
a significant part of the heat demand is in high­density areas [3]. As data centers are
typically situated in urban areas [6], they are an available and closely situated heat source.
Data centers produce an excess of heat and have an almost one­to­one conversion rate
for the electricity consumed to the heat produced [8]. Many studies have been conducted
on waste heat integration from data centers in the district heating system, and it has been
determined that it is beneficial for both the district heating and data center operator in
economic terms [11]. For example, in Espoo Finland, a data center waste heat recovery
project was able to decrease the district heating system cost by up to 7.3% [7].
As investment costs are high and the return on investment horizon is uncertain, sup­
porting policies are required to enter data center waste heat in the district heating system
[11]. Policies that have been unitized to incentivize socio­technological change are pub­
lic investment & planning, taxes, subsidies and regulation [12]. The low­carbon energy
transition in Denmark is an excellent example of such policy schemes directing socio­
technical change. By utilizing a mixture of state investments and taxes, Denmark was

able to transit from a 90% fossil sourced energy mix to a share of 15% renewables and
60% energy­efficient CHP plants in a time horizon of 30 years [13]. This analysis will

This project investigates the effects of data center waste­heat integration in various dis­

environmental potential of data center integration in the various considered district heat­
ing systems. The support measures utilized in this analysis will be evaluated regarding
their economic prospects on the cost of the systems and assessed for their potential in
decarbonizing the various considered district heating portfolios. The change in market
shares is analyzed, and the systemic change by introducing data center waste recovery
in the district heating systems is assessed.

Literature Review
To determine if waste heat recovery from DCs is a technically viable option, a study
identifying the potential of energy savings and reviewing key problem areas of air man­
agement in DCs was considered. It was determined that, of the total amount of electricity
consumed in DC, 97% is convertible to utilizable waste heat [14]. In addition, the au­
thors paper determined that the recovered waste heat temperature ranges from 40 ­ 43
°C [14]. These values have been taken from a DC not utilizing a free cooling setup. If cold
air from the surroundings is utilized to cool the DC, the total waste heat recovery will be
significantly lower [14]. The simulated DC, in this study, could heat 30916 non­domestic
buildings considering the average energy demand of this type of building in Finland [14].
It is representing that the integration of DCs in the DHS has significant potential.
The mentioned temperature range would be favorable regarding waste heat recovery
by an excess HP, however, this is an average value and the temperature varies between
cooling set ups and DC sizes [15]. In this study, it was determined that air­cooled DCs
typically showcase a temperature rise of 10 ­ 20 °C [15]. This represents the temperature
range the HP installed in the DC can utilize, to upgrade the waste heat to the required
temperatures in the DHS. As most DCs utilize an air cooling set up, the determined tem­
perature rises in the cold to hot aisle of the small and medium sized DC were used in this
analysis [15].
The second method utilized as a cooling setup in DCs is liquid cooling. A study was
reviewed assessing the waste heat recovery potential of such DCs [7]. The study deter­
mined that a cooling setup utilizing four servers connected in series by a liquid cooling
system achieves the most optimal conditions for waste heat recovery, with a waste heat
temperature range of 47­51 °C [7]. In addition, to identifying the optimal cooling setup
in a liquid­cooled DC, this study also determined the business case and economic ben­
efit of investing in a waste heat recovery project. The waste heat recovery from such a
project could reduce the CO2 emissions by supplementing a natural gas heating system
for a swimming pool by up to 60% [7]. As newer and subsequently larger DC designs
are associated with high power loads, too high to be sufficiently covered by an air cooling
system [15], a liquid cooling setup was applied in the large DC design in this analysis.
There are two main input factors to consider when assessing waste heat recovery
projects, the cooling setup and the location of waste heat recovery [16]. The study re­
viewed the most recent advancements in cooling technologies and determined the most
optimal waste heat recovery techniques for district heating. It was determined that the
most optimal location to capture waste heat in air­cooled DC is the rack exhaust [16].This
cooling setup’s optimized temperature range is 35­45 °C [16]. In a liquid­cooled DC, the
optimal location is the water exiting from the servers, with a temperature level of 75­80 °C
[16]. The determined optimal locations of waste heat capture were utilized in the analysis
to ensure the most cost­effective heat production in the DCs considered.
To determine the most optimal set up to integrate the waste heat from the DC into the
district heating system, a study was utilized that analyzed the financial and environmental
viability of three methods [8]. The waste heat upgrading setups reviewed were a HP and
two applications of borefield configurations [8]. They all were designed to meet the cooling

The viability of DC waste heat integration in the DHS from a cost of production per­
spective was reviewed. An economic investment analysis on a small, medium and large
DC, regarding waste heat utilization in the district heating system, determined an NPV of
­48000€, 1.04 M€ and 16.3 M€ for the small, medium and large DC, respectively [11]. It
was concluded that an investment proposal is viable for the medium and large DC. The
small DC does not have a viable economic outlook. However, it was also determined that
this investment analysis is sensitive to input variables such as the electricity price [11].
This project investigated the sensitivity from a cost of production point of view regarding
the electricity price and discount rates applied to the investments required, to enable heat
recovery from DC in the DHS. A techno­economic study conducted on an air­cooled 1000
kW DC was conducted in Barcelona, Spain [11]. The study evaluated the performance
of a CRAH plus chiller setup and a rear door cooling setup, regarding their potential for
waste heat utilization. Utilizing an HP to upgrade the heat to the required temperatures in
the local DH, of 90 °C, it was determined that only the rear door cooling solution is eco­
nomically viable [11]. However, this study also found that the analysis is sensitive to the
DHN analyzed and the electricity prices utilized [11]. This analysis investigates the cost­
of­production case of the three DC types under the technical characteristics of the Danish
DHS and the Danish electricity price under various heat production mixes, investigating
the potential of DC waste heat integration in the DHS utilizing different sets of producers.
The sustainability aspects of DC waste heat integration were review in [18], [19]. A
DC waste heat recovery project in northern China was utilized to determine the economic
and environmental benefits of such projects [19]. It was determined, that the project can
replace 5292 MWh of electric energy by utilizing waste heat, equating to 4289.2 tons
of CO2 emissions saved when considered as the substitute for standard coal­fired heat
production [19]. Assuming direct monetary gains from environmental benefits, the study
concluded that the project has an NPV of 10729 kCNY(1.5 M€), a BCR of 1.15, and an IRR
of 16.54% [19]. Accounting for environmental benefits in monetary terms is not applicable
in most real­world applications, but the CO2 reductions found in this study are substantial.
This master thesis will investigate the potential of CO2 reductions but will determine the
origin of the CO2 reductions, by comparing which type of producer is phased out of the
system due to DC integration in various district heating market portfolios.
The study [18], investigated the potential CO2 emission reduction of a large high per­
formance liquid­cooled DC. The scenarios considered in the analysis were utilizing the
waste heat from the DC directly in buildings near the project and, a second, utilizing a HP
to upgrade the waste heat to be fed in the return flow of the DHS. It was determined that
the set­up utilizing a HP returns a higher usage of waste heat and subsequent higher CO2
reductions, with a return of investment time of 10 years regarding the investment cost of
the HP installed [18]. The CO2 reductions achieved in the HP set­up are 720 t(CO2)/a,
equating to 4% of the total emissions of the Lichtwiese campus considered in the study.

A study investigating the barriers of entry for waste heat utilization from DC in the district
heating market has determined to be low­quality heat, high investment cost, lack of heat
demand, inconvenient infrastructure, unclear business models and differing expectations
regarding financial outcome of the DC and DH operators [10]. It is discussed that the
main issue is not technical, as viable solutions exist in the market, but instead that there
is no clear solution for DC operators to make a profit. Therefore, there is a need for real
data regarding waste heat utilization from DC in the DHN, to build a better understanding
of the possible business models [10].
A study investigating the DC as prosumers in the district heating system from an up­
stream renewable energy supply and downstream waste heat utilization has found that
regional climate studies are an effective way to optimize the integration on waste heat from
DCs [16]. It is stated that the research of DC waste heat integration is an extensively in­
vestigated topic [16]. However, the current state of research regarding downstream waste
heat usage in different DH market portfolios is still lacking and will be further investigated
in this thesis, to the authors knowledge.
A policy reviewed was the ”Erneuerbare Energien Gesetz (EEG)” in Germany [21].
The Feed in Tariff (FiT) for Photovoltaic (PV) Systems in Germany is part of the EEG.
The EEG was implemented in the year 2000 and constantly adapted to current demands
regarding the development and progress of renewable energies in the energy market and
changing requirements in the energy market [21]. Small rooftop PV­Systems for private
households receive a fixed FiT for each kWh produced and feed into the electricity grid.
At the time of implementation of the EEG in the early 2000s, the FiT was much higher
at more than 50 ct/kWh than the price for electricity consumed at about 15 ct/kWh [22],
[23]. Therefore private household owners with a rooftop PV­System were economically
incentivized to feed the entire electricity produced into the grid to receive a high payout. It
was financially more beneficial to meet their electricity demand with electricity consumed
from the grid since the FiT was higher than the electricity prices. This was necessary
as PV­Systems were expensive and economies of scale were not established. Once
the industry of scale for producing the required parts for PV­Systems was established,
and businesses specialized in the implementation of rooftop PV­Systems, the costs for
rooftop PV­Systems drastically reduced [24]. Therefore the subsidies could be adapted
continually to be less costly to contribute to a more stable electricity grid and be less
financial incentivized. One key adjustment was the continuous reduction of the FiT. Today

the FiT for rooftop PV­Systems is between 8­9 ct for each kWh fed into the grid. In contrast,
current electricity prices for households range from 33­48 ct/kWh depending on household

electricity consumed by the grid. As a result, only the leftover electricity production is fed
into the grid. This is much more beneficial for grid stability.
Although the model applied in this report cannot depict a FiT that could be applied to the
heat generated by the data centers, as only the cost of production was considered in this
analysis, a semblance of a FiT policy was applied in the investigation. An approximation
of a required FiT that would enable data centers to enter the district heating market was
determined in this analysis by iterating through monetary payments per unit of heat gen­
erated that would enable data centers to enter all considered market portfolios. Although
the initial value of this payment might be high, with time, it will decrease, and data centers
will become more competitive in the district heating network.
One major contributor to greenhouse gas emissions in Germany is the heating of pub­
lic and private buildings. Therefore the German government started a program to reduce
CO2 emissions in the heating sector [21]. The program subsidizes efficiency improve­
ments, for example, better thermal insulation and fossil fuel free heating technologies
such as heat pumps [26]. The initial investment costs for heat pumps are typically 2­3
times higher compared to conventional heating technologies such as oil or gas boilers
[27]. The operating costs for each technology largely depend on the fuel type and con­
sumption. Gas boilers are currently the technology with the highest operating costs, with
nearly double the amount compared to oil boilers and heat pumps. To offset the com­
paratively high initial investment costs and incentivize the installation of heat pumps, a
one­time subsidy of up to 40% of the initial investment costs is used [28]. Household
owners who purchase a heat pump receive the subsidy in the form of a one­time pay­
ment after the purchase. The exact percentage and amount of the subsidy depend on
the heating system being replaced and the type of heat pump being used. This correlates
directly to the reduction in CO2 emissions. To further incentivize the use of heat pumps,
special heat pump electricity tariffs have been introduced to reduce the operating costs of
heat pumps. To qualify for the lower heat pump tariff, the grid operator can disconnect the
heat pump for a limited number of hours from the grid to prevent grid overload. In turn,
the grid operator fees for the heat pump electricity tariffs are lowered. The tax rate for
electricity heat pump tariffs is lowered compared to general electricity tariffs as well [28].
These subsidies enable household owners to change to a fossil­free heating technology
without additional financial burdens. Secondly, these subsidies are meant to push and
accelerate the growth of the heat pump industry to reduce overall CO2 emissions in the
heating sector. This policy scheme was then utilized as a framework to determine the
optimal one­time payment subsidy that would incentivize data centers enough to enter
the district heating system in the analysis conducted.
To the author’s knowledge, there is currently a lack in research regarding environmen­
tal and economic effects of integrating DC into the district heating market given different
market portfolios. Therefore, in this thesis, conclusions will be drawn regarding the bene­
fits of implementing support schemes in the given market portfolio and how they compare
given the considered set of producers, in addition to investigating and comparing how the
different market actors are affected by the entry of data centers in all analyzed portfolios.

DH market portfolios
The heat dispatch is highly dependent on the heat generation technologies utilized in
the DHS. Therefore, to analyze the heat dispatch of data center waste heat integration,
the various market portfolios dominated by a fossil, biomass and electric heat generation
were developed. The following section describes how the individual market portfolios
were derived.
To generate realistic market portfolios of a biomass, fossil and electric district heating
system, the generation technologies and fuel consumption in the EU­27 countries were
reviewed [45]. Denmark, with the highest share of biomass fueled heat generation, was
used to derive the biomass scenario. Germany, with a high share in gas and coal, was
used as a basis for the fossil scenario. While other countries have a larger share of fossil
fuel heat generation in their district heating market, Germany also represents a relatively
established district heating network with a large domestic production and consumption.
Deemed to be more representative than an outlier country, such as Greece, which almost
exclusively relies on fossil fuels for its heat generation. Lastly, as there is no EU country
that relies heavily on electric heat generation, Sweden, with one of the highest shares of
electric heat generation was chosen to derive the electric scenario, the electric scenario
can be considered as a future outlook for all EU countries.
The electric market portfolio is based on the source [47]. To determine the plant types
subdivided by the type of fuel they use for the electric scenario, the overall heat production
by plant type for the district heating system in 2021 was determined from [47]. Secondly,
the type of fuel consumed in 2019 to heat the district heating sector was derived from
[48]. The percentage of the heat generation technology types was then further divided
into the respective percentage of total fuel usage in 2019 to determine the capacities of
the given technology type. As the technology generation type flue gas condensation was
not depicted in the model, this heat generation was added to the total heat generation of
the CHP plants. To determine the total capacities of each plant type, the percentage of
each technology type was multiplied by the peak demand of the demand profile derived
from [29], which was then divided by the peak loss coefficient to determine a conservative
estimate of the maximum sum of the capacity set utilized as a parameter in the model,
equating to 410 MWth. However, this did not result in a high enough electric heat gen­
eration capacity in the market portfolio. To alleviate this issue, an equal arbitrary amount
was subtracted from the largest shares, CHP biomass and WtE CHP, and added to the
industrial heat pump and electric boiler equally, to obtain a market portfolio that was more
than 50% electric heat generation. The resulting capacity mix is shown in the appendix
in table A.31.

5.0.3
Policy design
A major issue in the European district heating systems is that they have natural monopoly
characteristics [31]. The high investment cost on the production side and supply infras­
tructure disincentives new actors from entering the market [10]. For data centers, this is
mainly the investment cost in the necessary equipment to upgrade the waste head to the
required temperatures and the piping system connecting the data center to the district
heating network [10]. The following support measures were used in the analysis to iden­
tify optimal support schemes that could support the entry of data centers into the district
heating market.
One­time payment support
The one­time payment support is in simple terms the fuel and CO2 tax revenue from the
previous year of operation without DC integration distributed equally to the three clusters
of DC types. The percentage of the share that is paid out to the DC equals to the capacity
to be connected divided by the upper limit of capacity.
To determine the tax revenue for each portfolio the fuel tax code and carbon tax in
Denmark was applied. The danish fuel tax code was utilized as they implemented a law

Heat dispatch
To depict the effect that the DC WH integration has on the district heating dispatch order,

Biomass portfolio
Figure 6.1, shows the comparison of the market dispatch of the given producers in the
no integration of DC versus tax­aided integration of DC scenarios. It depicts the production
behavior over one year, with high demand during the winter period and low demand during
the summer period. The tax­aided integration is interesting to investigate as it showcases
where the DC types are in the dispatch order of the technology mix considered without aid
to their heat production. Figure 6.1b shows that the entry of DC in the biomass portfolio
phases out the Straw CHP plant, Electric boiler and Coal CHP plant. The back­pressure
gas plant sees an increase in capacity and becomes lower in the dispatch order.
The large DC enters the market as a base producer with a WAVG cost of 9.7 €/MWhth,
undercutting the Industrial air HP by a significant margin. This can be attributed to the
higher COP of the large DC. The small and medium DC types enter as peak producers
and replace the Coal CHP and the Electric boiler. With a cost of 15.1 €/MWhth and 13.23
€/MWhth, both are cheaper than the Industrial HP, which has a cost of 15.62 €/MWhth.
As the DCs replace the production of the HP during higher electricity prices, the WAVG
cost of the HP is reduced to 15.62 €/MWhth from 17.63 €/MWhth by the integration of
DCs in the DH production mix.
An unexpected result of the DC integration is that the Straw CHP plant is pushed out
of the system and mainly replaced by the back­pressure Gas CHP plant. It should be
mentioned that electricity production is the only possible profit plants can make in this
model; because of this characteristic of the model, the electricity production of all CHP
plants is maximized. As both plants are back­pressure plants, their electricity production
is linked to possible heat production. It appears that the entry of DCs reduces the required
need for heat generation capacity by the Straw CHP plant. This is due to the high nominal
investment cost of this plant type A.18, that could be saved by decommissioning after it is
displaced. All WAVG cost data for the two scenarios is depicted in tables A.39 and A.42.

Sensitivity Analysis
Two sensitivity analyses were conducted. The first sensitivity considered the invest­
ments made required for WH recovery from a private perspective. The second was a
high and low electricity price scenario.
To investigate the integration of DCs from a private investment perspective, in the vari­
ous market portfolios, a 15% discount rate was applied to the nominal investment cost of
the HPs installed and the required DH infrastructure to connect the DC to the DHN. The
discount rate of 15% was chosen as DC WH recovery projects typically have a discount
rate ranging from 10­20% [10], and the median value was used. It was found that no
integration occurs in any portfolio under a monetary payment scheme. For the one­time
tax payment, the same behavior as depicted in the result section is seen.
The second parameter considered is the electricity price. A 25% decrease and increase
in the average electricity price utilized in the analysis were applied. The high electricity
price was tested with the monetary payment subsidy to investigate if the payment was
high enough to incentivize DC integration in such a scenario. It was determined that no
investment in DC integration was made in any portfolio. The low electricity price was
applied to the tax­based support scheme to investigate the production behavior of DCs in
a low electricity price scenario.

Discussion
DC WH integration in all portfolios increases the system’s total heat production. This is
due to the higher losses, as transmission losses were only assigned to the DCs. There
is a linear increasing trend in heat production from the small to large DC. Economies of
scale in the DC WH recovery design lead to lower production costs in larger DC WH re­
covery projects. Secondly, the heat production in the larger DC sizes is more efficient
as they have a higher COP. This shows that the cooling setup in the DC is an important
factor to consider in WH recovery project. The heat production cost in all portfolios in­
creases except the biomass market portfolio due to the integration of DCs. For DCs to
enter market portfolios dominated by fossil and electric heat generation, support mea­
sures are required. DC heat production is highly dependent on demand. They are mostly
dispatched during high demand periods (i.e. the winter). The required demand for DC
heat generation decreases with the increasing sizes of the DCs. The heat production in
DC is not highly dependent on electricity prices but the production profile clusters around
lower electricity prices. This stands for both the electric and biomass market portfolio. In
the fossil portfolio, the DC heat production is highly dependent on low electricity prices.
The production of the DCs is independent of the seasonal changes of the COP. This is
because the demand is very low in the summer period, and the COP variation is not large
enough to significantly affect the cost dispatch in the various DC considered. From the low
electricity price scenario, it was determined that the one­time payment support scheme
is a better fit for DC WH recovery projects. The same scenario also concluded that, in a
future DHN with higher integration of renewable generators, DH WH integration results in
electrification of the DHS.
The DC WH integration in all portfolios was found to increase the system’s total heat
production. This trend applies to both support schemes considered in this analysis, how­
ever, it is more prominent in the monetary aided support scheme. In all three scenar­
ios, we see a large increase in heat production, but more significantly in the electric and
biomass portfolios. This is occurring due to the higher losses in the system, given the DHN
design considered in this analysis and showcasing that the distance required to connect
the DC to the district heating network is a determining factor in their heat production. In
addition to revealing the DC integration sensitivity regarding its CO2 reduction potential to
the carbon equivalence of the electric grid, the higher losses also equate to equivalently
higher CO2 emissions compared to other electric heat generators. This was shown in the
monetary subsidized electric portfolio, due to this trend, the emissions increased. This
is indicating that for a future electric scenario, a more environmentally friendly support
scheme is a one­time payment support scheme.
The heat production in DC is not highly dependent on electricity prices in the electric
and biomass portfolio. In the fossil scenario, we see an opposing trend to this result. This
suggests that a one­time payment subsidy is a better approach toward DC integration
than a support measure toward the production cost of the DC. This is also reflected in the
requirement of a high monetary payment to support DC integration in this analysis.
DC heat production is highly dependent on demand. They are mostly dispatched during
the high demand periods (i.e. the winter). The required demand for DC heat generation
decreases with the increasing sizes of the DCs. This trend holds for all market portfolios

There is a linear increasing trend in heat production from the small to large DC. Economies

that only certain types of DC, with a more favorable cooling setup towards WH recovery
in the DHN, are supported. This could also support systemic change in the development
of new DC projects to weight this design criteria with higher importance.
Reviewing the results of the two aid schemes in the Biomass portfolio, it can be con­

system is significantly reduced, although this can only be attributed to the integration of
the small DC. Additionally, relatively cheap per unit of CO2 reductions can be achieved.

market share is high and the profit of selling electricity on the day­ahead market is skew­

can reduce the cost of heat in this market portfolio.
DC integration in the fossil market portfolio is not cost­effective. It increases the cost

WH integration does not lead to long­term systematic changes. The monetary payment
scheme pushes the DC into the heat dispatch as competitive producers. This will only
last as long as the aid is provided, as they are not competitive in the cost of production
dispatch.

would be phased out of the system by both support schemes considered. The biomass

In the tax scenario, we see a reduction of the CO2 emissions as the DC takes over the
industrial HP’s heat production share. The HP in the DCs have a higher COP and, subse­
quently, are more efficient in producing heat than the industrial HP. The heat production
of the electric heat producers occurs to a higher degree during low electricity prices, given
that the utilized electricity price and equivalent carbon emissions data sets origin is Den­
mark. Low electricity prices correspond to high wind power penetration [58]. Due to this,
the integration of DC reduces the overall emission of the system. On the other hand,
the monetary subsidy per unit produced has a much lower cost of policy and lower cost
increase in the cost of heat production. However, the CO2 emissions are increased as
the dependency on low electricity prices is decreased with this support scheme, and as
such, the above­mentioned trend does not apply. Secondly, this support scheme leads
to higher utilization of the DC as they have higher losses than the other producers. The
total emissions increase in this scenario.
The sensitivity analysis revealed that the monetary payment per unit of heat produced
needs to be higher to warrant DC integration when viewing the required investments made
from a private perspective. As the subsidy is already unrealistically high, it speaks against
this approach and suggests that a one­time payment to overcome the high initial invest­
ment cost of such projects is more appropriate. It was determined that a lower electricity
price leads to higher heat production in the biomass scenario. However, this is due to the
chosen analysis approach of only considering the cost of heat production while selling the
generated electricity on the day­ahead market. This result depicts the dependency of the
model on the electricity price regarding electricity generation in the CHP plants. Includ­
ing the DH market price in the model would lead to a more accurate depiction of the DC
integration potential, given that CHP plants are included in the analysis. A significant find­
ing is that DC integration phases­out the biomass HOB given a reduced electricity price,
this is in accordance to [20]. This shows that in a future scenario where lower electricity
prices apply due to higher integration of renewable generators, DC integration electrifies
the DHS, in accordance to other findings [57].
Future work to consider is to include the district heating price in the model. This could
lead to a much more accurate depiction of the DC integration especially considering the
market portfolios with high CHP plant market shares, such as the biomass portfolio in this
analysis. Furthermore, an interesting parameter to further investigate is the fuel prices
utilized in this analysis. Considering that the DC integration is not reasonable due to
the low cost of the gas HOB in the fossil scenario and the recent gas fluctuations [59],
this could change the results of this analysis considerably. Lastly, as the case for DC
WH recovery is more prominent in north European countries [10], it would have been
interesting to see how the inclusion of free cooling would affect the DC integration in the
various market portfolios. It would be expected that it would reduce the viability of the
integration and require higher support measures for the DCs to enter the heat dispatch.

Conclusion
This study identified the economic and environmental befits of data center waste heat
integration in the district heating system. The gap in research this work alleviated is a
comparative analysis of the effects of data center integration given various district heating
market portfolios. Conclusions were drawn regarding support measures to aid data center
integration and their effectiveness is evaluated considering the various market portfolios,
in addition to investigating and comparing how the different market actors are affected by
the entry of data center waste heat in the heat dispatch.
A techno­economic model was developed and a cost­of­production analysis on the sup­
port scheme aided integration of data center waste heat was conducted under the con­
ditions of the various market portfolios considered. The market portfolios representing a
biomass, fossil and electrically dominated district heating system were derived from the
Danish, German, and Swedish district heat systems. A policy scheme analysis was con­
ducted on European legislation aiding new energy sources to enter energy markets to
determine possible support measures for data canter waste heat recovery in the district
heating system.
The conclusion of this work is that due to data center integration the cost of the system
is increased in both the electric and fossil scenarios under both support schemes consid­
ered. Only the biomass­dominated district heating system has a system cost decrease.
CO2 reductions occur in all portfolios due to DC integration under the two support mea­
sures, except the one­time payment aided biomass scenario and the monetary payment
per unit produced electric scenario. It was determined that, due to the higher losses asso­
ciated with data center heat production, an important consideration is the carbon equiva­
lent emission profile of the electricity consumed by the data center. A one­time payment
support scheme was determined to be more environmentally friendly, especially in the
future electric scenario considered. Due to the low dependency on electricity prices and
high dependency on demand regarding the heat production of data centers, the one­time
payment support is more optimal to aid the integration of data centers in all considered
market portfolios. An increasing trend in heat production was determined from the small
to large data center types, due to economies of scale and an increasing COP in larger
sized data centers. This trend should be considered when drafting supportive legisla­
tion for data center integration in the district heating system, as the potential cost and
CO2 reduction of the system can be increased by supporting larger data centers with a
more optimal cooling setup. Lastly, it was determined that, in a future scenario with a
lower electricity price due to higher penetration of renewable energy sources, data center
waste heat integration can electrify the district heating network.
