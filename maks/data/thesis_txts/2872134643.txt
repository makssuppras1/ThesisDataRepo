Abstract 
 
Denmark’s groundwater-based water supply is at risk, due to overexploitation, pollution, droughts and 
increasing demands. The Geological Survey of Denmark and Greenland (GEUS) provides a database of 
all wells and waterworks in Denmark, as well as the connections from wells to waterworks and from 
waterworks to water supply areas. A hydro-economic optimization model is implemented in this study, 
based on the water supply system data provided by GEUS, for the capital region of Denmark. In 
addition, an effort is made to map the water exchanges between utilities and a first water exchange 
model is implemented in the optimization model. The goal of this model is to optimize groundwater 
allocation and the water supply infrastructures to keep fulfilling the needs of the water users in the 
future, without harming the environment. The optimization model is a multi-catchment, multi-
temporal linear programming model. Optimal decision variables and shadow prices are analysed to 
provide recommendations for groundwater resources management. The study shows that meeting the 
demand without overexploiting the groundwater resources will be a challenge in the capital region of 
Denmark, and other water sources should be considered to reduce the pressure on groundwater 
resources. The study also highlights the value of increased connectivity of water exchange networks 
for improving the water demand fulfilment, and the resilience of the network against limited 
groundwater availability.

1. Introduction 
 
Denmark’s groundwater resources availability is threatened because of overexploitation, pollution and 
climate change. The Danish Environmental Protection Agency estimates that the groundwater 
resources on Zealand are overexploited, by 50% over their sustainable threshold (Danish 
Environmental Protection Agency, 2023b). Across the island, there are huge regional differences in 
groundwater exploitation between the capital region and the rest of the island. The quality of 
groundwater is also an issue: a lot of wells are polluted on Zealand, reducing the amount of available 
water for human uses (Jørgensen & Stockmarr, 2009, Laerke Thorling et al., 2024). Between 2020 and 
2023, pesticides have been detected in more than 50% of the wells monitored in the groundwater 
monitoring program of Denmark, and PFAS have been detected in 16% of them. (Laerke Thorling et 
al., 2024). Poor quality of groundwater poses a threat to freshwater ecosystems because of a 
groundwater - surface water interaction (Duque et al., 2023). In addition, droughts are becoming more 
and more severe and are also posing a threat in Denmark for the upcoming years by reducing 
groundwater availability and therefore having huge social, environmental and economic impacts 
(Naumann et al., 2021). On top of that, regional water demands in Denmark are increasing for some 
specific sectors, such as the industry, Power-to-X applications, and agriculture, and are expected to 
further increase in the future (Danish Environmental Protection Agency, 2023a). For all these reasons, 
public utilities need to better operate their water supply systems and make informed investment 
decisions to improve the system efficiently. In particular, hydro-economic optimization models can be 
used for providing policy recommendations and highlighting better management practices. 
Hydro-economic optimization models are used in many countries to address water management 
issues. Many have highlighted the relevance of using optimization models for groundwater 
management and have given an overview of the possible application for hydro-economic optimization 
models (Gorelick & Zheng, 2015, Singh, 2012, Harou et al., 2009). Such models aims to connect the 
natural hydrological system with the water distribution system and the water users. In hydro-economic 
optimization models, the water is given an economic value which depends on when and where it is 
allocated. Traditionally, the objective function is defined as the maximisation of the value, or the 
minimisation of the cost (Harou et al., 2009). It should be highlighted that the objective function can 
be an economic objective but can also focus on social aspects, being for example the maximization of 
the user’s demand fulfilment or the fairness of the water allocation among users. The term economy 
refers to the use of economic benefit as a metric to evaluate the value created by water allocation. 
Hydro-economic optimization models provide optimal decision for water allocation, given the current 
water supply infrastructure, and the physical constraints. They also provide the shadow prices 
associated with all the constraints of the model. The shadow prices give the change of value in the 
objective function per change of constraint, so they indicate whether a given constraint in the model 
is binding or critical (Vijay P. Singh, 2017). The shadow prices can be analysed to decide whether or not 
to build given infrastructure in the system, or whether or not to increase the abstraction capacity in a 
given groundwater reservoir, etc. Therefore hydro-economic optimization models can be used by 
decision makers to design water management policies. 
Hydro-economic optimization models have been widely used (Harou et al., 2009), and many different 
mathematical optimization methods can be applied to implement them (Labadie, 2004). In particular, 
a study published in 2021 in Denmark, used linear programming for optimizing groundwater 
abstraction in one catchment in Jutland (Danapour et al., 2021). The work shows a lot of similarities 
with the study described in this report, but at a smaller scale. In Danapour’s study, the focus was more

on modelling in detail the catchment and the surface water network. The work focused particularly on 
defining environmental flow requirements for 1484 stream locations in the catchment. 
In previous studies the focus has been mainly on groundwater allocation optimization, while this study 
aims to include a model of the water supply system and optimize both the groundwater allocation and 
the water supply infrastructures. Few examples of work using a hydro-economic optimization model 
at a basin scale for optimizing water supply systems can be found in the literature. A study conducted 
in California has optimized California’s water supply system (Jenkins et al., 2004). They built a large 
scale optimization model of the California’s water supply system, including a hydrological model of the 
area and around 3,000 decentralized water agencies. The study identified that water transfers can 
improve the flexibility of the water system, and they also identified which storages bring most value. 
They showed that a lot of improvement can be done to make the system more efficient. Another study 
conducted in the East Bay Municipal Utility District in San Fransico bay, an urban area, is using linear 
programming for optimization for water shortage management, by modelling the entire water supply 
distribution network including water transfers (Wilchfort & Lund, 1997). The goal was to reduce the 
impacts of water shortage by optimizing the water supply system. They concluded that this approach 
could be extended for a lot of urban water supply reliability problems.  
 
This study aims at showcasing a new management tool in Denmark, which combines a hydrological 
model and the water supply distribution system in one optimization tool for decision support. The goal 
is to implement a hydro-economic optimization model for the capital region of Denmark, in order to 
optimize water supply systems and groundwater allocation and recommend water management 
policies to ensure societal needs without harming the environment.

The hydro-economic optimization model is implemented using linear programming, a branch of 
mathematical optimization, as the optimization method. The optimization model is supplied with 
groundwater recharge data that come from the national hydrological model of Denmark (GEUS, 2019). 
Data extends from 1989 to 2022. The model also uses data about the Danish water supply system and 
data about the water consumption of the water users. The model is coded in python, using pyomo, a 
python-based, open-source optimization modelling package (William Hart & Jean-Paul Watson, 2008). 
A combined database required for using the model as well as the python script of the model are 
available in section A of the Data Appendix. 
 
Figure 1 shows the area of the study corresponding to a portion of the island of Zealand, covering the 
city of Copenhagen and its surroundings. This area includes most of the wells owned by the water 
utility company HOFOR for delivering water to the city of Copenhagen, where the water demand is  
highest in this region. This area is composed of 299 active wellfields and 279 active waterworks (GEUS, 
2025), as well as 255 active water supply areas (Schullehner, 2022). For the study, the area has been 
split into 32 catchments, called watersheds (Figure 1 and Figure 3).

2. Data and Methods 
2.1. Modelling approach 
2.1.1. Conceptual model 
 
Before setting up the linear programming optimization model, a conceptual model of the water 
abstraction and distribution system has been developed. Figure 2 shows the conceptual model. The 
sketch summarizes the main components of the model.  
 
Figure 2. Conceptual model of the water systems in the capital region of Denmark  
The area of the study has been split into 32 catchments (Figure 1 and Figure 3), that will be later 
described in section 2.2.1. On each catchment the aquifer has been modelled using one single linear 
reservoir, taking as an input the groundwater recharge and the pumping, and, returning as an output, 
the baseflow to the river. 
The pumping of water from the groundwater reservoir is implemented at the wellfield level. A wellfield 
is a group of wells spatially close to each other, which all send the water they pump to the same place. 
There can be multiple wellfields in one catchment.  
From the wellfields, the water is sent to the waterworks. A wellfield can be connected to more than 
one waterworks, and a waterworks can be connected to more than one wellfield. Connected wellfields 
and waterworks may be located in more than one catchment. 
At the waterworks level, the water can be stored, sent to other waterworks through water exchange 
links or sent directly to the water supply areas for distribution to the water users. A waterworks can 
be connected to more than one water supply area, and a water supply area can be connected to more 
than one waterworks. Again, connected waterworks and water supply areas may be located in more 
than one catchment.  
==PAGE:12==

Each water supply area is associated with a water demand value for all 4 categories of users: 
Households (HH), industries (Ind), public services (PS) and agriculture (Agri).  
Finally after being supplied to the users, the water returns to the environment. A percentage of the 
water will go to the sea, and the rest will return to the river and contribute to the baseflow in the 
rivers. Therefore, the baseflow in the rivers, is the sum of the outflow from the groundwater reservoir 
and the return flow from the water supply areas to the river after consumption. 
The sections 2.2 and 2.3 will describe the data sources for each component of the conceptual model 
described in Figure 2. 
 
2.1.2. Implementation of the model with linear programming 
 
A linear programming (LP) optimization model is implemented. The model integrates the entire 
conceptual model, including water abstraction and water distribution. 
Linear programming is a branch of mathematical optimization. The basic form of a linear programming 
problem is expressed in the following way:

Applied to this study, the linear programming optimization problem can be formulated as:  
Maximizing an objective function (water need fulfilment, total benefit, ...), by finding optimal 
values for the decision variables (weekly pumping, water allocation, …) under constraints (abstraction 
licence, maximum transfer capacity, minimum baseflow requirements, …).  
In a linear programming model, all relationships between variables must be linear. Multiplying two 
variables is not allowed. In this section, all the linear programming set-up is described, as it is 
implemented in python. Table 1, Table 2 and Table 3 list respectively the indices, variables and 
parameters of the linear optimization set-up.

The objective function is the product of the water allocation and the willingness to pay for each 
category of users minus the pumping cost and the water exchange cost. In this study, the pumping and 
the water exchange costs are set very low compared to the benefit of providing water to any user. This 
ensures that the model never over-pumps water if it’s not necessary and always finds the shortest way 
for water, from the groundwater reservoirs to the users, without going through many water exchanges 
if  not needed. Therefore, the linear optimization model will maximize the fulfilment of the need of 
the users in the most efficient way.

Water balance constraints 
The water balance constraints occur at three levels, the wellfields, the waterworks and the water 
supply areas.  
At the wellfield level, an equality constraint states that the pumping at the wellfield level is equal to 
what is supplied from the wellfield to the waterworks (Equation ( 6 )). The variable Supply_WF_WW 
contains the actual amount of water transferred from one wellfield to one waterworks. Equation ( 7 )

2.2. Boundaries of the study 
2.2.1. Catchments definition 
 
The selected perimeter of the study has been defined based on the catchment definition from the DK1 
perimeter in the water resource assessment report of GEUS (Henriksen et al., 2024). The perimeter 
corresponds to 5 of the 12 catchments used in the report, as shown in Figure 3. The 5 catchments from 
Figure 3 (a) have been subdivided into smaller catchments based on the stream network, in Figure 3 
(b). These smaller catchments are called watersheds in the rest of the study. Thirty-two watersheds 
have been defined within the spatial perimeter of the study.  
The watersheds have been defined based on the ID15 catchments provided by GEUS (section B of the 
Data Appendix) and the river shape. The ID15 catchments have been merged, when connected to the 
same stream, from upstream to downstream, leading to the definition of the watersheds. Therefore, 
the watersheds are primarily surface water catchments. The boundaries of the watersheds also fit the 
shape of the bigger catchments from GEUS resource assessment report, that correspond to 
groundwater geological reservoirs, except for one of the watersheds (watershed number 12). 
 
  
 
Figure 3. (a) Left: 5 Catchments among the 12 of the DK1 model catchments (b) Right: Watersheds catchments 
 
2.2.2. Optimization period 
The evaluation period lasts from 1989 to 2022, that corresponds to the evaluation period of the 
national hydrological model (GEUS, 2019) that has been used to compute catchment water balances. 
Thirty-three years of data are used in the model to account for the natural variation of the recharge, 
both seasonal variation but also multi-annual variations over longer periods. However, water use data, 
abstraction licenses, etc., are based on the most recent data, because the goal is to evaluate whether 
the current infrastructure can sustain the current water consumption, over an evaluation period of 
many years.

2.3. Data collection and processing 
 
This section describes input data for various model parameters and forcings. Data processing 
workflows are described and justified. The section is subdivided into 3 parts, which represent the 3 
core component of the model, i.e. the hydrological model, the water supply model, and the water 
users model.  
 
2.3.1. The hydrological model  
 
Data 
As presented in Figure 2, the model needs, as an input, the groundwater recharge. Groundwater 
recharge cannot be directly observed and therefore it needs to be computed from an already existing 
model. 
The model that is used to estimate groundwater recharge, is the national hydrological model (GEUS, 
2019). The national hydrological model is used to simulate a water balance on the 33 years period. The 
data are extracted from MIKE SHE software (DHI, 2025) using the water balance calculation tool. This 
tool returns a lot of water balance items, and some of them can be aggregated in order to get an 
estimation of the groundwater recharge. The list of the water balance items, with their description can 
be found in appendix (Table 6). 
The groundwater recharge, has been estimated by summing up ‘qrech’, the recharge flux from UZ to 
SZ and ‘qrechmp’, the recharge flux from UZ to SZ via macropores. The water balance calculation tool 
also provides the lateral groundwater inflow and outflow. This has been neglected in the study, but no 
evaluation has been done to estimate the impact of this assumption on the results.  
The water balance calculation tool also gives: 
• 
an estimation of the baseflow to the rivers for each catchment, by aggregating the categories 
‘qszdrtorivin’, the SZ drainage to MIKE SHE River Links inside of the water balance sub-area 
and ‘qszrivpos’, the baseflow from SZ to MIKE SHE River Links.  
• 
an estimation of the pumping for each catchment, contained in the variable ‘qszabsex’, the 
groundwater pumping from SZ. 
These data are being used to parameterize a linear reservoir surrogate model of the national 
hydrological model. The reason for doing that is that in the optimization model, the groundwater 
pumping is a decision variable, that can be modified by the model in each scenario, therefore the 
baseflow is impacted and it is not possible to simply use the baseflow computed with the water 
balance calculation tool of MIKE SHE from the national hydrological model. 
The water balance calculation tool also provides the surface runoff, which should be used in the model 
as it is contributing to the baseflow. As the surface runoff is very small compared to the outflow of the 
groundwater reservoir, it is assumed that it is negligeable.  No study has been conducted to evaluate 
the impact of this decision on the results.

2.3.2. The water supply model 
 
Data on the water supply system are partly extracted from the Jupiter database (GEUS, 2025), a 
database gathering information about all the wells and plants in Denmark. In the Jupiter database, a 
plant corresponds to either a wellfield or a waterworks . A plant is an object that receive water from 
wells, wellfields or smaller waterworks and there can be multiple levels of plants from the wellfield 
level to the biggest waterworks. 
 
Overview 
The water supply system in Denmark is represented in the figure below (Figure 6).  
The water is extracted from the groundwater at the wells level. A plant that receives water from a 
group of well is called a wellfield in the model. The water collected in a wellfield is sent to a 
waterworks. Ultimately the water from the waterworks is delivered to the users located in the water 
supply areas connected to the waterworks. 
 
Figure 6. Sketch of the water supply model 
 
All data regarding the water supply system have been provided by GEUS. 
The following data have been extracted from the Jupiter database: 
• 
Well database: the database of all the wells in Denmark with the connection to wellfields and 
the abstraction license 
• 
Hierarchy database: The connection between all plants of the Jupiter database (wellfields and 
waterworks)

During data processing, all the wells connected to the same plant ID are gathered and the 
corresponding plant ID is called a Wellfield. The Wellfield has an abstraction license, which is already 
defined at the plant level in the well database. The wellfield is assigned to the Catchment ID 
corresponding to the catchment ID of the first well in the group of wells composing the wellfield. Taking 
the catchment ID corresponding where the majority of the wells is located would be better, however 
due to some technical issues, this has not been implemented. This is something that could be changed 
to make the model more accurate. As the wellfields are always composed of wells very close in space, 
it would probably not make a big difference. 
A waterworks is defined as a plant that is connected to at least one water supply area.  
 
The above two definitions of wellfields and waterworks make it possible for the waterworks and the 
wellfields to be the same plant, with the same plant ID. This occurs when the water distribution is done 
at a local scale, with only one plant between the wells and the users, which is therefore defined both 
as a wellfield and as a waterworks. For modelling purposes, in this situation, there will be one wellfield 
and one waterworks created, with the same plant ID and connected to each other in the matrix 
containing the connection between wellfields and waterworks (WF_WW matrix). 
When this is not the case, then the Hierarchy database is used to find the link between the wellfields 
and the waterworks. For each wellfield, all the downstream connections are studied one by one until 
a waterworks is found, meaning until one of the plants is connected to a water supply area. Then a 
connection is created in the WF_WW matrix. Even when a waterworks is found, the algorithm does 
not stop here, it continues to go to the next upper plants to find maybe another waterworks and create 
a new link in the matrix. This is necessary because in a multi-level distribution system, the water 
distribution can occur at multiple levels and not always at the very top of the plant hierarchy.

After the waterworks, each connection between a waterworks and a water supply area from the water 
supply area database is translated into a matrix containing all the links between waterworks and water 
supply area (WW_WSA matrix).

ready to be used in the model.  
Some connections have been added by hand, by modifying the database, where missing links have 
been identified. This is the case for Slangerup waterworks (Plant ID 4230) that has been connected by 
hand to the plants 45627, 45628, 168484 in the Hierarchy database. Glostrup waterworks 106321 and 
107060 were also missing in the database, therefore they have been added by hand in the list of the

it is connected to the water supply area 3559, 3560, 3564, 3565, corresponding to the Copenhagen 
area. Finally, the total pumping capacity of HOFOR’s waterworks at Regnemark were lower than 
expected, because some of the wells are outside the perimeter of the study. Therefore 6,500,000 
m3/year of abstraction licence have been added by hand for Regnemark, to reach a total abstraction 
licence of 15,000,000 m3/year. These modifications significantly increased the quality of the model, 
but they are probably not the only mistakes in the databases. Therefore more work should be done on 
data quality to improve the robustness of the model.

2.3.3. The water users model 
 
Estimation of the water demand 
The water demand in the model is estimated based on the average water consumption in 2023, in 
Denmark. Data on water consumption have been retrieved from Danmarks Statistik (Danmarks 
Statistik, 2025c). The water consumption is divided in four categories: 
• 
Households 
• 
Industries 
• 
Public Services 
• 
Agriculture 
 
The raw data from the website are water consumption for the whole country. The water consumption 
has been scaled to Zealand, based on the population living on the island. The total population on 
Zealand is 2,513,981 inhabitants (Klimadatastyrelsen, 2024a) and the total population in Denmark is 
5,992,734 inhabitants (Danmarks Statistik, 2025b). 
Then the water consumption has been scaled to the water supply areas using the population density 
(Klimadatastyrelsen, 2024b) for the Households water consumption and the job density (Danmarks 
==PAGE:27==
Data and Methods 
27 
 
Statistik, 2025a) for industries and public services. The water consumption for agriculture is assumed 
to be zero in Zealand, as irrigation is not allowed on the island, by default.  More research should be 
done to estimate the real agricultural demand in Zealand, as irrigation is still authorized for some 
specific uses. 
At the end of the process, each water supply area is associated to a yearly water consumption for all 
four categories. The yearly water consumption is subdivided into a weekly average water consumption. 
This information is added to the water supply area database before being imported in the model. 
 
Adjustment of the water demand value 
The process described above, lead to a total water demand on the defined area of 134.4 million 
m3/year, split between the categories of users as follows: 
• 
Households: 81.1 million m3/year 
• 
Industries: 34.6 million m3/year 
• 
Public Services:  18.8 million m3/year 
• 
Agriculture: 0 m3/year 
Considering the fact the population over the whole modelled region is 2,052,864 inhabitants, this 
corresponds to an average consumption of 39.50 m3/pers./year by households and a total 
consumption of 65.49 m3/pers./year when accounting for all categories. 
These numbers do not match the data of Vand I Tal 2024 (DANVA, 2024), that states that the 
households consumption in 2023 is 35.89 m3/pers./year and the total consumption in the country is 
59.43 m3/pers./year.  
Therefore, the water demand was adjusted, to match the per capita water use numbers reported by 
Vand I Tal 2024. A global reduction factor has been applied. The water demand for households is 
multiplied by 35.89/39.50 = 0.909 and the water demand for industries and services is multiplied by 
(59.43-35.89)/(65.49-39.50) = 0.905. 
This reduces the total water demand in the model to 121.9 million m3/year, which is value used in the 
model for the rest of the study. 
 
Estimation of the willingness to pay of the different categories of users 
The willingness to pay for households has been estimated using data from two publications, Vand I Tal 
2024 (DANVA, 2024) and a publication from the thinktank Brundtland (Brundtland, 2025). In Vand I Tal 
2024 the average price of water for Households is estimated to be 77.25kr/m3. Brundtland, 2025 
quantifies the Danes willingness to pay for clean drinking water. The study states that the Danes are 
willing to pay 448kr more per year to get clean drinking water. Therefore, based on the Danish average 
annual water consumption of 35.89 m3 (DANVA, 2024). The additional cost people are willing to pay 
per m3 is 12.48kr/m3, raising the willingness to pay of households to 89.73kr/m3. 
No good estimation method has been found for estimating the willingness to pay for industries, public 
services and agriculture. Therefore, in the study, the willingness to pay of these user categories are 
expressed as a percentage of the willingness to pay for households. The willingness to pay of the 
category Public Services is set to 1/2 times the willingness to pay of the households. The willingness 
==PAGE:28==
Data and Methods 
28 
 
to pay of the category industries is set to 1/3 times the willingness to pay of the households. The 
willingness to pay of the category agriculture is set to 1/4 times the willingness to pay of the 
households.  
In this study, the willingness to pay are just used for prioritizing the users. The users with the highest 
willingness to pay will be prioritized by the model, compared to the other users. Therefore, in all 
scenarios, the households are prioritized compared to the public services, and the lowest-priority users 
are the industries. Regarding agriculture, as the water demand is set to zero, the willingness to pay 
does not have any influence on the results. 
 
2.3.4. Additional assumptions 
 
Additional assumptions have been made on the data, either because of constraints due to the use of 
the linear programming tool, or to simplify the model. Below is a list of assumptions that have been 
taken: 
 
• 
The groundwater recharge data derived from the national hydrological model contains some 
positive and negative values. The negative values are an issue for running the optimization 
model with a linear reservoir model for groundwater reservoir as it is the case in this study. 
The problem is that the reservoir could run dry and storage could become negative which is 
does not make physical sense. Therefore, for each groundwater data series, the data have 
been processed in the following way: 
For each timestep where the value of the recharge is negative, the value has been set to zero 
and the former negative value of this timestep is added to the next time step. If the next time 
step becomes negative because of that, the same operation is executed for this time step. This 
is done for all the data series. Therefore it removes all the negative values, without losing the 
information on the total water balance over the period, unless the last value of the series is 
negative. If the value at the last timestep of the series is still negative, the value is set to zero 
and the data is lost.  
 
• 
The minimum flow requirements are based on the yearly average flow value. The yearly 
average baseflow for each catchment should be at least 75% of the yearly natural baseflow for 
the catchment (the flow if there was no human intervention through pumping). 
 
• 
The storage capacity for all waterworks is 0, because the timestep of the model is one week, 
and most of the waterworks just have a storage capacity of around one day, for daily operation 
of the plant. We thus assume that WW storage is not relevant at the weekly timescale. 
 
 
• 
Tinghøj is a water storage reservoir of a capacity of 250,000 m3. The reservoir has been added 
manually, as a waterworks connected to no wellfield, but only connected to other waterworks 
that send water into it and connected to the water supply areas of Copenhagen. 
 
 
==PAGE:29==
Data and Methods 
29 
 
• 
By default, the percentage of wastewater returning to the river is assumed to be 30%. In 
Copenhagen catchment and Amager catchment (watersheds number 16 and 27) it is assumed 
that 100% of the water goes to the sea and 0% to the river, because all the wastewater 
treatment plants of this area are near the coast. 
 
 
• 
The pumping cost has been set to 1kr/m3 and the water transfer cost has been set to 1kr/m3. 
These values do not have an empirical basis, they are set to guarantee that the model does 
not pump or exchange water if those actions do not generate benefit.

2.4.2. Method for analysing of results 
 
There are two types of outputs the model provides, which can be displayed and analysed. 
The optimal decision variables. For each decision variable, the model returns the optimal value for 
this variable. (An example of a decision variable is the allocation of water to the households in WSA 
number 1039 at timestep 154.)  
The shadow prices. The shadow prices correspond to how much benefit would be gained by making 
the constraints more loose. Shadow prices are always associated with a specific constraint. (An 
example of a shadow price is: the benefit of changing the abstraction license to allow pumping of one 
additional m3 of water in the WF number 24 at  timestep 56 is 80kr.) The shadow prices are calculated 
by the model, by solving the dual optimization problem associated with the primal problem. Getting 
the shadow prices expressed in kr/(m3/week) is a direct consequence of using the willingness to pay 
expressed in kr/m3. However, in this study, the absolute value of the shadow prices should not be 
trusted, because the willingness to pay are not realistic, they are just used to prioritise the users. The 
shadow prices are useful to compare the impact of different constraints and find which constraints are 
the most limiting for the current system. Therefore they can be used to guide investment decisions.  
 
The optimal decision variables that are analysed are: 
• 
The pumping in at the wellfield level 
• 
The compensation pumping 
• 
The water exchanges at the waterworks level 
• 
The storage at the waterworks level 
• 
The water allocation to all four categories of users 
• 
The baseflow in the rivers 
There are more decision variables in the model, but for some of them do not provide additional 
information, and therefore are not presented in the results. 
 
The shadow prices that are analysed refer to the following constraints: 
• 
The pumping at the wellfield level must not exceed the abstraction licence, Equation ( 4 ) 
• 
The storage at the waterworks level must not exceed the storage capacity, Equation ( 9 ) 
• 
The water exchanges at the waterworks level must not exceed the transfer capacity, Equation 
( 10 ) 
• 
The baseflow in the river must be higher than the minimum flow requirement, Equation ( 14 ) 
• 
The total pumping in each catchment must not exceed the groundwater maximum abstraction, 
Equation ( 18 ) (only present in scenario 1)

3.3. Further development and future research 
3.3.1. Groundwater reservoir modelling 
 
The linear reservoir model, as presented in Figure 4, is not flexible enough to represent the behaviour 
of the aquifers, in the selected catchment. A more realistic approach would probably use multiple 
reservoirs connected to each other, some of them with a higher residence time constant. 
Therefore more work should be done to design and calibrate a better groundwater surrogate model. 
A suggestion for another model that could be used shows in Figure 24. In the figure, the hydrological 
catchment refers to the big catchments defined by GEUS in the water resource assessment report 
(Hans Jørgen Henriksen, 2023). 
 
Figure 24. Sketch of an alternative groundwater reservoir model 
==PAGE:45==
Results and Discussion 
45 
 
3.3.2. Water supply data quality 
 
More work is required on the data collection and processing regarding the water supply system. This 
study has shown that the water supply areas framework and the Jupiter database can be used to 
parameterize a large optimization model integrating every component of the water distribution 
system, from the wells to the users. However this work also highlighted that some data are missing in 
the different databases, making the final model a coarse approximation of the reality. 
More research is needed to: 
• 
Ensure that all water supply areas are connected to the correct waterworks. During the study, 
some water supply areas have been connected by hand to the relevant waterworks, because 
some connections were missing. 
• 
Ensure that a water supply area is connected to waterworks that can provide enough water to 
fulfil the demand, meaning that the total abstraction licence of the associated wellfields has 
to be higher than the demand. 
• 
Getting a better overview of the connections between the plants. Both the vertical 
connections: from a wellfields or a waterworks, to a bigger waterworks, and the horizontal 
connections: water exchanges between waterworks. 
 
3.3.3. Better estimation of the water demand 
 
Better estimation of the water demand needs to be achieved, both for the current and the future water 
demand at the 2050 horizon. This will allow to make a more realistic model, and to provide some 
insights regarding how to deal with the evolution of the water demand by 2050. 
Moreover, in this study the yearly water demand has been divided equally between the 52 weeks of 
the year. However, the water demand is varying each week of the year. Therefore, the water demand 
could be adjusted, with the water demand factor provided by HOFOR, in the appendix. (Table 8) 
 
3.3.4. Implement a wastewater network 
 
This study does not include the wastewater system. The water, after being consumed, returns to the 
sea, or to the river in the catchment where the user is located. 
However, the wastewater distribution system should be implemented, to get a better modelling of 
where the water ends up after consumption. This is especially important for environmental flow 
requirements because the fulfilment of these minimum baseflow objectives will be impacted by the 
percentage of water returning to the river in each catchment. 
Moreover, in the current method, the list of connections from all water supply areas to the catchments 
is needed. This is challenging, because the boundaries of the water supply areas do not match the 
shape of the catchments. Therefore the water supply areas are now allocated to the catchments with 
the bigger overlap. However, this is an approximation, and that could be improved. 
==PAGE:46==
Results and Discussion 
46 
 
3.3.5. Create new scenarios 
 
Once running correctly, the model should be used to study the impacts of various scenarios. Here is a 
list of scenarios that could be interesting to study: 
New connections between waterworks. The shadow prices can be used to determine whether a new 
pipe would be a good investment or not in some specific place. This could be used to suggest new 
connections between waterworks. 
Increasing demand. Water demand is increasing locally, with some industries. One could study how to 
fulfil the demand in a growing water demand situation. 
New environmental flow requirements. The environmental flow requirements in this study have been 
defined at a yearly level, and based on the natural flow of the river. Other definitions could be used 
and more research is needed to create environmental flow requirements that make more sense and 
that provide a better protection of the environment. 
Alternative water sources. Alternative water sources could be implemented in the model as 
desalination for supplying water to Copenhagen. 
Drought adaptation. Based on some historical records of drought in Denmark. One could study how 
to fulfil the water demand during drought events in the future.  
Water demand for agriculture. Watering fields is not allowed by default on Zealand, but depending on 
climate evolution in Denmark, there could be situations where watering becomes necessary. Therefore 
it could be interesting to study how to find enough water to fulfil a growing agricultural water demand 
on the island. 
 
3.3.6. Expanding the model to cover all Zealand. 
 
All data are already there for expanding the model to the whole island of Zealand, and not only the 
capital region. The steps for doing that would be to define new catchments for the whole island, then 
run the hydrological model on MIKE SHE with these new catchments, and then process the rest of 
the data for the new domain instead of the just for the capital region.  
Technical guidance for implementing it, is provided in section A of the Data Appendix.

4. Conclusion and Recommendations 
 
This study has demonstrated that building a groundwater allocation optimization model based on the 
national hydrological model, a model of the water supply system, and a water use model in Denmark 
is possible given the existing data. One main outcome of this study is to provide a combined database 
required for this type of model. For now, the model is only implemented for the capital region of 
Denmark but it can be extended to the rest of the country. With further development, the model could 
be made available for water supply companies or for the Danish government and be used for 
optimizing infrastructure and the groundwater resources management at a regional level. A headline 
result of this work is the evaluation of the water exchanges between waterworks at the capital region 
level. The current implementation of the water exchanges is very basic but could be developed more 
with data from the water supply companies and the municipalities about the water exchanges.  
Overall, the baseline model is only able to fulfil about 80% of the estimated water demand of the 
capital region, due to some simplifications. The main simplification being to model the groundwater 
reservoirs with single linear reservoirs. However, it provides valuable insights about future water 
management strategies. The model identified areas where abstraction licences should be increased to 
meet the demand. It also identified areas where meeting minimum flow requirements can be a 
challenge. The model is also useful to study water exchanges and determine which connections are 
useful, whether the transfer capacity should be increased or if new connections should be added to 
the network. For example, the results have highlighted that capacity of the connection between Sjælsø 
and Tinghøj is binding in many time steps and it would be beneficial to increase the capacity of the 
pipe. The two scenarios of the study have also provided interesting conclusions. Scenario 1 has 
highlighted that some catchments are overexploited, even though the demand is only met at 80%. 
Therefore, in reality, with a demand met at 100%, the groundwater could be much more overexploited. 
Prospective work needs to be done for the water utilities to find alternative sources of water, in order 
not to overexploit the groundwater resources. Scenario 2 has demonstrated that a dense local water 
supply network is very efficient to increase the total water demand fulfilment in the region. 
I recommend further research to consolidate the model and get more reliable results. The main areas 
where more work is needed concern the catchment definition and the groundwater reservoir 
modelling; the quality of the data about the water supply network, both vertical and horizontal 
connections; and the estimation of the water demand, both the current demand and the estimated 
demand up until 2050. Based on the current results, I recommend the water supply companies and 
the Danish government aim to densify the water supply network by creating connections between the 
local waterworks, therefore making it more resilient to resource depletion. I also recommend 
increasing the water abstraction licences in areas identified as high water availability areas in the 
model, and creating connections from these areas to the areas with high water demand.
