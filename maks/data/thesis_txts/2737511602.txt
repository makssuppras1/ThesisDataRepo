Abstract 
A major trend in current research is the search for sustainable solutions to 
environmental challenges, especially in polymer technology. The aim of this work 
was to synthesise and characterise two different composite materials using the 
sustainable reinforcing material of biochar. The matrix consists of two different 
polyamides. On the one hand, polyamide 12 (PA12) was synthesised by in-situ 
polymerisation with 12-aminolauric acid and various proportions of biochar were 
co-operated. In addition, polyamide 1010 (PA1010) pellets were blended with the 
same proportions of biochar as PA12 and processed by melt compounding. 
An in-depth analysis of the influence of biochar on the polyamide matrix was carried 
out, discussing the morphology, mechanical properties, crystallinity, thermal 
stability and viscoelastic behaviour.  
Among the results of these analyses was that the successful synthesis of PA12 and 
PA1010 was demonstrated by FTIR spectra. Similarly, the increase in crystallinity 
of the PA12 composites with increasing biochar content was observed by DSC 
analysis, indicating nucleation effects of the biochar. At the same time, a decrease 
in crystallinity with the addition of biochar was observed in relation to the PA1010 
composites, as the biochar particles may have caused a disruption in matrix 
formation, resulting in a less ordered structure. 
The SEM examination of the microstructure analysis showed that the biochar is very 
well dispersed in the polymer matrix and that the two components are very 
compatible and have excellent interfacial adhesion and physical bonding.  
The effects of the biochar filler are particularly evident in the increase in tensile 
strength and modulus. The particles have a large reinforcing effect due to improved 
interfacial interactions with the matrix. However, the material is more brittle with 
the additive and has a reduced impact strength. However, the thermal stability is 
significantly increased with the addition of biochar and higher decomposition 
temperatures are achieved. 
The DMA analysis confirmed the increased stiffness and flow resistance when 
biochar was incorporated into the polyamides. This is a further confirmation of the 
good dispersion of the biochar in the matrix and its good compatibility.  
A shear thinning behaviour could be observed by means of a rheological 
investigation, which is a good prerequisite for the 3D printability of the materials. 
Complex structures could even be printed with a high biochar content using a 3D 
pellet printer.  
This in-depth analysis of polyamide-biochar composites gives hope for many 
promising practical applications and highlights the potential of biobased plastics for 
sustainable material development. 
Keywords  PA12, PA1010, Biochar, In-situ polymerization, 3D printing.

1 Introduction 
Today, plastics are used in countless areas and their market share is constantly growing. At the 
same time, most conventional plastics are based on fossil raw materials, which have harmful 
effects on the environment due to the release of CO2 and methane as well as the pollution caused 
by microplastics. [1] 
This is countered by bio-based materials made from renewable raw resources, which can be 
obtained from agricultural waste, for instance, which can be used as substitutes for fossil fuels. 
One example of this is biochar, which is obtained from the pyrolysis of biomass or biowaste. It 
is a carbon-rich material that can be used as a filler and reinforcing material in composites, 
among other things, to ensure better mechanical and thermal properties and a lower carbon 
footprint. [2 
Polyamides (Pas) are an excellent choice as a matrix option for polymer composites. They are 
characterised by repeating units of methyl groups linked by amide bonds and hydrogen bridge 
bonds between the chains. Their molecular arrangement determines their advantageous 
characteristics such as their high thermal stability, good toughness and impact strength, good 
abrasion resistance as well as low gas and vapour permeability and moisture absorption. Their 
properties make Pas suitable engineering materials used in a plethora of applications. [3] 
Representatives of the long-chain polyamides are Polyamide 1010 (PA1010) or Polyamide 12 
(PA12), which have many of the properties mentioned coupled with a lower melting point than 
polyamide 6, making their processing less energy-demanding. [4] 
Additionally, PA1010 represents a sustainable alternative, since it can be obtained from 
renewable biomass, e.g. castor oil, which supplies the starting materials 1,10-decanedioic acid 
(sebacic acid) and 1,10-diaminodecane or 1,10-decamethylene diamine. [4] However, PA12 is 
derived from petroleum-based resources, which can cause environmental concerns as both the 
disposal and recycling of fossil-based polyamides are challenging and no beneficial solutions 
have been found yet. [5] 
Therefore a bio-based filler such as biochar is incorporated into the polymer matrix to replace 
petroleum-based materials and reduce the carbon footprint of the composites. Furthermore, 
biochar provides additional improvements in the mechanical properties and thermal stability of 
the composites, since its porous structure and large surface area ensure good matrix-filler 
interaction. [6] 
The incorporation of biochar into the polyamide matrix can be done by the commonly used 
melt compounding or by innovative in-situ polymerisation. The latter ensures a better 
dispersion of biochar particles in the polymer matrix during the polycondensation reaction of 
polyamides, while the polymer chains are formed, which leads to mechanical interlocking and 
better interfacial adhesion. [7] 
In this work, two polyamide-biochar composites were synthesised and characterized. First of 
all,  PA12 biochar composites were synthesised, for which an in-situ polymerisation of 12-
amino lauric acid with different concentrations of biochar was carried out. The second part was 
the production of PA1010 biochar composites, which were produced by melt-compounding 
PA1010 pellets and biochar in the same proportions as used for the PA12 composites. A 
thorough analysis of the influence of biochar on the properties of the PA matrix was carried out 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
8 
 
by morphological examination, evaluation of the mechanical properties, crystallinity, thermal 
decomposition and viscoelastic behaviour.  
The aim of the thesis was to produce two composite materials based on polyamides and biochar 
to achieve improved properties and also create a more environmentally friendly material. The 
intention was also to assess how well and to what extent biochar is suitable as a filler and how 
well PA1010 can compete with petroleum-based polyamides as a bio-based alternative. In 
addition, a presentation of the practical applications of the polyamide-biochar composite has 
been made employing 3D printing of complex objects through additive manufacturing by 
material extrusion. 
This diverse analysis and practical application of composites should reflect their potential for 
more sustainable solutions, evidenced by a separate LCA analysis. The aim is to reduce 
manufacturing costs while improving material and environmental impacts. 
2 Background 
This chapter deals with the theoretical background of the composite materials and the individual 
materials. It is intended to provide information on why the materials were selected for this work 
and to what extent their properties influence those of the resulting composite material. Firstly, 
the principle of composite materials in general, their components and which aspects are 
important for their success are discussed. This is followed by a discussion of polyamides, 
specifically polyamides 12 and 1010, and their production and physical, chemical, thermal and 
mechanical properties. This is followed by a categorisation of biochar, in particular its 
mechanical and thermal properties and how these are determined by the adjustment method. 
Finally, the possible applications and in particular the limitations of biochar, the polyamides 
and their joint combination are discussed, and it is made clear how they can enrich each other. 
2.1 General Introduction to Composites 
The development of composite materials is an important part of material innovation. The 
principle of composite materials is to combine two or more different phases, which must be 
bonded together but insoluble in each other. This strategic fusion serves to improve the desired 
properties while at the same time reducing the less favourable ones. The versatility of 
composites is evident in their wide range of applications and properties that are tackled such as 
increasing physical strength, chemical resistance or mechanical robustness. [8] 
The following section focuses on the option for the matrix in the form of thermoplastic 
polymers and the choice for reinforcement in this case particle fillers, as these options are used 
for the type of composite material investigated in this work. 
2.1.1 Matrix Options 
The matrix plays a central role in composites, giving the composite its shape by binding the 
fillers and facilitating the application and transfer of loads by properly aligning the reinforcing 
materials along the direction of loading where possible, which can be a critical factor in the 
overall performance of the composite. The matrix also protects the reinforcing material from 
environmental influences. [8, 9]  
==PAGE:17==
 
 
9 
 
The types of matrix used in composites can be divided into 3 main categories - polymer matrix 
composites (PMC), metal matrix composites (MMC) and ceramic matrix composites (CMC).  
Firstly, there are the polymer matrix composites, which utilise polymers such as thermosets, 
thermoplastics and elastomers. These are often combined with reinforcing fibres such as E-
glass, carbon monofilaments and polyaramide to create fibre-reinforced composites.  
Thermoset resins make up the largest part of the PMC market. These include epoxies, 
polyurethanes (PUs), phenolic and amino resins and polyimides. They form a robust three-
dimensional network after curing. The advantages of such systems are manifold, including their 
wide availability and ease of processing as well as low material costs. Their generally low 
viscosity ensures that they can penetrate the reinforcing materials well and thus sufficiently wet 
their surface. 
They are often used in combination with curing agents or hardeners and impregnation of the 
reinforcing material. After curing, however, these materials no longer change and can no longer 
be remoulded. This irreversible hardening is both an important feature and a limitation, 
depending on the application. [8] 
Thermoplastics currently play a subordinate role in the production of modern composite 
materials but offer very interesting application possibilities. Representatives of this category 
include polyamides, polycarbonates, polysulfones and cellulosics. Their advantages include 
their stability, fast processing and the possibility of post-processing to correct defects. However, 
they also have disadvantages, such as the requirements for processing at high temperatures, 
which can lead to problems such as poor fibre wetting and inconsistencies in the fibre-matrix 
dispersion, as well as the high coefficient of thermal expansion and the sensitivity of crystalline 
morphology to processing conditions. [8, 9, 10] Secondly, there is the option of MMCs, where 
metals such as aluminium, magnesium, steel and titanium serve as the matrix. These metals are 
often paired with ceramic fibres such as silicon carbide or boron nitride. Properties that 
characterise this type of composite material include its high rigidity, high strength-to-density 
ratio and resistance to corrosion and heat. Particularly valued are these properties in the 
aerospace and automotive industries, where performance and durability are of paramount 
importance. [8, 9, 10] 
Thirdly, ceramic matrix composites are the last of these categories characterised by the use of 
ceramics such as silicon carbide, silicon nitride, aluminium oxide and carbon. These materials 
are usually reinforced with short fibres or whiskers, which in turn are often made of silicon 
carbide or boron nitride. Thereby, the resulting composite materials are known for their 
exceptional resistance to high temperatures and corrosive environments, making them suitable 
for applications that require a long service life under extreme conditions. [8, 9, 10] 
2.1.2 Reinforcements Options 
In addition to the matrix, reinforcement plays a decisive role in composite materials. Their 
purpose is manifold and serves not only to reduce costs but also to increase or reduce the density 
or improve the thermal and electrical properties of the composite material. Furthermore, they 
should contribute to heat resistance or conductivity and increase mechanical properties such as 
stiffness. The basic criterion for reinforcement is that it must be stronger and stiffer than the 
matrix in order to give the composite the required strength. [8, 9] 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
10 
 
There are a variety of materials that serve as reinforcement and are selected depending on the 
desired properties of the end product. The most commonly used fibres are short fibres, long 
fibres and continuous fibres, the effectiveness of which is determined by their aspect ratio, 
volume fraction and orientation within the matrix. A high aspect ratio facilitates integration into 
the matrix and leads to higher stiffness and strength, while the volume fraction of the fibres 
increases the strength and stiffness proportionally to the fibre content up to a certain level, with 
the upper limit being around 80%. The orientation of the fibres is also decisive. Unidirectional 
orientation generally provides optimum stiffness when the load is applied parallel to the fibres, 
albeit at the expense of anisotropic properties. [8] 
In addition, fillers can also be used as reinforcement materials, including particulate fillers with 
an aspect ratio greater than one and microspheres (either solid or hollow), which are designed 
to replace part of the polymer matrix while ideally also improving the overall properties of the 
composite. These fillers come in various forms, e.g. as short fibres, spheres or powders. An 
overview of the specific properties of various filler types can be found in Table 1. Aspects that 
influence the final quality of the composites are their shape, surface treatments, the mixture of 
particle types, as well as their size and distribution in the matrix. The effects offered by this 
type of reinforcement include increased stiffness, heat resistance, stability, strength, abrasion 
resistance and porosity. However, they are limited in their manufacturing methods. [8]

2.1.3 Matrix-Reinforcement Interphase  
The effectiveness of a composite material is largely determined by its microstructure and the 
interphase, which is the interface between the reinforcing and matrix phases where chemical 
and mechanical interactions occur. Interfacial adhesion is a crucial factor as it significantly 
influences the mechanical properties of the composite. A strong bond at this interface will have 
a positive effect on interlaminar shear strength, as well as resistance to delamination, fatigue 
==PAGE:19==
 
 
11 
 
and corrosion. Similarly, the ratio and geometry of the reinforcing material and its orientation 
can contribute significantly to the overall quality of a composite. [10] 
When manufacturing composites, it is always of paramount importance to achieve complete 
wetting of the reinforcement components by the matrix, as this is the only way to ensure that 
the load can be effectively transferred and carried. This applies in particular to composites that 
are reinforced with short or shredded fibres or particles. The matrix can only successfully 
transfer the load if the reinforcing material is completely covered. The efficiency of this transfer 
depends on the quality of the fibre-matrix bond. [8, 9] 
With thermoplastic matrices, however, it can be a challenge to achieve strong interfacial 
adhesion with the reinforcement, as conventional adhesion promoters, such as silanes, are less 
effective, so there is a need for new, more effective adhesion promoters. However, improving 
adhesion can be achieved using other methods. These include removing weak boundary layers 
or impurities, improving the wettability of the fibre surface, introducing additional chemical 
groups and changing the surface topography to facilitate mechanical bonding. In terms of 
porous reinforcement material, for example, which is bonded to a molten thermoplastic, the 
extent of adhesion depends on the porosity of the material, the viscosity of the thermoplastic 
and the pressure and duration of the bonding process [8, 19]. 
2.1.4 Processing techniques for composites 
The processing techniques for composites are very diverse and depend on the choice of matrix 
and reinforcing material. Polymer-matrix composites (PMC) can again be divided into 
processes for thermosetting polymers and processes for thermoplastic polymers.  
The following processes are used for thermoset polymers, among others: 
• Hand lay-up of fibres, laminates and felts allows for careful placement and alignment 
of the reinforcement. 
• Filament winding is suitable for monofilaments, where the fibres are wound under 
tension over a rotating mandrel. 
• In pultrusion, the fibres are drawn through a resin bath and then through a heated die to 
form a continuous profile. 
• Autoclaving uses laminates where the composite is subjected to pressure and heat in an 
autoclave to cure the thermosetting resin. 
• For porous preforms for reinforcement, resin moulding and impregnation are essential 
to allow the resin to fully penetrate the reinforcing material. 
In terms of thermoplastics, techniques such as the following are used: 
• Stacking and membrane moulding are often used for laminates, where the layers are 
stacked and then shaped under heat and pressure. 
• Tape laying is suitable for ribbons and enables the automatic placement of pre-
impregnated tapes. 
• Injection moulding is a versatile technique for chopped fibres and particles, where the 
material is injected into a mould and takes its shape as it cools and solidifies. 
Each of these techniques is chosen for its ability to optimise the interaction between the matrix 
and the reinforcement and to ensure that the composite material meets the desired 
specifications. [10]

2.3.1 Synthesis and Processing Techniques 
The synthesis of polyamides is based on the polycondensation of diamines and dicarboxylic 
acids, whereby the by-product water is eliminated to form long polymer chains. PA11, for 
example, is produced by polycondensation of amino carboxylic acids as bifunctional 
monomers. The bio-based polymers PA6 and PA4 are in turn synthesised by another route, ring-
opening polymerisation of lactam. [25] 
The components for the synthesis of bio-based polyamides (bio-PA) can either be bio-based 
acids and petroleum-based amines, as in the case of PA 610 and PA 410 or can be obtained from 
the complete extraction of both components from biomass, as in the case of PA 1010 and PA 
510. [25] 
The polycondensation of PA1010 takes place with 1,10-decamethylenediamine and sebacic 
acid. [26] 
These chemicals for the production of commercially available biopolyamides can be obtained 
from castor oil, which provides ricinoleic acid - a substance with a double bond and a hydroxyl 
group that can be easily chemically modified. Thus, polyamide building blocks such as sebacic 
acid can be obtained from castor oil. [25] 
PA12 is synthesised from just one monomer, either by polycondensation of ω-amino lauric acid 
or by ring-opening polymerisation of laurolactam. [26, 27] 
 
2.3.2 Properties 
2.3.2.1 General Classification 
The intermolecular interactions, which decrease with increasing length of the polymer chain, 
are decisive for the properties of polyamides. The basic properties of polyamides are good 
thermoplastic properties, high thermal stability, toughness and impact resistance, coupled with 
excellent abrasion resistance and low permeability to gases and vapours. [3] 
In terms of performance, PA1010 is located between the long-chain PA12 and PA1010 and the 
standard short-chain polyamides PA6 and PA66. It offers a comparatively lower density, lower 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
14 
 
water absorption and lower strength and rigidity, which is also reflected in a lower melting point 
and a lower continuous service temperature than PA 6, for example. [25, 26] 
An improvement in the mechanical properties, in particular the stiffness and strength, as well 
as the thermal stability of long-chain polyamides such as PA1010 can be achieved by the 
addition of various fillers. The performance of PA1010 can be compared with the petroleum-
based PA12. [25, 28] 
PA12 is characterised by the fact that it has the lowest concentration of amide groups of all 
commercially available polyamides. This structural characteristic determines many of its 
properties, such as good processability and the wide temperature range between melting and 
crystallisation, which also gives it excellent 3D printing properties. However, PA12 is also a 
comparatively expensive material. [22, 28] 
2.3.2.2 Chemical Structure and Crystallinity of Polyamides 
The chemical structure of polyamides such as PA12 and PA1010 has a decisive influence on 
many properties; it also plays a decisive role in relation to the hydrophobic or hydrophilic 
properties of the polymer. This in turn is due to its structure, based on either lauric lactam or ω-
amino lauric acid, which has amide groups separated by 11 CH2 groups. This results in a longer 
aliphatic chain, a higher number of methylene groups and fewer hydrogen bonds per unit mass 
compared to other polyamides. [22, 26] 
For example, the moisture absorption of PA12 is only around 2 %, which gives it great 
dimensional stability with fluctuating humidity. This stability is due to the hydrophobicity, 
which results from the high ratio of methylene to amide groups per repeating unit. In addition, 
the high degree of crystallinity of PA12 is another reason for its low water absorption, as water 
interacts primarily with the amorphous regions where hydrogen bonds are more susceptible to 
moisture. PA12 is also known for its resistance to a wide range of substances, including fats, 
oils, fuels, various solvents, salt solutions and other chemicals. [22, 26] 
With regard to the functional groups and reaction tendencies of these polyamides, it can be 
stated that the amide groups are particularly important here, as they form hydrogen bonds that 
restrict the mobility between the chains. This leads to high melting temperatures and increased 
strength. However, the amide groups can also react with water, which would lead to hydrolysis. 
This chain scission reaction would reduce the molecular weight and have a plasticising effect 
by disrupting the intermolecular hydrogen bonding and increasing the segment movement. 
Nevertheless, the water absorption of PA12 remains relatively low even after prolonged ageing, 
although the effects are more pronounced at higher temperatures. [22, 26]  
The same observations can also be applied to PA1010, except that even lower water absorption 
can be observed here than with PA12. This ensures that its mechanical properties and high 
dimensional stability are only minimally affected by changes in ambient humidity. [26] 
Another important property of polyamides, particularly for their application, is their density, 
especially in relation to industries that value lightweight materials. Thanks to its long 
hydrocarbon chain, PA12 has a relatively low density of around 1.01 g/cm3. The density of 
PA1010 is slightly higher at around 1.05 g/cm3. [22] 
Crystallinity is another important aspect that significantly affects the performance of 
polyamides. This is because it has a major influence on many of the important properties of 
==PAGE:23==
 
 
15 
 
polyamides, such as their thermal attributes, which in turn are reflected in the melting 
temperature, but the mechanical properties are also dependent on it. PA12 is semi-crystalline 
and is characterised by crystalline lamellae connected to amorphous regions by non-entangled 
chains or strained binding molecules. The crystalline forms of PA12 are the γ form (with a 
hexagonal packing structure) and the α form (with a monoclinic lattice), and there are also the 
γ′ and α′ variants. Which of these forms is present depends heavily on the crystallisation 
conditions. The γ form is the most common and easy to obtain. Here, the chains are twisted so 
that a complete hydrogen bond is formed, as the chains are arranged in parallel. The α form, on 
the other hand, has antiparallel molecular chains, which makes it easy to form complete 
hydrogen bonds between the extended chains. The α-form and γ-form differ in the properties 
they induce, such as the melting point, which is lower for the α-form than for the γ-form. They 
also have a different influence on mechanical properties. The α-PA12 is strong but brittle, but 
the γ-PA12 has even better mechanical properties and is tougher. The γ form is also the most 
stable crystal phase; it is obtained by slow cooling from the melt, which causes the molecular 
chains to arrange themselves closely and achieve a higher crystallinity. [22, 29] 
PA1010 is also semi-crystalline and is therefore characterised by high mechanical strength and 
chemical stability. This structure forms chain-folded sheets in even-numbered polyamides, 
which enable linear hydrogen bonds between the amide groups. The C10 is occupied in equal 
parts by diamine and dicarboxylic acids. The regular spacing of these groups along the chains 
results in a high crystallinity of approx. 49.7 %, which gives PA1010 its robust mechanical 
properties and effective barrier performance. [26, 28] 
The process and temperature chosen are decisive for crystallinity. At room temperature, a 
completely amorphous state or a semi-crystalline state with one or more crystalline phases of 
different structures and stability is formed. The thermal behaviour is strongly influenced by the 
fact that a rigid amorphous layer forms near the crystal surfaces, which only devitrifies at 
temperatures above the glass transition temperature (Tg). This layer leads to a lower increase in 
heat capacity and influences the modulus of elasticity when the material is heated above Tg. A 
lower supercooling of the melt favours the formation of more perfect crystals and higher 
enthalpy, while a high supercooling of the melt close to Tg leads to poorer crystals. The 
perfection of the crystals can therefore be adjusted via the crystallisation temperature Tc [3]. 
2.3.2.3 Mechanical Properties of Polyamides 
PA12 has excellent impact strength, even at temperatures below zero, and is also highly resistant 
to stress cracking and abrasion. It also has a low coefficient of sliding friction, dampens noise 
and vibrations and has good fatigue resistance under cyclic loading. On the other hand, PA12 
has the lowest strength and heat resistance compared to other polyamides. [22, 26] 
PA1010 can have an even higher degree of crystallinity, which in turn is reflected in even better 
mechanical properties. It has a tensile modulus of 762.1±57.5 MPa, a yield strength of 45.4±3.3 
MPa and an elongation at break of 155.06±36.3 %, which indicates relatively good ductility. 
The maximum tensile strength is 71.2±5.8 MPa. Double yielding can occur during deformation, 
which can be explained by recrystallisation. This is also known as " strain hardening", in which 
the crystallinity and hardness are increased in the direction of the force.  Another reason for the 
double yielding could be the coupling of amorphous and crystalline areas. In the case of 
PA1010, the crystal orientation by tensile force accelerates the crystallisation rate, which is 
==PAGE:24==
 
 
16 
 
favoured by the weakened hydrogen bonding during stretching. This enables both mono- and 
biaxially-orientated PA1010 films, which improves the mechanical properties. [28] 
2.3.2.4 Thermal Properties of Polyamides 
The melting temperature (Tm) of polyamides increases with a higher concentration of amide 
groups. The strength and perfection of the hydrogen bonds also influence the melting 
temperature. These depend on how the chain segments are arranged in the crystal structure. Odd 
polyamides such as PA12 have a relatively low Tm due to the aforementioned long methylene 
sequences and a high ratio between methylene and amide groups in the chain. Furthermore, the 
glass transition temperature (Tg) of the amorphous phase also decreases with increasing length 
of the methylene sequence between the amide groups. [3] 
PA12 has the lowest melting temperature among these polyamides, which is around 178 °C. 
[22] 
In comparison with PA12, PA1010 can be categorised between PA6 and PA12 in terms of its 
thermal properties. It can be processed at moderate temperatures and remains stable during heat 
treatment below 200 °C. Its melting point is around 200 °C and only degrades at around 468 
°C. [28]

2.4.1 Introduction

Biochar is a solid material obtained by the thermochemical conversion of biomass in a low-
oxygen environment. An illustration of its structure can be found in Figure 1. Common sources 
==PAGE:25==
 
 
17 
 
include wood and various waste products of forestry, agricultural and animal origin. The process 
usually takes place at temperatures below 700°C and results in a product consisting mainly of 
carbon, volatiles, minerals (ash) and moisture. The exact proportions of these components 
depend on the type of feedstock and the operating parameters used during production. [30] 
The most important processes for the production of biochar are pyrolysis and gasification, 
whereby pyrolysis can be further subdivided into slow and fast. Other less common 
thermomechanical conversion processes are hydrothermal carbonisation (HTC), torrefaction 
and hydrothermal liquefaction. [30] The general process steps that the biomass has to go 
through are moisture evaporation and the decomposition and formation of carbonaceous 
products. [31 
During pyrolysis, the biomass is heated and thermally decomposed under anaerobic conditions, 
usually between 400 °C and 1200 °C. As no oxygen is present, the biomass can be heated 
beyond its thermal stability limit, resulting in a robust solid residue. The formation of charcoal 
is favoured by lower temperatures and longer residence times. In secondary pyrolysis, heavy 
chemicals are cracked to convert the biomass into biochar, with processes such as 
depolymerisation, fragmentation and cross-linking occurring at specific temperature points and 
affecting the state of the lignocellulosic components. [30] 
Reactors used in pyrolysis include paddle furnaces, fluidised bed reactors, wagon reactors and 
stirred sand rotary kilns. The biochar yield depends not only on the reactor type but also on the 
type of biomass. However, it can be stated that, as a rule, the yield of biochar decreases with 
increasing pyrolysis temperature. [30]  
The main components of the biomass - lignin, cellulose and hemicellulose - react differently 
during pyrolysis. Cellulose, for example, can be depolymerised into oligosaccharides to form 
tar, which is a mixture of ketones, aldehydes, organic liquids and charcoal. Lignin, on the other 
hand, primarily forms charcoal with a minimum of water as its condensed solid structure breaks 
down and the ß-O-4 lignin bond is cleaved, leading to the formation of free radicals and 
degraded substances. The amount of lignin present and its rate of degradation vary depending 
on the type of wood, with the lignin of conifers being more stable than that of deciduous trees, 
which in turn has a positive effect on charcoal yield and in addition also promotes carbonisation 
and increases the carbon and ash content of the biochar. Conversely, a higher cellulose content 
tends to result in less charcoal and more tar, but also more gaseous content. [30] 
In gasification, carbon-rich materials are treated at high temperatures of 700 to 900 °C and, in 
contrast to pyrolysis, not without oxygen but with a limited supply of oxygen and converted 
into synthesis gas, a mixture of gaseous products and hydrocarbons. charcoal is only a by-
product and therefore has a lower yield and is also accompanied by ash, tar and oil. The 
advantages of this method compared to conventional processes are lower emissions and higher 
synthesis gas production. Efficient gasification requires a low oxygen-to-carbon (O/C) ratio, 
which can be achieved by torrefaction as a pre-treatment step. [30] 
As mentioned above, the biomass can be pretreated to increase efficiency. There are different 
categories, which are physical, physiochemical/thermal, chemical and biological processes.  
In physical pre-treatment, mechanical energy is used, e.g. by grinding and shredding, to reduce 
the size of the biomass and fractionate the ash content. The finer the starting material, the finer 
==PAGE:26==
 
 
18 
 
the biochar particles, which in turn results in a lower nitrogen content and a larger surface area, 
as well as higher electrical conductivity and pH value. [30] 
During thermal pre-treatment, high temperatures and high pressure are used to cause sudden 
decompression through steam explosion, hydrothermal carbonisation (HTC) and hot water 
extraction, which can scatter the fibre material and break covalent bonds between hemicellulose 
and lignin. The steam explosion in particular increases the lignin content and creates a rougher 
surface with a larger specific surface area. [30] 
Regardless of the process, it can be stated that a longer residence time during pyrolysis leads to 
a higher degree of carbonisation and a higher calorific value of the biochar. This is also 
accompanied by an increase in the specific surface area due to the formation and expansion of 
the pore structure within the resulting char. Contrary to this, however, too long a retention time 
can result in a reduction in the surface area due to the possible collapse of the pore structure. 
[30]

2.4.2.1 Mechanical Properties of Biochar 
The mechanical properties of biochar, such as toughness and strength, are inherently linked to 
its rigid structure. Due to the honeycomb cell structure at the micrometre level of the wood, for 
example, the directional stiffness and strength of biochar are particularly high. At the nanometre 
level, the angle of the microfibrils is crucial for controlling stiffness and ductility in response 
to external stresses. During pyrolysis, the structure of the biomass is transformed into a 
carbonaceous material, but the orientation and nanocomposite properties of the original 
biomass are retained. It can be observed that at pyrolysis temperatures between 500 °C and 900 
°C, a structural order on the micrometre and nanometre scale occurs in the amorphous material, 
leading to the formation of a highly crosslinked 3D network of carbon layers. This network can 
in turn contribute to an increase in the modulus of elasticity and hardness of the biochar. [34, 
35, 36, 37, 38, 39, 40]  
The heating rate and temperature during pyrolysis are key drivers of the mechanical properties 
of biochar. Thus, at lower heating rates, the hardness and modulus of elasticity tend to develop 
towards lower values, as pyrolytic tars can be present on the surface. Conversely, at higher 
temperatures, the volatile elements are removed and pores are formed, which contribute to a 
higher hardness and modulus. [40] 
2.4.2.2 Physical and Chemical Properties of Biochar 
The physical and chemical properties of biochar are influenced by the pyrolysis process, in that 
the pyrolysis temperature determines the presence and variation of the functional groups in the 
biochar. For example, when the pyrolysis temperatures reach up to around 750 °C, the specific 
surface area, hardness, modulus and crystallinity of the biochar increase. At the same time, the 
content of combustible volatile substances and functional surface groups decreases. [32] 
A fundamental attribute of biochar Biochar is its surface area. This is influenced by changes in 
the element concentrations of carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, sulphur and nitrogen and their ratios. 
For example, H/C and O/C indicate the degree of aromatisation and maturation of the biochar. 
The element ratios also reflect the extent of pyrolysis and the degree of oxidative adaptation. 
The Brunauer-Emmett-Teller surface area (BET) also increases with increasing carbon burn-
off, which increases the pore volume and surface area. Similarly, the average pore size increases 
with residence time and pyrolysis temperature. [30] 
 
With regard to the morphology of the biochar, the structure of the starting material is very 
important.  If the biochar is obtained from cellulosic plant biomass, the pore diameter is usually 
around 1 µm and has a honeycomb appearance due to the original tubular structures in the plant 
cells. These developed pores contribute to a large surface area. Pyrolysis at higher temperatures 
results in a clean surface, as the process stabilises volatile hydrocarbons, smoothens the surface 
of the biochar and makes it suitable for environmental remediation in the same way as activated 
carbon. Conversely, pyrolysis at lower temperatures results in shaped structures with small 
pores and an uneven surface structure, which is beneficial for the regulation of fertiliser 
nutrients and the absorption of pollutants from the soil. [7, 30] 
The porous nature of biochar, in which the pores can range from micro to macropores, is 
particularly advantageous during polymerisation as it allows the monomers to penetrate the 
structure, creating a compatible and intimate interface between filler particles and polymer 
==PAGE:28==
 
 
20 
 
chains. This porous structure also promotes moisture retention, reduces bulk density, increases 
organic matter content and has a positive effect on cation exchange capacity. Depending on the 
application, the high porosity can also provide habitats for symbiotic microbes. [30] 
2.4.2.3 Thermal Properties of Biochar 
The production temperature of the biochar is decisive for its thermal properties. If the 
temperature is rather low, the volatile substances remain trapped in the pores of the biochar and 
act as fuel when exposed to heat again, leading to combustion. Biochar produced at higher 
temperatures, on the other hand, is characterised by a structure that consists exclusively of pores 
without volatile substances and also has strong covalent carbon-carbon (C-C) bonds, which 
makes it more resistant to combustion. [41] 
The particle size of biochar also has an impact on its combustion properties. Smaller particles 
have a higher combustion tendency than larger ones, as the larger surface area of smaller 
particles allows greater contact with oxygen during combustion. [41] 
Overall, biochar has excellent thermal stability, as shown by its glass transition temperature 
(Tg), melting temperature (Tm) and viscoelastic behaviour. These properties, which have very 
high values, suggest that biochar can maintain its structural integrity under different thermal 
conditions. [41] 
2.5 Applications and Limitations 
2.5.1 Polyamides 
Polyamides can be used in many areas of the food industry due to their versatile properties. For 
example, polyamides with their high barrier against vapours and gases are particularly popular 
in the food packaging industry. [28] 
Due to its high strength, durability and chemical resistance, PA12 in particular is a popular 
material in the automotive industry for components such as fuel lines, brake hoses and cable 
sheathing. Its mechanical properties also make it ideal for the industrial production of gearboxes 
and bushings. PA12 is also used in the electrical and electronics industry, e.g. for cable 
insulation and connectors due to its excellent electrical insulation properties. On top of this, 
PA12 also proves its suitability for 3D printing processes such as selective laser sintering, 
making it easy to manufacture products for various industries.  The material is already a popular 
choice for consumer goods such as sports equipment, tool handles and protective covers due to 
its durability and longevity. [42] 
In the case of PA1010, this can be used as a replacement for PA11 in areas such as coatings, 
where it is recognised for its durable, structural and long-lasting properties. PA1010 also has 
applications in other industrial sectors such as automotive, sports, medical and aerospace, where 
it is particularly suitable and appreciated for its bio-based nature, low density and low water 
absorption. With its properties, it effectively fills the gap between polyamide 6 and polyamide 
12. [4] 
Polyamides are often used in combination with composite materials, but they also have certain 
limitations in this respect. Environmental factors such as the physical effects of chemicals, 
hydrothermal effects and oxidation at high temperatures can attack the polyamide matrix and 
affect the performance of composites by causing softening, swelling, cracking and chain-
==PAGE:29==
 
 
21 
 
breaking reactions such as hydrolysis or oxidation. In addition, thermoplastics, including 
polyamides, are generally limited by their high temperature capability, where changes in 
internal stress due to thermal expansion and contraction can occur, which can affect mechanical 
properties and cause interface damage. The maximum continuous service temperature for 
polyamide is typically 120 °C. [8] 
Despite the widespread use of polyamides, there are concerns about environmental pollution. 
The main reason for this is the disposal of polyamides, as the decomposition of polyamides in 
landfill sites, for example, can release harmful gases and liquids. Incineration is also not a good 
option, as it can produce toxic gases such as CO and NOx and at the same time does not generate 
much energy. Recycling methods do exist, but they often require significant amounts of 
chemical reagents, which leads to further pollution. Therefore, the development of more 
sustainable reuse and recycling methods of polyamides is an important trend in research. For 
example, attempts are being made to develop polyamides that can be naturally degraded by 
copolymerisation and molecular design or to develop completely biodegradable polyamides on 
a biological basis. [5] 
2.5.2 Biochar 
The use of biochar offers many benefits in terms of sustainability and the environment, it 
promotes the reduction of CO2 emissions and reduces greenhouse gases.[30] 
The applications of biochar are diverse. It was originally used for soil applications, where it 
acts as activated carbon to help store and sequester carbon and improve soil performance. It is 
used here as a promoter of anaerobic digestion and as a catalyst in agriculture and horticulture, 
where it is used to improve soil and as a compost additive by increasing nutrient availability, 
reducing compaction and improving pH. Due to its high porosity, it facilitates microbial growth 
in the compost, resulting in accelerated recycling of nutrients.  Its adsorption properties make 
it an excellent solution for the bioremediation of heavy metals and polycyclic aromatic 
hydrocarbons in soils and offer a sustainable, cost-effective option for carbon sequestration. In 
addition to soil improvement, biochar is now also used for energy production and storage, 
sensors, water treatment, biochemical process stability and improvement, and as an 
environmentally friendly construction additive. [7, 30] 
In construction, it helps as a filler material in cement hydration. However, depending on the 
particle size, its addition can lead to microcracks and loss of strength. However, in terms of 
sustainability, it improves the immobilisation of organic contaminants and toxic elements and 
can bind atmospheric carbon dioxide in buildings, which could reduce greenhouse gas 
emissions by up to 25 %. Biochar is also appreciated in this area for its high pH value and water 
retention capacity. [30] 
Biochar also contributes to the composite materials sector by providing a cost-effective 
alternative to other carbon-based fillers and thus offers the possibility of reducing the overall 
production costs of composite materials. It has also proven to be highly compatible with 
extrusion or 3D printing. [7, 30]  
Despite these benefits, there are environmental concerns about the stability of biochar's carbon 
structure. There is a possibility that organic matter can dissolve, especially during water and 
wastewater treatment, which can release carbon from the biochar. In addition, biochar derived 
==PAGE:30==
 
 
22 
 
from sewage sludge can lead to heavy metal contamination in water, indicating that the stability 
of biochar needs to be further researched to ensure its safe and effective use. [43] 
2.5.3 Polyamide-Biochar Composites  
A general trend in research is the shift towards sustainable materials, driven by the depletion of 
oil resources and the increasing demand for environmentally friendly products. [7] 
A well-established example of this is wood-plastic composites, which are environmentally 
friendly but lack the mechanical strength and water resistance required for certain applications. 
Here, polyamides, especially when reinforced with materials such as plant fibres, cellulose or 
biochar, offer a robust alternative. The integration of biomass into synthetic polymers is an 
important trend that transforms renewable resources and agricultural waste into valuable fillers 
such as biochar. This not only reduces biowaste but also reduces CO2 emissions, contributing 
to a more sustainable production cycle. Furthermore, biochar has numerous properties that can 
benefit the composite material, for example, it can improve the thermal stability and mechanical 
performance of polyamides. [7, 44] 
In addition, a challenge with polyamides on their own is their low thermal conductivity and 
tendency to absorb water, which can lead to a plasticisation effect. For example, the inclusion 
of fibres such as carbon fibres can improve performance, but problems can occur at the interface 
due to the hydrophilic hydroxyl groups on the fibre surface. Biochar, on the other hand, is 
cheaper and has a large surface area, resulting in better adhesion. Another good reason for 
incorporating biochar into the polyamide matrix instead of conventional natural fibres is the 
high processing temperatures required for polyamides, which natural fibres usually cannot 
withstand, whereas biochar can. These composite materials are in turn well suited to many fields 
such as furniture, construction, packaging, automotive and aerospace. [6, 45] 
Biochar can be incorporated into polyamide matrices by methods such as in-situ 
polymerisation, which ensures excellent miscibility and uniform dispersion and improves 
mechanical properties as well as sustainability due to lower energy consumption. Other 
methods include melt compounding and solvent blending. [7] 
For processing, established methods such as injection moulding or dry mixing of biochar with 
polyamide and subsequent compression moulding are commonly used. However, a new method 
that is becoming increasingly popular is additive manufacturing, in particular 3D printing. It 
offers advantages such as the ability to create complex objects with high geometric complexity, 
cost efficiency, shorter production times, high customizability and fewer structural defects. [7, 
44, 46,  Gama et al., 2021) 
It can be seen that polyamide-biochar composites offer high potential as a more sustainable 
alternative in various industries, where they fulfil both performance requirements and 
environmental concerns.

3  Materials and Methods 
3.1 Materials 
12-aminododecanoic acid [95% (Sigma Aldrich, CAS: 693-57-2, 159247-25G, USA)], biochar 
[BC, Pyrolysis temperature ~650C, pyrolysis time <10 min, continuous process, particle size: 
<70µm (CARBOFLEX, Finland)], PA1010 [(Evonik, VESTAMID® Terra DS18 natural 
colour, up to 100% renewable resources, Batch: CN100819, China), sodium hypophosphite 
monohydrate [≥99% (Sigma Aldrich, CAS: 10039-56-2, S5012-100G, USA)] were used as 
received. 
3.2 Polymer Synthesis 
3.2.1 In-Situ Polymerisation for PA12 Composites 
The polymerisation process took place in a stainless-steel reactor with an overhead mixer and 
a heating jacket. Here, the monomer, 12-aminolauric acid, was mixed with approx. 0.1 % of the 
monomer amount of sodium hypophosphite monohydrate catalyst and 10, 30 or 50 wt.% of 
biochar, if necessary, and stirred at 240 °C for 4 hours under a nitrogen atmosphere. The 
synthesis product was then cooled and kept under a nitrogen atmosphere to prevent oxidation. 
With the addition of liquid nitrogen, the product was cooled down and embrittled to such an 
extent that it could be chiselled out. The resulting polymer chunks were ground into granules 
using a Retsch SM 300 granulator. To calculate the quantities of the polymerisation process, the 
by-products, such as water vapour, were determined theoretically so that a 100% yield of the 
product was obtained. 
The mixing ratios of PA12 to biochar are as follows: 100:0, 90:10, 70:30 and 50:50, labelled 
PA12, PA12BC10, PA12BC30 and PA12BC50 respectively. 
3.2.2 Melt Compounding for PA1010 Composites 
The PA1010 composites were produced by mixing PA1010 pellets with biochar using a counter-
rotating twin extruder (Labtech extruder model LTE 20-44). For this melt blending process, the 
extruder has five heating zones with temperature profiles of 220 °C, 230 °C, 240 °C, 240 °C 
and 240 °C, with a screw speed of 50 rpm. The resulting compounds are then pelletised into 
granules. 
The mixing ratios of PA1010 and biochar are the same as previously described for the PA12-
biochar composites.

PA12 Composites: 
Dog bone specimens measuring 3.2 mm × 2 mm × 2.2 mm (L × W × D) were produced for the 
tensile test using the injection moulding machine, Babyplast 6/12 from England. The machine 
was set to 240°C for this purpose.

The TGA model 5500 from TA Instruments was used for the thermogravimetric analysis (TGA)

3.4.5 Mechanical Testing 
The mechanical properties of the samples were evaluated by tensile tests on an Instron Model 
4204 universal tester in accordance with ASTM D638 Type V. Firstly, the samples were 
conditioned for 48 hours at 23 °C and 55 % relative humidity before testing, then loaded with 
a 5 kN load cell and stretched at a constant rate of 5 mm/s. The resulting stress-strain curves 
were analysed to determine important parameters. The respective tensile modulus (MPa), 
tensile strength (MPa) and elongation at break (%) were determined from the stress-strain 
curves of the samples. In addition, the notched impact strength was assessed using the Charpy 
V-notch test on the Charpy-Zwick-Roell device. 
3.4.6 DMA 
The viscoelastic properties related to the mechanical characteristics of the samples were 
investigated using dynamic mechanical analysis (DMA) over a temperature range of 0 °C to 
200 °C. For this purpose, a rectangular sample rod was punched from the hot-pressed film of 
the sample and analysed on a TA Instruments DMA model Q800 in a multi-frequency strain 
mode at preload, frequency and strain at 1 kN, 1 Hz and 1 %. The resulting storage modulus 
(G'), loss modulus (G") and loss factor (tan δ) were plotted against temperature and further 
analysed. 
3.4.7 Rheology 
To further characterise the viscoelastic behaviour of the samples, rheological measurements 
were carried out using an Anton Paar Physica rheometer model MCR 301. The samples were 
cut to a thin rectangular shape and melted at 230 °C, followed by a strain sweep test with a 
fixed angular frequency of 10 rad/s with shear strain logarithmically from 0.01 to 100 % to 
determine the linear viscoelastic range (LVR). A frequency sweep test was then carried out in 
the LVR (fixed shear rate of 1%) with an angular frequency from 0.01 to 628 rad/s. The 
measurements yielded the storage modulus (G'), the loss modulus (G") and complex viscosity 
(|η*|) as a function of the angular frequency, which were used to characterise the sample's 
rheological behaviour.

possibly resulting in improved alignment and packing of the polymer molecules, which leads 
to larger and better-ordered crystalline areas in the polymer matrix, which in turn increases the 
higher overall crystallinity.

4.3 TGA 
4.3.1 PA12 composites 
The thermal decomposition behaviour of the polyamides and their composites was investigated 
using TGA, as well as the particle dispersion of the biochar and the compatibility between the 
filler material and the matrix. 
As the biochar is already in a pyrolyzed state, its highly aromatic carbonaceous structure (> 50 
%) with low volatile components is thermally very stable. Even at very high temperatures of up 
to 800 °C, it only shows a minimal weight loss of approximately 11 %, as can be seen on the 
TGA weight loss curve together with the thermal behaviour of pure PA12 and the composites 
(Figure 8). 
The decomposition behaviour of biochar, pure PA12 and all composites is summarised in Table 
9. The decomposition temperatures increase significantly with increasing biochar content. 
While the decomposition temperature for 5% mass loss in the pure matrix (T5%) was at 416 °C, 
and at T10% at 426 °C and Tmax at 456 °C, the values for 50% biochar in PA12BC50 increased 
significantly and are at T5% at 434 °C, at T10% at 443 °C and for Tmax at 467 °C. 
The improvement in thermal stability can be explained by the fact that biochar has the capacity 
to act as a physical barrier in the matrix, preventing heat and oxygen diffusion, thus insulating 
the polymer matrix and retaining the volatile decomposition products within the composite. In 
addition, biochar can absorb a high proportion of the heat due to its high heat capacity, thus 
==PAGE:42==
 
 
34 
 
reducing the temperature gradient in the composite material during thermal decomposition and 
minimising the degradation of the polymer chains. [57, 58] 
No multiple decomposition peaks can be seen in the TGA  DTG curves (Figure 9), which 
speaks in favour of strong interfacial adhesion and minimal phase separation between matrix 
and filler. Thus, the observed increase in decomposition temperatures is also due to the robust 
interactions between the biochar particles and the PA matrix as well as good compatibility and 
excellent dispersion of the particles. The latter was mainly achieved by the in-situ 
polymerisation method. 
In addition, there is good agreement between experimental and theoretical values regarding the 
true mass composition. Calculated from the char residue at 800°C, the composites PA12BC10 
contain approx. 11% biochar, PA12BC30 approx. 25% and PA12BC50 approx. 50% biochar, 
which in turn is again proof of the good dispersion and incorporation of the biochar into the 
matrix.

4.3.2 PA1010 composites 
The PA1010 composites also confirmed that the incorporation of biochar increases the thermal 
stability of the PA1010 matrix, thus showing an increase in thermal decomposition temperatures 
for all composites. A summary of the weight loss data can be found in Table 10 and the TGA 
weight loss curve as well as the DTG curve can be found in the appendix (Appendix Figure 
12 and Appendix Figure 13). The same properties of biochar are at work here, with its large 
surface area and porous structure forming insulating barriers for the matrix consequently 
slowing down its decomposition.  
It should be mentioned that biochar can also have catalytic properties in the thermal 
decomposition process. For this scenario, the polymer matrix would decompose faster at high 
temperatures, which was not the case here. [59] 
Using the residue at 800 °C, the proportion of biochar in the PA composites was calculated 
again and resulted in 7% biochar for PA1010BC10, approximately 25% for PA1010BC30 and 
only 34% for PA1010BC50. This is proof of a good distribution and incorporation of the

4.4 SEM 
4.4.1 PA12 composites 
The filler-polymer interface interaction is crucial for the composite properties; therefore SEM 
images were taken to analyse the dispersion of biochar particles and the quality of interface 
adhesion.  
With regard to the images (Figure 10 (a and b)) of the biochar particles, it can be seen that they 
are present in different shapes and sizes; both spherical particles and irregular fragments, flakes, 
plates and agglomerations can be recognised. The particles all have a size of less than 70 µm 
since they were previously sieved. The surface of the biochar was rough and irregular with 
cracks, crevices and fissures in addition to a very porous morphology with micropores to 
macropores of different sizes and distributions. The porosity is likely a result of the release of 
gaseous products from the bulk decomposition of lignin and the degradation of cellulose and 
hemicellulose. This porosity provides a large surface area and thus favours the penetration of 
monomers or oligomers into the pores during polymerisation and the subsequent anchoring of 
the PA chains in the biochar sheets. The result is a physiomechanical interlocking between the 
polymer chains and the biochar surface improving the adhesion and stability of the composite 
and allowing good charge and stress transfer. This in turn is favourable for the mechanical 
properties. [57] 
Pure PA12 has a smooth and continuous surface and is characterised by its fracture and 
deformation-resistant properties. It displays no cracks or voids as can be seen in Figure 10 (c). 
The biochar composites (Figure 10 (d-f)) show a uniform dispersion of the biochar particles in 
the polyamide matrix. No cracks, large cavities, agglomerations or pull-out effects can be 
observed in any of the composites. This again indicates the good compatibility between the 
components and their effective mixing on a microscopic level, which speaks for the 
effectiveness of in-situ polymerisation as a processing technique. [7] These properties are in 
turn required for the mechanical integrity and performance of the composite.

4.5 Mechanical Testing 
4.5.1 PA12 composites 
The mechanical properties of the polyamides and composites were determined to further 
evaluate their suitability and performance for applications. The tensile strength and bending 
behaviour were measured in simple tensile tests. The resulting stress-strain curves were used to 
determine the tensile strength, tensile modulus and tensile elongation (Figure 11).  
The pure PA12 already exhibits a high tensile elongation of around 300±10%, tensile strength 
of 38±1 MPa and tensile modulus of 745±30 MPa, which is also in line with values from the 
literature [61, 62]. 
The addition of biochar increases the tensile strength and tensile modulus but reduces the tensile 
elongation. For example, with 50% biochar addition, the tensile strength increased to 54±2 MPa 
and tensile modulus to 2055±65 MPa, while the elongation at break decreased to only 8±0.3%. 
Thus, the biochar made the composite stronger and stiffer, but less ductile. This is summarized 
in Figure 12.  
Reasons for this increase could be that, as previously described, the polyamide chains infiltrate 
the pores of the biochar during polymerisation and thus provide physical/mechanical 
interlocking, so that the applied stress can be successfully transferred to the particles, resulting 
in higher stiffness. Another decisive factor for the improved mechanical properties is the good 
compatibility between biochar and polyamide as well as the good dispersion of the biochar 
particles so that the distance between the particles in the polymer matrix is reduced, which in 
turn increases the stiffness and strength of the composite. The lower elongation at break is 
probably due to the incorporation of the stiff biochar particles, which reduces deformability by 
minimising the mobility of the polyamide macromolecules. 
Similar influences on the mechanical properties of composite materials can be found in the 
literature. For example, C. Das et al. (2021) also found that their biochar/polypropylene 
biocomposites had significantly higher tensile moduli than pure polypropylene. In addition, the 
modulus increased even further with higher biochar content (from 25 to 35 %). They surmised 
that the biochar particles reduced the mobility and deformability of the polypropylene structure, 
thus increasing its stiffness. However, the tensile strength remained unchanged, as further 
functionalisation and coupling agents of the biochar were necessary for good interfacial 
bonding with the matrix. They also observed a reduction in elongation and ductility with the 
addition of biochar. [63] 
Minugu et al. (2021) also found a higher tensile strength in their composites. Here, only 4% 
biochar (from the Bael shell) in an epoxy resin matrix was found to have 183% higher strength 
than pure epoxy resin. This can also be attributed to the high proportion of hard elemental 
carbon and the large porous surface of the biochar, which favours interlocking between the 
matrix and the biochar and can therefore offer more resistance to the tensile load. However, in 
their case, the strength of the composites decreased from a biochar content of 6%, which they 
attributed to insufficient wettability between the particles and the resin as well as insufficient 
mixing of the components and thus reduced interfacial adhesion. [64] 
This in turn makes it clear that uniform dispersion of the particles is essential so that no stress 
concentration points can build up in the matrix. In this work, the effective mixing of the biochar

4.5.2 PA1010 composites 
The stress-strain curve of the pure PA1010 and its composites are shown in Figure 13 and the 
flexure stress-strain curves can be seen in Figure 14. A graphical overview of the mechanical 
properties can be seen in Figure 15.  
The pure PA1010 shows a very high elongation at break of approx. 325%, tensile strength of 
38.5±2.5 MPa and tensile modulus of 1025±40 MPa. Some of these values are slightly lower 
than those in the literature, but overall they match quite well. [65] 
Once again, the addition of biochar leads to a higher tensile strength and a higher tensile 
modulus, but also to a lower elongation at break. With PA1010-BC50, the tensile strength 
increased by approx. 6% and the tensile modulus by a full 106% compared to the pure 
polyamide matrix. However, this was accompanied by a 98% reduction in elongation at break. 
The reasons for the increase and decrease in the respective mechanical properties are the same 
as for the PA12 composites.  
However, it can also be stated that defects or discontinuities due to the biochar would be 
possible, which would cause stress concentration points and thus premature failure of the 
matrix, but this could not be observed in the SEM analysis. 
In contrast to the PA12 composites, three-point bending tests could also be carried out in this 
case to evaluate stiffness and strength. A significant improvement in the flexural modulus and 
flexural strength at 10 % elongation was observed with increasing biochar content. For 
example, the flexural strength increased from 848 MPa for PA1010BC to 2050 MPa for 
PA1010BC50, as biochar particles have high stiffness and strength properties, which provide 
effective reinforcement. The interactions at the interfaces are fundamental to load transfer and 
the biochar contributes to this through its high aspect ratio and surface-to-volume ratio. It can 
therefore be said that by improving flexural modulus and strength, biochar fillers are good for 
applications where high stiffness and structural integrity as well as load bearing capacity are 
required. 
In the impact test, the ability of the materials to absorb energy during plastic deformation was 
tested and thus the toughness and impact strength of the material was measured. The energy 
absorption correlates with the brittleness of the material. The pure PA1010 already has a 
relatively high impact strength of approx. 7 mJ/m2 and is therefore significantly higher than that 
of the composite materials. The impact strength of PA1010BC50 in particular was reduced by 
51% compared to the pure matrix.  
It can therefore be concluded that biochar impairs the composite's ability to absorb and release 
energy on impact. These changes in energy absorption can occur due to the altered 
microstructure caused by the biochar particles. Larger particles can lead to crack propagation 
and increased brittleness, resulting in a decrease in toughness and elasticity under dynamic 
loading. [66]

4.5.3.3 Stiffness of PA12BC-Composite 
A model was also created to evaluate the stiffness of the composites and their components. This 
is based on the article by Madsen et al. (2009), which follows a modified mixing rule in order 
to integrate the influence of porosity on the stiffness of the composites. This allows the 
maximum achievable stiffness of the composite to be determined using the volumetric 
composition of the composites. Modelling can also be used to make predictions about different 
component ratios. [68] 
To calculate the stiffness, the volume fractions of filler, matrix and porosity in composite 
materials must be determined. These are interdependent, while the filler weight fraction (Wf) is 
represented as an independent variable, as it is not influenced by the porosity produced and can 
therefore be easily controlled during the production of the composites. Thus, the filler weight 
fractions of the individual phases are used instead for modelling the stiffness of the composites.  
The model distinguishes between two regions of volumetric interaction of the composites. In 
region A, Wf is below a transition value (Wf trans), the volume fractions of filler, matrix and 
porosity (Vf, Vm and Vp respectively) are only influenced by the microstructure of the composite 
and the associated densities of the components and the constants of the filler- and matrix-
correlated porosity.  
In region B, Wf is above Wf trans and the filler arrangement is compressed to its minimum volume 
so that the volumetric interaction is limited to a maximum achievable filler volume fraction 
(Vfmax).  
In the transition between regions A and B, Wf is equal to Wf trans, characterised by an optimum 
of the material properties marked by the highest achievable fibre volume fraction with the 
lowest possible porosity at the same time.  
In this work, however, the focus is limited to region A before the onset of Vfmax, as Vfmax is 
unknown for this system and it is assumed that in the considered range up to 50% Wf it is 
unrealistic to achieve the maximum obtainable filler volume fraction, which is significantly 
below Vfmax. Equations (6), (7) and (8) are used for the volumetric interaction model for region 
A.

ratio in particular has a major influence here. In addition, the calculated stiffness of the biochar 
filler is the absolute stiffness of the material, while the literature values reflect the apparent 
stiffness. Thus, the calculated value only considers the solid material and not its pores and their 
size and distribution and number, orientation, etc., so that the value is higher than the literature 
values that do this. Porosity usually has a negative influence on stiffness (Liu et al., 2017). If 
the stiffness is now compared with a similarly carbon-heavy reinforcing material with very little 
porosity, such as carbon fibres, which have an e modulus value of 200-500 GPa, the calculated 
stiffness of the biochar no longer seems so absurdly high [69].  
As can be seen from the diagrams, the value shows good agreement between the experimental 
and theoretical values, so it probably reflects the real conditions sufficiently. This applies to the

4.6 DMA 
4.6.1 PA12 composites 
DMA was used to analyse the viscoelastic behaviour of pure polyamide and the composites to 
gain insight into the interactions between the biochar and polyamide matrix. For this purpose, 
the samples were heated to 200°C using the multi-frequency strain method and the storage 
modulus (G') and the loss factor (tan δ) were set as a function of temperature. 
In the storage modulus vs temperature diagram (Figure 22), it can be seen that in the case of 
pure PA12, the G' remained relatively constant at low temperatures due to the glassy, highly 
ordered state of the immobilised polymer chains, which causes the rigid structure. As the 
temperature increases, G' decreases, indicating increased mobility and relaxation of the polymer 
chains and thus the glass transition temperature (Tg), because near and above Tg, increased chain 
movement occurs in the transition from a glassy to a rubbery state, resulting in reduced stiffness 
and increased flexibility. [72] 
A very similar behaviour occurs in composites, except that here G' is significantly increased by 
the addition of biochar.  For example, G' was 1270 MPa at 30°C for PA12 and increased to 
4300 MPa at the same temperature for PA12BC50, resulting in a 170% increase. This increase 
indicates greater stiffness due to a presumably very homogeneous dispersion of the biochar in 
the matrix and the strong interactions between them, resulting in a robust network that limits 
the relaxation of the polymer chains and their mobility. [73] 
The loss factor tan δ is the ratio of the loss modulus (G") to the storage modulus (G') and 
provides information about the damping behaviour of the material. Where the peak value of tan 
δ is reached reflects the glass transition temperature at which the transition from the glassy to 
the rubbery state takes place. Its plotting against temperature can be seen in Figure 23. For pure 
PA12, the glass transition temperature was approx. 65 °C, which is also confirmed by similar 
values in previous reports [74]. 
With the addition of biochar, the Tg increases slightly, as it is a reinforcing material that reduces 
polymer chain mobility and has strong polymer/filler, filler/filler interfacial interactions [73]. 
This is a further indication of the uniform dispersion and good interaction of the biochar 
particles with the polyamide matrix facilitated by the in-situ polymerisation, 
At the same time, however, the Tg peak height decreases with more biochar content and the 
material dissipates less energy accordingly, as the restricted polymer chains have less movement 
at the glass transition. [75]

4.7 Rheology 
4.7.1 PA12 composites 
Further analysis of the polymer-filler interaction and dispersion by analysing the viscoelastic 
properties was carried out using rheology.  
Firstly, a strain sweep was performed at a constant angular frequency of 10 rad/s and 
logarithmically increased shear strain from 0.01% to 100% in order to identify the linear 
viscoelastic range, shown in Figure 26. At low strain rates, the storage modulus (G') and the 
loss modulus (G") behave constantly and independently of the strain amplitude; the material 
behaves viscoelastically and obeys Hooke's law. [77] A shear strain of 1 % was therefore 
selected for the subsequent frequency sweep test in order to remain in the linear viscoelastic 
range.  
For the subsequent frequency sweep, an angular frequency in the logarithmic range from 0.01 
rad/s to 628 rad/s was selected. The results (Figure 27) show the complex viscosity, the storage 
modulus (G') and the loss modulus (G"), plotted against the angular frequency. 
The behaviour of G' and G" as a function of frequency shows the influence of biochar on the 
viscoelastic properties of PA12. They increase with increasing frequency values, which means 
that the viscosity and therefore the stiffness of the material increase, as less time is available for 
molecular relaxation. [78] All samples exhibit a higher G" than G' over the entire frequency 
range, so the behaviour is more fluid-like, where energy is dissipated rather than stored. Only 
for PA12BC50 is G' higher than G", thus exhibiting a more solid behaviour with increased 
deformation resistance and higher ability to store energy rather than dissipate it. [78] 
==PAGE:64==
 
 
56 
 
When observing the complex viscosity as a function of the angular frequencies (Figure 28), it 
is noticeable that the pure PA12 and the composite material exhibit Newtonian behaviour at low 
frequencies, i.e. the complex viscosity remains relatively constant and viscous flow dominates 
over elastic behaviour. At higher frequencies, however, the viscosity decreases and the 
composites exhibit so-called shear-thinning behaviour due to the disruption of the polymer 
chain entanglement and the physical bonding within the material as well as the interfacial 
bonding between the polyamide matrix and the biochar reinforcement. [79] 
The same applies to composites, but with the addition of biochar the viscosity increases, here 
the transition from liquid-like to solid-like behaviour becomes apparent at lower frequencies. 
The biochar particles improve the elastic reaction and the solid-like behaviour of the polymer 
melt by creating a continuous rigid network. Thus, the storage modulus (G') in composites is 
higher than in pure PA and increases proportionally to the biochar content. This in turn indicates 
the good dispersion and interaction of the biochar within the polyamide matrix, where the 
polymer chains can infiltrate the porous structure of the carbon and thus form strong interfacial 
bonds through mechanical interlocking and van der Waals forces, resulting in a robust network 
with lower mobility for the polyamide chains and therefore higher flow resistance. [73, 80] 
Thus, an increase in viscosity at zero rate from 2.7∙1012 Pa∙s in PA12 to 3.7∙1013 Pa∙s in 
PA12BC50 can be observed.  At higher shear rates, however, the viscosity decreases and 
pseudoplastic shear thinning behaviour occurs. This behaviour is again good for extrusion/3D 
printing, as the material can then be extruded evenly.

4.8 3D Printing 
Additive manufacturing, or more precisely 3D printing, involves the layer-by-layer production 
of 3D objects from digital designs. This method offers unique design flexibility, rapid 
prototyping and customisation. [81] 
However, not every material is suitable for this technique, especially regarding extrusion-based 
machines.  The materials must have specific rheological properties for optimal flow through the 
printer nozzle and adhesion between the layers, as well as shear-thinning behaviour and thermal 
stability for uniform extrusion and few printing defects such as nozzle clogging or stringing. 
[82] 
To demonstrate the practical applicability of the PA composite, mesh objects were printed using 
a 3D pellet printer. The corresponding CAD design and photos can be seen in Figure 32 and 
Figure 33. As all samples exhibit shear-thinning behaviour, they proved their suitability for 3D 
printing as they have a lower viscosity under shear stress, allowing the material to flow evenly 
through the nozzle. Without this property, blockages or nozzle jams would probably occur or 
the material might not be extruded at all.  
The PA12BC50 and PA1010BC30 composite therefore exhibited good flowability under 
printing conditions and precise mesh objects with even layers could be printed. Excellent 
interlayer adhesion and good structural integrity were also observed. 98 layers could be printed 
without problems with the correct printing parameters without clogging, delamination, 
misalignment, deformation or large deposits. In addition, with a PA-3D print adhesive and a 
high bed temperature and additional outskirt layers, good initial adhesion to the print bed was 
achieved with high bed temperatures and only minimal deformation on the outer layer of the 
print without the printed material warping or peeling. However, the printed objects showed 
negligible shrinkage due to the rigid biochar particles in the material, which did not affect the 
structural integrity of the printed object.

5 Conclusions 
An investigation of novel PA-biochar composites was carried out using various analytical 
methods and their chemical, mechanical, thermal and morphological properties were evaluated.  
For the first time, polyamide 12 was synthesised in a biochar composite by in-situ 
polymerisation. In addition, an almost exclusively bio-based composite material consisting of 
polyamide 1010 and biochar was produced by melt compounding. 
In both variants, the addition of biochar changed the morphological and crystalline properties 
of the composites and significantly improved the mechanical strength and thermal stability.  
For example, the tensile elongation of pure PA12 of 300±10%, the tensile strength of 38±1 MPa 
and the tensile modulus of 745±30 MPa were reduced to a tensile strength of 8±0.5% by adding 
50% biochar, but the tensile strength increased to 54±2 MPa and the tensile modulus increased 
to 2055 MPa. A similarly drastic increase or decrease was also achieved for the PA1010 
composites, where the tensile strength increased by approx. 6%, the tensile strain decreased by 
a full 98%, but the tensile modulus increased by a full 106%, the impact strength by 51% and 
the flexure modulus by 142%. 
The good dispersion of the biochar particles in the polyamide matrix and the excellent 
compatibility between polymer and filler are particularly evident here 
A morphological examination by SEM showed that there was no cracking, particle 
agglomeration or phase separation in any of the composites.  
The effect of biochar on crystallinity was analysed using DSC and in the case of the PA12 
composites, it was concluded that biochar could act as a nucleating agent, as the crystallinity 
==PAGE:71==
 
 
63 
 
increased with increasing biochar content. In addition, the melting and crystallisation 
temperatures were increased. Concerning the PA1010 composites, it was observed that the 
biochar also increased the melting and crystallisation temperatures, but the crystallinity was 
reduced. The reason for this could be that the biochar hinders the mobility of the polymer chains 
during crystallisation, resulting in less ordered structures. 
The thermal stability of the composites was analysed by TGA and it was found that the biochar 
in each composition led to an increase in the temperature weight loss T5%, T10% and Tmax, 
and the decomposition of the materials was reduced accordingly. 
Using DMA and rheological analysis, the viscoelastic behaviour of the materials was 
investigated with the result that higher stiffness and viscosity were observed in the composites 
with biochar reinforcement, which in turn is good for 3D printing applications. 
Accordingly, detailed structures could be successfully 3D printed with the polyamide-biochar 
composite with PA12 and 50% biochar and PA1010 and 30% biochar. This confirms the 
practical suitability of these composites and their versatility in additive manufacturing. For the 
latter, however, further optimisation of the parameters is still necessary to achieve the best 
possible results, which could yield properties comparable to those of injection moulding.  
In conclusion, it can be said that the use of the bio-based filler in the form of biochar has 
improved the mechanical strength, thermal stability and viscoelastic properties of the 
polyamide matrix and that these composites are also characterised by good printability. Thus, 
the resulting materials are very promising for advanced and more sustainable applications that 
meet the evolving needs of the industry. It can be confirmed that the almost 100% bio-based 
PA1010 in the biochar composite can compete with the petroleum-based composites and thus 
offers great potential as a more sustainable engineering material. 
 
For future work, the PA1010 biochar composites could be produced by synthesis through in-
situ polymerisation and analysed to see if this brings a further improvement in properties 
compared to the results of this work obtained with melt compounding. 
It is also possible to use biochar from other sources and incorporate it into the composites. For 
example, the use of pure pyrolysed lignin promises a high yield of carbon as well as high 
stability and is also largely regarded as a waste product. 
Another exciting work could be the use of recycled polyamide and testing its reusability in 
composites.  
In any case, further perfecting the printing parameters for all the composites produced may be 
useful to produce even larger and stronger objects and make their applicability even more 
realistic.
