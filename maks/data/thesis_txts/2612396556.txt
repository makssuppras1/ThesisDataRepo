Abstract
The field of Road Condition Assessment have become an important part of maintaining a good infras-
tructure, by discovering when and where to do maintenance. This assessment is done nowadays using
specialized measurements vehicles, which has a limited use because they can’t be used in bad weather
and because of their cost. To improve on this a new way of assessing the road has been created, by
predicting the road condition using data from normal electric cars. This thesis is about this machine
learning problem of prediction the road condition.
To that end the problem is a classified as a time series regression problem, which is solved using the
deep learning technique of transforming the time series into an image and then applying a 2D-CNN on
the generated image.
This method was first introduced in [WO15], and is still remains relative unexplored. Therefore multi-
ple experiments on different parts of the method is performed, including on the specific transformation
technique. The thesis aims to define what constitutes a good time series image features, and how the
CNN impacts overall performance. It test the idea that using this method you can take advances of
computer vision and apply them to a time series problem.
The results show that the experiments done on the method improved the performance of the overall
method by 38.4 % compared to the initial model. And that it is possible to use a CNN pre-built for
computer vision to great effect, thereby confirming the idea. When the method was applied to a time
series regression benchmark is shows that it performed on par with state-of-the-art models.
In regard to the road condition problem, the method of transforming the time series into an image per-
forms well enough on a simulated dataset and on the specialized vehicles dataset to be able to predict
when there is an error in the road. But for the combined dataset using data from the electric cars the
results is inconclusive due to lack of data.

Resume
Vurdering af vejens tilstand er en vigtig del af at opretholde et funktionelt netværk af veje da det
giver mulighed for at bedømme hvor og hvornår vejen skal vedligeholdes. Den nuværende metode til
at vurderer vejens tilstand består en række køretøjer med specialiseret måleudstyr. Disse køretøjer af
begrænset af både vejr og deres begrænset antal. Dette betyder at der for visse veje kan være lang
tid mellem dens tilstand bliver vurderet med denne metode. For at overkomme disse problemer en ny
metode er ved at blive udviklet, der estimerer de specialiserede køretøjers målinger ud fra målinger fra
almindelige personbiler. Til at løse dette tidsrække regressions problem bruges metoder fra Machine
Learning feltet. Mere præcist bruges en dyb lærings model der transformerer tidsrække dataen til et
billede og benytter en 2D-CNN model til at lave regressionen ud fra det generede billede.
Denne metode blev først introduceret i [WO15] og er stadig forholdsvis ukendt. Af denne grund bliver
der eksperimenteret med flere aspekter af metoden, med specielt fokus på den anvendte metode til at
transformere tidsrække data til et billede. Det vil i opgaven blive undersøgt hvad der kendetegner en
god metode til at transformere tidsrækker og hvordan valget af dyb lærings struktur påvirker resultatet.
Der undersøges om det er fordelagtigt at gøre brug af modeller udviklet til Computer Vision til at
modellere tidrække data.
Der opnås en forbedring på 38,4 % på vores problem i forhold til den oprindelige model præsenteret i
artiklen. I en benchmark test opnår modellen resultater der er på niveau med det bedste modeller på
området.
I forhold til det konkrete problem at vurdere vejens tilstand opnår modellen gode resultater på det
simulerede data set og på data målt på Vejdirektoratets køretøjer. For det tilgængelige datasæt for
data fra personbiler er der ikke tilfredsstillende grundlag til at træffe en endelig konklusion.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Humans have been building roads for thousands of years. Building and maintaining an efficient road
infrastructure has become vital for any larger population. This is even more apparent with the increased
population growth and globalisation of the last century. The field of Road Condition Assessment has
become an important part of maintaining roads, by recognizing when and where to do maintenance. It
started of with manual surveys of the roads, which is still prevalent practice today. It was only in the
1980s, that standardized methods of categorizing the condition of a road were created. One method is
the Pavement Condition Index which still requires a manual survey of the pavement[SDK78]. Another
more automatic method is the International Roughness Index (IRI) [Pat86]. Which is calculated di-
rectly from a measured road profile. The road profile is the vertical aspect of the road, how it goes up
and down.[SGP86]. From this the IRI can be roughly understood as the instantaneous displacement of
the car in the vertical direction. Thereby the higher the IRI the more ”bumpy” a ride on the road is.
For further definition of IRI see section 5.1.
The Danish Road Directorate(DRD) [Vej] is responsible for maintaining all the highways in Denmark
as well as some other roads in Copenhagen. For this they need regular assessment of the road quality,
so they can make the decision of where and when to do the road repair. They directly measure the road
profile by driving specialized measurement vehicles on the roads. These vehicles uses mounted lasers to
create the road profile. The vehicles driven by the DRD is relatively expensive and only works in good
weather. This limits how often the condition of a specific road can be assessed.
A problem in the road can be categorised by its severity. The less severe a problem, the cheaper it is
to solve. If a problem in the asphalt becomes severe enough, it can impact the foundation of the road.
Repairing the foundation of the road is a lot more expensive than just the asphalt. Thereby assessing
the road more often in a cheaper way becomes a good value-proposition for the DRD.
The LiRA project [LiR] is a corporation between the Danish Road Directorate (DRD) [Vej], Green Mo-
bility (GM) [GM], Sweco [Swe] and the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), which aim to create
a method for more assessments. Green mobility is a electric free-float carsharing platform, which has a
lot of electrics cars in greater Copenhagen. The GM cars in total drive on average more than 10.000
kilometers a day in and around the city of Copenhagen. These cars are all the same model, and a few
have as part of the LiRA project been fitted with an accelerometer. The data gained from these cars
consists of the acceleration in all three axis as well as the normal car sensor such as yaw rate, speed, tire
pressure etc. The idea of the project is then to predict road condition measures like the IRI as well as
other factors like energy consumption and air pollution, by only using data from the GM cars as input.
This will allow the DRD monitor the road network live and let them measure much more frequently
than what the current DRD vehicles allow.

1.1
Problem Specification
The LiRA project is a multiyear project that has many sub-problems, such as aligning the GM data
and the DRD data, and delivering their end results to the consumer the DRD. The focus of the thesis
will be on the main machine learning problem of predicting the road condition using the IRI as the
target variable.
This will be done using three different datasets, the simulation data, the GM data, and the DRD data.
The simulation data is a simulation of a car driving over a pothole, a patch or a crack in the road,
with the output being the simulated vertical acceleration of the car. Because of this dataset’s availabil-
ity and large size, it is the main dataset for model experiments. A combination of the GM and the
DRD datasets with aligned data, is the foundation for solving the main road condition problem. An
exploratory analysis of each dataset will be performed before its use.
The IRI is measured over a segment of the road. The DRD does this in 10 meter segments. The sensors
in a GM car takes measurement in different specific frequencies. Because of the speed of the car can
vary, the time it takes the car to drive the 10 m will vary. This creates a situation where there unequal
amount of data-points per 10 meter segment. Thereby a variable number of input points for one target
variable.
Figure 1.1 shows our analysis of the problem. Where the blue path shows our chosen way to model
the problem. The car measurements are clearly taken in a sequence from the start to the end of the
road segment. We have chosen to model this sequence as a time series because it is a sequence of
discrete-time data, thereby by definition a time series. We have chosen to model the problem as a
supervised problem with the input data from the GM car, and the labels given by the DRD. This gives
a more complete image of the problem than if anomaly detection was done, where you would only be
able to distinguishes, if the road was normal(good) or not(anomaly). We have chosen to model it as a
regression problem, because of the one target variable of the IRI per segment. While it is possible to
analyse the problem as a classification problem, by cutting the continues variable IRI into segments,
it will not give as precise a result. You could also look at it as a time series forecasting problem, by
looking at a sequence of segments of the road and thereby the time series of IRI values. This is not done
because it does not model the road condition of each segment as well, and requires longer segments of
data, which makes the it harder to combine the datasets. In total we model the problem as a Time
Series Regression problem(TSR).
Figure 1.2 shows a tree of different models that can solve the TSR problem. The blue path shows our
choice. While TSR problems lends it self to a solution based on its time series nature, it can still be
solved using classic machine learning models that using extracted features. This will be used as baseline
models. The time series based methods can be divided into the classic methods and the deep learning
methods. An obvious choice from the classic methods is the Hidden Markov Model [RJ86]. With this
choice one could model the road condition as the hidden variable and the GM measurements such as the
acceleration as the observed variable. Another choice could be a ARIMA [Bar71] based model, which
could model the oscillating signal of the acceleration well. Using this understanding of the accelerations,
it could then predict the IRI.
While the classic time series models could solve this problem well, in this thesis the focus is on deep
learning methods. The reason is the assumption that because of the large size of the simulation dataset
as well as the expected large amount of data LiRA project will have available, the deep learning methods
will outperform the standard methods.
In deep learning methods two kinds of baseline models are normally used for time series data. The
first is Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) [ZSV14], which processes a variable length time series, one
time-point at a time, with the models hidden state vector carrying information from previous inputs in
the series. This could be used to create features of the GM data which then could be used to predict

the IRI. In general this method is more suited to a time series forecasting problem. Instead we have
chosen to use the other base model, which is a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). CNN models
have had much success when applied in a 2D setting on images, where they can exploit the patterns
between neighbouring pixels, and then assemble more complex patterns using these smaller and simpler
patterns. For time series there are three different ways you can apply CNN’s. The most widely used
is the 1D-CNN variant. They work exactly like a 2D CNN besides only in the one direction of the
time series. This can easily be applied to the road condition problem, and it will be used as a deep
learning baseline in the thesis. But in general literature 2d-CNN are where CNN’s traditionally have
shown great performance. Therefore the focus will be on a method of using 2D-CNN models on time
series. One way of doing this, which is only available to multi-variable time series problem,is to lay
the different variable on top of each other. Thereby creating a sudo-image of the multi-variable time
series. This method has the problem of deciding which variables are ”close” to each other. It can also
be ”fixed” by using a kernel size matching the number of variables, but in this case the difference to a
1D-CNN becomes minimal.
Instead the method explored in this thesis is the idea of transforming a time series into an image, and
then using a 2D-CNN on the generated image in order to do a classification task. This idea was first
introduced by [WO15]. Based on this paper other methods for transforming time series into 2D ’images’
was introduced in [DHG18] and [CS19]. The transformations can be applied on each input variable of
the data, which then creates different channels in the input image for the CNN, like the RGB channels
of a normal image. This is the model explored in the thesis to solve the road condition assessment
problem.

1.2
Contributions
This thesis will seek to investigate the following hypotheses:
1. We assume that using the deep learning method of transforming time series into image features,
and doing regression on those using a CNN will achieve better results on the road condition
problem than other methods.
2. We assume that deep learning method of transforming time series into image features will be on
par with other state-of-the-art methods for the TSR problem
3. We assume that the choice of technique for the transforming the time series into an image, will
greatly impact the performance of the method.
4. We assume that the choice of CNN architecture has a noticeable impact on the performance of
the method
5. We assume that pre-built CNN can achieve near optimal performance for the method.
6. We assume the method used to standardize the time series length has a noticeable impact on the
performance of the method.
7. We assume that larger images will increase the performance of the method at the cost of compu-
tation time

1.3
Related Work
Creating a cheaper alternative to the current road profilers by measuring the acceleration of a standard
vehicle is not a new idea but has been a subject of study for a number of years. Two different ideas for
the measurement of the acceleration data has proposed, using mobile phones and using sensors installed
in the vehicle. In [Fuj+05] and [Par+18] an approach using sensors installed in the vehicle are proposed.
In the latter of these it allows for the use additional features such as yaw rate of the car. This method
has the advantage over the mobile phone sensors that we know the orientation of the sensor in relation
to the vehicle and direction of travel. Using smartphones inbuilt sensors to measure the acceleration
and GPS position of the vehicle has been the more popular approach as seen in [MPR08], [Eri+08],
[Bho+12], [Ale+14] and [VMT19].
This approach has the advantage of using existing hardware in
smartphones to measure the needed data and as done in [Ale+14] as part of their SmartRoadSense
project[Bog+18] can easily be applied on mass scale via a smartphone app.
What characteristics of the road is being used to asses the condition of the road vary between differ-
ent studies but can be classified as doing either classification or regression. The studies [Eri+08] and
[Par+18] classify each segment of road based on if there is a road defect and what kind of road defect
is detected. The other approach is to do regression to get a standard road characteristic like IRI as is
done in [JJD20] and in [Ale+14] where they use their own scale for road roughness.
Multiple approaches to using the acceleration data to asses the condition of the road has been explored
in the literature. In one the earliest papers [Eri+08] a series of handcrafted filters, the parameters
of which was optimised using a small hand-labeled dataset, is used to classify road segments. In the
SmartRoadSense system discussed in [Ale+14] a signal processing approach is used. They use a method
utilising the Power spectral density and linear predictive coding to get a roughness index from a three
dimensional acceleration signal. The most popular approach by far is using machine learning methods.
The popularity of machine learning approaches can be attributed to the rise of deep learning methods
in the last years. This popularity has lead to a mix of deep learning models being used for this problem.
In [Par+18] a model utilising LSTM’s is used to classify road segments into 4 categories with a 94%
accuracy. A CNN based model is used in [JJD20] to estimate the IRI value of the road segment. Here a
2d-CNN is used on the the 3 different accelerations. Of interest is the paper [VMT19] as they compare
several different models at the road segment classification task. Among other they compare a CNN
model, a LSTM model, a reservoir computing model, a model based on Dynamical Time Warping and

a model using Naïve Bayes and K-means. It was found that in the studied tasks that the CNN model
achieved the best performance. A more classical machine learning approach is seen in the 2012 paper
[Bho+12] that uses a mix of supervised and unsupervised methods. They use a K-mean clustering and
support vector machine method together to classify segments of road as smooth or bumpy.
Going a bit more into depth with [JJD20] since its the closest comparison. They used a 2D CNN on
the three accelerations to predict the IRI. But they do not generate images, instead they lay the three

kernel size of 3 in the attribute dimension to predict the IRI. The data used was based on road profile

by the first 10.6 km of the trip for training-set the next for 4km for validation-set and the last 16.5
km for test-set. This split does not ensure that the distributions of the different datasets are similar,
and therefore their result of 0.565 IRI RMSE should be taken into that context. Even more so because
systemically larger IRI values can be observed in the training-set than the test-set.

CHAPTER 2
Baseline
To explore anything, be it a method, model or data-set, there is a need for something to compare to.
This is what the following chapter is for. This will include a section about the simulation dataset, which
is what is used to when exploring the methods. The chapter will also include a section on different

2.1
Simulation Data
The main data used for model exploration is simulated data. The simulations were carried out by the
Civil Engineering department at DTU [DTUb]. The simulation is a quarter car model [Say89], which
simulates a single wheel of a car as it travels along a 100 m segment of road. The road profile of the
road segment is ideal, which means there is no errors in the road besides the one selected defect. The
variable severity can be seen as the road profile, it is zero except when the car is driving over a defect,
at that point it describes the depth/height of the defect. Each simulation has one of three types of
defects. The simulated types of defects are: patch, pothole and crack. Each simulation also varies the
speed of the vehicle, as well as the type, width and depth of the defect. Using the quarter car model
and the road profile given by the severity, the vertical acceleration is then simulated.
The signals of interest for us, is the vertical acceleration of the car and the severity of the defect. The
severity is the target of the ML problem and the vertical acceleration is the input. While you could
use the tire force or suspension force used in the simulation as an input variable, it is not possible to
reliably measure in a real world setting and therefore they are not used.
2.1.1
Data Exploration
In figures 2.1, 2.2 and 2.3 we see example of the 3 different types of defects simulated, namely patch,
crack and pothole. The severity plotted is zero whenever there is not a defect and will at the defect have
a value equal to the height of the defect. It is clear from the examples that the acceleration very neatly
takes the form of a dampened harmonic oscillation, where the motion caused by the impact between
the road defect and the wheel is slowly dissipating.
As can be seen in the plots the correlations between the vertical acceleration and the severity is about
0.6 for the pothole and the patch, and about 0.4 for the crack.
The crack has the lowest correlation because of the low width, thereby making the part of the severity
time series that are different from 0 a lot smaller. But the oscillations still continue for some time
because of the relative high severity of the crack. But still there is clearly a connection between the
two variables. Another thing which can be seen is that the highest correlation is at lag 1-2, which can
also be seen in that accelerations start changing from zero 1-2 points before the severity does. This
is because the simulated tire contact patch (with the road) is about 250 mm in length. So while the

2.1.2
Preprocessing
In the real world the road conditions is estimated on much smaller windows than 100 meters, therefore
a sliding window with a width of 2 meters and step size of 1 was applied. From the plots 2.1, 2.2 and
2.3 its very obvious that it is a idealised road profile, with large segments where both the vertical accel-
eration and the severity is zero. Therefore many of the sliding windows contain only zeros, which makes
them extremely uninteresting and not representative of the real world. Hence these were removed as
well.
The real problem we are trying to solve in the thesis is a TSR problem, therefore the simulated data is
transformed into one as well. This was done by only looking at the maximum depth/height of a defect
in the sliding window, so the severity signal is reduced to the numerically largest value present in the
window. This gives us data that consists of time series of vertical acceleration data which each have
a single target value. With a sampling frequency of 300 Hz the time series have a varying length as a
result of the different simulated speeds of the car. As they also did in [JJD20], because signals from real
world data at speeds below 20 km/hr are dominated by starting and stopping of the car, we exclude
these data from the dataset to create a more realistic setting.
In figure 2.4 a histogram over the length of each time series after our preprocessing has been applied.
There is clearly a wide spread of different length. As some models need the input to have uniform
length, some method to increase or decrease the length of each time series is needed. The choice of
method for this will most likely have a significant effect on the performance due to the great difference
and wide spread of the data.
In the end the dataset is split into a training set of size 5.9 mil, validation set of size 2 mil and test set
of size 2 mil. Thereby there is more than enough data to train any type of model.

Extracting features from time series
Not all of the models that will be used for as baselines are designed for using the temporal aspect of the
data. It is possible to have models like a linear regression model take the time series as input, where the
model would understand each time point in the time series as input feature. This method suffers from
a severe drawback in the model in this case would see a time series shifted 1 point in either direction as
a totally different input. The model would struggle to capture any time dependent effect in the data.
So for the these kinds of models that are not well suited for time series data we will extract a number
of features from the time series that then will be used as input to the model. A wide range of features
are used, ranging from simple features like length of the time series, mean and standard deviation, to
more complex features there are derived from a Fourier transform of the time series signal. A list of
all the features used is visible in table A.6 in the Appendix. The features are calculated using the
Python package TSFEL [Bar+20]. This results in having 40 features per dimension of the input time
series, which are then added one at a time using a forward selection scheme, when training a model.
This feature selection step leads to a large amount of models being fitted, which is unfeasible to do
in a reasonable time on the full simulation data set. For the simulation data set the feature selection
process in instead done a small subset of the data consisting of 20000 time series. The final model is
then tested on the full simulation data set.
Standardizing the size of the time series

This means that the input time series needs to be made of uniform length. The most simple version of
this will be to pad the shorter time series with zeros until all time series have the same length. This is
under the assumption that 0 means no signal as is the case in the simulation dataset. This will make
all time series have the length of the previous longest time series. Due to how the computational cost
of training and running machine learning models scales with the length of the time series, it can be
beneficial to shortening the longer input time series instead. This can be done by averaging a number
of elements in the time series.

2.2.3
K-Nearest Neighbours
The K-nearest neighbours (KNN) [Pet09] is a classic model that is both intuitive and useful in a wide
range of cases. The model works by having any new points being determined by the points in the training
set that is closest to the new point. The most common used method for determining this distance is
using the euclidean distance. When used for regression the average of the ”k” closest neighbours is used
to determine the target value for any new points. This works by calculating the distance from any new
points to all the points in the training set, which can for large data sets be computationally expensive
making the method best suited for smaller problems. This is done using the extracted features on the
smaller dataset to keep it computationally viable.
2.2.4
Gradient Boosting Tree Regressor
Gradient boosting [Fri01] is a method combines multiple weak learners to form an ensemble with good
predictions. In this case the weak learners are regression trees. Regression trees are trees of decisions,
that are used to split the training set into small subsets. When used to predict the target value of a new
data point, it uses the ”decisions” of the new input to find the most similar subset of samples in the
training dataset, and then uses a mean of that subset’s target values as the prediction. The gradient
boosting method creates one regression tree at a time, where each new tree tries to improve on the
errors of its predecessor. This is done by fitting the new model to the residuals of the previous. This is
done with the extracted features on the smaller dataset.
2.2.5
Random Forest Regression
Random Forest [Bre01] is another method that combines multiple regression trees. It does this by
bootstrapping random training sets for each tree. Bootstrapping meaning sampling with replacement
from the original training set. Every split in the tree is also evaluated from a random subset of the
features. The prediction of random forest is the mean value of all the regression trees in the forest. These
random choices makes the model reduces the overall variance of the model and prevents overfitting,
thereby creating a good prediction. This does not scale well into high amounts of data, and therefore
it is also run with the extracted features on the smaller dataset.
2.2.6
Support Vector Regression
A Support Vector Machine (SVR) [SS04] creates a hyperplane on a high dimensional representation of
the data, which can then be used for regression, thereby becoming Support Vector Regression(SVR). It
uses a kernel to project the input features into the high dimension, therefore this also uses the extracted
features. Because of the feature selection step this is done on the small dataset. The kernel used is the
Radial basis function kernel.
2.2.7
Feed-Forward Neural Network
The first deep learning baseline is a feed-forward neural network (FNN) [SKP97], which consists of a
series of densely connected linear layers. This model does not take into account that the model is a
time series, and just use the values of the time series as different features. It does not use the extracted
features, because the model itself is complex enough to create those. FNN’s are very prone to overfitting.
To combat this, between every layer of the model we use both batch normalization and dropout. We
have chosen a pyramid like shape for the model with the first layers having the most hidden units and
the following layers having fewer and fewer hidden units. An illustration of the model structure can
be seen in the appendix A.1. This type of structure has been chosen as it is commonly used for the
output CNN models[Bas+20] and as such can serve as simple check for if the convolutional layers are
even needed.

Batch-normalisation
Batch-normalisation is the normalisation of the output of a layer in the network over the batch. It
works using the following formula:

The mean and standard-deviation are calculated over the batch. Across both the batch and the data
dimensions, thereby making the whole batch Gaussian. The mean and standard deviation is kept as
a running estimates with a momentum of 0.1. ϵ is a value added to the denominator for numerical
stability. The results of the batch-normalisation is that we reduce variance in the optimisation space,
as well as keeping outliers in the data under control and thereby minimising extreme value predictions.
In a CNN this would work in the same way, one mean and one standard deviation for the batch. This
makes the whole batch of images Guassian. This works splendidly for normal images, but can course
some problems in other instances as can be seen later in section 4.2.3.
2.2.7.2
Dropout
Dropout [Sri+14] in a Feed-Forward Neural Network is that idea that you randomly ”drop” activations
of certain neurons, by setting them to 0 regardless of the input. This is only done in the training phase,
and what it achieves it that it makes the Feed-Forward Neural Network use all its neuron and not just
a select few. This combats over-fitting in that you know effectively have many small models in your
model.
What is more interesting is dropout for CNN’s. CNN’s exploit the structure of the time series or image,
therefore randomly setting some parts of this to 0 will break that structure. Therefore the normal
kind of dropout is in general not advised for CNN’s. Instead what you can do for CNN’s is to drop
entire channels/features. For example if the input is a RGB image, you might randomly drop the Green
channel, making it all 0s.
2.2.8
1D-Convolutional Neural Network
A 1D-CNN model is constructed to compare against the 2D-CNN that is the focus of the thesis. The
1D-CNN model is heavily inspired by the 1D-CNN model introduced in [Tang]. Their model called
Omni-Scale 1D-CNN (OS-CNN) is designed to be used as a baseline. The idea behind the OS-CNN is to
concatenate several 1D convolutions with different kernel sizes. This leads to an enlarged receptive field.
The receptive fields is a term for, how much of the input time series a single neuron through connections
to previous layers has access to. The model uses prime numbers as kernel sizes up to 1/4 the length
of the input time series. We first implemented the OS-CNN model but saw that its performance was
lower than expected for the large data set, because of its relatively small size with only one, though
quite complicated layer. As such we made our own version by adding additional layers to both the
convolutional and the linear part of the model. The CNN layers are OS-CNN layers. An illustration of
the models structure can be seen in appendix in figure A.2 in the appendix.

Encoding Time Series as
Images
Inspired by the success of deep learning methods in the field of computer vision a new method of
analysing time series data was proposed [WO15]. The new idea was to encode the time series data into
into images and then use well understood computer vision deep learning models on the constructed
images. Several methods of encoding the time series data was proposed by Wang and Oates [WO15]
with more methods late being proposed in [DHG18], [CS19]. In all of these papers a CNN model was
used to successfully classify time series data from the UCR Archive [Dau+18] and obtained state-of-
the-art or comparatively results. All of the encoding methods discussed will preserve the temporal
dependencies by constructing a NxN image, from a time series of length N, such that time is along both
axis of the image.

3.3
Recurrence Plots
The recurrence plot is a visualisation technique for time series data dating back to 1987 [EOR87]. It has
recently been reused for time series encoding for use with deep learning models [DHG18]. A recurrence
plots shows the phase space trajectory returns to a previous level.
The phase space trajectory is
constructed from the time series X by a time delay embedding with lag 1. For a time series X =
[x1, x2, ..., xn] the phase space trajectory will have the form:
s1 = (x1, x2), s2 = (x2, x3), ..., sn−1 = (xn−1, xn)
(3.7)
The recurrence plot is then calculated by the formula:
Ri,j = θ(ϵ −||−→s i −−→s j||),
i, j = 1...(n −1)
(3.8)
where ϵ is a thresholding parameter and θ is the Heaviside function, that forces the output to 0 or 1.
The resulting image from this is a figure showing with black dots where the time series is close to a
previous state. This use of the Heaviside function results in loss of information that a machine learning
model could use. For this reason the Heaviside function θ and the threshold ϵ is dropped when the
method is used for encoding time series data. So the Recurrence plot used will be calculated using the
formula
Ri,j = ||−→s i −−→s j||,
i, j = 1...(n −1)
(3.9)
In figure 3.3 an example of an image made with the RP method is seen. The image is constructed based
on the shown sinus curve. It can be seen how the image is padded with a single row and column of
zeros due to the phase space transformation results in a sequence on shorter. Therefore if you want to
use this feature with any other NxN feature you need padding in the last row and column. Due to the
method using a distance measure the resulting image will be symmetric along the diagonal as can be
seen in the figure.

ResNet
A Residual Network(ResNet)[He+16] is a specific type of CNN. It works on the principle of resid-
ual(shortcut) connections between blocks of convolutions. This takes the input of a block of convolu-
tions and appends it to the output of that block of convolutions. What this does it it creates and easier
path for the gradient to be backpropergated through the network. It also allows the network to decide
if it wants to use each block or not. This architecture has made it possible to train way deeper networks
than possible without. The network we use as a baseline is ResNet18, which means there is 18 layers
of convolutions in the network. This gives a relatively deep network, that still is easy to train, which
makes it a good baseline CNN. The network is shown in appendix A.3.

Benjamini-Hochberg Algorithm
The problem of cumulative error of doing a lot of hypothesis tests, can best be explained by example.
If you are doing a single hypothesis test using α = 0.05 you would expect it has a 5% change of being
false. But if you did 20 hypothesis test you would expect on average that 1 test would be false. If you
would still like the chance of there being any error to be 5% you need to do something else. The easiest
correction is to put α = 0.05
N
where N is the number of tests. But this might be too restrictive for
some hypothesis. This is where you instead can use the Benjamini-Hochberg Algorithm as introduced
in [BH95]. This works as follows.
1. Sort all the hypothesis test by ascending p-value
2. Assign ranks to the p-values, the lowest has 1, second lowest 2 etc.
3. The critical value of the hypothesis with rank i becomes
i
N α
4. Now use the new critical values to reject or accept each hypothesis.

4.1.1
Kernel Functions Inspired Methods
The whole idea of transforming a time series into an image, is a way of projecting the problem into higher
dimensions. This is also the idea of kernel methods. The best performing methods the RP,GADF and
RPM all used the distance or the difference between individual parts of the time series to achieve this.
Therefore our idea is to expand on this by using the functions of kernels as distance metrics between
different points of the time series. This is NOT kernels, but hopefully the kernel functions have some
good properties for projecting data into higher dimensions that will also be useful in these methods.
All of our kernel function inspired methods work by setting the value (i, j) in the image matrix to the
distance between i′th and j′th value of the time series as given by the kernel inspired function. This
then creates the image X of size NxN from the time series x of length N. In table 4.1.1 all the kernel
function used can be found. In figure 4.1 examples of the generated images can be seen.

4.1.3
Understanding the performance of image generation methods
We have formulated the two main ways that we hypothesise the image generation methods can decrease
the performance of the overall method. The first is by introducing a loss of ”important” information
to the problem. The second is by constructing an image that cannot be decoded by the CNN. While
its impossible to quantify how large amount of important information is lost in the image generation,
we can quantify how much information the original time series has, and how much the generated image
has. We do this by using the concept of entropy see section 4.1.3.1. To understand if the problem lies
in the decoding done by the CNN is more strait forward. Since we have the original time series, we can
use the same CNN besides the output layer to reconstruct the original time series from the generated
image. Depending on the CNN’s performance on this task we then know if it is possible for the CNN
to decode the generated image(assuming the generated image contains all the information).
4.1.3.1
Entropy
Entropy is a word with many meanings, the one we are looking at here is the information theory
definition. This says that the entropy of a variable is the average level of ”information” inherent in the
variable’s possible outcomes. In other words the information in its distribution. Another way to look
at it is how much memory it takes to store the variable. It was mathematically defined in [Sha48] as:
H(X) = −
N
X
i=1
P(xi) logb(P(xi))
(4.1)
Here H(X) is the number of bits it takes to store X. Where the size of the bit is defined by the base b
of the log. N is the number of elements in X. P(xi) is the probability of xi given the distribution of X.
So if the image contained only 1’s, then P(xi = 1) = 1, and P(xi ̸= 1) = 0.
Entropy gives a way to compare the ”information” of different generated images. But it is depended
on the choice of log-base as for what it considers giving the most information. Because of the way its
formulated it can be shown that P(xi) = 1
b has the most impact on the size of H(X). We have chosen
the natural log, making the xi with P(xi) = 1
e ∼0.368 the most impact on the amount of information.
Higher bases will reward images with more different values, with lower rewarding the ones with more
constant values. e was chosen as the base as middle ground. So while entropy will be used to compare
the information in the generated images, it has to be taken with a grain of a salt, as there is no way to
know if its ”useful” information that is measured.
4.1.3.2
Entropy and Reconstruction Error of image features
As can be seen in table 4.3 there are features that have errors in both regards. The worst is the MTF
which has the lowest Entropy by far and also a bad reconstruction error, which probably stems from
the information loss. The only other two features with less entropy than the original time series are
Sigmoid and Histogram intersection. But Histogram intersection has the original time series in the
diagonal, so here we see the limitations of what can be concluded from the entropy alone. What we
do see is that the best performing features of GADF, RPM and Histogram Intersection all have a low
reconstruction error. This speaks for the problem mainly being this, that its hard for the CNN to
decode the more complex image features. That what is important about the image generation methods
is not if they create a good ”fancy” features, its just that they create a feature where the time series is
easily accessible. To this end we introduce some simple features in the following section.

4.2
Impact of a specific CNN on performance
In any deep learning model, the architecture of the network has a lot to say about the performance. But
how exactly this choice affects the performance of this method was not explored in any of the papers.
We explore this in two parts. Firstly we look at the idea with this method, that you can apply your
favorite pre-built CNN to the generated images and get good results. Then later we will create our own
CNN, looking at different parts of the CNN and how they each affects performance. In all these tests
will follow the baseline set up in section 3.5.1 besides the CNN being used.
4.2.1
Pre-built models
There are a lot of different pre-built CNN’s available for use. We will focus on some of them that
available in Pytorch. Another constraint we have is that we are using only a single GPU with 12 or 16
GB of memory. Therefore the model needs to fit in that, when using our training scheme see section
3.5.1. From these constraints we have 5 different pre-built models, where some have different versions.
ResNet
As mentioned in section 3.5.1.2 we have used ResNet18 as our standard model. ResNet is short for
Residual Neural Network and is a type of CNN structure developed for use in image recognition [He+15].
Besides the ResNet18, we will also run a ResNet34 and ResNet50. Even deeper ResNet models ran into
the memory constraint with our batch-size.
VGG
Models using the VGG achieved state-of-the-art results on the large scale image data set ImageNet
when it was introduced in 2015 [SZ15]. It further was shown to generalize well and achieved a good per-
formance on a wide range of tasks. The VGG architecture consists of 3x3 convolutional layers stacked
atop one another with maxpool and the output produced using fully connected layers and softmax. The
success of the VGG architecture showed that with sufficiently depth you do not need convolutional lay-
ers with a large receptive field but small 3x3 convolutional layers are sufficient. The VGG architecture
has different sizes, typically shown by a number added after, for example VGG13 which would be a
VGG model with 13 layers with weights. The downside of the VGG architecture is the large size and
slow training of the networks, which puts a practical limitation on the depth of network. The types of
VGG we will try are vgg11, vgg11_bn, vgg13, vgg13_bn. bn standing for batch normalization between
the layers. Larger VGG’s ran into the memory constraints.
DenseNet
The DenseNet architecture takes the concept of using connections between convolutional layers to the
extreme. In the network each convolutional layers takes the output from each of the previous con-
volutional layers as input [Hua+18]. This means that the last convolutional layer of the model will
have the features produced from all other layers as input, and that the image size will be the same
throughout all layers. All these previous layer features will quickly cause the number of input features
to explode at later layers. As such the authors of the original paper created their model using 3 blocks
consisting of several of these densely connected convolutional layers. Each block is then connected using
a regular convolutional layer and a pooling layer. This densely connected structure leads to a better
reuse of features and more compact model. In the original paper [Hua+18] it was found on test on the
ImageNet dataset that DenseNet models achieved the same performance as Resnet using around 1/3
of the parameters. Because of the decrease in number of parameters its possible for us to use a way
deeper model, which is why its the denseNet121 that is used. The even larger densenets still ran into
the memory constraint.
==PAGE:39==
4.2 Impact of a specific CNN on performance
32
Resnext
Resnext is an extension to the resnet architecture that changes a resnet block to include multiple CNN
paths. Where the resnet block includes the 3 convolutional layers and the skip connection, the resnext
block has a number of parallel paths all having 3 convolutional layers, the total number of features
for all paths roughly equals the number of features of a resnet block. Resnext still retains the skip
connection. The resnext we use is Resnext50_32x4d, which means that there is 50 layers, with 32
paths with 4 features in each path. Larger resnext ran into the same memory constraints as the other
networks.
4.2.2
Creating a CNN from scratch
When designing our CNN from scratch we took inspiration from the VGG architecture. This means the
structure of a number of blocks of a certain size, with decreasing image size and increasing number of
features for each new block. In the end we put a feedforward neural network, for doing the regression
using the CNN features. We made a selection of tests of variants of the architecture. This includes, the
size and depth of the feedforward part, the number of initial features, the size of the blocks, the number
of block, the activation function, the pooling function and testing global pooling between the CNN and
FNN parts. We also tested different regularization techniques, including dropout and normalization.
These where all done on the small part of the simulation dataset. Results of the different tests are
shown in the appendix A.1. For the final CNN see the CNN part of figure 4.5. One thing we discovered
was that batch normalization seems to make the performance worse, which seems to be also be the case
for the pre-buildt VGG model as can be seen in table 4.6. Why this is the case will be hypothesised on
in the following section.
4.2.3
Batch Normalization Vs Instance Normalization
A feature of batch normalization, is that it calculates one mean and one variance value over the whole
batch. While this work very well in most cases, there seems to be a problem when used on the generated
image. Since all these tests where done using the RPM image feature, the course could lay within it.
But since RPM is just based on differences between points in the time series this is not likely. It could
also be because the numerical scale of the input image from the RPM is uncapped, since the difference
is so. But since the normalization is used at the earliest after a convolution layer and an activation, it is
unlikely this is the case. The most likely cause is that model becomes a ”noise-sensitive application” as
described in [SK16], because of the generated features. This causes the noise introduced by the batch
normalization to be a problem, instead of a gain for the training. A way to fight this, which we did in
our own model is to use instance normalization instead.
Instance Normalization
Instance normalisation is an alternative to batch normalisation. Instead of calculating the mean and
variance over both the batch and the spatial dimensions, it calculates a different mean and variance
for each image in the batch. Thereby making each image gaussian but not the whole batch gaussian.
Thereby still combating extreme value predictions, but not introducing as much noise.

Methods of standardizing the time series length
As explained earlier the method requires standardized image sizes and thereby standardizing time series
length. We set up a baseline of PAA and 0-padding, which have been used for all earlier experiments.
But as could be seen in figure 2.5, PAA has some problems. 0-padding similarly has a problem in
that is skews the signal to noise ratio of the sample.
0-padding has an even worse problem if the
0-value does not mean no signal. Then it actually disturbs the distribution of the time series. All these
problems, while easy to formulate, their impact on the performance is hard to quantify. But through
the introduction of other length standardization techniques we try.
4.3.1
Noise padding
Noise padding is another form of padding, that should help with the problem of 0-padding disturbing
the distribution of the time series. The noise are Gaussian with a small amplitude, so while this still
impacts the distribution of the time series, the whole padding is not the exact same value. How low
the amplitude of the noise should be depends on the magnitude of the signal. What we implemented
was to have an amplitude of 5% of the standard deviation of the time series. But in general as long as
it is not too large, it will not matter much, since the model will learn to ignore it. See figure 4.3 for an
example.
4.3.2
Uniform Scaling
Uniform Scaling is a simple technique for upscaling a time series. Given a time series A of length N
you want to transform into a new time series B of length M, it works as follows:
B[i] = A[⌊i N
M ⌋] for i ∈[0, M −1]

4.3.3

Linear interpolation is a classic method to generate new points between 2 known points, thereby it can
be used to upscale a time series. Given a time series A of length N you want to transform into a new
time series B of length M, it works as follows:
i = j N

B[j] = (i1 −i)A[i0] + (i −i0)A[i1]

the interval [0, N −1].

4.3.4
Truncating parts of the signal containing no information
An idea, when you have a time series signal where you have a value or a threshold of values that means
noise or no signal, is to just cut of this part of the signal. This does have the possibility of being a loss
of information if the timing of when the time series goes from noise to signal matter. Therefore what
we propose is truncating, which means only deleting at the ends of the signal, thereby minimizing the
possibility of loosing relevant information. While this idea might not work in general, it does have a lot
of merit on the simulation data set, since large parts of the time series can be 0, until the simulated car
hits the defect. Truncating 0s in the data does not standardize the length of the time series, so this has
to be done afterwards with one of the other techniques. It must be said that if you truncated noise or
0s, it does not make sense to use a padding technique to standardize the length after. Since this would
likely just re-add the parts truncated.

CHAPTER 5
Data
We will in this chapter introduce two road condition datasets used to solve the road condition problem.
We also introduce a set of benchmark datasets for the problem of TSR, which will enable the comparison
of our model to models considered to state-of-the-art models on TSR.
5.1
International Roughness Index (IRI)
The International Roughness Index (IRI) has since its introduction in 1986 [SGP86] become one of the
most widely used measurement for evaluating the conditions of roads. The IRI is as the name suggest an
measure of the ’roughness’ of the road. More precisely the IRI is the accumulated vertical movement of
a standard vehicle’s suspension over a driven distance and has the standard unit m/km. The calculation
of the IRI is based on a longitudinal road profile, that is a profile of the height of the road along the
direction of travel. On this road profile is used a quarter car model[Say89] to simulate a car traveling
along the road. A quarter car model simulate a single wheel and corresponding suspension system of
a car. A specific set of parameter values for the quarter car model is chosen, the so-called Golden Car
[Gil80], in order the get a representative result. The simulation in the Golden car model is performed
for the car moving at 80km/hr. The vertical movement of the suspension is then accumulated and
divided by the length of the road profile to get the IRI value for that section of road. Most commonly
the IRI value is calculated in 10 meter intervals. For this project we will use the IRI measurements as
targets for the models. The IRI value data is supplied by the Danish Road Department. This data is
explained in detail in section 5.2. In figure 5.1 a scale for what IRI values describe for different types
of road/pavement.
5.2
Danish Road Department
For this project data from the Danish Road Department (DRD) have been made available for use in the
modeling. The data have been collected by the DRD for the LiRA project [LiR]. The data is collected
from a Greenwood P79 profiler vehicle driving different routes in the area around Copenhagen Denmark.
The routes of the vehicle include both highways and smaller roads in the city of Copenhagen. This gives
data from different type of roads and for a wide array of road conditions and road defects. The P79
vehicle measure several different parameters of its own movement and of the road it is driving on. The
main measurement equipment of the profiler is a bar in front of the vehicle which house 25 separate
lasers which measure the distance to the road. From this a 3d-profile of the road is constructed. For
this project we are interested in the the measurements from the lasers 5 and 21 which are mounted in
line with the wheels. The measured road profile from these lasers is used to calculate the International
Roughness Index (IRI) of the road. This means for each segment of road the P79 profiler has driven
across we have two IRI measurements, one for the left wheel path and one for the right wheel path. In
addition to this the driving speed, acceleration, and the vertical movement of the vehicle is measured.
The data is not measured using a fixed sampling frequency but a measurement is taken at every 0.1m
driven, resulting in a sampling frequency that is depending greatly on the speed of the vehicle. As the
International Roughness Index (IRI) is calculated based on the lasers in 10m intervals the dataset will
have consistently have 100±1 data points of acceleration for each set of IRI values.

5.2.2
Data preprocessing
Since we have two different IRI values at the different wheels, there is two target variables both explain-
ing something about the road condition. We want the best possible estimate of the real road condition
therefore we should use information from both wheels. As could be seen the in the plots, the two IRI
values are very close, and therefore we count their contribution to the overall road condition equal. To
do this we average the two values, thereby assuming to get a better estimate of the real road condition
while keeping the same scale of IRI for comparison. For this data set there is no need to create windows
as the data is already organized into 10 meter segments which nicely have almost the same length. In
the end the dataset is split into a training set of size 61103, validation set of size 12294 and test set of
size 18350. Each data sample containing the time series of the displacement, acceleration and speed of
the vehicle as well as the corresponding mean IRI value.
After the preprocessing we can look at how the different input variables of vertical displacement, accel-
eration and speed interact with the combined IRI. Results of this can be seen in table 5.1. What we see
is even better connection than in the simulation data set, in that there are three different variables all
with some correlation with the IRI. Therefore we conclude that it will definitely be possible to model.

6.2 Discussion
54
6.2
Discussion
In section 1.2 we set up different hypothesis we wanted to test about the problem and our model. The
following section will discuss how our results reflects on these hypothesis.
Starting with the hypothesis of the main method, we looked into different techniques for transforming
the time series into an image. Of the methods already introduced in [WO15], [DHG18] and [CS19],
we saw a large difference in performance between the best (GADF,RPM) and the worst (GASF,MTF).
With the introduction of our own image generation techniques, we got even more examples of differences
in performance. With this we can confirm our hypothesis that the choice of image generation technique
has a large impact on the overall method. Why this is the case we tried to investigate using Entropy
and Reconstruction Error. The Entropy results did not exactly give the answer we were looking for.
What we wanted to understand was if the image generation technique lost ”important” information in
its transformation. But since it only counted information in an abstract manor it was not exactly what
was needed. What it did confirm was that the MTF features has a very large loss of information, and
therefore probably also a loss of ”important” information. This can also be seen in the example figure
3.2. In total the difference in performance between the image features may still lay in the problem of
loss of information during the transformation. But no better way to quantify this was found.
On the other hand the Reconstruction Error did measure a difference between the different image fea-
tures. Because if we can’t reconstruct the original time series, then we can’t use all the information in
the time series to solve the problem either. This was mostly meant as a way to measure if the CNN’s
could decode the generated images. But in a way this is also a measure of the loss of information in the
transformation, because if too much information was lost it would be impossible to reconstruct the time
series. An example of this is again the MTF, where we know there is a large loss of information, and
likewise its reconstruction error is relatively bad. In general what we found was that the more simple
way you could represent the time series as an image, the better the performance of the overall method.
This is likely because the CNN’s itself is powerful enough to create arbitrarily complex features from
the simplest generated image, and therefore no need to do any complex, even if very useful, image
feature. This is also our answer to why the kernel inspired methods in general performed worse. In a
normal application of a kernel like a Support Vector Machine, the Kernel is the driving force behind
the complexity of the features used. But this complexity is not needed with the CNN, and is actually
an hindrance. In total what we found is that you want a very simple image generation technique for
the best performance of the overall method.
Another part of the method we tested was the CNN. We assumed, as is the case for all deep learn-
ing problems, that the architecture of the model does have a large impact on performance. The testing
of the pre-built CNN’s showed exactly this, with the Vgg11 performing worse than the deeper models.
At some point the deeper CNN’s did reach a plateau in performance improvement, showing that model
complexity been saturated for the problem. Our own model designed specifically for this problem and
method did perform 6% better than the pre-built. This speaks for one of the main idea behind the over-
all method does hold. In that you can use CNN architectures, made for normal image recognition tasks,
and apply them to a time series problem, using this image generation method. And the results achieved
will be near optimal, for this method. This also gives the possibility of the method being improved by
incorporating more and newer computer vision techniques. Another thing we found, was that batch
normalization did not work for the generated images. Our hypothesis was that the generated images
made the overall method a ”noise-sensitive application” as described in [SK16]. This means that the
extra noise added by batch normalisation which normally aids the stochastic gradient descent, instead
causes the training to be slower and or give a worse result. While we can’t prove that this is the cause,
we can be quite sure the problem exists since there was no problem when we applied our baseline FNN
which also uses batch normalization. It must also be taken into account that this ”problem” was found
using only one dataset, although using multiple different CNN’s. Our solution for our own CNN was
to instead use Instance normalisation, which seemed to solve the problem. In total the CNN does have
an impact on the overall method performance, and depending on the application the answer might be

to find the best performing pre-built CNN, instead of optimizing your own.
The last part of the method we tested was about the sizes of the generated images. Our results show,
as assumed that larger image sizes, and thereby more information, improve performance at the cost
of training and computation speed. This assumption has held for most machine learning methods in
general. The purpose of testing it serves more as a sanity check, since the original papers seemed to
randomly choose their image sizes.
The method of the original papers for standardizing the size of the time series using PAA, had some
large problems as shown in figure 2.5. Our solution of using linear interpolation for both up and down-
scaling seems to be the answer to these problems. Both visually and in terms of performance its large
improvements. There might still be some merit to padding as an up-scaling method, assuming you are
not doing any down-scaling. This is because the interpolation can change the frequencies of the time
series, which might be important to hold constant in some applications. For both ways of down-scaling
we have looked at it will change the frequencies not matter what, and therefore the preferred option
should just be to use linear interpolation for everything, if you have to down-scale.
Using all the small improvements as discussed, we created our model which can be seen in figure 4.5.
While this achieved results better than any single improvement, it did not improve by the sum of all the
improvement. This speaks for that there was some overlap in the problem solved. This is most likely
in the problem of loss of information. Be that in the image transformation, or the image size, or length
standardization method. Exactly this overlap was also why we only use two of our seven best image
features. We did do a forward feature selection to get the best combination for the simulation data-set,
but it is still not necessarily the best selection for all types of problems. Clearly we have found the
simple ways of encoding a time series into an image is the best. But beyond that its not known what
combination of features is exactly the best for a given problem. Even our feature selection does lag in
that we didn’t run it through fully, because of the computation time needed. In [WO15], they saw a
performance improvement when using multiple features, as we did. But this is probably because of the
loss of information in the transformations used. If a better simple image feature could be constructed,
there might not be any need to use multiple features at all.
Looking at the results of the TSR benchmark, we see that our model performs in the top of the state-of-
the-art models, and statistically on par with most of them. This means that there is definitely merit to
the method of transforming time series into images for the problem of TSR. This mirrors the results of
[WO15], [DHG18] and [CS19], that similar at the time had results on par for time series classification.
The main positives of this method, is that you can take improvements of the computer vision field to
improve on a time series task. And it is relatively easy to set up using a pre-built CNN to achieve near
optimal performance. The main downside of the method is that it works on images instead of directly
on the time series. This does mean it scales worse with longer time series or with more dimensions.
Our only solution to this is to scale the time series to a shorter length, which is a loss of information.
Which when the loss of information becomes large enough means, that other methods will outperform.
One such method is Rocket which the only method outperforming in the benchmark. Because of its
use of random 1D convolutions [DPW20], its computation time only increases linearly with the length
of the time series, and linearly in space consumption. In comparison our model during training will
scale polynomial in space consumption since derivatives are needed in relation to all pixels of the NxN
image, generated from the time series of length N. This constraint is why we use a maximal image size
of 256 for the benchmark, and also why some pre-built models could not fit on the 16 GB memory of
the GPU used. Larger cluster of GPUs can of course alleviate the problem for some type, but given
long enough time series or enough data dimension you will still run into the same problem. In total our
model achieves on par with state-of-the-art, and is very viable for our main Road Condition Assessment
task, since the data here is limited in length as it is bound by the length of the road segment and the
speed of the car.

6.2 Discussion
56
In the introduction we made multiple arguments for our choice of tackling the road condition problem.
While the arguments for our classification of problem still stand, we can now examine if the way we
chose to tackle the TSR problem was wise. Our arguments was that given enough data the deep learn-
ing models would out perform the classic models. This is clearly the case on the both the simulation
data-set and the dataset from the Danish Road Department(DRD). For the combined dataset this is
not the case, but here the premise of the argument falter, since 2200 time series is clearly not enough
data. We chose to use a CNN base approached, instead of an RNN based approach, because we are
doing TSR and not forecasting. While this argument has some merit in general, there still might be
RNN based methods that can rival or even exceed ours. We chose to use our method of transforming
a time series into an image, mostly by the argument that 2D CNN’s in generally perform better than
their 1D counterpart. While we have not explicitly tested this assumption, the results of the benchmark
as well as performance comparison between our model and the baseline 1D CNN, confirms that this is
a viable option.
So did we solve the road condition assessment problem?
While largest part of the thesis has been about improving our chosen model, the result on both the
simulation data as well as the DRD data shows that our method is definitely is viable for the road
condition assessment problem. For the simulation data we achieve a RMSE of 0.0101 m, which is about
twice the minimum difference in the simulated target variable. This means in the simulated data, given
the z-acceleration for a 2 meter segment with a defect in the road, our model can predict that maximal
defect height/depth in that segment with an average error of 1.01 cm. This is with simulated errors
spanning 0.5 cm to 30 cm. While our model might not be able to recognize the difference between two
similar defects, it will definitely be able to detect any major defect. For the DRD data our model has a
RMSE of 0.7083 IRI. This should compared to highways having 1 IRI for the good sections and 5 IRI
for the bad, and city roads upward of 30 IRI some places. In this case again our model will be able
to asses whether there is any major error in a road segment. Now for the real problem of using the
Green mobility data to predict the IRI values given by the Danish Road Department, our results are
not conclusive. The main reason is the amount data available, it just not enough yet. But the results
from the simulation and from using only the DRD data shows promise, that with more data the road
condition assessment problem will be achievable.

6.3 Conclusion
57
6.2.1
Future Work
This thesis has mostly been focused on our chosen model, and on improving it. Therefore while the
LiRA project as a whole has a lot of future work on the Road Condition Assessment problem, we will
focus our thought on what we have done.
We have made a lot of test of different models and different model configurations. In all of these test
and experiments we have used the simulation data set for training, validation and testing. The results
of these test and experiments were vital in how the final model is designed but due to the test only
being done on one data set we can not be sure that the results and the conclusions drawn from that
hold in general. It might be possible that some of the results are depending on some aspect of the
simulation dataset that is not present in another dataset originating from some other setting. That
being said we achieve good results on both the DRD dataset and in the benchmark tests indicating that
the general approach is working. In future work one could further study parts of the method, like if
you can find an even better image generation methods, or further investigate the different methods and
isolate how each method perform in different situations. We have for example found the MTF method
to perform very poorly but it is not impossible that it could perform better in another setting with
different data. Such a study will likely be able to find a version of our model that is more general have
a better performance across different data sets.
A technique that is often used to improve performance of 2D-CNN’s is data augmentation. This is
even more worth when you have a small amount of data, like our combined dataset. And while our
generated images does not have the invariance to rotation, mirroring, cropping or other standard data
augmentation techniques, it could be possible to use techniques on the time series before transforma-
tion. Things such as re-sampling through interpolation[OHJ20] and other techniques can be found in
literature[Wen+20].
Another way to improve on the smaller dataset is transfer learning. The idea here is that we could
have pre-trained the model on either the simulation dataset or the DRD dataset, before fine-tuning it
for the combined dataset. Thereby the CNN might learn the patterns of the generated images off the
large dataset, and then apply that knowledge on the smaller dataset. While this would work best if all
the datasets had the same input variables, which is not possible for us, it might still be able to learn
patterns introduced by the image transformations functions using other variables.
6.3
Conclusion
The main problem considered is the road condition assessment problem, which was approached using
deep learning methods. A method of utilizing 2D-CNN models on time series data by using transfor-
mation functions was explored. This allowed the use of powerful pre-built 2D-CNN architectures to be
used. This method was experimented on in multiple ways. Including the exact transformation tech-
nique, the CNN used, and how you standardize the length of the time series. Each experiment seeing an
improvement to the overall method. In total the experiments improved the performance of the method
by 38.4 % over the baseline version of the method, using the simulation data set. The considered model
was applied to a series of benchmark datasets, where the model achieved a good performance with only
one other model achieving a higher overall score. In applying the 2D-CNN method on data from the
Danish Road Directorate a good performance was achieved with an error rate of 0.7 RMSE IRI, which
is enough to predict errors in the road. The results on the combined data from Green Mobility and
the Danish Road department was inconclusive because of the low amount of data. The results for the
other datasets gives credence to the assumption that our method can solve it for the combined dataset,
given more data. Overall a good performance was seen from the approach of using 2D-CNN’s on time
series, with this model achieving better performance than the considered 1D-CNN.
