Abstract
Plasma-facing components (PFCs) in fusion reactors of the future must withstand extremely
high temperatures, which demand excellent thermal stability of the microstructure.
In this work, the microstructural evolution of two promising tungsten alloys have been inves-
tigated with respect to recovery, recrystallization, and grain growth: 1) Severely deformed
cold-rolled potassium doped tungsten, subjected to annealing treatments at temperatures be-
tween 800 ◦C and 1400 ◦C. 2) Tungsten-carbide reinforced tungsten subjected to annealing
treatments at temperatures between 1400 ◦C and 1550 ◦C.
The recovery process taking place in potassium doped tungsten sheets have investigated. A
strong and increasingly stronger rotated cube texture ({001}<110>) has been found. The
grain structure consists of a high fraction of low angle boundaries undertaking growth along
the normal to the rolling direction, until boundaries gets pinned by the potassium. Abnor-
mal grain growth (secondary recrystallization) have been observed, possibly due to potassium
loss.
The tungsten-carbide reinforced tungsten has obtained and maintained a random dispersion
of second-phase grains, and a random overall texture through all annealing treatments. The
alloy exhibit good microstructural stability with only limited hardness decrease after 102
days annealing at 1550 ◦C.

Introduction and Background
1
Introduction and Background
In the 1930s, physicist Ernest Rutherford and later his assistant Mark Oliphant showed the
world what tremendous eﬀect fusion of deuterium into helium would create. Ever since sci-
entists have been intrigued by the idea of creating a sun on earth, namely a fusion power
reactor which has potential as a limitless source of clean energy [6]. Till today a wide range
of reactor designs have been proposed for harnessing the fusion process. One of the most
promising being the tokamak type reactor, conceptualized in the USSR by physicists Igor
Tamm and Andrei Sakharov. It is a so called magnetic conﬁnement approach where hot
plasma is contained in a torus shaped magnetic ﬁeld restricting contact with the solid con-
tainers. And it is these containers or rather the ﬁrst inner wall of the container which is of
interest in this study [7] [6].
The road to fusion power has so far been long, packed with ups and downs and will undoubt-
edly be even longer, at least until several challenges has been resolved. In case of the tokamak
design, one of these challenges being the thermal stability of the plasma facing components
(PFC). With the extreme conditions where plasma temperatures reach 100 million degrees
Celsius and the ﬁrst wall be subjected to peak heat ﬂuxes up to 7MW/m2. It requires high
demands of the performance of the PFCs [6] [8].
For this tungsten has received a lot of attention – and rightfully. Tungsten has the highest
melting temperature of all metals, good thermal conductivity, high sputtering threshold and
high strength at high temperatures. Tungsten is mostly known as being the material with
the highest melting point (3422◦C). Application wise tungsten is known from the ﬁlaments
in old style light bulbs, a critical alloying element in high speed cutting tools as well as var-
ious high temperature application. tungsten carbides (eq. WC or W2C) are widely used in
jewellery and in coatings for machine tools due to its high hardness and wear resistance [5] [9].
However several challenges remain before tungsten is applicable in fusion reactors for PFC.
The potential PFC should withstand extreme heat loads and heat bursts from the plasma.
Tungsten suﬀers from embrittlement due to recrystallization wherefore ongoing research is in-
vestigating ways of retarding or slowing down the recrystallization of tungsten. This research
investigates two diﬀerent tungsten alloys, their resistance to various annealing phenomena,
and their microstructural evolution during annealing at high temperatures.

Tungsten as plasma facing material rises some technological challenges. One is related to the
intrinsic brittleness of tungsten with a high ductile to brittle transition temperature (DBTT)
originating from its BCC structure, and the intrinsic lack of closed packed planes. At low
temperatures tungsten behaves in a brittle manner. This is mainly due to a high activation
energy for glide of screw dislocations.
Deformation in tungsten is predominantly carried
out by motion of screw dislocations. In tungsten screw dislocations dissociate into partial
dislocations in order to minimise their energy. The partial dislocations have lower mobility as
they cannot cross-slip. In order for the screw dislocations to be able to slip thermal activation
is needed [11],[12].
Thus the brittleness disappears above a certain transition temperature (more correctly a
range of temperatures). The DBTT depends on mechanical, structural and chemical condi-
tions [5]. But the brittleness for polycrystalline tungsten is also attributed to the weakness
of the grain boundaries, which causes crack initiation of deformed as well as recrystallized
tungsten [13]. Failure by inter-granular fracture has shown to be the most common type of
failure for tungsten [13].
A study by Allen et al. [14] compared the ductility of single crystal and polycrystal tungsten
at room temperature. It was found that the presence of grain boundaries in polycrystalline
tungsten had an embrittling eﬀect. It is however not certain which role impurities at the
grain boundaries play in this manner. A general belief is that segregation of impurities at
grain boundaries weakens the grain boundaries, thus increasing the amount of inter-granular
fracture and embrittlement of tungsten. On the other hand, inter-granular fracture have also
been reported in high purity tungsten where impurities was not observed on the fractured

2.2.1
Potassium Doped Tungsten Sheets
Potassium doping is well known from manufacturing of tungsten wires for the lighting indus-
try. The doping is responsible for improvement of both, mechanical and thermal properties.
The potassium segregates as bubbles during sintering. The potassium bubbles predominately
situate themselves at grain boundaries where they pin the boundaries and thereby postpone
recrystallization [16], [17]. However there is a risk that these bubbles may grow and result
in voids due to diﬀusion of potassium [18]. In potassium-doped wires, the potassium forms a
long, elongated tube-like structure during deformation. At subsequent annealing they form
into series of aligned bubbles. This diﬀer somewhat for rolling, due to the plane-strain defor-
mation, potassium will form more disc-shaped structure rather than tube shaped structures.
The tungsten potassium alloy for this study has been cold-rolled. When rolling, the thickness
of the plate is reduced by passing the metal through a series of rolling steps. This will plas-
tically deform the metal whilst reducing its thickness, increasing its length and width (the
increase in width is only minor and usually neglected). Depending of the temperature, rolling
is termed cold, warm or hot-rolling. Cold if it is performed under the DBTT, warm above the
DBTT and hot rolled if it is performed above the recrystallization temperature (which is not
applied for tungsten) [19]. The principle of the potassium doping is that the bubbles will act

2.3
Deformation Structure
When a metal is plastically deformed, some of the work it is subjected to become stored in
the metal. Most of the work is quickly released as heat, however some of the energy gets
stored in the microstructure by increased defect density, approximately 1 %. Of this 1 % most
of it can be attributed to the accumulation of dislocations, as point defects do not contribute
signiﬁcantly. The dislocation density of tungsten varies between 1012m−2 and 1016m−2. The
increased dislocation density is the fundamental reason for any property change metals ob-
tain after deformation. An increased dislocation content is responsible for strengthening by
hindering dislocation motion by dislocation accumulation [5] [2].
Tungsten, similar to other bcc metals, primarily deforms by slip. The stacking fault energy
(SFE) of Tungsten is high; γSFE = 0.23 eV/˚A2 = 3.68 J/m2. This is the reason that twin-
ning is only rarely observed in Tungsten. Dislocation dissociation into stacking faults is not
possible as SFE is too high. [5] [21].
Slip in BCC metals takes place in the most densely packed directions < 111 >, and on the 3
plane families {110},{122} and {123}. The most favourable oriented slip system experiences
glide accommodating the deformation.
Dislocation accumulation is however not directly responsible for all of the stored energy in
the microstructure. During deformation of a polycrystalline metal the grains changes shape
in order to accommodate the macroscopic shape change. This results in an increased grain
boundary area. It is however related to the generation of dislocations as the retention of
the overall grain volume demands that this increased grain boundary area is accommodated
by the generation of dislocations from plastic deformation. The associated energy with the
boundary area increase makes out a signiﬁcant part of the stored energy, greater for cold
worked metals than warm worked and greater for small grains and large strains, due to the
deformation induced grain boundaries [2].
Furthermore, during deformation the microstructure undergo several changes. As explained
initially the grain shapes changes according to the macroscopic strain.
But secondly it’s
important to consider the internal structure within the grains, which may take form of en-
tangled dislocations, cells, or subgrains depending on the strain. At low strains dislocations

by dislcocation movement during deformation, whereas dislocation subgrains forms as a con-
sequence of dynamic-recovery. With increasing strain, cell structures starts to form. Cell
structures are dislocation structures consisting of regions with high dislocation density whilst
other regions are dislocation free. The walls of the cells are rather thick due to the accumu-
lation up of many redundant dislocations with opposite burgers vector (dislocation dipoles)
and consequently there is no or only very little misorientation between the cells (generally the
orientation diﬀerence between cells are below 2◦. The misorientation tends to increase with
increasing strain as cell walls becomes increasingly sharper). Deformation at higher strain
will also lead to dynamic recovery in which the cells gradually evolve towards continuously
sharper cells walls consisting of ordered dislocation arrays by means of even further disloca-
tion rearrangement and annihilation. The cells are now termed subgrains, where dislocations
arrange on top of each other in order to screen each others stress ﬁelds and thereby creating
a more thermodynamically stable state. This type of dislocation rearrangement occurs as a
consequence of deformation, termed dynamic recovery or due to thermal activation, termed
static recovery [2].
2.4
Annealing and Related Phenomena
The overall annealing processes referred to in this study are: recovery, recrystallization and
grain growth. Annealing in general is a heat treatment which can change the microstructure
in various ways. The following sections describe the mechanisms behind these three types
of microstructural changes. The overall driving force for both recovery, recrystallization and
grain growth are reduction of the stored energy in the deformation structure, although the
mechanisms by which they do this are diﬀerent. The microstructure can not undergo recov-
ery if recrystallization has already taken place. Although, if a signiﬁcant amount of recovery
has taken place, the driving force for recrystallization will be lower and the recrystallization
will occur with a lower rate. Recovery and recrystallization can therefore be described as
competing processes [2].

2.5
Recovery
Recovery is an annealing processes. As the term suggest, recovery refers to a microstructural
change which partially restores the properties prior to deformation. The primary driving
force for recovery is the reduction of the internal stored energy from deformation structures.
(Although recovery is not limited to plastically deformed materials.) [2].
There are 5 steps of the microstructural changes during recovery after deformation as illus-
trated in Figure 2.2. Usually these occur in this speciﬁc order allthough a signiﬁcant overlap
may take place. Whether all or only some of these microstructural changes will occur depend
on parameters such as the material, its purity, strain rate, deformation temperature, and
temperature during annealing.
Figure 2.2: Stages of recovery [2]
The steps in Figure 2.2 illustrates how the structure goes from the initially deformed state
with dislocation tangles through increasingly lower energy conﬁgurations, forming increas-
ingly ﬁner cell walls. The mechanism by which these 5 steps are able to take place is through
following processes:
1. Dislocation annihilation
2. Polygonization
3. Subgrain growth

4. Subgrain coalescence
Point defect annihilation is not limited to annealing, in fact it it also takes place at room
temperature and below although at a higher rate at elevated temperatures. Dislocation an-
nihilation and polygonization (rearrangement of dislocations) are the two primary processes.
Annihilation of dislocations requires that two dislocations of opposite Burgers vector meet,
for edges dislocations this is possible by dislocation glide and/or climb and for screw dis-
location this is possible by cross-slip. Polygonization is the re-arrangement of dislocations,
and describes how dislocations will rearrange in order to reduce their internal energy. This
is accomplished be screening each others stress ﬁelds, giving a more thermodynamic stable
state. One could describe it as a stacking of dislocations. Both dislocation annihilation and
polygonization is possible due to thermally activated dislocation glide, climb and cross-slip.
This corresponds typically to one third of the melting point. Dislocation annihilation and
polygonization are the most prominent and eﬀective processes during recovery, where the
combined eﬀect of dislocation annihilation and polygonization leads to formation of cells
with continuously sharper cell walls and eventually to the formation of subgrains [2].
Besides polygonization and point defect annihilation there are subgrain growth and subgrain
coalescence. The driving force for subgrain growth is the boundary energy. Reduction of
grain boundary area reduces the stored energy. However the growth of subgrains, is rather
small due to low mobility of low angle boundaries[2].
The ﬁnal step of recovery is referred to as subgrain coalescence. It covers the concept that
two neighbouring subgrains might rotate towards an average orientation and by that grow
into one larger grain. However, evidence of subgrain coalescence have been reported in several
studies, but the proof that subgrain coalescence is not just a thin foil eﬀect is not established
yet [22], [23], [24].
The governing fundamental mechanisms which usually control the rate of recovery is the
ability of dislocations to glide, climb and cross slip.
The latter makes the stacking fault
energy (γSFE) one of the most important material parameters with regards to recovery, as
it aﬀects the extent to which dislocations dissociate, which determines the rate of climb and
cross slip. Low stacking fault energy makes dislocation cross slip diﬃcult - which is why only
limited recovery is observed in metals as austenitic stainless steel and copper [2].

2.6
Recrystallization
As for recovery the driving force for recrystallization is the reduction in stored energy from the
deformation structures as well as the recovered microstructure. But it diﬀers from recovery
by its mechanisms. Namely, recrystallization is deﬁned as being the generation of new defect
free grains by movement of high angle boundaries.
Recrystallization is a three step process given by:
1. Nucleation
2. Growth
3. Impingement
It can be deﬁned as a restoration process in which generation and growth of new perfectly
ordered strain-free grains consume the deformed or recovered microstructure by migration of
high angle boundaries. Figure 2.3 shows a typical evolution of the recrystallized fraction.
Figure 2.3: Typical recrystallization kinetics during isothermal annealing. [2]
The evolution of recrystallization at isothermal annealing can be explained through the three
diﬀerent process steps; initially new defect free grains nucleate. However they are small in
volume and initially only few of them have boundaries which are able to migrate, thus the
rate of recrystallization is low in the beginning. At some point the recrystallized fraction

Some caution should be taken when describing the nucleation of new recrystallized grains.
The terms ”nucleation” and ”nuclei” are chosen for intuitive reasons, but should not be

the deformed microstructure. This was initially described by Cahn [28]. This also indicates
that there is a critical nuclei size (Equation 2.18) which must be fulﬁlled before growth is

• A minimum amount of deformation is needed to initiate recrystallization

2.6.1
Recrystallization of Two-phase Alloys
Many metals contain dispersed particles either present during deformation or precipitating
during annealing. Thus for this study some understanding of the recrystallization behaviour
of two-phase materials is essential.
Recrystallization of second-phase particles is aﬀected in several ways. Overall there are two

recrystallization promoting eﬀects. (i) The stored energy for recrystallization might increase
due to the particles. And (ii) large second-phase particles can act as nucleation sites. On the
other hand there is one main recrystallization hindering eﬀect that is due to the pinning of
particles on both low- and high-angle boundaries.
The ﬁnal outcome depends on which of the stated eﬀects is dominating, which in turn de-
pends on the volume fraction, size, shape and spacing of the particles.
During deformation, the microstructure is often highly aﬀected by particles. Particles in-
ﬂuence the overall dislocation density. Particles may cause an heterogeneous deformation.
Particles aﬀect the deformation structure in their vicinity possibly resulting in particle stim-
ulated nucleation.
Based on studies on copper and aluminium alloys it was found that an alloy that showed
retarded recrystallization after small strains may show accelerated recrystallization after large
strains.
This is possible as the driving pressure for recrystallization increases due to the
particles, while the pinning pressure remains constant, and thus the driving force becomes
higher compared to a single-phase material.
Several studies have shown that recrystallization was retarded or even inhibited by closely
spaced thermally stable particles. Having a suﬃciently small particle spacing it is possible to
preserve the deformed or recovered microstructure. The eﬀect of particles exerting a pressure
on low- or high- angle boundaries is known as the Smith-Zener drag (Smith [30]).

2.6.3
Particle-stimulated Nucleation of Recrystallization
Some studies of alloys with small and closely spaced particles have shown that the cell or
subgrain structure close to the particles is greatly aﬀected resulting in less misoriented cells
or subgrains [31].
It is generally established that nucleation will not take place in regions with uniform orien-
tation (i.e. at cells or subgrains), but rather at microstructural heterogenities such as HAB,
deformation bands, shear bands, and deformation zones near large particles, in other words;
regions related to large strain gradients.
Particle stimulated nucleation will only take place if the deformation beforehand is carried
out below a critical temperature or above a critical strain rate, this is because the distribu-
tion of dislocations and particles is temperature dependent. In many cases only one grain
nucleates at a particle. But there are evidence that multiple nucleation may occur at very

2.6.4
Recrystallization in Presence of Pores and Gas Bubbles
Gas bubbles exert a pinning eﬀect on boundaries similar to that of second-phase particles.
They tend to retard recrystallization, they might be draggged by the moving boundary. From
a study by Thomsen-Russell (1974) it was shown that the bubble dispersion was reﬁned with
increasing strain and thereby increasing the recrystallization temperature.
For alloys where particles, or in this case gas bubble may not be randomly distributed, but
rather concentrated in bands it is shown that the average pinning pressure is greatly increased
normal to the bands. This may be observed from the shape of grains or subgrains as illustrated

Recrystallization as described in previous section is also often more speciﬁcally termed pri-

abnormal grain growth). Either normal grain growth or secondary recrystallization might
occur in fully recrystallized materials. After primary recrystallization, the microstructure is
still not thermodynamically stable. There still exists a driving force for reduction in stored
energy - at this point the stored energy is only that of the grain boundarie, often the driving
force is about two orders of magnitude less than that for primary recrystallization, typically

Grain growth may be divided into continuous grain growth and discontinuous grain growth.
For continuous grain growth, termed normal grain growth, the grains coarsens in a uniformly

only a few grains grow rapidly on the expense of smaller grains. This results in a bimodal
grain size distribution. Eventually the large grains will impinge on each other and normal

Scanning Electron Microscopy
Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) has been utilized to obtain micrographs of the tungsten
alloys.
For this, the TableTop SEM Phenom has been used.
All micrographs has been
obtained with an acceleration voltage of 15kV. For both, WK80 and W/W2C, samples have
been prepared according to Table 3.6. In a SEM, it is possible to obtain images of the sample.
This is achieved by scanning of a focused electron beam of high-energy electrons across the
surface. The electrons interact with the atoms in the sample. Depending on the type of
interaction, diﬀerent signals are coming out and diﬀerent information is obtained. In SEM,
one diﬀerentiates between elastic or inelastic scattering. Elastic scattering of the incident
beam produces back scattered electrons (BSEs).
Inelastic scattering produces secondary
electrons (SEs). The interaction volume (the volume in which the electrons are generated or
interact and still are able to escape the sample) is small (5 −50nm) for SEs and detection
of those gives good topographical contrast, on the contrary to BSEs producing images of

high elemental composition contrast and grain orientation contrast from deeper regions (up
to 300 nm). The compositional contrast arises due to capability of the BSEs to escape from
the specimen depending on the atomic number.
Higher atomic numbers results in more
BSEs escaping. Thus regions with higher atomic number will appear brighter in greyscale
images.
However contrasts can arise from crystallographic orientation as well due to the
channelling eﬀect of the electron beam. The amount of BSEs that are able to escape depends
on the orientation of the crystallographic planes to the incident electron beam. Electrons
will penetrate deeper into the crystal, when the atomic packing density is low, the packing
density varies with the crystallographic plane. Thus areas where the electron penetration
is high will appear darker [4]. This is the type of contrast that is most signiﬁcant for the
micrographs of this work as no compositional contrast (or only minor) can be expected. The
Tabletop SEM used detects BSEs and not SEs which is suﬃcient as we are are not interested
in surface topography, but the microstructure in the particular grain structure .

Discussion on Experimental Work
Annealing treatments
Temperature distribution in the furnace might have inﬂuenced the annealing itself - as the
temperature is not constant through out the entire furnace. The distance to the thermostat
has in previous project been identiﬁed such that correct placement was possible [26].
Regarding the high temperature furnace used for annealing treatments of W/W2C it was
only possible to place one sample in the center of the furnace at the time. The temperature
distribution along the furnace tube is not known. The sample annealed for 30 days where
performed simultaneous with the 102 days and thus placed 90 mm (length of one ceramic
boat) away from the correct placement in the furnace. The values obtained from the W/W2C
sample annealed for 30 days should therefore be regarded more qualitatively than as an actual
quantitative value as the exact annealing temperature is not known.
Heterogeneity
Some factors may have inﬂuenced the ﬁnal results of this work. It is reasonable to expect
that the rolled sheets to some extent have a heterogeneous microstructure in its as deformed
state. This originates from the processing route e.g. from rolling having an nonuniform load
distribution, which would result in diﬀerent deformation thus work hardnening on diﬀerent
regions of the sheet. This initial diﬀerence in the as received state will aﬀect the annealing,
inherently inducing variations in the measured the results.
Sample cutting
It is likely that cutting of the samples have introduced work hardening on the RD/ND sec-
tion. In order to ensure as little or no deformation layer, preparation was performed in steps
with gradually ﬁner grit sizes ﬁnishing with OPS polishing.
Sample cutting also showed tendencies to induce delamination of the samples. All samples
used for the isochronal annealing treatment had been machine cut. It was chosen to apply
EDM cutting for possibly reducing the amount of delamination. Whether EDM cutting has
reduced the delamination of the samples has not been clear. The eﬀect of delamination was
taken into account when hardness measurements where performed why it was choosen to
keep a distance of 1.5 mm to the edge of the specimens.

Discussion on WK80
From the hardness plots presented in Figure 4.1 and Figure 4.2 it is clear that some or several
thermal activated processes takes place during the annealing causing the defect density in
the microstructure to decrease, and thus a decrease in hardness is measured. For the 2 h
isochronal annealing treatment the evolution in hardness was initially expected to originate
from static recovery described by Kuhlmann’s Type 1 recovery kinetics - as presented in
Section 2.5.1. From the linear recovery ﬁtting the activation energy related constant, A, was
found to be −0.157 K−1. This value give some indication of the recovery kinetics. Values for
the activation energy related constant has been obtained in previous project for (non-doped)
rolled tungsten for varying deformation rates [26]. Values was ranging between −0.103 K−1
and −0.269 K−1. None of these samples from the previous study has been deformed to the
same extent as the potassium doped sheets in this work. Solely based on the evolution in
hardness it may therefore be concluded that the potassium doping in fact has an diminishing
eﬀect on the recovery rate.
In order to get a better understanding of the actual processes taking place during annealing,
closer investigation of the microstructure has been executed. Analysis of the texture evolu-
tion, the misorientation and the grainsize evolution indicates that the recovery taking place
might consist of more than depletion of defects within the grains.
The following listed points are ﬁndings on the microstructural behaviour which will be dis-
cussed. It has been found that:
• Hardness does not change signiﬁcant during the various isothermal annealing treatments
(unless abnormal grain growth takes place)
• The grains grow primarily along ND during the isochronal annealing treatment and
primarily along RD during the isothermal annealing treatment
• The samples have a strong rotated cube texture in the as received state, which be-
comes even stronger during the isochronal annealing treatment. The volume fraction of
the rotated cube texture component does not change signiﬁcant during the isothermal
annealing treatment.
• The average misorientation descreases during the isochronal annealing treatment, but
does not change signiﬁcant during the isothermal annealing treatment.

Discussion on WK80
It is already stated that the hardness drop during the isochronal annealing treatment is due
to recovery related phenonema. However, if one only considers the microstructure obtained
with SEM, it could be misinterpreted as recrystallization or at least a mixture between re-
covery and recrystallization. The structure observed already after 2 h annealing at 1300 ◦C
is remarkable similar to what one would suspect to see from recrystallization, with gradually
more equiaxed grains. The results from EBSD changes this picture. Solely from the phase
maps showing misorientations it is clear that the microstructure consists of many LABs and
the average misorientation only decreases during the isochronal annealing treatment. This
does not ﬁt with recrystallization as it requires migration of high angle boundaries. What
is interesting to observe regarding texture at this part of the evolution, is the rather slight
change in the overall texture. The the initial texture consists primarily of a rotated cube
texture component, which during the isochronal annealing only increases in density. This is a
clear indication that recrystallization is not taking place at this stage. First of all, recrystal-
lization of the rotated cube component is expected to recrystallize into texture components
related to rotations around < 110 > [39]. This would result in rather weak textures with
densities close to random.
However the analysis of evolution in the linear intercept length together with the average mis-
orientation indicates that the thermally activated process may be deﬁned as subgrain growth
i.e. extended recovery. The extended recovery will lower the stored energy in the microstruc-
ture even further, and thereby suppressing recrystallization to even higher temperatures or
longer annealing times. Subgrain growth continues all through the 2 h isochronal annealing,
and for the isothermal 1300 ◦C until 96 h. However the nature of the growth changes between
the isochronal and isothermal annealing treatment. This may be observed in the change of
the aspect ratio, presented in Figure 4.51.
During the isochronal annealing treatment growth is primarily happening along ND whereas
the aspect ratio decreases until it is almost equiaxed at 1300 ◦C. From there on the aspect ra-
tio increases again, as growth along RD primarily takes place during the isothermal annealing
treatment. One explanation for this shift in the growth direction might be interpreted from
the potassium doping and the distribution of the potassium. As previously described in Sec-
tion 2.2.1 it is suspected that the potassium form into series of aligned bubbles. This would
imply that boundaries are only able to grow to a certain extent along ND until potassium

successfully starts pinning the boundaries. This oﬀcause depends on the spatial distribution
of the potassium. When the boundary pinning becomes eﬀectively, only growth along RD is
possible, either until enough thermal activation is reached for boundaries to migrate around
the potassium, or in the case of loss of potassium. The eﬀect from aligned bubbles of potas-
sium along RD would also explain the lamellar like grain structure observed in most of the
microstructures during isothermal annealing.
One may then ask, what exactly is the deﬁnition of this microstructural evolution? And this
is where there might exist a terminology problem. The microstructure evolution is somewhat
challenging to describe with classic terminology. It is unclear whether the microstructural
evolution can actually be described as subgrain growth. The common deﬁnition of subgrains,
is grains having misorientation below 15 ◦. However the original idea of subgrains was that of
having ’smaller’ grains consisting of low angle boundaries within larger grains, thus the term
subgrains. Those subgrains would be created during dynamic or static recovery as described
in previous sections. However the low angle boundaries observed in the boundary maps does
not originate from recovery processes, but from the rolling deformation creating the very
strong rotated cube texture, aligning all crystals and grains in the same direction with only
little misorientation between them.
One may choose to decscribe this growth phenonema in one or two ways: (1) One can choose
to stand strong on the deﬁnition of subgrains as ”all grains having LABs” then this could be
termed as extended recovery. (2) Otherwise one can choose to reject the deﬁnition of LAB
corresponding to subgrains, and instead solely deﬁne this phenomena as growth of low angle
boundaries.
The drop in hardness observed after 288 h of annealing may be described from the change in
microstructure; generally no diﬀerence is observed from the SEM images at 1300 ◦C or 1400
◦C annealing treatments. However, at 1350 ◦C after 336 h of annealing it is clear that some
extensive grain growth is taking place at and near the RD/TD surface, this is interpreted as an
early stage of abnormal grain growth (secondary recrystallization). Because the grain growth
is discontinuous and discontinuous grain growth requires that some grains exhibit growth
advantages compared to other grains. It is postulated that the abnormal grain growth is
caused and governed by potassium loss near the surface.

any deformation structure in the microstructures are not present. The thermally activated
process that the tungsten samples may undergo is grain growth driven by reduction of the
grain boundary area. The objective of the study of this alloy is therefore to investigate its

For the various annealing treatments the hardness of the samples has only been slightly af-

measured hardness is therefore deemed to be caused by varying microstructural state prior
to annealing or due to human error. The actual temperature of the sample annealed at 1550
◦C for 30 days is expected to be lower due to improper placement in the furnace, thus it is
expected that the hardness value would be somewhat lower if proper furnace placement had
been possible.
Grain Evolution
For both tungsten grain sizes and carbide grain sizes a signiﬁcant increase is observed from
the as received size. This is however not reﬂected in the hardness results, and thus raises
some questions. This could be interpreted as a condition for which the Hall-Petch relation-
ship does not have a big inﬂuence on the W/W2C samples. However conditions for which the
Hall-Petch relationship only have a small inﬂuence is normally limited to very large grains.
Furthermore, assesment of the microstructure from the micrographs obtained in SEM does
not support a signiﬁcant increase in grain size between the as received sample and the an-
nealed samples. It is therefore suspected that the EBSD information gathered for the as
received sample is not representative for the actual microstructure. In relation to this, it
should be mentioned that the as received EBSD map have been performed with half the map
size compared to the other annealing treatments. However, uncertainties remains as the grain
sizes of the W/W2C samples in general have been relative homogeneous. For future study it
is recommended to repeat the EBSD investigation of the as received state.

9
Conclusion
The aim of this work was to investigate the microstructural evolution due to thermally acti-
vated processes in two tungsten alloys being: 1) potassium doped tungsten and 2) tungsten
reinforced with tungsten semi carbide.
A successful procedure for preparation of the ND/RD section of 0.1 mm thick potassium
doped tungsten sheets have been obtained.
This include sheet cutting, electro-polishing,
proper embedding, mechanical and chemo-mechanical grinding and polishing applicable for
investigation with SEM and EBSD.
WK80
One 2 h isochronal annealing treatment has been performed and investigated with SEM and
EBSD. Three series of isothermal annealing treatments at respectively 1300 ◦C, 1350 ◦C, and
1400 ◦C has been performed, in which the isothermal series at 1300 ◦C has been investigated
applying EBSD.
Hardness measurements have been performed on the RD/TD on all annealed samples. The
measurement revealed decrease of hardness after recovery taking place during the isochronal
annealing treatment, as well as stabilization in hardness after the isothermal annealing treat-
ments.
The as received hardness of the severely deformed cold rolled tungsten sheets was 653 HV0.2.
After the isochronal annealing treatment the hardness drops. Initially during the isothermal
annealing treatment the hardness drops but stabilizes approximately at 520 HV0.2 to 530
HV0.2 similar to the isochroncal annealing treatment at 1300 ◦C for 2 h.
Linear recovery ﬁtting by the empirical relationship from Kuhlmann [25], indicated that ac-
tivation energy for recovery of the WK80 sample is lower than less deformed pure tungsten
investigated in previous study [26].
From in depth analysis of the microstructure applying SEM and EBSD it has been revealed
that migration of low angle boundaries initially takes place along ND until potassium pins
the boundaries and growth proceeds along RD.

Conclusion
Grain sizes have been described by the linear intercept method. Intercept lengths along ND
evolves from 0.3 µm at the as received state, and stabilizes around 0.9 µm from the 2 h
annealing treatment at 1300 ◦C. The intercept length along RD increases from 0.87 µm in
the as received state to 5.29 µm after 554 h annealing at 1300 ◦C with the dominating part
of the growth taking place during the isothermal annealing treatment.
Results from EBSD further revealed a very strong texture belonging to the rotated cube
orientation. The volume fraction of the rotated cube texture component increases from 67 %
in the as received state and reaches a maximum of ∼95% (measured by a 15◦misorientation
to the ideal orientation).
The average misorientation has been found to decrease during the isochronal annealing treat-
ment and stabilize during the isothermal annealing treatment. Hence correlating with type
of grain growth (along ND or RD respectively) and the increase of the density of rotated
cube texture component.
Abnormal grain growth was observed for three annealing treatments, which most likely have
been exposed to a deviant and unknown heating cycle. For which it is suspected that deple-
tion of potassium near the surface leads to the discontinuous grain growth.
The recovery process taking place may be deﬁned as a type of extended recovery i.e. grain
growth by migration of low angle boundaries.
For future study it is relevant to investigate the ductility and fracture toughness properties
of annealed WK80. As abnormal grain growth has been observed it is relevant to perform
annealing treatments at somewhat higher temperatures in order to obtain a better under-
standing of when and how this takes place. If the observations of signiﬁcant grain growth
near the edges after 336 h annealing treatment indeed is triggered by potassium loss, then
relevant annealing temperatures to study this phenomena may be around 1500 ◦C.
