Abstract
In 2001 large amplitude vibrations were observed in the vertical twin-hanger cables on the Great
Belt East Bridge in Denmark. The amplitude of the vibrations were close to 2 meter and investi-
gations showed that a possibility of ice accretion of the cables. This indicated that ice accretion
on bridge cables could cause or increase the magnitude of vibrations on cables, so that instability
could occur.
In this Master thesis, a two meter long cable section with identical cross section to the twin-hanger
cables from the Great Belt Bridge, were vertically investigated in a Climatic Wind Tunnel (CWT)
at FORCE Technology, Kgs. Lyngby, Denmark. The model was tested as a connected system,
where movement of one cable aﬀected the other cable directly. The tests was initially done in a
static test setup to acquire force coeﬃcients and afterwards dynamic tests were done to try and
reproduce large amplitude vibrations.
Ice accretion was made with a spray bar, designed to spray horizontal structures. The investigated
twin-cable was positioned vertical and the spray bar turned, which caused an uneven distributed,
with no ice in the top and a big ice coating at the bottom cable.
The goal of the static tests were to measure forces in the x and y direction, as well as moment
about the center line of the test setup. Tests were done for every 10◦from 0◦−360◦and for wind
velocities of 7 −29m
s , to calculate the force coeﬃcients for drag, lift and moment, which is the
parameters used in comparable literature.
The ice tests provided an increase in forces, compared with the dry tests, due to the change of mass
and volume.
The coeﬃcients were used to predict instability for the twin-cable system, by using two diﬀerent
Instability Models: The conventional 1-DOF Den Hartog Criterion (1932), using drag and lift, and
the DTU-developed 3-DOF Gjelstrup Model (2011) which also takes torsion, aerodynamic damp-
ing, stiﬀness and center oﬀset due to ice, into account. Both models predicted several instability
cases for the static tests.
The dynamic tests were used to try and reproduce instability, where it was predicted by the insta-
bility models. Unfortunately, the Instability Models mostly predicted instability for wind velocities
above 17m
s , which was higher than what could be tested, due to limits of the dynamic spring system.
Keywords:
Twin-hanger cables, Cable dynamics, Aerodynamics of hanger cables, Icing on bridge hanger cables,
Ice accretion, Torsional aerodynamics on cables, static and dynamic test

Sammenfatning
I 2001 blev der observeret vibrationer med stor amplitude p˚a de vertikale dobbelte hængerkabler
p˚a Storebæltsbroens østbro i Danmark. Amplituden var tæt p˚a 2 meter og undersøgelser viste
at der var sandsynlighed for at kablerne var dækket af et tyndt lag is. Dette indikerede at istil-
vækst p˚a brokabler kan forsage eller forøge amplituden p˚a kablernes vibration, hvorved instabilitet
forekomme.
I dette kandidatprojekt blev en to meter lang dobbeltkabelsektion, identisk med kablerne p˚a
Storebæltsbroen, undersøgt i en vertikal forsøgsopstilling i en klimatisk vindtunnel (CWT) hos
FORCE Technology, Kgs. Lyngby, Danmark. Modelen blev testet som et samlet system, hvor
bevægelse af det foreste kabel direkte p˚avirkede det bagerste.
Først blev modellen testet i en
statisk setup, for at ﬁnde kraftkoeﬃcienter og derefter blev dynamiske test udført for at forsøge at
vibrationer med stor amplitude.
Isdannelse blev lavet med en spraybar, produceret til at tilise horisontale konstruktioner.
Dobbelthængermodellen var konstrueret vertikalt og spray baren blev derfor vendt, hvilket re-
sulterede i at isen blive ulige fordelt over kabellængden, med meget is i bunden og nærmest intet i
toppen.
M˚alet med de statiske tests var at m˚ale de krafter i x og y, samt momentet om den vertikale cen-
terakse, som p˚avirkede modellen. Det blev gjort for hver 10◦fra 0◦−360◦og for vindhastigheder
fra 7 −29m
s , for at beregne kraftkoeﬃcienter for træk, løft og moment, hvilke er parametre sam-
menlignelige med andet litteratur.
Testene med islag havde øgede kraftp˚avirkning i forhold til test uden is, fordi b˚ade masse og volu-
men af systemet blev øget.
Kraftkoeﬃcienterne blev brugt til at forudse instabilitet for dobbeltkabelsystemet, ved brug af to
instabilitetsmodeller: Det traditionelle 1-DOF Den Hartog Kriterie (1932) og den DTU-udviklede
3-DOF Gjelstrup Model (2011), som ogs˚a medtager vridning, aerodynamisk dæmpning, structural
stivhed og ﬂytning af tyngdepunkt. Begge modeller foruds˚a instabilitet udfra resultaterne fra de
statiske tests.
De dynamiske tests blev brugt til at genskabe instabilitet de steder hvor modellerne foruds˚a det.
Desværre viste instabilitetsmodellerne og de dynamiske tests ikke de samme instabiliter, grundet
at den dynamiske forsøgsopstilling ikke var designet til de nødvendige vindhastigheder over 17m
s .
Keywords:
Dobbelte hængerkabler, Kabel dynamik, Aerodynamik p˚a hængerkabler, Tilisning af brokabler,
Vridningsp˚avirkede kabler, Statiske og dynamiske test

Chapter 1
Introduction
Main Authors: JS and NB
The hanger cables of the Great Belt bridge have been aﬀected by large amplitude vibrations since
the oﬃcial opening of the bridge in 1998, causing amplitudes of up to 2 meters.
The eﬀect of wind-induced cable vibrations is a considerable part of the design process in civil
engineering projects such as bridge and transmission lines. Cable vibrations can cause damage to
the structure itself due to increased loading on vital parts of the structure, as well as fatigue in
joints. There is also an element of discomfort for the users of the structures, if the vibrations have
large magnitude.
The purpose of this thesis was to investigate the reason for the large amplitude vibrations and try
to predict and recreate them in wind tunnel tests.

1.1
The Great Belt Bridge
The Great Belt Bridge is the largest bridge in Denmark, connect-
ing the islands Sealand and Fyn, and is supported in the middle
by the minor island Sprogø. The western part of the bridge is a
6.6 km long beam bridge and the eastern part is a 6.8 km long
suspension bridge with a 1624 meter span between the concrete
pylons, commonly known as the Great Belt East Bridge. The east
bridge cable conﬁguration consist of a main cable running between
the two anchor blocks, passing both the pylon-tops and the
highest peak of the bridge deck, see Figure 1.1. Between the main
cable and the bridge deck runs vertical twin-hanger cables for
approximately every 26 meters. The longest twin-hanger cables,
closest to the pylons, are 177.2 meter long and is connected by
minor cables for each 30-50 meters. These hanger cables are the
subject of interest in this master thesis. Figure 1.2 is a principle
sketch of the longest twin-hanger cables, stating the dimensions.
The investigated twin-cables consist of two cables of Type H3
(Appendix A) covered by black HDPE tubes with a 111 mm outer
diameter.
The inner cable is 98 mm in diameter, with a cross
section of 6470 mm2, consisting of multiple metallic strands. The
mass of the real cables are 54.35 kg
m .
The vibrations of the bridge hanger cables have been monitored
by COWI A/S since the opening in 1998. The data is presented in
Appendix B and consists of data for a large number of storms from
2000 - 2014. Large amplitude vibration up to two meter in am-
plitude, have been observed for 43 storm events (i.e 4 storms per
year roughly). The largest vibrations occurred in October 2000,
only two years after the bridge opened. The vibrations were un-
acceptably large, which caused COWI A/S to install tuned mass
dampers in 2003. This decreased the amplitude of the vibrations,
but did not resolve this problem entirely. The dampers have re-
cently been renovated, due to wear of the damper pistons, so the
problem still exists.

Chapter 1.
Introduction
1.2
Objective
The objective of this thesis was to be able to understand and possibly reproduce vibrations in a
test setup in a Climatic Wind Tunnel (CWT). This was done by making a series of static tests
where drag, lift and moment force coeﬃcients (CD, CL and CM) were measured for diﬀerent angles
of attack and wind velocities.
A quasi-steady assumption was done by using the static force coeﬃcients in The 1-DOF Den Hartog
criterion and The 3-DOF Gjelstrup Model. This was done to predict what combination of wind
velocity and angle of attack might cause instability of the cables, in a dynamic test setup.
Furthermore, the inﬂuence of ice accretion of the cables were evaluated, because previous research
have shown that ice has a great eﬀect of the possibility of instability.
The tests were conducted in a vertical test setup, with cables of the same diameter and spacing as
the actual ones of The Great Belt Bridge. The cross section area of the CWT allowed the cable to
have a length of 1.9 meters. The forces were measured at the top and bottom of the test setup, at
the center of the cable conﬁguration. This essentially means that the cable system was evaluated
as one connected system, not as two individual cables.
The tests were conducted for wind velocities between 6.5 −28.0m
s at all angles of a full 360◦, with
angle steps of 10◦.
The results from the static tests was taken into account when choosing which dynamic tests had
to be carried out.
The dynamic tests were conducted in the same CWT by use of a specially designed spring system.
The spring system allowed the test setup to move in both directions, as well as rotation around the
center axis of the cables. This made it possible to conﬁrm both the investigated Instability Models.
1.3
Structure of Report
The ﬁrst part of the report discusses the general theory behind cable aerodynamics needed to
understand how the vibrations in the cables occur. The chapter discusses diﬀerent instability phe-
nomenons and how the system attempt to damp these. Afterwards the study deﬁnes the diﬀerent
instability models evaluated in this thesis.
The second part of the thesis, studies in-depth what vibration observations have been made of The
Great Belt Bridge, and how this can be converted into a trustworthy test setup in the CWT.
Afterwards, the experimental work is split into three categories:
 Overall description of the static and dynamic test setup and how it was implemented in the
CWT, as well as the method behind the ice accretion of the cables.
 Preliminary tests, i.e a small test setup to evaluate the spring system, a decay test to determine
damping of the system and a force calibration check to verify the electrical system in the CWT.
 A test description that thoroughly explains exactly how all of the static and dynamic tests
were conducted.

Wind load on a system changes the ﬂow around the cylinders and creates aerodynamic forces that
causes vibrations. These vibrations are divided into several diﬀerent types of vibrations, caused

2.6.1

Vortex induced vibrations relates to the vortex shedding described in section 2.2. The vortices are
created ﬁrstly on one side of the cable and afterwards on the other side. This alternates for each
vortex, causing the lift force to oscilate between positive and negative values.
The closer the vortex shedding frequency (fv) is to the natural frequency of the cable, the big-
ger the vibration.
The vortex shedding frequency is found by using the Strouhal number, see
subsection 2.2.1.

parallel direction. This movement keeps amplifying until the cable eventually moves perpendicular

2.6.6

When a cable has an ice-coating, the cross-section, center of gravity, temperature and weight of
the cable is changed. Also the assumption of a perfectly circular cable with no lift force is not
applicable to a cable with ice-coating, due to changes of symmetry.
In recent years, ice-accretion has been investigated thoroughly, for example by Frederiksen and
Gandil, 2014 [13] who has shown how the thermal mass, i.e temperature changes of the cable
conﬁguration greatly inﬂuences results. Also Lund, 2013 [8] and Gjelstrup et al., 2011 [14] has
dealt with the changes of cross section caused by the ice, and the inﬂuence this has on the forces
on the cables. This study investigates the inﬂuence ice has on a twin-cable arrangement.

2.7.4
Damping of Bridge Cables
All vibrations that occur in bridge cables, are reduced by the damping of the cable. Some of the
damping is internal in the cable and some damping is applied to reduce vibrations. The internal
damping exists because of the friction between strings in the cable and friction between the cable
coating and the stings. This is also referred to as structural damping. External dampers, e.g.
hydraulic dampers, tuned mass dampers or even components that shorten the cable length, can be
used to increase structural damping.
Finally, the cables are damped by the air ﬂow around the cylinder. When the cable is set in motion
it will have to ”move” the surrounding air to move itself, meaning the air is essentially damping
the cable. This is called aerodynamic damping.

Instability Models
Main Author: NB, Edited and controlled: JS
The two instability models used in this thesis was The Den Hartog Instability Criterion which is a
1-DOF model using only the drag and lift force in the system and The Gjelstrup Model, Gjelstrup,
2011 [17], a 3-DOF model, also taking torsion into account.
The Den Hartog Criterion attempts to predict galloping instability of the system by use of the
gradient of lift force in the system.
The Gjelstrup Model is a more precise model taking into
account a variety of inputs, e.g damping, stiﬀness mass center oﬀset and moment of the system.
The Gjelstrup Model is also able to predict torsional galloping in the system.
The models uses a quasi-steady approach which is further explained in the following section.
3.1
Quasi-Steady Approach
Instability relates to excessive movement of the cable arrangement and thus creates large ﬂuctuat-
ing forces. Given that it is incredibly diﬃcult to be able to measure ﬂuctuating forces compared to
static forces, as well as the fact that a simulation in a computer program requires a series of steady
situations, a quasi-steady approach was used. The basic idea behind a quasi-steady approach is
that motion can be described by a series of frames or ”stationary situations”, where the prediction
will be more precise the more frames being put into the model.
In this study, the parameter being discretized was the angle of attack of the wind. The approach
was therefore to measure the static drag, lift and moment force coeﬃcients for a number of diﬀerent
angles of attack, α. Based on previous studies at DTU, e.g. Lund, 2013 [8], it was a fair assessment
that the model would be suﬃciently accurate with angle steps of α = 10◦.
3.2
The Den Hartog Criterion
In 1932, Jacob Pieter Den Hartog studied large amplitude vibrations of transmission cables.
The Den Hartog Criterion looked at a circular cylinder being exerted by a force, caused by wind.
For perfectly circular cylinders, wind always aﬀects a cable in its mass center and the force in the
cable will be in the direction of the wind. Since a lift force is perpendicular to the wind angle, this

will always be zero for an idealized system, Den Hartog, 1932 [18]. When the cable is aﬀected by
sleet weather and its cross section is no longer circular, the angle of attack may change and the
force exerted is no longer in the same direction as the wind. This creates both a lift and a drag
force on the cable.
Figure 3.1: Principle graph of The Den Hartog Criterion, Den Hartog, 1956 [4]
If the change in lift force with respect to change of angle becomes large enough, cf. Figure 3.1, the
cable will start oscillating transverse to the wind direction, in the direction of the lift force. The
system has a drag force which is essentially a damping force trying to keep the system from moving
extensively.
Generally, it can be said that instability of the cable occurs when the negative slope of the lift force
is greater than the damping induced by the acting drag force, deﬁned by Den Hartog in Den Hartog,
1956 [4].

3.3
The Gjelstrup Model
The Gjelstrup Model is a combination of a broad variety of diﬀerent instability models, as described
in the introduction of Gjelstrup, 2011 [17], and uses a number of input parameters to better predict
galloping instability.
The model studies a bluﬀbody as seen in Figure 3.2, that can move in the x,y and θ direction, the
latter being rotation. When ice is present on the cable, the cross section is no longer circular and
the mass center will have moved towards the direction of the ice accretion, described by Le, which
is the oﬀ-set from the original to the new center of rotation.
Figure 3.2: Gjelstrup principle, 3-DOF dynamic system
The model uses the parameters in Figure 3.2 and the force coeﬃcients CD, CL and CM to create
a prediction of unstable events . This is done by use of the general equation of motion, where the
aerodynamic damping of the system and displacement as function of Le is determined by a series
of matrix calculations. Afterwards the Routh-Hurwitz stability criterion is used on the damping
matrices. The imaginary solutions to the Routh-Hurwitz equations are the unstable regions of the
data series, see Gjelstrup, 2011 [17].
The input necessary to use the Gjelstrup Model are:
 Force coeﬃcients, CD, CL and CM
 Structural damping of the system in all directions
 Center oﬀset of the system, only relevant if the system is loaded with e.g. ice
 Stiﬀness of the system

happens when a body is placed in the test section of a wind tunnel and the ﬂow around the body
is constrained by the boundary conditions, e.g. the walls, roof and ﬂoor. This results in diﬀerences

6.2
Length of Carrying Wire
The wire taking the dead load of the structure
would have several implications on the system.
First of all it would create a pendulum eﬀect,
moving more in the bottom than the top, rather
than a system that moves the same in both ends.
An ideal wire would have an inﬁnite length,
so horizontal oscillations could happen without
vertical interference.
But the shorter the wire,
the bigger vertical forces is introduced.
Secondly the wire would try to stay at rest,
meaning that when the system was oscillating,
the wire would pull.
It was visually observed that a short wire re-
sulted in great damping and a pendulum eﬀect
of the setup, which is why the wire was made
as long as possible, i.e connecting it to the ceiling.
The increased damping of the system was diﬃcult
to counteract but an attempt at counteracting
the pendulum eﬀect was done by attaching a big,
pre-tensioned spring from the bottom of the lower
dynamic head to the ﬂoor, see Figure 6.7. This
had a good eﬀect and decreased the pendulum

8.2
Power Spectrum Density Analysis
To determine the cut-oﬀfrequency, a power spectrum density (PSD) analysis have to be carried
out. This is done by transforming the data from the time domain to the frequency domain to
evaluate which oscillations are relevant data and which oscillations are due to noise. This study
used the Welch power spectrum density estimation which uses a Fast Fourier Transformation (FFT)
to change from time domain to frequency domain. The Welch method was carried out in MatLAB as
[Sx,f] = pwelch(x,window,noverlap,f,fs)
where x is the time series that need to be investigated, window is the number of windows being
analyzed at the segments, noverlap is how much the windows overlaps, set to a default value of
50 %. f is the number of Discrete Fourier Transform points (DFT) and fs is the sample rate in
the data series.
The PSD is normalized by the geometric variance of the plot, meaning the area beneath the curve,
σ2:

8.3
Digital Filtering
The ﬁlter used was a Butterworth low-pass ﬁlter. A low-pass ﬁlter lets frequencies lower than the
cutoﬀfrequency pass and ﬁlters out frequencies higher. This way, the noise which is usually high
frequency static, is removed and the desired data is retained. To determine the ﬁlter coeﬃcients, a
and b, the MatLAB function butter is used:
[a,b] = butter (n,Wn,ftype)
Where n is the order of the Butterworth ﬁlter and Wn is the normalized cut-oﬀfrequency in the
range between 0 and 1. Finally, ftype deﬁnes if a low or high pass ﬁlter should be used.
This study uses a 6th order low-pass ﬁlter with a normalized cut-oﬀfrequency of

10.1
Comparability of Static and Dynamic Tests
Comparing static and dynamic tests must be done with consideration. A static test setup is always
in the same position with the same angle of attack, throughout a test and the drag and lift forces
are relatively stable through the course of the test. In a dynamic test, the initial angle of attack
is changed when the vibrations start to occur or movements of the entire setup happens. Because
of this, the two test types only have the same boundary conditions in the very beginning of a test.
After that, the dynamic setup will experience varies in α and the systems are no longer alike. From
this point, the dynamic test have to be evaluated with a quasi-steady approach with changing loads
and angle of attack.
The static test can therefore not give a precise picture of what will happen in the dynamic test,
but just an attempt of predicting if vibrations will occur in a given initial position. The success

10.3
Unpredicted Instability - Switching Flow
When testing the dynamic setup, several cases of switching ﬂow were observed, ﬁve of them with
rotational vibrations that kept increasing until the springs jumped of. Neither of the instability
models were able to predict this, because switching ﬂow is a ﬂow mechanics and can not be pre-
dicted by the use of force coeﬃcients.
Switching ﬂows instabilities occurred at 0◦for wind velocities U = [6.7, 8.5, 12.5, 15.5]m
s and
for 10◦at U = 8.6m
s . The explanation might be that the switching ﬂow phenomena is a ﬂow
mechanism, forcing the test setup to vibrate around its own center axis. It initial starts by wake
induced vibrations from upstream to downstream cable. When the cable system is rotating so the
air ﬂow travels ﬁrstly on one side of the cable and then on the other side of the upstream cable,
the vibrations kept increasing it reaches very large amplitudes.
In the cases where switching ﬂows were observed, the phenomena that started them were wake
induced vibrations from upstream to downstream cable. The videos 01-03 and 05-06 in Table 10.1
and 01-02 in Table 10.2 shows switching ﬂows, where the cable system slowly increases a twisting

Chapter 11
Conclusion
During the present thesis, tests have been performed in a climatic wind tunnel, on a twin-hanger
cable model with the same dimensions as the hanger cables on the Great Belt Bridge in Denmark.
Firstly, the goal was to investigate a full size cable model with the same dynamic parameters as the
real structure, in relation to ﬂow, forces and vibrations impacting the cables in diﬀerent environ-
mental conditions. Secondly, the test setup was designed so it allowed vibrations in both directions
and in torsion, to be able to implement both The Den Hartog criterion and The Gjelstrup Model.
To deﬁne the cable system, some preliminary tests were performed. A spring stiﬀness test was
investigated, to ﬁnd the optimal spring design for the ﬁnal dynamic setup. The ﬁnal design was
a six-spring system at the top and bottom of the cable conﬁguration, with four springs in one
direction with stiﬀness, k = 1k, and two springs in the other direction with k = 2k. This resulted
in a equivalent system stiﬀness with the smallest possible variations, for diﬀerent angles of attack.
A decay test was also made on the ﬁnal dynamic setup to verify that the natural frequencies were
as close to the real frequencies of the Great Belt Bridge hanger cables as possible. The real cables
have a frequency of fn,Bridge = 0.64 Hz while the tests showed a frequency of fn,y = 1.00 Hz and
fn,x = 1.10 Hz in respectively side-by-side and tandem position. The torsional frequency of The
Great Belt Bridge cables was not known, but was fn,z = 0.525 Hz in this test setup. The reason
why the real life and the model frequencies did not correspond, might be due to the individual
spring stiﬀness, which varied a bit from the needed stiﬀness. The related aerodynamic damping for
the two directions and the moment were ζy = 2.3 h, ζx = 1.4 h and ζMz = 3.5 h.
It is assumed that the real twin-cables can vibrate independently in speciﬁc situations, but the test
setup was decided to be simpliﬁed to a connected system, so the movement of one cable directly
impacted the other.
The ﬁnal design of the twin-cable system was ﬁrst tested in a static setup, for all angles between
0 −350◦in steps of 10◦and in wind velocities varying from 6.7 −28m
s . The output from the
these tests was force components in all directions, which was recalculated to the normalized force
coeﬃcients for drag CD, lift CL and moment CM.
The cable model was tested with two types of ice accretion, glaze and rime ice. Ice accretion on

it did not block the instability.

evaluated.
Ice accretion applied more mass and eccentric weight to the system, so more instability was ex-
pected, however this was not observed.

Chapter 12
Future Studies
During this thesis, the attention was lead to several subjects that could be improved to reach more
precise results and additional questions that need answers.
Special attention was given to the comparison of data from this study to ESDU. The main prob-
lem was that the lift coeﬃcients did not match the data from ESDU. In order to verify the the
data obtained in this study it would be interesting to improve the static test setup. The main
factor that would be of interest would be to test cables were the forces are measured on the ca-
bles and not on the system as a whole. This could be done by using cylinders of exactly 2 meters
in length to ﬁt into the CWT, and then mounting force transducers inside the ends of the two cables.
Improvements to the dynamic test setup would also be of great use, to be able to test wind velocities
at higher velocities. This could be done by using springs with a higher stiﬀness and better mounted
springs so they would be active throughout the course of testing.
In additional to the two speciﬁc suggetions, a summary of possible improvements in general are
listed below.
 The static test setup was not 100 % maintained in the same position during tests, small
movements might have occurred between the two wooden frames. A suggestion is to make a
more solid frame-structure of steel, but with a low weight so it is possible to rotate for two
people.
 The cable system could be more symmetric about the two center axes, so the force coeﬃcients
would be identical for each 90◦angle-runs.
 Instead of ending the tests at 350◦, a test could be done at 360◦, to be able to see if any
unexpected changes in the setup or logging system occurred, as α = 0 = 360◦
 Preparing the instability models before any static tests are done, so a static test with ice
accretion can be performed and analyzed, and the CWT kept cold until the dynamic tests
are made. This ensures that the ice in the two tests are identical.
 More time in the CWT for dynamic tests will make it more likely to discover galloping and
give time to improve the dynamic setup

Purpose
The purpose is the same as in test no. 02, but with ice. The wanted output

1.1
Test set-up
Static test set-up with variation in wind speed and angle of attack. Each
measurement was made in 30 seconds, in total 36 x 12 = 432 recordings of
30 seconds. Two measurements is erased, the starting zero reading and the
1500 RPM reading, because only one zero-reading was needed for all the
tests, and the 1500 RPM was removed because the CWT applied to varying
wind velocities at this maximum load.
The ice is made at 500 RPM in the CWT, corresponding to approximately
10.5 m
s . At this velocity, the spray cloud from the spray bar is not wide
enough to reach both cables. The cables is therefore disconnected from the
static head and placed so only one cable at a time is aﬀected by the spray
cloud. The ice accretion is then made in two times 30 minute runs at (-5)
to (-6) ◦C.

Rime ice is applied to the cables and the zero-reading and 1500-test was
cancelled.
