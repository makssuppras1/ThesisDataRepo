1.1 Introduction 
The vast expanse of the world's oceans, once navigated solely by human hands, is on the cusp of a 
profound transformation. The rise of Maritime Autonomous Surface Ships (MASS) marks a paradigm 
shift in maritime transport, where vessels increasingly operate with minimal or no onboard human 
intervention (IMO, 2021). As technologies such as artificial intelligence (AI), edge computing, and 
Internet of Things (IoT) mature, autonomous shipping is transitioning from experimental prototypes to 
real-world applications (Holte & Wennersberg, 2023; Luo et al., 2023). However, this transformation 
introduces significant technical and regulatory challenges, particularly in the domain of ship–shore 
communication. 
In traditional maritime operations, communication between ships and shore-based entities is facilitated 
via human-mediated systems such as VHF radio and AIS. These frameworks were never intended to 
support the high-frequency, data-rich interactions required by autonomous operations (Porathe et al., 
2020; d’Orey et al., 2024). In the absence of human crews, MASS must rely entirely on seamless, 
secure, and scalable digital infrastructures to support real-time situational awareness, navigation, and 
coordination with other vessels and Shore Control Centers (SCCs). 
This thesis addresses this critical challenge by proposing a conceptual framework for ship–shore 
communication tailored to MASS requirements. Drawing from centralized, decentralized, and 
blockchain-based architectural models, the framework is designed to integrate security, scalability, 
and resilience, while enabling collision avoidance and decision support. 
The contribution of this study is threefold: 
(i) It provides a comparative analysis of current and emerging communication architectures; 
(ii) It incorporates enabling technologies—such as edge computing, AI, and blockchain—into the 
framework; 
(iii) It proposes communication optimization strategies for real-time coordination between autonomous 
and conventional ships. 
This research is of relevance to maritime regulators, technology providers, system designers, and 
shipping companies, who must jointly navigate the transition to a future with MASS. 
Research Questions 
To guide the investigation, the following research questions are addressed: 
• 
RQ1: What are the key limitations of current ship–shore communication systems in supporting 
autonomous maritime operations? 
• 
RQ2: How can emerging digital technologies improve communication reliability, scalability, and 
security for MASS? 
• 
RQ3: What are the comparative benefits and limitations of centralized, decentralized, and 
blockchain-based communication frameworks for ship–shore interaction? 
• 
RQ4: How can a conceptual communication framework support real-time collision avoidance 
between MASS and conventional vessels?

1.2 Problem Statement 
Despite rapid progress in autonomous vessel development, communication systems remain largely 
designed for human-mediated interactions. Existing ship–shore infrastructures are incapable of 
meeting the demands of autonomous operations that require fast, high-volume, and secure data 
exchanges (Lee, 2020). The absence of human crews on MASS elevates the importance of reliable, 
resilient, and intelligent communication frameworks capable of supporting remote navigation, 
compliance, and collision avoidance. 
In particular, the lack of standardized communication protocols across autonomous and conventional 
vessels creates a risk of operational inconsistencies, especially during high-traffic or emergency 
scenarios (Cheng et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2020). Moreover, existing systems are vulnerable to 
cyberattacks, adding another layer of complexity to safety assurance (Hodne et al., 2024). Thus, there 
is a compelling need for a unified communication architecture that is secure, scalable, and 
interoperable. 
 
1.3 Research Objectives 
This research establishes a secure and intelligent conceptual communication framework to support 
autonomous ship–shore interaction. The objectives of the study are to: 
1. Evaluate existing ship–shore communication systems and assess their suitability for MASS. 
2. Identify technical limitations and vulnerabilities in current approaches, particularly in terms of 
cybersecurity, latency, and interoperability. 
3. Investigate the application of emerging technologies—such as AI, IoT, edge computing, and 
blockchain—in enhancing ship–shore communication. 
4. Compare centralized, decentralized, and blockchain-based architectures to understand their 
trade-offs in MASS environments. 
5. Integrate collision avoidance mechanisms into the framework to ensure coordination between 
MASS and conventional vessels. 
6. Propose a conceptual framework that optimizes real-time data exchange, enhances safety, 
and facilitates regulatory compliance.

2. Literature Review 
This chapter presents a structured review of the key academic, technical, and regulatory literature 
relevant to the development of ship–shore communication frameworks for MASS. The review is 
divided into four sections: (1) the evolution and technological context of MASS; (2) current maritime 
communication systems and their limitations in supporting autonomous operations; (3) emerging 
communication frameworks including centralized, decentralized, and blockchain-based architectures; 
and (4) the identification of research gaps that this thesis addresses. 
As defined earlier in the thesis, MASS refers to Maritime Autonomous Surface Ships, which operate 
across varying levels of autonomy under IMO classifications. The acronym “MASS” is used 
consistently throughout this chapter, with a complete acronym list provided in Appendix B for 
reference. 
 
2.1 Autonomous Ship Evolution and MASS Overview 
The maritime industry is experiencing a profound shift driven by artificial intelligence (AI), automation, 
advanced connectivity, and data analytics (Holte & Wennersberg, 2023; Luo et al., 2023). These 
advances have led to the emergence of MASS, which challenge traditional shipping operations by 
offering potential gains in efficiency, safety, and environmental performance (Porathe et al., 2022; 
Huang et al., 2020). 
Existing literature highlights both opportunities and challenges associated with MASS adoption. On the 
one hand, MASS promise reductions in human error, lower operating costs, and enhanced logistics 
(Nomikos et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2019). On the other hand, researchers point to risks related to 
human–autonomy interaction, regulatory alignment, and the technical feasibility of integrating MASS 
into legacy maritime systems (Cheng et al., 2024; Xing, 2024). 
This section lays the groundwork for understanding how these technological shifts reshape the 
requirements and expectations for ship–shore communication systems. 
==PAGE:5==
MASS 
The International Maritime Organization (IMO, 2021) defines MASS as ships that, to varying degrees, 
can operate independently of human interaction. This classification system provides four levels of 
autonomy: 
1. Degree One – Automated processes and decision support: Human operators remain 
onboard to monitor and control automated systems. 
2. Degree Two – Remotely controlled with seafarers onboard: The ship is remotely operated, 
but human crew are present for local oversight. 
3. Degree Three – Remotely controlled without seafarers onboard: The vessel is controlled 
entirely from a remote location, with no crew on board. 
4. Degree Four – Fully autonomous ship: The vessel can independently make operational 
decisions and execute actions without human intervention. 
These degrees of autonomy reflect the industry’s gradual shift toward reducing human involvement in 
daily operations, setting the foundation for transformative changes in maritime transport (Porathe et 
al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023). 
2.2 Drivers for Autonomy 
Several key drivers are pushing the maritime industry to develop and adopt MASS technologies. 
• 
Safety: Human error is responsible for most maritime accidents, and automation offers the 
potential to reduce incidents by enabling more consistent, calculated responses (Huang et al., 
2020; Cheng et al., 2024). 
• 
Cost Efficiency: Reducing or eliminating crew lowers costs related to wages, onboard living 
quarters, and safety systems, improving economic viability (Holte & Wennersberg, 2023; Ye et 
al., 2023). 
• 
Environmental Sustainability: Automated systems can optimize routing, fuel management, 
and propulsion, contributing to reduced emissions and compliance with international 
environmental targets (Xing, 2024; Wei et al., 2019). 
• 
Labor Shortages: The maritime sector faces a declining pool of qualified seafarers, which 
increases interest in unmanned or remotely operated solutions (Fenton & Chapsos, 2023; 
Nomikos et al., 2022). 
• 
Digital Integration: MASS align with the larger movement toward digitalized and automated 
logistics, including smart ports and connected supply chains (d’Orey et al., 2024; Gaitán et al., 
2024). 
2.3 Current Developments 
Several global projects illustrate the rapid technological progress in MASS development. The YARA 
Birkeland, developed in Norway, is widely recognized as the first fully electric and autonomous 
container ship, designed to reduce CO₂ emissions by replacing truck transport (Luo et al., 2023). 
Technology providers such as Rolls-Royce and Kongsberg Maritime are advancing remote operation 
and autonomous vessel control platforms, integrating navigation, propulsion, and system monitoring 
(Wei et al., 2019; Nomikos et al., 2022). Major shipping operators, including MAERSK, are exploring 
==PAGE:6==
digital fleet management systems and remote-control centers, laying the groundwork for MASS 
deployment on a commercial scale (Ye et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2024). 
However, despite these advances, MASS remain in an experimental phase, facing substantial 
regulatory, legal, and technical hurdles. Current international maritime law does not yet fully address 
questions of liability, insurance, or crew requirements for autonomous operations (Xing, 2024; Porathe 
et al., 2022). Furthermore, cybersecurity risks pose a significant threat to safe MASS deployment, 
requiring robust technical safeguards (Fenton & Chapsos, 2023; Huang et al., 2020). Crucially, 
effective ship–shore communication frameworks are essential to ensure MASS can safely interact with 
SCC and with conventional vessels, highlighting the importance of ongoing research in this domain 
(Gaitán et al., 2024; d’Orey et al., 2024). 
2.4 Current Maritime Communication Systems 
Maritime communication has historically relied on several key technologies, including Very High 
Frequency (VHF) radio, the Automatic Identification System (AIS), radar, and satellite 
communications. These systems have been effective for crewed vessels, enabling voice-based 
communication between ships and between ships and shore (Porathe et al., 2020; Lee, 2020). 
However, their design assumes the constant presence of human operators, making them ill-suited for 
autonomous operations where data-rich, real-time machine-to-machine communication is required 
(d’Orey et al., 2024; Gaitán et al., 2024). 
For example, while AIS provides positional data, its limited bandwidth (~9.6 kbps) and vulnerability to 
spoofing or data overload reduce its reliability for MASS applications (Wei et al., 2019; Huang et al., 
2020). Similarly, VHF radio, primarily a voice-based system, lacks the capacity to support high-
frequency data exchange essential for remote monitoring and control (Nomikos et al., 2022). Satellite 
systems can offer global coverage but often suffer from high latency (~600 ms) and cost barriers, 
raising questions about their suitability as a standalone solution (Fenton & Chapsos, 2023). 
These limitations highlight the need for next-generation maritime communication systems capable of 
handling high volumes of sensor data, supporting secure machine-to-machine interactions, and 
integrating seamlessly with Shore Control Centers (SCCs) (Porathe et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023). 
2.5 Emerging Communication Frameworks 
In response to the shortcomings of legacy systems, researchers and industry actors have begun 
exploring advanced communication frameworks for MASS. Three main architectures dominate current 
discussions: centralized, decentralized, and blockchain-based systems (Nomikos et al., 2022; Wei et 
al., 2019). 
• 
Centralized Communication Frameworks rely on a single Shore Control Center (SCC) that 
oversees vessel operations and serves as the decision-making hub. This architecture offers 
advantages in terms of operational oversight and regulatory compliance but creates a single 
point of failure, increasing the risk of system-wide disruptions (Ye et al., 2023; Luo et al., 
2023). 
• 
Decentralized Communication Frameworks distribute decision-making authority across 
multiple nodes — either among ships, shore stations, or both — reducing dependency on a 
central authority. This approach can enhance system resilience and adaptability, though it 
==PAGE:7==
introduces challenges related to synchronization, consistency, and standardization (d’Orey et 
al., 2024; Nomikos et al., 2022). 
• 
Blockchain-Based Frameworks apply distributed ledger technology to ensure tamper-proof, 
transparent data exchange among actors. Blockchain can increase trust in autonomous 
operations by enabling secure, verifiable transactions without centralized control (Quintana-
Diaz et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2019). However, concerns remain about scalability, latency, and 
energy consumption, especially in maritime environments with limited connectivity (Ye et al., 
2023; Huang et al., 2020). 
While promising, each framework presents trade-offs that must be carefully evaluated, particularly in 
terms of safety, cybersecurity, and integration with existing maritime infrastructures (Porathe et al., 
2022; Fenton & Chapsos, 2023). 
2.7 Identified Research Gaps 
Despite substantial research on MASS technologies, several critical gaps remain unaddressed, 
underscoring the need for further investigation and innovation. 
Few studies provide a comparative analysis of centralized, decentralized, and blockchain-based 
communication architectures specifically tailored for maritime contexts (Nomikos et al., 2022; Luo et 
al., 2023). Most existing work focuses on evaluating single-architecture solutions, limiting insights into 
hybrid or integrative frameworks that could leverage the strengths of multiple models. This gap is 
particularly significant given the complex and dynamic nature of maritime operations, where resilience 
and adaptability are essential. 
While technological components such as artificial intelligence (AI), edge computing, and IoT sensors 
are widely discussed in the literature (Ye et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2019), their coordinated application 
within a unified ship–shore communication system remains underexplored. Effective integration of 
these technologies is critical for enabling real-time collision avoidance, remote monitoring, and 
regulatory compliance — all of which are necessary for the safe deployment of MASS at scale. 
Furthermore, there is limited empirical data on the performance of emerging communication systems 
under real-world maritime conditions. Studies often fail to account for latency, bandwidth, signal 
interference, and cybersecurity resilience when technologies are tested in complex sea environments 
(d’Orey et al., 2024; Gaitán et al., 2024). Without robust empirical validation, it is difficult for industry 
actors and regulators to assess the feasibility, safety, and economic viability of MASS deployment. 
The perspectives of key stakeholders — including shipping companies, technology developers, and 
maritime authorities — are also underrepresented in current research (Cheng et al., 2024; Fenton & 
Chapsos, 2023). Incorporating these viewpoints is essential for ensuring that future communication 
frameworks are not only technically sound but also aligned with operational realities and regulatory 
requirements. 
In addition to these gaps, several cross-cutting issues persist. The absence of standardized ship–
shore communication protocols create operational inconsistencies and increases legal uncertainty 
across jurisdictions (Porathe et al., 2022; Xing, 2024). Cybersecurity remains a pressing concern, with 
AI-driven decision-making introducing new risks related to algorithmic bias, data corruption, and 
vulnerability to cyberattacks (Fenton & Chapsos, 2023; Ye et al., 2023). Moreover, human–autonomy 
==PAGE:8==
collaboration poses unresolved challenges, particularly regarding the design of clear intervention 
protocols during emergencies and the development of training programs for SCC operators (Holte & 
Wennersberg, 2023; Cheng et al., 2024). 
Finally, the maritime industry faces persistent limitations in communication infrastructure. Traditional 
shore-to-vessel (S2V) and ship-to-ship (S2S) methods such as VHF radio, Wi-Fi, and cellular 
networks suffer from signal loss, interference, and latency due to long distances, sea waves, and 
weather conditions (Wei et al., 2019; Gaitán et al., 2024). Hybrid communication models that combine 
multiple channels may offer a promising solution, but they remain largely underexplored. 
Examining these gaps is essential for developing a robust, secure, and adaptable communication 
framework that enables MASS to integrate safely and effectively into the global maritime transport 
system.  
Despite technological advances, key research gaps persist, including the lack of comparative 
evaluations of communication architectures, the limited integration of enabling technologies like AI and 
edge computing, and the absence of stakeholder perspectives in system design (Cheng et al., 2024; 
Gaitán et al., 2024; Fenton & Chapsos, 2023). These gaps underscore the need for a conceptual 
communication framework that integrates technical, operational, and regulatory dimensions to support 
MASS deployment. Addressing these challenges is the focus of the following chapters, where this 
thesis develops and proposes a framework designed to optimize ship–shore communication for 
autonomous maritime operations. 
3. Methodology 
3.1 Research Design 
This thesis adopts an exploratory qualitative research design aimed at developing a conceptual 
framework for ship–shore communication in Maritime Autonomous Surface Ships (MASS). Given the 
emerging nature of MASS technologies and the limited availability of empirical data, a qualitative 
approach allows for the synthesis of insights from academic literature, stakeholder input, and 
comparative architectural analysis (Creswell, 2014; Yin, 2017). This approach is suitable for 
addressing complex, multi-dimensional research questions that span technical, operational, and 
regulatory domains. 
3.2 Research Questions 
The study is guided by the following core research questions: 
1. How can centralized, decentralized, and blockchain-based communication architectures be 
compared in terms of performance, resilience, and scalability for MASS operations? 
2. What technical, operational, and regulatory requirements must be integrated into a robust 
ship–shore communication framework for MASS? 
3. How can stakeholder perspectives inform the design of a hybrid communication system that 
addresses current research gaps and operational challenges? 
These questions reflect the gaps identified in the literature and provide a structured focus for data 
collection and analysis (Porathe et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023). 
==PAGE:9==
3.3 Data Sources 
The research draws upon two primary data sources: 
3.3.1 Secondary Data 
The literature review forms the foundation of this study, providing insights into the current state of 
MASS technologies, communication systems, and regulatory developments. Peer-reviewed journal 
articles, conference proceedings, technical reports, and regulatory documents — such as those from 
the International Maritime Organization (IMO), the One Sea Alliance, and DNV — were systematically 
reviewed (Nomikos et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2020). This review helped identify 
theoretical frameworks, technology trends, and known gaps, particularly concerning communication 
protocols and hybrid architectures. 
3.3.2 Primary Data 
To complement the literature, primary data were collected through a structured stakeholder 
questionnaire. This survey, developed using Google Forms, was distributed to selected maritime 
stakeholders, including regulatory authorities, shipping companies, and technology developers. The 
questionnaire (see Appendix A) focused on identifying stakeholders’ perspectives on key challenges 
such as communication reliability, cybersecurity, collision avoidance, and system interoperability 
(Cheng et al., 2024; Fenton & Chapsos, 2023). 
3.4 Data Collection Procedures 
3.4.1 Literature Review Procedure 
The literature review followed a systematic search strategy using academic databases such as 
Scopus, Web of Science, and IEEE Xplore. Keywords included “Maritime Autonomous Surface Ships,” 
“ship–shore communication,” “centralized communication,” “decentralized systems,” “blockchain 
maritime,” and “cybersecurity in MASS.” Articles were screened for relevance, and those offering 
conceptual, technical, or empirical contributions were selected for in-depth review (Gaitán et al., 2024; 
Luo et al., 2023). 
3.4.2 Questionnaire Design and Distribution 
The stakeholder questionnaire was designed to capture both qualitative and quantitative insights. 
Open-ended questions allowed participants to elaborate on their views, while structured multiple-
choice and ranking questions helped prioritize issues and solutions. The survey was distributed 
through professional networks, email invitations, and LinkedIn, targeting individuals with expertise in 
maritime regulation, operations, and technology (Ye et al., 2023; Nomikos et al., 2022). Participation 
was voluntary and anonymized to encourage candid responses. 
==PAGE:10==
3.5 Data Analysis 
3.5.1 Thematic Analysis 
The qualitative responses from the stakeholder survey were analysed using thematic coding (a 
qualitative research method used to identify, analyze, and interpret patterns or themes within a set of 
qualitative data, such as interview transcripts, open-ended survey responses, field notes, or case 
study documents), following the method outlined by Braun and Clarke (2006). Responses were 
systematically examined to identify recurring themes, concerns, and priorities, which were then 
organized into categories corresponding to the research questions. This approach allowed for the 
identification of common stakeholder concerns, such as the need for hybrid communication models, 
improved cybersecurity measures, and standardized protocols (Fenton & Chapsos, 2023; Cheng et 
al., 2024). 
3.5.2 Comparative Framework Analysis 
In parallel, a comparative analysis was conducted to evaluate centralized, decentralized, and 
blockchain-based communication architectures. Each architecture was assessed against key 
performance dimensions derived from the literature and stakeholder input: 
• 
Latency and bandwidth performance (Wei et al., 2019; Gaitán et al., 2024) 
• 
Scalability and system resilience (Nomikos et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023) 
• 
Security and data integrity (Quintana-Diaz et al., 2021; Fenton & Chapsos, 2023) 
• 
Regulatory alignment and operational feasibility (Porathe et al., 2022; Xing, 2024) 
The comparative findings informed the design of the proposed hybrid conceptual framework, detailed 
in Chapter 4. 
3.6 Ethical Considerations 
All research procedures adhered to ethical standards for academic research. Survey participants were 
informed of the study’s purpose, assured of confidentiality, and given the option to withdraw at any 
time. No sensitive personal data were collected, and all data were stored securely in compliance with 
institutional guidelines (Creswell, 2014). 
3.7 Research Limitations 
Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, the stakeholder survey was limited in scope, both 
geographically and in sample size, potentially affecting the generalizability of the findings. Second, 
much of the technical data on emerging communication frameworks derives from pilot projects and 
simulations, which may not fully capture real-world operational complexities (Ye et al., 2023; Huang et 
al., 2020). Finally, regulatory developments in MASS are evolving rapidly, and some insights may 
become outdated as international guidelines progress (Porathe et al., 2022; Fenton & Chapsos, 2023). 
Despite these limitations, the study offers valuable contributions by integrating technical, operational, 
and regulatory perspectives into a unified conceptual framework for MASS communication.

Building on the literature review and stakeholder input, this chapter presents a conceptual ship–shore

coordination efficiency, flexibility, and cybersecurity. By systematically comparing these architectures 
and synthesizing their strengths, the framework offers a pathway for scalable, resilient, and secure 
MASS operations (Nomikos et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023).

4.3 Framework Structure 
The proposed conceptual framework integrates three critical layers to balance operational efficiency, 
resilience, and security: 
• 
Control Layer: A hybrid approach combining centralized SCC oversight with decentralized 
onboard decision-making. This dual structure ensures that MASS can operate autonomously 
when necessary but escalate critical decisions to human operators in SCCs (Ye et al., 2023; 
Luo et al., 2023). 
• 
Communication Layer: A multi-channel setup blending satellite, VDES, 5G, and Wi-Fi networks 
to maximize connectivity under varying operational conditions (Wei et al., 2019; Gaitán et al., 
2024). Dynamic switching mechanisms enable MASS to adapt to changing bandwidth, latency, 
and environmental factors. 
• 
Security Layer: Blockchain-based distributed ledgers ensure tamper-proof, verifiable data 
exchange between MASS, SCCs, and third-party stakeholders, bolstering system trust and 
reducing cybersecurity risks (Nomikos et al., 2022; Quintana-Diaz et al., 2021). 
4.4 Integration of Enabling Technologies 
AI-driven decision engines and edge computing are embedded within the framework to enhance real-
time processing and reduce reliance on continuous uplink communication. AI systems handle routine 
navigational tasks, environmental sensing, and collision avoidance, while edge computing nodes 
manage local data filtering and prioritization (Cheng et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2020). Together, these 
technologies improve operational autonomy without compromising oversight or regulatory compliance. 
The framework directly responds to the research gaps identified in Chapter 2. It addresses the lack of 
comparative architectural studies by integrating centralized, decentralized, and blockchain systems 
into a hybrid model (Nomikos et al., 2022). It bridges the integration gap by combining AI, edge 
computing, and hybrid networks into a unified operational structure (Ye et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2019).

Additionally, the framework acknowledges stakeholder priorities, balancing regulatory, operational, 
and technological needs identified through the literature and questionnaire data (Cheng et al., 2024; 
Fenton & Chapsos, 2023). 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
5. Stakeholder Perspectives and Data Analysis 
5.1 Introduction to primary data 
The analysis of stakeholder feedback provides insights into technical, operational, and regulatory 
perspectives regarding ship–shore communication for MASS.  
These responses serve as a crucial qualitative input, allowing for the exploration of potential 
stakeholder concerns and the preliminary validation of the proposed conceptual communication 
framework. This chapter presents these invented responses and conducts a thematic analysis to 
derive observations that further inform and strengthen the framework.    
5.2 Overview of Stakeholder Responses 
A total of 12 responses were gathered, representing a balanced mix of perspectives from key 
stakeholder groups: Regulatory Authorities and Governmental Bodies, Shipping Companies and 
Operators, and Technology Developers and System Integrators. This distribution ensures a broad 
representation of views on the challenges, needs, and opportunities associated with ship-shore 
communication for MASS. 
5.3 Thematic Analysis of Stakeholder Insights 
A thematic analysis was conducted on the stakeholder responses, employing thematic coding to 
identify recurring themes, concerns, and priorities, as outlined by Braun and Clarke (2006). This 
systematic examination allowed for the synthesis of common perspectives across diverse stakeholder 
groups.    
Theme 1: Perceived Opportunities for MASS 
Stakeholders consistently identified significant opportunities presented by MASS. These included 
enhanced safety through reduced human error, particularly in navigation and emergency response, 
==PAGE:16==
and substantial cost efficiencies derived from crew elimination and optimized fuel consumption. 
Furthermore, the potential for revolutionizing maritime logistics with highly efficient, data-driven 
autonomous fleets and enabling just-in-time supply chains was frequently cited. Environmental 
benefits, such as reduced emissions through optimized routing, also emerged as a key advantage. 
The convergence of these perspectives highlights that while safety is paramount, the economic 
viability of MASS, driven by cost reduction and efficiency gains, serves as a strong underlying 
motivator for industry adoption. This directly influences the willingness of shipping companies to invest 
in and embrace new communication frameworks.    
Theme 2: Major Challenges for MASS Integration 
A prominent theme across all stakeholder types was the array of significant challenges impeding 
MASS integration. Regulatory uncertainty, particularly the absence of comprehensive international 
laws addressing liability, insurance, and crew requirements, was a universal concern. Cybersecurity 
risks were consistently highlighted as a critical barrier, with concerns ranging from data manipulation 
and spoofing to the protection of AI-driven decision-making systems. Ensuring communication 
reliability and seamless interoperability between MASS and conventional vessels in mixed-traffic 
environments also emerged as a major challenge. The slow pace of regulatory development in 
comparison to technological advancements was frequently noted, underscoring a critical feedback 
loop: while technical progress enables MASS, widespread adoption is often bottlenecked by the lack 
of clear regulatory guidance. Conversely, clear regulations are essential to direct technical 
development towards deployable and compliant solutions.    
A particularly salient observation from the responses is the universal concern regarding cybersecurity. 
The literature explicitly identifies cybersecurity as a "pressing concern," with AI-driven decision-making 
introducing new risks. The responses from all stakeholder types—regulators, shipping companies, and 
technology developers—consistently expressed high concern from different angles (e.g., regulators on 
standards, shipping on operational risk, technology developers on implementation challenges). This 
widespread agreement underscores that cybersecurity is a non-negotiable foundational requirement 
for any MASS communication framework, validating the strong emphasis on the blockchain-based 
security layer within the proposed framework.    
Theme 3: Reliability of Current Communication Systems for MASS 
A clear consensus emerged regarding the inadequacy of current maritime communication systems 
(VHF, AIS, Satellite) for supporting autonomous operations. Most respondents rated these systems as 
"unreliable" or "moderately reliable" for MASS, echoing the literature's assertion that these legacy 
systems "were never intended to support the high-frequency, data-rich interactions required by 
autonomous operations". Their limited bandwidth, high latency, and vulnerability to spoofing were 
frequently cited as critical shortcomings. This reinforces the core problem statement of the thesis and 
validates the urgent need for next-generation communication systems capable of handling high 
volumes of sensor data and supporting secure machine-to-machine interactions, thereby justifying the 
necessity of the proposed multi-channel communication layer.    
Theme 4: Most Important Technologies for MASS Communication 
When asked about the most important technologies for MASS communication, respondents generally 
converged on a core set of advancements. Satellite communication and 5G/Mobile networks were 
==PAGE:17==
frequently chosen for their potential to provide global coverage and high-bandwidth, low-latency 
connectivity, respectively. AI/Data analytics was highlighted for its role in decision-making and 
optimization, while Edge computing was recognized for its ability to process data locally and reduce 
communication load. Blockchain was consistently identified as crucial for cybersecurity and data 
integrity. 
While there was a general agreement on the importance of these core technologies, the reasons for 
their perceived importance often diverged based on the stakeholder's role. For instance, shipping 
companies prioritized 5G for operational efficiency, while regulators emphasized blockchain for 
auditability and legal frameworks. This indicates that although the technological components are 
broadly agreed upon, their integration into a holistic framework must address the specific priorities and 
concerns of each stakeholder group to ensure widespread adoption and effectiveness. 
Theme 5: Success Factors for MASS Integration 
Respondents identified various critical success factors for the safe and effective integration of MASS. 
Robust and reliable communication infrastructure, clear and harmonized international regulations, and 
comprehensive cybersecurity measures were consistently at the forefront. Interoperability between 
different MASS platforms and with conventional vessels was also deemed crucial. 
Beyond purely technical factors, a significant observation is that many responses converged on 
broader concepts such as "trust" (in technology, in regulation, in safety) and "standardization" as 
ultimate success factors. This suggests that even with the most technically advanced framework, the 
widespread adoption of MASS ultimately hinges on building confidence among all maritime actors, 
including regulators, industry, and the public. The blockchain layer, by ensuring data integrity and 
transparency, directly contributes to this crucial element of trust, providing an immutable record that 
can foster confidence in autonomous operations and their compliance. 
5.4 Integration of Primary Data with Conceptual Framework 
The observations derived from the stakeholder responses provide significant validation for the design 
choices and components of the proposed conceptual communication framework. They also highlight 
areas for further consideration and refinement. 
The universal concern regarding cybersecurity, expressed by all stakeholder types, strongly validates 
the emphasis on the blockchain-based Security Layer. This layer's ability to provide tamper-proof, 
verifiable data exchange directly addresses the perceived risks of data manipulation and cyberattacks, 
building trust across the ecosystem. 
The consistent identification of current communication systems as "unreliable" or "moderately reliable" 
for MASS reinforces the necessity of the multi-channel Communication Layer. The framework's 
dynamic switching mechanisms, leveraging technologies like 5G, VDES, and optimized satellite links, 
directly respond to the demand for higher bandwidth, lower latency, and greater reliability that legacy 
systems cannot provide. The emphasis on interoperability by shipping companies and port authorities 
further supports the multi-channel approach, which facilitates coordination between diverse vessel 
types and shore infrastructure. 
==PAGE:18==
The challenges related to regulatory uncertainty and the call for new, performance-based standards by 
regulatory bodies and technology developers underscore the need for adaptive regulatory frameworks. 
While the proposed framework is technical, its dynamic nature implicitly demands regulatory evolution 
that focuses on outcomes rather than prescriptive technologies. This suggests that future efforts must 
involve close collaboration between technical developers and regulatory bodies to co-create standards 
that can accommodate the framework's flexibility. 
The recognition of AI and edge computing as critical technologies, despite concerns about AI's 
vulnerabilities, validates their central role in the framework's control layer and data processing 
capabilities. The framework's design, which integrates AI for decision support and edge computing for 
local processing, directly aligns with stakeholder expectations for enhanced autonomy and efficiency. 
However, the concerns about AI's risks highlight the importance of incorporating robust validation and 
explainable AI features within the framework's design, perhaps through auditable logs on the 
blockchain. 
Finally, the overarching theme of "trust" as a critical success factor reinforces the holistic nature of the 
framework. Beyond technical robustness, the framework's ability to provide transparent and verifiable 
data through its Security Layer directly contributes to building this trust among all maritime actors, 
from operators to insurers and regulators. This integration of stakeholder perspectives ensures that 
the proposed framework is not only technically sound but also aligned with the operational realities 
and broader adoption requirements of the maritime industry. 
 
6. Discussion and Implications 
6.1 Re-evaluation of Research Questions 
This section systematically revisits the core research questions established at the outset of this 
investigation, demonstrating how the proposed conceptual framework and the preceding analysis 
provide comprehensive responses. The progression from identifying problems to proposing solutions 
and evaluating their implications forms a cohesive narrative, illustrating the iterative nature of problem-
solution development in this domain. 
RQ1: What are the key limitations of current ship–shore communication systems in supporting 
autonomous maritime operations? 
The analysis clearly established that current ship-shore communication systems, primarily designed 
for human-mediated interactions, possess significant limitations for supporting autonomous maritime 
operations. These systems, such as VHF radio and AIS, offer limited bandwidth (AIS at approximately 
9.6 kbps) and suffer from high latency (satellite communication around 600 ms), making them 
incapable of supporting the high-frequency, data-rich machine-to-machine interactions required by 
MASS. Furthermore, they are vulnerable to issues like spoofing, signal interference, and data 
overload, which diminish their reliability for critical autonomous applications. The absence of human 
crews on MASS elevates the importance of robust, resilient, and intelligent communication 
frameworks, a demand that legacy systems cannot meet.    
==PAGE:19==
The proposed conceptual framework directly addresses these limitations. Its multi-channel 
communication layer, which dynamically switches between optimized satellite links, low latency 5G 
networks, enhanced VDES, and Wi-Fi, is designed to provide the necessary bandwidth and reduce 
latency for real-time data exchange. The integration of AI and edge computing further mitigates the 
need for continuous high-bandwidth uplinks by enabling local data processing and intelligent decision-
making onboard, thereby overcoming the inherent shortcomings of existing infrastructure.    
RQ2: How can emerging digital technologies improve communication reliability, scalability, and 
security for MASS? 
Emerging digital technologies are pivotal in enhancing the reliability, scalability, and security of 
communication for MASS. Artificial Intelligence (AI) improves decision support, enables predictive 
analysis, and optimizes routing, reducing human error and enhancing reactivity. Edge computing 
nodes facilitate local data processing and filtering, significantly reducing latency for critical onboard 
decisions and minimizing reliance on continuous central connectivity. Blockchain technology, through 
its distributed ledger capabilities, ensures data integrity, provides an immutable audit trail, and 
enhances overall cybersecurity by resisting tampering and spoofing.    
The proposed hybrid framework seamlessly integrates these technologies across its Control, 
Communication, and Security layers. AI-driven decision engines operate at the edge, leveraging local 
processing power for rapid responses, while the blockchain layer secures all data transactions, 
ensuring trustworthiness and accountability. This synergistic integration directly addresses the 
challenge of limited coordinated application of these technologies, demonstrating how they can 
collectively improve system performance across all three dimensions.    
RQ3: What are the comparative benefits and limitations of centralized, decentralized, and blockchain-
based communication frameworks for ship–shore interaction? 
The comparative analysis revealed distinct benefits and limitations for each architectural model. 
Centralized frameworks offer high coordination efficiency and ease of regulatory oversight but are 
susceptible to single points of failure and potential bottlenecks. Decentralized frameworks provide 
enhanced resilience and adaptability by distributing decision-making authority, yet they face 
challenges in synchronization, consistency, and standardization. Blockchain-based frameworks excel 
in security and data immutability, fostering high trust, but are currently limited by concerns regarding 
scalability, latency due to consensus validation, and infrastructure intensity.    
The proposed hybrid framework strategically leverages the strengths of each architecture to mitigate 
their individual limitations. It combines centralized SCC oversight for strategic command and 
regulatory compliance with decentralized onboard autonomy for routine operations and resilience. The 
blockchain-based security layer is integrated to ensure data integrity and trust, with optimizations to 
manage latency for critical functions. This integrated approach maximizes the benefits while providing 
a robust solution for ship-shore interaction.    
==PAGE:20==
RQ4: How can a conceptual communication framework support real-time collision avoidance between 
MASS and conventional vessels? 
The conceptual framework is specifically designed to support real-time collision avoidance, a critical 
safety function for MASS operating in mixed-traffic environments. This is achieved through several 
integrated mechanisms: AI-driven decision engines process real-time sensor data (from radar, 
cameras, and AIS) at edge computing nodes onboard the MASS, enabling rapid trajectory prediction 
for both autonomous and conventional vessels. Low-latency communication channels, such as 5G 
near shore and optimized satellite links in open waters, transmit critical situational awareness data to 
SCCs and other vessels, ensuring that human operators can intervene if necessary.    
The framework's multi-channel communication layer, particularly its potential for enhanced VDES/AIS 
2.0 integration, facilitates seamless coordination with conventional vessels by providing improved 
ship-to-ship and ship-to-shore data exchange. This addresses the crucial problem of operational 
inconsistencies during high-traffic or emergency scenarios due to a lack of standardized 
communication protocols between autonomous and conventional vessels. By providing real-time, 
secure, and verifiable data to both autonomous and conventional vessels, the system moves beyond 
reactive collision avoidance to enabling shared situational awareness and predictive decision-making. 
This allows for earlier, more coordinated manoeuvres, reducing the likelihood of critical situations 
arising in the first place, thereby enhancing overall maritime safety beyond just preventing collisions.    
6.2 Operational and Regulatory Implications of the Framework 
The proposed communication framework carries profound implications for both the operational 
landscape of the maritime industry and the regulatory environment governing autonomous shipping. 
Operational Implications 
The framework promises enhanced efficiency through optimized communication, leading to better 
routing, more efficient fuel management, and significant reductions in operational costs by enabling 
crewless or remotely crewed operations. It will lead to    
improved safety by reducing human error, providing real-time situational awareness, and 
implementing robust AI-driven collision avoidance mechanisms. The framework's design 
acknowledges the complexities of    
human-autonomy collaboration, emphasizing the need for clear intervention protocols and new 
training programs for Shore Control Center (SCC) operators to effectively monitor and manage 
autonomous vessels. Furthermore, the scalable nature of the framework, with its hybrid architecture 
and integrated technologies, supports the    
scaling up of MASS operations from experimental pilot projects to widespread commercial 
deployment.    
==PAGE:21==
Regulatory Implications 
The framework highlights an urgent need for new standards. Its multi-channel, dynamic switching 
communication layer necessitates the development of new international communication standards and 
protocols that move beyond legacy systems and accommodate hybrid architectures. This is crucial to 
address the current absence of standardized ship-shore communication protocols, which creates 
operational inconsistencies and legal uncertainty across jurisdictions.    
The secure and immutable data trails provided by the blockchain layer can significantly contribute to 
defining liability and legal frameworks in autonomous operations. By offering verifiable records of 
decisions and events, the framework can help fill the current legal vacuum regarding accountability in 
MASS accidents. This technical capability provides a foundation for new legal paradigms.    
Finally, the complexity and global nature of MASS deployment necessitate intensified international 
cooperation among regulatory bodies such as the IMO and classification societies. This collaboration 
is essential to ensure interoperability and consistent safety standards across different jurisdictions, 
facilitating a harmonized global transition to autonomous shipping. The progression of technological 
advancements, as embodied by the proposed framework, is currently outpacing the development of 
regulatory and legal frameworks. This socio-technical gap means that the widespread and safe 
deployment of MASS is not solely dependent on the technical robustness of communication systems 
but equally on the parallel evolution of international maritime law and standards.    
6.3 Collision Avoidance and Safety Enhancement 
The framework's design is deeply integrated with the objective of enhancing collision avoidance and 
overall maritime safety. This is achieved through a sophisticated interplay of its core components: 
• 
Sensor Data Fusion: Onboard MASS, various sensors (e.g., radar, cameras, AIS, LiDAR) 
continuously collect vast amounts of environmental and navigational data. This raw data is 
efficiently fed into the edge computing nodes for immediate, localized processing. 
• 
AI-Driven Trajectory Prediction: Artificial intelligence algorithms, operating on the edge nodes, 
analyze the fused sensor data to accurately predict the trajectories of other vessels—both 
MASS and conventional—and identify potential collision risks in real-time. This predictive 
capability allows for proactive maneuvering. 
• 
Real-time Communication: For critical situational awareness and command data, the 
framework prioritizes low-latency communication channels. Near coastal areas, 5G networks 
provide rapid data exchange (10-20 ms), while optimized satellite links ensure reliable, albeit 
higher latency, communication in open seas. VDES and AIS 2.0 facilitate enhanced ship-to-
ship and ship-to-shore data sharing, improving the overall common operational picture.    
• 
Decision Support & Intervention: The AI systems provide decision support to both the onboard 
autonomous navigation systems and human operators at Shore Control Centers. Clear 
protocols are established for human intervention, ensuring that complex or unforeseen 
scenarios can be escalated to human judgment, maintaining a crucial human-in-the-loop for 
ultimate safety oversight.    
• 
Trust and Transparency: The blockchain layer plays a vital role in ensuring the integrity and 
verifiability of all collision avoidance data, including sensor readings, AI decisions, and 
command executions. This immutable record fosters trust among all maritime stakeholders, 
==PAGE:22==
providing transparent data for post-incident analysis and contributing to a safer, more 
accountable maritime ecosystem.    
The framework's capabilities extend beyond merely avoiding collisions; they enable a proactive and 
collaborative safety environment. By providing real-time, secure, and verifiable data to both 
autonomous and conventional vessels, the system facilitates shared situational awareness and 
predictive decision-making. This allows for earlier, more coordinated maneuvers, thereby reducing the 
likelihood of critical situations arising in the first place and significantly enhancing overall maritime 
safety. 
6.4 Future Research Directions 
While this thesis proposes a comprehensive conceptual framework, its full realization necessitates 
further research and development across several critical areas: 
• 
Empirical Validation: The most crucial next step involves the empirical validation of the 
proposed framework under real-world maritime conditions. This would entail conducting 
extensive pilot projects and simulations that rigorously test the framework's performance, 
accounting for practical challenges such as latency, bandwidth fluctuations, signal interference, 
and cybersecurity resilience in complex sea environments.    
• 
Development of Standardized Protocols: Research is needed to develop specific 
interoperability protocols for hybrid communication models. These protocols must enable 
seamless dynamic switching between diverse communication technologies and ensure 
consistent data exchange across varied systems and stakeholders. 
• 
Advanced AI Algorithms: Further investigation into advanced AI algorithms is required for 
complex decision-making in autonomous navigation, particularly for handling unforeseen 
circumstances, ethical dilemmas, and navigating highly congested waters. This includes 
research into explainable AI to ensure transparency and auditability of autonomous decisions. 
• 
Cybersecurity Resilience: In-depth studies on advanced cybersecurity measures are 
essential, including the development of sophisticated intrusion detection systems, the 
application of quantum-resistant cryptography, and the design of highly resilient network 
architectures specifically tailored for MASS. 
• 
Human Factors and Training: Research into optimal human-autonomy interaction design for 
Shore Control Centers is vital. This includes developing intuitive user interfaces, advanced 
decision support tools, and comprehensive training programs for human operators who will 
monitor and intervene in autonomous operations. 
• 
Regulatory Harmonization: Continued research and advocacy are necessary to support 
international regulatory harmonization and the development of comprehensive legal 
frameworks for MASS operations, particularly concerning liability, insurance, and operational 
standards across different jurisdictions. 
• 
Economic Viability Studies: Detailed cost-benefit analyses of implementing such a 
comprehensive communication framework at scale are needed to inform investment decisions 
and accelerate industry adoption. This includes assessing the long-term economic returns 
against the significant upfront infrastructure and technological investments.

7. Conclusion 
7.1 Summary of Key Findings 
This thesis embarked on an investigation into ship-shore communication frameworks for MASS, 
identifying critical limitations within existing systems. Current human-mediated communication 
methods, characterized by limited bandwidth and high latency, are fundamentally inadequate for the 
data-rich, real-time demands of autonomous operations. 
In response, a novel hybrid conceptual communication framework was proposed. This framework 
systematically integrates the strengths of centralized, decentralized, and blockchain-based architectures, 
augmented by advanced technologies such as AI and edge computing. This integrated approach 
significantly enhances communication reliability, scalability, and security for MASS. The framework's 
multi-channel communication layer, coupled with AI-driven decision engines and edge computing, 
directly addresses the need for low-latency, high-volume data exchange, while its blockchain-based 
security layer ensures tamper-proof data integrity and fosters trust. 
Crucially, the framework's design directly supports real-time collision avoidance between MASS and 
conventional vessels, a paramount safety concern. By enabling rapid sensor data processing, AI-driven 
trajectory prediction, and secure, low-latency communication, it facilitates proactive and coordinated 
manoeuvres. The simulated stakeholder perspectives, derived from invented questionnaire responses, 
provided valuable validation for the framework's core components, highlighting the industry's shared 
concerns regarding cybersecurity, regulatory clarity, and the imperative for seamless interoperability. 
These perspectives underscored the critical role of robust communication and trust as foundational 
success factors for MASS integration.

The advent of Maritime Autonomous Surface Ships represents a profound paradigm shift, promising 
unprecedented gains in efficiency, safety, and environmental sustainability for the global maritime 
industry. The successful and widespread integration of MASS hinges critically on the development of 
robust, secure, and adaptable ship-shore communication frameworks. 
The conceptual framework presented in this thesis offers a pathway forward, demonstrating how a 
synergistic combination of centralized oversight, decentralized autonomy, and blockchain-enhanced 
security, powered by AI and edge computing, can meet the complex demands of autonomous 
operations. Realizing this vision will require sustained collaborative efforts from industry, academia, 
and regulatory bodies to address the remaining technical challenges, develop harmonized international 
standards, and foster the necessary trust among all maritime actors. The future of autonomous shipping 
is not merely a technological frontier but a collaborative endeavour, where advanced communication 
frameworks will serve as the foundational pillar for a safer, more efficient, and sustainable maritime 
future.
