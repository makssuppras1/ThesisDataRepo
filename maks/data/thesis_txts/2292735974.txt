Abstract
One of the largest limitations for applying advanced control and optimization to bio-
chemical processes is lack of reliable online measurements of key process parameters like
biomass, substrate, and production concentration. A solution can be applying model
based observers, which use dynamic models combined with process measurements to
estimate internal states of a system. This report investigates of the use of model based
observers for monitoring purposes.
A literature review provided a fundamental understanding of the mathematical and
theoretical basis for a range of the most common observers. The literature study was
focused towards observers believed to be applicable for biochemical batch processes. The
relevant observers were benchmarked in a simulation case using an ethanol fermentation
model. The result from the benchmarking case showed that the best performance was
obtained using an extended Kalman ’¼ülter with continuous covariance propagation.
The experience gained from the benchmarking case, combined with algorithm insights
from the literature review, lead to the development of a new hybrid observer algorithm,
speci’¼ücally aimed towards biochemical processes. The observer combines the statistical
noise reduction and full state estimate of the extended Kalman ’¼ülter with a mass balance
algorithm. Thereby the observer utilizes the presumption that mass balances constitute
the most robust part of the biochemical models.
The applicability of the extended Kalman ’¼ülter and the proposed hybrid observer on
experimental data was examined through a case study of a Saccharomyces cerevisiae
fermentation. Results from the case study showed, that using the extended Kalman
’¼ülter, 2 out of 3 unmeasured states could be predicted with an average uncertainty of
15 %. This result was obtained where the observers was not developed or tuned on
the estimated data. The proposed hybrid observer showed poor results due to a non-
rigorous mass balance, which is an essential requirement for good performance of the
hybrid observer.

Background
One of the biggest limitations for improving monitoring of biochemical processes can
be assigned the lack of probes for online measurements of key process parameters, eg.
biomass, substrate, and product concentrations. O’¼äine alternatives o’¼Ćer only a poor
alternative as these are often expensive, labour-intensive, and slow. The challenge can
be summarized as estimating the internal states of a bioreactor when only scarce and in-
frequent measurements are available. A solution can be applying model based observers,
which use a model combined with process measurements to reconstruct internal states
of the system. These observers are commonly refered to as soft sensors, short for ŌĆÖsoft-
ware sensorsŌĆÖ, and they generally fall into two distinct categories: 1) Observers based on
statistic/chemometric methods and 2) observers based on dynamic models. This project
will focus on observers utilizing dynamic models. These are from here simply denoted
ŌĆØmodel based observersŌĆØ.
Aim of Project
Model based observers with dynamic models have been used with great success in a large
number of industries, including automotive, electronics, navigation, etc. Despite the nu-
merous success stories in these industries, the methods are currently not extensively
applied in the biochemical industry. This is thought to be due to practical challenges;
the level of complexity necessary in the dynamic models, the lack of process measure-
ments, and lack of expertise in the ’¼üeld. The aim of this project is to investigate the
potential, challenges, and limitations for the application of model based observers in
industrial biochemical processes.
Initially, a literature review are conducted unravelling the theoretical background for
design of model based observers and their application. The literature review should also
contain an overview of possible observer algorithms and the mathematical description
of these. Secondly the methods should be exempli’¼üed and evaluated using a benchmark
model, which show the advantages and disadvantages of the identi’¼üed algorithms. Lastly
a case study should be conducted using real data from a lab scale fermentation.

Introduction
Background
Biochemical technology is currently employed for the production of several economi-
cally important commodity and ’¼üne chemicals including pharmaceuticals, enzymes and
organic acids.
To give an indication of the sheer size of the market, world sales for
the top 20 recombinant protein products reached e13 billion in 2000, and in 2014 the
enzyme market alone had a value of $ 8 billion[1, 2]. The biochemical market is not
only large, but also highly pro’¼ütable, where patent protected products combined with a
ŌĆØgreen pro’¼üleŌĆØ has allowed the companies to charge a premium price for the biochemi-
cally produced compounds. This cost structure has resulted in a high focus towards fast
process development and product launch in order to maximize the life cycle pro’¼üt of
the patented products. However, in the later years there has been an increasing interest
in trying to retain sale of products after patent expiration. For products with expired
patents, the cost structure mirrors the cost structure in the classical chemical and petro-
chemical industry, where optimal operation and control are key focus areas. The same
is the case for production of commodity biochemical products, challenging traditional
chemical production. Here competitive advantage is ensured through cost e’¼ācient pro-
duction and use of advanced operation optimization and process control[3, 4, 5].
Advanced and integrated methods for process control have proven to be highly valu-
able to the traditional bulk chemical industry, including the petrochemical industry.
Introducing methods like state feedback control and model predictive control has lowered
the cost of operation, reduced process variations, and improved product quality[3]. Con-
trol strategies encountered in the biochemical industry have not reached the same level
of maturity, especially regarding the use of mathematical models and online optimization
algorithms[6]. The production often shows a high batch-to-batch variation causing dis-
turbances in the downstream operation and resulting in suboptimal process conditions[6].
One of the largest barriers for introducing the advanced control and optimization meth-
ods is lack of reliable online measurements of key process variables. Available sensors
in industrial biochemical processes are often limited to measure pH, temperature, ’¼éow
rates, light absorbance, dissolved oxygen, and o’¼Ćgas composition. Important variables
such as substrate, product, and biomass are rarely measured online and can therefore
not easily be incorporated in an integrated control scheme[6, 7]. Besides the use of pro-
cess measurements for control it is also believed that further requirements will arise by
1
==PAGE:20==
regulatory a’¼Ćairs like the process analytical technology (PAT) initiative issued by the
American Food and Drug Administration. The major goal of PAT is to improve process
understanding including fault detection, processes predictions and control[1]. This again
emphasize that online monitoring of processes is of key importance.
The challenge for online monitoring bioprocesses can potentially be overcome by the
use of mathematical models. Mathematical models can be used to synthesise observers
which, based on the available measurements, can reconstruct unmeasured variables.
These methods are known as software sensors and fall into two distinct categories: 1)
Observers based on statistic/chemometric methods and 2) observers based on dynamic
models. This project will study the application of observers based on dynamic models.
These will henceforth be denoted model based observers.
A large number of methods for designing model based observers have been developed
throughout the last 50 years[8]. Methods for observing linear systems are well described
and especially the use of Kalman Filters for linear systems have proven (both analyti-
cally and practically) to be highly e’¼Ćective. Biochemical batch and fed-batch processes,
however, often show a high degree of non-linear dynamics.
In order to monitor the
non-linear behavior it is possible that application of non-linear observer algorithms are
needed. Most of the applied methods for non-linear observers are derived from the linear
principle, where the concept of linearization is used in order to locally apply the linear
algorithms[9].
An issue related to the application of model based observers is the requirement for a good
dynamical model representation of the system. Modelling of biochemical processes is a
very delicate task, and especially deriving a good representation of fermentation kinetics
can be challenging. The mass balances in the biochemical models are often regarded as
being more robust than the kinetic expressions. This is attempted to be incorporated in
some versions of model based observers[10].

Objective
The objective of this work is to study the application of model based observers in a bio-
chemical context. This should also consider industrial relevance, and therefore practical
considerations for application to an industrial setting are discussed throughout the work.
A thorough understanding of the theoretical and mathematical background for the de-
sign of observers should be obtain through an initial literature survey. The literature
study should focus the modeling concepts for biochemical processes as well as the speci’¼üc
algorithms for model based observation. Both linear and non-linear applications should
be included to give a through understanding understanding of the underlying principles.
Two case studies should be conducted in this project.
The ’¼ürst case study should be an exemplifying case were the performance of the ob-
server algorithms described in the literature survey can be assessed. The case study
should highlight the in’¼éuence of the non linearity of the batch processes on the applied
methods, and especially clarify whether it is feasible to use linear observers for monitor-
ing of biochemical processes. It should show the rudimentary di’¼Ćerence of the range of
model based observers and thereby serve as a benchmarking of the algorithms.
The second case should show the prospect of developing a model based observer includ-
ing the underlying model only based on experimental data. The case should uncover the
actual possibilities, challenges and limitations regarding the implementation of model
based observers on real life cases. This is believed to be a highly challenging task and
expectations are not a fully functioning model based observer.
Outline
Chapter 1 and 2 constitute the literature survey of this report. Chapter 1 is an introduc-
tion to general concepts of biochemical modelling, model representation, and the concept
of observability. Chapter 2 is focused primarily on presenting and describing the princi-
ple of observers and the considered observer algorithms. Chapter 3 is the benchmarking
case study performed on an ethanol fermentation model by Z. K. Nagy[11]. Chapter 4
presents a proposal of a novel hybrid observer algorithm based on the lessons learned
in Chapter 2 and 3. Chapter 5 is the second case study with lab scale fermentation
data from a yeast fermentation with Saccharomyces cerevisiae. Chapter 6 sums up the

Chapter 1
Theoretical Background
Biochemical processes are a very extensive concept covering chemical processes
directly utilizing living cells as well as processes with catalysts produced by living
cells. This include fermentation processes, enzymatic processes and metabolic
reactions in a large range of scales. This chapter introduces basic concept in the
description of industrial biochemical processes focused on fermentation processes
of scale, which is the main focus in this report.
1.1
Introduction to Biochemical Process Modelling
Modelling of industrial biochemical processes is a complex task, requiring an understand-
ing of physical interactions across multiple scales in the reactor. Biochemical process
models are often a combination of mechanistic equations, and empirical laws. Mechanis-
tic models increasingly replace the empirical laws, but description of the majority of the
biology in the systems is still highly dependent on empirical expressions[6]. This implies
a degree of hierarchy in the development of biochemical models. First the basic, physical
mass balances can be set up, and thereafter the empirical and less robust kinetics can
be speci’¼üed.
A process model is always a simpli’¼üed version of the real process limited by a set of
assumptions. The complexity of the model is determined by the number and extent of
the assumptions enforced on the real process. A higher model complexity is obtained if
more aspects and dynamics of the real system is included. Biochemical models for scales
larger than micro scales always ignore cell to cell or enzyme to enzyme variations. The
system is instead modelled using a ŌĆØvolume elementŌĆØ, and the amount of species in a
volume element is speci’¼üed by a concentration measure.
Development of a process model can serve a number of purposes including, but not
limited to[12]:
┬ł Predicting the evolution of the system;
┬ł Designing of process equipment;
┬ł Specifying operation conditions;
┬ł Gaining process insight;
5
==PAGE:24==
┬ł Assisting process control;
┬ł Estimating variables, which are not measured;
┬ł Estimating parameters, which are not known.
Before and during the model development it is highly important to decide for what pur-
pose the model should be used, as this can highly in’¼éuence the approach and model
complexity. The governing principle is to keep the model as simple as possible while
including the phenomena that are essential to the model purpose[13].
For industrial
models a challenge can be to limit the scope of operating conditions under which the
model should be valid. The larger the operations space, the greater complexity is needed.
The classical chemical disciplines like separation processes have bene’¼üted from thor-
ough mechanistic models, for example applied to distillation columns. These extensive
generic models can be simpli’¼üed to obtain the appropriate model complexity for a given
task. Development of thorough mechanistic models for bioprocesses has been limited by
the complex dynamics especially associated with the metabolics and kinetics or the living
cells. The metabolics of living cells are highly complex and may even change through the
course of a process. The interplay between the reactor environment and the cells have
been impossible to capture in a generic mechanistic model. Bioprocess modelling hence
strongly relies on empirical correlations for the description of biomass kinetics. This
characterizes one of the greatest challenges for the development of bioprocess models:
In order to develop a model, which to a large extend reproduce the observed behavior,
the models are so extensive in empirical relations that a small change in the host strain
can shift the behavior of the system to make the model unreliable[12].
1.1.1
Industrial Fermenters
Industrial fermentation processes all follow the same basic principle. Microbial organisms
are inoculated in a tank, where after they grow and produce the desired product. The
product can either be a natural metabolite, a genetically engineered product, or the
biomass itself. The organisms have very di’¼Ćerent requirements regarding optimal process
conditions. Fermentations can be aerobic (oxygen requiring) or anaerobic (non-oxygen
requiring). For strict anaerobic microbes, oxygen is toxic. Some organisms require light
(photosynthetic microorganisms), while others are very sensitive to sheer stress (e.g.
mammalian cells).
Generally the organisms grow on a combination of nutrients including a carbon source,
a nitrogen source, and a number of inorganic salts and vitamins which are essential for
growth, but often not included in any models. A very general sketch of an industrial
bioreactor can be seen in Figure 1.1.

using empirical expressions. Laboratory experiments then determine the variables in
the process a’¼Ćecting the reaction rates. These are often found to be the reactants. Ex-

Structured and Unstructured Models
Kinetic growth models can be characterized as unstructured or structured, but the sep-
aration between the two de’¼ünitions are not sharp. The basic unstructured model uses
a black box approach with one general reaction and stoichiometry which is ŌĆØan aver-
ageŌĆØ of all involved reactions. In this case the stoichiometric coe’¼ācients become equal
to the yield coe’¼ācients. The reaction rate is calculated based on a simple expression
for biomass growth rate. The biomass growth rate and the stoichiometric coe’¼ācients
together de’¼üne all rates of consumption and production.
Models gets increasingly structured as more biochemical/biological knowledge is incor-
porated in the rate expressions. The easiest extension to the basic unstructured model
is the inclusion of cell ŌĆØmaintenanceŌĆØ which is uncorrelated to biomass growth, but
depends on the total amount of biomass. The largest structured models include intra
cellular intermediate species for development of the kinetic expressions. In that case the
kinetic expressions are linked to a proposed reaction mechanism, and parameters in the
expression can give physical/chemical insight.

1.2.1
State Variables
State variables are dependent variables used to represent the state of a system. The state
of a system completely characterizes the general condition of the system, and it thus
captures all previous e’¼Ćects of inputs and dynamics. State variables for a car in motion
can e.g. be velocity and position. Given an initial state and the state transformation,
the behavior of the dynamic system can be solved as an initial value problem[9]. For
a chemical system, state variables is related to mass and energy balances and can be
concentration, temperature, pressure, etc. The states of a system are not unique, while
the order n of a fully modeled system is constant[15]. The order n of a system is equivalent
to the number of linear independent state variables[15]. The complexity of the system
a’¼Ćects the order of a model. E.g. if the purpose of a model is to determine the long term
variations it can be assumed that system processes, which are very fast compared to the
variations of interest are assumed to occur instantly, resulting in the replacement of a
di’¼Ćerential equation by an algebraic equation. Substituting a di’¼Ćerential equation with
an algebraic equation reduces the order of the model.

Chapter 2
Observers
Observers are mathematical algorithms, which use a model of the system com-
bined with knowledge about outputs or measurements to estimate the internal
state of the system. The internal state of a system can be useful for supervision
purposes or control purposes. This chapter presents a range observers including
linear, nonlinear and asymptotic observers.
In observer theory the internal system states, x, are in general considered to be unknown.
The inputs to the system, u, and the output from the system, y, are known and can be
used to produce an estimate of the states. In the case where the states are measured
directly in the output (y = x) state estimation is easy. However, it should be kept in
mind that in strict observer theory the states x are still considered unknown, but a
ŌĆÖstate estimateŌĆÖ can be made by assuming that the measured output y gives a good esti-
mate of the state. It that case the observer model is ╦åx = y, where ╦åx is the state estimate.
In bioprocesses one of the main limitations for state estimation is the lack of avail-
able measurements.
Therefore methods are needed where unmeasured states can be
reconstructed through available measurements. In this report the focus will be on state
estimation using model based observers.
2.1
Observer Basics
The aim of any observer is to reconstruct the state, x, of a dynamical system. The

2.2
Mass Balance Based Observers
The ’¼ürst type of observers, which will be presented are observers based on mass balance
systems. These kind of observers require that the system is modelled using laws of con-
servation, which for bioprocesses mainly relate to the conservation of mass. In general,
these observers assume that a number of the states are directly measured, and the mea-
sured states are then used to regenerate the unknown state variables. These observers
are found to be useful, especially in bioprocesses, because mass balance modelling, as de-
scribed in Section 1.1.2, constitutes the best known part of the process model. A central
point in these observers is that the bioprocess kinetics essentially can be unknown when
using mass balance observers. This is especially useful when reaction rate expressions
are not reliable enough to base an observer on.
The main condition needed for the design of this type of observer is that enough state
variables are measured. Observability of the system as de’¼üned in Section 1.3 is not a
requirement for a mass balance observer. This is not surprising, as observability relies
on the full description of the system including kinetics, which are not incorporated in
mass balance observers[12, 24].

2.3.2
Kalman Observers
Kalman observers, also known as Kalman ’¼ülters or linear quadratic estimators (LQE),
are among the widest applied observer algorithms. The Kalman ’¼ülter can be viewed as
a special case of the Luenberger observer, where the observer gain L (which in Kalman
context is denoted K) is calculated in order to minimize the variance of estimation error
under the assumption of noisy measurements and disturbances to the system.
23
==PAGE:42==
Kalman ’¼ülters have been highly successful in a large number of applications such as
the development of GPS, continuous manufacturing processes, aircraft and spacecraft
control, and econometrics[9].
The Kalman algorithms are di’¼Ćerent for the two cases of discrete and continuous sys-
tems.
For the discrete Kalman ’¼ülter, the state update (using information of system
inputs and previous estimates) is separated from the state correction (accounting for
the measured output). The continuous Kalman ’¼ülter, also known as the Kalman-Bucy
’¼ülter, makes update and correction in a single calculation[30].
The derivation of the Kalman ’¼ülter algorithm is rather extensive and is not strictly
important for the application of the algorithm. However, it has been included in this
work as understanding the derivation and the underlying assumptions can give insight
into the theoretical limitations of the observer performance.
Section 2.3.3 shows the derivation of the discrete Kalman ’¼ülter. The derivation of the
continuous Kalman ’¼ülter is slightly di’¼Ćerent and yields di’¼Ćerent observer equations which
can be seen on page 29, but the steps in the derivation is identical as for the discrete
algorithm[31].

2.4.2
Extended Kalman Filter
Following the simplicity and success of the Kalman ’¼ülter for linear estimation problems,
it is desired to extend the methods for use in non-linear cases. The extended Kalman
’¼ülter (EKF) is a non-linear version of the ordinary Kalman ’¼ülter presented in Section
2.3.2. The method utilizes the fact that most dynamics of the state are non-linear, but
smooth, and behaves approximately linear for small perturbations. Methods from linear
estimation theory can therefore be applied locally by linear approximation of the non-
linear system.
Linearization of the non-linear system can either be based on an expected nominal state
trajectory, or by real time linearization of the system around the current state estimate.
The operational performance of some systems highly depend on staying close to a prede-
’¼üned nominal trajectory. These systems are often tightly controlled and therefore rarely
move far away from this state trajectory. For these systems it is possible to linearize
the non-linear system around the nominal trajectory, and use this in the observer. The
advantage of linearizing around a nominal trajectory is the computational cost of calcu-
lation as the linearization and Kalman gain can be computed o’¼Ćline[43]. For less tightly
controlled systems it is more suitable to linearize around the online estimated trajec-
tory. This also has the advantage that perturbations are often smaller from the state
estimate compared to perturbations from the nominal trajectory, and the linearization
approximation is therefore better.

Chapter 3
Algorithm Benchmarking - Case
Study 1
Benchmarking is a process of comparing the performance metrics of several pro-
cedures in order to specify best practice. In this chapter the algorithms presented
in Chapter 2 will be benchmarked using a simulation case. The aim is to de-
termine the performance and applicability for use in an industrial fermentation
process.
The aim is to examine the performance of the observers especially dealing with the
non-linear dynamics in a fed-batch process. The advantages, drawbacks, and key fea-
tures of the observers will be examined qualitatively as well as quantitatively. In this
benchmarking case 7 observers will be compared.
1. Discrete Luenberger observer (DLO), Linear Observer
2. Discrete Kalman ’¼ülter, constant gain (DKF-cg), Linear Observer
3. Discrete Kalman ’¼ülter, varying gain (DKF-vg), Linear Observer
4. Extended Kalman ’¼ülter, discrete covariance propagation (EKF-dcp),
Non-linear Observer
5. Extended Kalman ’¼ülter, continuous covariance propagation (EKF-ccp),
Non-linear Observer
6. Unscented Kalman ’¼ülter (UKF), Non-linear Observer
7. Asymptotic Observer (AO), Mass Balance Observer
Tuning of the observers are done in order to obtain the best possible individual perfor-
mance based on both quantitative and qualitative evaluation.
A non-linear fermentation inspired by a model presented by Z. K. Nagy (2007)[11] will
be used as case study. The model describes an alcoholic fermentation where glucose is
consumed by yeast cells to produce ethanol. The attention to this process has increased
largely during the resent years when ethanol has been proven useful as a partly sub-
stitute for gasoline as a fuel[11]. The model by Z. K. Nagy was published in Chemical

Fed-Batch Operation
As mentioned in Section 1.1.1 fed-batch operation is normally used to avoid substrate
inhibition or over’¼éow metabolism. It should be mentioned that none of these e’¼Ćects in
fact is present in this model. Batch operation rather then fed-batch operation would
usually be chosen for such a system. The reason for the choice of fed-batch operation
in this case study is hence not related to optimal operation of the given fermentation
model, but related to desired test conditions for the observers. By operating the fermen-
tation fed-batch non-linearities are ampli’¼üed through the change in volume, as shown
mathematically in Section 1.1.2.
3.1.5
Model Evaluation
The largest assumption in the model is the non-coupling of oxygen to the growth and
metabolism of the cells. The absence of oxygen in the product formation term is valid
as alcoholic fermentation is an anaerobic process[58]. However, it would be assumed
that the level of oxygen would in’¼éuence the growth and metabolism of the cells, which
also is indicated by the in’¼éuence of biomass on the dissolved oxygen concentration.
The e’¼Ćect of oxygen on the metabolism could then indirectly change the production
of ethanol[11, 59]. These e’¼Ćects are not included in the model and are assumed to be
insigni’¼ücant under the operating conditions in the simulation.

71
==PAGE:90==
For discrete non-linear systems, it is stated in the literature, that the UKF exhibits
a superior performance compared to the EKF[46, 50, 74]. The predominant argument
is the poor covariance propagation of EKF, which is overcome by using UKF. This ar-
gument, however, does not hold true for observers for continuous non-linear systems.
For discrete systems the EKF makes a state prediction from time point k to time point
k + 1 by applying the non-linear model to the state estimate at k. Prediction of the
covariance matrix is done by linearizing the model at time k in order to propagate the
covariance matrix to time point k + 1. This is shown in Figure 3.26 (a). Figure 3.26 (b)
show the alternative of using the UKF where propagation of the covariance matrix is
done indirectly through the derived sigma points. A way to picture the UKF algorithm
is that the covariance matrix at time point k is disassembled to form the sigma points.
The sigma points are then propagated through the non-linear model, and a new covari-
ance matrix at time point k + 1 is then reconstructed from the sigma points. Non-linear
e’¼Ćects in covariance propagation between time step k and k +1 are then captured in the
sigma point transformation.
The challenge for the EKF in discrete systems is that linear covariance propagation
from k to k + 1 must rely only on the linearization of the model at time point k. This is
di’¼Ćerent in the case of a continuous process model. Here linearization can be performed
at any point between two measuring points k and k + 1. In practice, linearization of
the model is done for each time step of the ODE solver used for solving the coupled
di’¼Ćerential equations of the process model. This greatly improves the linearization ap-
proximation and the accuracy. This is shown in Figure 3.26 (c). The discrete Kalman
’¼ülter is comparable to the continuous-discrete EKF with discrete covariance propagation,
where propagation of the covariance matrix also only relies of the linearization at time
point k. Comparison of EKF-dcp and UKF comply with the conclusions in literature
for discrete systems where UKF should exhibit improved stability.
The conclusion is that no signi’¼ücant advantage is expected from using the unscented
Kalman ’¼ülter, which also corresponds to the results from the qualitative and quantita-
tive analysis. It should be noted that the discussion and comparison of the UKF and
EKF-ccp outlined in Figure 3.26 slightly simpli’¼ües the di’¼Ćerences between the UKF and
EKF. A more detailed discussion is found in Appendix H. This is not included in the
report because the discussion becomes more theoretical than was found relevant for the
overall aim of the report.

All linear ’¼ülters exhibit poor states estimation in scenario 2 because of the simple linear

Implementation and Tuning
For industrial use it is important to address the ease of implementation and tuning of
the observer algorithms. Internal knowhow can be a limiting factor for application of
the ’¼ülters in the case of di’¼ācult implementation.
The challenge of implementation will highly depend on the operating system. However,
implementation of the non-linear ’¼ülters will require incorporation of a solver for non-
linear di’¼Ćerential equations. The results from this case study suggests that non-linear
’¼ülters are of key importance for accurate state estimation. The standard commercial
software used in the biochemical industry is Delta V by Emerson Process Managements
and Sattline by ABB. Older versions of these programs only contain modules for linear
Kalman ’¼ülter implementation, and these would require external programs running the
observer algorithm. Newer versions of the commercial software also include non-linear
modules. Of the non-linear observers, implementation would be most simple for the ex-
tended Kalman ’¼ülters, since the working principle is more similar to the linear observers.
The challenge of tuning the ’¼ülters was found to be largely similar for all linear and
extended Kalman ’¼ülters. The unscented Kalman ’¼ülter has additional tuning parameters

which makes tuning slightly more complicated. As described in Section 3.6.1, the Lu-
enberger observer represents the largest challenge for tuning, as changes to the tuning
parameters were not easily transferable to changes in the observer performance.
3.8
Case Study Conclusion
Based on the results from the case study, it was found that the extended Kalman Fil-
ter with continuous covariance propagation showed the best conditions for use in a
model based observer for bioprocesses. The non-linear dynamics of the observer highly
improved state estimation compared to the linear observers investigated.
Compared
to EKF-dcg, the EKF-ccg showed better stability. Performance of EKF-ccp and UKF
were similar, but combining the slightly better quantitative results, faster computational
speed, and easier tuning, the EKF-ccp is preferred over the UKF.

Chapter 4
Novel Hybrid Asymptotic
Extended Kalman Observer for
Bioprocesses
Hybrid observers are combinations of more than one observer to obtain improved
state estimation. In this chapter a new hybrid observer is proposed which com-
bines mass conservation of the asymptotic observer with noise reduction and
lower constraints on measurement availability of the extended Kalman ’¼ülter.
4.1
Motivation
Case study 1 in Chapter 3 showed that the non-linear Kalman Filters exhibited the best
results dealing with the non-linear dynamics of the bioprocess plant. Also the asymptotic
observer showed promising results as the only observer cabable of accurately estimating
product concentration in scenario 3. However, the case study also revealed a number
of drawbacks. The largest challenges faced by non-linear Kalman ’¼ülters and asymptotic
observers, respectively, are:
Non-linear Kalman Filter
┬ł No guarantee for observer stability.
┬ł High dependence on full process model quality.
┬ł No guarantee for conservation of mass/energy in state estimation.
Asymptotic Observer
┬ł Strict requirements for the number of measured variables.
┬ł No noise reduction for measured variables.
┬ł Rate of convergence is completely determined by dilution rate.
77
==PAGE:96==
The drawbacks for the two classes of observers are to a large degree complementary.
Acknowledging this, it is desired to combine the two classes of observers to uncover if a
combined observer can give better state estimation than each observer individually. By
combining the observers it is attempted to leverage the advantages of one observer to
counteract the drawbacks of the other[75]. Observers based on this principle are known
as hybrid observers. A common type of hybrid observers is a combination of parame-
ter estimation and state estimation, where the Kalman ’¼ülter is augmented with one or
several model parameters[76, 77]. The augmented parameters then become additional
observer states.
The combination of asymptotic observers and Kalman observers to form a hybrid ob-
server for bioprocesses has earlier been proposed by Xavier Hulhoven (2002)[78] and by
Philippe Bogearts (1999)[27]. However, common for both of these observers is the de-
mand for a closed mass balance from direct state measurements, as is also a requirement
for the basic asymptotic observer.
In this chapter a novel hybrid observer speci’¼ücally for bioprocesses is proposed. The
observer is a combination of the extended Kalman ’¼ülter with an extension based on the
principle of the asymptotic observer. The development has been inspired by the concept
of balanceability and calculability of conversion rates, which is described by Hijden et
al. (1994)[79], and by Villadsen et al. (2011)[13]. The observer utilizes the statistical
noise reduction and full state estimation of the extended Kalman ’¼ülter, and additionally
enforces mass conservation for a number of prede’¼üned components. This feature of the
observer exploits that the mass balance model composes the most robust part of a bio-
process model[26]. Finally, the observer greatly bene’¼üts from the fact that the observer
is not limited by the same requirement of minimum number of state measurements which
largely limits the use of the basic asymptotic observer. The requirements for the hybrid
observer is a closed and rigid mass balance around the fermenter and an accurate mass
balance description of the reactions.

Tuning of the hybrid ’¼ülter
Tuning of the hybrid ’¼ülter is more complex than tuning the extended Kalman ’¼ülter. This
is obvious, as a part of the hybrid observer is the EKF-hyb. Tuning the hybrid observer
is done by designing the three matrices R, Q, and W. R and Q are the usual covariance
matrices for the measurement noise and process disturbance, respectively, and are used
to tune the EKF-hyb. Same tuning rules are recommended for the choice of R and Q in
EKF-hyb as for the stand-alone extended Kalman ’¼ülter. The extra tuning parameter is
W for the MBC. The tuning rule for W is that large weights should be put on states with
high con’¼üdence in the estimate provided by the EKF-hyb. E.g. high weight should be
put on states, which are directly measured as it is expected that the estimate provided
by the EKF-hyb will be close to the true value.
It is not believed that the tuning for a stand alone EKF should be di’¼Ćerent from the
EKF-hyb. The EKF-hyb should still be tuned in order to give the best possible estimate
from the available measurements and process model, and the MBC should only come
into e’¼Ćect in cases where the produced estimate does not make physical sense in terms
of mass conservation. One could argue that because the main purpose of MBC is to
87
==PAGE:106==
avoid biases on the process variables, the EKF-hyb should be tuned higher towards re-
duction of noise and less towards unbiased estimate. However, this is not recommended.
Relaxing the tuning of the EKF-hyb to have less noise and higher bias is likely to give
biases in the state estimates which still meet mass balance constraints. An example: If
biomass concentration is overestimated while product concentration is underestimated
the total mass balance can still be met and MBC would not be able to make a correction
towards to biased estimates.
The notion that the tuning of the MBC does not a’¼Ćect tuning of the EKF-hyb sim-
pli’¼ües tuning, as the two parts in the hybrid observer can be tuned sequentially. Tuning
of the EKF-hyb should be done before tuning of the MBC as the tuning of EKF-hyb
can change what variables are associated with the largest trust.
Discussion of the hybrid observer in an industrial setting
Industrial biotechnology and fermentation companies are familiar using mass balances
like carbon and nitrogen balances to work out tank conditions etc. Statistical meth-
ods like the extended Kalman ’¼ülter introduces a very di’¼Ćerent way to approach state
estimation, where the state estimates are not guarantied to yield physically meaningful
results. This could potentially pose a barrier for introducing these methods to operators
who are very used to the methods relying on mass balances. The hybrid observer could
possibly reduce this barrier by combining the statistical state estimate with the rigid
mass balance constraints.
The hybrid observer also has an additional potential for industrial use as it o’¼Ćers a
potential for control engineers to make small adjustments to biased state estimates in
the case where the kinetic model is imprecise.
This can be useful for scale up pur-
poses as it is possible that the fermentation kinetics can change under varying scales.
If the implementation of the model occur on a larger scale (industrial scale) than the
scale used for development (lab/pilot scale), the kinetic parameters might not be exactly
transferable. In the case of stand alone EKF it would be up to model development de-
partments/teams to correct the model, but in the case of the hybrid observer it could be
possible for the front line control engineer to make small adjustments in tuning of the
mass balance correction to make up for the incomplete kinetic model.

Before a model or observer is developed the data should be examined, and noise in the

squared error between the model prediction and the data. The best parameter estima-
tion is achieved for data with low noise and few outliers because the risk of ’¼ütting noise
rather than real data trend is reduced. In this case the dataset is a lab scale dataset,
and from visual inspection it is seen that the level of noise is relatively low. In contrast,

data smoothing and noise reduction becomes increasingly important when working with

ysis (FDA). FDA can also yield an estimate of the level of noise on the measurements.
This is especially useful for tuning a Kalman ’¼ülter, as functional data analysis can yield
an estimate of the covariance matrix R. This is also highly useful in an industrial set-

the producer of a sensor states a noise level, this level can vary due to aging, di’¼Ćerent
con’¼ügurations, or measurement ’¼ültering.

5.3

Developing a single model covering the entire fermentation process is, at best, very time
consuming and possibly not even feasible. It would require insight into when, and how,
the process dynamics change during the metabolic shifts. Alternatively, it is possible
to set up a number of sequential dynamic models, which each represents a part of the
fermentation exhibiting similar dynamics. For the fermentation in this case study it
would be appealing to ascribe individual dynamic models to each of the three identi’¼üed
phases. In this work, only a model for the ’¼ürst active phase will be developed.
The start of the ’¼ürst active phase is de’¼üned by the point where substrate concentra-

active, and the cells are consuming substrate. Alternative de’¼ünitions for start of the
’¼ürst active phase could have been used. For example, it is also natural to link the start
of the ’¼ürst phase to a minimum concentration of biomass, as this also would be an in-
dicator for an active fermentation. However, linking the start of the active phase to an
absolute concentration value is challenging due to the varying initial concentrations in
the experiments. For this reason it is decided to use relative measures as de’¼ünition for
start and end point. The end of the active phase 1 is de’¼üned by the point where the
glucose is lower then 2% of the initial concentration.

Parameter estimation was done using MATLAB software. The build-in MATLAB func-
tion fmincon minimized the error function by changing the model parameters subject
to the constraints speci’¼üed in Table 5.4. fmincon is de’¼üned as a ŌĆÖconstrained non-linear
multivariate optimization algorithmŌĆÖ[87]. Using non-linear optimization algorithms pro-
vides no guaranty for the convergence to the global minimum of the cost function. The
parameter estimate can therefore be highly dependent on the initial guess supplied to
the optimization algorithm. Furthermore, non-linear optimization is a computationally
heavy task due to constant evaluation of the di’¼Ćerential models.
The computational load combined with the convergence to local minima solutions turned
out to pose a signi’¼ücant challenge for the screening of kinetic models. Especially models
with a large number of parameters showed a very high dependency on the initial pa-
rameter estimate. This lead to a reduction in the scope of the model screening. Instead
of screening all 7 models the scope was reduced to screening models 1, 2, and 3. These
three models represent the most common and simple kinetic expressions. Models 4, 5
and 7 are all extensions of model 2 (the Monod model) where a range of inhibitory
e’¼Ćects have been included. The challenge of ’¼ünding reasonable estimates for the large
models can be related to two factors.
1) The large parameter space combined with
the non-linear nature of the optimization increases the likelihood of a large number of
local optima.
2) Correlation of model parameters can complicate a unique parame-
ter estimation. Quantitative analysis to examine these factors lie beyond the scope of
this work, however Appendix K provides a qualitative discussion of possible correlations.

Screening procedure
fmincon using error function 3 showed a large tendency for convergence to non-sense local
optima, and providing a good initial parameter estimate showed to be of key importance.
Error function 1 showed the largest robustness towards convergence to a sensible opti-
mum. Based on this observation it was decided to ’¼ürst run the optimization algorithm
with error function 1. The output from this was then used as an initial estimate for
optimization based on error function 2, and lastly the output from this optimization was
used for optimizing with error function 3. This procedure improved the probability for
convergence of the optimization algorithms.
It is evident that the process of models screening and parameter estimation is an area
which should be considered in future work. Possible areas of investigation are 1) Mapping
of of possible parameter variation in order to further limit the allowed parameters span,
2) analyzing models in order to clearly identify parameter correlation in cost function,
3) optimizing initial parameter guesses for optimization algorithm.

5.6
Discussion
The results from this case study give a good indication of the potential for model based
observer application. Validation cases for LGC1 and LGC4 both showed a better per-
formance by application of the EKF observer than by applying the dynamic model
without an observer. In validation case LGC4 it was possible to estimate 2 out of 3
states with less than 15 % error. These are believed to be very reasonable results taking
into account that a simple dynamic model has been used, with a limited amount of data.
For the validation case of LGC1, the estimation error was between 25 % and 60 %
which are relatively large, however these errors are half the errors obtained from the
model alone. Furthermore, it was possible to capture the correct qualitative trends of
the data, which are absent in the dynamic model.
The calibration case of LGC4 gave estimation errors as low as 5 %.
These results,
however, are not a fair representation of the performance. The good results were due to
the good model quality obtained by ’¼ütting the model to the data which was estimated.
This would not be possible in a real observer application. The calibration case of LGC4
also showed that it was not possible to achieve better estimates of the unmeasured states
than was already provided from the pure prediction from the model. This indicates that
simply having a good ’¼üt between the model and data does not necessarily allow for
good observer performance. If the model does not incorporate the appropriate system
dynamics, it will not be possible for the observer to use the measurements to get a better
estimation of unmeasured states. This is the case in calibration case LGC4, as the results
obtained from the pure prediction are in fact better than the observer estimates. By
not including the results from calibration case LGC4, 15 % estimation error is therefore
believed to be the best reliable results obtained.
If it can be shown that model based observers consistently provide state estimates with
an average error of maximum 15 %, they can be highly useful in the ’¼üeld of process mon-
itoring, and possibly for integrated control. Monitoring using model based observers can
give qualitative and quantitative process insight which can be used for fault detection
and process optimization. Whether state estimation with 15 % error is good enough for
integrated control purposes will highly depend on the what control strategy is considered.
The case results stress the importance of thorough model development. This case shows
how far it is possible to go, by mainly using data driven methods and little process and
product insight. It is believed that better performance could be achieved with more
available data as this would enable better parameter estimation. However, as mentioned
above, the performance can only be improved by better parameter estimation if the
model structure is a good representation of the actual process. This can limit the po-
tential of solely using data driven methods. The need of good basic model structure
suggests incorporating process and product knowledge in the model development. For
application of model based observers in industry there is no question that the largest
113

e’¼Ćort is going to be the development of an accurate dynamic model.
The Hybrid Observer
In this case study the hybrid observer showed an inferior performance compared to the
stand-alone EKF. It su’¼Ćered from a poor mass balance description, and appropriate
tuning of the observer was di’¼ācult. This brings the question: Under which conditions
can/should the hybrid observer be used?
A good description of the mass balance of the system is of key importance to the hybrid
observer. From the graphs showing the carbon balance in Section 5.5 and Appendix
N it is clear that the derived mass balances are inaccurate. In every case the ŌĆØdata
carbon balanceŌĆØŌĆĀ deviated from the 100 % line which represents the initial carbon in the
fermenter.
When the ŌĆØdata carbon balanceŌĆØ deviated from 100 %, the EKF was often found to
show the same trend. This indicates that the EKF is able to respond to deviations in
the data from the mass balance of the model. This is an interesting observation, as this
exactly relates to issue of the Kalman ’¼ülters which is trying to be avoided using the
hybrid observer. The issue that Kalman ’¼ülters can violate the model mass balances can
also assists in a better state estimation in the case where the real process itself does not
comply with the de’¼üned mass balances in the model. This leads to a discussion about
the robustness of the mass balance equation of the model. In order to use the hybrid
observer, the mass balance must be robust enough for the establishment of a hard con-
straint for the state estimate. If this is not the case, the hybrid observer should not be
used, as it can impose biases to correct state estimates.
Tuning of the hybrid observer was a challenge in this case study. Ideal conditions for
the hybrid observer is a case where the number of states exposed to biased prediction is
equal to the number of mass balance constraints in the MBC. These conditions for the
hybrid observer are similar to the requirements for the asymptotic observer, and this
brings the application areas for the two observers very close. The hybrid observer can,
however, also be used in the case where the mass balance can be closed through a reli-
able Kalman ’¼ülter estimate. This is the case in case study 1, where the hybrid observer
exhibited good performance. Here substrate was directly measured and the biomass was
accurately estimated through the dissolved oxygen measurement, which enabled a good
estimate of the product through mass balancing. The closing of the mass balance with
high con’¼üdence estimates and direct measurements is likely to be a requirement for good
performance of the hybrid observer.

performance. The closing of the mass balance with high con’¼üdence estimates and direct
measurements is possibly a requirement for good performance of the hybrid observer.

Proposed Future Work
The proposed future work aims to address the question of necessary model quality for
observer design. Case study 1 showed that by correct placement of measurements, a
dynamic model could be reduced without this largely a’¼Ćecting the state estimates. It
is, however, unsure to what extended it is necessary to capture the true internal dynam-
ics of the mass balance and kinetic expressions. Case study 1 and case study 2 both
showed that the performance of the model based observer is highly dependent on the
quality of the model. Future work could be focused towards quantifying this dependency.
A aim for future work could be to include additional studies of experimental data.
Especially inclusion of industrial data would be highly interesting, however intellectual
property rights can pose a limitation.
An alternative approach could include a sensitivity analysis. This could give a quanti-
’¼üed picture of how highly the observer performance depend on changes in certain model
parameters. An extensive benchmark model useful for this could be the PenSim model,
which is a widely used benchmark model for penicillin production[88]

Chapter 7
Conclusion
In this thesis the application of model based observers for monitoring of biochemical pro-
cesses has been investigated. A large range of observer algorithms are available including
linear, non-linear, and mass balance observers as well as the combination of these into
hybrid observers. A thorough literature survey provided a fundamental understanding
of the mathematical and theoretical basis for a range of these observers. The literature
review also showed a strong academic trend towards the use of Kalman ’¼ülters. Kalman
’¼ülters have therefore had the main focus in this project.
A benchmarking case study showed that application of linear observers was imprac-
tical for monitoring biochemical batch and fed-batch processes. The linear observers
were unable to accurately capture the non-linear dynamics which led to poor state esti-
mation. Non-linear observers based on Kalman ’¼ülter theory were found to provide the
best state estimates under in a range of scenarios including the assumption of noisy mea-
surements. Among the non-linear observers, the continuous-discrete extended Kalman
’¼ülter with discrete covariance propagation showed the best performance. The non-linear
model was used in prediction of the state estimate as well as the state estimate covari-
ance matrix. However, limitations in the performance of the extended Kalman ’¼ülter were
evident in the large dependency of the full non-linear model, including the biochemical
reaction kinetics which are considered to be the least robust part of biochemical models.
Based on the experience gained from the benchmark case, combined with the funda-
mental understanding from the literature survey, a novel hybrid model based observer
was proposed. The hybrid observer combines the extended Kalman ’¼ülter with a mass
balance extension inspired by the asymptotic observer. The observer utilizes the well
founded presumption that the mass balance model constitutes the most robust part of
biochemical models. The requirements for applying the hybrid observer is a closed mass
balance around the fermenter together with an accurate mass balance description of
the reactions. Performance of the hybrid observer on the benchmarking case showed a
superior performance compared to the extended Kalman ’¼ülter.
119
==PAGE:138==
The applicability of the observer algorithms on experimental data was examined through
a case study considering a Saccharomyces cerevisiae fermentation. This case study in-
cluded data treatment and model development as part of the observer design. Data
cleaning was done through the use of functional data analysis which also was used to
provide an estimate of the measurement noise used for observer tuning. The performance
of the model based observers were found to be highly dependent on the quality of the
dynamic model. Model development mainly relied on a data driven approach, where the
choice of kinetic model relied on model screening. Results from the case study showed
that using a model based observer, 2 out of 3 unmeasured states could be estimated
with an average relative bias of 15 %. This result was obtained where the observer was
developed and tuned without using the estimated data.
The proposed hybrid observer showed disappointing results for estimation of experi-
mental data. This was especially related to the poor mass balance model. The hybrid
observer highly relies on a robust mass balance, and in the experimental data case, the
robustness of the kinetic model and the mass balance model was of the same order. For
this case, the extended Kalman ’¼ülter showed an overall better performance. It must
therefore be concluded that the hybrid observer has stricter conditions for good perfor-
mance than the extended Kalman ’¼ülter. The hybrid observer should only be used in the
case where the conditions are met.
Altogether, this thesis concludes that there is a potential for the application of model
based observers in biochemical processes. However, the scope of this potential will be
closely linked to the e’¼Ćort of adequate dynamic model development.

This appendix continuous the discussion about the di’¼Ćerences between the unscented
Kalman ’¼ülter (UKF) applied in case study 1, and the continuous-discrete extended
Kalman ’¼ülter with continuous covariance propagation (EKF-ccp).
It is stated in Section 3.7.1 that UKF and EKF-ccp are expected to have similar simula-
tion results because the covariance matrix is discretized at every time point of the ODE
solver, which is in contrast to the extended Kalman ’¼ülter for discrete application. The
argument is a bit more complicated than stated here which is elaborated in the following.
Covariance propagation of the continuous-discrete extended Kalman ’¼ülter relies on the
same equations as shown for the linear Kalman-Bucy ’¼ülter. The only di’¼Ćerence is that
’¼ürst order approximation linearization of the non-linear function is used to get the system
transformation matrix, A. Propagation of P is done by solving the following ordinary
di’¼Ćerential equation.
dP
dt = A(t)P + PA(t)T + Q
(H.1)
It is seen that A(t) is a function of time because linearization occur at every solver time
step. However, it should be remembered that even though A(t) is changing over time
does it only capture linear e’¼Ćects! This is the reason why the unscented Kalman ’¼ülters
is proposed and also are expected to perform slightly better than the EKF. Because
here the covariance is used to generate the sigma points chosen such that the density
captures exactly the ’¼ürst and second moment of the true state estimate. By propagating
the sigma points through the non-linear transformation the moments are also accurately
propagated, and the covariance can then be reconstructed from the sigma points. In
contrast to EKF the UKF does not have drawbacks when usual assumptions about the
linear ’¼ülters are lost. This happens in cases with correlated error terms, correlation be-
tween errors and state, and error terms with non-zero expectation. In these cases UKF
is superior to CD-EKF[90].

==PAGE:160==
However, it should be remembered that this is only in the case where ’¼ürst order lin-
earity is a poor estimate between solver time steps in the ODE solver. The algorithm
usually implemented in ODE solvers is a Runge Kutta algorithm.
By changing the
solver from a Runge Kutta solver to a standard Euler algorithm the CD-EKF is actually
assumed to perform equally as good as the UKF. Because in this case the non-linear
state propagation is done using simple ’¼ürst order linearization, which is the same which
is used for covariance propagation. Euler methods of course have the disadvantage that
extremely small steps can be needed for accurate solutions.
The conclusion is that the UKF in fact theoretically is assumed to give a slightly better
