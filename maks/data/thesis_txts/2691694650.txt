Abstract
A path planning method for self-reconﬁguring modular robots is presented. Real-
world robots of this type are still in the very early stage of development. The method
presented here isn’t yet applicable to those real-world microbots, but may however
be in the future. Hence, a simulator is implemented to test the method on ensembles
of virtual modules, which will be referred to as micro robots or microbots for short.
What makes this method diﬀerent from other existing methods is the fact that the
microbots aren’t ﬁxed to the shape of a grid, all microbots move at the same time
and also mechanical stability of the ensemble at all times is taken into account. The
method is based on the divide and conquer method. In this case divide and conquer
is performed over physical time, i.e. the path planning problem is recursively divided
into two smaller path planning problems and are then recombined in the end. The
method is tested on diﬀerent virtual test setups, and while the results seem smooth
and mechanically stable, the implementation is only able to handle in the order
of 100 microbots within a couple of minutes.
For real-world applications where
enormous amounts of microbots are needed, the method would need to be much
faster. It could however be very useful for other types of robots, not necessarily
made out of tiny disconnected modules. Already existing robots like robotic arms
could take advantage of the method, since they don’t consist of as many movable
parts.

1
Introduction
Micro robots (or microbots for short) refer to robots on the micrometer scale. In this
thesis we’ll be looking at virtual microbots represented as spheres. Theoretically the
actual size of the them doesn’t matter, but in reality since one would usually consider
a swarm of microbots, it makes sense to think of each individual as being small. I’ve
worked with virtual microbots before[23], where I constructed an algorithm capable
of ﬁguring out how each individual microbot should move in order for the entire
ensemble of microbots to reach a destination. The project was originally inspired by
the movie Big Hero 6 and the animation technique used in it[19]. In this project I’ll
reﬁne the algorithm to achieve more physically correct results. E.g. the ensemble of
microbots should be mechanically stable.
Hence the the goal of this project is to model the formation of a structure of mi-
crobots, while at all times keeping the structure stable. The system of microbots
will be modeled as a truss structure.
1.1
Motivation
Microbots will make it possible to make structures in a similar fashion to what 3D
printers can. That is, microbots can collectively be seen as a kind of 3D printer. A
key diﬀerence between microbots and other types of 3D printers is that the microbots
don’t need to have been built for a speciﬁc type of structure. E.g. there exist 3D
printers that are good at printing bridges[22], but not necessarily good at printing
other types of structures. In principle microbots can construct any shape. Another
advantage of microbots is that they would be much faster at making a construction
than traditional 3D printers. The only thing that each microbot needs to do is
ﬁnding a way to its destination, and as soon as every microbot has done this, the
structure is complete. The structures that the microbots create don’t need to be
permanent either. E.g. you could imagine that one would need a scaﬀolding for a
period of time. The microbots would then be able to construct a scaﬀolding, and
when it weren’t needed anymore they would deconstruct it just as easily.
That
would make the scaﬀolding easier to put up and tear down as well as transport.
One of the downsides of microbots is that you need a lot of them. Since the precision
depends on how small they are, the more detailed you need the structure to be the
more microbots you need. So creating coarse structures will be a more feasible use
case than creating ﬁne structures.
Also, real-world microbots will probably be very diﬃcult to construct. Every single
microbot has to be able to move and attach to nearby microbots somehow. This also
means that all microbots has to have some sort of energy supply. The microbots also
need to know exactly where in space they are located and where they are heading
at every point in time.
So it will probably be a long time before we’ll see real microbots. But when that
time comes we need a way of ﬁguring out how they should move in order to reach
a certain target structure. That’s exactly what this project is about: Given the
1
==PAGE:6==
current positions of all microbots, which directions should they each move in to
make sure that the ensemble of microbots arrives at the given destination.
Even though real-world microbots don’t currently exist, it’s still relevant to develop
methods for how the path planning would work, if they did. If good algorithms
have already been developed for a technology that doesn’t exist yet, the incentive
to actually ﬁgure out how to build them will be much bigger, since one can be sure
that if we manage to build them, they will be very useful. Without good algorithms
they won’t have many useful applications.
The potential applications of microbots are plenty.
Just like traditional 3D printers, microbots can be used for rapid prototyping.
Whereas traditional 3D printers have only one nozzle though which all matter has
to be extruded, an ensemble of microbots just have to reconﬁgure which makes them
orders of magnitudes faster.
You could imagine having a 3D holographic display consisting of microbots. Just
like a traditional screen consisting of 2D pixels, this kind of display would consist of
microbots forming a 3D image.
Since this 3D display is made up of physical microbots you could potentially make
it interactive: a display where you can touch, feel and move the represented objects.
This would make design processes more seamless.
Structures made of microbots could be very resilient to environmental disturbances.
If e.g. a damage occurs at some part of a structure, since it’s made up of microbots,
it would would be able to easily repair itself.
Microbots could be used for all sorts of transportation. Instead of being limited
to railroads, highways or bicycle paths, an ensemble of microbots could potentially
move in any environment completely eliminating the need for roads. As long as
there’s something to hold on to or stand upon, the ensemble can move in the desired
direction, and at the same time be able to carry a payload.
Microbots would be ideal in space exploration. Since they can form arbitrary shapes,
they can easily be ﬁtted inside a space rocket, and upon arrival they can be put to
use. Since they could potentially reconﬁgured indeﬁnitely, once they had served one
purpose they could be reused for another purpose. This would make the overall
amount of mass that would be needed in space much smaller, hence making it
cheaper to send rockets into space.
When making constructions today, you often need some sort of support structure or
scaﬀolding. With microbots, this won’t be needed, since they are self supporting.
The microbots create their own support structure, and when the construction is
completed, they can easily dismantle the scaﬀolding, which can then be reused
elsewhere.
Microbots will also be useful in exploring unknown environments, e.g. caves or the
surface of other planets.
Since the microbots can reconﬁgure, the layout of the
environment doesn’t have to be known in advance.
2
==PAGE:7==
This would also be useful in search and rescue, since in these cases usually the
”environment” isn’t known in advance. E.g. someone could be trapped underneath
a collapsed building, and in order to get them out, you would need to be able to
move through the rubble quickly.
Microbots could also be useful in medical applications. If small enough, they would
be able to enter the body through a blood vessel and after reaching the desired place
in the body, they would be able to do all sorts of operations, e.g. heal a wound, inject
drugs, remove a blood clot etc.
Besides being useful in many applications, a key advantage of microbots is that they
are reusable. This means that they potentially could be very cheap and sustainable,
since you don’t need to keep producing enormous amounts of them.
1.2
Related work
In the literature we ﬁnd grid based methods for designing optimal structures[3] but
these are not suitable for ﬁguring out how microbots would have to construct a
stable shape. A particle based method would ﬁt better with a system of microbots.
We also ﬁnd methods for generating structures using moving particles[16], but the
result is still a grid structure.
Instead, we can model the system of microbots as a truss structure. A truss structure
is a mechanical structure made up of a network of trusses. If we represent a truss
structure as a graph, the trusses would be the edges, and the nodes would be where
the trusses meet. When modeling an ensemble of microbots as a truss structure,
each node of the graph represents a microbot and each connection represents a pair
of microbots in contact. Plenty of methods for generating optimal truss structures
exist[20;29;30]. We just need a way of keeping the system of microbots stable while
the construction occurs.
There exist methods for staged construction[1] and additive manufacturing[28], that
also take the process of construction into consideration, but these methods are made
for grid based structures and not truss structures.
Programmable matter is the term used for objects that can be programmed to change
their physical properties[15]. Using an ensemble of modular robots, one can obtain
a type of programmable matter referred to as self-reconﬁguring modular robots, i.e.
robots that are made up of modules that can move around changing the overall
structure of the robot. Claytronics refers to a class of self-reconﬁguring modular
robots that can change shape to take the appearance of a 3D object, just like you
can shape a lump of clay into almost any shape. Claytronic robots consist of a large
number of small modules called catoms (claytronic atoms). Every catom can attach
to its surrounding catoms forming a large structure. Since the size of these catoms
are so small, we can also refer to them as a type of microbots, i.e. robots on the
micrometer scale.
Claytronics doesn’t exist in the real world yet, but is a thing of the future. There are
various challenges involved in claytronics that need to be addressed before claytronics
3
==PAGE:8==
can be realized, e.g. how to localize every robot[14], how to communicate with the
robots to tell them what to do, how to distribute power throughout the entire
structure of microbots, how should microbots attach to their neighbors and, do you
plan which path each microbot has to take and how to ensure that the structure of
microbots is mechanically stable and won’t collapse.
Real-world prototypes of self-reconﬁguring modular robots have been made, some
of which consist of cylindrical robots and hence only operate in 2D[5]. These cylin-
drical robots use magnetism to attach to neighboring modules, and they also have
the ability to distribute power among each other. Others are able to move in 3D
space and attach to neighbors using mechanical latches[2].
However the sizes of
these real-world examples of modules are at the cm-scale and not the µm-scale, and
their movement is slow and unstable, so we’re still far from achieving real-world
claytronics. One of the challenges that needs to be addressed before we can achieve
real-world claytronics is that of path planning. That is, we need a way of ﬁguring out
how each individual microbot needs to move in order to make the ensemble change
shape. This is not a trivial problem and might sound a lot easier than it is. If you
want a microbot to move in some direction, you can’t have other microbots blocking
the way. Also, a microbot always needs to have other neighboring microbots that it
can attach to in order to move. Multiple attempts at designing a method for path
planning have been proposed[13;7;11;12]. Many of these methods only operate with
microbots arranged in lattice-structures. This means that they are very limited in
real-world use. They aren’t very resilient to external disturbances. E.g. if a part of
the structure were to be turned over making an angle with the ground that isn’t ex-
actly 90 degrees, we would no longer have the same axis-aligned lattice-structure and
reconnecting with the rest of the microbots would not be easy. Most methods also
don’t consider the problem of mechanical stability, i.e. the structure of microbots
needs to be able to support itself. One problem may be that the center of mass of
the ensemble is outside of the area in which it needs to be, which would make the
ensemble turn over. Another problem is that if one of the parts of the structure is
carrying more weight than it is able to without breaking the entire structure might
collapse. One article presents a method of ﬁguring out whether a certain structure
of microbots is stable enough which is useful if you already have a path planned and
want to check that it is mechanically stable[18]. However this method can’t be used
to actually ﬁnd the paths needed to be traversed by the microbots. Another article
presents a simulator for ensembles of robots, but this too is just that: a simulator[6].
It’s not able to perform the path planning. Hence, a method for ﬁnding such a path
which is mechanically stable is needed.
A key diﬀerence between most methods in the literature and the method presented
here is that this method is global, i.e. a solution for the entire ensemble is computed
in one master computer and afterwards communicated to the microbots, whereas
most other methods are decentralized, and each microbot computes where it has
to go while only communicating with its neighbors. The result of a local method
will be an emergent behavior of microbots creating the movement of the entire
ensemble.
This is similar to how multicellular organisms work (e.g. the human
body), where no master cell is in control of the entire body, but instead all cells
4
==PAGE:9==
have their own limited functions that collectively create an emergent phenomenon
called a human. Ant colonies is another example of emergent behavior in nature.
Every ant has its own set of simple rules that it follows, but the behavior of the
entire colony becomes seemingly intelligent, forming an underground network of
rooms and overground paths leading to food sources. In both of these examples
(multicellular organisms and ant colonies) the ensemble is made up of modules that
aren’t completely identical. Some cells are skin cells, some are brain cells and so on,
while some ants are worker ants, some are warrior ants and some are queen ants.
These types of ensembles of modules are called heterogeneous, while ensembles of
identical modules are called homogeneous[11].
In homogeneous self-reconﬁguring
robots it doesn’t matter where each individual microbot ends up as long as all
microbots end up in a goal location. We don’t care if on of the microbots swapped
place with another one. In heterogeneous systems however, it’s important that each
microbot ends up at a speciﬁc location, or in one of a select number of locations.
E.g. if some of the microbots have a certain type of sensor, we might want this
robot to be placed at a speciﬁc location where it is able to make a measurement.
The method presented here is made for completely heterogeneous systems, i.e. all
microbots need to be assigned an individual goal location for the method to work.
The assignment is not part of the method, but of course it could be extended to
handle assignment as well by e.g. minimizing the sum of squares of distances. Then
it could be used for homogeneous systems as well.
With a global method, instead of an emergent behavior, the entire solution to the
problem is computed on one central computer, so the solution is known even before
the robots start to move. The advantage of a global method is that you can be
sure that you don’t have any conﬂicts between microbots, e.g. one microbot might
try to take a speciﬁc path, but another one wants to take the same path thereby
blocking the ﬁrst microbot. With local methods you can easily get stuck in a certain
conﬁguration where no microbot is able to move. A disadvantage to global methods
however, is that the computational complexity grows quickly with the number of
microbots, and the instructions from the master computer need to be delivered to
all microbots, which also gets harder the more microbots there are. It might be
possible to distribute the computation of a global solution among the microbots
thereby mitigating these challenges. One article presents a programming language
dedicated to programming ensembles of robots[8]. However the focus of this project
is on how a global method for path planning would be constructed assuming it’s
possible to have a central computer that coordinates all movement.
In my previous work, the objective was to generate an animation of spherical mi-
crobots given a start and end conﬁguration. The constraints were simple: (1) over-
laps between microbots are not allowed, (2) the entire structure should be connected,
i.e. if we represent the conﬁguration of microbots as a graph where each microbot is
a node and a pair of microbots is connected if they touch, then the resulting graph
should be a connected graph. Here we also count the ground as a node. Besides
these two constraints the acceleration of the microbots were minimized. More ac-
curately, the sum of squares of accelerations of all microbots and all points in time
were minimized.
5
==PAGE:10==
The method used to solve this problem was also very simple: Start with the start and
end conﬁgurations. Then take the midpoint between the two and make sure that it
is a valid conﬁguration. Thus the problem has been divided into two subproblems:
(1) how to get from the start conﬁguration to the new middle conﬁguration and (2)
how to get from the middle conﬁguration to the end conﬁguration. I.e. a method
based on divide and conquer. In this case it was divide and conquer over time: each
time interval is divided into two smaller ones, and when we have enough subdivisions
the time intervals are combined to create an animation.

world. That’s the problem were going to look at in the following: how do you ﬁgure
out where the microbots should move while at all times maintaining mechanical
stability?
Speciﬁcally we’re going to consider the mechanical compliance of the
8

system of microbots. In short, by minimizing compliance, we maximize stability.
Another thing is that the previous method only considers completely ﬂat environ-
ments. While ﬂat environments do exist in the real world (ﬂoors, tables, roads, etc.),
most environments aren’t. So in order to make the method more useful, we’re also
going to look at how microbots could handle obstacles placed in a scene. For this
we’ll use a path ﬁnding algorithm.
In order to solve these challenges the ﬁrst thing we’ll do is to take a more mathe-
matical look at it. This will enable us to deﬁne rigorously what the problem is and
thereby we’ll be able to construct a method that solves it.

2
Problem analysis
Picture a ﬂoor with a bunch of tiny robots on it. These robots have arms in all
directions. This allows them to crawl along the ﬂoor or attach to other robots.
Let’s say that these robots are all controlled by a remote computer. E.g. if the
computer tells robot number 3141 to move 1 cm to the right, it will immediately do
that. If the robot is told to attach to a nearby robot and crawl on top if it, it will
do that too. So by controlling the central computer, you are able to control what
every robot does.
But a robot only understands simple instructions, like ”move 1cm to the right”. So
what if we want to give a robot a more complex instruction, like: ”ﬁnd your way
to robot number 3142”, or if we want to give all robots a collective instruction, like
”make a robot pyramid” - just like humans can make a human pyramid - then how
would we tell the robots what to do using only simple instructions that they can
actually understand?
If we knew the exact paths that the robots should take in order to make a robot
pyramid, then we would also know exactly what instructions to give each robot.
E.g. if we could tell robot number 3143 that during the ﬁrst tenth of a second it
should move 1 cm to the right, then during the second tenth of a second it should
move 1 cm forward, then after the third tenth of a second it should move upwards
by holding on to robot number 3144, and so on. By somehow converting a complex
instruction like ”make a pyramid” to simple instructions, we could tell each robot
exactly what to do in order to make the ensemble of robots make a robot pyramid.
So the question is: how do we convert a complex instruction to simple instructions
that robots can understand?
If we could somehow compute the exact locations for all robots at all times from
start to ﬁnish, then we would automatically know in which direction each robot
would have to move at any given time. So by ﬁnding the paths that all robots
should take, we can easily compute the instructions, that the robots need. But now
the question is then: given a complex instruction, how do we compute the exact
paths, that the robots should take?
Let’s start by deﬁning what ”a complex instruction” actually means. ”make a pyra-
mid” is an example of a complex instruction, but we could imagine other structures
that we might want the robots to form. So a complex instruction can be something
on the form: ”make this shape” where ”this shape” can be any conﬁguration of
robots. More speciﬁcally the wanted locations of all robots could be speciﬁed. E.g.
”robot number 1 should be in location (0,0,0), robot number 2 should be in location
(1,0,0), robot number 3 should be in location (1,2,3), ...” and so on for all robots.
So now the question is: given the wanted locations of all robots, how do we compute
the exact paths, that they need to take to get there?

2.1
Physical constraints
Before we can state the problem mathematically, we need to consider how we are
limited by the laws of physics.
Let’s start by deﬁning an idealized version of a
microbot that’s not too hard to model. The shape of a microbot is going to be
a perfect sphere. This isn’t an absurd idealization, since if the microbot should
happen to not be shaped like a sphere in the real-world, a sphere can still be used
as a bounding sphere, making sure that overlaps won’t occur. Also we’ll say that
microbots can have an arbitrary number of connections to to other microbots, and
that they are able to attach and detach instantly. In the real world, the connections
might be formed by electromagnets placed on the surface of the microbots. This
would probably be closer to the idealized version of the microbot than if the connec-
tion mechanism would be made of mechanical latches, since these would need time
to lock and unlock and perhaps the number of possible connections would be low.
For simplicity, we’ll also say that the microbots are able to attach to the ground
in the same way as with other microbots. In the real world, this could correspond
to a ﬂoor covered in programmable electromagnets. Finally we’re going to say that
all of the mass of the microbot is located in the center of the sphere. Hence, we
don’t need to consider the eﬀects of angular momentum. Now, even though this
is an idealized version of a microbot, we still have some limits. For example, two
robots can’t move through each other or below the ground, and they can’t attach to
each other if they’re too far apart. They can’t teleport, and they can’t ﬂy (perhaps
the are able to hover slightly if the connections are magnetic, but only at a limited
distance).
Mathematically speaking, not being able to teleport, means that there are no dis-
continuities in the function t 7→p(t) that maps from time t to the position of a
microbot at that time p(t). But as we’ll see later, we’re going to solve the problem
numerically, which means that the requirement of being continuous isn’t going to
have any impact on the solution. Also we don’t want inﬁnitely large accelerations,
so we’ll try to somehow minimize the accelerations of the robots at every time slice
of the solution. More speciﬁcally, the total sum of squared accelerations. By taking
the squares of the accelerations, we make sure that large accelerations are penalized.
Not being able to ﬂy is a bit more tricky to deﬁne. What we actually mean, is
that Newton’s laws of motion should apply. 1: If a robot isn’t touching anything, it
should move in a straight line until aﬀected by some force. In this case we have a
constant downward force due to gravity, so the robot should have a constant down-
ward acceleration. 2: The sum of forces applied to a robot should be proportional
to the acceleration. 3: All forces should should be accompanied by an equally big
and opposite force. E.g. if a robot pushes on another robot, the robot itself will be
pushed in the opposite direction.
But if we think about it, these constraints aren’t suﬃcient. Let’s say that the robots
have made the shape of a leaning tower. A tower that leans a lot. Let’s say 45◦.
This tower obviously won’t be very stable and would probably collapse before it
had even been built. But it still doesn’t violate any of the constraints mentioned
above. All of Newton’s laws are satisﬁed, since all robots are directly or indirectly in
11
==PAGE:16==
contact with the ground. The ground and all robots can in theory apply inﬁnitely
big forces. I.e. if we made the tower of an inﬁnitely strong material it wouldn’t
collapse. But since we don’t have an inﬁnitely strong material lying around, this
problem will need to be incorporated into the mathematical constraints.
What we’ll do, is to model the entire structure of robots as a truss structure. I.e.
whenever a pair of robots are connected we’ll model that connection as if it were a
truss. These trusses all have a stiﬀness, that says how much force it takes to deform
the truss. In the example with the leaning tower, the trusses on the inner side are
going to be compressed, while the trusses on the outer side of the tower are going to
be stretched. Hence, in this example the trusses in the structure are experiencing a
large deformation, and this is a sign that the structure might be unstable.

What we want is to make sure that no single truss either buckles, i.e. if compressed
a truss might experience such a big stress that it starts to bend perpendicular
to the loading direction, or yields, i.e. if the stress becomes too big the material
will no longer undergo elastic deformation but instead plastic deformation, hence
permanently damaging the truss, or in the worst case break it apart. Basically,
there are two ways to make sure that this doesn’t happen. Either make the material
stiﬀer and stronger or make the stress smaller. If we assume that we’ve already built
the microbots and they make connections with speciﬁc material properties that we

can’t change, the only thing to do is make the stress smaller. Since stress and strain
are proportional during elastic deformation, this is equivalent to making the strain
and hence deformation smaller.
What we’re actually going to minimize is the compliance of the system, which is a
measure of the amount of work done on the system by external forces. For a single
truss this work W will be equal to the strain energy:

times strain, and since stress and strain are proportional in an elastic material, we
also minimize both the stress and the strain on every truss. To keep things simple,
we’ll minimize the total compliance of the entire structure, instead of e.g. minimizing
the maximum compliance of all trusses.
In this project we will not take air resistance into account. So when a robot is
far away from all other robots and the ground, only gravity is acting on it. We’ll
also simplify by saying that the ground has inﬁnite mass. This makes it possible to
accelerate a robot without necessarily accelerating something else in the opposite
direction.

3
Path planning
What we want is a method that given the start and end conﬁgurations returns the
conﬁgurations for all time slices. I.e. planning the paths of all microbots.
3.1
The naive method
To solve this problem, the ﬁrst idea that comes to mind might be as follows: Start
at the ﬁrst time slice. To compute the conﬁguration for the next time slice, move
all microbots a tiny amount in the direction towards their respective destinations.
Then make corrections until the constraints are satisﬁed. E.g. if any of the microbots
overlap, move then apart. When this is done, we repeat this process to get the next
time slice and so on until all microbots have reached their destinations.
However, this method doesn’t work in general. Consider an example where we have
two separate towers of microbots: one to the left and one to the right. All of these
microbots already are where they should be. I.e. the start position of each microbot
is the same as its destination. Let’s say that on top of the left tower, there’s a
microbot whose destination is on the top of the right tower. How would the naive
method handle this setup?
Figure 6: Two pillars consisting of 4 and 3 microbots respectively. The top left
microbot has to get to the top of the other pillar. This is not going to be possible if
the microbot tries to move in a straight line, since there would be no other microbots
to support it.
First, the microbot on top of the left tower would be moved slightly to the right,
since its destination is in this direction. Then, if the microbot is still on top of the
left tower and hence still stable, we have found the conﬁguration for the next time
slice. This will be repeated until the microbot has moved so far to the right, that
it isn’t on top of the left tower anymore. Now, it’s just over the edge of the left
tower, and hence would fall down if we didn’t correct the conﬁguration. In this case,
correcting the conﬁguration might involve moving the microbot slightly to the left,
so that it’s again on top of the left tower. But in this case the naive method would
stall. The microbot would be moved slightly to the right, but then corrected slightly
to the left, eﬀectively leaving it in place.

==PAGE:22==
However, a correction isn’t limited to moving just the microbot that’s on top of the
left tower. All microbots making up the left tower could also be moved. We could
e.g. move the entire left tower slightly to the right to support the top microbot. The
naive method might do this, and hence it wouldn’t stall immediately. But when we
now want to compute the conﬁguration for the next time slice, we again encounter
a problem. Since the left tower is slightly more to the right than it’s destination
(which is where it started), all microbots that make up the left tower would be
moved back to where they were before, while the top microbot would continue to
move slightly to the right. Since the top microbot now isn’t supported by anything,
the correction step would have to move it to the left, and hence stalling the naive
method.
In short, the problem with the naive method is that the microbots don’t cooperate.
In the example above, the top microbot wants to move to the right. But it can’t
unless the left tower also moves to the right. But the left tower wants to stay in
place. Hence, the top microbot can never reach the top of the other tower. What
we need is a method that somehow can coordinate the movements of all microbots,
thereby avoiding deadlocks.
3.2
Pathﬁnding
Another idea might be to use pathﬁnding to ﬁgure out the bots’ paths.
In the
previous example with the two static towers and a single bot on top of the left
tower, a pathﬁnding approach would ﬁnd a path going down the left tower, across
the ground and ﬁnally up the right tower. So initially this method seems to avoid
the pitfalls of the naive method.
But like the naive method, the pathﬁnding method doesn’t work in general. Let’s
look at another example. Take for example a 3 × 3 × 3 box of robots, and let’s say
that the robot in the middle of this box has to get out. How would it ever be able to
do this, if the other robots just want to stay in place? This problem is not solvable
by either the naive nor the pathﬁnding method.

Both the native
method and the pathﬁnding method only considers each robot in isolation. What
we need is a method that can coordinate the movements of all robots.

4
Correction
Using the divide and conquer method, the hardest part is the ”correction” step. I.e.
after the middle conﬁguration has been computed, it needs to be corrected in order
to live up to the constraints posed by the problem. Since we want to map from
any conﬁguration of microbots to one which lives up the constraints we could also
have called it the projection step. I.e. we project from the unconstrained space to a
constrained subspace of conﬁgurations.
The correction involves 3 diﬀerent parts:
• Minimizing compliance
• Minimizing acceleration
• Resolving overlap
What’s going to happen is that after a subdivision has been performed, each of these
3 parts are run a number of times until the microbots settle at a ﬁnal conﬁguration.
I.e. we run the compliance minimization part a number of times, then the accelera-
tion minimization part a number of times and ﬁnally the overlap resolution part a
number of times.
In each of these steps, the microbots’ positions are moved a tiny bit in the right
direction. In the compliance minimization part the gradient of the compliance is
computed and then gradient descent is run. In the acceleration minimization part,
the microbots are moved slightly towards the positions that would minimize the
accelerations. In the overlap resolution part, we’re going to make sure that no pair
of microbots overlap. Hence, we’ll ﬁnd out which microbots overlap and move them
apart.
The compliance minimization part is the most complex, since it both depends on
space and time. At each time step the compliance depends on the entire structure of
microbots. Also the compliance depends on the accelerations of microbots at that
time step. Hence, for a speciﬁc time slice we’ll need to take all other microbots at
this time slice into account as well as all microbots one time slice before and one
time slice after.
The acceleration minimization part can be solved for each microbot separately, since
changing the position of a certain microbot, won’t aﬀect the accelerations of the
other microbots. Hence, in order to minimize the acceleration for a speciﬁc microbot
at a given time slice, we only need to take the position of that speciﬁc microbot into
account during the time slice, and the time slice before and after.
The overlap resolution part can be solved for each time slice separately, since updat-
ing the position of microbot at a given time slice won’t aﬀect the overlaps at other
time slices. Hence, to resolve overlaps for a speciﬁc time slice we’ll ﬁnd the pairs of
overlapping microbots at that time slice and move them apart.
The 3 parts will be described in detail in the following subsections. As we’ll see the
compliance minimization part is not very fast. Hence, we’re going to add a heuristic

4.2
Minimizing acceleration
Minimizing compliance ensures that we get the most stable structures. There’s just
one problem with the algorithm described above. Since each time slice is weighted
equally, this will result in microbots making discontinuous jumps.
Lets e.g. say
that we have a scene with a stable conﬁguration of microbots, and the destination
conﬁguration is also stable, but in order for the microbots to reach the destina-
tion, they have to temporarily make a slightly more unstable conﬁguration. Since
each time slice is weighted equally, the time slices in the middle conﬁguration will
move towards one of the more stable conﬁgurations, hence resulting in an anima-
tion, where during the ﬁrst half part we have the starting conﬁguration, and then
suddenly a discontinuous jump happens, teleporting all of the microbots to the end
conﬁguration, where they remain for the last half part of the animation.
To avoid discontinuous jumps like this, it would make sense to put some sort of limit
to how much the robots can move during a single time step. An example could be
30
==PAGE:35==
to put a cap on the velocities of the microbots, by saying that they can’t move more
than a certain distance during a single time step. I’d argue that a more natural
approach is to limit the accelerations instead of the velocities. Let’s say we’re in
an inertial frame of reference. If we limit the velocities of the microbots, then what
happens if we consider another inertial reference frame? The velocities depend on
the reference frame, hence limiting them in one inertial frame of reference isn’t
necessarily going to limit them in another. But if we instead limit the accelerations,
then this will be independent on the chosen inertial frame of reference. Let’s look
at an example: if we have a bunch of microbots on a moving train, and we want
them to make some sort of structure, then what does it mean to limit the velocities?
Since the train is moving compared to the earth, the microbots will try to move in
the opposite direction of the train in order to minimize their velocities. This isn’t
what we’re interested in. Conversely if we focus on the accelerations, the solution
the microbots ﬁnd is going to be the exact same as if they had been outside the
train standing still.
So let’s put some sort of limit to the accelerations. We could try to minimize the sum
of magnitudes of accelerations, however small accelerations aren’t really a problem,
and doing this would also not solve the problem of discontinuous jumps, since each
time slice would still be weighted equally. A better choice would be to minimize
the sum of squares of magnitudes of accelerations. This will put more focus on the
few places where a microbot has a very large acceleration. It also happens to be
easier to minimize algebraically, since we avoid a square root when computing the
magnitude of an acceleration.

4.4
A heuristic for improving performance of compliance
minimization
As it turns out, this method of ﬁnding the nearest valid conﬁguration isn’t very
fast. The thing is that a lot of iterations are needed if the minimum is far away
from the initial conﬁguration, and each step of the gradient descent requires ﬁnding
the gradient of the compliance, which in turn requires solving a large system of
equations.
What we can do, is to use a heuristic for obtaining a conﬁguration close to the
minimum. It is based on the following observation: The closer the microbots are to
each other the more stable the conﬁguration will be. Obviously, if all of the microbots
are gathered in one big pile, the structure doesn’t have any way of collapsing, because
it already is in a collapsed conﬁguration.
So instead of just using gradient descent for a large number of iterations, we’ll ﬁrst
use a heuristic that pulls the microbots closer together, followed by gradient descent
for a small number of iterations.
In the following, it’s worth noting that there are two types of ”connections” be-
tween microbots. One type is the physical connection that we are already familiar
34
==PAGE:39==
with. If microbots are connected via e.g. electromagnets they have a physical con-
nection. The other type of connection is purely mathematical and doesn’t represent
any physical connections. Before performing the correction step, we might form
these ”non-physical” connections between microbots arbitrarily far apart. What
this means, is that we want these microbots to be closer to each other at the current
time slice. As a result of the heuristic, they will be closer to each other, but not
because of any physical interaction. It’s just part of the path planning process. The
”non-physical” connections help us to determine where we want the microbots to
be at a certain time, not necessarily how to get there.
If we say that all microbots should be attracted to every other microbot, we’d end
up with a structure that seems to be too connected. The entire structure will turn
into a big pile of microbots before trying to reach the end conﬁguration. Turning
into a big pile is easy. You just need to collapse the structure. But afterwards, when
you need to generate the structure given by the end conﬁguration, it’s a bit harder.
If every microbot wants to stay connected to all of its neighbors, it’s not easy to
reach the end conﬁguration. The result of such a high connectedness will be that
the initial conﬁguration quickly collapses into a pile, where it stays until just before
the simulation is about to end, and then it will quickly generate the ﬁnal structure.
This is a problem, if we want the structure to be as stable as possible at all times.
Instead of saying that all pairs of microbots should be attracted to each other, let’s
create a graph connecting only the pairs of microbots that should contract. Now
the question is which pairs of microbots should be used as edges in this graph?
4.4.1
Minimum spanning tree
The ﬁrst idea is to use the minimum spanning tree as the graph. This will result
in n edges in the graph (we also count the ground as a node in the graph). Using
this graph the structure won’t collapse into a big pile as before, which is good. But
as seen in my previous work[23] this will lead to constructions that don’t seem very
stable. Everything is connected indirectly, but the connectedness is too low to have
the desired eﬀect.
4.4.2
Delaunay tetrahedralization
The next idea is to construct a Delaunay tetrahedralization (DT) and use that as the
graph for contraction. The DT is a supergraph of the MST. It has been shown that
the 3D DT can be computed in O(n
3
2) expected time without any assumption on the
distribution of the nodes, and in O(n log(n)) time for independently and identically
distributed nodes.[10] Since in this case we don’t have independently distributed
microbots but we also keep overlaps from happening, it’s reasonable to expect this
time to be somewhere in between the two.
Using this graph seems to solve some of the problems with the MST. If we e.g.
have two towers of microbots far from each other, there would be no connections
between them if the MST were to be used.
But with the tetrahedralization we
are guaranteed that everything in the scene will be connected. Not just indirectly
35
==PAGE:40==
through the ground, but also directly. Hence, in this example the two towers would
attract, making a more stable conﬁguration.
Nevertheless, this graph also seems to be too connected. Since we’re going to have
a lot of connections that don’t seem like they are actually needed for the structure
to be stable enough, we’re still going to end up with a big pile of microbots.

5
Obstacles
Until now we’ve only considered an environment consisting of a ﬂat ground. I we
want to simulate more interesting environments, one way to do it is to introduce
obstacles into the scene that the microbots have to get around.
5.1
Represent obstacles as ﬁxed microbots
A simple way to do that is to ﬁx some of the microbots and say that they represent
obstacles. Hence, we’ll have two types of entities in the scene: ﬁxed microbots and
dynamic microbots. The algorithms can be run on the entire collection of microbots
as normal, the only diﬀerence being that the ﬁxed microbots are held in place.
There’s only one problem. Since some of the microbots are now ﬁxed, the others
might get stuck trying to get around them.
E.g. consider a wall made of ﬁxed
microbots put together in a grid structure. If we have a single dynamic microbot on
one side of this wall and it has to reach the other side, then it will try to move in
a straight line through the wall. If the wall had been made of dynamic microbots,
they could easily have moved to make a hole in the wall for the single microbot to
get through. But if the microbots are ﬁxed then this won’t be possible.
5.2
Pathﬁnding used in subdivision
To solve this problem, instead of taking the midpoint along a line segment when
performing the subdivision, let’s ﬁnd a path that avoids all ﬁxed microbots and take
the midpoint of that. E.g. in the case of the wall and the single microbot, in the
ﬁrst subdivision we can ﬁnd a path around one of the two edges of the wall. The
midpoint will then be just next to either of these edges. The following subdivisions
will ﬁnd an almost straight line as the shortest path. Hence, the rest of the algorithm
will run almost as normal.
In practice, the path will be found by ﬁrst creating a grid of cells, then marking a
grid cell if it contains a ﬁxed microbot. Then the A* algorithm is run on this grid,
ﬁnding an approximation to the shortest path. The midpoint of this path will be
the one used.
This solution will work in general, but of course the step sizes need to be small
enough since too big step sizes during e.g. acceleration minimization might force
the path of the microbots to go through obstacles. It has to bee so small that the
following overlap resolution will make sure that the path is still valid.

Implementation
The algorithm is implemented using the TypeScript language[27]. The result of the
implementation is a visual program that can be executed in a web browser. The
library Three.js is used for displaying the 3D scene[25], while React is used for making
the user interface consisting of input ﬁelds and buttons[24].
An advantage of this, is that running the program requires no setup.
Anyone
can easily run the demo by visiting the website https://peulicke.github.io/

A disadvantage of using TypeScript as a language is that the execution time is larger
that that of other languages like C++, which is compiled to machine code before
being executed. Hence, if the algorithm were to be used on big problems, it may
be an advantage to reimplement the algorithm in another language. But for the
purposes of this project TypeScript seems like a better option, since it’s easier to
distribute a demo showing the possibilities of the algorithm.

6.5
Creating the animation
The interesting part of the program is found within the createAnimation function.
This function is responsible for actually solving the problem using the divide and
conquer method presented. The structure of the function looks like this:
1. Let start be the start conﬁguration and end be the end conﬁguration.
2. Start with an animation consisting of the time slices [start, start, end, end].
3. Perform the following steps until we have enough time slices in the animation.
(a) Subdivide the time intervals that contain a microbot having an acceler-
ation greater than the allowed.
(b) Perform the following a constant number of times.
i. Contract the structure using the contraction graph.
ii. Resolve overlaps.
(c) Perform the following a constant number of times.
i. Perform a compliance minimization step.
(d) Perform the following a constant number of times.
43
==PAGE:48==
i. Perform an acceleration minimization step.
ii. Resolve overlaps.
(e) Reset all ﬁxed microbots.
4. Remove the ﬁrst and last time slice.
5. The resulting sequence of time slices makes up the ﬁnal animation.
In step 2 the reason for the duplicated start and end conﬁgurations is that we
want to be able to evaluate the acceleration at the start and end time slices. I.e.
duplicating them means: the velocity during the ﬁrst and last time slices are 0.
In step 3a in practice what we do when determining if a time interval should be
subdivided is to use the acceleration at the ﬁrst time slice of the time interval. We
could also have used e.g. the mean acceleration of the ﬁrst and last time slice of the
time slice. This would have made the algorithm time symmetric. But the overall
eﬀect of using the ﬁrst time slice shouldn’t have a big impact on the ﬁnal result.
After step 3(c)i we don’t need to perform an overlap resolution step as we do in step
3(b)i and 3(d)i, since this is handled using an overlap penalty in the compliance
minimization step. In step 3e the ﬁxed microbots refer to the ones that are used to
represent obstacles. In step 4 we remove the ﬁrst and last time slice, since we only
needed them to be able to evaluate velocities and accelerations at the ﬁrst and last
time slice.
It’s possible for degeneracies to occur while e.g. computing the Delaunay tetrahe-
dralization. If for example all microbots lie on a perfect line, you won’t be able to
create any tetrahedrons from them. Also, when resolving overlap, if the microbots
happen to be located at the exact same point in space, equation (109) results in a
division by 0. In practice, these problems can easily be solved by slightly moving
the positions of all microbots in random directions. Mathematically speaking, the
probability that a degenerate case will occur is 0. Hence, after adding a tiny amount
of randomness to the setup, these cases won’t occur in practice.

Discussion
So now we’ve seen that using this method it’s possible to plan the movement of a
collection of microbots in such a way the they all take each other into account and
work together to accomplish the task they were assigned. The method stands out
from other methods in the sense that it uses one central computer that controls
where each microbots has to go. In most other methods presented in the literature,
the microbots are autonomous and they each try to ﬁnd the best path not necessarily
taking the other microbots into account, which can result in ineﬃcient solutions (i.e.
it takes a long time for the microbots to reach their destination) or in the worst case
deadlocks, where no microbot is able to move any further. Using these local methods
one will usually observe emergent behavior, i.e. the group of microbots behave in
a complex way even though every microbot only takes itself and its neighbors into
account. But the method presented here is global in the sense that the solution
is computed by a single computer. Hence, you could argue that the behavior of
the microbots that we see isn’t an emergent phenomenon, even though it seems
like a complex behavior. On the contrary one could argue that this behavior is in
fact emergent, since we start by having one time interval that we split into two
recursively, and using this simple rule we end up with a behavior that almost seems
intelligent.
The results that were obtained using the implementation of the method showed the
importance of the three parts of the correction step, namely: contraction, compliance
minimization and acceleration minimization. Without any of them the results had
vital ﬂaws. I.e. not generating results that would be mechanically stable or physically
possible. Of course, since the total compliance of the entire ensemble were minimized
it might not yield as stable results as if it had been the maximum compliance of all
trusses. Since every truss has a limit to how much stress it can withstand before
permanently deforming, minimizing the maximum of all trusses would yield more
stable results than minimizing the sum of all compliances. This seems like a harder
problem though which is why the sum of compliances were chosen here.
We also found that the implementation could only handle about 100 microbots
within a minute of computation time, or 1000 microbots within a few minutes when
compliance minimization was turned oﬀ. This is of course far from what would be
needed in the application of claytronics. Using another language like C++ would
perhaps have made this better, but still probably far from what is needed. Another
possibility would be to take advantage of distributing the computation across mul-
tiple cores of the CPU or perhaps to use the GPU. Maybe the method could be
optimized by creating some sort of hierarchical structure like others have already
written about[4] where the problem is divided into subproblems that are solved
independently and then recombined.
Perhaps, it would also be possible to take
advantage of the method of metamodules[9] where microbots are used to generate
larger modules called metamodules and an algorithm is then built based on these
modules. Since the metamodules are built in such a way that they are able to per-
form actions that microbots aren’t capable of, it’s possible to construct other types
of algorithms. In the paper presented, the metamodules are built in such a way
58
==PAGE:63==
that they can ”swallow” each other. I.e. a metamodule can enter the interior of
another metamodule, hence making it possible for these modules to move through
each other.
Being able to ”handle about 100 microbots within a minute” is also a bit misleading.
This would be the case if the microbots existed in an ideal environment, which
they do inside the simulator.
But in the real world, external disturbances and
uncertainty would require the microbots to update their paths on the way to their
goal locations. In the program that was implemented here, you have to option to
”disturb” the microbots, which eﬀectively just adds a bit of randomness to all of the
microbots’ positions. After having done this however, the entire algorithm has to
be rerun. This clearly wouldn’t be feasible in the real world, since the computation
time would exceed the actual time used by the microbots to move. It might be
possible however to reuse the old solution when computing a new after having been
”disturbed”, since the two solutions should be very close to each other. E.g. if the
microbots are disturbed only a tiny amount, instead of recomputing a solution from
start to end, you could compute a solution from the current location after being
disturbed to the previous location before being disturbed, and then prepend this
solution to the previous solution. I.e. after having been disturbed, you ﬁgure out
how to get back to where you were before you were disturbed, and then you continue
exactly as before.
But what are the challenges of building real-world microbots?
For starters, we
would need an attachment system for the microbots. I.e. we need to ﬁgure out how
a pair microbots would hold on to each other. One idea is to cover the surface of
every microbot with electromagnets. By turning these magnets on and oﬀyou’d
be able to attach and detach to neighboring microbots. Or instead of traditional
electromagnets one could use electropermanent magnets, i.e. magnets that don’t
require a constant stream of current to be active. They might only need electrical
power when turning on or oﬀ. This would be better for applications where you want
to make some sort of construction, since when it’s been completed it’s going to stay
in that conﬁguration for a long time. Hence, it would be a beneﬁt if it didn’t draw
any power in that state. Another option would be to create mechanical latches that
would have the ability to lock to it’s neighbors when in contact. This might create
a stronger connection. One problem though might be that since it would have to be
in physical contact while doing this the movement is vastly constrained. Another
problem might be that the locking and unlocking of a latch could take some time, and
this would need to be accounted for in the algorithm. The algorithm presented here
assumes that attachment and detachment are instantaneous operations. Another
similar idea that also requires microbots to be in physical contact is that of adhesive
surfaces similar to that of a gecko’s feet. This would perhaps result in a much faster
attachment and detachment that that of a latch mechanism, however it still has the
problem of requiring physical contact.
Another challenge of real-world microbots that needs to be overcome is that of
powering the microbots. Each of the microbots could have a battery inside of it
that it could draw power from. However, since the microbots have to be so small,
the battery must be very small as well, and it would probably not be able to contain
59
==PAGE:64==
enough power for anything serious. One could imagine that the microbots could
transfer energy to their neighbors e.g. using electrical transformers. Then you’d only
need one of the microbots to be connected to some sort of power source and it could
then redistribute the power throughout the entire structure. Another possibility
might be to transfer the power directly from the source in a wireless fashion to all
microbots at the same time. This might require extreme amounts of power to be
possible and also magnetic ﬁelds might disturb to microbots if their attachment
mechanism relied on magnetism. Having a central power supply would limit the
microbots movement by requiring them to always be nearby. However it might be
possible for the microbots to lift this central power supply and transport it with
them, such that they are able to go anywhere while still having access to power.
For the algorithm to work in the real world you’d also need a way of always knowing
the position of every microbot. One idea would be to use something like the GPS[17]
that is based on measuring signals from external emitters and then using multilater-
ation to compute the position in spacetime. This however doesn’t seem like it would
yield very good results since a lot of interference among the robots would happen.
For a system like GPS to work well it’s best to have a line of sight to the emitters.
Otherwise, the time for a signal to arrive may be greater than it would have been
had it been unobstructed and able to take a direct path through space. Another
idea would be for each microbot to somehow measure the distance to it’s neighbors.
Then gathering all these distances one would be able to triangulate the positions of
all microbots. I.e. constructing a network of triangles (or tetrahedrons in 3D) where
all side lengths are know. Using this information it should be possible to compute
the position of every microbot. Since all distance measurements are between pairs
of microbots close to each other, the estimates might be better than that of using a
system like the GPS.
In the application of constructing buildings, it might be beneﬁcial if you could some-
how make the robots solidify to make the structure more stable. E.g. if each microbot
were equipped with hollow tubes extending all the way though the microbot, they
would be able to make a network of tubes which could be ﬁlled with e.g. concrete.
I.e. ﬁrst the microbots would create the structure of the building. Then afterwards
you would pump the network full of concrete. Then, after the concrete had solidi-
ﬁed, the structure would be just as stable as any other building. You could use this
technique to build skyscrapers very quickly. I.e. start by creating the skeleton of
the ground ﬂoor, then ﬁll it with concrete and let it solidify. Then, you repeat with
the next ﬂoor and so on until the skyscraper is completed. Each new ﬂoor starts
out being weak, since it’s just a collection of microbots, but after the concrete has
been added this ﬂoor is going to be strong enough to support the construction of
the next one on top of it. One disadvantage of this technique would of course be
that it wouldn’t be easy to repurpose the microbots, since they would be locked in
place by the concrete.

Conclusion
To summarize: a method for path planning of microbots was presented. This method
was implemented as a program running in a web browser. This program was used
to generate simulations of example setups showing the possibilities of microbots.
The method is a variant of the divide and conquer method over the time domain.
I.e. time is subdivided recursively until we have a series of time slices resembling an
animation of the microbots. During each subdivision 3 diﬀerent tasks are performed:
contraction, compliance minimization and acceleration minimization. Each of these
parts help achieving a good result which is both eﬀective (i.e. the microbots reach
their destination fast) and mechanically stable.
The implementation has been tested on a wide variety of example setups, testing
the microbots’ abilities in construction, transportation and transformation. Both
example setups without obstacles and setups with obstacles have been tested, and
the microbots were able to navigate around these obstacles.
A big weakness of this method is the fact that it isn’t very fast. Only about 100
microbots could be handled by the program in less than a minute (1000 microbots
within few minutes with compliance minimization turned oﬀ).
Finally, the basic principle of using divide and conquer over time to solve mechanical
problems, could be used outside the domain of microbots. One could for example
imagine a mechanical arm, that needs to be programmed to pick up a certain object.
This could be done in much the same way as it was in this project. You take the
initial position and the target positions of all parts of the arm and compute the
average. Then you make sure that the arm is in a valid conﬁguration by possibly
correcting it. Then you recursively repeat this for each of the two new time intervals
until you have a smooth animation of an arm picking up an object. In this example,
computational performance wouldn’t be a big problem as with many millions of
microbots. Since the arm is probably made up of only of few joints, the algorithm
would be fast enough.
