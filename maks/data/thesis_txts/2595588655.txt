Preface
This report is a presentation of the Master Thesis developed by Peter Vilhelm Kern Nyholm and Henrik
Foss Jørgensen in the period from February 2020 to June 2020 at DTU Management on the Technical
University of Denmark (DTU) in collaboration with Novo Nordisk. The thesis was developed as the
ﬁnal project of the Master of Science (M.Sc.) degree Business Analytics (Optimization and datadesign)
and as part of the Honours Program.
The thesis deals with production scheduling in one of Novo Nordisk’s production facilities in Hillerød,
Denmark, where the plastic components used for assembling the Novo Nordisk FlexTouch® and Flex-
Pen® insulin devices, used by diabetics in the entire world, is produced.
It is assumed, that the reader has knowledge within several topics of operations research such as math-
ematical modelling, heuristics, mathheuristics and large scale decomposition. It furthermore assumes,
that the reader has a basic knowledge of algorithm runtimes and simple mathematics.
The thesis was developed with Thomas Riis Stidsen, DTU Management, as the supervisor from DTU,
and Niels-Christian Fink Bagger, Novo Nordisk Hillerød, as the supervisor from Novo Nordisk.

Abstract
This thesis presents an approach for determining a production schedule for the plastic components used
for assembling the Novo Nordisk FlexPen® and FlexTouch® insulin devices, that are used by millions
of diabetics around the world. The plastic components are produced at a Novo Nordisk factory located
in Hillerød, Denmark.
The thesis begins with a thorough description of the factory, the production system and the problem
associated with determining the production schedule. This is followed by a mathematical (MIP) model,
which, if solved to optimality, can determine the best production schedule for the factory in the con-
sidered time horizon. Due to the complex nature of the problem, the MIP model was not able to solve
within a time limit of 12 hours, which is the maximum amount of time available for determining the
production schedule. From investigating the structure of the problem, it is discovered, that the full
problem can be decomposed into smaller problems which can be solved independently. Some of these
smaller problems could be solved to optimality within a time limit of 1 hour, while others did not when
using a commercial solver.
Since the commercial solver was not able to determine the optimal production schedule, a greedy
heuristic is used for determining a quick but not necessarily optimal solution. The greedy heuristic
determined a fast feasible solution, which can be useful if Novo Nordisk is ever in a situation, where a
solution must be provided quickly.
Although the heuristic provided a quick solution, there is no guarantee of quality in these solutions.
Because of this, the mathheuristics Local branching and Cut and solve are used on the production prob-
lem to improve these solutions. Local Branching is a method, which initializes with a known feasible
solution and attempts to improve the known solution. The greedy heuristic solution was used as the
initial solution of the Local branching, and the improvement of the solution was signiﬁcant for some
problems, while in others, it did not manage to improve the solution within its short time limit. Cut
and solve is a mathheuristic, which in comparison to Local Branching, guarantees optimality if no time
limit is imposed and does not need an initial feasible solution. The Cut and solve showed great potential
for some problems while other problems were troublesome due to diﬃcult LP relaxations.
Since none of the above approaches resulted in a proven optimal solution, a Dantzig Wolfe decomposi-
tion approach is presented. A simple Dantzig Wolfe column generation managed to solve some of the
problems to optimality, but this was only the case for problems, that the MIP solver solved to optimal-
ity within the time limit as well. The harder problems were not solved by the simple algorithm due
to a variety of issues. These issues are investigated separately and diﬀerent techniques for solving the
problems are suggested and tested.
Even though the Dantzig Wolfe column generation algorithm did not manage to ﬁnd optimal solutions
or even converge for some problems, it still managed to determine decent results for many problems by
using heuristics for converting the Dantzig Wolfe solutions into integer solutions. For most problems,
these results were better than the ones returned by the MIP solver.
Overall the thesis found, that the hybrid of mathheuristics and Dantzig Wolfe column generation shows
potential, even though it requires a lot of tweaking, before it can outperform a commercial MIP solver.

1.1
Introduction
Ever since the industrial revolution, the ability to mass produce products has revolutionized many
industries of the world. Mass production has improved the lives of billions of people, and one of the
industries which has been revolutionized partly due to mass production is the medical industry. The
ability to mass produce medicinal products has given companies such as Novo Nordisk the possibility
of delivering life changing medicine to patients all over the world.
Novo Nordisk is a global company with more than 30 million patients in over 170 countries, who use
their diabetes products [1]. This means, that there are more than 30 million patients, who count on Novo
Nordisk to succesfully produce insulin and insulin devices in order to live a normal life with diabetes.
According to the World Health Organisation [2], the amount of people with diabetes rose from 108
million in 1980 to 422 million in 2014. This vast increase in the amount of diabetics in the world, means
that Novo Nordisk must constantly be adapting to the changes to the demand for their products, and
ensure that their production facilities are capable of living up to the task.
In Hillerød, Denmark, one of the factories, which produce some of the insulin devices, is located. There
are many aspects of producing the ﬁnal insulin products. The insulin will have to be produced, the de-
vices will have to be assembled and the smaller components of the ﬁnal device will have to be produced.
Two of the devices, which are made in Hillerød, are the FlexPen® and the FlexTouch® insulin pens seen
in Figure 1.1. The subject of this thesis is exclusively on the production of the plastic components of
these two pens.
The factory in Hillerød produces many diﬀerent plastic components in many diﬀerent colors and sizes,
24 hours a day, all days of the year. Production systems such as the one in Hillerød are in most cases
very complex and determining the optimal production schedule can be computationally hard. The focus
of this thesis is to develop a method, which can be used to generate a production schedule, that lives up
to the required objectives and desired output of the factory. There are several ways that a production
schedule can be determined, but the main tools of this thesis are within the area of operations research.
Operations research is a discipline that originates from World War 2 [3], and deals with analyzing com-
plex problems using mathematical modelling and advanced algorithms in order to ﬁnd optimal or near
optimal solutions for complex decision making problems. Production scheduling naturally involves
analyzing and optimizing complex systems. Operations research includes both exact and non-exact
methods to optimize a certain objective. Both exact and non-exact methods are attempted in this the-
sis.

1.2.1
Current production planning
To obtain an understanding of how the results of this thesis is related to the production planning at
Novo Nordisk, it is necessary to have an understanding of how the current production planning works
at the factory in Hillerød. Not more than a few years ago, the planning of the production was done
manually. However in recent years, a digitalization of the production planning has been prioritized
heavily, which has changed how the production planning is done signiﬁcantly.
There are currently three types of planning, which is carried out by the planners at Novo Nordisk.
Long-term planning with a time horizon of 1-1.5 years, medium-term planning with a time horizon of
18 weeks and short-term planning with a time horizon of 4 weeks into the future. The goal of the long-
term planning is to make decisions in order to prepare for long term strategical production changes.
These changes include, but are not limited to, investments in new production lines and improvements of
current production lines. Medium-term planning is a mix of several things. First of all, it is done in order
to determine which materials to buy and when to buy them, such that the production is not limited by
shortage of material. It is also used to determine more detailed production plans for the weeks in the
coming months, but these plans are naturally subject to change, since the production facility is a very
dynamic system.
The short term planning is the planning of the detailed production plan in the coming weeks. This
detailed production plan determines how the actual production should be carried out. This includes
aspects such as assigning staﬀto the diﬀerent areas of the production system and determining which
machines should produce which products at a given time of the day. Short-term planning is done on
an hourly granularity.
The current production planning is computed using a MIP solver, that outputs a production plan where
the granularity of planning is on a weekly basis. In order to gain insights into how reasonable the
current long-term production goals are, this model can be solved for 1-1.5 years. Solving this model
does not provide any speciﬁcs to what should be produced in the individual hours, but it does provide
how much of each product, that should be produced on each machine each week, which can indicate

Goals and scope

1.3
Description of production system
This section introduces the speciﬁcs of how the production system works. The description is limited to
what is important for the purpose of this thesis. As mentioned earlier, the production system considered
in this project produces the plastic items, which are used as components in building the Novo Nordisk
FlexTouch® and FlexPen® insulin devices. The amount of unique items, that the system produces is
108. It does not consider assembly of the items, but only the production of the individual items.
At the end of each week, Novo Nordisk must deliver a pre-deﬁned amount of each item that the system
produces. The amount that must be delivered varies from week to week and from item to item. The
main goal of this project is to ensure that the production system meets this demand for each item each
week by providing a production schedule, which results in the right amount of items.
1.3.1
Machines, moulds and items
Production of the items takes place on one of the 43 machines of the production system. Each unique
machine can produce one or more of the items. However, before a machine can produce an item it must
be equipped with the correct mould. The system consists of 73 unique moulds. Each mould can ﬁt into
one or more of the machines and together a unique machine/mould combination can produce a subset
of the 108 items. Some machine/mould combinations can only produce one type of item, while other
combinations can produce up to 19 of the unique items. A unique combination of a machine, a mould
and an item, that the machine and mould can produce, is known as a valid conﬁguration.
It is important to emphasize, that the machines and moulds (and time) are the primary resources of the
production system. This means that factors such as staﬀavailability and raw material inventory are
not considered explicitly in this thesis. In Figure 1.3, the concept of valid conﬁgurations is illustrated
by using a small example of such a system.

1.3.2
Production time, take down and setup
The production system produces items on an hourly basis. This means that when a valid conﬁgura-
tion (consisting of a unique machine, mould and item) is producing, it will be set to produce from the
beginning of an hour until the end of an hour. After an hour has passed the conﬁguration can either
continue to produce or stop production. The amount of items produced in an hour is constant and is
entirely dependent on the mould used in the conﬁguration.
Production takes place at all hours of the day, seven days a week, which means that a production
schedule must be provided for this situation. When planning the production, it will often be necessary
to either change which machines and moulds that are currently producing together or change the items
that the current machine / mould combinations are producing.
If a machine is currently combined with a speciﬁc mould and it needs to produce an item using a diﬀer-
ent mould, then there is an associated take down time from taking out the mould and an associated
setup time from putting in the new mould. When taking out the mould from the machine, neither
the machine nor the mould can be used for production during the take down time associated with that
speciﬁc mould. Similarly, the machine and the new mould can not be used for production during the
associated setup time after the take down has ﬁnished.

available staﬀ, which means that minimizing the amount of item start-ups is naturally something Novo
Nordisk would want to reduce, as this limits the staﬀrequirements.
Because of this, it was also made an objective of the problem to minimize the amount of times the
schedule begins production of the individual items. Even though this objective was included, the main
objectives were still to deliver the required demand and staying within the storage bounds.

1.3.5
Simplifying assumptions
Large production systems such as the one at Novo Nordisk are often complicated and the amount of
objectives can be very large and hard to prioritize. The problem of this thesis is no exception to this,
so in order to simplify the problem at hand, a number of assumptions were made. The assumptions are
summarized in the following:
- Moulds can instantly switch from producing one item to another item
When a mould switches from producing one item to another item, a worker is required to conﬁg-
ure the mould for the new items. This procedure usually takes around 15 minutes, but is assumed
to be instant. Furthermore, some moulds are able to produce items in several colors. When switch-
ing from one color to another, the mould must be cleaned in order to prevent color bleeding. It
is assumed, that this cleaning process happens instantly.
- Item prioritization
Novo Nordisk might have items, where the delivery of the demand or the storage is more im-
portant than for other items. These items could be more proﬁtable or be a major component in
many ﬁnal products. It is assumed, that all items are equally important.
- Workforce
The availability of the workforce in the factory can vary due to holidays and other factors. How-
ever, it is assumed, that there is suﬃcient manpower available to implement suggested production
schedules.
- Setup and take down periods
Novo Nordisk prefers a production schedule, where setting up and taking down moulds happens
during the day. This is due to the fact, that there are usually more workforce available during the
day compared to night hours. However, it is assumed, that it is possible to setup and take down
moulds from lines in all periods of the day.
- Material availability
In every production system, the availability of the material used for production is something
which should always be kept in mind. In this thesis it is assumed that there is always enough ma-
terial to produce the articles, that the production schedule demands. Niels-Christian Fink Bagger
from Novo Nordisk has assured us that this assumption is very close to the truth, which means
that this resource is not considered.
The methods of this thesis will provide production schedules, which do not consider the associated costs
of the above assumptions. An important thing to keep in mind is that the provided schedule might not
result in any issues with respect to the above assumptions, but when a schedule is provided, it should
be searched for any signiﬁcant problems that might occur when compared to the above. In Section 5.1,
some of these assumptions and suggestions for how they can be handled are discussed.

1.4
Literature study
Determining the optimal schedules for production systems is something, which has been widely re-
searched for many decades. Production systems vary greatly and it is therefore diﬃcult to achieve gen-
eral methods, which can be applied for all instances. However, many problems similar to the problem
of this thesis have been studied previously.
1.4.1
Production problem classiﬁcations
For a clear understanding of how the problem of this thesis relates to other production scheduling
problems, it is important to gain insight into how the problem at hand can be classiﬁed. The problem
described in Section 1.3 is a problem within the category of dynamic lot sizing problems. It is a lot sizing
problem in the sense that the production system delivers batches (lots) of the diﬀerent products, and it
is dynamic in the sense that the size of the lots changes over time. The range of diﬀerent types of lot
sizing problems are long, however [6] provides a overview which is summarized in the following:
- Discrete versus continuous production horizon
Lot sizing problems are deﬁned with either a discrete or continuous production horizon. Discrete
production horizons can range from hourly, daily, weekly, monthly or even yearly depending on
the problem at hand.
- Single-item versus multi-item
Lot sizing problems are deﬁned to either consider a single item, that needs to be produced, or a
group of items (multi-item problem).
- Single-level versus multi-level
Items in lot sizing problems can require multiple stages of production in order to reach a ﬁnal
product, which is referred to as a multi-level lot sizing problem. Single-level lot sizing problems
only requires an item to go through a single phase or operation in order to reach its ﬁnal state.
- Single-resource versus multi-resource
Items in lot sizing problems might have multiple resources, that can produce a given item, which
is the case for multi-resource lot sizing problems. In the case of single-resource lot sizing prob-
lems, one or more items share a single resource.
- Capacitated versus uncapacitated
Resources in production systems include machines, moulds, manpower etc. When there is no
restrictions on the resources, the lot sizing problem is classiﬁed as a uncapacitated lot sizing
problem. When the resources are limited the problem is known as a capacitated lot sizing problem.
The overall production problem presented in this thesis can therefore be described as a dynamic dis-
crete multi-item single-level multi-resource capacitated lot sizing problem. This is not a stan-
dard problem, however individual elements of the problem has been highly researched and will be a
great help, when trying to optimize the production at Novo Nordisk.
1.4.2
Related work in literature
A number of diﬀerent mathematical models of a single-item lot sizing with setup time using discrete
and continuous time horizons is discussed in [6]. These are modelled as mixed integer models (MIP).

==PAGE:25==

Furthermore, Belvaux and Wolsey, explores a number of extensions to the basic lot sizing problem,
presenting diﬀerent ways to model a multi-item, multi-resource, single-phase problem in [7]. It is dis-
cussed how to model safety stocks for items and add valid inequalities in order to tighten the bound
of a LP relaxation. Additionally, Belvaux and Wolsey, discuss ways to model start-ups, switch-oﬀs, and
changeovers on machines. Belvaux and Wolsey concluded that general MIP systems for lot sizing prob-
lems do not have the proper structure in order to allow for branching and searching feasible solutions,
however they were able to obtain tight bounds.
A dynamic programming approach to the single-item single-machine uncapacitated lot sizing prob-
lem with setup was developed by Harvey m. Wagner and Thomson M. Wittin [8], which computes
an optimal production plan in O(t2), where t is the number of planning periods. Furthermore, other
dynamic programming recursions has been developed to account for back-logging [9], constant pro-
duction capacity [10], lower bounds on production [11] and more. The drawback of the mentioned
dynamic programming approaches is, that they only consider a single item, and cannot easily be ex-
tended to multi-item problems.
Decomposition methods are also valid techniques, when dealing with multi-item lot sizing problems.
Multi-item lot sizing problems can be divided into smaller subproblems, that can be solved easily inde-
pendently. One must combine the solutions of the subproblems in order to ﬁnd a solution or approxi-
mation to the overall problem. One of the most popular approaches is to transform the MIP model into
the Dantzig-Wolfe Reformulation [12], consisting of a restricted master model and n smaller subprob-
lems, and use column generation in order to reduce the number of variables in the full master problem.
One of the ﬁrst people to utilize Dantzig-Wolfe decomposition to solve lot sizing problems was Ralph
E. Gomory and Bernard P. Dzielinski [13]. In each iteration of the column generation algorithm, they
solved a pricing problem for a single-item lot sizing problem for each item and added the column to the
restricted master problem. As the subproblems considers single-item lot sizing problem, the previously
mentioned dynamic programming approaches might be useful here. The restricted master problem only
contained linking constraints, which ensured that resources would not overlap. They found that cer-
tain problems required a large number of columns in order to converge and suﬀered from the tailing
oﬀeﬀect.
A math-heuristic Dantzig-Wolfe algorithm was developed in [14] for the multi-item capacitated lot
sizing problem with setups. The aim of this paper was to tackle challenging subproblems using math-
heuristics. This approach provided promising results both in terms of run time and solution quality.
Additionally, Benders’ decomposition has also been attempted for multi-item lot sizing problems in
[15].
Meta-heuristics is a popular technique for obtaining good solutions to complex combinatorial problems.
A wide range of diﬀerent meta-heuristics has been developed in order to optimize diﬀerent lot sizing
problems. The most popular ones for lot sizing problems are simulated annealing [16], tabu search [17]
and genetic algorithms [18]. All these algorithms provided good results for their respective lot sizing
problems, but could not guarantee optimal solutions.
Even though optimization of production systems has been widely researched, it has proven diﬃcult
to ﬁnd research, that attempts to solve the particular problem of this thesis. However, it is promising
that many operations research methods has been successfully implemented for many similar problems.
This is a good indication that the potential for providing good results is not an impossible task.

minimum, maximum and safety stock are represented in the next three terms, where the objective is

1.5.3
The objective function - Weights vs. Lexiographic
Choosing the right weights ϵi in the objective function of the above model is not a trivial task. Dif-
ferent values and ratios between the ϵi values can result in performance changes to the model and
provide diﬀerent results if not chosen correctly. The values must always be chosen such that the order
of importance is maintained in the ﬁnal solution. The values ϵi = [100, 16, 4, 1, 1] were suggested to
Niels-Christian Fink Bagger from Novo Nordisk, and he found these to be reasonable. It can be assumed
that these ϵ-values are used for any models and results in the remainder of this thesis.
An alternative approach to setting the values for ϵi is the so-called lexiographic (hierarchical) optimiza-
tion. Optimizing with lexiographic optimization splits the objective function into several objectives and
ranks them in the order of importance. The model is then optimized using only the ﬁrst objective and
the solutions is noted down. The second objective is then used for a new optimization, where the model
is constrained such that the optimal solution with respect to the previous objective is adhered to. In the

1.5.4
Intermediate results - Full model
The formulation presented in this section was implemented and optimized using the Gurobi solver
for the full system. It was found, that the Gurobi solver did not manage to determine any solutions
within the 12 hour time limit for 18 weeks. The solver did not manage to determine a gap, which
means that the LP relaxation of the system was not solved either. The model was also tested for fewer
weeks and it was found that even for a time horizon of 1 week, the solver did not provide an optimal
solution. Furthermore, the lexiographic approach of having multiple prioritized objectives was tested.
This approach did not manage to determine any solutions either within the time limit.
The fact that the model was not optimized within the time limit meant that other methods had to be
used. The remainder of this thesis will aim to provide high quality and ideally optimal solutions to the
18 weeks of planning.

2.1
Determining a high quality primal solution
In many real life problems, the optimal solution can be very diﬃcult and time consuming to determine.
Spending too much time on determining the best solution can in some cases not be worth the time, if
a high quality solution can be found quickly by other non-exact methods. The production system at
Novo Nordisk, Hillerød consists of lines, moulds and other things, which can break or be unexpectedly
unavailable for other reasons. Because of this, it is important to be able to adapt the production plan
quickly, such that the new situation can be adhered to.
Besides the fact that a fast solution can be useful, if time is an issue and a plan has to be made swiftly,
it is also an advantage, that these solutions can often be combined with exact methods. For some exact
methods, they can potentially speed up the run time to a signiﬁcant degree, such that the optimal
solution can be determined faster.
If non-exact methods are used for determining the ﬁnal production schedule, the planner should always
be aware, that these solution can potentially result in an inferior production plan, when comparing to
the optimal solution. To avoid this, it is often advantageous that the best found solutions from the
non-exact methods are compared with lower bounds achieved from any relaxation method, such that
the known potential loss can be quantiﬁed. This chapter introduces two heuristics, which were im-
plemented to get a fast solution. The chapter then presents the two mathheuristics, Local Branching
and Cut and solve, which were used and implemented as an attempt to achieve valuable high quality
solutions.
2.2
Heuristics
Heuristics are deﬁned as simple methods to achieve a quick, but not necessarily optimal solution. Apart
from the strength of being fast, it is also an advantage of heuristics, that they do not require access to
any expensive commercial MIP solvers such as CPLEX or Gurobi. Two simple heuristics are presented
in this section. The main purpose with these heuristics is, that they will be used as baseline solutions.
The solutions of these heuristics will therefore be used to quantify the performance increase, when
using more complicated methods such as mathheuristics and decomposition methods.
2.2.1
Zero production heuristic
The ﬁrst heuristic, which can be used to ﬁnd a feasible solution very quickly, is the zero production
heuristic. This heuristic provides the solution, where no production is carried out and no demand is
delivered. This is the solution, that would happen if Novo Nordisk were to suddenly shut down the fac-
tory and ignore the demands of their customers. Naturally this solution is always of very poor quality,
when considering the objective of the problem.
This heuristic corresponds to setting the values of zc,t and xa,t to zero for all periods t = 0 . . . |T| in
the mathematical formulation. This heuristic was not used for many purposes, but proved to be useful
when a feasible solution was needed for initializing other methods.

Because of the very poor nature of the zero production solutions, a greedy heuristic was developed to
obtain a better fast solution. The main idea of the greedy heuristic is, that it determines a production
plan for one week at a time, until a production plan has been made for all the necessary weeks. For
each week, the heuristic goes through three objectives, that it attempts to optimize on and then adds
production to satisfy these objectives as much as possible. The greedy part of the heuristics comes from
the fact, that the production is planned with only one week in mind. This means, that the heuristic
ﬁnds a greedy solution for a speciﬁc week and then moves on to the next week and does not consider
the following weeks, which most likely results in suboptimal (but maybe still high quality) solutions.

prioritize the diﬀerent articles and rank their importance. The pseudo code for the greedy heuristic can

2.3
Local branching - Improving a solution
Improving a solution is often valuable, if this can be done within a short amount of time. This can
be useful in situations, where the production plan can be easily improved, before using it as the ﬁnal
plan, but it can also be useful along with other methods such as Dantzig Wolfe decomposition. This
section outlines the so-called "Local branching" mathheuristic, which main purpose in this thesis is
to improve an existing solution. Local branching was ﬁrst proposed as a mathheuristic for improving
existing solutions by [19].
By taking an existing solution ¯x, Local branching improves this solution by searching the neighbour-
hood of the solution for any improvements. When the optimal solution has been found in the neigh-
bourhood, the neighbourhood of the new and better solution is then considered and searched for the
optimal solution. This process is repeated iteratively, until the allowed time has been spent.
This method requires that a commercial solver is available for determining the optimal solution in the
neighbourhood of the current solution. The fact that the method requires a solver is what classiﬁes the
method as a mathheuristic.
2.3.1
Description of local branching
The following description of the Local branching method will only consider binary integer program-
ming problems, since this is the only case, that will be relevant for this thesis. The ﬁrst question, that
naturally arises from the description above, is how the neighbourhood of a feasible solution ¯x is de-
ﬁned. In Local branching, the neighbourhood of a solution is deﬁned as the part of the solution space,
where a maximum of k of the binary variables can change their values from what they were in the
previous solution. This means, that at most k variables can be 0, if they were 1 in the previous solution
or 1 if they were 0. This is also known as a Hamming distance constraint.
As discussed earlier, the only binary variables in the model are the zc,t variables, which means that
it is only these variables, that are considered when deﬁning the neighbourhood, and it is only these
variables whose values are limited by the local branching. In practice, this means that the neighbour-
hood deﬁnes the solution, where at most k of the chosen production hours can be changed. It might
be that a producing Line/Mould combination is just changed to another time slot, a diﬀerent article or
many other things, but the actual change is not of interest. The only thing of interest is that the optimal
solution within the restriction of the local branching constraint is determined.
Given a solution where the set Z0 represents the set of the zc,t variables that takes the value 1 in the
initial solution, the local branching constraint can be written as follows:

2.3.2
Improving the Local branching method
The procedure described above can encounter several issues, which can signiﬁcantly slow down the
progress of ﬁnding better solutions. To avoid this, [19] proposes some techniques, which can assist
the local branching method in ﬁnding better solutions faster. This section will provide an explanation
of how these techniques are used, but the pseudo-code and implementation will only be available in
Appendix C.
The ﬁrst problem occurs, when the time it takes to determine the optimal solution of the neighbourhood
is too long. Even with a small choice of k, it can be a complicated problem to solve. A way to help with
this issue is to impose a time limit on the neighbourhood search. As outlined in Appendix C, this
situation creates two cases that needs to be handled if the optimal solution is not found within the time
limit:
1. If the best solution found after the time limit has been exceeded is better than the current solution,
constraint (2.3.1) is changed such that the new solution is used instead, and the neighbourhood
of this solution is searched instead. Note that the constraint (2.3.2) is not created in this case.
2. If the best solution found after the time limit has been exceeded is not better than the current
solution, the neighbourhood size is temporarily decreased by ⌈k
2⌉, in the hopes that the smaller
neighbourhood is more easily solved than the larger.
Another technique to improve the local branching method is what [19] refers to as "Diversiﬁcation".
Diversiﬁcation is a method, which is applied if the left node of the branching tree does not contain an
improvement to the current solution. Diversiﬁcation is divided into two types.
1. The "soft" diversiﬁcation step is the ﬁrst diversiﬁcation step taken in the case, where no better
solution exist in the neighbourhood. Soft diversiﬁcation takes the right hand side of constraint
(2.3.1) and increases it by ⌈k
2⌉. This increases the size of the neighbourhood, increasing the prob-
ability of ﬁnding a better solution.
2. The "strong" diversiﬁcation step is taken in the case where "soft" diversiﬁcation did not result
in a better solution. Since no better solution exist in the neighbourhood of the current solution,
strong diversiﬁcation just chooses the ﬁrst feasible solution, that the solver ﬁnds and sets it as
the new current solution, even though it is not better than the previous solution. This ensures
that a larger part of the solution space is searched.
The diversiﬁcation part of local branching is what makes it diﬀerent from a hill climber algorithm, since
it does not stop as soon as a local extrema has been found, but continues to search for better solutions.
The amount of diversiﬁcations in the local branching procedure can be limited such that the algorithm
terminates if this number is exceeded.
The improvement techniques described in this section were all implemented and used, since the local
branching procedure did not work well without them. The mathheuristic becomes signiﬁcantly more
complicated when using these techniques, which is why the pseudocode has been omitted here and put
in Appendix C.

2.3.3
Reasons for using Local branching
Local branching is one out of many techniques, that can be used to improve a given solution. The list
below outlines some of the reﬂections, which were made, when it was decided to try this method.
1. The mathematical model only contains positive continuous and binary variables, which simpliﬁes
the local branching approach. The amount of binary variables is also fairly low compared to the
total amount of variables.
2. Much of the solve time from the Gurobi solver might be due to symmetry issues in the model,
which are potentially reduced when adding the local branching constraint.
3. A time limit is a natural part of Local branching, which means that the time limit can be set such
that the algorithm returns the best found solution after a speciﬁc amount of time. This is useful,
since it is not necessarily the optimal solution, but merely a "good" solution which can be used
as the ﬁnal plan or as initial columns in the Dantzig-Wolfe decomposition algorithm.
4. Local branching does not need to determine a solution from the ground up, since it is given a
feasible solution.
As it is often the case within the area of operations research, these reasons by no means ensure, that
the method will work well. The results from using local branching are presented in Section 4.4.2.

2.4
Cut and solve
An alternative approach to the Local branching mathheuristic is the Cut and solve mathheuristic. Cut
and solve is a method, that has some of the characteristics of Local branching, but does not require an
initial solution. The cut and solve method works by determining the optimal solution in small parts of
the full solution space, and then "cutting oﬀ" the solutions of this solution space and saving the best
solution the method has found so far. This is continued, until an optimal solution has been found or the
time limit has been exceeded. The Cut and solve method is a method, that guarantees optimality if no
time limit is imposed. It can therefore be argued, whether Cut and solve can be classiﬁed as a heuristic
or not. If a relatively short time limit is imposed and the main goal is to get a fast solution, then the
method is used as a heuristic and not as a method for ﬁnding the optimal solution. Because of this, cut
and solve has been classiﬁed as a mathheuristic for this thesis.
This section presents the Cut and solve approach for determining quick solutions along with the re-
ﬂections regarding the strengths and weaknesses of the method. The presentation of the cut and solve
method will only consider how the method is applied to BIP problems. This section has been inspired
by [20], who, to the knowledge of the authors of this thesis, was the ﬁrst to propose the method.
2.4.1
Description of Cut and solve
The Cut and solve method begins with determining a solution to a relaxed version of the BIP problem.

Reasons for using Cut and Solve
Cut and solve has some properties, which are interesting when compared to Local Branching. First and
foremost, cut and solve does not require an initial solutions. This property removes the need for any
of the heuristics presented in the beginning of this chapter. The Local branching can potentially be
"misled" by the initial solution, if the solution is of low quality. If a low quality initial solution is used
in Local branching, it will take a long time and many iterations to determine a good solution. Cut and
solve does not suﬀer from this disadvantage.
Cut and solve is also a method that ensures optimality, if no time limit is imposed on the algorithm.
Solving many small (sparse) problem will in some cases be faster than solving one big problem. It is
also a possibility, that the cut-and-solve ﬁnds the optimal solution fast, since a lot of production hours
may not be needed. It is therefore interesting to see whether this approach is able to outperform the
MIP solver.
As mentioned, the cut and solve methods determines the optimal solution of a relaxation in each it-
eration. For the method to be eﬃcient, it is important that the relaxation of the model can be solved
within a short amount of time to decrease the time of each iteration. This should be kept in mind when
attempting to use this method.

3.1
Introduction to Dantzig Wolfe decomposition
Determining the optimal production schedule turned out to be diﬃcult, when providing the solver
with just the MIP model. The Gurobi solver quickly failed to determine optimal solutions, when the
problem became too complex, whether this was due to the amount of weeks, the amount of articles
or the complexity of the connections between the used lines and moulds of the articles. This means
that other methods had to be used, when trying to solve the problem. This chapter outlines how the
Dantzig Wolfe decomposition algorithm can be used as an attempt to decrease the total solution time
of determining the optimal production schedule.
The chapter begins with a brief description of the theory behind the Dantzig Wolfe decomposition
algorithm. This is followed by the reﬂections, that were made regarding the decomposition method
and a proposition for a decomposition of the mathematical model. After this, some of the algorithms
that were used are presented and discussed along with some of the techniques, which can be used to
improve the column generation approach. Since the Dantzig Wolfe algorithm does not ensure optimal
integer solutions, the chapter concludes with a discussion on some of the diﬀerent methods that can be
used if the solution turns out to be fractional.
3.1.1
Brief explanation of Dantzig Wolfe decomposition theory
The Dantzig Wolfe decomposition algorithm is a method, which can be used to determine a lower
bound of a MIP model. The decomposition method is especially useful, when the MIP problem has a
so-called block angular structure, where a set of coupling constraints can be identiﬁed and the rest of
the constraints can be decomposed into independent subproblems. It must be true for every variable in
one of the subproblems, that it does not have any non-zero coeﬃcients in any of the other subproblems.
Dantzig Wolfe decomposition splits the problem into what is known as the restricted master problem
and the subproblems. The master problem is constructed by examining the original formulation and
convexifying the constraints, which belong to the subproblems by applying the Minkowski-Weyl’s
Theorem. This formulation requires, that all corner points of the convexiﬁed constraints are known
before the model can be solved. However, in many cases, not all possible corner points / columnns are
required to solve the problem to optimality. Because of this, the master problem only begins with a few
(often non-optimal) columns and new columns are then added iteratively as needed, which is why the
problem is known as the "restricted master problem".
The columns, which are added to the restricted master problem, are found by solving a pricing problem
for each subproblem. The subproblems outputs the columns, which has the lowest reduced cost in
the restricted master problem at the current iteration. Subproblems are solved to optimality and if a
column has a negative reduced cost, it has the potential to improve the restricted master problem and is
therefore added. After adding the columns from the pricing problems to the restricted master problem,
the master problem is solved again. This will yield new dual variables obtained from the restricted
master problem, which in turn will change the pricing problem of each subproblem. In each iteration a
lower bound can be calculated using the objective value of the restricted master problem and the latest
know reduced costs of the subproblems.
This procedure is then continued until no more columns with a negative reduced cost can be added to
the restricted master problem. Once all columns with a negative reduced cost has been added, solving

If this solution happens to be integer, the optimal solution to the original model has been found.
3.2
Decomposing the problem
The ﬁrst and most important question when considering the Dantzig Wolfe decomposition algorithm,
is the question of how to decompose the problem. Ideally, the model is decomposed into many easy-to-
solve subproblems and a relatively small LP master problem which also can be solved quickly.
In the case of this thesis, there are three obvious ways that the model can be decomposed: decomposing
on the lines, the moulds or the articles, as all of these exhibit block angular structures. The subproblems
of the Dantzig Wolfe algorithm would then be to determine individual production schedules for each
lines, moulds or articles. These schedules can then be combined in the restricted master problem to
obtain the optimal solution of the Dantzig Wolfe relaxation.
So which one would be the best choice? Two important factors should be considered, when choosing
how to decompose. The ﬁrst is the solve time of the subproblems. In each iteration of the basic Dantzig
Wolfe decomposition algorithm, each subproblem will have to be solved. This means that a long solution
time for the subproblems will not only require a long time once, but will require a long time for each
iteration, so choosing a decomposition with easy subproblems is crucial. The subproblems for all the
lines, moulds and articles were solved individually for 18 weeks to investigate and give an indication of
which method provides the fastest subproblems. The results of this can be seen in Table 3.1. The time
limit was set such that the ratio between the number of elements (articles, lines or moulds) and the

were solved, were signiﬁcantly higher compared to the moulds and the lines. It is also clear, that the
solve time of the articles, which solved to optimality, was much better than the lines and the moulds.
This does by no means ensure that the decomposition will work best when doing it on the articles, but

Adding features to the simple CG algorithm
In many cases, the compound problems did not
manage to converge within the time limit of one hour, due to a variety of reasons which will be discussed
in Section 4.5.1. The following features were added to the column generation algorithm to investigate
whether this would add any beneﬁt to the results.
1. Allow a gap when solving the subproblems. When column generation terminates, run again but
with no allowed gap.
2. Solve the master problem between each optimization of a subproblem, and update the dual values.
3. Remove suproblems from consideration as soon as there are no columns with negative reduced
cost found. Reintroduce when all other subproblems have no new columns to be added.
As it was shown in Table 3.1, many of the subproblems do not solve to optimality within the speciﬁed
time limit. However, some of the problems did ﬁnd a solution with a very small gap. Instead of discarding
these columns and moving on to the next subproblem suggestion (1) allows a gap when optimizing the
subproblems. The column generation algorithm will then potentially ﬁnd all needed columns with
this gap without forcing the solver to prove optimality of the pricing problem solutions. After column
generation terminates, the gap is changed to 0% and column generation is continued to ensure that no
columns are missing.
Suggestion (2) changes the column generation such, that the columns are added to the master problem,
as soon as they are found instead of adding them in "batches". Optimizing the master problem between
each subproblem has the potential to avoid adding redundant columns, and is not very expensive com-
pared to optimizing subproblems. In the simple column generation algorithm, the master problem is
only solved after all the pricing problems have been solved. A downside to this approach is that the
columns found for each of the pricing problem does not take the eﬀect of the columns from the other
pricing problems into account for that speciﬁc iteration. This means, that some of the pricing problems
might add columns, which could have been excluded, if the master problem had been solved after each
pricing problem. In the proposed feature, the dual values are updated between the optimization of each
subproblem, which means that every column will always have negative reduced cost in the master
problem when added.
Suggestion (3) is a technique, which is used to avoid solving particular pricing problems in each iter-
ation, if they do not return any columns with negative reduced cost. Some subproblems ﬁnd all the
necessary columns in the ﬁrst few iterations, which means that solving them again and again is not
eﬃcient. Removing them from consideration, when there are no columns with negative reduced cost
avoids this issue. Reintroducing the subproblems again, when no more columns are found for all the
other subproblems is important, since the new dual values might result in columns with negative re-
duced cost.

Lower bounds from Dantzig Wolfe decomposition
When the Dantzig Wolfe decomposition algorithm converges, a lower bound has been found for the
problem. This lower bound will always be at least as tight or better than the lower bound from a LP
relaxation, which is an advantage of the Dantzig Wolfe approach by itself.
Another useful property of the Dantzig Wolfe decomposition is that a lower bound can be calculated
in each iteration of the algorithm. This means that even if the column generation does not manage to
converge within the given time limit, a lower bound can still be found, which can be useful in some
situations.
The lower bound in an iteration of the column generation is calculated by taking the objective value

3.5
Stabilization
For some problems, column generation might suﬀer from what is known as convergence issues. This
means that the amount of columns added is much larger than necessary and the time of determining
the optimal solution to the Dantzig Wolfe decomposition is increased signiﬁcantly.
This section presents a method known as stabilization, which is an attempt to reduce convergence time
of column generation. The main idea behind stabilization is to "stabilize" the dual values of the column
generation. When the restricted master problem is solved, it is sometimes the case, that extreme dual
values are found, which are far from what the ﬁnal solution should be. This means, that the generated
columns corresponding to the extreme dual values will often be extreme columns as well and be re-
dundant in the ﬁnal solution. This section presents two stabilization methods, which were attempted
to reduce the convergence time of the column generation algorithms.

3.7
Integer solutions
Dantzig-Wolfe decomposition is in no way guaranteed to provide optimal integer solutions, and will in
many cases simply provide a fractional solution, which provides a dual bound, that is better or equal
to the dual bound found in a LP relaxation. One can be lucky and obtain integer solutions from a
converged Dantzig-Wolfe decomposition if all the variables in the restricted master problem happens
to be integer, which in that case means an optimal solution has been found. However, more often than
not, the solution found from the restricted master problem is fractional and other methods must be used
in order to obtain integer solution. A number of diﬀerent approaches to determine integer solutions
will be discussed in this section.
3.7.1
MIP’ify heuristic
The ﬁrst approach to obtain feasible integer solutions, when column generation has converged is the
so-called MIP’ify heuristic. The MIP’ify heuristic is a simple approach, where the column variables
λi
a of the restricted master problem are set to be binary. The restricted master problem is then solved
again, and the resulting solution will result in a feasible solution to the problem, since it will only use
one column for each subproblem, and the columns will only be chosen, if they are feasible together due
to the linking constraints in the master problem.
Since this is a heuristic, it does not guarantee that the optimal solution is found. If a lower bound has
been determined, the gap between this and the MIP’ify solution can be determined as a measure of
quality of the solution. An advantage of the MIP’ify heuristic is, that it can always be used, even when
column generation has not converged. The quality of the solution will naturally always improve, the
closer column generation is to being ﬁnished, but it can be a useful tool to determine the best solutions
with the columns of the non-converged instance.
3.7.2
Rounding heuristic
The Rounding heuristic is a heuristic, which can be applied to determine heuristic solutions quickly,
when column generation has converged by using the information about the optimal solution to the
restricted master problem. Like the MIP’ify heuristic, this method does not ensure optimality either.
When column generation has converged, the rounding heuristic uses the ﬁnal solution to the restricted
master problem and ﬁxes the best column from one or more of the subproblems and then continues
column generation. As soon as a column is ﬁxed for a subproblem a ∈A, the subproblem is no longer
considered in the column generation. The "best" columns from the ﬁnal solution of the master problem
are considered to be the columns, where the value of the columns is one (the master problem uses
only that column from the speciﬁc subproblem), and then one more column which is the fractional
column closest to one. After ﬁxing these variables, the column generation continues and the columns
with negative reduced cost are added for the remaining subproblems. When the column generation
converges again, at least one subproblem is ﬁxed and this is continued, until an integer solutions has
been found. In Algorithm 7 the rounding heuristic procedure is outlined.
The rounding heuristic has its name from the fact, that it rounds the fractional values of the columns.
This heuristic is slightly more complicated than the MIP’ify heuristic and it requires more time, since it
continues the column generation procedure after convergence has been achieved. Whether the MIP’ify

4.1
Introduction to results
This chapter presents the results that were achieved from implementing the techniques presented in
Chapter 2 and Chapter 3. The section begins with a presentation of the performance of the Gurobi
solver on the compact models. This is proceeded by a section, that shows the reﬂections and results
from the heuristics and mathheuristics. The column generation results are then shown and the diﬀerent
improvement techniques and their respective results are shown and discussed. The section concludes
with a discussion of the methods for making the Dantzig Wolfe decomposition result integer as well as
a summary of the results found from the diﬀerent methods.
As mentioned previously, it is the main goal of this thesis to determine an hourly production schedule
for 18 weeks or more. Because of this, most results are shown for 18 weeks to see how the diﬀer-
ent methods perform on this amount of weeks. Furthermore, the amount of time to do the planning
should always be able to ﬁnish over night, which means that the total time of solving all the compound
problems should never exceed 12 hours. A time limit of 1 hour was therefore imposed on each of the
compound problems.
4.1.1
Testing setup
Every algorithm used in this thesis (heuristics, mathheuristics and decomposition) was programmed
from the ground up without the use of any existing code or external frameworks. The testing of the
algorithms was done in Python version 3.7.3 with the Gurobi API version 9.0.2 as the solver. The data
used for testing was an anonymized data set provided by Novo Nordisk.
The non-tuning result were performed on a personal computer with 16 GB of DDR4 RAM and an
Intel(R) Core(TM) i5-9600K CPU@ 3.70GHz (6-core) processor. Few of the results were "tuning" results,
which means that the results of these tests were primarily used to compare diﬀerent parameter settings
within the scope of that particular method. The result of these tests were not used to compare with
other methods, and were in some cases done on other computers.
4.2
MIP Solver Performance
Before attempting to implement any algorithms, the individual compound problems were optimized
individually using the Gurobi solver to investigate how a commercial solver performed by itself. The
results, that were obtained from this, were used as a reference performance point, since no methods
are worth implementing, if they do not outperform the commercial solver.
All of the 12 compound problems, that were presented in Section 1.6.2, were solved in 1 week increments
going from 1 to 18 weeks, where a 1 hour time limit was set for each of these optimizations. In Figure
4.1, the optimization time of the individual compound problems are shown for each week. For some
of the compound problems, the solver failed to determine the optimal solution, when the amount of
weeks became too large. In these cases the values are not shown in the ﬁgure. There are a few important

4.4.3
Cut and solve
In Section 2.4.1, it was discussed how the following three questions will have to be answered before
implementing cut and solve:
1. Choosing a relaxation method.
2. Determining which criteria to use for choosing the variables of S in the sparse problem.
3. Determining the amount of variables to include in S.
For the implementation of this thesis, it was decided to implement cut and solve using the LP relaxation
as the relaxation method. The variables of S was decided to be the zc,t variables that were in basis in
the corresponding LP relaxation. In [19], it was proposed to use the variables with reduced cost below
a certain value and include these in the sparse problem. The variables that are in basis will always have
a reduced cost of zero, which means that the strategy of choosing only the variables in basis reduces
the sparse problem to be relatively small.
Cut and solve was tested for all the compounds for two minutes using this strategy. As mentioned in
Section 4.3, the LP relaxation of many of the compound problems were very diﬃcult to solve. This
also means, that the initial relaxation in the Cut and solve algorithm was very diﬃcult to solve, which
meant that for many of the compound problems, a solution was not found within two minutes. It was
considered to reduce the amount of variables in the sparse problem by including even fewer variables
than the ones that were in basis in the LP relaxation. Even though this would be a viable strategy, it
was decided not to do it, since the main issues of the method was due to the LP relaxations and not the
sparse problems.
Since Cut and solve is a method, that ensures optimality if no time limit is imposed, it was also tested
how it performed with a runtime of 1 hour for each compound problem to see whether the method
would be able to determine the optimal solution or outperform the commercial solver. The results of
this can be seen in Section 4.7.1.

4.5.4
Solving subproblems with LP issues
When attempting to run the column generation algorithms, it was found that for some articles, that
the main bottleneck was solving the LP-relaxation of some of the subproblems. An example of this is
Article 55 from compound 2, which did not manage to solve the LP-relaxation even when given an hour.
As already described in previous sections, the time spent on a diﬃcult subproblem can be decreased
signiﬁcantly in the column generation by implementing diﬀerent tricks to avoid these subproblems
as much as possible. However, in the end, the diﬃcult subproblems will always have to be solved to
optimality to ensure that the correct solution to the Dantzig Wolfe decomposition has been determined.
This section investigates what characterizes a "hard" suproblem and what was attempted to prevent the
damaging eﬀects on the column generation algorithm.
It was found, that the subproblems, which were hard to solve, were often articles, that can be produced

Changing the Gurobi parameters
The initial attempt to decrease the optimization time of the hard
subproblems was to manipulate some of the built-in parameter options of the Gurobi solver. The Gurobi
solver allows the user to choose the algorithm for solving the LP relaxation of the model. The options

vides an option for how aggressively the optimizer should detect symmetry issues in the model. The
symmetry detection has three options, which are either "Oﬀ", "Conservative" or "Aggresive". Each of
the LP method / symmetry option combinations were attempted and the time limit was set to ﬁve min-
utes for each combination. It was found, that the LP relaxation did not manage to determine an optimal
solution for article 21 and article 55 for any of the conﬁgurations and there was no sign of improved
performance.

One of the methods, that were considered, was to use local branching on the existing columns of the

neighbourhood of k = 1 is a very small neighbourhood, and the objective gain from changing just one
production hour is limited. It was therefore decided, that this would not be worth implementing with
the column generation to handle the articles with diﬃcult LP problems.
Decomposing the subproblem
Unfortunately, none of the attempted approaches seemed to ﬁx the
issue of diﬃcult LP problems. It is possible, that combining some of the above methods could work, but
there was no indication that any of the methods had a noticeable impact on the solution time, so this
was not tested.
The last idea, which could maybe help, would be to decompose the subproblems once more. Since the
original subproblems were decomposed on the articles, it could be possible to decompose it further
on either the lines or the moulds. To test whether this could potentially be a viable option, the hard
subproblems were solved by only considering a single line or a single mould at a time.
When doing this on Article 55, it was found that the single line and single mould problems solved in
about one minute for each of them. If this is used as an indicator of how fast the subproblems would
solve in the second level of decomposition, then the subproblems would have to converge in very few
iterations for it to be viable. Because of this fact, the further decomposition of the subproblems was not
implemented or tested further, but could be an idea for future research.
4.5.5
Solving subproblems with gap issues
Some compound problems did not suﬀer from the issue of diﬃcult LP problems, but were rather limited
by the time spent determining the optimal solution to the subproblems after having solved the LP
relaxation. It was the case for several of the compounds, that the column generation algorithm managed
to do a few iterations, but due to a few diﬃcult subproblems (with optimization times of 2-5 minutes) the
time of doing the iterations was too long. This section presents the techniques, that were implemented
as part of the column generation to see whether they could speed up the iteration times.
Adding multiple columns for each subproblem
To this point, the strategy had been to solve
the subproblems to optimality and then add the resulting column, if the reduced cost was negative. A
strategy was attempted to try and add several columns for each optimization of a subproblem. When
the Gurobi solver optimizes a subproblem, it goes through a branching tree, where it might ﬁnd several
Page 72 of 108
==PAGE:83==
Chapter 4
4.5 Dantzig Wolfe decomposition
DTU Management - Master Thesis
good columns. Even though adding these "extra" columns will not increase the amount of iterations in
the column generation, it might reduce the amount of times the subproblems will have to be solved,
before column generation converges.
Adding several columns can be done in several ways. The following list presents the methods that were
attempted besides adding only the optimal column:
1. Add all columns with negative reduced cost that the Gurobi solver found, when determining the
optimal solution.
2. Force the solver to determine the best ﬁve columns and add the ones with negative reduced cost
to the restricted master problem.
3. Force the solver to determine at least ﬁve columns, that are not necessarily the ﬁve best. Add the
ones with negative reduced cost to the restricted master problem.
When the solver has found the the optimal solution, it also saves the suboptimal solutions, that was
found during the branching procedure. Suggestion (1) checks each of the found solutions and adds them
if they have negative reduced cost. Suggestion (2) forces the solver to ﬁnd the ﬁve best solutions to the
problem. The amount of solutions can be chosen to be less or more than ﬁve. Naturally, an increased
solve time is expected, when the ﬁve best columns need to be found, but it might be that this extra
time is worth it, if adding these columns results in the column generation not having to optimize the
subproblems as many times. The last suggestion (3) forces the solver to determine at least ﬁve solutions.
The quality of these solutions are not guaranteed except for the optimal one. This is computationally
less expensive than ﬁnding the ﬁve best columns, but might also return worse solutions.
In Figure 4.7, the results of implementing the above suggestions can be seen for compound problem 3.
These techniques did not seem to work very well with respect to converging faster. Suggestion (1) (blue)
performed close to how the original (red) performed, but was slightly worse. This was a bit surprising,
since the solve time is not increased with this suggestion. The worse performance was most likely due
to the dual values changing, when adding several columns and therefore spending more time in the
subproblems, but diagnosing the exact reason was diﬃcult.

subproblems was increased signiﬁcantly, when searching for the best ﬁve columns, which can be seen
by the amount of time between each drop in objective value. It was expected, that the time between

Removing subproblems if classiﬁed as "Hard"
One attempt of handling the "hard" subproblems
was to initially remove them from the problem, if they were found to be hard and then completing
columnn generation without them. The column generation could then be ﬁnished without being halted
by the hard subproblems, and the hope was that only a few columns would have to be added for the
hard subproblems, when reintroducing them in the column generation.
A proposition like this generated the need for a strategy for classifying problems as hard subproblems.
After investigating the solution time of the subproblems it was decided that hard subproblems should
be classiﬁed as the problems with an optimization time of more than two minutes.
This strategy was implemented and tested for compound problem 5, 6 and 8, as these compounds prob-
lems had certain subproblems, where the solve time was between two and ﬁve minutes. The testing
on these compound problems showed, that the amount of time it took to arrive at the point where
the gap was changed to zero was increased signiﬁcantly. However, this was mostly due to the column
generation ignoring the "hard" subproblems and as soon as the gap was changed to zero and the hard
subproblems were reintroduced, the column generation slowed down again. The end results of all of
these compounds were either worse or the same than without this addition to the algorithm.
Using ﬁrst found column with negative reduced cost if classiﬁed as "Hard"
An alternative
strategy to removing the hard subproblems was to optimize the subproblems and then terminating the
solver as soon as a column with negative reduced cost was found, if the subproblem was classiﬁed as
hard. The hope with this approach was, that the necessary columns could be found without always
ﬁnding the optimal column.
Before this could be implemented, it had to be decided what the time limit of the "hard" subproblems
should be. Two diﬀerent approaches were attempted: Imposing no time limit on the hard subproblems
(except for the remainder of the one hour time limit) and imposing a time limit of two minutes. The
idea of the ﬁrst attempt was, that the hard problems were given a lot of time, but they should still
return the ﬁrst columns with negative reduced cost that they found. The second attempt was based on
the idea that the "hard" problems would be given an extra chance of ﬁnding a column with negative
reduced cost. If a column with negative reduced cost was not determined within the two minute time
limit, then the subproblems were removed from consideration, until the gap was changed to zero for
all subproblems.
These attempts were also tested for compound problems 5, 6 and 8. The general issue with these ap-
proaches were, that more time than two minutes was required before columns with negative reduced
cost could be found. The two minute time limit could have been increased, but by increasing the time
limit of the compound problems, the time spent on the hard subproblems was also increased, which
meant that the amount of iterations became very low.

4.5.8
Discussion and summary of Dantzig-Wolfe results
Section 4.5 has shown the results achieved from using the Danztig Wolfe decomposition and column
generation approach for determining a production plan. It was quickly found, that a simple column
generation method was not suﬃcient for solving all the compound problems and it has been discussed
and tested how this algorithm could be improved, such that the column generation algorithm could
have a higher potential of converging.
Some of the compound problems had issues of diﬃcult subproblems, where the Gurobi solvers had
issues with determining the LP relaxation solution. Several techniques were attempted to help this issue,
but unfortunately none of them were successful. Diﬃcult LP problems is a critical issue, which can be
hard to ﬁx. Even when reducing the problems with diﬀerent methods, they were very diﬃcult to solve.
The recommendation, if LP issues occurs in the future, is to investigate the use of heuristics (that are
not dependent on a MIP solver) or further decomposition to ﬁnd columns for the diﬃcult subproblems.
The main issues with using heuristics is that the subproblems will always have to be solved at least
once to ensure optimality in order to converge. The compounds with diﬃcult LP problems will most
likely be hard to solve in the future as well, which means that Novo Nordisk will have to settle for
suboptimal solutions if using the methods of this thesis.
Other compound problems did not have issues with the LP relaxation for subproblems, but with de-
termining the optimal solution fast. For these problems, it was often the cases that the subproblems
were solved eventually, but much slower than necessary for the column generation to terminate. It was
investigated whether adding several columns to the master problem for each optimization of the sub-
problem would yield any performance increase, but this did not to work very well and only increased
the solution time of the subproblems. A strategy of diﬀerentiating between "easy" and "hard" subprob-
lems was proposed and tested. Diﬀerent techniques of handling the "hard" subproblems were tested,
but none of them made a big diﬀerence. The main issue with handling the "hard" problems were that
they always had to be solved to optimality at least a few times, which often used up a large amount of
the one hour time limit, such that the column generation could not converge.
The only "hard" compound problem, which did not suﬀer from diﬃcult subproblems, was the com-
pound problem 3. This compound problem managed to do a lot of iterations, but did have diﬃculties
converging. Due to these convergence issues, two stabilization methods were tested to check, if this
would help. The ﬁrst stabilization method, using weighted dual values in the subproblems, was only
well suited for the simple column generation algorithm. The stabilization method managed to stabilize
the dual values signiﬁcantly, but the algorithm did still not manage to converge and provided poor re-
sults. The second stabilization method, BoxStep stabilization, was implemented and tested. The results
from this investigation were diﬃcult to interpret and have therefore been put in Appendix G.
The alternative decomposition of the mathematical model showed some interesting characteristics com-
pared to the original. In some cases, the alternative decomposition performed much better than the
Page 79 of 108
==PAGE:90==

original. However, the alternative decomposition also showed issues with converging for easy prob-
lems. This means that there is an associated "risk" with using the alternative decomposition, since it
might be that the converge time is very long for easy problems. This trade-oﬀshould be considered
when choosing a decomposition.
Testing the impact of the diﬀerent improvement techniques was a time consuming process. Applying
every technique to all the compound problems would take a long time and for this reason, the diﬀerent
techniques were often only attempted on a subset of the compound problems, since applying every
technique to all the compound problems would not be possible. The problems that were tested, were

Many of the compound problems did not manage to converge with the column generation approaches of

4.6.1

been found. In fact, for all of the compound problems where the column generation converged, the
optimal solution was integer and therefore optimal.

4.6.2
Rounding Heuristic and MIP’ify
When the Dantzig Wolfe does not return an integer solutions, the methods of Section 3.7 can be used to
determine a feasible solution. The MIP’ify heuristic can always be used even when column generation
has not converged, since the "binarized" version of the problem was found to solve instantly in all found
cases. The rounding heuristic continues the column generation, which can potentially take a signiﬁcant
amount of time. This means that the rounding heuristic is less suited when column generation has not
converged.
To investigate the performance of the MIP’ify and rounding heuristic, the column generation was at-
tempted on Compound 3 for 1 to 18 weeks in 1 week increments. This test was done for two reasons.
The ﬁrst was to investigate how many weeks the column generation algorithm could converge within
an hour. The second was to investigate the resulting MIP’ify and rounding heuristic solutions to see
their performance. For each of the weeks, a lower bound was determined even if the column generation
did not manage to converge. This was done by solving all the subproblems at the end of the time limit.
In Figure 4.13, the distance of the MIP’ify objective and the rounding objective to the lower bound
can be seen for 1-18 weeks for compound 3. Note that the rounding heuristic is only shown from 1-11
weeks. This is due to the column generation not converging after 11 weeks.
Figure 4.13: Absolute value diﬀerence between lower bound and MIP’ify/Rounding heuristic objective
values.
The results for compound problem 3 showed, that the MIP’ify and rounding heuristic managed to get
very close to the lower bound for all weeks in the range 1-18. The rounding heuristic determined the
optimal solution for all except one week, while the MIP’ify method did slightly worse, but still did a
very good job. These results indicate, that if the column generation manages to converge, the results of
applying any of these two heuristic performs very well. It is emphasized, that it is diﬃcult to generalise
from this, but together with the fact that the column generation found the optimal solution for all the
easy problems, it is clear that the column generation gets very close to the optimal solution in many
situations.
As it was mentioned in Section 3.7, a branch and price algorithm was not implemented in this thesis.
The ﬁrst reason for not implementing a branch and price algorithm was, that the main focus of the
thesis was directed towards making the column generation converge. Without converged solutions, it
does not make sense to implement a branch and price algorithm. The second reason was due to the
results seen in this section. Since the column generation mixed with the MIP’ify/rounding heuristic
performs as good as they do, it was decided that implementing branch and price was not of much
interest. When this is said, there is no guarantee that this will provide as good results in the future and
Novo Nordisk should keep this fact in mind, when deciding which methods to use.

5.1
Future work
The methods that has been proposed in this thesis managed to determine high quality solutions in many
situations, but there is still room for improvement. The column generation did not manage to converge
for many situations, which is something that would be very valuable for future uses. Furthermore,
several assumptions were made when developing the mathematical model as described in Section 1.3.5,
which were not considered explicitly in this thesis.
This section presents some model extensions, which can be used to extend the model, such that it
adheres better to the structure of the production facility. Furthermore, some suggestions which were not
tested in this thesis are proposed, that might improve the column generation algorithm if implemented.
The suggestions of this section can be kept in mind by Novo Nordisk, if they are to implement the results
of this thesis in the future.

Model extensions
The compact model was developed with a number of assumptions and simpliﬁcations as seen in Section

As mentioned brieﬂy in Section 4.5.4, the subproblems with LP issues can poten-

columns if solving the LP relaxation is found to be very diﬃcult.

Dantzig Wolfe relaxation has been found, but it could potentially get the column generation closer to
converging.

Additional parameter tuning
Several of the methods which have been used in this thesis are dependent on parameters that are not
given from the start. Choosing the best values of these parameters is something which can potentially
increase the performance of the methods. The tuning of these parameters is something, which would
be valuable to do in the future. Especially since the data will most likely change signiﬁcantly.
The ﬁrst and perhaps the most important, parameters which could be investigated further are the ob-
jective coeﬃcients. These were chosen early in the progress and it could be interesting to see how the
developed methods would perform with other choices of objective coeﬃcients.
Furthermore the tuning of the k parameter in Local branching, the p value in weighted dual stabilization
and the box sizes and penalties in BoxStep stabilization are all values that could be investigated further.
This should ideally be done with diﬀerent datasets to get a fair performance measurement.

As mentioned in the beginning of this thesis, the main goal of this project was to investigate methods,

Denmark. At the beginning of this thesis, the following research goals were made:
1. Construct a mathematical model which can be used and extended in the future by Novo Nordisk
to determine detailed hourly productions plans.
2. Determine the best possible production plan with hourly granularity for up to 18 weeks within
the scope of this thesis.
3. Develop methods which can be used for determining the optimal or near optimal solutions to the

The ﬁrst goal of the thesis was to develop a good representative mathematical model. In Chapter 1,

a twelve hour time limit. It was proven diﬃcult to determine the optimal solutions to the mathematical
model within the time limit. Novo Nordisk should be aware that this is an issue with the model. Even
though the optimal solution was often diﬃcult to determine, the methods of this thesis can still be
used to determine very good solutions for most cases. The recommendation is that Novo Nordisk tests
whether the good solutions can be generalized by testing on more datasets.

5.3
Conclusion
This thesis has presented an approach for developing a production schedule for producing plastic com-
ponents for the insulin devices FlexPen® and FlexTouch® at a Novo Nordisk factory in Hillerød, Den-
mark. A mathematical model has been developed which, if optimized, produces a production schedule
on an hourly granularity for an arbitrary amount of weeks. When exploring the structure of the pro-
duction system, it was found that this mathematical model could be split into twelve smaller models,
which could be solved independently. The solutions found from optimizing these twelve problems sep-
arately could be combined to constitute a full solution. It was found, that the diﬃculty of these twelve
problems was very diﬀerent depending on how many items, machines and moulds, that were a part of
the problem. A commercial MIP solver was able to determine a detailed production plan for the next
18 weeks for ﬁve out of the twelve problems within an hour.
Since seven out of twelve problems were not solved to optimality by a commercial solver within the time
limit of one hour, other methods were used. The thesis has attempted to use heuristics, mathheuristics
and Dantzig-Wolfe decomposition for determining the best possible schedule. The greedy heuristic was
developed to determine a quick high quality feasible solution and produced fairly good solutions, which
were found to be very useful when combined with other methods. It was found that the Local Branching
mathheuristic was able to improve some of the solutions found from the greedy heuristic, but it was
not the case for all of the twelve problems. Local Branching was mostly useful for some of the easier
problems, while it did not provide a lot of improvement on some of the harder problems. The method,
Cut and solve, was also implemented and tested. The Cut and solve method managed to determine the
optimal solution for the easy problems, but did not provide any results for the diﬃcult problems, since
the LP relaxation of these problems were not solved within an hour.
The results found from the heuristics and mathheuristics were useful, but did not guarantee optimality.
With the goal of determining the optimal solution, a Dantzig Wolfe decomposition of the mathemati-
cal model was proposed. Many column generation algorithms were tested to determine an algorithm,
which was able to solve the Dantzig Wolfe relaxation within the time limit. A simple column generation
algorithm was able to converge for the easy problems, but not for the harder problems. The reasons
for not being able to converge were diﬀerent depending on the problem. The issues have been investi-
gated and diﬀerent solution techniques were proposed and tested. The techniques did not improve the
column generation algorithm to a degree, where the hard problems converged, but some of the harder
problems did get closer to convergence. It was found, that the Dantzig Wolfe relaxation in many cases
were either optimal or close to optimal. Because of this, some simple heuristics were implemented to
make the Dantzig Wolfe relaxation results integer. It was seen, that these results were very close to
optimal solutions by comparing the lower bound from the Dantzig Wolfe algorithm with the heuristic
solutions, and even performed better than the commercial MIP solver in many of the problems.
It was the goal to determine a method for determining a detailed plan for 18 weeks. The methods
proposed in this thesis can be used by Novo Nordisk to determine detailed production plans for this
amount of time. Determining the optimal solution has proven to be diﬃcult, but by using the methods
proposed in this thesis, the possibility of achieving high quality solutions are promising.

Appendix G BoxStep stabilization results
DTU Management - Master Thesis
G
BoxStep stabilization results
The euclidean distance from the last dual values to the all other dual values was plotted for all param-
eter setting for diﬀerent parameter setting for Boxstep stabilization. These distances were compared to
the euclidean distance from the dual values obtained from the original Dantzig-Wolfe decomposition
without any stabilization applied. The results of these can be seen in the following ﬁgures.
Across all parameter setting for the BoxStep Stabilization, the dual variables was much higher than the
dual variables without stabilization. This was unexpected, as it was expected that they would be lower
and more stable using the BoxStep stabilization. The results from this were therefore inconclusive and
was not used for further testing in this thesis.
