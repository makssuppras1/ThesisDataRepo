Abstract
Due to the rising cost of hydrocarbons, a tense geopolitical situation, and countries’ desire
to own independent energy sources, research on inaccessible geological formations is required.
Knowing that formation stresses are essential in well planning, hydrocarbon production, and
fluid injection, there is a need for more research into geomechanics and effective stress. In
order to correctly estimate the effective stress, the effective stress coeﬀicient must be included.
The purpose of this research is to investigate the effect of temperature on static and dynamic
effective stress coeﬀicients. The coeﬀicients were calculated using upper Jurassic sandstone
rock data from the North Sea obtained during the mechanical compaction experiment in uni-
axial conditions. Furthermore, the project calculates shear failure pressure to investigate the
effect of the effective stress coeﬀicient on borehole stability.
In ambient temperature, the majority of estimated static Biot’s coeﬀicient ”n” ranges from
0.2 to 1.2. The static Biot’s coeﬀicient has an average value of 0.7. Most static Biot’s coeﬀi-
cient ”n” in situ temperatures are comparable to those tested in ambient temperatures. The
dispersion of α, on the other hand, increases with temperature. The dynamic Biot’s coeﬀicient
increases with temperature and ranges between 0.4 and 0.6 α. With increasing axial stress,
the dynamic Biot’s coeﬀicient decreases as well. There is a possibility of an error in the estima-
tion of elastic velocities, which could influence the results. It is diﬀicult to determine whether
temperature influences Biot’s coeﬀicient based on the experiment and calculations.
Biot’s coeﬀicient appears to have a strong influence on shear failure pressure. The Biot’s co-
eﬀicients used in the calculations to estimate the minimum pressure required to avoid borehole
collapse ranged from 0.4 to 0.7, and they were compared to estimates with a Biot’s coeﬀicient
of 1. The shear failure pressure difference between 0.4 and 1 α is 38MPa. As a result, the
correct assumption of Biot’s coeﬀicient may have a significant impact on shear failure pressure
and borehole stability estimations.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Objective
Due to the increasing costs of hydrocarbons, a tense geopolitical situation and countries’ de-
sires to own independent energy sources, research is needed on hardly accessible geological
formations. Developing high-pressure and high-temperature hydrocarbon reservoirs can fulfil
some of the mentioned needs. Besides oil and gas, there is a raised interest in geothermal
energy. Furthermore, green sources of heat and electricity have recently become a priority due
to constantly rising Earth temperatures.
High-pressure and high-temperature reservoirs and subsurface areas with significant geother-
mal gradients can cause severe operations issues. Knowing that formation stresses are essential
in well planning, hydrocarbon production, or fluid injection, there is a need for further study
into geomechanics and effective stress.
Any subsurface changes in the stress field done by nature or human contribution may change
the effective stress of the rock. Effective stress estimation can be utilised to enhance drilling
procedures and predict depletion in the reservoir. Effective stress is a term that describes
the difference between the overburden stress and pore pressure inside the rock formation. To
correctly estimate the effective stress, including the effective stress coeﬀicient is crucial. The
effective stress coeﬀicient is the pore pressure fraction describing rock pores’ elastic deforma-
tion. Omissions or wrong calculations of effective stress coeﬀicients can affect in situ effective
stress estimation and lead to limitations in planning and operations.
One of the biggest risks while drilling a well is borehole instability which may lead to rock
failure. A drilling fluid, also known as drilling mud, maintains the correct pressure inside the
borehole. When the borehole pressure is too low, it may lead to borehole shear failure and
borehole collapse. The minimum borehole pressure needed to avoid shear failure of the bore-
hole wall can be estimated using an effective stress coeﬀicient.
In the first part, this research aims to study the influence of temperature on the static and
dynamic effective stress coeﬀicients. The coeﬀicients were calculated from rock data obtained
during the mechanical compaction experiment in uniaxial conditions. Two sandstone samples
were tested inside the oedometer. The first sample was tested at a constant ambient temper-
ature and the second at a rising temperature up to 165◦C. Tobias Orlander conducted the
oedometer test in 2017 at GEO (Maglebjergvej 1, 2800 Kongens Lyngby).

CHAPTER 2
Theory
2.1
Static and dynamic effective stress coeﬀicient
Effective stress estimations are utilised to improve drilling procedures, develop hydraulic frac-
turing, and predict the subsidence process. The theory of effective stress currently used in
practice was published by [Biot,1941].
According to [Biot,1941], to calculate the effective
stress of saturated rock, the pore pressure Pp multiplied by the effective stress coeﬀicient α
should be deducted from the overburden stress σ, as visible in Eq.2.1.
σeﬀ= σ −αPp
(2.1)
The overburden stress σ comes from the weight of sediments over the rock, and pressure
inside the pores Pp is assumed to be a hydrostatic pressure exerted from the surface by the
water column. In other words, pore pressure is the fluid pressure trapped in the pores coun-
teracting the stress from inside.
The effective stress coeﬀicient α, known as Biot’s coeﬀicient, describes the change of surface
contact between the grains. Put differently, Biot’s coeﬀicient defines the poroelastic deforma-
tion of the pores. The maximum value of Biot’s coeﬀicient is 1, corresponding to a rock with
no cement at the grain contacts [Olsen,2008]. The lower limit of Biot’s coeﬀicient illustrates
rocks’ porosity. Considering one pore, a free space volume between grains under the greatest
possible compaction before rock failure equals the rock’s porosity.
The static Biot’s coeﬀicient ”n” is based on the general theory of 3D consolidation presented
by [Biot,1941]. The theory explains the phenomenon called consolidation and stress prediction
in porous media. One of the ways to calculate the static effective stress coeﬀicient is to perform
mechanical loading on the rock sample in uniaxial conditions. The uniaxial conditions enable
us to consider the stresses and strain in the vertical direction. The coeﬀicient ”n” depends on
strain relations with pore pressure and differential stress.
In Eq.2.2 σd is differential stress, Pp is a pore pressure, and ϵa is an axial strain. The
differential stress results from pore pressure subtraction from axial stress σa. To successfully
calculated the static Biot’s coeﬀicient, based on Eq.2.2, it is mandatory to maintain separately
constant differential stress and constant pore pressure conditions. The derivation of the equa-
tion Eq.2.2 is based on [Todd,1972] derivation and can be found in [Bula,2022]. The precise
methodology behind the equation is explained in the section 5.2.

Stresses around the borehole
A precise assessment of pre-production in situ effective stresses is required to understand poten-
tial deformations that may affect borehole integrity during drilling and production [Regel,2017].
The stable wellbore is crucial during the drilling phase since it prevents damaging borehole
walls and enables smooth and successful operations. In the borehole, drilling mud (drilling
fluid) is responsible, among the others, for maintaining the pressure in the well. The pressure
is created by drilling mud column with a specific density. The influence of that pressure on
the borehole wall is visible in Fig.2.1.
Figure 2.1: The figure presents graphically how the borehole responds to low and high drilling mud
pressure. Too low pressure of the drilling fluid leads to breakouts and, eventually, hole collapse. Too
high mud pressure can create tensile fractures resulting in formation breakdown and significant mud
losses [J. Zhang,2019].
Underground formations are constantly pressured, mainly due to overburden and tectonic
forces. During drilling, the solid material in the form of cuttings is removed. The fluid pressure
supports the Oval shape borehole wall instead of the rock [E. Fjaer,2008]. Generally, the in-
situ formation stresses differ from the fluid pressure; as a result, there is stress redistribution
across the well. Insuﬀicient drilling fluid pressure may lead to rock weakening and failure.
If the vertical well is considered, the rock formation beneath the surface is exposed in
Cartesian coordinates to axial stress σz, maximum horizontal stress σHmax in the x direction
and minimum horizontal stress σhmin in the y direction.
However, to examine stresses around the borehole, we need to express σz, σHmax and
σhmin in cylindrical coordinates [E. Fjaer,2008]. The cylindrical coordinates are represented
by vertical stress σv equal to axial stress σz, tangential stress σθ and radial stress σr. The
tangential and radial stress combines horizontal stresses from Cartesian coordinates.

3.1
Effective stress coeﬀicient
The theory of effective stress is crucial in geomechanics. Initially introduced by [Terzaghi,1923],
the concept is based on a difference between overburden stress σ and pore pressure Pp (Eq.
3.1). Terzaghi investigated loose granular sediments in which the contact area between grain
surfaces is negligible. Later, [Biot,1941] developed the generalized poroelastic theory; however,
the effective stress coeﬀicient, known as Biot’s, was published by [Biot and Willis,1957].
σeﬀ= σ −Pp
(3.1)
In this project, a compaction experiment was carried out to calculate Biot’s coeﬀicient. A
precise description of the compressibility apparatus was presented by [Geertsma,1957]. More-
over, Geertsma’s research explains the volumetric changes in the porous rock by injecting a
fluid inside the compacted sandstone sample.
In [Qiao,2012], the authors focused on low-permeable Canadian Nikanassin sandstone.
They estimated the effective stress of the rock by calculating Biot’s coeﬀicient. The effective
stress would be used to calculate the magnitude of pressure needed to fracture the sandstone
and increase permeability. The publication describes the static Biot’s coeﬀicient calculations
as a ratio of the variation in permeability to pore pressure to that with respect to confin-
ing pressure [Qiao,2012]. Low permeable (0.002-0.014 mD) Nikassin sandstone had a porosity
range between 0.054 and 0.058. The static Biot’s coeﬀicients for the first sample were 0.54, 0.55
and 1. The average static Biot’s coeﬀicient for the second sample with similar permeabilities
was 0.2. The authors suggest that permeability measurement error influenced the difference
between the two values of Biot’s coeﬀicient.
[Yu,2020] studied the anisotropy of the effective stress coeﬀicient of sandstone under true
triaxial stress to predict the effective stress. The authors inserted a sandstone sample from the
Chongqing area into the triaxial apparatus. The test was conducted under changing axial and
horizontal stresses, from 20MPa to 50MPa, and helium-controlled pore pressures (6-10MPa).
The effective stress coeﬀicient was calculated with permeability as a function of effective stress.
The Biot’s coeﬀicient ranged from 0.45 to 1.1. The Biot’s coeﬀicient increased with pore pres-
sure when principal stresses remained constant.
Determination of the effective stress coeﬀicient for Brazilian sandstones was studied by
[Vasquez,2009]. They estimated dynamic Biot’s coeﬀicient with compressional velocity. The
five sandstone samples had density from 2.65[g/cm3] to 2.66[g/cm3], porosity between 13%
and 20% and permeability from 0.3mD to 16mD. The axial stress and pore pressure during the

conditions. The temperature has a greater decreasing influence on the Biot’s coeﬀicient at high
pressures than it does at low pressure in both static and dynamic conditions.

The second part of the report consists of calculations investigating the possible effects on

number less than 1 denotes a robust frame that is likely capable of withstanding greater pres-

5.1
Data calibration
The raw data from the test could be influenced by external factors and might not represent
actual results from rock samples. It could happen because the the system might slightly deform
during loading. Therefore, data calibration is needed to ensure correct measurements are taken
for calculations. The two data calibrations were done to ensure that the obtained strain values
came only from the specimen. One additional adjustment was performed on elastic waves to
compute correct elastic velocities.
5.1.1
Deflection of the system
One of the performed calibrations was to check if the oedometer system deflection did not
influence the recorded strain during mechanical loading. The method to investigate that was
to perform a test in the same way as explained for the sandstone samples Fig.4.5 but on a plug
made of steel. It was done to check if any additional deflection was coming from the system.
A plug made from steel was inserted between the pistons of the oedometer and connected to
LVDTs to measure deformation. The plug had a slightly smaller diameter than the tested
sandstone specimens. As a result, the plug was not touching the wall of the test ring. The test
was carried out in five consecutive loops.
The steel’s Young’s modulus stands at around 200GPa [Chen,2016]. It means that the
deformation of the steel plug is possible while reaching 200GPa in stress/strain ratio. The
calibration test was programmed to exert similar stress as during the experiment on rock spec-
imens. The magnitude of exerted axial stress was too small and could not deform the steel
plug. Nevertheless, sensors recorded a change in strain.
Thus, I presumed that the strain measured by the LVDTs came from the system, not the
steel plug. Moreover, deflection calibration is believed to be accurate for both rock samples.

visible in Appendix 9.0.1

7.0.1
Strain measurements
Fig.6.1 and Fig.6.2 represent the strain measurements at ambient and in situ temperatures.
The first strain loop differs slightly from the rest four loops in both temperatures. It could be
due to further sample adjustments in the steel ring since the sample experienced the maximum
axial stress and pore pressure for the first time in the first loop. It is less likely but possible
that both samples could not be fully saturated. So, increasing axial stress and pore pressure
could lead to increased saturation. In Fig.6.1, loops two, three, four and five are very similar.
The strain slightly decreases with every loop. It might be because the sample gets squeezed
many times, and the rock is weakened.
The test in Loop 1 was carried out on samples BB-1 and BB-2 under similar stress and
temperature conditions. The first loop of the test in rising temperature starts at 25◦C. As a
result, we can compare two samples within the first loop marked in purple. There was a visual
difference in initial strain when the test started, even though both sandstones come from the
same core and have similar physical properties. The strain of sample BB-1 at the beginning
reaches around 0.5%, and sample BB-2 1%. Since samples were intact before the test, the
dissimilarity could occur due to the samples’ slightly different mineralogy, which difference
was not discovered.
The maximum strain recorded under the reservoir temperature is markedly bigger than
at ambient temperature. A similar effect that temperature influenced the increase in strain
was observed by [Asadollahpour,2021] and [Hassanzadegan,2014]. The slight enlargement of
minerals with temperature could occur. Rising and declining differential stress when mineral
volume increases with temperature could create micro-fractures similar to the ones presented

7.0.3
Behaviour of Elastic Wave Velocities
Compressional and shear wave velocities in Fig.6.5(a) and (b) vary significantly compared to
temperature. S-wave velocity is relatively constant, and P-wave velocity decreases with tem-
perature markedly.
Based on [E. Fjaer,2008], the temperature may slightly influence fully
saturated rock and decrease velocity. Moreover, the behaviour of both velocities would be
expected to be similar to each other, like in [Hassanzadegan,2014] or [Vasquez,2009].
Both P- and S- wave velocities have reacted to changes in stress conditions during the test.
The velocities obtained in constant differential conditions are slower than in constant pore
pressure conditions. This change in velocities can probably be linked to compaction. During
constant differential stress conditions, the axial stress is counteracted by pore pressure. As
a result, the differential stress stands at 50MPa. When the condition changes in the second
part of the loop, the differential stress doubles and reaches 100MPa. The increased differential
stress could cause a squeeze of the sample, increasing contact between the grains and rock
compaction. As a result, a slight increase in density could lead to a sonic velocity increase.
This occurrence happens in Vp in all temperatures and Vs in two first loops, so in 25◦C and
50.◦C. Similar effects were noticed in [Asadollahpour,2021] and [Vasquez,2009]. In Fig.6.5(a),
the velocities are higher in loops 3, 4, and 5 when differential stress is constant.
A significantly different behaviour exists with temperature between P- and S- wave veloc-
ity. Moreover, Vs changes with temperature when initial velocities are higher with greater

7.0.6
Borehole stability
The shear failure pressure estimations show that Biot’s coeﬀicient significantly influences
the drilling window. According to several authors, [Yu,2020], [Mavko,2020], [Orlander,2020],
[Regel,2017], [Hassanzadegan,2014], and many others, Biot’s coeﬀicient of sandstone can often
appear below one. As a result, calculating shear failure pressure using an effective stress co-
eﬀicient less than one in the equation for the Mohr-Coulomb failure criterion reduces shear
failure pressure significantly. When the drilling window is narrow, using ”α” equal one may
cause SFP to be greater than pore pressure, further restricting the drilling window,[Regel,2017].
However, because the rock at the borehole wall is considered damaged while drilling, the value
of one is commonly used in the industry.
7.0.7
Future research
Based on the research presented in the report, possible corrections could enhance future re-
search on the influence of temperature on the effective stress coeﬀicient. One of the greatest
uncertainty in the report is connected with elastic wave velocities. The correct estimation is
essential in dynamic calculations. Performing a compaction test on a longer sample could be
the key to decreasing uncertainty. The longer sample could provide a later P-arrival time and
decline the uncertainty of picking the arrival time or estimating the delay time.

Moreover, the S-wave arrival could be chosen based on several signal amplitudes because
the shear wave would arrive later. Sometimes the compressional and shear wave overlap under
different angles, and later arrival time would allow tracking the waves. The increased sample
length could decrease the uncertainty of picking the right arrival times, and small uncertainty
would not affect the results significantly.
Another possibility is to use a rock sample built from minerals that have lower velocities.
One-inch samples with slower elastic velocities than sandstone could increase the arrival time
and decrease the uncertainty by making the changes in the signal more visible.
The dynamic Biot’s coeﬀicient calculations could be carried out only at ambient tempera-
ture to complete this research. The results indicate the difference between the velocities and
changing behaviour of S-wave velocity when initially faster in high differential stress increases.
However, it changes its velocity later and is faster when differential stress is declined. The
test in ambient temperature could explain if the results are linked to short elastic wave arrival
times, rising temperature or additional uncertainty that this report has yet to discover.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
This study aimed to investigate the influence of temperature on Biot’s coeﬀicient and check to
what extent the coeﬀicient influence borehole stability estimations. The report consists of a
theory and methodology to describe how the test was performed. Recommendations used in
the discussion should decrease the number of uncertainties and improve future studies. The
conclusions below are based on the results presented in this report.
The static effective stress coeﬀicient of sandstone from the Heno formation tested at am-
bient temperature stands at approximately 0.7 ”n”. Most of the static Biot’s coeﬀicient data
points tested in situ temperature are also located around 0.7 ”n”; however, the standard devia-
tion of the data increase with temperature and reaches +/- 2.3 ”n” at 165◦C. As a result, it is
diﬀicult to state if the temperature influences static Biot’s coeﬀicient. The rising temperature
visually affects the strain of the sample. The strain of the sandstone sample in the last loop
at 165◦C was 0.7% greater than at 25◦C.
The elastic velocities increase with rising differential stress between axial stress and pore
pressure. The P-wave velocity significantly decreases with temperature, contrary to S-wave
velocity, since shear velocity remains constant with rising temperature. There is a potential
uncertainty in elastic wave velocities that could influence the dynamic moduli.
The bulk density of the sandstone sample increases with increasing differential stress and
decreases with temperature. These changes could be explained by decreasing the sample vol-
ume while compacting and increasing the volume when minerals are expanded due to rising
temperature.
The bulk modulus decreases with the temperature, similarly to the P-wave modulus. The
P-wave velocity’s significant decrease with temperature affects compressional modulus and
bulk modulus.
Dynamic Biot’s coeﬀicient ranges between 0.4 and 0.6 ”α” and increases with rising temper-
ature. Its standard deviation remains similar throughout the test, equaling 0.05 ”α”. Moreover,
dynamic Biot’s coeﬀicient decreases with increasing differential stress.
Correct Biot’s coeﬀicient calculation may significantly influence borehole stability estima-
tions.
The value different from 1 α may markedly decline the shear failure pressure limit.
The industry could potentially be beneficial from this finding since the precise in situ stresses
estimations could lead to the smoothest operation and, consequently, cost decrease.
Besides effective stress coeﬀicient estimation in different temperatures, this research demon-
strates the importance of precision and consistency when it comes to dynamic moduli estima-
tions.
