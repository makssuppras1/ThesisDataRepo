Abstract 
Abstract 
This thesis presents the design, development, and validation of a prototype press 
machine tailored for micro-product applications, with a pressing capacity of up to 
5 kN. Initiated in collaboration with the Department of Design Engineering at 
Polman Bandung, the project supports the establishment of a mini factory 
laboratory aimed at enhancing the production-based learning environment. The 
proposed system qualifies as a mechatronic product according to the VDI 2206 
framework, integrating mechanical, electrical, and control subsystems. 
The development followed a structured design methodology based on the 
updated VDI 2206 V-model, guiding the process from requirements elicitation 
through to system integration. Key mechanical components, such as a servo-
driven ball screw system, were selected to ensure precision and repeatability, 
while the control architecture was implemented using a PLC and HMI interface to 
facilitate multiple operational modes. Ergonomic and safety considerations were 
incorporated based on relevant ISO standards to accommodate operator usability 
and compliance. 
Validation was performed through geometry and capacity testing using aluminum 
sheet materials and a progressive cutting tool. Results demonstrated that the 
machine could operate effectively within the specified force and speed range, 
fulfilling 16 out of 17 target requirements. This prototype lays a foundation for 
future enhancements in safety monitoring, load sensing, and tool modularity, 
enabling broader applications in micro-manufacturing environments. 
 
Keywords: Mechatronic System, Micro-Manufacturing, Press Machine, VDI 2206, 
Prototype Design

1 Introduction 
1.1 Background 
Department of Design Engineering in Politeknik Manufaktur Bandung (Bandung 
Polytechnic for Manufacturing), hereafter referred to as Polman, is currently 
developing a mini factory laboratory to support its future production-based 
academic curriculum. One of the key systems within this lab is a Press Machine 
designed for microproducts. 
The machine qualifies as a mechatronic system according to VDI 2206, 
encompassing three functional domains: mechanical, electronic, and control 
system. Since 2021, the preliminary mechanical design has been developed and 
partially manufactured. However, the system currently lacks complete design and 
integration of the electronic components and control system. 
For the press machine to be operational, all three domains must be cohesively 
integrated. This project addresses the integration of the electronic control system 
and information architecture to produce a fully functional prototype, contributing 
to future developments of the press machine. 
1.2 Design Methodology 
This project adopts the VDI 2206 methodology for the design methodology as it 
is tailored for a mechatronic product in macro level. It provides a systematic 
approach to solving a complex problem and prevents us from jumping to a 
conclusion without exploring possible solutions.  It also promotes ensuring all 
three domains are being considered iteratively rather than combining individual 
aspects at the end of the process. Thus, it is ensured that the “right” system 
(validation) is developed in the “right” manner (verification). [1]

Figure 1-1 The new V-model for VDI 2206 [2] 
The new V-model also introduces six checkpoints behind the three strands of the 
V-shape (main task and activities). These checkpoints consist of a list of 
questions that need to be ticked before going on to the next task and ensuring 
that the current task is completed. The six checkpoints mentioned are: business 
case, specification, architecture, implementation, integration, and transition. 
Unfortunately, the mechanical domain was designed and manufactured upfront 
with minimal considerations to the other two domains. This leads to some 
constraints inside the design methodology such as minimizing changing and 
modifying components which will be limited solutions. The detailed project 
development will be explained in the following section.

3.1 System Architecture and Design 
A system can be best understood by analyzing its fundamental inputs and outputs. 
As shown in the black box model (Figure 3-1), the primary function of the machine 
is to transform sheet metal into a finished product. This main statement will then 
act as a guide to develop its internal system. This leads to the question: how do 
energy, signals, and material flow within the system to produce a micro-part? 
Figure 3-3 maps the developed sub-functions needed to achieve the main

3.2 Implementation of System Elements 
System elements in this project are clustered into three big domains: mechanical, 
electrical, and control domain. While the mechanical domain was defined in a 
previous milestone, several undocumented variations and gaps in component 
analysis were identified and missing analysis of selected components which will 
be checked in this milestone. Control domain will mainly discuss modes that the 
machine must carry out for its operator. The electrical domain focuses on actuator 
functions and interfaces between mechanical components and the control system. 
It is important to mention that the installation and the programming were done in 
collaboration with a third party. 
==PAGE:19==
Implementation of System Elements

Figure 3-3 Transparent box showing components to fulfill the function unit 
 
Figure 3-3 illustrates the complete component connections required to fulfil each 
sub-function, along with the respective domains they operate in. Power is 
supplied to the PLC and servo driver, which then distribute energy to the other 
components. Operator inputs via buttons, the HMI, or optical sensors send control 
signals into the system. These signals are processed by the PLC, with user 
interaction and feedback managed through the HMI. The processed signals are 
then transmitted to other components to control the machine's operation. 
An external component, the press tool, is required for the machine to function and 
can be interchanged depending on the product being manufactured. This tool is 
installed between the Bolster and the Ram. However, due to time and fund 
constraints, certain features specifically the AOPD sensor and load cell were not 
implemented in this milestone.

3.3 System Integration  
The integration of mechanical, electrical, and control domains has been 
successfully realized to form a cohesive and operational press machine system. 
This integration ensures that power delivery, signal transmission, motion control, 
and user interaction are functionally synchronized to support the intended micro-
manufacturing processes. 
The system consists of three core subassemblies: the main body (see Figure 
3-6)., the control panel (see Figure 3-5), and the twin-button stand (Figure 3-7). 
They are interconnected using standardized socket connectors to allow plug-and-
play functionality and simplify assembly and maintenance during this 
development stage.

4.1 Geometry Verification 
The goal of geometry verification is to ensure the manufacturing and assembly 
quality of the machine components. The geometry checkpoints: flatness, 
perpendicularity, and parallelism are illustrated in Figure 4-1. The characteristics 
and tolerance specifications for each geometric feature are as follows: 
• Datum A is selected as the primary reference surface due to its longer 
length [13] and the difficulty of modifying it after assembly. It consists of 
two guide rail surfaces installed on the frame. The linear guides rest on 
this surface, which guides the Ram plate movement via the ball screw.  
• Datum B is designated as the secondary reference surface because it 
serves as the fixed interface for the press tool. 
• Flatness checkpoints 1, 2, and 3 are assigned a tolerance limit of 0.2 mm 
for a length range of 100–300 mm, classified under H class standards [13]. 
• Perpendicularity checkpoints 4 and 6 have a tolerance limit of 0.3 mm for 
lengths between 100–300 mm, also classified as H class [13]. 
• Parallelism at checkpoints 5 and 7 is the most challenging to control, as it 
is influenced by other geometric features. The specified tolerance limit is 
0.2 mm [13].

5 Discussion 
5.1 Challenges 
Despite the successful prototype development, the project faced several 
challenges across technical, organizational, and operational domains. These 
challenges influenced timelines, resource allocation, and system completeness. 
a) Technical challenges 
• 
Outdated component drawings: Ram and Bolster plate designs were 
changed without synchronized updates in documentation, which led to 
other problem in tool designing and ease of assembly. 
• 
Inadequate fastening features: Limited mounting points on Ram and 
Bolster made it difficult to securely install or align the press tool. Initially 
they are designed with T-slots and currently are equipped with threaded 
holes (see Figure 5-1 and Figure 5-2). 
• 
Cable routing errors: Misaligned and bent cabling within the control panel 
caused intermittent power losses, highlighting the need for stricter 
electrical layout planning. 
b) Organizational challenges 
• 
Limited in-house manufacturing: Many parts required external fabrication, 
introducing delays due to supplier schedules and availability. 
• 
Personnel limitations: Project progress depended on a small team with 
variable availability, slowing down integration and testing cycles. 
c) Procedural challenges 
• 
Improper application of VDI 2206: The mechanical system was developed 
in isolation before other domains were considered, making it difficult to 
implement cross-domain updates without redesigns. 
• 
Abrupt change of plan: Due to other challenges, some target on this 
milestone is pending and need adjustment without proper planning. 
 
These challenges underline the importance of an integrated design process, 
version-controlled documentation, and early-stage risk planning.

6 Conclusion 
The prototype press machine for micro-products has been successfully designed, 
integrated, and tested as part of a broader initiative to support hands-on 
manufacturing education. Using the VDI 2206 methodology, the project ensured 
a structured and interdisciplinary development approach, resulting in a functional 
system that combines mechanical precision, responsive control, and user-friendly 
interaction. 
Key objectives were achieved, including the integration of servo-driven motion 
systems, development of a PLC-based control interface with multiple operation 
modes, and adherence to several ISO standards for safety and ergonomics. 
Validation tests confirmed the machine’s ability to perform cutting operations on 
aluminum sheets with consistent dimensional accuracy and within the intended 
pressing force of 5 kN. 
However, several aspects remain for future work, including the installation of the 
load cell for real-time force monitoring, completion of the safety enclosure, 
enhancement of the HMI with intuitive visual and auditory alerts, and refinement 
of mechanical tolerances to address minor alignment deviations. These 
improvements will further elevate the machine’s reliability, safety, and adaptability. 
In conclusion, this prototype represents a significant step toward developing a 
fully functional, compact, and precise press system for micro-manufacturing, with 
the potential to support various research and educational needs in manufacturing 
environments.
