This Master’s thesis revolves around the emerging field of absolute sustainability
and the interrelation with minimal requirements for social sustainability and absolute
environmental sustainability. The study is intended to contribute to the scientific
literature, providing perspective and inspiration to further studies regarding social
growth, technological prioritization and other research in relation to the ecological
and health related thresholds which limit our options. Hence, this Master’s thesis
is structured as a paper in a scientific journal style and format, with the intend of
publication in the near future. Due to the choice of format, the thesis will consist of
only key findings, while supplementary information will be provided in appendices,
documenting approach, additional results and data sources.
The thesis has been conducted in the period from January till August 2023 for

Abstract
This paper investigates the impacts on absolute environmental sustainability thresholds
from meeting requirements for Decent Living Standards (DLS) for the global population.
A hypothetical scenario representing today’s world with activity levels conforming with
standards for decent living was created based on current technologies from available
data and reconfigured consumption patterns.
Results were calculated twofold, using the Planetary Boundaries-based Life cycle
impact assessment method and the Carrying Capacities normalization references in
ILCD-recommended impact categories.
The DLS scenario exceeds the thresholds for the wast majority of the control
variables, indicating unsustainable global pressure levels; Pressures on Climate change,
Ocean acidification, Biogeochemical flows - Nitrogen and Biogeochemical flows - Re-
gional phosphorus, Atmospheric aerosol loading and Biosphere integrity - Functional
biodiversity loss transgress Planetary Boundaries, while potential impacts on Climate
change, Freshwater eutrophication, Land use, Freshwater ecotoxicity, Particulate matter,
Resource use, fossil, and Resource use, mineral and metals exceed global Carrying Ca-
pacities. Meanwhile, Stratospheric ozone depletion remains at an absolute sustainable
level for both methods. Several control variables yielded inconclusive results due to a
lack of spatial disaggregation.
While sufficiency lifestyles could reduce global pressures, the results underline that
fundamental societal and technological transformations are needed to achieve absolute
sustainability. Technological propagation across nations is not a sufficient solution.
A radical transition in incentives for technological development is required for global
well-being within the Absolute Sustainability thresholds.

1
Introduction
This article seeks to uncover the potential burden on absolute environmental sustainability as a result
of reconfiguring global access to resources and energy in ways that provide decent well-being for
everyone as defined by [1]. Thus, the study contributes to scientific literature by broadening the
coverage from single or few impacts, to full coverage across Earth systems (ESs) and impact categories
recommended by the international reference life cycle data system (ILCD) ([2, 3]).
1.1
Background
The discussions pertaining to the concept of sustainability have been a rising trend in the past decades
with the roots of concern on an international level stemming from at least half a century ago, when
the United Nations (UN) held its first conference on conservation and utilization of resources([4]).
The most commonly used definition of sustainability today stems from the Brundtland Report from
1987, and reads:
"Sustainable development is development that meets the needs of the present without
compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs." ([5])
Here, the concept of ’needs’ is discussed as being difficult to define, culturally determined, and
arguably not fulfilled on a global level.
[6, 7] made groundbreaking work in regard to environmental sustainability, when they presented a
study that for the first time defined the load that Earth’s various earth system processes (biogeochemical,
hydrological, geospheric, and cryospheric) can tolerate before risking to push the Earth system into a
state markedly different from the Holocene epoch; the period of stability we have been in since the last
ice age. With this, the concept of a safe operating space for humanity was introduced. Unfortunately,
human industrialization was already then found to have caused exceedance of multiple boundaries.
This work was later updated by [8] with no better news to follow.
The field has since gained traction within the scientific literature. The quantification of absolute
thresholds has since been expanded, and more operational frameworks have been developed to enable
the possibility of carrying out Absolute Environmental Sustainability Assessments (AESAs). One
of such is the Planetary Boundaries-based life-cycle impact assessment method (PB-LCIA), which
characterizes elementary flows to make them relatable with different sets of absolute environmental
sustainability references (AESRs) designed for comparison of the potential contribution to impacts
due to the existence of a product/system, to the PBs of earth system processes ([9, 10]). Another
framework is the normalization factors based on Carrying Capacity (CC) of affected ecosystems, such
as AESRs designed with the intent of being compatible with the more conventionally applied LCIA
methods, which are commonly used in life-cycle assessment LCA ([11]).
Since then, further development on the coverage and approaches to sustainability references has
been done, including refinement and redefinition of previously established PBs and CCs, and including
boundaries for resource depletion and biodiversity ([12–15]), as well as expanding the sense of absolute
sustainability beyond environmental focus by introducing health-related references ([15]).
In terms of social sustainability, an array of studies have been conducted over the years, trying
to define and quantify the basic requirements necessary for an individual or a society to achieve a
standard of living remotely similar to what was initially sought in the Brundtland report ([5]).
On a theoretical level, the addition of the concept of justice was applied to the safe operating
space (SOS of [6, 7] by [16]). Introducing the now well-known doughnut interpretation with a social
foundation based on UN’s Sustainable Development Goals predecessor, the Millennium Development
Goals, Raworth created a conceptual connection between the two sets of thresholds. Other literature
introduces Decent Living Standards (DLS) as a basis for a conceptual framework to apply justification
for less developed countries to be held less accountable when universal environmental (in this case,
climate) mitigation propositions emerge. They propose a set of universal indicators, attributing
everyone basic rights in the form of a set of basic goods and activity levels which are needed for
decent well-being ([1]). Furthermore, the authors suggest that the field of defining at what level living
standards are perceived as "decent" and worth protecting is contentious and prone to disagreements,
and often defined in ways that are cumbersome to operationalize ([1]).

Social sustainability - as with every naturally occurring concept which is attempted to be socially
systematized - is impossible to fully define and quantify, and no singular quantitative indicator suffices.
Neither material flow, ability to perform certain actions, nor improvement of health-related endpoints
such as life expectancy can stand alone when determining whether social sustainability is obtained.
Hence the work in this paper is based on various measures present in the literature, such as Just
access ([17]), Just Ends ([18]) and the general intersections between the capabilities approach ([19])
and the basic goods approach upon which the DLS are built ([20] in [1]).
A study ([21]) estimated the final energy demand at the global level associated with activity
levels following the DLS. It is emphasized that individual consumption must be kept at a level of
sufficiency. The work primarily builds upon the elaborated work in [22] on the quantification of DLS.
While [21] made the preparatory development necessary to analyze the energy requirements of
the DLS activity levels, additional translation is required to create the life-cycle inventory (LCI) data
needed to quantify the potential contribution from DLS to the planetary boundaries (PB). [17] since
quantified the additional impact on four PBs (climate, water, land, and nutritional flows of nitrogen
(N) and phosphorus (P)) of achieving different levels of just access.
1.2
Purpose
Political decisions deserve to be taken on an informed basis, and the current basis still lacks information
on how radical improvements in social sustainability would impact the environment on a global level.
The analysis of global DLS enables the comparison of how a radical reconfiguration of affluence
in the well-known IPAT equation (1), assuming the population and technology level of the present
day, performs against the absolute thresholds for impact as well as for the current global affluence
([23, 24]).
Envrionmental Impact = Population · Affluence per capita · Technology factor
(1)
There is a need for quantitative data on which trade-offs appear from a multiple of socially
desirable scenarios, yielding information on what drivers of affluence and technology should be targeted
to safely arrive to a just world.
The purpose of this paper is to investigate the extent to which a global hypothetical sufficient
consumption level will result in changes in the anthropogenic pressures on the environment and
compare it to a series of AESRs. The scenario represents the world as we know it today in terms
of geographics, availability of natural resources, fauna, flora and demographics, but with activity
levels conforming (and only conforming) to the DLS, leaving poor people entitled to access to more
basic goods than the current state, while the abundance of goods found within the global minority is
universally decommissioned, leaving the entire global population to thrive in a sufficiency lifestyle.
As explained previously, although the study will follow the DLS framework, the literature which
operationalizes other social sustainability frameworks will be consulted to supplement areas where
DLS are not sufficiently defined ([25]).

Methods
This section presents the methods used to successfully conduct the hybrid absolute environmental
sustainability assessment of the global decent living scenario. Furthermore, the methodological choices
made throughout the study will be presented in brief, while detailed documentation is available in SI
1.
2.1
Design of the study
Figure 1: Diagram demonstrating the design of the analysis. The DLS segments are operationalized
to either make up environmental interventions in a process-based inventory, or as monetary demands
related to relevant sectors in the historical international economy. Following the upper track in the
figure (PB-LCIA), potential contribution to impact categories for the EEIO-based demand is linked
and characterized and the potential contribution of stress to earth system processes is calculated
Finally, the results are normalized to the SOS. Following the lower track (carrying capacity), both
models are characterized, linked and normalized to using CC-NR.
In order to perform the AESA, the DLS activity segments were analyzed one by one, to feasibly
operationalize the required activity levels to form an appropriate inventory (see 2.3.5). Part of the
inventory was calculated using data from SI 5.2 ([26]).
The general approach was to perform a hybrid AESA (see 2.3) to ensure an appropriate level of
inventory completeness, where process-based LCI modelling (see 2.3.1) was not feasible to conduct. In
spite of the fact that EEIO analyses feature high completeness levels, being a top-down modelling
approach, it does not suffice as the primary basis for this research, the main reasons for this being
related to the limited coverage and granularity. This is elaborated on in SI 1.1.
Hence, the EEIO using EXIOBASE will be used to supplement the calculations of this model,
and where the impact categories from EXIOBASE did not correspond directly to the PB-LCIA and
CC control variables, different measures of aggregation and proxies are made to ensure proxy coverage
of activities of concern for each AESA framework. See 2.3.2 for a description of the measures taken.
2.2
Absolute environmental sustainability assessment (AESA)
While Life-cycle assessment (LCA) is a widely used framework to assess the relative potential impacts
related to the introduction of a given product or system, it cannot determine whether it is sustainable
in absolute terms ([24]). To do this, it is necessary to identify and quantify which control variables
3 of 71
==PAGE:14==
best represent the states and changes in the performance of the earth system processes. Within
these control variables, the AESRs are determined, creating a threshold on a global, regional, or
local level, in how much stress is tolerated on the metrics of the control variable corresponding to an
earth system process. Having determined AESRs, a sustainability ratio (SR) is calculated by dividing
the impacts from the assessed system within each AESR with the threshold for these, ultimately
leaving an SR < 1 to be deemed absolute sustainable and vice versa. AESRs are established based on
scientific approaches, by calculated forecasting, measurements, or a combination thereof, which relate
to different environmental observed or expected changes of state ([10, 11]).
Usual LCA methodology demands a singular reference, a functional unit - to ensure systems
equivalence in order to facilitate comparability assertion for current, past, or future products or
systems. FUs are generally describing the needed function, quality, quantity and duration to fulfill,
and usually span the entire life-cycle of a given product or system. In this study, an SR is created by
comparison to scientifically deduced thresholds for temporal stressors. This leaves the need for the
life-cycle inventory (LCI) - which consists of exchanges within the technosphere as well as elementary
flows to and from the ecosphere - to be modelled as annual flows across all services, as described in
[10].
2.2.1
Planetary boundary-based life-cycle impact assessment (PB-LCIA)
PB-LCIA is one of several methods of conducting AESA, and is specifically designed to correspond to
the Planetary Boundaries and the zones of uncertainty of [8] and [6], providing Characterization Factors
(CFs) for climate change, stratospheric ozone depletion SOD, biogeochemical flows (P and N), land-
system change (LSC), freshwater use (FWU) and atmospheric aerosol loading (AAL). Furthermore,
PB-LCIA characterizes FWU based on river basin humidity (semidry, dry and humid), and LSC based
on primary forest biomes (boreal, temperate, and tropic). The planetary boundary for novel entities
is not considered in PB-LCIA, since no PB had been determined at the time of writing.
In regard to FWU, the best level of detail to accommodate these segregated CFs would be on
a river catchment area level. However, this study considers only the national assessment, and the
effort to regionalize the impacts (in this case, the deprivation of water resources) is outside the scope
of this study. Furthermore, the data that laid the basis for the calculation of the CFs for two of the
three basin types are limited to one country each. Thus, FWU will solely be considered on a global
level. Additionally, issues related to linking of elementary flows from EcoInvent processes prevent the
quantification of LSC in boreal and temperate areas. For these reasons, LSC will be considered only
on a global level as well.
[10] excluded the characterization of biodiversity, stating that at the state of how well the
magnitudes of cause-effect relationships that contribute to the change in biodiversity can be quantified
do not cover known stressors to a sufficient level at the time of writing. Ryberg argued that solely
quantifying a subset of impacts will introduce bias and lead to potential misinformation when
interpreting the results.
However, since the aim of this article is to feasibly cover broadly across earth systems (ESs) and
since biosphere integrity is perceived as central to increasing resilience to abrupt and gradual changes
to the ES ([8]), biodiversity is considered. While two boundaries for the ES biosphere integrity are
presented by [8], solely functional biodiversity is included. This implies that a known incompleteness
in the amount of CFs relating to impact of biodiversity will be present, hence the LCIA results
are expected to represent an underestimation or at least a minimum potential impact. To feasibly
include biodiversity, the approach of [13] is followed. In that study, PB-LCIA was applied, and a
complementary approach of characterizing contributions to the functional role of biodiversity was
suggested. The method calculates loss in mean species abundance (MSA) from elementary flows linked
to land occupation and climate change. MSA attributes all affected land areas the same weight, while
BII differentiates the contribution to its score across biomes and vegetation types ([27]). In this study,
MSA serves as a proxy for BII using the CFs (sheet 28 of SI 5.1) obtained from the approach of [13].

2.2.2
Carrying capacity-based normalization
Another method for relating the LCA methodology to PBs is the use of Carrying capacity normalization
references CC-NR introduced by [11]. This framework consists of a set of normalization references
which link midpoint indicators for impact categories with scientifically sound thresholds for what was
defined as carrying capacities;
"The maximum sustained environmental intervention a natural system can withstand
without experiencing negative changes in structure or functioning that are difficult or
impossible to revert." ([11])
The CCs differ from PBs, especially in the sense of choice of precaution and impact category
coverage. Where [6, 8, 9] defines the boundaries as presumed safe operating spaces applying high
precaution, [11] targets the best estimates to enhance the LCA applicability. This means that, in spite
of some control variables being highly related across the two frameworks, results transgressing the
PB-LCIA thresholds are not necessarily exceeding the corresponding CCs. That mentioned, several of
the CCs are calculated from the PBs, from which it would be expected that other factors apply in
case of marked differences.

2.3
Life-cycle inventory model - Hybrid AESA
As the intent of this study is to capture the potential impact of a (hypothetical) global consumption,
it is favorable to aim for model completeness rather than product specificity. This could lead one to
opt for simply taking a top-down approach across the entire system. However, the nature of available
top-down methods for quantifying impacts is not based on physical flows of materials, chemicals, or
energy, but rather on monetary exchanges across sectors. For this reason and for reasons presented in
SI 1.1, simply relying the results of the study on an LCI built from a top-down modelling method,
such as environmentally extended input-output (EEIO), would introduce bias to a level where the
risk of not being able to produce meaningful conclusions appears. For this reason, a process-based
inventory will form the backbone of this model, while activity segments that are deemed infeasible to
operationalize will be calculated through EEIO to fill in the known completeness gaps.
Attributional LCI modelling was applied rather than comparing the DLS systems to an existing
background system, the DLS scenario represents a completely different hypothetical system. EcoInvent
3.7 Cut-off was used, as this was the data available for the software used, due to licensing limitations.
This means that the model will not necessarily consist of the latest technology processes. However,
while the newest available database is version 3.9.1, the evolution of the databases is quite limited,
and the use of a slightly outdated database is not expected to have significant impact on the results.
2.3.1

specification that the life-cycle inventory (LCI) phase of the LCA, is created as a sum of defined
material and energy flows, as well as emissions related to a unit processes, i.e., a pLCI ([24]). See the
pLCI results in sheet 26 in SI 5.1 and the LCI process documentation in SI 5.6.

2.3.2
Application of EEIO
For the EEIO part of the analysis, the ’inventory’ does not consist of a set of flows and processes, but
rather a final demand of a monetary value is applied for each industry within a given region. This
final demand is defined for each region, forming a demand vector that compiles the life-cycle inventory
for the defined product/system.
Ultimately, the industry output needed to meet the final demand is multiplied by the CFs
provided by EXIOBASE as environmental extensions, which represent the impacts related to meeting
the final demand.
For this analysis, the EXIOBASE industry input-output table was used for the year 2022. The
script can be obtained through SI 5.3.

respectively. Different approaches were needed, as explained in 2.1, due to the different nature of the
frameworks in question. In the following sections, these linking approaches are presented.

2.3.3
Parameterizing - the ’average national citizen’
Because the model is intended to output spatially differentiated results on a national level, many
repetitive data inputs are required for the model.
The best approach to handle this is to parameterize as many of the activities as possible and
structure a way to differentiate them.
For this reason, the core model consists of "all activities related to the well-being of an average
citizen of the nation x", where x is any specific country or area under national observation by the UN.
To obtain a national impact contribution, the core model was scaled by its population. The impacts
of nations then ultimately sum up to a global impact contribution.
This citizen’s required activity levels for decent living are determined by the demographic and
technology-related statistics of this nation. Parameters such as distribution of age, urbanization rate,
building type distribution, population density, etc. For a complete list of parameters, see sheet 27 in
SI 5.1. The script can be obtained through SI 5.5.
Preliminary results showed to be sensitive to the choice of electricity technology and water
consumption-related processes. To ensure the best possible differentiation of these technologies,
all available spatially dependent technologies for low-voltage (everything below 1 kV) electricity

consumption in EcoInvent were identified. All nations studied were linked by their ISO-alpha2 code
and the corresponding available EcoInvent geographies codes. In cases where no specific geographies
match, a hierarchy of links was implemented in the order: "country →region →RoW →Global
(GLO). The nature of this hierarchy ensures that a match with EcoInvent will be reached (see SI 4.2
and sheet 1 in SI 5.1).
This procedure spatialized the LCI for all 249 nations, into 82 regional and 28 global (GLO)

2.3.4
Accounting for uncertainty - Monte Carlo simulation
In order to assess the reliability of the predicted DLS impacts, Monte Carlo simulation (MC) was
applied. MC is a method which calculates multiple scenarios based on probabilistic distributions
of input parameters to a model, creating itself a simulated probabilistic distribution of the chosen
model output variable. This enables the analyst to obtain a simulated confidence interval by inputting
information about the uncertainty, variability, or miss-specification within individual parameters.
In this study, all unmodified generic processes contain uncertainty information provided by
EcoInvent. For all model parameters, the consideration for uncertainty information was taken, and
included when relevant. See sheet 27 in SI 5.1, SI 5.6 and SI 1.4 for details.
Due to the complexity of the model and the design of the software, the Monte Carlo analysis
was made for 27 selected nations and scaled to represent world uncertainty. See SI 1.2 for details.

2.3.5.1
Nutrition
The Nutrition segment was divided into three measures of activity level; Food, storage, and meal
preparation.
The food was modeled on the basis of the universal recommendations of the EAT Lancet
commissions of healthy reference diet ([29]), in which the target nutritional value is appr. 2500 kcal.
However, to operationalize the physical flows related to food, the macronutrient intake in g / day laid
the basis for the model instead. For every type of food in the reference diet, fitting generic EcoInvent
processes were included in the model. In cases where several generic processes were available for a
recommended food type, even distribution was assumed. Therefore, the mass of the food in all types
of food was contained rather than its calorific value. The treatment of food waste is assumed to
be 20% [54] of all the produce that reaches the consumer, while upstream food waste is taken into
account by background processes. Cold storage was modelled as a generic refrigerator process, scaled
to the size of the household and its useful life. Lifetime was assumed to be 20 years. The efficiency of
electricity was set to 120 kWh per refrigerator per year.
The preparation of food was modelled as a cookstove, electricity consumption, and waste
treatment. Cookstoves were modelled similarly to refrigerators. The energy used for heating food was
assumed to be all electric. The energy requirement was modelled to be 6 MJ per kg food ([30] in [51]).
It was assumed that half of all food would be cooked.
2.3.5.2
Shelter
The shelter segment represents the domestic part of ’living’ and includes dwelling construction
(sufficient housing space), thermal comfort, illumination, and furniture.
Sufficient housing space was modelled to represent a steady-state activity level of yearly dwelling
construction. Separate models were made for multi family housing (MFH) and single family housing
(SFH). For each nation, the prospected urban and rural share of the population was determined ([55]).
In the 20 cases, where no data was available (mainly small islands and military bases), a half-half
distribution was applied.
Furthermore, the nations were matched with data, at a regional level based on [32], showing the
share of SFH and MFH in urban and rural areas, respectively ([31]). This segregated the demographic
data into shares of total MFH and SFH in urban and rural areas, respectively.
The housing was modelled as the stock of construction materials in residential buildings based
on a study by [33]. To create the model for MFH with the type Apartment Buildings and SFH with
Detached Houses in [33] were matched. The model therefore consists of production and transport
for steel, concrete, wood([34]), copper, aluminium and glass. Furthermore, metal working energy
and materials for the three metals were modelled. All construction and waste flows were distributed
equally over the assumed lifetime of the buildings of 50 years.
The total sufficient housing space was calculated to 69m2 per household and ∼14.1 per person,
using definitions from [22] and household size data from [35].
Thermal comfort was modelled as the materials and energy needed for residents to maintain
thermal comfort indoors and was based on the work in [21]. The baseline interval for thermal comfort
was set to 15.6 to 21.1◦C, which means technological assistance for temperature regulation is modelled
for ambient temperatures outside this interval.
Illumination
Energy needs for illumination were modelled following the approach suggested in [21], providing
125 lumen · m−2 for 6 hours daily on a yearly average basis, with one third of the household being lit
at a time, not discriminating across nations.
Furniture
Furniture was crudely assumed to contain a constant stock of 1 tonne of wooden furniture per
household, with a lifetime mode of 25 years.

2.3.5.3
Hygiene
The hygiene segment contains the general need for hygienically related consumption needs. Water
supply and heating of water is explicitly modelled, while waste infrastructure is spread out as intrinsic
parts of all activity levels considered.
The supply of clean water was modelled using generic EcoInvent tap water, as explained in
section 2.3.3 encapsulating infrastructure, as well as water consumption. The activity level for water

The wastewater is assumed to be treated at municipal wastewater treatment plants.
Water heating
For heating water, it was discovered that the use in [21] of air temperature as a proxy for shallow
groundwater temperature for the start temperature of the inlet water was possibly offset by a few

The share of energy for water heating was assumed to be 50% electricity provision (at the end
consumer), 25% district heating from natural gas, and 25% district heating from other energy sources.
The model does not consider the heat loss fraction, thereby the additional energy need for the cases of
district heating.
2.3.5.4
Clothing
The clothing segment contains the amount of garment needed for people to maintain thermal comfort
outdoors, and arguably some current social requirements are present as well. Furthermore, the
maintenance (washing) and the equipment needs for this are included as well.
The garment was modelled according to the material garment flow scenarios of [21], linking the
clothing items with relevant generic EcoInvent processes.
Washing facilities were modelled as the manufacturing and transportation of washing machines,
operation, and waste treatment. The model assumes that one machine per household to conform with
[21]. The lifetime of the machine is assumed to be 20 years, corresponding to ∼2.9 tons of washed
clothes.

2.3.5.6
Communication & information
The Communications segment represents the individual need to interact with social and political
movements ([22]), and consists of partly multidirectional telecommunication (phones and computers)
and information/entertainment broadcast (television, which to some extent is obtainable through the
aforementioned devices).
The share of phones attributed to every country depends on the definition of need. The definition
in [22] is quite ambiguous, stating in one place that every adult (leaving out a definition of what
makes an adult) should have a phone, while further through the article, 1 phone per household is

The phones were modelled as material need, transformation and waste processes, as well as
yearly energy demand.
As a computer has the possibility to serve multiple functions, including to receive broadcasts of
information via internet connection, the model assumes this DLS activity level to be solely covered by

the latter defines the DLS for information access as 1 television or internet device per household.
The usage of the computer is assumed to be 2 hours per person per day in a household.
2.3.5.7
Mobility
The mobility segment entails the access of people to ’adequate’ means of assisted transportation. It
includes motorized transport and bicycle commute, adding the latter to the defined requirements of
[22]. It is important to note that as all freight operations are inbuilt to every activity segment, the
’mobility’ segment solely concerns personal transportation and the freight transportation related to
the infrastructure behind it.
The mobility model to a vast extent follows the approach of [21], as is well documented in [51],
and is modelled by recalculation of the required transportation distances as a function of population
density.
The calculated total mobility levels amounted to the upper and lower limits of 14233 and 4900
km · year−1 · pers−1, respectively.
The shares between modes of transportation were directly adopted from [51], leaving mobility
activity level for air transport and bicycling constant across nations, while car, bus and rail transport
vary.
The cars were modelled as a combination of 20% of propulsion in electric vehicles as opposed to
internal combustion engine vehicles.

4
Discussion
In the following sections, the impact of the results on the research questions outlined in section
1.2 will be discussed. Mainly, whether the PB-LCIA and CC methods indicate DLS to be absolute
sustainable or not. Then, it is discussed whether the DLS exceeds the current pressure level. After
that, the most relevant uncertainties and related limitations will be discussed. Additionally, the results
of the study will be perspectivized to relevant studies. Furthermore, some considerations will be
presented regarding how challenges and possible mitigation initiatives could be assessed. Finally, some
recommendations for further study are suggested.
4.1
Main findings
The main findings on whether the DLS scenario is absolute sustainable, and how the pressure
from providing DLS will exceed the current global pressure level will be presented. In general, the
expectations for the results are that DLS will generally yield a lower anthropogenic pressure across
all ES and impact categories than the current pressure levels, since similar studies such as [40] and
[57] suggest that less than half the current global energy consumption is needed to obtain DLS.
Additionally, unexpected results will be discussed in extension to the main findings.
4.1.1
Absolute sustainability
The results suggest that 5 of 8 assessed earth systems were receiving pressure to levels causing
exceedance of the planetary boundaries, while 8 of 17 carrying capacities are exceeded. Furthermore,
in the cases of climate change, ocean acidification and both control variables for biogeochemical flows,
the DLS scenarios provide pressure well beyond the zone of uncertainty, suggesting the need for
prioritization. Similarly, the analysis of the carrying capacity suggests an exceedance for Climate
change and phosphorus-based Freshwater eutrophication while the DLS pressure on nitrogen-related
marine eutrophication reaches just roughly 44% of the carrying capacity. The analyses furthermore
agree on the global freshwater use, which is half of the PB and ∼32% of the CC, respectively, but
disagree on the control variables related to land use, where Land system change constitutes less than
0.1% of the PB and Land use is 6.7 times as large as the carrying capacity.
The two analyses roughly agree on the levels of stratospheric ozone depletion, with non-worrying
pressure levels. Comparing these levels with the current pressure towards the carrying capacity, the
DLS model is much lower. Albeit, as the baseline for the current pressure levels is quite dated, the
effect of the Montreal protocol is expected to have increased since then, leaving the presented pressure
levels seemingly overestimated.
Figure 7 shows us that there are large differences between the nations regarding the CO2eq
intensity which follows the activity levels required to meet DLS, and furthermore, that all assessed
nations exceed the PB for climate change - Energy Imbalance. The consequences are discussed further
in 4.4.
4.1.1.1
Marine eutrophication issue
Two important points in regard to the difference in results related to nitrogen are 1) that the control
variable for nitrogen, as portrayed in Table 1 is related to intentional nitrogen fixation, while the
indicator for marine eutrophication is an indicator at midpoint and is the result of just a subset
of the potential environmental pressures from nitrogen fixation, and 2) the impacts related to the
nitrogen cycle are not only of global importance. Since much of the inventory model is spatially
generic and since PB-LCIA is spatially generic for this control variable and since the LCIA for marine
eutrophication was not regionalized, the results are highly uncertain in regard to real environmental
relevance for both methods. [58] proposed ways to relate the nitrogen cycle with PBs using several
spatially dependent indicators.

==PAGE:29==
4.1.1.2
Land-system change issue
The implausibly low contribution to the LSC ES is important to address. This is due to the extent of
the applied CFs, which primarily relate to transformations to and from forest and crop in boreal and
taiga biomes, disregarding several other transformation flows relevant to LSC. Hence, the coverage
of the impact on LSC is very limited. This issue is also found in [59]. While forest biomes are
considered paramount to maintain balanced climate feedbacks ([60]), [61] suggested to include two
control variables based on the climatic response (surface air temperature and precipitation). These are
recognized as drivers for, together with anthropogenic perturbation, the creation of positive feedback
loops toward system instability ([61]). Furthermore, the nature of the current version of PB-LCIA
(as described above) implies that any LCI containing input flows of non-forest land transformation
and outputs forest transformation will create imbalance, leaving the result unrepresentative. For the
reasons mentioned above, this study has insufficient results to infer transgression or the opposite in
terms of LSC.
4.1.2
Comparison with current pressure
All impact categories except Freshwater ecotoxicity and Resource use, mineral and metals suggest
DLS to be a markedly less impactful scenario, compared to current pressure.
As the results for the impact categories Freshwater ecotoxicity and Resource use, minerals and
metals were expected to be lower than current pressures, the driving contributions for the impacts
were investigated.
Given the nature of the model, no top-down contribution analysis is feasible to conduct, as no
combined results across nations can be extracted. The contribution analyses in the following paragraphs
are based on data from the same set of countries laying the basis for the segment distribution for the
PB-LCIA results. For quantitative data, refer to the tables in sheets 24 & 25 in SI 5.1.
4.1.2.1
Freshwater ecotoxicity issue
The process contribution analysis (PCA) showed that the impacts stem mainly from the various
processes related to food production (dairy, rice) and copper. The main contributors here are related
to pesticides (potassium chloride production) and electricity consumption in various categories for
nations with a less developed electricity grid. See sheet 25 in SI 5.1 for details.
Investigating the distributions of DLS model contribution for India using EF 3.0, 37% of the
contribution to FET (sheet 25 in SI 5.1 and Figure 11) all feeds into electricity grids. Repeating the
analysis using the LCIA method ReCiPe 2016 Midpoint (H) (Figure 6 and Figure 12), a different
picture appears. While a large share stems from electricity (∼26%), the main subcontributors are
related to the copper used to construct the electricity network. Furthermore, in contrast to the analysis
using EF 3.0, Shelter is the main contributor to FET and is predominantly driven by copper as well.
The substance contribution analysis (SCA) showed ∼40% of the impact contributions coming
from sulfur due to potassium chloride production and aluminium due to blasting related to coal
production. Furthermore, aluminium emission to soil contributed in some areas.
As the electricity consumption processes are spatialized, an increase of electricity in countries
where the electricity grid contains a large share of non-renewable energy sources, such as black coal,
will lead to a combined global increase of the impacts to the relevant categories.
The two analyses of freshwater ecotoxicity show some connection to freshwater ecotoxicity
from DLS being dominating compared to the current stressors. While the model arguably contains
limitations, part of the difference might be related to the intrinsic uncertainty of the LCIA method.
The USEtox LCIA method holds the recommendation level "recommended, but to be applied with
caution" by ILCD ([2]). The DLS results using ReCiPe confirm this, being twice as high as the EF
3.0-based results (Figure 6). It is relevant to further investigate the relationship between the DLS
model and the contribution to freshwater ecotoxicity, and especially to investigate whether the impact
of increasing demand in less developed nations is higher than the avoided impact of decreasing activity
levels in developed nations.

4.1.2.2
Resource use, mineral and metals issue
The PCA showed a primary impact from shelter and nutrition with 1/3 of the impact each. For
shelter, metal materials (copper and aluminum) for housing, as well as zinc, needed for the production
of heat pumps, drive impacts. For nutrition, production for different sorts of food, which are electricity
intensive drive impacts. Hence, the resources for electricity production drive the contribution of
the nutrition segments to this impact category. Furthermore, gold and zinc within the digital logic
elements of the model for phones drive some contribution (Figure 13 and Figure 14).
The SCA showed ∼75% impacts from gold impacted by zinc, silver, and gold mining, respectively.
Furthermore, Tellurium accounted for approximately a fifth of the impacts. Tellurium is deprived in
relation to copper mining.
The analyses uncovers that there might be either an overestimation of materials used in phones,
or an underestimation in other electronic appliances. The former is most likely, as: 1) the modelling
approach for phones is rather crude, and 2) the impacts are partly driven by the model for phones.
Similar conclusions can be drawn from the modelling of housing. The DLS results using the ReCiPe
method however, suggest elements of sensitivity to the choice of methods as well, as these impacts
are lower than the current level - and, in fact, barely exceed the carrying capacity. The listed points
suggest the need for refinement of the mentioned parts of the model and to generally assess the model
through multiple LCIA methods.
4.2
Uncertainty and limitations
This section discusses the main sources of uncertainty and limitations within the study, organized by
their implications for the credibility and interpretability of the results.
4.2.1
Inventory model uncertainties
As the scope of this study is quite broad, the model is necessarily crude. This yields more uncertainty
that can impact conclusions. The generic and high-level nature of the inventory data constrains the
precision and accuracy of the results.
Several parameters and assumptions in modelling the different activity levels across segments
involve uncertainties.
As explained in section 2.3.5, the model accustoms 1 washing machine per household, following
[21] in spite of the descriptions for DLS in [22]. This is arguably a modelling choice favoring current
western consumption behavior and is quite far from the defined DLS. Arguably, the attempt to
maintain conservative approximations in modelling can raise questions on whether this improves the
model representativeness to the sufficiency-based society or includes too much unnecessary privilege.
However, as Clothing is not dominant in any ES, the results are not expected to be sensitive to a
reduction in washing machine production.
Arguably, the modelling of phones could benefit from more attention to detail. Since the results
from both freshwater ecotoxicity and mineral resource depletion proved to be markedly contributed by
the phone model, refinements are due. Several full-fletched LCAs exist on current/modern smartphones,
and thorough adoption of LCIs would increase representativeness of the current market for phones.
The share of EVs representing 20% in year 2023 hardly represents the actual current conditions.
However, since the technology is proven ready to a greater degree than is currently implemented
([53]), the DLS with a high share of EV technology is considered quite representative of current
technology. Additionally, while only about 2.5% of the global stock of registered road vehicles (in
different categories) are electric vehicles, the market share is rapidly increasing with an annual market
share of ∼10% in 2021([53]). Ultimately, the model is not intended to represent real-world stock, but
a scenario of the current technology in use. This was considered and implemented in the MC (2.3.4).
Thus, the results of the study are not sensitive to the fraction of EVs in the model for DLS.
The size of the household could have been differentiated closer to local levels, to accustom
culture differences by, e.g. using data from [62]. Since multiple parameters regarding shelter provision
and household-specific availability of appliances depend on the household size. As above, this was
implemented in the MC, leading to similar findings.

4.2.2
Limitations of the impact assessment method
To test the robustness of the choice of the impact assessment method, recalculations using other
methods are due. For PB-LCIA, the Planetary Boundary Allowance impact assessment method by
[63] could be another way to compare the results of PB-LCIA. This method assigns characterization
factors (CFs) directly to EcoInvent 3, normalizing the personal allowance based on the principle of
equal allocation per capita, similar to the sustainability ratio (SR) with an equal-per-capita allocation
principle for PB-LCIA results. The CFs are in most cases created differently, hence the results would
not be expected to be identical. As the data treatment required to perform this check is quite extensive,
the analysis was not initiated.
For CC, the LCIA-method ReCiPe 2016 Midpoint (H) was applied to the extent that the units
of the midpoint indicators matched those of EF 3.0. Three exceptions were made, in part to facilitate
comparison with the issues highlighted in 4.1.2.1 and 4.1.2.2 and to create a broader coverage of
the analysis. In these cases, the CFs from EF 3.0 corresponding to the indicator unit from ReCiPe
was applied, creating an additional characterization to the characterized ReCiPe results (see sheet
6 in SI 5.1). Naturally, this increases uncertainty in the credibility for those indicators. Albeit, the
assessment was found necessary for the given reasons. The result of the analysis suggests, as stated in
4.1.2.2, that the interpretability of this study is not robust against the change of LCIA method. Since
part of the scope of the CC framework in general is compatibility with ILCD-recommended impact
categories, the act of assessing impacts using impact categories which are not recommended by ILCD,
challenges the concept of the intended standardized practices. Given the constraints outlined above,
consideration of the lack of robustness is recommended to make, if assertion is to be made from this
study. Additionally, conducting further analyses by applying more LCIA methods is recommended.
4.2.2.1
Limitations of global-scale assessment for spatially variable impacts
The reason for leaving out the evaluation of spatially differentiated water consumption control variables
from the PB-LCIA method was primarily due to the limited data foundation the CFs were built
upon for certain climate conditions. The results from the study through the assessed methods show
the DLS to constitute about half of the PB and a third of the CC. Similarly, the comparison to
the current pressure reveals a pressure level at roughly half of the carrying capacity. In [64], it is
found that 1.5 billion people cannot source enough local water to fit the needed consumption of
∼400L · cap−1 · day−1 to escape poverty. The DLS requires on average 678.5L in comparison, implying
the need for spatialized assessment. The study also shows that the water catchment areas with
insufficient recharge cover 5.3% of the world, 19.3% of Africa and ’only’ 8‰ in North America. This
suggests that global water consumption, considered in isolation, may not be a relevant control variable
for assessing absolute sustainability.
However, assessing the difference from spatially differentiated water recharge, and the DLS
consumption rate has the potential to provide more relevant insights than the current results. This
will not be investigated further in this study. For the described reasons, the results are limited to
infer global average consumption, while local exceedances are expected but cannot be inferred with
the available resolution of the assessment.
In terms of eutrophication and acidification, the thresholds for the exceedance of carrying
capacity are of global relation. Controversially, the impacts are of regional or local relevance. Hence,
similarly as in the case for freshwater use, the analysed lack of exceedance in these categories does not
necessarily infer that the DLS scenario is absolute sustainable. For functional biodiversity, as stated
in [8], it is relevant to assess it by biomes or large regional areas. However, in this case, the global
average threshold is exceeded, meaning that some areas will necessarily not be absolute sustainable.
4.2.2.2
Limitations of modelling a drastically transformed economy
All calculations contained in the model assume history-based conditions; that the tiered supply for any
demand of the EEIO is based on historical monetary flow within/across industries, and similarly that
the generic processes of the process-based LCI are based on the industry in terms of both monetary
exchanges and physical market-driven spatialization of processes. This means that a vastly different
consumption than business-as-usual on a global level becomes less representative by the magnitude.
It could be argued that the current availability of technology would be shifted vastly in the
DLS scenario, as the requirements are much more bound to the population density, rather than the
21 of 71
==PAGE:32==
differences in consumption patterns (as the differences are much less significant in the DLS scenario).
Furthermore, it could be argued that the current technology available would be tiered for prioritization,
with endorsement of the best technologies. I.e. the energy sector would likely prioritize the renewable
energy production to the extent the capacity is already there. As DLS requires less energy, it is
expected that the share of renewable energy constitutes a higher share of energy mixes.
Naturally, some of the best technologies are spatially bound; as hydropower needs water streams,
wind energy needs consistent wind conditions, and photovoltaic panels need a good amount of sun
to be efficient. Hence, the model contains bias towards higher impacts, and the biases contain some
restrictions, making the model unable to anticipate technological advances that may result from
meeting reductions in demand in a more efficient way.
Similarly, water recovery technologies are spatially dependent to a higher degree, which would
limit the bias of the results further than for energy production. The limitations of modelling with
data representing a vastly different demand mean that the results should be interpreted with caution.
4.2.3
Monte Carlo analysis offset issue
As seen in Figure 9 and Figure 10, the Monte Carlo analyses almost consequently overestimated
results compared to the deterministic model, which is a product of several factors, including: Inherent
uncertainty in the background processes as well as issues with mass balance in variable process inputs
and fixed outputs with processes feeding into themselves.
To illustrate the issues, two experiments have been made: 1) Testing whether MC of a single
unaltered EcoInvent process will yield an offset and 2) Testing whether other LCA software will cause
similar offset on a similar calculation setup. The deterministic and MC simulation results for both
tests are presented in Table 8 and Table 9.
Both tests confirmed the expectations; for test 1, the MC model overestimated the results by ∼
8% for climate change, and test 2 showed SimaPro to not yield an offset with a similar calculation
setup. Therefore, the test results suggest that the choice of LCA software matters when calculating
stochastic uncertainty.
Since EcoInvent is proven to be especially developed to conform with SimaPro, and since
accessibility of EcoInvent databases to OpenLCA happens through third-party translation, it is
inclined to conclude that OpenLCA does not conduct MC-test on EcoInvent processes correctly.
However, it is expected that the lack of uncertainty data related to the incompleteness of the inventory
would spread the confidence interval further, ultimately making the offset less significant. Ultimately,
only what is measured contains uncertainty data. Thus, a fully complete DLS scenario is expected to
contain higher impacts and wider error bars. These issues limit the study to suggest borderline results
which makes the outcome inconclusive and should be interpreted with caution.
4.3
Perspectivation of results to other studies
As no previous study has applied identical methods to evaluate absolute sustainability of global
sufficiency scenarios, there is no study to which it can be directly compared against to unfold the
plausibility of the results. However, studies applying slightly different methodology, focusing on
subsets of the aim, can be used to provide limited basis for comparison to aid putting the broad-scale
findings into context and to identify gaps which show the need for further investigation.
Comparisons of the distribution of contributions to impacts from the individual DLS activity
segments were therefore made against the findings of closely related studies.
A cautious comparison was made between the DLS scenarios contribution to climate change
and the contribution to Decent Living Energy (DLE). The DLE contribution can be seen in Figure 3b
of [57], where the stacked contribution bars show the different energy contribution from the activity
levels defined in that study.
The DLE results also show Nutrition to constitute plurality, but in a smaller manor (∼22%).
However, the DLE study assesses a slightly different set of activities than for this study. Noticeably,
retail and freight activity is a separate part of the DLE study, while it is intrinsically modelled here,
as described in SI 1.4. Hence, climate change related to the freight of Nutrition in this study is not
represented in the DLE study. For the Shelter, mobility, Communications & information and Clothing,
both studies agree. DLS levels for Hygiene is equivalent to Water in the DLE study, and both studies

==PAGE:33==
are fairly in agreement. Lastly, public services of the DLE study encapsulates the Healthcare and
Education segments in this study, where this study estimates ∼17% from this segment while the DLE
study estimates ∼12% of global energy use.
In addition, comparing the total magnitude of the result to other studies, [18] estimated in a
recent study the potential ’overshoot’ on pressure from meeting minimum access in an unequal world
with ’just access’ for all. In Figure 5 of [18], the worlds total emissions of CO2eq year−1 is 26.9 Gt.
In this study, the EF 3.0 characterizes the DLS scenario to 24.4 Gt CO2eq year−1.
Both comparisons show fairly relatable results, which indicate some level of credibility in the
DLS model as a feasible indicator at the LCI level for at least the climate change related ES or impact
categories. While the results of the comparisons are encouraging on a broad scale, the comparison also
suggests that the DLS scenario model should initially be interpreted as a novel and interim approach
to indicate sufficiency lifestyle impacts on an LCI level, providing a first take on comparison, to be
further refined through more differentiated models.
However, as highlighted in section 1.1 the field of working with planetary boundaries is still
novel and in rapid development. The framework still contains significant knowledge gaps within the
definitions of AESRs, including identification of the right impact pathways to represent all relevant
ESs. Hence, this limits the degree of scientific consensus achievable within the scope of this study.
[65] elaborated on [18]s work on ESB. In the recent paper, they defined a differentiation of safe
and just ESBs, respectively, while recognizing the need for an access foundation, forming a ’safe and
just corridor’ ([65]). The paper does not quantify the access foundation but keeps it as a conceptual
lower threshold. As my research expands on a similar concept, the results suggest that given the
current technological state, the access foundation in multiple occurrences exceeds the safe biophysical
ESBs, making the ’corridor’ collapse from within.
4.4
Considerations on mitigation
As the results suggest that an abrupt transition to sufficiency lifestyles will not be absolute sustainable,
we must consider the transformations needed to enable the required scales of mitigation and examine
what focuses should be made to mitigate the impacts from anthropogenic activities to a level compatible
with planetary stability. [66] stresses that a positive feedback loop occurs between human identity
and the PBs through self-identification with nature, while decreased tendency to account for the
environmental mitigation will eventually lead to decreased mental health and decreased resource
availability, which prompt a decrease in cooperation in any manner, including environmental mitigation.
This means that public awareness and engagement in itself will affect the socioeconomic mitigation
aspect regardless, from which activity transitions from an informed basis will lead to a reduction of
impacts.
Looking back at the IPAT equation introduced in section 1.2, it is now clear that ’I’ as a product
of non-negotiable human well-being under current technology and demography circumstances is not
likely to be absolute sustainable.
While [17] concluded that
"radical redistribution of resources is needed to achieve ’just access’ within the a stable
earth system"
, while this study suggests that redistribution will not suffice. Historically, technology has been
developed to facilitate economic growth, whereas the study reveals the need to forcibly direct
technological development to facilitate transformations in alignment with PBs.
The study reveals a significant variability across nations in the degree to which an impact
intensity follows the DLS scenario. In Figure 7 is shown a world heatmap of the sustainability ratio of
the climate change - Energy imbalance contribution per capita. As seen in the figure, no nation is
absolute sustainable, and the worst set nations require more than twice the footprint per capita to
meet DLS than most favorable set nations.
Noticeably, the figure shows Africa and especially Asia to be in the upper end of the scale, which
necessitates considering the population sizes. India and China constitute more than a quarter of
the global population, and the results show that adopting current technologies of ’best performing’
nations, i.e. with a larger fraction of renewable energy, will likely remain insufficient to appropriately
mitigate impacts.

While forcible mitigation of population size carries the potential to logically balance the IPAT-
equation, it is strongly invasive, and the results of this study do not cover the required considerations
to construct sound recommendations on this topic.
However, there is a need for technological advances that are effective in the areas where the
contribution to impacts is largest. Simultaneously, capable nations have to lead innovation on how to
develop more sustainable technology levels. Assessing further the current technological evolution with
respect to all the individual control variables can aid in identifying the potential to develop the most
favorable solutions while avoiding trade-offs.
4.5
Recommendations for further study
While recommendations for mitigation on political level is beneficial, concise topics for recommendation
has been revealed, aimed for the scientific community, in order to collectively increase understanding
of the interconnections between sufficiency lifestyles and absolute sustainability:
• Refining the basis for the life-cycle inventory related to DLS. As the resources for this study were
primarily limited by other factors than the importance of the research, there is the potential to
increase consistency, representativeness, and robustness.
• Investigating the relevance of regionalization of impact assessment from non-globally dependent
control variables, by collecting further spatialized inventory data. Examining where the damages
from overconsumption occur will enable prioritization for relevant mitigation in the future.
• Investigating across control variables, the relationship between increment of demand within
nations with more impact-intensive technologies outweighs the avoided impacts from decrement
of demand within historically over-consuming nations.
• Investigating, within each nation, the relationships between the distribution of sources that
cause environmental impacts for the DLS scenario and the potential for improvement within
each contributing segment for that nation. Thus, creating incentives for improvement on a
disaggregated level while considering each nation’s unique limitations and opportunities.

5
Conclusion
This study applied two methods for characterization and normalization; Planetary Boundaries-based
Life Cycle Impact Assessment, and ILCD-recommended impact assessment with Carrying Capacities
Normalization as a first order baseline for comparison, rather than a definitive evaluation of absolute
sustainability from decent living.
The main conclusions are 1) that providing material needs to conform with Decent Living
Standards under current technological and demographic conditions is not absolute sustainable, and 2)
that resulting pressures were generally lower than the current global pressures, which indicates that
sufficiency lifestyles could provide a substantial global average reduction in anthropogenic impacts.
The DLS scenario exceeded the Planetary Boundaries and Carrying Capacities for several
indicators, indicating unsustainable pressure levels. Atmospheric aerosol loading and Biodiversity
intactness index transgressed the Planetary Boundaries, while Climate change, Ocean acidification,
Biogeochemical flows - Nitrogen and Biogeochemical flows - Regional Phosphorus further transgressed
the upper thresholds for the zone of uncertainty. Climate change, Freshwater eutrophication, Land
use, Freshwater ecotoxicity , Particulate matter, both Resource use, fossil, and Resource use, mineral
and metals transgressed carrying capacity.
The DLS scenario was found to be absolute sustainable for Stratospheric ozone depletion, using
both methods.
The results for Terrestrial eutrophication, Terrestrial acidification, Freshwater depletion, Photo-
chemical ozone formation, Carcinogenic human toxicity, Non-carcinogenic human toxicity and Ionizing
radiation were considered inconclusive due to lack of spatial disaggregation.
There is a need for rapid technological development in a direction which decouples human
well-being from impacts on the biophysical, health related and resource consumption related thresh-
olds. Thus, technological propagation of the average current technologies from the best performing
nations to the other nations is not enough. Drivers for technological advancement are required to
be fundamentally changed, so economic incentives are sourced from providing benefits for universal
well-being and the ecological environment, in a way that innovation for sustainability is prioritized.
In summary, even though sufficiency lifestyles would constitute a large part of the solution,
it cannot be reached from current business-as-usual circumstances. For this, fundamental change
in societal-environmental engagement supported by radical transition in incentives for technological
development is necessary to pave the way toward global well-being in an absolute sustainable fashion.

Supplementary information 1
SI 1
Methods related information
SI 1.1
EEIO excluded as base-model
The following points argue why the inventory model excludes the use of EEIO using EXIOBASE to
lay the foundation of the modelled scenario.
1) The chosen multi-regional input-output (MRIO) database - EXIOBASE - holds 200 products
and 163 industries( [67]). In spite of this being quite detailed in comparison, the granularity of this
is not sufficient to represent the activity segments to an appropriate degree. 2) The coverage of
impact categories neither aligns fully with the PB-framework and is not sufficiently covering all the
ILCD-recommended midpoint indicators of the CC-frameworks. 3) EXIOBASE covers 49 regions,
while this study covers all 249 countries and areas (from here on: nations) observed by the UN.
Thus EEIO it will be used to supplement the pLCA model, where no data can be obtained.
SI 1.2
Selection of nations for MC proxy
Due to the design of the software (2.3.6), performing MC is both memory-consuming and time-
consuming, since the results should be simulated individually for each nation. For this reason, a
selection of representative nations was made, to form approximated simulation results. The selection
was made by the following approach.
For all UN geographical regions, the nation with the highest and lowest ’lived density’, along
with the most populated nation of the region was chosen. This ensures variability across several
parameters as well as most of the world population to be assessed with only few nations. A total of 27
nations were selected, and MC with 250 runs were made for each for PB-LCIA results. The MC results
were weighted by the corresponding nations population, and scaled by the relative contribution of
impact to the deterministic impact from the same nations, so they would represent global simulation
results. The 27 selected nations constitute ∼68% of the world population. See sheet 4 in SI 5.1.
SI 1.3
Current pressure on Planetary Boundaries
In order to compare the results of the DLS model to today’s world, comparable metrics have to be
applied. Consult sheet 22 in SI 5.1 for calculations.
The indicators presented in [8] as "current level" will not suffice, as several determine the current
state, due to previous pressure, as well as natural background, while the metrics needed for comparison
is the annual inventory of anthropogenically caused emissions or withdrawals, pressuring the ESBs.
Ideally, to avoid bias, the inventories for each ES, should consider the exact same set of compounds
as implemented in PB-LCIA. However, such assurance was not found feasible to make. Similarly, it
was not possible to find sufficient data to consider all ESBs. Hence, best available data will be used
to map the pressures for climate change, while extending the data collection is advised as further
research.
The general approach was to determine which emissions were characterized for each ES with PB-
LCIA and identifying current (or recent) inventory data. Then characterize the emissions individually,
using corresponding CFs for PB-LCIA and aggregating the results. The ES calculations for climate
change are documented below.

Details on system modelling per activity segment
In general, all processes used as part of the pLCI was chosen as market activities, in contracts to
production processes. The former takes into account the trades and material losses occurring between
producers and the consumers, aggregated in geographically dependent consumption mixes. For this
reason, in regard to transport of goods, freight is included, which means that a full model of freight in
isolation would lead to double counting. In the manor this was modelled, the model incompleteness
concerning freight consistently follows the incompleteness within general activities.
All products modelled are modelled as close to the global market (GLO) as possible, taking the
current market as given, rather than the shifted market, which would be expected to exist if DLS
were applied globally.

Nutrition
The Nutrition segment was divided into three measures of activity level; Food, storage, and meal
preparation.
Food
Food was modelled from the basis of the EAT Lancet commissions universal healthy reference diet
recommendations ([29]), in which the target nutritional value is appr. 2500 kcal. However, in order to
32 of 71
==PAGE:43==
Supplementary information 1
operationalize the physical flows related to food, the macronutrient intake in g/day laid basis for the
model instead.
For every food type in the reference diet, fitting generic Ecoinvent processes were included in
the model. In cases where ranges for a type of food was mentioned in the healthy reference diet,
these were included as uncertainty ranges. Triangular distribution was generally used, while uniform
distribution was used in the cases where the presented range starts from zero.
In order to make the food model as broad as possible within the limits of available generic data,
all relevant food market processes were evaluated, and in cases where several processes fit a presented
food type in the reference diet, an even mass distribution among these was made. This means the food
category for whole grains, containing rice, wheat, corn, etc. dictates an amount value of 1
n · mi, where
n is the amount of food types that fits those presented by [29] and mi is the mass of a certain food
type i. This might over- or under-represent the calorific value in some cases, as the calorie intensity
varies more in certain food types than in others.
However, the uncertainty ranges cover both comparatively small and large macronutrient intakes.
Waste treatment of food is assumed to be 20% ([54]) of all produce which reaches the consumer (all
pre-consumer waste is accounted for upstream). All food waste is assumed to be treated as kitchen
and garden biowaste.
Cold storage
Cold storage was modelled as a generic refrigerator process, scaled to the size of the household and
lifetime. Lifetime was assumed to be 20 years, with a triangular uncertainty range from 10 to 30
years. The electricity use was spatially modelled, as explained in section 2.3.3, and the electricity
efficiency was set to 120 kWh per refrigerator per year with uncertainty range normally distributed
around 150 kWh with a standard deviation of 30. This uncertainty range challenges the rather energy
efficient deterministic model. At the end-of-life stage, the refrigerator is treated for materials recovery
of domestic refrigerators.
Preparation of food
Preparation of food was modelled as a cookstove, electricity consumption and waste treatment. The
cookstove was modelled similarly to the refrigerator. The energy used for heating was assumed to be
all electric, for feasibility of the study. The energy requirement was estimated by [30] in [51] to be 5-7
MJ per kg food. Hence 6, MJ/kg was used. It was assumed that half of all food would be cooked.
For uncertainty analysis, a standard deviation of 0.1 was chosen, making the model test scenarios for
roughly 33-66% food cooked globally. This amounts to an range of 155-310 kg food cooked per person
yearly.
For the waste treatment no specific process were obtained. Hence the scaled mass of the cookstove
was treated as waste electric and electronic equipment.
SI 1.4.0.2
Shelter
The shelter segment represents the domestic part of "living", and includes dwelling construction
(sufficient housing space), thermal comfort, illumination and furniture.
Sufficient housing space
Sufficient housing space was modelled to represent a steady state activity level of yearly dwelling
construction. Separate models for multi family housing (MFH) and single family housing (SFH) were
made. United Nations world urbanization prospects for 2023 [55] was used to determine what share of
the individual nations population lives in urban and rural areas. For each country, the prospected
urban and rural share of the population was determined. In 20 cases, where no data was available
(mainly islands and military bases), a 50-50% distribution was applied.
This data set does not necessarily coincide with the share between actual household types, but is
rather based on various definitions of how urban areas are defined in the data available. This is usually
based on local population density, but in some cases other aspects, such as occupation (agricultural
vs. non-agricultural work), and even formal convention.

Furthermore, the nations were matched with data showing the share of SFH and MFH in urban
and rural areas, respectively ([31]). The data granularity was not available on a national level, but
instead aggregated into 11 geographical regions from one to four countries per region([32]). Hence,
the data was linked for each nation to create figures for the share of total MFH and SFH in urban
and rural areas, respectively.
As a measure of feasibility, the housing was created based on a study on construction materials
stock in residential buildings by [33]. While the cited study segregates dwellings to four types, this
study matches the data for MFH with Apartment Buildings and SFH with Detached Houses. The
model therefore consists of production and transport for steel, concrete, wood, copper, aluminium and
glass. Furthermore, metal working energy and materials for the three metals were modelled.
All construction and waste flows were distributed over the assumed lifetime of the buildings of 50
years. It is necessary to stress that it is not a true depiction, that the actual impacts from construction
is equally distributed over the course of its lifetime. However, in this case, the steady state scenario of
buildings being replaced and maintained over the course of a year is regarded as feasible. It is fair to
assume the model should represent the average potential impacts from the building for each year in
its lifetime. Naturally, this assumption requires the population to maintain constant as well, which is
not the case. Therefore it is assumed that the materials for construction would be underestimated.
The data for steel was modelled as 90% unalloyed steel, as this covers carbon steel, used for structural
beams, and 10% reinforcing steel for strength in concrete. Wood was modelled as sawn wood beams,
glued laminated timber and plywood, using density key of 480, 480 and 490 kg · m−3, respectively
([34]).
Considering the DLS ([22]), a person needs 10 m2/capita and a functional space of 20 m2,
which might or might not be shared with others. The amount of building materials needed to make a
household should equate the aforementioned requirements for square meterage of shelter, and follow
the equation:

used for uncertainty analysis. The total sufficient housing space thus amounts to 69m2 per household.
Thermal comfort
Thermal comfort was modelled as the materials and energy needed for residents to maintain thermal
comfort indoors and was based on the work in [21]. The baseline interval for thermal comfort was set
to 15.6 to 21.1◦C, which implies that technological assistance for temperature regulation is modelled
for temperatures outside this interval.
The model of this study uses the calculated values for direct energy intensity (in MJ/m2/year)
based on a set of parameters, including a historical average heating- and cooling-degree days (HDD
and CDD, respectively) and the technology level "advanced new" of a no more available tool by the
Global Buildings Performance Network called Building Energy Performance Scenarios (BEPS), in
which energy intensities for various scenarios can be generated for a set of building types withing
different climate zones.
Since this study considers 249 nations, which present the need for further data than available at
the point of writing, national values from the [21] study were matched to the corresponding nations,
while nations with no data were interpolated region-wise with the remaining countries. Four energy
intensities for every nation were thus developed, and assigned to the model.

Materials for thermal heating were assumed to be comparable to a 4kW heat pump EcoInvent
process, from which the annual material flows was obtained by scaling by the lifetime of the buildings
(as previously explained) and the assumed heat pump lifetime of 15 years. For uncertainty, lifetimes
of 10 and 30 years were used in a triangular distribution.
Illumination
Energy needs for illumination were modelled following the suggested approach in [21], providing
125 lumen · m−2 for 6 hours daily on a yearly average basis, with one third of the household being
lit at a time, not discriminating across nations. The electricity need for illumination to operate is
thus 0.0118GJ · m−2 · year−1 The model assumes all this lighting is provided by light emitting diodes
(LEDs) and the material need assumed roughly 30 LEDs per dwelling, which at ∼35g creates 1 kg of
LED bulbs per dwelling. The lifetime of LEDs is assumed to 20 years, while uncertainty range is 10
and 30 years in a triangular distribution.
Furniture
Furniture was crudely assumed to contain a constant stock of 1 tonne of wooden furniture per household,
with a lifetime mode of 25 years, and an uncertainty range of 15 and 50 years, representing the large
variability of when the necessity for decommissioning furniture presents itself. This encapsulates the
possibility of households trading furniture among each other, when different needs occur in a human
life (i.e. the demand for cribs and beds shift as the years go by).
SI 1.4.0.3
Hygiene
The hygiene segment contains the general need for hygienically related consumption. In previous
studies, waste treatment has been part of the hygiene segment. However, while water supply and
heating of water is explicitly modelled, waste infrastructure is spread out as intrinsic parts of every
activity level considered, and is not explicitly part of the model. This choice of modelling, could
potentially lead to model incompleteness, a topic which is holistically touched upon in section 4.
Water supply
Supply of clean water was modelled using generic EcoInvent market processes, which includes, as part
of the process documentation, a network assuming distance and loss of "... generic medium-sized town
transporting 5334 m3/day over a 69.53 km network ...". No feasibly operationalizable data was found,
spanning global water infrastructure, hence, this process was implemented unaltered, despite the fact,
that it would not be fully representative within its use. The activity level for water use is set to 50L
per capita per day as defined in [22]. Tap water processes include distribution and various current
technologies of water cleaning.
To ensure best possible differentiation of technologies, all available spatially dependent technolo-
gies for tap water in EcoInvent was identified. These amounted to seven geographical areas, including
five countries, the European continent, and the rest of the world (RoW). To link these to available
processes in EcoInvent, a similar approach as the one for electricity grids explained in section 2.3.3
was used.
Garden related watering activities are not included in the model due to several reasons; 1) First
and foremost, none of the stated literature states non-food-related irrigation as a requirement for
sufficient living. Hence, artificial gardening for aesthetic reasons is not considered a requirement, and
2) the water needed for irrigation on a private scale could be argued to be obtained from other sources
than tap water, hence not being represented by the infrastructure modelled.
It is assumed that all residential wastewater is treated at a municipal wastewater treatments
plant (WWTP), which includes Swiss data for infrastructure materials, transports, dismantling and
land use burden related to WWTP operation.
Water heating
While [21] bases the starting temperatures on spatially differentiated values, they stem from the
work of [38], and represent averaged air temperatures rather than shallow ground water temperature

(GWT). Air temperature is a fairly volatile measurement, and according to [39], the offset to GWT
is rarely validated. Although [39] instead investigates land surface temperatures (LST), and finds
an average offset of 1.2 ± 1.5K, the data availability of this proxy was not sufficient to adopt in this
current study. Hence, the values provided in [21] are used in our model. The share of energy for water
heating was assumed to be provided by either electricity (at the end consumer), district heating from

The deterministic model assumes 50% electricity provision, while the other 50% is distributed

Clothing
The clothing segment contains the needed amount of garment needed for people to maintain thermal
comfort outdoors, and arguably some current socially requirements are present as well. Furthermore
the maintenance (washing) and the equipment needs for this are included as well.
Clothes
[22] described the need for clothing for daily activity in the local climate. In the study, they did
not define the amount, but mentioned metered products of garments as indicators. [40] translated
this requirement based on data from India and some average global values, This model differentiated
global north/south with ∼3.33 and 2.2 kg clothes per year, respectively, based on the work by [21]
who assumed the under/overestimation from making a single model will cancel out to a sufficient
degree compared to the expected impact contribution from this segment. The study created scenarios
for key garment materials, lifetime, washing frequency and weight of each clothing item, amounting to
a yearly demand of ∼3 kg.
In this study, the clothing items are linked with relevant generic production processes, in which
tops, bottoms and underwear is assumed to be cotton, Shoes are assumed to be synthetic rubber
which jacket and jumpers are assumed to be made of fleece (polyethylene material).
More diverse materials and weight fractions is arguably used for clothing, however, the limitations
of the database available lead to the presented distribution.
The model assumes the entire mass is contained within the garments throughout its lifetime,
and 100% reaches waste treatment facilities.
Washing facilities
Washing facilities were modelled as manufacturing and transportation of washing machines, operation,
and waste treatment. Even though [22] defined the DLS for washing clothes to be based on joint
facilities (one machine per 1000 people), the model assumes one machine per household to conform
with [21]. The lifetime of the machine is assumed to be 20 years, corresponding to ∼2.9 tonnes of
clothing. For waste treatment the machines is assumed to weigh 75 kg and to be treated as electronic
waste.

Communication & information
The Communications segment represents the individuals need to interaction with social and political
movements ([22]), and consists of partly multi-directional telecommunication (phones and computers)
and information/entertainment broadcast (Television, which to some extend is obtainable through the
aforementioned devices).
Phones
The share of phones attributed to every country depends on the definition of need. [22] definition is
quite ambiguous, stating one place that every adult (leaving out a definition of what makes one an
adult) should have a phone, while further through the article, 1 phone per household is considered
sufficient. [21] assumes a threshold of 10 years of age.
Determining at what age it becomes a necessity to own a phone is a matter of the level of
alternatives for obtaining exposure to the needed cultural and political engagement.
Whether the age of 10 years represent adulthood is disputable. As is the debate on whether it is
ethically correct or not to provide ’younger adults’ with access to the smart-devices, which are now -
and in the future inevitably connected with ’phones’. in a paper about adolescent mental health, [50],
writes:
"Policies restricting adolescents’ access to new technologies are advocated, but may be
ill advised if new technologies are being used as a valuable source of social support or
are required in order to build digital and interpersonal (digitally mediated) skills for
economies of the future." ([50])
According to Odgers, 80% of 9-16 year-olds across seven European countries owned a cellphone
in 2014. This leaves reason to believe that 10 years is as good an age as any, and it is the intent of the

Hence, this values was adopted in the model of this study as well.
For each Nation, determining the share of the population at age 10 years or more at the year 2021

Computers
As a computer has the possibility to serve multiple functions, including to receive broadcasting of
information via internet connection, the model assumes this DLS activity level to be solely covered by
computers. Hence, no televisions are included. This is in alignment with both [21] and [22], for which
the latter defines the DLS for information access to be 1 television or internet device per household.
Computers are modelled as laptops using EcoInvent operation process, which includes manu-
facturing, internet infrastructure, electricity consumption and waste treatment. The lifetime of the
laptops are assumed to be 10 years, conforming with [51], The usage of the computer is assumed to be
2 hours per person per day in a household, as this also covers potential time spent working from home,
and streaming information (substituting television). However, this assumption is rather speculative.
It is assumed that all laptops are waste treated at the end-of-life stage.

Mobility
The mobility segment entails the access of people of ’adequate’ means of assisted transportation. In
[22] it is solely described as motorized transport, however, this model assumes some degree of pedal
assisted transport as well.
The mobility model to a vast extent follows the approach of [21], as is well documented in [51],
which structures the required distances and modes of transportation, based on a certain part of the
required distance being inversely correlated to the concept of ’lived density’ (LD). LD represents
population density considering agricultural and urban land area, and excludes e.g. snow-covered area.
The variable factor of the mobility requirement equation is based on individual country deviancy
from a base lived density LDbase. In [51] LDbase was assumed to be the median of all calculated LDs.
In this model, the LDbase calculation was substituted with a new approach, to increase temporal
representativeness to the current average world LD. The world bank source data for land area,
percentage of agricultural land area, and population density was extrapolated to represent present
demographics, yielding the world average LD, hence the new LDbase to be defined by

AUL
AL + ρAL
, where σpopworld is the worlds population density (people per km2, AUL is the worlds urban
land area in km2, AL is the worlds land area in km2 and ρAL is the fraction of agricultural land from
AL. In 39 cases, no data for LD was obtainable for a given nation. In all these cases, LDn = LDbase
were assumed (See sheet 8 SI 5.1 for details).
Total mobility levels were then recalculated, amounting to upper and lower limits of 14233 and

Cars
Cars were modelled as a combination of electric vehicles (EVs) and internal combustion engine vehicles

uncertainty range for the share of EV vs. ICE propulsion is uniform across the whole range from 0 to
100% of either.
The share of EVs representing 20% of year 2023 hardly represents the actual current conditions.
However, as the technology is proven ready to a larger degree than is currently implemented ([53]),
the DLS with a high share of EV technology is deemed quite representative of current technology.
Additionally, while only about 2.5% of the global stock of registered road vehicles (across categories)
are EVs, the market share is rapidly increasing with a annual market share of ∼10% in 2021([53]).
Ultimately, the model is not intended to represent real world stock, but a scenario of the current
technology available.
The electricity consumption during operation of EVs was spatialized the same way as explained

Trains and Busses
Train and bus propulsions were modelled without modification to global market processes. This means
no busses were modelled to be electric, despite current share is already electric, and the stock is
predicted increase to 5% share of busses globally i 2025 [53]. Additionally, part of the background
processes for train propulsion is modelled as electrically driven trains, however, as the spatialization
of the model is limited to low voltage electricity consumption, the train propulsion is in no way
specialized. A share of trains in the model is electrically driven. This is not spatialized. However, as
trains run on much higher voltage, the need for electricity transformation is arguably less than for low
voltage grid electricity. Hence the resulting change in impact from this choice is not expected to have
great effect.

SI 2.3
List of assumptions
This section contains a non-exhaustive list of assumptions and related limitations regarding the study.
• The model assumes that the mass of food is representative of the calorific value of food. This is
indicated by the choice of modelling mass-based distribution as a feasibility measure. Naturally,
this is not the case, hence the study is only representative in the not yet known cases where
mass distribution of food types and calorific value of food type correlate.
• The energy used to heat food was assumed to be entirely electric. This is a vast assumption
in terms of representation of current situations. However, as the DLS model already provides
electricity grid for everyone, and furthermore relies on technological advancements in electricity
provision, the assumption is not deemed unrealistic.
• All construction and waste flows were distributed equally over the assumed lifetime of the
buildings of 50 years. This is a well-knows fallacy in the field of LCA. The discussion of where
in a product lifetime it makes sense to attribute the most impact is widespread. In this case,
evaluating annual impacts, incentivizes repetitive assessments on a yearly basis, eventually
getting ever closer to change of "stock" in the case of little innovation. Although this is not a
perfect approach, it is outside the scope of this study to refine this matter.
• Houses are assumed to consist of six materials and some metal working. This implies some
underestimation and perhaps some overrepresentation og individual modelled materials. Since
the Shelter activity segment contributes a bit to both FET and RUM, the impact categories
yielding unexpected results, this assumption is further empathized as overly crude, and the
model needs refinement.
• Garden related watering activities are not included in the model due to several reasons; 1) First
and foremost, none of the stated literature states non-food-related irrigation as a requirement for
sufficient living. Hence, artificial gardening for aesthetic reasons is not considered a requirement,
and 2) the water needed for irrigation on a private scale could be argued to be obtained from
other sources than tap water, hence not being represented by the infrastructure modelled.
However, water depletion is naturally a result of irrigation, hence, including such a model will
need another water-provision sub-model to facilitate assessment of private irrigation.
• The share of energy for water heating was assumed to be 50% electricity provision (at the end
consumer), 25% district heating from natural gas, and 25% district heating from other energy
sources. Naturally, this is not the case globally. However, the MC results show that this does
not have a vast impact to the conclusions of the study.
• The model does not consider the heat loss fraction, thereby the additional energy need for the
cases of district heating. This implies some underestimation to be expected.
• For clothing: bottoms and underwear are assumed to be cotton, shoes are assumed to be
synthetic rubber, jackets and jumpers are assumed to be made of fleece (polyethylene material).
This is a simplification, and similar to Housing, some materials are necessarily over-represented
at the cost of materials with unavailable data. This is not expected to have an impact on the
conclusions.

• In regard to thermal comfort for public services, private intensity is assumed to be multiplied by
3 to account for non-hospital healthcare operation. This is a very crude assumption, which is
adopted directly from [21], and in reality, variabilities in healthcare facilities dictate the energy
needs. As healthcare does not drive impacts, it is not expected to impact the conclusions of
the study.
• The DLS model assumes there is no infrastructure for treating human waste (excrement), the
no impacts related to this are quantified. This yields a completeness gap in the model.In
[72], it is stated that post food-consumption emissions has potential to drive the impacts on
eutrophication. The study also recognizes a high sensitivity to food types. Hence, in relation to
further refinement of the Nutrition sub-model, it is recommended to expand it with metabolic
processes as well.
