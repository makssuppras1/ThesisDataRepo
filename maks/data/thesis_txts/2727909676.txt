Abstract
This thesis project aims to investigate mobility patterns and test the validity of the ”travel
time budget” hypothesis using large­scale mobility data. The study explores into various
aspects of users mobility, including demographic information, to gain comprehensive in­
sights.
The initial phase involved data pre­processing to ensure its suitability for subsequent anal­
yses, where filtering techniques were applied to improve data quality.
We implemented a spatial analysis of the data, where explored the overall distances cov­
ered by users, both within the hours of the day and the days of the week. Additionally,
the temporal aspect of individual movements was examined, focusing on the duration
of users’ trips. Then we conducted a critical data sanity test which revealed consistent
movement patterns among users.
Furthermore, we created an algorithm to identify the location of users’ homes and work­
places, which enabled the exploration of their commuting trips. The study paid particular
attention to commute times, revealing remarkably stable median values across working
days and months. This finding coupled with the consistent movement patterns, suggests
the presence of a travel time budget.
Incorporating demographic data into the analysis allowed us for a thorough exploration of
user patterns. There was observed a gender gap in commuting time, where males tend to
travel longer than females, while the other demographic data did not show significant pat­
terns. Subsequently, the correlations between demographics and commuting times were
examined, and a regression model was applied to further investigate their connections.
This study sheds light on mobility behavior and contributes valuable insights into the ”travel
time budget” hypothesis using large­scale mobility data. The findings have implications
for understanding commuting patterns and their relationship with demographic factors,
ultimately contributing to the field of mobility research.

1.1
Human behaviour ­ mobility
The study of human behavior has been a topic of interest since ancient times, with philoso­
phers attempting to explain human behavior for centuries. However, the development of
systematic and scientific approaches to understanding human behavior began to take
shape during the late 19th and early 20th centuries. In the context of this study, we are
interested in human mobility, which is a specific aspect of human behavior connected to
the social and cultural context. The research of which as a distinct field began to gain
momentum in the late 20th century.
The emergence of human mobility research can be attributed to various factors related to
the complexity of modern cities and societies. Urbanization and transportation develop­
ment, the shift toward sustainable and efficient transportation, and the policy and planning
of public infrastructure projects have contributed to the growth of this research field. Tech­
nological advancements and the availability of data have also played a significant role
in accelerating research progress. Interdisciplinary approaches from geography, anthro­
pology, sociology, urban planning, and computer science have enabled a holistic under­
standing of human mobility, incorporating social, spatial, technological, and environmental
dimensions.
Today, human mobility research continues to evolve, driven by ongoing societal chal­
lenges and technological advancements. The field covers a wide range of topics, in­
cluding travel behavior [1], epidemic spreading [2], transportation planning [3], migration
[4][5], urban mobility and accessibility[6] [7]. The goal is to develop more efficient and
sustainable mobility systems that meet the needs of individuals and communities while
addressing the challenges of an increasingly interconnected world.
Research on human mobility aims to gain insights into how individuals move and inter­
act within different contexts. In the past, researchers collected data through surveys,
interviews [8], ethnographic studies , and observations of individuals [9]. Although these
studies provided high­quality data, they were limited in terms of sample size. However,
with recent technological advancements, new methods of data gathering have emerged.
Current studies often utilize data from sources such as mobile phones [4], GPS tracking
[10], and social media [11], providing rich spatial and temporal information. The utiliza­
tion of these large­scale datasets has enabled quantitative studies, contributing to a more
comprehensive understanding of human mobility.
The field of human mobility research has gained increased attention, particularly due to
the recent COVID­19 pandemic. Simulations of mobility patterns have proven useful in
preventing the spread of the virus, leading researchers worldwide to intensify their efforts
in this field. Benita [12], in a review on the boost in academic research fields inspired by

1.2
Travel Time Budget (TTB) Hypothesis
In this dissertation, we focus in a specific subject of human mobility, we aim to test the
”travel time budget” (TTB) hypothesis, by using a large­scale mobility dataset. The con­
cept of constant travel­time budgets (TTBs) was initially proposed in 1961 [13], and it has
since gathered significant attention in the transportation literature, following some influen­
tial work done in the 70’s and 90’s [14][15][1]. The hypothesis suggests that individuals, on
average, allocate a fixed amount of time for daily commuting, typically averaging around
1­1.3 hours per person per day [16].
Understanding the behavior and decision­making processes of individuals is crucial in
transportation research for developing effective policies and infrastructure solutions. One
prominent theory that has shaped our understanding of travel behavior is the ”Travel Time
Budget” hypothesis [17] [15]. Proposed by economist Yacov Zahavi in the 1970s, this
hypothesis posits that individuals have a predetermined, fixed amount of time that they
are willing to allocate for travel activities on a daily basis.
According to the Travel Time Budget hypothesis, people strive to maintain a consistent
level of travel­related activities, such as commuting, running errands, and recreational
travel, regardless of changes in travel speed or the availability of transportation alterna­
tives. The hypothesis suggests that any improvements in travel speed or accessibility are
offset by longer travel distances or increased activity participation. In other words, individ­
uals have a specific ”budget” of time for travel and adapt their behavior accordingly.
1.3
Dataset, our approach
While the Travel Time Budget hypothesis has been widely acknowledged and utilized
in transportation planning and policy­making, empirical evidence supporting its validity
remains mixed. With the recent availability of extensive mobility datasets, there is an
opportunity to test this hypothesis on a large scale. This thesis aims to examine the Travel
Time Budget hypothesis by leveraging a large dataset that contains data from various
geographical regions, travel modes, and demographic characteristics.
Our approach will primarily involve quantitative analysis. We have obtained access to a
massive dataset comprising anonymized GPS location data for approximately 5,000,000
individuals. This dataset was collected by a global smartphone and electronics company
between 2015 and 2019 through a smartphone app. The data consists of anonymized
user information and has been extracted for research purposes.
By analyzing this large­scale mobility data, we aim to address several key research ques­
tions. First, we seek to determine if individuals maintain a relatively stable travel time
budget across varying conditions, including changes in transportation infrastructure, socio­
economic factors, and technological advancements. Additionally, we aim to explore how
travel time budgets vary across different segments of the population, taking into account
factors such as age, gender, and residential area. Furthermore, we will investigate if and
how these demographic factors are connected to commuting time.
Overall, this thesis aims to contribute to the existing knowledge on the Travel Time Budget
hypothesis through the analysis of large­scale mobility data. By shedding light on the

Literature Review
In recent years, the field of human mobility has gathered significant attention due to the
availability of large­scale datasets capturing human mobility patterns. This chapter aims
to provide a review of the existing literature on human mobility, with a specific focus on
the concept of the travel time budget (TTB).
The chapter is divided into three sections. The first section presents studies that explore
human mobility patterns. The second one is focused more on the research around the
topic of the travel time budget. While in the last part we are presenting some research on
demographics on human mobility and commuting times.
2.1
Human Mobility patterns
The field of human mobility has gathered significant attention due to its relevance in var­
ious applications such as estimating migratory flows, traffic forecasting, urban planning,
and epidemic modeling. The COVID­19 pandemic further intensified interest in this field,
as understanding human mobility patterns became crucial in curbing the spread of the
virus.
One highly influential study in the field conducted by Brockmann, Hufnagel, and Geisel
[18] focused on the dispersal of bank notes in the United States. The study revealed that
the dispersal of bank notes can be described by a continuous­time random­walk process
with scale­free jumps and long waiting times between displacements. The trajectories
of bank notes exhibited characteristics akin to scale­free random walks, known as Lévy
flights, suggesting anomalous dispersal behavior. Importantly, the authors demonstrated
that the dispersal characteristics observed in bank notes also apply to human travel be­
havior, providing a quantitative assessment of human travel statistics.
In contrast to the findings above, a later study by González, Hidalgo, and Barabási [4] pre­
sented contrasting results. Their research indicated that human mobility patterns exhibit
high regularity and reproducibility, despite the diverse travel histories of individuals. They
observed that each individual has a time­independent characteristic travel distance and a
significant probability of returning to a few highly frequented locations. The study further
demonstrated that individual travel patterns converge into a single spatial probability dis­
tribution, suggesting that humans follow simple and reproducible patterns that differ from
the random trajectories predicted by models like Lévy flights and random walks.
Both studies highlight the scale­free nature of human mobility, indicating that it remains
unaffected by the size of the cities individuals reside in. However, Alessandretti, Aslak,
and Lehmann [19] propose an interesting perspective on these scale­free results. They
suggest that the scale­free patterns observed in previous studies may be artifacts of data
aggregation within geographical containers, where they appear as power laws. Instead,
they argue that day­to­day human mobility exhibits scales that correspond to different
levels of areas, such as rooms, buildings, and neighborhoods. This perspective sheds
light on the hierarchical nature of human mobility patterns within different spatial scales.
2.2
Commuting patterns
The travel time budget theory, initially proposed by Tanner [13] in 1961, suggests the ex­

istence of constant travel­time budgets (TTBs) among humans. Subsequent research by
Szalai [14] examined travel time to work across different countries and found an interest­
ing result, that there is minimal variation in travel time among various cities and countries.
Zahavi made significant contributions to the understanding of the travel time budget hy­
pothesis, as he focus his research on the topic. His work at this period, spanning publica­
tions such as [17], [15], and [20], focused on establishing well the hypothesis that people
seem to have a travel time budget. Zahavi’s model postulated that individuals allocate
time for travelling and other activities, and he observed relative stability in the mean daily
time allocated to travel across several cities worldwide. Based on this stability, Zahavi
proposed that daily time allocated to travel is independent of the other activities.
However, there is a discrepancy in the usage of the term ”travel time budget” in past stud­
ies. Initially, the term referred to the time individuals allocate in their schedules for com­
muting. However, most studies have focused on the actual time people spend commuting
rather than the time they are willing to spend. A more precise term, ”travel time expendi­
ture” (TTE), has been introduced to differentiate between the two concepts. Goodwin [21]
analyzes these terms and highlights that ”budget” implies stability, whereas ”expenditure”
does not, making it more susceptible to changes.
The question arises as to whether past research on travel time budgets had significant
shortcomings. Stopher and Zhang [22] suggest that studies analyzing travel time bud­
gets using highly aggregated data from various metropolitan areas around the world have
likely drawn correct conclusions. On the other hand, studies that have undertaken more
disaggregated analyses of travel time expenditures but mislabeled them as budgets are
more clearly in error. Mokhtarian and Chen [3] support this statement in their review of
27 TTB studies. They examined both aggregate and disaggregate studies between 1972
and 1999, and concluded that at an aggregate level the travel time expenditures are con­
stant. At a disaggregate level, the TTE is connected to various characteristics and factors,
and this is the reason why these studies fail to finds constancy in studies like [23], [24],
[25]. Thus, the underlying mechanisms explaining that regularity in aggregate studies are
not well understood.
The research community’s focus on finding results related to travel time budgets (TTBs)
contrasts with the availability of data on travel time expenditures (TTEs). Observing a
budget in practice presents challenges. Banerjee, Ye, and Pendyala [26] attempted to
calculate TTB using a proxy term called ”travel time frontier” (TTF). They did that by cre­
ating a stochastic frontier model.
These early studies laid the foundation for understanding the travel time budget hypothe­
sis and its implications for human mobility. They established the concept that individuals
allocate a relatively consistent amount of time for travel and demonstrated that this travel
time allocations are stable across the different countries and cities. As mentioned by
Ahmed & Stopher [16] in their comprehensive review of travel­time budget (TTB) studies,
the TTB has been reported in most researches almost as a universal constant across
time and space at 1–1.3 hours per person per day. Of course, across different studies
the exact number and variance differs, but the average of researchers suggest this time
to be between 60 and 80 minutes.
Marchetti’s intriguing study [1] expanded the timeframe of research to thousands of years
in the past. He proposed a theory known as ”The Marchetti Constant,” suggesting that
the travel time budget has been inherent to humans for millennia. Marchetti’s findings
explain that cities have functioned based on distances where people needed an average

Some studies suggest an increasing trend in travel time budgets or expenditures in recent

on massive and precise data are not found a lot in the literature. The key results from this

ability observed across different contexts and regions.
2.3
Mobility patterns & demographics
In addition to studying overall travel time, researchers have also examined the relationship
between travel time and demographic variables.
Lenormand et al. [29] conducted a study analyzing mobility patterns based on age, gen­
der, and occupation status using credit­card records from Barcelona and Madrid. Their
findings indicate that there are some small differences among these groups under certain
circumstances.
Elias et al. [30] also reported differences in mobility patterns between genders. They
concluded that women tend to travel less than men in terms of both the number of trips
and the total time spent traveling. This gender gap in travel behavior has been observed
in various studies conducted across different countries [31], [32], [33], [34], suggesting
that male workers tend to have longer commuting times or distances compared to female
workers.
The research by Newbold [35] on the topic of commuting distance by age, has shed some
light in the variability of commuting regarding age. They discovered that individuals in
their fifties tend to travel the furthest for work, while younger and older age groups tend
to have shorter commuting distances. However, in the research conducted by Giménez

that commuting behaviors may not depend on the age of workers.
A recent study by Bunten et al. [36] highlighted racial differences in commuting times.
They found that in 1980, black commuters spent an additional 50.3 minutes commuting per
week compared to white commuters. Although this difference decreased to 22.4 minutes
per week by 2019, it still exists.
These studies provide insights into the connection between travel time and various demo­
graphic factors, such as gender, age, and race. Understanding these relationships can
contribute to a better understanding of commuting patterns and help inform transportation
and urban planning strategies.

patterns, the travel time budget hypothesis, and the connection between mobility patterns

and highlight the importance of considering various factors when studying commuting
patterns and travel time expenditures. In the subsequent sections, we will further explore

ing steps, as outlined in the research paper by Alessandretti, Aslak, and Lehmann [19],
involved using the Infostop algorithm to identify stop locations in the trajectories.
The Infostop algorithm, developed by Aslak and Alessandretti [37], is a scalable algorithm
designed to detect stop locations in multi­user mobility data. It converts raw time series
data into sequences of destinations where individuals stayed at the same place for a
period of time. The algorithm utilized specific parameters, including r1 = 30 meters, r2 =
30 meters, tmin = 10 minutes, and tmax = 24 hours.
Description of the parameters:
• r1, the maximum roaming distance allowed for two points within the same stay.
• r2, the typical distance between two stays in the same destination.
• tmin, the minimum duration of a stay.
• tmax, the maximum time difference between two consecutive records for them to be
considered within the same stay.
While we do not have access to the specific details of the preprocessing steps beyond
the utilization of the Infostop algorithm, the aforementioned research papers provide ad­
ditional information on the process and its parameters. It is important to note that the
preprocessing steps were conducted to transform the raw data into a format suitable for
analysis and to identify stop locations for further analysis of human mobility patterns.

4.2
Home & work location detection
In order to detect the home and work locations of the users, we implemented a simple
heuristic algorithm based on common assumptions about people’s daily routines. The
algorithm followed several steps to identify the locations where individuals spend most of
their nights (home) and mornings (work), taking into account potential changes in living
arrangements and job transitions.
Testing the ”travel time budget” hypothesis using large­scale mobility data
21
==PAGE:29==
Here is an overview of the steps involved in the home and work location detection process:
1. The detection was performed for each month separately, recognizing that people
may change their residence or workplace over time.
2. To determine the home location, we identified the location where the user spent the
most nights per month (specifically at 3 a.m.). Similarly, for the work location, we
identified the location where the user spent the most mornings per month (specifi­
cally 11 a.m.).
3. In cases where the maximum occurrence of nights or mornings was a null value
(indicating that the user was mostly moving during those times), we considered the
second maximum value instead.
4. To address gaps in the data where the home or work location was undetermined
for certain months, we filled in the missing locations based on the previous or next
detected month. If the home location was determined for a particular month but
not the previous month, we assumed the home location remained the same and
assigned it to the undetermined month. This process was repeated until most of the
gaps were filled.
5. A minimum threshold was set for the number of days per month that the user was
expected to be at home or work. If the maximum occurrence fell below this threshold
(e.g., less than 3 times per month), the location detection was considered unreliable
and filtered out.
By following these steps, we aimed to capture the most frequent locations where indi­
viduals reside and work, considering potential changes and data gaps. This approach
provided a simple but useful algorithm for home and work location detection.

Finally, a machine learning task was conducted to explore the potential connection be­

predictors of commute duration. However, initial results did not yield promising outcomes,
and further exploration was limited.
To prepare the data for the regression task, a dataframe was created, consisting of the
independent variables, including urbanization level, age, gender, height, and weight, and
the dependent variable, duration. To ensure compatibility with regression models, all vari­
ables were converted into numerical values. The gender variable was mapped to numeri­
cal values, with ”MALE” assigned as 0 and ”FEMALE” as 1. Additionally, null values were
removed from the dataset to facilitate accurate algorithm performance. We also calcu­
lated each individual’s median commute duration in order to make the task more feasible
for the model.
To gain a better understanding of the data and its interrelationships, we calculated Pear­
son’s correlation scores for each pair of columns. The resulting heatmap, presented in
Figure 5.16, displayed notable correlations among age, gender, and height, with the high­
est score observed between gender and height (­0.7). However, the correlations of inter­
est were between duration and the other variables, which were found to be insignificant,
with scores below 0.3 on both the positive and negative scales.
Despite the discouraging correlation results, the regression task using a random forest
algorithm was pursued in an attempt to uncover any underlying complex connections.
The random forest algorithm was chosen due to its simplicity and effectiveness in regres­
sion problems. To implement the algorithm, we transformed the input data (age, gen­
der, weight, and height) into a single vector using the VectorAssembler transformer in
PySpark. Subsequently, we employed the random forest regressor, coupled with k­fold
cross­validation.

Discussion
In this section, we will discuss the results of our analysis and comment on both the ex­
pected and unexpected findings. We will also provide insights into what these results
mean for the city of Tokyo and how they contribute to the broader field of human mobility
research.
6.1
Spatial Analysis
In the initial phase of the analysis, we explored the spatial aspect of human mobility by
investigating the distances users covered in the city of Tokyo. We computed the average
distances traveled on different days of the week and over the entire time period. The re­
sults revealed distinct patterns of covered distances between weekends and weekdays,
with longer average distances on weekdays, indicating more active mobility during work­
ing days.
As anticipated, we observed a slightly higher average distance on Mondays, which can
be attributed to errands and activities postponed during the weekend, as people tend to
rest during this period. Notably, the analysis of national holidays displayed clear spikes
in the distance chart, implying a connection between movement patterns and the cultural
aspects of society.
Furthermore, we identified a prevalent tendency for very short moves, indicating either
short trips or round trips, which aligns logically with the nature of the dataset. Additionally,
we noticed a downward trend in the data, indicating that longer distances were less prob­
able to be covered by individuals. This suggests that people tend to move within a radius
of distance around their usual locations.
A particularly interesting finding in Figure 5.4 was the presence of two distinct cycles of
movement in an individual’s working day. Long distances were covered in the morning,
followed by the same distances being gradually covered again in the late afternoon and
night. Unexpectedly, between 12 p.m. and 5 p.m., the median distances covered were
even lower than during nighttime, suggesting very limited movements during that time
period. On weekends, we observed a different pattern, with people not traveling far except
for a notable peak between 12 a.m. and 1 p.m., which we interpret as the time when
individuals return home after a Friday night out, supported by the fact that most trains
stop working around that time.
6.2
Temporal Analysis
In our temporal analysis, we focused on the distribution of move and stop durations, pro­
viding insights into the temporal patterns of mobility in Tokyo. The results indicated that
people primarily engaged in short stops and trips lasting 10 to 20 minutes, with an addi­
tional tendency for trips lasting around 0.5 to 1 hour, which aligns with later findings of the
duration of daily commuting trips.
We also observed a preference for stops lasting 9 to 12 hours, which likely corresponds to
the time when individuals rest at night. An unexpected discovery was a small peak in move
durations lasting 12 hours, which we traced back to specific users, possibly professional
drivers, whose behavior could have skewed the aggregated data.

Furthermore, when comparing move and stop patterns between weekdays and weekends,
we found that on weekends, people engaged in more short­duration trips and stops, while
on Fridays and weekends, they tended to have longer stops lasting over 13 hours. This
pattern can be attributed to reduced overall mobility on weekends, with individuals spend­
ing more time at home.
Analyzing move and stop durations throughout the day, we identified that trips starting
between 4:30 p.m. and 7:30 p.m. tended to last longer than 1.5 hours. This finding
is consistent with the results from the commuting analysis, indicating that individuals with
longer commutes tend to start their trips earlier. We also observed shorter move durations
around 12 p.m., suggesting that individuals allocate sufficient time for meals during their
lunch breaks.
An intriguing observation was that after noon, move durations on weekends were shorter
than on weekdays, in line with the overall reduced movement on weekends. However, an
unexpected peak of move duration on weekdays around 2 hours at 12:45 a.m. was noted,
which we attributed to isolated incidents.
In the distribution of stops durations on the weekends, we found that there is a peak at
8:30 p.m., where the stops last more than 8 hours. This corresponds to the time when
people return home for the night. On working days, beside the same peak that exists
but starts slightly earlier, there were two additional peaks in stop durations, one at 8 a.m.
and a smaller one at 1 p.m., likely indicating the times when people arrive at work in the
morning and after lunch.
A significant step in our analysis involved a sanity test, where we assessed the random­
ness of moves in the dataset. There we saw that if the moves of this dataset are randomly
distributed, they are heavily affected by the many small values and thus the distribution
very narrow around the mean or median. This compared to the real user’s distribution
gives us the information that people are consistent to their movement patterns. This, is
an argument supporting the idea that people have a Travel Time Budget (TTB).
6.3
Commuting Analysis
In the subsequent commuting analysis, we looked into the characteristics of commuting
trips in the city of Tokyo. There we observed a tendency for commuting trips to last be­
tween 45 and 65 minutes on regular working days. On weekends the commuting times
were shorter, as expected because far less people work on weekend, so the traffic is
lighter.
Examining the times when people started their commuting trips, we found clear patterns of
morning start times and more varied return times in the evening and night. The distribution
of commute trip durations throughout the day had significant fluctuations but it was mostly
fluctuating between 30 minutes and 1 hour.
Our investigation reveals that the median duration of a single trip commute is approxi­
mately 53 minutes. This value surpasses the average duration reported in a related study
[40]. However, it is crucial to acknowledge that the accuracy of our findings is compro­
mised by the absence of validation data for the detection of home and work locations. As
a consequence, we should be cautious with the 53­minute commuting time as a definitive
and precise outcome.
Exploring the influence of different factors on commuting time, such as the time of the year
and days of the week, we observed remarkable stability in commuting time at around 53

In analyzing the relationship between commuting time and demographic data, we found a

spend approximately 2̃5% more time commuting than women, consistent with the findings

correlation between commuting time and demographic factors.

7
Conclusion
In summary, the results of our analysis shed light on the spatial and temporal patterns
of human mobility in Tokyo. Our findings contribute to a better understanding of human
mobility in Tokyo and may be valuable for urban planning and transportation manage­
ment. Furthermore, our analysis highlights the importance of considering demographic
factors when studying commuting patterns, but also indicates that commuting time might
be influenced by various other factors beyond demographics.
The aim of this thesis was to explore a large­scale mobility dataset towards mobility pat­
terns and more specifically to investigate on the ”travel time budget” hypothesis. We
achieved this, through comprehensive analyses of both temporal and spatial aspects of
the data, along with the analyses into commuting behaviors and demographic factors.
Then, through the data sanity test that revealed consistent movement patterns, coupled
with the remarkably stable median commute time over days and months, we additionally
achieved to find indications of the travel time budget.
This dissertation contributes to the field of human mobility research through an extensive
quantitative analysis of mobility data. The study provides a comprehensive and detailed
description of human movement patterns, shedding light on how individuals move around
in the city of Tokyo.
The analysis provides valuable insights on an aggregate level into human mobility patterns
and their tendencies. The declining curves in the distance, duration, and trip distributions
per day suggest that people generally prefer to move less, which could be attributed to
various factors such as convenience, urbanization, or changes in the business industry.
Further research into the underlying reasons for this trend could shed light on how soci­
eties are evolving and adapting to modern challenges.
The clear distinction in movement patterns between working days and weekends high­
lights the importance of considering different mobility needs on weekdays versus week­
ends in urban planning and transportation management. This finding could influence de­
cisions regarding public transport schedules, infrastructure development, and policies to
accommodate different commuting behaviors.
The observation that people are consistent in their mobility patterns implies that individuals
tend to establish regular routines in their daily lives. This is in accordance to the ”travel
time budget” (TTB) theory, suggesting that people have a specific daily time budget.
The finding that commuting time is stable on average but varies among individuals empha­
sizes the need for personalized commute solutions. Recognizing individual differences in
commuting behavior can help employers, policymakers, and city planners develop more
efficient transportation strategies and improve work­life balance.
The gender gap in commute time between men and women highlights an area for further
investigation and potential action.
The attempted prediction of commute time and the lack of significant correlations with de­
mographic variables underscore the complexity of this issue. It suggests that additional
factors beyond demographics, such as traffic conditions, weather, or individual prefer­
ences, play crucial roles in determining commute times.

Although our focus was primarily on the city of Tokyo to ensure a comprehensive and
robust investigation, the methodology can be adapted with slight modifications to explore
more extensive geographical regions. By extending the number of included cities and
regions, we can have a more generalized view on the topic.
It is essential to note that the home­work location algorithm we employed has some lim­
itations, as we lacked validation data. Future research in this area could benefit from
incorporating additional data from the users.
In future research, the study has the potential to be extended by incorporating velocity
analysis. As depicted in Appendix A.4, an initial distribution of velocity is presented. How­
ever, it is essential to note that for this particular analysis, the data requires cleaning, a
process that was not feasible within the constraints of the limited timeframe of this thesis.
The scope of the thesis project limited the extent of our investigation towards commuting
time forecasting. Given more time and resources, a more comprehensive analysis of
the regression model could give valuable insights into finding the factors that influence
commuting duration.
