increasing attention because of their worldwide distribution, toxicity, and persistence.

1 Introduction 
Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) are organic contaminants that are the result of human 
activity and have a fluorinated carbon chain connected to a functional group. For more than 60 years, 
these materials have been employed in a wide range of products, such as food packaging, surfactant 
agents, insecticides, paints, stain- and water-resistant clothing, and in fire-extinguishing 
formulations (Arvaniti & Stasinakis, 2015; Cousins et al., 2016; Lau et al., 2007; Prevedouros et al., 2006; 
Wang et al., 2018). However, because of their persistence (Hale et al., 2017; Houtz et al., 2013; Kannan, 
2011; Xiao et al., 2015), propensity for bioaccumulation (Conder et al., 2008; Kwak et al., 2020; Liu et al., 
2014; Yang et al., 2017), and toxicity (Apelberg et al., 2007; Giesy et al., 2010), they have come to be 
recognized as environmental contaminants of international significance. 
 
The diverse sources of PFASs include point sources, such as industrial sites, airports, fire stations, 
military bases, and PFAS waste disposal sites (Bräunig et al., 2019; Y. Li et al., 2018a; Lorenzo et al., 2019; 
Xiao et al., 2015) and diffuse sources, such as agricultural land where insecticides have been applied 
or where recycled wastewater has been used for irrigation or the application of digested sewage 
sludge has taken place (G. R. Johnson, 2022; Zareitalabad et al., 2013). Leading to pollution with PFASs 
in the soil and groundwater among other environmental matrices. 
 
PFAS are widely distributed in the environment and are taken up by species, including humans 
(Ahrens, 2011; Giesy & Kannan, 2001; Yeung et al., 2006; Zareitalabad et al., 2013). The extreme surface-
active characteristics of PFAS, characterized by their hydrophobic and oleophobic poly- or 
perfluorinated carbon chain (tail) in combination with the hydrophilic, typically anionic, functional 
head group—distinguish them from other persistent organic pollutants (POPs) in terms of how they 
interact with the environment. PFAS behavior is influenced by both hydrophobic, oleophobic and 
electrostatic interactions (Higgins & Luthy, 2007). Due to the complexity of PFAS chemistry, sorption 
of these substances cannot be predicted from a single sorbent bulk property, such as organic carbon 
(OC) content (Barzen-Hanson, Davis, et al., 2017; Y. Li et al., 2018a). Furthermore, there are still 
significant uncertainties regarding how different sorbent specific properties, such as pH and surface-
bound cations, interact to determine the binding of PFAS to soils (Y. Li et al., 2018a). To assess the risks 
associated with PFAS, it is important to understand the sorption behavior of these chemicals and 
how they migrate through soil. 
==PAGE:8==
2 
 
The sorption of PFAS to soil/sediment materials depends on the perfluorocarbon tail's length and 
the head group's functionality. The distribution coefficient increases with each -CF2- moiety, 
indicating that the main sorption mechanism for the tail is absorption into the organic content of 
the soil/sediment, while the head is hydrophilic (Knight et al., 2019). 
 
A model currently used to predict the sorption behavior of PFAS, is a model forecasting the sorption 
dependency of PFAS on electrostatic and hydrophobic interactions with the organic matter, it was 
evaluated for OC ranging from 0.6 to 9.7% (Higgins & Luthy, 2006a, 2007). Knight et al., 2019 tested 
100 soils with OC content ranging from 0.1 to 3.5%, for PFOA sorption and found that given the 
variety of soil phases that influence sorption, normalizing 𝐾𝑑 measurements simply using soil OC 
content is not adequate, but that a range of soil properties such as OC, silt, clay, surface roughness, 
grain sizes, exchangeable cation concentration, carbonate content and pH is required. 
 
Also, Nguyen et al., 2020 found that sorption of PFAS is better explained by multiple soil parameters 
than just by one. And that anionic, nonionic, and zwitterionic PFASs all exhibited varying responses 
to soil characteristics in terms of sorption. That the solution pH changes both soil chemistry and 
PFAS speciation with regard to electrostatic processes and surface complexity. These findings show 
that PFAS and soil surfaces interact in complex ways, and that in order to describe how PFAS migrate 
through the subsurface, it is necessary to consider both the type of PFAS and soil characteristics.

2 Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) and their characteristics 
 
2.1 Structure and classification 
 
Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) cover a wide range of fluorinated compounds with at 
least one fully fluorinated methyl (-CF3) or methylene group (-CF2-), without any H/Cl/Br/I atom 
attached (OECD, 2021). It is a heterogeneous group with varying levels of fluorination, number of 
carbon atoms and other chemical groups present. 
To distinguish which organofluoride compounds are PFAS and which are not, an illustrative scheme 
is drawn, se figure 1. 
 
Figure 1: Illustrative scheme of which organofluoride compounds are PFAS, modified from (OECD, 2021). 
 
PFAS is a highly complex group of chemicals with diverse molecular structures consisting of a polar 
functional group attached to at least one saturated fully fluorinated carbon moiety/-ies., either with 
or without aromatic rings, it can be non-polymeric or polymeric, be neutral, anionic, cationic or 
zwitterionic (OECD, 2021). 
 
==PAGE:11==

PFAS is grouped into two main groups non-polymeric and polymeric molecules. Non-polymeric PFAS 
is further split into two categories perfluoroalkyl and polyfluoroalkyl substances. Perfluoroalkyl 
comprises a hydrophobic carbon chain that is fully fluorinated, and a polar functional group, see 
figure 2 (OECD, 2021; Panieri et al., 2022). While non-polymeric polyfluoroalkyl compounds have at 
least one (but not all) carbon atoms fully fluorinated.
 
Figure 2: General structure of non-polymeric, perfluorinated PFAS on the left and polyfluorinated on the right 
(Panieri et al., 2022). 
 
The polymeric PFAS is a large group of compounds and include: 
a) Fluoropolymers, compounds in which fluoride atoms replace most, if not all, of the hydrogen 
atoms in the carbon chain (e.g., PFTE also known as Teflon). 
b) Side-chain fluorinated polymers, compounds with per-/polyfluoroalkylic side chains and non-
fluorinated carbon chains of varying content (e.g., fluorinated acrylate polymers). 
c) Perfluoropolyethers, compounds with atoms of fluoride and oxygen directly bonded to the 
carbon chain in the major backbone (e.g., PFPE also known as Krytox, a lubricant). 
Figure 3: Examples of polymeric PFAS: the (CF2-CF2)n moiety can is repeated up to thousand times, the (CF[CF3]-
CF2-O)n moiety is repeated 10-60 times (Panieri et al., 2022). 
 
The polymeric PFAS are outside the scope of this study, and not discussed further.

The functional group and the number of carbons on the alkyl chain provide the nomenclature for 
each distinct PFAS congener. 
 
PFAS are often further divided into sub-groups according to chain length, due to diverse actions in 
environment and organisms and an increased predisposition for bioaccumulation. However, the cut is 
made at different points. OECD distinguish between different PFAS: perfluoroalkyl carboxylic acids (PFCA) 
with ≥ 7 as fluorinated moieties is characterized as “long-chained”, and for perfluoroalkyl sulfonic acids 
(PFSA) it is set at ≥ 6 fluorinated moieties, and the precursors follows what they are precursors to. This 
is not strictly followed in the scientific community, where it is study dependent, e.g., Campos Pereira et 
al., (2018) referees to “long-chained” having >9 perfluorinated carbons, and Fabregat-Palau et al., (2021) 
split it up in “short-chained”, ≤5 perfluorinated carbons, “mid-chained” 6-9 perfluorinated carbons, and 
“long-chained" >9 perfluorinated carbons. In this study the OECD guidelines is followed. 
 
2.2 Physiochemical and biological properties 
PFAS molecules have very high thermal and chemical stability due to the exceptional strength of the 
C-F bond caused by the strong electronegativity and small atomic size of the fluorine atom, thereby 
enhancing the properties of the molecules (e.g., increased surface activity at extremely low 
concentrations, stronger acidity, stability, and/or increased oil and water repellent properties). The 
physicochemical properties like the presence of hydrophobic and hydrophilic properties as well as 
==PAGE:13==

variations in the carbon chain length and chemical composition, generates an enormous range of 
different uses (Panieri et al., 2022; Z. Wang et al., 2017). As a result, PFAS have been effectively used 
in a wide range of consumer/industrial products spanning over more than 100 applications. Some 
of the most popular uses are pesticide formulation, firefighting foams, textile coating, cosmetics, 
medical products, food processing and packaging, surfactant agents, paints, building and 
construction, paper and packaging, and electronics (Arvaniti & Stasinakis, 2015; Cousins et al., 2016; Lau 
et al., 2007; Panieri et al., 2022; Prevedouros et al., 2006; Q. Wang et al., 2018). 
 
A large number of the frequently encountered PFAS have an anionic (negatively charged) functional 
group (e.g., PFAA as: PFCA, PFSA, FTCA, and FTSA groupings), due to the incredibly low acid 
dissociation constant (pKa) values of PFCAs (<1.6) and PFSAs (<0.3), in ambient conditions (normal 
pH of 4–9) (Goss, 2008; Vierke et al., 2013). Though, cationic (positively charged) and zwitterionic 
(bearing both charges) PFAS have also been found at contaminated sites (Barzen-Hanson, Roberts, 
et al., 2017a).

frequent detection/presence in high concentrations or both in Danish soils.

Regulation of PFAS began in the late 1990’s due to findings of PFOS in the blood plasma in the general 
population and testing on animals showed adverse effects. This has led to establishment of quality 
criteria for PFAS compounds and recommendations of analysis packages for studies and monitoring 
of PFAS compounds in environmental samples. The quality criteria and analysis packages are 
continuously revised in line with new knowledge (VMR, 2022). 
Several PFAS compounds are considered to be endocrine disruptors, at the same time they are 
suspected of being reproductively toxic and carcinogenic (Schrenk et al., 2020). The health effects 
depend on exposure route, the PFAS composition, duration and levels of exposure and vulnerability 
of the exposed individuals (age, health, gender, etc.). 
Epidemical studies indicate that exposure to PFAS compounds can cause immunosuppression (NTP, 
2016), elevated blood cholesterol, influence of the thyroid gland, liver and possibly kidney and 
testicular cancer, lower birth weight and delayed development of mammary gland in newborns 
(Fenton et al., 2021; Schrenk et al., 2020).

5 Sorption of PFAS 
 
Sorption to soils and sediments is one phase partitioning behavior that controls the behavior and fate of 
a chemical in the environment. Many variables affect the sorption-desorption processes, such as the 
physical and chemical characteristics of the chemical, the characteristics of the solid and solution phases 
that the chemical interacts with, and the time the chemical and sorbent phase interact. 
 
Sorption occurs through various types of interactions with the solid phase in soils and sediments. Soil 
contains both positive and negative charges, as permanent and variable charge surfaces. Permanent 
charges are isomorphic substituted metal ions in the mesh structure of silicate clay minerals. Variable 
charges in soils are a result of dissociation of functional groups, which are influenced by the pH of the 
solution, the ionic strength, and interactions with anions and cations (Bolan et al., 1999). 
 
Sorption of PFAS to soil/sediment depends on the tail’s length and the heads functional group, as well as 
the soil properties, such as pH, exchangeable cation concentration, organic carbon (OC) and grain size 
(sand, silt and clay fractions). Due to PFAS dual functionality, derived from its hydrophobic tail and 
hydrophilic head, it may interact with both the hydrophilic and hydrophobic sites of clay minerals as well 
as organic matter. Luft et al., (2022) found similar binding affinities for PFAS adsorption to clay and OC 
(using model humic compound) following the same trend PFOS>PFOA>PFBA. Short-chain PFAS (PFBA) 
was more likely to be discovered separately, while larger and less ionic molecules aggregated strongly 
inside the humic structure, therefore the greater adsorption of long-chained PFAS (Luft et al., 2022). 
 
The distribution of PFAS in the aqueous phase is determined by PFAS hydrophobic and oleophobic 
properties. The water solubility and sorption affinity of PFAS in soil particles determine their 
bioavailability. Short chain PFAS accumulate more in plants than long chain PFAS, most likely as a 
result of their lower molecular size and greater water solubility, which allow them to more easily 
pass through the cell walls of roots (Adu et al., 2023). 
 
The main processes of PFAS sorption are electrostatic and hydrophobic interactions, while hydrogen 
bonding and van der Waals interactions probably are minor (Du et al., 2014). Clay and OC content, 
indicates possible binding sites for electrostatic interaction but does not indicate the surface charge 
of the soil. 
==PAGE:20==

The charged functional head are responsible for the electrostatic interactions, while the hydrophobic 
interactions are controlled by the tail and the soil organic matter (SOM) (Mei et al., 2021) figure 6.  
 
Figure 6: Overview figure over possible sorption behaviors of PFAS in the soil environments (an example of 
anionic PFAA) (Mei et al., 2021). 
 
At environmental pH values many PFAS usually exist as anionic species due to their low 𝑝𝐾𝑎 values, 
therefore an electrostatic attraction can form between the negatively charged functional head of the 
PFAS and the positively charged surfaces of the adsorbents. Under environmentally relevant 
conditions the soil is negatively charged, due to dissociated carboxylic and phenolic acid groups 
present (Y. Li et al., 2018a), therefore divalent/multivalent cations serve as bridge between the 
negatively charged functional head of perfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) and the negatively charged 
surfaces of sorbents (Du et al., 2014; Higgins & Luthy, 2006b). 
 
The underlying geochemical variety of natural soil elements results in varied PFAS adsorption by soil. 
A number of soil characteristics, such as specific surface area, the amount and character of organic 
carbon, the amount of metal oxides (Fe and Al), the mineralogy of the clay, and the shape of the 
intraparticle pore, can affect the extent of PFAS adsorption. For instance, the hydrophobic 
fluorinated carbon tail can interact with the soil's organic carbon fraction, and the polar head group 
can electrostatically interact with charged clay or metal-oxide fractions to adsorb PFAS (Higgins & 
Luthy, 2006b). 
==PAGE:21==
15 
 
Due to PFAS amphipathic nature it might result in the formation of micelles or hemimicelles, when 
dissolved in water. Since PFAS molecules have a hydrophilic polar head and a hydrophobic carbon 
tail, as previously indicated, they may aggregate in clusters and bind to particle surfaces as 
admicelles or hemimicelles, with the hydrophobic tail pointing toward the center, to avoid coming 
into contact with water. The formation of micelles happens when the concentration exceeds the 
critical micelle concentration (CMC). The CMC concentrations are well over the concentrations used 
in this study and typical Danish ground water concentrations (CMC values for PFOS and PFOA are 
about 4573 and 15696 mg/L (Yu et al., 2009)), the highest concentration in this study is 0.3 mg/L. 
 
Y. Li et al., (2018b) found that the sorption behavior of PFAS may not be adequately described by pH, 
OC, or clay alone by conducting a review of published data. Some of the factors that affect PFAS 
sorption is provided below.

5.2 
Electrostatic sorption 
 
The amount of clay in the soil, is a significant physicochemical factor that can influence the sorption 
of PFAS, 𝐾𝑑 increased with increased clay content, due to the increased electrostatic interactions 
and that clay governs PFAS sorption especially in low OC soils (Fabregat-Palau et al., 2021; Li et al., 
2018). Although the amount of clay (along with OC content) in the soil does not directly correlate with 
its surface charge, it does indicate possible binding sites for electrostatic interaction (Y. Li et al., 2018b). 
 
Investigating different size fraction of contaminated soil, showed that the fine sand fraction (0.063 
to 0.5 mm) contains the greatest amount of PFAS (approximately 60%), while the silt and clay 
fraction (< 0.063 mm) accounted for approximately 25% (Hubert et al., 2023). This emphasize how 
crucial it is to understand the sorption mechanisms at work for the various size fractions as these 
will have an impact on the overall sorption.

5.3 pH effect 
 
The presence of PFAS in anionic, cationic, or zwitterionic states means that electrostatic interactions 
that are sensitive to the pH of the surrounding environment are anticipated to be significant (Higgins 
& Luthy, 2006b). 
 
PFCA and PFSA are weakly acidic substances, therefore their sorption is reduced as the pH rises due 
to an increase in the number of anionic molecules. Changes in pH can have an impact on the 
sorbent's surface qualities in addition to its charge characteristics. The average net surface charge 
on mineral particles decreases with increasing pH (R. L. Johnson et al., 2007). 
Solution parameters such as pH or ionic strength is expected to affect sorption behavior of ionizable 
substances. The hydrophilicity of the sulfonate or carboxylate head is expected to electrostatically 
influence the sorption (Du et al., 2014; Higgins & Luthy, 2006b). 
 
Adsorption can take two forms: nonspecific adsorption, which happens when the positive and 
negative charges balance, and specific adsorption, which involves the creation of chemical bonds, 
the anions and ions on the soil surface create a chemical connection (Bolan et al., 1999). According 
to Bolan et al., (1999), specific sorption happens across a broad range of soil pH values, whereas 
nonspecific sorption is typically associated with significant sorption at low pH and decreasing 
sorption with increasing pH. 
 
Due to increasing electrostatic repulsion, the sorption of the anionic PFOS is reduced as the pH 
increases and the average surface charge on goethite and kaolinite became less positive (R. L. Johnson 
et al., 2007). When results for PFOS are compiled from several datasets, an overall decrease in 𝐾𝑑 with 
increasing pH is observed (figure 8), although the relationship is not significant (𝑅2=0.06) (Y. Li et al., 2018b).

The variability observed in the pH data can be explained by the intricate nature of PFAS sorption 
behaviors, which can be influenced by multiple parameters at different pH levels. These effects may 
include the potential of organic matter or the release of ions in solution (Higgins & Luthy, 2006b; Oliver 
et al., 2019). 
5.4 Cation bridging 
 
In general, the sorption of ionizable, anionic, organic contaminants is promoted by a decrease in pH 
and by an increase in solution cation concentration. This has been supported for PFAS by studies 
such as, (Chen et al., 2009; Higgins & Luthy, 2006a; C. Zhang et al., 2013) regarding the influence of pH 
and Ca2+, and Wang et al., 2015 regarding the enhanced sorption effects of Mg2+, Fe3+, and Al3+. 
However, Li et al., 2018 conducted a review on sorption data and found no evidence of a significant 
association with Ca2+ for both PFOS and PFOA (𝑅2 = 0.11, n = 32 and 𝑅2 = 0.14, n=7, respectively) or 
with 𝑁𝑎+ (𝑅2 = 0.05, n = 23 and 𝑅2 = 0.08, n = 6, respectively). 
 
Monovalent cations like Na+ and K+ have not been shown to have major impact on sorption (Higgins 
& Luthy, 2006a; Li et al., 2018; Nguyen et al., 2020; F. Wang et al., 2015). 
==PAGE:25==
19 
 
However, there is a challenge in conducting experiments to evaluate the effect of ionic strength as 
changes in ionic strength also can alter pH, making it challenging to determine the specific 
parameters effects on sorption (Higgins & Luthy, 2006a). 
 
A few theories have been proposed to explain the increased PFAS sorption with cations: 
1. The neutralization of the negative charge on the sediment surfaces, which leads to a 
decrease in electrostatic repulsion. 
2. The "salting out effect," which causes the water molecule to become more structured with 
dissolved ions and thereby reduces the cavities. 
3. Ligand bridging: The divalent cations could create a bridge between the soil or sediment 
particles and the PFAS anionic functional group (You et al., 2010). 
4. Surface complexation reactions: Involving hydrous oxides on iron and aluminum oxides, 
which then form hydrogen bonds with either the oxygen atom of the functional group or the 
F atoms in the perfluorocarbon chain (in which case, sorption increases with chain length) 
(Feng et al., 2017). 
 
The primary mechanisms for adsorption onto iron and aluminum oxides have been reported to 
include surface complexation and ligand exchange (the O atom is replaced by, for example, the 
sulfate ion in the functional group) (F. Wang & Shih, 2011).

8 Discussion 
 
8.1 Variation of 𝐾𝑑 
 
When the sorption is adequate the 𝐾𝑑 values derived by the Mayer et al.-method, correlates with 
the Freundlich isotherm at similar low concentration. 
Overall, the 𝐾𝑑 values across soils are in good agreement with those determined in the literature, 
both in terms of absolute values and range (figure 27) (Høisæter & Breedveld, 2022; Hubert et al., 2023; 
Nguyen et al., 2020). 
 
Figure 27: This studies results (OECD and Mayer) across soils in comparison with results from (Høisæter & 
Breedveld, 2022; Hubert et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2020) 
 
𝐾𝑑 max for PFHxS is slightly out of range (Mayer results), this could be due to the low concentration 
used in this setup. 
8.2 Comparison of approaches 
 
The Mayer et al.-method is a good approach for determination of 𝐾𝑑 values, when the appropriate 
soil:water ratio is not defined. The soil amount is the parameter changing here, and as such the 
appropriate soil:water ratio will be found. But as the concentration is fixed, the sorption dependency 
of concentration, cannot be assess here. 
For the OECD-method the appropriate soil:water ratio has to be known, to achieve adequate 
sorption. As here the sorption concentration dependency is assessed. 
Therefore the 2 methods complement each other in the determination of the sorption coefficient 
and its concentration dependency. 
==PAGE:53==
47 
 
8.3 Uncertainties 
 
Measures were taken during the laboratory procedure to guarantee the accuracy of the findings. 
Measurement was performed in triplicate to ensure that outlier can be found. Human factor is also 
a source of uncertainty, there was a lot of pipetting, therefore a calibrated pipette was used to 
ensure minimal variance in the amount pipetted, and the vial was weighted every step during the 
experiment setup to minimize errors. 
Even though the LC-QQQ is reliable, one has to predefine the detection accuracy. Mayer et al.-
method was run with low concentrations, so the detection had to be high. However, the OECD-
method was run with different concentrations, but the setting was not adjusted accordingly. That 
resulted in many outliers around 10%, that was discarded (outlier is when one of the triplicate 
measurements differ more than 25% from the other two). 
Rotating time was 72 hours, F. Li et al., (2019) found that 48 hours is sufficient to reach equilibrium 
for a soil with 3.3% OC, but there is no data assessing if the time needed to reach equilibrium differ 
for different OC content. 
The inadequate sorption especially for sand Industrivej, resulted in 𝐾𝑑 with very poor coefficient of 
determination (𝑅2). This means that there is a big uncertainty on the value. 
This study consists of only 6 soils (2 sand, 2 topsoil and 2 clayey till), and as seen from the soil 
characterization, that even if it is the same soil class, it is very different from each other (different 
OC, clay, cation content). Therefore, no 𝐾𝑑 value for sand, topsoil or clayey till could be determined.

9 Conclusion 
 
Sorption of PFAS to soil/sediment depends on the tail’s length and the heads functional group, and 
the soil properties, such as pH, exchangeable cation concentration, organic carbon (OC) and grain 
size (sand, silt and clay fractions). 
The sorption behavior of PFAS is not adequately described by pH, OC, or clay alone. Multiple factors 
affect the sorption of PFAS, and further studies is needed to find out how the different soil properties 
contribute to the sorption of PFAS. 
PFAS sorption to OC is not a reliable parameter to use exclusively for transport predictions. 
Therefore, the assumptions made by VMR (2022) in this regard and the unifying of the sorption 
coefficient across soil is insufficient to estimate PFAS mobility. A 𝐾𝑑 value cannot be determined for 
each PFAS, as it also depends on the soil properties. 
Distribution coefficients are important to predict the fate and movement of PFAS in the environment 
but shouldn't be the only consideration. Accurately predicting the fate and distribution of PFAS 
requires detailed site-specific knowledge. Any prediction of the environmental fate and transport of 
PFAS should incorporate the following: 1) a geohydrological model that describes the in-situ water 
phase; 2) pH, soil charge data, OC, and protein content measurements; and 3) field-derived 
distribution coefficients from each soil type contained in the soil profile.

B Appendix: Grain size distribution 
 
Laser diffractometer is used to determine the amount of clay present in the corresponding soil 
samples. 
The soil sample particles must have a diameter of less than 0.063 mm in a 10 mL suspension in order 
to be used with the laser diffractometer. 
Approximately 100 g soil was weighed into a 250 mL plastic flask and 100 mL of tap water was added. 
The flasks were rotated for 12 hours to disintegrate aggregated particles. Once the samples are 
completely suspended, they were run through a 0.063 mm (63 µm) sieve placed on top of a plastic 
funnel into a one-liter glass bottle. Between samples, the sieve was properly cleaned. Tap water was 
added once again to the 250 mL flask to help dislodge the sediment remnants at the bottom, this 
was repeated until all the soil was transferred. After the majority of the silt and clay passed through 
the filter, the remnants (particles larger than 0.063 mm) were placed in a pre-weight empty beaker 
and dried for 12 hours at 105°C in a heating cabinet and weighted once cooled down, to get the 
total mass of the coarse fraction. The remaining suspension of clay and silt is set aside, while sieving 
the coarse fraction. 
Nine sieves with the mesh sizes 2.0-1.0-0.6-0.355-0.250-0.180-0.125-0.090 and 0.063 mm were 
employed to characterize the grain sizes in the coarse fraction. The sieves were weighed and placed 
in a sieving machine for 15 minutes at amplitude 1.5. Each sieve was measured again and the 
fraction below 0.063 mm was added to the one-liter bottle with the clay and silt fractions. 
Table B1 show the ISO-standard grain size classification (OECD, 2017). To distinguish the different 
soils, the cumulative weight % of the grain size distribution are plotted against the particle size on a 
logarithmic scale.

C Procedure with different soil/water ratios (Mayer et al.) 
1. Label and pre-weigh 216 pieces 66 mL vials with caps. 
2. Prepare a stock solution of around 10 mg PFAS/L. 
a. 0.5 mL PFAS of each of the 7 PFAS is added a 50 mL flask with tap water (46.5 mL 
tap water and 3.5 mL PFAS (7*0.5 mL) (stock solution A of 10 mg PFAS/L) 
b. 0.5 mL of stock solution A is added a 50 mL flask with tap water (stock solution B of 
100 µg/L) 
c. 3 mL of stock solution B is added a 1 L flask and 15 mL is added a 5 L flask both with 
tap water (PFAS solution of 0.3 µg/L) 
d. The stock solution is stored in a refrigerator (5°C). 
3. 3 vials (vials without soil added) are filled with PFAS solution to test the start concentration, 
one from the 1 L flask and the other 2 from the 5 L flask to be sure the concentrations are 
the same. 
a. the vials are weighted. 
b. the vials will be treated as the next X vials. 
4. 90 g of each soil is weighed in triplicate (30 g in each vail), here 30 mL of tap water is added 
to have a starting point (if the soil already contains PFAS). 
5. 387 g dry soil of (mull from 2 locations, sand from 2 locations and 2 moraines from 1 
location different depth) and 1 moraine from another location were weighted for the 
different batch experiment (see Table) 
a. 1.5, 3, 4.5, 6, 9, 15, 21, 30 and 39 g of dry soil is added to the respective batch vials 
(see Table), and the vial is weighted afterwards. 
6. 30 mL PFAS solution is added to the rest of the vials (189 vails). Make sure the soil samples 
are saturated. To get the clay lumps suspended a vortex mixer can be used. 
7. All the filled vials are weighted. 
8. The batch vials are rotated for at least 3 days (in a 10°C room) 
9. The vials will always be stored in a 10°C room, when not worked on, since equilibrium 
might be temperature dependent. 
10. The batch vials are removed from rotation and left in the 10°C room for the clay particles to 
settle down. The chosen vails cannot be centrifuged.

D Procedure with different concentrations (OECD) 
1. Label and pre-weigh 228 pieces 66 mL vials with caps. 
2. Prepare a stock solution of around 10 mg PFAS/L (Lab. stock solution 1 mg/mL). 
a. 0.5 mL PFAS of each of the 6 PFAS is added a 50 mL flask with tap water (47 mL tap 
water and 3 mL PFAS (6*0.5 mL) (stock solution A of 10 mg PFAS/L)(Without PFOS) 
b. 0.5 mL of the stock solution A is added a 50 mL flask with tap water (stock solution 
B of 100 µg PFAS/L) 
c. 0.5 mL PFOS is added a 50 mL with tap water (49.5 mL tap water and 0.5 mL PFOS 
(PFOS stock solution A of 10 mg PFOS/L). 
d. 0.5 mL of PFOS stock solution A is added a 50 mL flask with tap water (PFOS stock 
solution B of 100 µg PFOS/L) 
e. The stock solutions are stored in a refrigerator (5°C). 
3. 495 g dry soil (1:1 soil-water ratio) + 75 g dry soil (0.15:1 soil-water ratio) (topsoil from 2 
locations, sand from 2 locations and 2 moraines from 1 location different depth) were 
weighted for the different batch experiment (see Table) 
a. 33 g of dry soil is added to the respective batch vials (see Table), and the vial is 
weighted afterwards. 
b. 5 g of dry soil is added to the respective batch vials (see Table), and the vial is 
weighted afterwards. 
4. Tap water is added to all the 228 vials so the final water volume is 30 mL. In the vials with 
soils this is done in order to saturate the soil samples. To get the clay lumps suspended a 
vortex mixer can be used. (Think just vortex mix is enough to saturate the soil). 
5. The first 12 vials (vials without soil added) are addressed as followed: 
a. Vails 1-3 are added 100 µL PFAS stock solution B. 
b. Vails 4-6 are added 1000 µL of PFAS stock solution B. 
c. Vials 7-9 are added 100 µL of PFAS stock solution A. 
d. Vails 10-12 are added 1000 µL of PFAS stock solution A. 
e. the vials are weighted. 
f. the vials are all set aside for later LC-QQQ measurements, to test if any loss (i.e., 
precipitation occurs). 
==PAGE:77==
71 
 
6. Vails 13-24 (vials without soil added) are also filled with stock solution in the same manner 
as in step 5 (13-15 100 µL PFAS stock solution B, 16-18 1000 µL PFAS stock solution B, 19-21 
100 µL PFAS stock solution A and 22-24 1000 µL PFAS stock solution A) 
a. the vials are weighted. 
b. these vials will be treated as the next vials in order to test the start concentrations 
stock solution. 
7. Vails 25-114 (Vails with 33 g soil) 100 µL and 1000 µL of the PFAS stock solutions B and A is 
added to the respective batch vials containing soil. The vials are closed after the application 
of stock solution and weighted. 
8. Vails 115-126 (vials without soil added) are also filled with stock solution in the same 
manner as in step 5 and 6 (115-117 100 µL PFAS stock solution B and 100 µL PFOS stock 
solution, 118-120 1000 µL PFAS stock solution B and 1000 µL PFOS stock solution B, 121-
123 100 µL PFAS stock solution A and 100 µL PFOS stock solution A and 124-126 1000 µL 
PFAS stock solution A and 1000 µL PFOS stock solution A) 
a. the vials are weighted. 
b. the vials are all set aside for later LC-QQQ measurements, to test if any loss (i.e., 
precipitation occurs). 
9. Vails 127-138 (vails without soil added) are filled like in step 8 (127-129 100 µL PFAS stock 
solution B and 100 µL PFOS stock solution, 130-132 1000 µL PFAS stock solution B and 1000 
µL PFOS stock solution B, 133-135 100 µL PFAS stock solution A and 100 µL PFOS stock 
solution A and 136-138 1000 µL PFAS stock solution A and 1000 µL PFOS stock solution A) 
a. the vials are weighted. 
b. these vials will be treated as the next vials in order to test the start concentrations 
stock solution. 
10. All the filled vials are weighted, and water is added to have 33 ml solution in all vails. 
11. The batch vials are rotated for at least 3 days (in a 10°C room). 
12. The vials will always be stored in a 10°C room, when not worked on, since equilibrium 
might be temperature dependent. 
13. The batch vials are removed from rotation and left in the 10°C room for the clay particles to 
settle down. The chosen vails cannot be centrifuged.

14. 1 mL of the water phase is transferred to LC-QQQ analyses vails. 
15. The water phase is analyzed for PFAS on the LC-QQQ.
