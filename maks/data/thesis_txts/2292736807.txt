ABSTRACT  
Natural fibers offer the benefits of a smaller environmental footprint, sustainability and 
lower costs when compared to traditional synthetic fibers. However, natural fibers 
currently suffer from low tensile strength, a deterrent for expanding use in structural 
applications for composites. Understanding their behavior and potential is therefore of 
great interest.  
The aim of this work is to further knowledge of the effect of defects on the mechanical 
properties of single fibers. Polarized light microscopy is used to visualize defect areas 
present in the cell wall. Image analysis tools process the digital images and deliver the 
relative defect area, defined as the area of defects relative to the total fiber area. Initial work 
has improved the protocol to quantify defects by developing a process to more accurately 
determine defect area. 
The latter work investigated two treatment methods, which were directed at reducing hemp 
fiber defects and improving tensile properties to allow their successful use as replacements 
of synthetic fibers to reinforce composite materials. First, physical treatments placed single 
fibers under tension in order to realign the cell wall microstructure. This method reduced 
defects and increase fiber stiffness. One treatment saw a 68% increase in stiffness. 
Furthermore, these treatments affect the linearity of deformation. There are three stress-
strain behaviors commonly seen in natural fibers with unique characteristics. This work 
objectively assigns a curve to one of the three behaviors.  
The second treatment was a three step chemical treatment to separate single fibers from 
the surrounding plant material as an alternative to damaging mechanical processes. The 
steps extracted pectin, lignin and then hemicellulose from cell wall. Each step reduced the 
stiffness and ultimate stress. Most notably during lignin removal, the stiffness dropped from 
21GPa to 12GPa. The final step, to remove hemicellulose caused an increase in defect 
content from 2.1% to 3.5%.

1. INTRODUCTION 
1.1 NATURAL FIBERS 
Natural fibers have long been used in the textile industry, providing a base of knowledge on 
their behavior. However, the industrial focus was not on the mechanical properties that 
natural fibers can provide [1]. Utilizing natural fibers as a structural component deserves 
further consideration and development. 
Natural fibers are generally weaker than their synthetic fiber counterparts are, but they 
have similar specific mechanical properties to glass fiber. Therefore, they are primarily 
considered to replace glass fiber. Downsides to natural fiber include inconsistent properties 
from one plant to another and from harvest to harvest. Additionally, natural fibers are very 
hydrophilic resulting in dimensional instability and poor interfacial bonding [1].  
Glass fiber and carbon fiber composites provide good strength properties, but are costly 
both environmentally and financially. Perhaps the greatest benefit of natural fibers is their 
minimal environmental impact. 
1.1.1 GREEN INITIATIVE 
Many countries worldwide are focused on green initiatives such as recycling, renewability, 
etc. This includes the energy sources as well as the materials used to harness the energy. 
Wind turbines have the potential to provide clean energy and to utilize renewable materials. 
The composites used in the blades are commonly glass or carbon fiber reinforced, but 
natural fiber reinforced composites are a viable alternative [2].  
- 
Life Cycle Analysis of glass fiber composites and natural fiber composites often 
conclude that the latter are less environmentally damaging than the former [3].  
- 
Manufacturing glass fiber is very energy intensive, partially due to the high processing 
temperatures. There is some energy consumption during the processing of natural 
fibers, but most of the energy consumed by natural fibers comes directly from the sun. 
- 
Glass fiber production emits greenhouse gases such as carbon dioxide, NOx and Sox. 
Plants consume carbon dioxide, but nitrates and fertilizers are commonly used during 
plant growth. 
- 
Replacing glass fiber with natural fiber often results in weight savings of the final 
composite. The specific mechanical properties of the two are similar. To provide the 
same stiffness, less natural fiber weight is necessary.  
- 
At the end of life, natural fibers can be incinerated and provide energy and carbon 
credits.  
1.1.2 APPLICATION 
Many natural fiber reinforced composites used in the industry today are for non-structural 
support e.g. in the car industry as a component in door panels, instrument panels, parcel 
shelves and headliners [4]. Natural fibers have a lower density than glass fibers; therefore, 
there are often weight savings associated with combining natural fibers with composites.  
==PAGE:8==

Wind turbine blades, commonly made of glass composites, are one of the few components 
of a wind turbine that are not recyclable. The stiffness of a blade controls the deformation 
of the structure. The most important parameters for the blades are a very stiff material with 
low density and resistance to degradation. Wood is a lightweight material, but has low 
stiffness. Glass fiber has higher stiffness and is more dense. Natural fibers have a nice 
combination of moderate stiffness and low density [2]. These properties along with their 
environmental benefits make a strong argument for using natural fiber reinforced 
composites in wind turbine blades.  
1.1.3 DEFECT DEFINITION 
Natural fibers have a heterogeneous structure that lends to imperfections in the 
microstructure. The secondary cell wall imperfections are presented in literature with a 
variety of names such as defects, dislocations, kink bands, nodes, slip planes, and micro-
compressive defects [5], [6]. 
The origin of defects has been extensively studied. Nyholm [5] suggests defects are caused 
by the weight of the plant, wind, mechanical or chemical impacts.  
Their presence has been observed in untreated fibers, which indicates their introduction 
occurs during growth. Thygesen and Asgharipour [7] isolated hemp fibers from plants 
grown in three conditions: wind free, wind and drought. All three conditions showed 
defects, though there were more defects on the wind and drought. Compression causes 
buckling in the S1 layer that may damage the S2 layer [5].  
Another source of defects is the mechanical processing to isolate the fibers from the plant 
stem. After harvesting the plant, the stalks undergo retting, the process in which the tissue 
surrounding the fibers is degraded. This is followed by mechanical extraction to further 
separate the fibers [8]. This is done in a number of steps which involve breaking the stem 
and pulling the fibers through rollers. Hanninen [9] measured the defect content of raw flax, 
after retting, after two mechanical processes and after three mechanical processes. There 
was an increase in defect content after the mechanical processing steps.

One source of the poor strength is defects, or imperfections on the fiber wall that introduce 
localized weak points.  Breaks in the fiber tend to occur at defects, which was observed via 
in-situ microscopy by Baley [10]. It was also observed that the maximum strength decreases 
with increasing damage [11].  
These defect regions are also detrimental on the composite scale. Defects are more 
chemically reactive and may bond poorly with matrix material [12]. Matrix adhesion is very 
important characteristic for the composite properties. Defects can initiate debonding 
between the fiber and matrix as well as initiate the formation of cracks in the matrix 
material [10].  
1.3 OBJECTIVE 
The implementation of natural fibers is as a reinforcement material in composites. The 
scope of this study only entails fiber properties because a full, working knowledge on the 
fiber level is necessary to understand how the composite will behave. 
The objective of this thesis is to investigate defect content and its effect on the fiber 
mechanical properties. It is believed that defects have negative effects on the fiber 
properties as well as the composite properties. Further understanding of the relationship 
between these properties would be beneficial to the design and application of natural fiber 
reinforced composites. 
The investigation of defect content starts with the protocol to determine defect content 
using polarized light microscopy and image analysis set by Mortensen and Madsen [13]. 
Additional consideration is given to the important effect of the microscope intensity.  The 
brightness of the light passing through the fiber affects the measured defect area, such that 
at higher brightness levels there is a larger measured defect area [14]. A procedure is 
designed to negate that effect. The second addition to the protocol is an uncertainty analysis 
of the method. 
Two treatments are analyzed in an attempt to minimize the defect content of a single fiber. 
The first, a physical treatment is intended to reduce the quantity or severity of defects. 
Image analysis and tensile testing will be performed. The second a series of chemical 
treatments to extract the cortical tissue surrounding the fibers. This method should avoid 
the introduction of defects because it is less mechanically damaging than industrial 
processing methods.

1.4 LIMITATIONS 
This study is significantly limited by time. There are numerous avenues of investigation that 
surround the properties of natural fibers, but only a select few were chosen. Time also 
limited the types of  tested fibers, thereby excluding several flax fibers. 
The accuracy of the defect content measurements is limited by the ability of the microscope 
and the operator. It limits the level of detail observable on a fiber.  
The defects are on the order of micrometers, so small increases or decreases are difficult to 
perceive. If there are not very many defects, then perhaps these changes are negligible or 
within the uncertainty of the measurement.

Optical microscopy and scanning electron microscopy can show large defects that affect the

The number of defects on a fiber varies as well as the area of these defect regions. The

This way the effect of defects on other fiber properties can be scientifically determined. 
Defect content is a way to quantify the fiber quality.

The defect content (𝐷) is defined as the ratio of the area of defects in the visible fiber 
segment (𝐴𝑑) to the fiber segment area (𝐴𝑓). The value represents the percentage of the 
fiber area covered with defects [17], [18]. The defect content is 0 for a fiber free of defects 
and equal to 1 for a fiber completely full of defects.

The accurate quantification of defects requires two steps. First, the quality imaging of an 
isolated fiber, projected in two dimensions as a rectangle and second, a robust and reliable 
image analysis tool to measure area.

2.3.1 OPTICAL MICROSCOPY  
The fiber area can be determined using dark field microscopy. The dark field image shows 
scattered light, rather than the transmitted light visible with light field microscopy. The area 
outside the fiber does not reflect any light and thus appears black. At low brightness levels, 
only the fiber edges are illuminated as in Figure 2.5. An image analysis code can be applied 
to look for the fiber’s edges and calculate the area.  
 
Figure 2.5 Dark field image to determine fiber area 
The defect area can be determined using bright field settings and polarized light. Polarized 
light is commonly used in the textile industry to identify fibers and examine their 
macrostructure and microstructure. It can also be used as a method to determine 
birefringence or observe the damaged areas of fibers [19].  
A polarizing light microscope has two polaroids: a polarizer and analyzer. Each is essentially 
a filter and reduces the direction that a light ray can travel. Figure 2.6 is an illustration of 
the effect of one polarizer on light, called plane polarization. At O the light can travel in all 
three directions; this is illustrated as the shape of a canoe from O to 𝑃. The polarizer at O’ is 
oriented vertically from P to P’ such that only light parallel to PP’ can pass. Now the light 
only vibrates in the vertical direction and in the direction of motion (to the right). An 
analyzer placed perpendicular to the polarizer and to the direction of motion would then 
limit the light vibration to a single direction. 
 
Figure 2.6 A polarizer at plane 11 filters the light coming from O such that the polarized light can only vibrate 
parallel to PP’ [20] 
When the polariods are arranged perpendicular to one another, they are denoted crossed 
polars [19], [18]. Each eliminates a possible direction of light vibration such that after 
passing through one polaroid the light is vibrating in only two dimensions. 
A birefringent material is optically anisotropic and therefore, has more than one index of 
refraction. Double refraction means that a ray of light passing through the material creates 
two light rays. If the light is polarized, then one refracted light ray is visible and the other is 
filtered [20].  
==PAGE:14==

The crystalline cellulose in the cell wall of natural fibers is birefringent. It refracts a light ray 
into two perpendicular components. Therefore, the cellulose is only visible when one of 
these components is parallel to one of the polars. There are four orientations where this 
occurs and the vibration direction of the cellulose, which is along the longitudinal direction 
of the fiber, is parallel to the direction of one of the polars.  
 
Recall that the microfibril angle of defect regions are greater than the microfibril angle of 
the bulk cell wall. At an offset of 45°, neither of the components passes through the polars 
thereby creating a dark image; this orientation is called an extinction position [18]. In an 
extinction position, the regularly aligned microfibrils are dark, but the defect region refracts 
light at a different angle, which can pass through the polars. The longitudinal direction of 
the fiber is parallel to one of the polars. Rotation 45° from the extinction positions produces 
the opposite: the regularly aligned microfibrils are bright white like in the corner images 
where the fiber area is completely white. The misaligned microfibrils are do not reflect any 
light, but the corner images are so bright that the dark defects are not visible. These 
orientations are shown in Figure 2.7. The polarizer is placed at 90°/270° and the analyzer 
is oriented at 0°/180°.

2.3.2 IMAGE ANALYSIS 
There are three common image analysis techniques to determine the area of the defects or 
the fiber. A greyscale image contains pixels of values between 0 and 255. The purpose is to 
convert this into a black and white image. The first two image analysis techniques are so-
called thresholding techniques, which perform an operation to divide the greyscale image 
into a foreground (1) and a background (0). The third image analysis technique is the 
seeded region growing method in which a seed point is chosen and then its neighbors are 
classified as similar or dissimilar in greyscale intensity.  
The first thresholding technique, the coefficient of variance method uses a threshold value 
to divide the greyscale pixels into the foreground or the background. It checks each possible 
threshold value and finds the one that yields the most stable foreground [17].  
The second thresholding technique is called Otsu’s method. It analyzes the histogram of an 
image and aims to select a threshold value that minimizes the within-group variance. There 
can be more than one threshold value if the histogram is not bi-modal. The total variance

The final image analysis method is the seeded region growing method. “The general 
procedure is to compare one pixel to its neighbor(s). If a criterion of homogeneity is 
satisfied, the pixel is said to belong to the same class as one or more of its neighbors” [24]. 
The seeded region growing method starts from a seed point and looks for a boundary edge 
(a significant change in greyscale intensity) that is the fiber edge. By placing a seed above 
and below the fiber, the method determines the top and bottom edge of the fiber. The large 
computing time of this method is a major drawback, but it provides a reliable result [17]. 
The seeded region growing method is a good alternative to thresholding techniques when 
there is noise or fuzzy boundaries in the image.

The international standard, ISO 1973 [30] regarding the determination of linear density 
using the vibroscopic test notes that “the vibroscopic method may not be applicable to 
hollow and flat fibres.” Natural fibers may be considered hollow due to its lumen core. 
However, the standard provides no explanation of the reason for this statement. Gupta and 
Broughton [31] studied hollow polyester fibers and successfully tested these fibers using 
the vibroscopic method. Additionally, hollow carbon fibers of known material density were 
tested with the vibroscopic method by Morschel et al. [29]. Therefore, it is assumed that the 
vibroscopic test is valid on natural fibers.  
2.4.3 STRESS-STRAIN BEHAVIOR OF FIBERS 
Natural fibers behave in three characteristic stress-strain curves as shown in Figure 2.8 
[15]. The first type, TI, exhibits linear deformation. The second type, TII, has an initial linear 
region and then a nonlinear bend, followed by linear deformation until failure. This 
nonlinear bend is similar to the yield point seen in metals, but here is also called the “knee 
point.” Finally, the third type, TIII, is characterized by a region of linear deformation, a 
nonlinear decrease in slope, and then a linear region with a higher slope than the initial 
region [15], [26], [32], [27].  
 
Figure 2.8 Stress-strain behaviors of natural fiber [15] 
The representation of the three behaviors is not equal between fiber batches and 
environmental conditions. Numerous authors found that a majority of tested fibers acted 
like TIII [15], [25], [27]. At high relative humidity, the percentage of TIII curves increased to 
70% [33]. The majority of the hemp fibers Duval [26] tested presented TII behavior. Aslan 
et al. [32] found only TI curves in green flax fibers, whereas more than half of the cottonized 
flax fibers had nonlinear behavior (there was no distinction between TII and TIII).  
Many authors attribute the nonlinear behavior seen in TII and TIII to the misalignment 
between the microfibrils in the secondary cell wall and the longitudinal fiber direction [25], 
[26], [15], [27]. During loading in the nonlinear section, the cellulose microfibrils increase 
in length and slide into alignment with the fiber axis, which is a decrease of the mean 
microfibril angle. A lower microfibril angle results in linear behavior.  
==PAGE:18==
Background 
18 
 
The stick-slip mechanism states that at a certain loading, the shear stress between the 
microfibrils ruptures the hydrogen bonds allowing the amorphous matrix to flow in a 
viscous manner. The microfibrils and matrix will not flow back into its previous position, 
but lock in its new position due to re-forming hydrogen bonds [15], [25].This irreversible 
stiffening is the motivation for the physical treatments described in the next section.  
2.5 PHYSICAL TREATMENT OF NATURAL FIBERS 
If one accepts the hypothesis that regions of poorly aligned microfibrils produce fibers with 
low mechanical properties, then a treatment should be designed to reduce this 
misalignment.  
Defects have been observed during straining under polarized light microscopy [17], [25], 
[6]. Strain caused stretching and realignment of cellulose microfibrils and reduced fiber 
defects. Thygesen et al. [6] found that the dislocations returned two months after the initial 
straining; Mortensen [17] saw that the dislocations return immediately after releasing the 
strain. 
Devries [34] successfully wetted and then dried hemp yarn under tension for improved 
mechanical properties. He found an increase in dynamic modulus after drying with a low 
tension of approximately 1-2g. Meyer and Lotmar [35] attained almost 25% increase in 
stiffness by drying under tension. The stiffness results are presented in Table 2.2.

The environment, especially temperature and humidity, are very important to the behavior 
of natural fibers. The effect of heat treatments on natural fibers has been extensively studied

properties of the fibers. These high temperatures degrade the cell wall and significantly 
weaken the fiber.  
There may be a temperature at which the cell wall is not degraded, but is allowed to realign 
the cellulose microfibrils. The softening of wet lignin (the cell wall matrix material) occurs 
at 60-70°C. At these temperatures, the fiber swells and allows changes in the fiber wall [37]. 
2.6 CHEMICAL TREATMENT OF NATURAL FIBERS 
The industrial manufacturing process used to isolate natural plant fibers from the stem 
begins with retting. This is either done in the field, in a controlled water bath or by an 
enzymatic treatment. The purpose of retting is to breakdown the cortical tissue that holds 
the single fibers in the stem. Next, a series of mechanical processes break the stems into 
smaller pieces and extract the fibers from the dry stems [8].

Chemical treatments are an alternative method to remove specific materials such as pectin,

3. METHODS AND MATERIALS 
This chapter documents the fiber origins, fiber nomenclature, the testing setup and 
procedure, and details regarding the treatments. Testing was limited to hemp (Cannabis 
sativa L.). 
3.1 MATERIALS  
3.1.1 TEXTILE HEMP  
The textile hemp yarn used in this study was purchased from HempAge (Germany). The 
yarn was ring spun, which is the conventional spinning method. In this method, one end of 
the fiber is clamped and the other end rotates [39]. The fiber was wet spun in boiling water. 
This weakens the pectin bonds, which makes the fiber softer and more disposed to 
extension [39].  
Isolation  
Careful fiber isolation is necessary to measure the defect content of a single fiber. Soaking 
the fiber bundle in distilled water softens the fibers and reduces friction between the fibers 
and the cortical tissue allowing separation of single fibers. The assumption is that distilled 
water does not chemically affect the fiber. It is believed to be less damaging than isolation 
of dry single fibers [17], [40], [41].  
The fibers were isolated mechanically with precision tweezers similar to the method used 
by Thygesen and Asgharipour [40] and Burgert et al. [42]. Effort was made to pull a single 
fiber along the axis of the fiber yarn or bundle rather than peel a fiber away from the yarn 
at an angle.  The latter method potentially introduced damage to the fiber. 
3.1.2 FIELD HEMP FIBERS 
Hemp variety USO-31, was sown at a rate of 45 kg/ha in 2013 in France by hemp cultivation 
company (Bafa Neu GmbH, Germany). The seeds were sown with the seed drill 3 – 4 inches 
deep. The hemp plants were fertilized with 80 kg/ha N, 45 kg/ha K and 45 kg/ha P. The 
monoecious hemp plants were harvested at seed maturity [43]. 
Isolation  
There was one important difference in the isolation method of the field hemp fibers used 
here, and the textile yarn. The cortical tissue surrounding the fibers acts like glue, holding 
the fibers together and making separation difficult. This created issues to separate a single 
fiber and have a fiber clean enough to obtain quality microscopy images. In the image 
analysis method, cortical tissue attached to the fiber would be included in the determined 
fiber area. Cortical tissue also makes focusing on the fiber edges more difficult.  
An ultrasonic bath was used to break up the fiber bundle and to “clean” the fibers by 
removing cortical tissue [41], [44]. The fibers were placed in a beaker filled with distilled 
water and shaken in an ultrasonic bath for 20 minutes. This greatly improved the single 
fiber isolation and microscopy process. The ultrasonic bath potentially introduces more 
damage to the fibers. However, the effect of the ultrasonic bath on fiber defect content was 
tested on fiber 4d1b and proved minimal in the Results chapter.

3.2 DEFECT CONTENT 
The defect content is determined by imaging a single fiber under two conditions. First, for 
defect area imaging, bright field microscopy was paired with cross-polar polarization. 
Second, the fiber area was imaged using dark field microscopy to see the light scattered by 
the fiber edges.  
The focus and light intensity are important parameters for the quality of an image. To make 
a valid comparison, these and other settings must be held constant for all tests. 
3.2.1 MICROSCOPE SAMPLE PREPARATION 
A single fiber must be isolated from the batch as described previously and taped with 
double-sided tape to a glass slide. A droplet of distilled water is placed on the fiber and 
another glass slide is firmly placed on top. The slides must be pressed together to squeeze 
out excess water. The fiber is surrounded by water and sandwiched between two glass 
slides.

3.2.2 IMAGE ANALYSIS 
Mortensen [17] compared the image analysis methods for the fiber area image and for the 
defect area image. The coefficient of variation and Otsu method for fiber area detection 
frequently return an inaccurate fiber area. Cortical tissue attached to the fiber will cause the 
image edges to be blurry, which seeded region growing method is able to analyze accurately 
and more easily than thresholding techniques. 
To determine the defect area, Mortensen showed that the seeded region growing method is 
not able to deliver reliable results. The coefficient of variation method is less sensitive and 
therefore less aware of small differences in defect area than other methods [17]. Sensitivity 
is advantageous when comparing different fiber batches. Therefore, Otsu’s method was 
selected as the superior choice. 
It is difficult to determine the accuracy of the these measurements because the true value is 
unknown. The results can be compared to literature, but the microscope and camera 
settings effect the output. However, this process is very good at comparing between fiber 
batches on the same setup because the settings can be consistent [13].

2. Capture fiber area images at 4, 5 and 6V

4. Check the standard deviation criteria of the defect area images and of the fiber area 
images 
5. Defect content of the measurement, location on the single fiber, is the mean of the three 
defect area images divided by the mean of the three fiber area images

4.3 STRESS-STRAIN CURVE SHAPE 
Recall the three stress-strain behaviors of natural fiber introduced in the Background. No 
attempt has been found in the literature to define the three curve shapes quantitatively. 
Thus far, the shapes have only been described qualitatively using terms such as linear, 
nonlinear, and plateau. Therefore, in the present work, criteria are set forth to distinguish 
between the behaviors.  
4.3.1 TI STRESS-STRAIN BEHAVIOR 
Many materials initially deform elastically and then yield into plastic behavior. ASTM 
standards define the yield stress as the onset of plastic deformation. This point is 
==PAGE:43==
 
 
Results and Discussion 
 
43 
determined by duplicating the initial elastic portion of the curve, but offsetting by 0.2% 
strain. The point of intersection between the curve and this line is the yield stress.  
TI deforms elastically until failure. The criterion for TI is an absence of a yield point. A linear 
regression was performed from 0.20% to 0.65% strain, shown between the red ticks in 
Figure 4.16. The linear fit line is then shifted ±0.2% strain, shown as the two green lines. If 
the data do not deviate outside the shifted lines, then the curve was said to exhibit TI 
behavior. For example, Figure 4.16 shows a tensile test result, the linear regression and the 
shifted linear regression lines. Since the curve does not exceed these lines, it is TI.  
 
Figure 4.16 Type I behavior: linear deformation 
4.3.2 TII STRESS-STRAIN BEHAVIOR 
TII has an initial linear deformation region followed by a knee, which is then followed by 
another linear region until failure. This provides three characteristics – two linear regions 
and a knee – of TII behavior. Theoretically, the stiffness versus strain plot shows a plateau 
during the linear deformation because the derivative of a linear line is constant. This plateau 
would be followed by a decrease in stiffness, and then another plateau that represents the 
second linear region.  
However, the initial plateau was nonexistent, as seen in the lower plot of Figure 4.17. The 
initial linear region was approximated by visually inspecting the stress curves for strain 
range before the knee. 0.1% to 0.3% was chosen.  
The secondary linear part was determined from the plateau present in the stiffness vs strain

5. CONCLUSION 
5.1 OVERLOOK 
This study is motivated by the poor tensile properties of natural fiber with a focus on 
understanding the correlation with defect content. There are improvements to the method 
of defect content determination to provide more accurate results. Two treatment types are 
investigated to improve mechanical properties. The first involves physical treatments on 
hemp yarn. Second, chemical treatments to replace the damaging mechanical extraction 
processes common to industry.  
This study contributes to the knowledge and understanding of the mechanisms seen in 
natural fibers. It supports the further development and implementation of natural fiber 
reinforced composites in structural applications. 
5.2 DEFECT CONTENT 
Defects in the cell wall of natural fibers are defined as regions of local misalignments in the 
microfibril angle with respect to its neighbors. These regions are visible under polarized 
light because the light reflected from the neighbor microfibrils is filtered out by the polars, 
while the light from the defect region is not.  
Defects are suspected to be the root cause of weak mechanical properties of the fibers 
because the cellulose in the secondary wall is both the source of structural strength and the 
location of defects. The initial work of this thesis improves upon the previously developed 
quantitative method of determining defects by [14] and [17].  
The major addition to the previous work is the measurement of defect content at three 
brightness levels. The visibility and detection of areas by optical microscopy are dependent 
on the brightness intensity. By imaging at three levels, the effect of brightness is considered 
in order to reduce the uncertainty of measured defect contents.  
The uncertainty of the defect content of one measurement is determined with the error 
propagation equation which accounts for the standard deviation obtained from the three 
brightness images. There is a strong correlation between the mean defect area and the 
standard deviation of the defect area of the three images. The cause of this correlation is 
most likely due to the way the image analysis technique segments the greyscale image to 
black and white. Perhaps it is simply inherent in the measurements as an effect of polarized 
light. The correlation between mean defect content and uncertainty of defect content of 
measurements is defined as a first order, linear equation. To determine the uncertainty of 
the defect content of multiple measurements, the mean of the measurements is substituted 
into this equation. 
There is difficulty in measuring significant changes in defect content because observations 
are on the order of microns. The treatments must have a big effect on the defects to be 
noticed. The large scatter within the measurements provides additional difficulty in 
detecting changes.

5.3 TENSILE TEST 
The vibroscopic test provides promising initial results. It proves capable of measuring the 
diameter of long hemp fibers consistent with the microscopy measurements. The 
overlapping stress-strain curves in Figure 4.13 are a good indication of the accuracy of the 
vibroscopic test because one expects the curves to overlap when the individual diameter is 
used instead of an average diameter.  
The three stress-strain behaviors are quantitatively determined with the proposed criteria. 
The distinction of TI and TII behaviors are clear and easily implemented. TI is an entirely 
linear curve with no yielding point. The ASTM standard dictates that the yielding point 
occurs at an offset of 0.2% strain. If no yielding point can be found with this criterion then 
the curve is assigned TI. TII has a yielding point, called a knee. Unlike steel, plastic 
deformation in the fibers does not occur after this yield point. In TII curves the fiber deforms 
linearly until failure. The characteristics of TIII are more complex. The criterion is the 
location of a minimum stiffness between 0.2% and 1.3% [15]. This criterion was manually 
checked for all tests. The minimum stiffness usually occurs at the end of TI and TIII curves, 
at strain levels of 1.9%.  
5.4 PHYSICAL TREATMENT 
5.4.1 PRELIMINARY TEST 
The tension magnitude is based on the preliminary tensile test, which showed the two 
regions of linear deformation. The tension above and below the knee point is applied in the 
treatments. There was a small decrease in defect content from the untreated fibers 
(2.0±1.0%) to the low tension treatment (1.6±1.0%) and a further decrease in defect 
content for the high tension treatment (1.2±0.7%). Unfortunately, comparable images could 
not be taken for the oven treated fibers, so the effect of this treatment on the defect content 
is unknown.  
5.4.2 TREATMENTS  
The low tension treatment have a mean defect content that is 0.4% lower than the untreated 
fiber. This is accompanied by a 13GPa increase in stiffness, from 19GPa to 32GPa. This seems 
significant. The high tension treatment decreases the mean defect content another 0.4% but 
only increases the stiffness 9GPa, to 27GPa. There is not a very clear correlation between 
the change of defect content and the change of stiffness in these results. The increases in 
strength are similar to those of stiffness – larger change in strength for the low tension 
treatment than the high tension treatment, and a large uncertainty in the oven, low tension 
treatment.  
None of the physically treated fibers exhibit TIII behavior. While this may be due to the small 
sample size, it could very well indicate that drying under tension realigns the microfibrils in 
the cell wall and allows them to deform more linearly. It is beneficial to have primarily TII 
because these curves have the highest mean stiffness (28GPa) and strength (500MPa).  
5.5 CHEMICAL TREATMENT 
5.5.1 ISOLATION METHODS 
The degree of damage introduced during single fiber isolation was minimized whenever 
possible, but it is unknown. The ultrasonic bath may introduce defects, but it was 
==PAGE:62==
Conclusion 
62 
 
experimentally shown to be negligible. There is probably an effect from peeling a single fiber 
away from the bundle rather than pulling along the fiber bundle axis. Peeling bends a single 
fiber away from the bundle at an angle greater than 90 degrees. This method is harsh, but it 
was difficult to avoid when dealing with some of the chemically treated fiber types. 
5.5.2 DEFECT CONTENT 
The first two chemical treatments: pectin extraction and lignin extraction have no effect on 
the defect content. Only the third treatment for hemicellulose removal causes an increase 
in defect content. The defect content remained just above 2.0% until 4d1c when it jumped 
to 3.5%. There appears to be something unique in this latter treatment that causes more 
damage. One possibility is the chemical solution used to extract hemicellulose. Another is 
the additional mechanical damage imposed by handling and drying the fiber. 
5.5.3 MECHANICAL PROPERTIES 
Fiber stiffness and failure stress decrease with each chemical extraction step. The stiffness 
decreases from the 4d1 batch by 19%, 54% and 77% for each treatment 4d1a, 4d1b and 
4d1c respectively. The average ultimate stress for the untreated, 4d1 is 575MPa. The 
ultimate stress for 4d1a, 4d1b and 4d1c is 318MPa, 281MPa and 159MPa, respectively. It is 
difficult to distinguish if the reduced mechanical properties were due to the chemical 
processes or the mechanical processes that occurred simultaneously. The fibers were 
wetted in a hot solution and then dried without tension for each treatment. It is expected 
that additional damage be introduced during the handling and transportation of the fibers.  
Regions of defects in the fibers are more susceptible to chemical invasion. The solutions are 
intended to remove specific components of the fiber wall, but may change the chemical 
orientation in the cell wall of defects. Any such chemical degradation would weaken the 
fibers [12].   
An attempt is made to provide a correlation between stiffness and defect content Figure 
4.15. The results do not conclude a strong correlation, but with more data a trend may 
emerge.

6. FUTURE WORK 
Further exploration on the results work is full of potentially developments. A number 
interesting ideas are proposed here. 
- 
Further development of the image analysis tool to include the severity of defects.  
For example, a method to classify defects by shape such as X, V or simple strip [10]. 
- 
Correlate the defect content and fiber diameter. Literature found a correlation 
between strength and fiber diameter [26], [33]. Determine if this correlation 
extends to defect content. 
- 
The compressive properties of hemp fiber via a single fiber compression test in-situ 
polarized light, for example the loop test [16]. Observe the development of new and 
existing defects due to compression loading.  
- 
A comparison of the fiber diameter determined by the vibroscopic test and optical 
microscopy. Microscopy is the most common method to determine fiber area, but it 
requires assumptions for the fiber cross-sectional geometry. It is also limited to 
view the fiber in two dimensions. Vibroscopy does not limit the dimensions. 
- 
More robust criteria to numerically quantify the stress-strain behavior types. This 
study isolated a single defining characteristic of each behavior. TII and TIII have 
nonlinear deformation that is more difficult to distinguish. 
- 
Extend the duration of physical treatments. This study attained very promising 
results on the physical treatment using low tension. Perform the low tension 
treatment for a shorter and longer amount of time. Investigate how quickly the 
microfibrils realign.  
- 
It is suspected the strain rate has an effect on the results of tensile testing – both in 
magnitude of stiffness and behavior. This study performed all tensile tests at 
1mm/min.  
- 
The dimensional changes of wetting and drying (latent recovery) of hemp fiber. 
Each chemical treatment included wetting in a solution and drying with no tension. 
Determine if the dimensional changes are affected by drying with or without 
tension.
