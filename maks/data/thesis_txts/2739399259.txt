Abstract
This thesis details the development and validation of a computational model for an-
alyzing tunnel-soil interaction in the spatial frequency domain, with particular em-
phasis on validating analytical closed-form solutions. The model was implemented
in ABAQUS, supplemented by automated Python scripts, enabling the systematic
analysis of soil-structure interaction. The study focuses on two primary scenarios:
an infinite depth case, where the structure is buried deep within the soil, and a finite
depth case, where the structure is located near the surface.
In the developed model, displacement inputs with different modes and frequencies
are applied, and the resulting real stress outputs are extracted. These stress values
are then processed using the Inverse Discrete Fourier Transform (IDFT) to derive
frequency-dependent stiffness coeﬀicients. The extracted stiffness coeﬀicients are
then applied to a pipeline, modeled under the Euler-Bernoulli beam assumption, to
assess its response to tunneling-induced ground movements.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
The utilization of underground spaces has significantly increased over recent decades.
A 2019 survey by the International Tunneling and Underground Space Association
highlighted a 9% annual growth in the tunneling market, substantially surpassing the
global construction growth rate [1]. However, underground construction often leads
to soil movements that can stress existing structures, such as buildings, pipelines,
or tunnels, which were not designed to withstand these stresses and are vulnerable
to damage.
Consequently, the calculation of the deformation and stressing of buried pipelines
must be considered in the design process of new tunnels in urban areas [2]. The
concept is illustrated in Figure 1.1. In particular, the tunnel excavation generates
soil settlement around the pipe, causing it to deform. The tunnel above suffers an
additional bending moment. The magnitude of tunnel deformation and the changes
in bending moment depend on the distribution of soil settlement due to tunneling
at the tunnel level and the relative stiffness between the tunnel and the surrounding
soil.
Figure 1.1: Illustration of the tunneling effect on buried infrastructures showing
settlements of soil and existing structures due to tunnel excavation.[3]
1
==PAGE:10==
Over the past 30 years, several approaches have been suggested to evaluate the
potential damage caused by the construction of new tunnels to existing buried in-
frastructure such as tunnels and pipelines. In this aspect, analytical solutions are
particularly valuable as they provide fundamental insights into the governing mech-
anisms of these problems and serve as validation tools for approximate and numer-
ical methods. Moreover, analytical methods are computationally eﬀicient/fast and,
therefore, support the development of inverse analysis schemes and model-guided
techniques guided by optimization algorithms that entail re-executing the underlying
model many times over.
Attewell et al. [4] were the first to suggest a mixed analytical-empirical approach
that uses the greenfield condition as an explicit input to an analytical solution
of the soil–pipeline interaction problem using a Winkler model.
Klar et al.
[3]
offered a closed-form solution to Attewell’s problem, and in addition, developed
an elastic continuum solution to the problem under the same input of greenfield
displacement. Moreover, some work has been done to explore the effects on tunnels
using the Timoshenko beam theory [5], which is more appropriate for tunnels and
large-diameter pipelines.
Although the proposed beam models have some merit they are still crude approx-
imations.
This is because tunnels are three-dimensional structures that interact
with the soil along their entire contact surface and can deform locally, not just one-
dimensionally as beams do. Additionally, these solutions often omit soil resistance
to cross-sectional rotation and ground displacement variation along the structure’s
height, which might be acceptable for conventional pipelines but not for tunnels and
large pipelines.
Klar et al. [2] developed a new elastic continuum solution for the continuous pipeline
problem. The solution involved the representation of the pipeline deformation and
the interaction with the soil by Fourier integral and series expansions. The approach
allowed for full compatibility between the pipeline and the soil (both in the longitu-
dinal direction and in the cross-section) and may be said to be more rigorous than
previously published solutions which were based on certain assumptions regarding
the pattern and distribution of interaction forces.
This work is motivated by the need for a computational model to support the de-
velopment of analytical models for studying the complex interaction problem of
tunneling effects on existing tunnels and large pipelines.
Specifically, the thesis
focuses on the development of a method suitable for the verification of analytical
models. The model is based on techniques that extend spatial frequency relations
and represent both displacements and stresses as Fourier series. Due to its capability
of solving a wide variety of engineering problems, the Finite Element Method (FEM)
is selected as a numerical tool to establish the proposed computational model.
1.2
Study objectives and Methodology
The objectives of the current work were threefold:
(O1) Develop a versatile and validated Finite Element model in ABAQUS capable
of capturing spatial frequency relations.
2
==PAGE:11==
(O2) Develop an automated and parameterized script to extract the frequency-
dependent stiffness matrices from the ABAQUS model.
(O3) Demonstrate the proposed method by applying the extracted results to a
pipeline experiencing tunneling effects.
Successful completion of these objectives will provide researchers with a tool capable
of studying the complex interaction problem of tunneling effects on existing tunnels,
and therefore validate, as well as further develop analytical solutions for the same
purpose.
Figure 1.2 describes the methodology used to develop the proposed computational
model. The methodology is schematically depicted and it consists of three elements,
i.e. :
1. Displacement modes, formulated on sine-cosine form and used as input to the
ABAQUS model. Finite element analysis was used to establish a tunnel soil
interaction response model that produced stresses as an output.
2. Inverse Discrete Fourier Transform used to determine the frequency-dependent
stiffnesses.
3. Utilize frequency-dependent stiffnesses to calculate the pipeline response, due
to the tunneling effect.
Figure 1.2: Schematic illustration of the Methodology.

2.3
Finite Element Method
The Finite Element Method (FEM) is a powerful computational technique used for
solving a wide range of engineering problems. It divides a complex structure into
smaller, manageable elements interconnected at nodes, where the governing equa-
tions are approximated. This method is particularly useful for solving engineering
problems.
In this thesis, we utilize linear static analysis within the FEM framework using
the Abaqus software.
The linear static analysis assumes that material behavior
is linear elastic and that deformations are small enough such that the equilibrium
equations remain linear. This simplification allows for the superposition of effects
and is suitable for many engineering problems where the response is expected to
remain within the linear elastic range.
2.3.1
Governing Equations
The fundamental principle behind FEM is the discretization of the structure into
finite elements, leading to the formation of a set of algebraic equations. The govern-
ing equations for a linear static analysis are derived from the equilibrium equations,
constitutive equations, and compatibility conditions.
Equilibrium Equations
For a static system, the equilibrium equation can be expressed as:
Ku = F
where:
• K is the global stiffness matrix.
• u is the displacement vector.
• F is the force vector.
Constitutive Equations
These relate the stress and strain in a linear elastic material:
𝜎𝑖𝑗= 𝐶𝑖𝑗𝑘𝑙𝜖𝑘𝑙
where:
• 𝜎𝑖𝑗is the stress tensor.
• 𝐶𝑖𝑗𝑘𝑙is the elasticity tensor (for isotropic materials, it depends on Young’s
modulus 𝐸and Poisson’s ratio 𝜈).
• 𝜖𝑘𝑙is the strain tensor.

Discretization
The discretization process involves subdividing the domain into a finite number of
elements. Each element has a predefined shape and is characterized by a set of
nodes. The displacement field within an element is approximated by interpolation
functions (shape functions) that are defined in terms of the nodal displacements.
For a linear element, the displacement field 𝑢can be expressed as:

where:
• 𝑁(𝑥) is the shape function matrix.
• u𝑒is the vector of nodal displacements for the element.

3.5
Model discretization
Model discretization, or so-called meshing, is a crucial step in the FEM, as the
accuracy of the results is highly dependent on the quality and density of the mesh.
A denser mesh typically yields more precise results, but this comes at the cost
of increased computational time. Therefore, determining the appropriate number
of elements, particularly in three-dimensional problems, requires a careful balance
between minimizing error (i.e., deviation from the exact solution) and optimizing
computational eﬀiciency.
In this analysis, a structured mesh has been employed, designed to maintain the
elements as close to a cubical shape as possible (i.e., minimizing element distortion),
as illustrated in Figure 3.6a. This approach helps to ensure consistency in element
quality, which is essential for accurate stress and strain calculations.
Additionally, the mesh density is refined around the buried structure, as shown in
Figure 3.6b. This refinement is necessary because the stress distribution around this
area is of particular interest and will be closely analyzed. As the distance from the
buried structure increases towards the boundaries, the mesh is gradually refined to
a coarser resolution. The number of elements in the vicinity of the structure must
13
==PAGE:22==
be suﬀicient to accurately capture the analytical solution while also ensuring the
correct representation of frequency-dependent stiffness values.
(a) Structured mesh.
(b) Mesh refinement.
As discussed in Chapter 2, for the Inverse Discrete Fourier Transform (iDFT) anal-
ysis, the minimum number of elements along the 𝜃and 𝑥axes should be at least
twice the maximum number of modes (2n). In this analysis, the maximum number
of modes 𝑛considered was 5, necessitating at least 10 elements along these axes,
which have been adhered to. The total number of elements utilized in this analysis
amounts to 65,664. Furthermore, a convergence analysis was performed to demon-
strate the impact of increasing the number of elements on the final results, as will
be elaborated in Chapter 4.

5.1
Introduction
This chapter offers a solution for assessing the impact of tunneling-induced ground
displacements on buried pipelines within the soil elastic continuum, using the frequency-
dependent stiffnesses derived in Chapter 4. It is assumed that the pipeline behaves
as an Euler–Bernoulli beam, and thus is governed by the bending stiffness, ignoring
any shear flexibility.
5.2
Soil-Pipeline interaction

formed and referred to as the ”greenfield” condition, which is used as an input to
the more advanced analysis of the soil-structure interaction (see Figure 5.1). The
greenfield ground displacement is decomposed into an infinite series of trigonometric
functions. The response of the soil and the pipeline to these trigonometric functions
are then established and used to derive a solution for the global pipeline response
using the superposition principle [2].

6
Conclusions
In this project, a computational model was developed to validate analytical closed-
form solutions within the spatial frequency domain for tunnel-soil interaction prob-
lems. The model was implemented in ABAQUS and supplemented with automated
Python scripts.
In this model, displacements with varying modes and frequencies were applied as
input, while real stresses were extracted as output. These stresses were then used
to perform an inverse discrete Fourier transform, enabling the extraction of Fourier
coeﬀicients (i.e., frequency-dependent stiffnesses). Two scenarios were investigated:
one where the buried structure was located at an infinite depth, and another where
it was located near the surface.
Finally, assuming the buried structure (e.g., pipeline) adhered to the Euler-Bernoulli
beam assumption, the frequency-dependent stiffness coeﬀicients were employed to
calculate the pipeline’s response.
The main findings of this research include:
(i) The Inverse Discrete Fourier Transform (IDFT) can effectively be used in a
Finite Element (FE) problem due to the discretized nature of the FE method.
(ii) The number of elements in a 3D FE problem is crucial, requiring careful
engineering judgment to balance the acceptable error against computational
time.
(iii) The accuracy of FEM analysis is significantly influenced by the choice of mesh
structure and element order. A structured mesh ensures consistency and re-
duces computational errors, while the use of second-order elements enhances
the precision of the results.
(iv) The coupling or decoupling between modes is dependent on the depth of the
buried structure.
(v) The Euler-Bernoulli beam assumption for the pipeline provides a good first
approximation, though it comes with certain limitations.
Based on these findings, the following recommendations are proposed:
(i) Consider developing a symmetrical model in both the 𝑦and 𝑥directions, i.e.,
using half of the pipeline’s cross-section and half of the wavelength (𝐿/2). This
approach allows for a denser mesh, leading to more accurate results.
(ii) The current Python parametric model, which parameterizes 𝜔, 𝑛, and 𝑢𝑟, 𝑢𝜃,
and 𝑢𝑥, could be further enhanced by incorporating depth as an additional
40
==PAGE:49==
parameter. With this enhancement, the script would be complete, enabling
the automatic generation of frequency-dependent stiffness matrices for any
given depth, resulting in a fully functional, user-friendly mini-software tool.
(iii) More advanced assumptions could be explored for the pipeline, such as the
Timoshenko beam theory, or even represent the pipeline as a shell structure.
