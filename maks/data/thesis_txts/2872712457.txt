Predicting performance of intumescent coatings on large steel structures using small-scale tests and modeling 
Abstract 
Intumescent coatings are applied to enhance the fire resistance of structural 
steel. Their fire performance is typically assessed through full-scale furnace tests, 
which are both costly and time-consuming. During the development phase of new 
coating formulations, it becomes essential to rely on fast, cost-effective, and reli-
able screening methods to identify promising coating systems efficiently. 
 
In this context, a laboratory-scale screening test method was developed using a 
small electric furnace. To ensure the relevance of the test conditions, temperature 
and heat flux measurements were performed, confirming that the thermal envi-
ronment closely resembled that of full-scale test furnaces. A simplified heat trans-
fer model was employed to estimate the thermal performance of coatings across 
a range of dry film thicknesses (DFTs). 
 
To evaluate the method, three coatings were tested using different heat flux loads 
to simulate the incident heat flux distribution on I/H-sections. The samples con-
sisted of 3 mm thick steel panels with coating thicknesses ranging from 0.47 to 
3,66 mm. 
 
Predicted temperature-time curves confirmed that increasing dry film thickness 
(DFT) generally enhances fire resistance performance. However, limitations in 
the laboratory-scale model, such neglecting char degradation or radiative heat 
transfer, may lead to overestimations at higher DFTs.  
 
For the tested coatings, the method provided reasonable predictions for H-sec-
tions with a section factor of 220 𝑚⁻¹, except in the case of the water-based coat-
ing, which showed greater deviation. Further validation is needed to improve pre-
diction accuracy for higher DFTs and other section factors, and to ensure con-
sistency with full-scale fire test data.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Background 
 
Fire safety is a critical aspect of building design. During a fire, extreme heat can 
compromise the structural integrity of a building by affecting the materials used 
in its construction.  
 
Different materials respond to fire in various ways:  
 
- 
Wood: Burns and contributes with heat to fire. 
- 
Concrete: Can experience spalling, reducing its structural capacity.  
- 
Structural steel: Does not burn but loses mechanical strength as temper-
ature increases, which can lead to deformation and building collapse.  
To mitigate these risks, buildings are often designed with a specified fire re-
sistance time, allowing sufficient time for safe evacuation before structural failure 
occurs. (Lidman Olsson et al., 2025a) 
 
1.1.1. Fire Protection of Steel Structures 
 
Steel structures are widely used in modern construction due to their strength, 
durability, and flexibility. However, their mechanical properties at high tempera-
tures poses a significant challenge during a fire. As steel heats up, it loses its 
mechanical properties (reduction in yield strength, loss of stiffness and elastic 
modulus, expansion and thermal stresses), increasing the risk of structural fail-
ure. To mitigate this risk, two primary fire protection methods are employed: ac-
tive and passive fire protection. (Mariappan, 2016)

Active Fire Protection  
 
Active fire protection systems are designed to detect, suppress, or control fires 
through mechanical or electronic means. These include:  
 
• 
Sprinkler Systems – Automatically release water to suppress flames.  
• 
Fire Alarms – Alert occupants and emergency responders.  
• 
Gas Suppression Systems – Use chemical agents or inert gases to extin-
guish fires.  
 
While highly effective, active systems have notable limitations:  
 
• 
High cost and complex installation.  
• 
Regular maintenance is required for reliability.  
• 
Limited effectiveness in large fires.  
• 
Smoke stratification can reduce visibility and hinder evacuation.  
  
Passive Fire Protection  
 
Unlike active systems, passive fire protection does not require system activation.  
Passive systems are categorized into non-reactive and reactive solutions.  
 
Non-Reactive Passive Protection  
 
These materials insulate steel from heat without undergoing physical changes. 
Examples include:  
 
• 
Boards (gypsum, cement, or mineral fiber) – Provide durable, rigid insula-
tion.  
• 
Cementitious Spray – A thick fireproofing layer applied to steel surfaces.  
• 
Mineral Fiber Insulation – Fire-resistant panels that slows down heat pen-
etration.

• 
Aesthetic limitations – Steelwork can appear bulky and unattractive.

Reactive Passive Protection: Intumescent Coatings  
 
Intumescent coatings have gained widespread adoption in recent years due 
to their aesthetic appeal, ease of application, and superior fire resistance. 
These coatings expand when exposed to heat, forming an insulating char 
layer that prolong the time it takes for the steel to heat up.   
Intumescent coatings are increasingly used in buildings that require high fire 
protection, such as stadiums, skyscrapers, and multi-story buildings. These 
coatings are commonly applied to steel-framed and high-rise structures to 
protect them from fire. (Yasir et al., 2020)    
 
Advantages of Intumescent Coatings:  
 
• 
Aesthetic benefits – These organic coatings swell significantly when ex-
posed to heat, providing a high-quality finish that can be top coated for outdoor 
use. However, they may sometimes form a fluffy char that can dislodge at high 
wind speeds. (Puri & Khanna, 2017) 
• 
Speed and ease of application – Can be applied offsite for better quality 
control.  
• 
Corrosion protection – Some coating systems can provide corrosion pro-
tection and thereby enhance the longevity of steel structures.  
• 
Suitable for various structures – Used in airports, shopping malls, hospi-
tals, stadiums, and office buildings, where modern architectural designs pri-
oritize exposed steel elements. (Mariappan, 2016).

1.1.2. Large-Scale Testing of Intumescent Coatings 
 
Large-scale fire resistance testing is essential to verify that intumescent coatings 
perform as expected under real-world conditions. These tests are not only nec-
essary to demonstrate their effectiveness but are also required by regulations to 
ensure compliance with safety standards. (European Committee for Standardi-
zation, 2002) (ASTM International, 2011). 
 
Large-scale fire resistance tests require significant resources, making them ex-
pensive and time-consuming. Full-scale testing must account for a wide range of 
steel configurations and coating thicknesses to meet regulatory approval, it is la-
bor-intensive, and it consumes great amounts of fuel. When developing new coat-
ings, small-scale test methods are therefore needed in the early phases of the 
coating development. 
 
Predictive lab-scale methods can minimize the need for full-scale testing, reduc-
ing costs and saving time in the early phases of coating development. Computa-
tional simulations have been developed to reduce the need for large-scale tests, 
but they require extensive validation. Numerical models depend on accurate ma-
terial properties, which vary based on fire exposure and coating composition. 
(Weisheim et al., 2020)

Challenges of I/H-sections 
 
For I/H-sections it is important to take into account the shadow factor. This factor 
refers to how the flanges can partially block thermal radiation from reaching the 
web, leading to uneven heat distribution. The web areas covered by the flanges 
receive less radiation, resulting in slower heating and potentially different thermal 
expansion behavior in the coating compared to areas exposed to direct radiation. 
This effect is rarely considered in laboratory-scale testing, where more idealized 
conditions are assumed. A method to simulate this in the lab would therefore be 
valuable.

2. Aim, objectives and scope of the thesis 
Large-scale testing of intumescent coatings is expensive and time-consuming. 
When developing new coatings, small-scale test methods are therefore 
needed. This study aims to develop methodologies that can predict the perfor-
mance of commercial intumescent coatings on large steel structures, specifically 
I/H, using small-scale tests conducted in an electrical furnace, combined with nu-
merical modeling.  
 
The following objectives are defined:  
 
1. Develop a test method for flat steel panels that can be used to assess how the 
shadow factor on I/H-sections affects the coating performance. 
2. Develop a numerical model for 1-dimensional structural elements that use test 
data from point 1 as input. 
3. Identify the validity and limitations of the developed models by comparing the 
results with existing full-scale test data.

3. Literature review 
3.1 Principles of intumescent coatings 
 
In the past, inorganic intumescent coatings based on soluble alkali silicates were 
popular due to their low cost, but they have significant drawbacks, such as poor 
durability and water resistance. Today, organic thin-film intumescent coatings are 
more popular due to their better aesthetics and performance.  
 
An organic intumescent coating typically contains the following ingredients:  
 
• 
Carbon donor (char former)  
• 
Acid donor (catalyst)  
• 
Blowing agent  
• 
Binder 
 
 
3.1.1 Chemical Behaviour and Thermal Expansion Mechanisms  
 
The intumescence process consists of a series of physical and chemical trans-
formations that occur when the coating is exposed to high temperatures. Initially, 
the polymeric binder softens and melts, preparing the matrix for expansion. Sim-
ultaneously, inorganic acids (typically released from compounds such as ammo-
nium polyphosphate) catalyze the dehydration and carbonization of char formers 
like pentaerythritol (PER). This forms the basis of a thermally stable carbon layer. 
 
At the same time, blowing agents such as melamine begin to decompose and 
release gases. These gases become trapped within the molten matrix, causing 
foaming and expansion, which results in a multi-cellular, insulating char layer. 
This expanded carbon-rich layer significantly reduces heat transfer to the under-
lying metal substrate and delays structural failure by forming a protective thermal 
barrier. (Mariappan, 2016)

3.1.2 Key Components of Intumescent Coatings  
Acid Source / Catalyst (APP - Ammonium Polyphosphate) 
Ammonium polyphosphate (APP) acts as an acid donor and dehydrating agent 
during fire exposure. At temperatures above 200 °C, it decomposes to release 
polyphosphoric acid and ammonia, initiating the carbonization of char-forming 
components. APP functions both as a catalyst and a blowing agent, contributing 
to the formation of the protective intumescent foam layer. Its low water solubility 
enhances durability after weathering. However, its main limitation is poor mois-
ture resistance, which can lead to gradual degradation over time (Mariappan, 
2016) 
. 
Carbon Source / Carbonific 
The carbon source reacts with the acid to form an ester and provides carbon for 
the formation of a protective char layer. The effectiveness of the carbon source 
depends on the number of carbon atoms and hydroxyl groups. A higher carbon

Common carbon sources include Polyfunctional alcohols (PER, sorbitol), sugars,

PER best compromise between carbon and hydroxyl content

Blowing Agents / Spumific 
The blowing agents are organic compounds that decompose to release gases

The decomposition temperature of the blowing agent must be the same as 
char/dehydration temperature. In order to obtain thicker foam it’s common to use 
two blowing agents with slightly different decomposition temperatures that ex-

Low-temperature blowing agents includes amides and amines (urea, glycine, 
MEL, guanidine). 
High-temperature blowing agents includes chlorinated paraffin (CP), which stabi-

For effectiveness, the melt formed during dehydration of the polyol must be 
foamed via the evolution of non-flammable gases. Blowing should occur after the 
melt forms and before gelation occurs (Mariappan, 2016).

Binders 
The binder forms a protective skin over the foam, and ensures a continuous and 
uniform foam structure throughout the mass. 
The binder can be thermoplastic (used for both waterborne and solvent-based 
intumescent coatings) or thermosetting resins (used for the solvent-less intumes-
cent coatings, but  most of them do not produce good intumescence) 
The resin must retain its integrity on aging and must not become so hard that 
intumescence is prevented. 
A binder that melts at relatively low temperatures facilitates better foam expan-
sion. However, to maintain the structural integrity of the char at higher tempera-
tures (>200 °C), the binder must also exhibit sufficient viscosity and flexibility to 
support the expanding foam layer (Mariappan, 2016). 
 
3.1.3 Mechanisms of Intumescence  
 
Intumescent coatings protect against fire by expanding and forming a thick, heat-
resistant char layer with low thermal conductivity.  
The formation of this thick char layer, along with its high carbon concentration, 
high viscosity of the pyrolyzing melt, and low heat penetration, makes intumes-
cent systems highly efficient in reducing flammability and the release of toxic 
gases. To ensure that intumescent coatings provide sufficient heat insulation for 
structural steel, two critical properties must be considered: the expansion of the 
coating and the retention of the char layer. The formation of char is a complex 
process influenced by several factors, including its viscoelasticity, charring chem-
istry, and thermophysical properties that limit heat and mass transfer. Another 
key aspect of intumescent coatings is the mechanical strength of the intumesced 
char, which plays a crucial role in their overall effectiveness. (Mariappan, 2016)

3.2 Types of Fires and Their Impact on Steel Structures 
 
3.2.1 Cellulosic Fires 
 
Standards for cellulosic fires apply when the primary fuel source consists of cel-
lulose-based materials, such as paper or wood, commonly found in residential 
buildings, offices, or warehouses. This fire is a model of a ventilation-controlled 
natural fire, that is, fires in a normal building, and shows a slow temperature rise 
to 927°C after about 60 min(Mariappan, 2016). The global standard for fire testing 
is ISO 834, which defines the furnace temperature over time when using liquid or 
gaseous fuels. The equation describing this temperature is: 
𝑇𝑓= 20 + 345 log10(8𝑡+ 1) 
 
Where Tf is the furnace temperature in ºC and t is time in minutes. 
 
The coefficient of heat transfer by convection is commonly assumed to be 25 
W/m²K. (European Committee for Standardization, 2002) 
 
3.2.2 Hydrocarbon Fires 
 
When the main fuel source consists of hydrocarbons, the heat release rate is 
significantly higher than for cellulosic fuels, leading to a faster temperature rise 
and higher peak temperatures. Hydrocarbon fires typically occur in locations such 
as petrochemical plants or offshore oil platforms. Hydrocarbon fires show a rapid 
temperature rise to 1000°C at about 4 min.

release in hydrocarbon fires:

3.3 Current Standards and Methods for Fire Testing 
 
3.3.1 Large-Scale Fire Testing  
 
• High Costs and Time-Intensive Nature  
Large-scale fire resistance tests require significant resources, making them ex-
pensive and time-consuming (Lidman Olsson et al., 2025b). Full-scale testing 
must account for a wide range of steel configurations and coating thicknesses to 
meet regulatory approval (European Committee for Standardization, 2002).  
 
• Dependence on Standard Fire Curves  
Current testing methodologies mainly rely on standard temperature-time fire 
curves. However, these do not always represent real-world fire scenarios, leading 
to questions about their applicability (Dreyer et al., 2021) 
 
 
==PAGE:23==

• Variability in Heating Conditions  
Intumescent coatings respond differently to various heating rates. Slow-growing 
fires or low heating regimes can result in incomplete swelling, reducing effective-
ness(Lucherini & Maluk, 2019). Conversely, high heating rates can alter char for-
mation, affecting thermal performance (Dreyer et al., 2021). 
• Influence of Environmental Factors  
Factors like oxygen concentration, turbulence, and convective flows in testing 
environments can impact coating adhesion (Dreyer et al., 2021), delamination, 
and overall performance (Lucherini & Maluk, 2019). Standard furnace tests may 
not accurately replicate real-life conditions. 
• Long-Term Durability Issues  
Intumescent coatings may degrade over time due to environmental exposure 
(moisture, temperature fluctuations, UV radiation). However, most testing proce-
dures assess coatings in their "fresh" state, without considering long-term perfor-
mance (Mariappan, 2016). 
• Assumption of Fire Condition Independence  
However, the current design framework wrongly assumes that the thermo-physi-
cal properties of intumescent coatings are independent on the fire conditions. 
This oversimplification can lead to unreliable predictions of coating performance, 
particularly under non-standard fire scenarios. (Lucherini & Maluk, 2019) 
• Numerical Modeling Challenges  
Computational simulations have been developed to reduce the dependence on 
large-scale tests, but they require extensive validation. Numerical models depend 
on access to accurate material properties, which may vary based on fire exposure 
and coating composition. (Weisheim et al., 2020)

4. Small-Scale Fire Testing  
Various small-scale test methods have been developed to study intumescent 
coatings, each with its own characteristics, advantages, and limitations. Among 
them, Bunsen burners and impinging flames are the simplest and fastest meth-
ods to get an initial impression of how a coating reacts to high temperatures. 
However, they present challenges such as non-uniform heat distribution across 
the sample surface, difficulty in comparing results from different researchers, and 
the inability to follow standardized temperature-time profiles. 
Another alternative is radiative heaters, which expose the sample to a homoge-
neous and typically constant heat flux. However, since intumescent coatings ex-
pand when heated, the decreasing distance between the coating surface and the 
heater increases the incident heat flux during the experiment. Some approaches 
have been developed to address this issue, such as H-TRIS, a movable radiative 
heater panel that adjusts the thermal exposure over time (Dreyer et al., 2021). 
Despite these improvements, radiative heaters have several limitations, including 
uncertain heat loss (both radiative and convective), insufficient power to simulate 
prolonged hydrocarbon fires, and differences in atmospheric conditions, as ex-
periments are conducted in air while fire resistance tests require reduced oxygen 
environments. Comparative studies between a modified cone calorimeter and an 
oil-fired furnace have highlighted the limitations of radiative heaters, such as the 
absence of high-temperature reactions, differences in heating rates, and lower 
char surface and steel temperatures. 
To better simulate full-scale fire resistance tests, small-scale electric and hydro-
carbon-fired furnaces have been developed. These systems can follow at least 
some standardized temperature-time curves and are significantly cheaper and 
faster than their industrial-scale counterparts. Examples include the STT Mufu+, 
used for testing coatings under cellulosic fire temperature-time curves, and the 
Hempel Coatings Science and Technology Centre (CoaST) furnace at DTU, Den-
mark, which can reach 1093°C within 5 min (Dreyer et al., 2021). Additionally, the

Université de Lille (UMET, France) developed a hydrocarbon-fired furnace for 
testing samples under the same temperature-time curve. 
Despite their advantages, these furnaces also have some drawbacks. One of the 
main limitations is that they require cooling between experiments, which signifi-
cantly reduces the number of tests that can be performed daily. One way to in-
crease efficiency is to test multiple samples simultaneously in the same furnace, 
although this may affect the representativeness of the recovered chars. It may be 
challenging to replicate some of the full-scale furnace conditions in a small-scale 
furnace, for example the gas composition and the ratio of convective to radiative 
heat flux. However, compared to radiative heaters and impinging flame setups, 
furnace setups can more easily be controlled to follow the same standardized 
temperature-time curves that are used in full-scale tests (Lidman Olsson et al.,

For a small-scale test method to provide reliable performance predictions for full-
scale tests, it must be designed to quantify and compensate for key differences. 
These include heat loss, the steel section factor (the ratio of fire-exposed surface 
area to the volume of the steel), and the dry film thickness (DFT) of the coating, 
all of which can influence the intumescence process. (Dreyer et al., 2023) 
4.1 Existing small-scale electric furnace 
 
At the Technical University of Denmark (CoaST), a small electric furnace was 
previously used to test intumescent coatings under cellulosic or hydrocarbon fire 
curves. In (Lidman Olsson et al., 2025b) describes subsequent modifications 
made to improve the small electric furnace reliability for small-scale testing.  
To overcome the limitations, a series of modifications were implemented to im-
prove its reliability for testing intumescent coatings. First, the sample holder was 
redesigned to eliminate the tunnel-like configuration, thereby increasing the direct 
exposure of the coated sample to thermal radiation and avoiding any physical 
restriction to char expansion. Additionally, thermal insulation was added around 
the sample holder and the rear face of the steel substrate to reduce heat losses,

particularly at elevated temperatures (above 500 °C). This modification helped 
improve the accuracy of the steel temperature-time response during testing. Fi-

curate and stable furnace temperature regulation. These enhancements allowed 
the furnace to produce small-scale test results that are more representative and 
reproducible, aligning better with outcomes observed in large-scale fire testing.  
 
4.2 Effective thermal conductivity

coating in full-scale fire scenarios. This is because they cannot realistically cap-

reflects coating behavior independently of test configuration. While thermal con-

5.3 Expansion  
 
The expansion as a function of time will be extracted and postprocessed from the 
test to estimate an apparent thermal conductivity.    
To measure the expansion, an optical set up based on a camera and a beam was 
added to the back of the furnace. The beam projects a horizontal line on the 
sample, the beam projects it with a certain angle, so when the coating starts to 
expand the horizontal line beam moves up, the camera is in charge of recording 
the complete movement during the test. After that the file created by the camera 
is postprocessed with a MATLAB code developed previously (Ulusoy et al., 
2023). The image analysis process combines basic principles from image pro-
cessing, geometry, and statistics to calculate the expansion of the coating based 
on frames extracted from a video. 
First, the video is divided into individual images by sampling frames at regular 
time intervals, where the frame rate is determined from the total number of frames 
and the known test duration. 
In each image, the horizontal beam is detected by identifying the point of maxi-
mum intensity in each vertical column, this allows the distance to be counted. At 
the beginning the user can select the area of the frame that needs to analyze. To 
reduce the influence of noise, the detected points are filtered using a statistical 
outlier removal method. Points that deviate significantly from the mean are ex-
cluded to improve the reliability of the data. 
==PAGE:32==
32 
Predicting performance of intumescent coatings on large steel structures using small-scale tests and modeling 
After this, a manual correction step is included. A graphical interface allows the 
user to adjust specific points on the surface profile. This interaction ensures that 
irregularities or detection errors can be corrected by visual inspection. 
Once the surface position is finalized, the distance in pixels is converted to milli-
meters using a calibration factor.  
Because the camera is positioned at a 14.9° angle from vertical, the vertical dis-
placement seen in the image does not directly represent the true expansion. A 
trigonometric correction is applied to obtain the actual expansion using the rela-
tion: expansion equals vertical displacement divided by the tangent of 14.9 de-
grees. 
For each frame, the mean and standard deviation of the corrected expansion 
values are calculated. These statistical measures summarize the average coating 
expansion and its variation across the surface. 
Finally, the expansion is normalized by the initial coating thickness. This produces 
a relative expansion value, which is dimensionless and indicates how much the 
coating has grown compared to its original thickness.

The predicted fire resistance times demonstrate the expected trend of increasing 
time to reach a steel temperature of 500 °C with increasing dry film thickness 
(DFT) for all three coating types: water-based, solvent-based, and epoxy-based. 
However, it is important to acknowledge several limitations and assumptions in-
herent in the predictive methodology, particularly when extrapolating perfor-
mance to higher DFT values.  
Note that the data obtained from the model covers a range of 60 minutes for all 
the samples and the full-scale data for water-based coating and epoxy-based 
coating covers a range of 90 minutes for the studied section factor, for solvent-
based coating covers a range of 60 minutes for the studied section factor. 
A comparison between the predicted fire resistance times and the full-scale test 
data (EN 13381-8 CEN, 2013) reveals noticeable differences in prediction accu-
racy depending on the type of intumescent coating. For water-based coatings 
(Figure 40), the prediction underestimates the fire resistance time. This suggests 
that water-based coatings may exhibit more favorable behavior under realistic fire 
conditions (enhanced expansion or better adhesion to the substrate) which the 
model does not account for. In contrast, the results for solvent-based coatings 
(Figure 41) show better agreement between prediction and full-scale test data.  Ac-
cording to (Lidman Olsson et al., 2025b), caution should be taken when extrapo-
lating the results to higher DFT, to make the comparison a higher section factor 
must be studied because there is no data before 60 min for 218 𝑚−1. The epoxy-
based coatings (Figure 42), show a good agreement.  
 
Firstly, the model used for prediction assumes that the effective thermal conduc-
tivity is not affected by DFT. This simplification may not hold at larger film thick-
nesses, where a greater mass of char is formed during fire exposure. Thicker 
char layers are more susceptible to mechanical instability due to increased grav-
itational forces and internal stress, potentially leading to delamination or cracking, 
which would negatively affect thermal insulation performance. 
 
==PAGE:73==
      
 
Predicting performance of intumescent coatings on large steel structures using small-scale tests and modeling 
73 
Another important factor is the variation in heating rate (𝑑𝑇/𝑑𝑡) experienced 
across the coating thickness. While the outer layers are directly exposed to fur-
nace conditions and heat up rapidly, the inner regions of thicker coatings heat 
more slowly. This temperature gradient can result in reduced expansion in the 
lower parts of the film, leading to less char formation than predicted. Conse-
quently, the fire resistance time could be overestimated for higher DFTs. 
 
Furthermore, at higher temperatures, radiative heat transfer through the char 
structure becomes more significant, particularly in the pores of thick coatings. 
Since the laboratory-scale model does not explicitly account for this radiative 
component, thermal conductivity may be underestimated, again leading to opti-
mistic predictions at high DFT. 
 
Finally, the laboratory-scale method does not capture mechanical interaction be-
tween the coating and steel substrate during thermal expansion. In real fire con-
ditions, the ability of the coating to remain adhered (stickability) may decrease 
with increasing DFT, particularly under mechanical deformation. This limitation 
further adds uncertainty to the extrapolated predictions.

7. Conclusions 
In this work, a laboratory-scale method was developed and partially evaluated to 
predict the fire resistance time of intumescent coatings. The methodology is 
based on determining the effective thermal conductivity of the coating using a 
small-scale furnace test. The effective thermal conductivity was determined for 
different heat flux loads to simulate different shadow factors of an I/H-section, 
combined with a one-dimensional heat transfer model to predict the thermal re-
sponse as a function of dry film thickness (DFT). For the evaluated samples, the 
method was shown to provide reasonable predictions of fire resistance time for 
H-sections with a section factor of 220 𝑚⁻¹, particularly when the modeled DFT 
is similar to that of the specimen used in the laboratory-scale test, except in the 
case of the water-based coating, where larger discrepancies were observed. 
 
While the method can be used to generate predictions for DFTs other than those 
directly tested (for a given section factor of 220 m⁻¹), caution must be exercised 
when extrapolating to significantly higher thicknesses or in cases where char ad-
hesion is uncertain. To reduce the need for extrapolation, the model could be 
expanded to incorporate laboratory data from multiple specimens with varying 
DFTs, thereby improving its applicability across a wider range of coating thick-
nesses. 
 
In order to fully validate the methodology, it is necessary to expand the model to 
predict fire resistance for a broader range of section factors and to extend the 
duration of the prediction. This would ensure that the resulting data covers the 
full range of times needed for direct comparison with full-scale fire test results. 
With these improvements, the methodology could be more robustly evaluated 
and potentially refined to increase its predictive reliability for different DFT and 
section factors.

Heat flux code  
The code begins by importing, NumPy for numerical calculations, matplotlib.py-
plot for plotting, and pandas for handling data tables. Subsequently, it defines the 
gauge temperature, the convective heat transfer coefficient, the emissivity of both 
the gauge and the fire, and the Stefan-Boltzmann constant. After defining these 
parameters, it sets a reduction factor to account for a decrease in radiative heat 
flux, which is later used to compute the total heat flux while considering the view 
factor. 
To proceed with the calculations, the code generates a time vector consisting of 
100 points between 0 and 60 minutes. Based on this time vector, it calculates the 
gas temperature curve using the cellulose fire model, following a logarithmic ex-
pression. Using this temperature profile, the code then computes the convective 
heat flux via the convective coefficient and the radiative heat flux using the 
Stefan-Boltzmann law. These two components are summed to obtain the total 
heat flux. 
To simulate a reduced heat exposure scenario (caused by shadow effects), the 
reduction factor is applied only to the radiative component, while the convective 
part remains unchanged. Both the original and the reduced heat flux curves are 
then plotted for comparison. 
Following this, the code calculates the equivalent gas temperature that would re-
sult in the same reduced total heat flux. This is achieved through an iterative 
process at each time point, where an initial temperature guess is progressively 
refined until the computed total flux closely matches the reduced value.
