Abstract
This thesis deals with the investigation of the electromagnetic forces in LV busbar systems,
and the physical factors that have an impact on the amplitude of these forces. The
electromagnetic forces were calculated on three systems using three diﬀerent methods. One
of the three systems however, are calculated using ﬁve methods.
The ﬁrst system to be investigated, is the L1-L2-L3 system. This system consists of three
busbars, each carrying a phase, which are horizontally displaced from each other. This
system is calculated using COMSOL, Finite Diﬀerence Method through Matlab, Ampere’s
Law, the IEC 60865-1 standard, and also measured through lab exercises.
The second system to be investigated is the L1-L1-L2-L2-L3-L3 system. This system
consist of six busbars, where the phases are grouped so that two busbars share a phase.
The system resembles the L1-L2-L3 system in its positioning, with the diﬀerence of each
phase being split into two. The IEC 60865-1 standard only included multiple busbar phases
that was grouped like the second system, and only returned the largest electromagnetic
force towards another phase, instead of the largest electromagnetic force in general. This
system is calculated using COMSOL, Finite Diﬀerence Method, and Ampere’s Law. The
IEC 60865-1 standard was not eligible in order to ﬁnd the largest occurring electromagnetic
force, and the lab setup was not implemented to measure the electromagnetic forces of
this system.
The third and last system to be investigated is the L1-L2-L3-L1-L2-L3 system. This system
consists of two L1-L2-L3 systems, placed horizontally side by side. The electromagnetic
forces for this system, is not described in the IEC 60865-1 standard. This system is
calculated using COMSOL, Finite Diﬀerence Method, and Ampere’s Law. A lab setup
was not implemented to measure the electromagnetic forces of this system.
Each of these three systems are investigated using three diﬀerent dimensions for the busbars.
The three dimensions are implemented, so that they represent a vertical form factor, a
horizontal form factor, and a squared form factor. The result of each form factor and each
alignment are then compared.
The results indicated that the system with the L1-L2-L3-L1-L2-L3 alignment produced
the lowest electromagnetic force. This was proven to be consistent, regardless of busbar
form factor. The L1-L1-L2-L2-L3-L3 system however, produced the lowest electromagnetic
force in the direction that would result in two busbars carrying diﬀerent phases to collide.
The form factor however, proved to have a larger impact as distance shortens, and current
rises. Busbar systems using horizontally shaped busbars proved to generate the lowest
electromagnetic forces.

Introduction 1
The modern power grid today is based on the fact that any current generates an
electromagnetic ﬁeld, and a resulting electromagnetic force. This phenomena was discovered
by Hans Christian Ørsted in 1819, and supplemented by the fact that two wires carrying a
current in the same direction attracts, and repels if carrying opposite direction currents
in 1820 by André-Marie Ampère[1]. These discoveries lead to a series of other discoveries
and inventions that would later on become the backbone of the modern electrical grid,
which supplies more or less the whole planet with electrical power. The electrical grid
consist of a network of power cables connected in nodes, which are commonly referred as
power stations. These stations connect the electrical cables through busbars, and transfers
the electricity to a lower voltage level through transformers to another set of busbars.
The busbar is therefore one of the major components in a modern power grid, and is
subjected to a large amount of electrical current based on the consumption and generation
of electricity.
The power demand has risen as time goes past, and becomes more and more hazardous
to handle under speciﬁc situations, due to the increase of electrical current. The rise in
electrical current results in a higher electromagnetic ﬁeld, hence stronger electromagnetic
force. The study of these forces are therefore a topic which should be respected. A failure
to respect the electromagnetic forces can lead to faults in busbar systems, especially low
voltage systems due to the higher current. A fault can be two busbars colliding and
thus creating a phase-to-phase short circuit. Such fault can give major mechanical faults,
melt power components, and ultimately creating a life threatening environment for any
person in its proximity. A fault like this can also lead to permanent damage to the power
components, which would take a signiﬁcant amount of time to repair, thus creating larger
blackout incidents.
The design of a system containing busbars, especially in low voltage system should, due to
the reasons mentioned above, include the potential electromagnetic forces generated as a
part of the design process. This can be done quickly by using the IEC 60865 standard as a
point of reference, yielding uniform solutions throughout the electrical grid. It is therefore
interesting to determine whether the IEC 60865 standard reﬂect the actual electromagnetic
forces which are generated, or if there are other alternatives which gives a quick and swift
value.
Having a correct way of calculating the electromagnetic force is one part of the story, the
other part of the story is to investigate the design actions that can be made to minimize
these forces. These design decisions can vary from deciding the busbar dimensions, the
physical positions from the busbars to each other, or any other factors that have an impact

1.1
Project Goal
The purpose of this project is to have an in-depth analysis of the diﬀerent factors that
aﬀect busbar system design. The theory behind recognized standards will be reviewed and
evaluated if useful with real life experiments. A special project was completed prior to this
project, which developed a test system that is able to measure the electromagnetic forces
that occur on a three phase system.
The ﬁrst objective is to implement a series of methods that are capable of calculating the
electromagnetic force in the cases that are desired to be investigated. These methods will
later on be veriﬁed.
The second objective is to improve the existing test setup that had been used in the prior
project. The setup has to be improved, so the results are comparable to analytically
achieved values. Having a precise test setup would be capable of verifying the models
which are implemented, thus enabling the student to evaluate the methods implemented.
The third objective is to use the veriﬁed methods to investigate the diﬀerent factors that
have an impact of electromagnetic force generation. The impact of the factors will then
be used to determine the best strategies that can be applied to minimize the generated
electromagnetic force.
1.1.1
Project description
The main objective of the project is to investigate the electromagnetic forces that occur in
a low voltage busbar system using diﬀerent approaches:
• Experimental approach using a test system
• FEM Modelling using COMSOL
• Filament analytical method
• Veriﬁcation of the applied IEC standards
The project is limited to following physical boundaries:
• Maximum of two 3-phase systems
• Measuring up to 15 N
Initially, the student must describe the theory behind generating short circuit currents
and measuring of these and the mechanical forces that occur with them.
The student must compare the measured electromagnetic forces and busbar displacement
with the solutions achieved with each method as a comparison.

Ampere’s Law works as long as the conductors are of cylindrical shape, and has an even
current density throughout the material, which is assumed in this example. The reality

correctly model the skin eﬀect that occurs using diﬀerent form factors on the busbars. The
Finite-Diﬀerence Method divides the conductors in subdivisions that works individually,
and calculates the current density in these. The electromagnetic force can then be found,
yielding a more precise calculation of the force. The ﬁlaments are illustrated in Figure 2.5

and proximity eﬀect on conductors, but will in this project be used to accommodate for

Busbar Deformation
The use of metal busbars are used in the electrical grid, especially copper busbars, which
is also used in this example. Any solid material that is subjected to a force, reacts to it by
bending to the direction of the force. This deformation varies from material to material,
and is an important issue to highlight when designing high current setups, due to the high
electromagnetic forces. The deformation of the metal is an important issue to address,
to ensure that the metals do not bend excessively, resulting in the phases colliding and
having a contact. An understanding of the material properties of the used copper busbars
are therefore a useful information to have.

2.6
FEM Modelling
The modelling process to achieve results from the ﬁelds generated in a physics problem
can often be a challenging matter. The vast majority of problems that can be calculated
are too complicated to be done manually by hand. The geometries that enable calculation
by hand are limited to three types:
1. parallel plates
2. co-axial cylinders
3. Concentric spheres
Anything beside these geometries require a mathematical approach called FEM modelling[8].
FEM modelling stands for Finite Element modelling. It is an approach that calculates
the ﬁeld in a ﬁnite number of triangles. There is a range of software that are available
for this purpose, the software COMSOL Multiphysics is used in this project. COMSOL
requires a set of inputs to successfully model the scenario. Primarily some modelling
to determine the geometries that the scenario applies to, and the number of elements.
COMSOL concentrates the amount of elements on areas that are prone to a concentration
of a said ﬁeld, while the user only has a say in the resolution of the simulation. The elements
are calculated between the elements in each triangle. There is a linear development of
the ﬁeld between each points in the mesh that is created by COMSOL. The force is the
calculated by using the Lorentz force ﬁeld. The results can either be shown individually at
each angle, or plotted in a 1-D line plot.
2.6.1
Geometry
The geometry of the COMSOL model can be described in either 2D or 3D. The 2D method
is suﬃcient when calculating the eﬀects of straight busbars in any busbar alignments,
this is however another case with busbars that have a 90◦corner. Geometries that aren’t

2.6.3
Physics
The simulations in COMSOL requires a minimum of one physics environment to be declared.
This physics environment will clarify which physical limitations, boundaries, laws, and
approximations that can be made. Physics modules in COMSOl are categorized in a set
of categories. The ones which are used in this projects are the AC/DC and Structural
Mechanics categories.
2.6.3.1
Magnetic Fields
The Magnetic Fields physics is categorized under the AC/DC category. It is used to
calculate and visualize the magnetic ﬁelds, Lorentz Force distribution, current density,
and the electromagnetic force. The Magnetic Fields physics is also used to generate an
AC current with a frequency declared in the solver. This means that any skin eﬀect and
frequency related eﬀects are included as well. The electromagnetic force that is calculated
using the magnetic ﬁelds physics with the frequency domain solver, is the one that is being
compared to other means of electromagnetic force calculation.
2.6.3.2
Solid Mechanics
The solid mechanics physics is categorized under the Structural Mechanics category. It
is used in this project to compute and visualize the deﬂection of the busbars as a result
of the electromagnetic forces. The deﬂection is calculated by using the maximum force,
and the other electromagnetic forces for that angle, in a stationary solver. The frequency
solver is not used, due to the fact that the electromagnetic force is altering in a sine wave.
The frequency solver would use the mean value of the sine wave to calculate the total
deﬂection. The total deﬂection is not desired, because it represents the mean deﬂection
over a period, the middle busbar as an example would be practically zero, even though it
is the busbar that experiences the largest deﬂections. The stationary solver is therefore
more of use, since it can depict the actual largest deﬂection, using the electromagnetic

2.6.3.3
Electric Currents
The deﬂection of a straight busbar was calculated straight forward with the use of the
electromagnetic forces at the angle of the highest occurring electromagnetic force. This
force however, was an evenly distributed electromagnetic force, which isn’t the case with
the busbar that has a 90◦corner. The current density here, is signiﬁcantly diﬀerent at
the corner, which results in a much diﬀerent electromagnetic force and the distribution of
such. The Electric Currents physics proves useful in this problem, due to the fact that it
can have a steady input current, which can be coupled with the magnetic ﬁelds physics.
The Lorentz force distribution can then be coupled with the solid mechanics physics to
calculate the deﬂection in a stationary solver. This has the downside of excluding the skin
eﬀect, hence the reason that a small squared busbar is used when comparing the busbar
systems.
2.6.4
Solvers
Each study on COMSOL uses a minimum of one solver. The solver is used in collaboration
with the physics interfaces to calculate the desired values. The following subsections to
this section will explain the diﬀerent solvers that are used in this project.
2.6.4.1
Coil Geometric Analysis
The magnetic ﬁelds physics can generate currents in two ways. One of the two ways are by
using an external current density, which can be coupled with the current density of another
physics, this method does not require the Coil Geometric Analysis solver. The second
method is by use of coils. The busbars can be perceived as coils with a single winding in
the magnetic ﬁeld physics, which means that they can be used to generate current. The
Coil Geometric Analysis method is used combined with the frequency domain solver, to
perceive the busbars as electric coils.
2.6.4.2
Frequency Domain
The frequency domain solver is being used with the magnetic ﬁeld physics to correctly
calculate the electromagnetic force, including the skin eﬀect. The frequency domain solver
returns values that are desired, depending on an input frequency, which is set in the solver
settings.

Conclusion 9
The purpose of this thesis was to investigate the electromagnetic forces in a low voltage
busbar system, and the methods measuring them. The physics of magnetic ﬁelds and
electromagnetic forces was explained at the beginning of this thesis. This was done so
that a better understanding of the applied methods could be achieved. The theory helped
with the understanding of the methods that were implemented. During the study, it was
discovered that the majority of the research that could be found were more focused on
the L1-L2-L3 case. The information that could be found for the dual systems, which were
investigated in this thesis, were hard to come by. This resulted in the need for a common
ground for comparison of the methods, since information to compare was hard to come by.
The lab equipment provided was not suﬃcient to create large electromagnetic forces either.
This was however, enough to verify the methods that was implemented in the L1-L2-L3
case, most importantly the FEM method through COMSOL.
Having conﬁrmed the values calculated through COMSOL, resulted in a common ground
for evaluating the other methods. The IEC 60865 method performed very well in the
L1-L2-L3 case. The eﬀective distance parameter, which replaced the centre line distance
normally used, proved to be a useful parameter in including the skin eﬀect. The values were
even closer to the values from COMSOL when the eﬀective distance was diﬀerent from the
centre line distance. The case of a dual three phase system with the L1-L1-L2-L2-L3-L3
system however, did not reﬂect the highest electromagnetic force that was generated.
The Standard is therefore to be used with caution when being applied on these type
of systems, since the result only reﬂect the electromagnetic force in the direction of a
phase-to-phase short circuit, and ignores the signiﬁcantly larger electromagnetic force
between two busbars carrying the same electrical phase. The IEC 60865 method did
also not include the L1-L2-L3-L1-L2-L3 alignment, which means that the standard is not
suitable for calculating the electromagnetic forces in that system.
The L1-L2-L3-L1-L2-L3 alignment is made of two L1-L2-L3 systems, which are placed
horizontally beside each other. This system generates less electromagnetic force between
the busbars. The IEC standard would return a value that is is too high, if the method
was used. Having too high values will indicate a need for stronger materials, thus higher
costs. The FDM implementation however, could be used in any form factor and any busbar
alignment desired. The downside of the FDM implementation in this project, was that the
skin eﬀect was inaccurately represented. The skin eﬀect was represented by distributing
the current densities away from the centre of the conductor, with no respect to the sides.
Having this method resulted in the current densities being pushed into the corners of
the busbars, instead of all the sides. This meant that the FDM method was inaccurate
for higher frequencies, and lesser accurate for squared conductors. The FDM was more
67
==PAGE:76==
9. Conclusion
accurate than applying Ampere’s law without the use of FDM, which meant that the
method worked for the 50 Hz frequency. The idea behind the method is good, but a better
implementation of the skin eﬀect is needed.
There was however, no method that directly computed the deformation/displacement of
the busbars. This was done by using a simple equation. The physics behind the equation
explained that the deformation/deﬂection was directly proportional with the force applied.
The equation was veriﬁed with COMSOL simulations. The equation however, only worked
as long as the material was within the elastic region. The electromagnetic forces that was
achieved through the simulations and calculation in this thesis, was not high enough for
the material applied to reach its plastic region. The electromagnetic force was therefore, a
direct value of comparison for the deformation/deﬂection.
Verifying the methods resulted in a common ground for comparison of the diﬀerent busbar
alignments, and the parameters that aﬀected the magnitude of the electromagnetic force.
A comparison of the simulations and calculated resulted in the fact that the L1-L2-L3-
L1-L2-L3 busbar alignment, with a horizontal form factor of the busbars, returned the
lowest electromagnetic force. There is no doubt that the horizontal form factor of the
busbars are best, especially due to the fact that the electromagnetic forces are highest
horizontally, hence a lower deﬂection. The direction of the L1-L2-L3-L1-L2-L3 busbar
alignment however, was in the direction of a busbar carrying a diﬀerent phase current,
which would result in a phase-to-phase short circuit if they were to collide. The largest
electromagnetic force on the L1-L1-L2-L2-L3-L3 was in the direction of another busbar
carrying a current with the same phase current, which wouldn’t result in a phase-to-phase
short circuit. The highest electromagnetic force in a busbar the direction of another busbar
carrying a diﬀerent phase current, was the lowest of all busbar alignments investigated. A
calculation of the electromagnetic forces and the resulting deﬂection showed that a 90◦
corner on the busbars signiﬁcantly changed the current densities, resulting in a higher
concentration of the electromagnetic forces on the corner. This resulted in a signiﬁcantly
higher deﬂection, which indicated that those corners should be reinforced. This type
of busbar was not included in the comparison of the models, due to the fact that no
other method could calculate this. The busbar alignments were only evaluated using the
electromagnetic force as a point of comparison.
9.1
Future Work
Continue the veriﬁcation process of the methods using lab readings
The process of validation through an experimental setup was performed in this project,
with some signiﬁcant limitations. The current was not suﬃcient enough to have a visible
deﬂection and electromagnetic force. The equipment was limited to only having one three
phase system instead of the maximum of two, which was deﬁned in the project description.
A major part of this report is based on the fact that the COMSOL readings proved to
be reliable after comparison with lab results for the L1-L2-L3 case. A more in-depth
comparison of the values, using a better executed lab setup, would improve the credibility
for the validation of the methods, which are being used in this project.
Investigate the eﬀect of heat distribution for diﬀerent busbar alignments
68
==PAGE:77==
9. Conclusion
The eﬀect of the heat distribution and temperature rise-time is an interesting topic to
investigate. The electromagnetic force is one side of the story, where actual temperature
rises would be interesting to investigate as well.
Implement other phases in the K matrix in the FDM implementation
This thesis used a single K matrix, and made current density matrices out of this single
K matrix to simulate each phase. This only includes the skin eﬀect on the conductors,
and neglects the proximity eﬀect. This approach was used to minimize the computation
time and simplifying the method. Including the K matrix on the other phases enables the
FDM method to include the proximity eﬀect, which in theory would give more precise
calculations.
Investigate the FDM implementation and ﬁnd a new way to include the skin
eﬀect
The FDM method which was included in this thesis implemented the skin eﬀect by pushing
the current densities to the corners from the center, instead of the sides. This resulted in
the electromagnetic forces being higher as a result to the skin eﬀect, instead of lowering it,
which is the case portrayed in COMSOL. COMSOL had a correct representation of the
skin eﬀect, and it is therefore presumed to be more reliable. A method that implements
the skin eﬀect in a way where the current is being pushed to all sides, would give a better
representation, thus resulting in a better implemented method.
Experiment with vertical placement of busbars
The busbars were all placed in a horizontal line in this thesis. The IEC 60865 does also
only explain this type of busbar alignment. It is known that transmission lines can be
placed in a triangular alignment to even out the magnetic ﬁeld between each other. The
same approach could be applied on busbar systems. A triangular placement of the busbars
would introduce a stronger Y component on the electromagnetic force in a a XY plane,
which by Pythagoras theorem should decrease the amplitude of the electromagnetic force.
This type of alignment could be experienced on systems that contains multiple three phase
systems, to determine which alignment would work best.
Transient short circuit simulation
The currents that are used in this theory are steady state balanced line currents. It could
an interesting aspect to simulate and calculate on high transient- and sub-transient short
circuit currents, since these are the situations that gives the highest peaks of electromagnetic
forces. The study could be performed en three phase short circuits, dual line-to-ground
short circuits, and single line short circuits. This would give unbalanced currents, which
would be interesting to investigate. The case could be performed with three phase currents
with and without diﬀerent types of grounding.

1. Research electromagnetic forces in a three-phase system
• The electromagnetic forces and the theory of the diﬀerence methods to measure
or calculate these were described in chapter 2 - Background. The methods that
were used to measure these were brieﬂy explained, and individually investigated
in their own respected chapters.

• The origin of the busbar deformation and deﬂection was explained in section 2.4-

of the diﬀerent stages a material has is described as well. The equation for
calculating the deformation was later on veriﬁed with a FEM simulation in
chapter 5 - Simulating the Three-Phase System Using FEM modelling.
3. Understand the eﬀect of diﬀerent form factors on the electromagnetic force between
phases
• The diﬀerent form factors were all simulated and calculated upon in chapter 7.
The results that were achieved from there was analyzed in chapter 8 - Comparison
of Investigated Busbar Alignments. The eﬀect of the form factors on the busbars
on the electromagnetic forces were analyzed as well. The cross-section of the
applied form factors were more or less equal, so that the cross-section would not
be a parameter that distinguishes the analyzed electromagnetic forces between
them.
4. Suggest design guidelines for low voltage busbar systems to pass certiﬁcation
• The L1-L1-L2-L2-L3-L3 and L1-L2-L3-L1-L2-L3 alignments were both simulated
and calculated upon in chapter 7 with three form factors. The results that were
achieved from there was analyzed in chapter 8 - Comparison of Investigated
Busbar Alignments.
The eﬀect of the form factors on the busbars on the
electromagnetic forces were analyzed as well. The results were compared with
the single three phase system alignment as well. The knowledge obtained from
these analysis paved the way for suggestions to minimize the eﬀects, which are
listen in the conclusion at Chapter 9. Following these suggestions would help
decrease the electromagnetic force.
5. Perform tests and validate results
• Chapter 7 - Results includes some testing of the electromagnetic ﬁeld for the L1-
L2-L3 system. This system was the only one which had laboratory calculations,
due to insuﬃcient equipment. The results however, were enough to validate
that the methods were precise enough, most importantly, validate that FEM
through COMSOL was precise. COMSOL was therefore applied as a common
ground for comparison for the remaining scenarios, due to the fact that the
equipment available was insuﬃcient for measuring the remaining cases.
6. Compare analytical solutions with measurement results
• This learning objective was fulﬁlled by comparing all the methods in chapter 7
for the L1-L2-L3 case. The comparison proved satisfyingly precise, so that the
remaining cases could be compared to COMSOL.
