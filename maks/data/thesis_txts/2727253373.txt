Abstract
Indeed, in today’s rapidly changing business landscape, organizations must
increase their flexibility to promptly adapt to their customers’ evolving needs and
maintain a competitive edge. As a result of servitization, many business processes
cross the boundaries of individual organizations and become multi-party, which
results in goods being owned and potentially changed by multiple manipulators.
Since organizations cannot control the externalized parts of the process, the ex-
ecution of the process can deviate, leading to delays and other problems. To
address this, some research has designed an artifact-driven monitoring platform
capable of reliably monitoring the execution of outsourced business processes.
This platform monitors process execution from both the perspective of the activ-
ities being performed and their dependencies and the evolution of artifacts. To
configure the monitoring platform, a method of converting the BPMN collabo-
ration diagram into an E-GSM model and mapping criteria is already available.
However, this transformation process is currently only semi-automated.
This article aims to further automate this transformation process by auto-
matically enriching the BPMN collaboration diagram and deriving an artifact-
oriented BPMN process view.
To enrich the diagram, we gather information
from the artifacts involved in the process and integrate it as data objects into
the BPMN collaboration diagram.
For the derivation process, we automati-
cally transform the enriched BPMN collaboration diagram into a well-structured
BPMN process model, suitable for generating the E-GSM model and defining
mapping criteria by defining and implementing transformation rules. Through
implementation and evaluation, we have shown that our approach effectively au-
tomates both stages of the transformation process and produces models that can
be applied in the monitoring platform.
Keywords— Artifact, BPMN, Process Monitoring, Well-structure, Automation

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Traditionally, Business Process Management Systems(BPMSs) provide monitor mod-
ules that could automatically track the executions of processes for organizations to monitor
the execution of business processes. It is easy and straightforward to monitor single-party
business processes by BPMSs.
However, the monitoring modules based on BPMSs have problems that cannot be ig-
nored. On the one hand, BPMSs are intended to monitor full-automated, single-party busi-
ness processes. But as organizations become more competitive and service-oriented, it has
become more common for multiple organizations to be involved in the same business process.
More and more business processes became multi-party processes so that usually one orga-
nization relies on different service providers and is involved in different business processes.
Hence, it is difficult for BPMSs to provide monitoring modules of business processes in which
multiple organizations are involved. On the other hand, a BPMS requires operators to send
notifications manually when operating unautomated activities. The tasks will be difficult to
perform as the operator is likely to be distracted by manually sending start and end notifi-
cations to BPMSs. Meanwhile, it may be unreliable for monitoring modules to monitor the
execution of outsourced business processes that should be notified as quickly as the processes
start or end.
To autonomously and real-time monitor multi-party business processes in, instead of
using BPMSs monitoring modules, smart objects which can collect information on the en-
vironment they operate and exchange information with humans, information systems, or
other smart objects rely on Extended-GSM(E-GSM), which is an artifact-centric modeling
language. Deriving the E-GSM models from the well-known BPMN collaboration diagrams
is a method to help non-expert users to monitor a process by using existing process models
and modeling tools. However, translating BPMN collaboration diagrams to E-GSM models
is not full-automated.
This project addresses the previously mentioned needs by proposing a full-automated
method to enrich and extract BPMN collaboration diagrams from an artifact-oriented process
view by mining information from sensor data logs.

Background
To better understand the advantages and importance of fully automating the process
diagram with artifacts information from the BPMN collaboration diagram, this section will
first introduce the Business Process Management (BPM) lifecycle and related technologies
and tools for business process monitoring.
Subsequently, we introduced Business Process Model and Notation (BPMN), a crucial
specification for describing business processes. We outline the basic elements of BPMN,
which will also be used in the following sections. We also elaborate on the various types of
BPMN diagrams. Similarly, we provide an overview of the Extended Generalized Stochastic
Petri-net (EGSPM) model, along with its basic elements.
SMARTifact is a successful artifact-driven monitoring platform, the components of the
platform and how to configure it will be described in this section. It is important to note that
the goal of our project is to provide an automated method of obtaining process diagrams for
part of the configuration of the monitoring platform.
2.1
Business Process Monitoring
2.1.1
BPM lifecycle
The BPM lifecycle provides a path for the systematic and continuous enhancement of
the business process. The BPM lifecycle consists of six phases as in Figure 1. The details of
each phase are shown below:
• Process identification: The BPM lifecycle starts with the Process identification
phase. This phase enumerates and prioritizes the business processes in the organiza-
tion.
• Process discovery: The aim of this phase is to discover the process and capture such
information in a process model.
• Process analysis: The phase uses the as-is process model and associated documen-
tation gathered during the process discovery phase to detect and quantify weaknesses
in business processes.
• Process redesign: The goal is to improve the model by using the as-is process model
obtained in the previous phase and the insights and weaknesses in the model to help
the model solve possible issues.
• Process implementation: Process implementation covers two aspects: the automa-
tion activities and the organizational change management around the process change.
• Process monitoring and controlling: Process monitoring is facilitated by a number
of methods. By implementing the executable process model obtained in the previous
steps, relevant data is collected and analyzed to determine how well the process is
performing. And process monitoring is the part we focus on.

2.1.2
Event data logging in BPM
Event data logging, which allows the collection and storage of events relating to artifacts
and resources that interact with processes, generates event data.
Different artifacts will
generate different event data records, for example, a GPS artifact will continuously and in
real-time detect the location of the artifact and record that location data.
The initial aim of process mining is to reproduce the real process of a business process
by analyzing the logs generated by the operation of the workflow and using this information
to analyze and optimize the workflow etc. Collecting event data from sensors, information
systems, or other applications relevant to the process and mining useful information from
the event data logs is an important part of determining data objects in BPMN collaboration
models and inferring which artifacts take part in a process.
2.1.3
Conformance Checking
Conformance checking is a family of techniques to analyze the modeled behavior based
on the event data logs and the process model. There are two types of conformance checking:
replay-based conformance checking and align-based conformance checking.
Replay-based techniques mean replaying the event data log to detect if there is any
conflict or error, or more often, there is an exception that is never captured in the process
model. Align-based techniques, on the other hand, capture executions in the event log and
then compare the executions with all the executions that are admissible in the process model.

2.2

If an execution in the process model can match the execution captured, there is no violation
of the process model. If there is no execution model that can be matched with the captured
one, it means there is a violation of the execution of the process.
2.1.4
Compliance Checking
Compliance Checking is very similar to conformance checking since both of these two
types of process mining techniques are used to analyze the execution of the process model.
But there still have some identical aspects.
Compliance checking is based on the event
data log and a set of business rules containing semantic constraints representing regulations,
guidelines, policies, and laws instead of a process model and checks if the event log fulfills
the business rules. If the event log can not match the business rules, they show the violations
of these rules.
2.1.5
Runtime Performance Analysis
When a process is executed, runtime performance analysis can diagnose performance
information from the execution of the process. Runtime performance analysis is based on
real data coming from realistic process executions. Instead of using estimation data or ran-
dom data, runtime performance analysis can extract information about the process model’s
performance.
2.2
Business Process Model and Notation
Business Process Model and Notation (BPMN) is a graphical standard notation and
notation convention for describing business processes. It provides a common way to model,
visualize, analyze, and execute business processes.
The BPMN offers multiple diagrams
which are defined for the people who designed the business process.
2.2.1
Basic Elements
The BPMN model in this project consists of the following main elements:
• Swimlane: organizes activities and responsibilities of the business process.
– Pool: indicates the main players in the process and is used to separate the
different organizations.
– Lane: categorizes activities by function or role.
• Flow Object: represents the core elements of a business process diagram.
– Activity: is the task performed in the context of business processes.
∗Task: indicates an atomic work or activity within a business process.
∗Sub-Process: is a composite activity consisting of a collection of other tasks
and sub-processes.

2.2
Business Process Model and Notation
– Event: describes an event that takes place throughout a process.
– Gateway: controls the flow of processes by determining the diversion and aggre-
gation of paths based on conditions.
• Connecting Objects: connects the core objects in the BPMN model.
– Sequence Flow: represents the order in which activities and events are per-
formed.
– Message Flow: represents the flow of messages between different organizations.
– Association: describes connections between flow objects and artifacts.
• Artifacts: is mainly used to provide additional information about the business process.
– Text Annotation: adds an explanation or description of the business process.
– Data Object: indicates the data required or generated in the business process.
The state attribute is used to represent the state of a data object.
2.2.2
BPMN Diagram Types
Business Process Model and Notation (BPMN) provides a standardized graphical rep-
resentation for specifying business processes in a business process diagram (BPD). Here are
the main types of BPMN diagrams:
• Collaboration diagram: These diagrams show interactions between different par-
ticipants (e.g., people, systems) in a process. They include everything that process
diagrams do, but they also have "pools" and "lanes" to represent different participants
and their interactions.
• Business Process Diagram: These diagrams represent the sequence of activities
that make up a process. They include events (start, intermediate, and end events),
activities (tasks and sub-processes), gateways (for making decisions), and sequence
flows (to show the order of activities).
• Choreography diagram: These diagrams focus on the exchange of information (mes-
sages) between participants, rather than the sequence of activities. They show who
interacts with whom and in what order.
• Conversation diagram: These diagrams are a high-level view of the communications
within a business process. They show participants and conversation nodes, where each
conversation node aggregates a set of message exchanges.
Our projects are mainly related to the first two BPMN diagrams. The main objective of
the project is to design and implement a fully automated platform for transforming BPMN
collaboration diagrams from ordinary business processes into process diagrams enriched with
information from intelligent components, thus providing the basis for a process monitoring
platform based on the EGSM model. The BPMN collaboration diagram is therefore used as

2.3
E-GSM
E-GSM is an extension of the Guard-Stage-Milestone (GSM) declarative language for
process monitoring. It can address the issue of monitoring whether the order of execution
of monitored activities is executed in the order defined in the process model or whether the
artifacts are managed in the expected way by representing the process to be monitored.
2.3.1
GSM
GSM is one of the artifact-centric languages which focus on the business artifacts par-
ticipating in the process. The following are the major components of the GSM lifecycle:
• Stages: It presents activities in charge of interacting with and altering the state of
artifacts.
• Guards: It depicts the situation at the beginning of the execution of the stage.
• Milestones: Milestones are important nodes or key achievements. It represents the
condition that ends the execution of the stage.
• Information Model: The construction shows the current state of the artifact. It
includes data attributes and status attributes. While status attributes provide the
stage and milestone, the data attribute describes the value of the attribute.
Each stage can be closed or open. It will change according to the following rules:
• The stage is initially closed.
• When a stage is closed, it will become open if one of its guards is activated.
• When a stage is open, it will become closed if one of its milestones is activated.
• When a stage is closed, it will be re-open if one of its milestones is invalidated.
• When the stage is closed, its nest stage is closed as well.
The GSM can be used to keep track of executing activities and to determine when activities
start or end by using the condition of the guard and milestone. Because it is artifact-centric
language, it can model the process from an artifact perspective and can keep track of the
state of the artifact as well as the activities being executed. However, GSM does not have
the constructs to define the expected execution flow and determine when an activity is
incorrectly performed.

2.3.2
Extending GSM
E-GSM can support monitoring platforms by addressing the shortcomings of GSM. The
main components include not only stages, milestones and the information model which are
the same as GSM, but also some extended components, as follows:
• Data flow guard: When its conditions are satisfied, the stage will be opened.
• Process flow guard: The process flow guard which is a boolean expression models
the expected execution flow. If the expression is true, the stage complies with the
expected execution and vice versa.
• fault logger: When the condition is satisfied, it means that the stage is faulty. Even
though the stage is faulty, it does not terminate the execution of the stage.
Compared to the GSM, E-GSM does not modify its operation semantics but builds on it
with new extensions.

Internet of Things
The Internet of Things (IoT) is a paradigm that anticipates physical items that can be
individually recognized. IoT is used for sending messages and can be used to interact with
their surroundings and communicate with each other and conventional computing devices.
• Enabling Technologies of IoT: Enabling technologies of the IoT that make it pos-

• Ontologies for the IoT: To integrate smart objects and middleware produced by

3.1
Data Log Classification
To automatically enrich BPMN collaboration diagrams, it is necessary to track changes
in artifact states that influence the start and end of an activity, thereby configuring data
objects. Machine learning algorithms can use classification to distinguish how the state of
an artifact differs when the activity in a data log changes, and use the classification results
to identify data objects.
Aljabri et al. in [3] describe the use of different machine learning classification algorithms
to train classification models on collected firewall logs and to perform performance analysis.
The research concludes that it is possible to use machine learning techniques to automatically
classify firewall logs in a reliable and fast way. Thus, it is feasible to use machine learning
algorithms to analyze and classify data logs.
Andrews et al. in [4] evaluate the performance of eight machine learning models using
the same real-world dataset, which consists of HTTP log data collected from a business
network over a six-month period. After experimentation, they concluded that the K Nearest
Neighbors algorithm was more accurate. Therefore, when classifying the data log in this
project, the K Nearest Neighbor algorithm can be tested and its precision compared to
other classification algorithms.
According to [5], Ferreira et al. describe an approach to find out why some process
instances in real-world business processes take longer than usual to complete. The method
entails converting the event log into a logical form before classifying process instances ac-
cording to duration using decision tree induction. Along with dividing those instances into
various subsets, each branch of the tree produces a rule that explains why a certain subset
of instances has a different average duration from other subsets of instances. The literature
provides some inspiration for finding factors that affect the start or end of an activity in a
data log, which is similar to finding factors that affect the delay of a process instance.
Chidanand et al. in [6] presents the application of decision tree and rule induction in
data-mining. The article mentions the existence of rule induction procedures that are com-
plementary to decision tree solutions, whereby first generating a tree and then transforming
it into a set of rules. This article gives the possibility to use the decision tree algorithm to
classify data logs when solving the RQ1.1 which is mentioned in section 4, and then extract
the rules classified by the decision tree as the states of the artifacts attached to the smart
objects to try to achieve the goal from the event data logs automatically determining data
objects.
Aditya et al. in [7] design a method to classify the dataset using different machine
learning classifiers and different imputation methods, with and without feature selection
and deep learning from the Kaggle dataset. They used the correlation method for feature
selection or no feature selection and then tried to train the model using Logistic, SVM,
Random Forest, decision tree and KNN machine learning algorithms. One of the results
is that feature selection before machine learning classification can improve the accuracy of
classification. Therefore, we probably refer to their article to select features for the dataset
before classifying the data log in our project.

3.2
Structuring BPMN Diagram
The process model, as described before, represents the sequence of activities that make
up a process. As discussed in [8], there are two types of process models, well-structured
(also denoted as block-structured) and unstructured.
A well-structured process model is
constituted of blocks, which can be nested but can not overlap, and the model must not
have rigid nodes. To derive a process diagram from a collaboration diagram, we must ensure
the process diagram obtained is well-structured. Since the process diagram will next be used
to generate an E-GSM model.
In recent years, research aimed at detecting and transforming unstructured process
models into structured ones was carried out. We compared the work and outline current
limitations, trying to find a BPMN diagram optimization restructure strategy that may work
for this project.
Vanhatalo et al. in [9] present how a process model decomposition into SESE regions
as process structure tree. Single-entry, single-exit (SESE) regions can be a single activity or
a nested block. Furthermore, SESE regions possess the capability to either be sequentially
composed or remain disjointed. A well-structured model, as we described before, must only
contain SESE regions.
The concept of block-structuring is well-known in structured programming languages
and can be found in process modeling and execution languages like BPEL.
Wil M.P. et al. in [10] presents a mapping from Workflow Nets (WF-nets) to BPEL.
The mapping could be used for mapping BPEL to the BPMN model based on the rich
theory of Petri nets. They also implemented the algorithm they proposed in a tool named
WorkflowNet2BPEL4WS. Wei Zhao et al.
in [11] discusses an approach called Regular
Expression Language (REL) that can be used for compiling controlled code complexity
with a business process model that is irreducible with unstructured loops to statements in
structured languages. Converting a BPMN model to a structured programming language
model is one of the restructuring approaches.
Rong Liu et al. in [12] described a taxonomy that provides a framework for analyzing
unstructured workflows. The framework organized unstructured workflows from two aspects:
improper nesting and mismatched split-join pairs. They also described two situations: well-
structured workflows and unstructured workflows and discussed unstructured workflows that
mapped structured workflows.
Hauser, Rainer Friedrich, et al. in [13] introduced a concept for a workflow model,
named region tree(RT), which can be used for the central data structure for not only workflow
analysis and workflow transformation. This work provides fundamentals for structuring the
BPMN diagram.
Polyvyanyy et al.
in [14] provided techniques for transforming hidden unstructured
regions into block-structured ones. They defined a classification of arbitrarily structured
process models based on techniques of the hierarchical process model decomposition and
identified a structural class of models that consist of block-structured patterns. The complex
behavior could be defined by examining the loops in hidden unstructured regions of control
flows and the issues for defining an unstructured model could be analyzed by the complex
behavior.

This section presents the main issues of automatically generating artifact-oriented pro-
cess models from BPMN collaboration diagrams and the main research questions.
4.1
Research Challenges
To generate artifact-oriented process models from BPMN collaboration diagrams au-
tonomously, the following main issues have to be addressed:
• C1: Enriching the BPMN Process model with artifacts autonomously. Smart
objects that are provided sensing capabilities, computational power, and transmission
interfaces by the IoT paradigm can collect information from the environment and
contact other smart objects, operators, and systems. Changes in smart objects can
detect the state of the artifacts. Relying on the smart objects, BPMN collaboration
diagrams can be enriched by using standard BPMN data objects. for BPMN process
model enrichment, it is crucial to autonomously and accurately mine information from
processes and data logs to determine and add data objects. The first major challenge
will be how to obtain the required information from known data logs and collaboration
diagrams and how to encapsulate the information in data objects and add it to the
model.
• C2: Extracting the artifact-oriented process view without human opera-
tors. In a BPMN collaboration diagram, different organizations and business units
are responsible for executing different activities defined by the pool and lane constructs.
However, an artifact is involved with multiple organizations and participates in activ-
ities relying on multiple organizations and service providers. To model a process from
the viewpoint of an artifact, irrelevant activities, events, or other elements should be
excluded, message flows should be replaced with sequence flows, and an activity can
be involved with multiple organizations. Following the previous steps to transform the
enriched BPMN collaboration diagram into an artifact-oriented process view diagram
can be more efficient than defining a new model from scratch. However, there is no
automatic and accurate approach to converting the BPMN collaboration diagram into
a process diagram. The second challenge is to fully automate the conversion of BPMN
collaboration diagrams, which already have information on artifacts(data objects), into
a well-structured BPMN process diagram with an artifact perspective.

Enriching BPMN Collaboration Diagram
A BPMN collaboration diagram illustrates the involvement of multiple organizations
and business units in a business process. Different organizations and business units carry
out different activities and events. The connections and dependencies between these organi-
zations and business units are depicted through pools, events, and message flows in a BPMN
collaboration diagram.
However, such a BPMN collaboration diagram lacks information concerning the relevant
smart objects which are responsible for monitoring the process.
In this section, we use
data logs obtained from various smart artifacts to mine the relationships between these
artifacts and the various events and activities in the BPMN collaboration diagram. Then
the information obtained is used to automatically enrich the original BPMN collaboration
diagram. The source data logs and BPMN diagram should satisfy the following conditions:
• DR1: The data logs need to contain data from at least one artifact. As the enrichment
process relies on features related to artifacts, this condition is fundamental for the
effectiveness of the enrichment.
• DR2: The data logs need to contain timestamps, process instance numbers, and
corresponding activities referring to the BPMN diagram or at least can derive the
corresponding activities from other data. To obtain states that can be used in the E-
GSM model for the activities corresponding to the BPMN diagrams that can interact
with the artifacts, a large amount of time-stamped data that has been manually defined
for the activities is essential.
• DR3: The BPMN diagram is well-structured. Since the enrichment process adds to
the BPMN diagram by modeling the artifacts with data objects, it is crucial that the
BPMN diagram is well-structured.
• DR4: The BPMN diagrams and the data logs must be consistent. As mentioned in
DR2 and DR3, it is essential for the data logs to contain activities that correspond to
the elements in the BPMN diagram.
• DR5: All activities in the data logs must correspond to the activities in the original
BPMN diagram.
Additionally, the preceding and following activities must directly
correspond to the preceding and following activities in the original BPMN diagram.
The data that conflicts with the sequence flow or message flow of the BPMN diagram
or is missing intermediate activities will have an impact on the accuracy of the clas-
sification. Consequently, it is essential to ensure that the data is consistent with the
original BPMN diagram in order to assure correctness.
If the source data logs and BPMN diagram satisfy these conditions, an enriched BPMN
diagram suitable for deriving an artifact-view process model can be automatically produced
by following the steps.

5.2
Classification of Activities
The categorization process bifurcates into two integral components: the comparative
analysis of diverse classification algorithms, and the procurement of classification rules.
5.2.1
Different Classification Algorithms
In the context of our scholarly discourse, the dataset, which has been subjected to
extensive preprocessing, is meticulously analyzed with a distinct objective: to identify and
categorize disparate attributes inherent to each intelligent component encapsulated within
the data. This procedure is integral to our pursuit of understanding the underlying structure
and potential correlations within the complex body of data.
Following the analytical phase, the dataset is strategically partitioned into two discrete
subsets. The first subset is designated as the training set, intended to be used for the
purpose of developing and refining our model. The second subset, the test set, is set aside
to evaluate the performance and reliability of the model, thereby maintaining the integrity
of our procedural methodology and avoiding any potential bias or overfitting. The process of
segmenting the dataset into training and testing sets is not conducted via complete random
selection. As described above, the dataset is made up of logs from multiple process instances,
and if it is divided randomly, there is a risk of uneven distribution, which could result in
an activity or event not being tested. Consequently, the test set is constituted by selecting
logs from one or more randomly selected process instances in their entirety. This approach
ensures the comprehensiveness and accuracy of the tests.
To carry out the classification, we employ a diverse array of classification techniques,
each with its own strengths and potential applications as described in section 2.
These

5.3.3
Merging redundant conditions in JSON file
There are two issues that arise when creating the rules for the decision tree: the first is
that some redundant text features may exist, and the second is that numeric columns may
be compared more than once during classification, leading to a lot of redundant conditions
in the rules (for example, speed > 41 and speed > 50 may exist in a rule at the same.
Therefore, each condition of a rule in the decision tree needs to be processed according to
the following rules before it can be used as the state of a data object in order to address
these two issues:
• If the column of the condition is a text feature, the following situations will be handled:
– If the value of the text feature is True. The conditions in that rule that have a
False value for that text feature should be removed.
For instance, If there is a condition in rules with city_Coqulles = True, all
other conditions with a text feature of the city and a value of False in that rule
will be removed.
• If the column of the condition is a numeric feature, there are two ways it will be handled
– If there is more than one condition with the same numeric feature greater than
certain values, the condition with the smaller value will be removed.
For instance, if speed > 40 and speed > 50 are both in the same rule, then
speed > 40 will be removed.
– If there is more than one condition with the same numeric feature less than certain
values, the condition with the larger value will be removed.
For instance, if speed <= 40 and speed <= 50 are both in the same rule,
then speed <= 50 will be removed.

6
Deriving BPMN Process View
In the previous section, we obtained a fully automated BPMN collaboration diagram
enriched by artifacts through an original BPMN diagram and data logs. The collabora-
tion diagram obtained has pools and lanes to show the organizations and business units
associated with and responsible for each activity. In addition, the BPMN collaboration di-
agram has corresponding message and sequence flows to coordinate the interdependencies
between the different organizations and business units responsible for the different activities.
Such a BPMN collaboration diagram has many advantages: the roles and responsibilities of
the different actors can be clearly defined, enhancing the overall intuitiveness of the BPMN
diagram, and facilitating more straightforward analysis and modification for the model. Fur-
thermore, it offers a transparent viewpoint on the interplay between various organizational
entities and business units. But for this project, as deliberated previously, the artifacts are
free to move between the diverse organizations and business units. Various organizations can
collaboratively utilize the same set of artifacts. For a project that uses artifacts to monitor
processes, it is not necessary to know which organization or business unit is responsible for
the activity the artifact performs at each moment in a process. On the other hand, for
different artifacts, not all activities and events are associated with that artifact. Activities
and events that are not related to the artifact cannot be monitored by the information of
the artifact.
Therefore, in this section, we propose a set of rules for the automated extraction and
modification of the previously derived BPMN collaboration diagram to remove information
related to the organization and the business unit as well as activities that are not related
to the artifact. For different artifacts, a well-structured process view with different artifact
perspectives will eventually be obtained completely automatically.
In order to obtain a flowchart with an artefactual perspective from the BPMN collab-
oration diagram enriched in the previous chapter, the following four steps are required: (i)
Make the activities not limited to the organization or business unit, (ii) Make the message
flow and sequence flow indistinguishable, (iii) remove irrelevant activities and events, and
(iv) give the resulting flowchart a better structure.
By adhering to the steps outlined, the transformation from a BPMN collaboration
diagram to an artifact-centric process diagram can be completely automated, as long as the
source BPMN collaboration diagram satisfies the following conditions:
• DR1: The BPMN collaboration diagram is enriched with data objects. Relying on
the standard BPMN data objects, the BPMN collaboration diagram can be used to
present information about the different artifacts and process interactions. Since the
transformation rules rely on the enriched BPMN collaboration diagram, this condition
is critical for the transformation to work.
• DR2: The artifact that needs to generate the process view must be deterministic.
Since in a BPMN collaboration diagram there is at least one artifact with which to
interact. Specifying the process diagram for the viewpoint of the required artifact is
one of the conditions.
Page 29 of 88
==PAGE:35==
Figure 9: BPMN collaboration diagram enriched with information on the artifacts that
participate in the process
• DR3: The input BPMN collaboration diagram must be sound. The soundness of
a BPMN diagram is encapsulated in three fundamental characteristics: (i) a process
instance can complete for any situation once the execution starts, (ii) when an instance
of the process ends, there must be no activity still running or enabled, and (iii) the
process model can not contain a dead activity that can not run for all the traces.

• DR4: The source diagram is well-structured. Since the derivation can not change the
behavior of the BPMN diagram, if the input diagram is unstructured, it is unexpected
that the out diagram is a well-structured BPMN diagram.
Assuming the source BPMN collaboration diagram meets these conditions, it is possi-
ble to automatically generate a well-structured artifact-view process diagram that can be
subsequently converted into a final E-GSM model for use in the process monitoring platform.

• If there is more than one end event, an end event is introduced to indicate the end
of the process and is called "Process End" and a merging parallel gateway is added
before the new start event. The recorded intermediate throw events converted from

flow are the intermediate throw events and the target is the merging parallel gateway
we created. Connect the merging parallel gateway and the super end event by another
new sequence flow. This way we add a super end event to the BPMN model.
• Record the added events and they will be used in rules for removing irrelevant elements.

6.3.5
Removing Events
As with activity, events constitute a pivotal part of the BPMN diagram, often repre-
senting a significant occurrence that can affect the process flow. Removing irrelevant events
proves to be substantially more complex than removing irrelevant activities since events not
only can interact with artifacts but also represent the binding and unbinding of smart ar-
tifacts to the process. The rules and conditions concerning the removal of irrelevant events
are detailed explicitly by case, under the assumption that the process view of the Artifact
Ar that is to be derived:
Rule Events not interacting with Ar are removed.
• If the events have one or more input or output data objects which are related to Ar, and
these data objects have a corresponding data state, such events are not responsible for
binding or unbinding Ar to the process. Instead, they represent these events directly
interacting with or affecting Ar a smart artifact. Consequently, such events will be
preserved.
On the other hand, if Ar’s data objects associated with the events are not specified
with a particular state, these events are responsible for signaling the initiation or
termination of Ar’s interaction with the business process. Since the process view to
be derived is Ar’s viewpoint, there is no necessity for events to specifically highlight
the start and end of the interaction in this model. Other elements that interact with
or are influenced by Ar are already monitored by the corresponding Ar’s data objects.
Therefore, such events are no longer needed and relevant and will be removed from the
BPMN diagram. Upon the removal of an event, the element before it and the element
after it are connected by a new sequence flow, and the corresponding condition of the
original sequence flows is retained.
• If the event has one or more input or output data objects which relate to Ar’ (unlike the
smart artifact Ar given above) and these data objects lack a corresponding data state,
then such event is responsible for binding or unbinding Ar’ to the process. Since Ar’ is

6.4
Re-strcuturing BPMN Diagram
After the preceding steps, we have essentially provided an artifact-oriented process view.
However, due to the numerous operations handled within the BPMN collaboration diagram:
(i) adding several elements (e.g. start event, end event, parallel gateway, etc.), (ii) changing
the type of many elements (e.g. converting start event to intermediate catch event), (iii) and
removing numerous elements (e.g. unrelated events, activities, etc.), the resulting diagram
is not necessarily well formed. The next step involves processing the gateways and flows, op-
timizing the BPMN diagram with the BPStruct tool, and finally obtaining a well-structured
BPMN diagram.
6.4.1
Removing Repeated Flows
Rule Repeated sequence flows are removed.
• If more than two sequence flows have the same source element, target element, and
condition, then these sequence flows are duplicates.
• Iterate through all the sequence flows in the BPMN model and remove the duplicate
sequence flows. Make sure that each sequence flow is unique.
• If two sequence flows have the same source element and target element, but they have
different conditions. Consequently, the two sequence flows should be merged based on
the conditions, keeping the source and target elements.
6.4.2
Removing Sequence Flows between Two Parallel Gateways
In the previous step, several sequence flows and paralleled gateways were inevitably
added. paralleled gateways are divided into splitting paralleled gateways and merging par-
alleled gateways. when the corresponding splitting parallel gateway and merging parallel
gateway are directly connected by a sequence flow, there is no condition for the splitting
parallel gateway to choose a path.
This means that the sequence flow between the two
gateways will receive a token and pass it directly to the merging parallel gateway after the
splitting parallel gateway fires.
However, this is meaningless since the merging parallel gateway needs to wait for all the
other tokens from other incoming sequence flows of the same instance before it will generate
a token in its outgoing flow. Hence, sequence flows are removed following the rules:
Rule Sequence flows which directly connect a splitting parallel gateway and a merging
parallel gateway.

then the type of the two parallel gateways will be checked.
• If the source parallel gateway is a splitting parallel gateway and the target parallel
gateway is a merging parallel gateway, then the paths between the two parallel gateways
will be checked.
• If there is another path between the two parallel gateways to reach the merging parallel

Prepare for BPstruct Restructuring
BPStruct stands as an ingeniously devised instrument purposed for the restructuring of
unstructured Business Process Models and Notation (BPMN) diagrams into ones of struc-
tured diagrams. BPStruct transforms unstructured models into structured ones by applying
a set of restructuring operations that preserve the behavior of the original model.
As described in section 3, we compared several BPMN restructure projects and tools
and finally chose BPStruct as the tool to restructure the BPMN diagram for several reasons:
• The conversion process and correctness of BPStruct have been verified to output well-
structured BPMN diagrams as long as the input BPMN diagram is eligible.
• The project code for BPStruct is open-source and the source code can be found on
GitHub or Google Driver. The entire project runs on an older version of the JAVA
environment but can be used through compatible deployment and re-writing code.
• BPStruct is also used in several other projects and inspiration can be found through
other open-source projects.
To use BPStruct, The original BPMN diagram needs to meet the following conditions:
• BPMN 2.0: BPStruct supports BPMN 2.0 diagrams.
• Flat Process: BPStruct works with flat processes, i.e., the process should not contain
sub-processes.

• Single Start and End Event: The process should have exactly one start event and
one end event.
• No Boundary Event: The process should have no boundary event.
• Sound Model: The process model should be sound, i.e., it should be free from

not satisfy the input required conditions of BPStruct. Therefore, additional processing is
necessary before the BPMN diagram can be used as the original diagram for BPStruct:
• Boundary Event: If there is one or more boundary events within the original BPMN
diagram, then depending on the type of boundary events the following needs to be
done to it (similar to handling boundary events in removing phase):
– boundary non-blocking events convert into a temporary intermediate event, with
the exclusive gateway converting the temporary intermediate event and the ele-
ment after the attached activity using the no condition branch.
– boundary blocking events convert into a temporary intermediate event, with the
inclusive gateway converting the temporary intermediate event and the element
after the attached activity using the no condition branch.
– The original boundary events and associated elements are recorded and can be
used after BPStruct restructuring.
• Sub-process: If the original BPMN diagram contains one or more sub-process, then
the sub-process activity needs to be collated as a normal activity. activities, events,
gateways, and sequence flows within the sub-process will not appear in the input pro-

in the BPStruct, respectively, and the type, name, and id are entered into the BPStruct as
information. sequence flows correspond to flows in the BPStruct. Automatic completion of
the well-structure process by BPStruct and acquisition of a new process diagram. The new

8.2
Evaluation for Derivation
In order to validate the derivation part of the deployment and implementation, 20
already published or public BPMN diagrams that meet the input requirements for a realistic
process were selected for simulation and validation. The derived diagrams were analyzed
from different perspectives (i) whether they were syntactically correct, (ii) whether they
conformed to the rules, and (iii) whether they did not conflict with the traces of the original
diagram. The original and derived BPMN diagram (with the source) used in the validation
section will be shown in the appendix.
8.2.1
Test Diagram Source
The BPMN collaboration diagrams used for validation were all sourced from published
journals, magazines, books, or publicly available repositories. Compared to manually gen-
erated BPMN collaboration diagrams, diagrams sourced from published articles or publicly
available databases ensure that BPMN diagrams are well-structured and well-behaved. Such
BPMN diagrams satisfy the requirements of correctness, soundness, and effectiveness, and
they are free from issues such as dead tasks or lack of synchronization. Moreover, the major-
ity of these BPMN diagrams are obtained from real processes in real-world scenarios, making
them more relevant and less detached from reality. In the validation section, it is essential
to be strict about the requirements of BPMN diagrams used for validation, as inputting
a BPMN diagram with bad behavior leads to a similar BPMN diagram with bad behav-
ior, thereby making the validation section pointless. In summary, to ensure that the input
BPMN collaboration diagrams are the correct model, we have chosen to use the published
or public BPMN diagrams.
On the other hand, not all BPMN diagrams can be used as input images for evalua-
tions since there are several conditions the diagrams must satisfy. We select proper BPMN
diagrams in our diagram library based on the following criteria:
• The BPMN diagram must be a BPMN collaboration diagram, which means the BPMN
diagram must have pools and each element must be within a pool.
• The BPMN diagram must have at least one data object.
Since our derivation is
based on the artifact perspective, the BPMN diagram must have activities or events
interacting with the artifacts.
• The BPMN diagram must be from a known source. If a BPMN diagram cannot be
traced back to its source, it cannot be used as a test BPMN diagram for evaluation.
Page 74 of 88
==PAGE:80==
8.2
Evaluation for Derivation
The BPMN diagrams selected by the above criteria will be used as the test BPMN
diagrams for the evaluation section. In the appendix, these BPMN diagrams are presented
in turn, with the source of each model indicated. However, the models used for evaluation
cannot be used directly because many of them, although they meet the criteria mentioned
before, do not satisfy the conditions of the original diagram used as the derivation section
shown in section 6.
For each of these diagrams we have carried out the following pre-
processing:
• If the model only has a display image of the model, the model is reverted and modeled
in the Camunda modeler based on the display image. The modeling requires that
the structure, text, and elements of the original model are not changed.
• If there is already a .bpmn file corresponding to the model, the file is checked to see if
it conforms to the input format. If it does not conform to the input format (e.g. if it
is not a BPMN 2.0 standard model), the model is restored and rebuilt in Camunda
modeler based on the existing model. The modeling requires that the structure, text,
and elements of the original model are not changed.
• If the data objects in the BPMN diagram do not have states, the states are added
to the data objects in the model randomly; the addition of states does not affect the
behavior and structure of the BPMN diagram.
• If there are no events in the BPMN diagram that are responsible for binding or un-
binding the indicated artifacts to the process, they are added manually.
• If the model misses gateways or other elements(conditions, element names, etc.), they
will be added manually. The added elements cannot change the behavior of the original
diagram.
The BPMN diagrams that meet the input criteria are automatically derived to obtain a
new BPMN process diagram, which is also shown in the appendix after the original BPMN
diagram.
8.2.2
Syntax Checking
In order to verify that the models obtained through the automated derivation opera-
tions we have deployed are syntactically correct, rules-compliant, and well-structured, the
following analysis will be performed on each diagram:
1 The output model will be checked with Camunda Modeler and Signavio. For Camunda
Modeler check, if, after inputting the model, no warnings or errors are found and the
BPMN diagram is correctly displayed, the input model is semantically correct. The
process diagram obtained by fully automatic derivation conforms to the BPMN rules.
If a warning or error occurs or is displayed incompletely after the input model, then
the model does not conform to the BPMN rules. For Signavio, choose Check Syntax
and Signavio can check the syntax automatically. If the Signavio shows Plausibility
check successful, it means the model is semantically correct.

2 If the output model does not contain pools and lanes, and there is no information

3 Check if the output model contains message flows and see if the XML file in Canmunda

4 Manually compare the output model with the original model to see if all extraneous

8.2.3
Tracking and Comparing Traces
In order to verify that the BPMN diagram and the original model by automatic deriva-
tion are models with the same behaviors and traces, we checked and compared the traces of
the original and output models.
As the tools for the BPMN model (e.g. pm4py, proM, Camunda, etc.) do not support
checking the traces of the BPMN collaboration diagram. Otherwise, these tools do not give
Page 77 of 88
==PAGE:83==
8.2
Evaluation for Derivation
all the traces directly, instead, these tools can execute an instance of the model and output
the traces of the execution process. Hence, the tools should execute multiple times of the
process and still may not show all the traces. The process of checking the traces was done
manually.
On the other hand, validation and comparison of traces should be bi-directional. It is
essential to confirm that all traces found in the original BPMN collaboration diagram can be
accurately found in the derived BPMN process diagram. Similarly, it is equally important
to ensure that all traces within the derived BPMN process diagram are indeed compatible
with the original BPMN collaboration diagram.
1 Open the derived model and the original model in Camunda Modeler. Since we
already check the syntactical correctness of the derived model as we mentioned before,
the derived model can be read and presented by the tool.
2 Check all the traces one by one in the derived model and record them.
3 For each of the recorded traces, we replay it in the original diagram to check if the
trace has any conflict with the trace of the original model. If all the recorded traces
are compatible with the original diagram, then we can verify the behavior and traces
of the derived diagram are not changed after using the automatic derivation tool we
implemented before.
4 Check all the traces one by one in the original model and record them. But for the
original diagram trace tracking, we ignored the elements that are removed in the derived
diagram.
5 For each of the recorded traces, we try to find a trace in the derived model that can
match the recorded trace. If all the traces are matched with a trace in the derived
process diagram, we can verify all the traces are not lost from the original diagram to
the derived diagram.

9
Conclusion
This thesis presented an approach to automatically enrich a BPMN collaboration dia-
gram with artifacts and then derive an artifact-oriented process diagram which means that
the first two steps of the configure monitoring platform are automated.
In order to automate the enrichment, the BPMN collaboration diagram needs to add
data objects with and without state. The data object with states determines when an activity
has started or ended by using the state while the data object without a state determines
the binding and unbinding of artifacts. The BPMN collaboration diagram requires that
state data objects are added to the diagram based on the results of the classification of
activity pairs by the decision tree. The binding and unbinding of the artifact in the BPMN
collaboration diagram are determined based on the added data object with states.
The
data object without a state is then used to enrich the BPMN collaboration diagram with
information from the binding and unbinding artifacts. The outcome of enrichment is an
enriched BPMN collaboration diagram which can be used when deriving the artifact-oriented
process view.
To achieve the derivation part, the enriched BPMN collaboration diagram obtained in
the enrichment part will be used as the input of the derivation part and the derivation will
transform it into an artifact-oriented process model. When designing and implementing the
deviation part, elements in the diagram that are not related to artifacts will be deleted or
replaced. In order to make the BPMN process diagram well-structured, the tool BPStruct
is used to do the optimization. The outcome of derivation is a well-structured process model
which can be used when processing the transformation.
For the evaluation of the enrichment part, the SMARTifact monitoring platform is
used to simulate the experiment. The performance of enriching the BPMN collaboration
diagram is validated by comparing the results of the process execution of the E-GSM engine
in the SMARTifact platform monitoring the manually generated enriched BPMN model
and rules with our automatically generated ones. The experimental results prove that the
automatically enriched BPMN collaboration diagram and the manual ones are consistent in
the monitoring platform for process execution.
For the evaluation of the derivation part, the experiment is tested from two aspects. The
first aspect is to test whether the syntax of the generated BPMN process model is correct.
The second perspective is to determine whether the original process collaboration diagram
overwrites traces of the generated BPMN process model and has the same behavior. The
experimental results show that the generated artifact-oriented BPMN process model satisfies
these two aspects.
The result of both evaluations showed that the automation of the BPMN collabora-
tion diagram and the automatic derivation of the artifact-oriented BPMN process model is
compliant with the requirements. Therefore, the method and implementation of automation
designed for this project are feasible and reasonable.

9.1
Answers to the Research Questions
• RQ1: How to enrich the BPMN collaboration diagram by mining artifact/sensor data
from process logs automatically? By pre-analyzing the event data logs and adopting
the classification algorithms, the data objects can be automatically generated.
– RQ1.1: How to automatically determine data objects from event data logs?
Through the utilization of multiple classification algorithms, event data logs can
be classified based on the attributes of the artifacts. By comparing the precision,
recall, and f1-score value, the optimal algorithm for the classification of event
data logs can be identified. The decision tree is the best-performing classification
algorithm among decision tree, random forest, KNN, gradient boosting decision
tree, and multinomial naive bayes, and is therefore utilized for obtaining the
required data objects. the output of the classification rules after classification by
the decision tree is used as the state of the data objects. Hence, following the
aforementioned methodology, the determination of data objects can be automated
via event data logs.
– RQ1.2: How to add data objects to the BPMN collaboration diagram? How to
ensure the number of the input data object and the output data object of each
artifact and each activity?
Based on the original BPMN diagram and the event data logs, obtain the activity
pairs for each process instance from the data logs, which contain two activities
corresponding to the BPMN diagram, and add the data objects containing the
activity pair and the classification rules one by one to the BPMN diagram. The
activity pairs also guarantee that an activity will have at least one input data
object and one output data object. After adding the stated data objects, the
traces of the BPMN diagram are tracked and eventually no state data objects are
added which indicates that the artifacts and processes are bound and unbound.
– RQ1.3: How can added relationships and associations between data objects and
other elements have no conflicts with message flows and sequence flows?
In the process of adding data objects, the trace of the BPMN diagram is checked.
The trace of the BPMN collaboration diagram is not only considered sequence
flows but also sequence flows, as different Pools interact with each other via mes-
sage flows. Not all data objects are added to the BPMN diagram, those that
have associations that do not match the trace are not added to the BPMN. This
ensures that the relationships and associations added between data objects and
other elements do not conflict with message flows and sequence flows.
• RQ2: How can derive artifact-driven BPMN process view from existing BPMN collab-
oration diagram?
By designing and implementing the rules on how to derive the BPMN process dia-
gram from the artifact’s viewpoint, the original BPMN collaboration diagram can be
automatically derived.

– RQ2.1: How to exclude the activities and events that do not interact with the
artifact?
Design detailed rules to distinguish whether different activities and events interact
with artifacts. The elements in the BPMN collaboration diagram are checked one
by one based on the rules and if they meet the condition of interacting with
artifacts, they are kept, if they do not interact, they are considered irrelevant and
removed. By strictly enforcing these rules, it is ensured that irrelevant activities
and events are removed.
– RQ2.2: How to make activities not be confined within a single organization?
The BPMN collaboration diagram uses pools and lanes to represent the different
organizations in that different BPMN elements are involved and the interactions
between them. Removing pools and lanes in BPMN collaboration diagrams, en-
suring that activities and events no longer belong to a separate, specific organi-
zation.
– RQ2.3: How to replace message flows with sequence flows?
In the BPMN collaboration diagram, as elements between different pools interact
with each other, the interaction between them is achieved through message flows.
With the removal of BPMN pools, the distinction between message flows and
sequence flows is no longer necessary and message flows will naturally need to
be removed. However, deleting message flows directly will result in the loss of
interaction information between BPMN elements in different pools. Therefore,
message flows are replaced with sequence flows, and all information about message
flows is recorded and kept.
– RQ2.4: How to make the BPMN diagram become well-structure?
A series of rules were designed to optimize gateways, flows, and loops, comple-
mented by the deployment of the BPStruct tool for final acyclic process model
refinement. This approach facilitated the automatic optimization of unstructured
BPMN diagrams, culminating in the output of a well-structured BPMN process
diagram.

While we have accomplished comprehensive automation in enrichment and derivation,
numerous limitations and shortcomings persist. Future endeavors will strive to analyze these
deficiencies, optimizing the present automation process to yield superior and more suitable
outcomes.
9.2.1
Limitation
The limitations of enrichment and derivation are analyzed as followed:
• Limitation of Enrichment:

Current Limitation and Future Work
In the pre-analyzing section, activity pairs are identified and used to classify each
process instance. While the usage of activity pairs ensures that every activity possesses
both input and output data objects, it also constrains the generation of data objects.
Specifically, for a data log, in instances where the process model is intricate with
multiple concurrent activities, there exists a risk of conflict.
For a given activity in the process model, there might exist multiple input or output
data objects.
If these data objects are referred to interact with different artifacts,
defining how to amalgamate these diverse data objects as initiating or concluding
conditions for the activity becomes challenging.
The rules derived from the classification algorithm are not directly applicable to the
E-GSM models. Consequently, these rules must be transformed into a distinct label
that can function as states for the data objects.
• Limitation of Derivation:
We rewrote and deployed an open-source project, named BPStruct, to restructure the
acquired process diagram and consequently attain a well-structured process diagram.
Despite the age of the project, the BPStruct tool proficiently transforms diagrams that
satisfy the input prerequisites into well-structured ones. However, due to the project’s
published time, it necessitates a comparatively old configuration environment. While
we have successfully integrated this project into our implementation, and it functions
satisfactorily in the majority of examples, there are still cases where BPStruct is not
available.
9.2.2
Future Work
The future work of enrichment and derivation are analyzed as followed:
• Future Work of Enrichment:
Future research may follow two potential directions to address the problem associated
with the enrichment section concerning data object generation and classification. One
approach could be to persist with the added attribute of activity pairs. More con-
straints can be added to the pre-analyzing of obtaining pairs to obtain activity pairs
that fit the model better and ultimately generate more appropriate data objects. Al-
ternatively, there could be an exploration of different methods to ascertain associated
activities for the generated data objects.
For the automation of rule conversion into states applicable in the E-GSM model, a
potential avenue could involve leveraging machine learning algorithms for an extensive
learning-based transformation of rules into states.
• Future Work of Derivation:
There exist two plausible solutions and work directions for the optimization of process
diagrams.
Firstly, we could persist in utilizing BPStruct, delving into the reasons
why certain diagrams can not function as expected, and subsequently optimizing and
