Abstract
In the initial phase of the project, the curved photovoltaic (PV) module underwent a thor-
ough evaluation to ensure no physical damages post-transportation. This evaluation in-
cluded electroluminescence testing and thermal imaging to detect internal defects and
uniformity tests across the x, y, and z planes, adjusted for the module’s curvature us-
ing a reference cell. High-capacitance adjustments were made before characterizing the
module through flash-tests. Field-tests involved mounting the module on a PV array and
performing IV-tracing over two separate days, with data corrected to Standard Test Condi-
tions (STC) using DS:EN 60891:2021’s Procedure 1. The second part focused on deriv-
ing characterization values from the curved module to recreate measurement scenarios
from field and flash tests. This process involved utilizing an open-source Python library
and PVlibs De Soto Single Diode Method for model validation, demonstrating a com-
prehensive approach to assessing and modelling curved PV modules for solar energy
applications.
Incorporating MotionLab and Energy Yield Model insights, the project further examined
the analysis of energy yield predictions.
Despite the models performing satisfactorily
across various IV-curve model tests, the MotionLab data revealed a notable discrepancy,
where the Energy Yield Model fell short of actual measured values. This discrepancy,
despite validation through field and flash tests, underscores the challenges in accurately
predicting energy yields in real-world conditions. The divergence highlights the necessity
for model refinement, particularly in enhancing the predictive accuracy of energy yields,
to better align with empirical observations.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
The relentless pursuit of a sustainable future has accelerated the integration of renewable
energy sources into a variety of daily technologies. This is not just a consequence of tech-
nological evolution, but it is an direct response to global environmental challenges and the
urgent need to reduce our carbon footprint in every sector. One significant advancement
that stands testament to this green revolution is the emergence of Vehicle-integrated pho-
tovoltaic (VIPV) panels within the automobile sector.
Unlike their conventional counterparts, which adorn our rooftops and vast solar farms in
planar arrays, VIPVs have evolved in design to cater to vehicle application. They often
manifest in a distinctive curved form, an ingenious adaptation that allows them to meld
perfectly with the aerodynamic and aesthetic contours of modern vehicles. This integra-
tion not only enhances the vehicle’s energy efficiency but also signals a shift in how we
envision the cooperation of mobility and sustainable energy[1, 2].
With this innovation new challenges approach. The unique curvature of VIPVs, while ad-
vantageous for vehicle design, welcome new challenges for power generation, and affect
the solar income and electrical properties of the PV module. It introduces complexities
in their characterization, electrical properties/behaviour, performance evaluation, and ef-
ficiency metrics, setting the stage for some of the central issues this study seeks to ad-
dress[3, 2].
VIPV systems represent a significant advancement in the integration of renewable energy
sources with automotive technology. The unique aspect of VIPV systems is their ability
to incorporate PV panels into the surfaces of vehicles, such as roofs, hoods, and doors.
This integration not only contributes to the vehicle’s energy generation but also presents
unique challenges in terms of panel curvature, aesthetic considerations, and performance
under variable irradiance conditions[1, 2].
Curved PV panels pose significant challenges for characterization and modelling due to
their non-uniform irradiance profile, which results from their curved surfaces. Traditional
methods used for flat PV panels fall short in accurately capturing the performance of
curved panels. The development of a collimated solar simulator, as detailed in the work
of Vallerotto et al.[4], represents a significant advancement in this area, offering a method
to characterize these panels under conditions that closely mimic natural sunlight.
1.2
Problem Statement
The integration of VIPV, presents a unique challenges due to their curved nature, contrast-
ing significantly with traditional planar PV panels. This curvature necessitates a reevalua-
tion and adjustment of existing characterization and measurement methodologies, as the
solar geometry’s impact on these curved panels leads to a non-uniform irradiance distri-
bution across the all the cells in the module. Traditional flash-testing methods, struggle to
account for the three-dimensional curvature, making them inadequate without significant
adjustments. Also, the dynamic operational environment of vehicles introduces additional
complexities. VIPV face constantly changing irradiance levels, alternating shadow and
obstacle profiles, and variations in angle relative to the sun, also when parking in urban
areas, all of which affect each cell within a module differently. This dynamic scenario

1.4
Objective of the Study
1.4.1
Research Objectives
The primary objectives of this study are:
• To implement and evaluate the curved module flash testing procedure proposed in
the round robin.
• To determine the STC IV parameters of the curved PV module samples.
• To evaluate the IV/electrical performance of the modules under field operating con-
ditions.
• To develop a model for the curved panels for reproducing their IV characteristic
under variable conditions and to assess its accuracy.
• To estimate the energy harvesting potential of the curved panel in a realistic scenario
– notably, a car parked in a parking lot(MotionLab).
• To construct and validate an energy harvesting/performance model based on the
above scenario.
This project seeks to characterize a curved PV module, particularly the curved PV mod-
ule used in the Toyota HPV vehicle provided by the Round Robin for this study, through
adapted flash-test methods for flat panels, considering specific constraints from a Round
Robin experiment. It aims to achieve accurate IV characteristic and field performance
data for curved PV modules under variable outdoor conditions.
In addition, the project incorporates Electroluminescence (EL) imaging to assess the state
of the curved module post-transport, identifying potential cell damage and/or defects.
Thermal imaging is also utilized to examine the thermal profile of the modules, pinpointing
areas of potential hotspots due to mismatch or other anomalies. Furthermore, leveraging
the MotionLab’s capabilities, the curved PV module is mounted on a vehicle’s roof, sim-
ulating realistic parking scenarios in various locations, logging data to assess the curved

Theory
In this chapter, the theoretical basis for solar energy, photovoltaics, and further, the inves-
tigation into the fundamentals of characterization and modelling of a PV module, with a
focus on designing for vehicle integration, is established. This theory and research dives
into some theoretical principles needed to understand what VIPV systems are about, and
their potential, as well as focusing on a curved PV module that presents unique challenges
and opportunities in the field of renewable energy.
The chapter outlines the evolution and current state of photovoltaic technology, and with a
particular emphasis on its integration into vehicles, and how the structure of the PV mod-
ule impact the energy generation. This segment highlights the significance of transitioning
from traditional planar panels to curved designs, a shift necessitated by the aerodynamic
and aesthetic requirements of automotive applications. The chapter extends to the chal-
lenges in characterizing and modelling such curved PV modules, particularly given their
unique geometry which affects the angle of incidence (AOI) of the solar beams upon and
across different cells.
2.1
Solar Irradiance - Principles of Solar Irradiance
Solar irradiance refers to the power per unit area received from the Sun in the form of
electromagnetic radiation, measured in watts per square meter ( w
m2 ). Instruments such as
pyranometers and pyrheliometers are commonly used for these measurements[5]. This
measure is critical in understanding solar energy’s potential and is a fundamental con-
cept in solar technology, no matter the solar module technology in question, and climate
studies[6, 7, 5]. The amount of solar radiation reaching a point on Earth’s surface varies
depending on the angle of the sun over the course of the year, the geographic location
of the examined point, and atmospheric conditions[8]. Darwish et al. (2022)[9] reviewed
various applications of photovoltaic (PV) devices, highlighting the conversion of solar ir-
radiation into electricity as a basic operating principle. This can include conversion of
solar energy to electricity in solar energy farms, Buinding integrated photovoltaics (BIPV)
for building applications, and as stated in this thesis, Vehicle Integrated Photovoltaics
(VIPV), to mention some of the uses. The last mentioned, and the focus of this study, is
a newer applied PV technology, which is seeing more and more applications in the world,
every day[1, 2].

The concept of air mass (AM) is essential in understanding solar irradiance at the Earth’s
surface. It represents the path length that sunlight takes through the atmosphere, quan-
tifying the reduction in solar radiation power as it passes through. An increase in air
mass leads to a decrease in solar radiation reaching the surface, which in turn affects the
performance of solar modules. For example, AM 1.5, commonly used as a standard for
solar testing, represents the sun’s rays passing through 1.5 times the atmosphere’s mass
compared to the zenith point (as if the sun was directly above, or on an 90◦angle to, a
horizontal point of examination on earth). The air mass if the sun was directly above the
point of examination, would be AM 1[6, 13, 14, 5].
For solar modules, an understanding of solar irradiance is critical for calculating efficiency
and power output, also in this thesis. Photovoltaic cells are engineered to optimize the
absorption of sunlight and convert it into electrical energy. The efficiency of this conver-
sion is influenced by the spectral distribution, air mass, and intensity of the irradiance, as
well as by the temperature and material properties of the cells, under these conditions.
Variations in solar irradiance, due to atmospheric effects, can significantly impact the per-
formance of solar modules. The reduction in certain wavelengths and overall intensity of
sunlight can affect how much energy photovoltaic cells can capture and convert. Thus,
atmospheric effects and the resulting solar spectrum, significantly influence the overall
performance of solar energy systems[5].
2.2
Solar Cell and Module Fundamentals
2.2.1
Types of Solar Cells, and Solar cell technology for Vehicle
Integration
Solar cells are designed to turn sunlight into electrical energy through the photovoltaic
effect. Their key components include:
• Semiconductor Layer: As mentioned in the previous section, this is the heart of the
solar cell, where light absorption and charge carrier generation (electrons and holes)
occur. A semiconductor is a type of material that has electrical conductivity between
that of a conductor and an insulator. The semiconductor layer is generally doped to
form a p-n junction, which result in the electric field to separate and mobilize charge
carriers.[6]. They need an appropriate band gap to absorb sunlight efficiently, stabil-
ity to endure operating conditions, to be, in most cases, cost-effective and available
for widespread use, and to have high conversion efficiency to be practically useful.
Silicon remains the dominant material due to its years of application research, and
balance of the mentioned requirements. But semiconductor material used in thin-

film is also starting to integrate its way more and more into the marked, due to the
balance of the same requirements, boosting the production of these[15].
Crystalline Silicon (c-Si): Crystalline Silicon (c-si) includes both monocrystalline and
polycrystalline silicon. Monocrystalline silicon cells, made from a single crystal, provide
higher efficiency due to their uniform structure which allows optimal electron mobility.
However, they are more expensive to produce compared to polycrystalline silicon cells,
due to the extra manufacturing stage to gain their single crystal structure. Polycrystalline
silicon cells are made from multiple silicon crystals fused together. These have slightly
lower efficiencies due to their grain boundaries between crystal, that can obstruct electron
flow, but are cheaper to manufacture.
Amorphous Silicon (a-Si): Used to be used primarily in thin-film solar cells. This non-
crystalline form of silicon offers advantages in terms of flexibility, cost, lower temperature
dependency, but its efficiency is lower due to less effective light absorption from the solar
spectrum, and charge carrier mobility[16, 17].
• Front and Back Contacts: These metallic layers on top and under the cell, when
connected, allow electrons to flow in and out of the solar cell, forming an electrical
circuit. The front metallic contacts (negative contact) are consisting of finger and
busbars. Fingers are the small metal strips that transport the generated electrons to
a bigger metallic collector strip called the busbar. The back contacts (positive con-
tact) serve as the pathway for the charge carriers to complete the electrical circuit[6],
usually a metallic layer that provides good electrical conductivity.
• Anti-Reflective Coating: Applied to the top surface to reduce light reflection and
increase light absorption. Reduces the reflection of sunlight off the surface of the
cell, allowing more light to be absorbed.
In contrast to crystalline silicon, thin-film solar cells diverge substantially in their structure
and composition. These cells are characterized by their thin layers of photovoltaic ma-
terial, only a few micrometers thick, deposited onto a substrate such as glass, metal, or
plastic. This method allows for a wide variety of materials to be used, including cadmium
telluride (CdTe), copper indium gallium selenide (CIGS), gallium arsenide (GaAs), and
amorphous silicon (a-Si), each material offering unique advantages in terms of efficiency
and application. Unlike the thicker wafers of c-Si cells, the slim profile of thin-film cells
leads to greater flexibility and a broader range of uses, including direct applications on
curved surfaces or lightweight structures. This makes them suitable, interesting, and a
good contender to technology used in BIPV and VIPV. Thin-film have also proved to be
better in low-light conditions, as well being less temperature dependent, which is beneficial
for solar cells of different applications[17][6]. The process of creating thin-film cells, in-
volving techniques like sputtering and chemical vapor deposition, is generally less energy-
intensive than the production of crystalline silicon cells, potentially offering cost savings
and environmental benefits in the manufacturing sector. But compared to silicon which
can be extracted from sand[6], the semiconductor materials used in thin film, are often
more scarce, and therefore more expensive. This leads to the prices of manufacturing
the solar cells as a whole, might be higher, when looking at the whole process. Therefore

The structural differences between these two types of solar cells result in distinct perfor-
mance characteristics. Crystalline silicon cells typically boast higher efficiencies at low
production cost, making them well-suited for applications where space usage is a impor-
tant consideration and maximum power output from a limited area is essential. Meanwhile,
the versatility, flexibility and low production costs of thin-film cells make them attractive
for large-scale installations and integrated photovoltaic solutions, despite their generally
lower efficiency compared to their crystalline counterparts. This contrast underscores the
importance of selecting the appropriate solar cell type based on specific project require-
ments, like in this instance integration onto vehicles, including efficiency, cost, installation
environment, and aesthetic considerations. All of which is important to consider for VIPV,
which is the focus of this thesis.
Beyond the regular crystalline silicon and thin-film cells, other technologies include per-
ovskite solar cells, which have shown high efficiencies due to their excellent light ab-
sorption and charge carrier transport properties. Perovskite solar cells (PSCs) are at the
forefront of photovoltaic research due to their potential for high efficiency and low manu-
facturing costs. Recent studies indicate progress towards bringing PSCs closer to com-
mercial market readiness, but as of research today, this technology is not yet ready for
commercial use[18, 19, 20]. Therefore PSCs potential beneficial use in VIPV, is not avail-
able[2]. Dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) and organic photovoltaics (OPVs) are other
emerging technologies that offer benefits like lower manufacturing costs and flexibility,
but the efficiency is not near other commercial and mature solar cell technologies. These
are not as of a mature technology yet, to output enough energy in space constrained ve-
hicle area for feasible use in VIPV[21, 2]. The figure 2.4 below, made by NREL, shows
the efficiency improvement and development over a timeline of most types of solar cells

As mentioned when it comes to solar cell technology for Vehicle Integration, the efficiency
of the solar module, is one of the most critical factors. Newer technologies in solar cells
focus on junction designs to improve efficiency[1]. Single-junction cells have one p-n junc-
tion, limiting their efficiency due to the Shockley-Queisser limit. The Shockley-Queisser
limit, set at about 33.7%, defines the maximum theoretical efficiency of single-junction
solar cells under standard conditions[6]. It is based on semiconductor physics, account-
ing for the semiconductor material’s bandgap and light conversion thermodynamics. This
limit reveals that only photons with energy exceeding the bandgap contribute to electricity
generation, with any surplus energy dissipated as heat[23][6].
Multi-junction solar cells are designed to overcome the efficiency limits of single-junction
cells, and is, as the name state, a technology with multiple junctions in a solar cell. By
stacking multiple semiconductor layers, each made to absorb different portions of the solar
spectrum, the cells can capture more energy from light. The architecture typically involves
a combination of different materials all with varying bandgaps, to ensure that each layer
absorbs different wavelengths of light efficiently. However, a big challenge is the current
of the cell matching between layers, and also the complex manufacturing process, making
this technology costly to produce and use. Recent advancements in multi-junction cells
is to stabilize and improve efficiency, as well as reducing cost[24].
A heterojunction solar cell is a type of solar cell that utilizes a junction formed between
two different materials, or two different types of semiconductors. The term ”heterojunc-
tion” refers to this interface between two dissimilar materials, separating the solar cell
technology from single junction. These two different materials have distinct bandgap en-
ergies, that determines how the material absorbs sunlight and converts it into electricity.
The heterojunction design exploits the advantageous properties of each material at the
interface, enhancing the cell’s overall efficiency.
HIT (Heterojunction with Intrinsic Thin layer) cells are a specific type of heterojunction so-
lar cells. HIT solar cells are made by combining amorphous silicon (a-Si) and crystalline
silicon (c-Si) to create the solar cell structure, and are known for their high efficiency and
performance[17]. This is also the technology used in the curved module for vehicle inte-
gration studied in this thesis. The base of the cell is a crystalline silicon wafer. Similar to

traditional silicon solar cells, this wafer is responsible for the bulk of the light absorption
and electron-hole pair generation. Surrounding the crystalline silicon, are thin layers of in-

occur at the surface of the wafer. To complete the cell, there are doped (n-type and p-

the electric field needed to separate and direct the charge carriers towards the cell’s elec-

and near-infrared region, while amorphous silicon can absorb shorter wavelengths more

contributing of a-si also acquire better temperature coefficients for the HIT cells, to perform
better under high temperatures than standard silicon cells. The efficiency of HIT cells de-
creases less with temperature, making them more suitable for conditions where the cells
experience higher temperatures[17]. Due to the combination of structure in the layers of
the HIT cells that prevent charge loss, they are also classified as high capacitance solar
cells[25].

2.2.2
High Capacitance of Solar Cells in Solar Cell Design
The concept of ’capacitance’ in solar cells, especially in high-efficiency variants, relates to
their ability to store electrical charge[32]. High capacitance solar modules, such as those
utilizing PERC (Passivated Emitter Rear Contact) and Panasonic HIT (Heterojunction with
Intrinsic Thin-layer) technologies, represent a major advancement in the efficiency of con-
verting light to energy in photovoltaic technology. Driven by the goal for greater solar
energy conversion efficiency, these modules are characterized by their enhanced ability
to store electrical charge, therefore giving the character of ”high capacitance modules”.
This feature is a result of their physical structure and the utilization of advanced mate-
rials. The improved design of these modules, which includes enhanced charge carrier
separation and reduced recombination rates, allows for more effective solar energy con-
version. Such advancements are vital in various applications, including specialized areas
like vehicle-integrated photovoltaics (VIPV), where maximizing energy capture within a

limited space with dynamic environment, is essential[30, 17, 33].
Advanced Cell Architecture: The architecture of high capacitance solar modules like
PERC and Panasonic HIT cells is designed to maximize light absorption and minimize
energy losses. The rear passivation layer in PERC technology plays a pivotal role in
reflecting unabsorbed light back into the cell, thereby enhancing light absorption and con-
version efficiency, as the ability to create electron-hole pairs is enhanced. This design
aspect is particularly beneficial in applications with variable lighting conditions, ensuring
optimal performance under different light angles and intensities[34].
Silicon heterojunctions in Panasonic HIT cells significantly enhance charge carrier dynam-
ics, facilitating efficient separation of charge carriers and reducing recombination losses.
This boosts overall cell efficiency, which is especially important in dynamic and ever-
changing environments like for mobile applications, and for fluctuating weather conditions.
The enhanced charge separation ability ensures a more consistent performance and high
energy yield, even under less-than-ideal conditions[35, 36].
Passivation Layers: The application of passivation layers in technologies like PERC is
crucial. These layers, typically made of silicon oxide or silicon nitride, enhance light ab-
sorption and energy capture, and protect the cell from environmental factors that can de-
grade performance over time. Their effectiveness is pronounced in modules with unusual
geometries or exposed to harsh environmental conditions[37].
Capacitance Effects on Testing: High-capacitance cells, including Panasonic HIT mod-
ules, result in unique challenges during flash testing. The high internal capacitance of
these cells can distort I-V measurements during regular flash testing with equipment not
suited for high capacitance, as described in the guide by SolMetric[38]. The standard
flash testing methods may not be appropriate for these cells due to their slower response
times, leading to inaccuracies and distortion in performance measurements, an issue that
is exacerbated in modules with complex geometries[39, 40].
Adaptations for Accurate Measurements: Adjusting measurement methodologies is key
for accurately assessing the performance of high-capacitance modules. Techniques like
longer pulse durations in solar simulators and continuous light simulation methods, bet-
ter suited for the electrical characteristics of high-capacitance cells, enable more precise
and accurate measurements [39, 38]. Equipment capable of producing longer light pulse
duration, and/or continuous light simulation, is not always available for every research fa-
cility and regular solar simulator must be used. This is the case for the Risø Campus, and
the testing of the curved module in this thesis, as newer equipment with these technolo-
gies are not available. Without the use of these advanced solar simulator/flash-testers,
IV-curve result fluctuations or other anomalies might occur.
2.2.3
Solar Modules and operation
Solar modules, which harness the power of the sun to generate electricity, are composed
of interconnected solar cells. The manner in which these cells are connected - either
in series, parallel, or a combination of the two - plays a big role in the module’s overall
performance, efficiency, stability and suitability for various applications. For the case of
the curved module, the module have 56 HIT solar cells that are connected in series, with
no parallel connections.
In a series connection, solar cells are linked end-to-end, which results in the voltages
of each cell adding up while maintaining the same current as a single cell. This type of
connection is beneficial for applications requiring high voltage outputs to reach a high
Characterization and modelling of curved photovoltaic panels for vehicle integration
13
==PAGE:24==
energy output, for example for VIPV on limited space, or for applications such as grid-
connected solar systems. It simplifies the module’s wiring and is efficient for high-voltage
applications. However, a significant downside to series connections is their susceptibility
to shading. In such a setup, and the case for the curved PV module in this thesis, the
performance of the entire string can be drastically reduced by a single shaded or under-
performing cell, as the current in a series circuit is determined by the least performing cell.
This limitation lead to mismatch losses where some cells do not operate at their maximum
potential[41], and unfortunately, this is a reality of curved PV modules due to different cell
light exposure. Therefore, optimally chosen VIPV cell technology is a highly important
matter.
On the other hand, parallel connections, where cells are connected side by side, increase
the overall current output as the currents of each cell accumulate. This configuration
is advantageous for low-voltage, high-current applications and offers better resilience to
shading. Each string in a parallel configuration operates independently, rendering the
system less vulnerable to the performance of individual cells, as it only limits the affected
string. However, this setup brings its own set of challenges and downsides, including
potentially less electric energy generation, more complex wiring requirements and the
need for robust management systems to handle the increased current flow[41].
The choice between series and parallel connections significantly impacts the module’s
efficiency, especially under varying environmental conditions, and is also a crucial choice
to make when considering curved PV modules. The aim is to find an optimal balance
between voltage and current to maximize power output, considering the specific require-
ments of the system, environmental conditions, and the characteristics of the solar cells.
Proper configuration ensures not only maximized energy harvest, but also the safety and
longevity of the solar module and the entire system. High voltages require robust insu-
lation and safety measures, while high currents necessitate thicker wires and better heat
management. Moreover, the configuration must align with the load requirements and stor-
age capabilities, such as charging the battery for electric or hybrid vehicles, or supplying
power to household appliances, or the grid.
To mitigate the drawbacks of series connections, particularly their sensitivity to shading,
bypass diodes are integrated into solar modules in the junction box. These diodes allow
the current to take a alternative route in the module, to bypass the shaded or malfunc-
tioning cells that is limiting the module as a whole, thus preventing significant drops in
module output and protecting cells from hot-spot heating due to cell mismatch. Strategic
placement of bypass diodes significantly reduces the negative effects of partial shading
on a series string, ensuring more consistent performance and extending the life of the
solar module[6]. For example, one bypass diode connected in parallel with each set of
18 cells is common practice, but there can be more depending on the configuration and
appliance of the PV system.
Maximum Power Point Trackers
Maximum Power Point Tracking (MPPT) technology is the tool used to optimize the extrac-
tion of power from solar modules by dynamically adjusting the electrical operating point to
stay close to the maximum power point, extracting the maximum power from the module.
This point varies with sunlight intensity, temperature, and the state of the solar module,
and the efficiency of the tracker depends on how fast it can adjust to changes. MPPT
ensures that the solar system operates at its maximum efficiency, thereby improving the
performance and energy yield of solar module[42, 43]

2.2.4
Mismatch in Solar Modules and the Effect on a Curved PV module
In photovoltaic (PV) technology, ’mismatch’ refers to the variation in performance char-
acteristics between individual solar cells within a module, arising from several factors.
This phenomenon leads to suboptimal performance of the entire module, as the overall
output is often limited by the weakest cell[6]. Particularly in the curved solar module, the
design can amplify the effects of mismatch. Since each cell is experiencing a different
irradiance, because of the 3D curvature nature of the module, the module will always
experience mismatch in the cell performance. Therefor configuration in curved modules
is a very important balance between different power output expectations. Either from
connecting in series and parallel and have output less subject to mismatch, but also a
chance to gain lower energy output due to the limited space - or have everything in series
to potentially reach higher energy output, but more subject to mismatch limiting the whole
module.
Not all mismatches in the PV module is due to the curvature. One primary cause of mis-
match is manufacturing variances. Solar cells from the same production batch can exhibit
slight differences in their electrical properties. These variances, though often minimal, can
lead to mismatch when the cells are assembled into a module, as explored in research by
Wang et al., who studied the power losses caused by solar cells’ micro mismatch in solar
modules[44].
Environmental influences also contribute significantly to mismatch. Factors like dust ac-
cumulation, rain droplets, and shadows unevenly affect solar irradiance on different cells
within the same module. This variation in light exposure translates to performance in-
consistency among the cells. Sahoo et al. assessed mismatching in series and parallel
connection of PV modules, identifying shading, environmental stress, and module orien-
tations as common reasons for mismatch[45].
Cell damage, resulting from mechanical stress, thermal cycling, or material flaws, can im-
pair individual cell functionality, further exacerbating mismatch. Curvature in solar mod-
ules leads to non-uniform exposure to sunlight across the module surface, causing cells
to receive differing amounts of irradiance and leading to variations in electrical output.
Mismatch impacts module performance in various ways. Reduced power output is a direct
consequence, as the overall module is constrained by its least efficient cell. Mismatch can
also cause hot spots within a module, areas of excessive heat that accelerate solar cell
degradation. Chattopadhyay et al. quantified this thermal mismatch in PV modules, cor-
relating it with power degradation and highlighting the importance of addressing mismatch
to ensure module longevity and efficiency[46].

Solar Geometry and Impact on Curved PV for Vehicle

Since solar cells and modules utilized the photons from sun light to produce electric en-
ergy, knowing the solar resource incident on the module is the key to understand how
much energy the module can produce. It is crucial to understand and know the suns
position, and which irradiance intensities the module is receiving to calculate this energy
output.
Due to Earth being in motion, the relative position to the sun varies throughout the day,
from day to day, and seasonally, but it can be accurately calculated and modelled. The
sun is repetitive and predictable, and we can calculate the suns positions in the sky at a
given time on any given day, to a fairly accurate point, with a set of equations to calculate
the different angles needed for solar resource assessment[6].
Solar geometry is crucial to understand and use when one want to estimate the solar en-
ergy components incident on a surface, either the surface is horizontal or on a slope[47].
For the Curved PV module investigated in this thesis, each cell will experience differ-
ent solar angles due to difference in curvature across the module, even if the attached
base/frame of the module is horizontal. The irradiance will therefore always need to be
obtained using the geometric relationships between the sun’s position and the orientation
of the inclined surface of the cells. Understanding at which angle the suns beams and
diffuse light hit each of the different cells of the curved module are the cornerstone to
calculating and model the sun light the module, as a whole, experience and can utilize.
Getting these values for each cell throughout a period of time, are the reason we can
calculate the energy output of the curved module, respecting its 3D curvature. Therefore
calculating the solar resource for a curved module, is more complex, compared to cal-
culating the solar irradiance on a flat planar module where all cells experience the same
amount of sun at the given time, even though the concepts are the same[48][3].
All the angles that define the sun position will be described, illustrated and further ex-
plained below[49][50][51].
Solar Angles:
• Declination Angle (δS, [◦]) - : The angle between the Sun’s direction and the equa-
torial plane (is the plane of orbit of the earth around the sun, also as seen in figure
??.). In the northern hemisphere, δS varies smoothly from +23.45◦at summer sol-
stice, to -23.45◦at the winter solstice, and is 0◦at the Equinoxes.
• Hour Angle (ωS or h [◦]) - the angle through which the Earth has rotated since solar
noon. Since the Earth rotates at 360º/24 hour = 15º/hour. The hour angle is positive
in the evening and negative in the morning. The hour angle can be calculated as
ωS = (Solar Time - 12)*15◦.
• Solar Time (ST) and Local Time (LT) - As Earth’s Time system is based on Time-
zones, the ”local time” will have the same value for different locations across the
same timezone, even if the sun is moving across this zone differently. Take a sun-
watch for example. The sunwatch will not showcase the same time for every loca-
tion, even if they are indeed in the same timezone. This is due to the suns position
over a day. Since the Sun will not be at the same place for all locations over a
Timezone, a different time value for Solar Time must be addressed, to account for
this.

Photovoltaic (PV) module characteristics are fundamental to the design, analysis, and
optimization of solar energy systems. These characteristics determine the performance
of a solar module under various environmental conditions. A solar module, essentially a
collection of solar cells connected into a single unit have characteristics that are important
to understand to know its performance. The performance of solar modules is influenced
by a variety of factors including material properties, design aspects, like a module with 3D
curvature from the Round Robin, and installation parameters like configuration and mount-
ing onto a car roof. Essentially the number of cells connected in series and paralell also
define these characteristics. Key characteristics that define a PV module’s performance
include its efficiency, power output, voltage-current (IV) characteristics and temperature
coefficients. The IV-curve, which describes the most important characteristics when the
sun hits the solar cells, are the relationship between the current and voltage output of a
PV module. The IV-curve is vital for understanding the optimal operating point where the
module delivers maximum power, also expressed through a PV-curve[6, 56, 57, 58]. An
example of the IV- and PV-curve is presented in figure 2.8 above, and the main parame-
ters and important points of the characteristic curves are explained below:
• Short circuit current (Isc, [A]): The current that flows when the module’s termi-
nals are short-circuited (meaning it has no voltage drop across it and resistance is
zero). It represents the maximum current the module can deliver under standard
test conditions (STC), and is directly proportional to the intensity of the incident light
as represented in the Figure 2.10. Isc is an important parameter for assessing the
peak current capability of the module.
• Open circuit voltage (Voc, [V]): This is the maximum voltage that the solar module
can produce when it is not connected to any load (open circuit), meaning when
the current through the module is zero. Voc is influenced by the temperature and
the semiconductor material of the module, as illustrated in figure 2.11. It gives an
indication of the maximum voltage output under STC.

• Maximum Power point voltage (Vmp, [V]): The voltage at which the module pro-
duces its maximum power output. This is a specific point along the voltage axis of
the I-V curve, at the maximum power point (MPP).
• Maximum Power point current (Imp, [A]): The current at which the module pro-
duces its maximum power output. This is a specific point along the current axis of
the I-V curve, also at the maximum power point (MPP).
• Maximum power point (MPP ,Pmp, [W]: This point on the I-V curve represents
the condition under which the solar module produces its maximum power output,
Pmax. At maximum power point, the product of current Imp and voltage Vmp is at its
maximum, Pmp, see figure 2.8. The location of MPP is crucial for the design of PV
systems because operating at or near this point maximizes the efficiency of energy
conversion, as mentioned earlier in the chapter, MPP Trackers are responsible for
this operation. The maximum power point can also be seen in the illustrations in
2.10 and 2.11, and how it is dependent on irradiance and temperature.
• Fill Factor (FF, [%]): is a measure of the ”squareness” of the I-V curve and is de-
fined as the ratio of the actual maximum obtainable power (Pmax) to the theoretical
power (Voc x Isc), represented by a square. The percentage is a measure of how
much the ”square” fills the area below the IV-curve. A higher fill factor indicates a
more efficient solar module, as it signifies that a larger area of the I-V curve is useful
for producing power.
• Series resistance (Rs, [Ω]): is a parasitic resistance which is in series with photo-
generated current. It accounts for the resistance current is subject to when moving
between emitter and base of the cells, the resistance between the device’s metal
contacts and the resistance between the metal contacts and the semiconductor.
In order to make as much of Iph reach the load, this resistance should have the
smallest possible value. High values of Rs will affect the slope of the curve, and
decrease the Fill Factor, as shown in figure 2.9(a).
• Shunt resistance (Rsh, [Ω]): represents a parallel current path within the PV cells
and module, that decrease the output and efficiency of the photovoltaic device. It
accounts for manufacturing defects and impurities wihtin the cell, such as leakage
currents through the junction at the bulk. Its value should therefore be high in order
to prevent the photo-generated current from going through this path. Decreasing
values of Rsh will affect the slope of the curve, and decrease the Fill Factor, as
shown in figure 2.9(b).
• Photo-generated current (Iph, [A]): is the current which is generated when the cell
or module is exposed to light with enough energy, with excited electrons moving
from the n-side to the p-side and holes moving in the opposite direction. Electrons
and holes are referred to majority carriers.
• Reverse saturation current (I0 [A]): the diode leakage current density in the ab-
sence of light. It is the current resulting from continuing flow of minority carriers,
and is a measure of the recombination in a device when the cell or module is re-
versely biased in the dark: for this reason it is also referred to as ”dark saturation
current”[59].
• Module Efficiency (ηm [%]): is the ratio of the electrical power output of the solar
module to the incident light power, expressed as a percentage. It’s a critical param-
eter for evaluating the performance of a PV module. Efficiency also depends on the

2.4.1
Flash-Testing Characterization of PV Module
To accurately characterize a solar module and develop a reliable model of its performance
and behavior, conducting a solar simulation is essential for gathering the electrical param-
eters. Flash-testing is a solar simulation technique in the photovoltaic industry for deter-
mining the electrical performance of solar cells and modules under standard test condi-
tions (STC). This method involves exposing the PV device to a brief, intense light pulse
that mimics and simulates sunlight, allowing for the measurement of its current-voltage
(I-V) characteristics. The I-V curve obtained from flash-testing provides a comprehensive
overview of the solar device’s performance, including some of the key parameters stated
previously. To ensure consistency and comparability of performance metrics across differ-
ent solar modules, flash-testing is conducted under Standard Test Conditions (STC). STC
specifies a light intensity of 1000 W/m2, a spectrum equivalent to air mass 1.5 (AM1.5G),
and a cell temperature of 25°C[61]. The results and parameters achieved from testing a
module, and obtaining an IV-curve, can be used further to directly model it’s behaviour in
different conditions, and in the end get the energy generated in different irradiance and
cell-temperature scenarios.
For curved solar modules, the characterization process becomes more complex due to
the geometric variations that affect the light absorption and, subsequently, the electrical
output. The normal protocol for flash-testing flat solar modules, must be adjusted, to
ensure consistency and comparability in solar simulation of curved modules as well[4].
Uniformity tests of the simulated light before flash-testing the curved module in question,
must be conducted as well, to announce the non-uniformity each cell is experiencing. This
is usually done by a small reference cell, placed in the same positions as each cell of the
module[4, 62, 63]. There are several more factors to be announced and taken into account

2.4.2
IV-tracing Field measurements of PV Modules
As mentioned, PV devices display variability in performance due to their non-ideal na-
ture, especially in curved modules, which become clear under real-world operational
conditions. Therefore, in the dynamic nature of PV technology, the characterization of
PV modules through outside Field IV-tracker measurements stands as a crucial method
for assessing module performance directly under operational conditions. This approach
involves capturing the current-voltage (IV) and power-voltage (PV) curves of PV mod-
ules under illumination from the actual real-time solar spectrum, providing invaluable in-
sights into their efficiency, operational characteristics, operational health, and potential
defects[4, 62, 57].
The IV-curve offers a snapshot of the electrical behavior of PV modules by depicting the
relationship between current output and voltage under varying loads. As mentioned ear-
lier, key parameters derived from this curve under operating conditions, such as the max-
imum power point (MPP), open-circuit voltage (Voc), short-circuit current (Isc), and fill
factor (FF), are essential for evaluating module operation, efficiency and diagnosing is-
sues like shading, soiling, or internal defects, and other dynamic conditions. As these can
vary significantly from the tests under perfect and static conditions, and STC conditions
rarely ever happen outside in real-life, correction-to-STC-values procedures are made to
showcase these based on outside performance. Procedures for correcting to STC values,
are often different from another, and can vary from advanced to simple. The simplest, but
accurate, procedure 1 described in DS/EN- IEC60891:2021[64] is the preferred and used
procedure in this thesis. The PV-curve, calculated from the IV data, illustrates the mod-
ule’s power output across different voltage levels, pinpointing the MPP where efficiency
peaks. This curve is crucial for optimizing system components, including inverters and
MPPT controllers, to maximize energy capture.
Characterizing PV modules using outside Field IV-tracker measurements in real-world
conditions ensures the accuracy of performance data, taking into account environmental
factors such as temperature and irradiance from the actual solar spectrum. This method
not only aids in understanding the PV module behaviour, and the design and maintenance
of PV systems, but also supports the development of robust, efficient, and reliable solar
energy models. Recent studies and methodologies, outlined in literature, underscore the
significance of advanced diagnostic tools and characterization techniques in enhancing
the understanding of PV module behavior, ensuring the longevity and reliability of the solar
installations[62, 57].

2.4.4
Thermography Imaging of PV Modules
Thermography imaging, or thermal imaging, is another non-invasive technique used to
characterize PV modules. It involves the use of infrared cameras to detect and mea-
sure the thermal radiation emitted from the surface of the module while illuminated. This
method provides a temperature profile of the module, highlighting areas of abnormal heat
generation under operation, which can indicate potential problems such as electrical mis-
matches, defective cells, or poor solder joint quality.
The primary reason for conducting thermography imaging on PV modules is to identify hot
spots that can significantly reduce the efficiency and lifespan of the module. Hot spots can
result from various conditions or defects, including shading, soiling, or physical damage
to the cells. This method is important, as it characterises how the module handles heat
and temperature under various condition. By identifying these issues early, maintenance
can be performed to prevent further damage and ensure the module operates at optimal
efficiency. Thermography imaging is also valuable for validating the thermal management
designs of PV modules, ensuring they can dissipate heat effectively under various oper-
ating conditions. Information about this can significantly help improving the technology, to
improve the heat management, as the results are important for better and more efficient
operation and energy outcome. Thermographic imaging are generally done outside, in
conditions where the solar irradiance in the plane of array is above 600 W/m2, to see how
heat distributes across the cells in a real life operating conditions.

Extraction of Single Diode Model Parameters with System Advisor Model
The System Advisor Model (SAM) is a tool developed by NREL, to analyze the perfor-
mance of renewable energy systems. SAM is widely used for the simulation of PV sys-
tems, among other renewable energy technologies, with performance models. SAM also
have a performance model function within its system, named ”CEC performance model
with user-entered specifications”. This function can be used to extract single diode pa-
rameters of a PV module, by only entering module characteristics that are usually found
on the module datasheet, like Voc, Isc, Vmp, Imp, and the modules temperature coeffi-
cients. The method used by the this function, of the software created by NREL, is based
on literature presented by Dobos (2012)[68].
The paper by Dobos (2012)[67] presents an improved algorithm for calculating the six
needed parameters for the Single Diode Model method. The ”CEC PV Calculator module
performance model” is based on the five-parameter model developed at the University of
Wisconsin-Madison. The coefficient calculator by Dobos uses a combination of empirical
regressions and heuristic methods to achieve automated convergence for the module
placed into the CEC database. Specifically, the paper details the method for calculating
key points on the I-V curve, including constraints derived from short circuit conditions
(Isc), open circuit conditions (Voc), and the maximum power point condition (Vmp and
Imp). The method involves solving six nonlinear constraint equations at the same time in
an iterative fashion, using a globally convergent variant of Newton’s method. This root-
finding algorithm computes values such that the function equals zero, considering both
the variables and the function as vector quantities[67].
In summary, the extraction method used in the ”CEC performance model with user-entered
specifications”, in SAM, relies on an algorithm that combines empirical regressions and
heuristic methods to calculate the parameters of the single diode method from standard
module specifications. This method allows for further characterization of modules and
diode parameters extraction, for modelling module behaviour for further use in IV-curve -
and energy yield models[67].

Before flash-testing the curved PV module and the reference cell, the Endeas’ xenon

flasher is not made for the 3D curvature, each cell of the curved module might not receive
the same light exposure from the flasher because of their individual curvature as well.
This means it is not sure to say if all cells are receiving STC irradiance conditions during
flashing. This is a mechanical limitation of testing a curved panel in a flash-test made for
planar modules.

infrared temperature meter. When ready the curved PV module was connected, thanks

3.2
Electroluminescence Tests - Electroluminescence
Imaging of the Curved Photovoltaic Module
Electroluminescence (EL) imaging is a non-invasive diagnostic technique widely used
in photovoltaics for detecting damages, defects and faults in solar cells and modules.
EL imaging is conducted by putting the module in forward bias (feeding current into the
module), causing radiative recombination, and the cells in the module to emit light in the
infrared spectrum. Doing this causes the cells to appear bright in EL imaging due to the
current, and damages, defects and faults will appear as ”dark/dimmed” areas/spots/cracks
due to the current through these areas being suboptimal. In this study, EL imaging was
utilized to detect if there were any of these faults present in the module jeopardizing the
power generation and causing power loss, small or severe, and characterize a hetero-
junction 56-cell curved photovoltaic module, through the results of these images. It was
also performed to ensure integrity after shipping. The EL imaging was conducted in a dark
room EL lab at DTU Risø Campus to ensure clear EL images without the interference of
background lighting.
Equipment
1. DSLR Camera (Modified for EL Imaging): A standard DSLR camera was modi-
fied for EL imaging. Modifications included removing the IR filter to allow EL signal
detection and adding a visible light filter to reduce exposure where the detector is
most sensitive. Due to the high efficiency of the HJT solar cells in the module, shut-
ter time has to be a lower value than what is normally used for other standard cell
technologies, as high efficiency modules appear brigther during EL imaging than
lower efficiency modules.
2. InGaAs-based Camera: For fast acquisition EL images and enhanced sensitivity
in the short-wave infrared (SWIR) region, an InGaAs-based camera was used. This
camera covered the full emission spectra from crystalline silicon, facilitating quicker
exposure times and video capability with EL signal.
3. Power Supply Unit (PSU): A DC power supply unit (BK precision 9205) was con-
nected to the curved PV module in forward bias to drive current through it, inducing
electroluminescence.
4. Computing Equipment: A computer was connected to the camera, and was used
for image data acquisition and camera settings control.
Procedure
1. Image Acquisition:
• The curved PV module was connected to the PSU.
32

==PAGE:43==
• The PSU was set to a voltage of 48V, a voltage slightly higher than the sug-
gested open-circuit voltage (Voc) of 41.8V, to ensure current flow.
• EL images were captured at 100% and 10% of the initial guess short-circuit
current (Isc) using both DSLR and InGaAs-based cameras.
• For the DSLR camera, focus, ISO, and exposure time were adjusted. The
shutter time was adjusted to a shutter time of 1.6 seconds for 100% of Isc, and
8 seconds for the 10% of Isc. For the InGaAs the shutter time was adjusted to
1 ms for both 100% and 10% of the Isc, as higher shutter times would result in
too bright cells for this image capturing.
2. Image Processing:

• The processed images were used to identify and analyze potential faults and

3.6
Model - All Models
The Goal of the Energy Yield Model was to try to accurately simulate the IV-curve for
each cell in a module through an IV-curve model that balances simplicity with precision,
achieved through careful assumptions and detailed parameterizing.
It does this by representing each cells’ individual tilt, and azimuth angle based on ori-
entation, and calculating the GPOA of each cell from the individual angle of incidence
combined with the shadow profile, over the course of a day. It aimed to produce energy
yield predictions closely mirroring actual obstruction conditions and parking scenarios by
adding the modelled IV-curves of all the individual cells together, accounting for mismatch
with the current decided by the GPOA of the most limiting cell.

The Weather data (Timestamp, DNI and DHI) and the obstruction data (elevation angle

model, and the PVlib library in python acquired the solar position based on the weather
data. The needed solar angles, along with the tilt of each cell, and the azimuth of each

3.6.6
Curved PV Module IV-curve Model and Energy Yield Model
The IV-curve Model was created by setting up the Single Diode Model parameters derived
from the SAM extracted values of the STC values of the flash-test and STC corrected field
test data. The Model was made to use the De Soto Single Diode Model function provided
within the PVlib python library. The IV-curve Model calculated the IV-curve for each cell
with the use of each cell’s modelled input GPOA/GPOA,shaded derived from the weather data,
validated for each experiment. The temperature input for the IV-curve model dependent
on which experiment the model was compared with and based on. For the flash-test 25◦
was used for STC values, and the input temperature for the field measurement compar-
isons, was the recorded cell temperature. For the MotionLab, the IV-curves was modelled
with the temperature input of the highest cell temperature out the two of the recorded cell
temperature values.
IV-curve Model created and modelled the module IV-curve for GPOA/GPOA,shaded and tem-
perature values of the solar cells for each timestamp, by adding the modelled IV-curves of
all the individual cells together, accounting for mismatch with the current decided by the
lowest GPOA of the most limiting cell. The total voltage, resulting in the Voc voltage, was
calculated by the model by adding the resulting IV-curve Voc voltage of each cell together.
This mean as stated previously in the report, that the IV-curve Model does not account
for bypass diodes present in the module. The Single Diode method in the IV-curve Model
is the De Soto Model which is mostly used for IV-curve modelling of flat cells and mod-
ules. This essentially also mean that the method used, presume every cell as a flat cell
that individually experience different conditions. The Model then account for/presume the
curvature by adding all different resulting IV-curves together.
The Energy Yield model was made simple, and takes the maximum power point of mod-
elled IV-curve and multiply it with time duration. The Energy Yield Model was used to
calculate the total Watt-hours (Wh) produced throughout the day for the MotionLab. This
was achieved by multiplying the Pmp value, derived from the IV-curve model at each spe-
cific time of day, by the duration of the time intervals between weather data timestamps.

Due to the non-uniformity, not all cells are subjected to STC values, which can significantly
impact the output IV-curve during flash testing. The discrepancy of STC values across
individual cells lead to a mismatch among the solar cells during the testing procedure, lim-
iting the full potential, and extend to the curved module as a whole. The resulting module
IV-curve will be limited by this mismatch creating uncertainty in the test setup. Conse-
quently, when subjected to flash testing, the curved module is likely to be characterized
by a performance value that falls short of what could be expected under ideal conditions
where uniform irradiance is assured for all solar cells.
Furthermore, the module’s high capacitance, along with the Endeas flash-tester’s lack
of a sufficiently long sweep time setting for HIT solar cells, further distorts the flash-test
outcomes, adding another level of uncertainty to the IV-curve output. In alignment with
findings reported in Blum et al.[25], the required pulse length and sweep duration for flash-
testing n-type HIT cells to yield an accurate IV-curve without discrepancies necessitate a
pulse duration of 2436 ms. This specification significantly exceeds the capabilities of the
Endeas testing equipment.

Conclusion
In this master thesis project the characterization and modelling of a PV module with three-
dimensional curvature, the Vehicle Integrated Photovoltaic(VIPV) module belonging on
the roof of the Toyota Prius HPV, have been undertaken. The results acquired in this
thesis hope to pave the way and aims to take a stance on better understanding of the
modelling of curved modules on vehicle in urban settings, and aim to assist in greater
understanding of a more accurate way of modelling energy yield of these curved modules.
This master thesis has systematically explored the characterization and modelling of a
high capacitance curved photovoltaic panels for vehicle integration through, employing
different characterization approaches for curved modules which included Electrolumines-
cence (EL) imaging, thermal imaging, flash testing with uniformity test, field testing, and
MotionLab(vehicle in a parking lot) measurements. EL imaging and comprehensive ex-
amination confirmed the integrity of the curved PV module post-transport, ensuring that no
damage was inflicted on the curved photovoltaic module during transportation. Through
thermal imaging and EL tests, the operational and structural integrity of the module was
validated.
The comprehensive analysis conducted in this thesis critically evaluates the performance
and modelling accuracy of curved PV modules through a series of field-tests, flash-tests,
and MotionLab data recording. Field-testing, employing field-mounted IV-tracing, pro-
vided a rigorous characterization of the module’s real-world performance. Flash-testing,
with varied voltage bias to stabilize high capacitance effect was performed, and unifor-
mity tests across the x, y, and z planes, ensured the module’s uniform performance was
within acceptable range. The non-uniformity was 2.4% (Class A). These methodologies
revealed the different impact of environmental conditions on module efficiency, particu-
larly highlighting non-uniformity concerns.
Flash- and field-testing provided a comparative analysis, highlighting the differences in
module characterization under controlled and real-world conditions. Specifically, flash
testing offered a baseline for module performance, while field testing underlined the vari-
able real-world conditions’ impact.
A pivotal aspect of this research was the comparison between model predictions and em-
pirical measurements. The IV-Curve Model was validated against real-world data, show-
casing its high accuracy in replicating actual module performance across several metrics
and instances. However, discrepancies in the short-circuit current (Isc) were noted, at-
tributed to the exclusion of bypass diodes in the model. For future work, this should be
considered critical consideration for future model adjustments for modelling IV-curves of
curved modules.
Furthermore, the Energy Yield Model, when compared to MotionLab’s empirical data,
highlighted an overestimation in Energy Yield Model predictions from the IV-curve model
despite its accuracy at Pmp across other validation processes. This variance underscores
the potential limitations of the MPP tracker algorithm in the charge controller of the Mo-
tionLab, that the model in the thesis did not account for, suggesting it may default to lower
power values than optimal max power point. Despite these discrepancies, the model’s
predictions under the low irradiance conditions typical of Denmark’s autumn months were
deemed tolerable for practical applications. This acceptability suggests the model’s util-
ity in estimating energy yield in parking scenarios, though it calls for further refinement

to enhance accuracy, especially accounting for the presence of bypass diodes, and MPP
