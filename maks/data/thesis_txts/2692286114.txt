Abstract
A method based on machine learning models to predict the wind speed uncertainty, as a
way to improve WRF wind modeled data is investigated in this work. In order to get the
best candidate model, four different machine learning algorithms (ANN, ERT, GBT, RFR)
are studied to obtain both the optimal input data and the best hyperparameters. The study
was realized with data from the Wind Atlas for South Africa (WASA) project, where the
best model turned out to be a ERT with average MAE of 1.42 m/s and EMD of 0.76 m/s
for the predictions made at the reference stations.
The fitted model uses WRF model output data alone, including wind speed, wind direction,
wind shear, temperature, and bulk Richardson number, as well as the hour of the day and
month. These parameters provided good results, and the possibility to map the wind
speed bias over entire regions during different time periods. The obtained results are a
useful source to estimate the wind speed bias over the South African territory, and assess
the regions where WRF­modeled wind is overpredicted or underpredicted, giving as well
an idea on what to expect at a site.

1
Introduction
This chapter outlines the motivation, objective, and scope of this thesis project. It briefly
revises the current status of the wind energy and its necessity for the global energy mix,
that drive the fundamental reason of this project.
1.1
Motivation
As of 2020 wind energy accounts for an accumulated installed capacity of 743 GW on­
shore and 35 GW offshore. Despite disruptions in the global wind energy market due to
COVID­19, 2020 had the best year­over­year growth of 53% or 93 GW globally. These
shares of the global wind energy are expected to increase 469 GW onshore and 94 GW
offshore with new installations planned for the following years until 2025 (Lee and Zhao
2021).
The wind farm planning and design process, among other procedures, is based mainly
on an accurate wind resource assessment at the given site. Reducing the uncertainty in
the wind resource assessment decreases costs in a project as it helps later stages of it to
be improved, for example, choosing the optimal wind turbine for site’s conditions. Accord­
ing to Gasch and Twele 2012, a correct estimation of the wind resources can impact the
economical feasibility of the projected wind farm. Before validating with in­site measure­
ments, mesoscale weather models are used to pre­assess the wind resources. The New
European Wind Atlas (NEWA) (Hahmann, Sile, et al. 2020; Dörenkämper et al. 2020) and
Wind Atlas for South Africa (WASA) (Hahmann, Floors, et al. 2021) are sources where
a preliminary wind resource verification can be done. These two sources are available
for specific regions, and are based on Weather Research and Forecasting (WRF) simu­
lations which are then downscaled using the WAsP methodology to obtain maps with a
better spatial resolution and a higher fidelity to the site’s characteristic topography and
location.
Wind power relies on atmospheric flow conditions, therefore a better prediction of the
wind speed contributes to a better power forecasting. Depending on the goal, numerical
weather prediction models (NWPs) are used for either short­term wind power forecasting
or long­term wind resource assessment. Nevertheless, their accuracy depends heavily
on the site, the surrounding terrain, and the local meteorology. This means that is not
uncommon that these models fail to predict the wind speed properly, and consequently the
wind power. Alternatively, statistical based models, such as machine learning models, are
likely to be useful to provide improved models that overcome the limitations and provide
a better wind assessment (Vassallo, Krishnamurthy, and Fernando 2021).
Given the high dependency between the heterogeneous wind resource and the geo­
graphic characteristics of the wind farm, it is a great challenge to improve the understand­
ing of atmospheric physics among the flow physics at a wind farm (Veers et al. 2019).
While mesoscale weather models are used to assess the wind resources, it is of high in­
terest to see if the use of machine learning models can determine model’s wind speed bias
given the atmospheric flow conditions predicted by WRF, including atmospheric stability
parameters. Therefore, this work focuses on the development of a method that estimates
the wind speed bias between mesoscale weather modeled wind data and measurements
at given sites, verifying the utility of the predictions coming from the proposed models.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1.2
Scope
The compromises made by South Africa as a signatory of the Paris Agreement, added to
an undergoing energy crisis, has enforced the country to set important targets to reduce
its greenhouse gases emissions, aiming to be net zero carbon emitter by 2050. Important
targets in renewable energy are under development, including the installation of 30 GW
additional capacity by 2030. An important share of this additions are 14.4 GW of planned
new onshore wind capacity, that will join the current operative 2.5 GW around the country
by 2020 (Lee and Zhao 2021).
These commitments had put on the spotlight the South African wind energy market, with
wind farm projects being developed to achieve the goals. Additionally, the South African
government, through institutions such as South African National Energy Development
Institute (SANEDI), supports projects that promote energy research to take full advantage
of the national resources. An important project is the WASA, that aims to map the wind
resource in South Africa, developing, verifying and employing methods that help the large
scale exploitation of wind energy in the country.
This project uses observation data measured in South Africa added to modeled data for
the same sites made for the WASA project, making it a resourceful dataset. For this
reason, the project only uses data from South Africa, following a very specific geographical
extension, while using a methodology that could be applied to other regions where the
same kind of information is available.
1.3
Objective
As mesoscale weather models assess wind resources, which are validated at measure­
ment sites, there is a genuine need for a method to estimate these estimates’ error. Ac­
cordingly, if machine learning models are successful to determine the relation between
model error and environmental conditions, these will make possible to decrease the error
and increase the model’s accuracy. For this project, this method is investigated with fitted
models based on specific sites in South Africa that not only take into account common
atmospheric variables (e.g. wind speed, wind direction), but also atmospheric stability pa­
rameters such as Richardson number or Obukhov length, to improve the fitted model. A
machine learning implementation that can predict the wind speed bias at given sites rises
the possibility to predict the wind speed bias everywhere else in the model’s domain.

Chapter 3 the machine learning models employed to handle the data are briefly described;
afterwards, the results obtained from the machine learning applications are shown (Chap­
ter 4) and discussed (Chapter 5). An application of this project is described in Chapter 6,
followed by project’s conclusions in Chapter 7 and a short Chapter 8 about possible im­
provements and further work.

2.3
Modeled data
The WRF mesoscale model dataset is from WASA3, and uses the same simulation method
as in WASA1 and WASA2 (Hahmann, Lennard, et al. 2014; Hahmann, Pian, et al. 2018).
The simulations have three nested spatial domains that are shown in Figure 2.2, with a
grid spacing of 3.33 km for the inner domain D3 and a 1:3 ratio for the following domains,
resulting in spatial resolutions of 10 and 30 km for D2 and D1 respectively. As a good
practice, the model top was set to 50 hPa. Temporal resolution of the D3 dataset is 30­
minute, simulating over a 30 year span covering from 1990 to 2020. However, not all the
data is being used during the evaluation of the results as it is limited by the observations’
availability.
It is important to notice that simulated data used in this project is the output data that
showed the best combination of model setup and parameterizations in Hahmann, Floors,
et al. 2021 for the region, which is the YSU­V381­MOS. In other words, it uses Yonsei
University PBL scheme (YSU), with WRF version 3.8.1, and mosaic vegetation (subtiling
option). Other model’s parameters can be consulted in Hahmann, Floors, et al. 2021,
were details related to the simulations are explained.
From the available WRF model output data, the variables selected from D3 to be used
along the project are: wind speed (at 120, 100, 62, 60 and 10 m), wind direction (at
60 m), surface heat flux, surface RiB, the sectorial reference roughness length z0 and
temperature (at 2 m), as well as the derived wind shear parameter (between 60 and 10 m).

Correlation
A correlation matrix is able to tell how all the available variables both from the model
and the observations are related to each other. With a first glimpse, it is possible to detect
which may be the most important variables to consider as predictors in a machine learning
model predicting the wind speed bias.
In Figure 2.3, it can be verified that the highest correlations occur between the wind speed
measured and modeled at different height levels. This is a good sign, as WRF is repro­
ducing the wind with correlation values of around 0.8, at least for WM04. This value can
however drop down to 0.5 in certain stations such as WM19, which could be explained by
different factors such as the site’s topography or the ability to deal with the local climate.
Correlations between relative humidity, temperature gradients and temperatures are also
notorious.
Nevertheless, the most important correlations to check are the ones for the wind speed
bias. It can be seen that the values are not that high, but this might mean that the relation
between all the other variables and the bias is not linear. To have the highest correlations
with wind speeds is no surprise as the bias is derived directly from these. Additional
variables that show some linear correlation to the bias are the wind direction, temperature
gradient, wind shear, temperature and heat flux. For these reasons these variables are
taken into account to be used as predictors in the machine learning implementation. The
RiB is also considered to study the influence of having a stability parameter as a predictor.

3
Methods
In this chapter, the different machine learning models used throughout the work, as well
as evaluation scores are briefly explained to give a consistent idea of the basics behind
them.
3.1
Machine learning models
For this work, different machine learning models are used to evaluate their performance
predicting the wind speed bias, utilizing the implementations in scikit­learn Python pack­
age version 0.24 (Pedregosa et al. 2011). The four machine learning models used in
this work are chosen based on other works (Optis and Perr­Sauer 2019; Buturache and
Stancu 2021), studying wind power forecasting applications, and are applied for super­
vised machine learning regression.
3.1.1
Artificial Neural Network
An artificial neural network (ANN) is a two stage regression model, which can be described
by diagram in Figure 3.1. In the diagram, all input predictors are transformed with the
chosen activation function to be the inputs at the hidden layer composed by a determined
number of neurons. The process will repeat as many times as hidden layers the model
has until it reaches the output layer where the predicted output variable is estimated.
After selecting an error metric for the predicted variable, the weights and biases of each
input per neuron are learned (Optis and Perr­Sauer 2019). The main idea is to find linear
combinations of the inputs and model the target as a non­linear function of the input data
(Hastie, Tibshirani, and Friedman 2001).
For this work, the back­propagation ANN is used, which is scikit­learn’s implementation
using a multilayer perceptron, a model that employs units that use an activation function
(e.g. threshold, piecewise linear, sigmoid). Fitting this model carry some issues such as
the amount of layers and neurons per layer needed, network performance outside train­
ing, and overfitting (Jain, Mao, and Mohiuddin 1996). To address these issues, different
number of hidden layers had to be studied with an amount of neurons no larger than 100,
and a regularization parameter to avoid overfitting.
3.1.2
Random Forest Regressor
A random forest regressor (RFR) is a regression model that combines tree predictors, in
which each tree depends on the values of a random vector sampled independently and
with the same distribution for all trees in the forest (Breiman 2001). The error converges
as the forest increases its size, increasing its accuracy, deriving in an ensemble model
that is robust with respect to noise, and that can also give an internal estimate of each
variable importance. The diagram in Figure 3.2 shows how the RFR works, given an
input, each of the trees makes its own prediction based on a random sample of the data,
and then all the predictions made by the trees are taken into account to give the average
value as the prediction from the forest.
One of the known disadvantages of RFR is that sometimes it tends to overfit some datasets.
Nonetheless, it is also an efficient model with large databases, as well as effective to es­
timate data missing in a dataset.

Developed by Friedman 2001, the gradient boosting trees (GBT) ensemble model is
based on regression trees. This type of model fits the data using a simple regression

Extremely Randomized Trees
Extra trees or extremely randomized trees (ERT) is another ensemble model based on

Some of the advantages of using ERT are an increase in the accuracy, but also compu­

values of the learning sample in an extreme case. This method that greatly randomizes
the input variables, as well as the cut­point when splitting a tree node, tend to decrease
while increasing the bias. To avoid the bias issue, the trees could include more attributes
as seen in Geurts, Ernst, and Wehenkel 2006.

Two metrics are used to evaluate the performance of the machine learning models pre­

Mean absolute error
The mean absolute error (MAE) is a typical regression metric
defined as the average difference between the target variable ˆyi and the predicted variable
yi (the one coming from the machine learning model), for n number of instances predicted.
Its mathematical definition is shown in Equation 3.1.

Earth mover’s distance
The earth mover’s distance (EMD) is a score used to evaluate

Werman 2008).

4
Results
This chapter presents and discuss the results obtained from the machine learning cross­
validation at each site taking into account the four models mentioned before (ANN, GBT,
RFR and ERT), as well as different input variables as predictors. The discussion follows
the utilization of the best two fitted models in the preliminary performance evaluation to fit
both of them using data from all sites to get a single fitted model from each one of them.
The best from these models is chosen based on its overall performance predicting the
wind speed bias at the stations.
4.1
Machine learning preliminary performance evaluation
The machine learning preliminary performance evaluation uses scikit­learn’s default pa­
rameters for the four models, which can be found in Table 3.1. This preliminary phase
consists in a 5­fold random cross­validation with 0.8/0.2 train­test data splitting, meaning
two things: the temporal information is downgraded to simple predictors that will help to
define the actual conditions without considering the previous state, and that the models
are trained with 80% of the data available at each station. Additionally, it should be no­
ticed that a test phase occurs two times, one with 20% of training data to evaluate the
different candidates fitted on 80% of training data to get the best; and the second to get
an overall estimation of the model’s performance with the remaining 20% of 'unseen'data.
Before fitting the models, all data inputs are standardized using a standard scaler that
subtracts to each column the respective predictor’s mean and divides the result by the
predictor’s standard deviation. The use of this standard scaler is necessary to avoid faulty
model’s performance.
Different sets of predictors are used to revise their contribution to the predicted wind speed
bias, as well as their influence on the MAE. These different cases are presented in the
following subsections in addition to their results. Maps of the geographical distribution of
the feature importances are included in Appendix A.3, for all the cases analyzed.
4.1.1
Default predictors
The first case utilizes the for now on called default predictors that include the wind direction
at 60 m, wind speed at 62 m, bulk Richardson number, wind shear, temperature at 2 m and
the temporal variables hour and month. These variables are defined as default because
they consist of a small set of predictors while still having the necessary information about
the wind, the atmospheric stability, as well the temporal reference for daily and seasonal
effects.
In Figure 4.1a, the MAE is shown for all the stations and the four selected models. It
can be seen that ERT and RFR are the models with the best performance with average
values of 1.28 and 1.34 m/s respectively. On the other hand, with similar results both GBT
and ANN have the worst performance, with ANN having the highest value of 1.9 m/s at
WM10. This site together with WM11 consistently have the highest MAE values along all
the models, very likely due to their topographic complexity in the surroundings (Hahmann,
Floors, et al. 2021). In general, the MAE values do not vary considerably between the
different stations, showing that each of the models deliver consistent predictions.

To weigh the contribution of each of the predictors to the predicted wind speed, a permu­
tation feature importance methodology is used. This method consists on randomly drop
a predictor to verify how much the model score decreases, showing how much the model
output depends on the chosen predictor (Breiman 2001). Based on permutation feature
importance results, Figure 4.4 maps the reliability of each of the models on the predictors
over South Africa. Due to the way the permutation feature importance is calculated, the
exact contribution of each of the predictors should not be taken as a rule, but just as an
approximation of the dependence of the model on each of them.
In the case of the ERT (Figure 4.4a), the model relies mainly on the wind speed, but it has
a very reasonable share of the reliability between the rest of the predictors at most of the
stations. In contrast, both GBT and ANN seem to rely heavily on the wind speed, which
contributes to even more than 50% of the importance at most sites. This might be a sign
of these model’s deficiency to use the other predictor’s information to generate a better
prediction. It is no surprise that for all the models the wind speed appears to be the main
driver of the wind speed bias predictions. However, for the GBT wind direction actually
plays the major role at WM05 and WM08, very possibly due to their coastal location.

4.2
Fitted general model over South Africa
To be able to map the wind speed bias allover the country, it is unpractical to have several
different models fitted to different regions around South Africa, therefore this section fo­
cuses on finding the best possible model to use taking into account data coming from all
the described sites. Only two out of the four initial models are used in order to enhance
the hyperparameter optimization, therefore both the ERT and ANN are selected to vary
their hyperparameters and get the combination that gives the minimum MAE. The ERT is
chosen as it is the model that consistently obtained the best MAE values for all the cases
using the default hyperparameters (Section 4.1). Despite being one of the poorly per­
forming models, ANN is also included in this analysis, giving it the possibility to perform
better when the hyperparameters are changed. Additionally, given the amount of data,
that using all the information available represents, the ANN is expected to improve its
predictions of the wind speed bias. The GBT and RFR algorithms are not used anymore
as only one tree­ensemble based model is to be studied (the ERT) in addition to the ANN
which has a different type of architecture.
In order to find the best set of hyperparameters for each of the models, a halving grid
search with a 10­fold random cross validation is carried out for each of the models (keeping
the same 0.8/0.2 train­test data ratio). In this procedure, all the possible combinations of
hyperparameters are trained on a small subdataset, which increases its size iteratively,
while selecting the best candidates and increasing the size of the dataset used to fit the
model. In the end, only the best performing candidate is selected and its hyperparameters
are considered to be the ones to be used on the available reference data.
For both algorithms, the set of possible hyperparameters are defined based on Optis
and Perr­Sauer 2019 and listed in Table 4.1. The total amount of ANN models that are
inspected is 14 (2 activation functions × 7 hidden layer sizes), and 144 for the ERT (4
maximum depths × 3 minimum sample splits × 4 minimum samples per leaf × number of
estimators) which is high in comparison to the ANN due to its higher amount of possible
of hyperparameters to vary.
Also, both the ERT and ANN are tested on two different datasets, one that includes all the
available data (approximately 1.6 million instances) offering a wide range of conditions,
and a second dataset that is limited by the shortest amount of instances a station has
(which is WM10 with around 20,000 instances) totaling approximately 380,000 instances.
Despite the second dataset being more than four times smaller than the other dataset, it
guarantees that each of the stations is equally represented avoiding bias in the models
due to one or various stations being overrepresented, contributing to the possibility of
finding a better model.
In the case of the ANN, the best model fitted using all the data available corresponds to the
one using ReLU as activation function and (100, 50, 25, 9) as hidden layer configuration.
In contrast, the best ANN fitted for the limited amount of data also uses ReLU as activation
function, but the hidden layer configuration is (100, 50). On the other hand, ERT fitted on

Good prediction case (WM04).
From Hahmann, Floors, et al. 2021, it is retrieved that
WM04 is located near the seashore, surrounded by slightly complex agricultural and fore­
stal areas, and a low z0 ≈0.02 m. These characteristics showed that the station is driven
by the sea­land effects due to its location, and make it easier for the WRF model to repre­
sent better observed data. Both the modeled temporal cycles and the wind distributions
have a nice match in regard to the observations, specially the directional distribution and
the temporal cycles, with just slight differences but good timing during the year and the
day.
The combination of low terrain and good timing representation with WRF model at WM04
results in better predictions of the wind speed bias, with similar probability and temporal
characteristics compared to the observed wind speed bias (Figure 5.1a, Figure 5.2). This
good performance is assessed with a MAE of 1.21 m/s, which is the lowest value and
also very close to the 1.10 m/s value from the model fitted using single station data alone
(Figure 4.1a); and an EMD value of 0.55 m/s.

such as estimating the bias along the time over an specific region. Mapping the wind

Conclusions
Based on the necessity of correcting wind speeds obtained from models such as WRF,
in this work, different machine learning algorithms (ANN, ERT, GBT, RFR) were tested
using cross validation methodology, and their hyperparameters and set of input variables
optimized for the task of predicting the wind speed bias over South Africa. From the
different sets of variables, the optimal set of inputs found was compound of the wind
speed at 60 m, wind direction at 62 m/s, wind shear, temperature at 2 m, bulk Richardson
number, as well as the hour of the day and the month.
ERT with optimized hyperparameters showed the best results in the final evaluation stage
of candidate models, for this specific region and dataset. This model got in average a MAE
of 1.42 m/s and an EMD of 0.76 m/s for the predictions made at the reference stations.
However, along the stations this evaluation metrics varied between 1.21 to 1.68 m/s and
0.54 to 0.96 m/s for the MAE and EMD respectively. In the case of the worst performing
stations, the results can be attributable to its topography as they are mainly located in
mountainous regions.
Using this method is advantageous as it can be extended for the regions near the ref­
erence sites, expanding the range of bias predictions to regions as large as the WRF
model’s domain, but always limited by available observations to fit the machine learning
model. In this work, the wind speed bias prediction included all the South African terri­
tory, proving its scalability and usefulness, specially for sites where only modeled data is
available.
The wind speed bias generated over South Africa proved to be helpful to see local and
temporal effects on the bias, and consequently the possible quality of the wind speed pre­
dictions made with WRF model. On this first approach exploring the possibility of machine
learning models trained in WRF data alone, surges the question on which is the correct
set of parameters taken from the simulations that, will represent the local characteristics
in the best possible way, as models reproducing the mesoscale effects are good already.
Further improving this aspect, would make the presented method more reliable.

Further work
As the set of WRF output variables was limited to the ones stored during the simulations,
which were mainly variables traditionally used for wind applications, it is left to explore if
other variables are useful to improve the predictions, such as precipitation. For the same
limitation, other stability parameters, such as TKE, were not studied in this work, but might
be of great benefit to model, as they are reported to boost the performance of forecasting
applications for instance. While the inclusion of directional roughness length did not seem
to improve the predictions considerably, other static variables coming from the model input
data might offer a better representation of the local effects, hence bettering the results.
Another possibility opened by this work, is the analysis of using a machine learning model
trained with different sources of modeled weather data, such as reanalysis (MERRA­2,
ERA), or even WRF ensembles using different parameterizations.
One of the weak points about this work is the lack of a reference or an evaluation metric to
assess the obtained results in an optimal way. As the wind speed bias predictions’ MAE
values are somehow abstract and difficult to relate with, another metric would make it
easier to compare the results, with methodologies attempting to catch the local effects to
correct mesoscale model’s output. This would also ease the way to compare the current
method used in this work to WAsP for example.
The method developed in this work can be extended for evaluation in other regions such
as Mexico, where a project similar to WASA is under development (Wind Atlas for Mexico
(AEM)), offering the possibility to test the method in a region with different characteristics.
