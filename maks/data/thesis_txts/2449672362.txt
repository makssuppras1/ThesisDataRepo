Abstract
The increasing interest and need to protect own military territory on land as
well as under water engaged the investigation of a passive harbour surveillance
system. Opposed to active sonars where a signal is transmitted and received by
the same transducer, the passive system is based on hydrophones only, includ-
ing no transmitting element. This prevents foreign forces from detecting and
possibly destroying the system.
This project studies the possibility of measuring diﬀerent sound sources and
thence derive the time delay to facilitate localization of the emitting source.
Two methods to derive the time delay have been investigated numerically. The
localization method is based on the Time Diﬀerence of Arrival (TDoA) between
two signals and uses 2D trigonometry to back trace the source.
Field tests
have been conducted using a pinger and explosives as sources. The data have
been processed using the cross correlation and the localization method, sound
ranging. The pinger data found a unique solution deviating from the measure-
ment location, while the measurements from the explosives did not ﬁnd a unique
source location.

Chapter 1
Introduction & Motivation
In both 2017 and 2018 it has been estimated by the Secret Service of the Danish
Defense that the threat coming from Russia is rising.[1] They are especially
expanding within bombers and submarines for which reason increasing needs of
security around own military territory are present.
During World War I it was realized that detecting and locating enemy posi-
tions should not only rely on air reconnaissance from observation posts, as the
enemy’s ﬁeld artillery often deployed some distance back from the front line,
making them invisible.[2] For this reason the interest of new localization meth-
ods based on acoustic observation arose.
This became the beginning of the
development of sound ranging. Many experiments were started independent of
each other, and in 1916 the French Army tested and implemented the ﬁrst sys-
tem successfully.[3] The initial system was based on manual observations using a
timer, which limited the system’s accuracy. After the ﬁrst World War the tech-
nological development continued and lead to some well implemented methods
used today.
Nowadays it is possible to detect when foreign objects approach an area above
the surface with high accuracy. However, detecting objects underwater is more
complicated as the sound velocity can change rapidly due to diﬀerent factors.[4]
These changes has to be included in the modelling which means that well-
established mature techniques applied above the water mirror cannot be directly
applied for underwater signals.
Through decades radar technology has been a well-known and widely applied
technology both within telecommunication and for military application. Op-
erating and processing signals in air has its challenges but applying similar
technologies under water complicates the signal processing due to the possibil-
ity of raid changes in the sound velocity. By the end of World War II, the ﬁrst

sonars were developed and able to detect submarines at distances up to 1 or 2
km.[5] Nowadays sonars are widely used in navies all over the world for diﬀerent
purposes.
Both radar and sonar systems are based on active sensors emitting a sound
wave and detecting a target based on the diﬀerence between the transmitted
and reﬂected signal. However, using an active detection method also enables
the risk of letting the foreign forces know that they are under surveillance.
1.1
Underwater history
The earliest recording of realizing that sound propagates in water is dated back
to Aristotle (384-322 BC). Also Leonardo da Vinci has written about underwater
acoustics stating that “If you cause your ship to stop and place the head of a
long tube in the water and place the other extremity to your ear, you will hear
ships at great distances”.[4]
However, initial experiments were not started until the beginning of the 19th
century. In 1826 the speed of sound in water was measured by Daniel Colladon.[4]
The real interest for underwater acoustics gained speed during the two World
Wars and especially during the cold war this was an important topic. The fun-
damentals of the sonar technology known today originates from the time of the
world wars.[4]
1.1.1
Wave basics
Sound is often described as a longitudinal pressure wave propagating through
a medium.
When visualizing the measured signal of a wave in e.g.
Matlab
the wave appears as a transverse wave instead.[5] The propagation medium
can be e.g. air, or as in this case, water. Many properties proven for sound
propagation in air can be applied in underwater technology, too.
However,
some assumptions have to be made regarding the structure of the water, speed
of sound and potential noise sources in the surroundings.
Considering two sine waves with the same frequency, the sum will be another
sine wave, with diﬀerent amplitude and phase than the two original waves. If
the frequency is not the same for the two sinusoid, the sum will not be another
sinusodial wave. By adding together an inﬁnite number of sound waves e.g.
a squared looking wave can be created.[6] In this way every possible periodic

waveform and function in existence can be generated by adding together diﬀerent
sets of sinusoidal waves. The density of frequencies can be measured and will
often be higher around some frequencies than others. This is called the frequency

from the nature of the signal by including both the frequency spectrum and the

1.2
Underwater characteristics
Sound waves travel faster through water compared with propagation in air. De-
termining the exact speed of sound involves diﬀerent factors such as; season,
salinity, depth and temperature.[5] Besides these direct eﬀects on the velocity,
propagation loss is also an important factor in underwater technology. This loss
may be due to both spreading and attenuation.[4] Furthermore is the propaga-
tion loss frequency dependent and higher frequency signals tends to have more
loss than signals at low frequencies.
Based on data from the Defence Center of Operational Oceanography (FCOO)
the mean value of the speed of sound in seawater is estimated to be c = 1500 m/s.
However, the velocity may change over short distances due to thermocline and
halocline layers.
Also sound channels may exist, in where sound can travel
with almost no propagation loss.[4] These channels are called Sound Fixing
and Ranging Channels (SOFAR) and are commonly used for communication of
marine animals and to detect earth quakes at distances up to 1500 km.[7]
To get a better estimate of the speed of sound assuming no sound channels are
present, a variety of empirical formula exist. Diﬀerent parameters are considered
and according to Leroy the speed of sound (given in m/s) can be calculated by

Furthermore, may the transmitted signal be aﬀected by the surroundings, which

that has to be taken into account.[4] Some noise sources can be separated and
classiﬁed into diﬀerent frequency ranges, and hence be eliminated with respect
to the signal of interest.

1.2.1
Detection method
In [8] suggestions of four common methods of estimating the distance to a source
are given; 1. the angle of arrival (AoA), 2. the time of arrival (ToA), 3. time
diﬀerence of arrival (TDoA) and lastly 4. the received signal strength indicator
(RSSI). Here the ﬁrst two methods are based on active localization, which is
commonly used in many commercial available devices. Figure 1.1 shows the
basic concept of active localization, where a signal is transmitted from e.g. a
ship. If an object is present, the signal will be returned as an echo to a receiving
transducer. The receiving transducer will often be the same as the transmitting
transducer.[5] On the other hand does a passive system only listen to noise from
the surroundings, thus basic systems give no information about the range of the
object.[5] As this thesis focuses on passive detection methods only, the TDoA
is considered as the optimal applied method.
1.3
State of the art
The harbour surveillance systems found on the marked today all depends on
active elements containing transducers, with both transmitting and receiving
elements.
These devices uses the time diﬀerence between the outgoing and
incoming sound wave to determine whether a foreign object is present or not.
The distance to an object is determined from the ToA of the sound wave.
Some commercial passive sonars can be found on the marked today. However,
these are all based on a towed recording arrays and are very expensive. Fur-

1.4
Motivation
Since no completely passive system for harbour surveillance can be found, it
is desired to look into diﬀerent opportunities of a simple but suﬃcient passive
setup for underwater protection.
Designing a completely passive system serves multiple application for the Danish
Navy. First of all the main purpose is to built and test a system applicable for
harbour surveillance due to the rising threats from foreign forces.
Here the
system aims to detect and potentially identify an approaching object, without
being detected them-self.
Furthermore, the setup is desired as a localization setup. Both when a foreign
object is detected, but also for training purposes. Around Odden at Zealand a
training area for the navy is located. When training with artillery from the ships
it is desired to know the location of the impact. The current methods are based
on visualization, which is not a very precise detection method on its own. Thus,
a passive sonar system could help locate the impact more precisely by using a
mathematical model rather than individual estimation from the observer.
Lastly, the passive setup is expected to help identifying own equipment and to
verify the safety distance for a diver, when a detonation takes place.

1.4.1
Objective of the thesis
The objective of this thesis is to investigate the opportunity of creating a passive
detection system for harbour surveillance in collaboration with the Danish Navy.
The system aims only to use passive devices such as hydrophones. By laying out
hydrophones at known coordinates it will be investigated if foreign objects with
a well deﬁned frequency, can be detected, identiﬁed and located using the time
diﬀerence of arrival (TDoA) and sound ranging. Basic Digital Signal Processing
(DSP) tools will be applied to identify the frequencies speciﬁc for a given source
and to ﬁnd the time delay between the diﬀerent recorded signals.

2.1
Basic concepts of underwater sound
Sound can be described as regular motion of molecules of an elastic medium.
Due to the elasticity of the medium, a motion of particles will initiate movement
of adjacent particles.[4] In this way a sound wave is propagated outward from
the source initiating the sound. In a ﬂuid the particle motion is parallel to the
direction of propagation.[4] A plane pressure wave travelling through water is
detected by measuring the wave pressure at a given time using a hydrophone.
The pressure of a plane sound wave is given by
p = ρcu,
(2.1)
where ρ is the density of the water (kg/m3), c is the sound wave propagation
velocity in water (m/s) and u is the ﬂuid particle velocity (m/s).[4] The intensity
of the pressure wave is normally used to describe the sound. In underwater
acoustics a reference unit of micro Pascal is often used.[4]
The sound level is normally expressed in the number of decibel (dB) by which
its intensity (or energy ﬂux density) diﬀers from the intensity of the reference
sound wave. The dB scale compares intensities (or energy densities) rather than
using a direct acoustic pressure and is in underwater acoustics often written as
dB re 1 µPa.[4] This will be elaborated more upon later in this chapter.

2.1.1
Background noise of the sea
When measuring underwater sound and detecting foreign objects it is important
to be able to identify and eliminate the background noises originating from the
sea itself. In [4] some of the main sources have been deﬁned and categorized.
Figure 2.1 shows the noise level in some bays and harbours as a function of
frequency. The data is recorded in diﬀerent locations during World War II.[4]
The CC line shows an average of the recorded data. The shaded area is the
noise level at subsonic frequencies.
First noise source to mention is the ambient noise. The ambient noise refers
to the noise originating from the sea itself. In other words this is deﬁned as
the “left over” noise. Even though the ambient noise originates from all sides it
is anisotropic.[4] The ambient noise level is the intensity given in dB measured
with a non-directional hydrophone and is always reduced to a 1 Hz frequency
band, also referred to as the ambient noise spectrum level.[4]
The sea contains lots of diﬀerent noise sources. Some examples are; cable strum-
ming, splashes of waves against diﬀerent objects and identiﬁable distant noise
sources including e.g. individual ships. Ambient noise often sounds like a low
rumble at low frequencies and like crackling hiss at high frequencies.[4]
Other noise sources to be mentioned are the tides and hydrostatic eﬀects of
waves. Most hydrophones are pressure sensitive and will respond to any changes

in ambient pressure no matter whether it has an acoustic origin or not. Tides
and waves causes hydrostatic pressure changes with relatively large amplitude
at very low frequencies.[4]
As hydrophones are built from piezoelectric transducers, which are also pyroelec-
tric, a change in voltage will occur as a result of temperature changes caused by
the tides. The voltage change can be up to 12 V/◦C.[4] A pyroelectric material is
a naturally polarized crystal containing a large electrical ﬁeld. This material has
the ability to generate a temporary voltage when the material is either cooled
or heated. The temperature change modiﬁes the atom position slightly within
the crystal structure which causes a change in polarization. If the temperature
changes to a new constant temperature the pyroelectrical voltage will gradu-
ally disappear due to leakage currents. This means that hydrophones always
requires a thermal shield to avoid this eﬀect. However, for very low frequencies
the thermal shield will not be eﬀective making it impossible to distinguish be-
tween thermal and pressure eﬀects.[4] This is not assumed to be a problem in
this project as data sheets conform the thermal shield.
Surface waves is another source of hydrostatic pressure changes.
In shallow
waters a rough surface can become a dominant source of low frequency back-
ground noise. This is a relevant noise source to include in this thesis due to the
application in harbour entrances which are considered shallow waters.
Lastly to mention is oceanic turbulence which will also causes additional back-
ground noise. For more information of potential sources of back ground noise,
the reader is referred to [4].
2.2
Digital signal processing
The formula stated in this section is with reference to [10] unless otherwise
stated.
Nowadays signals are most often divided into two categories; analog and digital
signals. This thesis only considers digital signal processing (DSP) as it has some
advantages and is the most widely used technique within signal processing. Some
advantages to be mentioned is that it is cheaper than real-time processing and
DPS hardware often allows more reliable and ﬂexible solutions with a higher
accuracy.[10] However, digital processing of analog signals has some drawback
due to the sampling of the signal.
Related to quantization of the sampling,
distortion arises and may make it impossible to reconstruct the original analog
signal. This eﬀect can be minimized by choosing the right sampling rate.

pendent variable (often time). These signals are everywhere in the everyday
life. Some examples are; rainfall data for a given period, stock prices and the
grade distribution for a particular course. Another application of discrete time
signals can be when simulating continuous time systems.[10] If a signal cannot
be described by a speciﬁc function, it may be possible to describe it by a sum

Since most signals in science and engineering are analog (a function of a contin-

is when values of an analog signal is selected at a discrete time instants.[10]
Taking the analog representation of a basic sinusoidal signal
x(t) = A sin(ωt) = A sin(2πft),
where A is the amplitude, ω is the angular frequency (in rad/s) and f is the

Here ω0 is the digital angular frequency in rad/sample, θ is the phase and f0 is
the digital frequency in cycles/sample.
The discrete time signal is the result from sampling the continuous time signal
at regular intervals. This can also be written as
x(n) = xcont(nTs),
Ts = 1/fs,
with Ts being the sampling time in seconds and fs is the sampling frequency
given in samples/sec or Hertz [Hz]. Furthermore the sampling frequency relates
the analog and digital frequencies to each other by f0 = f/fs. f0 is also known
as the normalized frequency. This means that the digital angular frequency can
be found from ω0 = 2πf/fs.
When reconstructing a signal the result can be ambiguous due to aliasing. Alias-
ing is when a high frequency signal is sampled at a too low sampling rate, then
the sampled signal does not look like the original signal and will appear at a
much lower frequency. This eﬀect can be avoided by choosing fs suﬃciently

After the discrete time sequence has been collected or sampled, the processing

be compressed which is very relevant nowadays with the huge amount of data

properties of the signal. This information is often kept in the phase, amplitude
and frequency of the signal.
The purpose of signal processing is to extract
this sort of detailed information. In this project a very interesting and crucial
property to analyze is the frequencies. Lastly the manipulation of the signal
helps reduce noise in order to create a better and more sharp signal picture.[10]

2.2.3
Signal-to-Noise Ratio
The Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) is the ratio between the power of the signal
and the power of the noise. The SNR can be calculated in diﬀerent ways but
most often it is convenient to express value in dB, which is commonly used
within sound and signal engineering. The SNR is often deﬁned as being the
average signal power relative to the average noise power.
SNRpower = Psignal
Pnoise
,
SNRdB = 10 log10(SNRpower).
(2.22)
Here Psignal is the average power of the signal, while Pnoise is the average power
of the noise. The power is found using the right-hand side of (2.20) when using
the DFT.
A large SNR means that the actual signal of interest is not covered in noise
while a negative SNR is when the power of the noise is greater than the power
of the signal itself, which will be impossible to distinguish in time domain.
When calculating the SNR it is always deﬁned in a given bandwidth. To compare
diﬀerent SNR the bandwidth has to be stated. Having a big bandwidth of the
receiver means that the noise spreads out over more frequencies and the noise
level increases, which results in a lower SNR than for a receiver with small
bandwidth.[12]

Another thing to notice when determining the SNR from experimental data, is
that the signal measurement often includes noise, too. This means that instead
of getting the S/N ratio the actual ratio found is the signal plus noise to noise
ratio, (S+N)/N. Assuming the signal power is much larger than the noise, this
diﬀerence is without much inﬂuence.[12] When calculating the theoretical SNR
this eﬀect can be omitted by ﬁnding the signal power from data without added
noise. For this reason it will not be discussed further in this thesis.
2.2.3.1
Bandwidth
Just as signals can be classiﬁed based on their time-domain characteristics, they
can also be classiﬁed from the frequency-domain characteristics, which in some
cases can be an advantage. Signals are often divided into three categorize; low-
frequency signals, high-frequency signals and band-pass signals. Illustrations of
the three cases can be found in Appendix A. Besides these three classiﬁcations
it is often an advantage to express quantitatively the range of frequencies over
which the power or energy spectrum is concentrated.[10] This measure is called
the signal bandwidth BW. A narrowband signal is deﬁned as a signal with a
much smaller bandwidth (a factor 10 or more) than the median frequency. The
opposite case is called wideband. A bandlimited signal is a signal with zero
spectrum outside the frequency range deﬁned by the bandwidth |f| ≥BW.
Similarly a signal can also be deﬁned as time-limited if
x(t) = 0,
|t| > τ.
(2.23)
Due to the reciprocal relationship between the time duration and the frequency
domain of a signal, it can be claimed that no signal can be both time- and band-
limited simultaneously.[10] As an example, if a signal is a short rectangular time
domain pulse, the frequency spectrum will have a width inversely proportional
to the time duration of the pulse. Thus it is not possible to make the time-
bandwidth product arbitrary small for any signal.[10]

2.2.5
Correlation
Another well-known way to determine the time delay between two signals is
by computing the correlation. This method is often used in radar and sonar
technology, where the time delay between the transmitted and received signal
is used for source localization.
Assuming an object is present to reﬂect the
transmitted signal x(n), a receiver will record a signal of the form
y(n) = αx(n −τ) + ω(n).
(2.35)
Here α is an attenuation factor representing the loss of the signal, τ is the time
delay and ω(n) is the noise caused by any electrical components and ampliﬁers
contained in the front end of the receiver.[10] In the case of no present target,
the received signal will only contain noise and no correlation will be found. A
challenge within radar and sonar technology is that the delayed signal x(n−τ) is
often corrupted by additive noise from the surroundings in such a way that the
received signal y(n) does not show the presence nor the absence of the signal
reﬂected from the object.
Here correlation is an important tool in order to
extract the desired information from the signal.
Since this project covers a passive localization system only, the correlation be-
tween transmitted and received signal, which is transmitted and received by the
same transducer is not possible. Instead the correlation between the received
signal of diﬀerent transducers/hydrophones can be used. This requires a diﬀer-
ent localization method, which will be discussed later in this chapter. However,
the method of deriving the time delay between two signals is the same as used
in radar and sonar systems.

2.3
Localization of an object
Looking at the history, source localization is a well-known problem within the
military. Especially during the World Wars new localization methods based on
acoustic observations was developed, as it was desired to localize the enemies
location and artillery, using better and more precise methods than visualization
only.
Nowadays both radar and sonar technology are well developed technologies and
they have been known for decades. However, these techniques uses active local-
ization methods based on the diﬀerence in the transmitted and received signal.
Using an active system enables the risk of letting the enemy know that a surveil-
lance system is enabled, which is undesired by DALO.
2.3.1
Sound ranging
One completely passive localization technique developed during World War I is
sound ranging. The method was ﬁrst developed in air and is based on simple
geometric observations together with the diﬀerence in time of arrival of the
sound emitted by a source (e.g. gunﬁre) received at diﬀerent receivers. It is
crucial to know the exact location of the receivers to achieve the best possible
result. The method only requires three observation stations.[3]
This thesis aims to apply the same method under water using hydrophones as re-
ceivers and a well-deﬁned source as transmitter. A hydrophone array containing
three hydrophones are considered.

2.3.1.1
Advantages and disadvantages
Using sound ranging includes both some advantages and disadvantages. A cou-
ple of advantages to be mentioned are that the method is passive, which means
that since no sound is emitted from the setup itself and it cannot be back-traced
to the hydrophones by a potential enemy. Secondly; the setup is often small
and does not require large antennas or large amount of power.[14]
Some drawbacks of using a passive method is that the speed of sound varies
with temperature, salinity and depth, which can make it hard to ﬁnd the correct
distances based on the derived time delays. When using an active method the
transmitted and received signal passes the same distance so the variation in
sound speed in diﬀerent layers cancels out and is not considered a big problem.
Due to the nature of the water the sound speed might also vary for the diﬀerent
receivers, due to diﬀerent paths of the signal. This can also give rise to error in
the ﬁnal location. Furthermore, sources that does not emit any sound cannot be
detected using a passive method. Also an accurate location of the hydrophones
is crucial.
Another drawback of sound ranging is that it does not allow 3D localization,
which means the depth of the source cannot be derived. Furthermore, if the
hydrophones are placed the same distance above the sea ﬂoor having varying
depth, the hydrophone depths relative to the surface will diﬀer and require 3D
localization. However, it is assumed to be without a bigger inﬂuence in this
project due to the relatively small variations in depth which will be discussed
later.
Lastly it should be mentioned that the three (or more) circles do not necessarily
intersect in one unique point when using sound ranging. This can be caused
by diﬀerent reasons. In [16] four cases with no unique intersection is worked
through.
It is shown how a unique point can be determined by integrating
eﬃcient localization algorithms suited to each of the four cases. This will not
be discussed further in this project and the reader is referred to [16] for further
clariﬁcation.

3.1.4
Phase derivation using Hilbert transform
Another way to derive the phase from a delayed signal is by using the Hilbert
transform. Having two signals; yi = cos(x) and yq = sin(x) a combined signal
can be deﬁned as a complex signal from where x can be derived using the phase
angle.
y = yi + iyq = cos(x) + i sin(x).
To apply this method it requires the two signals are precisely synchronize when
measured, which is very hard to achieve in practice. Instead yq can be approxi-
mated by the Hilbert transform.
The derivation for a cosine with input x = ωt + φ is shown in Section 2.2.4 to
be a sine having the same input. This method should be more stable and easy
to apply as it only includes time domain signal. The signal Y (t) is computed
in Matlab for the sinusoid deﬁned in (3.1). For a sinusoid with no added noise,
the derived phase equals the deﬁned phase ˆθ = θ = π/3, while a deviation is
observed when adding noise with amplitude A = 0.5 Pa. The derived phase is
then ˆθ = 1.0328 which is a deviation of 0.0144, which is lower compared to the
deviation using the frequency spectrum.
The method is also called the Time domain approach, while deriving the phase
using the Fourier transform is called the Frequency domain approach. As for
the Frequency domain approach the derived phase diﬀers more the greater the
noise introduces.
This method requires a well-deﬁned known frequency in order to derive the
phase. For the simulations performed in this thesis, the frequency is always
well-deﬁned, and thus the method can be used. However, the problem is much
more complex for real time measurement and will not be investigated further.

3.2
Time delay derived from the cross correlation
When calculating the time delay from the derived phase, problems occurred
separating the target from the shadow points. For this reason another approach
to derive the time delay has been tested.
In radar and sonar technology a well-known method is to derive the time delay
using the cross correlation between the transmitted and received signal. Since
this project only considers passive methods, no signal is transmitted from the
hydrophone and thus the method cannot be used in the same way. However,
the cross correlation between the signals received on diﬀerent hydrophones can
be used instead as the transmitted signal originates from the same source.[19]
The source used in the experiments is assumed to emit a sinusoidal pulse with a
Gaussian envelope rather than a non-modulated sinusoidal pulse. For this reason
the cross correlation method of sinusoidal signals is not developed. Instead time
derivation using cross correlation is tested on three type of signals; a single
Gaussian modulated sinusoidal pulse, a pulse train consisting of three Gaussian
pulses and lastly for signals with a super Gaussian pulse.

3.3
Chapter summary
This chapter has conﬁrmed that the time delay can be derived from the signal
phase using simulated data. A new localization method was proposed when
deriving the time delay from the frequency domain. This method showed dif-
ﬁculties with distinguishing the real source location from additional found so-
lutions. The method was rejected and the time derivation was found using the
cross correlation between two signals. This method showed promising results
for both signals containing a single Gaussian modulated sinusoidal pulse, signals
with a Gaussian pulse train and signals with two super Gaussian pulses.

Experimental Setup & Data Processing
This chapter describes the equipment used to perform the experiments. Further-

4.1
Experimental setup
To test the theoretical models using real recorded data, experiments have been
conducted recording underwater sound from diﬀerent sources at diﬀerent loca-

Due to daily activity in the military harbour entrance, it has not been possible
to lay out the equipment in a real harbour entrance.
Instead the place for
the experiments has been chosen such that the conditions of a harbour entrance
have been imitated in the best possible way. The location of the experiments has
been chosen to be in a calm area to minimize disturbance from other vessels.
Furthermore, the chosen area has depths varying from 3-10 m, which is also
comparable to the depths of a harbour entrance. The setup is located at the
outside of a pier so reﬂections will be present from one side. This condition is
simpliﬁed compared to a real harbour entrance, where reﬂections will be present
from two or more sides. The chosen pier is located in a bay (Isefjorden) which
has calm conditions compared to a harbour entrance facing the open sea (e.g.
naval base Fredericshavn or naval base Korsør). Additionally, it is the base of
two naval units who assisted with distributing the equipment and performing
the measurements.

hydrophones have been used.

Chapter 5
Discussion
In this chapter the results found in the preceding chapters will be discussed and
proposals will be given for further development of the project, including both
suggestions for further experiments and other methods to derive the time delay
and to localize a source.
5.1
Sources of error
When conducting ﬁeld test in an open environment as for this project, it is cru-
cial to gain as much information about the area and equipment as possible to
minimize the sources of error. In this project the experiments were conducted
in the open sea, which made it impossible to control all factors from the out-
side with high accuracy. Furthermore, it was hard to gain full control of the
experimental setup as the surroundings had a big impact.
5.1.1
Precision of GPS coordinates
When performing measurements for the purpose of source localization, it is
crucial to known the exact position of the placement of the hydrophones, as
well as for the individual measurements, because otherwise inaccuracy may be
introduced in the derived location of the source. In this project a Garmin GPS
logger (model GPSMAP 64s) has been used. The positions of the measurements
were logged while being on a moving rubber boat. The GPS was placed on top
of the line connected to the pinger, while logging. Lowering the pinger into the
water might change the position to not being exactly below the GPS logger, due
to e.g. wind or current.
==PAGE:110==
102
Discussion
Also the locations of the hydrophones are crucial to know. A diver attached a
buoy to each of the frames, which was used as reference to log the coordinates.
However, due to wind and current the exact position of the buoy may drift com-
pared to the hydrophone introducing inaccuracy in the location. Furthermore,
may the position of the hydrophone change during the experiments. This eﬀect
was tried to be minimized placing a sandbag over each frame. Still the location
may vary up to ±1 m in both the NS and EW direction, which will also eﬀect
the results. A way to minimize the inﬂuence of the current is to bury the frame
with the hydrophone into the seabed and secure the frame even more than with
one sandbag.
5.1.1.1
Explosion data
For the measurements performed with explosives, it has not been possible to log
the exact coordinates, as the explosives were deposited into the water from the
pier. This means that the actual location remains unknown, hence the derived
locations cannot be either veriﬁed nor rejected. Only an approximate location
is known from the area where the explosives were originally disposed.
Since it has not been possible to detect the location, it is not possible to estimate
the safety distance for a diver. The pressure amplitude can be found from the
time domain of the recordings, but since the distance to the detonation remains
unknown this cannot be taken any further using the collected data.
For further investigation it is important to know the location of the source,
whether it is explosives or a well-deﬁned sound source like the pinger. Without
knowing the position the results cannot be veriﬁed, which makes them unusable.
5.1.2
Sound velocity
Another important source of error is the sound velocity in water. Throughout
this thesis the velocity is assumed constant with a value of c = 1500 m/s. This
estimate is based on models provided by FCOO.
Another eﬀect regarding the velocity is the presence of thermocline and halocline
layers. These can be very profound and have a big inﬂuence on the propagation
of a sound wave. For future experiments these eﬀects should be investigated
further, in order to obtain more precise results. Even though the models are
good, the velocity can be found even more precise by measuring the properties
at the exact day. This can be done using a Sound Velocity Proﬁler (SVP) which
==PAGE:111==
5.1 Sources of error
103
measures the sound velocity in the entire depth. For future experiment it is
highly recommended to used the SVP in order to eliminate the sound speed as
a source of error.
5.1.3
3D localization
Several parameters may aﬀect the results, such as the sound velocity in wa-
ter, the precision of the logged coordinates and the varying depth of the hy-
drophones. The hydrophones are placed approximately 1 m above the seabed,
which has a varying height in the area used to perform the experiments. Hence,
the hydrophones are placed in varying heights relative to the surface. Since the
pinger is lowered into the water at a depth ∼1 m from the surface, there is also
a variation in depth of the detection of the transmitted signal. This may also
have an impact on the ﬁnal localization since sound ranging is a 2D method
only. However, the depth variation is assumed to be without a bigger inﬂuence
in this project due to the relatively small variations which at maximum is ∼7 m.
To eliminate this as a source of error other localization methods should be
considered. In [22] hyperbolic localization is used as a 3D method. However,
this requires at least four hydrophones and an optimal estimator for the initial
guess of location should be provided to the algorithms. [23] investigates diﬀerent
eﬃcient estimators for hyperbolic localization.
5.1.4
Distance estimation
Since the localization of a source is a two part problem, the method used to
derive the time delay should also be considered as a potential source of error.
This thesis tests two methods to derive the time delay; namely using the phase
and the cross correlation. The ﬁrst method uses the phase and frequency spec-
trum, which introduced a signiﬁcant number of shadow points, that cannot be
separated from the actual solution. The second method tested is a time domain
approach using the cross correlation between two signals. The results showed
more promising results in the simulations, where a unique solution was found
by using four hydrophones. However, no unique solution was found for the data
from the explosives. In spite of derivations of up to 15 m for the pinger data,
the found solutions were promising for both the NS and WE measurement. The
deviation can be caused by one or more of the mentioned sources of errors.
In [24] it is suggested to used the Generalized Cross Correlation (GCC) between
two groups of signals. The GCC is the weighted cross correlation, which high-
==PAGE:112==
104
Discussion
lights the relevant signal components and suppress components disturbed by
noise.[24] The GCC also shows promising results for noisy signals.[25] Thus the
next step of this project could include to test the GCC as a method to derive
the time delay if the cross correlation does not show suﬃcient accuracy in future
processing.
5.1.5
Array pattern
The array pattern used in this thesis is chosen based on considerations of how to
minimize the number of shadow points appearing from symmetry in the setup.
For this reason the tested array is arbitrary asymmetric.[26] A more optimal
array pattern may be found. Several articles ([25, 27]) uses a linear microphone
array to localize an acoustic source. Using a linear array of hydrophones can
potentially make the localization problem easier. Also the background noise
and echo from the pier might be more aligned dependent on the exact location
relative to the pier.
5.2
Experimental data
In Chapter 4 data processing for two types of measurements were worked through.
The pinger data turned out to have a good signal to noise ratio of 32.5 dB for
the NS measurement and 46.0 dB for the EW measurement.
Both SNR are
found over a bandwidth of BW = 1.5 kHz. The frequency spectra still showed
presence of background noise most apparent at low frequencies. This is the same
tendency as seen in the spectrum of classiﬁed sources of ambient noise, shown
in Figure 2.1. Further studies and ﬁeld work has to be carried out in order to
get the full appreciation of the ambient noise spectrum and its inﬂuence at the
received signals.
Despite the relative good SNR of the pinger signal ﬁltering can also be considered
as a tool tested in further processing. Only brief tests with a bandpass ﬁlter
have been conducted for the numerical data. The initial studies did not give a
better precision and optimal ﬁltering of underwater signals gives rise to a whole
new study, for which reason it has not been taken any further in this thesis.
Even though the SNR of the pinger signals was high, the correct location of the
source was not found. The deviations to the actual location of the measurement
were 15 m and 9 m for the NS and EW measurement, respectively.

For the measurements of Explosion 1, neither of the derived solutions are close
to the actual detonation area, while for Explosion 2 two solutions are placed
within the area of the detonation. Both cases shows that the expectation of
ﬁnding one unique solution does not apply.
The deviation in the found results can be caused by many diﬀerent factors,
including unknown objects at the seabed causing undesired reﬂections or scat-

that can help understand the deviating results and hopefully eliminate another
source of error by either remove these objects or account for them during the
data processing.
Reﬂection from the pier also has to be implemented in the model as these might
give rise to detection of the same signal more than once. Here interference of
the signals can be a problem.
5.3
Other applications
As brieﬂy mentioned earlier this setup serves the possibility of being used for
other applications than harbour surveillance.
One of them is to help increase human safety by verifying the theoretical safety
distances for divers during a detonation. This requires that one can determine
the exact distance from the source to the recorder, where the sound pressure
is measured. To verify the theoretical values, more measurements have to be
carried out. Performing these experiments it is important to know the water
structure and hence the sound velocity, in order to ensure no underestimation
takes place. Other problems that can arise is if the pressure amplitude exceeds
the limitations of the system and thus cannot be measured.
Furthermore the recording of the detonatins gave rise to another possible ap-
plication of the system; namely detection of impacts from the ships. Around
Zealnad’s Odde the navy has a naval shooting range close to the coast. This
area is used for training the abilities of hitting a predeﬁned target with artillery.
At the moment the impact is only detected by mans eye, which is not very
precise. By laying out a system similar to the one built and tested in this the-
sis it will potentially be possible to detect and locate the impact with a much
higher precision. However, it requires a lot more testing and optimization of lo-
calization method before a fully operational setup is a reality. When detecting
impacts used the artillery can vary. Both the size of the projectile and the type
of explosive may vary resulting in diﬀerent emitted pressure waves.

A third possible application of the system besides harbour surveillance, is to

Chapter 6
Conclusion
This thesis has investigated the possibility of creating an only passive system
for the purpose of harbour surveillance and localization of foreign sources.
The applied theory of Digital Signal Processing has been explained, including
how to derive the frequency domain of both periodic and aperiodic signals and
giving a veriﬁcation of Parseval’s theorem in the diﬀerent cases. Calculation of
the cross correlation between two signals has been addressed and it has been
showed how to localize a source from a three and four hydrophone setup.
Two diﬀerent methods to derive the time delay has been simulated. The ﬁrst
method used the frequency domain to identify the source from the signal fre-
quency and hence derive the time delay from the signal phase. This method
showed several additional shadow points, when trying to localize the source.
From the simulations it was shown that the correct location could not be dis-
tinguished from the shadow point, thus no unique solution was found.
The second method investigated only depends on the time delay.
The time
delay between the incoming signal at two diﬀerent hydrophones was found by
computing the cross correlation.
The time delay is derived from the maxi-
mum correlation between the signals. Having a three hydrophone setup showed
that localizing a source could be done by applying the geometric method sound
ranging. Sound ranging uses simple geometry setting up two equation with two
unknowns. If two solutions were found, the model showed that a unique solu-
tion could be found by adding an extra hydrophone and setting up an additional
equation.
Field tests were conducted in the beginning of the project. An array consisting
of six hydrophones was built and distributed in an area with similar conditions
as a harbour entrance. Three types of experiments were conducted; one using a
==PAGE:116==
108
Conclusion
pinger as source, one measuring explosions and one trying to ﬁnd the detection
range of an engine.
For the experimental data only the time domain method using the cross corre-
lation was applied. Two measurements using the pinger have been analyzed by
computing the cross correlation and applying sound ranging. The exact loca-
tion of the measurement was not found for either of the pinger measurements.
However, a solution deviating 15 m was found for the measurement along the
North-South (NS) route, while for the East-West (EW) route the deviation to
the logged location was 9 m. The SNR was calculated for both signals giving a
maximum value of 32.5 dB and 46.0 dB for the NS and EW data, respectively.
Both SNR are found over a bandwidth of BW = 1.5 kHz.
After investigating the pinger data, the data from the explosions were pro-
cessed. For the ﬁrst data-set no mathematical solution was found in the area of
the detonation, however, for the second data-set two potential locations of the
detonation were found. Due to the execution of the experiments it has not been
possible to conclude if any of the solutions are correct.
Lastly the detection range of an engine was brieﬂy investigated. At a distance
of > 500 m the noise from the engine could still be detected.
All in all a functional model was developed to derive the time delay and ﬁnd a
unique solution for the location. The experimental data gave some challenges,
but since the experiments were performed in a open environment many param-
eters might aﬀect the results.
6.1
Further studies
Since no unique solution was found for either the pinger data nor for the explo-
sion data, future experiments should be more focused on eliminating potential
sources of errors.
By repeating more measurements using explosions, the objective should be to
eliminate some sources of error and hence to increase the precision of the local-
ization. However, this would require meticulous attention to the exact position
of the explosion, as the actual location of the detonation should be detected
more precisely. A way to log the correct location can be by placing a target
with known coordinates and aiming to hit the target. This might require lots of
experiments to get just one aim. Another approach is to mount the explosive
at a know location and remotely detonate it.
Executing the experiments this way would also focus the purpose of the project
==PAGE:117==
6.1 Further studies
109
to source location for training at Zealand’s Odde.
If the pinger is used as the source, a signal proﬁle should be investigated further.
This could be done by place the pinger close to the hydrophone in both a basin
and in open waters. In this way it could be investigated how the source proﬁle
changes dependent on the background noise and scattering eﬀects from the walls
of the basin. Using the pinger it is important to ensure the logged locations are
correct and does not deviate remarkably due to current or other environmental
eﬀects. Additionally, the calibration of the pinger is very important as this can
reduce the inaccuracies of the measurements.
Another thing to investigate in the future is the optimal array pattern for the
system. This can be done by testing both linear and arbitrary arrays. Due
to the construction of the setup, the array can easily be rearranged for further
tests.
When ﬁnding the optimal array pattern and the localization method has been
optimized for a stationary source, it is desired to test the setup on a moving tar-
get such as an Autonomous Underwater Vehicle (AUV) or an approaching diver.
This might give rise to new challenges due to a low SNR and low operational
frequency, which is expected to be hard to distinguish from the background
noise.
Even though the frequency spectrum is not used to derive the time diﬀerence of
arrival, knowing the frequency of a source can be very useful in identiﬁcation and
classiﬁcation of material. Optimally a database could be generated containing
the sound level of diﬀerent vehicles, ships, types of diving equipment etc. and
in which frequency domain they can be classiﬁed. In this way the frequency
spectrum can also be used for initial identiﬁcation as it is often more easy to
see if an incoming signal originates from an object rather than the background
noise using the the frequency spectrum.

At low frequencies, the sensitivity of a hydrophone is the

Traceable Calibration
The sound pressure level in the coupler volume can be
monitored with a ½ microphone (see Fig. 7), enabling the
calibration to be traceable to international standards.
Hydrophone Calibration System Type 9718
Type 9718 uses LAN-XI data acquisition hardware and dedicated hydrophone calibration software based
on PULSE. Calibrations performed using Type 9718 are in accordance with IEC 60565 with optional phase
response calibration.
Type 9718 is a scalable system that can be configured for:
• Primary calibration of frequency response by the reciprocity method
• Secondary calibration as receiver by the substitution method using a known reference hydrophone
• Calibration of the projecting response, for example pressure in Pa/V versus frequency 
• Directivity calibration at multiple customer defined frequencies

Signal Conditioning and Data Acquisition
In some cases, it is necessary to route the high-impedance output signals through a signal-conditioning
amplifier. Although the international standards call for calibration of the hydrophones’ voltage sensitivity
(using a voltage amplifier), it is often more practical to use a charge amplifier, which will result in a charge
sensitivity independent of the actual cable length. 
Recommended Signal Conditioner
Although the entire Brüel & Kjær range of accelerometer preamplifiers
is suitable, the NEXUS™ Range of Conditioning Amplifiers (Product
Data: BP 1702) are recommended because they feature sensitivity
adjustment (for convenient output values: 0.1 or 1 V/Unit), have a wide
frequency range and have adjustable low- and high-pass filters.
Type 8106
The built-in preamplifier provides an output signal that does not require further conditioning. 
Discontinued Brüel & Kjær Signal Conditioners
The following signal conditioners are no longer manufactured but can be used: 
• Conditioning Amplifier Type 2635, requires a cable terminating with a proprietary connector, referred to
as a B&K 7-pin connector such as Cable AO-0390 
• Measuring Amplifier Type 2525, requires a BNC to TNC adaptor such as Adaptor JP-0226
Charge to CCLD Converter 
In case you wish to connect a charge output (for example, output from
Types 8103, 8104 and 8105) to CCLD input, you can use Charge to CCLD
Converter Type 2647 (Product Data: BP 1874).
Data Acquisition Hardware
The recommended LAN-XI front-end module for hydrophones is Type 3161 (Product
Data: BP 2215). The module features one input and one output channel with
frequency ranges of DC to 204.8 kHz.

Hydrophone Types 8103, 8104 and 8105 can be used
as sound transmitters (projectors), for both
measurement and reciprocity calibration. Just as the
piezoelectric effect produces an electrical output from
mechanical excitation, applying alternating voltage to
the transducer will produce mechanical excitation in
the piezoelectric element. 
Transmitting responses to applied voltage in water are
shown in Fig. 11. Note the 12 dB/octave slope which
is typical for piezoelectric hydrophones driven with
constant voltage input.
Amplification of the Projected Signal
When hydrophones are used as projectors as with Hydrophone Calibration System Type 9718, the signal
requires power amplification. Power Amplifier Type 2713 (Product Data: BP 0228) is suited for driving
signals transmitted from hydrophones, and it can be used to drive other highly reactive loads. 
Type 2713 is a low-noise 100 VA power amplifier with a frequency range extending from 10 Hz up to
200 kHz, and a gain continuously adjustable between 0 and 60 dB over six 10 dB ranges. It also features
selectable maximum output voltage limits, as well as extensive protective functions to prevent damage to
both the amplifier and the transducer.
