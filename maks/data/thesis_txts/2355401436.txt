Abstract
Gas journal bearings are becoming more and more popular in turbo-machinery owing to their
many advantages. The gas bearings can operate at higher rotational speeds compared to many
other bearing designs, while generating almost no noise or heat. The bearings are contact free
meaning they have no mechanical wear. Furthermore, the lubricant, air, is cheap, abundant,
non-ﬂammable, and clean.
However, gas journal bearings also have some disadvantages. Due to the low viscosity of the lu-
bricant, load carrying capacity is low. Furthermore, gas bearings have low damping properties,
which means that running close to or crossing the critical speeds can lead to big instabilities
of the system.
In this thesis, the dynamic and static characteristics of an externally pressurised active gas
journal bearing with four supply oriﬁces are investigated, both numerically and experimentally.
The ﬂuid dynamics of the gas bearing are modelled and simulated using the Computational
Fluid Dynamic (CFD) software OpenFoam. OpenFoam is used to analyse and characterise
the injection ﬂow by achieving velocity proﬁles, pressure proﬁles, and mass ﬂow rates of the
injection nozzles of the gas bearing for diﬀerent eccentricities, injector valve openings, injection
pressures, and journal angular velocities.
The mass ﬂow rate from Modiﬁed Reynold’s Equation (MRE) and the mass ﬂow rate from the
CFD will be used to obtain a discharge coeﬃcient, Cd. The discharge coeﬃcient is necessary
in order to compensate for the assumptions of the Modiﬁed Reynold’s Equation, which does
not ﬁt to the physical characteristics of the injection nozzles of the active gas bearing. A Cd
function is created from all of the discharge coeﬃcient values for the diﬀerent cases. This is
done in order to have a function that can interpolate Cd coeﬃcients between cases. The Cd
function is implemented in the MRE model in order to achieve a more precise prediction of the
dynamic bearing coeﬃcients. The ﬂuid ﬁlm reaction forces are obtained from the corrected
MRE along with the damping and stiﬀness coeﬃcients of the bearing.
The damping and stiﬀness coeﬃcients are used in a Finite Element (FE) model of the rotor-
bearing system to determine theoretical natural frequencies, frequency response functions, un-
balance responses and stability maps. With this the theoretical operational range of the system
is determined. These results are validated by comparison with experimental results from a test
rig.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Bearings are a crucial part of rotating machinery. Within the regime of bearings, the gas bear-
ing is very interesting, as it holds a lot of advantages. The gas bearing is suitable for high speed
applications, has no mechanical wear, has low levels of noise and temperature development,
and the working ﬂuid is air, which is cheap, clean, abundant, and non-ﬂammable. Despite the
many advantages of the gas bearing, it has very low damping, which decreases stability and
causes high amplitude responses when crossing critical speeds.
Diﬀerent gas bearing designs have been proposed in order to improve this matter. In this work,
the focus will be on externally pressurised bearings. In a self-acting gas bearing, the relative
motion between the rotor and bearing wall induces a wedge ﬁlm eﬀect that supports the load
[1]. The external pressurisation improve start-up and shut-down properties by lifting the shaft
in the absence of rotation. It also increases the stiﬀness of the gas bearing and improves load
carrying capacity.
However, the externally pressurised bearing is prone to unwanted whirl
instability [1].
1.1
Development of the studied active gas bearing
In an attempt to overcome instabilities Morosi and Santos [2] introduced an active exter-
nally pressurised gas bearing, where the injection was PD-controlled (Proportional-Derivative
controller) and tuned empirically. They demonstrated, that this eﬀectively reduced lateral
vibrations.
In 2014, Theisen et al. [3, 4] established a reduced model for the same system studied by
Morosi and Santos [2], by using grey-box identiﬁcation tools. The model formed the basis of
a new H∞control design, which signiﬁcantly increased the damping ratio of the rotor-bearing
system, and allowed the ﬁrst two critical speeds to be crossed.
As opposed to the presented works [2, 3, 4] using an experimental and empirical approach,
Pierart [5] made a control design for the same active gas bearing system based on a previously
proposed mathematical model anchored to physical laws. Pierart improved the mathematical
model, a modiﬁed Reynold’s equation (MRE), by implementing a correction function based
on Computational Fluid Dynamic (CFD) analysis. It was proved to be in good agreement
with experiments by means of journal equilibrium position [6]. Subsequently, the dynamic
behaviour of the rotor-bearing system was investigated by means of constant injector input
values. Experimental and theoretical values were in good agreement with respect to natural
frequencies and damping ratio [7]. In [8, 9] Pierart proposes three diﬀerent control designs
based on the corrected mathematical model. Two of the controllers increased the damping
ratio of the active gas bearing signiﬁcantly, and made it possible to cross the ﬁrst critical speed
and up to 50% of the second, without encountering instability problems.

1 Introduction
1.2
Aim of this thesis
In this work, the discussed active externally pressurised gas bearing is studied further. The
externally pressurised ﬂuid injection can be regulated dynamically, which allows to control the
characteristics of the bearing ﬁlm and thereby improve the properties and stability limits of
the bearing. However, in order to make a fair control design of the injectors, it is necessary to
know the bearing ﬁlm behaviour and how it aﬀects the rotor-bearing system dynamics.
It is the aim of this thesis to extend the work by Pierart [6, 7] and improve the CFD model,
which supports the mathematical MRE model, such that it is valid in the high speed range
(≤30, 000 rpm). In this respect, a new correction function for the MRE is sought. It is further
the aim to perform an analysis of the rotor-bearing system dynamics and stability, and validate
these theoretical results by experiments.
1.3
Method
By supporting a mathematical MRE model with a CFD analysis, it is possible to make a precise
theoretical prediction of the active gas bearing dynamic coeﬃcients. The ﬂuid dynamics of the
gas bearing will be modelled and simulated using the CFD software OpenFoam. A statistical
parameter analysis is performed on the CFD results in order to deﬁne a new correction function,
which is implemented into the MRE model.
With the gas bearing stiﬀness and damping
coeﬃcients at hand, the natural frequencies and stability threshold of the rotor-bearing system
will be obtained by the Finite Element Method. Lastly, experiments will be carried out in
order to compare and validate the theoretical results.

CHAPTER 3
Computational Fluid Dynamic Analysis
The ﬂuid dynamics of the gas bearing and injector nozzle will be modelled and simulated using
a Computational Fluid Dynamic (CFD) analysis. As the mathematical MRE model makes
assumptions not applicable for the physical system, the CFD analysis is necessary in order
to study and correct these assumptions numerically. To make such a correction the value of
interest of the CFD analysis is the mass ﬂow rate of the injector. However, the CFD analysis
is also interesting as it simulates the ﬂow and gives a complete understanding of how the bear-
ing and ﬂow behave. Pressure and velocity proﬁles will be presented along with relevant ﬂow
visualisations. Simulations will be performed for diﬀerent values of injection pressure, angular
velocity, valve openings and journal eccentricities.
The ﬁrst section of this chapter will present the CFD model. In this section the solver will
be presented along with geometry considerations and a grid independence analysis. The sub-
sequent section describe the convergence criteria. Finally, all the results of the CFD analysis
are presented and analysed.
3.1
The CFD Model
In this section the CFD model will be described. The CFD model used in this project is based
on the model developed by Pierart [5]. The CFD computations are carried out using the and
open source CFD software, OpenFOAM version 2.0.1. The solver implemented in OpenFOAM
is the rhoSimplecFoam solver, which is a steady-state SIMPLEC solver for laminar and turbu-
lent ﬂow of compressible ﬂuids.
Figure 3.1a shows how the gas bearing is modelled in OpenFOAM. In order to decrease com-

The ﬁgure also shows the main boundary conditions for the system. The inlet pressure is set
as a constant pressure corresponding to the injection pressure, and the outlet pressure is set
to atmospheric pressure. The bearing wall is set as ﬁxed wall, while the rotor surface is a
moving wall. Figure 3.1b shows how the geometry has been simpliﬁed by ”unwrapping” the

Relaxation Factors
The correct choice of relaxation factors are critical in order to obtain the best combination
of stability and computational time. Using an excessively high over-relaxation will lead to in-
stability, while choosing too much under-relaxation will lead to excessive computational time.
Generally for the SIMPLEC solver, the relaxation factors are set to 1.0 which decreases com-
putational time of convergence. However, for some cases a relaxation factor of 1.0 introduces
instability, which makes it necessary to reduce the factors. It is not easy choosing the right
==PAGE:33==

factors, and often users are resorted to trial-and-error adjustments in order to obtain the best
results. For the OpenFOAM model used in this project, the best relaxation factors were found
to be 0.7 for velocities and enthalpy, 0.1-0.7 for the pressure, and 0.3-0.7 for the density.
Selecting proper relaxation can be a diﬃcult and frustrating experience for users of computa-
tional ﬂuid dynamics software. The relaxation depends on the speciﬁcs of the problem being
solved, which may change during the evolution of a problem.
Unfortunately, there are no
universal guidelines for selecting relaxation because it depend not only on the physical pro-
cesses being approximated, but also on the details of the numerical formulation. Many CFD
programs have a standard set of recommended relaxation factors, but users must often resort
to trial-and-error adjustments to get good results.

Tolerances
Selecting the optimal convergence tolerance criteria is critical. If choosing too loose values, the
results will be poor, while on the other hand choosing too strict values will result in excessive
computational time. The solver is iterative and based on reducing the equation residual which
is a measure of the error in the solution. So the smaller the residual, the more accurate the
solution.
After each iteration the residual is evaluated, and the solver stops if any of the
following conditions are fulﬁlled:
• The residuals are lower than the deﬁned solver tolerance of each component (tolerance).
• The ratio of current to initial residual falls below the relative solver tolerance (relTol).
• Maximum number of iterations are reached (maxIter).
• The residuals of speciﬁed ﬁelds are lower than corresponding ﬁeld tolerance (residualControl).
In order to obtain the most satisfying solution with regards to precision for our key parameter,
the mass ﬂow rate, and computational time, a tolerance convergence study was performed.

Linear Solver
In OpenFOAM each linear solver (also known as iterative solver) is also deﬁned for each dis-
cretised equation, where the term linear solver refers to the numerical method of solving the
matrix equation. Whereas the application solver, in this model rhoSimplecFoam, describes
the entire set of equations and algorithms used to solve the problem[14, p. U-124]. The linear
solvers for this model has been set to the GAMG solver for the pressure, the velocities and the
enthalpy.
GAMG is an abbreviation for Geometric Agglomerated Algebraic Multigrid.
It is a devel-
opment of the algebraic multi-grid methods. The principle is that the solver generates a quick
solution on a coarse mesh and then maps the solution on a ﬁner mesh using the coarse mesh as
an initial guess to obtain an accurate solution on the ﬁne mesh [14]. This can be done in two
ways, either by geometric coarsening of the grid (geometric multi-grid) or by using algebraic
multi-grid, where the same principles are applied directly to the matrix, not considering the
geometry [15]. It is necessary for the user to set which coarsening or agglomeration algorithm
that should be used, the geometric or the algebraic. The geometric agglomeration algorithm
has proved superior regarding computation time, and is thus used in this model. The iteration
scheme that GAMG carries out [15] is as follows:
1. Obtain ﬁneness of grid from mesh speciﬁcation
2. Agglomerate cells based on the cells faceWeights (based on area of faces or matrix coef-
ﬁcients)

2.1. For each individual cell, calculate faces and go through the faces to produce clusters.
2.1.1. Check faces to ﬁnd neighbour cell with largest face weight.
2.1.2. Check if current cell is face owner or neighbour
2.1.3. When matching cell has been found, collect necessary data in order to produce
cluster, or else ﬁnd best matching neighbouring cluster and add cell
2.2. Conﬁrm all cells are part of clusters, if not create single cell clusters
2.3. Reverse map ordering to improve next level of agglomeration
3. Agglomerate the faceWeights ﬁeld and iterate from step 1 unless the user-speciﬁed ap-
proximate mesh size at the most coarse level (nCoarsestCells) or the maximum number
of grid levels has been reached

able to capture the gradients better.
Hence, the mesh analysis points to the sharp corner causing the convergence problems. In order
to add stability to the solutions and improve convergence, a mesh reﬁnement of the corner is
desired. This is proposed to be done by adding a small ﬁllet.
Fillet
A ﬁllet at the sharp corner is desired in order to improve the mesh and thereby remove high
velocity magnitude boundary cells at the sharp corner and improve convergence and residual
behaviour.
As a change of geometry and mesh is very cumbersome and time-consuming to do in Open-
FOAM, a combination of SolidWorks and ANSYS was tried ﬁrst, but was unsuccessful, see
Appendix A.1 for more information on this. Because of this, it was decided to create the ﬁllet

by the use of OpenFOAM.
A ﬁllet is added to the mesh in OpenFOAM by creating a wedge along the edge where the
injector and the ﬁlm meet. An arc is added to the wedge, and the remaining mesh is adjusted
such that two new mesh regions can be inserted on each side of the wedge, see 1 and 2 in
Figure 3.13a. Mesh block 1 is inserted at the very bottom of the injector pocket and connect
the cells of the ﬁlm and pocket in the y- and z-direction. The resolution in the x-direction can
be chosen freely so that it ﬁts the desired resolution of the ﬁllet. Mesh block 2 is annulus-
shaped and runs just below the ﬁllet along the circumference of the injector. As such, mesh
block 2 allows the desired resolution of the ﬁllet in the y- and z-direction. By introducing the
two new mesh blocks it is possible to control the mesh resolution of the ﬁllet without likewise
changing the entire mesh domain.
The wedge is created as a hexahedron by collapsing two pairs of vertices, see Figure 3.14. In
this example the input for blocks will then become hex (0 1 2 3 4 5 5 4); i.e. using two
vertices twice and triggering OpenFOAM to collapse the points.
This method does however create problems when it comes to meshing. The sharp corners at
each end of the ﬁllet cannot be meshed by hexahedrons without encountering the mesh failure
of non-orthogonality from the checkMesh. This failure is not negligible and leads to large
errors and convergence problems. In order to obtain maximum accuracy for the diﬀusive ﬂux,
the straight line connecting the two neighbouring cell centres must be orthogonal to the cell
face and pass through the cell face centre, see Figure 3.15. If this is not the case the ﬂux
integrated over the surface is poor [13]. In a situation where the distance between crossing
point and centre point is too large, the mesh should be reﬁned until the distance is reduced to
an acceptable limit, equivalent to Figure 3.15a.
It has not been possible to create a satisfactory and error-free mesh of the ﬁllet using blockMesh
in OpenFOAM. As another approach, the arc is removed from the ﬁllet, hence creating a
chamfer type corner.
This geometry is easier to mesh and does not give any errors with
the checkMesh. By introducing the chamfer, the ﬂuctuations of the simulations are reduced
signiﬁcantly. Figure 3.16 shows the chamfer type corner.

axial direction, such that the density of cells is largest closest to the injector. The mesh with
and without grading in axial direction can be seen in Figure 3.17.

3.2
Convergence Criteria
In numerical simulations it is important to assure the validity of the solution. In this respect,
for a steady state simulation, the solution has to fulﬁl four conditions:
• The value of residual errors have reduced to an acceptable limit - typically in the range
10−4 or 10−5. The tolerances in this study are stated in Section 3.1.1.
• The values of interest have reached a steady solution. Meaning that the value remains
fairly constant as the number of iterations increases.
• Imbalances of the domain are below 1 %.
• The solution must be grid independent.
Each condition is elaborated further in the following.
Obviously, it is important to look at the residual error values, as these directly quantify the
error of the solution of the system of equations. The residuals will never equal zero but they

are below the deﬁned tolerance they are considered to be converged. The pressure residuals

The value of interest is in this case the mass ﬂow rate of the injection nozzle. For a converged
solution of a steady state simulation, this quantity of interest should not change from iteration
to iteration. Thus, monitoring this value can indicate whether the solution has converged or
not. As the residuals decrease, the values of interest change less and less between iterations.
But it is important that the values reach a steady solution and stopping the simulation too
early will produce misleading results.
Figure 3.20 shows the mass ﬂow rate as a function of iterations. It is concluded that the mass
ﬂow rate has reached a steady solution after 201 iterations.

fulﬁlled in the ﬁnal solution. The error in conservation is the so called imbalance of the system.
The imbalance will never be exactly zero, but for a converged solution, it is typically required
to be 1% or lower.

3.3
Results
This section contains the results of the CFD simulations. The value of interest is the mass
ﬂow rate, which is used to make a discharge coeﬃcient function for the MRE-model. The CFD
results are also interesting as they provide valuable information of the ﬂow behaviour. Pressure
proﬁles, velocity proﬁles and ﬂow visualizations are presented to describe and understand the
ﬂow.
All simulations have been run on the Technical University of Denmark’s computer cluster.
Through a performance of the most optimal number of cores, it has been concluded that for
the base mesh, 4 cores oﬀers the best performance. Furthermore, a consistency study of the
mass ﬂow rates for diﬀerent number of cores has been carried out to make sure the results were
the same. For theory and in-depth results, see Appendix A.2.2.
To further decrease computational time, bandwidth reduction of the sparse coeﬃcient matrices
has also been looked into. The ﬁnding of this study was, that by using bandwidth reduction, the
computational time could be decreased by as much as 50%. For theory on mesh renumbering,
as well as further results for diﬀerent mesh sizes, visit Appendix A.2.1.
The mass ﬂow rate has been evaluated in a cross section sliced 15 µm above the ﬁlm, see Figure
3.22. The height of the slice was chosen in this way, in order to avoid the bad valued boundary
cells at the sharp corner. All of the post processing results presented are based on the cell
data as opposed to the point data. The reason for this is that cell data oﬀers a more true
representation of the simulation.

The four main parameters aﬀecting the mass ﬂow rate are the journal eccentricity, injector
valve opening, injection pressure and journal angular velocity.
As for the eccentricity, simulations are carried out with an eccentricity in the X-direction, eX,
of 5 µm, 10 µm and 15 µm respectively. The injector is positioned in the bottom of the gas
bearing, see Figure 3.2a. The diﬀerent levels of eccentricity corresponds to an eccentricity of
20%, 40% and 60% as the nominal clearance of the ﬁlm is 25 µm. This eccentricity range is
selected based on a typical operational range of gas bearings [6]. Eccentricity in the Y -direction
is not considered.
When the injector valve is fully open, the vertical height of the valve opening, hinj, is 45 µm,
see Figure 3.2b. The opening is determined by the maximum elongation of the used piezo-
actuators opening and closing the valves. Three diﬀerent valve positions are simulated: fully
open (hinj = 45 µm), half open (hinj = 22.5 µm) and almost fully closed (hinj = 4.5 µm).
The maximum supply pressure of the test rig is 8 bar. Simulations are carried out for an
injection pressure, pinj, of 3, 5 and 7 bar.
As the objective of this project is to analyse the dynamics and stability of the active gas bearing
setup in the high speed range, the journal angular velocity, Ω, is varied from steady state,
Ω= 0 rpm, and all the way to Ω= 30 krpm. Subsequently eight diﬀerent rotational speeds are
simulated: 0 krpm, 2 krpm, 5 krpm, 10 krpm, 15 krpm, 20 krpm, 25 krpm and 30 krpm.

An explanation for this behaviour is found in the ﬂuid dynamics of the ﬁlm. The ﬂow in the
ﬁlm is assumed to be a combined Couette and Poiseuille ﬂow - the Couette ﬂow induced by
the rotor and the Poiseuille ﬂow induced by the injector.
A Couette ﬂow is a surface-driven ﬂow between two parallel plates. Because the ﬁlm thickness
is much smaller than the radius of the rotor and bearing, curvature eﬀects can be neglected
and the surfaces are assumed parallel.
To simplify the equation of motion, derived from
the Navier-Stokes equations, the Couette ﬂow is assumed to be steady, uni-directional in the
circumferential direction and with no pressure gradient.

CHAPTER 4
Discharge Coefficient
In this chapter the mathematical MRE model is compared with the simulations of the CFD
model. Assuming the CFD model to represent the true behaviour of the gas bearing, a coef-
ﬁcient is introduced to the MRE model in order to correct the discrepancies. This coeﬃcient
is the so-called discharge coeﬃcient, Cd. With the injection mass ﬂow rate as the chosen key
parameter, Cd is calculated with the aim of minimizing the diﬀerence of injection mass ﬂow
rate between the MRE- and CFD model.
Instead of doing CFD analysis for each case, the Cd coeﬃcient is found such that a Cd func-
tion may be implemented in the MRE model. This Cd-function should be able to interpolate
between the outer boundaries of the pressure, the angular velocity, the opening, and the eccen-
tricities. In this way a lot of time consuming and computationally expensive CFD work can
be avoided.

proﬁles, injector mass ﬂow rates and aerodynamic forces.

4.2
Discharge Coeﬃcient Function
The discharge coeﬃcient is necessary in order to compensate for the assumptions of the mod-
iﬁed Reynold’s Equation which do not ﬁt to the characteristics of the active gas bearing. In
order to correct the diﬀerences between the MRE and CFD model, a correction factor is cal-
culated and included in the MRE model for annulus injection, Equation (2.7). This correction
factor is the so called discharge coeﬃcient, Cd.
The expression for the injection ﬂow, Equation (2.5), applies to incompressible and laminar
ﬂows through a constant annular cross section. However, compressible ﬂow can not be ne-
glected and neither can the appearance of turbulence. The real geometry of the injector is also
more complex than the one of the model, see Figure 2.1. Reynold’s equation assumes inertia
eﬀects to be negligible, but CFD simulations have proved inertia eﬀects to have a signiﬁcant
impact. Furthermore, in the MRE formulation there is no such thing as a valve gap height,
hinj. In reality, when the input voltage to the piezoactuator increases, the valve gap height is
reduced, and so is the inlet mass ﬂow rate. To take this into account in the MRE model, the

In this chapter the Finite Element Model is introduced. The model is used for the analysis
of the rotor-bearing system dynamics. The corrected MRE model describes the interaction
between the rotor and the gas bearing by means of the stiﬀness and damping coeﬃcients. It
is shown how the dynamic coeﬃcients of the gas bearing vary with speed, injection pressure
and injector valve opening. Stability maps, Campbell diagrams and unbalance responses are
presented to characterise the system and assess the stability.

forward mode, the gyroscopic eﬀect essentially acts like an increasingly stiﬀspring, increasing
the natural frequency. For the backward mode on the other hand, the gyroscopic eﬀect reduces
the eﬀective stiﬀness and the natural frequency. This is however only a generalisation. As the
dynamic coeﬃcients of the gas bearing change with Ω, it is possible for the cross-coupled
coeﬃcients to actually make the forward mode decrease with speed and the backward to
increase. In fact, this phenomenon is observed for each case, Figures 5.7, 5.8 and 5.9.

Experimental modal analysis is used to validate the proposed mathematical model of the sys-
tem. Experimental modal analysis is the process of analysing the dynamic properties of a
structure in terms of its natural frequencies, damping ratios and mode shapes, based on exper-
imental data. The data is analysed using a steady state analysis in the frequency domain. The
system is excited and the response in terms of both input and output are measured. For this
experiment an impact hammer is used to excite the system between the disc and the gas bear-
ing, while proximity probes measure the displacement response of the rotor. The fundamental
idea is to excite the structure at a range of frequencies including the resonances, where the
magnitude of the response rapidly approaches a sharp maximum value, and the phase of the
response between the excitation force and displacement shifts by 180◦. However, as rotating
machinery is rarely proportionally damped, and the modes are generally complex, rather than
real, the phase angle is frequently not 0◦or 180◦. This complication is generally seen in lightly
damped structural modes [21]. This is also evident from Figures 6.10, 6.11, and 6.12.
6.2.1
Steady State Analysis
One way of characterising modal parameters is with a frequency response function (FRF). In
steady state analysis the FRF is obtained by exciting the structure with a stationary random
input, while the response is measured. The input and output signals are used to estimate the
FRF by comparing the two through a Fourier analysis. It is important to excite the structure
in all frequencies of interest for the results to be valid in the whole domain.
Sample Rate and Aliasing
When performing digital analysis on a stationary random signal it is important to know the
required time it takes to measure enough samples to calculate a useful statistical value. This
time is given by the auto-correlation function

where x(t) is the measured signal and τ is the time between each sample of x(t). If the sample

frequencies will appear as low frequencies. This phenomenon is called aliasing. Aliasing can
be avoided by using a sample rate twice as fast as the highest frequency of interest, and by
applying a low-pass ﬁlter that only allows frequencies lower than half the maximum frequency
of interest; the Nyquist-frequency. For this experiment a sampling rate of 2500 Hz is used, and
a Butterworth low-pass ﬁlter with a cut-oﬀfrequency of 1237 Hz is applied.
Averaging and Leakage
In signal processing of a random signal, simply performing Fourier transformation on the raw
data is insuﬃcient. Some kind of averaging of the signal over a speciﬁed amount of samples is
required to obtain statistically reliable results and minimize noise contamination. In this case
averaging is done on blocks of samples, using an overlap on the blocks so every data point is
used more than once, and in this way producing a more smoother data spectra. Choosing a
ﬁnite sample period gives rise to another problem, because for the signal to be ﬁnite, it requires
that it is cut oﬀat an integral multiple of its period. This is diﬃcult to do in practice for a

complicated signal with many diﬀerent frequencies. The problem is that a Fourier transform
of a ﬁnite period assumes that the signal is periodic in the sample length, hence if a signal is
cut oﬀmid-period, the frequency will ”leak” into erroneous frequencies that are not real [22].
To correct for this a window function called the Hanning function is used, where the signal is
multiplied with a window function that forces the signal to be 0 outside of the sampling period.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The purpose of this project has been to investigate a current hybrid gas bearing test rig, both
numerically and experimentally. The test rig was the same as investigated by Piearart in [5],
but with a smaller and lighter disc.
Speciﬁcally, the aim was to extend the mathematical model, describing the dynamic coeﬃ-
cients of the externally pressurised active gas bearing, such that it is valid in the high speed
range (≤30, 000 rpm). This means to extend the CFD model equivalently and based on this
obtain a new correction function. It was also within the scope of the project to employ the
new model and make a stability analysis of the system in the new range by using the Finite
Element Method. Theoretical results shall be validated by experiments on the actual test rig.
The numerical/theoretical study can be divided into three categories: computational ﬂuid
dynamics, correction of the modiﬁed Reynold’s equation, and ﬁnite element model analysis.
The most signiﬁcant ﬁndings will be summarised below.
Computation Fluid Dynamics Analysis
In Chapter 3, the starting point of the CFD analysis was an already existing model produced by
Pierart[6]. The model had problems with incorrect pressure and velocity distributions, as well
as high gradients of these. To resolve this, the geometry of the injector was improved and untrue
geometry was corrected, see Section 3.1.2. Furthermore, a comprehensive grid independence
analysis was carried out, showing that the mesh was in fact not grid independent. As shown
in Table 3.4, the number of cells in the mesh needed to be increased by 73% compared to the
original.
Moreover, in order to reduce ﬂuctuations in the solutions, and remove high gradients, a chamfer
was added to the sharp corner at the injector-ﬁlm zone. This decreased ﬂuctuations in mass
ﬂow rate from 4.5% to 0.01% and signiﬁcantly reduced residuals and gradients, which made the
CFD model far more stable and convergent. The mass ﬂow rate of the test case was reduced
by 20.2%, which is in better agreement with expectation.
Correction of the Modiﬁed Reynold’s Equation
In Chapter 4, the Modiﬁed Reynold’s Equation is corrected by discharge coeﬃcient function,
deﬁned by comparing the mass ﬂow rates of the modiﬁed Reynold’s equation and CFD. This
was introduced in order to reduce the numerical diﬀerence between the two. Two diﬀerent Cd
functions were proposed.
The functions, however, turned out to be infeasible to use due to negative Cd values. The
reason for negative Cd values was because of MRE being unable to handle values of Cd > 1
properly. Instead the Cd function introduced in [8] was implemented in MRE, because it al-
lowed for control of voltage, and in some degree, pressure. Through experiments performed on
the test rig, it was shown that MRE with Cd was able to give good estimations of equilibrium

positions for the steady state, and for the rotor turning.
Finite Element Model
After having validated MRE against experimental equilibrium positions, stiﬀness and damp-
ing coeﬃcients were extracted for use in the Finite Element Model introduced in Chapter 5.
The experimental natural frequencies for steady state were held up against the ones found in
the FEM model. Adjustments, due to manufacturing tolerances and uncertainty of material
properties, were made to decrease the discrepancy as much as possible, however, they could
not be reduced below 10%.
The analysis showed that when adding an unbalance mass to the disk, an injection pressure
of 3 bar was able to hold the highest unbalance without exceeding the nominal bearing ﬁlm
clearance. This is also supported when looking at the damping coeﬃcients in Figure 5.5, where
3 bar has the highest damping.
The critical speeds for all pressures were also found. 7 bar were found to have the highest
critical speed at 16,600 rpm. This is explained by the bearing having the highest stiﬀness for
7 bar, according to Figure 5.4.
Based on the above ﬁndings, it can be concluded that the aim of the thesis has only been
partially fulﬁlled. The CFD model was expanded to the high speed range and improved by
means of stability and convergence. This achievement was however obtained at a late stage
of the project. For this reason, the proposed Cd functions were based on poor CFD results of
the early stage CFD model, why the functions were not accurate. This is why the aim of the
thesis is considered only partially fulﬁlled.

A.2.1
Bandwidth Reduction
When the partial diﬀerential equations are discretised using the Finite Volume Method, sets
of linear algebraic equations with large sparse coeﬃcient matrices are obtained. The matrices
are then to be solved with iterative methods. Using bandwidth reduction, the large sparse
coeﬃcient matrices can be greatly reduced by renumbering the cell label list.
The bandwidth of a matrix is found as the bandwidth of each row of the matrix, where the
bandwidth of a row is the number of matrix entries between the ﬁrst and last nonzero entries
in the row. The problem is then to reduce the bandwidth, for a given symmetric sparse matrix,
M (nxn), by permuting the rows and columns such that the non-zero elements of matrix M
are moved as close as possible to the diagonal. An example of bandwidth reduction for an 8x8
matrix is shown in Figure A.2.

The structure of the adjacency graphs is identical, the only diﬀerence is the node labelling.
In this way it can also be seen as a graph labelling problem. A solution for solving this was
proposed by E. Cuthill and J. Mckee in 1969 [23].
The Cuthill-Mckee (CM) algorithm will be described below [24] for a given graph G(n), e.g.
the left graph in Figure A.3. Before starting, it should be stated that the degree of a node in
a graph is the number of nodes adjacent to it.
Step 0 Initiate an empty queue matrix, Q, and an empty results matrix, R.
Step 1 In graph G(n), select the node with the lowest degree that has not been inserted in
the result matrix previously, name it p (for parent).
Step 2 Add p in the ﬁrst free position in R.
Step 3 Add all nodes adjacent to node p in the queue matrix,Q, in the increasing order of
their degree.
Step 4.1 Extract the ﬁrst node from the queue in order to examine it, name it c (for child).
Step 4.2 If c has not already been added to the results matrix, add it in the ﬁrst free position.
Add the neighbouring nodes of c to the Q matrix, but only if they are not already in the
results matrix. As previously, they should be added in increasing order of their degree.
Step 5 Repeat from Step 4.1 until the queue matrix, Q is empty.
Step 6 Repeat from Step 1 until there are no more unexplored nodes
By applying the algorithm to the left matrix in Figure A.2 and on the left graph in Figure A.3
the corresponding right matrix and graph should be obtained.

A.2.2
Parallel Computing
This subsection will describe how OpenFOAM runs in parallel on distributed processors. In
OpenFOAM parallel processing is achieved using domain decomposition. Generally speaking
domain decomposition works by breaking the geometry and the associated ﬁelds into pieces
and allocating them for separate processors.
Domain Decomposition
One may think that implicit methods, such as SIMPLEC, are not well suited for parallel
computing, because of the dependency upon other values. However, domain decomposition
parallelisation is quite eﬃcient, both in time and space. In OpenFOAM, the decomposition
performed is geometric. The spatial decomposition works by splitting the solution domain into
sub domains. The goal is to maximise eﬃciency by creating sub domains where each proces-
sor has an equal amount of number-crunching. In order to solve the problem, the iteration
procedure needs to be modiﬁed. In domain decomposition, the global coeﬃcient matrix, A, is
split into a system of diagonal blocks, Aii, that contains the elements connecting the nodes
that belong to the ith subdomain, and oﬀ-diagonal blocks , Aij i ̸= j, representing interaction
between the sub domains i and j[13]. Figure A.4 shows an example of a 2D square solution
divided into four sub domains. In order to be most eﬃcient, the inner iterations should have
as little data dependency as possible, meaning a reduction in the inter-process communication
between the neighbour domains. For the example in Figure A.4 the processor for domain 1
needs data from the processors of sub domain 2 and 3 in order to solve the system diﬀerential
equations.

FEATURES
CHARACTERISTICS
These transducers are used for the contactless
measurement of the relative displacement of moving
machine elements. They are particularly suitable for
measuring the relative vibration and axial position of
rotating machine shafts, such as those found in
steam, gas and hydraulic turbines, as well as in
alternators, turbo-compressors and pumps.
The 
TQ 102 
non-contacting 
gap-to-voltage
transducer 
and 
its 
matching 
IQS 603 
signal
conditioner form a calibrated system in which each
component is interchangeable.
The active part of the transducer is a coil of wire that
is moulded inside the tip of the device, which is made
of Torlon (polyamide-imide). The target material must,
in all cases, be metallic.
The transducer body is made of stainless steel and is
available with metric or English thread. It has an
integral coaxial cable terminated with an AMP-type
connector. 
Due to the tolerances of the coaxial cable
specifications, "electrical trimming" of the integral
cable nominal length is necessary to optimize system
performance and transducer interchangeability. 
If required, the TQ 102 can be used with an extension
cable from the 913-101 series. Optional junction
boxes and housings are available to provide
mechanical protection to the AMP connectors on the
integral cable and extension cable.
