Summary
Soundscape analysis helps in measuring and understanding the sonic environment of
a given place. It helps characterize the various sounds available and helps achieve
a realistic representation of the acoustic environment. Even though it is useful the
process of doing a soundscape analysis is tedious and time consuming and thereby
also costly, the aim of this project is to develop a method that would be a lighter
version that would help obtain the key data with fair amount of accuracy within a
shorter period of time

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Sound is part and parcel of our daily lives. We use it to communicate, to assess
and interact with the environment, to sense danger and so on. Sound becomes a
problem when it turns out to be unnecessary. These sounds are called noise. The
environment of sound surrounding a given location is called a soundscape analogous
to land and landscape. While land can be seen, measured, quantified and analysed
with relative ease the same does not true for sound. Existing methods to understand
sound surroundings are of two kinds; namely, the classical methods and soundscape
method.
Both these methods approach the situation differently. The soundscape
method is a holistic approach while the classical methods are easy and straight forward
to implement. The purpose of this thesis is to try to find some middle ground between
the two that could serve as a quick and efficient way to fairly estimate the soundscape
scenario of a given place. It should have the practicality comparable to that of the
classic methods while having a broad point of view like the soundscape method. Some
of a few salient features of the existing methods are
1.1
Classical sound pressure level measurements
Classical sound pressure level measurements was based on as the name suggests based
on the sound pressure level. It assumes that the level of annoyance is directly pro-
portional to the sound pressure level. It is based only on the physical value without
considerations to the other conditions and aspects that may also contribute to a
person’s sensing of the surrounding.
1.2
Soundscape Analysis
The soundscape analysis is a detailed method which encompasses all the different
aspects involved in one’s perception of the surrounding and is very detailed. It has
sound measurements and calculation of loudness, sharpness, roughness and fluctua-
tion but also considers psychological aspects, subjective opinions, types of sources,
etc. which are obtained through surveys, listening tests, walks and personal inter-
views. While this is a very precise analysis the problem is that the time and the cost
involved in the carrying out of said method is pretty high.

been standardised and can be measured and quantified with utmost accuracy and

human sensation of the surroundings.

2.1.4
Drawbacks of Existing Noise Standards
The biggest drawback of exiting noise standards is that they are all mostly dependant
on the A-weighting of the dBA scale while the human hearing response is dynamic
changing according to the level of the sound [14]. Fletcher and Munson plotted the
equal loudness contours mapping the human loudness response across the audible
spectrum. While the Lden method takes time into consideration, none of these meth-
ods take into consideration the type of source or its characteristics into account. The
sense of loudness depends on the spectral quality of the sound as well. For example,
a pure tone sounds louder than a broadband noise of the same sound pressure level.
2.1.5
Loudness
Loudness modelling gives a better representation than frequency weightings as it takes
into account the human physiological response too. To model the loudness it is not
only sufficient to know the frequency response at different levels it is also important
to understand how the human ear works.
2.1.5.1
Phons
Sound Intensity is the closest physical parameter to loudness. Now considering the
level of 40dB SPL at 1kHz, the intensities of different frequencies to sound at the
same loudness would form a curve across the spectrum, this curve is the 40 Phons
curve, across which all frequencies (pure tones) sound at the same loudness. Similarly
the 50 Phons for a tone would mean the tone has the same loudness as a 1kHz tone
at 50dB though the intensity might be different.

Other parameters

2.2
Soundscape
A soundscape is defined by the ISO 12913 standard as
”Acoustic environment as perceived or experienced and/or understood by
a person or people, in context” [4]
As discussed earlier it is very complex to model perception based on a measure-
ments, so the soundscape method makes use of several methods in unison to achieve a
holistic representation of perception. It considers a number of key components namely
people, acoustic environment and context [7] and the subsequent contributions. Un-
like other methods it doesn’t only concern annoyance but also on pleasantness.
2.2.1
Data Collection Methods
Soundscape analysis is the integration of several different methods that together give
a complete picture. Data collection is therefore vital and done under several different
methods. The methods mentioned in the standard[7] are as follows
==PAGE:20==
10
2 Literature review
2.2.1.1
Soundwalks
Soundwalks are walks conducted by a group of people while understanding and as-
sessing the acoustical conditions at predefined locations in the area to be assessed.
It requires at least 20 participants a moderator. It is carried out by 4-5 people at a
time. A questionnaire is handed to the participants with predefined questions and
scales with which the analysis is carried out.
2.2.1.2
Questionnaires
Questionnaire method is aimed at the person who live in the certain area that is to
be assessed. Regular and long-term exposure to sounds under daily settings leads to
a different outcome than someone who is witnessing the soundscape for short periods
of time. Thus it is vital to get the information from the residents of the target area.
This is best achieved with the help of surveys. Standardised questionnaires that help
get a quick assessment of how one feels about the soundscape
2.2.1.3
Narrative Interviews
Narrative interview are a more in-depth version of the questionnaire method. Here
people who have lived in the area to be analysed are in a more personal way, with
question regarding living space, how the neighbourhood has changed over the years,
Moods, Daily routines, Comfortability and so on. This helps obtain a more personal
and highly detailed subjective response to the soundscape that is being assessed
2.2.1.4
Sound Source Taxonomy
Here the various kinds of sound sources are classified according to the standard
and put into groups based on the type of environment (eg. indoor/outdoor or ur-
ban/rural), type of source(eg. naturally ocurring/human activity), the characteristic
source(motorised sounds/human voice or so on or wind/rain or so on). This helps to
get a better overview of the sources and thereby the soundscape as well
2.2.1.5
Binaural recordings and measurements
Binaural recordings are a good way to recreate a sonic environment elsewhere. The
recording can then be used for listening tests and for measurements as they imitate
more or less how one would hear the sound. In these recording either a dummy head
and torso model is used or a person of height 1.6±1m wears the binaural microphone
headset.

CHAPTER 3
Case study
The case study was carried out in the inner city (indre by) of Copenhagen city in the
block encompassed by Gothersgade, Store Regnegade, Ny Østergade and Christian
IX’s Gade. The area is a mixed dwelling with residential dwellings on the first, second
and third floors and commercial property on the ground floor and basement. There
is also a large parking lot within the block. The are 6 bars and 8 shops in the block.
The block is exposed to different kinds of noises over a regular week. Traffic noises,
music from the bars and the park (Kongens Have) nearby, construction noise due to
road works happening in the area

3.1
Description
Over the course of the week the block is exposed to different kinds of sounds. During
the weekday there in mainly traffic noise, construction noise, roadwork noise, road
cleaners(machines that clean the pavement) and all other associated noises, during the
weekend there is also the sound of constant people chatter, music from Kongens Have
and late at night people screaming and playing loud music in the streets. Due to the
aforementioned complex nature of the sounds available in the given area, it is pretty
hard to measure the noise annoyance based on the simple sound level measurements
and the fact that it is relatively small in physical size though it has so many traits
makes it an ideal place for the scope of this project.
3.2
Behavioural Pattern
The analysis starts with familiarisation with the surroundings. Based on the analysis
of the pattern of the city block the day is categorised and divided into 5 sections.

one of the relatively quieter areas when there are no road works. While the corner of
Gothersgade and Store Regnegade are silent during the day, it is the region of most
activity at night.
3.3
Measurement
The measurement was done in the form of binaural recordings. Stereo microphones
were worn on the ear to get the recordings with the HRTF (Head Related Transfer
Function). This method was preferred to the use of a Head and Torso model as that
would draw attention and affect the measurements. The measurements were done on
the streets at different times on different days under different weather conditions in
order to obtain a vast array of diversities.
3.3.1
Measurement Setup
The measurement setup consisted of three parts
• Stereo microphone [Sennheiser MKE 2002]
• Hard disk recorder [Sound Devices 722]
• Microphone calibrator 94dB @ 1khz [Brüel & Kjær Type 4231]

3.3.2
Measurement Method
The microphone was worn while the hard disk recorder was kept concealed in a back-
pack in order not to draw too much attention. At the beginning and at the end of
the every recording the tone from the calibrator was played into the microphones in
order to help with reproduction to recorded data at original levels. The recording
was taken all around the block. Sounds that represent the every day common habits
are part of the soundscape of the area are recorded, extraordinary sounds that are
of rare/special occurrences are avoided (eg, New Year fireworks, Sound from Distor-
tion, Nørrebro). While collecting samples, the background noise is also tried to be
recorded as it helps perceive the environment during the listening tests. Traffic noise
was measured along Gothersgade and Christian XI’s Gade while most of the people
noise was measured along Gothersgade and Store Regnegade. The background noise
recordings were mostly taken at Ny Østergade. But recording were in general done all
around the block while slowly moving around taking pauses at areas of high activity.

CHAPTER 4
Light version
To conceptualise a light version of the soundscape method it is necessary to prioritise
the important parts. This is also where current standards and legislation fall short.
Secondly it is necessary to understand who would be using this method, here this
method is aimed at tackling city noise so legislators and law makers can understand
and monitor noise annoyance. Finally it must be simple, quick and fairly accurate. It
is important to realise that this is not a replacement or alternate to the full soundscape
method. It helps to get a rough picture quickly and can also act as a precursor for a
full blown soundscape method helping to identify areas of interests. Thus it’s relation
to the soundscape method can be seen analogous to the relation between Type 1 and
Type 2 sound level meters, where the latter is cheaper and easier to implement while
the former is more accurate. In order to focus and streamline the method there would
need to be a few assumptions.
4.1
Assumptions
• The negative aspects of sound on feeling is of main concern, namely annoyance.
• Binaural recordings/measurement provide a sufficiently clear picture of the en-
vironment
• The background of the person taking part in the listening test is not of major
concern provided they have normal hearing
• The effect of training (getting used) to a certain environment is overlooked and
things are assessed as is.
• The annoyance of the sounds on the road is comparable to the one in dwellings,
i.e. the reaction to different kinds of sources is the relative
• Only air-borne sounds are considered
4.2
Methodology
For the light version a few different methods from different points of views are explored
to see which would give desired results.
In order to develop a method it is first

4.2.1
Regression Analysis
Regression analysis is a method used for estimating between input parameters(independent
variables) and the output (dependent variable). Here in this method we use linear
regression which means assumes that all parameters contribute uniquely to the out-
put and there are no cross-dependencies. This is an important point to consider for
parameter selection. If an output variable y is to be estimated with n parameters x1,
x2, x3...xn that contribute uniquely to the output, then using regression analysis the
output y is equal to
y = β0 + β1x1 + β2x2 + β3x3 + ... + βnxn
(4.1)

where β0,β1,β2...βn are the unknown parameters that are calculated in order for the
values of y, the simulated output to be closest to the actual output.
Regression analysis also helps to find out input parameters that contribute very
little if not none and therefore can be omitted in order to make the model more
efficient . Based on the regression values obtained a model can be obtained, this
model once validated under different conditions can be used to predict the annoyance
of further samples
4.2.2
Listening Tests
Listening tests are conducted to estimate the human response to the environment,
here the response is annoyance. This method to collect the data is chosen over the
other methods available is because, it helps make sure that for a given sample everyone
hears exactly the same thing. In this case study, the subjects were young adults (age
18-28) and therefore all the models represent the annoyance of young adults to the
sound environment and not the actual annoyance of the demographic group of the
population living in the studied block.The same method can be applied for different
or mixed age groups.
The sounds are presented during the listening tests are all calibrated and repro-
duced at the actual levels. Background tracks recorded and a few sample sounds
are played first until the subject gets acclimatised to the sound environment. The
background track is also played between the samples to keep the reference the same.
It is very important to remember that in order to avoid the ”order effect” the order of
the samples be randomised. In this study the groups of samples by time (5-9 followed
by 10-15) are presented in the same chronological order but the samples within the
group(eg. Weekday 5-9) are randomised
The annoyance is obtained from the subject using the 5-point scale based on the
ISO 15666 standard [13]. Therefore the following is presented
• 1 - Not Annoying
• 2 - Slightly Annoying
• 3 - Moderately Annoying
• 4 - Very Annoying
• 5 - Extremely Annoying
From the results of this method the annoyance percentage is calculated and used for
the rest of the methods. The results of these tests can be seen in Appendix A

4.3
Psychophysical Method
The Psychophysical method is based on what can be measured and/or calculated
from the measurement. This includes SPL level, Peak values, Spectrum and values
that can be derived from the same like Loudness, Sharpness, Roughness. Here in
this study loudness, sharpness and spectral centroid(loudness spectrum) are used as
input parameters, these are very simple to calculate and can be measured real-time.
Loudness is measured in sones and the RMS(root mean square) value is used. The
calculation of loudness has already been discussed. The calculation of sharpness is
also based on Zwicker’s method [1] and is calculated in acums.
Based on the values that are calculated the regression model is run and the mod-
elling is done, this model can be used to predict annoyance for the same location the
study was done.
4.4
Word Elicitation
Word Elicitation is the process of using words to describe the various facets of sounds
being analysed. This is a form of direct elicitation because it makes use of words
relating to a certain perception. In order to use words to describe a sensation using
words it requires a large lexicon to be able to describe those distinct features that
stand out. In this method the lexicon from the paper ”The Semantic Space of Sounds”
by Torben Holm [10] is used. The method to obtain the words was adapted from the
suggestion of Bech S. and Zacharov N. [15] the process is split into five phases
• Phase 1 Listening and description: The various different kinds of samples are
listened to and then later described using the words from the lexicon
• Phase 2 Removal of duplicates: Words of similar meaning are removed
• Phase 3 Further reduction of words: Words with ambiguos and unclear mean-
ings are removed
• Phase 4 Scaling: Words selected are defined and scaled, i.e. definition of what
’0’, ’10’ and anything in between mean on the 0-10 scale
• Phase 5 Fine Tuning: The scaling is tested and verified to be consistent, while
showing some differences for almost similar signals
The words and definitions obtained now can be used for the perceptual method.
4.5
Perceptual method
The perceptual method is done by modelling the annoyance based on perceptual
tags that are unique to the types of sound available, characteristics that describe

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
6.1
Narrative Interview
A narrative interview was conducted with a resident of an adjacent block to the block
studied. The subject had been a resident for 25 years working in various time shifts
including night shifts and complained in loss of comfort and high annoyance and
poor performance at work. The biggest concern was large amounts of ’bass’ from
the bars. Something that is absent in all measurements. The subject also claimed
it was capable to hear average human talk as high as up in the 4th floor.
This
could probably mean that there is probably a lot of structure borne sound and very
poor sound isolation of walls (the subject also claimed to be able to hear neighbours
clearly). This would be something outside the scope of what was considered for this
project. The project could be extended to structure borne sound but that would no
longer be a light version. A full soundscape model is the only way to analyse these
conditions. The subject also agreed upon the assumptions made for indicators and
behavioural patterns.
6.2
Measurement
The measurement setup could be made a bit more simpler by minimising the the
experimental setup. There were many technical issues especially due to the presence
of two batteries in the experimental setup. The presence of such a large setup meant
that on site setup was a bit more tedious and attention drawing. Also the presence of
small switches like the input switch for (Mic/Line source) were easily tripped leading
to a lot of data loss. This was even more a bigger problem because the recorder could
not be kept outside and the measurement could not be monitored using headphones
since the microphones were sitting on the ear. There were also issues when the hard
disk recorder ran out of power as it was not discovered until after the measurement
because the bag cannot be moved during the measurement.
Simplification of the
experimental setup would make it a lot easier. Something as simple and discrete
as a handheld device capable of providing on-screen information would make the
measurement process less demanding and more efficient.
==PAGE:42==
32
6 Discussion
6.3
Listening tests
The listening tests were conducted on a sample size of 10 subjects, which is rather
small from a statistical point of view. In this case since the method was used to
predict the outcome and not represent actual conditions, the sample size significance
is slightly lower, but for any measurement for real world situations the sample size
should be fairly large, at least 30 and should also be representative of the living
population (age,gender,etc.), thereby the outcome of the listening test is more repre-
sentative of the studied location
6.4
Psychophysical Method
The fit obtained by the psychophysical model was found to be in better fit with the
data than dBA. This data is also instantly available like dBA. It consistency could
be considered and developed and standardised to get quick annoyance estimate. But
again this would not be able to map complex stimuli like weather noises. Thus while
it still an improvement over the usage of SPL levels in order to qualify for a light
version of the soundscape method this method falls short. On the other hand the the
use of indicators along with psychophysical parameters could help achieve a slightly
slower but more representative value. Since the Lden method uses penalties that act
like indicators it’s response is better than the fits without indicators, while there is
improvement over the dBA method.
6.5
Perceptual Method
Despite the the fact that well defined scaling methods were used there were found to be
some discrepancies in the values obtained from the test. This could have been avoided
if the listeners had been trained to have the same reference before the beginning of
the experiment. Though the average values did give a good fit, a better fit could have
been obtained. Another reason for these discrepancies could be the effect of fatigue
as these measurements required utmost concentration skill for the good part of two
hours. This could be made simple if the 0-10 scale was replaced by a 5 point scale.
The fits obtained purely by the perceptual method were found to be better than all
existing methods
6.6
Indicators
The use of the indicator proved rather effective helping to achieve a tighter fit with
very little of extra effort and in the aim to develop a light and quick version has
contributed really well. It could be something that could be made further use of
==PAGE:43==
6.7 Limitations
33
in further iteration of these methods. Indicators add a dimension of input to psy-
chophysical methods making it more viable to use and probably be considered as a
light version. The indicators used can be improved by using relatively scaled values
instead of arbitrary values.
6.7
Limitations
The models can never really completely model one’s annoyance in real life scenarios
because that depends on several personal factors like stress, fatigue, mood, preferences
etc. all of which can vary from person to person but what it can do is represent what
a general population would react as a whole.
The second limitation is because only listening tests are used structure borne
sound will not be considered.
Households are are not completely silent and the
sounds produced by devices and people provide some amount of masking in real
living conditions this also is not considered.
6.8
Further development
A standardised window and room simulation can be used to simulate the indoor listen-
ing experience and thereby be get more accurate annoyance data. The psychophysical
model could be developed into a mobile application to work along with some mobile
audio interface and a microphone or just the phone microphone to measure and cate-
gorise the sounds, while compensating for the phone. This would also allow the input
of the indicators and thereby getting quick annoyance levels which would be more
accurate than the SPL levels. This could be used by the night sound guards who
patrol the city.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
In the search for a practical soundscape method approach for city noise several meth-
ods were considered and inputs from different methods were combined and used to
model and predict noise annoyance in cities. The two methods using psychophysical
and perceptual method with indicators could be used under different circumstances
to quickly get an idea of the soundscape. While the perceptual method can be used
to properly analyse and predict values to a considerable accuracy, the psychophysical
method under some development can be used to quickly get a rough assessment on
field conditions and could also serve as a precursor to the perceptual method which
in turn could act as a precursor to a full soundscape method.
The psychophysical method without the indicators was not as good as the Lden
method but with the presence of the indicators proved to be an improvement. This
method could be used for real-time monitoring situations but its significance in de-
tailed studies is limited
The perceptual method without the indicators is significant on it’s own but it
improves with the use of indicators. The perceptual method with calculated loudness
and indicators makes use of input of different kinds of methods and combines them
to give an output, this thus would therefore be worthy of qualifying as a the light
version of the soundscape method both with respect to accuracy and principle that
would be suitable for city noise.
