Abstract 
Ensuring food safety in innovative fermentation processes is essential for public health 
and product quality. This study evaluated microbiological, chemical and physical hazards 
in fermentation process applied at Myco4Food (M4F) by applying Hazard Analysis and 
Critical Control Points (HACCP) principles and conducting microbiological analyses on 
brewers’ spent grain (BSG) before and during solid-state fermentation. Through qualita-
tive risk assessment, the most significant hazards were identified based on their severity 
and likelihood of occurrence. Key pathogens of concern included Bacillus cereus, Lis-
teria monocytogenes, Clostridium botulinum, Shiga toxin-producing Escherichia coli 
(STEC), and hepatitis A virus. 
 
An exposure assessment was performed using recent data on contaminant concentra-
tions in barley grains and the consumption of hybrid sausages containing 10% fermented 
BSG, as M4F’s product is intended for further processing before consumption. Among 
chemical hazards, aflatoxins posed the greatest risk of contamination in hybrid sau-
sages. Regarding physical hazards, foreign bodies such as metal fragments and stones 
were identified as primary concerns to be addressed in the HACCP plan. 
 
To complement the qualitative risk assessment, microbiological analyses on BSG before 
and during fermentation were performed to assess the real prevalence of selected mi-
croorganisms in the product. Among them, B. cereus emerged as a primary concern due 
to its natural presence in all the nine samples analyzed and its potential to reach harmful 
levels during fermentation. On unfermented BSG, Bacillus cereus sensu lato reached 
levels from 1.7 to 3.7 log CFU/g. However, it was observed an increased in Bacillus 
microbial load in the beginning of the fermentation, reaching a maximum value of 4.9±0.2 
log CFU/g after 20 hours of fermentation. 
 
Microbial dynamics during fermentation were also investigated, with a focus on interac-
tions between Rhizopus oryzae and B. cereus. In the solid-state fermentation process at 
M4F, a Rhizopus oryzae spore solution at the concentration of 6 log CFU/g is inoculated 
into BSG, where it proliferates and forms a white mycelial network. This network is re-
sponsible for enhancing substrate structure while improving BSG digestibility. 
 
Results revealed that while B. cereus initially proliferated, its population declined as fer-
mentation progressed, reaching a minimum value of 2.3±0.3 log CFU/g by the end of the 
third day – potentially due to substrate competition or organic acid accumulation. How-
ever, by the fourth day of fermentation, a resurgence of Bacillus was observed, with final 
concentrations reaching 4.0±1.5 log CFU/g. Moreover, coliform growth was detected in 
the last two days of fermentation. These findings suggest that, under current raw material 
conditions, fermentation should be limited to three days to ensure product safety and 
quality.  
 
To mitigate microbiological, chemical, and physical hazards, various control strategies 
were proposed. A high-temperature heat treatment at 120 °C for 15 minutes was identi-
fied as a Critical Control Point (CCP) for reducing spore-forming bacteria while prevent-
ing toxin production. Additionally, key Operational Prerequisite Programs (OPRPs), such

as chilled storage at 5 °C, raw material inspection, and chemical monitoring, were rec-
ommended to minimize contamination risks. Alongside the HACCP plan, the implemen-
tation of Prerequisite Programs (PRPs) – including hygienic design of the laboratory and 
bioreactor, Good Hygiene Practices (GHP), and the selection of appropriate Food Con-
tact Materials (FCM) – was advised as the foundation of the food safety risk management 
program.  
 
While this study provides a solid foundation for microbiological risk management in the 
M4F process, further research is needed to refine control strategies, validate microbial 
reduction methods, and explore cost-efficient alternatives. By integrating scientific risk 
assessments with robust food safety management, this research contributes to the de-
velopment of safe and sustainable fermentation practices, supporting the valorization of 
BSG in food applications.

1. Introduction 
Approximately one-third of the food produced globally is lost during processing or wasted 
(Tan et al., 2019). As the global population continues to grow, food production and the 
associated generation of waste are expected to rise, leading to substantial amounts of 
food being discarded annually without proper consumption or utilization. This scenario 
poses significant environmental, economic, and climate challenges, underscoring the in-
creasing importance of research and projects focused on waste valorization. 
 
Amidst the food industry, the brewing sector generates significant amounts of waste 
throughout the year, with brewers' spent grain (BSG) accounting for up to 85% of the 
total by-products. The remaining waste includes spent hops and surplus yeast (Tan et 
al., 2019). As a result, large quantities of BSG are produced annually. In 2023, beer sales 
in Denmark reached approximately 16.5 million liters (Ridder, 2024), resulting in the pro-
duction of roughly 3,300 tons of BSG. 
 
However, despite its potential as a valuable biomass resource, the utilization and broader 
application of BSG remains limited due to various challenges. BSG's high moisture con-
tent makes it susceptible to rapid microbial growth and spoilage, significantly reducing 
its shelf life (Castro & Colpini, 2021; Mitri et al., 2022). Additionally, logistics and trans-
portation present challenges, as BSG is produced in large quantities and often far from 
potential users, increasing costs and complexity (Castro & Colpini, 2021). 
 
Furthermore, BSG composition varies depending on the grain type, brewing process, 
and individual brewery practices, resulting in a lack of standardization and quality control 
for its use as a raw material. Regulatory and safety concerns, including microbial and 
chemical contamination, further hinder its broader application. 
 
In this scenario, Myco4Food (M4F) – a biotech company with expertise in solid-state 
fermentation (SSF) and plant-based substrates – is developing a fermentation process 
for the efficient utilization of BSG. By applying fungal fermentation, the company aims to 
enhance BSG's digestibility and nutritional profile, repurposing it as a protein-rich food 
ingredient. 
 
However, food safety remains a concern, as the BSG used as a substrate in M4F’s pro-
duction line is still subject to various food safety challenges. One key issue is the natural 
presence of Bacillus cereus, a foodborne pathogen that can pose a potential health risk 
if not properly controlled in the final fermented product. 
 
Additionally, other chemical, physical, and biological hazards may arise in the absence 
of adequate food safety practices. Therefore, a comprehensive Hazard Analysis and 
Critical Control Points (HACCP), aligned with scientific evidence and EU regulatory 
standards, is essential to mitigate risks and ensure a safe product for further utilization 
and consumption.

2. Objectives 
This project aimed to conduct a qualitative risk assessment of key microbiological, chem-
ical, and physical hazards associated with brewers’ spent grain. Hazard identification 
and characterization were based on a comprehensive literature review and on-site ob-
servations at Myco4Food’s facility. 
 
To complement the qualitative assessment, microbiological analyses were performed on 
both fresh and fermented BSG to detect the natural presence of potential pathogens, 
with particular focus on Bacillus cereus, which had been previously identified as a con-
cern by the company. Additionally, microbial dynamics during fermentation were evalu-
ated to assess changes in population levels and potential safety risks. 
 
Building on both qualitative and quantitative findings, a HACCP plan was developed for 
the Myco4Food process line. This plan identifies and discusses Critical Control Points 
(CCPs) and Operational Prerequisite Programs (OPRPs) designed to minimize contam-
ination risks and ensure the safety and quality of the final fermented product. 
 
3. Scientific background 
3.1 The brewing process 
Beer is a fermented beverage primarily made from water, malt, hops, and yeast. It is one 
of the oldest and most widely consumed alcoholic drinks globally (Mitri et al., 2022). The 
brewing process begins with the treatment of barley grains, converting them into malt, 
which is then processed into beer. The key stages of the brewing include malting, milling, 
mashing, lautering, boiling, cooling, fermentation, and maturation (Kunze, 1996; Mosher 
& Trantham, 2021). 
 
The malting process (Figure 1) transforms barley grains into malt through three stages. 
The first stage, known as steeping, involves soaking the grains in water at 5-18 °C for 
approximately two days to increase the moisture content up to 42-48% (Mussatto et al., 
2006). In the subsequent germination stage, the grains are transferred to a germination 
vessel, where it is exposed to a humid air stream that maintains the temperature between 
15 and 21 °C. 
 
Germination typically lasts 6 to 7 days and is meant to activates enzymes that facilitate 
the release of sugars from the malt (Mosher & Trantham, 2021). Enzymes such as glu-
canases, proteases, and amylases are produced, breaking down cell walls, proteins, and 
the starch in the grain’s endosperm. The final stage of malting is kilning, where the grains 
are dried at 40-60 °C to reduce the moisture content to 4-5%, preventing microbial con-
tamination and enhancing flavor development (Kunze, 1996; Mussatto et al., 2006). 
 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
 
13 
 
 
Figure 1. Schematic representation of the malting and brewing processes up to the separation of BSG 
from the wort, adapted from Mussatto et al. (2006) 
 
 
In the brewing process (Figure 1), the malt is first milled to break open the husk and 
expose the starch in the grain, facilitating better sugar release. Afterwards, during mash-
ing, water is added to the milled malt, called grist, and the mixture is gradually heated to 
activate enzymes that release fermentable substrates. Proteases, alpha-amylases, and 
beta-amylases become active at different temperatures during this process. The mash-
ing step typically lasts 60 minutes, and the temperature range is usually kept between 
60 and 68 °C  (Mosher & Trantham, 2021). Additionally, a pH range around 5 to 5.5 is 
naturally established during this step (Mosher & Trantham, 2021). 
 
Once the enzymatic reactions are complete, a mash-out is performed by raising the 
mash temperature to halt enzyme activity and preserve the sugar profile (Kunze, 1996; 
==PAGE:14==
 
14 
 
 
Mosher & Trantham, 2021). This higher temperature – usually around 80 °C – also sig-
nificantly reduces the viscosity of the mash (Mosher & Trantham, 2021). The subsequent 
step is lautering, which involves rinsing the mash (i.e., wort and grist) with hot sparge 
water to increase the sugar content in the liquid fraction. 
 
Lautering can be performed using different methods. Typically, the entire mash is 
pumped into a vessel called a lauter tun, where the grains are rinsed, and the wort is 
slowly drained. In other cases, when the grist is finely ground like a powder, the mash is 
pumped into a mash filter (Mosher & Trantham, 2021). In this case, the wort is first 
squeezed from the grist, followed by the addition of sparge water. In smaller breweries, 
both mashing and lautering can also be carried out in a combined vessel. 
 
After filtration, the residual grist, now called brewers’ spent grain (BSG), is collected as 
a by-product, while the wort proceeds to the subsequent stages of the brewing process. 
The subsequent steps for beer production involve boiling, cooling, fermentation, and 
maturation. 
 
During the boiling step, hops are added to the wort, and both enzymes and microorgan-
isms are inactivated. After boiling and cooling, yeast is introduced for fermentation. De-
pending on the yeast strain, fermentation can last from a few days to several weeks. 
Once fermentation is complete, the beer undergoes maturation to develop its final flavor. 
 
3.2 Brewers’ spent grains 
Typically, for every kilogram of malt used during brewing, approximately 1.8 kilograms 
of BSG is generated. Its composition consists of approximately 70% lignocellulosic fi-
brous material, 20% proteins, and 10% lipids, along with vitamins, minerals, amino acids, 
and phenolic compounds (Mitri et al., 2022). However, despite its potential as a biomass 
resource, BSG remains underutilized. Currently, around 70% is used as animal feed, 
10% in biogas production, and the remaining 20% is discarded in landfills (Mitri et al., 
2022). 
 
Given its abundance and nutritional content, and to maximize the utilization of BSG, there 
is a strong interest in finding alternative uses for BSG, such as in nutritional applications 
or for the extraction of valuable macromolecules (Castro & Colpini, 2021). In this context, 
the solid-state fermentation of this biomass emerges as a promising approach to en-
hance the product value of BSG. 
 
3.3 Fermentation 
The production of fermented foods is one of the oldest food processing technologies, 
with records of fermenting milk, meat, and vegetables dating back to 6,000 BC (Caplice 
& Fitzgerald, 1999). Certainly, fermentation methods were entirely artisanal in ancient 
times, with no understanding of the crucial role microorganisms played in the process. 
 
It was due to the rise of microbiology as a science in the 1850s that the biological basis 
of fermentation began to be understood. This led to the discovery of the essential roles 
of bacteria, yeasts, and molds in producing fermented foods, allowing for more controlled 
and efficient fermentation processes (Caplice & Fitzgerald, 1999). 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
 
15 
 
Fermentation offers multiple benefits for food products. It can extend shelf life and pre-
serve foods by producing organic acids, alcohol, and/or antimicrobial compounds, or 
through competitive exclusion of harmful microorganisms. Additionally, fermentation en-
hances both texture and flavor profiles. The process can also remove allergens and toxic 
substances from foods, as seen in cassava fermentation. Furthermore, fermentation im-
proves the digestibility and nutritional value of foods by converting nutrients into more 
bioavailable forms through microbial activity (Caplice & Fitzgerald, 1999). 
 
There are various methods of fermentation, each involving different microorganisms and 
processes. For instance, lactic acid bacteria (LAB) are used in acid fermentation to pro-
duce yogurt and cheese, while yeasts are employed in alcoholic fermentation to create 
beer and wine. Bacillus subtilis is involved in alkaline fermentation, such as in the pro-
duction of natto. Additionally, acetic acid fermentation, carried out by Acetobacter spp. 
or Gluconobacter spp., is used to produce vinegar. Fermentation can also be categorized 
by the systems used, including submerged (SmF) and solid state-fermentation (SSF), 
each with its specific applications and benefits. 
 
3.3.1 Solid-state fermentation 
Solid-state fermentation is the fermentation system in which microorganisms grow on 
solid materials in the absence or near-absence of free flowing aqueous phase. SSF is 
an alternative to liquid or submerged fermentation for producing various value-added 
products, including antibiotics, enzymes, single-cell proteins, organic acids, aroma com-
pounds, biopesticides, and biofuels (Bhargav et al., 2008). Compared to SmF, the use 
of SSF offers several advantages in terms of cost and sustainability, such as simpler 
implementation, minimal water requirements, reduced wastewater treatment, and lower 
energy consumption (Myco4Food, 2024; Tan et al., 2019). 
 
Several factors influence the SSF process, including the choice of microorganisms, sub-
strate, and process conditions. Bacteria, yeast, and filamentous fungi can all be utilized 
in SSF, but it is particularly well-suited for filamentous fungi due to the minimal presence 
of free-flowing liquid. Unlike other microorganisms that typically thrive in liquid environ-
ments, filamentous fungi naturally grow on solid substrates like wood, seeds, and roots 
(Bhargav et al., 2008). 
 
In terms of substrate choice, SSF is regarded as a promising alternative for the biological 
pretreatment and utilization of solid residues, particularly food and agro-industrial by-
products. Sugar cane bagasse, apple pomace, wheat bran, and brewers’ spent grain are 
some of the substrates that can be used in SSF. 
 
Various process parameters also play a critical role in the efficiency of the process. Fac-
tors such as moisture content, pH, water activity, substrate pretreatment, incubation tem-
perature, agitation, aeration, and nutrient supplementation must be optimized to ensure 
the optimal growth of the microorganisms and maximize the desired product yield.  
 
3.3.2 Rhizopus spp. 
Rhizopus is one of the most promising fungal genera for solid-state fermentation (SSF). 
Several species within this genus are utilized in various industrial applications, including 
==PAGE:16==
 
16 
 
 
the production of enzymes such as glucoamylases, cellulases, and tannases, as well as 
organic acids like lactic acid and fumaric acid (Ibarruri et al., 2019). These enzymes and 
acids are crucial in a range of biotechnological processes. Additionally, Rhizopus spe-
cies are commonly used in animal feed preparation, contributing to the enhancement of 
nutritional value. 
 
Some key advantages of using Rhizopus sp. in SSF include their simple nutritional re-
quirements, the ability to grow in a wide temperature range (25-45°C), their tolerance to 
pH levels between 4.5 and 7.5, and the adaptability to diverse fermentative substrates 
(Ibarruri et al., 2019).  
 
Two of the most well-known species are Rhizopus oligosporus and Rhizopus oryzae, 
which are widely used in the production of fermented foods like tempeh. In this process, 
soybeans are fermented with Rhizopus sp., leading to the formation of a mycelial network 
that binds the soybeans together, creating a nutritious and easily digestible product. The 
fermentation not only improves the flavor and texture of the soybeans but also enhances 
their nutritional profile (Martín-Miguélez et al., 2025). 
 
R. oligosporus and R. oryzae are molds that are generally regarded as safe in food pro-
duction, as they do not produce mycotoxins or any other hazard related to food safety 
(Martín-Miguélez et al., 2025). Both species are widely used in industrial applications for 
the processing of various substrates due to their capacity to metabolize a broad range 
of carbon sources and produce valuable enzymes. 
 
Furthermore, fermentation with R. oryzae and oligosporus has been shown to enhance 
the anti-inflammatory properties of substrates. This improvement occurs through the in-
creased bioaccessibility, bioavailability, and bioactivity of antioxidants, making these 
fungi not only beneficial in terms of food processing but also for health-promoting prop-
erties (Martín-Miguélez et al., 2025).  
 
3.3.3 Myco4Food 
Myco4Food (M4F) is a biotech spinout from Aalborg University (AAU), founded in 2022 
by Peter Lübeck, Mette Lübeck, and Jesper Schierbeck-Hansen. The company has as-
sembled an experienced team with expertise in utilizing fungi for the solid-state fermen-
tation (SSF) of plant-based substrates. By developing unique bioreactor systems for SSF 
using edible microfungi, M4F aims to upcycle a wide range of organic residues from the 
food and agricultural industries to produce protein-rich food ingredients and products in 
a cost-effective and sustainable manner (Myco4Food, 2024). 
 
M4F has successfully developed an SSF process for the fungal fermentation of brewers' 
spent grain using Rhizopus oryzae. This process has been established at laboratory 
scale and in bioreactors ranging from 200 to 300 liters, with plans to scale up to 3,000-
liter systems. However, in addition to further technical advancements in the bioreactors, 
attention must be given to food safety management due to the potential presence of 
microbiological, chemical, and physical hazards.

3.4 Food safety 
Over the past two decades, there has been a significant increase in efforts to protect 
consumers from foodborne illnesses and food poisoning (Notermans & Batt, 1998). This 
has resulted in a heightened focus on ensuring food safety throughout the production 
process. Key developments include the implementation, continuous improvement, and 
rigorous inspection of Good Manufacturing Practices (GMP), Good Hygiene Practices 
(GHP), and food safety management systems based on the principles of Hazard Analysis 
and Critical Control Points (HACCP). These frameworks are essential for identifying po-
tential hazards, monitoring critical control points, and ensuring that food is produced in a 
safe and hygienic manner.  
 
3.4.1 Risk analysis and the European Union agents 
According to the Codex Alimentarius (CAC/GL 30-1999), risk analysis is a system con-
sisting of three interconnected components: risk assessment, risk management, and risk 
communication. Risk assessment is a science-based approach that focuses on identify-
ing and characterizing hazards, as well as determining the level of risk to human health 
through exposure assessments and risk characterization. It provides an impartial source 
of scientific advice to risk managers and supports communication about risks associated 
with the food chain (EFSA, 2024a; FAO and WHO, 2021). At the European level, the 
European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) is responsible for conducting risk assessments, 
while the DTU National Food Institute fulfills this role at national level in Denmark. 
 
Risk management involves evaluating policy options in consultation with relevant stake-
holders. It considers the findings of the risk assessment, along with other legitimate fac-
tors, and, when necessary, implements appropriate prevention and control measures 
(FAO and WHO, 2021). The European Commission oversees risk management at the 
European level, while the Ministry of Food, Agriculture, and Fisheries of Denmark man-
ages it at national level. 
 
Risk communication encompasses the interactive exchange of information and opinions 
throughout the risk analysis process. It involves risk assessors, risk managers, consum-
ers, food and feed businesses, the academic community, and other stakeholders (FAO 
and WHO, 2021). This communication includes explaining risk assessment findings and 
the basis for risk management decisions. One of the tools used in the European Union 
is the Rapid Alert System for Food and Feed (RASFF), which facilitates the exchange of 
information between member countries, enabling swift action by food safety authorities 
when public health risks arise from the food chain (European Commission, 2024). 
 
3.4.2 Food safety hazards and management systems 
A food safety hazard is any biological, chemical, or physical agent in food that has the 
potential to render it unsafe for human consumption, potentially resulting in adverse 
health effects. To prevent these hazards from affecting consumers, it is essential to im-
plement a food safety management system across the entire food production chain. 
 
As outlined in ISO 22000 specifications, the key components of an effective food safety 
management system include interactive communication, system management, prereq-
uisite programs, and the application of HACCP principles (SafetyCulture, 2024). These 
==PAGE:18==
 
18 
 
 
elements work together to ensure that food safety risks are effectively identified, as-
sessed, and controlled throughout the production process. 
 
3.4.3 Prerequisite programs 
Prerequisite programs (PRPs) form the essential foundation for safe food production and 
are a prerequisite for the successful implementation of HACCP. These programs ad-
dress the fundamental conditions and activities that an organization must maintain to 
ensure food safety. Key PRPs include supplier and raw material control, hygienic design 
– which involves the appropriate selection of equipment, facility’s design, food contact 
materials, and cleaning methods – good hygiene practices, and good manufacturing 
practices. 
 
By establishing these basic conditions, PRPs help create a safe and sanitary environ-
ment for food production, laying the groundwork for more specific hazard control through 
the development of an HACCP system. 
 
3.4.4 HACCP system 
The HACCP plan is a systematic approach used to assess hazards and establish control 
measures that prioritize prevention over end-product testing. It is structured around 
seven core principles designed to ensure food products remain safe for human consump-
tion. 
 
1. 
Conduct a hazard analysis by identifying and evaluating potential hazards that could 
compromise food safety 
2. 
Determine Critical Control Points (CCPs) and Operational Prerequisite Programs 
(OPRPs) by identifying key steps in the process where hazards must be controlled 
3. 
Establish critical limits and action criteria by defining measurable parameters to en-
sure control measures remain effective 
4. 
Establish a monitoring system by implementing procedures to track control 
measures at CCPs and OPRPs 
5. 
Establish corrective actions by defining necessary steps if a CCP or OPRP deviates 
from its critical limits 
6. 
Establish verification procedures to confirm that the HACCP system is functioning 
effectively 
7. 
Establish documentation and maintain detailed records of all procedures to ensure 
compliance and traceability 
 
The hazard analysis (Principle 1) aims to identify and assess potential hazards, deter-
mining whether they need to be eliminated or reduced to an acceptable level. This pro-
cess also involves defining appropriate control measures to prevent or mitigate risks. 
Additionally, hazard characterization evaluates both the likelihood of occurrence and the 
severity of potential health effects associated with each hazard. 
 
Following the hazard analysis, Principle 2 identifies CCPs and OPRPs, which are critical 
stages where control measures must be applied. CCPs are specific steps in the produc-
tion process where hazards must be eliminated or reduced to safe levels. At these points, 
critical limits are defined (Principle 3), and process parameters are monitored to ensure 
==PAGE:19==
 
 
 
19 
 
food safety. OPRPs are preventive control measures that reduce the likelihood of haz-
ards in the final product. These measures inhibit microbial growth or prevent contamina-
tion rather than eliminating hazards entirely. To ensure their effectiveness, action criteria 
are set (Principle 3), and monitoring procedures are implemented. 
 
Monitoring CCPs and OPRPs (Principle 4) involves systematic measurements or obser-
vations to verify that control parameters remain within safe limits. If a deviation occurs, 
Principle 5 requires the implementation of corrective actions to restore control and pre-
vent unsafe products from reaching consumers. 
 
Finally, Principles 6 and 7 emphasize verification procedures to ensure the HACCP sys-
tem is functioning effectively and comprehensive documentation to maintain transpar-
ency, compliance, and traceability throughout the process. 
 
3.5 Microbiological hazards 
Microbiological hazards refer to harmful microorganisms, including bacteria, parasites, 
fungi, and viruses, that can contaminate food. They may develop due to improper han-
dling or be introduced from external sources such as air, soil, and water. These microor-
ganisms are classified into three groups based on their interaction with the human body 
and their role in foodborne illnesses (FAO and WHO, 2021). 
 
The first category – i.e., toxico-infectious hazards – includes bacteria that are ingested 
through contaminated food and subsequently produce toxins in the host’s intestinal tract. 
In these cases, both the bacteria and their toxins contribute to illness. 
 
The second category is called toxicogenic hazards. This group consists of microorgan-
isms that produce toxins directly in food before consumption. Foodborne illness occurs 
when these pre-formed toxins are ingested, rather than from the presence of the live 
microorganisms themselves. 
 
The third group are infectious hazards. This category encompasses a broad range of 
microorganisms, including bacteria, viruses, parasites, and fungi, which invade and mul-
tiply within the human body. Once established in the host, they cause disease. 
 
Understanding these pathogens is essential for implementing effective control measures 
to reduce the risk of foodborne illnesses and ensure food safety. 
 
3.5.1 Toxico-infectious biological hazards 
3.5.1.1 Bacillus cereus 
Bacillus cereus is a rod-shaped, Gram-positive, aerobic-to-facultative bacterium widely 
distributed environmentally. It is found in reservoirs such as decaying organic matter, 
fresh and marine waters, vegetables and fomites, and the intestinal tract of invertebrates, 
from which soil and food products may become contaminated (Bottone, 2010). Like other 
species from the genus Bacillus, it has the ability to produce endospores, allowing it to 
survive in harsh environments. 
 
==PAGE:20==
 
20 
 
 
Bacillus cereus forms spores when environmental conditions become unfavorable for 
growth. For instance, the exposure to sublethal heat (i.e., temperatures that are not high 
enough to kill the cells) can induce stress responses in B. cereus, leading to sporulation. 
These spores develop within the mother cell compartment during the sporulation process 
and are released into the environment when the mother cell undergoes lysis. In their 
dormant state, these spores are highly resistant to various environmental stress factors, 
including heat, desiccation, radiation, and chemical disinfectants, allowing them to sur-
vive in harsh conditions for extended periods (Ehling-Schulz et al., 2019a; Setlow, 2014). 
 
Spore resistance and dormancy in Bacillus cereus are attributed to its unique structural 
components. As depicted in Figure 2, spores possess several specialized layers not 
found in vegetative cells. The outermost layer, the exosporium, provides protection, while 
the coat layer plays a crucial role in resistance to chemicals and predation. Between the 
exosporium and the coat lies the interspace. Beneath the coat is the outer membrane, 
followed by a peptidoglycan cortex, which is essential for maintaining dormancy and 
structural integrity. The germ cell wall, which is similar to the cell wall of a growing bac-
terium, lies beneath the cortex (Setlow, 2014). 
 
Next is the inner spore membrane, which contains most of the key proteins involved in 
spore germination. At the core of the spore is the central core, where essential compo-
nents such as DNA, ribosomes, and enzymes required for spore formation are located. 
The core's low water content (between 25% to 50% of its wet weight) is believed to be a 
key factor in the spore's minimal metabolic activity, contributing to its dormancy and re-
sistance to environmental stress (Setlow, 2014). 
 
 
 
Figure 2. Representation of the B. cereus spore structure, adapted from Setlow (2014). 
 
The germination of Bacillus cereus occurs when its dormant spores encounter favorable 
environmental conditions, such as suitable temperatures, moisture, and nutrients, allow-
ing the bacterium to grow, multiply, and potentially produce toxins. Before germination 
can begin, spores must be activated by exposure to specific environmental stimuli, such 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
 
21 
 
as heat, chemical, or nutrient exposure (Ehling-Schulz et al., 2019a). Following germi-
nation, the outgrowth converts the germinated spores into growing cells (Setlow, 2014). 
 
The Bacillus cereus group can grow within a temperature range of 4 to 50°C, with optimal 
growth occurring between 28 and 35°C. Its generation time is relatively short, ranging 
from 18 to 27 minutes. The bacterium also grows across a wide pH range, from 4.9 to 
9.3, in water activity levels above 0.912, and can tolerate salt concentrations of up to 
7.5% (Ehling-Schulz et al., 2019a; FDA, 2012; Notermans & Batt, 1998). The spores are 
rather heat resistant, although the D-values tend to be variable. Typically, D100 values 
range from 2.7 to 3.1 minutes. The germination process is rapid and can occur in some 
strains within 30 minutes (Notermans & Batt, 1998). 
 
In food safety, B. cereus can be classified as both toxico-infectious and toxicogenic path-
ogen, leading to two different types of illness: diarrheal syndrome and emetic syndrome, 
respectively. The pathogenicity of Bacillus cereus, both intestinal and non-intestinal, is 
closely linked to the production of tissue-destructive exoenzymes (Bottone, 2010). Addi-
tionally, B. cereus is a significant food spoilage organism, contributing to food degrada-
tion and loss (Notermans & Batt, 1998). 
 
The first type of illness caused by this pathogen is known as diarrheal syndrome. In the 
small intestine, vegetative cells of B. cereus, ingested as either viable cells or spores, 
produce and secrete a protein enterotoxin, which triggers the associated symptoms (Bot-
tone, 2010). This syndrome is characterized by abdominal pain, diarrhea, and rectal te-
nesmus, typically occurring 8 to 16 hours after consuming food contaminated with B. 
cereus (Notermans & Batt, 1998). 
 
While research over the years has shown that B. cereus produces one or more toxins 
that cause a diarrheal response, the exact nature and mechanism of these toxins have 
yet to be fully elucidated. It is known, however, that the enterotoxin disrupts the mem-
branes of epithelial cells in the gastrointestinal tract, leading to symptoms that can last 
between 12 and 36 hours. Enterotoxins are typically found in foods such as meat, fish, 
poultry, dairy products, and vegetables (Notermans & Batt, 1998). The second type of 
illness caused by Bacillus cereus is associated with the production of an emetic toxin 
known as cereulide, which will be further detailed in Section 3.6.2. 
 
Both types of B. cereus diseases are generally associated with mild to moderate symp-
toms, but the severity can increase under certain conditions, particularly in vulnerable 
populations such as young children, the elderly, pregnant women, or immunocompro-
mised individuals (YOPI) (FDA, 2012; Notermans & Batt, 1998). Although mortality from 
B. cereus contamination is rare, emetic syndrome has been linked to liver failure and 
death, even in healthy individuals. Additionally, in 2011, a cytotoxin isolated from B. ce-
reus have been the cause of a significant outbreak in the Netherlands, resulting in three 
deaths (FDA, 2012). 
 
Most foodborne outbreaks caused by Bacillus cereus are linked to concentrations ex-
ceeding 5 log CFU/g (colony-forming units per gram), which indicates active growth and 
proliferation of the bacterium and is often associated with foodborne illness (EFSA, 2016; 
==PAGE:22==
 
22 
 
 
FDA, 2012). In the European context, EFSA does not set a specific critical limit for B. 
cereus applicable to all food categories (EFSA, 2016). Concentrations lower than 5 log 
CFU/g may still be acceptable if the food undergoes further processing or heating before 
consumption. 
 
3.5.1.2 Clostridium perfringens 
Clostridium perfringens is another Gram-positive, spore-forming, rod-shaped bacterium 
that produces an enterotoxin. Although primarily anaerobic, it can tolerate exposure to 
aerobic conditions. The spores of C. perfringens are resilient and persist in soil and areas 
contaminated with human or animal feces, making them easily spread in the environment 
(FDA, 2012; Matthews et al., 2017). 
 
This bacterium is commonly found in a variety of foods, including meat and poultry, veg-
etables, spices, herbs, and in both raw and processed foods (FDA, 2012; Food-
Safety.gov, 2025). Similarly to Bacillus cereus, the most concerning trait of C. perfringens 
is its ability to produce heat-resistant spores, which can survive cooking and later germi-
nate under favorable conditions (FDA, 2012; Matthews et al., 2017). 
 
C. perfringens can grow at temperatures ranging from 20 to 50 °C, with optimal growth 
occurring between 43 and 45 °C. Growth rates decrease rapidly at temperatures below 
15 °C, and no growth occurs at 6 °C (Matthews et al., 2017). The bacterium can tolerate 
salt concentrations up to 5% and grows best at pH levels between 5 and 8 and water 
activity higher range of 0.93-0.97 (Novak & Juneja, 2002). Even though vegetative cells 
are destroyed at temperatures above 60 °C, C. perfringens spores can withstand tem-
peratures over 100 °C (Akhtar et al., 2009; Matthews et al., 2017). 
 
There are two forms of foodborne illness caused by Clostridium perfringens: gastroen-
teritis and enteritis necroticans. Both forms typically result from the ingestion of large 
numbers of C. perfringens vegetative cells or spores, which germinate and multiply more 
quickly than most bacteria. After ingestion, the vegetative cells produce an enterotoxin 
in the intestines that results in the illness. The symptoms normally last 12-24 hours and 
include abdominal cramps, diarrhea, nausea, and occasionally vomiting (FDA, 2012). 
 
The gastroenteritis form is usually mild and self-limiting. However, in rare cases, severe 
gastroenteritis can cause significant damage to the small intestine and may lead to fa-
tality. Enteritis necroticans is a much more severe form of the illness, often leading to 
extensive damage of the intestinal tissue, and is frequently fatal (FDA, 2012). 
 
Like for B. cereus, EFSA does not set a specific critical level for C. perfringens (EFSA, 
2016). Most foodborne outbreaks caused by C. perfringens are linked to concentrations 
exceeding 5 log CFU/g, typically in cooked meat dishes and catering meals, as the path-
ogen thrives in protein-rich substrates. 
 
3.5.2 Toxicogenic biological hazards 
3.5.2.1 Clostridium botulinum 
Clostridium botulinum is an anaerobic, Gram-positive, spore-forming, rod-shaped bacte-
rium known for producing a highly potent neurotoxin. Like Bacillus and C. perfringens, 
==PAGE:23==
 
 
 
23 
 
its spores are heat-resistant and can survive in foods that are improperly or minimally 
processed. The bacterium and its spores are widespread in nature and commonly found 
in protein-rich food sources that provide an anaerobic, low-acid, and moist environment 
(FDA, 2012; FSANZ, 2020; Rahman, 2007). 
 
C. botulinum isolates are categorized based on the type of botulinum neurotoxin (BoNT) 
they produce, with types A, B, E, and F being responsible for causing botulism in humans 
(FDA, 2012). Foodborne botulism symptoms usually appear 18 to 36 hours after con-
suming the toxin-contaminated food and may include double vision, blurred vision, 
drooping eyelids, slurred speech, difficulty swallowing, dry mouth, and muscle weak-
ness. If left untreated, the condition can progress to flaccid paralysis of muscles, includ-
ing those of the respiratory tract. The mortality rate is high, ranging from 5 to 10% of 
cases if treatment is not immediately administered (FDA, 2012). 
 
Depending on the temperature ranges for growth, C. botulinum can be divided in two 
groups: proteolytic mesophilic (Group 1) and non-proteolytic psychrotolerant (Group 2). 
Group 1 grows between 10 and 50 °C, with an optimal range of 35-40 °C. Group 2 grows 
between 3 and 45 °C, with an optimal range of 18-25 °C. The bacterium prefers neutral 
to slightly acidic conditions, in a pH range between 4.6 and 8.5, and requires an oxygen-
free environment to grow. It can also tolerate salt concentration up to 5-10%, and water 
activity higher than 0.93 (FDA, 2012; Matthews et al., 2017). 
 
There is no specific regulation setting quantitative microbiological criteria for the man-
agement of C. botulinum in food safety within the EU. However, the regulations do pro-
vide guidelines and requirements that focus on controlling factors such as storage tem-
perature, salt concentration, pH, and food processing methods to prevent the growth and 
toxin production of C. botulinum (Matthews et al., 2017). These preventive measures are 
essential to minimize the risk associated with this pathogen.  
 
3.5.2.2 Staphylococcus aureus 
Staphylococcus aureus is a Gram-positive, nonmotile, catalase-positive, cocci-shaped 
bacterium capable of producing a variety of enterotoxins, many of which act as virulent 
factors affecting human health. This pathogen is frequently linked to food poisoning, par-
ticularly in foods that require significant handling during preparation and are kept slightly 
above proper refrigeration temperatures for extended periods (FDA, 2012). 
 
It grows in temperature ranges from 7 to 47.8 °C, the optimum growth at 35 °C. The 
growth pH lays between 4.5 and 9.3, with an optimum growth around 7. It also tolerates 
water activity as low as 0.83 and high salt concentrations, up to about 10-15%. It is widely 
distributed in the environment and is one of the most resistant non-spore-forming human 
pathogens, able to survive for extended periods in a dry state (FDA, 2012; Matthews et 
al., 2017). 
 
Staphylococcal food poisoning occurs when food is contaminated with enterotoxins pro-
duced by S. aureus. Upon ingestion, these enterotoxins can cause symptoms such as 
nausea, abdominal cramping, vomiting, and diarrhea. In more severe cases, individuals 
may experience dehydration, headaches, muscle cramping, and temporary changes in 
==PAGE:24==
 
24 
 
 
blood pressure and pulse rate. Although the illness is typically mild and usually lasts from 
only a few hours to one day, fatalities can occur, particularly among the elderly, infants, 
and individuals with weakened immune systems (FDA, 2012). 
 
According to Commission Regulation (EC) No. 2073/2005, staphylococcal enterotoxins 
should not be detected in 25 grams of products such as cheeses, milk powder and whey 
powder. In addition, coagulase-positive staphylococci must not exceed the critical limit 
of 2 log CFU/g in most dairy products. The regulation also emphasizes that if S. aureus 
is detected in products that support its growth, improvements in production hygiene and 
selection of raw materials must be done, and if values greater than 5 log CFU/g are 
detected, the food batch has to be tested for staphylococcal enterotoxins. 
 
3.5.3 Infectious biological hazards 
3.5.3.1 Listeria monocytogenes 
Listeria monocytogenes is a Gram-positive, rod-shaped, facultative bacterium commonly 
found in a variety of food products, including ready-to-eat (RTE) refrigerated foods, dairy 
products, cheeses, meat and poultry, as well as raw vegetables and fruits (FDA, 2012; 
Matthews et al., 2017). 
 
It exhibits notable resistance to salt, acidic conditions, and cold temperatures. It can grow 
within a temperature range of 0 to 45 °C and has the capability to grow slowly at lower 
temperatures. This bacterium tolerates salt concentrations up to 10-12% and can thrive 
in environments with pH levels as low as 4.4. Additionally, the minimum water activity for 
growth is typically 0.93, with optimum growth at 0.97 (Matthews et al., 2017). Although 
L. monocytogenes cannot survive heat processing, it is particularly difficult to eliminate 
its contamination from food plants because it adheres to food contact surfaces and forms 
biofilms in hard-to-reach areas (Matthews et al., 2017). This makes proper sanitation 
difficult. 
 
Listeria can cause two forms of diseases (i.e., listeriosis) and is one of the leading causes 
of death from foodborne illness (FDA, 2012). The first form of listeriosis ranges from mild 
to intense symptoms of nausea, vomiting, aches, fever, and, sometimes, diarrhea that 
typically last from few days to one week. The second and more deadly form occurs when 
the infection spreads through the bloodstream to the nervous system, resulting in men-
ingitis, septicemia, and other potentially fatal problems (FDA, 2012). The duration of ill-
ness can be prolonged and may last several weeks. 
 
Some growth of L. monocytogenes may be allowed in the food as the pathogen has a 
high infective dose. According to Commission Regulation (EC) No 1441/2007 on micro-
biological criteria for foodstuffs, amending Commission Regulation (EC) No 2073/2005, 
RTE foods that can support the growth of Listeria monocytogenes (e.g., cheeses, 
smoked fish, pâtés, and meat products) must comply with a critical limit of 2 log CFU/g 
at the end of the product's shelf life.  
 
3.5.3.2 Salmonella spp. 
Salmonella spp. is a genus of non-sporeforming, Gram-negative, rod-shaped bacteria 
from the family Enterobacteriaceae. The genus is divided into two species that can cause 
==PAGE:25==
 
 
 
25 
 
illness in humans: S. enterica and S. bongori. Its contamination is commonly associated 
with raw or undercooked eggs, poultry, and meat, but can also be found in fruits and 
vegetables, seafood, dairy products, and even dry food like spices and nuts (Matthews 
et al., 2017). 
 
This bacterium is highly resilient to both temperature and pH variations, allowing it to 
adapt to extreme environmental conditions. Some strains can grow at temperatures as 
high as 54 °C, while others exhibit psychotropic abilities that enable growth at 2-4°C, 
thereby ensuring survival in refrigerated environments. It also tolerates pH levels ranging 
from 4.5 to 9.5, with an optimal range of 6.5 to 7.5. Although Salmonella is generally 
inhibited by 3-4% salt, its tolerance increases with temperature in the range of 10 to 
30 °C. Additionally, foods with a water activity below 0.93 do not support its growth (FDA, 
2012; Matthews et al., 2017). 
 
Salmonella spp. is a leading causes of foodborne bacterial illnesses in humans (FDA, 
2012) and is associated with two types of disorders: gastrointestinal illness (i.e., nonty-
phoidal salmonellosis) and typhoid fever. Gastrointestinal illness caused by Salmonella 
typically presents mild to moderate symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, ab-
dominal cramps, and fever. These symptoms generally last from a few days up to one 
week (Matthews et al., 2017) and usually do not require hospitalization. 
 
Typhoidal illness, caused by serotypes like S. Typhi and S. Paratyphi, is more severe 
and can result in high fever, diarrhea or constipation, body aches, headache, lethargy, 
and sometimes a red skin rash. This condition is serious and can be fatal, with mortality 
rates reaching up to 10% in untreated cases (FDA, 2012). Due to its severity and very 
low infective dose – as low as one cell, depending on age and health of host and strain 
differences (FDA, 2012) – the microbiological criteria for Salmonella in foodstuffs is to be 
absent in 25 grams of the food product, according to Commission Regulation (EC) No 
2073/2005. 
 
The Annual Report on Zoonoses in Denmark (2023) indicates a rise in Salmonella out-
breaks, increasing from 11 outbreaks in 2022 to 18 in 2023. This surge was largely at-
tributed to Salmonella Enteritidis (non-typhoidal type), which was responsible for eight 
outbreaks in 2023, a significant jump compared to the 0 to 2 outbreaks reported annually 
between 2018 and 2022. 
 
3.5.3.3 Shigatoxigenic Escherichia coli (STEC) 
Shigatoxigenic Escherichia coli (STEC) or Verotoxigenic Escherichia coli (VTEC) are 
strains of the bacterium E. coli that produce Shiga toxin, also known as verotoxin. Only 
a minority of E. coli types can cause illness in humans (FDA, 2012; Matthews et al., 
2017), and STEC is one of the six pathogenic types. It can be found in various food 
sources, such as raw or undercooked ground beef and poultry, unpasteurized milk and 
juices, vegetables, fruits, and contaminated water (FDA, 2012). 
 
Among STEC, a small group known as Enterohemorrhagic E. coli (EHEC) is the major 
cause of foodborne illness, being responsible for most cases of severe disease and food-
borne outbreaks worldwide (FDA, 2012; Matthews et al., 2017). E. coli O157 is the most 
==PAGE:26==
 
26 
 
 
predominant STEC/EHEC strain, accounting for 75% of the infections worldwide (FDA, 
2012). EHEC can cause severe complications, including hemorrhagic colitis and hemo-
lytic uremic syndrome (HUS), a serious condition that can lead to kidney failure (Mat-
thews et al., 2017). 
 
E. coli O157:H7 is tolerant to acidic environment, being able to grow at pH levels 
around 4. According to literature (Matthews et al., 2017), when inoculated at high levels, 
E. coli O157:H7 has survived fermentation, drying, and storage of fermented sausages 
(pH 4.5) for more than 2 months at 4 °C. Moreover, it has developed resistance to multi-
ple antibiotics. On the other hand, it has low resistance to heat, with D60 values around 
2-3 minutes. 
 
EHEC has low infective dose, being able to cause illness from 10 to 100 cells in the case 
of O157:H7 (FDA, 2012). According to Commission Regulation (EC) No 1441/2007, the 
critical levels of E. coli vary depending on the type of food product. For most meat prod-
ucts, the limit is set at 500 CFU/g. For dairy products, the acceptable levels range be-
tween 2 to 3 log CFU/g, while for ready-to-eat fruits and vegetables, the critical threshold 
is 3 log CFU/g. However, due to its low infective dose, STEC must be absent in 25 g of 
RTE products. 
 
3.5.3.4 Campylobacter spp. 
Campylobacter spp. is a non-sporeforming, Gram-negative, with an S- or spiral- rod mor-
phology. The organism is motile, with a flagellum at one or both of the polar ends. The 
bacteria in commonly associated with food sources such as poultry, unpasteurized milk, 
contaminated water, red meat, seafood, and fruits and vegetables (FDA, 2012; Matthews 
et al., 2017).  
 
Most Campylobacter species, particularly C. jejuni and C. coli, are associated with intes-
tinal tract infections called campylobacteriosis. Fever, diarrhea, abdominal cramps, and 
vomiting are the major symptoms. In general, the minimum number of ingested Campyl-
obacter cells that can cause infection is in the range of 4 log CFU/g, but the infectious 
dose as well as the severity of symptoms may vary depending on the type of contami-
nated food consumed and the general health of the exposed person. Most cases of cam-
pylobacteriosis are self-limiting, but severe cases can be fatal (FDA, 2012; Lassen et al., 
2023; Matthews et al., 2017). 
 
This pathogen typically grows at low oxygen concentrations (i.e., lower than atmospheric 
concentration), with optimum growth at 3-5% O2. Thus, these bacteria generally are quite 
unstable in the ambient environment (FDA, 2012). Moreover, C. jejuni is susceptible to 
drying, heating, freezing, disinfectants, and acidic conditions. 
 
C. jejuni is estimated to be the third leading bacterial cause of foodborne illness in the 
United States (FDA, 2012), and the third main cause of foodborne illness in Denmark in 
2023, only behind Salmonella and Norovirus (NoV) (Lassen et al., 2023). Like STEC and 
Salmonella, Campylobacter spp. is associated with a low infectious dose, so a small 
number of cells can cause illness. According to Commission Regulation (EC) No. 
2073/2005, the presence of C. jejuni must be absent in 25 grams of fresh poultry meat.

3.5.4 Foodborne viruses 
3.5.4.1 Norovirus 
Norovirus (NoV) is a highly contagious virus and one of the leading causes of acute 
gastroenteritis worldwide, accounting for the majority of foodborne illness outbreaks 
(FDA, 2012; Lassen et al., 2023; Matthews et al., 2017). NoV is notably resilient and can 
be transmitted via contaminated food and water, as well as through direct person-to-
person contact and interaction with contaminated environmental surfaces. 
 
There are six recognized NoV genogroups, with three (I, II, and IV) known to infect hu-
mans. The viral particles are hardy, capable to withstand storage, refrigeration, freezing, 
and acidic conditions. Moreover, these viruses generally have an extremely low infection 
dose, as few as 1 to 10 viral particles (FDA, 2012; Matthews et al., 2017). 
 
Symptoms of NoV-induced gastroenteritis typically manifest within 24-48 hours of expo-
sure and include acute-onset vomiting, watery non-bloody diarrhea, abdominal cramps, 
and nausea (FDA, 2012). Although most cases are mild to moderate and self-limiting, 
the severity and potential mortality can be influenced by factors such as age and under-
lying health conditions, making vulnerable groups, like the YOPI population, more sus-
ceptible to complications.  
 
NoV infection can affect individuals of all ages, and outbreaks are particularly challenging 
to prevent and manage due to the virus's high transmissibility (Matthews et al., 2017). 
According to the Annual Report on Zoonoses in Denmark (2023), NoV accounted for 13 
foodborne outbreaks in 2023 in Denmark, maintaining a similar trend from 2022 and 
2021, which reported 14 outbreaks each year. Most outbreaks were linked to a healthy 
virus carrier among kitchen staff, predominantly in canteens or catering facilities. 
 
The EU regulations do not specify a critical limit for NoV in food. However, keeping strict 
personal hygiene, and following proper GHP and GMP throughout the food processing 
are essential to minimize contamination risk. 
 
3.5.4.2 Hepatitis A virus 
Similar to NoV, hepatitis A virus (HAV) is a resilient organism that can infect individuals 
through contaminated food and water, and it is often transmitted via direct person-to-
person contact. Although HAV cannot replicate outside the human host, it remains stable 
under a wide range of environmental conditions, including freezing, heat, exposure to 
chemicals, and desiccation (FDA, 2012; Matthews et al., 2017). 
 
There are six known HAV genotypes, with three (I, II, and III) capable of infecting hu-
mans. The infective dose of HAV is very low, typically ranging from 10 to 100 viral parti-
cles. The incubation period after ingestion can last up to 50 days, during which an in-
fected individual may shed the virus for 10 to 14 days before exhibiting any symptoms 
(Matthews et al., 2017).  
HAV infections can be asymptomatic – particularly in young children – or symptomatic. 
Typical symptoms of HAV infection include fever, anorexia, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, 
myalgia, hepatitis, and often jaundice. Symptoms generally last from 1 to 2 weeks, alt-
hough rare, prolonged cases may continue for up to 6 months (FDA, 2012). More severe 
==PAGE:28==
 
28 
 
 
HAV infections can lead to massive hepatic necrosis and acute liver failure, which carry 
a high case-fatality rate. But fortunately, a hepatitis A vaccine is available to help prevent 
infection. 
 
As mentioned for NoV, there are no EU regulations that outline a critical level for HAV in 
food. Instead, the focus is on prevention. Maintaining strict personal hygiene and follow-
ing proper GHP and GMP throughout the food processing and supply chain are essential 
to minimize contamination risk. 
 
3.5.5 Spoilage microorganisms 
Food spoilage is a metabolic process that makes foods undesirable or unacceptable for 
human consumption due to changes in their sensory characteristics. Various microor-
ganisms can cause food spoilage, including bacteria such as lactic acid bacteria (LAB), 
Bacillus spp., and Pseudomonas spp., as well as molds and yeasts (Rawat, 2015). 
 
Although spoiled foods may sometimes be safe to eat and may not cause illness due to 
the absence of pathogens or toxins, changes in texture, smell, taste, or appearance often 
leads to their rejection. Additionally, some spoilage microorganisms can produce toxins 
that may have adverse health effects. To prevent the presence of these organisms 
throughout a product's shelf life, it is crucial to implement control measures that minimize 
the likelihood of microbial growth in both raw materials and the final product. 
 
3.6 Chemical hazards 
In food safety, chemical hazards refer to substances that may either naturally occur in 
food or be introduced during production, processing, or handling, posing risks to human 
health when ingested. These hazards include contaminants such as environmental pol-
lutants, process contaminants, or migrating substances that are not intentionally added 
to the food. They also encompass residues from pesticides, veterinary drugs, or pro-
cessing aids deliberately used in food production. Additionally, allergens, food additives, 
or adulterants that may be present or added, contributing to potential risks. These haz-
ards can be toxic, carcinogenic, allergenic, or harmful to body systems if consumed at 
dangerous levels. 
 
3.6.1 Mycotoxins 
Mycotoxins are toxic secondary metabolites produced by different types of fungi that can 
contaminate food. These molds can thrive on various food items, including various cere-
als such as barley, corn, and wheat, as well as oilseeds, dried fruits, nuts, and spices. 
Mycotoxins enter the food chain due to mold growth in crops both before and after har-
vest. They may also grow during storage, transportation, or directly on the food, espe-
cially in warm and humid conditions. Furthermore, most mycotoxins are chemically sta-
ble, allowing them to persist through food processing. Exposure to mycotoxins can hap-
pen either directly by eating infected food or indirectly from animals that are fed contam-
inated feed (Alshannaq & Yu, 2017; WHO, 2023). 
 
Mycotoxins pose a significant threat to food safety and public health, with the potential 
to cause a range of acute and chronic health issues in humans, including gastrointestinal 
disorders, kidney damage, immune deficiencies, and even cancer (EFSA, 2024d). To

3.6.2 Cereulide 
Cereulide is a toxin responsible for the emetic type of B. cereus food poisoning (EFSA, 
2016). This toxin is a plasmid-encoded cyclic peptide synthesized when B. cereus con-
taminates food products, leading to emetic syndrome upon ingestion of the product con-
taminated with toxin (Bottone, 2010; FDA, 2012).  
 
Cereulide is produced during the stationary phase of bacterial growth and is commonly 
found in foods rich in starch, such as rice products and pasta (Bottone, 2010; FDA, 2012). 
This indicates that barley, a grain rich in starch, and its industrial byproducts, such as 
BSG, can be sources of cereulide. Additionally, the toxin is highly heat- and pH-stable, 
allowing it to persist in the food even after processing, preparation, or storage (Matthews 
et al., 2017). 
 
==PAGE:30==
 
30 
 
 
Based on data from animal studies, the minimum intoxication dose is estimated to be 
between 8 and 10 µg/kg of body weight. However, further research is needed to deter-
mine the exact emetic dose for humans. Recent foodborne outbreaks have reported toxin 
concentrations in leftover food ranging from 2 to 6 µg/g (EFSA, 2016). 
 
Symptoms of emetic syndrome, which include nausea and rapid onset vomiting, typically 
appear in 15 minutes to 6 hours after consuming food containing the preformed toxin 
(EFSA, 2016) and can last between 6 to 24 hours (Notermans & Batt, 1998). The emetic 
form of B. cereus poisoning is generally mild and self-limiting in healthy individuals, but 
more severe cases can occur when large amounts of the toxin are ingested. More severe 
intoxications are reported requiring hospitalization and intensive medical care, including 
liver transplantations (EFSA, 2016). 
 
Currently, EU regulations do not establish specific maximum levels for cereulide. How-
ever, Bacillus cereus typically begins producing this toxin after reaching high population 
densities, usually exceeding 5-6 log CFU/mL (Ceuppens et al., 2013; Matthews et al., 
2017). In the absence of defined regulatory limits for cereulide, food businesses must 
implement HACCP-based control measures to mitigate the risks associated with the 
pathogen and its toxins, particularly in high-risk foods. 
 
3.6.3 Process contaminants 
Process contaminants are substances unintentionally formed in food or food ingredients 
during chemical changes that occur in processing methods such as fermentation, smok-
ing, drying, refining, and high-temperature cooking (EFSA, 2024c). 
 
In terms of barley processing into malt, the formation of process contaminants is primarily 
linked to the possible roasting of malt at high temperatures. Acrylamide and furan are 
the main food process contaminants that may form when temperature and time is not 
controlled (Food Safety Magazine, 2007). However, since furan is a highly volatile com-
pound and evaporates easily, the primary food safety concern in BSG is the potential 
presence of acrylamide. 
 
Acrylamide is a chemical that forms naturally in starchy foods during high-temperature 
processes. This formation primarily occurs through the Maillard Reaction, a non-enzy-
matic reaction involving proteins and amino acids, particularly asparagine, reacting with 
reducing sugars (EFSA, 2024c). The Maillard Reaction is intentionally utilized in food 
preparation to enhance color (browning) and flavor. However, if processing parameters 
like temperature, time, and pH are not adequately controlled, the risk of acrylamide for-
mation increases (Food Safety Magazine, 2007). 
 
Acrylamide is an animal carcinogen and human neurotoxicant at high levels of exposure, 
affecting the nervous system (Food Safety Magazine, 2007). Moreover, it is classified by 
the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) as a probable human carcino-
gen. Commission Regulation (EU) 2017/2158 about the presence of acrylamide in food 
established benchmark levels for the presence of acrylamide in cereal-based products 
between 150 and 300 µg/kg.

3.7 Physical hazards 
Physical hazards in food are foreign objects or extraneous materials that may cause 
injury or discomfort when consumed. These contaminants can originate from raw mate-
rials, processing equipment, packaging, or improper handling. Common physical haz-
ards include metal fragments from equipment – in this case, potentially from the brewery 
or M4F equipment – as well as stones and dirt introduced through raw materials, such 
as barley grains used for malt production. During the filtration process, when wort is 
separated from BSG after mashing, these foreign bodies can be carried along with the 
BSG and may remain in the final fermented product.

4.4.2 Sample preparation 
To perform the first BSG dilution (i.e., 10-1 dilution), 25 g of the thawed BSG were mixed 
with 225 mL of buffered peptone water (BPW) in a stomacher bag with a filter and ho-
mogenized for 1 minute using a stomacher machine. Subsequent serial dilutions were 
prepared by transferring 1 mL of the BSG solution into 9 mL of maximum recovery diluent 
(MRD) to create 10-2, 10-3, and 10-4 dilutions, as illustrated in Figure 4. 
 
 
Figure 4. Schematic representation of the performed serial dilution. 
 
To  analyze B. cereus spores, 7 mL of the filtered BSG stock solution at 10-1 dilution was 
heat treated for 10 min in a water bath at 80 °C. This pre-treatment aims to induce spor-
ulation when BSG is contaminated with B. cereus vegetative cells. Afterwards, the same 
10-2, 10-3, and 10-4 dilutions were performed as mentioned before. 
 
For the hygiene indicators – i.e., coagulase-positive Staphylococci, coliforms and E. coli 
– as well as for the pathogens L. monocytogenes and Salmonella spp., it was also per-
formed the enrichment of the samples. B. cereus was not analyzed with enrichment as 
it was already identified in all samples during the initial analyses. For the enrichment, the 
BSG stock solution at 10-1 dilution was incubated at 37 °C for 24 hours. After that, 100, 
10, and 1 µL of the filtered BSG stock were spread on agar plates and incubated for 24 
hours. After the incubation period, the agar plates were checked to see if colonies were 
present or not.  
 
4.4.3 Medium preparation and dispersion method 
With the exception of TSC agar for Clostridium perfringens and MRS for lactic acid bac-
teria, which require pour plate with overlayer method, the agar plates were previously 
prepared and dried in the laboratory prior to sampling. Agar media were weighed in a 
sterile environment and mixed with 500 mL of distilled water. The mixtures were auto-

5. Results and discussion 
5.1 Fermented BSG and raw materials 
This project investigated the process employed by Myco4Food (M4F), which utilizes 
solid-state fermentation of brewers' spent grain (BSG) in a bioreactor with the filamen-
tous fungus Rhizopus oryzae. The primary goal of fermenting BSG is to enhance its 
digestibility and nutritional value, with the aim of repurposing it as a protein-rich food 
ingredient. The fermented BSG produced at M4F is intended for further processing into 
different kinds of products, such as hybrid and plant-based sausages, sourdough breads, 
and crackers. 
 
Brewers' spent grain is the primary raw material used in the M4F process and is a pre-
processed by-product of the brewing industry. As illustrated in Error! Reference source n
ot found., BSG is derived from barley that first undergoes malting, followed by the brew-
ing steps of milling, mashing, and lautering/filtration. During beer production, malted bar-
ley is subjected to both mechanical and heat treatments before being discarded as BSG 
after wort separation. While the type of malt used may vary depending on the beer recipe, 
the mechanical and heat treatment conditions typically remain consistent across pro-
cesses. 
 
BSG typically has a high moisture content, ranging from 77 to 81% (w/w), and a pH 
between 5 and 6  (Castro & Colpini, 2021; Mitri et al., 2022; Mussatto et al., 2006). These 
values are susceptible to significant variation due to the quality and type of malts used 
during the brewing process. Additionally, BSG typically exhibits a high water activity (aw), 
between 0.95 and 0.97, due to its elevated moisture content. Table 10, adapted from 
Castro & Colpini (2021), illustrates the composition of BSG measured during their study. 
 
Due to its high moisture and fermentable sugar content, BSG is a highly unstable mate-
rial that is prone to microbial growth and rapid deterioration. In the M4F process, BSG is 
supplied by Slowburn Brewing Co-op, located in Albertslund, and is transported to M4F's 
facility at 50-70 °C.

The second raw material used by M4F is a Rhizopus oryzae spore suspension. To pre-
pare this suspension, a mixture of barley grains and water serves as the cultivation me-

isolated culture. After incubation, the spores are collected using autoclaved deionized 
water, and the final concentration is adjusted. 
 
5.2 M4F process line 
The process line begins with the reception of a fresh batch of BSG from Slowburn Brew-
ing Co-op. Approximately 30-40 kg of spent grain is transported from the brewery to M4F, 
a 10-minute journey. The BSG is collected right after the mashing step at Slowburn at 
approximately 70 °C and arrives at M4F at a temperature of 50 °C. Upon receiving the 
BSG, M4F either initiates fermentation with the fresh BSG or portions for storage and 
future use. 
 
The fresh BSG is divided into 1-2 kg portions, sealed in plastic bags, and stored in a 
freezer at -20 °C. When ready for fermentation, the frozen BSG is thawed in a microwave 
for 15 minutes. The defrosted BSG is then mixed with Rhizopus oryzae spore suspension 
at a concentration of 106 spores per gram of BSG. 
 
The suspension must be prepared in advance and stored at M4F. For this process, 100 
grams of slightly grounded barley is mixed with 65 grams of water in membrane plastic 
bags suitable for autoclaving. The substrate mixture is thoroughly homogenized and then 
sealed for sterilization in an autoclave at 121 °C for 15 minutes. 
 
After sterilization, the substrate bags are cooled to room temperature and inoculated with 
1 mL of pre-prepared Rhizopus spore suspension or an isolated culture from a Petri dish. 
The inoculated medium is carefully homogenized again and sealed for incubation, en-
suring the membrane is left unobstructed to allow for proper aeration during incubation. 
 
==PAGE:43==
 
 
 
43 
 
Incubation takes place under aerobic conditions at 26 °C for approximately 7-10 days, 
during which Rhizopus oryzae black spores develop in the medium. To collect the sus-
pension under sterile conditions, 50-100 mL of autoclaved deionized (DI) water is added 
and mixed periodically. The suspension is then transferred to Falcon tubes. To ensure 
the removal of any remaining growth medium, the solution is filtered using autoclaved 
Miracloth filter. After filtration, the spore count is performed, and the concentration of the 
suspension is adjusted. The RO spore suspension can be stored in a refrigerator for 
several days prior to its use. 
 
After adding the spore suspension to the BSG, the mixture is thoroughly homogenized 
and evenly distributed into aluminum trays, each containing approximately 800 g. The 
bioreactor, which has a capacity for nine trays (Figure 5, Section 4.5), is then loaded, 
and fermentation proceeds under controlled conditions. The process is maintained at 
temperatures between 25 and 35 °C, with saturated humidity and air circulation set to 
15-20% O₂ and 2% CO₂. However, these parameters are still being studied and adjusted 
to optimize fermentation conditions. 
 
During solid-state fermentation, the temperature gradually increases within the BSG, cre-
ating favorable conditions for fungal proliferation. The fermentation is typically stopped 
after three or four days – but can be extended if necessary. The extent of fermentation 
is assessed both visually, by observing mycelium development in the substrate, and 
quantitatively, through dry matter loss calculation, which typically ranges between 20 to 
30% after three or four days of fermentation. 
 
Currently, no heat-inactivation step is performed after fermentation to reduce the risk of 
potentially pathogenic microorganisms that may have developed during the previous 
steps. After fermentation, the trays are cooled to room temperature and the fermented 
BSG is collected into 10-15 kg plastic bags and stored in a freezer at -20 °C. 
 
The final M4F product is subsequently transported to the DMRI center at the Technolog-
ical Institute and to the University of Copenhagen, where it is further processed into a 
variety of protein-rich food products. It is valid to highlight that the fermented BSG is not 
commercialized as an ingredient yet, and its applicability and safety are still under study.

M4F.

5.3 Further processing 
At the Technical Institute and the University of Copenhagen, significant efforts are being 
dedicated to exploring various food products incorporating fermented BSG, including 
sourdough bread, crackers, plant-based pâté, and hybrid sausages. As part of this pro-
ject, a technical visit to the DMRI group at the Technical Institute was conducted to as-
sess the further processing of fermented BSG. At DMRI, research and development ef-
forts are primarily focused on formulating hybrid sausages using the fermented BSG 
from M4F. 
 
==PAGE:45==
 
 
 
45 
 
While the formulation may vary, hybrid sausages made at the Technical Institute are 
typically produced using a combination of fat, additives, meat, and 5-10% fermented 
BSG as a meat replacer. In this process, the whole fermented BSG is incorporated with-
out separation into solid and liquid fractions or undergoing any pre-treatment, such as 
grinding or drying. This approach ensures the complete utilization of the fermented grain 
as an ingredient. 
 
The development process at the Technical Institute begins with the reception of fer-
mented BSG, which is transported frozen in 10-15 kg plastic bags from Myco4Food to 
DMRI. Since both buildings are located within the same development park, the short 
transport time – approximately two minutes – ensures that the product remains frozen 
and does not thaw during transport. Upon arrival, the BSG is divided into 1 kg portions, 
vacuum-packed, and stored in a freezer at -20 °C until further use.  
 
Sausages are produced in the pilot plant, which has a capacity of approximately 10 kg 
per batch. The process begins with defrosting the fermented BSG and meat, which are 
then blended in a meat mincer with the other ingredients such as fat, water, dry season-
ing, and additives. The mincer is manually fed, and all the ingredients are thoroughly 
mixed at a controlled temperature of 10-12 °C until the blend achieves a uniform distri-
bution and desired texture. 
 
Once the blending is complete, the mixture is manually transferred to a sausage filling 
machine, where the stuffing step is performed. Before stuffing, pH measurements are 
taken for quality and safety control. The sausage casings are kept in cold water until 
utilization. As in the blending step, the stuffing step is partially manual, as the casings 
are manually filled with the sausage mixture and then sealed. Figure 8 illustrates the 
blending and stuffing equipment and handling during sausage production at the Tech-
nical Institute. 
 
 
Figure 8. From left to right: blending and stuffing steps during sausage production in the pilot plant at the 
Technical Institute.

In some cases, after stuffing, the sausages undergo smoking, which serves to dry and 
cure the blend while imparting distinctive flavor and aroma to the final product. Smoking 
also introduces components that can inhibit bacterial growth, enhancing the safety and 
shelf life of the sausages. 
 
However, the hybrid sausages made with meat and fermented BSG are currently not 
subjected to the smoking process. Instead, they are hung in oven cabins and steam-
cooked for 15-20 minutes at 80 °C, reaching a core temperature of 75 °C. After cooking, 
the sausages are cooled in the same cabin using rinse water until they reach a core 
temperature of 40 °C. Finally, the final sausages are vacuum-packed and stored in the 
freezer at -20 °C. 
 
5.4 Food safety and prerequisite program 
As mentioned in Section 3.4.3, prerequisite programs (PRPs) consists of essential prac-
tices and procedures that establish the fundamental conditions required for ensuring food 
safety. Within this framework, the choice of adequate food contact materials (FCM) and 
the appropriate implementation of good hygiene practices (GHP) and hygienic design 
are critical actions to be emphasized in the M4F processing line for fermented BSG. 
 
5.4.1 Food contact materials 
According to EFSA, food contact materials (FCM) are substances or objects that come 
into direct contact with food during its production, processing, packaging, or storage. 
These materials include packaging materials, processing equipment, storage tanks, and 
utensils. 
 
To minimize contamination risks, FCM must comply with food safety regulations that 
specify requirements for their selection, maintenance, and cleaning. Table 11 presents 
the primary materials that come into contact with BSG during its collection, processing, 
and storage at Myco4Food.

limited attention has been given so far to identifying the contact materials and ensuring 
that they are food-grade and used under appropriate conditions. Therefore, it is important 
to highlight the key requirements that must be followed to ensure the safety of the final 
fermented product. 
 
The main EU legislation on the matter of FCM is Regulation (EC) No 1935/2004 con-
cerning all materials and articles intended to come into contact with food. The regulation 
states that FCM must not transfer constituents to food which could endanger human 
health, change the composition or deteriorate the organoleptic characteristics of the food. 
It also states that materials acceptable for food contact must have full traceability 
throughout the supply chain and must be identified by a suitable system – applying 
proper labeling and linking relevant documentation.  
 
Although most of these requirements are intended to FCM manufacturers, it is important 
for food producers such as M4F to understand how to identify materials approved as 
food grade, and to request relevant documentation from the suppliers. One easy way to 
identify materials that are safe for food contact is through the international symbol for 
‘food safe’ material, depicted in Figure 9. The symbol indicates that the product follows 
legal requirements and that the material used in the product is considered safe for food 
contact.  
 
 
Figure 9. International symbol for ‘food safe’ material. 
 
Nevertheless, the presence of the ‘food safe’ symbol does not guarantee food safety 
under all conditions. The composition of the FCM is not the only factor that affects the 
risk of hazardous substance migration to the food. Other factors, such as the temperature 
of the food product, its fat content, and the total time of contact with the surface, also 
influence the safety and performance of the FCM. Therefore, identifying all FCM materi-
als and understanding the limits of their applicability are essential to ensuring that no 
contamination risks or deterioration of food properties occur.

5.4.2 Good hygienic practices and hygienic design 
Another important aspect to consider for reducing contamination risks in the M4F pro-
cessing line is GHP and enhancing the hygienic design of both the laboratory and biore-
actor. Maintaining high hygiene standards must be an integral part of all planning and 
processing steps to ensure food safety. 
 
As illustrated in Figure 7, M4F’s process begins with the collection of fresh BSG from 
Slowburn on the same day a new batch of beer is brewed. The BSG is directly collected 
from the mashing tank using a stainless steel shovel and transferred into two polypropyl-
ene transport boxes that have been pre-disinfected with 70% v/v ethanol. However, eth-
anol alone is not effective against bacterial and fungal spores, raising concerns about 
the adequacy of the cleaning procedures. Additionally, the sanitation protocols for the 
brewery’s equipment (e.g., the mashing tank), utensils (e.g., the shovel), and factory site 
have not been assessed. 
 
Moreover, microbiological experiments revealed that Bacillus cereus and its spores are 
naturally present in the BSG before fermentation at M4F, emphasizing the need for 
stricter hygiene measures. 
 
To minimize microbiological cross-contamination between batches, the disinfection of 
transport boxes and the shovel before BSG collection should be improved by incorporat-
ing a more effective sanitation agent, such as 2.4% sodium hypochlorite, which has 
proven efficacy against B. cereus spores. Additionally, disinfection with sodium hypo-
chlorite should also be implemented at M4F processing stations before handling BSG 
upon arrival to further reduce contamination risks.  
 
M4F’s laboratory is divided into two designated areas: one room dedicated to spore so-
lution preparation, substrate handling, and storage, and another room housing the bio-
reactor, its control systems, and additional incubators. While this separation helps 
streamline the workflow, it also underscores the need for strict hygiene protocols, partic-
ularly in the bioreactor area. 
 
The bioreactor is surrounded by cables and a laptop, making thorough external cleaning 
challenging. Additionally, the absence of a filter in its air circulation system increases the 
risk of contamination through uncontrolled airflow. The incubators also create an envi-
ronment conducive to microbial proliferation, emphasizing the need for continuous and 
effective cleaning measures to maintain hygiene and prevent cross-contamination. 
 
Finally, personal hygiene procedures should be reinforced in the laboratory for both em-
ployees and visitors. Emphasizing the mandatory use of lab coats, proper handwashing 
and disinfection before and after handling materials, and the removal of jewelry and other 
accessories can help minimize contamination risks. Implementing clear hygiene guide-
lines and regular training sessions would further enhance awareness and compliance 
with these best practices.

According to the information presented in Table 12, the most severe and life threatening 
pathogens identified in the hazard analysis of BSG were the bacteria C. botulinum 
(grade 4), STEC (grade 4), and L. monocytogenes (grade 3), as well as hepatitis A virus 
(grade 3). For all these cases, symptoms are severe and complications in the absence 
of appropriate treatment can lead to severe consequences, including mortality. 
 
In contrast, B. cereus and C. perfringens, both spore-forming bacteria, were classified 
as mild severity (grade 1) since the diseases they cause are typically mild and self-limit-
ing over a short period. On the other hand, their occurrences (Table 13) were rated as 
high (grade 3 for B. cereus) and moderate (grade 2 for C. perfringens) due to the resili-
ence of their spores, which can withstand harsh conditions and persist in food. 
 
B. cereus was considered of higher occurrence than C. perfringens due to its higher 
probability of being found in grains such as barley. Moreover, despite the theoretical 
basis of the initial hazard analysis, microbiological testing of BSG from Slowburn re-
vealed the natural presence of B. cereus in all tested samples, underscoring its very high 
occurrence in M4F’s processing line. 
 
Similar to C. perfringens, L. monocytogenes was considered to have a moderate occur-
rence. Although it does not form resistant spores and is susceptible to heat treatments, 
it can persist on surfaces and equipment due to its ability to form biofilms that adhere to 
food contact surfaces and hard-to-reach areas (Matthews et al., 2017). This biofilm for-
mation makes L. monocytogenes particularly difficult to eliminate in processing plants.  
 
C. botulinum is also a spore-forming bacterium and could, therefore, be considered 
higher occurrence based on the selected criteria. However, its growth and toxin produc-
tion require an anaerobic environment (FDA, 2012; Matthews et al., 2017). As a result, 
its occurrence in barley and other cereals was classified as low probability. 
 
Nevertheless, C. botulinum may become a greater concern during the further processing 
of fermented BSG into hybrid sausages. This risk is particularly relevant if the final sau-
sages are vacuum-packaged for storage, as this could create an anaerobic environment 
conducive to pathogen growth and toxin production. Therefore, strict control measures, 
including proper chilled storage conditions, should be considered to mitigate this risk in 
the intended product made from fermented BSG. 
 
Other bacteria such as S. aureus, STEC,  Salmonella spp., Campylobacter spp. and was 
also classified as low-occurrence (grade 1) due to its not common natural presence in 
grains such as barley and its susceptibility to heat inactivation, which significantly re-
duces its risk in BSG. 
 
It is valid to highlight that pathogens such as S. aureus, Hepatitis A virus, and norovirus 
are not directly associated with key food sources such as barley grains, but they can be 
easily transmitted through contaminated individuals, increasing the risk of cross-contam-
ination. However, they can be controlled with strict hygiene practices and good manu-

facturing practices, reducing their likelihood of occurrence to low probability. Thus, en-
suring proper handling, sanitation, and employee hygiene is critical in minimizing the risk 
of contamination in the processing environment.

5.6 Chemical risk assessment 
In Section 3.6, the primary chemical hazards potentially present in barley grains, malt, 
and both raw and fermented BSG were identified. These include mycotoxins, Bacillus 
cereus emetic toxin, heavy metals, acrylamide, and pesticides. On the matter of FCM 
discussed in Section 5.4.1, no risk of migrant transfer from contact materials is expected. 
This statement is based on factors such as the composition of BSG, which is a solid 
==PAGE:56==
 
56 
 
 
material low in lipids, and the contact time and temperature, which, particularly for plas-
tics, remain below critical thresholds that could promote the migration of monomers, ad-
ditives, or other compounds. 
 
Mycotoxins are commonly associated with grains stored in warm, humid conditions, such 
as BSG – which is stored and transported at high moisture and around 50-70 °C before 
arriving at M4F. While Rhizopus oryzae used in solid-state fermentation at M4F is not a 
producer of mycotoxins (Martín-Miguélez et al., 2025), proper environmental controls are 
essential to prevent contamination by other molds that do produce them. Uncontrolled 
moisture or temperature can lead to such contamination.  
 
Recent RASFF notifications showed that Ochratoxin A was detected in barley products 
across the EU in 2023, posing a potential risk. Additionally, the GEMS/Food database 
confirmed the presence of Ochratoxin A and Aflatoxins in barley samples from 2020, 
although the concentrations were significantly below the maximum permitted levels. 
 
Process contaminants such as acrylamide are most likely to occur in BSG derived from 
roasted malts, as high roasting temperatures can promote its formation due to the pres-
ence of the amino acid asparagine and reducing sugars in barley. However, no RASFF 
notifications related to acrylamide in malt and/or BSG were reported from 2020 to 2024. 
In a recent study, Gutiérrez-Barrutia et al. (2022) confirmed that acrylamide levels in 
BSG remained unchanged after extrusion at 122.5 °C, staying significantly below the 
benchmark level for cereal-based products. 
 
Heavy metals are not intentionally added to crops but are often found in grains such as 
barley due to their widespread presence in the environment, including water, soil, and 
air. Nevertheless, no RASFF notifications related to heavy metals in barley and/or BSG 
were reported from 2020 to 2024. According to GEMS/Food database, the presence of 
cadmium and lead were attested in barley samples in 2020, but in concentrations con-
siderably lower than the maximum levels.  
 
Pesticides are intentionally applied to barley crops to protect against diseases and infes-
tations. The latest Foodwatch report (2023) revealed that around 40% of tested barley 
samples contained at least one pesticide, though most residues were within the MRLs. 
Similarly, the 2022 European Union Report on pesticide residues in food found that 47% 
of samples contained detectable pesticide residues. However, 98.4% of these samples 
complied with MRLs, and only 1.6% exceeded the legal limits (EFSA, 2024e). The two 
most recent RASFF notifications (from 2021 and 2023) on this matter stated the pres-
ence of chlorpyrifos in barley grains, even though the contaminant is currently banned in 
the EU. 
 
Exposure levels of the mentioned contaminants were estimated. For that, relevant con-
centration data on contaminants in barley was collected. Table 15 presents the data ob-
tained from the GEMS/Food database, RASFF Window, and/or scientific publications 
from 2020 to 2024 across the European Union. It is valid to highlight that it was selected 
the maximum reported concentration values for each chemical hazard, and just six of 
the twelve identified hazards were found in barley and reported.

In terms of quality indicators, coagulase-positive Staphylococci were not detected in any 
of the samples. However, coliform bacteria were observed in one batch (BSG H). The 
colony-forming units followed the dilution rate, suggesting that the bacteria either origi-
nated from the raw material supplied to the brewery or resulted from cross-contamination 
during brewing or prior to collection for M4F. After collection, the untreated BSG did not 
come into contact with handlers until the microbiological assay, which was conducted 
under strict adherence to good hygiene practices. 
 
Regarding pathogens, the presence of Listeria monocytogenes, Salmonella spp., and 
Clostridium perfringens was not detected in any of the tested samples. However, it is 
possible to notice that Bacillus cereus is naturally present in all samples, indicating that 
it most likely comes from the barley malt sourced to the brewery.  
 
The total count of B. cereus in log CFU/g ranged from 1.7 to 3.7, while spore count 
ranged from 1.4 to 3.5. The similarity between these counts indicates the heat treatments 
applied during mashing (60-65 °C) and mashing-out (80 °C) and the freeze-thaw cycle 
appear insufficient to eliminate B. cereus; instead, some of these conditions may pro-
mote its sporulation and subsequent germination. 
 
As mentioned in Section 3.5.1.1, most foodborne outbreaks associated with Bacillus ce-
reus are linked to concentrations exceeding 5 log CFU/g (EFSA, 2016). However, EFSA 
scientific opinion does not establish a critical limit for B. cereus across all food categories. 
Concentrations lower than 5 log CFU/g may be acceptable, especially if the food is sub-
jected to further processing or heating prior to consumption. 
 
Although the BSG in this study is intended for fermentation and subsequent processing 
into hybrid sausages and bakery products, the natural presence of B. cereus in the raw 
material poses a potential food safety risk. Consequently, further research is essential to 
evaluate bacterial growth during fermentation and its effect on the quality and safety of 
the intermediate product (i.e., fermented BSG).

Figure 10 illustrates the anaerobic mesophilic total count for the fresh BSG (0 BSG), the 
fresh BSG after the addition of the Rhizopus oryzae spore solution (0 BSG+RO), and the 
fermented samples at various time points. The TSA plates were incubated under anaer-
obic conditions to minimize the overgrowth of Rhizopus oryzae, ensuring more accurate 
measurement of the anaerobic mesophilic microbial load. Despite these efforts, mold 
growth was still observed on the TSA plates, even under anaerobic conditions. 
 
The fresh BSG replicates in Figure 10 showed log CFU/g values of 6.1, 5.5, and 5.4, 
resulting in an average of 5.7 ± 0.4 log CFU/g. When compared to the average aerobic 
count of 4.6 ± 0.9 log CFU/g for defrosted BSG samples discussed in Section 5.7, this 
higher value for the fresh (i.e., not previously frozen) batch suggests a substantial micro-
bial load under anaerobic conditions, likely even higher under aerobic incubation. This 
result aligns with expectations, as the freeze-thaw cycle may reduce or eliminate micro-
organisms that are sensitive to temperature stress. 
 
The anaerobic mesophilic total count remains relatively stable during the first two days 
of fermentation, with log CFU/g values ranging from 4.7 to 5.6. However, from the third 
day onward, these values increase, reaching 7.1 to 8.7 log CFU/g by the final day of 
fermentation. This rise is expected, as fermented foods typically exhibit high microbial 
loads due to the proliferation of fermentative microorganisms. Fermented foods typically 
exhibit microbial loads ranging from 6 to 9 log CFU/g (Rezac et al., 2018), though this 
can vary significantly depending on the product type, fermentation process, and any sub-
sequent treatments.

To better assess whether the increased microbial load impacts on the safety and quality 
of the fermented BSG, hygiene indicators such as E. coli and coliform levels were ana-
lyzed, as shown in Figure 11. E. coli and coliform are commonly used as an indicator of 
fecal contamination and overall hygiene during food processing. While Commission Reg-
ulation (EC) No 2073/2005 does not set specific limits for coliform bacteria as a group, 
their presence is often monitored in order to identify lapses in GHP and prevent the pro-
liferation of pathogenic bacteria. 
 
Notably, no E. coli colonies were detected in any of the samples, with only coliform bac-
teria observed as a group, particularly from the third day of fermentation onward. Coli-
form colonies detected in sample FBSG D4 AM 1 (i.e., replicate 1 after 92 hours of fer-
mentation) were classified as ‘too many to count’ (TMTC); for graphing purposes in Fig-
ure 11, an estimated value of 1000 colonies at the 10-3 dilution was used. 
 
The elevated coliform levels observed on the final day of fermentation (i.e., 5.4 and 5.8 
log CFU/g), combined with the increased total anaerobic microbial load, suggest that to 
maintain the safety and quality of the fermented BSG, the fermentation process should 
ideally be limited to a maximum of three days.  
 
As shown in Figure 12, during the fermentation process, Rhizopus oryzae – initially in-
troduced at a concentration of 6 log CFU/g – showed a decline on the first day, followed 
by an increase on the second day. By the final day of fermentation, its population stabi-
lized at 7.3–7.4 log CFU/g. One possible explanation for the initial reduction in fungal 
count might be the presence of antifungal compounds in the substrate, potentially pro-
duced by naturally occurring Bacillus cereus. For instance, Iturin A – a cyclic lipopeptide 
synthesized by several Bacillus species – exhibits strong antifungal activity and acts as 
a biosurfactant that disrupts fungal membranes (Besson, 1987; Geissler et al., 2019). 
 
Figures 13 and 14 illustrate the behavior of the naturally occurring Bacillus cereus total 
count and spores, respectively. Unlike the frozen-thawed BSG analyzed in the initial ex-
periments, the fresh BSG collected at Slowburn and immediately tested at M4F did not 
exhibit a similar correlation between the total count and spores. Furthermore, the total 
microbial load of B. cereus was higher in the fresh samples compared to the frozen-
thawed samples. 
 
The fresh BSG used for fermentation had total Bacillus counts ranging from 3.6 to 3.7 
log CFU/g and spore counts between 1.9 and 2.4 log CFU/g. One possible explanation 
for this is the period following the mashing-out process, which occurs at approximately 
80 °C. Afterward, the BSG batch remains in the tank as the temperature gradually de-
creases. During this cooling phase, spores that may have formed due to the high tem-
perature could have had time to germinate before microbiological analysis was per-
formed in the laboratory. 
 
Additionally, only one sample of fresh BSG was tested, compared to eight samples of 
frozen BSG. Therefore, further analyses using different batches of fresh BSG are nec-
essary before drawing definitive conclusions. 
 
==PAGE:66==
 
66 
 
 
Bacillus cereus spores remained stable at levels ranging from 2.1 to 2.8 log CFU/g during 
the first 26 hours of fermentation. In contrast, the total count showed a significant in-
crease during the first day, reaching a peak average level of 4.9±0.2 log CFU/g. This 
was followed by a decline during the second and third days, with a minimum average 
level of 2.3±0.3 log CFU/g observed after 74 hours of fermentation. 
 
When compared to the behavior of Rhizopus oryzae (Figure 12), these results suggest 
an initial lag phase for fungal growth, whereas Bacillus sensu lato began growing imme-
diately. However, from the second day onward, this trend reverses: Rhizopus oryzae 
growth increases and stabilizes, while Bacillus cereus total cell counts decline, possibly 
due to the end of Rhizopus lag phase, competition for the same substrate, or the pro-
duction of organic acids during fermentation. 
 
Interestingly, by the fourth day of fermentation, a resurgence of Bacillus was observed, 
with final concentrations reaching 4.0±1.5 log CFU/g. These findings corroborate with 
the suggestion that, under current raw material conditions, fermentation should be limited 
to three days to ensure product safety and quality.  
 
5.8.2 Correlation between process parameters and microbial load 
During the fermentation process, chemical parameters such as substrate pH and tem-
perature, as well as ambient conditions including temperature, relative humidity, and ox-
ygen levels, were monitored. The bioreactor is equipped with an automated control sys-
tem to regulate temperature, airflow, and relative humidity, intended to ensure optimal 
fermentation conditions. However, the substrate’s temperature and pH are influenced by 
the fermentation process itself, as the microbial activity and production of metabolites 
lead to dynamic changes in these parameters over time. The recorded and measured 
values are presented in Figures 15 to 17.

slightly around 21%. In Figure 16, the temperature profile during fermentation is shown. 
Initially, fermentation began at a lower temperature, ranging between 20 and 25 °C. By

The comparison in the temperature profile with fungal growth in Figure 12 reveals that 
the increase of temperature from the second day onward significantly influenced fermen-
tation progress. The growth of Rhizopus oryzae shows a substantial rise from the second 
day, indicating that the initial temperature on day 1 was below its optimal range, which 
delayed fermentation development. As fermentation progressed and temperatures in-
creased, Rhizopus oryzae growth accelerated, highlighting the critical role of tempera-
ture in shaping the fermentation outcomes.  
 
Another notable observation is the correlation between pH variation and the progression 
of fermentation. Rhizopus oryzae fermentation can result in the production of organic 
acids, such as lactic acid, fumaric acid, and succinic acid, depending on the substrate 
and fermentation conditions. These acids contribute to a reduction in the substrate’s pH. 
On the other hand, Bacillus cereus can either decrease or increase the medium’s pH, 
depending on its metabolic activity and environment stresses, such as the availability of 
nutrients in the substrate (Desriac et al., 2013). 
 
Figure 17 illustrates the pH dynamics throughout the fermentation process. Initially, the 
substrate pH decreases significantly, from 5.66±0.01 to 3.81±0.04 during the first day of 
fermentation, likely due to the production of organic acids. However, from the second 
day onward, the pH begins to rise, reaching a final value of 6.22±0.17. This pH increase 
may indicate a shift in metabolic activity of Bacillus cereus. 
 
As shown in Table 10, sugars make up approximately 3% of the dry weight composition 
of BSG, while proteins account for about 18%. One possible explanation for the observed 
pH increase in the substrate is that, as fermentable sugars become limited, Bacillus ce-
reus may shift to its arginine deiminase (ADI) metabolic pathway. This shift leads to the 
production of ammonia and other alkaline byproducts through amino acid degradation 
(Desriac et al., 2013). This suggests a dynamic interaction between the metabolites pro-
duced by Bacillus and Rhizopus oryzae, which influences both the pH and the overall 
fermentation process.

opment into mycelium is clearly visible on the surface of the substrate in Figure 18, which 
is characterized by its distinctive white coloration. This development is responsible for 
providing a firmer structure to the BSG substrate.

However, as shown in Figure 19, the mycelium did not fully penetrate the internal inter-
stices of the BSG grains as expected under optimal fermentation conditions. The black 
spots observed amidst the mycelium in Figure 18 are attributed to the formation of Rhi-
zopus oryzae spores. The significant presence of spores further suggests that the fer-
mentation process did not achieve optimal performance. 
 
Additionally, high coliform levels – reaching up to 5.8 log CFU/g – were observed on the 
fourth day of fermentation, along with an increased total anaerobic microbial load of up 
to 7.7 log CFU/g. These elevated levels indicate a potential decline in both the quality 
and safety of the final product. 
 
Two main factors were identified as limitations to optimal fermentation: the high microbial 
load in the raw material, especially due to Bacillus cereus natural presence, and the 
lower temperature during the first day of fermentation. Both factors compromised fungal 
growth, particularly in the early stages of fermentation. Due to the suboptimal tempera-
ture, Rhizopus oryzae experienced delayed growth, allowing B. cereus to proliferate 
more rapidly. 
 
One approach to improving both fermentation performance and the safety of the final 
fermented product is to apply a heat treatment to the BSG prior to fermentation. This 
reduces the microbial load, minimizing interactions with Rhizopus oryzae growth. Vari-
ous heat treatment methods, such as boiling, steaming, and autoclaving, could be ap-
plied to BSG. However, it is important to consider that very high temperatures may de-
grade macronutrients, reducing the nutritional value of the product and potentially alter-
ing its texture and functionality. 
 
5.8.4 Heat inactivation of spore-forming pathogens 
Based on D-values of Bacillus cereus and Clostridium perfringens, Byrne et al. (2006) 
studied the thermal inactivation of vegetative cells and spores of both bacteria in pork 
luncheon roll. Their study concluded that a heat treatment at 70 °C for 12 seconds and 
1.3 minutes achieved a 6-log reduction of B. cereus and C. perfringens vegetative cells, 
respectively. To achieve an equivalent reduction for spores, heating was required for 36 
seconds at 105 °C and 110 °C for B. cereus and C. perfringens, respectively (Byrne et 
al., 2006). 
 
Another study (Akhtar et al., 2009) suggests that the most efficient method to inactivate 
C. perfringens spores in poultry meat consists of an initial heat treatment (80 °C, 10 min) 
to activate spores for germination, followed by cooling to 55 °C in about 20 min, and 
further incubation at the same temperature for 15 min for spore germination. Finally, the 
inactivation of germinated spores is carried out by pressure-assisted thermal processing 
(586 MPa at 73 °C for 10 min). 
 
It is also valid to underline that C. perfringens can usually stand heat stress better than 
B. cereus, as shown in Byrne et al. (2006) research. However, the heat resistance of 
spore-forming pathogens can vary considerably depending on their strains and the me-
dium (Labbe & Juneja, 2017).

5.9 HACCP plan 
5.9.1 Selection of most significant hazards 
As shown in Table 14, five of the ten identified hazards were classified as having mod-
erate significance, either due to their severity as foodborne pathogens or their likelihood 
of occurrence. These hazards include Listeria monocytogenes (grade 6), Clostridium 
botulinum (grade 4), STEC (grade 4), Bacillus cereus (grade 3), and Hepatitis A virus 
(grade 3). 
 
One limitation of the selected method is its tendency to prioritize the most severe patho-
gens, even when their occurrence is relatively low. This can misrepresent the actual risk 
posed by these pathogens in the process line. C. botulinum, for instance, can cause 
severe disease. However, as an anaerobic bacterium, it requires a low-oxygen environ-
ment for growth, and its spores can be effectively inactivated by thermal processing (Mat-
thews et al., 2017). This significantly reduces its risk of occurrence and, consequently, 
its overall significance. 
 
According to the Codex Alimentarius (CAC/GL 30-1999), risk characterization is defined 
as “the process of determining the qualitative and/or quantitative estimation, including 
attendant uncertainties, of the probability of occurrence and severity of known or poten-
tial adverse health effects in a given population based on hazard identification, hazard 
characterization, and exposure assessment.” In this project, microbiological risk charac-
terization was assessed qualitatively, assigning severity and occurrence grades based 
on literature evaluation rather than real quantitative data. As a result, the assigned oc-
currence grades may not fully reflect the actual prevalence of hazards in the studied 
scenario. 
 
Qualitative risk assessments are commonly used for screening risks and determining 
whether further investigation is needed (FAO and WHO, 2021). They are particularly 
useful in preliminary risk management activities, such as the development of a HACCP 
plan. Therefore, the selection of the most significant microbiological hazards in the M4F 
process must be based on both the initial hazard analysis and characterization discussed 
in Section 5.5, as well as the microbiological analyses presented in Sections 5.7 and 5.8. 
 
For example, B. cereus is a genuine concern for M4F, as it was naturally present in all 
analyzed BSG samples used as raw material. Therefore, its significance should be con-
sidered even greater than that of L. monocytogenes. It was observed that, during fer-
mentation, Bacillus cereus sensu lato achieved microbial levels exceeding 5 log CFU/g 
in some cases (Figure 13), which, according to EFSA scientific opinion, is sufficient to 
pose a risk of cereulide production and potentially lead to emetic syndrome (EFSA, 
2016). 
 
While L. monocytogenes was not detected in BSG, its ability to form adherent biofilms in 
the process line and the potential severity of its disease justify its inclusion among sig-
nificant hazards. Other pathogens targeted in the HACCP plan include C. botulinum and 
STEC, primarily due to the possible severity of their associated diseases and, particularly 
for C. botulinum, due to the very low infective dose of its toxin, as shown in Table 12.

In the following HACCP plan, all identified microbiological and chemical hazards will be 
addressed, demonstrating how their presence and growth can be controlled or eliminated 
through appropriate measures. However, it is important to emphasize that the primary 
focus of the HACCP plan is on the pathogens highlighted in this section.

The first control point in the HACCP plan for M4F is the visual inspection conducted 
during the portioning of fresh BSG, following its collection at Slowburn. This OPRP aims 
to reduce the likelihood of foreign bodies, such as metal fragments and stones. While 
more advanced methods, such as electromagnetic metal detectors and X-ray screening, 
offer greater precision in detecting physical hazards, they are also more costly. Given 
that M4F is still optimizing its production process at the pilot scale, visual inspection by 
trained employees remains the most practical and cost-effective approach at this stage. 
 
The second OPRP in the production process involves separating raw material samples 
for chemical analysis. This step aims to minimize the risk of chemical hazards while en-
suring compliance with expected safety and quality standards. Monitoring should be 
based on the maximum allowable levels established by EU regulations or suggested in 
the Codex Alimentarius (CXS 193-1995). In cases of non-compliance, corrective actions 
– such as product recall, disposal, or switching BSG supplier – may be necessary. These 
measures are crucial, as the identified chemical hazards are persistent in the product 
and cannot be reduced or eliminated through additional processing steps in the produc-
tion line. 
 
It is important to note that the pesticide chlorpyrifos was not included in the HACCP plan, 
as its use has been banned since 2020 (Eurofins, 2024). Consequently, no maximum 
residue level has been assigned to its concentration in food, and no residues are ex-
pected in the raw materials sourced for M4F. Similarly, EU regulations do not specify 
maximum allowable levels for cereulide. However, by effectively killing or reducing B. 
cereus in the product to safe levels, the risk of cereulide production can also be mitigated 
(Ceuppens et al., 2013). 
 
The chosen CCP for the process line is a heat treatment at high temperature – i.e., 
120 °C for 15 minutes. This step can be carried out in an autoclave or other suitable 
high-temperature processing equipment, and it is essential to monitor whether the core 
temperature of the BSG bags reaches the critical limit of 110 °C. This CCP was chosen 
because two of the main targeted microorganisms are spore-forming bacteria. Other-
wise, a heat treatment at a lower temperature – for example, 90 °C for 15 minutes – 
would most likely be enough to eliminate the possible pathogens. 
 
As mentioned in Section 5.8.4, bacterial spores are highly resistant to inactivation, and 
various methods have been explored in recent years. Some studies suggest that the 
most effective approach to eliminating spore-forming bacteria involves initially promoting 
sporulation, then stimulating spore germination, and finally applying a targeted inactiva-
tion treatment (Akhtar et al., 2009). However, considering that the two spore-forming 
bacteria targeted in this HACCP plan (i.e., B. cereus and C. botulinum) are also toxin 
producers, there is a risk of inducing toxin production during the time required for spore 
germination. To mitigate this risk, a rapid high-temperature heat treatment was chosen 
as the preferred control measure. 
 
Another important consideration is that the complete elimination of a pathogen is not 
always necessary; in many cases, reducing its concentration to an established safe level 
==PAGE:77==
 
 
 
77 
 
is sufficient. For instance, a boiling step for 15 minutes might be enough to lower patho-
gen levels to a safe threshold. However, further studies are needed to evaluate the ef-
fectiveness of such alternative methods and determine whether they offer a more cost-
efficient approach while maintaining food safety. 
 
After heat treatment, the pathogen-free BSG must be cooled to room temperature before 
the addition of Rhizopus oryzae spore suspension, as high temperatures would also in-
activate the fungus. Once cooled, the solid-state fermentation can proceed in the biore-
actor, which must be effectively cleaned before and after fermentation. 
 
The final control point (OPRP 3) in the processing line is chilled storage at a targeted 
temperature of 5 °C. This OPRP plays a crucial role in preventing microbial growth, 
thereby minimizing the risk of pathogen proliferation in the final fermented product. The 
action criterion for this stage is to maintain the refrigerator temperature at or below 7 °C, 
with continuous monitoring to prevent extreme deviations that could compromise the 
safety and quality of the final product. 
 
6. Conclusion and perspectives 
This study provided a comprehensive assessment of microbiological, chemical, and 
physical hazards associated with the solid-state fermentation of BSG at Myco4Food. By 
integrating qualitative risk assessment, microbiological analyses, and HACCP principles, 
key safety concerns were identified, including the potential proliferation of foodborne 
pathogens including Bacillus cereus, Listeria monocytogenes, Clostridium botulinum, 
STEC, and hepatitis A virus, as well as risks related to aflatoxin contamination and for-
eign bodies. 
 
While the qualitative risk assessment provided valuable insights into potential hazards, 
it also underscored the need for quantitative validation of the pathogen’s prevalence in 
BSG samples through microbiological analyses. Among the identified hazards, microbi-
ological results confirmed that B. cereus was naturally present in all tested BSG samples, 
with microbial load fluctuations observed throughout fermentation. 
 
Microbial dynamics during BSG fermentation were investigated, with a particular focus 
on Rhizopus oryzae and B. cereus. The findings revealed a complex interplay between 
fungal and bacterial populations. Initially, B. cereus sensu lato exhibited rapid growth – 
reaching a peak of 4.9±0.2 log CFU/g – while R. oryzae experienced a short lag phase. 
As fermentation progressed, R. oryzae growth stabilized, while B. cereus populations 
declined to 2.3±0.3 log CFU/g by the end of the third day – potentially due to substrate 
competition or the accumulation of organic acids. However, B. cereus and other micro-
organisms, including coliforms, showed an increase by the fourth day of fermentation, 
indicating that, under the current raw material conditions (i.e., high microbial load), fer-
mentation should not exceed three days to maintain product safety and quality. 
 
To mitigate microbiological, chemical, and physical hazards, various control strategies 
were proposed. A high-temperature heat treatment at 120 °C for 15 minutes was identi-
==PAGE:78==
 
78 
 
 
fied as a CCP for reducing spore-forming bacteria while preventing toxin production. Ad-
ditionally, key OPRPs, such as chilled storage at 5 °C, raw material inspection, and 
chemical monitoring, were recommended to minimize contamination risks. Alongside the 
HACCP plan, the implementation of PRPs – including hygienic design of the laboratory 
and bioreactor, good hygiene practices, and the selection of appropriate food contact 
materials – was advised as the foundation of the food safety risk management program. 
 
While this research provides a strong foundation for microbiological risk management in 
the M4F process, further studies are necessary to refine control strategies and assess 
the effectiveness of alternative, cost-efficient control methods. Future research should 
focus on validating hazard reduction techniques proposed in the HACCP plan, optimizing 
fermentation conditions, and ensuring that HACCP measures remain adaptable to differ-
ent production scales. 
 
By integrating scientific risk assessment with robust food safety management and pro-
cess control, this study contributes to the advancement of safe and sustainable food 
fermentation practices. Ensuring food safety in innovative processes, such as M4F solid-
state fermentation of BSG, not only protects public health but also supports the viability 
of sustainable food solutions that repurpose valuable by-products.
