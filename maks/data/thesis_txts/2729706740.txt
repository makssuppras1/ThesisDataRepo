Abstract
The employment of spatial variability in fisheries management is exiguous and local
migration rates between sub-areas within fish stocks is seldom accounted for. Stock
assessment models frequently apply a single Fcap across the entire spatial domain of fish
communities and neglect localised population dynamics. In particular, sandeel (Ammodytes
marinus) are a highly variable species which yield inconsistent annual spawning stock
biomass, catch and recruitment. To address this issue, Fcap is distributed across a
disaggregated spatial domain to sandeel in the North Sea (SA1r). Using an existing
age-based operating model this thesis configured the population dynamic parameters
inclusive of spatial variation using a distributed F of 0.5 over three spatially explicit areas.
The results forecast SSB, R, catch and distributed F scenarios over the next twenty years. All
scenarios were above Blim from the latest ICES advice and produced relatively high SSB and
catch rates. Without consolidating more interdisciplinary functions within stock assessment
models, inclusive of spatial variability, future maximum sustainable yield of sandeel in the
North Sea and growth in stock assessment advice for sandeel remains unknown.

Introduction
1.1.
Sandeel in the North Sea
The sandeel (Ammodytes marinus) play a central role in the North Sea ecosystem, where they
can form great shoals at the edges of sand banks, such as at Dogger Bank within ICES area
SA1r. The small, thin, eel-like fish are known to be of great importance to higher trophic
levels in marine ecosystems i.e. prey fish for predators such as seabirds, dolphins, whales
and fish (JNCC, 2023) but also highly correlated with primary production at the lower
trophic levels (Eliasen et al., 2011). Historically speaking, Sandeel has been fished on an
industrial scale and in 2002 for example total catches were over 600,000 tonnes (ICES, 2023d).
Sandeel today is still amongst some of the most ecnomically important pelagic fisheries in the
North Sea primarily for its use for reduction purposes (i.e. oils and fish meal) (ICES, 2022c).
Spawning stock biomass, landings and net worth value has rapidly declined since early 2000s
due to increased effects of climate change and industrial fishing methods (ICES, 2018a &
Dickey-Collas et al., 2014 & Lindegren et al., 2018) but also because sandeel are extremely
sensitive to a changing environment (DEFRA, 2022). The preferred habitat for sandeel is
extremely selective since they require burial during low light conditions (night and winter),
emerging only briefly to spawn during winter (Wheeler and Du Heaume, 1969) and high
activity during the summer months is correlated with light (Winslade, 1974) and when tidal
currents are strong (Kooĳ, Scott, and Mackinson, 2008), to shoal and graze on plankton and
return to the sediments during the night (Scottish Government, 2021).
Dogger bank in the North Sea provides a large sandy substrate ranging from 20 meters deep
in the southern part and down to 60 meters further north towards the special area of
conservation in the UK and is an important spawning ground for sandeel (Engebretsen,
Haver, and Myrhaug, 2016). It is widely accepted that when sandeel reach adulthood, mixing
between banks tends to be confined to that area (Wright et al., 2019) and passive particle drift
secures long term spatially stable retention (ICES, 2018b) by bringing food right to their
doorsteps via tidal currents. Additionally, the tidal current also acts as a transport
mechanism allowing for passive larval drift (Henriksen et al., 2021). Larval drift and otolith
models are fully incorporated within the HAWG sandeel stock annex (ICES, 2018b) however
the potential exchange between different sand banks is not yet fully implemented within the
HAWG sandeel stock assessment model. Further, in the context of climate change, studies on

increased variability and temperature changes that can influence spatial distribution are well
documented yet changes in movement rates or spatial dynamics are rarely accounted for in
stock assessment models (Jacobsen et al., 2022).
With a more comprehensive knowledge of spatial population structures, scientists are now
more accepting of potential spatiotemporal mismatches within biological and ecological
processes and how that can affect the robustness of regional fisheries management (Berger
et al., 2016). For example Wright et al. (2019) uses larval transport and otolith chemistry to
discover that movement between pre-settled sandeel greater than 200km apart is seldom and
limited to length at maturity and year class and stress that biological processes should be
matched with management actions. In failing that, less productive populations may be
overfished and more productive populations may be underfished (Jardim et al., 2018 in
Wright et al., 2019).
1.2.
Aims & Objectives
The aim of this project is to simulate and project the effects of different scenarios of spatial
closures of certain sandeel banks to evaluate the effects on the stock in relation to stock
sustainability and local depletion, fishing mortality (F), area specific partial F, and spawning
stock biomass (SSB)/total stock biomass (TSB) and catch. This simulation is done under
different scenarios of recruitment (R) and migration patterns between banks.
To achieve the aim of this project, the following objectives are as follow;
• To configure population dynamic parameters in an existing age-based operating model
• To analyse population dynamics inclusive of spatial variation together with historical
sandeel data
• Investigate how spatial variation plays a vital role in the management of sandeel stocks
in the context of Brexit, located in area SA1r in the North Sea.

1.3.
Stock Identity and Distribution
The following stock identity and distribution section will highlight some of the major
historical changes to sandeel areas that have taken place during the June 2016 Benchmark
Workshop on Sandeel Stocks (WKSAND) in the North sea (ICES, 2017).
1.3.1. Stock Identity
In the North East Atlantic Sea the sandeel stock definition has undergone several revisions
over the past two decades that are primarily based on Christensen’s et al (2008) bio-physical
model of larval transport. In June 2016 WKSAND added alternative area divisions and
produced the final figure seen below (1.1) as part of the Herring Assessment Working Group
2018. Area SA1r has remained more or less unchanged during the various area revisions and
as the proceeding paragraphs will unfold why, a logical decision in the area selection of SA1r
was made to use as a study site within this report.

areas in the north sea and as seen using larval backtracking (1.2), the western part of SA1r is
more or less self-sustained with, according to ICES (2018b), a high degree of retention,

1.3.2. Demography among stock assessment areas
The 2016 WKSAND data preparation workshop suggested that since groups with different
mortality and growth parameters reflect different stocks then it should be presumed that age
and size composition should also differ between the different stocks. In a similar fashion
ICES (2018b) and Bergstad, Høines, and Jørgensen (2002) both found that there were regional
variations of weight at age and size at age and that size at age was higher in the central to
north east fishing areas where waters are deeper and warmer (ICES, 2018b). During the
WKSAND in 2016 it was agreed upon that North Sea sandeel areas should conform to the
previous figure 1.1 above making a total of 8 potentially isolated stocks. It was previously
mentioned in ICES (2018b) that in future studies, "One could be to look at recruitment
dynamics within and between areas" (P.8) using North Sea dredge and CPUE data and that is
what this report intends on exploring further with the potential of suggesting scenarios for
future fisheries management. Finally, figure 1.3 from the latest International Council for the
Exploration of the Sea (ICES) stock advice for North Sea sandeel (Ammodytes spp.) reveals
how the stocks divisions have yet been revised once again. The major changes appear to be
that Area 3b is now part of Area SA1r.

1.5.
Ecosystem Function & Trophic Interactions
1.5.1. Bottom Up Effects
Bottom up effects are a limiting factor with juvenile sandeel whereby phenology and
abundance of zooplankton and phytoplankton is strongly limited by climate changes
(Edwards and Richardson, 2004) and thus fluctuating plankton abundances will limit sandeel
growth and survival during critical periods (Frederiksen, Furness, and Wanless, 2007).
Further studies that support bottom up effects of sandeel productivity and recruitment in
area SA1r have modelled different fishing and climate scenarios and found that spring sea
surface temperature and the abundance of Calanus finmarchicus (common copepod and
component of zooplankton in the North Sea) were both highly correlated with successful
recruitment (ICES, 2018b).
1.5.2. Top Down Effects
The North Sea Sandeel are a crucial prey fish for several top predatory marine mammals,
seabirds and fish. For example, Cury et al. (2011) demonstrate how prey abundance can
become a limiting factor for productivity of several seabirds if abundance drops below
approximately one-third of the maximum prey biomass. According to commercial catch data
during August and April, catches decrease significantly which provided the initial indication
that sandeel are within their overwintering period that generally lasts for around 8 months.
==PAGE:13==
1.6. Fisheries System

During the overwintering period, sandeel are submerged in the sand are therefore, for the
most part, inaccessible to predators, especially seabirds. Sandeel are usually only disturbed
by spawning during December and January and on occasion by foraging predatory fish
(ICES, 2018b) hence why in this report, sandeel data is taken outside of the overwinter period.
When coupling the extensive sensitivity analysis of sandeel predators by Engelhard et al.
(2014) with the previous bottom up and top down effects of sandeel it is possible to find
indications of significant relationships between sandeel biomass and predator performance,
for example the Atlantic common puffin (Fratercula arctica).
1.6.
Fisheries System
1.6.1. Exploitation level
Sandeel in SA1r, in particular Dogger Bank, is one of the most heavily exploited fishing areas
in the North Sea (ICES, 2023b). Since 2011 the MSY approach has been applied and catches
have been on average been relatively lower than the period 1983-2011 ICES (2023d).
Figure 1.4: Sandeel in divisions 4.b–c, Sandeel Area 1r. Summary of the stock assessment. Assumed recruitment
for 2023 is shaded in a lighter colour (ICES, 2023d).
1.6.2. Fisheries
Germany, UK, Sweden, Norway and Denmark make up the participants of the sandeel
fishery where Denmark on average takes 73% of landings (ICES, 2018b). Geographically
speaking the sandeel fishery varies annually and seasonally where the majority takes place
from spring through summer, in the westerly part of Dogger Bank and during the third
quarter, catches tend to move further towards the easterly fishing grounds of SA1r. It was
during the 1970’s that the sandeel fishery first developed with peak landings at 1.2 million
tonnes and since then, 880 000 tonnes (average) between 2002 to 2004 and 300 000 tonnes
(average) between 2003 to 2016 (ICES, 2018b).

1.6.3. Fleet
The Danish fleet has changed considerably in its size distribution where vessels have become
fewer and larger, consisting today of approximately 100 large vessels. A similar trend can
also be seen for the Norwegian fleet. These new, larger vessels are equipped with more
advanced technology and can on average produce considerably higher yields over fewer
fishing days and visiting less potential fishing areas than traditional smaller vessels. It has
been argued that since the fishery has undergone such rapid changes in vessel size and
landings it is a concern for the lack of spatial coverage (ICES, 2022b) and how that might
affect future modeling since data from both fleet scenarios (more smaller vs less larger) is
being used in current stock assessments.
1.6.4. Areas
The fishing area of interest used in this report is within ICES division 4.b and 4.c, Sandeel
area SA1r (central and southern North Sea, Dogger Bank) (ICES, 2022c) however areas SA 2r,
3r, 4, and 6 are all potentially open to other fishing activity (ICES, 2022a).
1.7.
Current Management
The management of sandeel in the north sea falls between three major governing bodies;
Norway, EU and the UK. All three have access to the advice from ICES, however, since Brexit
there have been question marks as to how the sandeel stocks will be managed going forwards,
since as mentioned previously and as will be elaborated upon later on, the majority of the
fishing is conducted on the western part of Dogger Bank, which falls within the UK EEZ.
1.7.1. Regulations
As part of the stock annex in 2018, North Sea sandeel can only be fished with a minimum of
95% target species at <16 mm mesh size or a minimum of 90% target species including
maximum 5% of any mixture inclusive of saithe, haddock and cod at 16 to 31 mm mesh size.
Since 2011 the fishery has been regulated using TAC by area and, since 2005, commercial
sandeel fishing has been disallowed for Danish Vessels before 31 March per year. (ICES,
2018b). There is no ’until’ or after date mentioned in ICES (2018b) but it is assumed that
because sandeel are burrowing down during the winter and spring, no fishing takes place.
1.7.2. Management Advisory Methods
ICES is currently managing SA 6 using the data limited stocks approach and SA 1r, 2r, 3r and
4 are subject to analytical stock assessments. Excluding the biological sampling carried out by
ICES, the Danish fishery is the only fishery who participate in sampling despite ICES
encouraging all nations who use a monitoring TAC should be involved (ICES, 2022a). During
2014 - 2015 catches were misreported and since then it has been mandatory for Danish
fisheries to report catches from one fishing area per trip which is also encouraged to other
nations.
The current advice from ICES follows the escapement strategy, particularly for a short-lived
species in attempts to maintain SSB above MSY Btrigger post fishing. Unfortunately, however,

1.7.4. Sandeel Stock Recruitment Relationship
The SSB/R relationship used in this report depends on the assumptions in stock and
recruitment. There is a slight relationship between the two in the estimation model, however
it is so small that one can assume the two are independent estimates. The assessment model
is not spatially explicit, and therefore does not know where certain fish might be spawning
from. The ’n_age_space’ (R data file) contains observed sandeel – their relative abundance in
contrast to the estimates from the assessment model depend on the catchability from the
survey data. It is often assumed that there is a relationship between stock and recruitment in
a stock. In the case of this report, two assumptions can be made; 1) The more spawners there
are, the more recruits can be expected; 2) Density dependence: for many fish species there is
density dependence in the stock and in fisheries science it is often assumed that density
dependence stems from stock-recruitment, i.e, an infinite amount of spawners will not lead to
an infinite amount of recruits due to demographic effects such as competition, food
availability and cannibalism, especially for 1 yr old sandeel (M. V. Deurs et al., 2009). With
sandeel in particular, they are a highly variable species and very sensitive to physical changes
in the sand banks including fishing activity. It is therefore not unreasonable to assume that
the entirety of the SSB would be tied to new recruits. Further, it is also very likely that each of
the three areas in this report have their own S/R relationship since for the most part, sandeel
tend to resolve in the place that they spawned in or were moved to via tidal currents (Wright
et al., 2019 & ICES, 2018b) however, calculating the recruitment for each area in an
assessment model is beyond the scope of this project.

1.9.
Brexit
Motivating this paper are some of the environmental and fisheries developments following
Brexit, which warrants further investigation into SA1r and the sandeel population. Following
Brexit, the EU-UK Trade and Cooperation Agreement has granted EU vessels “full access to
UK water for an adjustment period lasting until 30 June 2026” at which point annual
consultations will determine EU vessel access to British waters (Think Tank, 2022). During
the adjustment period, the EU fishing quotas for several shared stocks will gradually be
reduced on an annual basis. While the 2022 written record of fisheries consultations between
the UK and the EU for sandeel continues to apply in 2023, meaning the TAC shares for UK
and EU remains 2.86% and 97.4% respectively, these are set to be re-negotiated annually
(European Commission, 2022; European Commission, 2023; Think Tank, 2022).
In March 2023, Defra, UK’s Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs announced
considerations to partially or fully ban sandeel fishing in UK Waters (DEFRA, 2023).
Underlying these considerations is the objective to benefit two of England’s most endangered
seabirds, the kittiwakes and puffins, amongst others (IUCN, 2023a; IUCN, 2023b). As part of
public consultation events, Defra presented three scenarios for public consideration: 1) Full
closure of UK EEZ SA1r, SA3r and SA4; 2) Closure of UK EEZ SA3r and SA4; 3) Closure of
UK EEZ SA1r. Over 90% of the engaged public opted for a full closure of all three areas,
which was emphasised by 138,890 signatures from a Greenpeace petition and 32,000
coordinated emails from the Royal Society of the Protection of Birds (RSPB) (DEFRA, 2023).

2
Materials and Methods
The following methodology section presents the steps required to condition and function an
operating model (OM) for the purpose of disaggregating single stock data into a series of
potential sub-populations. Forming a better understanding of the intricate relationships of
migration between sub-populations enables us to forecast more accurate scenarios of fishing
mortality under different management regimes. Data extraction, standardisation and
pre-analysis of survey and catch data is required followed by the definition and establishment
of realistic fishing, management areas and sandeel sub-population areas. Furthermore,
developing, structuring and conditioning of the OM allows us to mimic different recruitment,
fisheries and management areas, as well as different migration patterns between these. This
opens up for the potential to utilize different population dynamic parameters between areas
(e.g. Growth, Maturity, Natural mortality (M), etc.) in the future. The conditioning and
parameterisation of the OM is so the perception of the development over time in SSB by age
and area (and recruitment) aligns with the development and variability in survey CPUE by
age and area. Once this is achieved, scenario evaluation of different spatial migration and
recruitment patterns can be made including the scenario evaluation of different spatial
fishing pressures according to different EEZ or management plans. This methodology
development allows for the generic evaluation of different and potentially new areas and
accordingly the opportunity to change migration and recruitment patterns between them
and/or different spatial fishing patterns e.g. distribution of fishing mortality (F). Utilising a
successfully tuned operating model offers the opportunity to establish and implement a
generic management evaluation framework with typological scenarios.

collect and store survey data for the purpose of increasing data quality and availability by the
standardisation of formats and calculations (DATRAS, 2014b). Figure 2.1 below is the annual

• ii) To choose sandeel areas that share more than one exclusive economic zone for the

• iii) To ensure coherence with the aims and objectives in this report

2.5.
Operating Model
The OM is an age based stock assessment model inclusive of movement. Movement can be
thought of to represent migration between hypothetical sub populations of sandeel in the
North Sea. One could consider the age based stock assessment model as a sum of the three
areas (potentially more) described previously and using the above methodology, attempt to
disentangle that sum into a spatial, aggregated operating model. For example, the current
stock assessment assumes a variety of parameters that are based on historical fishing data,
biological and ecological data and natural mortality. The total data that goes into the
assessment model is then summed across all spaces to represent one stock. The configuration
is to then take both models and attempt to fit them together so that the OM can be used to
make scenarios of predicted of F, Spawning Stock Biomass (SSB), Recruitment (R), and Catch
in numbers in a given time period (C). To achieve a successful fit the OM must be
conditioned as well as possible to mimic the current stock assessment model that is provided
by ICES. When choosing a combination of movement parameters the number of spatial
variants (i.e. defined geographical area) can easily be changed in the OM and are determined
by the number of spatial variants in the CPUE data. The OM fits three interlinked spatial
variants to the output from an aged based stock assessment model. The current assessment
model used for sandeel in the North Sea is an age based stock assessment model. The
assessment model is used as guidance for fisheries since it is the best to our knowledge of the
actual sandeel stock size for the whole of area SA1r. For example if it is assumed that we have
1 million recruits in area 1r, those recruits could potentially come from more than 1
disaggregated spawning group which in this case reflects the 3 different areas used in this
study. The OM uses a logistics function which is the maximum value (i.e. a higher age) of a
saturating function. This function will not have much effect on the recruits (Age 0). The
theory behind it being that new spawns are unable to swim over long distances when they
are smaller in size however in some cases, currents could drag new recruits to different areas
and recruitment is based on SSB within the particular spatial area.

2.7. SSB/R Relationship
26
2.7.
SSB/R Relationship
It was discussed that for the OM stock recruitment relationship a hockey stick (using
segmented regression in R) could be fixed to the data points and the Blim would be the
’break point’ of that hockey stick. If the curve is a dome shape, it suggests there is carrying
capacity (accounting more for a density dependant (cannibalism) regulation in the stock). If
the SSB becomes very large, the sandeel are potentially cannibalising the offspring or in many
cases where there are is lack of spawning habitats, the curve starts to flatten out.
If it cannot be determined whether to fit a Ricker or Beverton Holt, a hockey stick can be
chosen where, if the curve runs below the change in the slope (i.e. Blim) then the status of the
stock is heading in an unsustainable direction but above that point, any SSB is going to
produce a good recruitment. The hockey stick can also be used if it is thought that the
environment is much more important than the SSB. For sandeel in the north sea whom are a
short lived species, it is well documented that the environment is very important for their
survival and a relationship between SSB and recruitment is rarely seen (M. v. Deurs et al.,
2023). Alternatively, a Ricker model was fit to the stock recruitment relationship and it
appeared to fit better that the hockey stick.

ages one and two only, divided into the three areas. The OM gives the maximum fishing
mortality so it is a saturated curve and Fbar in every year is set to F 1 and 2 (Ages) only. This is
because in the case where a fishing mortality is inclusive of all age groups that are potentially
not fished very much it might not be well represented. For example the zero groups that are
not recruited to the fishery because of selectivity or it could be the older age groups or where
there are so few individuals. If for example in biological sampling, the fish that are sampled
are very few and 4 or 5 year old’s by coincidence then the fishing mortality would be very
high for those age groups and that is not representative of the fishing mortality on the stock.
Instead the OM uses the Fbar as the fishing mortality on age groups that are most abundant
in the stock and for example with short lived species such as sandeel and norway pout, the 1
and 2 groups are used to calculate their fishing mortality because they are fully recruited to
the fishery and they are the most abundant meaning it is a more robust estimate. It is also a
way to summarise the fishing mortality across all the age groups and each age group has a
slightly different fishing mortality in every year. The reason for this is because if you have an
area that is not fished at all, then the population level fishing mortality becomes quite low. In
the stock assessment an equal distribution of F within the three areas is assumed and known
as the fishing mortality, (not the extraction). It is the proportion of the stock, that is taken out
or has died due to fishing and is also proportional to the effort (ICES, 2018b). This report will
run scenarios of the distribution of F over the three chosen areas that sums up to a total Fbar
of 0.5. This is an assumption because if there is a very skewed distribution of the stock
between the areas, the F could be highly variable between the areas. For example, in an
extreme scenario where the full stock is distributed in the UK area with an Fcap of 0.5 and at
the same time a zero F is used in areas EU1 and EU2 then then the result will be far from the
Fcap because it is highly likely that not all of the fish are in the UK area. Therefore Fcap of 0.5
assumes a relatively equal distribution of the stock if it should be sustainable according to the
overall Fcap of 0.5 from the current assessment advise (ICES, 2023d). If the F is raised above
that number, there is a >5% chance that the stock will crash however that is not assuming any
area division and the divisions of Fcap are essentially Fcap divided by three. This
assumption can easily be checked by plotting the exploitation rate (catch divided by the
exploitable biomass). If that is in equilibrium, then that is the same as the fishing mortality
and if not it will give a different number. In regards to the divisions of F, it makes sense to
have a higher fishing mortality in area EU 2 than EU 3 because EU 2 has higher biomass and
that is where the fishing will take place first to fill their quota. The purpose of setting the
choosing the right area and how it is distributed is for advising area specific management
where the Total Allowable Catch (TAC) can be set according to the area specific Fcap. A
baseline must be devised in order to simulate further scenarios relative to that baseline. To
achieve a baseline, effort data can be used to see what the distribution of effort is within the
three selected areas in ths report. For example as seen in figure 7.3, there is approximately
less than half of the effort in area EU 1 as there is in area UK and very low effort in area EU 2.
If business as usual continues, there will generally always be a low distributed F in area EU 2.
To move forwards with the business as usual scenario, future scenarios can function to scale
with the baseline e.g. double the F in area UK than in area EU 1 and low in area EU 2. This
can be justified by the effort data on how area 1r (SA1r) has been fished historically.

4
Discussion
The outcome of this research has provided the background for the configuration of an aged
based stock assessment model that takes spatial variability into consideration when
forecasting future sandeel catch and SSB in SA1r, for the purpose of projecting the effects of
distributed fishing pressure in relation to maximum sustainable yield on a single stock. The
research set out to;
• Investigate how spatial variation plays a vital role in management of sandeel stocks
following Brexit.
• Configuring population dynamic parameters in an existing age-based operating model.
• Analysing population dynamics inclusive of spatial variation in the context of historical
sandeel data.
The following section will discuss the implications of the results and provide
recommendations for further research.
4.1.
Investigating the initial data
Section 2 covered the preparation associated with the development and running of the OM
used in this paper. This initial step informed the area selection process by utilising both the
CPUE data and the hypothetical quadrats across different EEZs. The CPUE data in itself
provided an initial overview of the distribution of sandeel clusters per age group, per area in
SA1r and provided insights to the distribution of the stock from both an age and geographic
perspective. All three initial spatial configurations based on the initial CPUE plot indicated
that zero-year-olds were distributed across all three geographical areas, whereas one- and
two-year-olds appeared more abundant in the west and southwestern parts of SA1r, which
falls primarily within the UK EEZ. The catch data from 2004-2022 also indicates that the
majority of sandeel catches were in the UK area, following the same trend observed from the
effort data. The evidence showing that most stock abundance and fishing activity is within
UK EEZ, coupled with the sandeel clusters that seemed to partly aggregate within the same
area, led to the decision of assigning UK EEZ as one group, with the same pattern followed
for area EU1 and EU2. The OM is designed to execute one or multiple areas in a given run
and allows for the addition or complete modification of areas. While this research primarily
focuses on the parameters R, movement and distributed F, these can be changed if and when

population dynamics and exchange rates between areas and further allows for the generic

4.2.
The conditioned model
Compiling the CPUE data, reconditioning population dynamics and disaggregating a single
stock of sandeel into three potentially separate sub-populations was a complete success.
Figure 3.4 displays how well the the OM mimics the CPUE data. Firstly it meant that a
movement parameter added to an existing aged based model was possible for this stock and
second that the OM was then equipped to move forwards with forecasting. It was decided
that the selected areas should operate towards a potential management shift that would
result in two different governances; The United Kingdom and The European Union in
relation to the likelihood of Brexit and how important that information may be on 30th June
2026 when the annual consultations for determining EU vessel access to British waters begin
(Think Tank, 2022). Using the finalised movement and R parameters, fitted with a ricker stock
recruitment relationship, a number of different combinations of distributed F were analysed.
Five mean and summed scenarios of forecast SSB, catch, recruitment and F were initiated
which allowed for the comparison and discussion of total catch and SSB from 2022-2042.
4.3.
Future Scenarios of F
The BASELINE scenario uses a distributed F of UK 1.2, EU1 0.32 and EU2 0.014 from the
relative distribution of effort. In this study, BASELINE is considered ’business as usual’ (see
figure 3.6) and was configured to mimic on average the last 5 years of fishing effort for
sandeel in SA1r. Assuming a constant distributed Fcap from 2022-2042, BASELINE yields on
average 183,611 tonnes SSB per year and 169,423 tonnes average catch per year which is
almost 50,000 tonnes higher than recommended catch corresponding to stock assessment
advice (ICES, 2023d). When looking at figure 3.11 the most important result related to
BASELINE is that the total SSB, which accounts for the sum of all three areas (i.e. the whole
stock) is above Blim from the latest 2023 ICES advice which is also calculated for the whole
stock, not to mention that of the five scenarios, BASELINE is the lowest total SSB. In the latest
ICES advice there are many years that use the same or similar Fcap of 0.49 where SSB the
following year is highly variable e.g. some years its higher than Blim relative to that year and
some years its lower. However when applying the same Fcap but instead distributing it
between three spatially disaggregated areas, all of the scenarios stay above both Blim and
Bescapement. Of course the spatially explicit scenarios cannot be directly compared to
previous years SSB since Fcap is distributed differently but what it can do is demonstrate that
applying a distributed F across three areas of one stock instead of applying a total Fcap to the
whole stock could be an alternative fisheries management strategy since the fishing pressure
is more controlled, according to different population dynamics inclusive of spatial variation.

and catch. In the most recent ICES advise for example, when the MSY approach is applied i.e.
using an Fcap of 0.49 where Maximum F, results in < 5% probability of SSB < Blim (ICES,

scenario ’FIVEFOLD’ is 119,245 tonnes in 2023 using a distributed F of UK 0.3, EU1 0.1 and
EU2 0.2. This scenario then produces a consistently, relatively high average catch and SSB
going forwards twenty years into the future, SSB being the second highest of the five groups.
In the context of one stock that has been dissagregated into three separate areas that are
governed by different population dynamics that have been calculated via the OM, this could
be a good choice of distributed Fcap. The relatively high R from area UK in theory maintains
a healthy SSB and leads to high catches in future years however this would assume 0.3 Fcap
in area UK which may be closed to fisheries in the future, and the management strategy will
therefore need to be re-evaluated as new decisions are made regarding TAC quotas and
potential prohibition for sandeel fishing in the area, as elaborated in the next section
(European Commission, 2023).
4.5.
Potential Local Depletion
The 2023 consultation on spatial management measures for industrial sandeel fishing in
English waters proposed three management measures to, as stated by DEFRA (2023)
“increase the resilience of sandeel stocks and the wider ecosystem”, with the majority of
responses agreeing with full closure of the area. The Zero F scenario in the OM reflects this
becoming a reality, where the UK EEZ fishing zones are closed and assumes that EU1 and
EU2 continue to business as usual. It appears that with a maximum Fcap of 0.5 (distributed
over three areas), closing the UK EEZ to fisheries will benefit the sandeel stock in SA1r
because it serves to produce enough recruits to sustain the majority when it is considered one
stock with no spatial consideration. In fact, the closure would also serve to help produce
more recruits to area EU1 and potentially also to EU2 since in the model, 10% of sandeel
migrate from the UK to EU1. However as demonstrated in figure 3.9, if a relatively high F is
then applied to area EU1, the disaggregated SSB for future years in EU1 is close to zero.
When sandeel are fished their habitat is often destroyed by the fishing gears (SEASATRISK,
2022) and since sandeel are already an extremely variable species at base, fishing in EU1 with
a high F could lead to an overcrowded and reduced habitat area however overall, according
to the OM, the sandeel stock will remain above current Blim but there may be some local
depletion in area EU1.

4.6. Brexit’s silver lining

4.6.
Brexit’s silver lining
EU1 group has low SSB when the movement parameter is set to 0.7 (70%) which is to be
expected since many fish are assumed to be moving out and according to the HAWG stock
annex, sandeel tend to stay where they end up if, in the instance they are carried passively
via larval drift (ICES, 2018b). When looking at the larval drift simulations (using connectivity
matrix from the biophysical model in the annex) it appears that if there is any drift or
migration in SA1r, it usually starts on the westerly side of Dogger Bank and heads in a north
easterly direction. When coupled with the large amounts of commercial catch (7.2), effort
(7.3) and relative abundance from the CPUE data (3.3) in western Dogger Bank, this would
then presume that area UK is an important spawning ground for area EU1 and potentially
EU2 which supports Gurkan et al. (2013) in that Dogger Bank is a large retention area for
settling sandeel meaning that essentially if area UK is closed as a result of Brexit, it would be
highly beneficial to sandeel stocks in the EU EEZ’s.
4.7.
Future Perspectives
To improve the area selection process in this thesis, further statistical modelling in the
correlation between sandeel clusters, the implementation of larval and otolith data and the
inclusion of temperature fluctuations is perhaps the next step to an otherwise simplified
selection process that was fitting for the scope of this thesis. It has been displayed that when
applying a distributed F to a single stock of sandeel in SA1, recruitment and SSB are above
current Blim. This is under the assumption that sandeel are migrating between sand banks at
a given age however movement is not necessarily explicit to active migration but could also
be related to other forces which function to the same effect. For example differences in
density or climate change e.g. where Wright et al. (2019) finds significant differences in local
productivity and mortality in site attached populations of sandeel and how Jacobsen et al.
(2022) discovers that changes in temperature and increased variability is altering
environmental conditions within marine populations retrospectively. The issue that is
presented in this thesis, whilst taking the former concerns into consideration is that coupling
biological and ecological research on a localised scale with stock assessment models is
paramount for fisheries management advice going forwards. Not to mention in light of the
recent developments encouraged by Defra, NGO’s and the general public in the UK, it would
seem important for both EU and UK to encourage further research into the management of
sandeel stocks so that the impact on sandeel sub-populations can be mitigated, high catches
of sandeel can reach MSY and local depletion can be avoided.

Conclusion
Over the past decade, studies have shown that sandeel is one of the fundamental,
interlinking species within marine ecosystems that plays a central role in consuming
zooplankton at the lower end of the food web (Eliasen et al., 2011) and in turn acting as a key
food source to the higher end for predators such as fish, birds and mammals (Otto, Opitz,
and Froese, 2019). From an economic perspective, sandeel provide great economic value to
EU registered vessels, especially from Denmark where between 2015 – 2019, £41.2m worth of
sandeel was harvested on average per year, roughly equating to 354,412,736 Danish kroner
today (DEFRA, 2022). Historically speaking, from between 1983 and 2002, Denmark was the
major fishery for sandeel landings contributing 73% of total landings (ICES, 2018b). The
current challenges surrounding the management of sandeel are a lack of understanding in
the sandeel stock structure (RSPB, 2023) and reports of severely declining stocks (NatureScot,
2023), which has prompted further investigations and public consultations from the UK
following Brexit (DEFRA, 2023). To better understand the intrinsic phenomena observed
within sandeel population dynamics, research has attempted to utilise models that mimic
sandeel population dynamics and based on the output make projections for MSY scenarios in
stock assessments. However, many assessment models used in fisheries management today
tend to aggregate the data and ignore the spatially explicit, localised interactions (Berger
et al., 2016) and it appears that progress in integrated population models (IPMs) that
incorporate spatial dynamics to calculate fisheries advise is slow (Goethel. et al., 2020). This
thesis has highlighted some of the issues surrounding current stock assessments of sandeel
in the North Sea and identified some of the gaps between sandeel biology and stock
assessment models. It was possible to achieve its objectives by successfully configuring the
population dynamic parameters of an existing age based model inclusive of spatial variation
and to mimic annual CPUE data for sandeel in SA1r. The conditioned model allows for the
investigation of how spatial variation plays a vital role in the management of sandeel stocks
in the context of Brexit and presented a potential solution that yields high average SSB and
catch rates, twenty years into the future.

Key Words and Abbreviations
• Biomass (B): The total weight of a given stock. Could for example be in tonnes or
thousands of tonnes.
• Catch per unit effort (CPUE): Can be viewed as fish abundance and looks at the
quantity of fish caught per standard unit of effort. For example, catch in tonnes per
hour trawling.
• Catch in numbers in a given time step (C): For example, if the time step is annual, then
C refers to the number of fish caught between January 1st of a given year until
December 31 of that same year.
• Effort: Sometimes referred to as fishing effort or Ut in equations. Effort refers to the
effort in a given time-step, for example fishing hours.
• Exp: The exponential function.
• Fbar: Average fishing mortality calculated across pre-specified age-groups.
• Fishing mortality (F): A common way of expressing fishing pressure, as it is the
proportion of the average stock that is caught over a year.
• Fish stock: A fish stock is a group of fish of the same species that are “reproductively
isolated in space or time” (Jennings et al, 2001: 178). The growth and mortality of the
individual stock thus has a greater effect on stock dynamics than the birth and death
rates of neighbouring stocks.
• Length: Refers to the length of the individual fish or the average of fish length of fish in
the stock within a given age group.
• Management targets and reference points: Based on the management objectives and
provides ways of evaluating certain management strategies and actions. It may include,
for example, a critical lower SSB that we wish to keep the stock above.
• Maximum Sustainable Yield (MSY): Denotes the largest yield that can, under existing
conditions, be taken from a stock.
• Natural mortality (M): Natural mortality is the mortality not attributable to fishing, but
rather from predation, and follows exponential decay.
• Number of fish in the stock (N): Mostly N refers to the number of fish in the stock
within a given age-group (numbers at age). We often see the usage of Nt or N(t) which
refers to the number of fish in a time step.
• Total mortality (Z): Combines natural mortality with fishing mortality. Total mortality
can apply to any time step and can be aggregated across time steps.
47
==PAGE:53==
48
• Recruitment (R): The number of young fish entering the fish stock. It can be the number
of settling young-of-the-year individuals or the number of fish entering the fishery for
the first time.
• Spawning stock biomass (S or SSB): The biomass of the reproducing segment of the
stock.
• Time step (t): The time interval utilised. Often the time step is also relate to age (see
description of N).
• Weight (W): Sometimes this is referred to as weight-at-age, which refers to the average
weight of a given age-group in the stock. Can also be the weight of the individual fish
at a given age.
• Yield: Total catch in biomass across all age groups. If no fish is discarded, then yield is
equal to landing.
