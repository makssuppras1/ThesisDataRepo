Abstract
The expansion of wind energy in Denmark has in recent years fallen short of expectations
[1, 2], partly due to public opposition which is often rooted in concerns about visual impact
(VI), particularly for nearshore wind farms [3]. This thesis presents a framework for optimizing
wind farm layouts based on a coupled assessment of Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE) and
VI. Although VI is subjective, this study focuses on its quantification by creating a model that
integrates turbine visibility analysis among other VI parameters. The model is applied to a
Danish nearshore case study, the Lillebælt Syd wind farm [4], to explore how different layout
configurations balance LCOE and VI, and to identify which turbine arrangements emerge as
optimal under varying optimization priorities.
The coupled optimization is implemented in the open-source TOPFARM framework. VI is
quantified by combining turbine visibility with population data and survey-based weights,
while LCOE is estimated using a predefined cost model and a power production calculator. A
methodology is developed to optimize both LCOE and VI, while also enabling investigation of
the trade-offs between the two. The VI optimization accounts for multiple viewpoints, and the
importance assigned to each viewpoint is easily interchangeable within the model. The results
of the case study show that pure VI optimization results in clustered and randomly seeming
layouts when viewed from above, but the layouts appear more ordered when experienced from
the viewpoints, with turbines aligned in rows to reduce VI. Although effective in minimizing
VI, this approach generally results in significantly higher LCOE. Minimizing VI and LCOE si-
multaneously yields layouts that resemble those of pure LCOE optimization in terms of overall
turbine spread. However, these layouts are more deliberately oriented towards viewpoints to re-
duce turbine visibility, achieving a balanced compromise between cost-eﬀiciency and aesthetics.
This work contributes to improved wind farm layout planning by integrating VI considerations
and provides new insights into the scientific field of VI optimization.

1
Introduction
Denmark is widely recognized as a global leader in wind energy, with ambitious goals of achiev-
ing climate neutrality by utilizing renewable energy technologies [5]. As part of this transition,
the country is expanding both onshore and offshore wind capacity, including large-scale projects
in the North Sea and Baltic Sea. However, despite its strong international reputation, Den-
mark’s rollout of new wind installations has in recent years fallen short of expectations [1, 2].
Planning delays, rising costs, and challenges due to local opposition have complicated progress,
making the present study both timely and relevant.
To improve the feasibility of wind power projects, there has historically been significant focus on
economic factors in the design process, including designing the wind farm layout configuration.
The design of wind farm layouts is often based on developer experience, optimization of eco-
nomic objectives or a combination of the two. As a rule of thumb, offshore turbines are typically
arranged in a grid formation, with a spacing of seven rotor diameters in the dominant wind
direction and three in the non-dominant direction [6]. Modern wind turbines, often around 150
- 250 meters tall [7], significantly reshape the visual landscape wherever they are installed. As
turbine size and national installed capacity increase, so do public concerns, especially in scenic
or populated areas. Although social acceptance of wind projects is a complex topic [8], many
appeals to the Danish Energy Appeals Board cite VI as a reason for opposition [9], suggesting
that addressing VI in the design process could improve public acceptance.
A 2022 analysis by the Danish Energy Agency (DEA) highlights growing support for tender
models that incorporate not only price, but also sustainability and environmental criteria [10].
While VI is not explicitly mentioned, the emphasis on societal value and non-price factors
suggests that visual and social concerns may become more prominent in the near future. The
DEA 2022 site screening similarly flagged areas within 20 km of the coast as having medium
visual concern and recommended prioritizing more distant sites [11]. This again reflects in-
creased awareness of VI. Politically, the 2022 Climate Agreement [12] introduced more holistic
tender models that integrate environmental, social, and governance criteria. The 2025 offshore
wind tender further mandates life cycle assessments, environmental product declarations, and
socially responsible supply chains [13], potentially paving the way for the inclusion of VI in
future evaluations.
Recent studies also highlight the relevance of multi-criteria decision-making (MCDM) in wind
planning. Abdullah et al. (2025) reviewed MCDM applications for wind farm site selection and
demonstrated its potential to balance technical, environmental, social, and economic factors
[14]. Although focused on macro siting, these principles could support the integration of VI
into layout design as well.
Offshore wind farms are typically located at ranges from shore where turbines appear smaller
due to the distance. On the other hand, nearshore wind farms are located much closer to shore,

farms that are close enough to land to produce a comparable visual presence, as seen with the
2024 Vesterhav Syd project [15].
Despite the growing interest in reducing VI in wind projects, optimization of wind farm layout
with regard to VI is a very limited scientific field. VI is one of many subjective influences on
public opinion [16], and as Bishop notes, local communities are unlikely to fully accept quan-
titative visual assessments as definitive [16]. However, integrating VI with economic analysis
in the wind farm design process can offer valuable insight in an area where limited research
has been done, and may help guide the development of more visually, and thereby socially,
acceptable wind farms.
In response, this thesis proposes a coupled optimization model that simultaneously balances
LCOE and VI in wind farm layout planning, aiming to harmonize economic and aesthetic
considerations. Applied to a Danish nearshore case study, the model draws on both technical
and social science insights. The purpose of this thesis is to help bridge the gap between economic
eﬀiciency and social acceptance in wind farm layout design, with the potential to inform future
research and planning guidelines.

This section provides an overview of regulations and research related to wind farm layout and
VI. It starts with an overview of how layouts are commonly optimized, followed by a review

research on how VI is assessed and how it can be reduced. The focus is on studies that are

2.1
Wind Farm Layout Design
The layout of a wind farm plays a central role regarding its performance, cost-eﬀiciency, and
possibly its public acceptance. For nearshore wind farms, typically located a few kilometers
off the coast in relatively shallow waters, the layout is often constrained by factors such as
seabed conditions, local water depths and wind conditions. Offshore wind farm layouts were
traditionally based on regular grid patterns, but this is no longer always the case [17]. As
illustrated in Figure 1, the shift toward more irregular and optimization based layouts has
become increasingly common in the literature recent years. Although the figure dates back to
2019, it effectively highlights the evolving design trends over the past decade.
Figure 1: ”Development of wind farm patterns over time.” [17].
The offshore wind farm Kriegers Flak, commissioned in 2021, features a fairly irregular layout
design. Regarding nearshore, the Lillebælt Syd nearshore project (establishment permit in 2024
[4]) adopts a regular grid, while the nearshore project Jammerland Bugt [18](establishment per-
mit in 2024) employs a more irregular layout.

When designing wind farm layouts, the optimization process can target different objectives,
although maximizing the annual energy production (AEP), minimizing the cost of energy or
LCOE are commonly used [17]. LCOE is a widely accepted metric for evaluating the cost-
effectiveness of energy projects. It captures the total lifetime costs (including capital expen-
diture, operational expenditure, and decommissioning) relative to the total expected energy
production. Many of the cost drivers, such as inter-array cable length, foundation type, and in-
stallation logistics, are directly influenced by turbine placement. In addition, power production
can be significantly affected by wake losses if turbines are positioned in a way that disrupts the
wind flow to downstream turbines.
2.1.1
Optimization Objectives
When optimizing wind farm layouts, the focus is most often on economic objectives [19, 17,
20]. In contrast, studies that address VI typically focus on evaluating the VI of existing wind
farms or creating decision tools, rather than optimizing the layout design with respect to VI.
To the authors’ knowledge, only one study has performed an optimization of VI of nearshore
wind farms. It is a coupled optimization of wind farm layout with respect to both VI and
LCOE. The study was conducted by Gonzalez-Rodriguez et al.
[21] and applies a multi-
objective optimization framework. This approach generates a set of non-dominated solutions,
where improving one objective, such as reducing LCOE, inevitably leads to trade-offs in the
other, such as increased VI. The method only investigates regular grid layouts seen from a
single viewpoints. Another method proposed by Guan [22] can be used to optimize the VI of
onshore wind projects, although it was not applied to an iterative optimization process in the
study.
2.2
Regulations and Guidelines for Wind Farm Layout
While there are national guidelines addressing the layout of wind farms in Denmark, there are
no legal requirements specifying how turbines must be arranged to address VI. According to
the Danish ’Executive Order on Planning and Permits for Wind Turbine Installation’, ”wind
turbines installed in groups must be arranged in a geometrically coherent pattern that is easily
perceptible in relation to the landscape” [23]. However, it is not defined from which location
the pattern should be easily perceived, which may be challenging when addressing near-shore
wind farm layout, since the wind farm may be visible from various angles.
As previously mentioned, many complaints about wind farms under development emphasize
concerns about the anticipated VI of the wind farm. A complaint regarding the VI of the
Vesterhav Syd Offshore Wind Farm permit was rejected by the Danish Energy Appeals Board,
as there are currently no legal regulations addressing VI for offshore wind farms under ’The
Renewable Energy Act’ [24].
In the Oﬀicial Guidelines for Planning and Permitting Wind Turbine Installation [25], it is
stated that the evaluation of the VI associated with a wind farm project is discretionary and

inconclusive recommendations.
2.3
Quantification of Visual Impact
The VI of a wind farm is subjective [16, 22], but it can be generalized using different methods.
One approach is the Hedonic Pricing Method [27, 28, 29], which estimates how wind farms affect
property values. Another is to gather survey data based on visualizations of the wind farm from
different viewpoints [26, 3]. However, the most commonly used methods are linear evaluation
models based on categorization [30, 31, 32, 22]. Across different studies, the same quantitative
and objective parameters are often used to evaluate the VI of existing or planned wind power
projects. These include turbine dimensions and orientation, viewer distance and exposure, and
the landscape type surrounding the viewer and wind farm, which represents the scenic quality of
the area. These parameters are easily defined and are commonly used in models that calculate
total VI. In this report, they are grouped under the acronym DODEL (Dimensions, Orientation,
Distance, Exposure, Landscape). A detailed description of the DODEL parameters is provided

2.3.1
Hedonic Pricing Method
The hedonic pricing method estimates how specific factors influence property values, including
external factors such as views or nearby infrastructure. Several studies have used this approach
to evaluate how the visibility of wind turbines affects residential property prices. A common
finding is that visible turbines lead to a reduction in property value, with stronger effects at
shorter distances [27, 28, 29]. Guo et al. [28] found that in the U.S., property prices drop by
an average of 1.12% if at least one turbine is visible within 10 km. Dong et al. [29] reported
a 2.2–2.5% drop when investigating only New England, USA, with even greater reductions in
urban and coastal areas. In Denmark, COWI [27] showed that visible turbines within 3 km
significantly lower property values, especially when multiple turbines are visible.
These findings are based on onshore wind turbines but suggest that both distance and line of
sight from residential areas are important factors in relation to VI.

turbine visibility from the village, village visibility from the wind farm, relative view of the
wind farm geometry, distance to the village, and population. The model uses GIS data and
basic formulas to quantify each coeﬀicient. An overview of the method equations can be seen

project aims to address.

on several factors, and distance to the turbines is particularly important, as larger distances

2.4
Research Questions
Most wind farm layout optimization studies focus primarily on economic objectives. However,
VI is an important factor for public opposition and there is very limited literature on layout
optimization that considers VI. Although modern wind farm layouts are no longer limited to
regular grid patterns, the only known method in the literature that optimizes both LCOE and
VI assumes a regular layout structure and only one viewpoint. This gap motivates the following
research questions:
How does coupled optimization of LCOE and VI influence the layout pattern of a nearshore
wind farm?
• How can multiple viewpoints be incorporated in the VI estimation?
• What are the trade-offs between LCOE and VI in layout optimization?
• When using multiple viewpoints in VI estimation, how can their relative importance be
defined, and how does this influence the resulting layout configuration?

one-time costs incurred before the wind farm becomes operational. Operational expenditures
(OPEX) denote annual costs in year t, including maintenance, insurance, and other recurring
expenses. AEP is the annual energy production in year t. The parameter r represents the

3.1.1
Cost Model
DTU has developed a Python library that can be used to estimate wind farm costs and calculate
LCOE. The library, named CostModel [36], includes a model called DTUOffshoreCostModel,
which is used in this thesis as the LCOE estimator. The model estimates pre-tax LCOE in
nominal values by accounting for inflation through the use of a nominal WACC as the cost
discount rate. The model was developed by Kenneth Thomsen in 2019, based on initial work
by Henrik Stiesdal. TOPFARM also includes a built-in cost model, but according to our su-
pervisors, it is somewhat outdated compared to the DTUOffshoreCostModel. Therefore, the
built-in model was not used in this study.
The general functioning of the cost model is now described with input parameters in italic:
The model uses turbine characteristics (rated power, rotor diameter, rated rotor speed, and hub
height) to estimate the cost of the type of turbine used in the wind farm. This cost is then
multiplied by the number of turbines. The foundation type must be specified, and the associated
cost depends on water depth, as deeper waters require taller and more expensive foundations.
Electrical infrastructure is estimated as a cost per installed MW and represents expenditures
related to e.g. cabling and grid connection. The project lifetime is used to estimate both the
total OPEX and the total AEP. A nominal WACC is applied as the discount rate when cal-
culating the LCOE using Equation 2. A table overview of all input parameters is provided in
Table 13 in Appendix B.
Some additional input parameters of the cost model are, due to simplification, assumed to be
zero. Modern offshore wind farms often exhibit a negligible AEP decline factor over their oper-
ational lifetime, particularly when using high-quality turbines and following good maintenance
practices [37]. Therefore, the AEP decline factor is set to 0. As mentioned, ABEX is diﬀicult
to estimate and is therefore set to 0. Turbine seller profit, which can be used to adjust the
turbine cost, is also set to 0 for simplicity.
As previously mentioned, water depth influences CAPEX through its impact on foundation
design. To account for this, a bathymetry map is used to determine the water depth at each
turbine location. Since the primary focus of this thesis is to develop a model incorporating
VI, water depth is assumed to be the only cost parameter among the total lifetime cost in
Equation 2 dependent on the turbine layout. This is a simplification of reality, as other cost
components such as cable length, installation costs, and seabed conditions can also be affected
by the layout configuration [38].

on factors such as the local wind resource, turbine specifications, and wind farm layout. This

Wind Resource Data and Modeling Approach
An accurate characterization of the wind resource is essential for reliable estimation of the
AEP. Wind speed and direction are commonly recorded as time-series measurements using 10-
or 30-minute averages to capture short-term variability and seasonal patterns. To facilitate
energy yield calculations, these time-series are statistically modeled, often by fitting a Weibull
distribution to the wind speed data, which is a widely accepted approach in wind energy
analysis [39]. The Weibull distribution is described by two parameters: the shape parameter k
and the scale parameter A. Together, they define the probability distribution of wind speeds at
a site, enabling representation of the wind climate with a compact statistical model. To capture
directional variation, Weibull distributions are commonly generated for multiple wind direction
sectors, often represented by the wind rose showing the directional frequency distribution, which
is crucial for precise wind energy assessment.

Wake Modeling
This study applies the Bastankhah Gaussian wake model, originally developed by Bastankhah
and Porté-Agel [41], to estimate the wind speed deficit caused by turbine wakes. The model
assumes a Gaussian distribution of the velocity deficit, with the greatest reduction at the wake
center and a gradual recovery toward the freestream velocity at the edges. It is based on con-
servation of mass and momentum, yielding physically consistent and widely accepted results.
The Bastankhah model was selected for its general applicability and strong theoretical founda-
tion. It was recommended by the thesis supervisors as an appropriate standard model for this
analysis. Accordingly, alternative wake models were not investigated, as their evaluation falls
outside the scope of this study. The full derivation and governing equations of the model can
be found in the original publication by Bastankhah and Porté-Agel [41].
The wake deficit is calculated as a function of the turbine’s thrust coeﬀicient, which varies with
wind speed and is obtained from the turbine’s thrust coeﬀicient curve, similar to how the power
curve specifies power output. This enables the model to reflect changes in operational turbine
behavior under different wind conditions.
A key parameter in the wake model is the wake expansion coeﬀicient kwake, which governs how
quickly the wake widens downstream. This reflects the degree of turbulent mixing between the
wake and the surrounding flow. In this study, the default value kwake = 0.0324555, as provided
in PyWake, was used. This represents a moderate expansion rate suitable for typical turbulence
conditions. A higher kwake value results in a faster-spreading but shallower wake, while a lower
kwake leads to a narrower and deeper wake with stronger downstream effects. To maintain focus
on other aspects of the study, this default kwake-value was retained without further calibration.

3.2
Visual Impact
This subsections firstly elaborate in VI as a metric and then describes how it is calculated in
the model.
The VI of a wind farm depends on both objective and subjective factors. To reduce VI, several
objective design elements are commonly addressed through industry standards. These include
turbine color, blade rotation direction, and synchronized rotation speed across the wind farm.
As these features are already widely optimized and standardized in modern projects, they are
not investigated in this study. The same applies to other turbine characteristics such as the
number of blades and the shape of the rotor, tower, and nacelle. While turbine type can be
defined as a design variable in TOPFARM, as demonstrated by [19], we chose to focus exclu-
sively on layout design, as this approach is more commonly used in both academic literature
and industry applications.
The spatial layout of the wind farm is another important objective factor regarding VI. Turbine
layout influences how visible and orderly the wind farm appears. Layouts where turbines align
or partially overlap can reduce visual clutter [21]. The landscape surrounding the wind farm
and the observer also influences VI. Wind farms tend to have a greater VI in scenic, undevel-
oped areas compared to locations where other large-scale infrastructure is already present, such
as rural areas with transmission lines, roads, or industrial facilities.
In addition to physical characteristics, VI is influenced by subjective factors such as individual
attitudes toward wind energy, familiarity with wind turbines, place attachment, and political
views [8, 3, 31]. These personal perceptions vary between individuals and locations, making VI
a complex and context-dependent concept. In the literature, many studies estimate the VI of
wind farms using linear evaluation models based on categorization schemes, typically applied to
wind farm where the final layout is defined. In contrast, this work estimates VI based on turbine
visibility, by assessing the amount of visible turbines seen from a set of selected viewpoints. Our
VI estimation is heavily inspired by Gonzalez-Rodriguez et al. [21], and incorporates multiple
of their equations, but with some adjustments. While our VI estimation approach is similar,
a major difference lies in the optimization setup. In contrast to their study, which focuses
on regular layouts and visibility from a single viewpoint, we optimize turbine visibility across
multiple viewpoints and allow for irregular layouts.

4.3.2
VI Weights
As outlined in Section 3.2.2, each viewpoint is assigned a weight, which may be applied de-
pending on the specific scenario. These weights can be based on various factors, but in this
model, only two are considered:
1) Population size. The area around Lillebælt primarily consists of natural landscapes and
summerhouse zones, so most viewpoints have a population count of zero. According to Statis-
tics Denmark (Danmarks Statistik) [62], only 4 of the 14 locations have residential populations.
2) Public attitude. In connection with the Lillebælt Syd project, a Megafon survey was
conducted in the municipalities of Sønderborg and Assens to gauge local attitudes [63]. The
survey found that 17% of the residents of Assens were negative or very negative towards the
project, compared to only 9% in Sønderborg. Based on this, it is assumed that neighboring
municipalities, Haderslev, Aabenraa, and Faaborg-Midtfyn, align more closely with Assens,
while Sønderborg, as the project initiator, stands out. These negative response percentages
form the basis of the so-called “attitude scores.”
Originally, the plan was to use detailed attitude data from a national survey by Jacob Laden-
burg, which examined Danish public sentiment toward various renewable energy technologies
across demographics such as age, gender, housing type, and location. These data had the po-
tential to assign customized attitude scores to individual households. Furthermore, data could
inform weighting schemes based on factors such as urban versus rural residence. Although
considerable time was spent exploring this approach, the data was ultimately not used in the
final model due to its complexity, the limited significance of the findings, and the diﬀiculty in
linking aggregated survey responses to specific viewpoints within the case area. As a result,
Megafon attitude scores were used instead as a simpler and more practical proxy.
Each viewpoint is therefore assigned a population count and an attitude score, reflecting its
relative importance. To ensure comparability, both values are normalized between 0 and their
respective maximums and weighted equally. Their average forms the final weight, which is mul-
tiplied by the corresponding turbine visibility score to determine that viewpoint’s contribution
to the overall VI score. An overview is provided in Table 6, with further details in Table 14 in
Appendix D.1.

A reason for the solver being stuck at four rows and not achieving the optimal two rows is
that VI is only reduced by hiding a visible turbine behind another turbine, or moving turbines
backward. The driver is therefore good at creating rows, but tends to be ineffective in reducing
the number of rows, as removing a turbine from a row leads to an increase in VI unless the
turbine is completely covered by another row in the following incrementation. In Figure 20,
one of the 4 rows (the row to the left in the Minimized VI layout as seen from Å Strand)
contains only two turbines. These two turbines could easily fit into the adjacent row, which
would significantly reduce the VI. But if the driver does not move all turbines in a row in the
same direction, it will only experience that the VI increases when moving a turbine towards
another row.

Verification Summary
The verification of LCOE and VI confirms that the model components work as intended. When
minimizing LCOE, the model lowers total water depth and wake losses to improve AEP, effec-
tively reducing LCOE. When minimizing VI with respect to a single viewpoint, turbines are
arranged in rows seen from that viewpoint to reduce visibility. However, both objectives are
sensitive to the initial layouts, often leading to local optima. This emphasizes the importance
of testing multiple initial layouts to explore the solution space more thoroughly.

6.1.3
Inclusion of Dominant Wind Direction
When creating visualizations in EIAs, it is considered best practice to render the rotor planes
perpendicular to the observer’s view direction. This orientation presents the turbines in their
most visually prominent form. Following this principle, the turbine visibility calculations are
based on a “worst-case scenario”, where rotor planes are oriented toward each VI point to
maximize their apparent visibility. However, it is physically impossible for the rotor planes to
face all viewpoints simultaneously, as they are located in various directions.
Orienting the rotor planes according to the dominant wind direction in the turbine visibility
calculation has been suggested by Gonzalez-Rodriguez et al.[21] as a direction for future work,
specifically recommending the use of wind rose. Instead of using the full wind rose, we initially
implemented a simplified approach, where the fVI(x,y) function considered the dominant wind
direction. As shown in Section 4.2.3 and Figure 13a, the wind rose indicates that the wind at
Lillebælt Syd most frequently comes from the west, with 270◦as the dominant wind direction.
To illustrate the effect of including the dominant wind direction in the visibility calculation,
the Developer Layout is visualized from Å Strand with and without it in Figure 24.

6.6
Sensitivity
Given the usage of a newly developed model, a sensitivity analysis was conducted to assess
how it responds to different input parameters and choices of normalization bounds. The focus
was on internal model sensitivity rather than external scenario variations, aiming to identify
particularly influential parameters and to perform a general sanity check of model behavior.
Although an uncertainty analysis would have been valuable, it was not pursued due to time
constraints. Uncertainty analysis could involve a Monte Carlo simulation, varying the random
seeds used to generate the 100 initial layouts to estimate the uncertainty in LCOE and VI
for the investigated layouts. Alternatively, a manual comparison using a small set of different
initial layouts could help assess whether general layout tendencies remain consistent. Although
this was not pursued in the present study, it is considered a valuable direction for future work.
6.6.1
LCOE - Model Sensitivity
To illustrate the sensitivity of the LCOE model, we analyze the Developer Layout as an ex-
ample. It has an LCOE of 70.0 €/MWh, total water depth of 208 m and AEP of 748 GWh,
corresponding to a total energy production of 18,700 GWh over 25 years. Marginal changes
reveal the following sensitivities:
• A 1% increase in water depth leads to a 0.05% increase in LCOE.
• A 1% increase in AEP leads to a 1.0% decrease in LCOE.
Assuming a linear relationship, this implies that AEP has approximately 20 times more influ-
ence on LCOE than water depth. At Lillebælt Syd, the Baseline Minimized LCOE reduced total
water depth by 13 % and increased AEP by 0.4 %. A linear estimate of the LCOE improvement
would be:
∆LCOE = −0.05 % · 13 + (−1.00 % · −0.4) = −1.05 %
This corresponds to an estimated reduction from 70.0 €/MWh to approximately:
70.0 €/MWh · (1 −0.0105) = 69.3 €/MWh
However, the Baseline Minimized LCOE layout had and LCOE of 69.0 €/MWh which indicates
that the relationship between LCOE, AEP, and water depth is non-linear and that optimization
delivers compounded benefits beyond those predicted by a linear model.
The relative importance of AEP and water depth is also site specific, and whichever parameter
shows greater potential for improvement will typically dominate the LCOE reduction. To fur-
ther explore this, we varied water depth and AEP individually by ±20 % around the Baseline
scenario and analyzed the corresponding optimized layouts. This sensitivity test was conducted
first with 10 initial layouts, and then repeated with 100 initial layouts, all optimized solely for
LCOE. Full results are presented in Appendix G.

7.1
General Discussion of Results
The results indicate that LCOE and VI are conflicting objectives, as demonstrated by the
Pareto fronts. A low LCOE typically corresponds to a high VI, and vice versa. This trade-off
is also reported by Gonzalez-Rodriguez et al. [21] in their coupled optimization of LCOE and
VI. Since Minimizing VI layouts often result in high LCOE values and may therefore be unfea-
sible, and given that LCOE-based layout optimization has already been extensively addressed
in previous studies, the following discussion mainly focuses on the Even Trade-Off layouts.
A notable observation across the Even Trade-Off layouts is that the turbines are widely dis-
tributed across the wind farm area to reduce wake losses and are arranged in intersecting,
non-parallel rows aligned with key viewpoints to reduce VI. This is an interesting finding, as
it appears to be a practical design solution not commonly seen in existing wind farms. It may
offer a compromise that is both visually considerate and technically feasible within modern
wind farm development. The model suggests that such a layout can significantly reduce visual
impact without substantially increasing LCOE.
The Even Trade-Off results showed reasonable LCOE values between 69.7 and 70.6 €/MWh
and VI scores ranging from 0.7 to 1.1 in all scenarios. In comparison, the Developer Layout,
as evaluated by the proposed model, has an LCOE of 70.0 €/MWh and a VI score between
3.2 and 4.6. This suggests that, based on the given VI definition, the developer could have
selected an alternative layout with a comparable LCOE but significantly lower visual impact.
The Even Trade-Off layouts all appear irregular to some extent, and, as noted by Sickler et
al. [17], wind farm designs are increasingly shifting away from strict grid formations. There-
fore, it should not be considered a limitation that the model suggests using relatively irregular
layouts to reduce VI. National guidelines state that wind farm layouts should follow an easily
recognizable pattern, but this is arguably a vague requirement, as it does not specify from
where the pattern should be recognizable. By applying the method proposed in this thesis, a
more technical and quantifiable approach can be used to reduce VI as seen from key viewpoints.
With regard to viewpoints, the use of those defined in the EIA appears to be both a reasonable
and a practical approach. EIAs commonly include a set of predefined viewpoints to assess VI,
often selected in collaboration with local stakeholders. In this study, the use of the 14 Lille-
bælt Syd EIA viewpoints produced satisfactory results; however, using fewer viewpoints could
potentially lead to layouts that reduce VI even more significantly for each individual viewpoint.
Although Abdullah et al. [14] focus on including social factors when choosing wind farm loca-
tions, similar ideas could also be useful when designing the layout. In this context, layouts like
those in the Even Trade-Off scenario could help include non-economic objectives in wind farm
planning.

7
DISCUSSION
June 26th, 2025
A key question is whether the findings of this case study can reveal anything general about
near-shore wind farm layouts. In short, only to a limited extent. Overall, while this study does
not provide general rules or proved ideal nearshore layouts, it does investigate how different
design assumptions influence layout design. The results suggest that widely spreading turbines
while aligning rows according to key viewpoints offers the best trade-off between LCOE and
VI, at least in the case of Lillebælt Syd.
The layout results were, in general, closely related to their specific configuration and weighting
of the VI points, which in this case were tailored to the particular conditions of the Lillebælt
Syd wind farm. As a result, general conclusions, such as whether certain layout patterns (e.g.,
circular formations or compact clustering) reliably minimize VI, would be premature. Findings
like these would likely only be relevant to wind farms with similar characteristics to Lillebælt
Syd, including prevailing wind directions and location of VI viewpoints. Furthermore, since no
directly comparable models were identified in the literature, it was not possible to benchmark
the findings against previous studies. Rather than prescribing a universally optimal layout
type, this study provides a methodological framework: A method to enhance layout designs
for reducing VI, dependent on the unique viewpoints defined for the site and the possibility to
change the relative importance of these.
7.2
Model Assumptions and Design Choices
The results are influenced by several key assumptions in the modeling and optimization-related
choices. These include the definition of objective function, the optimization algorithm (driver),
the quantity and distribution of the λ values and initial layouts. Although these parameters
were chosen with care, in-depth exploration of their effects and alternatives are beyond the
scope of this study. For example, using λ = [0, 0.01, 0.49, 0.5, 0.51, 0.99, 1] could have resulted
in different investigated layouts, or the use of smart start (e.g. finding initial layouts using a
greedy algorithm) could lead to investigated layouts with lower LCOE and VI. The primary
focus has been to establish a methodological framework and subsequently develop a model based
on it, which has been successfully achieved. Enhancing computational eﬀiciency would be a
logical next step to reduce runtime and make further model investigation less time consuming.

The normalized objectives, f norm

and ease of implementation and the the investigated Even Trade-Off layouts performed well in
regards to both LCOE and VI.

The use of the COBYLA driver may limit the performance of the optimization, as it is a local
optimization algorithm and is therefore sensitive to the choice of initial layouts. Exploring
alternative optimization drivers could potentially yield better results. For example, using a
global optimization method such as a GA or Particle Swarm Optimization may help overcome
the sensitivity to initial guess.
Moreover, due to the complexity of the model, it is diﬀicult to determine whether the non-

solutions exist that would dominate those found.
Since it is likely that there exist better
solutions, it could be interesting to perform a Monte Carlo simulation using random gener-
ated initial layouts (e.g. 1000 model runs, each with a new set of 100 random initial layouts)
to understand the uncertainty of the results.
This was not performed since it would take
1000 · 140 min = 97 days with the current model using the computer available for this thesis.
The number of significant digits used in the results has intentionally been limited to 3, since
the uncertainties of many input parameters are unknown and a model uncertainty analysis has
not been performed. The lack of uncertainty analysis is again a reason for investigating layout
tendencies instead of a specific layout. It was also found that using the model on different
computers produced different results, which may be due to differences in the installed Python
versions.
Estimation of Levelized Cost of Energy
The LCOE estimation used in the model is relatively simple compared to other studies. In
addition to total water depth and AEP, other factors such as cable costs also depend on the
turbine layout, as the length of inter-array cables generally increases with larger spacing or
irregular configurations. Including these additional layout-dependent costs could potentially
alter the trade-offs observed in the current model results. However, based on correspondence
with the developer, water depth and AEP appeared to be the most influential parameters for
Lillebælt Syd.
7.2.2
Assumptions and Limitations in the Visual Impact Metric
The VI metric is subject to several assumptions that influence both the optimization process
and the interpretation of the results. These assumptions shape how the model defines a ”better”
or ”worse” VI and may limit the metric’s ability to fully reflect real-world aesthetic preferences.
The current implementation assumes that it is always visually preferable for wind turbines to
be positioned directly behind one another seen from a viewpoint. This approach effectively
rewards alignment or clustering along the observer’s line of sight, without accounting for sit-
uations where wider spacing may in fact reduce perceived VI. In particular, this assumption
ignores the possibility that aligned turbines might amplify the visual presence of each other.
For example, when multiple turbines are positioned directly behind the front turbine, their
visibility may be compounded, particularly under certain weather conditions such as low cloud
cover, where this alignment may enhance contrast and visibility. In contrast, layouts in which

or appear more diffuse, thereby reducing perceived VI.
In the Baseline Weighted scenario seen in Figure 26, both the Even Trade-Off layout and the

think that the VI-optimal layout with turbines clustered into 2 stretched rows appears to be
more structured and pleasing to the eye. This raises the question of whether the current VI
metric fully captures qualitative visual preferences, such as symmetry or grouping of turbines.
A possible option would be to refine the VI metric to reward orderly groupings of turbines
or to penalize scattered layouts. However, this study also intentionally explores the potential
for more irregular layouts, with the aim of investigating whether such configurations might be
favored through VI-based optimization. This highlights a broader question in the field: What
truly defines a ”good” VI? Without a universally accepted standard or perceptual baseline, the
definition of an optimal VI layout remains subjective and is heavily influenced by the authors’
opinions and the limited number of existing studies.
However, the review of the literature on VI quantification suggests that the use of all or some of
the identified DODEL parameters is the most common approach. In this proposed model, both
dimensions, orientation, distance, and exposure are incorporated to some extent. Additionally,
the landscape type could easily be included by assigning it a weight within the VI calculation
if desired. We as authors, and Gonzalez-Rodriguez et al. [21], agree that basing the VI metric
on turbine visibility is a valid approach, although it represents a strong simplification of reality.
Visibility Assumptions and Line-of-Sight Limitations
Another simplification lies in the assumption that all VI points have an unobstructed line of
sight to the wind farm. In practice, local obstructions such as buildings, terrain, or vegetation
may partially or completely block views from certain locations I. These elements are not in-
cluded in the current model, which may lead to an overestimation of VI from some viewpoints.
However, this exclusion was considered reasonable in the context of the study. Rather than
modeling highly specific and localized conditions, each The VI point broadly symbolizes the
visual experience of its surrounding area. This generalization helps avoid overfitting the opti-
mization to particular obstructions that might not be permanent or may vary seasonally (e.g.,
tree foliage). Nonetheless, it remains a limitation that should be acknowledged, particularly if
the model is to be applied in detailed planning processes or in public acceptance studies where
actual view obstructions can play a significant role.

8
Conclusion
In this study, a novel model was developed to optimize wind farm layout by considering both
LCOE and VI. LCOE was estimated using a cost model and power production calculator, while
VI was quantified based on mean turbine visibility from a set of viewpoints derived from the
Lillebælt Syd EIA visualization points [52]. Then a coupled optimization of the wind farm
layout was performed using a weighted sum approach within the TOPFARM framework [35],
and three representative layout types were investigated: Minimized LCOE, Minimized VI and
Even Trade-Off. The developed optimization model shows that targeting either LCOE or VI
leads to markedly different layouts, as the two objectives are generally conflicting. When both
are equally weighted in the optimization, the model produces a third, distinct layout - the Even
Trade-Off - that balances cost and aesthetics through water depth, AEP and turbine visibility
optimization. These layouts typically reduce VI significantly while increasing LCOE by only
1–2% compared to the Minimized LCOE layout.
Several factors influence the model’s behavior and the resulting layouts, including wind re-
source, bathymetry, and the location and viewpoint importance. The relative importance of
viewpoints was varied across scenarios by considering factors such as distance to the wind
farm, population size, public attitude, or by assigning equal importance to all. The optimized
layouts from using these scenarios showed significant deviations from the original Developer
Layout, highlighting the method’s potential to improve both economic and visual performance.
Notably, layouts optimized for VI tend to appear more clustered and random when viewed
from above; however, this apparent randomness is much less pronounced from ground-level
viewpoints, which are ultimately more relevant for assessing VI. The results show that VI opti-
mization is highly sensitive to both the location of the VI points considered and their assigned
importance.
The Even Trade-Off layouts, on the other hand, consistently favor turbine dispersion, spread-
ing turbines as much as the site allows while still taking the VI points into account, often
resulting in a semi-structured irregular pattern. This behavior is observed consistently across
scenarios and sensitivity analyzes, suggesting that the model effectively captures layout trends
in response to VI considerations. The use of EIA viewpoints proved both practical and rele-
vant, and we recommend that future studies adopt a similar approach to ensure consistency
with real-world assessment practices.
Overall, the model offers a flexible and innovative framework for exploring nearshore wind farm
layout optimization with respect to both economic objectives and aesthetics.

8.1
Proposed Future Work
• A systematic uncertainty analysis could provide valuable insight into the relative relia-
bility of the model results. This could involve Monte Carlo simulations with variations
in initial layouts and key input parameters such as AEP, allowing for an assessment of
uncertainty in both LCOE and VI outcomes.
• The current VI estimation only rewards turbines that are directly hidden behind others.
Future versions could encourage placing turbines closer together more generally, poten-
tially leading to very clustered layouts in Minimized VI optimization. For Even Trade-Off
layouts, this adjustment might result in more compact groupings, fewer rows with more
turbines in them, while maintaining reasonable wake losses and reducing VI. Another
potential modification could involve using TOPFARM’s functionality for grid-based lay-
out optimization. It would be interesting to investigate what VI scores the model could
achieve with turbine placement restricted to a regular grid.
• Finally, a useful extension to this research would be to apply the model to another
nearshore project, such as Jammerland Bugt, which could help test its broader applica-
bility. This site shares key characteristics with Lillebælt Syd but features an irregular
developer layout. According to developer European Energy, the design aimed to align
rows toward populated viewpoints, similar to the proposed model’s behavior. Developing
a consistent benchmarking framework for such comparisons would also strengthen the
generalizability of the model.

8.2
Learnings
• Coupled optimization turned out to be less straightforward than expected. At the begin-
ning of this thesis, we anticipated focusing primarily on the technical aspects of VI. In
practice, however, our efforts were more evenly split between VI and optimization related
technicalities. We had not fully anticipated the many important and time-consuming
decisions involved, such as choosing an optimization driver, tuning that driver, selecting
a coupled optimization strategy, and deciding how to normalize LCOE and VI. These
steps proved to be far more demanding than originally assumed.
• Describing and categorizing pattern types also proved to be a challenge. It was not always
clear when something should be labeled as ”regular” versus ”irregular”, ”spread” versus
”clustered” etc. Identifying trends or grouping patterns became diﬀicult when the designs
shared similarities and differences. The line between different categories was often blurry,
which made communicating these patterns more complex than anticipated.
• On a more practical level, we also gained hands-on experience with several tools that
were new to us at the start of the project. These included TOPFARM, DTUOffshore-
CostModel, and PyWake, which were essential for the developed model, while tools like
QGIS and WindPlanner were used for supplementary visualization and spatial context.

C
PyWake Implementation
This appendix presents a more detailed and technical description of the wind resource model-
ing and wind farm layout methodology, implemented using the Python library PyWake. The
objective is to provide a transparent foundation to support replicability and potential exten-
sions of the approach. Please note that the full script underlying the proposed model was not
originally developed for public distribution. As such, it may not be fully intuitive or adhere to
best programming practices for broader usability. However, if you are interested in reviewing
the code, you may contact XX and XX for access and further information.
Wind Resource Modeling with NEWA and PyWake
The wind resource is derived from the New European Wind Atlas (NEWA) mesoscale time-
series, provided as a NetCDF file with a spatial grid resolution of 3x3 km and a temporal
resolution of 30 minutes. The data spans five years and contains time-resolved values for wind
speed (WS) and wind direction (WD) at multiple heights.
To construct the wind climate input for PyWake:
1. The timeseries data is cleansed for NaN values ans loaded using xarray.open_dataset().
2. The wind conditions at each grid point are aggregated into directional Weibull distribu-
tions using a custom function (NEWA_timeseries_to_Weibull_localwind). This func-
tion computes the Weibull A and k parameters, along with sector frequency, for each 1◦
wind direction bin.
3. The resulting data is structured into a format compatible with PyWake’s XRSite.from_pwc()
method, enabling the creation of an XRSite object.
4. The global_weibull speedup method is used when constructing the XRSite, which com-
putes directional speedup maps by comparing each location’s mean wind speed to the
median mean for the direction. This normalizes local variations while preserving Weibull
shape consistency across the domain.
This approach enables high-resolution wind resource modeling using statistically derived wind
climates, tailored to the spatial grid of the NEWA data.
Wind Turbine Modeling and Layout Definition
The turbine model is defined in PyWake using the WindTurbine class, where manufacturer-
provided power and thrust curves as a function of wind speed are specified. These curves are
used during simulation to compute both energy output and wake effects.
Turbine positions are defined as coordinate pairs (typically in meters) representing the spatial
layout of the wind farm. These are passed to a WindFarmModel object, which combines the
wind resource (XRSite) and turbine definitions to simulate farm-wide performance.

Wake effects are modeled using a specific wake model. The wake model calculates the wind
speed deficits and added turbulence at each turbine based on the upstream flow conditions and
turbine locations.
Finally, the Annual Energy Production (AEP) is computed using the WindFarmModel.aep()
method, which runs the simulation over all defined wind sectors, wind speeds, and turbine
positions, returning energy yield estimates that account for wakes, directional variation, and
local wind speed differences.
