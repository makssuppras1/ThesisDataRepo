Abstract 
Frameless window design showed significant improvements in terms of reduced heat loss and energy 
consumption. The fixed frameless window is designed by incorporating the glazing unit with optimized 
spacer and reduced polysulfide in the prefabricated walls. This creates an opportunity to avoid window 
manufacturers, which limit the improvements of energy efficient windows and results in extra expenses.   
When the glazing unit is incorporated in the prefabricated wall, the heat loss of the regular frame is 
eliminated, and low thermal linear losses in the connection between the wall and glazing unit is achieved.  
Quadruple glazing units were designed for further improvements of the thermal performance. The results 
showed that the biggest improvements were achieved when removing the frame, and the further 
optimization of the thermal performance when using quadruple glazing compared to triple glazing were 
small. The savings in insulation use is not cost effective due to the current extra expenses of quadruple 
glazing, compared to triple glazing that already exist on the market.  
To address the need for venting, 3 solutions for frameless windows were created.  
Solution 1: Fixed glazing and optimized energy efficient hatches that provides effective ventilation, without 
the risk of burglary or penetration of rain.  
Solution 2: Fixed glazing and frameless sliding windows using magnetic seal to open and close. 
solution 3: Fixed glazing and sliding door using same principle as sliding window. 
The new design of the improved windows and hatches were implemented in the final design of a 150 m2 
long house, that has the same floor plan as the single-family house from the collection of buildings in the 
building regulation. This type house is used to determine the cost efficiency of the standard for Nearly Zero 
Energy buildings, which concluded that it is not cost efficient to build according to it.  
This type house is chosen as the reference house that assembles the design of current type houses in 
Denmark.  
This thesis analyzes the reference house in terms of the use of insulation according to CCE (Cost of 
conserved energy) to reach a maximum of 20 kWh/m2 per year in energy consumption, while achieving the 
required comfortable indoor climate. The cost of insulation use is compared to the optimized models. The 
purpose was to prove that cost-effective Nearly Zero Energy houses can be achieved with frameless 
windows, effective ventilation through hatches/sliding window/door, optimized prefabricated wall 
elements and roof elements constructed of wood I-profiles that reduces the heat loss from the studs.   
The indoor climate was investigated for the models to ensure that overheating is limited to under 100 
hours of 27 °C a year, according to the thermal requirements of BR18 and DS/EN 15251. 
==PAGE:4==
An airflow rate of 6 Air change per Hour (ACH) was applied at the maximum rate to avoid draught.  
Overheating was avoided and a comfortable indoor climate was achieved, due to an effective ventilation 
setup with optimized mechanical ventilation and smart opening function of the designed hatches and 
sliding windows, and high daylight intake but lower radiation area due to high Light transmittance of the 
glazing. 
The final design with solution 1 with hatches was designed and analyzed, to determine the insulation use 
needed to achieve a maximum of 20 kWh/m2 a year according to CCE.  
The calculated average value of the thermal bridges around the edges of the glazing and the hatches is 
equal to 0.05436 W/mK.  
Compared to the reference model, the optimized model resulted in savings of 34560 DKK for windows and 
around 10000 DKK for cost of insulation.   
15 cm thinner walls were achieved for the optimized model, compared to reference walls constructed of 
brick stone and concrete inner walls, which influences the amount of daylight that enters the rooms.  
The final design with solution 2 and 3 with sliding windows/door was designed and analyzed to determine 
the needed insulation use to achieve a maximum of 20 kWh/m2 a year in energy consumption. The average 
value of the thermal bridges around the edges of the glazing’s is 0.05292 W/mK.  
The results were compared to a reference model with identical optimized building envelope and window 
layout but used typical windows. This comparison highlighted, that just by removing the frame, the thermal 
performance of the building improved and resulted in savings of nearly 13000 DKK for insulation. Since the 
glazing unit is incorporated in the prefabricated walls, window manufacturers are avoided, which resulted 
in savings around 30000 DKK for windows.  
The prefab building elements shortens the building process and project time, which results in further 
savings. The amount is not identified in the report.  
The purpose of this master thesis was to prove the potentials of optimizing the design methods of the 
building envelope and windows. The optimized design solutions presented in this thesis, create a more 
cost-effective nearly zero energy modern type house, with classic simplicity and beautiful architectural 
impact. 
The continued improvements of the components in the building sector, relies on making the building code 
for Nearly Zero energy house the minimum requirement.

1 Introduction  
The introduction chapter will introduce the motivation behind the thesis, aim of the thesis and the 
structure of the thesis. 
For the following thesis, frameless window designs will be developed based on the optimization of the 
architectural value, air tightness, indoor environment, energy consumption and cost benefits.  
A single Nearly Zero Energy family type House of the collection from the building regulation will be 
optimized, with focus on frameless window solutions and developed prefabricated components of the 
building envelope. The results are afterwards compared to the reference model, to determine the 
improved energy performance and cost efficiency.  
1.1 Motivation 
With all the climate changes happening around us, which influences our daily lives, it is necessary to do 
something to lower the CO2 emission in the atmosphere. Buildings are responsible for 40% of the energy 
consumption, and 36% of CO2 emissions in EU [1], which represents a great responsibility to improve the 
energy performance of new buildings.  
The master Thesis from June 2018, written by Camilla Bech, and Hildegunn Skogstad showed lots of 
potential in decreasing the energy use, when the standard frame solution of the windows is removed, since 
it reduced the heat loss through the window assembly remarkably.  
The building regulation BR18 still only has an optional building class for NZEB buildings. This influences the 
product market and their motivation to improve their products and maximize the efficiency. 
The motivation behind this thesis is to challenge the perspective of making the planned energy frame of 20 
kWh/m2 official, by showing ways of improvements of typical Danish Houses that could be integrated in the 
designs and would have a positive effect in the energy frame.  
One way of reducing the magnitude of the demand of heating in the cold season, is through better window 
design. In the existing market, there is already a large offer of energy efficient glazing units that are 
developed to achieve improved thermal comfort within buildings, while simultaneously reducing the 
energy use. The window manufacturers on the other hand are not committed to improve the thermal 
properties of the frame of the windows, which is around 20% of the window area and increases the heat 
loss of the window because of their bad insulation coefficient.  
 A frameless window construction is an important aspect to enhance the thermal performance of windows. 
This thesis will focus on the new concept for frameless windows that reduces the heat loss through 
windows significantly, by completely removing the frame around the windows and incorporate the glazing 
unit directly in the prefabricated walls. Different solutions of venting will be designed.  
The architectural value of the solutions is of importance to visually attract the buyers.

1.2 Aim  
The aim of this thesis is to design and analyze solutions for frameless windows with a high architectural 
value, to remove the high amount of heat loss through a standard window frame, which in the end will 
decrease the energy use of the building. This will create an opportunity to design prefabricated energy 
efficient modern Danish buildings, which fits the future building regulations requirement of a maximum 
energy use of 20 kWh/m2, with less use of insulation material. This will reduce the thickness of the 
construction parts and therefore be a more cost effective and a more attractive solution for a Nearly Zero 
Energy House. With these developments, this master thesis aims to influence the decision behind making 
the 2020 building regulation a requirement instead of optional, so that the building sector is required to 
continue to improve the energy efficiency of their products.

2.1 Development of standard type Houses  
In the discussions of the connections between buildings and the surroundings made by architects, they 
highlight that most traditional buildings separate the interior space from the outdoors. Seen from a 
historical view, buildings functioned primarily as shelter from the elements and from strangers. Advances in 
technology enabled modern architects of 100 years ago to eliminate the walls in the interior and create 
wide open window-walls of glass. [1] 
Danish people are known to be very preoccupied with their homes. Danes love their homes which is 
experienced through there many conversations about it, and the large amount of their income they spend 
on it. Danes spend most of their time indoor and the biggest reason for that, is the North Atlantic climate in 
Denmark with low temperatures, especially in the months from October-April. The popular word hygge 
(cozy time) indoors which is the favorite activity for Danes sums it up.  
Single family houses are the most popular type of housing in Denmark since they represent almost 50% of 
the total dwellings. [2] 
The architecture of typical Danish houses is highly diverse due to the various eras characterized as the 
classic villa, the master building House, the bungalow and the package-deal house. These type houses are 
found in any town in Denmark, but it is common that the classic villa dominate the areas of single-family 
houses. [2] 
The single-family houses in Denmark gives the people a great degree of freedom of design and use, since it 
presents few constrains in terms of the decorations people can choose and how they use their homes. But 
many clients prefer the comfort of traditional styles of architecture, since their house is a life investment 
and a key element in their ’life project’, which shouldn’t differ too much from the market when it´s time to 
sell again.   
Danish type Houses can vary in the design form, but what characterizes it is the brick facades. Many 
different colors of brick are commonly used. It provides an aesthetic, protective and long-lasting value that 
is highly valued in the Danish Type House market.

2.2.1 Multipaned energy-efficient glazing system and methods for improved edge seal unit  
Research has shown that 30-50 % of transmission losses through building envelopes occur through 
windows. [5] A significantly amount of energy can be saved by improving the energy performance of the 
windows.  
 Many improvements in glazing systems have been achieved the past decades, as low emissivity coatings 
and gas fills of insulating gas units. In highly insulated glazing, a special insulated gas is used between the 
panes for the reduction of convection and heat conduction. These reduces the heat loss through the 
glazing. Insulating glazing units consist of individual components, which includes the glass panes, gas fill and 
edge seal. [5]. The edge seal unit which consist of a spacer bar, a desiccant and a sealant, has a main 
function to keep an equal distance between the glass panes, while prevent infiltration of water vapor or 
exfiltration of the gas filled cavity between the glass panes. The sealant structurally bonds the glass panes 
and spacer bar together. It provides moisture vapor and gas diffusion resistance on a high level. Glass 
movements is accommodated due to its flexible material. [5] 
Figure 2.2.1 illustrates an example of the components of the edge seal in a double pane glazing unit. 
Figure 2.2.1: An example of a double pane glazing unit with illustration of the edge seal unit [6] 
 
The Spacer bar has a typical width that varies between 4 mm and 8 mm. The spacer bar thickness that is 
most common on the market are between 12 mm and 14 mm. 
The design of the spacer bar and its properties will be discussed under the presentation of the ´two box 
model´. Desiccants can be used to absorb moisture and organic vapor that may be in the interpane space, 
to prevent the inside glass surfaces from fogging because of condensation. Desiccants that are commonly 
==PAGE:17==
Side 12 af 217 
 
used in the IG industry are molecular sieves or silica gel blended with molecular sieves. Desiccant can be 
integrated in the edge seal design. It can also be used as a fill in hollow spacer bars.  
Research has shown that the heat loss through multipaned windows can be reduced significantly by 
improving the edge seal. [5]. 
Edge seal unit as a two-box model 
A two-box model with an equivalent thermal conductivity has been developed with the approach to 
simplify the model of an edge seal unit. The edge seal as illustrated in Figure 2.2.2 is simplified and 
modelled as two boxes, such that it results in the same heat flow as the actual edge seal construction. The 
box with secondary sealant defines the polysulfide sealant with a thermal conductivity of 0.4 W/(mK). The 
second box replaces the spacer profile with the equivalent thermal conductivity as for the detailed model.  
Figure 2.2.2: Illustration of a two-box model with equivalent thermal conductivity [5] 
 
The method of the two-box model was tested for several edge constructions, where the equivalent thermal 
conductivity was found by having the two-box model replace the actual edge construction with a spacer 
profile. Results showed that the difference between the values calculated with the two-box model and the 
detailed edge construction were less than 3.1 %. [5] The method is considered reliable.  
For the calculation of the thermal conductivity of the edge seal, a two-dimensional simulation program 
(COMSOL) will be used.

2.2.3 Glazing with integrated solar shading 
The modern design of buildings is defined by light-filled spaces, clean lines and uninterrupted views to 
maintain a visual connection between outside and inside, while forming a barrier to factors like wind and 
rain. This is the reason why glass is such an important component in the facades, which is why architects 
strive for greater transparency and a high light transmittance (LT). 
Due to todays increased use of glass in architecture, it is imperative to consider the occupants comfort in 
the buildings. An issue of discomfort is overheating that is caused by excessive solar heat gain from the 
glass facades. Solar control coating can be used to minimize solar heat gain.  
Pilkington offer ´´a range of solar control products with a wide range of visible light transmittance, reduced 
solar transmittance and low emissivity in one product´´. [15] 
 
 
Figure 2.2.12: An illustration from Pilkington of solar coated glass [15] 
 
The solar coated glass is made to manage the solar heat radiation by three mechanisms as shown in Figure 
2.2.12. The reflectance which represents the proportion of solar radiation that gets reflected back to the 
exterior. The direct transmittance that defines the proportion of solar radiation that gets transmitted 
directly through the glass. The absorptance defines the proportion of solar radiation absorbed by the glass. 
[16] The solar gain is represented by the g-value which is lower since the coating reflects away a large 
degree of the suns heat, while allowing sunlight to pass through the window, which is represented by the 
light transmittance LT value.  
==PAGE:25==
Side 20 af 217 
 
The use of solar coated glazing helps reduce overheating, but the main challenge is that it also causes a 
reduction in light transmittance, which results in a larger glazing area to compensate for it.   
Investigations of the impact on indoor climate and energy consumption of a type house building, when 
using different coating were made in the master thesis ´Multi pane glazing in frameless windows´ by Hajan 
Farhan. [10] It was found that a good balance between the g-value and LT of the glazing when using 
quadruple glazing was obtained by using Optitherm S3 OW from Pilkington with an LT value of 70% and g-
value of 0.47, [17] that reduced overheating, without the need of huge glazing area to obtain sufficient 
daylight in the rooms. [10]

2.2.5 Prefabricated Houses and elements  
Prefabricated elements have shown many benefits in a time where energy efficient and cost-optimal 
solutions are essential to fulfill Class 2020 buildings that are the Danish Nearly Zero energy Buildings.  
Following the need for a higher productivity within the building industry, it is believed that there will be an 
increase in the use of prefabricated solutions in the coming years.  
Prefabrication have solutions to reduce time, materials and labor, which are three factors that the overall 
cost of a building project can be simplified to depend on.  
Production that happens in a factory increases productivity and have advantages over traditional on-site 
construction, which leads to shortened project time. This is caused by the fact that prefab modular and 
prefab building elements are built indoor and therefore doesn’t get affected by the weather. The units are 
of higher quality because it is closely supervised. The work on site can be done at the same time as the 
units are being built in the factory. [20] 
Prefabricated wall elements potentially save material costs, because they are delivered based on a pre-
defined need, which reduces the materials in a project compared to the work on-site, and because of 
better precision in the work in the fabric, there is less waste in the off-site production.  
Prefabricated elements that are transported to the site means less work to be done on site. This reduces 
the extent of the activities on site which results in less need for human resources. 
There are economic advantages in the reduced construction time and project time due to reduced 
expenses as electrical power, rigs, scaffolds, barracks and containers for storage, which are costs that are 
paid per day or week [20].  
Prefabrication is not a new idea and the prefab/modular industry has been around for decades with the 
purpose to be cost-reducing in the housing market. The post-war prefab buildings had that same purpose 
of precise modern structures created in clean factories and shipped to site. But in reality, they were often 
poorly designed and poorly constructed. [21] 
Prefabrication is experiencing a resurgence in the 21st century. In recent years prefabricated Houses has 
generated more attention because of the more creative and edgy designs from popular architects. Prefab 
pioneers have in recent years been working on developing the modular method into a viable, alternative 
system of construction, with focus on lower cost and quicker construction, while still maintaining excellent 
craftsmanship and quality, which in recent years has got attention seen from the number of readers and 
interests of the top modular housing articles from BUILDER over the past years. [21] 
It is reported in a 2011 Globe and Mail article that while “in Scandinavia up to 80 per cent of housing is 
prefab, and modular housing of this type is common in Germany and other parts of Europe.” [20]

4.2 Air tightness and moisture control 
Air leakage through the building envelope results in infiltration of cold air through various openings and 
venues into the building interiors and warm air out. This has a considerable impact on the demand of 
heating which effects the energy use and thereby energy costs of building. Infiltration can result in 
moisture accumulation problems, since moisture in the air transports through the leaks and can cause

The building envelope must be secured against accumulation of moisture that is caused due to moisture

4.3 Thermal performance 
The thermal performance of a building refers to the various heat exchange processes of the envelope 
insulation and window performance, which controls the indoor environment from the external 
environment and climate fluctuations. To obtain the requirements for energy savings, requirements for 
minimum insulation has been made to decrease heating and cooling demand, which plays an essential part 
of the overall energy consumption in the 2020 energy frame.  
Thermal transmittance, thermal bridge and thermal capacity contributes to the thermal performance of a 
building and will be further explained in the following. [33] 
 
4.3.1 Thermal transmittance  
It is of high importance to ensure that the building envelope has an acceptable thermal performance. The 
minimal requirement for the thermal transmittance for nearly-zero energy buildings for one floor buildings, 
is that the dimensioning transmission loss per m2 climate screen is maximum 3.7 W. The transmission 
losses through the area of windows and doors are not included in the calculation. 
The specific thermal loss is calculated for every component of the housing-unit. The sum of the calculated 
specific thermal losses determines the total specific thermal losses of the bearing components and the 
ventilation loss for the whole envelope. [34]

between the invested improved materials and the cost of energy saved.  
 
4.3.2 Thermal bridges 
Thermal bridges within the building envelope is an area with increased heat flow density compared to the 
surrounding materials in the cross-section. Thermal bridges are classified as point or linear thermal bridges. 
Thermal bridges results in increased energy use due to increased heat flow, which is normally 
perpendicular to the surface and is changed because of an increase or decrease of heat transfer in or out of 
the envelope.  [36] 
The effect of the thermal bridges becomes more substantial in a well-insulated building and plays a crucial 
role regarding building energy efficiency.  
The thermal bridges that causes thermal losses of the building can be minimized, and one solution is to 
insulate the junctions that is sensitive with materials of low conductivity.  
4.3.3 Thermal Capacity  
The thermal mass in a building is represented by the effective heat capacity, also referred to as the thermal 
capacity. [37] The thermal mass describes the materials ability to absorb and store heat energy. High 
density materials such as concrete, bricks and tiles are considered to have greater thermal mass, whereas 
lightweight materials such as timber have low thermal mass. A calculation of a type house with both high 
and low thermal mass were made in DTU BYG Report R-042, where they concluded that the energy savings 
that is achieved due to a building envelope with greater thermal mass, compared to a building envelope of 
light weight construction is relatively small. However, the thermal capacity does affect the overheating.

4.5 Daylight 
An essential factor in ensuring a good indoor environment is daylight.  
Documentations of sufficient daylight in the rooms is required. Sufficient daylight is according to BR18 
obtained when the glazing area corresponds to 10 % of the floor area, if there are no shadows from the 
surroundings. The specified glazing area has to be corrected for shading from surroundings and reduced 
light transmittance. [40] 
An alternative way to document that there is sufficient daylight based on the Spatial Daylight Autonomy 
(sDA) is, when the illumination intensity of daylight in 50% of the floor area of the rooms for minimum 50% 
of the daylight hours is minimum 300 lux, which is equal to a daylight factor of 2.1%. [41] 
The daylight factor describes the ratio of the illuminance on a plane surface inside a room and the external 
horizontal surface under an overcast sky, that only takes the diffuse radiation into account. [40] 
It is believed that sufficient daylight is obtained with a glazing area of 17% of the floor area when the light 
transmittance LT is equal to 75%. The area of the glazing has to be corrected according to formular (4.5), If 
the LT is reduced.

5.2.1 Improved design of the glazing unit  
Research has shown that 30-50 % of transmission losses through building envelopes occur through 
windows. A significantly amount of energy can be saved, by improving the energy performance of the 
windows. [5]   
The designed glazing type for this project as seen in Figure 5.2.1, is based on the prototype of a quadruple 
glazing presented as part of the MEM4WIN research, which was presented in the state of the art.  
The glass type of the 4-pane glazing is made of the developed thin glass membranes. The thin glass sheets 
of high strength and flexibility prevents breakage losses, that would occur due to four layers of untreated 
glass. The glass has a thickness of 2 mm for inner and middle panes, which is due to current availability of 
low-e coated glass. The outer panes are 4 mm due to the local wind load requirements. This thickness is 
determined by the smallest dimension of the window geometry, which is 1.1 m.  
The research done in the state of the art, showed the importance of using the proper cavity thickness and 
material. The lowest U-value for the MEM4WIN prototype was achieved, by having 20 mm thick cavities of 
90% argon and 10% dry air between the four panes. [1] The four glass panes are structurally held together 
by an edge seal along the perimeters that consist of a spacer and sealant.  The design of the edge seal is 
inspired by the research Van Den Bergh et al. made in Energy and Buildings 58. [5] Research showed that a 
potential area to improve the overall performance of the IG edge seal unit is a ´corrugated edge seal 
design´.   
A corrugated spacer design as shown in Figure 5.2.1 increases the edge seal length for heat transfer, since 
this new design accommodates the quadruple glazing, which normally requires 3 spacer profiles. This 
design proved to contribute to better thermal performance in the edge-of-glass region. This design requires 
a development of a corrugated material that provides structural stability and lower conductivity.

6 Solution 1 of frameless windows with hatches for ventilation 
Chapter 6 contains a description of the design of the fixed frameless window and the new design of hatches 
for venting, an analysis of thermal capacity, and an analysis of the architectural impact of solution 1 of 
frameless windows with hatches for ventilation.  
The thermal capacity of the solution is calculated in the multiphysics program Comsol. 
 
6.1 Description of the design solution with the frameless window and the venting hatch 
The purpose of these solutions is to create energy-efficient and beautiful windows, especially for the design 
of type Houses, to help improve lives and protecting our planet. Since the frame around the windows 
increases the heat loss, the new solution lead to a new concept of creating windows without frames. 
The fixed frameless window as described in previous chapter is presented in a 3D illustration below.  
 
Figure 6.1.1: 3D view of the fixed window  
 
The wall plate will either consist of a 20 mm wood wall plate or 15 mm vacuum panel and 5 mm PVC cover 
depending on the results of the analysis of the heat transfer. The 3D picture above shows the vacuum-
panel and the PVC cover as a wall panel.  
==PAGE:64==
Side 59 af 217 
 
This solution prevents the window from opening and require an alternative way to ventilate and avoid 
overheating. It is required to have a ventilative cooling architectural aperture that is highly insulated, to 
make it an attractive and energy efficient solution to ventilate.  
This solution will develop the already existing ventilative cooling components that 
are on the market. As we saw in the state of the art, the concept and design of a 
louvre acoustic-grille has many benefits, but for this solution to be considered, the 
heat loss coefficient must improve. In order to make them attractive for the type 
House market, they must meet requirements as good insulated, mechanical 
openings and fire safety escape.  
 As for the windows where the frames caused a significant amount of heat loss and 
energy loss, the frame of the hatch as shown in Figure 6.1.2 which causes the 
hatch to open, results in a high heatloss coefficient factor.  
To meet the requirements for the fire safety, the hatches must be able to open 
and function as fire escape. This means that the apertures that is designed for the 
purpose of rescue, shall have a clearance height h of at least 0.6 m and a free 
width b of at least 0.5 m as mentioned in the state of the art.  
 
6.1.1 Ventilative architectural aperture (hatch) 
The ventilative architectural aperture also referred to as hatch, is designed with focus on minimizing the 
heat loss, while maintaining the several characteristic performance indicators that it has. Such as protecting 
the room from rain penetration, sound and burglary, while allowing the passage air in and extend the 
possible operative hours of ventilative cooling. 
The new design of the hatch unit which is illustrated in Figure 6.1.3, consist of the acoustical louvre grill 
component on the outside, and the mechanical opening component that consist of a vacuum-panel 
covered by GFRP (3-6) on the inside as seen in the step by step illustration. 
The insulating vacuum panel consist of a rigid core that is surrounded by a gastight enclosure, which is 
covered by a 2mm Glass fiber reinforced polyester box and plates, to protect the vacuum-panel against 
atmospheric pressure. Vacuum insulation panels (VIP) has an impressive performance that increases energy 
efficiency cause of its extremely low thermal conductivity of 0.004 W/mK [45]. 
The thermal conductivity of the vacuum panel inclusive its barrier film is found to be 0.007 W/mK. The 
thinnest aluminium folie is 7.5 my meter which equals 7.5/1000 mm = 7.5 10ି଺ m with a heat transfer of 
160 W/mK from VACUPOR will be used for this project. [45]

6.3.1 Interior design with two escape doors for the occupied rooms

with moveable/sliding inner walls as seen in Figure 6.3.2 that shows the interior design of the plan drawing.

Moveable partitions maximize the layout options because it gives the ability to physically rearrange a space 
and make it easy to reconfigure, which provides spatial opportunities. Flexible spaces support the needs of 
an ever-changing family and accommodate a variety of uses. Flexible homes are attractive because it 
responds to the changing circumstances that happens in a lifetime of a family or when it is time to sell. The 
architecture transforms, adapts, moves and interacts with the end user and makes the design 
multifunctional.

The energy performance of the building is also depended on the occupant behavior. Several field test 
studies that has been made all over the world shows, that the energy consumption of buildings is higher in 
reality than what was expected from calculations. [49] This gap could be bridged by implementing sensors 
in the hatches, that read the inhabitants movements and open and closes according to it.  
It is becoming more and more common within our current society to incorporate intelligent building 
systems, which makes the building able to react and interact accordingly to its inhabitants to provide the 
best possible conditions in terms of comfort, safety, energy use and efficiency. It is therefore of interest to 
design ventilative cooling apertures that is not manual but with incorporated censors. This would be 
possible to incorporate for this design of the hatches, since the interior design of the rooms meets the fire

with the same size, slides in front of the fixed glazing unit.

9 Reference type house building  
Several evaluations of the cost-efficiency of the energy requirements in the Danish building regulation has 
been made, with the attempt to analyze the savings potential in relation to tightening the requirements, to 
improve the energy efficiency of buildings.  
The analysis of the cost optimality of the energy requirement 2020 building code according to ´Cost-optimal 
levels of minimum energy performance requirements in the Danish Building regulations´, is based on the 
Single-family type house building seen in Figure 9.1.1.  [51] 
The reference building will be optimized to reach the 2020 requirements, by increasing the insulation in the 
building components with the method Cost of Conserved energy (CCE), and later be compared to the 
improved final solutions, with the purpose of highlighting the saving potentials of the new cost-effective 
design method of nearly zero energy type houses.

kitchen, utility room and two bathrooms.   
The external wall consists of 100 mm light concrete for the inner wall and 100 mm stone brick in the 
external façade with insulation in between. 
The ground slap is constructed of 100 mm concrete with floor heating and insulation below the concrete.

10 Prefabricated building envelopes for the final design  
Prefabricated envelopes play an essential role in terms of designing cost effective buildings, that can make 
it possible to make the integration of 2020 building code as the energy standard in Denmark economical.   
Chapter 10 includes a description of the prefab walls, floor and prefab roof that is used for the final designs, 
including the heat losses of the building envelopes.  
Finally, a description of the IDA ICE inputs of the building services for the final design is made.  
10.1 Prefabricated walls  
The prefabricated walls for the final design are constructed of I-profiled timber studs, with brick cladding on 
the exterior and gypsum on the interior. The I-profiled studs showed to be the most energy efficient and 
environmentally friendly wall element, since it reduces the volume of the studs, which decreases the heat 
loss and achieves the slimmest construction, while providing the necessary structural strength. [23]  
The vapor barrier is an important part of the building envelope. Moisture accumulation problems is the 
results of moisture problems. Condense is caused when moisture in the air, transports through the leaks. 
This has an impact on the indoor quality and increases the risk of rot wooden structure.  
 
 
Figure 10.1.1: Horizontal section of a 1.2m wall for the final design  
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:145==
Side 140 af 217 
 
 
Figure 10.1.2: Vertical section of the wall for the final design 
 
A continuous vapor barrier towards the interior is placed behind the (45x45mm) wood internal battens pr. 
600 mm to allow for cables, without the risk of damaging the vapor barrier. 
A 25 mm ventilated air gap under the exterior cladding is used for the external walls, to establish a two-
step seal against driven rain. A windboard from cembrit is used. The board is constructed of grey cement 
and limestone filler. It is reinforced with a fiber material to increase its strength. The board has a very low 
vapor transmission resistance, which enables the moisture that is on the inside of the building to pass 
through. [54] Cembrit highly recommends having a maximum of 600 mm between the wood battens, which 
the Cembrit wind stopper is fixed to. The plates are taped to ensure a tight connection. This is illustrated in 
their data sheet brochure and shown in Figure 10.1.3. The load bearing I-profiles is placed with a distance 
up to 1200 mm. This required to have horizontal wood battens (45x45mm) every 600mm that is attached 
to the I-beams as illustrated in the horizontal section of the wall for the final design in Figure 10.1.2.

10.3 Prefabricated roof  
The prefabricated flat roof is constructed as a ´box element`. The load bearing timber I-profiles is placed 
every 600 mm and is attached to ply wood plates, that help transfer the load from the roof and the I-
profiles to the load bearing I-profiles in the exterior walls. The slightly ventilated cavity between the 45x45 
wooden battens of the I-profile ensures a proper ventilation. The box element is closed off with an OSB 
plate covered by asphalt roofing. The second layer of 45x45 mm wood battens with the OSB plate that has 
the required slope of 2.5 cm pr. m is delivered separately to be installed on the building site. This optimized 
design of the prefabricated ventilated flat roof can prevent damage of the ´box element´ since leaks of the 
second layer can be spotted and changed, before damage happens to the whole construction.

10.4.3 Optimized mechanical ventilation system  
This thesis applies an improved mechanical ventilation system that is efficient, robust and performs better. 
The mechanical ventilation is implemented in IDA ICE by defining an Air Handing Unit (AHU) as seen in 
Figure 9.4.1, that has a total Specific Fan Power (SFP) of 0.8 kW (m3/s), which corresponds to a value of 800 
J/m3. The AHU consists of two fans that each has an efficiency of 60% and 0.4 kW (m3/s) as Specific Fan 
Power (SFP).  
==PAGE:158==
Side 153 af 217 
 
 
 
Figure 9.4.1: Air Handling Unit (AHU) defined in IDA ICE 
Based on the requirement from BR15 of 0.03 L/s/m2, the total air flow of the system for 150 m2 is 45 L/s. It 
is considered very rare that all the wet rooms would be occupied at the same time, which would need a 
total air flow of 60 l/s for two bathrooms, a utility room and a kitchen according to the requirements. This 
situation is therefore not taken into consideration.  
The Heat Exchanger that controls the air temperature has a heat recovery efficiency of 85% and the 
maximum flow is set to 0.045 m3/s which is equal to 45 l/s.   
The strategy consists of only supplying the dry rooms which is the bedrooms, living room and dining room, 
and extracting air from the wet rooms such as the kitchen, bathrooms and utility room. 
Acoustic dampening air vents are integrated into doorways and interior walls, so that the supplied air to the 
´ dry rooms´ moves towards the ´ wet rooms´ where it is extracted. 
The vents in IDA ICE was emulated by the door’s leakages. The default value for the doors between 
corridor, kitchen, dining room and living room is set to 0.02 m2, because a large exchange of air is expected, 
whereas for the other doors, a reasonable value is set to 0.01 m2.

the total number of ducts which is 8. The manifold has 8 duct attachments. The ventilation system has 8 air 
terminals for both supply and exhaust. The kitchen, living room and master bedroom have two ducts, 
whereas the rest of the rooms have one duct.

10.5 Natural ventilation  
The designed ventilation hatches and sliding windows are openable and provides the rooms with natural 
ventilation of a minimum air-change of 0.5 h-1 and a maximum of 6 h-1 when it is completely open.  
The hatch was created as a glazing unit in IDA ICE, but with a minimal Light transmission and g-value that is 
close to 0.  
The setup of the controller system that is used for the opening of the hatch and the sliding window is 
illustrated in Figure 9.5.1.  
Three stages are defined which identifies the summer period, transmission period and winter period. The 
opening of the hatches and windows for each of the periods is determined on signals that is given in IDA ICE 
as Rule A, B and C, which tells the controller system to open by a fraction of the area. 
Rule A defines the period where the heating is activated, whereas the hatches/windows are closed all the 
time, since it is relied on the mechanical system to provide a comfortable indoor climate. It will only open 
when the temperature exceeds 25 °C.  
Rule B defines the transition from summer to winter that is defined from 01 May to 15 June and from 01 
Sep to 01 Oct. This transmission period is necessarily to define, in order to avoid negative interaction 
between the systems. The opening elements completely open when the temperature exceeds 25°C, but 
closes completely when the temperature inside drops below 23°C.   
Rule C defines the summer period from 15 June to 01 Sep. This is the period where no energy is used for 
heating and mechanical ventilation since they are deactivated. The hatches/opening window systems are 
designed (manually openings) to provide the air flow rates to obtain a comfortable indoor climate.

14 Summary and discussion 
Huge potentials of savings were found by optimizing the current design process and methods of 
constructing the building envelope and windows, which results in more cost-efficient design of nearly zero 
energy type houses. 
The current process of designing a type house as for the analyzed reference house, involves the steps as 
illustrated in Figure 14.1.  
 
Figure 14.1: Illustration of the current process for a typical type house design with rational windows and bricks on the exterior  
 
The new design of the frameless windows and the design of prefabricated walls creates an opportunity to 
avoid the window manufacturers, by incorporating the glazing unit directly to the walls in the factory. This 
is illustrated in Figure 14.2. The cost analysis showed that around 30000 DKK is saved by this developed 
process and design. The reduced heat loss due to the removed frames, tightens the building envelope and 
reduces the energy consumption. The cost analysis of the insulation uses in the building envelope, to reach 
a maximum of 20 kWh/m2 pr. year, results in savings around 13000 DKK.  
The prefabrication of the elements of the building envelope and the prefab modules of the bathrooms 
reduces project time, materials and labor, which results in a reduced overall cost of the building project. 
These further savings are not calculated in this thesis but is an important factor of the cost optimal nearly 
zero energy type house. 
==PAGE:196==
Side 191 af 217 
 
Figure 14.2: Illustration of the optimized process and design that avoids window manufacturer by using frameless windows and 
prefab building elements which results in more cost efficient nearly zero energy type houses 
 
The frameless window offers uninterrupted view by removing the frame of the window, which helps 
dissolve the boundary between exterior and interior, and allows the residents to experience the full 
potential of connection to the outside. This helps creating living environments with clean lines, timeless 
stile, comfort and elegance clean lines. 
The new designed hatch provides an effective ventilation, without the risk of burglary during night venting 
or when venting during unoccupied hours. The hatches provide a unique touch to the façade. Another 
solution is the designed frameless sliding windows that provides a more modern look.  
The layout of the glazing that is the most cost effective and maximizes the daylight in the rooms, results in a 
==PAGE:197==

classic simplicity that gives a beautiful architectural impact. The long-lasting value and visual impact of the 
exterior in the existing type houses, is obtained by using brick cladding. The use of the optimized materials 
for the walls, results in 15 cm thinner walls compared to reference walls constructed of brick stone and 
concrete inner walls.    
 
These optimized solutions create cost-effective, energy-efficient and comfortable modern homes with 
classic simplicity and beautiful architectural impact. The optimized results combine functionality with 
aesthetics and creates innovative and stylish living spaces with prefabricated elements.

15 Conclusion 
It is concluded that a more cost effective Nearly Zero Energy Buildings design that saves close to 43000 
DKK, compared to reference nearly zero energy houses, is obtained by implementing these parameters: 
 Replacing typical three pane wood-alu windows with three pane frameless windows, with a high LT 
value and optimized layout that maximizes the daylight in the rooms. This was found to be the 
major saving potential with savings of around 30000 DKK for the fixed windows alone, and savings 
around 13000 for insulation cost due to improved tightness of the building.  
 The use of hatches for effective ventilation, without the risk of burglary during night venting or 
when venting during unoccupied hours. It is also possible to use the designed frameless sliding 
windows to obtain a more modern aesthetic look.  
 Prefabricated roof and wall elements constructed of load bearing wood I-profiles that reduces the 
heat loss of the studs and reduces the thickness of the components. 
 Brick cladding to obtain the architectural impact and long-lasting value of brick facades, while 
reducing the thickness of the walls with 15 cm, compared to reference walls constructed of brick 
stone and concrete inner walls.    
 The prefab building elements shortens the building process and project times, which saves money 
when creating cost-effective nearly zero energy modern type house, with classic simplicity and 
beautiful architectural impact. 
The purpose of this master thesis was to prove the potentials of optimizing the design methods of the 
building envelope and windows, which results in more cost-efficient design of nearly zero energy type 
houses. The continues improvements of the components in the building industry, relies on making the 
building code 2020 the minimum requirement.

15 Future work 
Future work should develop mock-ups to test the presented concepts of frameless fixed windows and 
hatch or sliding window for venting. It is ideal to produce the prototypes to prove the possibilities of 
creating frameless windows where the glazing unit is integrated in the walls and address some potential 
structural concerns. It is also an opportunity to address potential concerns, in terms of using magnetic seal 
to open and close the hatches and sliding windows. 
 
Furthermore, it is of potential to research and develop windows with very high light transmission, since it 
can provide a sufficient amount of daylight in the rooms with less glazing area. Less glazing area equals less 
radiation of heat which showed to have a significant effect on overheating.  
 
The development of the spacer design and material to achieve lower conductivity is interesting for future 
research.
