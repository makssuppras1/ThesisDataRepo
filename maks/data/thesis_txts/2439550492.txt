Abstract
Remote sensing technologies provide opportunities to monitor surface water resources
even in poorly gauged basins and remote areas. This study explores the potential of
remote sensing data usage in the Kafue Flats region. The study area is especially inter-
esting due to its ecological and socio-economical importance. Using data freely available
from ESA Sentinel missions, a detailed water mask from Sentinel-1 radar imagery, sup-
ported by Sentinel-2 optical imagery, was derived. The obtained surface water extent
enabled the extraction of altimetry water level observations from Cryosat-2 satellite
mission.
51 Sentinel-1 SAR and 32 Sentinel-2 MSI images were processed to obtain the dy-
namic water mask of the Kafue Flats region. The processing of Sentinel-1 SAR included
calibration, speckle filtering and terrain correction. NDVI indices ware calculated to
obtain useful water to non-water content information from Sentinel-2 MSI images. The
images were then processed with the water masking algorithm, such that only two classes
of pixels were left: water and non-water areas. The common part of the SAR and NDVI
mask allowed to obtain 13 dynamic water masks of the Kafue Flats regions.
The obtained water masks have shown that with the use of the masking algorithm
even the parts of the river with a width below 20 m and reservoirs with a surface below
1 km² can be extracted. The attempt to map the selected area of the Kafue Wetland
has shown the potential of SAR in extracting vegetation-covered areas of water bodies.
Once the surface water extent of the Kafue Flats was mapped, the Cryosat-2 observations
were extracted over the water bodies. For all the studied Kafue Flats water bodies, the
surface water elevation was examined using the static water mask. The assessment of
the dynamic water mask was evaluated on the biggest reservoir of the Kafue Flats- the
Itezhi-tezhi.
3456 single Cryosat-2 observations were extracted over the water bodies. Water level
observations of all the studied reservoirs were modeled using R package tsHydro. The
obtained precision of mean water level of 0.7 cm for the Itezhi-tezhi Reservoir and 3.6 cm
for Manyeke Lake, generally agrees with the precision obtained for studies with similar
conditions. The consistency was found in seasonal water level variations of the reservoirs.
The impact of El Niño event on the system was observed, suggesting that the obtained
results are realistic.
The Kafue River’s level profile was investigated, showing the agreement with the
topography. The tested approach on the selected area of the Kafue Wetland has shown
the potential of using Cryosat-2 over the wetlands. The water storage for the Itezhi-tezhi
was computed using the reservoir’s area from the water mask and the modeled water level
values. The result has shown the impact of the dynamic area on the changes in storage.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Changes in the availability of freshwater resources is one of the most pressing challenges
globally [10]. With an increase in population and developing human activities, the de-
mand for water already exceeds the supply in many parts of the world and more areas are
expected to face the same problem in the near future [44]. Continental water resources
are a pivotal supply for irrigation and clean water for drinking [3]. Different sectors such
as agricultural, industrial, household, recreational and the environment require water for
its use. Especially developing and poor countries are in urgent need of effective water
management plans, so that the water stress can be reduced [44]. Just as water scarcity
is an issue, its excess can also be a challenge. Floods can destroy crops, houses and
infrastructure. The same places that undergo flooding are often exposed to droughts
and drain droughts [17]. Therefore, monitoring water level and its extent is essential to
get accurate estimates of the available water resources and ensure optimal management.
At the same time, while demand for water has increased, the availability of measure-
ments of in situ water levels and discharge have declined [46]. In situ gauges provide
precise water levels [32], but are expensive and challenging to establish and maintain
due to insufficient infrastructure, inaccessibility in remote areas or undisclosed data [45].
Satellite radar altimetry has become an essential supplement to monitor inland water
bodies over the last twenty years [46]. The radar altimeter measures water surface el-
evation from space. Regular and global water elevation information obtained from the
satellite is independent of infrastructure and local policies, and therefore it can greatly
contribute to the fields of hydrology, climate change and flood or drought assessment
[46]. Initially developed to monitor water level changes over the oceans, today satellite
altimetry is a crucial contributor in monitoring changes in continental water levels - not
only lakes and reservoirs, but also rivers and wetlands [31]. The water level observation
is used to obtain surface storage changes, estimate river discharge, determine hydrologic
parameters of poorly gauged river basins and monitor the impact of climate change on
surface water [41]. As more remote sensing data is globally and publicly available, and
as water scarcity issue is increasing, there is a need to work on the most optimal and
accurate solution for monitoring water level over the globe.
This paper explores the potential of Cryosat-2 altimetry data usage over inland water
together with Sentinel-1 radar data and Sentinel-2 multispectral imagery. The explicit
objective of this study is presented in Section 1.2. Previous research with respect to the
topic is presented in Section 1.1.

1 Introduction
1.1
Context and background
Monitoring water levels from space has now been applied for more than 20 years [32]. The
altimetry measurements provide valuable results of surface water elevation estimation
that can be used for different purposes related to water management or climate change
detection.
Water level is the key quantity in hydrological research, closely correlated to rivers
discharge and water storage in lake and reservoirs. Remote sensing data has universal
spatial coverage and it is accessible freely, thus, satellite remote sensing products have
advantage over traditional observations. With high spatio-temporal resolution, it allows
to monitor water levels in lakes and rivers of a sufficient width [17].
To precisely extract remote sensing measurements over the water bodies, the surface
water extent has to be known. Water body extraction is an important part of water
resource management, thus, a number of studies investigated this research for over two
decades [21].
High-resolution water masks, derived from the remote sensing imagery, provide valu-
able information about the area of a water body[17].
Optical imagery is a common approach to get such a water mask. Ref. [27] used a
water mask based on Landsat imagery to extract Envisat altimetry data over the Zam-
bezi River basin. This allowed to obtain accurate measurements for rivers down to 80
m wide. In [26] authors explored the potential of using altimetry data from six Envisat
virtual stations to improve modeled discharge in Brahmaputra River Basin. The sur-
face water extent was also based on optical data from Landsat imagery. Ref. [25] used
Envisat observations over the Zambezi River Basin to assimilate the data to improve
model producing forecast of the river discharge.
Satellite measurements allow to monitor river levels, flood evolution and estimate
river discharge for the areas where in situ measurements are not available or not acces-
sible [18].
In [45] the performance of a drifting orbit satellite Cryosat-2 in Ganges Brahmaputra
River basin was investigated. Authors relocated annual signal observations to a series of
virtual stations. The MODIS land-water mask was used to identify water bodies. The
comparison to repeat-orbit satellite Envisat revealed that similar signal was captured by
both satellites. Therefore, a conclusion of the possibility to monitor river level by only
Cryosat-2 was made.
Since the aforementioned study, many others examples of river monitoring by Cryosat-
2 altimetry were performed. In [3], Cryosat-2 observations over the Mekong River Basin
were validated with: Cryosat-2 observations with developed classification approached, in
situ gauge data, and Envisat data. The estimated surface water elevation showed advan-
tages of using the classification approach in the region of study. However, the authors

suggested that, for some parts of the Mekong River Basin, better results of Cryosat-2
could have been obtained if high resolution-seasonal water-land mask was used (e.g. de-

Ref. [40] was one of the first studies that demonstrated Cryosat-2 observations over

Authors used Landsat 7 and Landsat 8 imagery to obtain a dynamic water mask. How-

Apart from proving a valuable resource of information on river levels, satellite altime-

tracks. Surface water extent was obtained using Landsat 8 and Landsat 7 optical im-

3.1
Surface Water Extent
Mapping and monitoring surface water bodies has gained importance with the progress
of remote sensing techniques [20]. Global information about the variations of the area of
freshwater resources is however limited [22]. Water monitoring plays an important role
in water resource management, thus, water bodies extraction has been an important
part of remote sensing science for over two decades [21].
To better understand the impact of both, anthropogenic activity and climate change
on water bodies, detailed monitoring of water bodies has to be assured [12]. The contin-
uous long-term information on surface water extent is crucial to study changes in surface
water dynamics [43].
The seasonal and long-term information on surface water extent can provide crucial
data on the basin hydrology over poorly gauged watersheds.
The obtained informa-

construction or irrigation schemes. Also, the insight on livestock watering and crop irri-

of vulnerable water-related ecosystems [1].
While working with small inland water bodies, the information about the type of
the terrain measured by the radar altimeter is not available. Therefore, accurate water
mask for the area of interest is necessary, to separate measurement taken over water
from observations over the land.
With the availability of repetitive satellite measurements the dynamic detailed mask
of surface water bodies was created. SAR imagery from Sentinel-1 mission supported
by optical imagery from Sentinel-2 MSI mission were analyzed and processed in order

3.1.1
Sentinel-1 SAR
Sentinel-1 is an imaging radar mission launched by ESA in 2014. Based on a constellation
of two SAR satellites; Sentinel-1A and Sentinel-1B, it provide continuous all-weather,
all-night C-band observations. Two satellites orbit with 180◦phase difference, at 693
km altitude. Each satellite has a 12-day repeat orbit cycle, allowing for the complete
coverage of the Earth with an interval of 6 days [36].
The mission includes a variety of SAR operational modes with different resolution
and coverage. The mode used for the project, that satellites operate primary over the
land, is Interferometric Wide swath (IW) with a swath width of 250 km and the ground
resolution of 20 m x 5 m [28]. Different modes of Setinel-1 mission can be seen in Figure
3.1.

A Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) is an imaging radar mounted to a moving plat-
form. It transmits electromagnetic waves, which after hitting the ground target can be
absorbed, scattered or reflected. The backscatter echoes collected by the radar antenna
represent a measure of scene reflectivity. This directly reflected signal is described as a
normalized measure of the reflective strength of the ground target and it is represented
as backscatter coefficient σ0. The value of σ0, apart from radar observations parameters,
depends on the physical and electrical properties of the imaged object, therefore it is a
crucial signature when it comes to distinguishing between water and land target points
[29].
Water bodies have smooth surfaces, thus strong specular reflection of them is ob-
served. The energy of the SAR beams is reflected away and therefore the backscatter
signal is likely to return only as a small portion of the transmitted signal from the satel-
lite. As a consequence, water surfaces with low backscatter coefficient should appear as
dark surfaces on the SAR imagery [4].
C-band frequency of the instrument ensures all-weather, day and night imaging ca-
pability to provide measurements at high and medium resolution. The facility to ”see
through the clouds” enables to monitor even dynamic processes on the Earth’s surface
in a reliable, continuous and global way [29]. The satellite operates in dual polarization
using two simultaneously receiving channels (HH/HV or VV/VH) [28]. The polarization
refers to the orientation of the satellite transmitter or receiver with respect to the Earth’s
surface. Letters H and V correspond to horizontal and vertical radar pulses; the first
letter describes the polarization of the emitter, while the second represents the receiver’s
polarization. Images for this project were acquired in interferometric wide swath mode
and with a dual polarization in VV and VH.
Different acquisition modes and the level of processing are available for the Sentinel-
1 SAR observations. All the data acquired for this project are Level-1 GRD (Ground
Range Detected) in high spatial resolution (HR) 20 m x 22 m (range x azimuth). SAR
multi-looked products are projected to the ground range with respect to the Earth ellip-
soid model WGS84 and include complete geo-reference information [7].
All the Sentinel-1 SAR imagery data were obtained through Copernicus Open Access
Hub server that provides free access to Sentinel-1, Sentinel-2 and Sentinel-3 user prod-
ucts. Images from April 2015 to March 2018 were downloaded over the Kafue Flats area
and pre-processed in ESA SNAP processing toolbox, that is a specific tool for processing
and analysis Sentinel data. While downloading images of the study area, it appeared
that the whole region of the Kafue Flats consists of four SAR scenes. Thirteen dates
were selected - for these dates, three images were always taken from the same orbit, at
the same day, while the fourth image, that covers the Kafue Gorge Dam, was always
taken from the different orbit, usually with a shift of +/- 5 days. Two of the dates are
missing part/all of the Kafue Gorge Dam scenery, as no data for this region was avail-
able. Nonetheless, it was decided that for the purpose of the project, this data would
still be analyzed. In total 51 SAR images were downloaded and processed.

The first step of pre-processing SAR images in SNAP is to apply the orbit file. Since
the satellite flying orbit track is being detected by many sensors, like mounted gyro, GPS,
and ground observations, it takes time to calculate out the precise orbit data. Therefore,
orbit data are not included in many SAR satellite data bundles and it is necessary to
apply orbit files as the first step of the pre-processing [7].
Next, the border noise was removed. Sentinel-1 GRD Level-1 products have noise
artifacts at the image borders that may affect clean and consistent time series of backscat-
ter [2]. ”Sentinel-1 GRD Border Noise Removal” algorithm available in SNAP toolbox
allowed to remove this noise.
Once the orbit file is applied and the border noise removed, the calibration is made.
This process ensures that the measurements on SAR images correspond to the intensity
of radar backscatter of the reflecting surface. The adjustment removes the outcome
of the incidence angle and product’s relative level of brightness [7]. After calibration,
images from the same orbit are assembled. The ”Slice assembly” option from SNAP
toolbox allows for the smooth combination of the calibrated scenes, including metadata,
into a merged product [8].
Further on, speckle filtering is applied. SAR images are affected by speckle that
is a granular effect with random spatial variations due to the interference of waves re-
flected from many single scatters. As a consequence, classification and segmentation of
an image is less precise [7]. Figure 3.3 presents a scene of the Kafue Flats region, before
and after pre-processing. Three images show: before calibration (3.3a), after calibration
(3.3b) and after speckle filtering (3.3c). The presented images are mirrored since the
terrain correction and reprojection were not yet applied.
Finally, Range-Doppler terrain correction was applied. This step removes the in-
fluence of topography on backscatter values. When the side-looking radar images the
rugged terrain, the geometric distortion appears. It may cause the geolocation errors,
therefore the terrain correction has to be made in order to maintain proper spatial rela-
tionship of images pixels [14]. The calibrated images can be then reprojected, mosaiced
and concatenated into one big scene that covers the whole region of the Kafue Flats.
Figure 3.4 shows terrain corrected, reprojected and mosaiced Sentinel-1 scenes of the
Kafue Flats.
All the pre-processing was made in SNAP using a Python interface, so that all the
steps could be done automatically. The code used for the processing (developed by Cé-
cile Marie Margaretha Kittel, DTU) was adjusted to the project’s purpose. Once the
pre-processing was done, all final results - calibrated and corrected Kafue Flats scenes -
were copied to DTU clusters of high performance computers (HPC). With the usage of
HPC the water mask of the study area was extracted in a much faster and convenient
way. Since the images store all the geo-spatial information, they require large amounts
of space and computing power to process them.

Both polarizations of the images, VH and VV, were analyzed. VH polarization has
higher contrast when comparing water and non-water areas, while VV polarization gives
better distinction over rough water areas.
The images processed with a masking algorithm allowed to obtain water mask for
the study area. The aim of the algorithm was to create black and white image based
on the original image. The algorithm is based on the Otsu’s thresholding method. In
principle, the Otsu’s method assumes that the input image contains only two classes
of pixels.
It calculates the optimum threshold that separates two classes from each
other. The segregation of water pixels from non-water pixels is based on the histogram
of the backscatter coefficient values. Otsu’s thresholding method finds the value of the
threshold that should lie in between the two peaks of the histogram [24].
To assure precise recognition of the threshold, additional steps were taken before
applying the Otsu’s method. First, the scene was multiplied by a negative factor that
decreases the value of each pixel linearly. Next, the exponential of the result was com-
puted, such that the pixel classes were spread out. As a consequence, the contrast of
the image was enhanced and the difference between the water and non-water pixels was
augmented. Consequently, it was easier to set up the threshold between the two peaks,
leaving only white and black pixels on the image. The effect of the masking algorithm
application on the histogram is shown in Figure 3.5.

3.1.2
Sentinel-2 MSI
Sentinel-2 European mission was launched in 2015. Twin satellites fly at the same orbit
with a phase difference 180◦, at altitude of 786 km, with a revisit frequency of 5 days. It is
wide-swath (field of view = 290 km), high resolution mission carrying the MultiSpectral
Instrument (MSI). The instrument measures the reflected solar spectral radiances in
thirteen spectral bands ranging from 10 meters to 60 meters spatial resolution, from the
visible to the shortwave infrared bands (SWIR) [8].
The main purpose of the mission is to ensure regular monitoring of global landuse/-
landcover change at local and global scale. The monitoring includes observations of
vegetation, soil and water cover, inland waterways or coastal areas. With assured high
resolution in the most visible and all of the near-infrared (NIR) bands, MSI allows to
obtain valid observations with pure water pixels even in smaller water bodies such as
narrow rivers and streams [33].
Similarly as the SAR imagery, MSI scenes were downloaded through Copernicus
Open Access Hub server. As opposed to SAR, MSI imagery is sensitive to weather con-
ditions, as the instrument cannot penetrate the clouds cover. During the rainy season
this could be particularly observed. Consequently, it was only possible to collect data
from nine dates that correspond to the obtained SAR data. As Sentinel-2 started oper-
ating in 2015, no data before August 2015 could be collected. For the whole region of
the Kafue Flats different combination of scenes were used - either two scenes comprise
the area, or similarly to the SAR data, four single images comprise the whole region. In
total 32 scenes were downloaded. Table 3.1 shows all the data that was collected for the
project and that was processed in order to get the water mask of the Kafue Flats region.

ensure values of top-of-atmosphere reflectances of the observations [8]. Due to the time
constraint, and after prior approval of the Supervisor, this data was not additionally

A test on the selected area of the Kafue Wetland
A slightly different approach was tested regarding the vegetated wetland. Wetlands,
inundated by water and covered by vegetation of aquatic plants, show different backscat-
ter characteristics from other surface water bodies. When vertically growing vegetation
is surrounded by a visible, smooth water surface, and the right angle between them is
preserved, the incoming radar signal is reflected from both surfaces, so that most of the
signal is reflected back to the sensor. The effect is called double-bounce and its occur-
rence in wetlands can be also combined with volume backscattering [47]. Therefore, the
backscattering coefficient is larger in comparison to open water surfaces and wetlands
areas appear bright on SAR images. Thus, for these formations distinctive wetland mask
was required.
As a particular example, part of the SAR Kafue Flats Wetland scene was analyzed.
To support the wetland area determination, MSI image from the same period with true
color composition was studied. Once the part of the image with wetland occurrence was
ascertained, the wetland mask was obtained. The analyzed wetland test area consists
of a river branch that is partially covered by vegetation. The recognizable shape of a
river curve was determined and the selection of the river branch was made. Manually
adjusting the threshold on the histogram, bright areas with high backscatter coefficient
values where enhanced. The obtained opposite-mask was polygonized, so that the river
covered by vegetation comprised the polygon. Applying the standard procedure, the
open water river was polygonized and combined with the vegetated river polygon, so
that the whole river branch was polygonized. For the tested area of the wetland, the
performance of Cryosat-2 was examined with regards to the type of the surface measured

3.2
Water Surface Elevation
To map water surface elevation of the study area, radar altimetry observations from
Cryosat-2 satellite altimetry mission were extracted over the Kafue Flats surface water
bodies.
Cryosat-2 satellite was launched by ESA in 2010. The altimeter onboard is the delay-
Doppler SAR Interferometric Radar Altimeter (SIRAL) operating in three modes: LRM
(Low Resolution Mode), SAR mode, and SARIn (SAR Interferometric) mode [31]. The
satellite orbits the Earth at an altitude of ca. 720 km with a drifting orbit inclination
of 92◦and a repeat cycle of 369 days, with 30-days sub-cycles [35]. This distinctive
long-repeat ground track pattern results in an unusually short between-track distance,
approximately 7 km at the equator. LRM resolution is comparable with conventional
radar altimetry, with pulse limited footprint of approximately 1.5 km. SAR and SARIn
modes have along-track footprint of ca.
300 m [32].
Originally designed to observe
the cryosphere, with the high along-track resolution and small footprint in SAR/SARIn
modes, the satellite proved to be useful for observing water levels over oceans and inland
waters [40].
==PAGE:41==
3.2 Water Surface Elevation
25
The satellite altimeter collects surface heights by measuring the two-way travel time
of an electromagnetic pulse between the altimeter and the surface. With dense ground
tracks, it is possible to get more frequent overpasses over observed bodies, which makes
it possible to monitor smaller water bodies in comparison to previous altimetry mis-
sions [18]. Since the satellite always follows the track, that is slightly shifted from the
previous one every 30 days, spatially distributed time series of water bodies height can
be obtained. Due to this special ground-track pattern, high spatial coverage is assured,
giving frequent overpasses of water bodies [17].
The data used for this project is Cryosat-2 Level-2 data obtained from National Space
Institute, Technical University of Denmark (DTU Space) for the period from 29/07/2010
to 15/04/2018. The provided dataset is referenced to WGS84 ellipsoid and projected
onto EGM 2008 geiod model. The data has been processed using Narrow Primary Peak
Threshold [16].
The Kafue Flats region is covered only in LRM, but its longitudinal location ensures
frequent Cryosat-2 overpasses, due to the satellite along-track direction that is perpen-
dicular to the Kafue River flow. In consequence, hundreds of orbits crossed the Kafue
Flats region within the study time period.
Due to the large ground footprint of the altimeter, the observations over the narrow
rivers (less than few hundreds meters in width) can be contaminated by the surrounding
wave surface. Also steep river valleys make it difficult to get a precise waveform reflec-
tion from the water surface [17]. Although the Kafue River width does not exceed 100
m in many parts of its basin, the Kafue Flats topography was considered as an advan-
tage. Thus, the study on the water surface elevation throughout the river was examined.
3.2.1
Water level
As a part of the study, water surface elevation of the biggest reservoirs of the Kafue
Flats was mapped: the Itezhi-tezhi Reservoir, the Manyeke Lake and the Kafue Gorge
Dam. Additionally, the Kafue River water level profile was examined, and water surface
elevation of the selected area of the wetland was investigated.
From the obtained surface water extent maps, the most precise water mask (static
water mask) was chosen and Cryosat-2 altimetry observations were extracted over this
static water mask. The observations were then assigned into specific water bodies, so
the water surface elevation of these bodies could be investigated.
The extracted water elevation observations over the reservoirs were modeled using
the R package tsHydro. Since an observation can either represent the correct water
level or a response from of-nadir target, it is assumed in the model that the observations
follows a mixture distribution between a Gaussian and a Cauchy distribution. As a
consequence, the extreme observations are less influential on the estimated mean. tsHy-

Thus, the tsHydro model allows to obtain reliable predictions by taking advantage of
the correlation between the observations close in time [32].
tsHydro model estimates the mean water level and the corresponding standard devia-
tion. Standard deviations (SD) are related to the individual track. Model also computes
standard deviation of the average water level (SDAveH) that describes precision of the
mean water level.

Observations over the Kafue River were extracted with respect to the static water

Following March the flow is decreasing, and hence water accumulates in the reservoir.
The discharge of the reservoir is controlled by the dam, so the maximum outflow from
the reservoir is reached when the dam is open. As mentioned before, efforts have been
made to restore the natural runoff regime by adapting the dam [37], so the water level
variation of the Itezhi-tezhi Reservoir should mimic the natural flow of the water. Nev-

modified due to the construction of the dams. Consequently, the presented data cannot
be interpreted as a natural level variations, but impacted by anthropological needs of
hydroelectric power production.
The lowest water values can be observed in years 2016 and 2017. This behavior might
be explained by the El Niño current of 2015 that brought conditions drier than average
in southern Africa. In consequence, the rainy season 2015/2016 had the lowest total
annual rainfall record since records were kept starting in 1904. The dry conditions were
followed by above average temperatures in October-December, resulting in extensive
drought in many countries in southern Africa, including Zambia. Since Zambia relies
heavily on hydropower to generate electricity, the reduction of usage was observable,

4.3
Discussion
Due to the time constrains, this project was limited to the specific choices of remote
sensing products and the processing applied. In the following section the discussion
about the decisions that were made is presented and recommendations for further studies
are introduced.
4.3.1
Surface Water Extent
The process of water mask extraction consisted of the combination of two distinct
datasets: observations from Sentinel-1 SAR and Sentinel-2 MSI. While the project orig-
inally assumed using only SAR data, firsts results of the water masks obtainment led to
the decision to include also MSI data.
For all the water masks obtained from SAR, the false positives can be observed.
While some of the areas can indeed represent water pixels, most of it is probably wrongly
categorized as water by the algorithm. However, since the river and the largest reservoirs
(considered as the main objectives of the project) were precisely extracted on the masks,
the algorithm was considered reliable.
The intersection between the water masks obtained from SAR and NDVI threshold-
ing, respectively, was extracted. As a result, only the largest reservoirs of the Kafue
Flats and the Kafue River were mapped as the surface water.
==PAGE:65==
4.3 Discussion
49
Despite correct classification of water bodies based on NDVI images, some limitations
of MSI images have been identified during the work on the project.
First of all, for almost all wet seasons there are no available MSI images without
cloud cover. Since the instrument cannot penetrate clouds, its usability is restricted by
the weather conditions. This leads to a problem with availability of reliable data that,
especially for the dynamic water mask, might result in insufficient numbers of images
that can be processed to obtain the water mask.
Some of the masks are missing the north part of the Kafue River. Also the Itezhi-
tezhi Reservoir was not completely mapped in some cases. This behavior was caused
by sparse clouds that covered parts of the water bodies excluding them from the water
mask (Figure A.5).
While comparing masks obtained from SAR and MSI, the results consistently showed
that masks obtained from NDVI have smaller areas of the water bodies than the ones
obtained from SAR.
Figure 4.24 shows two close-ups on the Itezhi-tezhi Reservoir border from January
2018, obtained by SAR and MSI. The bottom image illustrates the comparison map of
the water mask. Areas interpreted as water exclusively by SAR (in red color) include
the borders of the reservoir. Looking at SAR and NDVI images it can be observed that
the borders of the lake are covered by vegetation. However, the wet patches, that can
be seen to the west of the reservoir, suggest that the shore consist of water, which is a
part of the lake.
Masks obtained from NDVI index calculated for MSI images contain false negatives.
This behavior is especially pronounced during the months when the vegetation is flour-
ishing. Thus, parts of the total water extent are lost with this approach due to the
misinterpretation of vegetated areas covering the borders of water bodies as land.
This leads proves the potential of using SAR images to mask wetland areas in partic-
ular. Monitoring wetlands water level is critically important due to wetland’s significant
importance for hydrological and ecological services [37]. Therefore, an algorithm capable
of precise interpretation of vegetated water bodies could substantially support the mon-
itoring of inland water bodies levels. While SAR water masks interpreted a substantial
part of the Kafue Wetland as wetlands area - MSI water masks included almost none of
the wetlands of the region. More detailed examination would have to be made in order
to examine which of the water pixies truly represents water bodies.
Studies [37], [9], [30] suggest that parts of the wetland are flooded during the dry sea-
son, supporting that part of the SAR-only classification is actually correct. Thus SAR
images can potentially be used to extract a wider range of water bodies if an appropriate
water mask algorithm is used.

4.3.2
Water Surface Elevation
Extracted altimetry observations over the specific water bodies show that, Cryosat-2 can
provide reliable measurements, even in LRM for smaller water bodies and rivers with
a width smaller than 100 m. Undoubtedly, in this case, the Kafue Flats topography
should be considered as an advantage.
For studied examples of reservoirs tsHydro model computed predictions of the water
surface elevation, taking the most extreme observations as less influential.
The Kafue River river observations were plotted over the river chainage in order to
examine the river level profile.
While analyzing the water surface elevation of the reservoirs: Itezhi-tezhi and Manyeke
Lake, the seasonal variation agrees with the mean precipitation and the runoff from the
Itezhi-tezhi Reservoir. The Itezhi-tezhi time series reflects that water flow through the
dam is controlled (Figure 4.15. It can be seen that water is released around the same
period of time, each year. The period when the water level starts to increase occurs
around the same time each year. Thus the altimetry derived water level observations
are consistent with known trends in the catchment and the expected management of the
reservoir.
While seasonal water level variation can be still observed, the plot of the Manyeke
Lake modeled water level (Figure 4.17) does not shows such regular fit as the biggest
reservoir’s. The Manyeke Lake, located on the Kafue Wetland, opposite to the Itezhi-
tezhi Reservoir, is not a regulated reservoir. The lake is naturally recharged by pre-
cipitation and groundwater and the storage naturally decreases during the dry season.
The runoff from the Itezhi-tezhi Reservoir also contributes to recharging the lake. The
modelled surface water level illustrates these behaviors, so consistency with the known
trends in the catchment is observed.
On both plots El Niño event is recognizable. Years 2015-2017 were the drier years
among all years analyzed. The global minimum water surface level can be observed for
each of the reservoirs during that period.
While analyzing results for specific water bodies it can be seen that the standard
deviation of the average water level is equal to 0.7 cm for the Itezhi-tezhi Reservoir and
==PAGE:68==
52
4 Results and discussion
3.6 cm for the Manyeke Lake. This precision is in agreement with the precision obtained
in studies [31], [32], [18] for objectives of similar conditions.
Since, Cryosat-2 follows a drifting orbit, it was not possible to get modeled water sur-
face elevation over the Kafue River. However, treating the river as a continuous stretch,
information on water level throughout the river was obtained. Due to the altimeter
footprint size, the most narrow parts of the river are not observed by the satellite. How-
ever, even for the river widths between 20 m and 300 m, the obtained results agree with
the information acquired from topography. A linear decreasing trend can be observed,
corresponding to the west to east flow.
Due to the insufficient width of the river that could ensure cross-sections, some ob-
servations was missing throughout the river basin. With superimposed SAR or SARIn
Cryosat-2 modes, the smaller footprint could possibly be obtained. Thus, the availabil-
ity of additional observations could support creating more detailed river profile. Also,
SAR/SARIn modes ensure pulse-limited observations, so that less noise is detected than
in LRM mode. This could especially improve the precision in the eastern part of the
Kafue River, where steep land topography contaminated the returned echo.
Although the test on the selected area of the Kafue Wetland would have to be signif-
icantly explored in order to get reliable results, the consistency with the known trends
can be observed. There is a potential to develop an algorithm that could classify vege-
tated wetlands as water bodies on the water mask. Once the areas of wetlands would
be classified as water bodies, Cryosat-2 altimetry data could be extracted, exploring the
satellite potential over the wetlands. To author’s knowledge, no studies used Cryosat-2
observations to monitor wetlands yet, so such research could bring valuable input to
water resource management field.
In addition to water level observations, the cumulative water storage of Itezhi-tezhi
Reservoir was calculated. It can be seen that using mapped surface water extent and
water surface elevation, the water storage change can be calculated. Calculated val-
ues (Table A.3) agree with the information from source [34] regarding water capacity
abstracted for power generation and the reservoir’s storage loss due to the evaporation.
While cumulative storage behavior, based on the static area, basically mirrors the
water level variations of the reservoir, more attention could be paid to values computed
using the dynamic water mask. Since observations of the reservoir were extracted only
for months with corresponding dates of a dynamic mask, a precision of the tsHydro
model for such small dataset could be discussed. Further on, the dynamic mask, as an
intersection of both: NDVI and SAR masks, was missing certain reservoir’s parts, due
to the clouds cover that interfered with NDVI mask. Also NDVI misinterpretation of
vegetated areas that cover the borders of water bodies was observed. Thus, the reliabil-
ity of such mask, at least for some months, could be debatable. However, even using
limited dataset (ca. 44 observations for each mask) over the obtained masks, the water
storage values fit quite well to the static model frame.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
This thesis explored the potential of remote sensing data usage in the Kafue Flats region.
Using data from ESA Sentinel missions a detailed water mask from Sentinel-1 radar
imagery and Sentinel-2 optical imagery was derived. The obtained surface water extent
enabled the extraction of altimetry observations from Cryosat-2.
In poorly gauged basins and remote areas, the water mask can highly contribute to
the water resource management field. As currently the demand for water is increasing,
monitoring water resources is crucial to get accurate estimates of the available water
resources and to ensure optimal management. Freely accessible Sentinel-1 and Sentinel-
2 data, with high spatial and high temporal resolution, can be used to monitor water
bodies surface extent. Applying methods used in this study, the detailed dynamic water
mask can be defined. The project showed the potential of such a mask usage, revealing
the probable changes in the area of the biggest controlled reservoir of the Kafue Flats -
Itezhi-tezhi.
The static water mask used in the study has shown that the detailed mask of the Ka-
fue River and the biggest reservoirs can be produced using the presented water masking
algorithm. The static surface water extent included even the parts of the river with a
width below 20 m and reservoirs with a surface below 1 km². The attempt of mapping
the selected area of the Kafue Wetland has shown the potential of SAR in extracting
vegetation-covered areas of water bodies.
Using the detailed water mask, Cryosat-2 altimetry observations were extracted over
the water bodies. The reservoirs’ observations, modeled using tsHydro, enabled mapping
water surface extent of the biggest reservoirs with a precision of mean water level that
agrees with the precision obtained for studies with similar conditions. The analysis of
water level variations showed consistency and revealed the trends that seem very realistic
with regards to the knowledge about the system. Water storage calculated using the
dynamic water mask showed the influence of the changes in a body’s area on the water
body storage.
Due to the Kafue River’s flow in the east-west direction, regular observations by the
drifting satellite over the river were assured. The river’s level profile was investigated,
showing the agreement with the topography. The tested approach regarding the selected
area of the wetland has shown the potential of using Cryosat-2 over the wetlands.
Progress in remote sensing technologies, instruments and extraction algorithms al-
lows to obtain observation in data-sparse regions. Continuous long-term information
creates possibility to monitor surface water variations over the globe. This valuable
information is needed for freshwater monitoring and management, the essential resource
for billions of people.
