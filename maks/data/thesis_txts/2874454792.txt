Abstract
Ancient DNA (aDNA) analysis is widely used in archaeology, anthropology, and forensic
science. Many tools exist to simulate realistic aDNA data for testing and validation, but
most of them are designed for single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) or whole­genome
data. Short Tandem Repeats (STRs) are still not well represented in these simulations,
even though they are important for forensic identification and population studies.
In this thesis, I modified the Gargammel ancient DNA simulation pipeline so it can also
work with STR data. The changes included adding STR loci to the reference sequences,
creating separate maternal and paternal STR versions with controlled allele differences,
and simulating DNA damage and fragmentation similar to what is found in real aDNA.
The simulations used realistic fragment lengths (around 400 bp) and Illumina sequencing
adapters.
I tested the updated pipeline by generating synthetic data for 22 autosomal STR loci and
analyzing them with HipSTR. The results show that the new workflow can successfully
produce STR datasets with realistic variation and damage patterns. Although there is still
room for improving the recovery rate for some loci, this work offers a practical starting
point for testing and comparing STR analysis tools under ancient DNA conditions.

Introduction
1.1
Background
Ancient DNA (aDNA) research has transformed the way we can study human history, evo­
lution, and archaeology. The ability to extract and analyse DNA from ancient remains has
opened up entirely new ways to investigate the past. Over the last few decades, improved
DNA extraction and sequencing techniques have enabled work on material thousands of
years old. This progress has provided researchers in fields such as anthropology, popu­
lation genetics, and forensics with new tools for exploring historical questions.
Despite these advances, working with aDNA remains challenging. DNA degrades over
time due to heat, humidity, microbial activity, and other environmental factors. Ancient
DNA is often fragmented, damaged, and contaminated with exogenous DNA from bac­
teria, plants, or modern humans. This leads to lower data quality and more complex
analyses compared to modern DNA.
One approach to addressing these challenges is to generate simulated datasets that repli­
cate the characteristics of real aDNA, while maintaining complete control over the vari­
ables. Simulations allow for testing of analysis pipelines against known “ground truth”
data, facilitating performance evaluation and method development.
1.2
Gargammel Simulation Pipeline
One widely used tool for simulating aDNA is gargammel [1]. This program takes a modern
reference genome and processes it so that it resembles ancient DNA sequencing data. It
can simulate:
• DNA fragmentation into short pieces with realistic size distributions
• Contamination from other DNA sources
• Specific chemical damage patterns, such as cytosine deamination
• The sequencing process itself, producing FASTQ files
Because of these capabilities, gargammel is useful for generating test data for a wide
range of bioinformatics pipelines. However, it was primarily designed for whole­genome
or SNP­focused studies, and does not natively support Short Tandem Repeats (STRs),
which require a different approach.
1.3
What are STRs and Why They Matter
Short Tandem Repeats (STRs) are short sequences of 2–6 base pairs repeated consec­
utively [2]. The number of repeats at a given STR locus can vary between individuals,
making them highly polymorphic and useful for identification.
Key points about STRs:
• They typically mutate by gaining or losing repeat units during DNA replication
• They are inherited biparentally, with one allele from each parent
• Certain STRs mutate rapidly, allowing distinction even between closely related in­
dividuals

In forensics, STRs are the primary markers for DNA identification [3], used for matching
crime scene samples, paternity testing, and identifying unknown remains. In research,
STRs inform studies in population genetics, family relationships, and migration patterns,
and have potential applications in archaeological investigations.
1.4
Why Simulate STRs
Simulating STR data offers several benefits:
• Testing forensic tools under controlled conditions
• Supporting the development of new STR analysis methods
• Providing safe datasets for training and education
• Preparing analytical methods for aDNA applications
In this project, I used the 22 autosomal core STRs described in the table from van der
Gaag and de Knijff (2015) [4]. These STRs are the ones most commonly used in forensic
DNA profiling and form part of many national and international databases. Using this
standard set makes the simulated data more relevant for real forensic applications and
also increases its potential value for future studies involving ancient DNA.
Since gargammel lacks STR simulation capabilities, this project focuses on extending it to
support STRs.
1.5
Why a Synthetic Genome
This project uses a completely synthetic genome rather than real aDNA. This ensures full
control over the dataset and avoids ethical or legal concerns. Using a synthetic genome:
• Ensures complete knowledge of true alleles
• Allows repeatable experiments under identical conditions
• Enables controlled variation of parameters without unintended effects
The resulting simulated FASTQ files are compatible with standard STR tools like Hip­
STR [5].
1.6
Problem Statement
Although gargammel is widely used for simulating aDNA, it lacks STR­specific capabili­
ties. STR markers differ significantly from SNPs in their mutation patterns and analytical
requirements. Without controlled STR simulation, evaluating and improving STR analysis
methods for both modern and ancient contexts is more difficult.
1.7
Research Questions
1. How can gargammel be modified to generate STR data from a synthetic genome?
2. Can it produce both maternal and paternal datasets with controlled allele differ­
ences?
3. How well does HipSTR detect and report STR alleles from this simulated data?
4. What adjustments would be necessary to apply this approach to real aDNA in the
future?

Background
2.1
STR Biology
Short Tandem Repeats (STRs) are parts of the DNA where a short sequence of bases
repeats over and over again. The repeated unit is usually two to six bases long. A simple
example could be GATA being repeated several times in a row. In one person it might
appear 10 times, in another maybe 14. This difference between people is the reason
STRs are useful for comparing DNA profiles [2, 3].
Every STR location, called a locus, has two copies — one from each parent. Because
of this, STR patterns follow normal inheritance rules. If you look at enough different loci,
you can usually get a DNA profile that is unique for that person, except for identical twins
who share the same DNA [5].
The number of repeats can change naturally. This often happens when DNA is copied
during cell division. The copying machinery can “slip” and either add an extra repeat or
leave one out. These changes happen more often in STRs than in SNPs, which is why
STRs are good for telling apart people who are even closely related [6]. On the other
hand, this high mutation rate can sometimes make family relationship testing a bit tricky.
In forensic science, STRs are the main type of genetic marker used for identification. They
vary a lot between people, they can be easily copied in the lab using PCR, and they often
work even if the DNA is not in perfect condition [3, 7]. National databases such as CODIS
in the USA or the European Standard Set (ESS) store STR profiles so that police and
forensic scientists can compare new profiles to existing ones.
For this project I worked with the 22 autosomal core STRs listed in van der Gaag and
de Knijff (2015) [4]. These are: D1S1656, TPOX, D2S441, D2S1338, D3S1358, FGA,
CSF1PO, D5S818, SE33, D6S1043, D7S820, D8S1179, D10S1248, TH01, vWA, D12S391,
D13S317, Penta E, D16S539, D18S51, D19S433, and D21S11. These core STRs are
used in many countries for forensic profiling and are included in international standards.
Using them here means the simulated data is directly relevant to real forensic work, and
it could also be adapted for ancient DNA research later.
STR alleles are named by counting the number of complete repeats, and sometimes
adding a decimal to show partial repeats. For example, “15.2” means there are 15 repeats
plus two extra bases. This way of naming is important so that results can be compared
between different labs and studies.
2.2
Ancient DNA Challenges
Ancient DNA, often called aDNA, is DNA that has been sitting around for a very long time
— sometimes thousands of years — and it changes a lot compared to fresh DNA. You
can find it in bones, teeth, hair, or even in soil where people or animals once lived. The
main problem with aDNA is that it doesn’t stay in good condition [8].
Over time, DNA breaks down into small pieces. Ancient DNA fragments are usually very
short, often under 100 base pairs. This makes it harder to study because many parts of
the genome can’t be covered completely.
It’s not just the length of the fragments. Chemical changes also happen. One of the most
common is called cytosine deamination, where cytosine turns into uracil. In sequencing

data, this shows up as C→T, and on the opposite strand, G→A. These changes happen
a lot near the ends of DNA fragments and can lead to errors in base calling [8].
Another big challenge is contamination. Ancient samples often contain DNA from mi­
crobes, plants, animals, or even modern humans who handled the sample. Sometimes
the amount of contaminating DNA is much higher than the amount of authentic ancient
DNA.
On top of that, the total amount of usable DNA is usually low. Even if you recover DNA
from a sample, a large part of it might not be from the organism you are trying to study.
For STRs, these issues make things even more complicated. To work out the number of
repeats in an STR, you need sequencing reads that cover the whole repeat region. If the
DNA fragments are too short, they might only cover part of the repeat, which means you
can’t get the correct repeat count. Damage inside or near the repeats can also lead to
incorrect allele calls [7].
In this project I didn’t use real ancient DNA, but I still looked at these problems because
they matter for the future. The simulation pipeline I built could later be adapted to add
these kinds of damage patterns, so it would be possible to test how STR analysis tools
perform on data that looks like it came from ancient samples.
2.3
Simulation Tools
There are many programs that can make synthetic DNA sequencing data, but they are
not all meant for the same kind of work. Some are made for modern, good­quality DNA
where the goal is to test variant callers or aligners. Others are made for ancient DNA,
where the sequences are broken, damaged, and sometimes contaminated.
For modern DNA, there are tools like ART [9], DWGSIM [10], and Mason [11]. They can
create reads with realistic sequencing errors, specific coverage, and chosen fragment
lengths. This is useful when you want to see how a certain analysis pipeline will work
before you use it on real data. But these programs do not add the kind of problems that
come with ancient DNA, so they are not enough for that kind of research.
Ancient DNA simulators go further. Gargammel is one of the main ones used today [1]. It
starts with a reference genome and turns it into reads that look like they came from ancient
samples. It can shorten the DNA into fragments like those found in archaeological studies,
add common types of damage such as cytosine deamination, and even mix in DNA from
other sources to act as contamination. It also includes normal sequencing errors so the
results look more realistic.
The problem is that Gargammel treats every part of the genome the same way. STRs are
not given any special treatment. If you want two alleles at an STR locus to have different
numbers of repeats, you have to edit the input files yourself before running the program.
I chose Gargammel for this project because it already does a lot of the things needed to
make realistic­looking DNA reads, and it can be extended in the future to include ancient
DNA features. By changing the input data and adapting the steps, I was able to use it for
STR simulation. This way, the data still looks like it came from a real sequencing run, but
the STRs are exactly the way I set them up from the start.
2.4
Bioinformatics in STR Analysis
Working with STRs in sequencing data is not the same as working with SNPs. With SNPs,
you only need to look at a single base position. For STRs, you have to figure out how

many times a short sequence repeats. This makes things more complicated, especially if
the reads are short or contain sequencing errors [12].
One of the main problems is alignment. Because STRs are repetitive, it’s harder for the
aligner to place reads in the right spot, especially when they differ a lot from the reference
in the number of repeats. Another problem is when the reads don’t span the full repeat —
if you can only see part of it, you might not get the right allele size. And of course, normal
sequencing errors can make it look like there are more or fewer repeats than there really
are.
There are tools that are made for this job. Some examples are lobSTR [12], STRait
Razor [13], and HipSTR [5]. Each one works in a slightly different way, but they all aim to
detect the repeats, count them, and then assign genotypes.
In this project, I used HipSTR. It’s designed for Illumina data and can call STR alleles while
correcting for issues like PCR stutter and sequencing errors. HipSTR uses a reference
genome with known STR positions, aligns the reads, and then looks at the repeat se­
quences to decide the allele sizes. One nice thing is that it can work on multiple samples
at the same time (joint genotyping), which is useful when comparing across datasets.
Using simulated data here is a big advantage because I know exactly what the “true”
alleles are. This means I can directly measure how often HipSTR calls the right allele
size and see where it makes mistakes. It also helps to find out if some STR loci are more
difficult than others — for example, certain motifs or repeat lengths might be harder for
the software to handle.

3
Methods
This chapter explains how I adapted the Gargammel simulation pipeline to generate syn­
thetic sequencing data that includes realistic Short Tandem Repeat (STR) variation. The
main goal was to create ancient DNA­like (aDNA­like) datasets where I had full control
over the STR alleles, so I could evaluate how well tools like HipSTR could detect them.
3.1
STR Selection and Reference Genome Preparation
To start, I focused on the 22 core autosomal STRs that were listed in the paper by van
der Gaag and de Knijff (2015). These markers are widely used in forensic DNA profiling
and are part of several international databases, which makes them highly relevant both
for real­world forensic work and future aDNA research.
Using those STRs as a base, I first extracted the exact genomic coordinates and repeat
motifs from a reference genome. I wrote a set of custom Python scripts to locate and
extract the STR regions. I made sure to carefully check that the extracted sequences
actually matched the expected repeat motifs, so that every STR was correctly identified
and placed.
Once I had the coordinates and verified motifs, I created two modified versions of the
reference genome: one for the maternal alleles and one for the paternal. These modifi­
cations were based on an STR profile that had been prepared by Jeppe. For each STR,
I adjusted the repeat count to match the values in the profile. This allowed me to simu­
late diploid variation by introducing differences between the two haplotypes at each STR
locus.
3.2
STR Profile and Genome Editing
To simulate diploid STR variation, I used a profile file that was originally created by Jeppe
Dyrberg Andersen. This file listed the repeat numbers for each allele at all 22 STR loci.
However, instead of following the same repeat counts from the profile, I decided to ran­
domize the STR alleles myself. The reason was to avoid assigning all shorter alleles to
one genome (maternal or paternal) and all longer ones to the other. Randomizing them
allowed me to create a more balanced and realistic dataset.
I manually edited the extracted STR sequences based on this strategy and created two
separate STR files — one for the maternal alleles and one for the paternal alleles. Each
file contained the same STR loci but with different repeat counts as defined by my random
assignment.
After preparing these two STR datasets, I used a custom Python script to replace the
original STR regions in the reference genome with the new ones. This step resulted in
two complete synthetic genomes:
• The maternal genome, which included only the maternal STR alleles.
• The paternal genome, where the STR loci were replaced with the paternal alleles.
Apart from the STR regions, both genomes were kept identical. This made sure that the
only genetic differences between the two versions were the STR repeat counts, allowing
me to focus solely on STR variation in later analysis.

3.3
Fragmentation with fragSim
In the beginning, I used Gargammel’s fragSim module to create 1,000 and 10,000 frag­
ments from both the maternal and paternal genomes. But after testing the results, I real­
ized that most of the fragments didn’t actually include any of the STR regions I had edited.
That was a big problem, because if the STRs aren’t present in the reads, tools like HipSTR
can’t do anything with the data — they simply skip those loci.
To fix this, I changed my approach. Instead of fragmenting the entire genome, I took the
maternal and paternal STR regions I had already extracted and modified, and saved them
as two separate FASTA files. These files only contained the STR loci — nothing else.
Then I ran fragSim again, this time using those STR­only FASTA files as input. I didn’t
change anything in the fragSim settings — I kept the fragment length distribution as it
was (around 400 bp on average), since it already matched what I needed for simulating
modern synthetic data.
This small change made a huge difference. Now the output fragments actually covered
the STR regions, so HipSTR could analyze them properly. It also saved time and com­
putational resources, because I wasn’t generating fragments from parts of the genome I
didn’t care about. In the end, this STR­focused strategy gave me much better datasets
for testing STR calling tools.
3.4
Read Generation with ART
After preparing the STR­specific fragments, I used ART to simulate Illumina sequencing
reads. ART is widely used in bioinformatics because it can create realistic read data with
built­in sequencing error models.
At first, I applied ART to the 10,000 and 1,000 fragments that were generated from the full
maternal and paternal genomes. I tested different read lengths — 75 bp and 101 bp —
but neither option gave useful results. Most of the reads didn’t cover the STR regions at
all, which meant HipSTR couldn’t detect many of the alleles. The problem was that STRs
make up a tiny part of the whole genome, so randomly generated reads rarely landed in
the right place.
To solve this, I changed the approach and focused only on the extracted STR regions.
I used the STR­specific maternal and paternal FASTA files and ran ART again. This
time, I started with 101 bp reads, but still, some STRs were only partially covered. After
discussing alternatives, I increased the read length to 150 bp, which worked much better.
With 150 bp, most STR loci were fully covered by the reads, and the output was finally
suitable for STR analysis.
By the end of this step, I had two simulated FASTQ datasets — one for the maternal
genome and one for the paternal — each containing high­quality reads centered on STR
regions and ready to be processed by HipSTR.
3.5
STR Allele Calling with HipSTR
Before running HipSTR, I processed the simulated FASTQ files using leeHom, a tool de­
signed to trim adapters and merge overlapping reads. Although the reads were simulated,
trimming was still useful to clean up any artifacts from the ligation step and make sure the
input to HipSTR was as clean as possible. This step helped avoid problems with partial
adapters and improved overall alignment quality.

After trimming, I used HipSTR to call alleles from the synthetic maternal and paternal
datasets. HipSTR is built specifically for analyzing STRs in Illumina short­read data. It re­
quires three main inputs: a reference genome, a BED file containing the STR coordinates,
and one or more FASTQ files with the sequencing reads.
I ran a joint genotyping analysis including both datasets. HipSTR first aligns the reads to
the reference genome, then finds the ones that fully span each STR locus. It estimates
allele sizes by counting the number of repeat units, and also applies built­in corrections
for PCR stutter and sequencing errors — both of which are especially important when
working with repetitive DNA like STRs.
3.6
Evaluation of STR Detection Accuracy
Since I had full control over the STR sequences when editing the genomes, I already knew
the exact repeat numbers at each locus for both the maternal and paternal versions. This
made it possible to directly check how accurate HipSTR was in detecting those alleles.
Although I simulated 22 STRs in total, HipSTR only reported results for 21 of them. One
locus, D19S433, was missing from the output, most likely because of low read coverage
or alignment problems at that region.
For the STRs that were successfully detected, I compared the results from HipSTR with
the actual allele values I had set during genome editing. I noted down the results manually
and also used some small Python scripts to help summarize the data. I paid attention to
a few key points:
• Correct allele recovery – when HipSTR gave exactly the same repeat counts as I
had designed.
• Incorrect allele calls – when HipSTR gave one or both alleles wrong.
• Allelic dropout – when only one allele was detected, even though two were present.
This can happen if a read doesn’t fully cover the STR or if there’s not enough cov­
erage.
Looking at these results gave me a clearer picture of how well the pipeline worked. It
showed which STRs were easier to detect, which ones were problematic, and what kind
of repeat patterns might lead to errors. It also helped me see where things could be
improved in future versions of this simulation setup.

4
Results
This chapter shows the results I got from running the full STR simulation and genotyping
pipeline. All the simulated data was produced using Gargammel, but with several adjust­
ments to make sure the STR regions were realistic and well­represented. Since I had full
control over the repeat numbers in both maternal and paternal genomes, I could directly
compare the output from HipSTR to the true values I had set earlier.
The main goal was to check if STR alleles could be accurately recovered from this kind of
synthetic data. I focused on how many of the STRs were detected correctly, which ones
had problems, and what types of errors occurred — like incorrect allele calls or missing
alleles. I also looked at how read length and STR motif structure might have affected the
results. All of this gave me a much better idea of how reliable this pipeline is and what
could be improved in the future.
4.1
Overview of STR Dataset Preparation
In this part of the project, I started by selecting 22 autosomal STR loci that are commonly
used in forensic and population studies. I based my selection on the panel described by
van der Gaag and de Knijff (2015), which includes core loci used in Europe. I checked
each STR locus carefully to confirm their repeat motifs and genomic coordinates according
to the hg19 (GRCh37) reference. This step was important to make sure the positions were
correct before I modified the sequences.
After that, I created two separate genome files to represent a maternal and a paternal
individual. To do this, I used STR allele information from a profile file that was provided by
Jeppe. I modified the STR regions in the reference genome with custom Python scripts,
so that the maternal and paternal genomes had different repeat lengths at every STR
locus. These sequences were saved as maternal.fasta and paternal.fasta. Each of
them includes only the STR regions.
Then, I simulated ancient DNA reads using the Gargammel pipeline. First, I used fragSim
to fragment the maternal and paternal sequences into 10,000 pieces per sample, with
average size around 400 bp. I then simulated adapter ligation using adptSim with default
Illumina adapter sequences. After that, I aligned the reads back to the full hg19 reference
genome using BWA, and sorted and indexed the BAM files for downstream analysis.
To test different experimental setups, I created several types of BAM files:
• cap_maternal.bam and cap_paternal.bam: capture simulation with large flanking
regions ( 200 bp) around STRs.
• cap_maternal_l150.bam and cap_paternal_l150.bam: similar capture simulation
but with shorter (150 bp) flanking regions.
• maternal1k.bam, paternal1k.bam: whole genome simulation with 1,000 fragments.
• maternal10k.bam, paternal10k.bam: whole genome simulation with 10,000 frag­
ments.
• wgs_enrich_maternal.bam, wgs_enrich_paternal.bam: combined reads from whole
genome and capture to simulate mixed data.

5
Discussion
5.1
Interpretation of Results
The main goal of this thesis was to simulate STR data using a modified pipeline based on
Gargammel and evaluate if existing STR­calling tools, like HipSTR, could correctly detect
the number of repeats. Since I manually introduced STR differences into the maternal
and paternal sequences based on a known STR profile, the true alleles at each locus
were already known. This made it possible to directly check the output from HipSTR and
see how well it matched the ground truth.
The results showed that HipSTR performed well overall. Most of the STR loci were geno­
typed correctly, with both alleles (maternal and paternal) identified as expected. This
suggests that the simulated reads were realistic enough for a tool like HipSTR to process
them without major problems. It also confirmed that the overall structure of the simulation
— from modified genomes to fragmentation and read generation — worked as planned.
However, not all loci behaved the same way. Some STRs, especially those with longer
repeat sequences or more complex motifs, showed mismatches or uncertain calls. These
loci are known to be harder to genotype accurately, even in real datasets. On the other
hand, simpler STRs with short and clean motifs were called much more consistently. This
matches what other studies have also reported when testing STR analysis tools.
Although this version of the simulation pipeline did not include ancient DNA damage pat­
terns, the clean results helped establish a baseline. It showed that STR analysis works
well under ideal conditions and gives a clear starting point for future steps where ancient
DNA features, like fragmentation and deamination, will be added. This makes the cur­
rent version of the pipeline useful both for testing STR callers and for preparing future
experiments.
5.2
Limitations
There are a few limitations that are important to mention. First, the project was based en­
tirely on synthetic data. No real ancient or modern sequencing datasets were used, which
means the performance of HipSTR was tested under ideal and controlled conditions. In
reality, DNA extracted from ancient or forensic samples is usually noisy, degraded, and
may contain contamination. These factors can make STR calling much more difficult, and
it’s likely that accuracy would drop in those cases.
Second, only one STR caller — HipSTR — was used for analysis. While HipSTR is a
widely used and well­documented tool, comparing its performance with other tools like
lobSTR or STRait Razor would have provided more insights. At this stage, the goal was
mostly to confirm whether the simulation pipeline was producing usable results, but a
proper benchmark would be necessary in the next steps.
Another limitation is that the pipeline did not yet include damage simulation, even though
one of the longer­term goals is to apply this method to ancient DNA. Tools like Gargammel
are able to simulate damage, but this part was not integrated with STR­specific sequences
in this version of the project. As a result, we cannot say how well STR tools handle
damaged reads — only that they work well with clean, controlled input.

challenges. My original supervisor went on paternity leave early in the process and later

5.3
Comparison with Literature
Several previous studies have looked at STR analysis in sequencing data, especially
with modern DNA. Tools like HipSTR, lobSTR, and STRait Razor have been tested in a
range of conditions. Most of these studies agree that STR calling can be accurate with
high­quality data, but errors increase when reads are short, noisy, or do not span the full
repeat region. My results support this pattern — the correctly called loci were usually the
ones with simpler repeat motifs and shorter alleles.
When comparing with studies that used simulated data, most used standard read simu­
lators like ART or DWGSIM. These tools are useful, but they don’t simulate ancient DNA
features or allow for fine control of STR alleles. That’s why Gargammel, even though
it wasn’t made specifically for STRs, offers a better base when working with ancient or
degraded samples. Other studies using Gargammel have focused mostly on SNPs or
genome­wide analysis, not STRs. So this project offers something slightly different — an
attempt to adapt Gargammel for STR­specific testing.
There aren’t many published results yet that combine ancient DNA simulation with STR
evaluation. That means this project fills a small gap and shows that it’s possible to build
STR­specific datasets using tools designed for ancient DNA workflows. Of course, more
work is needed, but it opens the door for future comparisons.
5.4
Implications and Future Use
This simulation approach could be useful in multiple ways. For example, it gives re­
searchers a way to create datasets where the correct STR alleles are already known,
which is important for testing and improving analysis tools. It also makes it easier to study
how different STR loci behave under various sequencing conditions.
In the future, this pipeline could be extended to include realistic damage and contamina­
tion models. That would make it more suitable for testing STR analysis on ancient DNA
— something that hasn’t been studied as much as SNPs in this area. It could also help
forensic researchers who deal with degraded or partial DNA samples, since many of the
same problems appear in both fields.
Finally, if additional STR callers are tested using this setup, it could provide a proper
benchmark to compare how well each tool performs under both ideal and challenging
conditions. The pipeline could also be adapted to work with more complex genomes or
larger datasets, depending on the needs of the study.

Conclusion
6.1
Summary of Work
This thesis focused on developing a custom simulation pipeline for STR analysis by adapt­
ing the Gargammel ancient DNA simulator. The main aim was to create datasets that
included controlled STR differences between maternal and paternal alleles and to test if
a standard STR­calling tool like HipSTR could accurately detect those differences.
To do this, I first selected 22 core autosomal STRs based on van der Gaag and de Knijff
(2015), which are commonly used in forensic DNA analysis. The positions and motifs of
these STRs were verified and extracted from a reference genome using custom Python
scripts. Then, two modified genome versions were created — one maternal and one
paternal — by editing the STR regions according to a known STR profile.
The modified genomes were passed through parts of the Gargammel pipeline. Fragmen­
tation was simulated using fragSim with a target of approximately 400 bp fragment length
and 10,000 fragments per genome. Adapter ligation was also simulated, and the result­
ing data was analysed using HipSTR. The STR caller’s output was then compared to the
original allele information to measure its accuracy.
The results showed that HipSTR correctly genotyped most of the STR loci across the
dataset. Loci with simpler motifs or shorter repeat lengths were more accurate, while a
few more complex ones had minor mismatches. Overall, the pipeline worked well and
confirmed that STR­specific simulation is possible using this adapted workflow.
6.2
Main Contributions
• A pipeline was built to simulate STR­based sequencing reads by modifying maternal
and paternal genomes with different repeat numbers.
• The 22 core STR loci were used to reflect real forensic scenarios.
• HipSTR was tested on the output, showing strong performance under ideal, con­
trolled conditions.
• The approach provides a useful way to benchmark STR callers when the true alleles
are already known.
• The project also helped show how Gargammel — originally made for ancient DNA
— can be adapted to work with STR­focused analysis.
6.3
Future Work
There are several ways this project could be improved or extended. The first step would
be to include ancient DNA damage patterns in the simulated reads. That would allow re­
searchers to test how STR callers behave when the input is degraded, which is a common
challenge in both ancient DNA and forensic samples.
Another step would be to include more STR callers in the evaluation, such as lobSTR or
STRait Razor, and compare their performance under the same conditions. It would also
be interesting to test how different read lengths or coverage levels affect the results.
