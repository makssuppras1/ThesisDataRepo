Abstract
Background: Antimicrobial resistance is one of the largest public health threats today. The bacterium
Escherichia coli is the primary cause of invasive infections in Denmark and globally it is the pathogen
with the highest counts of global deaths attributable to and associated with bacterial antimicrobial re-
sistance.
Methods Using a literature review and TRACE (TRAnsparent and Comprehensive Ecological model-
ing), already published models are gathered to make a new model. A compartment model is used to
simulate the transmission of E. coli colonisations and predict the burden of disease. This transmission
model is also modelled with the extension of vaccinations.
Results The literature review and TRACE found relevant articles with a focus on the transmission of
E. coli and that none of the articles fulfilled all elements of TRACE. Our model was able to simulate
the transmission of E. coli in Denmark. Further, the burden of disease in DALYs (Disability-Adjusted
Life Years) for the baseline model was found to be 38 per 100,000 population for ESBL-producing E.
coli and 79 per 100,000 population for non-ESBL-producing E. coli. Vaccination was also successfully
implemented, where the DALYs for the best-case model were found to be 28 per 100,000 population for
ESBL-producing E. coli and 70 per 100,000 population for non-ESBL-producing E. coli and for the worst-
case they were found to be 34 per 100,000 population for ESBL-producing E. coli and 76 per 100,000
population for non-ESBL-producing E. coli.
Conclusion The data extraction and TRACE showed data gaps in the area of E. coli transmission mod-
els. These methods were also a good way to assess similar models and get an idea of what to do in our
modelling process. The introduction of vaccinations could potentially lower the burden of disease for
E. coli and AMR.

Introduction
Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) has become one of the largest public health threats of the 21st century.
Today, approximately 700,000 people worldwide are dying each year due to resistant infections. This
number has been estimated to increase to 10 million by 2050 [1]. AMR challenges our ability to treat
severe, but also common, infectious diseases, resulting in prolonged illness, disability and death. This
is especially due to the overuse and misuse of antimicrobials in humans as well as animals leading to
high levels of resistance [2].
In general, the awareness of the issue with AMR has increased over the last couple of decades and
action plans have been established [3]. For example, reports across organisations and ministries in dif-
ferent countries [4][5][6] emphasise some of the same points. We need to increase awareness of AMR
and the prevention of infections in the general community. Improve hygiene and decrease the risk of be-
coming infected in the health care system. Keep track of the infections and antimicrobial usage (AMU).
Find and develop alternative treatments to antibiotics, for instance, vaccines. Further, antibiotics should
only be used if necessary.
Escherichia coli (E. coli) is the primary cause of invasive infections in Denmark [7]. Further, E. coli is by
far the most common cause of community- and hospital-acquired urinary tract infections (UTIs) and
bloodstream infections (BSIs). In 2021 more than half of the isolates of E. coli were resistant towards at
least one antibiotic, causing E. coli to be the most frequent resistant bacteria causing infections in 2021.
Further, the number of resistance cases with E. coli has increased by 52% from 2012 to 2021 [5]. Over the
last decades, resistance types, such as extended-spectrum β lactamase (ESBL), have increased radically
and have by the World Health Organization (WHO) been announced to be in the top three largest health
threats against society [8].
One way to measure the burden of diseases, such as E. coli infections, is to calculate the disability-
adjusted life-years (DALYs). A systematic analysis was made in 2019 of the global burden of bacterial
AMR [9]. This analysis showed DALYs calculated globally and for the Global Burden of Diseases, In-
juries, and Risk Factors Study (GBD) super regions. The global DALYs were found to be 2477.7 per
100,000 population and for high-income countries, it was calculated to be 946.7 per 100,000 popula-
tion for infections associated with bacterial AMR. These are based on the counterfactual of no infection.
Further, for infections attributable to bacterial AMR, the DALYs were found to be 618.7 per 100,000 pop-
ulation globally and 222.3 per 100,000 population for high-income countries. These are based on the
counterfactual of drug-sensitive infections. In this global analysis, it was also found that BSIs and UTIs
were among the leading causes of deaths attributable to and associated with bacterial AMR. Further,
E. coli were also found to be the pathogen with the highest counts of global deaths attributable to and
associated with bacterial AMR [9].
This thesis contributes to a work package concerning a systematic review of AMR models including
transmission, interventions and secondary infections. This work package covers several pathogens, in-
cluding E. coli. The work package also contributes to an international project initiated by the European

2.1
E. coli
E. coli is a gram-negative bacterium and is one of the most common food-borne pathogens. E. coli be-
longs to the Enteriobacteriea family and is therefore, among others, closely related to Klebsiella pneumoniae
(K. pneumoniae). E. coli is commonly found in the intestines of humans as well as in many animals. Not
all strains of E. coli cause illness, but certain types are associated with several food-borne outbreaks
[5][12]. Extraintestinal pathogenic E. coli (ExPEC) denotes infections outside the intestines, most often
leading to UTIs, which can progress into pyelonephritis, and BSIs. In the intestines, the bacteria can
cause diarrhoea [13]. For this thesis, we refer to ExPEC when denoting E. coli infections and colonisa-
tions.

2.1.3
Treatment
Antibiotics are antimicrobials for defeating bacterial infections. Antimicrobials include antibiotics, an-
tivirals, antifungals and antiparasitics, and are generally used to prevent and treat infections [21]. In
Denmark, ciprofloxacin, mecillinam, nitrofurantoin, and co-trimoxazole consisting of sulfamethizol and
trimetoprim, are the most often used antibiotics against UTIs [16]. Ciprofloxacin is most often the first
choice [21]. All of these are in the so-called access group of antibiotics, except mecillinam which is cat-
egorised in the watch group. This grouping of antibiotics was developed by WHO. The access group
includes antibiotics that are active against a wide range of common bacteria, and are showing lower
resistance potential. Further, the watch group is reserved for antibiotics with higher resistance potential
[22][23]. Lastly, the reserved group includes antibiotics that should only be used as a last resort for cur-
ing the infection [24]. Even though antibiotics are in the access group, it does not indicate that resistance
has not developed against the antibiotic [21][25].
When looking at BSIs, piperacillin and tazobactam, imipenem and cilastatin, or meropenem may be used
as a treatment. Further, combination therapy with antibiotics that cover E. coli plus levofloxacin and
clindamycin or metronidazole can be used [4]. Most of the antibiotics mentioned above are preserved
in the watch group, except clindamycin and metronidazole, which are in the access group [22][23].

2.2
Antimicrobial resistance
AMR is the result of pathogens developing the ability to resist an antimicrobial, that was intended to
kill them. AMR occurs naturally over time when pathogens are exposed to antimicrobials [21]. Once
a bacterium has developed AMR, it has the ability to spread this trait. The spread to humans can hap-
pen between humans, from animals to humans, including via the animal-based food we eat, and from
the environment to humans. The consequence of using antimicrobials is that the AMR organisms and
antimicrobials contaminate solid and liquid waste that further can travel into our sewer systems and
waterways, from where it can accelerate the AMR problem by spreading [2][21].
There is an increasing tendency of AMR worldwide. The overuse and misuse of antibiotics are part
of it, but the general lack of prevention of infections and diseases by good hygiene, medicines and
vaccines is accelerating the development. Further, the lack of awareness and knowledge about AMR
and antimicrobials in general, and lack of action in the form of legislation in the usage, also take part in
the accelerating development [21].
As an example, in 2021 the resistance to ciprofloxacin for treating UTIs caused by E. coli had a median
at 43.1% for the countries reporting to WHOs Global Antimicrobial Resistance and Use Surveillance
System (GLASS). Further, the median for resistance against co-trimoxazole was found to be 54.4%. This
indicates that the resistance to normally used antibiotics in the access group for treating UTIs, is alarm-
ingly high [21][25].
2.2.1
ESBL resistance
In regard to the antibiotic β-lactams, one resistance mechanism is the production of β-lactamases. One
class of β-lactamases is ESBLs, these are often defined as enzymes that can degrade later generations
of β-lactams, such as third-generation cephalosporins. In general, ESBL-producing bacteria is char-
acterised by being able to break down at least one of the following β-lactam antibiotics: penicillins,
cephalosporins, carbapenems and monobactams. ESBL-encoding genes reside on plasmids and can
therefore be transferred between similar bacteria e.g. among the Enterobacteriaceae [8].
In Denmark, and generally in the world, an increase in the occurrence of ESBL-producing bacteria has
been seen over the last decade, especially for E. coli and K. pneumoniae. This increases the risk of closely
related bacteria acquiring the resistance gene(s), such as Salmonella bacteria. Further, it is not uncommon
that ESBL-producing bacteria are resistant towards other antibiotics than the ones in the β-lactam group,
such as gentamicin and fluoroquinolones. There is no standardised guide for treating an individual
with an ESBL-producing bacteria infection, as the resistance genes can be different. In general, it is
a huge problem for bacteria to have resistance towards β-lactam antibiotics, as these are commonly
used antibiotics. In Denmark, the three most used antibiotics for humans are penicillins with extended-
spectrum, β-lactamase sensitive penicillins and β-lactamase resistant penicillins in primary and hospital
care [5]. An infection with an ESBL-producing bacteria forces the doctors to give antibiotics less suited
for the infection, and that may have a higher chance of side effects and a higher risk of environmental
consequences with further development of resistance [8]. In the most serious cases, doctors are forced
to use carbapenem antibiotics, which are reserved for highly resistant infections [26].

2.2.2
Interventions
In the danish healthcare sector, there are six important general national guidelines for decreasing the
number of infectious disease cases:
1. Hand hygiene; disinfection or wash of hands before procedures. Further, instruction for patients
and visitors.
2. Work clothes; clean work clothes for healthcare personnel, which should be changed if wet or
contaminated.
3. Personal protection equipment; Used for healthcare personnel’s protection and includes gloves,
disposable aprons, masks, protective glasses and visors. These should be switched out between
each patient.
4. Reprocessing of equipment; disinfection of equipment with heat or disinfectant after each use.
5. Cleaning of patient rooms and inventory.
6. Handling of patient secretions and samples; Avoidance of contamination of sample packaging,
proper packaging and transport of samples, and use of personal protection equipment.
These guidelines are incorporated into all procedures to reduce transmissions [27]. Despite these inter-
ventions already implemented in the healthcare sector, an increase in the number of cases is still seen,
as described in section 2.2. This suggests that further interventions are required. Currently, others in-
vestigate the impact of hand hygiene, isolation, the number of contacts and AMU. Further, vaccines are
a possible intervention that could result in a decreased number of infected individuals [15][28].
A vaccine is a way to trigger the body’s natural immune response by injecting weakened or inactive
fragments of antigens or instructions for producing the antigen into the body. This helps the immune
system to build specific antibodies, which can be used when the actual pathogen enters the body. A
term often used in relation to vaccines is herd immunity, where a large enough part of the population is
vaccinated. This causes individuals who can not be vaccinated to be protected if they live among vacci-
nated individuals [29]. This depends on multiple factors: the vaccine’s efficacy, coverage and durability.
The efficacy is an expression of how efficient the vaccine is to prevent individuals from getting infected.
The coverage is a measure of how large a portion of the population is estimated to receive the vaccine.
The coverage of vaccines can differ greatly depending on the type of disease. The Global coverage of
the Haemophilus influenza type b vaccine is estimated at 71%, three doses of the Hepatitis B vaccine are
estimated at 80% and a rotavirus vaccine is estimated at 49%. The coverage of most approved vaccines
ranges from approximately 50% up to 80% [30].
The durability of the vaccine expresses how long the vaccine has a noticeable effect on the pathogen.
Meaning, if the duration of the vaccine is long, individuals will be protected against the pathogen for a
longer time. The rate of being infected when having the vaccine is given as (1 −e f f icacy). If the efficacy
of a vaccine is low, the rate of infection will be higher, whereas if it is higher, fewer will become sick.

A vaccine consisting of several heat-killed uropathogens, among which are of six strains for E. coli, is
showing promising results in clinical trials. It was shown that three doses of vaginal Urovac delayed
the time to reinfection for women affected by recurrent UTIs. When using six doses in the vaccination

2.3
Transmission models
Transmission models are broadly used to predict outcomes of infectious diseases. They are used to pre-
dict the duration and magnitude of an outbreak. The model is often defined by the question of interest.
The models can be very specific and can account for specific pathogens, variations in the incubation
period, symptoms severity and multiple transmission pathways [31].
A susceptible-infected-susceptible (SIS) model is based on the principle of susceptible-infected-removed
(SIR) models. These are transmission models used for infectious diseases. The models of SIR consist of
a system of three coupled non-linear ordinary differential equations (ODEs) [32]. The equations of the
simple SIR model are:

ble (S), infected (I) and removed (R). N is the sum of S, I and R, and therefore the total population. In
this type of model, no individuals will enter the model, meaning N will be constant. The first equation
denotes S and as there is only a negative part in the equation, the population for S will only decline.
The second equation denotes I, and this includes two parts. The part from S, which is now positively
charged, indicates that the population can move from S to I. And secondly, a negatively charged part.
This part is a positively charged part in the equation denoting R, indicating that the population can
move from I to R.
S denotes the population susceptible to the disease. I denotes the population that is infected with the
disease. Lastly, R is the population which have recovered from the disease or has been removed from
the system. R can denote people that cannot get sick from the disease again due to immunity, and the
ones that have died [32]. Other models, including the SIRS model, implement that immunity is not per-
manent. This means individuals will be moved back to S with time.

Infections like UTIs and BSIs have symptoms and disease outcomes with different severity. A way to
compare these is by calculating DALYs.
DALYs are a metric which describes the healthy life years lost. This metric can be calculated by adding
the adjusted number of years lived with disability (YLDs) and the number of years of life lost due to
premature mortality (YLLs) [33].
DALYs = YLDs + YLLs
(3)
Where YLDs are given as number of cases × duration × DW, where DW denotes the disability weights, and

TRACE, short for TRAnsparent and Comprehensive Evaludation modelling documentation, is a way
of evaluating and validating models [34]. Originally TRACE aimed at documenting good modelling
practice [35][36], but has later been redefined as a tool for planning, performing and documenting good
modelling practice [34]. Normally, TRACE will be performed by the authors, and has the purpose to
ensure that the model can be replicated by external users afterwards. Further, it helps the clients of
the model to understand the model and assess the quality and hence the reliability of the results. This
ensures that scientists are more easily able to use other authors’ work in their own, so there is no need to
start from scratch every time a model is developed [34]. The elements of TRACE and a short description
are listed in table 1.

3.3.1
Deterministic modelling
When modelling dynamical models, two broad classes are used; deterministic or stochastic. Determinis-
tic models are commonly used to describe biological phenomena. This class of models assumes that the
future is entirely determined by the model, which also causes the outcome of the model to be the same at
every simulation. Further, these models do not account for fluctuating environments. Stochastic models
are where random events affect the biological system. These are therefore a prediction of the probability
of various outcomes in the future, as the parameters of the system are not constant for each simulation
[39]. The models of this thesis are based on a deterministic approach. The models are simulated and the
analyses are made using the software R, version 4.2.2 and Rstudio, version 2022.12.0.
Ordinary differential equations (ODEs) are used to formulate the model. These are differential equations
with one or more unknown functions of one variable and involve derivatives of those functions. The
term ordinary indicates that there can be more than one independent variable [40].
When solving ODEs in R the function ode [41] from the package deSolve, version 1.34 is used. This
function takes as input:
• The initial state values for the system.
• A sequence of time for which the output is wanted.
• A function that computes the values of the derivatives in the ODE system.
• The parameters that should be used in the function.
The function returns a matrix with the number of rows equal to time steps and the number of columns
equal to the number of initial state values in the model plus a time column [41]. The ODEs and the
inputs of our model will be described further in the following sections.
3.4
Baseline model
Our baseline model is based on the principle of SIR models, described in section 2.3. The model attempts
to reflect the situation of E. coli in Denmark.
Overall, the model has three different settings: community (com), hospital (hos) and ICU (icu). The flow
diagram of the model is visualised in figure 4. The population is divided into three main stages: sus-
ceptible (S), colonised with an ESBL-producing E. coli (CE) and colonised with a non-ESBL-producing E.
coli (C). Throughout this thesis, colonised means infected and replaces the I compartment in traditional
SIR / SIS models. The stages of S, C and CE occur in all three settings, giving us nine options for the
individuals to be in. Additionally, we have one compartment reflecting the recurrence period for the
colonisation in the community (SR).
In general, across all three stages, individuals can move from the community to the hospital, back again
to the community, or be admitted to the ICU. From the ICU, individuals can move back to the hospital

3.5
Sensitivity analysis
Sensitivity analysis is performed on models to measure the relative effect of the different variables. The
analysis also explores the stability of the model and how likely the outcome is when changing any of the
parameters. In general, there are two overall types of sensitivity analysis: a local and global sensitivity
analysis.
3.5.1
Global
In our model, the global sensitivity analysis indicates which parameters influence the number of colonised
and the mortality for both non-ESBL-producing and ESBL-producing E. coli.
As a part of the global sensitivity analysis, scatter plots with correlations are computed using the func-
tion pairs [51]. This function is a part of base R. The function takes a data set as input and makes a
matrix of plots, with the number of columns and rows equal to the number of input variables. These
types of plots were used to show correlations between the model parameters and populations [51].
The partial rank correlation coefficient (PRCC) for each parameter in relation to the number of colonised
individuals and the mortality for individuals with non-ESBL-producing and ESBL-producing E. coli was
computed. The value of the PRCC indicates the importance of the parameter in relation to the outcome.
The values of PRCC are between -1 and 1, and a PRCC numerically close to 0 indicates minor impor-
tance of the parameter to the outcome of the model. The sign of the PRCC indicates the qualitative
relationship between the parameter and the outcome [52].
The function used to compute the PRCCs in R is epi.prcc [52], which is a part of the R package epiR.
This function takes a data frame which contains the parameters of the model simulations and the output
in the last column. Further, the function takes two inputs besides the data frame, if it is a one- or two-
sided test and the confidence level. The one-sided test is used to evaluate whether or not the PRCC is
greater than zero and the two-sided test is used to evaluate whether or not the PRCC is greater or less
than zero. For our computation of the PRCC, the two-sided test and a confidence level of 95% were
chosen [52].
This function gives a data frame with five elements as output. The first one is the point estimate of the
PRCC between each parameter and the model outcome. The second and third are the lower and upper
boundaries of the confidence interval for the PRCC. The fourth is the test statistic which determines the
significance of non-zero values of the PRCC and the last output is the p-value for each PRCC.
3.5.2
Local
A local sensitivity analysis explores the local impact of changes in the input parameter, by only changing
one parameter’s value, at a time. Further, it studies how small changes in input parameters affect the
model output [53]. To test the sensitivity of our parameters, we chose to test only the parameters that
were found to be most influential in the global sensitivity analysis. The parameters chosen for this
analysis were βCcom, νS and νC. These values were then increased and decreased one at a time by 10%.

3.7
DALYs
As described in section 2.4, DALYs are a metric which describes the healthy life years lost or lived with
disability. In this thesis, it is used to quantify the burden of E. coli colonisations. Some assumptions were
made to calculate DALYs, these are:
1. The models in this thesis do not take age or gender into account, so the distribution of these was
used from the ”Antimicrobial resistance surveillance in Europe report” from 2022 by WHO [55]. Based
on this report, the gender distribution is equally split, and the age group distributions are:
• 0 to 4 years: 3%
• 5 to 19 years: 1%
• 20 to 64 years: 20%
• +65 years: 76%
2. The DWs used are from the study ”Assessing disability weights based on the responses of 30,660 people
from four European countries” by Haagsma et. al [56]. These are divided into mild, moderate and
severe acute episodes of infectious diseases.
3. As described in section 3.4.2, it is assumed that individuals have a UTI in the community, pyelonephri-
tis in the hospital and a BSI in the ICU. This has been assumed in relation to the calculations of
DALYs, as the colonised in the community are then assumed to have mild colonisation, colonised
in the hospital are assumed to have moderate colonisation and colonised in the ICU are assumed
to have severe colonisation.

Model description
The model simulations were performed in the
statistical programming software R. The concept
of this model is not new.
The authors have
gotten inspiration from several previously pub-
lished models. The model is described in two
overall settings: the community and the hospi-
tal. Within both settings individuals can be sus-
ceptible, have acquired ARB in the community
and have acquired ARB in the hospital.
The
model simulates a population with 100,000 indi-
viduals, with 0.25% being in the hospital at the
start. The model is deterministic and has discrete
time.
The model is divided into two overall settings:
exposed and not exposed.
These settings are
then further divided into households, nursing
homes and hospitals for the exposed and the
general population for the not exposed.
The
proposed structural model falls within the SEIS
class (Susceptible, Exposed, Infected, Suscepti-
ble) of compartment models, where exposed has
been replaced with colonized. The model is a
dynamic model, which uses both probabilistic
and deterministic quantification depending on
the subpopulation size. The probabilistic quan-
tification is used for small-sized subpopulations,
which are hospitals and nursing homes.
An
adjusted deterministic quantification is used for
large-size subpopulations.
Data evaluation
A range for each parameter is found in articles
and reports or estimated, and from here an ac-
ceptable value is chosen based on calibration. It
is not further described how this is performed.
Analysis of the model indicates that there are
several major key drivers for both the propor-
tion of individuals acquiring ARB in the hospital
population and guiding the prevalence of resis-
tance in the community. The drivers are antibi-
otic exposure, the rate of clearance of resistance
in the community and LOS at the hospital.
The data used to construct this model was from
five prospective studies. These five studies were
approved by the Institutional Review Board
at the University Hospital Virgen Macarena
(Seville, Spain) and consent was obtained for us-
ing the data. The data is local data and therefore
it will be difficult to use the model in very differ-
ent settings, as the parameters would have to be
changed to values in ranges that match the envi-
ronment and epidemiology of the area.

It was
here generated 10,000 parameter sets to explore

Discussion
5.1
Data extraction and TRACE
First of all, the models selected to be used as the basis for our model, rely on the larger systematic review
to catch relevant articles. It is very difficult to catch all relevant articles and some important models may
be missed.
Our goal with this thesis was to merge other models together, to attempt to build a more complex model.
The idea was that it was possible to take different elements from different models and combine them.
The issue is that the models’ base assumptions are not the same and in only one case, the code for the
model was provided. When the basic principles are not the same and the model is not described well
enough or provided by the code, it is hard to understand the different parts of the model to split it apart

and use them individually. Further, none of the found models implemented vaccinations. The selec-
tion of models looking at UTIs and/or BSIs was limited. Further, when we examined the articles using
TRACE, we discovered that most of the articles lack at least two of the TRACE elements. This makes
the replication, expansion and quality assessment process of the already existing models difficult. For
this thesis, it has been an aim to fulfil all eight TRACE elements. As authors of this thesis, we cannot be
objective, when saying that we are convinced that this aim is fulfilled, but the result of our TRACE can
be seen in table C.
In general, from TRACE it seems that scientists are much more focused on the results, rather than mak-
ing sure the model is able to be recreated giving the same results. If the model does not fulfil the TRACE
elements, it can result in fewer worked-through models and greater risks of implementing errors. Fur-
ther, it can also make it difficult to follow the thoughts behind the models.
In our process of developing the model, we have written a modelling notebook, see Appendix F. We
have written down each time we made larger changes to the model. This should make it easier for
external individuals, to understand why our model looks like it does, what we have tried to implement,
and why we did go with it or removed it again. We found this part surprisingly hard because we
moved on to a new model in the process of many small changes. We had a difficult time going back,
remembering and understanding the thoughts behind the previous version of the model. In future
model development, we would make it a habit to write down the alterations and thoughts each time we
would save a script.
5.2
Model type and our model
Our starting point was to use the well-known SIR and SIS systems, to build our model. These describe
transmission systems, and in the development of our model, we decided to replace the characteristic
transmission part, with a linear self-transmission function. This transmission was implemented as a
rate, directly on the susceptible individuals. The assumption is therefore that the number of colonised
individuals in the society does not depend on how many are colonised and able to spread the bacteria.
This further ensures that our baseline system becomes much more simple, and the interactions between
the different compartments, are more stable. This is also seen when performing the sensitivity analysis
on the baseline model, see section 4.3.2. Compared to earlier versions of the model (see Appendix F), the
inclusion of the self-transmission part, changed the system from being quite sensitive to small changes
in especially βCcom and νC to becoming stable for all the parameters. So the removal of the classic SIR
transmission part is both a simplification of the model and an assumption of how most of the E. coli in-
fections occur in the community. This change in the model led to a more stable model, but also a model
that deviated from the general SIR and SIS principles. This raises the question if the SIR and SIS models
in general are the best models to reflect infections that are not driven by person-to-person transmission.
For our thesis, we used compartment models to describe the transmission of E. coli in the population
of Denmark. This could also be described using an agent-based model, where the interactions between
individuals would be simulated. This type of model would be useful in the hospital and the ICU, as
transmissions between individuals may have a larger impact on the number of infected and colonised
individuals. This is especially due to the high number of contacts in the hospital and ICU, between
patients, nurses and doctors. It is expected that this will increase the rate of transmission between indi-
viduals [47][50].

The only way of going from having a non-ESBL-producing E. coli colonisation to having an ESBL-
producing E. coli colonisation, is assumed to be due to the treatment with antibiotics. This was done
as the transmission of E.coli in relation to UTIs are primarily due to self-infections in the community.
This was also done for the hospital and ICU, but here the implementation of catheters constitutes a ma-
jor risk of transmission. A Danish study showed that hospital admission appeared to be the primary
source for the acquisition of ESBL-producing E. coli and especially the number of different departments
visited increased the risk [73]. Another Danish study indicates that there are alternative routes for ac-
quiring UTIs caused by E. coli. For example, their study indicates that meat and production animals
may pose a zoonotic risk for individuals [74]. The transmission between individuals and additional
transmission routes in these settings could be explored in future work.
The approach of the model is deterministic. This has been chosen, as it is most often easier to imple-
ment compared to stochastic approaches. A deterministic model also gives the same output for every
run, whereas a stochastic model can give different outcomes. In a stochastic model, variations in the
parameter values will generate a more complex model and are more likely to give a better representa-
tion of reality. Most of the articles including our key points from the data extraction used a deterministic
model. The implementation of gender, age and comorbidity for the individuals, should be considered in
a further model. Potentially, the stochastic approach could help with this. This implementation would
be important for the understanding and visualisation of the risk factors associated with E. coli colonisa-
tions.
5.2.1
Parameters
In general, this project was limited by the fact that specific parameter values can be hard to find. We
decided initially to focus on E. coli infections resulting in the infection outcomes: UTI, pyelonephritis
and BSI. It had the purpose of making the model more specific and relevant. Further, many of the
parameters were hard to find for specific infection types and bacteria. Most of our parameters are based
on estimations of the number of cases and data from Denmark. From this, we found that there are data
gaps both globally and nationally. Some of the data gaps may be hard to fill, such as the number of
unreported cases of infections, since this would require a lot more surveillance of the infections for the
general population. Further, some of the data gaps could be filled by more models of the infections,
which could be used for further research and to compare models and knowledge.
We attempted to mostly use data from before 2020, due to the risk of Covid-19 influencing our values. It
was thought that especially the admission numbers for the susceptible could have been influenced and
that the number of admissions for the susceptible could affect the E. coli cases in our model to deviate
from the real number of cases.
5.2.2
Assumptions
Generally, one of the largest limitations often seen for models is the high number of assumptions made
for them. Assumptions are a way to limit the model, as the reality that the models try to imitate is often
too complex for a model. In the following part, the assumptions made for this model, written in section
3.4.2, will be discussed.
The stage of non-ESBL-producing E. coli is assumed to cover both resistant and non-resistant E. coli
colonisations. For some of the parameters, the values are assumed to be a bit lower for non-ESBL-

producing E. coli colonisations, this may not be the case, as some resistance types included in the non-
ESBL-producing group may have the same properties as ESBL-producing E. coli. In general, a commonly
used antibiotic against UTIs is ciprofloxacin. This type of antibiotic is often used for the treatment of
a wide range of community-acquired and nosocomial infections. In addition, resistance is commonly
seen for ciprofloxacin [75]. This indicates that there are other resistance types included in non-ESBL-
producing E. coli, which could increase the parameter value for these compartments.
The assumption of the split of infections in the community, hospital and ICU is a large simplification
of the reality. Numbers from Statens Serum Institut indicate that approximately 1,100 individuals get a
UTI each month in the hospital in Denmark [71]. This suggests that many of the cases in the hospital
may be due to UTIs and not pyelonephritis. For uncomplicated UTIs in the community, there may be a
larger number of unreported cases. This may be the case, as some of the infections are asymptomatic,
described in section 2.1.1, and some resolve on their own without treatment.
Antibiotic treatment is assumed to be given to all colonised individuals with non-ESBL-producing E.
coli, but as described above some cases do not need treatment to resolve and some are asymptomatic.
So in an extension of our model, it could be more realistic that only some of these colonised individuals
would be given antibiotic treatment. Or that individuals are divided into two separate groups, concern-
ing asymptomatic individuals with no treatment, and symptomatic ones that do get it. Further, it is also
assumed that the chance of being colonised with ESBL-producing E. coli, due to treatment, is the same
in all settings. But as mentioned in section 2.1.3, antibiotics used against BSIs, are more likely to be in
the watch or reserved group, compared to the treatment for UTIs, which most often is from the access
group. Further, it must be assumed that there in general are more antibiotics being used in the hospital
and ICU, making it more likely to acquire a resistant type of infection from the surroundings. Further,
due to treatment, there is an increased chance of susceptible bacteria dying and resistant bacteria sur-
viving and dominating the system [76].
Our model does not include natural death or birth, individuals can only die due to colonisation. This
causes the total population without dead individuals to decrease over time and go towards zero. Fur-
ther, the model will then not become completely stable. If the model is simulated over many years, it
will cause a large part of the active population to be removed from the system. Natural death and birth
could be implemented in future work, which could increase the stability of the system and may cause
the system to be more realistic.
5.2.3
Vaccination model
In section 3.6.2, the assumptions of the vaccination model are described and in the following, these will
be discussed.
In our model, we vaccinate individuals from both Scom and SRcom. As described in section 2.2.2, some
vaccine candidates show promising results targeting the recurrence individuals and some show promis-
ing results for new infections in the susceptible individuals.
In our model, it is assumed that vaccinated individuals still have the possibility of becoming colonised
and the severity of the colonisation is assumed to be the same for vaccinated individuals as for unvac-
cinated individuals. This may not be the case for these types of vaccines, where it is possible that it can
cause the vaccinated individual to have partial immunity. This could cause the colonisations to be less
severe for vaccinated individuals and could be explored further in future work. Further, this has the

possibility of influencing the disease burden calculations, which will be discussed later.
Related to this, it is assumed that individuals can still move from being vaccinated to being susceptible
in the hospital. This will happen with the same rate, γShos, as the movement from community to hospital
happens for a susceptible. This indicates that the immunity of the vaccine against E. coli colonisations,
are not transferred when entering the hospital. This is a huge simplification of the system and of the
efficacy of the vaccine.
The transmission of E. coli in our model is assumed to be due to self-infections. This causes the vaccina-
tion model to not be able to achieve herd immunity, as the vaccinated individuals cannot cause general
immunity in the society. But, it would be possible to reduce many of the cases, if the primary target
groups are more prone to be recolonised multiple times and have a more troublesome process. For ex-
ample, in Denmark, the target groups of vaccination against influenza infections, are primarily children
between two and six, pregnant women, healthcare workers, individuals with chronic diseases and el-
derlies above 65 years old [77]. This will reduce the number of severe cases associated with influenza,
and will, as in our case of E. coli, not result in herd immunity. For the case of E. coli colonisations, it
seems appropriate to vaccinate similar target groups as done for influenza. Elderlies, smaller children,
pregnant women and chronically sick individuals which often get admitted to the hospital. In addition,
women will most likely be overrepresented in the vaccinated group. Most of the cases in young women
with UTI are mild, and some are further asymptomatic. This indicates that the reach of herd immunity
against UTIs caused by E. coli may not be as necessary, but the focus should be on the vulnerable groups
most likely to progress to a disease with a higher burden.
In contrast to other SIR models including vaccination, we have described this part as a logistic part.
Normally, this will simply be described as a constant number of people, moving at each time step [78]. To
give a more realistic implementation of this, we ensured that the new number of vaccinated individuals
depends on how many are already vaccinated, and how many are possible to become vaccinated. This
ensures a more dynamic relation of how many newly vaccinated there is each time step.

cases for each compartment comes from different sources. This gives the possibility of some groups of

As seen in table 3, the parameter values used in our model are from different sources. All parameters are

data or models. This weakens the model’s reliability, and therefore our models. But, we have managed
to compare the results against other data, therefore verifying our model and its output.

5.4
DALYs
To calculate the DALYs both for the baseline model and the vaccination models, we made several as-
sumptions, described in section 3.7. We assumed a very small percentage of colonisations in the age
group 5 to 19 years, based on the ”Antimicrobial resistance surveillance in Europe report” from 2022 by
WHO [4]. This may not be realistic for all infections, since UTIs often affect young women, as described
in section 2.1.1. This split of populations is only for the reported cases of E. coli infections, where many
UTIs in young women may not be reported as infections by this pathogen.
The DWs used to calculate the DALYs are for mild, moderate and severe acute episodes of infectious dis-
eases. These values are not specifically for E. coli infections or colonisations, which may cause the DWs
to be a bit lower than DWs for E. coli. This may be the case as E. coli has been found to be the pathogen
with the highest counts of global deaths attributable to and associated with bacterial AMR, as described
in section 1. Further, the DWs have been assumed to be the same for both non-ESBL-producing and
ESBL-producing E. coli colonisations. These may not be the same, since infections with resistant bacteria
can be harder to treat, which could lead to the duration being longer and the severity being higher for
the disease [21]. This could cause the DWs to be higher for ESBL-producing E. coli, resulting in higher
DALYs.
The assumption in section 3.7 about the split of the life expectancy in Denmark, may cause the larger age
groupings to have lower values since these values are the mean values of the entire age group. Further,
the assumption about the calculation of YLDs and the use of LOS as ”the average time a person lives
with the disease before remission or death”, may also cause the values of DALYs to be lower or higher
than expected. This may be the case as an individual can be colonised longer than they are admitted
in real life, where it is assumed in the calculation that the LOS is also the clearance rate of colonisation.
Further, it was assumed that the LOS for the ESBL-producing E. coli infections were longer, compared
to the non-ESBL-producing E. coli. This makes the DALYs larger for ESBL-producing E. coli.
Our results of the calculations of DALYs from the baseline model output, described in section 4.3.3,
showed that most of the values for women were higher than for men. This is due to the fact that the
life expectancies used to calculate DALYs are higher for women than men. Further, the results showed
that the DALYs per 100,000 population were lower in the mild cases, which is due to the assumption
that individuals can not die in the community. This causes the YLL to be zero for the mild cases and
therefore the DALYs to only depend on the YLDs. The DALYs per case for ESBL-producing E. coli
follows the pattern of what we have expected, where the lowest DALYs per case are seen for mild cases,
then moderate and severe. Further, for non-ESBL-producing E. coli the DALYs per case for the moderate
cases are the highest, this is most likely due to the fact that the number of dead individuals is higher for
the moderate cases compared to the severe cases, where it is the opposite for ESBL-producing E. coli.
As mentioned in the introduction, DALYs have been calculated in other studies. These showed DALYs
for high-income countries to be 946.7 per 100,000 population for infections associated with bacterial
AMR and 222.3 per 100,000 population for infections attributable to bacterial AMR [9]. When comparing
the DALYs from the study to our results of the baseline model, we see that our results are significantly
lower for ESBL-producing colonisations. Our results show DALYs per 100,000 population of approx-
imately 38 for ESBL-producing E. coli and 79 for non-ESBL-producing E. coli when adding DALYs for
both men and women.
The values from the literature may be higher due to the difference in the settings of the data, where

of moderate and severe but are most likely due to small differences in the amount of dead and infected
individuals, as the vaccination does not change the progression of the infection, only the likelihood of it
occurring.
5.5
Strengths and limitations
All models have several strengths and limitations. The most noticeable ones in our model are:
Strengths:
1. Fulfilled the TRACE elements, see table C, and it is, therefore, easier for external users to
reproduce the model.
2. The model is based on other published and peer-reviewed models.
3. The inclusion of three settings representing disease severity; community, hospital and ICU.
4. The implementation of a recurrence stage in the community, which is a large part of the
disease burden.
5. The separation of ESBL-producing and non-ESBL-producing E. coli into two stages to com-
pare the burden of resistance.
6. Calculation of DALYs and comparison of our results of DALYs to other studies, which showed
that our results are comparable to other studies [79].
7. Implementation of vaccination as an intervention to reduce the burden of disease, which is
compared to our baseline model.
8. The model has been simulated using R, which is a free software and the code can easily be
shared and further developed by other researchers.

Limitations:
1. The model is not separated by age, gender or comorbidity. As for gender, we assume equal
occurrence across gender, although UTIs are predominant in women.
2. The model does not differentiate between the likelihood across the settings of the infection
developing resistance due to antibiotic treatment. Each compartment has the same likelihood
of developing resistance throughout the LOS. This would be assumed to be higher in the
hospital and ICU.
3. It is not implemented in the model, that colonised can be asymptomatic and symptomatic.
The model and DALY estimation do not account for unreported cases.
4. The sources used for estimating the number of cases for each compartment is found from
different sources, resulting in the population may not be completed.
5. The parameter values used for the model, are not exclusively for Denmark. This could influ-
ence the values as Denmark is thought of as a leading country in surveillance and AMR.
6. The model does not include nursing homes as a setting. This is a limitation, since the burden
of disease may be higher for elderlies and elderlies with underlying conditions.
7. Partial immunity following infection or vaccination is not included.
5.6
Future perspectives
When making these transmission models, there are always things that could have been done differently.
In this section, we will consider some of the changes that could be made to this model in future work.
For further alterations and additions to our model, it should be considered to divide the individuals
into gender, age and comorbidity specific. For example with a stochastic approach. Especially the im-
plementation of age could make a difference, as younger individuals - especially women - most often
have an uncomplicated disease course. Whereas for smaller children and elderlies, most often gives
uncharacteristic symptoms, making it more likely to induce a more severe disease course. Further, the
implementation of several stages describing the disease should be considered. Suggestions would be to
implement the infection to be asymptomatic and symptomatic. This could also be a way to distinguish
between individuals that get treatment. The considerations of age, gender, comorbidity and differenti-
ation between infected and colonised, were also among the consideration for future models denoted in
Knight 2018 [37]. They state that they have not implemented it, due to a lack of sufficient data, which
we can confirm. But, the solution to this, could be to look at other models for corresponding or similar
bacteria, which may have included this. Due to the fact that it is especially elderlies that get more severe
disease courses, it would be obvious to implement a new setting, nursing homes. Inhere, the rate of
getting treatment could be increased compared to elderlies in the community. Further, the rate of the
infection found may also be higher, and therefore the risk of a more severe disease course could be lower
here. Other models, such as the model by Talaminos 2016 [38], have included such a state.
In our vaccination model, it is not possible to retain the immunity of the vaccination if an individual
move to the hospital as a vaccinated person. This could relatively easily be implemented by adding two
additional compartments. One for vaccinated individuals at the hospital and one for the ICU.
In our model, we implemented vaccinations as an intervention to decrease the number of colonisations
and the burden of the disease. As shown in our data extraction in table 5, many models implement

other types of interventions, such as hand hygiene, monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) and isolation. Hand

fewer cases of transmission between patients, even though the transmission between individuals was
not accounted for in our model. mAbs could be useful to decrease the number of antibiotic agents used
in all settings, which has the possibility to decrease AMR generally. The mAbs could also decrease the
burden of disease, as they do not cause resistance and may be used to treat infections and colonisations
with resistant bacteria. So to wrap up, there is still much further work, to be done for exploring and

6
Conclusion
With this thesis, we aimed at developing a mathematical model to investigate the disease burden of
ESBL-producing and non-ESBL-producing E. coli and measure the effectiveness of the implementation
of vaccination against E. coli infections.
To develop this model, we used a literature review and TRACE to assess the already published models.
From the literature review, it could be concluded that there are several models describing E. coli and
interventions against the infections. But none of them included vaccinations. The TRACE evaluation
discovered that none of the 18 articles fulfilled all elements of TRACE. This made it clear that there is a
large need for improvements in this area and in data for the models generally.
Our model is based on multiple already published models. During the building of this model, we found
that there are data gaps in model building and in relation to parameters. Further, the model developed
throughout this thesis has been found to be able to simulate the transmission of E. coli in Denmark. We
have found that our baseline model’s yearly number of colonised for ESBL-producing and non-ESBL-
producing E. coli, matches the expected numbers we have found based on sources from Denmark. When
looking at the DALYs calculated from this model, it is found to be a bit lower compared to sources for
high-income countries. Generally, the burden of disease for ESBL-producing E. coli have been found to
be higher based on DALYs per case. Further, the DALYs from a study with data from Denmark [79]
were found to be close to our results.
The vaccination model results in a decreased number of cases across all settings, both for ESBL-producing
and non-ESBL-producing E. coli, compared to the baseline model. This is seen in both the best- and
worst-case scenarios. Further, a decrease in the results of DALYs is seen for these models, compared to
the baseline. This indicates that we achieve to implement a vaccine in the model, which gives a decrease
in the burden of disease. Further indicating, that this could lead to a decreased AMU, and therefore be
a step in the right direction for resolving the problem of AMR.
Even though our model is not representing the golden solution, it helps to include more relevant models
out there, regarding E. coli infections. This contributes to making the parameter-finding process and
model layout, easier. Further, with the increasing number of accessible models, it makes it easier to
validate the models and their outputs.
The model developed in this thesis is further contributing to the larger international project PrIMaVeRa,
where the aim is to answer the question about the effect of vaccinations for E. coli infections, and how
this will contribute to the AMR and the burden of disease.

Model description
This TRACE element provides supporting information on: The model. Provide a detailed written model description. For
individual/agent-based and other simulation models, the ODD protocol is recommended as a standard format. For complex
submodels, it should include concise explanations of the underlying rationale. Model users should learn what the model is, how
it works, and what guided its design.
The model is described in the article, and further details about the model equations, dynamics and sim-
ulation are described in the supplementary. The model simulations were performed in the statistical
programming software R.
The concept of this model is not new. The authors have gotten inspiration from several previously pub-
lished models, which have similar settings. The previous models describe the ARB movement between
hospital and community settings. The focus has previously been on the strains from the community
invading the hospitals, or the driver mechanisms of the maintenance of resistance in the hospital. The
overall purpose of this model is to simulate the proportion of individuals that acquired ARB in the hos-
pital.
The model is described in two overall settings: the community and the hospital. Within both settings
individuals can be susceptible, have acquired ARB in the community and have acquired ARB in the
hospital. Susceptible individuals in the community can acquire ARB either by transmission or due to
antibiotic treatment. With time these individuals will become susceptible again. Both susceptible and
individuals who acquired ARB can move from the community settings to the hospital settings. In hos-
pital settings susceptible individuals can acquire ARB by either transmission or by antibiotic treatment,
exactly like in the community. But, in the hospital, the likelihood of being transmitted when meeting
with an individual that had acquired ARB is higher, compared to in the community. On the other hand,
the likelihood of meeting an individual with ARB in the community is higher, compared to the hospital.
Again, people no matter the state they are in, are able to move from the hospital to the community, and
the other way around. It is only possible to become susceptible again in the community, no matter if the
acquisition of ARB happened in the community or the hospital. The system is showed in figure A.1.

This TRACE element provides supporting information on: The model. Provide a detailed written model description. For
individual/agent-based and other simulation models, the ODD protocol is recommended as a standard format. For complex
submodels, it should include concise explanations of the underlying rationale. Model users should learn what the model is, how

The model has been described in the article, and further details about the model equations and pa-
rameter values have been described in the supplementary. The programming language of the model
simulations has not been specified in either the article or the supplementary, the authors only mention
that the model will be using a high-level programming language.
The concept of this model is not new. The authors considered the epidemiological background of ST131,
data from ad-hoc studies and various models that are available that addresses the transmission of infec-
tious diseases. The previous models describe the transmission of infectious diseases and often analyze
the healthcare and community settings separately. Whereas this work aims to emphasize the interactions
among different populations liable to suffer contagion by ESBL-producing and non-ESBL-producing
ST131. Therefore, an epidemiological framework and mathematical model which explains the spread
of ESBL-producing and non-ESBL-producing E. coli ST131 have been constructed, using a population-
based perspective and the authors further investigate the potential impact of control interventions.
The model is divided into two overall settings: the exposed and the not exposed. These settings are
then further divided into households, nursing homes and hospitals for the exposed and the general
population for the not exposed. The household setting is categorized as the population where at least
one individual in the household was colonized. Both the household and the hospital setting have the
same structure, where there are five compartments; Susceptible, ST131 colonized, ST131 infected, ESBL-
producing ST131 colonized and ESBL-producing ST131 infected. Further, the general population only
has one compartment, which is susceptible, as they are moved to other settings if they get colonized.
The nursing homes are split into three compartments; susceptible, ST131 colonized and ESBL-producing
ST131 colonized. The proposed structural model falls within the SEIS class (Susceptible, Exposed, In-
fected, Susceptible) of compartment models, where exposed has been replaced with colonized. The
largest part of the population is considered part of the susceptible compartment in the general popula-
tion. The flow between the compartments and the structure of the model can be seen in figure B.1.
The overall purpose of the model is the predict the occurrence of new cases of infection caused by ESBL-
producing and non-ESBL-producing E. coli ST131 based on epidemiological data.

A stepwise approach was used to develop the model. First, they analyze possible interactions between
the different variables, taking cause-effect relationships into account. This will lead to a mathematical
model of the system, by describing the relationship via mathematical equations. The second objective
is to implement the mathematical model using a high-level programming language. The third is to val-
idate the model with the dynamics observed. The fourth and last objective is to exploit the model by
simulating different scenarios to help analyze the effects of possible strategies for the interventions.
The entities in the model are individuals either in the household, the hospital, the general population,
or the nursing homes. The state variables include susceptible individuals, colonized individuals, and
infected individuals. The largest part of the population is considered to be part of the susceptible group
in the general population. The size of the population was determined based on local demographic data
and the size of the population in each compartment in one year was 800,000 for the general population,
70,000 for the hospital population and 7,000 for the nursing home population. The household popula-
tion were assumed to have the initial proportion of susceptible (non-colonized) to colonized individuals
3:1 and the average number of individuals in a household was four. The simulation length covers a
period of one year.
The model’s quantification was based on the law of mass preservation, where state variables correspond
to the population under consideration (households, nursing homes and hospitals). These enabled rela-
tionships in the flows of individuals between the different compartments. The dynamic behaviours of
the population states were proposed by a set of first-level coupled differential equations. The general
state equation was expressed as:
dDpi

= Fap −Fdp
Where Dpi represents the population state of compartment I and Fap or Fdp represent the flow of indi-
viduals entering or leaving the specific state.
The model is a dynamic model, which uses both probabilistic and deterministic quantification depend-
ing on the subpopulation size. The probabilistic quantification is used for small-sized subpopulations,
which are hospitals and nursing homes. An adjusted deterministic quantification is used for large-size
subpopulations. The deterministic quantification was adjusted using the least squares method.

F.3

The thought was that some will get infected from non-ESBL producing to ESBL producing E. coli, due
to resistance developing from treatment or simply by getting infected by ESBL producing E. coli. This
may be a way to help the system in the right direction. As mentioned, we did not have as many values
for the parameters describing non-ESBL-producing E. coli. Therefore, in the cases where we did not
have a value from literature, we chose a value in the same range as the parameter range for ESBL-
producing E. coli. In most cases, we chose to use the smaller value for non-ESBL and a larger value for
ESBL-producing E. coli. This was done with the intuition of individuals with ESBL-producing E. coli
had a higher risk of getting more severely sick, dying and having a long process with the infection than
individuals with non-ESBL producing E. coli. This was assumed as non-ESBL producing E. coli includes
both sensitive E. coli strains and other resistant strains.

F.4
Introducing mortality and birth to the model
A birth and mortality rate was then introduced to the model. Individuals in this model can only be born
into the susceptible group in the community, as it was assumed that the number of births for individuals
that are infected, hospitalised or in the ICU is very low, and were therefore omitted. The birth rate was
multiplied with the entire population of the model at each time step and added to the population of
susceptible individuals in the community. The mortality rate introduced in this model was the death
of individuals not caused by an E. coli infection. This mortality rate was introduced to all parts of the
system, as it was assumed that individuals can die due to other causes than E. coli infections in all stages
and all settings.
The addition of birth and mortality to the model did not create a stable model and the model did not
reach equilibrium. The parameter values for this model are the same as in the section above, Appendix
F.3.
We found that the system with mortality in all the settings and across the three groups, where too
complex. This system was very hard to stabilise. And as the initial thought, was that it should help
us with the distribution of individuals in the hospital and ICU, it did not work. It was hard to ensure
enough infections to keep the system running. In this system, we had the same mortality rate for all
groups in the community, with the assumption that an individual will enter at least the hospital if the
infection gets out of control. We then found the general mortality rate for the hospital and ICU in general
in Denmark. And we found mortality rates for ESBL and non-ESBL-producing E. coli in both the hospital
and ICU.
In general, we found that the balance between having a system, where individuals get colonised and can
die, and one where everything is dying, is a bit tough to find. In most cases, the individuals colonised
with E. coli, was decreasing, and after simulating the model for 10 years, the number of cases was close
to zero across all settings.

F.6
Reevaluate the amount of colonised
In the process of performing this project, it came to our attention that we had underestimated the num-
ber of yearly infections due to E. coli. In the further process of evolving the model, the values listed in
table 8, are used as a guideline. To this point, we have calculated with a factor 10 lower in the com-
munity for both CE and C, and around 10,000 less in the hospital for C and a factor 10 less for CE. The
amount of colonised individuals in the ICU was too thought to be lower.
These new guidance values ensured that we could not use most of the values describing β, as we simply
could not make enough colonised in the system.
It was tried to make the system from the previous section fit the new requirements. This was done with-
out luck. The system does now become very unstable. In this process, we both try to fit the parameter
values, but also the initial values. The system stayed unstable. Especially the system was sensitive to
alterations in the βCcom and νC.
Due to the steady unstable system, and the new thinking, about the more reasonable numbers of infec-
tions each year. The transmission part of the equations was rethought too.
F.7
Remove transmission
For this system, it was decided to remove the transmission part describing the movement from the S
populations to CE and C. Even though we are using a transmission model system as a baseline, this
was done as UTIs are most often caused by transmission from the host themselves, self-transmission.
Therefore, this was replaced with a simple rate. Due to this, the transmission due to interaction between
colonised from C to CE, denoted κ, was removed too. This was partly due to that κ had a limited impact
on the system.
The transmission alteration ensured that the system become way more stable than previously. This new
system was not as sensitive for changes in especially βCcom and νC. The initial values and parameter
values are visualised in table F.4 and F.5.
