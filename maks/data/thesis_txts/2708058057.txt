Abstract
In recent years, the use of additive manufacturing in the construction industry has become
increasingly popular with the rise of modern 3D printing technologies.
Commonly used
materials for 3D construction printing uses a high amount of cement which is harmful to
the environment. Hence, the construction industry and its wider socio-economic context are
continuously recognising the necessity for environmentally friendly alternatives.
This thesis evaluates the possibility of substituting a cementitious binder in 3D printable
concrete with a novel material that contains a biologically based polymer. The focal point
of this thesis is to test and evaluate rheological properties of biopolymer concrete and its
suitability for additive manufacturing. Biopolymer concrete must obtain certain rheological
properties, characterised by suﬃcient extrudability and buildability through thixotropic-
and thermoplastic behaviour. Two polymers, Agar and κ-Carrageenan, are each mixed with
water and used as binders. The two materials are subject to comprehensive testing and
comparison to one another as well as conventional 3D printable concrete.
Firstly, a preliminary visual evaluation of workability, extrudability and buildability was
conducted to identify the optimum binder concentration, binder-to-sand-ratio and material
temperature for each binder.
Secondly, the two best mix designs for each binder and a conventional 3D printable concrete
were further investigated. A vicat apparatus test was used to evaluate the open time of
the material. The buildability was evaluated by the yield stress development, which was
found using a shear vane apparatus and an unconﬁned uniaxial compression test over time.
Thixotropic- and thermoplastic behaviour along with other rheological characterisations were
evaluated using an oscillatory rheometer.
The test program indicated that Agar as a binder is not suited for 3D printing due to a
substantial degree of segregation. However, κ-Carrageenan displayed a faster yield stress
development than 3D printable concrete while also showing promising extrudability proper-
ties.
Ultimately, the test program ranked the tested mix designs according to three parameters;
open time, extrudability and buildability. The highest ranked mix design was optimised in
accordance with performed tests and ﬁnally used in a full scale test print.

Introduction
1.1
Research motivation
Recent years have shown a signiﬁcant improvement in large-scale, constructional 3D print-
ing development. Since additive manufacturing have been introduced to the construction
industry, along with 3D printing technologies, several advantages are found by comparing
it to traditional construction methods [1]. One advantage is reduced cost, as both human
resources and construction time on-site are lowered by using 3D printing technologies [2].
Additionally, it is found that around 60% of the constructional cost can be reduced by re-
moving formwork, which traditional concrete projects are highly dependant on [3].
The
implementation of constructional 3D printing will also enable increased eﬃciency as well
as higher ﬂexibility regarding design in the project [2]. 3D printing can decrease the CO2
impact from a construction project due to the optimized shapes and precise placement of
material - thus providing a much more environmental-friendly solution. Additionally, it is
seen that additive manufacturing creates less waste and requires less material for the job,
which results in less environmental damage caused by the construction industry [2].
As the aforementioned advantages will surely clear the way for future building projects, some
challenges still occur in construction 3D printing. As of today, the signiﬁcant challenges rely
on the material used for 3D printers, as 3D printable concrete (3DPC) must be ﬂuid enough
to be printed, but should also obtain a fast hardening in order for the subsequent layers to
be printed on top of one another [2]. These requirements rely on the rheological properties
of 3DPC, which is highly dependent on the additives utilised for the speciﬁc mixture, and
an increased amount of cementitious materials compared to conventional concrete [1]. As
cement plays a signiﬁcant role in regards to the environmental impact coming from the
construction industry, high interest is emerging to ﬁnd reliable alternatives for 3DPC.
Ongoing research at the Department of Civil Engineering at the Technical University of
Denmark has investigated the possibility of utilising biologically based binding material for
constructional 3DPC [4]. The investigated material is known as a biopolymer, resourced
from industrial waste products. Products such as bone glue and animal tissues can now
be used as a replacement fully capable of replacing cementitious material. The binder has
shown a quick change in viscosity after extrusion, which is a desired property in the ﬁeld of
constructional 3D printing. As this polymer has shown promising results for utilisation in
the constructional 3D printing industry, it is desired to investigate other polymers for the
same rheological properties to ﬁnd more environmentally friendly alternatives.

2.1
General process of 3D printing
The following section aims to describe the technologies used for 3D printing within the
construction industry. Firstly, the general characterisation of 3D printing will be described.
Subsequently, a more detailed description will be made for an extrusion-based process within
the construction industry.
2.2
Constructional additive manufacturing
The ﬁeld of additive manufacturing within the construction industry is getting more attention
than ever before. Automation is still at a fairly immature stage in the construction industry.
However, new 3D printing technologies are being developed for construction sites to increase
project eﬃciency. Converting manual-heavy work tasks into automated processes within
construction can decrease construction cost, building time and lead to less material waste
and lower energy consumption [5].
Additive manufacturing can simultaneously enable companies to obtain more architectural
freedom regarding the fabrication of complex projects and structures [5, 1].
2.2.1
General concrete construction
Today, concrete is the most commonly used building material in the world. The raw materials
used for concrete production are cheap and easily accessible, which makes it useful all around
the globe. Raw materials for concrete production generally includes a ﬁller of sand, gravel
or similar granulate materials, which is bound by a concentration of water and cementitious
materials.
The cementitious material is usually cement but can be cement mixed with
cement-replacers such as ﬂy-ash, limestone ﬁller, slag etc. [6]. Concrete as a construction
material is strong in compression, as well as durable, ﬁre resistant and easily modiﬁed in
regards to shape, as it is cast as a ﬂuid, which hardens over time [6].
As a result of the widely used concrete around the globe, alternative ways to optimise the
concrete to achieve higher strength, lower CO2 footprint, and other performance increasing
properties are being studied. These studies involve using and optimising additives, aggregates
and diﬀerent cementitious materials suitable to obtain speciﬁc properties of the material [6].

Methods that involve additive manufacturing, (i.e. 3D printing) have been explored since

2.2.2
Constructional 3D printing system - Material extrusion
The majority of traditional 3D printing techniques are based upon thermo-dependent prop-
erties. This allows for materials to obtain a rapid solidiﬁcation and fast printing rate. Figure
3 shows a traditional 3D printer.
Figure 3: Example of a traditional 3D printer - Prusa i3 MK3S+ 3D printer [8].
Traditional 3D printing techniques cannot be directly applied on cement-based materials,
but instead, the setup needs to be adapted for this kind of printing purpose [2].
3D printing methods involving material extrusion is the most commonly used technique
in the 3D construction industry. The method involves an extrusion/deposition process, in
which the structure is made from depositing fresh cementitious material along a deﬁned path
as layer-by-layer on top of one another [5]. Figure 4, showcases a constructional 3D printing
system.

The gantry printer allows the printing head to move both horizontally and vertically. The
printer is placed on a steel frame, which is scaleable depending on the printed project [1]. A
robotic printer provides the ability to print in 6 diﬀerent axes, which allows for more complex
printing projects to be conducted.
One of the main beneﬁts of using a gantry printer is the ability to scale the printer in
accordance with diﬀerent project requirements. This is not the case for a robotic printer.
When using a robotic printer, the size is often ﬁxed and therefore harder to scale when
printing. However, the robotic printer can print at a higher velocity and has more degrees
of freedom than the gantry system [7]. This makes the robotic printing system suitable for
complex printing assignments, while a gantry printer is typically used for simpler- and larger
projects, as the cost is lower for a gantry printer compared to a robotic printer.
2.2.3
Challenges of 3D concrete printing
Unfortunately, 3D printing concrete structures does not come without challenges and impli-
cations. Challenges are often linked to the architectural design, the limitation in regards to
reinforcement, as well as a limited range of materials, which can be used for the job. The
forthcoming section delineates the aforementioned.
Limitations related to the architectural design of the construction
Architectural freedom is one of the beneﬁts of using 3D printing technology in construction.
There is, however, some limitations regarding the design. At the moment, the 3D printing
concept for concrete construction only applies to low-rise projects [1], and the size of the
project is restricted to the size of the utilised 3D printer [2].
Limited ability to reinforce the structure
Reinforcement is an issue in the ﬁeld of construction 3D printing. Currently, 3D printed
concrete structures struggle to replace conventionally used methods, as it is not possible to
integrate conventional reinforcement into 3D printed structures. 3D printed structures will
therefore be weak in a tension situation, compared to conventional concrete structures [1].
This is an evident limitation for 3D printed structures, as it is not possible to use printed
structures that will be exposed to bending or tension.
Scholars suggest using ﬁber-reinforced concrete that is printed along with the concrete struc-
ture [11]. In such an instant, the ﬁber-reinforcement is printed alongside the concrete and
thus replace the longitudinal reinforcement from the conventional concrete structure. The
ﬁber-reinforcement can potentially increase the strength of 3D printed structures in tension
and bending.

2.3
Utilised materials
Limited range of compatible materials for 3D printing
As the concrete industry continuously explores more environmental-friendly solutions, ma-
terials suited for substitution are being examined and researched like never before. Never-
theless, when it comes to 3D printing, it is a limited ﬁeld for compatible materials, which
can be used for construction purposes. It is one of the main challenges to ﬁnd the correct
balance in regards to the consistency of the mixture in order for it to work with a concrete 3D
printer. The cementitious mixture should feature properties such as easy-ﬂowing throughout
the system as well as easy-pumping, easy-deposition, dimension stability, and low-shrinkage
[1]. The rheology of the mixture plays a signiﬁcant role in the success of the printed project.
The rheology deﬁnes the shape of the material, which is essential as a 3D printed structure
is constructed without performing any formwork. The mix design must be optimised for the
concrete to run smoothly through the pumping system and extrude through the nozzle. It
should also have a suﬃcient setting time and yield stress to be ready for subsequent layers,

Rheological principles
Rheology deﬁnes the theory based on the ﬂow and deformation of a given matter. Many
properties can be given for materials, but rheology generally tells how the material responds
to an imposed force. Most often, the force in rheological theory is stress - i.e., the ratio
between the force and the surface area of application. The material response is measured in
either deformation, strain or deformation rate (also known as strain rate) [19].

3.1.5
Temperature-dependent behaviour
The ﬂow and deformation of diﬀerent materials may vary regarding time-dependent be-
haviour, but some materials will also depend on the temperature variation. Temperature-
dependant behaviour frequently shows how some materials may melt or soften due to heating,
while other materials may solidify, crystallize or even turn into a gel when cooled down [19].
Viscosity can be found from temperature-dependant viscosity curves. In most cases, viscos-
ity will decrease if a sample is heated. When heating up, the most interesting points are the
softening and melting temperatures [20]. When cooling, the solidiﬁcation temperature can be
determined, and for some materials, the congealing temperature. Temperature-dependency
theory and the behaviour of given materials are an important aspect of 3D printing polymer
concretes, as it often deﬁnes the desired consistency of the 3D printable solution.

Cement-based materials
In additive manufacturing a structure is build layer upon layer based on a 3D model. This
requires a material with very speciﬁc rheological properties.

3.2.3
Polymer gels
Polymer gels are usually considered chemically or physically cross-linked and form a three-
dimensional network structure. They are usually a matter swollen up with an appropriate
solvent, and can possess many properties of a solid, but contains only very little solid material
[36].
Hydrogels are hydrophilic cross-linked polymer which does not dissolve in water but can
obtain large amounts of water while maintaining its structure. Hydrogels are used in bio-
fabrication and show promising capabilities for printing complex composite tissue [37]. When
hydrogels are exposed to speciﬁc physical or chemical changes, they respond by a signiﬁcant
volume phase transition or gel-sol phase transition. These physical or chemical exposures
can be temperature, solvent composition, pressure, al., while the chemical stimuli can be PH,
ions and speciﬁc chemical compositions. In most cases, the transition is reversible, meaning
that the hydrogel will go back to its initial state after the external physical or chemical
stimuli is gone [38].
For this thesis, the focus will be on hydrogels that respond to the thermal gelation method.
The advantages of being able to use the thermal gelation method are that the reversible
interactions ensure constant viscosity of the material while printing and a rapid reassembly
to gels after printing [39]. The temperature can also be controlled throughout the whole
process, making it easier to ensure the appropriate viscosity at the right time.

variation of the printable range in diﬀerent studies is related to the characteristics of the
speciﬁc printing head.
Open time
Throughout the studies, the open time is addressed either by Vicat apparatus test or slump
loss over time.
Buildability
A common approach for testing the buildability of a printable material is printing a speciﬁed
amount of layers and checking for collapse and deformation of the bottom layers. As for
the visual examination of extrudability, no rheological properties will be achieved by this
method. Therefore, most studies will use diﬀerent tests in order to predict the buildability
of a printable material without actually printing.
Generally, the literature uses one of two diﬀerent material failure-criteria to predict the
buildability of a printable material. The methods are known as the Mohr-Coulomb failure
criterion and the yield stress of non-newtonian ﬂuid. When using the Mohr-Coulomb failure
criterion the direct-shear test is used. Here the cohesion, C, and the friction angle, ϕ, is used
to ﬁnd the shear yield stress.
τy = C(t) + σn · tan(ϕ(t))

where, τ is the shear yield stress and σ is the normal stress.
When using the failure criterion of an yield stress non-newtonian ﬂuid, the rotational-
rheometer or a shear vane tester is used over time in order to determine the static yield
stress over time.
The results from the failure criterion are often compared and validated with either an un-
conﬁned uniaxial compression test or a wall print test.

Experimental test program
Based on the state of the art methodologies presented in Section 3.4, an experimental testing

A preliminary visual evaluation of the workability, extrudability and buildability is con-

strength of the material. The two tests are used for evaluating the buildability properties of
the diﬀerent samples.

and thermoplastic properties of the samples.

The Vicat apparatus test (VAT) is usually used to test the setting time of cement. The
method is used for assessing if a speciﬁc cement conforms with its speciﬁed setting time

and soundness. In this thesis, the VAT is used for assessing the open time of a 3D printable

time. The workability loss is a result of the thixotropic- and thermoplastic behaviour of

The testing procedure for the SV test is as follows:
• The heated material is poured into four diﬀerent molds, each covered with a plastic

4.7.2
Print recommendations and adjustments
Based on the diﬀerent test results, important adjustments and recommendations for printing
with Car-4%WV0.5BS85D will be explained below in order to perform the best possible full-
scale printing experiment.
The minimum layer time found from GS test was determined as 38s and for the SV test as
50s. A layer time of 50s would therefore be preferred.
From the VAT, it was seen that after 11.5min a signiﬁcant change in the penetration resis-
tance occurred. Therefore, no print pauses should exceed 11.5min, as it could lead to a loss
of interlayer bonding.
From initial temperature measurements seen in Table 6, it was found that actual temperature
was between 62 −68◦C when the processing temperature was 85◦C.
From the temperature sweep presented in Figure 79, a rapid increase in the G∗was found
when reaching a temperature of 50◦C. Therefore, the extrudability temperature should be
kept above 50◦C, and preferably between the extrudability temperature range, which is
54◦C-66◦C.
As the actual temperature is placed in the higher end of the preferable extrudability range, it
should be allowed to decrease by 8◦C. A decrease in temperature will change the consistency
of the BPC, which could be preferred according to the print.
It is essential that the actual extrudability temperature does not go below 50◦C, as the
signiﬁcant change in G∗could lead to clogging or segregation in the nozzle.

5.1
Conclusion
This thesis aimed to determine speciﬁc rheological parameters of biopolymer concrete com-
posites. This was done through experimental investigation of rheological properties needed
for suﬃcient extrudability and buildability. Two diﬀerent natural polymers was used as bio-
based binders. Through an iterative preliminary printability assessment, the optimal mix
designs for each bio-based binder was found.
Based on the state of the art methodologies, an experimental testing program was planned
for the BPC. The aim of the experimental investigation was the open time, extrudability,
buildability, and further rheological characterisations.
This following section encompasses overall conclusions in regards to the investigations carried
out, their results and appertaining discussions. The following points have been concluded
upon experimental investigation.
• Due to frequently occurring segregation, it was concluded that the biopolymer based
composites containing Agar was not suitable for 3D printing. The conclusion relies on
the mixing- and processing methodology presented in this thesis.
• From preliminary printability assessment, it was found that higher processing temper-
atures and higher percentage binder weight-to-volume of Agar, not exceeding 10%WV,
led to less segregation.
• From preliminary printability assessment, a printable area was found for mix design
containing 3%WV- and 4%WV κ-Carrageenan. The printable area showed satisfying
extrudability- and buildability properties.
• Green strength and shear vane testing methodologies showed similar results regard-
ing the yield stress development of the materials. From the results it was possible to
estimate a minimum layer time for each material. The results showed a higher de-
velopment of yield stress for BPC containing κ-Carragenan compared to conventional
3DPC and BPC containing Agar.
• Amplitude sweep showed a shear-thinning viscoelastic behaviour of the BPC containing
κ-Carrageenan.
• Oscillatory test of the thixotropy, showed a thixotropic behaviour for BPC containing
κ-Carrageenan but a faster structural build-up for conventional 3DPC.
• Temperature sweeps showed that thermoplastic behaviour for BPC containing κ-Carrageenan
exceeded the structural build-up for conventional 3DPC. This ensured a signiﬁcant in-

• The temperature sweeps also showed a signiﬁcant increase in the complex shear modu-

This thesis has investigated the use of biologically based polymers as binding material in 3D
printable composites. The results showed overall great potential within the ﬁeld of construc-
tional 3D printing, as an environmentally friendly alternatives to cementitious materials.
5.2
Recommendations for future work
As biologically-based concrete is a newly developed and studied material, further investi-
gation of properties is needed in order to complement the rheological investigation found
throughout this thesis.
By using the mixing procedures employed in this thesis, it was not possible to develop a BPC
containing Agar suitable for 3D printing. It would be of interest to try changing the mixing
temperature, mixing time, heating time or temperature of the binder prior to mixing. This
could enhance the BPCs’ resistance against segregation.
A printable area was detected through the preliminary printability assessment for the κ-
Carrageenan. Four diﬀerent mixes passed the preliminary printability assessment, whereas
two were tested for further rheological properties. It would be of interest to further inves-
tigate the inﬂuence of additional concentrations and temperatures in terms of rheological
properties for 3D printing. Optimisation of open time could prove to be a signiﬁcant area
of further research to avoid the problem regarding interlayer bonding seen at the showcase
print.
Due to the thermoplastic properties of the BPC, the temperature has a vital impact on the
printability properties. Therefore, it is of interest to further investigate the temperature
behaviour of the material in a printing situation. A greater understanding of the material
temperature in the extruder and after extrusion would allow a better interpretation of test
results and a better adaption of testing methodologies.
As shown in this thesis, the early age behaviour of the BPC containing κ-Carrageenan
showed better buildability properties than two diﬀerent conventional 3D printable concretes.

Diﬀerent tests have been performed and discussed throughout this report.

6.5.1
Alternative mixing procedure
The mixing methodology used in this project, is described in Section 4.2.1. In this mixing
methodology, the mixed BPC was stored in a cooling chamber at 5 degree until use at
maximum 3 days.
To ﬁnd out if there is an eﬀect of the storing conditions, a mixture of Agar-5%WV-0.5BS-85D
is made. After mixing the material is placed directly into the oven instead of placing it in the
cooling chamber. The sample is then compared in terms of extrudability and buildability to
the same sample which was kept in the cooling chamber for 3 days. The results for the two
samples are shown Figure 107 and 108.
Figure 107: Agar-5%WV-0.5BS-85D without cooler storing.
Figure 108: Agar-5%WV-0.5BS-85D with cooling storage for 3 days.
As seen in the picture, no signiﬁcant diﬀerence in the two samples was found. For both

6.5.2
Substitution of sand in Agar samples
The aim of this test is to minimize the amount of segregation when printing samples con-
taining Agar. To avoid segregation the original sand was substitute with ﬁner grained sand.

design.
By reducing the grain size, the water and Agar solution should be able to have a better
binding eﬀect to the sand.
A sieving analysis of the two diﬀerent sand are found in Figure 109.

focus on the segregation happening at extrusion.
