Preface and Acknowledgements
This thesis is submitted as partial fulﬁllment of the requirements for the degree of Master
of Science in Civil Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark. The project is
credited to 30 ECTS points.
The purpose of this study is to evaluate horizontal stresses within a soil sample dur-
ing the shearing phase of direct simple shear tests using high resolution distributed ﬁber
optic sensing. Professor Assaf Klar has developed a Fourier based optimization that can
relate strain measurements from distributed ﬁber optic sensing to contact stresses. The
investigation aims to demonstrate this approach.

Abstract
This study presents an approach for evaluating the development of horizontal stress within
simple shear tests using high resolution distributed ﬁber optic sensing. The horizontal
stress is evaluated using a new formulation where contact load on an element is repre-
sented by a Fourier series expansion. The formulation relates distributed measurements
of strain to contact load on a structural element. Coefﬁcients of the Fourier series ex-
pansion is determined by minimizing the difference between the measured strain and the
analytical strain. From the minimization, the coefﬁcient can be found by a linear set of
equations, which can be solved for a measured strain proﬁle. The formulation is vali-
dated from experiments measuring the ﬁber optic strain of a ﬁber installed on a simply
supported beam. The contact load obtained from the formulation is compared with the
contact load obtained from the second derivative of the moment equation. The horizontal
stress is evaluated for a clean Fortainebleau sand in six drained direct simple shear tests.
A new 3D printed conﬁning ring with a ﬁber optic embedded is used in the direct simple
shear device. The strain proﬁle of the conﬁning ring during shearing is measured and re-
lated to stress along the inner circumference of the conﬁning ring. Assuming plane strain
conditions, the stress on the conﬁning ring is equal to the stress applied to the soil sample.
From the evaluated horizontal stress, a new stress path can found. This stress path can be
used to plot the Mohr circle at failure and ﬁnd the rotation of principal stresses. For all
six tests during shearing, it is observed that the horizontal stress increase, the principal
stress rotates, and failure occurs along horizontal planes.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Distributed ﬁber optic sensing has been recognized as one of the potential technologies
that can revolutionize monitoring and testing within structural and geotechnical engineer-
ing (Klar et al. 2019). In structural and geotechnical engineering distributed ﬁber optic
sensing is most commonly used for structural health monitoring and monitoring of ground
deformation. Within geotechnical engineering distributed ﬁber optic sensing can also be
used for monitoring in situ conditions such as warning systems for detecting landslides
and sinkholes. The advantages with distributed ﬁber optic strain sensing compared to
conventional point strain sensors, are the low-cost of ﬁber optics, easy installation, and
durability (Rui et al. 2017). Another beneﬁt of distributed ﬁber optic sensing is the possi-
bility of obtaining distributed measurements of strain along the structure. The distributed
measurements of ﬁber optic strain can give the full proﬁle of strain for a structure which
can be directly related to curvature or bending moment (Mohamad et al. 2007). Recent
advancements within distributed ﬁber optic sensing allows for measurements in the order
of 1 microstrain (µε), which enables new possibilities for utilizing this technology within
studies of fundamental geotechnical problems in small-scale and laboratory tests (Skar
et al. 2019). The high spatial resolution of distributed ﬁber optic sensing has the poten-
tial to serve as a viable alternative or additive to linear variable differential transformer
(LVDTs) or strain gauges in laboratory tests (Uchida et al. 2015).
This thesis aims to present a new approach evaluating the horizontal stress in a soil
sample during simple shear tests. A unique formulation using strain measurements obtain
by ﬁber optic sensing to evaluate contact load on a structural element is presented. The
new formulation relates the measurements of strain to contract load using a Fourier se-
ries based optimization, which optimize the coefﬁcient of the Fourier series to obtain an
agreement between measured strain and analytical strain. The analytical strain can either
be derived numerically for general structure using a ﬁnite element software or analyti-
cally if a closed-form solution exists. The Fourier based optimization procedure is used

The beam experiments are performed to validate the Fourier base optimization for

stress path can be obtained using the Fourier based optimization procedure. The stress
path is not possible to obtain through conventional measurements of direct simple shear
tests and can be used to plot the Mohr circle from one direct simple shear test. From the
Mohr circle, the angle of shearing resistance and rotation of principal stress for sand can
be found.

small scale experiments, and laboratory tests. Secondly, the literature study focuses on

Fiber optic sensing is a technology that has been developed upon over the last 60 years.
Over time the concept of ﬁber optics sensing has become more commercialized and is
now based on a variety of optical phenomena which can be used to measure a broad
range of physical and chemical parameters. In general, ﬁbers used for measuring are
made much more sensitive and susceptible to external mechanism than the ones used for
telecommunication (Krohn et al. 2014). The most important application for this project is
the capacity of measuring ﬁber optic strain. The basic principle behind ﬁber optic sensing
is that the change in the measured light intensity of the emitted light waves can be related
to changes in an external parameter such a strain or temperature. The light waves change
in intensity due to external inﬂuences (strain, temperature etc.) changing the properties
of the ﬁber optic cable.
The sensing element modulates parameters of the optical system (intensity, wave-
length, phase, etc.), this introduces a change in the characteristics of the optical signal
which can be measured by a detector. A ﬁber optic sensor correlates the parameter of
interest (strain, temperature, etc.) to the change in the affected light-guiding characteris-
tic. Hence, the basic principle of ﬁber optic sensing is to measure an external physical
parameter, such as strain, by introducing changes in the optical properties of the light
traveling inside and along the ﬁber. The ﬁber is, therefore, both the sensing element
and transmission medium (Krohn et al. 2014). In the simplest form a ﬁber optic sen-
sor is composed of a light source, a sensing element, a detector, and a ﬁber optic, see
Figure 2.1. There are three different types of ﬁber optic sensors: a single point sensor,
a multiple point sensor, and a distributed sensor. They all operate by altering the light
intensity in a manner that is proportional to the parameter being sensed such tempera-
ture, strain, etc. Some of the advantages with the use of ﬁber optic sensing is: it is often
cheap, small and lightweight, easy to install, high accuracy is obtained and it can facil-
itate distributed sensing (Krohn et al. 2014). The basic categories of ﬁber optic sensors

2.1
Distributed ﬁber optic sensing
Distributed ﬁber optic has been recognized as a technology that can revolutionize moni-
toring and testing within geotechnical and structural engineering (Klar et al. 2016). Soga
& Luo (2018) details that distributed ﬁber optic sensing is a promising tool for structural
health monitoring. It can be used to measure temperature, strain, or vibrations of struc-
tures. According to Soga & Luo (2018) the development of ﬁber optic sensing goes in
two directions: point sensing or distributed sensing. The most common use of ﬁber optic
sensing, within civil engineering, is for structural health monitoring where the strain or
temperature can be measured from local ﬁber optic sensors (interferometric ﬁber optic
sensors), quasi-distributed ﬁber optic sensors or distributed ﬁber optic sensors, see Figure
2.2. Interferometric ﬁber optic sensors measure a point on the ﬁber, the quasi-distributed
sensors measures several points along the ﬁber optic and the distributed sensors measure
many points along the ﬁber optic allowing the distributed strain/temperature proﬁle to
be evaluated. The distributed ﬁber optic sensing allows for measurement at any point

Klar et al. (2016) highlights that strain is a fundamental component of design within

low-cost, high resolution sensing technology with current applications allowing for strain

km ﬁber optic cable (Ravenzwaaij et al. 2018). Barrias et al. (2016) also highlights that
the distributed ﬁber optic sensing has a vital role in structural health monitoring due to
the potential of this technology. It offers superior performance, lower cost of installation
and maintenance than the conventional sensors applied in structural health monitoring.
Distributed ﬁber optic sensing enables distributed strain measurements along the length
or height of the structure, which can help reveal the global behavior of a structure (Barrias
et al. 2016).
According to Barrias et al. (2016) current assessment of civil structures, such as build-
ings, bridges, slopes, dams, tunnels, settlements of buildings, etc. are carried out by visual
inspections which vary based on the person conducting the investigation. If the accuracy
of the assessment has to improve electric strain sensors, accelerometers, inclinometers,
etc. can be used. These standard monitoring practices are based on a limit and relatively

2.2
Application of distributed ﬁber optic sensing within
geotechnical engineering
In recent years geohazards have occurred frequently, and ﬁeld monitoring is becoming
increasingly important to mitigate these risk. In geotechnical engineering, most research
within distributed ﬁber optic sensing focuses on large scale problems to gain knowledge
about these geohazards. Fiber optic sensing has the advantage that it is robust and can
enable long-distance geotechnical monitoring, which leads to the potential in replacing
point-mode monitoring techniques. Therefore, distributed ﬁber optic sensing can be suit-
able for real-time monitoring of geohazards (Zhu et al. 2017). Examples of research on
large problems are given in this section.
Pile foundations
Rui et al. (2017) used distributed ﬁber optic sensing to evaluate the temperature of the
concrete in a bored pile. The distributed ﬁber optic cables were embedded in the pile
during casting, as shown in Figure 2.3. The ﬁber optic sensing was used to measure the
change in temperature in the concrete during curing. The research was conducted as a
ﬁeld test on a 51 m long test pile in London. The ﬁber optic cables were attached to
the reinforcement cage of the pile, the change in temperature was obtained along the pile
from distributed ﬁber optic sensing. From back analyse of the measured temperature data
the change in diameter of the pile with depth could be modelled. The results showed that
the pile diameter varied in the range 1.40 - 1.56 m with depth. Rui et al. (2017) highlights
three advantages with the use of distributed ﬁber optic sensing: It provides a high spatial
density of data, it is easy to install and has a very low failure rate.
Gao et al. (2019) used BOTDR distributed ﬁber optic sensing to monitor the strain
of a bored pile. The strain measurement was used to study the deformation of the pile.
The ﬁber optic cable was installed in the reinforcement cage, similar to Rui et al. (2017).
Gao et al. (2019) highlights the distributed ﬁber optic sensing is more suitable for pile
strain monitoring, due to the traditional strain sensors (strain gauges, etc.) being prone to
rust damage, giving insufﬁcient data representation and they are easily destroyed whereas
the distributed ﬁber optic sensing is lightweight, ﬂexible, easy to install, not damaged
easily and can monitor the strain efﬁciently in real time. Another example of the use of

Monitoring of landslides
Distributed ﬁber optic sensing can also be used for monitoring of landslides. Schenato
et al. (2017) uses distributed ﬁber optic sensing to measure the strain within a large-scale
physical model that produces a shallow landslide triggered by rainfall. The BOTDA tech-
nology with a spatial resolution of 10 mm was used. The distributed ﬁber optic sensing
showed promise as a reliable warning and forecast system due to the effectiveness and
sensitivity of the ﬁber optic. Schenato et al. (2017) states that one of the main issues with
the use of ﬁber optics in real ﬁeld condition would be the installation of the cable. Other
research projects, such as Liu et al. (2010) and Iten & Puzrin (2009), has also performed
research using distributed ﬁber optic sensing for monitoring of landslides. Zeni et al.
(2015) conclude based on laboratory and ﬁeld tests that the BOTDA based distributed
ﬁber optic sensing technology shows excellent potential in the monitoring of slopes and
foundations.

Monitoring of secant pile walls
Mohamad et al. (2007) used BOTDR distributed ﬁber optic sensing technology to mea-
sure the full deformation proﬁle of a secant wall. The distributed strain proﬁle could be
used to obtained axial and lateral movement of the pile. The measurement for the ﬁber
optic was seen to be in good agreement with inclinometer data for the wall. The ﬁber
optic measurement gave a better ﬁt than inclinometer when analyzing moment and shear
force. Mohamad et al. (2007) highlights the advantages of using distributed ﬁber optic
sensing compared to an inclinometer. One of the beneﬁts using BOTDR is that the strain
measurements gives a direct curvature/bending moment distribution, which is better than
needing to differentiate the gradient from inclinometer data. Another beneﬁt is that the
deﬂection of the wall can be monitored after the construction is ﬁnished since the optical
cables can be buried. Furthermore, the axial response of the structure could be observed.
2.3
Small scale application of distributed ﬁber optic
The high spatial resolution of few millimeters to centimeters within distributed ﬁber optic
allows for the use of ﬁber optic on a smaller scale such as on-sample measurement during
laboratory tests, soil proﬁling and characterization in small scale tests. According to Li
(2017) Rayleigh scattering based ﬁber optic sensing is ideal for use in laboratory test due
to its low spatial resolution of 1 µε and 0.1◦C, respectively. Brillouin scatter however
is better suited for a large scale application (long-distance up to 30 km) with the highest
spatial resolution over a two-kilometer sensing length of 2 cm and the longest sensing
being 150 km with 2 m spatial resolution Bao & Chen (2011).
Klar et al. (2016) used high-resolution distributed ﬁber optic to estimate the shear
modulus (small strain stiffness) distribution with the depth of a soil proﬁle. The soil is
composed of a sand layer between two clay layers. The ﬁber optic cable was installed in
a similar manner of a wick drain using wick drain installation machine to drive the ﬁber
optic cable into the ground. The ﬁber optic strain was measured during a loading test to
obtain a strain proﬁle of the soil. The paper presents a recursive calculation relating the
measure strain proﬁle to shear modulus of the soil. The suggested shear modulus proﬁle
was compared to that obtained from conventional testing and showed excellent agree-
ment, which indicates that the idea could be implemented in geotechnical engineering.
Uchida et al. (2015) presents an approach for obtaining on-specimen strain measure-
ment using high-resolution distributed ﬁber optic sensing in laboratory testing. The paper
investigates a basic setup of a uniaxial compression test. Uchida et al. (2015) highlights
that the distributed ﬁber optic has the potential to be a viable alternative to the conven-
tional measuring device such as strain gauges or LVDTs where one of the advantages

2.4
Direct Simple Shear (DSS)
The DSS test is a commercially available laboratory test where simple shear deformation
is applied to soil sample (Gutierrez et al. 2009). There are two basic types of devices
for performing DSS tests; the complex Cambridge simple shear apparatus (Roscoe 1953)
and the Greonor simple shear apparatus (Bjerrum & Landva 1966) which was developed
by the Norwegian Geotechnical Institute (NGI) (therefore it is also referred to as the
NGI device). The NGI device uses a cylindrical specimen conﬁned in a wire-reinforced
membrane whereas in the Cambridge simple shear device the specimen is conﬁned by a
stack of rings (typically made of aluminum or metal). The purpose of the DSS apparatus
is to allow rotation of the principal stress and strain to a soil sample in a plane strain
condition. The Cambridge device achieves rotation of principal axis by applying normal
and shear loads to the top or bottom of an initially cuboidal sample. In the NGI device,
a circular cylindrical soil sample is tested (Wood et al. 1979). According to Budhu &
ASCE (1985), the behavior of several practical problems experience rotation of principal
axis can be closely simulated by a simple shear test.
As a result of the rotation of principal stress directions during shearing, the orientation
of the failure planes is not known (Gutierrez et al. 2009). Budhu (1988) has examined
the failure planes in sands for DSS tests. Wroth (1984) and Budhu (1988) highlight two
failure mechanisms; rotation and sliding along vertical planes and sliding along horizontal
planes. Budhu (1988) observed that failure for dense sand happens close to or at the
horizontal orientation.
According to Gutierrez et al. (2009), a drawback of the DSS test is that the results are
difﬁcult to interpret. More than one test is necessary to evaluate the strength parameters
of the sand. Determination of the rotation of principal stress requires measurements of
lateral conﬁning stress during shearing. Carraro & H. (2017) has performed tests using
a simple shear device with cell pressure conﬁnement where the cell pressure is equal
to the horizontal stress during consolidation. As soon as the sample experience shear

2.5
Fontainebleau sand
For this project, Fontainebleau sand is used. Latini & Zania (2017) examined the inﬂu-
ence of relative density on the strength and deformation characteristics of Fontainebleau
sand. The study on Fontainebleau sand was done performing compression triaxial tests.
The sand was well-sorted, clean with a particle size ranging from 0.063mm to 0.25 mm
and uniformly index of U < 2. The study showed that the angle of shearing resistance
ranging from 37.0◦to 42.4◦for a relative density ranging from 50% to 80% — the angle
of shearing resistance increased with increasing relative density.
Gutierrez et al. (2009) states that the angle of shearing resistance of sand is highly
affected by the orientation of principal stresses where the angle of shearing resistance
decrease as the principal stresses rotate. Therefore, the angle of shearing resistance of
sand from a DSS test is examined (it was not possible to ﬁnd a study of Fontainebleau
sand). Takahashi & Jardine (2007) performed DSS test on a loose and dense sample for
three types of sand; Shepperton, Chertsey, and Ham. The tests were performed under a
vertical load ranging from 50-400 kPa. For all types of sand, a peak and residual angle
of shearing resistance were found. For Shepperton sand, the peak and residual was 38.7◦
and 30.3◦, respectively. For Chertsey sand, the peak and residual was 37.8◦and 30.9◦,
respectively. For Ham sand, the peak and residual were 40.0◦and 32.0◦, respectively.

Final remarks
From the literature study, it is evident that distributed ﬁber optic sensing has the poten-
tial to revolutionize monitoring and testing within geotechnical engineering and structural
engineering. The primary use of distributed ﬁber optic sensing within geotechnical engi-
neering is on large projects, where it is used for structural health monitoring and warning
systems. Uchida et al. (2015) highlight that distributed ﬁber optic can be a viable alterna-
tive to conventional point measuring devices, and Li (2017) found that Rayleigh scattered
ﬁber optic technology was ideal for laboratory testing. The applicability of Rayleigh
scattering based ﬁber optic sensing to laboratory tests are used in this thesis for measur-
ing strain on in the DSS apparatus. The measured strain can be related to the horizontal
stress in the soil sample by using the nature of the distributed ﬁber optic sensing, where
the measurements can be expressed as a distributed function. The measurements of hor-
izontal stress during shearing can, according to Gutierrez et al. (2009) help to a better
interpretation of DSS result, making it possible to obtain parameters such as the rotation
of the principal stresses.

CHAPTER 3
Theory and Methodology
The main focus of this project is evaluating the horizontal stress within DSS tests by using
high resolution distributed ﬁber optic sensing. Firstly, experiments were performed on a
simply supported beam; a ﬁber optic was installed along the top and bottom ﬁber of the
beam for measuring the strain. The experiments were performed to validate the concept of
the Fourier based optimization for evaluating contact load. After the concept was proven,
the process of evaluating the horizontal stress within simple shear tests was undertaken.
The strain proﬁle of the conﬁning ring had to be measured to evaluate the contact stress
between the conﬁning ring and soil sample. For this propose new conﬁning rings were
designed with a small groove for installation of the ﬁber optic. For both the experiments
on the simply supported beam and the DSS tests, the strain was measured by distributed
ﬁber optics sensing using Luna’s Optical Backscatter Reﬂectometer (OBR) 4600 based
Rayleigh scattering technology (Klar et al. 2016).
In this chapter, the theory and methodology used within the experimental procedure
of this project, together with the experimental setup, are presented. Firstly, a new for-
mulation for evaluating contact load on a structural element is presented. Secondly, the
equipment used for the distributed ﬁber optic sensing is described. Then the experiment
performed on the simply supported are presented. Finally, the modeling of the new con-
ﬁning ring and the produce of DSS tests are described.

Direct Simple Shear (DSS)
The DSS test is a commonly used laboratory test for evaluating the geotechnical proper-
ties of a soil sample. The test is most commonly used to determine the shear strength of
the soil sample. In compliance with ASTM (2007), the soil sample is ﬁrst loaded vertical
and consolidated in steps then sheared at a constant rate. During the consolidation phase,
the soil sample is conﬁned laterally allowing one-dimensional consolidation. When the
soil sample is fully consolidated for all steps, the shearing phase can be initiated. The soil
is sheared by moving the bottom piston relative the top piston introducing simple shear
mode, see Figure 3.5. Four parameters can be controlled or monitored during the DSS
test, namely: vertical stress (σz), shear stress (τ), vertical strain (ϵv) and shear strain (γ).

In the DSS test, the principal axes of stress and strain increments are free to rotate
during the shearing phase. The rotation of the principal axis is a major difference from
the oedometer and conventional triaxial tests where the principal axes are prevented from
moving. Since in situ soil elements experience rotation of principal axes during shearing,
one of the advantages with the DSS test is that the soil element also experiences rotation
of principal axes. According to Wroth (1984), there are two shear failure mechanisms.
The conventional understanding where the associated deformation occurs due to sliding
of the horizontal planes within the specimen. The alternative shear failure mechanism
where the vertical planes rotate and slide relative to each other within the specimen. The
shear failure mechanisms are shown in Figure 3.6. In general, if the boundary conditions
allow it, the soil element will choose the mode that offers the least resistance (Wroth
1984).

horizontal plane (vertical stress). Therefore, it is impossible to deduce the complete stress

5.1
Beam experiments
The beam experiments were performed to validate the output of the Fourier based opti-
mization, which should produce a contact load that matches the measured strain proﬁle.
For a Euler–Bernoulli beam, the contact load can be found as the second derivative of the
moment equation, as already mentioned. The idealized strain proﬁle (Figure 4.2) showed
that the Fourier based optimization produce gave a somewhat accurate display of the con-
tact load on the beam. The approach was also capable of detecting the reaction, which
indicates that the Fourier based optimization procedure is independent of the geometry
of the structure. The only demand of the formulation is that the structure obeys the ex-
ternal equilibrium conditions. The double second derivative of the moment equation is
the most accurate solution of contact load on a beam that can be obtained from the strain
proﬁle. The Fourier based optimization shows similar behavior compared to the second
derivative of the moment equation for both the idealized strain proﬁle and the measured
ﬁber optic strain. From the idealized case, it can also be seen that a higher number of
elements (higher frequencies) was necessary to obtain the abrupt shift in the amplitude of
the contact load proﬁle at the location of the actual load.
For the Fourier based optimization and the second derivative of the moment equation,
it was observed that the noise from the high resolution distributed ﬁber optic strain read-

CHAPTER 6
Conclusions
In this thesis, a new approach for evaluating horizontal stress in a soil sample during sim-
ple shear tests was presented and examined. The approach utilizes a developed Fourier
based optimization for evaluating contact load, which relates high resolution distributed
ﬁber optic strain proﬁles to contact stress on a structural element. The contact stress
distribution within the soil sample was represented as a Fourier series, where the coef-
ﬁcients are optimized by minimizing the difference between a measured strain and the
linearly elastic strain response of a structural element. The formulation resulted in a clas-
sic quadratic objective function for which an optimal solution that brings a minimum
value of coefﬁcients for the Fourier series expansion can be established as the solution of
a linear set of equations. The solution can be written in matrix form, where N coefﬁcients
of the Fourier series can be found by multiplying the inverse of a fully populated N ×N
matrix with an input vector. The matrix is ﬁxed and represent the response of the element,
while the input vector relates to the measured strain proﬁle.
Experiments on a simply supported beam were performed to validate the Fourier
based optimization for evaluating contact load. The strain proﬁle was measured with
a ﬁber optic along the top and bottom of the beam. For the case of the beam, the output
of the formulation was in terms of a line load (kN/m). The beam was subject to bending
by a triangular load. The analysis of an idealized strain proﬁle showed a good agreement
between the second derivative of the moment equation and the Fourier based optimiza-
tion for evaluating contact load. It was also seen that a signiﬁcant number of elements
was needed in Fourier series summation to obtain the abrupt shift in amplitude at the lo-
cation of the load. The Fourier based optimization procedure also deﬁnes the reactions
at the location of the supports. The formulation seems to be independent of the global
geometry. Therefore, one of the conditions of the formulation is that the structure has
to be in equilibrium. Analysis of the measure strain proﬁle shows that the results were
highly effected by higher frequencies introduced by noise on the distributed ﬁber optic

high frequencies affected the second derivative of the moment equation in the same way.
The higher frequencies were ﬁltered from the strain proﬁle using a ﬁlter based on Fourier

the contact load, which did not match the applied load. A lower number of elements did
not ﬁt the second derivative as well as for 9 elements.
The 14 drained DSS tests were performed on a loose to medium dense, clean Fort-
ainebleau sand with a relatively low angle of shearing resistance, below 30◦. For demon-
strating the approach of evaluating the horizontal stress in the soil sample, only six tests
where considered. All of the tests showed a dilating behavior and no or a small strain
softening. For the DSS apparatus, new conﬁning rings with an embedded ﬁber optic ca-
ble were designed using 3D printing. For the six considered tests, a ring with a ﬁber optic
embedded at the top and bottom of the ring and base align the conﬁning rings with the
top piston was used. Increasing bending effected in the rings were observed throughout
shearing. The bending contribution to the strain proﬁle was reduced by superimposing
the ﬁber optic strain from the top and bottom ﬁber. The bending contribution to the strain
proﬁle was reduced before it was introduced to the Fourier based optimization procedure
because the formulation does not include such modes of deformation. Using the new
approach, it was possible to calculate the horizontal stress in the soil sample throughout
the shearing phase of the DSS tests. From the new information of horizontal stresses, the
stress path of horizontal stress (σx) and shear stress (τxz) could now be established allow-
ing for the construction of the Mohr circle for each test. The Mohr circle at failure was
constructed from the stress path of (σx, τxz) and the stress path of vertical stress (σz) and
shear stress (τzx). The Mohr-Coulomb tangent failure line could be plotted on the Mohr
circle where it showed that the soil in these tests failed along horizontal planes ((σz, τzx)
reach the failure line). The angle of shearing resistance was also found as the slope of the
Mohr-Coulomb tangent failure line. Finally, the rotation of principal stresses, which is a
unique feature of the DSS, was also found using the pole of the Mohr circle.
The new approach shows great promise for the evaluation of horizontal stress in a soil
sample. The capacity of obtaining the horizontal stress can help to understand the failure
mechanisms and rotation of principal stress in DSS tests better. The assumption of failure
along horizontal planes is appropriate for the case of these tests where it is assumed that
τzx/σz = tan φ′. This assumption is not always correct, in undrained (zero volumetric
strain) tests the failure occurs along vertical planes, and the maximum measured stress is
not always the greatest stress experienced. For some tests, the vertical nor the horizontal

6.1
Further work
In this thesis the concept of an approach for evaluating horizontal stress within a soil
sample during simple shear tests has been presented. Further work can be done to improve
the suggested approach. For example, studies of the PLA 3D printed conﬁning rings
could be performed to obtain a better understanding of the response of rings and material
properties. Investigations could also be performed to verify the assumption of isotropic
behavior of the rings. Constructing the modiﬁed conﬁning rings in a material with higher
bending stiffness such as aluminum or metal might limit the bending contribution to the
strain proﬁle.
Further investigations could be performed trying to locate the optimal position of
the ﬁber optic cable within the conﬁning ring. An investigation into different bending
contribution to the rings could be performed to reduce the bending effects further. It
should also be considered investigating the utilization of other high resolution sensing
technologies.
Further attempts to limit the friction within the experimental setup should be consid-
ered, which could be examining the effect of the membrane and the friction between the
conﬁning rings and membrane. Installation of more conﬁning rings with an embedded
ﬁber optic should also be considered to obtain the variation in horizontal stress along the
vertical interface. Furthermore, investigations into the consolidation phase should be car-
ried out to validate the assumption that K0 conditions prevail at the end of consolidation.
Finally, investigations into more complex soil behavior could also be performed.

Abstract: The paper presents an approach for evaluating the horizontal stresses that develop in
1
geotechnical Direct Simple Shear (DSS) tests through the use of high resolution distributed ﬁber optic
2
sensing. For this aim, ﬁber optics have been embedded in 3D printed rings used for conﬁning the soil
3
in the test procedure. An analytical approach linking between the measured spatially distributed
4
strain proﬁle and the internal soil-ring contact stresses is developed in the paper. The method is based
5
on representing the contact stresses by a Fourier series expansion, and determining the coefﬁcients of
6
the series by minimizing the difference between the measured strain and the analytical strain within
7
the linear elastic ring. The minimization problem results in a linear set of equations that can easily
8
be solved for a given measurement. The approach is demonstrated on a set of drained DSS tests of
9
clean sand specimens. Stress paths using the evaluated horizontal stresses are plotted together with
10
Mohr circles at failure. These illustrate how, in these speciﬁc tests, the horizontal stress increases and
11
principal stress direction rotates, until failure occurs along horizontal planes.
12
Keywords: Distributed ﬁber-optic sensing; Direct Simple Shear; Geotechnical Engineering; Contact
13
problems; Soil Properties

1. Introduction
15
Distributed ﬁber optic sensing has been recognized as one of the potential technologies that could
16
revolutionize structural and geotechnical monitoring and testing (e.g. [1–3]). Most of the research focus
17
in the area has been placed on large scale problems such as, pile foundations [4–7], pipeline stressing
18
[8–11], stressing and deformation of secant pile walls [12,13], landslides and sinkholes localization
19
[14–17], tunneling stressing [18], strain measurement and detection of cracks in asphalt pavements
20
[19–22], and evaluation of tunneling induced ground deformations [23,24]. These applications are
21
based on static, and low (˜1 m) sensing resolution limiting themselves to large scale problems.
22
New sensing developments, however, allow for higher spatial resolution of a few millimeters to
23
centimeters [25–29], and these developments open the window to new possibilities of smaller scale
24
applications, such as on-sample measurements [30], high resolution proﬁling [31], and key-points
25
characterization methods [32].
26
This paper suggests an additional new application for high resolution ﬁber optic sensing, aiming
27
to enrich the information obtained from a Direct Simple Shear (DSS) test. DSS test is a common soil
28
laboratory test for evaluating the geotechnical properties of geomatertials. In the test, a soil sample
29
is being sheared in a simple shear mode such that parallel planes remain parallel and maintain a
30
constant distance, while translating relative to each other (as demonstrated in Fig. 1). Guidelines for
31

32
and the associated deformation occurs due to sliding of horizontal planes, other modes of shearing
33
can lead to kinematically compatible deformations, as illustrated in Fig. 1.
34
The test can be performed either under a ﬁxed vertical stress (allowing the soil to compact or
35
dilate), or under ﬁxed vertical strain (in which case the vertical stress is measured and may decrease
36
or increase). Overall 4 parameters are controlled or monitored in the test procedure, namely: the
37
shear strain γ, the shear stress τ, the vertical stress σv and the vertical strain ϵv (which supposedly
38
constitutes the volumetric strain in this speciﬁc mode of shearing). There exist two types of DSS device,
39
the ﬁrst being the complex, non commercial, Cambridge cuboidal simple shear apparatus [34], and the
40
other the Geonor DSS device [35], which uses a cylindrical specimen conﬁned by a wire reinforced
41
membrane. As the complete stress condition is not known due to the rotation of principal stresses and
42
strains(not event sufﬁcient to construct a Mohr circle of stresses), measurements need to be performed
43
to evaluate the value of the horizontal stresses at the time of the shearing.This is achieved by load cells
44
[36] in the Cambridge device, while a resistance wire has been sometimes applied [37] to the Geonor
45
device, for evaluating the average radial stress using a Wheastone bridge. However, typical DSS
46
devices available commercially do not have these capabilities. This hinders the interpretation of the test
47
results, as certain assumptions need to be made regarding the stress state at the time of failure. Over
48
the years various models have been suggested to analyze the test results based on mix of analytical
49
and empirical relations.For example, [38? ] developed semi-empirical expressions for calculating the
50
horizontal stress, based on the direct proportionality between the inclination of the principal stress
51
axes and the shear stress ratio. [39] have used collinearity of stress and strain to develop an analytical
52
incremental expression that allows evaluation of the horizontal stress, and to interpret test results. [40]
53
have coupled 3D computer simulations of DSS with the measured response to calibrate the constitutive
54
behavior. It is clear that measurements of the horizontal stresses within the sample could contribute to
55
the interpretation of the test results, regardless of the exact test conditions (i.e. drained, undrained,
56
monotonic, cyclic, etc.) or type of soil (i.e. sands or clays).

This paper presents a new approach for evaluating the horizontal stresses in the sample, and
58
present ﬁrst steps of its development. The approach utilizes recent advances in distributed ﬁber optic
59
sensing technologies that allow for high accuracy and resolution sensing, together with an approach
60
for determination of the contact stresses. More speciﬁcally, the approach links between spatially
61
continuous proﬁles of the developed strain in the apparatus conﬁning rings with an analytical solution
62
of the rings’ response to contact stresses. The contact stress is resolved by an optimization problem for
63
which the solution can be obtained by a set of linear equations. The paper presents both the analytical
64
development as well as demonstration of the approach on the DSS apparatus.

The paper is composed of 5 main sections. First, the approach of evaluating the contact stresses
66
by an optimization problem of measured high resolution distributed strain proﬁle and an analytical
67

68
ﬁber embedded 3D printed rings, is described. Third, test results of direct simple shear of clean sand
69
are presented. Fourth, analysis of the measured strain proﬁle and interpretation of the results are
70
presented. Finally, discussion and conclusions are provided.

75

3. Experimental setup
106
To demonstrate the approach, the DSS apparatus by Geocomp Corporation [42] was modiﬁed to
107
include ﬁber embedded rings. This DSS device uses conﬁning rings to support the soil while sheared,
108
as opposed to an NGI DSS device [35] which uses a reinforced membrane to conﬁne the soil. The
109
inner diameter of the rings is 64.51 mm and the external diameter is 101.5 mm. Fig. 4 shows the
110
original Teﬂon coated aluminium rings next to the 3D PLA printed rings. The rings were printed
111
with a thickness of 3mm, except for the central one which had double the thickness, to provide higher
112
bending resistance. Fig. 5 shows a ﬁber optic embedded ring. A single mode tight-buffer ﬁber optic
113
cable was placed in the 3D printed groove, prestrained and glued with a cyanoacrylates adhesive.
114
The ﬁbers were installed on top and bottom of the ring. Earlier experiments involved a one sided
115
ﬁber embedded ring, but it was found that the measurements were sensitive to potential bending of
116
the rings. Consequently, ﬁbers were installed on both sides of the rings allowing for compensation
117
of bending response by summing the strain from both proﬁles. Other installation conﬁgurations
118
are possible, but their investigation is beyond the scope of this paper which merely presents and
119
demonstrates the new concept.
120
Note that the same 3D model used for 3D printing was also used for generating the Γi(x) response
121
functions in the ﬁnite element code COMSOL Multyphysics [43], as described in the previous section.
122
For the interpretation of the results, the PLA properties were taken as E = 3150MPa following the
123
mechanical properties provided by the technical data sheet of manufacturer (Ultimaker). The rings
124
were printed under 100% ﬁlling conﬁguration to create a true continuum structure.

6. Conclusions
210
The paper presented a new approach for evaluating the horizontal stress condition in direct
211
simple shear tests. The approach utilizes high resolution distributed ﬁber optic sensing to evaluate the
212
proﬁle of the developed strains in the conﬁning rings. These strain proﬁles depend on the interface
213
contact stresses, which are of interest (i.e the stresses applied to the soil as reactions). By considering
214
the linear elastic response of the conﬁning rings, and representing the contact stress distribution by a
215
Fourier series expansion, an optimization problem was formulated aiming to evaluate the best stress
216
proﬁle (in terms of Fourier coefﬁcients) generating an analytical stress proﬁle that would match the
217
measured strain proﬁle. The formulation resulted in a quadratic objective function for which a solution
218
can be obtained by a linear set of equations written in a matrix form; that is, the N coefﬁcients of the
219
Fourier series expansion are obtained by multiplication of a fully populated N × N matrix with an
220
input vector. The fully populated matrix is ﬁxed and represents the ring, while the multiplied vector
221
relates to the measured strain proﬁle.
222
The approach was demonstrated with a commercial DSS apparatus, for which a new set of ﬁber
223
optic embedded conﬁning rings was produced using 3D printing. Six drained direct simple shear tests
224
were performed on clean sand to demonstrate the applicability of the approach. It was demonstrated
225
how stress path and Mohr circles can be evaluated with the new information derived from the ﬁber
226
optic sensing. In speciﬁc, it was shown that in these speciﬁc tests the soil truly fails along horizontal
227
planes, when considering a Mohr Coulomb yield condition.
228
The main focus of the paper was to conceptually develop a suitable approach for utilizing high
229
resolution ﬁber optic sensing for laboratory geotechnical testing, and to demonstrated its feasibility.
230
Clearly, further research could be performed to improve the suggested approach. For example, studies
231
could be performed for seeking the best position of the ﬁber optic within the rings. Investigation of
232
different ring materials or shape could be considered, as well as utilization of other high resolution
233
sensing techniques. These investigations, as well as investigations into more complex soil behavior, are
234
beyond the scope of this paper which is focused on ﬁrst steps of advancing the use of high resolution
235
ﬁber optic sensing for laboratory geotechnical testing.
