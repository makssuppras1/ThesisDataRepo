ABSTRACT
Environmental concerns are leading the energy sector towards a less carbon intensive
system. In order to reduce greenhouse gas emissions, an increasing share of energy is
produced from Renewable Energy Sources (RES). This comes with new challenges for
the operation of a reliable and secure power system, as essential ancillary services are
traditionally performed by coal or oil-based units, while non-dispatchable RES such as
wind or solar increase the unbalance between electricity production and consumption. It is
therefore important that renewable-based technologies’ ability to provide frequency and
voltage control is developed. Thanks to their ﬂexibility potentials, biogas power plants are
a promising solution to provide frequency control. The biogas produced by the anaerobic
digestion (AD) of organic material can be stored until it is used by Combined Heat and
Power (CHP) units to produce electricity and heat. Moreover, the biogas production itself
can be modulated by implementing demand-driven feeding. Eventually, it can be upgraded
to biomethane for use as fuel in transportation or injection into the gas grid.
This thesis analyzes the provision of primary frequency control from a biogas plant in
Bornholm’s power system. An existing model of a biogas plant is used, which is able for a
given infeed of raw material to characterize the AD process and calculate the power output
and the level of storage. This model is implemented in Matlab-Simulink, and represents a
3 MW biogas power plant located in Bornholm, Eastern Denmark. This existing model
has been extended by adding a controller that modulates the power output depending on
the grid frequency. In Eastern Denmark, primary frequency control comprises two services:
frequency-controlled normal operation reserve (FCR-N), activated linearly with no dead
band for all frequency deviations within ±100 mHz, and frequency-controlled disturbance
reserve (FCR-D), activated when frequency drops below 49.9 Hz or goes over 50.1 Hz
(as expected to be in force from 2022). Four cases have been tested, providing diﬀerent
combinations of these services, and with diﬀerent conﬁgurations of capacity reserves. Real
data of November 2020 have been used for input feeding, output generation and grid
frequency measurements.
It has been demonstrated that the production from upward and downward regulation
are balanced over the considered time period, so keeping a capacity reserve for provision
of frequency control results for the power plant in a lower energy production, and thus
in higher levels of storage. Depending on the type of service provided, the diﬀerence
compared to the actual operation is not the same: provision of FCR-N requires small-sized
capacity reserves and thus little diﬀerence, while FCR-D requires large-sized reserves. This
causes large losses regarding power outputs, which imply lower revenues, and less usage of
the generated gas. In terms of economical results, the revenues for frequency control are
negligible compared to the subsidies perceived by the plant for its electricity production.
However, these subsidies tend to decrease, and a sensitivity analysis has shown that in
that case, provision of frequency control would become more proﬁtable economically.

INTRODUCTION
This project originates from the European Project, INSULAE, which aims at decarbonizing
European islands by implementing innovative energy solutions [1]. One of the islands
involved in this project is Bornholm, Denmark, where one of the use cases investigates
the potential of a renewable-driven Virtual Power Plant (VPP) embracing diﬀerent energy
domains (electrical, heat, gas, transportation) for ﬂexibility provision to the island’s energy
system. The VPP comprises diﬀerent types of energy sources and loads, some of them
being dispatchable (batteries or Electricle Vehicles (EVs) for example), and some of them
not (wind turbines or domestic loads for example). Among the connected units, a biogas
plant may play a key role in this VPP as it is able to provide ﬂexible electricity production.
Indeed, biogas plants have several ﬂexibility potentials that allow them to contribute to
more ﬂexibility and stability on the grid. A description of these ﬂexibility potentials can be
found in [2], [3] and [4]. First, the gas storage tank can be used to store the gas produced
until it is needed. Second, what is called feeding management, that can change the input
feeding to the digester so that biogas production can be adjusted, which can help to
modulate the production without having huge gas storage tanks. Gas can also be upgraded
to biomethane in time of over production and eventually be used for transportation or gas
grid injection.
In order to have a sustainable production of biogas, it should however be produced from
recycled biomass, that is to say biomass that would have emitted its share of carbon
dioxide anyway if not used for biogas production. It can be, for example, animal manure,
household waste or other recycled products. From a more holistic point of view, another
aspect that makes biogas plants a sustainable source of energy is that the returned digested
materials can serve as fertilizer for the farmers’ ﬁelds, thus is thus reducing the need for
chemicals [2].
Thanks to these ﬂexibility potentials, biogas plants could help the system operator maintain
the balance by providing ancillary services, and more speciﬁcally frequency control. This
study tries to analyze how these ﬂexibility potentials can be used by the plant manager to
adjust its production depending on what is needed by the power system.
1.1
Frequency control
An electric power system is characterized by two main important parameters: voltage and
frequency. In order to keep the expected operating conditions and supply energy to all the
users connected, it is important to control these two parameters within predeﬁned limits,
to avoid unexpected disturbances that can create problems to the connected loads or even

cause the system to fail [5].
In Europe, the nominal frequency is 50 Hz. If there is too much production, the frequency
tends to increase, while if there is not enough production it tends to decrease. Therefore,
frequency control is performed so that the grid frequency stays close to this nominal value
[5].
Presently, frequency control is mostly provided by conventional units, which are ﬂexible
production units, and can rapidly adapt to frequency disturbances of the grid. However,
these conventional units run mainly on oil, gas or coal. Therefore, they will be eventually
phased out because of their high emissions of greenhouse gases and thus their impact on
climate change, and will not provide frequency control anymore. The increasing share of
renewable energy sources also brings new challenges because most of renewable energy
sources are not fully predictable and have production proﬁles that do not match the
consumption proﬁles. As a result, they accentuate the unbalance between production and
consumption.
Therefore, frequency control is an important aspect of transition towards a renewable
energy system. Biogas plants become interesting in this perspective as they can have a
ﬂexible production, and so provide frequency control. Solutions based on other renewable
energies are studied in addition to conventional units. For example, EVs are considered as
a promising source of power system services: provided that they are correctly aggregated,
they can adapt the charging/discharging process and perform vehicle-to-grid services, such
as primary control [6]. Energy storage systems can also be used to balance the variable
production of other renewable units: Battery Storage Systems can control the state of
charge depending on the grid frequency and thus provide primary frequency control [7].
Various types of micro-grids combining renewable-based energy sources, among which
biogas power plants, have been modelled and studied in order to assess the stability and
reliability of renewable-based power systems, and more speciﬁcally their ability to provide
frequency control [8], [9]. The dynamic of biogas plant in the perspective of controllable
power outputs have been studied [10], [11]. In the case of the German energy system,
frequency control by biogas CHP plants is already in place [10]. However, the biogas plant
that is investigated in the INSULAE project does not provide any frequency control.
Hence, the goal of this project is to assess the provision of frequency control from a biogas
plant in the context of the Danish framework, and more particularly for the Bornholm’s
power system, which is presented in the next section.
1.2
Bornholm’s power system

of Denmark. It is one of the three demo sites of INSULAE, and is used as a full scale
laboratory for research and demonstration activities for a number of green energy-related
projects [12]. The power system in Bornholm comprises both conventional and renewable
energy based generation. Table 1.1 lists the generation technologies on the island.

1.3
Overview of biogas and biogas plants structure
In the recent years, the biogas sector has developed and gained relevance. Biogas is produced
from the anaerobic digestion of organic material by microorganism, but it can also be
upgraded to biomethane for gas grid injection or as fuel for transportation. Production
of biogas from recycled material such as residues of agriculture and industry, municipal
organic waste and sewage sludge has become one of the most attractive renewable energy
pathway. Both biogas production plants and upgrading to biomethane are expanding in a
number of countries, aspecially in United States and Europe. European Union is the world’s
leader in biogas electricity production and the worlds leading producer of biomethane [13].
The possibility to upgrade the biogas produced to biomethane is seen as the next growth
driver for the sector by the authors of [14]. Indeed, development of renewable electricity
is essential to reach the targets of greenhouse gas reduction. However, a system relying
only on electricity would not be cost-eﬀective and technologically impossible. The optimal
option could be a dual gas and power network. In this perspective, biogas production
by AD is a propitious solution as it is already a mature technology, that is traditionally
used to produce power via CHP units, but that would also allow the upgrade of biogas to
biomethane. It is interesting to note that biogas production can be done at several scales,

The complete biological decomposition of organic matter to methane (CH4) and carbon
dioxide (CO2) under oxygen-depleted conditions – i.e. anaerobic – is complicated. It is an
interaction between a number of diﬀerent bacteria that are each responsible for their part
of the task. Aerobic (oxygen-rich) decomposition is relatively easy to operate compared
to anaerobic digestion [16], [17]. However, the end-product of aerobic digestion is carbon
dioxide, water and a range of lower molecular weight organic compounds, which does not
correspond to the composition of biogas and therefore, cannot be used to produce it [17],
[18]. The biogas process is often divided into three steps: hydrolysis (long-chain molecules
are broken down to smaller molecules), acidogenesis (fermentation) and methanogenesis
(production of methane) [16].
There are diﬀerent parameters that have to be taken into account when optimizing the
process: the anaerobic environment, the temperature, the acidity (pH), the C/N ratio
(should be less than 30/1), the organic load, the type of substrate, the comminution
(reduction of the size of fragments), the dry matter content and the stirring. There are
also various substances that might inhibit the gas process, such as nitrogen, organic acids,
or antibiotics. The diﬀerent types of biomass used are animal manure, industrial waste,
sewage sludge, household wastes and energy crops. Each type of biomass results in a
diﬀerent yield. The whole biogas process is described in section 2 in [16].
There are diﬀerent types of plant design but the most common in Denmark for the
decomposition of both agricultural residues and sewage sludge is the CSTR (Continuously
Stirred Tank Reactor), as stated in [16] and [19]. The general structure of a biogas plant
is presented in Figure 1.1.

1.4
Thesis objectives
The thesis consists of extending an existing model of a biogas plant located in Bornholm
by adding a frequency controller. The goal is to assess the potentials of the biogas plant
to provide frequency control. The thesis aims at answering the following main research
questions:
• What is the regulatory framework for the provision of frequency control
in East Denmark? In order to provide frequency control, a production unit has to
comply with speciﬁc regulation. The focus will be on East Denmark, called DK2,
because this is where the biogas plant under study is located, and on power plants of
type C, as this is the type to which the biogas plant belongs.
• How much and under which conditions can a biogas power plant provide
frequency control?
A controller that modulates the active power production
depending on grid frequency variations will be designed. However, this modulation
can be both limited by the amount of gas available in the storage tank and by the
capacity of the plant (frequency provision requires capacity reserve). Diﬀerent study
cases will be explored, and both the active power production and the gas storage
level will be analyzed.
• How proﬁtable is frequency provision in terms of revenues for the biogas
plant? The change in active power production will necessarily impact the revenues
of the plant, as the payments for normal production and for participation to ancillary
services are not the same. A biogas plant will only be interested in participating to
the frequency provision if it is proﬁtable from an economic point of view.

2.1
Electricity markets
There are diﬀerent types of markets that secure the balance between supply and demand
from a range of participating entities, as explained in [21]:
• the Capacity Market is for the system operator to ensure that suﬃcient generation
capacity is present for reliable system operation in future years and at competitive
prices [22],[23]. It becomes of special interest in a context where more volatile and
unpredictable renewable generation plants come on stream. It has been implemented
in some countries, listed in [22], but is not in force yet in Nordic countries [24],
• the Energy Market is the central place for the optimal scheduling and settlement of
energy exchanges,
• The Ancillary Services Markets comprise any type of service that supports power
system operations, directly bought by the system operator (such as primary reserve).
There are three relevant energy markets for the Nordic system, ordered by time frame:
day-ahead, intra-day and regulating/balancing (real-time) [25],[26] . These markets are
connected temporarily, and each one can correct the imbalances of the previous one. There
are also markets, called futures and forwards, that allow to trade energy for delivery in
the future, up to many years in advance, but these are out of the scope of the project.
The following paragraphs describe how these markets work in Nordic countries and more
speciﬁcally in Eastern Denmark, DK2.
As shown on Figure 2.1, the market operator runs the day-ahead and intra-day markets,
while the system operator manages the ancillary services and the balancing market.
Nordpool is the market operator in the Nordic countries, and so in Denmark, while the
Danish Transmission System Operator (TSO) is Energinet. Figure 2.1 and 2.2 also describe

The day-ahead market (spot) is the market where most of the energy is traded. This is
a pool trading where customers can buy or sell energy for the next 24 hours. The market
is cleared one day before the delivery date and the closure time is twelve hours before
delivery. The day is divided in 24 periods of 1 hour. For each period consumers can place
their demand proﬁle in the market, and producers can bid generation proﬁle according to
their marginal costs. They cannot see other bids. Merit order is created and a price for
the system is calculated. This spot price is denoted λspot. It is worth noticing that the day
ahead market is a ﬁnancial market and that transactions are ﬁnancial, that is to say there
is no obligation to generate or consume directly.
There are diﬀerent types of support schemes, that provide ﬁnancial support for renew-
able energy technologies and other environmentally friendly energy technologies to make

The intra-day market is centrally organised like day-ahead, but is based on bilateral
contracts. It works between day ahead gate closure time and 1 hour before delivery time
approximately. It allows to correct for errors of estimation in the day-ahead market. It is
especially relevant for high shares of volatile renewable generation that rely on weather
forecasts.
The regulating/balancing market is a real time market that maintains the balance
between supply and demand [27].
Failure to regulate power can produce frequency
deviations or even load shedding, so there is a need for up and down regulation. The
balancing stage combines (though we eventually see it as a single market mechanism) the
regulation market, for the TSO to obtain necessary regulating power prior to the delivery
hour, and the balancing market, linked to the real-time operations, and yielding balancing
payments based on actual metering.
If the system is imbalanced, depending on the sign of this imbalance, there can be a need
for upward or downward regulation. At the balancing market, supplier can bid energy
volumes and prices, and the market is cleared the same way than in the day-ahead market,
resulting in a balancing price denoted λB. The price of day-ahead market is denoted λspot.
Depending on the sign of the imbalance, the balancing price will be higher or lower than
the spot price. The imbalance on the market is denoted ∆Pmarket, it is the diﬀerence
between production and consumption.

If the imbalance is negative, the system needs the suppliers to produce more and the
balancing price is higher than the spot price. If the imbalance is positive, the system needs
the suppliers to produce less and the balancing price is lower than the spot price. The
general principle is that if a producer has a positive imbalance, it receives a payment for
its additional production of energy, while when it has a negative imbalance, it has to pay
back to the system operator for the energy that it did not produce.
The pricing method used on the balancing market is the two-price imbalance settlement,
that penalizes the participants putting the system oﬀ-balance but not giving extra rewards
to those who (unintentionally) support the system [26].

Diﬀerent regulations apply to diﬀerent plant categories. As the plant studied here has a
capacity of 3 MW, it belongs to category C, which includes all the plants above 1.5 MW
up to and including 25 MW. (section 1.2.5 in [29])
Normal operating conditions for this plant’s category (section 3 in [29]) are ±10%
in term of voltage and between 49 Hz and 51 Hz in term of frequency. Units can trip
out of 47-52 Hz. In terms of tolerance of frequency deviations, the plant must be able to
withstand transient frequency gradients (df/dt) of up to ±2.5 Hz/s in the connection point
without disconnecting. Moreover, between 49 and 47.5 Hz it can reduce the active power
production of 6% per Hz (3.3.1 in [29]). In the event of islanded operation the plant must
remain connected and be able to maintain the frequency in normal operation range.
Category C plants have to perform all the control functions types (section 5 in [29]):
Frequency response, frequency control, load regulator, ramp rate limit, Q control, power
factor control, voltage control and system protection. Q control, power factor control and
voltage control will not be detailed here since they are not related to frequency control.
The active power control functions are presented here.
Frequency response (called Limited Frequency Sensitive Mode – Underfrequency (LFSM-
U) and Limited Frequency Sensitive Mode – Overfrequency (LFSM-O)) (section 5.5.1 in
[29]) is deﬁned as the automatic upward or downward adjustment of active power as a
function of grid frequencies below or above a certain reference frequency fR with a view to
stabilizing the grid frequency. The technical regulations are:
• Frequency response must commence within 2s,
• Droop value must be in range of 2-8%
• The minimum and maximum frequencies fmin and fmax that deﬁne the interval
where frequency response should be provided must be in range 47.00-52.00 Hz,
• There can be a deadband between minimum and maximum response frequencies,
denoted fR,min and fR,max respectively, and fR,min must be in 47-49.90 Hz while
fmax must be in 50.1-52 Hz, standard values are 49.8 and 50.2 Hz.
Frequency control (called Frequency Sensitive Mode (FSM)) (section 5.1.2 in [29]) is
deﬁned as the control functions that can provide frequency control in order to stabilise
or restore the grid frequency. Parameters can vary (depending on service, geographical
location), the speciﬁc parameters are stated in the speciﬁcation for ancillary services [28],
and in the following section.
The updated numerical values of the intervals of LFSM and FSM are speciﬁed in [30]
and [33]. It is written there that in Nordic countries, FSM should be provided in the
interval [49.5 Hz; 50.5 Hz], while LFSM should be provided outside this interval, that is
to say in [47.5 Hz; 49.5 Hz] and [50.5 Hz; 51.5 Hz]. It is also speciﬁed in [33] that droops
used for both LFSM and FSM can be in range of 2 to 12%.

The technical conditions to comply with in order to be able to provide ancillary services are

• Frequency-controlled disturbance reserve (FCR-D),

We are interested here only in primary reserve, that is to say FCR-N and FCR-D.
FCR-N secure that the equilibrium between generation and demand is restored, keeping
the frequency close to 50 Hz in the event of frequency deviations. FCR-N is automatic
regulation provided by generation or demand units which, by means of control equipment,
respond to grid frequency deviations. It is provided as symmetrical reserve, where upward
and downward regulation reserves are procured together, and:
• Must be supplied at deviations up to ±100 mHz, that is to say between 49.9 Hz and
50.1 Hz
• Deliveries must be made without deadband
• at minimum linearly between 0 and 100 mHz
• Must be supplied within 150s, regardless the size of the deviation
Each bid entered must be of a minimum of 0.3 MW.
If the frequency goes outside the interval [49.9,50.1] Hz, FCR-N should remain activated:

as the product of λspot, the price on the day-ahead market in €/MWh, Pref, the power
reference, which is the power actually sold on the day-ahead market in MW, and ∆t, the
market time unit in hour, which is actually equal to one hour in DK2 :
pspot = λspot · Pref · ∆t
(2.1)
Let pFCR−N denote the payment given to the supplier for the participation to frequency
control under normal operation. It comprises a price for capacity reserve, which is the
product of Cres,N, the capacity reserve kept for provision of FCR-N in MW, and λres,N,
the price bid for the capacity reserve in €/MW, but also a price paid for energy production,
which is a function of λup ad λdown, the prices of the balancing market in €/MWh, and Eup
and Edown, the energy actually produced for upward and downward regulation in MWh.
The price paid for upward regulation is positive because the producer gets paid for an
additional production of energy. On the contrary, the price paid for downward regulation
is negative because the producer has to pay back the system operator for the energy it did
not produce:

pFCR−N = λres,N · Cres,N + λup · Eup −λdown · Edown
(2.2)
If the plant is providing FCR-N on top of its production on the spot market, it will receive
a payment:
p = pspot + pFCR−N
(2.3)
2.3.2
Payment and pricing for FCR-D
FCR-D (section 1.5.2 in [28]) is presently procured as upward regulation power, but will
be procured as a symmetrical reserve from 2022 [31]. The regulation is similar to FCR-N.
The reserve is procured at daily auctions. It can be submitted hourly or as block bids, and
must state an hour-by-hour volume and a price for the day of operation. Volumes may
change from hour to hour, but the price must be ﬁxed. Each bid must be entered for a
minimum of 0.3 MW and must always be stated in MW to one decimal point. The price
must be stated in DKK/MW or EUR/MW to two decimal points.
All accepted bids for upward regulation receive an availability payment, corresponding to
the players’ bidding price (pay-as-bid). No calculation is made of energy volumes supplied
from frequency-controlled disturbance reserves. Indeed, the energy delivered from FCR-D
does not receive any payment because the service is very energy limited. In other words,
the suppliers do not receive a payment for the energy procured, but only for the capacity
reserved. The payment for FCR-D provsion, pFCR−D, is the product of the capacity reserve
kept for FCR-D provision Cres,D, in MW, and the price bid for this capacity reserve λres,D,
in €/MW:
pFCR−D = λres,D · Cres,D
(2.4)
The numerical values of prices for capacity reserve for FCR provision can be found
on Energi Data Service [34]. The values are provided on an hourly basis. These are
only average prices, not even weighted, otherwise the diﬀerent market players would start
bidding according to the most expensive price.
The numerical values of regulation prices on the realtime market can be found as well
on Energi Data Service [35]. The prices are given on an hourly basis for up and down
regulation.

SIMULATION MODEL OF A BIOGAS PLANT
A simulation model that comprises the main dynamics of the internal processes of a biogas
plant is used to conduct the study. This chapter aims at explaining the basic principles of
this biogas plant model.
4.1
Model of the biogas plant used in this study
The speciﬁc biogas plant model that will be used during the thesis is presented in [2].
This model includes the most relevant dynamics from an electrical engineering perspective
and is used to provide an overview of the potentials of biogas power plant in providing
ﬂexibility towards islanded systems. For a given infeed of raw material, it characterizes
the anaerobic digestion processes and quantify the energy output. The model is validated
against real data from a 3 MW biogas power plant on the island of Bornholm, Denmark.
The presented biogas power plant model comprises four distinct processes with their speciﬁc
dynamics: (a) the digestion of organic material under anaerobic conditions, (b) the storage
of biogas, (c) the burning of gas for generating combined heat and power, and (d) the
auxiliary self-consumption of the plant. Figure 4.1 presents the processes and their possible
ﬂexibility options in a block diagram.

4.2
Model of the biogas plant in Simulink
As explained in [2] and [20], the model of the biogas plant is designed in Simulink. It is
composed of ﬁve main blocks, presented on Figure 4.2:
• the Infeed handling block that calculates, given the substrate composition and its
characterization, the share of volatile solid and the yield of biogas. For the moment,
the substrate composition is ﬁxed;
• the Anaerobic digestion process block that corresponds to the fermenter and
characterizes the anaerobic digestion process. Given the input feeding, the share of
volatile solid, and the biogas yield it is able to output the biogas production rate.
Whenever the storage tank is full, the upper protection is turned on and the inﬂow
to the gas storage is set to zero, this can be interpreted as that the biogas is burned
in a torch before entering the storage;
• the CHP units that take as an input the actual power generation to calculate the
electrical generation and, given the heat-to-power ratio and the eﬃciency of the
unit, the heat production and the losses. Whenever the storage tank is empty, the
production is shut down to zero. The CHP units correspond to two generators of
same size, 1500 kW each. A ramp rate limit is added to the CHP block in the model.
The technical speciﬁcation of the engine actually used in the power plant writes that
the engine can ramp up from 0 to 100% power output in less than two minutes [41].
A ramp rate limit of 100%/120s is therefore used for the simulation;
• the Storage tank block that, given the amount of gas produced and the amount of
gas used by the CHP units to produce power, calculates the level of storage. When
the storage is full, i.e. the State Of Charge (SOC) is 100%, the upper protection is
turned on, and when the SOC comes back to 90% it is turned oﬀ. When the storage
tank is empty, i.e. the SOC is 0%, the lower protection is turned on, and when it
comes back to 10% it is turned oﬀ;
• the Self-consumption block that retrieves to the power produced by the CHP units
what is consumed by the plant itself and outputs what is actually sent to the grid.
• the Controller block, that is the part that will be added to the original biogas plant’s
model to assess the provision of frequency control.

4.3
Input data: feeding and power generation
The two time series that are inputs to the system are the input feeding, which is the
amount of biogas fed over time, and the power generation, which corresponds to the actual
power production of the CHP units when there is no frequency controller.
In reality, the feeding is put in the fermenter every three hours. The amount of biomass
fed each time is approximately the same, resulting in a total feeding over the month of
11480 tons. In the model, a daily feeding is used, that corresponds to the same total
quantity of feeding, so 11480 tons divided by the 30 days of November, which approximately
equals 383 tons per day. This infeed has then been adjusted so that the gas storage level of
the model corresponds to the actual gas storage level of the biogas plant in November. The
composition of the substrate is assumed to be constant and is an average of the substrates
actually used, that vary over times.
The power generation corresponds to the values of the power actually generated by the
CHP units. There is no direct access to the power produced by the generators so the data
collected by the SCADA system are used. They correspond to the energy injected to the
grid. The power generation of the CHP units is the sum of the power injected on the grid
and the self-consumption of the power plant. As the energy used for self-consumption
is known, the power generated by the CHP units can be obtained. The measures from
the SCADA system for November are used: the time resolution of these measurements is
varying between one measurements every 2 minutes to every 15 minutes, but the values
have been interpolated and then aggregated to one hour. Figure 4.3 presents these aggre-

There are various aspects that impacts the level of production. The amount of gas sent

It is a simple control that open/close a valve leading the gas to the CHP units whenever
the storage level crosses these limits. However, there are other reasons why the level of
production may vary. Variations in the quality of gas or incomplete burning can cause
variation in the level of production. The output of one of the generators can be decreased
or even turned oﬀin order to run the other one at higher eﬃciency. The generation could
be also turned oﬀfor maintenance or if the DSO asks for it. All these parameters are not
directly modelled, but instead the real power generation is taken as an input so that its
variations are represented.
In order to model the biochemical reactions and the electrical and thermal generations,
the simulation has been broken down in smaller parts and careful assumptions were made.
These assumptions consist in the time resolutions of the feeding, the dynamic substrate
composition, the simpliﬁcation of biochemical reactions and the neglected pressure–volume
dependency. Hence, the outputs of the model do not correspond to the real ones with the
highest accuracy but allows to have results that can be generalized to other time periods
than the one tested (September 2020) and to other biogas plants (if the corresponding
parameters are adjusted). These assumptions were also made depending on the level of
accuracy that was envisioned by the modelling. For example, the assumption of daily
feeding (instead of 3-hours feeding) is accurate enough when aiming at obtaining hourly
electrical set points. All in all, the model gives a good estimate of a biogas plant’s behaviour,
that can be used to assess its ﬂexibility potentials. [20]
The goal of the thesis is to add a control to the power input of the CHP units so that it can
react to frequency variations. A power deviation ∆P is added to the power input, as shown
in red on Figure 4.2. However, to model this control, it is assumed that the production
can be changed instantly, the only limitation to the changes in power production being
the ramp rate limit of the CHP units, which, as explained earlier, is of 100% of maximum
capacity per 120 s. Other limitations are not taken into account, such as, for example,
the fact that it could be not possible to obtain the desired gas production because of
incomplete burning of gas.

MODEL OF THE CONTROLLER
This chapter presents the model of the frequency controller that the existing Simulink-based
biogas plant model has been supplemented with. The chapter starts with a presentation
of the global design of the frequency controller. Then it explains why there should be
separated blocks in the controller for provision of FCR-N and FCR-D. Afterwards, it
exposes why and how should capacity reserve be kept. Finally, it presents the complete
model of the controller as implemented in Simulink.
5.1
Global design of the frequency controller
Presently, the CHP units’ block gets as an input the power generation Pgen, which indicates
how much power should be produced. The amount of gas needed is deduced from that,
and compared to the level of gas of the storage, so that if there is enough gas left, this
amount can be produced; otherwise, the production is shut down. The model calculates
how much electricity, heat and losses are produced by the CHP units.
The controller will calculate a new power generation, that will be denoted Pset and called
’power setpoint’. It corresponds to the power output of the CHP units that is desired.
This power setpoint will be calculated as the sum of the power deviation ∆P and a power
reference Pref (5.1). This power reference Pref is obtained from Pgen, the speciﬁcs will be
further detailed in sections 5.3 and 5.4. The power deviation is directly calculated from the
variation in grid frequency and a droop kdroop (5.1). The grid frequency fgrid is compared
to a reference frequency fref that can vary depending on the range of frequency where
the controller is activated. This will be further explained in the following sections. The
frequency variation is then ∆f = fgrid −fref.

both. Several cases will be considered, and it is therefore needed that the distinction is made
between these ranges of frequency and that the controller can be activated accordingly.
It is worth noticing that for the set of frequency data considered, which corresponds to the
frequency measurements in November 2020, the frequency never went out of the interval

5.3
Capacity reserve
Given the regulations in DK2 detailed in section 2.2, in order to participate to frequency
control, a power plant needs to keep a capacity reserve. This way, it is able at any moment
to produce the energy needed in case of a frequency deviation. It means that the power
reference Pref should stay under an upper limit, so that it is always able to provide up-
regulation, and over a lower limit, so it is always able to provide down-regulation. In DK2,
the power plant has to keep symmetrical capacity reserves, as explained in 2.2.2, so that it
can provide both upward and downward regulation. When providing frequency control, the
power production from the controller ∆P has to be linear with respect to frequency devia-
tion. Therefore, the capacity reserve has to correspond exactly to the power production at
maximum frequency deviation for a given interval (FCR-N or FCR-D) and for a given droop.
For example, if the plant wants to provide FCR-N with a droop of 4%, the maximum

6. Results
6.1.5
Comparison of technical results of the four cases
Four Cases were tested, combining diﬀerent possibilities of provision of frequency control:
providing both FCR-N and FCR-D (Cases 1 and 2) or just FCR-D (Cases 3 and 4), keeping
an additional capacity reserve (Cases 2 and 4) or not (Cases 1 and 3).
For all the cases, the energy produced from the controller is small compared to the total
energy production: the case where the ratio is the highest (in absolute value) is in Case
2 using a droop of 2%, the ratio is then equal to -0.12% of total energy produced. In
all cases, the production from the controller is negative, which means that it is actually
lowering the production of the power plant. These results are consistent with what was
expected. Indeed, the analysis of frequency showed that the variations around the reference
frequency were balanced over a month with a slight tendency for going over 50 Hz. As
power deviations implied by the controller are proportional to grid frequency deviations
and of opposite sign, there were expected to be small and negative. This is also veriﬁed
when the controller is not activated all the time (Cases 1 and 3).
As a result, the energy production is very similar to the energy production at reference
level Pref. As explained before, when an additional capacity reserve is kept, the mean
value of Pref is lower than Pbase. Therefore, in Cases where an additional capacity reserve
is kept (Cases 2 and 4), the total energy production is lower than when it is not (Cases 1
and 3). This lower production implies higher levels of storage.
Technical results also have shown that FCR-D is energy limited compared to FCR-N. When
making a reserve for FCR-N (Case 2), the total energy production from the controller over
the month is still small, but looking at upward and downward regulation seperately, it can
be seen that the capacity reserve is often used. On the contrary, when keeping a reserve
for FCR-D (Case 4) it is almost not used. Capacity reserves for FCR-D are larger than
those of FCR-N and thus result in levels of storage that are even higher and often reach
the upper limit of the tank.

Comparison of economical results

conclusions can be drawn.
At this stage, it is also interesting to come back to the
results of the quick calculation presented in 6.2.1 to see how much accuracy was gained by
running these simulations.
When the biogas power plant keeps the same power reference that it would have had if
not providing frequency control, it is sure to receive the same revenues from the support
scheme, and an additional revenues from ancillary services. Simultaneously, it tends to
produce less (because of the tendency of frequency to go over 50 Hz). As a result, it is
sure to have higher revenues.
When the biogas power plant keeps an additional capacity reserve, the average production
is lower. Therefore, both revenues from support schemes and costs are lower, and revenues
from ancillary services are higher. It appears that the revenues from the support schemes
are large compared to the other revenues and the costs. The lower revenues from support
scheme can be balanced by the additional revenues from ancillary services only for small
sizes of capacity reserves. For example, reserves for FCR-N are small enough for almost all
the droops to be proﬁtable, but the reserves for FCR-D are too big and never proﬁtable.
The payment from support schemes is quite high, so it is interesting how changes in their
values would impact the result. A sensitivity analysis will be conducted, presented in
section 6.2.8.
Regarding the quick calculation, it appears that case A corresponds to Base Case ad Case
B is similar to Case 2. The diﬀerence between the two is that Case B is taking into account
only FCR-N, while Case 2 take into account both FCR-N and FCR-D. But FCR-D does

CONCLUDING REMARKS
This thesis focused on assessing the provision of frequency control from a biogas power
plant. This chapter concludes the report. It starts with a summary of the main results,
answering the research questions listed in section 1.5 and discussing the most interesting
points. Then, it presents further investigations that could be carried out to analyse more
deeply the provision of frequency control from a biogas plant.
7.1
Discussion and conclusion
In order to assess the capability of a biogas power plant to provide primary frequency
control, the speciﬁc model of a 3 MW biogas power plant in Bornholm, DK2, was extended
to include a frequency controller. The regulation on provision of primary frequency control
in DK2 speciﬁes that a control action has to be provided in a speciﬁc frequency interval,
between 49.5 Hz and 50.5 Hz. This interval is divided in two parts, that correspond to
two types of primary frequency control. The ﬁrst one is between 49.9 Hz and 50.1 Hz,
and corresponds to Frequency-Controlled Normal operation Reserve, denoted FCR-N. The
second one is between 49.5 Hz and 49.9 Hz and between 50.1 Hz and 50.5 Hz, it corresponds
to Frequency-Controlled Disturbance Reserve, denoted FCR-D. The power plants can
choose to provide only one of the two or both at the same time. They must be able to
adapt linearly their power output to the variations of grid frequency on the whole interval
considered. Therefore, appropriate capacity reserves must be kept by the power plants
that want to provide primary frequency control. The size of the capacity reserve depends
on the droop used and the frequency interval considered.
The implemented controller allows to provide one of the two types of frequency control
or both. Several cases have been considered, combining diﬀerent options: provision of
FCR-N and FCR-D or only FCR-D, as well as keeping an additional capacity reserve or
not. For each case, the value of the droop that relates the power output variations to the
grid frequency variations has been varied between the extreme values authorized, that is to
say between 2 and 12%. The model has been tested using real-life data of grid frequency,
feeding and power production of CHP units for November 2020.
The biogas power plant is able to provide frequency control for all the speciﬁed droop
values. For the data considered, it is not limited by the storage level, nor by the ramp rate
limit of the CHP units, which has almost no eﬀect on the power output. As the controller
is activated only when the needed capacity reserve is available, power can be adapted
linearly to the grid frequency. However, the minimum size of capacity reserve that can
be bid in DK2 is 0.3 MW, which in the case of the 3 MW power plant is larger than the
reserve needed in most of the cases. When providing FCR-N, it is equal to 0.3 MW only
when using the most aggressive droop, 2%. When providing FCR-D, it is equal or larger
than 0.3 MW for droops equal or smaller than 8%. However, all droops have been tested,

because the results in p.u. could be applied to larger power plants.
The power production of the CHP units, Pset, is the sum of the production at a reference
level, Pref, and the production from the controller, ∆P. The power reference used is
derived from the actual power production of the biogas power plant Pbase. Pbase is often
quite close to maximum capacity, because it is in the interest of the plant to maximize its
production. Therefore it does not always leave room for an upward capacity reserve. On
the contrary, the downward capacity reserve is available almost all the time. Two options
have been tested: use directly Pbase as the power reference, or keep an additional capacity
reserve. With the ﬁrst option, depending on the value of the droop used and thus on the
size of the capacity reserve, the control action can be activated only for some hours in the
month. With the second option, it can be activated all the time (except when the plant is
shut down) but it results in a production from power reference that is lower than in the
ﬁrst option.
The grid frequency in November 2020 is balanced around 50 Hz and stays close to this value,
slightly above. This behaviour can be generalized to larger time period as it similar results
were obtained for the ten month studied (March to December 2020). Therefore, when
considering a time period of a whole month, the production from upward and downward
regulation of the controller is balanced, and slightly negative. The total production of the
power plant is therefore close to the production from power reference. In case an additional
capacity reserve is kept, the power reference is lower as is the energy production of the
plant. This impacts the level of storage, which is higher.
In the cases studied, provision of FCR-N implies sizes of capacity reserve in a range that
allows the gas storage level to stay below the upper limit. Moreover, even if the total
production from the controller over the month is small, looking separately at upward and
downward regulation, it can be seen that the capacity reserve is often used.
On the contrary, sizes of capacity reserves that correspond to FCR-D are larger and result
in a gas storage full almost all the time. The provision of FCR-D is limited in energy, so
the reserve kept is not exploited extensively. When the storage tank is full, the surplus
of biogas is wasted, so the amount of feeding is not appropriate. In case the power plant
wants to provide this type of control, it should adopt a strategic feeding, in order to adapt
the biogas production to the electricity production desired.
The economical results depend on the cases considered. Generally, the proﬁts are quite
similar to the case where no controller is used. When no additional capacity reserve is kept,
it is always beneﬁcial for the plant to provide frequency control. Indeed, it receives the
same revenues for the reference production, and, additionally, revenues for capacity reserve
when available. However, the revenues from ancillary services for provision of frequency
control are negligible. When an additional capacity reserve is kept, it results in an average
total production that is lower, and thus revenues from reference production are lower. It is
beneﬁcial only when the payment for capacity reserve is larger than the loss of revenues
from reference production. This is only the case for small sizes of capacity reserve, in this
case equal or lower than 0.05 p.u. Keeping a reserve for FCR-D is the only case where the
proﬁts are signiﬁcantly lower, this type of frequency control is therefore not interesting for
the plant.
The FIT paid by the support schemes to the biogas power plant are large compared to the
prices paid for frequency control, and thus, even when frequency control is beneﬁcial, the

additional gain is quite negligible. The sensitivity analysis showed that the plant would
not be able to have positive proﬁts without these FITs under the assumed LCOE. To be
self-suﬃcient when selling its production on the spot market, the LCOE from the power
plant should be reduced to be lower than the average spot price, which in November 2020,
corresponds to a reduction of the LCOE of almost 50%. Looking at the proﬁts, the value
of FIT needs to be closer to the LCOE before the additional revenues from the provision
of frequency control starts to make a real diﬀerence. For example, in the case where a
capacity reserve is kept for FCR-N only, the provision of frequency control is at least 3.5%
more valuable (depending on the value of the droop) when FIT is 40% lower than current
value.
To conclude, with the current levels of prices and costs, that is to say FIT larger than the
others, it does not bring a real economical beneﬁt to provide frequency control for the
investigated biogas plant under the current regulatory framework. Indeed, provision of
frequency control requires from the power plant to keep a capacity reserve, which implies
lower production and a surplus of biogas to be stored. It has been seen that the plant
does not produce at its maximum all the time, for example the production was lower in
October than in November, but it tends to maximize its production to maximize revenues.
Provision of frequency control would be more proﬁtable if payments from support schemes
and ancillary services were more similar.
In the speciﬁc case of the biogas plant studied during this thesis, provision of FCR-N
seems feasible, as the the capacity reserve is actually used by the control action, and the
economical results are satisfactory. However, with the current requirement on the minimum
size of 0.3 MW of capacity reserves, only the most aggressive droop (2%) can be used.
On the contrary, provision of FCR-D requires too large reserves, and the corresponding
production is not adapted to the infeed. The surplus of biogas stored is an important
aspect: if the capacity reserve becomes too large compared to the size of the storage tank,
other solutions need to be considered. The size of the gas storage tank could e.g. be
increased, demand-driven feeding could be implemented, or the surplus of biogas produced
could be upgraded to biomethane.
7.2
Future works
This section presents some aspects that have not been studied in the scope of this thesis
but would be interesting for further investigation.
7.2.1
Evolution of subsidies for biogas power plants
The revenues of biogas plants in Denmark, and more globally in Europe, are dependent on
the subsidies paid by the support schemes. It is expected that they would decrease in the
future, pushing the biogas sector to increase its sustainability and reduce its costs [14]. In
this perspective, a sensitivity analysis was conducted to have a broader overview of the
possible revenues from frequency control. However, the trends of subsidies should be further
analyze to have more accurate results and an understanding of the interdependencies of all
relevant parameters.

7. Concluding remarks
7.2.2
Islanded operation
One of the assumptions made in this thesis is that the contribution of the biogas power
plant is negligible compared to the other generation units, so the controller was modeled
as an open loop. It can be explained by the fact that, for the data considered, Bornholm
was connected to the Swedish power system, and in that case, the size of the plant, 3 MW,
is negligible compared to the other generation units.
In case the island needs to be self-suﬃcient, it would operate in islanded mode. Then, the
biogas plant’s contribution would sensitively impact the grid. Therefore, it would have
been interesting to model Bornholm’s power system and the frequency controller as a
closed loop. The methodology used would have been to model a simpliﬁed model of the
power system of the Danish island of Bornholm to assess the impact of frequency control
from the biogas plant, similarly to what is presented in [6], which implements a single-bus
system in order to asses the impact of primary frequency control from EVs.
Moreover, the results could be applied to another biogas plant, possibly of larger size. Its
impact on the grid should be taken into account as well, by modelling a closed loop system.
7.2.3
Gas storage level control
In some of the cases studied, the level of storage often hits the upper limit of the gas
storage tank. Therefore, it would have been interesting to add a second layer of control,
in order to adapt the power production to the gas storage level: the production could be
lowered when the storage tank is depleted, and inversely.
7.2.4
Feeding management
Thanks to their ﬂexibility potentials biogas plants are an interesting option to supply
demand-driven electricity. In this thesis, the gas storage tank was the element allowing for
ﬂexible production. Another way of providing ﬂexible production is to modulate the biogas
production by implementing a demand-driven feeding [3] [4]. The study in [3] demonstrated
a high degree of intra-day ﬂexibility and a potential for an electricity shutdown of up to
3 days by ﬂexible feeding in full-scale. The ﬂexible feeding did not aﬀect negatively the
long-term process stability and proved that it could enable signiﬁcant savings in terms
of the required gas storage volume (up to 65%) and permit far greater plant ﬂexibility
compared to constant gas production. Such feeding management could be applied to the
biogas power plant under study in this thesis, especially in cases where the size of the gas
storage was not appropriate.
