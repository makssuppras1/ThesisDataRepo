Abstract
Peripheral neuropathies are typically diagnosed through complex clinical examinations
conducted by trained professionals with specialized equipment. In practice, limited ca­
pacity and long waiting times hinder early detection, which is crucial for effective treat­
ment. A previous project at the Technical University of Denmark demonstrated that an
acceptable diagnosis can be achieved without dedicated hardware by employing struc­
tured, self­administered assessments on a mobile device. Their resulting Neuropathy
Grading Tool was validated in a clinical setting but faced limitations in scalability due to
its local­only data storage and device­bound distribution model.
This thesis builds on that foundation with the objective of enhancing maintainability, mod­
ularity, and data­collection capabilities to support research on peripheral neuropathies.
The codebase was physically restructured, the application’s architecture and state man­
agement were redesigned, and a secure login flow with informed consent was integrated.
Using the Copenhagen Research Platform framework, support was added for uploading
examination results to cloud storage. The updated system was evaluated through soft­
ware quality metrics, performance analysis, and a small­scale usability study with invited
participants. The findings confirm that the revised application operates effectively in prac­
tice, is capable of distributed deployment, and can serve as a reliable tool for remote data
collection in neuropathy research.

Introduction
Diagnosing Peripheral Neuropathies typically relies on a complex clinical examination
performed by trained professionals with access to specialized equipment. In practice the
system struggles with capacity: clinics face long waiting lists, yet early detection is crucial
for effective treatment. To address this gap, a project had been created by students of the
Technical University of Denmark. [1] Based on a field study, the authors argued that an
acceptable diagnosis can be captured without dedicated hardware by leveraging struc­
tured, self­administered assessments on a mobile device. They also noted the growing
role of patient­reported outcomes in the patient­centered healthcare model.
On this basis they designed a mobile health application aimed at widening access to
neuropathy screening. A small­scale validation suggested the approach is feasible and
potentially useful, while clearly indicating that further research is required due to the limited
sample and scope. Their work establishes both the motivation and an initial technical
baseline on top of which the present thesis is built.
The baseline, the conclusion of their work ­ the original Neuropathy Grading Tool ­ had
been validated and used at the clinics. The application had been installed only on a couple
of devices, which were provided for the clinics. All the patients had to use the same device
this way, and their data was stored in the devices’ local storage. These factors contributed
to limiting the solution’s scalability.
The original project focused on the design and clinical accuracy of the application. This
approach satisfied their goals, but made it hard to follow­up­on the application and to
maintain it. While functioned well as a standalone diagnosis tool besides the shortcom­
ings of distribution, the stakeholders expressed their intentions to extend the applications
capabilities with the option to collect medical data ­ the outcome of examinations ­ from
people, to be able to foster data­based research on peripheral neuropathies.
Building on these aspirations, to prepare the application for modularity and long­term
maintenance, a physical restructuring of the codebase was performed, followed by an
architecture redesign and a change of the state­management approach. Subsequently,
using a framework provided by the Copenhagen Research Platform ­ which framework
had been used in the original application as well to build the examination ­ was introduced
to enable user login, including an informed­consent session and support for the upload of
examination results to the cloud. The impact of these changes was evaluated by analyzing
software quality metrics, conducting a performance analysis, and running a small­scale
usability test in which the application was distributed to invited participants. The invitation
process and the study task itself not only formed the basis of the usability evaluation, but
also served as a verification that the application functions in practice and can be used to
collect data.

The primary research question of this project is as follows:
How to make the Flutter mHealth Neuropathy Grading Tool application ready to collect
data for research purposes?

2.1
Introduction
The project had been started to enhance and extend an existing Mobile Health (mHealth)
application with additional functionality and capabilities, approaching the issue from a
software engineering perspective. While functionality was utmost importance from the
beginning, the goal was to achieve it in a way that enables future researchers to further

2.1.1
Neuropathy grading tool
The first and main component of the project is the Neuropathy Grading Tool that had
been developed in 2023 by Julia Gabriela Makulec and Mads Westermann as part of
their Master Thesis at the Technical University of Denmark, which later also had been
published as a research article. [1] Their project was focused on creating a standalone
mobile application to provide patients with a tool to self­assess peripheral neuropathy.
The study explores the medical background in a throughout way, discussing sympthoms
of neuropathy and its diagnostic measures in depth. It emphasizes user­centered design
and takes an iterative approach to develop the final questioneer, which is implemented
and validated in their study. The implementation itself uses the Flutter framework to create
a cross­platform solution, making it available to an even broader audience by investing
in the localization solution within the project. In conclusion they mention that more data
would be needed to validate that the application could be used at home to carry out self­
assessment and detect early neuropathy. The implementation makes good use of Flutter
packages developed by the Copenhagen Research Platform, which is explored in more
detail next.
2.1.2
Copenhagen Research Platform
The Copenhagen Reseach Platform (CARP) is an open­source project that offers both
frameworks and server­side services to help researchers with mHealth application de­
velopment and digital phenotyping.[2] It offers a wide range of packages supported by
extensive documentation and tutorials. Besides information accessible on their website,
their software is available on Github under MIT license [3], as well as they have multiple
packages published on pub.dev website [4] which is the official package repository for
Flutter and Dart.
The offered domain model, development packages and server­side infrastructure are all
great building blocks that can be used to develop an mHealth application from scratch, or
alternatively to extend an existing one with new features.

2.2.1
Classic layered architecture
Layered architecture ­ often called n­tier or multi­layered ­ structures an application into
a stack of horizontal layers, each charged with a single, well­defined responsibility. A
major rule that is defined is the one­way dependency flow: every upper layer may depend
only on the layer immediately beneath it, never the other way around. In practice there is
differentiation between logical layers (the conceptual slices) and physical tiers (the runtime
deployment units). Several logical layers can — and in mobile applications usually do ­
co­exist inside the same physical tier without violating the model.[8]
The canonical three­tier variant of layered architecture maps cleanly to Flutter develop­
ment:
• Presentation layer: Flutter’s widget tree and navigation stack.
• Business­logic layer: domain rules, use­cases, and state managers (e.g. blocs,
providers).
• Data­access layer: APIs, local databases, secure­storage, or any persistence mech­
anism.
Additional slices (e.g. a service layer for cross­cutting concerns or an application layer
for orchestration) can be added to increase separation of concerns and keep individual
components small and testable.
Isolation makes each slice replaceable, unit­test friendly, and resilient to changes in neigh­
boring layer. The clear contracts ensure that changing specifics e.g. an API client or
refactoring business rules should rarely affect the UI to bring an example. Scalability also
improves because whole tiers can be replicated or off­loaded without touching the rest of
the stack. On the contrary the design introduces latency: every call must traverse one or
more boundaries. Another issue is that it is conflicting with Flutter’s reactive, declarative
paradigm, which requires more flexibility. The strict top­down flow can feel limiting when
the framework would rely on near­real­time UI updates.
In practice these issues are mitigated by exposing reactive interfaces at layer edges:
Streams for continuous data, Futures for operations, or callback channels that transfer
effects upward without breaking the dependency rule. These asynchronous operations
preserve the layered model while letting the UI rebuild instantly when fresh state arrives,
and this achieves the integration of the classical architecture with Flutter’s event­driven
nature.
2.2.2
Model­View­ViewModel (MVVM)
Model­View­ViewModel (MVVM) extends the classic layered stack with an explicit presentation­
logic slice that matches the Flutter framework’s reactive nature. The pattern divides the
code­base into three roles, which map well to Flutter:
• View: Flutter widgets that render the UI and forward user intents.
• ViewModel: An adapter that transforms domain objects into view­ready state, and
exposes commands.
• Model: the underlying business layer (use­cases, repositories, data sources).

Data flow is unidirectional in the model. A gesture triggers an intent, the ViewModel issues
a command against the domain layer, and the resulting state flows back to the View, which
rebuilds the UI. By inserting the ViewModel between UI and data, MVVM preserves the
strict dependency rule of layered architecture while adding a reactive element that wel­
comes naturally several popular state management approaches (e.g setState, Provider,
Riverpod, or BLoC).
Flutter’s own documentation promotes MVVM as a default mental model [9], mapping
clean­architecture [10] terminology almost one­to­one:
• View ­> presentation
• ViewModel ­> presentation logic
• Use­cases(Model) ­> application
• Repositories(Model) ­> data
The result is high testability (ViewModels compile without a widget tree), modular feature
slices, and predictable scaling for medium­to­large applications.
The trade­offs are similar in most structured patterns. In the case of MVVM the View­
Models can balloon fast into large objects which is impossible to maintain, unless their
responsibilities are aggressively pruned. The immutable state classes, mappers, and dis­
posal hooks add boilerplate, long­lived streams or controllers demand careful cleanup to
avoid memory leaks.
For prototypes or small apps that overhead usually outweighs the gains, but otherwise
when the goal is clean presentation logic, reactive UI updates, and hard boundaries that
survive refactors, MVVM offers a natural fit for Flutter’s declarative style and the state­
management libraries that power it.
2.2.3
MVC
First talked about in the 1970s, MVC later quickly became a renowned approach for object­
oriented user­interface code [11]. In its canonical form the Model holds data and business
rules, the View renders that data to the screen, and the Controller interprets user input,
updates the Model, and selects an appropriate View. All interaction flows in a single
direction, from View to Controller to Model, which keeps responsibilities clearly separated
and makes the pattern easy to teach, reason about, and prototype.
The MVC Flutter adaptations follow the same outline with a few substitutions: widgets
serve as Views, the State class often doubles as the Controller, and the Model is split into
domain entities (pure business objects) and application models (DTOs, UI). As it is visible,
the fit can be made, but it is not seamless. Flutter encourages reactive data binding,
whereas MVC’s originally is imperative, and as a result MVC remains less common than
other approaches. When used without caution, in this approach the Controller (State) can
be the one which grows way out of it’s scope hoarding navigation, validation, and logic.
This issue have been observed in several other patterns on this list, and it hurts scalability.
To make MVC work in the Flutter framework, it is advised to keep the controller thin:
translate UI intents, delegate to use­cases in the Model layer, and let immutable state
objects modify the View. If this can be achieved, MVC can be a fine choice for small
applications, classroom examples, and solo prototypes where understandability and rapid
development pace outweighs long­term maintainability. For medium or large use­cases
­ which usually demand more­and­more native reactivity — it is better to choose other

solutions which integrate better with the Flutter framework by design (e.g., MVVM, layered
clean architecture with a dedicated state engine). [12]
2.2.4
Clean Architecture
While clean architecture as the concept already have been mentioned in the MVVM para­
graph, this section is mainly about how the original concept works in­ and translates to
Flutter based on a research paper [13] on the clean architecture and their released FLut­
ter package for it (at the time of writing the package itself is not too popular with 900
downloads in the past 30 days, but that is not a reason not to explore it [14]).
Clean Architecture organises a code­base into concentric rings whose dependencies
point strictly inward. At the centre there are Entities and Use­Cases: pure­Dart busi­
ness rules that are isolated from outer rings. Surrounding layers become progressively
more concrete: first interface adapters (presenters, DTO mappers, repository gateways)
and finally the framework and driver ring that houses Flutter UI, navigation, local storage,
network clients, and any third­party SDKs. The inversion is deliberate: the domain owns
the abstract interfaces, while the outer layers supply implementations that are injected
back toward the core.
In Flutter this blueprint translates to four layers:
• View: responsible for widget tree plus navigation code.
• ViewState: contains immutable UI state objects.
• Controller: captures user events and orchestrates Use­Cases.
• Presenter: converts pure­Dart Use­Case output into ViewState.
The folder structure should mirror the rings so boundaries stay visible at a glance, and no
Flutter types are allowed to leak into the domain.
Compared with MVVM, the emphasis shifts: MVVM centres its ViewModel (a hybrid of pre­
sentation logic and state), whereas Clean Architecture centres Use­Cases and enforces
dependency inversion. MVVM offers faster execution but risks a bloated ViewModel, while
Clean architecture imposes more ceremony yet achieves stronger decoupling and clearer
ownership.
The benefits are substantial for medium­to­large, long­lived products: clear unit testing of
business rules, safe refactoring, parallel development across rings, and the freedom to
swap outer components without modifying the core. To provide this, it comes with sub­
stantial overhead: extra layers, indirection, interface boilerplate, and steeper on­boarding.
For these reasons it is not recommended for solo developers or throw­away apps: these
are usually better served by lighter patterns, where delivery speed is superior to archi­
tectural purity. On the other hand when multiple teams share a code­base, scalability,
portability, and high test coverage matter, Clean Architecture provides a disciplined ap­
proach.
2.2.5
BLoC­centric architecture
Since it was mentioned that architecture patterns can be built on top of state management
approaches, this paragraph aims to present that using the BLoC approach (later explained
as a state management approach as well) as the base.
BLoC­centred architecture organizes each feature into a slim three­layer stack: presenta­
tion, domain and data. It delegates orchestration to the presentation layer’s logic compo­
nent that can be a Bloc, Cubit, Riverpod notifier or any reactive tool. Interaction remains
6

unidirectional: a widget forwards a user intent to the component, which invokes a use
case inside the pure­Dart domain layer. That use case calls a repository interface whose
concrete implementation lives in the data layer and drives local or remote data sources.
Results are received as immutable view­state objects, triggering a rebuild and keeping
widgets thin and declarative.
Like Clean Architecture, the domain owns the contracts and never imports Flutter types,
but the overall scheme is lighter: fewer indirections and less boilerplate. The upside is
a modular, test­friendly code­base where back ends can be swapped and state remains
predictable.
The hazards are familiar too: here the presentation layer can couple all logic. Because
of the separation it also has the mapping overhead.
In practice the pattern excels on medium­to­large, long­lived products where several
teams work together, yet they want to avoid the heavier ceremony of full Clean Archi­
tecture. Prototypes or very small apps might benefit from using lighter models. Projects
that demand compile­time enforcement of boundaries may still prefer the stricter concen­
tric rings that is offered by Clean Architecture. [15]
2.3
State management
State management in flutter is a complex and actively researched field by the flutter com­
munity. Effective state management is critical in flutter development as it has significant
implications on both performance, scalability and maintainability. The flutter documenta­
tion itself lists 20 different approaches to choose from with plenty of resources and exam­
ples on all of them. [16] Flutter is declarative, the user interface is built up considering the
state of the application. Opposed to imperative frameworks, flutter rebuilds the UI from
scratch on every frame if that is needed, instead of modifying elements directly.
In Flutter, two main types of states can be distinguished: ephemeral state and app­wide
state. The first one is named after that it has a relatively short life span, contained within
a single widget, thus frequently also called UI­ or local­state. In general this type of data
do not need to be shared with the widget tree e.g. stages of an animation or text in an
input field. On the contrary, app­wide state affects multiple parts of the application e.g.
language settings or an authentication session.
While there are multiple studies comparing state management approaches in a bulk or
comparing them against each other in specific settings, it is not possible to declare one
superior over the others: each has their advantages and disadvantages, thus choosing
one should be largely dependent on the use case. This project aims to present a well­
rounded preview on the methods available. In order to try to create a ranking, the related
Flutter packages’ download count had been used presented by pub.dev, the official pack­
age repository for Flutter. The website only provides a sum of the downloads within the
past 30 days, but due to the frequent updates of the packages maybe this is even more rel­
evant than the total number of downloads. It is possible to observe the number of weekly
downloads in a larger time­frame and these usually show consistency magnitude­wise,
making it a fair point of comparison for the packages. While popularity does not necessar­
ily correlate with the packages quality and usefulness, it is an important factor considering
broader support by the community and materials available on more popular packages.

the past 30 days (as of 2025­07­29), starting off with 2 unranked (not package related)
low­level approaches, which are important to understand for state management.

2.3.1
setState
setState is a simple method that is part of the flutter/material base package, basically a
built­in solution for state management. [17] The method excels in managing ephemeral
state (it is possible to use it for app state management as well, however not recommended
by the documentation), which essentially doesn’t require the use of higher level state
management approaches. It is a convenient and easy to use solution when data only
needs to be updated within a (Stateful) Widget.
Calling this method notifies the framework to schedule a rebuild for the state object (marks
it as outdated) thus the associated sub­tree. It is usually used as a wrapper for changing
state objects, making it possible to reflect changes in state objects on the user interface
on the next frame. The rebuild is considered before the next frame only, meaning multiple
calls within the same frame only causes performance loss due to it’s direct overhead, and
has no actual use, thus should be avoided in tight loops, as it is also mentioned in the
previously cited documentation. The call is always synchronous, so the state change is
set in time.
To clarify, changing state objects is possible outside or without notifying the framework,
but then it won’t be reflected on the interface until another rebuild is triggered. The widget
should be checked if mounted (if it is still part of the widget tree) in order to call this method
(as the widget can be disposed during an async operation causing an error in execution).
2.3.2
InheritedWidget
InheritedWidget is a foundational abstract class in the flutter framework [18]. It is used
when data needs to be downwards propagated in the widget tree. Essentially the widget
exposes data for the descendants. It needs to be referenced in the child object to access
the data (state), and it is also capable of triggering rebuild in the descendant.
It is worth noting that InheritedWidget is immutable and does not manage state directly,
but it serves as a tool to expose it for descendants. It is usually combined with a Stateful­
Widget that rebuilds and re­injects a new instance of the InheritedWidget when the data
changes.
Using InheritedWidget directly in smaller applications is usually good practice, but it can
be verbose as the application grows as it requires careful manual widget tree planning
and wrapping. It also requires spending a lot of time on boilerplate code creation, as
manual definition of its methods are needed. It is also quite error­prone, as setting the
correct references and update notifiers can be confusing in case of larger applications.
For above mentioned reasons it is often wrapped or abstracted by more ergonomic and
advanced state management libraries such as Provider, Riverpod and BLoC as well.
Similarly to setState, rebuilding via InheritedWidget is also limited to the next frame, but
due to its reliance on it’s subtree and subsriptions, it is generally more efficient when the
goal is to broadcast shared state changes to multiple child objects.
2.3.3
Provider
Provider is currently the most­adopted, package­based state­management solution in the
Flutter ecosystem. At the time of writing, it has 4.67 million downloads in the past thirty
days, and it is the only state­management package that consistently surpasses the four­
million­download mark in that period. [19] It is also the recommended approach for new
Flutter developers in the official flutter documentation when it comes to state management.
[20]
Its popularity stems from the fact that it wraps the low­level InheritedWidget in a far more
8
Mobile Data Collection Tool for Neuropathy
==PAGE:17==
ergonomic interface, thus eliminating much of the boiler­plate and cognitive overhead
associated with manual tree­injection. In addition to the wrapper itself, Provider offers
convenient possibilities for resource allocation and disposal, lazy­loading, and good in­
tegration with the Flutter DevTools, all of which make following the flow of state during
development considerably easier.
Conceptually, a Provider (e.g. ChangeNotifierProvider, FutureProvider, or a custom im­
plementation) is placed high enough in the widget tree to expose a state, while descen­
dants obtain or listen to that state through one of three context extensions: context.watch,
context.read or context.select, or by using a dedicated Consumer widget. Once the
basic pattern is understood the workflow feels intuitive and aligned with Flutter’s declar­
ative widget model: once the subtree is wrapped, the listener UI rebuild based on state
once the state is modified if configured correctly. The package supplies a MultiProvider
helper to group several providers together, reducing nesting when multiple independent
pieces of state must be injected.
The main drawback of Provider is that it remains tightly coupled to the widget BuildContext.
Any call to context.read or context.watch must therefore be guarded by a mounted­
check inside asynchronous code paths, otherwise a disposed widget can trigger an excep­
tion. Besides using MultiProvider, deeply nested provider trees can still become verbose
and difficult to reason about in very large applications. For that reason Provider is gener­
ally considered an great fit for small to medium­sized projects, or for the early stages of a
larger codebase before more opinionated architectural layers (such as BLoC or Riverpod)
are introduced.
2.3.4
Riverpod
Riverpod is currently the second­most downloaded, package­based state­management
library on pub.dev—roughly 2.35 million installs within the last month, which numbers
proved stable for more than a year now. [21]
Created by the author of Provider, Riverpod was designed to correct two structural pain­
points in Provider: its tight coupling to the Flutter widget tree and a related issue to this,
its reliance on BuildContext. In Riverpod every state exists in a Dart provider object, com­
pletely detached from UI concerns. Widgets have to obtain a handle (WidgetRef) when
they need to read, watch, or listen to that state. This inversion makes Riverpod framework­
agnostic, which eliminates the need for context­related guard clauses (context.mounted).
It helps developers to think in terms of stateless widgets that respond to external state up­
dates.
Beyond the architectural shift, Riverpod improves regarding several modern conveniences
that were not existent in Provider: native handling of loading and error states, pull­to­
refresh semantics, and automatic rebuilds that only target the widgets that actually de­
pend on the mutated provider, often leading to performance gains compared to provider
[22], especially in large­scale applications. The library further reduces boilerplate through
code­generation (riverpod generator) and by eliminating the need for manual widget wrap­
ping (MultiProvider, Consumer, etc.). State is declared once and can be accessed from
anywhere in the codebase.
The main drawback of Riverpod is probably a steeper learning curve. Riverpod intro­
duces its own system (e.g., Ref, ProviderBase, family and auto­dispose modifiers) and
encourages a more difficult dependency­injection style, which is more verbose compared
to the simplicity in Provider (context.watch). In small applications these abstractions
can cause over­engineering, but they shine in medium­to­large codebases where state

dependencies can influence dozens of features. For such projects Riverpod’s decoupling,
fine­grained rebuilds, and testability offers a good solution, and based on the download
numbers it is still better adopted for teams which outgrew Provider, but do not want to use
heavier, opinionated patterns like BLoC.
2.3.5
BLoC
BLoC (Business Logic Component) has the third spot among package­based state­management
libraries on pub.dev [23]. Its Flutter wrapper, flutter_bloc, logs just over 2.29 million
downloads in the last thirty days, almost at par with Riverpod and comfortably ahead of
all other contenders.
At its core BLoC is not a package but an architectural pattern that enforces a unidirectional
data flow: events are used as input, and states as output. A Bloc receives events from the
UI, transforms them (often asynchronously) and emits new states through a Dart Stream.
Widgets such as BlocBuilder, BlocListener, or BlocConsumer subscribe to that stream
and rebuild or react accordingly. The result is a highly predictable update cycle where
every visual change can be traced back to a discrete event, greatly simplifying debugging
and automated testing. For state­only scenarios a convenient class in the package ­
Cubit ­ removes the event layer while preserving BLoC’s reactive behaviour. Both blocs
and cubits can be freely nested or composed to model complex feature graphs.
The drawback of bloc is that it’s verbosity and complexity. Each feature typically de­
mands separate event and state classes plus a Bloc/Cubit implementation, and wiring
these pieces together and keeping stream subscriptions, disposals, and async error han­
dling correct can be difficult. It has non­trivial boilerplate and a steeper learning curve
than either Provider or Riverpod. Consequently BLoC can be over­engineering as well
for small projects, but it is great for medium­to­large applications where teams value strict
separation of concerns, deterministic state transitions, and exhaustive unit­test coverage.
When considering those as priorities during development, the extra structure helps with
maintainability and long­term scalability. [24]
2.3.6
GetIt
GetIt is not a state­management approach in itself as others on this list, but it must be
mentioned due to the immense popularity ­ 1.88 million downloads of the pub.dev package
in the past 30 days at the date of writing ­ when pairing it with Provider, Riverpod, Cubit,
or plain ValueNotifiers to wire up reactive updates [25].
Functionally it is a service locator: a single registry where singletons, lazy singletons,
factories, or Future­backed async instances are created, cached, and disposed without
relying on BuildContext. Look­ups run in constant time making it fast, and accessing a
dependency is very convenient and easy(getIt<Repository>()), so the UI tree can stay
free of injection widgets.
GetIt only creates and holds objects. It does not emit change notifications, so another
mechanism must drive reactive UI updates. Global access can also blur dependency
boundaries—overused singletons, hidden coupling, and life­cycle bugs are common when
using it (usage of scopes and disposers can mitigate this).
GetIt shines at binding interfaces to concrete implementations, sharing heavyweight re­
sources (e.g., HTTP clients, database pools), or adding a small feature onto an existing
code­base without having to interfere with context chains. In medium­to­large apps it of­
fers a fast, context­free solution, but in very small apps it may be overkill to use it if there
is already a stand alone state management solution is in place. It has a shallow learning
curve that allows for quick adoption for developers.

2.3.7
GetX
According to its pub.dev page [26] GetX is an ”extra­light and powerful” all­in­one frame­
work for Flutter, bundling state management, navigation, and dependency lookup behind
a single, mostly context­free API. The package has roughly 681k downloads over the last
thirty days, which makes it the lowest included approach in this list, but still places it firmly
amongst popular ones (it also claims to be the most liked Flutter package on it’s page).
For state management, GetX offers two approaches.
The reactive solution revolves
around Rx<T> variables wrapped in Obx widgets, which automatically wires listeners and
triggers rebuilds. The second solution is recommended when tighter control is required.
For this GetBuilder relies on manual refresh that trades continuous reactivity for deter­
ministic updates. Both approaches rely on GetxController classes to hold view state
and business logic. These controllers expose lifecycle hooks (onInit, onClose) and
”workers” that automate common reactive patterns. Because controllers use the same
service­locator registry, any part of the app can fetch them in constant time without Build­
Context. This same singleton store backs the built­in router, letting code push or replace
routes with a single Get.to() call. This results in fast prototyping with minimal boilerplate,
targeted low­resource rebuilds, and shared global access to state.
Drawbacks are similar to other global locators as this concept lives in the core of GetX.
The shared access hides dependencies, complicates unit testing, and exposes the risk to
couple too much responsibility in the controllers, creating massive ViewModels.
Because GetX both manage routing, DI, and reactivity in its own structure, migrating or
addition of another patterns later can prove to be hard: essentially the project becomes
locked into GetX’s framework and conventions and drifts away from the clean­architecture
close guidance found in Flutter’s official recommendations. For larger projects the ab­
sence of clear layer boundaries makes it harder to maintain.
It is recommended for small­to­medium applications, solo projects, where GetX delivers
great speed and performance, but once complexity of an application grows, a more mod­
ular approach (e.g., Provider, Riverpod, or BLoC in a layered architecture) usually works
better. [27]
2.4
Code quality metrics
Software quality metrics exist to convert code and the semantics of it quantifiable. By
assigning values to otherwise abstract attributes, teams can reason about the current
state of a code­base, compare alternatives, track and measure the effect of changes in a
simple way.
The most well­known first articles about the topic are from Boehm (1976) [28] and McCall
(1977) [29], and it shows how the idea is almost as old as software development. Today
the ISO/IEC 25010:2023 standard codifies a comprehensive product­quality model for
modern ICT systems.
Most schemes in modern software quality engineering adopt a Goal/Question/Metric (GQM)
[30] discipline: begin with a concrete improvement goal, refine it into answerable ques­
tions, and then pick or design the metrics that supply those answers. A list of features
usually measured include: reliability, maintainability, efficiency, compatibility, stability, us­
ability as intrinsic product qualities, and lead time, cycle time, or deployment frequency as
development or flow indicators. When applied strategically, these numbers can surface
trends, reveal bottlenecks, and allow risks to be flagged early rather than discovered later
in production.

==PAGE:20==
Important to note that these quality metrics are not perfect: many operate as proxies for
latent qualities (e.g. complexity will not translate directly to comprehensibility) and are
highly context­sensitive. A figure that signals poor maintainability in one domain may be
perfectly acceptable in another. An often cited statement borrowed from economics is
Goodhart’s Law [31](“when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good mea­
sure”), which warns that teams can over­optimize numbers and metrics while making
the underlying reality worse. Effective metric programmes therefore treat the figures as
guides, not goals, always supported by qualitative judgement and domain knowledge.
2.4.1
Traditional code quality metrics
The following list summarizes popular traditional code quality metrics, which are applica­
ble for dart (contains unmarked references and citations from DCM metric docs [32]):
Code­level metrics:
• Cyclomatic complexity: counts how many different paths a program can take during
execution. [33]
• Halstead volume: based on the numbers of operators and operands. [34]
• Coupling and cohesion: assess the relationships between different parts of a soft­
ware system. [35]
• Code duplication: monitors duplicated code.
• LOC: Lines of Code, counts the number of lines in a software project’s source code.
• Churn: measures how often code changes (over a period of time).
• Lint rule violations: checking for overly long functions, inconsistent indentation, or
unclear variable names.
• Maintainability index: a combined score based on complexity, code length, and
comments, usually shown as a color (green/yellow/red). [36]
• Technical debt: estimates how much time is needed to repair the code compared to
how long it took to write it (based on comments, deprecations, suppressing rules,
etc.).
Testing related metrics:
• Test coverage: how much of the code is covered by unit or integration tests.
• Mutation score: the effectiveness of a test suite in detecting faults within the code.
• Flakiness rate: reflects the reliability of automated tests.
• Defect density: measures the number of defects found in a specific unit of code.
Popular mobile specific metrics
• Startup time: measures the time it takes for an app to produce its first frame.
• Jank/frame drops: refers to application frames that take too long to render.
• Memory usage: measures Random Access Memory used.
• Battery usage: measures battery consumption of the application.

2.4.2
Dart analyze
dart analyze is the language’s built­in static analyzer, driven by the Dart Analysis Server
that most IDE plug­ins use. Each compilation unit is parsed, type­checked, and inspected
against a list of diagnostics that covers the usual type errors, unreachable or dead code,
null­safety violations. Besides that it also checks unused imports, style and formatting
mismatches, API misuse, and an ever­growing set of Flutter­specific lints. The rule­set
is declarative: an analysis_options.yaml file lets developers enable, disable, or rank
individual lints so that warnings escalate to errors if needed. In the editor the server
streams findings in real time, yet the same command can run headless as the part of
Continuous Integration (CI), turning the report into a hard quality gate that must pass
before a build releases.
Because every issue is emitted with a machine­readable severity, rule­ID, and line num­
ber, pipelines can aggregate simple metrics (e.g. total findings, rule breakdowns, issue
density per KLOC) without extra tooling. These numbers are great to expose worsening
trend lines and spotlight hot files or dangerous patterns before they start to cause issues
(if acted upon).
Static analysis ­ as usual ­ has it’s limitations: some lints generate false positives or miss
project­specific issues, and enforcing a strict rule set can feel oppressive if the developer
team’s conventions diverge from those. Best practice is to curate a context­aware config­
uration, treat the output as one signal among many, and always use it in union with unit
tests, code reviews, and runtime telemetry. If used properly, dart analyze delivers fast
and cost­free feedback while it also fits into a broader quality­assurance process.
2.4.3
DCM ­ Dart Code Metrics
Dart Code Metrics [37] is a third­party static­analysis tool that is built on top of the native
Dart Analysis Server, extending capabilities from lint violations to extensive code­health
analytics. Whereas dart analyze ships with a few hundred rules, DCM layers on hundreds
more (+425 according to the website) plus specialized detectors for localization drift, dead
or duplicated code, architectural­layer violations, and other smells that are hard to catch
with lint rules alone. Configuration is set up in an augmented analysis_options.yaml,
where teams can tailor and toggle rules. It is also possible to supply numeric thresholds
(e.g. cyclomatic complexity ≤5, nesting depth ≤2, public­API number ≤3) to turn abstract
quality targets into objective fail or pass gates. DCM also has several ready­made presets
to provide support for projects that want sensible defaults without investing time in the
details of the metrics.
The headline feature of the tool is reporting. Via CLI commands it is possible to generate
an interactive HTML dashboard or a PDF that charts the key metrics according to the
configuration. These rich indicators can help to decide where to invest refactoring effort. In
effect DCM bundles classic maintainability constructs (e.g complexity, coupling, cohesion)
inside a Flutter context, and displays them in a way that is easier to digest and act upon.
The open­source package was archived in mid­2023, leaving a restricted model: the basic
rule­set remained, but advanced dashboards and historical tracking requires a commer­
cial licence since then. As with any static analyzer, false positives, context­blind rec­
ommendations, and the impossibility of measuring intent mean that DCM should com­
plement, not replace, engineering judgement, code reviews, and runtime telemetry. For
developers willing to invest (or satisfied with the free possibilities), it offers a deeper view
of code quality than the built­in dart analyzer, and the visual dashboards greatly increase
understandability of the shortcomings of projects.

2.5
Software testing
Testing in general complements the above mentioned code­quality metrics. While the
metrics identify which parts of the code­base could potentially cause issues, testing pin­
points the faults of the application. Software testing is formalized and standardized by
ISO 29118 [38].
2.5.1
Automated testing in Flutter
The Flutter documentation [39] divides automated testing into the following three cate­
gories:
• Unit test: to test a single function, method or class. It has to stay focused on the
piece of logic tested, while dependencies are mocked out. It usually has to be iso­
lated completely detaching it from user inputs, rendering and data handling (reading
and writing to storage).
• Widget test: to test a single Widget. In this case the widget requires a test environ­
ment ­ an own lifcycle context ­ where it can receive and respond to user intents,
create it’s child widgets. It is much more comprehensive than a unit test with addi­
tional build­ adn runtime­cost.
• Integration test: to test either a large part of the application or the complete applica­
tion. Used to verify that the components of the system work well together, typically
isolated from the driver code running in a test­device or emulator. It is sufficient to
monitor performance of the application.
Considering the testing pyramid a well­tested app should have numerous unit and widget
tests, and few integration tests to cover all main use­cases[40], which than can translate
to the test­coverage metric introduced in the Code quality metrics section.
2.5.2
Usability tests
Considering the scope and the nature of planned changes, for usability tests the project
only considered the following 2 lightweight tests which are considered great for small­
scale studies:
System Usability Scale ­ SUS:
The System Usability Scale (SUS) is a technology­agnostic ten­item questionnaire that
asks respondents to rate statements on a five­point Likert scale. By alternating positively
and negatively worded items it reduces response bias.
It mainly focuses on perceived usability and learnability. SUS is royalty­free as long as its
exact wording remains intact, and it takes only one to three minutes to administer, making
it adoptable in quick studies.
Scoring converts raw answers to a single 0–100 index: higher is better. To give some an­
chor points, scores below roughly 51 signal severe usability trouble, while scores above
75 indicate above­average ease of use. With small samples the metric excels at forma­
tive testing, guiding iterative tweaks and with large samples it supports even cross­product
benchmarking because it is widely adopted in both industry and academia, providing ref­
erence distributions.
Researchers and practitioners alike value SUS when speed and simplicity matter: com­
paring prototypes, tracking releases, or any time a quick and credible simple usability
feedback is needed. Its limitations mirror its compactness: interpretation is highly context­
sensitive and the number alone only reveals that a problem exists, but not what the prob­
lem is. [41]

Post­Study System Usability Questionnaire ­ PSSUQ:
Originated from IBM, the Post­Study System Usability Questionnaire (PSSUQ) extends
SUS by trading compactness for diagnostic depth. Administered immediately after a task­
based session, it asks users to rate either 16­19 (based on variation, the first version
had 18 questions [42]) statements on a seven­point Likert scale (1 = strongly agree, 7 =
strongly disagree). Ratings feed three subscales plus produce an overall mean:
• System Usefulness (SU)
• Information Quality (IQ)
• Interface Quality (INT)
Lower numbers mean better perceived performance, with scores around 2–3 considered
healthy and anything consistently above 4 signaling friction (though context reliant). Be­
cause items probe efficiency, task completion, help­text clarity, and layout consistency,
PSSUQ localizes pain points that SUS can only flag generically, yet the extra granularity
costs time: completion typically runs three to five minutes and the longer list risks respon­
dent fatigue or inapplicable questions.
To sum up, PSSUQ is best reserved for formal, task­based studies where actionable
detail outweighs speed, whereas fast­cycle evaluations may prefer SUS and its single
composite score.[43]

meaning choosing a simpler approach would be more beneficial.
• GetIt: Using the service locator with SetState is the go­to approach of the legacy
application. While it could be adopted, the new features that are considered to be
added (e.g. Login system) would probably bloat the singleton­base used. Con­
sidering that these hide dependencies, for better understandability and testability a
change from this system is justified.
• GetX: Definitely an appealing solution, in general it fits the specific case well, how­
ever this would only be the case if the legacy application would have started with
this solution, but as an integration/refactoring option it seems to be way too heavy.
As the legacy application has parts reliant on external packages, it is safer not to
commit to this fully separate framework.
In conclusion, if the application had to be develope from scratch, probably GetX would
offer just the right capabilities to create it as fast as possible with a unified over­arching
framework.
If the time­frame for the project and lack of experience would not be a limiting factor,
Riverpod would be a great­future proof solution.
Due to this, the final choice for state management is Provider: it offer a simple yet pow­
erful solution for the context, with great support from the documentation and community.
Also it is worth to mention that it naturally works well with the chosen MVVM architecture
as well.

4.3
Changing state management
4.3.1
Legacy code solution
The legacy code does not utilize a clear state management approach. It manages state
with a combination of tools:
1. The UI pages are StatefulWidgets, meaning they store the state by themselves.
2. Global singleton are created to hold app­wide information.
3. The UI pages fetch the global state, which is essentially created by the previous
step, using the GetIt service locator when their state is initialized.
This approach in the end gets the required information, but the widget tree is pressured
with pulling and pushing data on demand to the shared singletons. Since the data is
cached on each page, the approach risks state duplication while the process itself hurts
performance. Since flutter uses asynchronous operations, the lack of canonical source
of truth can cause issues when concurrent pages want to load or modify the same data.
Safeguarding these options inevitably causes the caller UI elements to wait for each other
with loading (on the contrary, the UI elements could load in advance while the data han­
dling runs in the background). Figure 4.5 presents the state management approach of
the legacy version of the application.
Figure 4.5: Legacy code usual state management approach
4.3.2
Implementing provider and main entry point changes
Before adding provider as the state management solution, first the main entry point of the
application was reconstructed, as otherwise it was unclear how to position the initialization
of the providers.
In the legacy code main.dart instantiated the stateful widget MyApp. The state of the wid­
get orchestrated widget orientation, localization and async initialization of storage. This
coupled a lot of different purpose tasks into one file, so instead of this in the new version
these were split into 3 new files:
• main.dart, which ensures device orientation only and serves as the entry point for
the application (MyApp).
• app.dart, MyApp, is the pure UI root in the application.
• bootstrap.dart, orchestrates the dependency injection and the async initialization.

• languages.dart, which separated the localization related tasks in main and had
been moved into the localization folder in core layer, to provide clear separation and

This new setup helped to decrease rebuilds while debugging: main never had to be mod­

aration enabled the seamless addition of provider into the newly created bootstrap.dart,

wrapped by a MultiProvider, which created the persistence of the application.
In the new setup the Multiprovider replaced the need for the GetIt service locator fully,
allowing the removal of this package from the project. Now, the provider structure initiates
the database using Sembast, then Proxyproviders built on top control how data is written,
changed or deleted. These functionalities and the data stored gets exposed to the widget­

4.3.3
Widget conversion
The legacy code had to rely on stateful widgets almost exclusively. With the new ap­
proach, a broad state­lifting had to be implemented. The newly created stateless wid­

state. This process has been done manually, going through all UI pages to implement the
changes, separating logic from UI as a result. It is important to note, that some widgets
had been kept as Stateful, in these two cases:
• In case a widget contained controllers e.g. a user input field, or similar features that
controlled live changes on the page. This is because these changes do belong on
the UI side, and it is indeed best practice to use Stateful widgets in these cases.
• The examination flow and its steps also kept their internal state management: as
hinted in the Routing changes section 4.2.2, the whole examination flow heavily re­

that are out of control and on external package side. Instead only the beginning and

4.4.2
The login flow
The Login View Model is the provider class that exposes all login related operations to the
UI. It also coordinates the user lifecycle and manages the related CARP services. As a
provider class, it bundles the login related state­flags (e.g., whether login succeeded, con­
sent is present, and which study is active). Besides the authentication, it is also responsi­
ble for the informed consent flow, and data upload. It’s asynchronous steps are wrapped
in notifier updates, ensuring that UI components receive consistent updates about the
login state and that routing is refreshed accordingly.
On the login page the user can choose to continue as a guest, leaving the application
standalone functionality untouched as it was requested by the clinic. In case the user
does choose to log in, first the login view model tries to authenticate them, looking for
their entered username and password combination. If the user is found, the view model
fetches the available invitation tied to the user in the CARP server, meaning discovering
if the user is not only registered, but invited to this study. Either of those would fail, a
pop­up error message breaks the flow and the user stays on the login page. If both are
successful, then the login view model checks if the user agreed to the data collection (if
they filled out an informed consent). If they did previously, then they are redirected to the
home page immediately where they can start an examination and see past ones (these
are not cloud­synced, the examinations themselves are stored on the device). If the user
did not fill out an informed consent document previously, then an informed consent page
and flow is displayed, which redirects to the home page after the consent is reviewed,
accepted, and signed. Figure 4.7 summarizes the login flow.

6
Discussion
Software development does not end once an application is released. Even once every
metric is optimized, every possible feature is added, there is always room for improve­
ments, and if not, constant maintenance to keep up with ever evolving technology and
hardware. Thus several limitations had to be considered during the development process,
refining the scope as new opportunities were created with each modification. These limi­
tations are presented in this chapter, after which there is suggestions and considerations
for future work and possible outlook for the project.
6.1
Limitations
Hardware limitations: Flutter is a cross­platform solution, which is specifically advanta­
geous to deliver applications for multiple platforms at the same time, reaching more peo­
ple. This could not have been utilized, due to the lack of access to Apple devices, which
are required for both building and testing if one wants to truly develop cross­platform.
Software limitations: The popular practice among Flutter developers to utilize a lot of
packages ­ just like in the case of the Neuropathy Grading Tool ­ leads to a lot of vari­
ables that are very hard to control, in the image of depreciation. From the start, external
packages’ constraints limited the usage of new packages.
Limitations on the clinic­feedback issues: There was no verification of the German
translation, which would be nice to have before distribution to German­speaking users.
A crucial pain­point outlined both in the GitHub issues and in the clinicians’ feedback,
is that there is a part in the examination which is copyrighted and can only be used with
exact wording. However this exact wording is meant to be used in a clinical setting, where
the professional asks these questions from the patient. This means that during at­home
usage the questions should be rephrased to make sense as a self­diagnosis question ­
which is prohibited by the copyright.
Limitations of Git repository: Since the repository is owned by CARP only used by
the author during the thesis project, the author was hesitant to make major modifications
and while a version 2.0 release had been created at the end of the project, the branches
were not fully cleaned up, as this would be dependent on how the CARP team wants to
maintain the project in the future.
Limitations regarding folder structure: In the folder structure there have been some
files left, which make sense from a logical perspective, but clearly violate the uni­directional
flow. To make an example, both the bootstrap responsible for initialization and the routing
is at the core folder, however both use ­ and for them it is necessary to use ­ feature layer
components. A proper solution would have been to move these into a separate folder of
their own, but based on engineering judgement it made sense to leave it in core, as these
affect the whole application.
Navigation limitations: As it is mentioned in the Implementation chapter, the external
packages used inaccessible explicit instructions which broke navigation when the Go­
Router approach had been implemented, meaning the legacy navigation had to be kept
in some cases.
QA limitation: To determine the SUS score, 21 responses had been evaluated, however
this still is considered a small­scale study, and do not provide high confidence.

App distribution: During app distribution there were multiple participants reaching out
that the application was flagged as unsafe by the system and displayed a worrying warn­
ing message when they tried to install it, which caused them to interrupt their participation
and to reach out (from various participants from age 25­60). This is because Android
does not allow installation from external sources, unless forced. While it was added to
the instructions for further participants to the invitation, that this message is normal and
they should proceed (as it was also communicated to them when they have reached out),
many participants remained concerned: the vibrant warning of the system discouraged
participants, and they perceived it ’dangerous’ or ’illegal’ to proceed (besides communi­
cation to proceed from developers). Probably the real solution to this issue is to make the
application available on android­trusted services. (IOS testing have not been executed,
but on IOS external sources are not allowed at all, thus eliminating this issue.)
6.2
Future work
There are several improvements that could be made to make the application better in
general but were out of scope of the thesis.
GitHub and code documentation: The code­base still lacks proper helper documenta­
tion for functions and classes. Also a proper CI/CD integration would be beneficial if the
application long­term maintenance is considered.
CARP improvements: The study management (protocols, informed consent, deploy­
ments, authentication methods, consent translations) are controlled on the CARP website.
The configuration of the study and participant management had been quite cumbersome
and potentially would be a challenge for a researcher inexperienced with the system.
Continuous maintenance: After the projects dormant status for only 1.5 years, the pack­
age deprecations caused the project not to compile anymore: Flutter is evolving in a very
rapid pace and constant maintenance of the app is required to keep up with it.
Extended QA: High test­coverage is essential for quality development. While this was out
of scope for the thesis and only some examples were provided with presentation value,
ideally for all classes and methods should exist unit test, for all widgets widget­tests with
proper mocking. Also further integration tests should be implemented for optimal QA.
Metrics improvements: The code quality metrics had been only used to confirm results,
while they do point out potential issues with the code. All metrics should be brought to
satisfy software development standards, with targeted code­changes.
App distribution: To cover as many neuropathy patients as possible, the creation of an
IOS build would be necessary. This leads to the restriction on apps from external sources
on IOS, meaning distribution could only be achieved with publishing the app on the IOS
App Store. While now android distribution is possible ­ as the test scenario proved it ­
probably publishing the app to the Play Store would also contribute greatly to the ease of
distribution.

7
Conclusion
This thesis addressed the modernization of a legacy, patient­facing Flutter application
used in a clinical setting where reliable, consent­bound data collection is essential. The
project focused on:
• Changing the physical folder structure and logical architecture to VMMV with a fea­
ture centric 4 layer folder structure.
• Migrating from setState + GetIt patterns to a Provider­based state management.
• Integrating authentication, informed consent, and cloud upload so examination data
can be used for research.
• Inspect code quality metrics, performance and perceived usability of the application.
The research question ­ How to make the Flutter mHealth Neuropathy Grading Tool ap­
plication ready to collect data for research purposes? ­ had been answered via the above
outlined delivered solutions, which in the end created an application ready for data col­
lection based on measurements presented in Chapter 5.
Summary of results:
• Regarding maintainability, the refactoring reduced static issues and simplified de­
pendency structure. dart analyze decreased from 3 errors and 55 warnings to 0.
Dart Code Metrics showed good improvement on Coupling and Cohesion, but only
a slight increase in Maintainability Index. The codebase now avoids singletons and
hides dataflow behind scoped providers.
• Regarding performance, profiling on the target device showed faster average UI
frame build time during simple task completion, however while rastering caused a
bottleneck limiting these improvements in practice.
• Regarding usability, the System Usability Scale (SUS) with n = 21 participants yielded
a score of 80.357, which falls into the best category of usability when measured with
SUS.
For the collaborating clinic, the application now had been improved based on the feed­
back provided based on the first­round of usage. German translation to use for research
in Germany, authenticated sessions and explicit informed­consent capture tied to exami­
nation results data had been added. The possibility of distribution had been examined as
well during a live test.
This project intentionally prioritised restructuring and integration over comprehensive QA.
Automated tests were kept to a minimal level, and performance evaluation focused on
startup and first­screen responsiveness and minimal profiling. Chapter 6 outlines concrete
next steps that would increase confidence and external validity.
In conclusion, the thesis delivers an updated Flutter application that uses recognized Ar­
chitecture and State management patterns for increased maintainability. It performs well
on a small­scale perceived usability test while enables consent­aware data collection. The
approach and documentation provide both immediate value to the clinic and a reference
for engineers structuring similar mHealth applications.
