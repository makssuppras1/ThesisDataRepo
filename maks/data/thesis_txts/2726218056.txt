Abstract 
     Piscine orthoreovirus (PRV) is a virus that threatens salmonid aquaculture worldwide. 
The genotype 3 (PRV-3) was detected for the first time in Norway and later in Denmark. 
Earlier studies suggest that the transmission path of the virus around the Danish farms 
follows an expected pattern when egg and fish trading occur throughout the country. 
Therefore, in this study, the potential vertical transmission of PRV-3 is investigated in 
combination with the prevalence of the virus in the larval stage as well as the broodstock.  
     Blood from 60 females and 5 males brood fish are tested for PRV-3. 18/65 samples show 
some detection of PRV-3. All the Ct values are high. 5/65 samples show Ct values below 35, 
while 13/65 have Ct values fluctuating between 35 and 40. 20 samples with ovarian fluid 
from the tanks with the most positive results and 5 samples with sperm are tested. There is no 
detection of PRV-3 RNA in the gonadal fluids. No disinfection occurs in the fertilized eggs 
except formalin. Similarly, no traces of PRV-3 are found in the 86 larval samples.  
     Every sample is tested additionally for a true vertically transmitted virus, IPNV. This virus 
is found in 6/65 blood broodstock samples with high Ct values (>40). Even though there is no 
detection of IPNV in the gonadal fluids, the virus reappears in the larvae in 9/86 samples with 
high Ct values, indicating that vertical transmission is not a major route for PRV-3.  
     The prevalence of PRV-3 in the broodstock is estimated between 7.7-27.7%. The sample 
size is calculated for both the gonadal fluid and larval sampling. The former is based on a 
hypothesized estimation of 20% prevalence while the latter on a prevalence between 3-4%. 
The results from both samplings indicate that either there is no PRV-3 in those sample types 
or the prevalence of PRV-3 is below the estimated ones.

1. Introduction 
1.1. 
Importance of aquaculture   
     Aquaculture plays a significant role in food production and it is constantly growing as it is 
the main protein producer. Fish and aquatic animals are vital for food security and nutrition 
(FAO, 2020). The consumption of aquatic animals varies around the world. In 2019, it 
reached 158 million tons globally (FAO, 2021b) and in 2020 the production of aquatic 
animals that is directly for human consumption reached 157 million tons, where aquaculture 
produced 87.5 million tons (FAO, 2021b). There is an urgent need for expanding aquacultural 
production to maintain the natural fish stocks and prevent overfishing.  
 
Figure 1: Fisheries capture and aquaculture production (FAO, 2021b) 
     According to (Albrektsen et al., 2022), salmonids is a very important cultivated fish 
family as it includes species of great economical and nutritional significance, such as Atlantic 
salmon, rainbow trout, Arctic char, etc. With more than 4 million tons of production in 2020, 
the salmonid species are in the top 10 species group in the global aquaculture ranking (FAO, 
2022). The total aquaculture production of rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss) worldwide, 
is approximately 960 thousand metric tons for the year 2020 (FAO, 2021a).  
     Reliable data on the global aquaculture production of rainbow trout originated from 
Recirculating Aquaculture Systems (RAS) and flow-through systems (FTS) is limited. 
However, it is estimated that RAS systems account for a small but growing share of the 
global rainbow trout production. RAS technology allows for the cultivation of fish in a 
closed, controlled environment with minimal water usage, making it popular method due to 
its potential for sustainable and environmentally friendly production (EUMOFA, 2020). On 
the other side, it is estimated that flow-through systems account for most of the worldwide 
production (FAO, 2021a). FTS technology involves the use of water that is continuously 
flowing through the fish tanks, with the water being discharged into a nearby water source 
==PAGE:7==
7 
 
after passing through the tanks. This method is widely used, particularly in areas with 
abundant freshwater resources.  
 
Figure 2: Aquaculture production of rainbow trout in Denmark for the period 1950-2020 (FAO, 2021a). 
     Approximately one third of the worldwide rainbow trout production comes from European 
countries, with a total amount of approximately 353 thousand tons in 2020 (FAO, 2021a). 
Some countries, such as Norway and Denmark, have shown an increase in RAS-based 
production in recent years. In Denmark the total aquaculture production of rainbow trout for 
2020 was 32821 tons as can be seen in Figure 2 (FAO, 2021a). Even though RAS systems 
have increased, most of the aquaculture production of rainbow trout comes from flow-
through systems (EUMOFA, 2023). 
1.2. 
Growing rainbow trout and life cycle 
     There are approximately 275 fish farms in Denmark that are producing rainbow trout in 
freshwater (Fiskeridirektoratets Akvakulturregister, 2009). Rainbow trout started as an 
endemic species of the Pacific drainages of North America but has expanded to all the 
continents except Antarctica due to recreational angling and aquaculture purposes. The main 
producers of rainbow trout are in Europe, North America, Chile, Japan and Australia (FAO, 
2022).  
     The aquaculture production of rainbow trout is very extended due to the fact that it is a 
species that spawns easily, grows fast and can tolerate a wide range of different environments 
and temperatures. The females can produce up to 2000 eggs/kg of body weight, which have a 
large diameter (3-7 mm), compared to other species. The larger fry can be weaned on to an 
artificial diet easily. Even though most rainbow trout spawn once per year during spring, 
selective breeding has led to strains that are able to spawn several times per year.

     Rainbow trout cannot spawn in aquaculture system; thus, eggs are artificially spawned 
from fully mature brood fish. The reproduction of rainbow trout is conducted manually. The 
eggs are removed from anaesthetized females by applying pressure from the pelvic fin to the 
vent area or by air spawning (stripping). They are kept dry until the milt is extracted from the 
males. Water is added only when the milt and the eggs are mixed in order to enhance 
reproduction (water hardening). The eggs should be kept in dark conditions because the light 
is fatal for the embryos. Generally, broodstock maintenance is very expensive and requires 
much labor, so many farms choose to have the eggs delivered from disease-free broodstock 
farms. In that case, the number of broodstock needed can be back-calculated by taking into 
account the survival rate of the fish in every life stage and the fecundity of the brood fish. 
Trading of eggs requires disinfection with iodine after delivery. After hatching, the fish reach 
the yolk-sac fry stage, where feeding is not needed since it is provided by the yolk-sac. This 
stage lasts approximately 2-4 weeks until the yolk is absorbed and the fish reach the swim-up 
stage. When the fry reaches the length of 8-10 cm they are moved to grow-out facilities. 
Every farm follows different strategies at this stage. At 30-40 cm in length, they have reached 
the marketable size and are ready to be harvested. The majority of the trout is ready in 9 
months, but there are fish that might need up to 20 months to reach larger sizes (FAO, 2022).  
1.3. 
Diseases in aquaculture  
     Fish in captivity seem to develop a variety of diseases caused by different pathogens like 
bacteria, fungi, viruses or parasites. Even though those pathogens are part of the ecosystem in 
nature with limited effects on the fish, they can cause problems under stressful circumstances. 
Aquaculture of high intensity, highly crowded tanks and low water quality in fish farming 
facilities may lead to disease outbreaks. Fish in natural environments get exposed to a wide 
variety of different pathogens which helps them establish a diverse and balanced microbial 
community in the fish gut. However, this is not possible for fish in captivity. In recirculating 
==PAGE:9==
9 
 
systems, bacterial and viral infections cause the biggest issues in fish production, due to 
limited bacterial flora in the fish body (Austin, 2006). Both types of infection can be 
challenging to manage in fish farms since they are highly infectious and can cause high 
mortality (Serrano et al., 2017).  
1.3.1. Diseases of rainbow trout in Denmark 
     The main pathogens to be found in Danish rainbow trout farms are some bacteria like 
Aeromonas salmonicida subsp. salmonicida that causes furunculosis in fish and Vibrio 
anguillarum, which is the agent of vibriosis. The former is developed in both marine and 
freshwater fish farms, while the latter in marine farms. Yersinia ruckeri, is a bacterium that 
causes enteric redmouth syndrome (ERM) in rainbow trout and found in freshwater farms 
(Dalsgaard & Madsen, 2000; Pedersen et al., 2008). In addition to the main pathogens found 
in rainbow trout farms, the bacteria Flabobacterium psychrophilum, which is the cause of 
rainbow trout fry syndrome, the Renibacterium salmoninarum, an agent for bacterial kidney 
disease (BKD) and IPN virus that causes infectious pancreatic necrosis are often present in 
rainbow trout aquaculture in Denmark (Pedersen et al., 2008). This report focuses on a 
recently detected virus, called Piscine orthoreovirus (PRV), and more specifically on the 
PRV genotype 3 (see section 1.4.1).  
1.3.2. Transmission of diseases 
     Most diseases get transmitted horizontally through various means, including water, 
equipment and infected fish. However, studies have shown that some bacteria and viruses get 
transmitted vertically. Vertical transmission of a pathogen means the transfer from the 
parents to the offspring through the eggs or the milt. Depending on the type of the pathogen, 
it is located either inside the egg (true vertical or intra-ovum transmission) or outside the egg 
(egg-associated transmission) (Bovo G., 2005).  
Some of the pathogens that are transmitted both horizontally and vertically have already been 
mentioned above. Among the bacteria that get transmitted vertically, is the Renibacterium 
salmoninarum, which gets transmitted from an infected female to its eggs and can lead to 
high mortality in early life stages and reduced growth and performance in surviving fish 
(Fryer & Sanders, 2003). Regarding Flavobacterion psychrophilum, vertical transmission 
occurs through infected eggs or ovarian fluid from an infected female (Brown et al., 1997).  
     A vertically transmitted virus, IPNV, is passed from infected broodstock to the progeny, 
causing mortality that reaches levels of 90% in fry, deformities and reducing performance 
(Bovo G., 2005; Bullock et al., 2011). It is a non-enveloped, double stranded RNA virus 
(dsRNA) infecting Atlantic salmon and rainbow trout in aquaculture facilities located all 
around the world (Bootland et al., 1991). This virus is found inside the eggshell when it 
passes from the brood fish to the offspring, meaning that true vertical transmission occurs 
(Bovo et al., 2005). Among viruses that have been investigated for vertical transmission is the 
viral haemorrhagic septicaemia virus (VHSV) from the Rhabdoviridae family. Early study 
failed to detect the virus either in ova or fry after experimentally infecting ova and milt of 
rainbow trout, indicating that the possibility of vertical transmission for VHSV is rather low 
(Munro & Gregory, 2010). However, according to Eaton et al. (1991), VHSV was isolated 
from ovarian fluid and milt from wild Coho salmon, suggesting that there is potential for 
==PAGE:10==
10 
 
vertically associated transmission in some salmonids. Research on another virus from the 
same family, Infectious haematopoietic necrosis virus (IHNV), tested wild sockeye salmon 
(Oncorhynchus nerka) for potential vertical or egg-associated transmission of IHNV. No 
traces of the virus were found either in the egg or the fry originating from infected female 
broodfish (Traxler et al., 1997). Lastly, piscine myocarditis virus (PMCV), the etiological 
agent of cardiomyopathy syndrome (CMS) shows similar results to IHNV. According to 
Mikalsen et al. (2020), it seems that vertical transmission is not an important transmission 
pathway since PMCV was not detected in the progeny of infected broodstock. 
1.4. 
Piscine orthoreovirus   
     Piscine orthoreovirus (PRV) is a virus that belongs to the Reoviridae family and genus 
Orthoreovirus. It was first discovered in 2010 in farmed Atlantic salmon (Palacios et al., 
2010). PRV has a non-enveloped, double layer capsid with a 70-80 nm diameter. Its genetic 
material is double-stranded RNA and its total genome size is 23 kb divided into 10 smaller 
linear segments (Palacios et al., 2010).  
     Three genotypes of PRV have been discovered and associated with diseases. PRV-1 has 
taken the name from PRV-Ss since it is infecting Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar), causing 
heart and skeletal muscle inflammation (HSMI) (Kongtorp et al., 2004). PRV-3 was 
previously named PRV-Om because mainly infects rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss) and 
has been associated with an HSMI-like disease in rainbow trout (Vendramin et al., 2019). 
Lastly, PRV-2 causes erythrocytic inclusion body syndrome (EIBS) in Coho salmon 
(Oncorhynchus kisutch) in Japan (Takano et al., 2016). 
     Studies have found that PRV is infecting primarily the red blood cells, which remain 
nucleated after differentiation compared to the mammalian ones. The virus is using the 
cellular mechanisms of the erythrocytes to replicate (Wessel et al., 2015). They can reach 
high loads which can lead to diseases in the recirculatory system (Finstad et al., 2014). Fish 
with HSMI were found anorectic and lethargic and they showed inflammatory changes to the 
myocardium (Kongtorp et al., 2004). 
1.4.1. Piscine orthoreovirus: genotype 3 (PRV-3)  
     PRV-3 was first detected in 2013 in a flow-through rainbow trout farm in Norway (A. B. 
Olsen et al., 2015b). In December 2017, the virus was found for the first time in Denmark in 
a rainbow trout pond farm (Dansk Akvakultur, 2019), where there was a disease outbreak 
causing high mortality rates (Dhamotharan et al., 2018). After PRV-3 was found in Denmark, 
many studies were conducted and showed that the virus could successfully get transmitted 
horizontally and cause HSMI like pathology (Hauge et al., 2017). However, in Norway fish 
were found positive for PRV-3, but without showing any disease symptoms (Gjevre et al., 
2016). PRV-3 also showed a host preference for rainbow trout, since this species was more 
susceptible to that virus (Hauge et al., 2017).  
     The prevalence of the virus was examined in different rainbow trout farms around 
Denmark. The results showed that the prevalence of PRV-3 is very high in Denmark, as 
71.7% of the examined farms, were found positive for the virus. The risk of PRV-3 infection 
seems to depend on the type of production, as it was found in 100% of the tested RAS farms, 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
while 50% of the flow-through systems were found positive. Additionally, PRV-3 was 
detected in 87% of grow-out farms compared to 47% of the positive broodstock facilities 
(Sørensen et al., 2020). However, the percentage of the farms that developed associated 
disease was 26% and they were all RAS of different levels of intensity, while there are no 
flow-through farms to have developed the disease yet (Sørensen et al., 2020). It appears that 
PRV-3 primarily threats RAS farms. Data from farms in Norway showed that PRV-3 is 
present in all stages of marine production, including the hatcheries, even though they were 
not using marine water (Olsen et al., 2015).  
     When examining the way of transmission, the natural spread was excluded since the virus 
was detected in farms all around the country. Thus, it was considered that trading of live fish 
that were asymptomatic and non-disinfected eggs was the route of transmission (Sørensen et 
al., 2020). The main mode of transmission of PRV-3 is horizontal transmission through direct 
contact with infected fish or contaminated water (Hauge et al., 2017). There is no published 
data that supports a possible vertical transmission of the PRV-3. However, there were 
indications of the vertical transmission of PRV-1 because the virus was present in freshwater 
hatcheries, originating from contaminated reproductive fluids of the parents (Polinski et al., 
2020). Later studies showed a failure to detect the virus in disinfected with Buffodine 
fertilized eggs and fry coming from infected broodfish. The conclusions so far, suggest that 
the virus is not found internally in the eggs and in case of external infection of the eggs, 
studies have shown that iodine-based disinfection is efficient against PRV-1 (Polinski et al., 
2020). Similarly, research about another recently discovered salmonid virus called Piscine 
myocarditis virus (PMCV), known for causing cardiomyopathy syndrome in Atlantic salmon, 
showed that vertical is not a major route of transmission (Wiik-Nielsen et al., 2012). In 
contrast to PRV-1 and PMCV, IPNV is known to get transmitted vertically. Sperm seems to 
be responsible for transmission since the virus was detected in fry coming from contaminated 
sperm and IPNV-free females (Bovo G., 2005). These three viruses have a common structural 
characteristic; none of them is enveloped. That makes them robust and resistant, increasing 
the risk of transmission when the disinfection of pipelines and equipment is not adequate 
(Løvoll et al., 2012). 
1.5. 
Egg disinfection 
     Egg trading and transportation are a big part of fish farming which increases the risk of 
bacterial or fungal infections between the farms, through contamination. Fish eggs can be 
carriers of various pathogens that can infect the fish and potentially spread diseases to other 
fish in the same environment. For this reason, disinfection has been used for many decades to 
eliminate the spread of different pathogens and at the same time increase the hatchability 
rates of the eggs and the survival of the fish in later life stages.  
     Many different protocols have been tested through the years, using different chemicals, 
concentrations or duration of the process. Iodophors are mostly used for egg disinfection. 
Iodophor toxicity is pH related. As the pH is getting higher, the toxicity of the iodophors 
decreases. So, it is considered a safe disinfectant for eyed eggs (Amend, 1974).  
     Some studies examined how single and double exposure to iodine, which is an iodophor, 
and H2O2 works as disinfectants and whether it affects the mortality of the eggs. The results 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
showed that iodine was pH dependent, and it was less toxic as the pH got higher (Amend, 
1974). On the contrary, H2O2 was not as effective as iodophors in killing bacteria and it was 
impossible to increase its concentration due to its toxicity. When a double exposure was 
tested of these two chemicals it was found that no significant change was noted to the iodine 
efficacy. On the other hand, the effect of H2O2 was improved due to the elimination of the 
recoverable bacteria (Wagner et al., 2012a). Other chemicals have been tested too. According 
to Wagner et al. (2012b), tannic acid was examined as a potential disinfectant for rainbow 
trout eggs and even though the results showed that it is safe to use, the efficacy against 
bacterial growth was not as effective as that of iodine.  
     The disinfection protocol for the eyed eggs that is currently recommended is a povidone-
iodine of 100 mg/l concentration for 10 min (Amend, 1974). The efficacy of povidone-iodine 
was tested in Coho salmon water-hardened eggs and even though it was very effective against 
the pathogens of the egg surface, it was proved to be completely ineffective against intra-
ovum infections (Evelyn et al., 1984).  
     Another chemical compound that is widely used is formalin, which was introduced for the 
first time as an egg disinfectant by Watanabe M. (1940). Most of the time formalin refers to a 
34-38% formaldehyde solution and it is considered effective against bacterial, fungal, 
parasitical and viral infections (De Swaef et al., 2016). However, an earlier study that tested 
the efficacy of this disinfectant against IPNV, suggests that formalin does not show 
promising results in removing IPNV from the outer layer of the eggs (Elliott & Amend, 
1978).  
     As far as PRV is concerned, due to the lack of available vaccines, fish farmers base the 
disease prevention and control by applying biosecurity measures, including disinfection of 
water and equipment. Some potential prophylactic methods were tested by Wessel et al. 
(2020) in an effort to inactivate the virus. The results showed that heat treatment is not an 
efficient prophylactic measure since the virus appears to be very resistant to it. Regarding 
UV, it was found that 50 mJ/cm2 is an effective concentration to inactivate the virus, as well 
as the pH extremes (pH 3 and pH 12). Furthermore, known disinfectants like Virocid and 
Iodine were found to be effective in the inactivation of the virus, since they can break the 
transmission cycle and prevent HSMI outbreaks (Wessel et al., 2020).  
1.6. 
Aim of the thesis 
     From the above, we conclude that PRV-3 is a ubiquitous virus that causes HSMI-like 
symptoms for rainbow trout. Mortality rates in RAS facilities are 20% on average. However, 
in severe cases, PRV-3 can lead to the loss of a whole batch in the RAS systems (Vendramin 
N., 2018). In addition, high morbidity levels affect the growth and welfare of the fish which 
can severely impact the fish farms. Broodstock farms in Denmark are getting surveilled for 
the presence of different pathogens including IPNV and Renibacterium salmoninarum in 
order to be able to trade disease-free eggs. Consequently, since the PRV-3 outbreaks, it has 
been a necessity for the rainbow trout broodstock farms to be considered PRV-3 free after 
disinfection (Sørensen et al., 2020). This thesis aims to examine a potential vertical 
transmission of PRV-3 through the eggs and assess the prevalence of the virus in different 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
life stages of rainbow trout in a broodstock farm after disinfection with formalin. Lastly, 
disinfection is evaluated as a procedure to support biosecurity measures.

2. Materials and methods 
2.1. 
Sample collection 
2.1.1. Testing the parents (blood sampling) 
     In December 2022 we visited the rainbow trout broodstock farm Aquapri in Farsø, in 
North Jutland in Denmark and conducted non-lethal blood sampling in 5 male and 60 female 
adult fish. Those broodfish are kept in the RAS part of the farm. The males originated from 
the same tank, while the females came from 6 different tanks and belong to 3 different year 
classes. Each year class refers to a different stripping year accordingly. To be more specific, 
20 females were sampled from each year class. The age of the females belonging to the first, 
second- and third-year class, were 3, 4 and 5 respectively. The aim of that was to examine 
which of the year classes shows the highest numbers of viral detection, in order to get eggs 
from specific tanks later.  
     Firstly, the fish were submerged in 4% benzocaine solution in 70% ethanol for a couple of 
minutes until they got anesthetized. 2 ml syringes were used to collect blood from the caudal 
vein and heparin coated tubes for storage until transport to the lab. After sampling, the fish 
were released back into the tanks.  
The blood samples were stored in ice and transferred to DTU in Lyngby, where they were 
kept in the fridge until they were further analyzed. The samples were centrifuged at 2000g for 
10 min at a temperature of 4℃ and 25 μl of the supernatant were transferred to 2 ml 
Eppendorf tubes and diluted with 175 μl PBS and they were ready for RNA extraction 
(Figure 4).  
 
Figure 4. Sampling and preparation of blood samples for RNA extraction. Created in Biorender. 
2.1.2. Sampling gonadal fluid 
     In January 2023 during stripping but before fertilization, sperm and eggs, before rinsing, 
were sampled. More specifically, 20 tubes were filled with eggs and ovarian fluid that 
originated from random second year class females. Milt from 5 different males was also 
sampled. The samples were shipped to DTU and directly processed. 250 μl of ovarian fluid 
and 200 μl milt were collected out of each sample in Eppendorf tubes. The samples that 
contained the ovarian fluid were ready for RNA extraction, while the sperm fluid samples 
were very dense. One of them was homogenized in 300 μL of PBS with a stainless-steel bead 
and tissualized for 2 min at 25 Hz and centrifuged for 5 min (Figure 5). 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
 
Figure 5. Sampling and preparation of ovarian fluid and sperm for RNA extraction. Created in Biorender. 
2.1.3. Sampling larvae 
     After stripping there were created two egg batches. One of the groups was originated from 
negative females and positive males (group 1), while the second group was from positive 
females and positive males (group 2). The fertilized eggs were placed in long plastic 
raceways. Each raceway held 5-6 trays of eggs Figure 6. They were treated with formalin as a 
disinfectant to prevent overgrowing of Saprolegnia sp. from day 2 after fertilization until day 
35. 500ml of 23% formaldehyde was used daily per raceway. The disinfectant was dripping 
slowly in the inlet. The eggs were kept in the flow-through section of the farm whose water 
supply is independent of the one that supplies the broodstock. Eggs were incubated in the 
dark with a temperature of 8-9℃ and 6.8-7 pH.  
     The eggs from group 1 (negative female x positive male) with a total number of 900.000 
were divided into 10 trays that consisted of approximately 7.5 l or 90.000 eggs per tray. On 
day 29-30 the incubated eggs were cleaned from lumps of fungus. Dead and non-fertilized 
eggs were removed. Close to the hatching time the cleaned eggs were redistributed into more 
trays, reaching a number of approximately 65-85 trays, consisting of 1-1.5 l or 10 – 15.000 
eggs per tray. This group would provide the eggs that are about to be transported to other 
farms. Regarding group 2 (positive female x positive male), it included around 100.000 eggs 
that were divided after disinfection into 5 trays with 2 l or 20.000 eggs per tray. However, the 
number of trays increased to 6-8 when the hatching time was approaching. Each tray had the 
same number of eggs as group 1. Group 2 had significantly fewer eggs because the new male 
spawners would come from it and there was no need for bigger production. The eggs started 
hatching around seven weeks after stripping.  
     Around day 42, the eggs started hatching. As far as the sampling of the newly hatched 
larvae is concerned, two pools were made from each tray for group 2 and one pool occurred 
out of tray 1,3,5 and 6 for group 1. Each pool consisted of 5 fish. 86 tubes filled with 2 ml 
EMEM were used for the sampling and were shipped to DTU, where they were stored in the 
freezer (-20 ℃) until further analysis.   
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
 
Figure 6. Visualized example of larval sampling 
     The samples were ground in the lab and diluted with 500 μl MEM. After that, they were 
centrifuged for 15 min at a temperature of 4℃ and a speed of 4000 rpm. The supernatant was

4. Discussion 
     Rainbow trout RAS farms experience severe losses due to PRV-3. This situation affects 
egg trading. Therefore, in this study, we examined the prevalence of the virus in the early 
stages of rainbow trout when originating from PRV-3 positive broodstock, as well as the 
transmission routes of PRV-3 and whether disinfection would be an adequate measurement to 
produce PRV-3 free rainbow trout eggs.  
4.1. 
Explanation of the PCR results  
     As mentioned above, positive results are considered the samples that noted Ct values 
below 35 Ct, while samples with Ct value above 40 are considered negative. The samples 
with Ct above 35 but not higher than 40 are under suspicion about their positivity. These need 
further investigation because they might be false positive. There is a possibility that traces of 
RNA after disinfection are detected but not the actual virus. For this reason, their 
amplification curves are checked for sigmoidity (Figure 14). In case the curves appear to be 
sigmoid, the samples could be considered positive.  
     Even though it was mentioned earlier that samples with a Ct value above 40 are 
considered negative and thus discarded, the majority of the samples tested for IPNV, that 
showed some viral detection, had Ct values above 40. An earlier study examining larvae of 
brook trout (Salvelinus fontinalis) concludes that they are not reliable on IPNV diagnosis 
before reaching the first feeding stage (Bootland et al., 1991). This might affect the viral load 
that is detected in this sample type, while another study shows that leukocytes in the blood 
provide more sensitive detection of IPNV in asymptomatic adult rainbow trout (Rodriguez et 
al., 2001). Consequently, the high Ct values in the larval stage are probably the result of the 
low viral load in the blood of the broodstock. 
     Notably, if it was only one sample with that level of Ct value in our study, it could 
probably be a random incident and we would have considered it negative. However, the RNA 
of IPNV is detected in approximately 10% of the samples in two life stages of rainbow trout 
(broodstock and larvae). Multiple detections of the IPNV, as well as the fact that it is present 
in both broodstock and larvae, suggests that there is a high possibility we detected the actual 
IPNV in those samples, even though the viral load of those is very low.  
4.2. 
Prevalence of PRV-3 
     Part of this study was to estimate the prevalence of PRV-3 in different life stages of 
rainbow trout. Chronologically, we started with broodstock. After testing the blood of adult 
female and male rainbow trout, we can see in Table 3, that 5/65 have Ct below 35, while 
18/65 in total show some detection of RNA. The prevalence of the virus fluctuates from 7.7% 
to 27,7% from positive to the total number of samples with Ct. Knowing that each tank has 
approximately 150 fish, the sample size was 65 coming from tanks with a total number of 
~1000 fish. Solving Equation 1 for confidence level (p), we found that the prevalence is 
between 7.7-27.7% with a confidence level of 99.5-100%.

     From the above broodstock, we used the tanks with the most positive females (2nd year-

Equation 1 we can calculate that the confidence level is again very high (99.7-100%).  
     As far as the gonadal fluid samples are concerned, we tested the hypothesis that the 
prevalence of PRV-3 would be proportional to the one from the blood samples of the selected 
group. Choosing the lower possible prevalence (20%), we used a sample size calculator to 
estimate that we need to sample 25 gonadal fluid samples to detect a prevalence of 20% with 
a 95% level of confidence (Dunkan, 2023). However, the results showed that all the samples 
were negative. That means that either the prevalence is below 20% or that this particular 
sample type is not preferable by PRV-3. 
     For the detection of PRV-3 in the larvae population we set the prevalence very low 
between 3-4% in order to mitigate the risk of missing it (Dunkan, 2023). By using Equation 
1, we calculated the size of the sample for the larvae with a 95% level of confidence 
(Dunkan, 2023). For the estimation of 3% prevalence, we should conduct 98 pools, while for 
4% prevalence the size of the sample should be 74. 86 pools were conducted. That is why the 
prevalence is between 3-4%. The results showed that all the samples were negative. That 
practically means, that with a confidence level of 95% either the virus is not present in that 
life stage of rainbow trout or the prevalence of PRV-3 is below 3-4%.  
4.3. 
Transmission of pathogen 
     PRV-3 is known for its horizontal transmission from contaminated fish to susceptible ones 
(Hauge et al., 2017). However, the possibility of vertical transmission has been under 
investigation. In this study, the PCR results showed some positivity for PRV-3 in the blood 
samples but all the samples of the ovarian fluid and sperm as well as the larval samples were 
negative Figure 23. These findings agree with the findings of unpublished data of M. 
Polinski, K. Garver and N. Vendramin where PRV-3 could not be detected in rainbow trout 
after egg disinfection with iodine. Since iodine disinfects only the surface of the eggs, PRV-3 
is not carried in the internal of the eggs (M. Polinski, K. Garver and N. Vendramin, 
unpublished data). In this study, no disinfection occurred, so egg-associated vertical 
transmission does not seem to be a possible route for the virus to get transmitted either. 
     Another way to explain the results of this study is to compare them with the PCR data for 
the infectious pancreatic virus (IPNV). It is known that IPNV is transmitted vertically (true 
vertical transmission) as it is found inside the eggshell (Bovo et al., 2005). When our samples 
were tested for IPNV detection, some of them showed high Ct values in both blood and larval 
samples, but not in ovarian fluid and sperm. These results are expected according to the 
definition of intra-ovum transmission. Comparing the PRV-3 results with the ones of IPNV, 
it is obvious that they follow different patterns. This suggests that PRV-3 is not transmitted as 
==PAGE:30==
30 
 
IPNV. In other words, the results indicate that there is no evidence of vertical transmission of 
PRV-3 from the infected broodstock to the progeny that was all negative. Conversely, even 
though IPNV was found in relatively higher Ct values and lower prevalence than PRV-3, it is 
also found in the larval samples in contrast to PRV-3.   
     Similar studies for PRV-1 show that both viruses follow similar patterns, where the brood 
fish were positive for PRV-1, but no traces of the virus were detected in the fertilized eggs or 
the larvae. The conclusion of Wiik-Nielsen et al. (2012) suggests that vertical transmission is 
not the major route for PRV-1 to get transmitted.  
     The viral load of PRV-3 at both fish and farm level is worth considering when discussing 
the transmission of the virus. Under perfect PCR conditions, a Ct value of 32 would refer to 1 
copy of the viral RNA or 1 unit of the virus. The results from this study showed that the 
lowest Ct value for PRV-3 is 32.76 in one of the blood samples of the broodstock. As it is 
mentioned above, the blood samples are a solution of 25 μl of blood supernatant and 175 μl 
PBS. So, taking into perspective the dilution of the positive samples, as well as the proportion 
of blood in a 5 kg fish (average weight for broodstock) we could estimate the viral load of 
PRV-3 at the fish level. It is known that approximately 6.2% of the rainbow trout’s body 
mass is blood (Smith, 1966). That means that a 5 kg fish contains around 310 ml of blood. 
Based on this study, in 25 μl only one virus was detected. So, in the total blood volume, there 
are 12400 particles of this virus. This amount is relatively low and below the number of virus 
units where the fish are considered infected. According to Løvoll et al. (2010), the Ct values 
for PRV in Atlantic salmon during an HSMI outbreak peaked at 16 and 18 in samples from a 
variety of organs which corresponds to hundreds of thousands of copies. 
     Following the same principle about the perfect PCR conditions, we could estimate the 
viral load of PRV-3 at farm level. According to the Ct values of the samples in this study, 
5/65 fish had Ct value below 35, which refers to 5 copies of RNA or 5 particles of PRV-3 
found in the blood that is the target organ for PRV-3. Extending those numbers to the total 
population where we sampled from, approximately 77 PRV-3 units were detected in a 
population of 1000 rainbow trout. This is considered a very low viral load. Furthermore, 
when we tested the gonadal fluids, where there was virtually no blood, we found no traces of 
PRV-3. No traces of the virus were detected either in the progeny. Consequently, we could 
conclude that considering this level of infection, the virus does not pass to the progeny.  
4.4. 
Disinfection 
     Disinfection is a crucial procedure in aquaculture to support biosecurity. Iodine-based 
disinfectants are dominating in protocols of different countries by rinsing the eyed eggs for a 
few minutes in a specific concentration of disinfectant (Bovo et al., 2005). The disinfectants 
are designed to kill pathogens in the outer membrane of the eggs without penetration or 
damage to the eggs (Bovo et al., 2005). As mentioned above, all the eggs in this study were 
treated with formalin as a disinfectant to control saprolegnia sp. However, when a 
concentration of 0.2% of formalin was tested against another double stranded virus, IPNV, 
the results were not promising (Elliott & Amend, 1978). Based on this study and considering 
that IPNV and PRV-3 have similar physicochemical properties we consider that formalin has 
a negligible effect on these viruses. Interestingly we could not detect PRV-3 in the progeny 
==PAGE:31==
31 
 
from PRV-3 infected broodstock regardless of the lack of disinfection. Regarding this level of 
infection in the broodstock and without contamination of the eggs with blood it seems that 
the virus does not pass to the progeny. 
     As mentioned earlier, the viral load of PRV-3 in the target organ of blood (Wessel et al., 
2015) is considered relatively low. Subsequently, traces of PRV-3 were detected neither in 
the gonadal fluids, very likely because there is no contamination of the eggs with blood 
during fertilization, nor the larvae. For this reason, we believe that the transmission has been 
broken since there is no blood contamination of the eggs. We reach this conclusion, because 
after testing a true vertically transmitted virus (IPNV) and detected it in the larvae, even 
though it was found with a lower prevalence than PRV-3 in the broodstock. Assessing the 
necessity of egg disinfection, we could say that it would make no difference regarding only 
PRV-3 and this specific level of infection. 
     On the other side, there might be a risk, during fertilization, that PRV-3 is found outside of 
the eggshell. Considering blood contamination, it would make sense to disinfect the eyed 
eggs, in order to eliminate such a possibility. Disinfection of fertilized eggs with iodine is 
widely used and important among salmonid species. According to (Wessel et al., 2020) even 
5 min of 50 ppm is adequate in PRV inactivation. However, iodine is a halogen and its 
efficiency reduces when there is the presence of organic compounds, such as blood cells 
(Gottardi, 2014). This fact should not be a problem when disinfecting according to the 
standard procedures because the eggs that get disinfected are standing in water, so they are 
considered clean of organic compounds. In other words, contamination with blood is a good 
way to transfer the disease but it does not affect the efficacy of disinfection.  
     Egg disinfection should always be conducted by the farmers since it does not only remove 
PRV-3 but is designed to kill all the pathogens found on the outside of the eggs. In general, in 
Denmark, it is suggested that iodophors are used for eyed egg disinfection with a 
concentration between 50-100 ppm for 10 minutes followed by rinsing the eggs in water 
(Bovo et al., 2005).   
4.5. 
Interference of PRV-3 and IPNV 
     It is known that infections trigger an immune response. In nature, but especially in fish 
farms of high intensity and crowded tanks, co-infections are very common. Co-infections 
refer to the infection of a host by two or more harmful pathogens (Cox, 2001). Concurrent 
infection of viral pathogens has also been reported. Studies have examined the co-infection of 
rainbow trout with IPNV and Infectious hematopoietic necrosis virus (IHNV). It was shown 
that the type of infection was antagonistic since IHNV was suppressed but there was no effect 
on IPNV (Alonso et al., 2003). Co-infection of IPNV and PRV-3 has additionally been 
reported in juvenile brown trout (Salmo trutta L.) in France causing severe losses (Bigarre, 
2016). However, this combination of viruses has not been found in rainbow trout. On the 
contrary, earlier study detected both PRV-1 and PRV-3 in rainbow trout farms, suggesting 
the occurrence of a co-infection caused by these two viruses (Cartagena et al., 2018).

4.6. 
Comparison between RAS and flow through systems  
     The broodstock, where the blood samples and gonadal fluids were collected, is held in the 
RAS part of the rainbow trout farm, while the fertilized eggs are kept in trays in flow-through 
system until they reach the first feeding stage. The two systems are supplied with water by 
different sources which limits the possibilities of contamination and the spread of diseases. 
The PCR results of this study showed detection of PRV-3 in blood samples coming from the 
RAS part of the farm, but traces of the virus were not found in the larval stage of flow-
through system. Even though the sample type is not the same between the two different 
aquaculture systems, the results seem to follow the same pattern as the surveillance program 
by Sørensen et al. (2020), where PRV-3 was found in 100% of the tested RAS farms while 
only half of the flow through farms were tested positive in that virus.

Conclusion  
     In this study, blood samples from broodfish, gonadal fluids and larvae were tested for 
PRV-3. It was revealed that PRV-3 is not detected in gonadal fluids and larvae originating 
from infected broodstock. Even without disinfection against PRV-3, it seems, that with this 
level of infection, the transmission of PRV-3 from the broodstock to the progeny breaks. This 
does not imply that disinfection is not necessary. On the contrary, disinfection is crucial, 
since there are many harmful pathogens found in aquaculture facilities.  
     To back up the results in this study, all the samples were tested for IPNV, which is known 
for true vertical transmission. IPNV was found in the broodstock and the larvae but not in the 
gonadal fluids. The two viruses follow different patterns of transmission, verifying that PRV-
3 is not transmitted vertically. Regarding PRV-3, our findings are significant for all the farms 
that trade eggs since they can provide PRV-3 free eggs. 
     However, a supplementary sampling, at the first feeding larval stage would be the ultimate 
proof of whether PRV-3 is passed on to the progeny or not. In case these samples are 
negative for PRV-3, then we could conclude that the viral load is beyond the detection limit. 
In the meantime, farmers have several options for minimizing the risk of passing PRV-3 to 
the offspring, including screening of the broodstock and selection of PRV-3 free parents for 
the next generation of rainbow trout, eliminating the risk of blood contamination by 
following the standard disinfection procedures.

Appendix 1: Protocols 
1.1 RNA extraction protocol 
 
- 
For the preparation of the VXL, 100 μl  buffer VXL, 400 μl buffer ACB, 25 μl 
MagAttract Suspension G (vortexed) and 1 μl Carrier RNA (1μg/μl) are mixed for 
each sample 
- 
700 μl of the washing buffer AW1 are added in the 2nd column of the plate (W1) 
- 
700 μl of the washing buffer AW2 are added in the 3rd column of the plate (W2) 
- 
100 μl of the elusion buffer are added in the 4th column of the plate (Elu) 
- 
20 μl of Proteinase K are added in the bottom of the 1st column (Ly) 
- 
200 μl of the sample are added in the 1st column (Ly) 
- 
500 μl of VXL mix (vortexed) are added in column 1 (Ly). 
- 
The blocks with the samples and the buffers are placed in the extraction machine 
(IndiMag 48). 
- 
The program is chosen and the RNA extraction starts. 
- 
The extracted RNA is in column 4 (Elu). 100 μl are transferred to eppendorf tubes 
 
1.2 PCR protocol 
 
For the preparation of the master mix (every volume corresponds to 1 reaction/sample) 
- 
8.625 μl RNAse free water 
- 
6.250 μl 4X TaqPath  
- 
0.500 μl forward primer for internal control Ef1a 
- 
0.500 μl reverse primer for internal control Ef1a 
- 
0.125 μl Probe for internal control Ef1a 
When testing for PRV-3, the following are also added 
- 
1.750 μl forward primer for PRV-3 (L1PRV3) 
- 
1.750 μl reverse primer for PRV-3 (L1PRV3) 
- 
0.500 μl Probe for PRV-3 (L1PRV3) 
When testing for IPNV, the following are also added 
- 
1.000 μl forward primer for IPNV (SP VP2) 
- 
1.000 μl reverse primer for IPNV (SP VP2) 
- 
0.500 μl Probe for IPNV (SP VP2) 
20 μl of master mix and 5 μl of the extracted sample are added to each PCR well 
Positive and negative controls are used for both PCR and the tested virus  
After removal of the bubbles, the wells are placed in the PCR machine, the program is set and 
the procedure starts.
