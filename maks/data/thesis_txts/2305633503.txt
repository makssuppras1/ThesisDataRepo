Abstract 
 
The final goal of this thesis is to develop a system model for an offshore wind energy park from a 
structural point of view. First, the development of a system model for the support structure of a 
single wind turbine is proposed, and since the wind turbine structures are in a high degree identi-
cal in their construction, the obtained result of the support structure is further integrated in a sys-
tem model of an offshore wind energy park. 
The structural system models proposed fulfil the safety regulations imposed by the Eurocode. The 
codes are giving target reliabilities based on the buildings importance and the type of damage the 
reliability is accounted for (ultimate limit state and serviceability limit state). The failure proba-
bilities of the wind turbines support structure are calculated considering the ultimate limit state as 
defined by the Eurocode, and for the wind energy park a serviceability limit state is defined based 
on an economic criteria. 
An analysis of different foundation possibilities for offshore wind turbines was carried out, and 
monopile foundation was chosen to be applied to the system, because of its simplicity and the 
wide use in the market. The series system is applied for the wind turbine, because each of the 
structural components is critical, starting from the monopile foundation to the top of the tower, a 
failure to any of the components leads to serious damage, or possible collapse of the structure.  
The offshore climate (wind speed, wave heights and tidal current speed) was defined considering 
annual extreme events. The distributions proposed are not based on measurements from a specific 
location, but are created based on statements in codes and compared to real measurements. Like-
wise, a correlation between the wind speeds and wave heights is assumed.  
The mechanical properties of the materials used in the structural components (steel and grout) are 
described based on the probabilistic model code JCSS, as well as correlations of the material 
properties based on the production process and location in the structure. The components of the 
wind turbine are considered to have the characteristics of a typical 5 MW wind turbine.    
The reliability analysis of the systems and structural components was done using the numerical 
method Monte Carlo. The reliability analysis of the structural steel components was done based 
on the buckling limit state and plastic limit state, following the procedures in Eurocode, but with 
random variables for the mechanical properties of steel and no safety coefficients. The same prin-
ciple was followed for the reliability analysis of the grouted connection between the monopile 
and transition piece, but in this case the procedure followed is from the design code for offshore 
wind turbines in DNV. For the reliability analysis of the park system also fatigue damage is taken 
into account as well, although values are only assumed in order to create the system.  
The structural components are designed in an iterative process until the target reliability con-
ditions are fulfilled, and all the components within a wind turbine have approximately the 
same reliability (approximately 4.2 for a reference period of 1 year).

1. Introduction 
 
In this first chapter of the thesis are presented the motivation, the project description with the 
simplifications assumed and the outline of the thesis.  
1.1. 
Motivation 
 
Engineered structures have to be designed and built with respect to life-time economy, finan-
cial and health risks. In Europe the code collection Eurocode supports civil engineers with 
guidelines in order to design structures that fulfil above mention qualitative requirements. 
The Eurocode defines quantitative requirements and provides methods to quantify important 
properties of the structure. One of the most important properties of a structure is its reliabil-
ity. The failure, e.g. collapse, of a structure can have serious effects on individuals as well as 
on larger groups. This can range from a disturbance with an economic impact to the loss of 
life.  
With regards to offshore wind energy parks failures result predominantly in economic dam-
age because persons are rarely present during the operation phase of such a park.  
The Eurocode contains target reliabilities of members or components of structures. Such 
members are e.g. columns or beams which are combined to a system in order to constitute a 
structure with a certain purpose such as building, bridge or wind turbine tower. Independent 
of the function of the structure its reliability is not only governed by the reliability of its sepa-
rate constituents but also by their interaction in the structure. The system theory provides 
methods to establish mathematical descriptions of systems and their components which can 
regard logical and also mechanical interactions.

2. Literature Study  
 
The first step was to get more familiarized with the concepts that are going to be used in the 
thesis. The starting literature study consists of following 3 main topics: system models, relia-
bility and sensitivity analysis, and foundation structures for offshore wind energy turbines. 
The most relevant information gained is summarized and also some starting decisions based 
on the literature study. More emphasis is placed on the concepts that are used in the project 
and for more detailed explanations of other concepts references are pointed out.   
2.1. 
System Theory 
 
One of the first steps in the thesis was to study system reliability theory and methods, in order 
to define a system for the support structure of a wind turbine, and then to integrate the wind 
turbine system into another system of a wind park. The literature study is based mainly on 2 
papers by R. Rackwitz [18], [19] and the JCSS Probabilistic Model Code [1].  
―In general, a system is understood as a technical arrangement of clearly identifiable com-
ponents whose functioning depends on the proper functioning of all or a subset of its compo-
nents‖ [18]. The components of a system can have 2 states: either functioning or failed. This 
type of system, with two possible outcomes, is called a ―Boolean system‖. The components 
cannot have a middle functioning state, and because of this, a clear definition of failure 
should be stated. For example, in structural engineering the failure of a beam can occur when 
actual rupture happens, or when the displacement of the beam is higher than a certain value. 
In this case if either one of the two criteria is reached, the beam system has failed, however it 
is not necessary for the beam to be ruptured. In structural reliability the failure criteria of the 
component is called limit state. 
Depending on the component arrangement, two main types of systems can be distinguished: 
the parallel system and the series system [18]. The failure of a series system is reached when 
any of the system components has failed; the most common example is the chain failure in its 
weakest link, while the parallel system fails when all the components have failed.

The redundancy is the property of a system to maintain its functionality with some failed 
components. By looking at the definition of the parallel and series systems, can be said that 
the parallel system is redundant and the series system not. Redundancy of system is met in 
civil engineering and known as robustness: the ability of the building to keep its integrity 
without some of the structural elements [2].  
Another structural redundant system is the Daniels system. The Daniels system shown in 
Figure 2-2, is formed by n components and a load that is equally distributed to all the compo-
nents. In the original Daniels system the resistances of the components are independent, have 
the same distribution and are perfectly brittle [19].

One of the main characteristics of the Daniels system is redundancy. If different mechanical 
behaviour is introduced and different correlations are assumed in between the components, 
the redundancy can have a negative or positive effect on the system reliability as follows 
[19]: extra reliability can be achieved due to redundancy if the components have a ductile be-
haviour, the correlation in between the components is low and the coefficient of variation be-
tween the load and resistance is small; on the other hand, if the number of components is 
small and they have a brittle failure, the effect is negative on the system reliability [19]. The-
se are just two examples of how different properties affect the reliability of a Daniels system, 
and more detailed information can be found in [19].  
In the thesis will be used the Daniels system with its original assumptions (brittle components 
and equal redistribution of the load) in the wind park system modelling (see chapter 6.1). 
The analysis of a system is done in four steps [18]: the first would be to define the systems 
logical architecture, which involves engineering analysis and it can be the most difficult [18]; 
the second one is to reduce the system to minimal sets, for example if one set is included in 
another one to take it as one set instead of two; the third is to calculate the probabilities of 
failure and the last one is to analyse the sensitivity and importance of parameters and compo-
nents [18].

2.1.1. Component structural reliability analysis 
 
Before the probability of failure for a structural system can be calculated, the components 
need to be analysed. The component analysis is done according to the theory from JCSS [1] 
and is shortly described here. In the next chapters of the thesis more details are present, since 
a major part of the thesis consists of structural component reliability.    
The procedure in analysing structural components consists of the following five steps: 
1) “Select appropriate limit state function” [1]; 
Like in the example with the failure of the beam, the failure modes have to be defined. This 
step is the same in any type of design procedure, not only probabilistic. The limit state func-
tions for the wind turbine components are defined in chapter 5.  
2) “Specify appropriate time reference” [1]; 
The time reference for which the probability of failure is calculated is important, especially 
for loads, which have higher effects for longer return periods and if degradation of materials 
is considered, time affects the resistances as well. In this project the reference period is 1 year 
for calculating the probability of failure of the wind turbine and 20 years for the wind park.  
3) “Identify basic variables and develop appropriate probabilistic models” [1]; 
Probabilistic models have to be defined for everything that contains uncertainties: the loads, 
the resistances, the dimensions and model uncertainties accounting for other random effects 
or calculation imprecisions. All the probabilistic models used in this project are defined and 
explained in chapters 3 and 4.   
4) “Compute reliability index and failure probability” [1];  
Monte Carlo simulation is the numerical method used in this project to calculate the failure 
probabilities. The method is described in chapter 2.4. There are also many other methods to 
solve structural reliability problems, such as: ―First-Order Reliability Method‖, ―Advanced 
Second-Moment Method‖, ―Importance Sampling Method‖, ―Conditional Expectation Meth-
od‖, ―Antithetic Variates Method‖ [17]. 
5) “Perform sensitivity analysis”[1]; 
Sensitivity analysis is used to determine the influence degree of the input parameters to the 
final result [20]. This step is done not only in probabilistic modelling, and in the design of the 
wind turbine proposed, this step was performed by iterating different possible inputs (mainly 
dimensions and resistances) in order to see the influence on the result and to be able to im-
prove the design until an acceptable model was reached.

2.2. 
General information and foundation types for offshore wind turbines 
 
In the following subchapters are presented shortly the main components of a typical offshore 
wind turbine, the different foundation concepts and the justification for choosing a monopile 
foundation for the structural system of the wind turbine.    
2.2.1. Offshore wind turbine components 
 
Based on an analysis of an online database [15] containing general information on construct-
ed wind turbines around the world, typical offshore turbines projects with a capacity greater 
than 5 MW, finished after 2010, consist of the following principal components: the support 
structure, the rotor (rotates about the horizontal axis) and the nacelle. In Figure 2-3 is shown a 
typical offshore wind turbine on a monopile foundation [9].   
 
Figure 2-3 Offshore wind turbine components on monopile foundation [9] 
==PAGE:18==
System Model of an Offshore Wind Energy Park 
 
9 
 
The support structure of a wind turbine generally is formed by the foundation structure, the 
transition piece and the tower. The foundation structure, which can be a monopile, a tripod 
structure, a jacket structure and others depending on the depth of the water and the soil condi-
tions. The transition piece has the role to connect the foundation with the tower and to correct 
the vertical alignment, because during the installation of the foundation, small misalignments 
usually appear. Depending on the foundation type and the installation precision, the transition 
piece is not necessary, but this is a very rare case. For example, at Scroby Sands project the 
tower is placed directly on the monopile foundation [16]. The other main component of the 
support structure is the tower. The tower is made of cylindrical or conical steel elements 
welded or bolted together. The diameter and the plate thickness are decreasing with the 
height of the tower, and the height above the sea level of the tower plus the transition piece 
can range between 70 m and 100 m, or even more depending on the power of the turbine [9].  
Through the rotor the kinetic wind energy is transformed into mechanical power, which will 
be further transformed into electricity. The rotor consists usually of 3 blades made of light 
material: glass fibres or carbon fibres reinforced plastics [10].   
The nacelle accommodates the electrical generator and other machinery and prevents direct 
exposure of the electrical equipment to the environment, and it also transfers the loads from 
the rotor to the tower [10]. 
2.2.2. Foundation types 
 
The offshore foundations for wind turbine are governed by the water depth and the soil type, 
within a small perimeter (for example a wind park). The offshore environment (wave heights 
and wind speed) is also an important factor, but the water depth and soil type can change rad-
ically within a small area, while climate differences are strongly noticed on larger distances 
[11].   
 
Figure 2-4 Typical foundation concepts [11]

In Figure 2-4 are shown typical foundation concepts and in the following paragraphs are 
shortly described the concepts along with their use percentage reported to the commercial 
projects finished as of December 2012, based on a statistics provided by the “European Wind 
Energy Association”[11]. 
a) Gravity-based foundations (Figure 2-4.a) are usually preferred in shallow waters (up 
to 10 m) and usually if monopile foundation is not suitable because of different soil 
conditions; they represent 16% of the market up to December 2012. Disadvantages 
for this type of foundation are the large amount of concrete needed and the necessity 
of expensive equipment in order to be installed.  
 
b) Monopile foundations (Figure 2-4.b) are the most common, found at 74% of the off-
shore wind turbine projects until 2012. This foundation option is usually preferred in 
water depths up to 25 m. The diameter of a monopile is usually between 4.5 m to 5 m 
for water depths up to about 10 m at lowest astronomic tide [4]. The popularity of the 
monopile is because of: the high speed of installation; simple yet robust design by 
comparison with other foundation concepts; it is “well suited to mass-fabrication and 
the installation method, based on conventional impact driving, is robust in most soil 
conditions” [11]. On the other hand, if the depth is higher, dynamic problems can oc-
cur because it forms a very slender structure together with the tower and special atten-
tion is needed in the ultimate and serviceability limit states with the induced vibra-
tions and dynamic amplifications of the loads. Because of the previous advantages 
presented for the monopile, intensive research is on-going in order to reach higher wa-
ter depths and positive results are obtained. Successful designs are completed for a 6 
MW wind turbine for a water depth of 35 m, having a monopile with 7.5 m diameter, 
and monopiles with diameters up to 10 m are ―claimed to be feasible in water depths 
up to 60 m‖[11]. Another problem with monopiles is the noise and vibration level dur-
ing the installation that can affect the marine wild life.  
 
 
c) In Figure 2-4.c is shown a combination of monopile and suction caisson foundation. 
The suction caissons are relatively new in the offshore wind turbine industry, and in 
this combination with a monopile, are still a concept only. The caisson is installed by 
means of pressure difference between its interior and exterior and practically it drives 
its self into the soil. Compared to the monopile alone, the foundation can be more 
economic in terms of steel consumption and the noise pollution of the marine envi-
ronment is no longer a problem.  
 
d) A tripod foundation with piles is shown in Figure 2-4.d and is used in about 5% of the 
projects until 2012. Larger depths than 30 m can be reached with this solution. In Fig-
ure 2-4.e is the same principle but with suction caissons instead of piles [7].  
 
e) The last type of foundation that is fairly used in the industry is the jacket foundation 
(see Figure 2-4.f). This is the most robust structure and depths larger than 60 m can be

2.2.3. Foundation structure chosen for the turbine system 
 
In order to choose one foundation concept to be used in this thesis for the system model of 
the wind turbine, more attention was given to the monopile and jacket foundation. The reason 
of this is the simplicity in production and installation of the monopile, and the high depths 
that can be achieved with the jacket foundation. 
The monopile is fabricated by bent steel shells, welded vertically to obtain the cylindrical 
structure and the cylinders are welded together. In Figure 2-6 the welded components can be 
seen. 
Looking at the monopile as a system of components and the modes it can fail, this resembles 
the most to a series system. In a series system the failure occurs when there is a failure in any 
component (like the chain brakes in its weakest link) and by looking at the monopile, in case 
any part fails, the load cannot be redistributed resulting in failure of the whole structure. Each 
component has to be very strong in order to make a reliable system. As described in chapter 
2.1.2, the correlation between the component resistances has a big influence on the probabil-
ity of failure, higher the correlation, smaller probability of failure. Correlation of the steel

components is achieved in the production process depending on the number steel mills and 
batches within the same mill (see chapter 4.2.2) [1].    
 
Figure 2-6 7.5m diameter monopile used for a 6MW offshore wind turbine [11] 
On the other hand the jacket structure is a more redundant system, normally if there would be 
a failure in a member of the jacket, the structure should not collapse and the load can be re-
distributed to the other elements. By looking at the failure mode of the jacket structure, it re-
sembles to a modified Daniels system (Figure 2-2) with the redistribution of the load to the 
surviving members, but the redistribution is not equal and it depends on the static system.   
From the two choices described, the monopile was finally selected to continue with the sys-
tem modelling of the wind turbine in this project, due to its simplicity, the wide use in the 
market, the research that is currently done to achieve higher water depths and easier analyti-
cal design steps. The jacket structure was not chosen because a large amount of time would 
be required to obtain the load redistribution to the surviving members.

Target reliabilities 
The reliability of the structural system and the target reliability values are chosen and calcu-
lated according to JCSS [1]. The definition of the reliability index  , is given by the invers 
standardized Gaussian distribution function  , of the survival probability   . 
 
          
2.9 
 
Figure 2-7Reliability index significance on the standardized normal distribution 
The target reliability is defined as the minimum required reliability index of a structure. The 
target reliability for a structure is given by its consequence class. The consequence class is 
given by the ratio of construction costs plus the failure costs over the costs of the failure. It is 
more controversial when loss of human lives is possible and the associated cost of a human

Monte Carlo Simulation  
 
Monte Carlo simulations are numerical methods used to calculate probabilities of failure. The 
method was introduced around the year 1940 and it became more and more popular with the 
evolution of the computer [17]. For solving structural reliability problems the random param-
eters are generated and the limit state function is analysed, step repeated for a certain number 
of times, while the failures of the limit state function are counted. The probability of failure 
will be the ratio of the number of failures to the number of simulations. The advantage of this 
method is that it can be accurate when it comes to strongly nonlinear and complicated limit 
state functions. The disadvantage is that it requires a large number of simulations for small 
probabilities of failure and it can require powerful computers and longer periods of time to 
finalise the simulation.

The short-term description of wind refers to the wind speed distribution within periods of 10

distribution, but many times it deviates strongly from it. The turbulence intensity    of the 
short-term wind speed distribution is described as a ratio between its standard deviation σ and 
the mean wind speed  ̅. The turbulence intensity and the wind speed distribution strongly 
depend on the height above the ground and on the terrain type: for example in the middle of a 
city, a forest, offshore, etc. The turbulence intensity is smaller at higher altitudes above the 
ground since the wind speed does not fluctuate so much. There are smaller turbulences also in 
an offshore environment compared to a city, since there are no obstacles for the wind. This is 
also an advantage to build offshore wind turbines.

4. Components and materials  
 
In this chapter are presented the structural components and materials used for the proposed 
support structure of the wind turbine. 
4.1. 
Components 
 Monopile 
The monopile is the foundation structure of the offshore wind turbine. It has a diameter of 6.8 
m and a 41 mm shell steel thickness. The component is produced onshore by smaller steel 
cylinders which are welded horizontally and vertically together to form the entire piece (see 
Figure 2-6). The pile is transported to the site and driven into the soil. The embedded length 
of the pile depends on the soil conditions, but the geotechnical aspects are not considered in 
this thesis. The pile is seen as fixed at the mud line. In the component analysis the monopile 
is taken as one critical component because it has the same thickness over the length, and the 
critical verifications of the component are done at the base, where the maximum stresses oc-
cur.     
 Transition piece and the grouted connection 
The transition piece is modelled according to DNV [3]. The transition piece is the connection 
between the monopile and the tower. It is sleeved over the monopile and due to this the verti-
cal alignment errors that can occur in the installation of the monopile can be corrected. The 
pile and the transition piece are fixed together by a grouted connection.

times the diameter of the pile, while the shell steel thicknesses and the grout thickness on the

Materials  
 
The materials are characterised by their mechanical models (e.g. yielding model) and random 
parameters. Usually, a mathematical relationship can be established to approximate a me-
chanical model based on another known property, for example between the yielding stress 
and ultimate stress of steel. Models should be created in such a way that the mechanical de-

ble, based on the characteristic strength of the material [1]. 
The characteristic strength of the material fk is defined as the quantile p in the material distri-
bution F [1]. In most codes is stated the characteristic strength should not be the 5% quantile.

4.2.2. Correlation between steel components 
 
Material properties have a different variation in properties based on their modelling scale. In 
[1] are defined four scales of variation fluctuation: micro, meso ((local), meso, and macro 
(global). The micro scale refers to the correlation in the material at the aggregate level space, 
the local meso scale refers to one element divided into unit volumes (aggregate level) and the 
correlation is defined between the unit volumes. The meso scale refers to a set of elements 
and the macro scale is seen as a set of structures.     
Likewise, in [1] is defined the correlation for structural steel bars as follows: within one steel 
bare the correlation can be assumed 1 if the bar is no longer than 10 m. If the bars are from 
the same mill and same melt (batch), having equal diameter, the correlation can be assumed 
0.9. If the steel bars are from different melts (batches) and are used in different places in the 
structure, the correlation between them is 0.4 
Based on the previous statement the correlation for the proposed model between steel ele-
ments is assumed 0.4. This choice was done considering that all the elements come from the 
same mill, but are treated as different batches since the elements are large and have different 
shell thickness.

For the ultimate limit state design, considering the annual maximum load effects in the struc-

As described in chapter 4.1, the steel components are composed of cylindrical shell elements, 
and in Eurocode 1993-1-6 are defined the following (relevant for this project) limit states for 
a steel shell structure: the buckling limit state, the plastic limit state, and the fatigue limit 
state. Fatigue damage is not considered in this part of the project, since it is accumulated over 
longer periods of time, and in this part only the response of the structure to extreme loading is 
of interest. Based on this, the remaining 2 limit states will be defined for the steel components 
of the wind turbine. 
For the grouted connection, in the limit state the stresses are checked so they not exceed the 
compressive strength and the tension strength of the grout, as well as the fatigue damage 
(which is not considered in this part for the same reasons like the steel components). 
Because 2 limit state functions are used to describe the failure criteria for each element, in the 
system model each of these parts will be seen as a subsystem with 2 components connected in 
series: the failure will occur if either buckling, or plastic failure is present for the steel com-
ponents and for the grouted connection if either the compression or tension strength is ex-
ceeded.

6. System model for an offshore wind park  
 
The last part of the thesis consists of developing a system model for a wind energy park. A 
wind energy park consists out of several structures. The largest amount of structures are the 
wind energy turbines which are to a high degree identical in their construction. For the pro-
posed model of the wind park, failure probabilities are calculated for the serviceability limit 
state of the wind farm. In the system model of the support structure of the wind turbine the 
probabilities of failure for the ultimate limit state are calculated, and in this part new limit 
states are defined for the serviceability or the functionality of the farm. The system model of 
the support structure is integrated completely in the wind farm model, and all the calculations 
are done based on the already obtained results. 
The wind energy park proposed consists of 10 wind turbines arranged in one line, in such a 
way that wake effects are not affecting any of the turbines. In the model only the wind tur-
bines are considered, the substations are not included. The main structural differences be-
tween wind turbines within a park is at the foundation level, since the ground can have differ-
ent properties on the area of the park [11], but the supper structures are approximately identi-
cal since are mass produced. In this project the differences between ground conditions and 
water depth are simplified and considered to be the same, and all wind turbines are identical 
to the one considered until now, described in chapter 4. 
In the following pages are presented all the calculations and assumptions for reaching the 
failure probability of the wind park. First, the new limit state is presented, which is consider-
ing economic assumptions in relation to the structural integrity of the wind turbines consider-
ing 20 years of life time of the park. Since the new reference time is 20 years, the wind and 
water current distributions are changed considering this new reference time. Correlations of 
the loads between wind turbines are defined based on the distance between them, and correla-
tions between the resistances of the components in between wind turbines are defined as well. 
Basic facts about fatigue damage to offshore wind turbines in general are presented, and fa-
tigue damage is assumed. Finally, the results and the influence they have in the initial design 
of the wind turbine are presented.

6.4. 
Fatigue 
6.4.1. General aspects regarding fatigue  
 
For wind turbines in general and especially for offshore wind turbines, fatigue damage is a 
very important factor in the design process and many times the design of the structural design 
is governed by fatigue, rather than extreme events [4]. The welded connections and the grout-
ed connection are the elements that accumulate the most fatigue damage in time. This type of 
damage is caused by cyclic loading and unloading of the structure in time.  
For offshore wind turbines the cyclic loading is generated by 3 main loads: wind, waves and 
rotor excitations [11]. In this thesis are defined distributions of the wind speed and wave 
heights only for extreme events, while in fatigue design all the loads are contributing to the 
accumulated damage, so distributions or measurements of the wind speed and wave height 
for shorter reference periods are of interest in this case. For example, the distribution of the 
mean wind speed with a reference period of 1 hour and distributions of all the reference wave 
heights.      
Another important property of the wind turbine, which has a big influence on fatigue, is the 
natural frequency of the structure. In this only the quasi-static response to extreme loads of 
the support structure was analysed, and the dynamic amplifications of the loads were not con-
sidered. For fatigue the natural frequency of the structure is very important and it should be 
tuned in such a way that there are no big amplifications of the loads due to dynamics. For ex-
ample, the maximum base bending moment is not necessarily bigger for higher waves. Like-
wise, for smaller haves if the wave period coincides with the period of the structure the dy-
namic amplification factor is high, and the maximum moment does not come from the quasi-
static loading on the structure, but from the top mass (nacelle plus rotor) excitations [4].

6.4.2. Assumptions for fatigue damage 
 
Fatigue damage analysis is not done in this project. However, for a better modelling of the 
wind park system fatigue damage is assumed. The failure of a structure due to fatigue damage 
is not sudden, and is caused by cracks in the welds which in time, due to the cyclic loading, 
are getting larger and weaken the cross section [1]. Most likely the collapse of the structure 
will occur during an extreme event as well (e.g. storm or high wind), but because the cross 
section is weaker, the load does not have to be very high.   
Based on this assumption a log-normal distributed coefficient with mean = 1 and CoV = 0.3 
is introduced. Based on the value of the random coefficient, the steel shell thickness is re-
duced. The shell thickness of the steel is reduced in the following way: the thickness reduc-
tion applies if the coefficient is larger than 1 and it goes from 1 (0% reduction), linearly until 
it reaches 0 (100% reduction of the thickness) if the value of the random coefficient is 4.

Wind park system results

the model uncertainty coefficients, due to the identical design. In one full iteration, all the 
wind turbines are taken one by one and the results for each turbine is stored (failed or not 
failed). In the end, by looking at the limit state function, it can be observed that if two tur-
bines or more have failed during one iteration, the park has failed.    
The Monte Carlo simulation for the wind park contains 105 iterations (see chapter 2.4), and 
the following results are obtained: the failure probability of the wind park considering 20 
years reference period is: 0.0014 with the corresponding reliability index 2.98, close to the 
imposed target reliability of 2.9.

1 year reference by dividing the total number of failures for a given wind turbine to the total 
number of iterations times 20 (to get one year reference), and obtaining a reliability index of 
3.98. The new probability of failure is larger compared in the design of a single wind turbine 
due to the fatigue assumption, which reduces the reliability.

7. Conclusion  
In conclusion, it can be said that a system model was created for the support structure of a 5 
MW offshore wind turbine. The monopile foundation type was chosen (through the literature 
study) to be used because of its simplicity and the wide use in the market. The annual target 
reliability of 3.7 is established based on JCSS [1] for the ultimate limit state structural relia-
bility analysis of the wind turbine. The offshore climate (wind speed, wave heights and tidal 
current speed) was defined considering annual extreme events. The extreme distributions pro-
posed are not based on measurements from a specific location, but are created based on state-
ments in codes and compared to real measurements. Also a correlation between the wind speeds 
and wave heights is assumed.  
The components for the support structure of the wind turbine are chosen based on popular 
solutions used in industry: cylindrical or conical steel shells, and grouted connection between 
the monopile and transition piece.  
The probabilistic modelling of the mechanical properties of the structural steel ―S350‖ and 
grout ―Ducorit S5‖ are defined based on JCSS [1]. The structural reliability analysis of the 
steel components considers the buckling and plastic limit state according to Eurocode [31]. 
The series system is applied to the support structure, and the obtained probability of failure is 
1.8 10-5 with the respective reliability index of 4.13, higher than the target reliability defined. 
The structural reliability problem was solved using the numerical method Monte Carlo, due 
to the ability of the method to analyse strongly nonlinear limit state functions, and easily ac-
counts for correlation between the components.  
The wind turbine’s support structure system model was further integrated into a system for a 
wind energy park. A serviceability limit state function of economic nature was defined for the 
failure criteria of the wind energy park with a period of 20 years. Correlations of the loads 
between different structures are defined based on the distance between them, and correlations 
of the mechanical properties of steel are defined based on the production process and the 
component location in the structure.  
The system model of the park is created as a Daniels system and the obtained probability of 
failure for the economic serviceability limit state assumed is 0.0014 and the respective relia-
bility index of 2.98, higher compared to the target reliability imposed 2.9, for a reference pe-
riod of 20 years. Monte Carlo simulation was used as well, for calculating the failure proba-
bilities of the wind energy park.  
Regarding future work on the project a main topic can be a more detailed analysis of the fa-
tigue limit state, and to obtain for the structural response to extreme events a reduction coef-
ficient considering fatigue damage. More research can be done also on the dynamics of the 
wind turbine, and serviceability limit states regarding vibrations in the structure produced by 
the offshore environment and rotor excitations. Also a system model of a wind energy park 
can be created, but considering different foundation structures in-between wind turbines.
