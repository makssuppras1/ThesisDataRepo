Project description:  
This thesis aims to develop a research framework based on literature surrounding bicycle parking 
infrastructure, behaviours of cyclists, and behavioural theories. The framework will provide a lens for 
which behaviour can be observed, and data collected. With this observed behaviour and collected 
data, an intervention will be developed in the form of a product. The product will be developed for a 
future context which will also be designed within the thesis.

ABSTRACT 
Copenhagen has a problem with bicycle parking practices. The infrastructure is often poorly used, if 
at all. Copenhageners have consistently ranked bicycle parking infrastructure with the lowest 
satisfaction against other metrics in a cycling related survey, such as the state of cycle lanes and effect 
on urban culture. The City of Copenhagen wants to increase this satisfaction to 70% by 2025. Their 
current strategy is to increase the quantity of bicycle stands in and around the city. Though, even with 
a huge increase in bicycle parking spaces in the past few years, bicycles are still parked haphazardly 
throughout the city. Habits and products which enable fly-parking also play their part in the reason 
for this. 
Using a research framework built from relevant behavioural theories, an investigation into the current 
behaviours associated with bicycle parking in Copenhagen will be undertaken, with interviews and a 
questionnaire to support it. This thesis illuminates various problems related to the current 
infrastructure. Copenhageners often poorly use the infrastructure, or lock their bicycles to urban 
furniture, when they want to be able to securely park their bicycles. Moreover, the current 
infrastructure acts as a form of centralised parking. Though this style is required at busy stations, 
shopping centres and supermarkets, this style breaks down when considering the narrow commercial 
streets. There is a clear lack of infrastructure which enables safe and secure bicycle parking in these 
areas. 
Using the Vision in Product Design methodology, this study presents a future context which is designed 
in alignment with the literature and future plans by The City of Copenhagen. Understanding individual 
and social theories of behaviour, and behavioural change methods, a product named The Droplet was 
developed. The feature acts as a simple solution to enable a safe and secure place to park a bicycle, 
particularly in places where space is a constraint. Its main principles are to redistribute parked bicycles 
so that all parties benefit, that it requires low ability but high motivation to be used, and that it nudges 
Copenhageners to choose more socially favourable places to park. 
An experiment was designed to assess The Droplet’s success. The test presented scenarios with and 
without the feature in it, and the subjects chose where they would park. The findings from the 
experiment found that there was a 9.3% increase in socially favourable parking choices in the scenarios 
with The Droplet present. Though the experiment was a simplified abstraction of a real dynamic 
scenario, the experiment does indicate that The Droplet would be successful.

MOTIVATION FOR THE THESIS 
The author learnt to ride a bicycle at the age of three. Ever since then cycling has been a core 
component of his life for transport, leisure, exercise and adventure. Whilst deciding on a topic for his 
Master Thesis, the author sought out academic areas of interest and wanted to apply them to things 
he is passionate about.  Naturally, he wanted to find a problem space to do with bicycles and cycling. 
As an avid cyclist in Copenhagen, one of the most bicycle friendly cities in the world, the author 
realised one thing that did not fit the bicycle friendly mantra. Bicycle parking. 
The author identified two interlinked factors which presented a problem space. Firstly, as a user of 
the existing infrastructure, the design of the most common bicycle stand in Copenhagen was awkward 
and not user friendly. Secondly, people did not use the stands very well or at all. Having learnt about 
behavioural design, the author decided that he wanted to understand explore how behavioural 
methods could be used to define and understand the problem. Based on this understanding, the 
author asked himself whether a new product could be developed for a future of better bicycle parking.

1 INTRODUCTION 
Cities such as Copenhagen and Amsterdam have almost always had a strong cycling culture. However, 
the resurgence of cycling cultures in other western cities has been somewhat unanticipated. For a long 
time city planners, architects and governments have prioritised other modes of transport and types 
of commuters (Vivanco 2013). However, cycling is returning as the new ‘old thing’ and is becoming 
increasingly desirable. With this in mind, it is becoming it is becoming increasingly important to 
understand key factors influencing the cycling community (Muñoz, Monzon, and López 2016), 
especially when documents like the Green Paper on Urban Mobility (Commission of the European 
Communities 2007) are promoting cycling as a sustainable alternative to motorised transport. 
A common practice among cities with regards to mobilities is how they are carefully planned, designed 
and ‘staged’ from above. However, they are also performed, used and lived by people as they are 
acted out from below (O. B. Jensen 2013). This is particularly prevalent in Copenhagen, where the city 
has a strong and robust infrastructure in place for cyclists, and boasts itself as arguably the best cycle 
city in the world. However, Copenhageners’ practical engagement with the infrastructure creates a 
sense of something ‘more-than-designed’. This user relationship has enabled the city to develop and 
innovate with the cyclist in mind, but it also means that planners and politicians need to be aware of 
their user base (J Larsen and Funk 2017). 
Larsen and Funk argue that future studies need to pay close attention to how bicycle specific designs 
are appropriated and re-appropriated through everyday use. Bicycle parking facilities are known to 
reduce barriers to entry for cyclists and promote cycling as a useful mode of transport within a city 
(Biernat, Buchholtz, and Bartkiewicz 2018; A. Jensen 2013; Aldred and Jungnickel 2013; Pucher and 
Buehler 2008). So why, in such a pro-cycling city like Copenhagen, do they have so many problems 
regarding bicycle parking? And why does The City of Copenhagen not provide better facilities for 
bicycle parking (Jonas Larsen 2017b)? 
Satisfaction of bicycle parking infrastructure in Copenhagen is consistently at the bottom of the list 
when compared to other factors such as satisfaction with state of cycle tracks (%), satisfaction with 
impact of bicycle culture on urban life (%) and more (Københavns Kommune 2017b). Figure 1 shows 
that satisfaction has constantly stayed below 40%, but has a target of 70% for 2025 (Københavns 
Kommune 2018a). Considering that the quantity of bicycle parking spaces has seen massive growth in 
recent years, why are more than 100,000 bicycles per annum parked outside of the stands? Why are 
45% of the stands in Copenhagen left unoccupied?

1.1 SCOPE 
Given the high level of satisfaction with bicycle parking at work and at home, this study will exclude 
them from its investigation. It will also exclude stations as these have been widely covered in a number 
of new constructions. For example, the brand-new bicycle parking facility at Nørreport station 
designed by COBE Architects which opened in late 2014 and provides approximately 1,900 bicycles 
parking spaces, serving the 250,000 daily commuters (Charlotte Kjær 2012). Though the same 
problems persist here, the need for high-density bicycle parking outweighs any other motive.  
Instead, this study brings the focus to the narrow streets and commercial areas of Copenhagen. Here, 
the problem is exacerbated by high-density stands being placed into small commercial streets where 
bicycles are commonly incorrectly or inefficiently locked, one of the focus areas of the Bicycle Parking 
Priority Plan (Jakob Schiøtt Stenbæk Madsen 2018). 
Copenhagen has many places to park bicycles yet so many left empty, and with such poor satisfaction 
of bicycle parking infrastructure. There is a clear link between how, where and why Copenhageners 
park their bicycles and the satisfaction of the infrastructure, with special emphasis on how. Jonas 
Larsen, a professor at Roskilde University, who has been prolific in literature and research about 
cycling in Copenhagen, states: 
“A circumscribed focus on infrastructure that pays no heed to how cycling is practiced 
will struggle to understand how new designs and policies may fail to attract new 
cyclists or avoid defection (Jonas Larsen 2017a).” 
Based on this, this thesis aims to take a holistic view of how bicycle parking is practiced in Copenhagen, 
in an effort to design a new product which could help to improve satisfaction of bicycle parking 
infrastructure. There will be a special focus on the narrow streets by the shops, which is highlighted 
as the most problematic area (Københavns Kommune 2017c).

1.2 AIMS 
To understand why satisfaction of bicycle parking infrastructure is so low and why Copenhageners do 
not use the infrastructure. By having this understanding, can a product improve the situation of bicycle 
parking in Copenhagen? This splits the aim into two parts: 
1. Understand the behaviours associated with bicycle parking in Copenhagen. 
2. Develop a product to influence and improve bicycle parking behaviour. 
The sub-sections for the first aim are: 
1.1. Build a framework to observe and understand behaviours on both an individual and social 
level. 
1.2. Model the behaviours of Copenhageners to understand how, why and what they need. 
1.3. Map the observed behaviour into an understandable graphic. 
The sub-sections for the second aim are: 
2.1. Create a design vision for a future context based on the observed behaviour. 
2.2. Develop a product for the future context. 
2.3. Validate the product. 
1.3 READING GUIDE 
The thesis has been written to be read sequentially from Section 1 to Section 9. Supporting material 
can be found in the appendices in Section 11. Material found in this section will typically be 
paraphrased or consolidated in the many body of the thesis. References can also be found at the end 
of the thesis in Section 10. 
In brief the structure of the thesis is as follows: 
Section 2: Methodology – presents the methodology which the thesis follows. 
Section 3: Literature review explores the current literature surrounding bicycle parking infrastructure, 
related behaviours associated with cycling, behavioural design methods and theories. An overview of 
behavioural design methods and theories will allow for the selection of the most appropriate ones. It 
culminates in a research framework which acts as a lens for the thesis. 
Section 4: Observations and data collection – takes a snapshot of the status quo, providing a holistic 
overview of behaviour and how it links to all aspects of society, from the attitudes to the policy 
affecting it. 
Section 5: Design process – walks through the process of using the Vision in the Product Design 
methodology to define a future context. It draws from theory, principles, laws, and so on, all of which 
could factor into the future. 
Section 6: The design – describes the product developed from the previous sections, The Droplet.

3.2 BICYCLE PARKING INFRASTRUCTURE 
Scientific studies have shown this time and time again, that cycling is a healthy activity (Pucher, Dill, 
and Handy 2010).  Many studies have shown positive impacts cycling has on health, far outweighing 
the risks from traffic injuries. Moreover, increasing the number of cyclists has allowed cities across 
Europe to better manage air quality (European Commission 1999) and can have an economic impact 
(Deenihan and Caulfield 2014). For cities to truly be lively, safe, sustainable and healthy, cycling should 
be encouraged (Gehl 2010). Infrastructure, programs, and policies enable people to cycle by reducing 
barriers to entry. Among these initiatives it has been shown that improving bicycle parking 
infrastructure promotes cycling (Pucher, Dill, and Handy 2010; Pucher and Buehler 2008; Hunt and 
Abraham 2007; Rietveld and Daniel 2004; Biernat, Buchholtz, and Bartkiewicz 2018). 
Parking is the terminal component for any mobility system, whether it is planes, trains, cars or boats 
(Hannam, Sheller, and Urry 2006). If you could not park a car, a car would be useless. This holds true 
for cycling too: without being able to affirmatively park a bicycle, the bicycle would be useless. To 
make cycling a useful mode of transport to people, bicycle parking infrastructure needs to be 
accessible to everyone. It also needs to be convenient, functional and appropriately located. These 
are often taken for granted in pro-cycling cities such as Copenhagen and Amsterdam, but in countries 
trying to improve cycling such as Poland, where cycling is an occasional activity, accessibility to basic 
parking facilities is commonly noted as means of reducing barriers to cycling (Biernat, Buchholtz, and 
Bartkiewicz 2018).

3.2.1 
Current bicycle parking infrastructure 
As cycling increases in popularity and is promoted by governments, the problem of parking starts to 
appear. Storage capacity in cities for bicycles becomes an issue (Van der Spek and Scheltema 2015). 
Particularly in Copenhagen and Amsterdam, bicycles are left scattered across the city parked ‘illegally’ 
(Jonas Larsen 2017b). “The random parking of bikes in public spaces can obstruct pedestrians on 
sidewalks and is considered by some to be a visual eyesore. (…). Somewhat similar to car parking in 
the USA, there never seems to be enough bike parking” (Pucher and Buehler 2008). It goes on to 
reiterate the point made by Gehl, that management of bicycle parking infrastructure is critical to 
keeping city centres liveable. Within literature, there has been emphasis placed on improving the bike-
ability of cities. However, though bicycle parking infrastructure is a core component, it is seldom 
mentioned or examined. 
3.2.1.1 
Location 
Simply put, mobility infrastructures need to take people where they want to go, regardless of mode 
(Merriman 2016). However, most of the literature around the location of bicycle parking infrastructure 
focuses on train stations, shopping centres, the workplace and home (Martens 2007; Pucher and 
Buehler 2008). This focus on specific areas negates the cyclists ability to cycle almost anywhere, 
anytime (Litman et al. 2009; Aldred and Jungnickel 2013). 
This is reflected in various guides for bicycle parking infrastructure planning. The Association of 
Pedestrian and Bicycle Professionals distinguishes between short-term and long-term parking, stating 
that short-term parking should be visible or close to the entrance it serves (Broom 2015). The Danish 
Cycle Federation furthers this, stating that there are key aspects to the location of the facility such as 
visibility, access in relation to infrastructure and distance to the destination (Celis and Bølling-
Ladegaard 2008). It also adds the aspect of ‘en route to the final destination’, which allows for cyclists 
lock their bicycles in natural places to park on the way to their destination. The purpose of these 
aspects is to allow the continual flow of the cyclist. 
Most importantly, if bicycle parking facilities are poorly located, they are not used (Celis and Bølling-
Ladegaard 2008). This results in wasted resources and can produce a false positive with regards to 
improving bicycle parking infrastructure in a city. Facilities should be visible to cyclists to try to make 
them aware of where bicycles can be parked. Poor location can also be with regards to conflicting with 
pedestrian and automobile areas. 
3.2.1.2 
Quantity 
Making sure there enough spaces for people to use is massively important. Guidelines are vague in 
how much bicycle parking infrastructure is needed for an area. Some places set requirements in 
coherence with zoning, setting a certain quantity of stands per percentage of automobile parking 
spaces, or based on land use (Montgomery County Planning Department 2016). In New York, new or 
renovated buildings are required to install one bicycle parking space per 7,000 – 10,000 square feet 
(Martens 2007). Other guides vaguely state that a desired quantity of places should be decided before 
a new building is built (Celis and Bølling-Ladegaard 2008; Andersen et al. 2012).  
When considering commercial streets, Andersen et al. suggests that to calculate how many spaces are 
required, the number of bicycles parked on a street at a given time should be calculated. This makes 
the case for street specific design plans. Whereas others suggest that a standard should be used, such 
as one to two spaces in the immediate vicinity of the shop (Celis and Bølling-Ladegaard 2008). On top 
of this, there should be 25% to 50% extra stands with the possibility to expand (Andersen et al. 2012; 
Cambridge Cycling Campaign 2008), so that as bicycle population grows there is still room for 
expansion.

Stands that are good for all applications are the Inverted U and the Post & Ring. These allow the bicycle

3.2.1.4 
Policy from around the world 
Around the world bicycle parking takes on various forms, each with their unique twist on promoting 
cycling. New York City has massively expanded its bicycle parking infrastructure over the past two 
decades. However, as with most cities around the world, New York City’s supply of bicycle parking 
infrastructure does not match the demand (Pucher et al. 2010). However, the governing body NYC 
DOT has been continually running many projects to improve bicycle parking. Such projects take the 
form of converting car parking spaces, installing covered parking facilities and having a new bicycle 
stand named the HOOP stand – designed by two Danish designers (Pucher et al. 2010; Pucher, Buehler, 
and Seinen 2011; Andersen et al. 2012). 
Compared to university campuses across1,2 the United States, New York City has few laws regarding 
illegal bicycle parking. The only policy is a financial cost which is incurred from locking a bicycle to a 
tree or stair railing at a Subway3. Policies regarding illegal bicycle parking can be found around the 
world. These policies are most extreme in East Asia, with bicycles being confiscated if found illegally 
parked. However, there is no literature stating the impact of these policies, only that the problem 
persists (Fujii 2005). 
One area where the problem of illegally parked bicycles has been highlighted and concentrated is 
within bicycle sharing programs. Dockless bicycles are being abandoned or parked in socially 
unfavourable places that pose a danger to others (Lazo 2017). However, unlike governmental policies 
bicycle-shares corporate policies can police people to park bicycles properly. For example, Donkey 
Republic bicycles can only be left in designated bicycle stands (Winslow and Mont 2019), otherwise 
they are unable to be locked. This has led to their bicycles consistently being parked properly. 
However, few cities have been able to emulate the success Donkey Republic through policy. 
Overall the role of policy within existing literature is underdeveloped and overlooked. Though some 
authors such as Larsen, Pucher and Buehler do allude to the importance of policy, there was no 
literature on the effects or outcomes of policies regarding where bicycles can and cannot be parked. 
3.2.2 
Conclusive points of Section 3.2 
Bicycle parking infrastructure is important for mobilising people and making cities liveable on a human 
scale. As with car parking spaces for cars, bicycle parking infrastructure enhances the bicycle as a 
useful mode of transport. When deciding on and designing bicycle parking infrastructure, there are 
four key components which should be considered carefully: 
1. Location 
Location of bicycle stands is an important factor, particularly regarding if they will be used or not. 
Simply put, if they are poorly located they will not be used. They should be located in a way that 
reflects the function and freedom that the bicycle enables. 
2. Quantity

Similar to location, the quantity of bicycle stands needed to service an area or population is very 
important. Though there are no concrete studies to calculate how many spaces would be needed 
for a street, there are vague estimates and safety factors to guide the decision-making process. 
3. Type 
There are many different types of stands with different benefits and downsides. The best stands 
are those that allow the user to secure their bicycle to the stand, can accommodate a variety of 
bicycles, does not stress the wheels and is intuitive to use. Typically, this is the Inverted U stand 
or the Post & Ring, which provide resting points and locking points to be used as the cyclist seems 
fit. 
4. Policy 
As with laws regarding car parking, laws and policy can also dictate bicycle parking. However, 
these range from extreme policies such as mass confiscations of bicycles, to nothing at all. In 
general, policies find little success unless continually enforced. Corporate policies for bicycle-
shares have found success in forcing people to park in favourable places. 
Though there is literature, reports, articles and more covering each point, there is little to no literature 
linking the four components in relation to bad bicycle parking behaviours, or even good bicycle 
parking. This highlights a gap where research identifies and acknowledges the problem, but does little 
to explain it in meaningful terms. In short, the conversation about bicycle infrastructure is largely 
around urban design and cycle lanes, whereas parking is rarely addressed (Jonas Larsen 2017b). 
Human behaviour is seldom mentioned or considered in these studies, which seems peculiar as the 
four components all influence human behaviour and links the points together. 
Approaching the problem of bicycle parking infrastructure with a lens on human behaviour will offer 
new insights as to how and why problems occur. Putting the cyclist first this thesis will take a bottom-
up perspective, rather than the traditional top-down approach. 
3.3 BICYCLE PARKING INFRASTRUCTURE IN COPENHAGEN 
Copenhagen is considered one of the most cycle friendly cities in the world (Pucher and Buehler 2008). 
It has more bicycle commuters and kilometres of cycling lanes (relative to population) than any other 
major city (Pucher and Buehler 2012). Copenhagen has long since been developing and continually 
reproducing mobility infrastructures for cyclists, attracting more and more people to cycle. Planners 
and policy makers have made it their mission to make cycling an easier and realistic alternative to 
motorised vehicles. One downside to the successful acceptance of cycling, is that it has led to a culture 
of short cuts and gentle law breaking such as fly-parking (Jonas Larsen 2017a). This creates an 
environment where pedestrians paths can be obstructed, cluttered with bicycles and seen as a visual 
eyesore (Pucher and Buehler 2008). 
3.3.1 
Current situation 
The facts and figures paint a conflicting image of bicycle parking infrastructure in Copenhagen. Whilst 
on the one hand there are approximately 180,000 public bicycle parking spaces, little under 100,000 
are actually used – with just over 100,000 bicycles being parked outside of the stands (Københavns 
Kommune 2018a). Coupled with a general satisfaction of 37%, the situation is dismal. 
For decades Copenhagen has utilised a Grid stand, manufactured by VEKSØ, for nearly all its bicycle 
parking facilities. This has been due to a matter of preference and practice, with the Head of Bike 
==PAGE:24==

Planning saying that it was a ‘question of routine’ and the head architect endorsing the design 
aesthetics (Jonas Larsen 2017b).

In 2014 the municipality found that approximately one third of bicycles were fly-parked in public – 
being parked outside of an formal space (Bentsen 2014). Four years later with a constant expansion 
of facilities the problem persists. Clearly something is wrong with the current situation of bicycle 
parking infrastructure. 
3.3.1.1 
Fly-parking 
The quick and easy attitude towards cycling is very common in Copenhagen. Cycling is seen as a mode 
of transport which is popularised by its means of reducing travel time and hassle. This time reduction 
concern is highlighted with regards to parking. As previously mentioned many bicycles are fly-parked, 
one of the main reasons for this phenomenon is the flexibility it gives the cyclist (Jonas Larsen 2017a). 
Fly-parking is the easiest and most individualistic means of parking, as it can be done anywhere, in 
particularly with the O-lock which dominates the Danish bicycle lock market (Jonas Larsen 2017b).

Bicycle theft is a major problem in Copenhagen, and has been highlighted as barrier to entry for cyclists

3.3.1.2 
Future planning 
With constant low satisfaction of bicycle parking infrastructure over the past decade (Københavns 
Kommune 2017a), in 2017 the government decided to make a plan to promote cycling in Copenhagen 
(Københavns Kommune 2017c). One of the initiatives is a bicycle parking priority plan which identifies 
three urban types or focus areas. These areas range from large stations down to local streets. Though 
it is a comprehensive look at the city, it has the traditional outlook of being staged from above (Jonas 
Larsen 2017b; O. B. Jensen and Lanng 2017). There is little focus on how the infrastructure is acted 
out from below (used by the people), and few funds going towards the development of new bicycle 
stands. It is likely that the VEKSØ bicycle stands will be used, which could lead to the same problems 
previously presented. It is striking that Copenhagen, for all it’s fantastic bicycle infrastructure, does 
not provide better stands for its cyclists (Jonas Larsen 2017b). 
3.3.2 
General behaviour of cyclists 
In Copenhagen bicycles are considered a normal part of daily life. The activity as a mode of transport 
has long since been popular, with networks of cycle lanes dating back to the 1940s (Gössling 2013; A. 
Jensen 2013). Though there have been bouts of popularity, it is currently in fashion with increased 
==PAGE:26==
14 
 
climate concerns (J Larsen and Funk 2017). The general behaviours of cyclists in Copenhagen are linked 
to the attitude towards cycling and what it means for the cyclist. Flexibility and complete freedom of 
mobility means people do not think about cycling. It is a passive activity which happens without 
thought, and for the majority of people, out of habit and routine. Even the head of the Copenhagen 
Cycle Department likens cycling to ‘brushing your teeth’ (Jonas Larsen 2014, 2018). 
This is highlighted in things like what people wear when they cycle and how they partake in “gentle 
law breaking”. Cyclists in Copenhagen are often clad in casual wear or even suits, as their commute is 
relaxed and not strenuous (J Larsen and Funk 2017; Jonas Larsen 2017a). This is perpetuated in how 
cycling is viewed as generally faster than other modes of transport in the city. It has created a culture 
of short-cuts where cyclists utilise the bicycle as the fastest mode of transport, regardless of place or 
situation (J Larsen and Funk 2017; Jantzen 2014; Andersen et al. 2012). 
The problems arise here as there is a disconnect between how these infrastructures are designed and 
how they facilitate practices. Infrastructures and practices are typically staged ‘from above’, yet when 
people interact with these infrastructures, the cyclist will find practical ways to utilise it which suits 
them (J Larsen and Funk 2017). 
3.3.2.1 
Cyclists parking behaviour in Copenhagen 
Bicycle parking is heavily influenced by the speed aspect, and the notion that the practice is so 
subconscious (Gössling 2013; J Larsen and Funk 2017; A. Jensen 2013). The routine has occurred for 
so long and so regularly that it is entrenched into the cyclist’s way of life. The result of this is 
widespread “illegal” parking, with bicycles being parked wherever they please (Van der Spek and 
Scheltema 2015). It is in this area of “illegal” parking which is particularly blurred in the literature. It is 
promoted that it is easy to find a space to park your bicycle in Copenhagen, yet when it is cluttered 
and obstructing it is deemed illegal. There is no clear line which clarifies when parking your bicycle 
anywhere becomes “illegal”. Some cyclists stated that they thought it was legal to fly-park their bicycle 
anywhere (Jonas Larsen 2017a).

3.3.2.2 
Locking bicycles to something 
In most cities it is common practice to lock your bicycle to something so that it is more difficult to be 
stolen. But in Copenhagen this style of locking is only practiced among a few cyclists – typically with 
more expensive bicycles. A part of this reason is because the O-lock, which is so common, does not by 
itself permit the cyclist to secure their bicycle to something. Moreover, the bicycle stands which are 
the standard for Copenhagen make it hard for cyclists to lock their bicycle to the stand, as it only 
supports the front wheel. Even by the central station, a place known for bicycle theft, the majority of 
bicycles are not secured to something. They are simply locked with an O-lock or chain to themselves 
(Jonas Larsen 2017b). A way to explain this is that Copenhageners typically do not have expensive 
bicycles, and that insurance companies easily pay out for stolen bicycles. So there is no monetary 
incentive to secure a bicycle to something (Jonas Larsen and Christensen 2015; Schiøtt et al. 2013). 
Those that do cycle expensive bicycles such as cargo, racing, vintage or electric bicycles must lock it to 
something. Larsen found that Copenhageners with such bicycles always search for urban furniture to 
securely park their bicycles, sometimes in socially unfavourable places. Under the current 
infrastructure this group of cyclists, which is increasing in size and popularity, are completely excluded 
and would applaud a stand which caters for secure parking (Jonas Larsen 2017b). 
3.3.2.3 
Habits 
The bicycle parking priority plan identifies that habits are a factor in bicycle parking behaviour, but 
does little to give insight into how and why. The insights are limited and superficial, stating that 
different bicycles require different parking needs, there is a distinction between short-term and long-
term parking – with short-term having a focus on distance to destination, and that clearly marking 
pedestrian areas and bicycle parking areas helps nudge cyclists to park better (Københavns Kommune 
2018b). The city will promote the use of the bicycle stands through their staged from above approach, 
but the reality of their use is very different. The bicycle stands in Copenhagen are continually 
undermined due to the O-lock and kickstand. The habit being that cyclists can park their bicycle 
anywhere, and not lock it to anything. Taking the view of the government and urban planners who 
design the city, bicycle stands are designed to create an orderly space to park bicycles. But as they are 
acted out from below, Copenhageners opt for the fly-parking method (Jonas Larsen 2017a).

Conclusive points of Section 3.3 
Copenhagen has acknowledged that it has a problem with bicycle parking infrastructure and has since 
created a plan to do something about it. However, due to a matter of routine, it is likely that the same 
bicycle stands manufactured by VEKSØ. Seeing as there has already been a huge increase in bicycle

The main reason the bicycle parking infrastructure in Copenhagen is not used is because it is 
undermined by the freedom of mobility. This attitude is enabled by the O-lock and kickstand, which 
has long since been a habit and routine for Copenhageners. The behaviours associated with this

especially for those who want to lock their bicycle to something. The current stand does not easily 
enable secure parking, and the municipality is doing very little to change that. 
3.4 BEHAVIOURAL MODELS AND THEORIES RELATING TO THE PREVIOUS CONTEXTS 
Having understood the current literature about bicycle parking infrastructure, Section 3.4 aims to 
illuminate a variety of behavioural models which could be used to understand the behaviour of cyclists 
when parking their bicycles. A combination of the Super Cool Analysis Tool (Angel and Kristensen 
2017) and The Intervention Design Tool (Nielsen and Aakerlund 2016) were used as a library for 
behavioural models and theories. Having read their reviews, individual and social models of behaviour 
were selected, researched, and presented in Section 3.4.1 and 3.4.2 respectively. Further research 
was also conducted to bring in models that were not presented in the two theses. The reason for 
exploring different models was to evaluate which would best serve as a lens into the behaviour of 
Copenhageners parking their bicycles. 
As there are limited cases of behavioural design in practice (Cash, Hartlev, and Durazo 2017), there is 
no precedent to follow. This is one of the main motivations behind the exploration of behavioural 
models, as an overview and evaluation will allow for the best models to be selected. A part of the 
reason for there being few case studies to follow is because behavioural design is a relatively young 
field of study. Thus, this thesis will act as a case study for behavioural design as it will go through the 
entire process, from selection of models to validating the final product. 
3.4.1 
Individual models of behaviour 
To gain an insight into different ways of understanding individualistic behaviour, selected models, 
theories and frameworks were explored. They were used to see what current models explain choices 
made by the individual. 
3.4.1.1 
A-B-C model of behaviour 
The antecedent-behaviour-consequence (A-B-C) model of behaviour is a framework for collecting 
data. It stipulates three categories in a sort of timeline in which a behaviour can fit into. The 
antecedent are the circumstances that precede the behaviour. This incorporates a variety of factors 
from events, actions and conditions (Pratt and Dubie 2016). Then there is the behaviour itself, and 
==PAGE:29==
17 
 
ultimately the consequence. The framework is good for recording data via observations when an in-
depth study cannot be done. It is best used to describe the narrative of negative behaviour as it is 
limited in expressing causality due to the observed events are related in a descriptive way (Eckert 
2005).  
3.4.1.2 
Dual process theory 
Dual process theory, or dual systems theory, is a means to understand the decision-making process. 
There are many different versions of these theories covering types, systems, and modes, though they 
all have the same basic concept. In general, type one process is intuitive, not requiring working 
memory and is autonomous, whereas type two process is reflective, it requires working memory, 
cognitive decoupling and mental stimulation (Evans and Stanovich 2013). Dual process theory is useful 
in providing a level of depth to actions and behaviours, presenting them as conscious or unconscious 
decisions.  
3.4.1.3 
How habits work 
How habits work is a framework, like the A-B-C model, splitting up a behaviour and a habit loop into 
three stages. Firstly, the cue or trigger – an event that starts the habit loop. Secondly, the routine – 
the behaviour itself. Finally, the reward – a consequence of the behaviour. The theory argues that by 
visualising and identifying these three parts to a habit loop, one can gain control over the habit and 
proactively try to change it (Duhigg 2012). Where it differs from the A-B-C model is with regards to 
consequence or reward. Typically, Duhigg’s model of a habit loop looks at a reward of a behaviour and 
argues that a positive reward is why the routine is reinforced. The A-B-C model simply narrates the 
timeline in a linear fashion, giving no causality to the relationships between events. 
 
3.4.1.4 
Theory of heuristics and biases 
The theory of heuristics and biases considers three heuristics in which bias may occur. Availability 
looks into memory-based judgements of frequency or probability, where a bias could occur due to 
risks that are easily remembered. Representativeness suggests that bias could occur due to judgments 
of likelihood of instances belonging to a category. Anchoring and adjustment suggest that bias could 
occur when estimates are made based on an initial value which is adjusted to get the final value. These 
three heuristics suggest a causality to specific behaviours that are seemingly random (Tversky and 
Kahneman 1974). 
3.4.1.5 
Theory of planned behaviour 
The theory of planned behaviour models behaviour as the sum of attitudes, subjective norms and 
perceived behavioural control creating intentions, which influence behaviour. The theory incorporates 
some central concepts to explain complex behaviours. Attitudes, subjective norms and perceived 
behavioural control are all linked through subjective, normative and control beliefs, but the exact 
relation is still unknown. The theory links both perceived behavioural control with the intentions to 
account for variance in behaviour. The theory is not without issues, it highlights the impact of past 
behaviour and how it may run side-by-side the behaviour and have an impact, but does little to reason 
this (Ajzen 1991). It has been more recently argued that the predictability of behaviour through this

3.4.1.6 
Theory of interpersonal behaviour 
Where the theory of planned behaviour falls short, the theory of interpersonal behaviour excels. It 
states that behaviour is the sum of intentions and habits under the influence of facilitating conditions. 
Intentions being made up of attitudes, social factors, and affect. Where the theory of planned 
behaviour is criticised for assuming people to be rational, the theory of interpersonal behaviour 
accepts that people can act under irrational motives (Triandis 1977). The theory has been built upon 
and adapted to show interlinks between the parts which make up the intentions and show how habits 
(under facilitating conditions) can bypass the rationality assumed by the theory of planned behaviour. 
Where it most differs from the theory of planned behaviour is rather than being a framework to 
predict behaviour, it focuses more on being a framework to understand behaviour (Egmond and 
Senternovem 2007).

distinguishes between intrinsic motivation (one’s internal motivation for an activity) and extrinsic 
motivation (external motivation for an activity). The theory also incorporates both conscious and

3.4.2 
Social models of behaviour 
Understanding individual behaviour finds the basis for the following section. Individuals act within a 
society or a group, so the following section explores different models, theories and frameworks which 
help explain societal effects and relationships. 
3.4.2.1 
Social cognitive theory 
Social cognitive theory explains behaviour as a trio of factors interacting with each other, cognitive or 
personal factors, environmental factors and behavioural factors. Using core theories, it explains that 
behaviour can be influenced by personal experience and also observations, through different modes 
of human agency (Bandura 2012). The core driver for motivation in this theory is self-efficacy – the 
desire to have control over one’s functionality (Bandura 1994). A central issue that arises from 
applying this theory is that behaviour change is a process not an event. An intervention may not 
instantly be effective but will start the ball rolling for long term change (Glanz and Bishop 2010). 
 
Figure 12: Social cognitive theory 
3.4.2.2 
Social cognitive theory of self-reflection 
Where the social cognitive limits motivation and behaviour to a trio of factors, the social cognitive 
theory of self-reflection goes on further to say that it is regulated by self-monitoring. This self-
regulation is categorised into self-observation, judgmental processes and self-reflection, each with 
their own further sub-divisions (Bandura 1991). What is important to note is that self-regulation is 
affected by social interactions. 
3.4.2.3 
Social ecological model 
Like the social cognitive theory, the social ecological model has the idea that behaviour is influenced 
by its social surroundings. In particular, the model states there are five circles of influence that affect 
the individual (Eddy 1981; Bronfenbrener 1979). The model acts as a framework in identifying 
relationships between different individuals, settings, and wider environmental contexts. There is also 
space for highlighting the cultural impacts on an individual or other systems (Eddy 1981). The model 
starts with the individual in their immediate environment (microsystem) and works its way up through 
interlinking systems to the political and cultural influences on the individual (macrosystem and 
chronosystem) (Bronfenbrener 1979). Like other theories there is the reciprocal nature of influence, 
where the individual has the power to influence the systems which impact them.

3.4.2.4 
Socially responsible design theory 
When considering concerns about good and bad behaviour the socially responsible design theory 
identifies two separate parts. The individual concerns, where a user or product interaction influences 
behaviour due to internal and individualistic concerns. The other is the collective concerns, which is 
when social factors influence behaviour with what is right or wrong in a social setting (Tromp, Hekkert, 
and Verbeek 2011). It has similarities with other models of behaviour considering external and 
extrinsic motivations from the environment and social settings. But also considers the individual 
concerns and states that there can be a disconnect between behaviour that is good for an individual 
and what is good for society.

then have seven intervention types on a spectrum from user to product. The different interventions

3.4.3.2 
Elaboration likelihood model 
At its core the elaboration likelihood model does four things. First, it highlights that behavioural 
change can happen through two degrees of thought. Second, it identifies two routes of thought, the 
central route and peripheral route. Third, it considers which route the persuasion should occur at. 
Fourth, it organises the many specific processes and possibilities into a set that operate at different 
levels (Petty and Cacioppo 1986; Lange, Kruglanski, and Higgins 2011, chap. 11). The two routes of 
information processing utilises the dual process theory (Evans and Stanovich 2013) in its 
understanding of information processing. 
3.4.3.3 
Fogg’s behaviour model 
Fogg’s model for persuasive design states that three simultaneous events must occur. A person must 
be sufficiently motivated, have the ability to perform a behaviour and be triggered to do so. Within 
the model it implies that motivation and ability can trade-off, showing that high motivation or low 
ability may be an overriding factor in behaviour (Fogg 2009). The model also values the importance of 
an appropriate trigger, stating that the trigger must be noticed, associated with the desired behaviour 
and happen at the right time – when the person is motivated and has the ability to perform the desired 
behaviour (Fogg 2009). 
3.4.3.4 
Nudging 
Nudging is a way of changing behaviour without using an economic incentive (Thaler and Sunstein 
2009). It utilises a part of the dual process theory by attempting to influence the subconscious process 
of thought (Evans and Stanovich 2013). Nudging can be facilitated in a wide range of designs, however 
there are ten important nudges. These can be categorised into default rules, simplification, uses social 
norms, increasing ease and convenience, disclosure, warnings, pre-commitment strategies, 
reminders, eliciting implementation intentions and informing a person of their past behaviour 
(Sunstein 2014). Again, this illustrates that behaviour change can intervene on a spectrum from user 
to product. Nudging is often used with general success in public spaces and the public sector (Rainford 
and Tinkler 2010). 
3.4.3.5 
Theory of influence 
The theory of influence states there are six shortcuts that influence human behaviour. If understood 
and applied, the theory states that it is more likely for a person to be persuaded into partaking in the 
desirable behaviour. The six shortcuts are reciprocity – people often return favours, scarcity – people 
want something more if it is rare, authority – people follow statues of power, consistency – people 
repeat things they say or do, liking – people follow statues of popularity, and consensus – people copy 
each other to figure out what to do (Cialdini 1984). 
3.4.4 
Conclusive points from Section 3.4 
Models of behaviour can be used to categorise and name elements of behaviours. They can also be 
used to link and suggest a causality to certain behaviours. Individual models largely focus on specific 
behaviours of a person, whereas social models tend to link these specific behaviours to a larger picture 
and show that society has an impact on an individual’s behaviour. One common theme across both 
individual and social models is the environment in which the behaviour occurs in as a key influencer 
in behaviour. This in combination with the power of habitual behaviour can be an overriding factor 
(Albarracin and Wyer 2000).

Though these models provide great insight into describing and understanding behaviour, integrating 
with them theories of behavioural change is important when considering how to change behaviour. 
The theories of behavioural change examined all aid the designer in contemplating different aspects 
of behaviour, but they are ambiguous as to how to implement the interventions. Due to the complex 
nature of brain processing and behaviour, designers should employ a variety of models and theories 
to predict behaviour. 
3.5 RESEARCH FRAMEWORK 
Given the context of bicycle parking in Copenhagen, there are two clear motives for design of bicycle 
parking infrastructure. They show synergy with the problems faced with the notion that bicycle 
parking infrastructure is designed and staged from above yet acted out from below. To build a 
framework which sufficiently covers these aspects a combination of individual and social models of 
behaviour will be combined. 
The two main frameworks will be the theory of interpersonal behaviour, for understanding how and 
why bicycle parking infrastructure is acted out from below. Whereas the social ecological model, will 
be used to understand how an individual is influenced and influences their environment and social 
behaviour. Supporting theories will be used to help explain more specific points. The A-B-C model and 
how habits work will be used in a similar way to give a timeline and narrative to the habitual nature

theory which will be used to model the behaviour of cyclists as best as possible. It should provide a

4.4 QUESTIONNAIRE SETUP 
The purpose of the questionnaire was to understand what people think they would do in a 
hypothetical bicycle parking situation. The questions related to attitudes towards bicycle parking in 
Copenhagen (Triandis 1977). The self-completion style questionnaire was sent out through various 
channels, such as Copenhagen based Facebook groups, student forums, and directly to people. 
Participants were then asked to share the questionnaire to try to spread it around as large a population 
as possible. 
The population of the survey was anyone who has cycled and subsequently parked their bicycle in 
Copenhagen. This large population was chosen as the survey was designed not to discriminate in any 
way, especially because all types people cycle in Copenhagen (Københavns Kommune 2013a). The 
sample frame of the population was primarily those who use Facebook and are a part of the groups 
that the survey was posted in. An attempt to rectify this coverage error was taken by asking 
participants to share the survey in their own circles (though there was no control over this), as well as 
the survey being sent out via other channels (Harvard University 2006).  
There were 76 respondents from the questionnaire, meaning there was a large coverage and 
nonresponse errors (Harvard University 2006). With regards to the nonresponse error, the 
questionnaire may have appealed to those who consider bicycle parking a problem in Copenhagen, or 
those who are more pro-cycling than others. This may have created a bias as the respondents may be 
more pro-cycling than the general population. 
The key findings from the questionnaire can be found in the Section 4.5. The full questionnaire, with

3. The City of Copenhagen wants parked bicycles to look tidy, but understands that this 
contradicts the freedom of movement promoted. 
When talking about how the Inverted U stand is commonly applauded across other cycle friendly 
cities, Stine mentioned how she saw those stands as being a source of a messy look. But she also said 
that the stands in Copenhagen are also a source of the messy look. She agreed that attitudes to bicycle 
parking infrastructure are likened to that of cars. For cars uniform parking infrastructure is easily 
achieved due to the tessellating rectangular shape of them. But with bicycles, due to their many 
differences, the same uniform infrastructure does not work. Kira mentioned how often the uniform 
style allows for Copenhageners to park their bicycles in between other bicycles, as seen in Figure 21, 
which creates frustration for the bicycles already parked inside the stand.

Freedom of movement whilst on a bicycle is at the Copenhageners core. Both Stine and Kira talk of a 
sense of entitlement which cyclists have, and this is furthered by the political stance “Politically, people 
want to promote riding and parking anywhere.” Also, Kira said “we are strong environmentalists 
because of cycling, then as individuals we tend to tell ourselves that it is okay to park badly as at least 
“I don’t pollute” or “I am picking the better/healthier option”. So not parking in the most socially 
responsible way is not that important as cycling is already doing something good in society.” This 
highlights the attitudes of Copenhagen cyclists as that of prioritising their individual concerns. 
4. Only if the guidelines are changed will the design of the bicycle stand be changed. 
Stine sternly said how a new concept would not be considered for many reasons, but mainly due to 
the law of the guidelines. For a new bicycle stand to be implemented across Copenhagen, the 
guidelines would have to be influenced accordingly. This is unlikely to happen as it is such a minor 
problem in the grand scheme of the political landscape. Though pilot studies have been undertaken 
(see next point), none of them have been subsequently implemented. Moreover, alternative means 
of parking facilities, such as silos, will not be considered as they would interfere with the vision of the 
city architect. 
5. Policing/monitoring and moving badly parked bicycles would be a costly task and with 
questionable success. 
Kira described in detail a trial project which involved moving bicycles from bad places to designated 
places. Some of the findings from the trial were that the sheer amount of signage required was 
deemed ineffective. This was partly down to a lot of cyclists being unaware of road signs in the same 
typography for drivers being for cyclists. Also, often cyclists are in a hurry and so do not have time to 
stop and read the details of what is and is not allowed with regards to parking. Mainly, the trial was 
deemed ineffective due to how often the moving of bicycles had to occur for it to change behaviour. 
On days where bicycles were not moved, the behaviour of Copenhageners resorted back to the bad 
parking habits that were trying to be changed. 
This constant policing and monitoring of bicycle spaces is also a reason for the flex-parking spaces, 
parking spaces shared between drivers and cyclists, to not be implemented across the city. It would 
be too costly to hire a team to constantly check and move bicycles according to the laws. Only outside 
of schools and other institutions, where a janitor, employee, or team could undertake the task, is it 
deemed possible to work. 
6. Design for manufacture and maintenance is a key concern. 
Both Stine and Kira talked about points regarding design for manufacture, maintenance and 
replacement. Stine talked about how now the bicycle stands are not painted, as it was too costly to 
constantly repaint the damaged stands. Also, Kira talked about how having fewer parts is better. This 
was mainly down to simplicity encouraging less maintenance. Moreover, from an operational flow 
perspective, the bicycle stand should not interfere with cleaning equipment and processes which 
often sweep the streets. This is something that the original VEKSØ stand did not take into account, 
resulting in the redesign and remaking of components.

4.6 ANALYSIS OF RESULTS 
Section 4.6 will present the key results from the observations, interviews and questionnaire. Where 
possible, relevant theory from the literature review will be brought in to try and explain the observed 
or intended behaviour. 
1. If there are visible and easy open spaces people tend to use it. 
Observations across Copenhagen have found that in general, if a space in a bicycle stand is visible and 
is easily accessible then it will be used. However, there is the interesting point of how many free spaces 
in a row influence behaviour. If there are three spaces in a row, shown in Figure 31, then the middle 
space will be used. If there are two spaces in a row, shown in Figure 30, then the side with the least 
intrusion from the neighbouring bicycle (smallest handlebars, no basket, etc.) will be used. But, in the 
case that there is a single space, as seen in Figure 29, the slot is infrequently used. If there is an easier 
space visibly available, as previously described in Figure 30 and Figure 31, then those spaces will be 
used. It was more common that people opted for parking their bicycle outside of the stand, mostly 
free standing or resting against the nearest piece of urban furniture, than using the single space. 
==PAGE:49==
37 
 
 
Figure 29: Single free bicycle space 
 
Figure 30: Double free bicycle space 
 
Figure 31: Triple free bicycle space 
==PAGE:50==
38 
 
One could argue that this is an example of the facilitating conditions, described in the theory of 
interpersonal behaviour (Triandis 1977), constituting an overriding factor of behaviour. If using the 
bicycle rack is perceived to be difficult, people tend to subconsciously look for an easier option, hence 
fly-parking. Moreover, the role of social factors is prominent. If there were bicycles already parked 
outside of the stand, people would usually follow suit regardless of their being free spaces or not. 
Generally speaking this is because it looked easier to do, as fly-parking is much easier than fitting a 
bicycle into a tight space – especially if there was only a single slot free. 
2. If there is not a bicycle stand directly outside the destination, then that location becomes the 
parking space. 
While walking the streets of Copenhagen, it is clear that fly-parking is rampant. Anywhere there are 
shops, cafés, bars and restaurants there are bicycles fly-parked outside. The questionnaire 
corroborated this as it showed that almost everyone feels as if they have complete freedom to cycle 
anywhere in Copenhagen, and that feel like they can park their bicycles almost anywhere. With 
regards to specific laws, only one respondent correctly cited that you can legally park anywhere – even 
if a shopfront sticker says you cannot. 
When considering these factors about bicycle parking, it is clear that people’s attitudes towards 
parking their bicycle is very open. Copenhageners believe that they can park their bicycles almost 
anywhere, and that it is down to them to decide what a good or bad place to park is. The questionnaire 
found that 90.8% of respondents felt like they have near or complete free mobility when on a bicycle, 
and 65.8% of respondents felt like they can park almost or anywhere they please (not only in the 
specified places). This goes to show that Copenhageners attitudes towards where they can park their 
bicycle is that of being able to place it anywhere. This point was reiterated by Kira from 
Cykelhåndtering, when she stated that the government feels that Copenhageners have an entitlement 
to park as they please due to them using the ‘better option’ when considering driving.  
Respondents from the questionnaire also stated that if they had a bad experience with a bicycle stand, 
then it would make them not want to use them in the future. This implies that Copenhageners expect 
bicycle parking to be easy and stress free, agreeing with the idea of frictionless mobility (A. Jensen 
2013). 
3. Copenhageners want to lock their bicycles to something, but they do not do so out of habit 
The O-lock is by far the most popular lock in Copenhagen, though more and more cyclists are opting 
for chains, cables, folding or D-locks if they are buying a new one. However, the habit of the O-lock 
persists. This was first identified in the literature review in Section 3.3.2, and now confirmed through 
observations. Approximately half of all observations used an O-lock when parking their bicycle. 
Moreover, when exploring and observing the streets of Copenhagen, nearly all cyclists use this type 
of lock. The habit routine associated with this type of lock is fly-parking. The literature also found that 
cyclists who formerly own O-locks tend to carry the habit over to their new locks. This was also found 
to be true. Practically all observations of cyclists who did not use an O-lock, never locked their bicycle 
to the stand or urban furniture. One reason for the O-lock habit persisting into alternative lock types 
is that the infrastructure is not suited to easily locking bicycles to it. 
Though only 6% of the observations saw Copenhageners lock their bicycle to something, the 
questionnaire found that 50% of respondents said they did. This was dwarfed by 80% of respondents 
saying they wanted to be able to lock their bicycle to something. The key point here is that the 
infrastructure in Copenhagen simply does not cater for those who want to be able to lock their bicycle 
to something.  
==PAGE:51==
39 
 
4. If other bicycles are parked outside of a bicycle stand then others follow suit 
The most common assessment of the observations was the influence of social factors on behaviour. 
Social norms in Copenhagen are that of widespread fly-parking across the city. If others have parked 
bicycles outside of the stand or in front of a commercial space, then it is likely that people will follow 
suit. This idea is a common factor in behavioural theories, where the environment and social norms 
influence behaviour. Building on this, the social norm of fly-parking promotes the idea that it is a very 
easy practice. It is quick, hassle free, and with a kickstand can be done anywhere. This is one way that 
the behaviour spreads, as it shows that a low ability is required for the behaviour to be practiced. 
 
Figure 32: Fly-parked bicycles outside of a shop 
This was supported with evidence, as approximately 70% of observations saw cyclists fly-parking or 
parking their bicycles against urban furniture when others had already done so. The remaining 30% of 
cyclists tended to use the stands properly as there were clearly visible and spacious free slots. This 
mentality of following suit and adhering to social norms has been tested and documented by others 
(Colville-Andersen 2018). 
5. Copenhagen values aesthetics over user experience 
The use of the VEKSØ bicycle stand has been widespread practice for a long time, to the extent that it 
is a part of the Copenhagen architecture manual. The interviews found that this was because the City 
of Copenhagen has a vision of the city where everything is in aesthetical harmony. They are aware 
that the satisfaction of the infrastructure is low but will not change the design of the stand. 
==PAGE:52==
40 
 
Considering the social ecological model and the socially responsible design theory, this is a clear clash 
of concerns, as the government (macrosystem) overrides the concerns of the others. Whilst 
Copenhageners want to be able to park their bicycle anywhere they please, the government want the 
bicycles to be parked in a uniform fashion.  
Both of the interviews found that the government promotes the idea that people can cycle and park 
their bicycles anywhere they please, to the extent that it is legal to fly-park a bicycle on any public 
land. If a shop or restaurant put up signs saying, “do not park here”, they are not legally allowed to 
enforce any consequence if someone does. It is illegal for them to move the bicycle as the bicycle has 
the legal status of private property. Prior to this and based on the literature review, it had been 
thought that there were places which were deemed illegal to park bicycles in, however findings from 
the interviews highlighted that there are almost no illegal places to park. A better way to describe the 
majority of “illegally” parked bicycles is socially unfavourable. In the next point more details are 
provided as to where is deemed illegal to park. 
In a pilot study conducted at Nørreport station, where road signs indicated where people could and 
could not park, the City of Copenhagen found that typically cyclists did not understand the signs. Kira 
stated that most cyclists in Copenhagen do not know how to drive a car, and consequently may not 
know the meaning of many road signs. They may also not know that the signs are intended for them. 
Or they simply do not look at them whilst cycling. 
6. Politics promotes fly-parking and sees the current stand as an identifier 
The political landscape is such that cycling is promoted as a free way of traveling with near complete 
flexibility. The only laws regarding where you cannot park is if your bicycle is concerning blocking a 
fire escape, operational flows, or is endangering others. Though it is the police’s jurisdiction to enforce 
this, they rarely do. Moreover, the stand itself is considered an aesthetical identifier of Copenhagen 
like the bin, street lamps, bench, and pavement (Københavns Kommune 2007). Only in certain cases 
will a new stand be considered – if the surrounding environment is deemed independent enough of 
Copenhagen or has a strong historical or aesthetic value. 
The political landscape shows that it is unlikely for the design of the current stand to change. Though 
changes have been made, these were incremental changes to marginally improve satisfaction – like 
the indent to protect magnetic lights. The design manual for public spaces is not going to change 
anytime soon, and the replacement of all stands in Copenhagen would be a too costly and a time-
consuming project. No politician would back either of these as it would be politically undesirable. 
Moreover, recent cuts to budgets mean that large scale projects such as replacing current 
infrastructure are less likely to occur. It is highly unlikely that any negative consequence will be 
introduced to curb undesirable bicycle parking, as this would go against the pro-cycling mantra. 
Through the EU Handshake programme the City of Copenhagen is aware of the benefits of a negative 
consequential law being introduced. Amsterdam has fairly strict policies regarding where you can and 
cannot park which in the city streets is widely accepted and adhered to.

4.8 CONCLUSIONS OF SECTION 4 
The findings from Section 4 reaffirmed the findings from the literature review. The disconnect 
between high level policy, design and the behaviour of users is prevalent. The main conclusions can 
be presented in the following four points: 
1. The City of Copenhagen values uniform aesthetics above all else. 
The design of the bicycle rack that is in widespread use across Copenhagen is not going to be 
changed without a long string of politics and lobbying. The design is considered a visual 
identifier for Copenhagen, and only changes in some cases where the urban landscape 
justifies a new aesthetic. Some design changes have been made to it, but these are only 
incremental improvements on functionality for the user. Some design considerations have 
been made which makes the design more attractive to other areas of the government, such 
as operational maintenance. The administration in charge of placing new stands in 
Copenhagen are aware of the behavioural downsides of the design but do not prioritise user 
behaviour. 
2. Space in Copenhagen is an issue, particularly in the inner city. 
Space in Copenhagen is highly contested. Narrow streets are shared with many stakeholders 
each with different amounts of power. Cyclists have the most visible power in the sense that 
they often (legally) park in car parking spaces when bicycle stands are full or overcrowded. 
What was previously thought to be illegal parking is better described as socially unfavourable, 
as this type of fly-parking often causes issues amongst stakeholders, as access can be 
restricted, pedestrian spaces obstructed, shop fronts damaged, and more. Even among 
cyclists the spatial contest causes upset. When bicycles are hard to retrieve from stands, 
cyclists get annoyed. The City of Copenhagen is aware of this spatial problem but has only 
managed to pilot a few projects with regards to bicycle parking, and with little success for 
widespread implementation. 
3. Cyclists will do whatever they want and park wherever they want. 
A lot of cyclists in Copenhagen do use the infrastructure provided, but not all the time. All too 
often it is easier, faster, more accessible or less frustrating to fly-park in Copenhagen. 
Moreover, this mentality is promoted and ingrained in habit. For a long time, the O-lock has 
reigned as the leading product to secure a bicycle. The behaviours associated with this has 
trickled down to subsequent locks, with cyclists often locking their bicycle to itself. However, 
Copenhageners are aware that it is safer to lock a bicycle to something fixed. A large portion 
of the population also want to be able to lock their bicycle to something. But the infrastructure 
provided does not make this easy. 
 
 
==PAGE:56==
44 
 
4. The bicycle parking infrastructure in Copenhagen does not reflect cyclist behaviour. 
Copenhagen is one of the few cities which opts for a Grid style stand as the main piece of 
infrastructure. However, this stand is known to damage bicycles and be difficult for cyclists to 
lock their bicycle to. The stands are also routinely a source of frustration due to overcrowding, 
leading to Copenhageners not wanting to use them. The stands are also placed in a centralised 
manner, whereas Copenhageners feel and want to be able to park all over the city and as close 
to their destinations as possible. This is a prime example of the disconnect between social 
concerns and individual concerns (Tromp, Hekkert, and Verbeek 2011). 
These four points show a reluctance from the City of Copenhagen and designers to radically rethink 
the approach to bicycle parking infrastructure. The focus for too long has been on incremental 
improvements to existing designs. More importantly, the way of thinking with regards to how cyclists 
will use the infrastructure has not changed. The bicycle stands in Copenhagen are designed in the way 
that car parking bays are, where cyclists are assumed to park uniformly. However, bicycles and cars 
are very different, particularly in their tessellating properties not enabling uniform parking. The City 
of Copenhagen is unwavering with regards to changing the design of the bicycle stand. So that the 
thesis still holds relevance, there will be a shift towards coming up with a new product which can work 
alongside the existing infrastructure rather than redesigning the VEKSØ stand.

5 DESIGN PROCESS 
5.1 INTRODUCTION 
To try to radically rethink bicycle parking infrastructure, an untraditional approach should be used to 
come up with something new. It would be easy to design another Inverted U or Grid stand, but these 
markets are saturated with small differences to distinguish between products. Moreover, The City of 
Copenhagen has made it clear that they will not change the design of the existing bicycle stand. 
What this project aims to achieve is to bring something new to the market, to try to design a new 
product which could work alongside existing infrastructure. To do this, elements from the Vision in 
Product Design methodology will be taken to explore the problem and its future in a new light. 
5.2 VISION FOR THE FUTURE OF BICYCLE PARKING INFRASTRUCTURE 
Leaning on the Vision in Product Design (ViP) theory (Hekkert and Dijk 2011), a domain and timeframe 
for the design vision will be used to establish a picture of what the future will look like and when it will 
be. The reason this method was utilised was to try to counter the results from the interviews in Section 
4.5.2. The traditional approach to designing public spaces follows an architectural manual which, due 
to politics, will not be changed soon. The ViP theory can circumnavigate this by making allowances for 
subjective opinion, where factors influencing the context can be based on beliefs and opinions which 
are general and/or subjective. 
Domain: enabling frictionless mobility5 whilst allowing secure bicycle parking 
Starting with the literature review and being reiterated in the collected data, Copenhageners want 
frictionless mobility which is highly flexible. Being able to park their bicycles anywhere is something 
that Copenhageners live and breathe. This should be reflected in the infrastructure that enables 
mobility – parking included. Given the wider global context bicycle parking should be secure; a trend 
which is also becoming more prevalent in Copenhagen. 
Timeframe: in line with the Bicycle Parking Priority Plan 2018 to 2025 
Aligning this project with the bicycle parking priority plan grounds the project. It means that the 
project can still use the goals set out by the City of Copenhagen but does not have to adhere to the 
same design philosophies. Though the field of public spaces is not exactly innovating as of now, a 
general shift towards behavioural design6 and design thinking7 is showing promise that in the future 
these methodologies will be employed. Therefore, aligning the project with the 2018 to 2025 
timeframe gives it a certain legitimacy.

attributes imposed by bicycles, theory related aspects and findings from Section 4.

or nice bicycles. For this group, they will often search the local vicinity for places to lock their bicycle

Larsen 2017b). For them the problems are simply that there is no formal infrastructure to easily lock 
a bicycle to. Often the only pieces of urban furniture to lock a bicycle to are in socially unfavourable 
places, such as blocking pavements and doorways, obstructing benches or the bicycle stand itself. 
The clear target market is for those who want to lock their bicycle to something. Currently, there are 
largely only socially unfavourable places to lock a bicycle to. This is mainly down to there being no 
formal infrastructure being designed for their needs.  
5.3.2 
Aligning collective and individual concerns 
The vision sees alignment between the collective concerns with the individual concerns, yet with the 
focus being on the individual. It is apparent from Section 4 that the concerns are somewhat aligned, 
as cycling is promoted as a means of limitless mobility, and Copenhageners treat it this way. This 
symbiotic relationship is realised by the rampant and legal fly-parking in Copenhagen. However, to 
satisfy the City of Copenhagen’s goal of increasing satisfaction with bicycle parking infrastructure, this 
project aims to design a product which could fit into the existing infrastructure. It should allow for 
more widespread and secure parking. By enabling those who want to be able to lock their bicycle to 
something to do so, bicycle stands should become less crowded. All parties would benefit from this 
better distribution of parked bicycles, subsequently improving the situation and satisfaction of bicycle 
parking. Though this approach may not instantly change behaviour, it will start the process and enable 
long-term behaviour change (Glanz and Bishop 2010). 
To take a radical new approach to bicycle parking infrastructure, the individual cyclist will have their 
needs, concerns and values prioritised. The current process is for the government, designers and 
policy to dictate where individuals can park. These decisions are usually made with socially favourable 
parking places in mind, typically centralised banks with bicycles parked in a uniform fashion. However, 
this can be done in a way to influence decision making so that they choose more socially favourable 
places to park. If a cyclist does not want to use the current infrastructure, then there are no other 
options to do so. Particularly if the cyclist wants to be able to lock their bicycle to something, then the 
easy options are to use urban furniture or poorly use the bicycle stand. There is a clear solution space 
for a product intervention which enables those who do not want to use the current infrastructure, 
and want to be able to lock their bicycle to something, to make socially favourable parking decisions.

5.3.3 
Ability vs motivation trade-off 
Considering Fogg’s behavioural model, the current practices can be depicted on the axis of motivation 
and ability. This tool can be used to paint the picture of where the vision and solution space needs to 
be. The model states that for a behaviour to occur, there must be a non-zero level for both motivation 
and ability (Fogg 2009). Currently, ability for fly-parking is extremely low. So for some people, 
regardless of a product intervention, they will continue to fly-park. However, the vision should reflect 
this near zero ability factor in the solution. 
The Theory of Influence (Cialdini 1984) states that not only do people follow statues of power, they 
also tend to follow the actions of each other. This line of thought is represented in many of the theories 
explored in Section 3.4. As found in Section 4, Copenhagen is not likely to introduce policies which 
restrict bicycle parking options. Therefore, the product should consider the role that policy could have, 
but not rely on it for success. In Copenhagen it is socially acceptable to fly-park (almost) anywhere. 
Therefore the vision for the solution space needs to have a higher motivation than the fly-parking 
option. Things to consider is what the product intervention can offer, such as safe and secure parking, 
easy to use, close to the final destination and being socially favourable option.

5.3.8 
Copenhagen aesthetic 
Copenhagen has a strict architectural policy and design code which has a focus on cultural history and 
identity. There is also a large focus on user centred design, sustainability, liveable cities and human 
scale (Københavns Kommune 2010).  The current bicycle stands are all made from hot dipped 
galvanised steel. If there are coloured components they are typically grey, green or blue, unless special 
cases are made for a location (Københavns Kommune 2007). All these components build into the idea 
of a Copenhagen identity, which stand upon the four pillars of the bench, bin, lighting and pavement. 
However, these policies and codes also state that urban furniture which solves a functional need on a 
human scale, with detail and an original design, also builds into the story of the city’s development 
(Københavns Kommune 2012). The product should reflect this, assimilating if necessary or otherwise 
trying to add to the story of Copenhagen’s development. 
5.3.9 
Current infrastructure 
This thesis is focusing on narrow streets, predominately used for commercial purposes. A part of the 
reason why this is the focus is that the current products on the market simply cannot cater for these 
areas. The VEKSØ Grid stand, or Inverted U stands found in cities across the world, are still needed in 
some places. There is a need for high capacity facilities which create a sort of order for cyclists. 
However, no products exist for the narrow streets. This is why cyclists regularly turn to urban furniture 
or, in the case of Copenhageners, use a kickstand and O-lock. This highlights a gap in the market which 
will need to be filled to fulfil the vision for the future context, as presented in Section 5.4. 
Also, it is easy to understand why bicycle stands are often overcrowded. It is because there is usually 
only one formal piece of infrastructure. If you consider car parking options, there is a wide variety of 
places to park, each with their own benefits and restrictions, which allow for a better distribution of 
parked cars. For example, if there were only car parks and no other car parking infrastructure, the car 
parks would be overcrowded. Parallels can be drawn to bicycle parking infrastructure. Options which 
suit different user needs and requirements need to be introduced to better distribute bicycles and 
cater for all cyclist types. 
5.4 FUTURE CONTEXT 
To give the future vision some context, the following section describes what bicycle parking 
infrastructure should be and who it should cater for. These context areas were developed from the 
studies conducted to support this thesis, which include observations, interviews, facts, theories, laws, 
opinions and thoughts (Hekkert and Dijk 2011). They were also developed from personal experiences 
gained whilst cycling in various cities around Europe, notably Amsterdam, Copenhagen, London, 
Berlin, Prague, Sheffield, Leeds and Dresden. Each subsection represents a structured take on the 
context areas, combining developments, trends, states and principles (Hekkert and Dijk 2011). 
5.4.1 
The final inch 
Cycling as a mode of transport gives freedom, flexibility and mobility to the user. For people cycling in 
a city, this means that they should be able to park their bicycle as close to their final destination as 
possible (Colville-Andersen 2018). Of course, there is a need for centralised bicycle parking 
infrastructure at places that see large influxes of cyclists, places such as stations, shopping centres and 
museums. But there is a need for decentralised facilities which can facilitate secure parking closer to 
smaller destinations, such as shops, salons and cafés. 
5.4.2 
Speed and ease 
Bicycle parking infrastructure should be intuitive, effortless, and quick to use. Parking as the terminal 
component of cycling can occur under all sorts of circumstances, sometimes where speed is of the 
==PAGE:66==
54 
 
essence. If caught in the rain, late for a train or in a hurry, parking and locking a bicycle should be a 
speedy exercise. 
Though it was not formally documented, the time spent for someone to lock their bicycle (in the 
observations), was usually less than 30 seconds. In the questionnaire, over 75% of respondents said 
they spend less than a minute finding a space to park their bicycle. In a world where instant 
gratification is so common, and everyone wants to live in the here and now, small interactions such 
as bicycle parking need to be quick and easy. This is so it does not detract from the person’s 
momentum. Thus, in the future bicycle parking spaces need to be easily seen and easy to use. 
Moreover anyone, regardless of where they are from and how old they are, should know how to use 
the product. 
5.4.3 
Security 
Bicycle theft is a problem around the world, especially in Copenhagen (Jonas Larsen 2017b). Securing 
a bicycle to an object is therefore important. Bicycle stands that only support the front or back wheel 
are simply not enough in the realms of the context. Though in Copenhagen this is rarely practiced, in 
other cities such as Amsterdam it is extremely uncommon to not lock your bicycle to something, and 
this is reflected in their style of bicycle stand. Both in the interviews and general observations saw that 
a common reason for poor parking behaviour within bicycle stands was to enable secure locking. 
Cyclists would sometimes block three or four formal spaces just so that their bicycle could be locked 
to the stand easily. 
5.4.4 
A family of parking infrastructure 
Spoiling cyclists with places to park their bicycles is a way cities and governments can show that they 
care about and are thinking cyclists (Celis and Bølling-Ladegaard 2008). No one product will satisfy all 
cyclists, there are simply too many niches, attitudes, and types of cyclist. What cities need to provide 
is a family of products which cater for all. Both centralised and decentralised facilities need to be 
provided. This family of products should be scattered across cities, ideally in a coherent style or design 
language. 
The products within the family of parking infrastructure need to be obvious what they are for. This 
goes back to the intuitive to use point. Users need to know what products are in their urban landscape 
and how to use them. The products should stand out, so that cyclists who act on what they see in the 
urban environment are aware of the facilities and products designed for them. 
5.4.5 
Policy in the context 
As with lines on roads indicating where you can and cannot park a car, there needs to be clear policies 
to indicate where cyclists can and cannot park. Though this seemingly contradicts with the freedom, 
flexibility and mobility highlighted earlier, policies can be introduced in a way which benefits all. The 
urban landscape is shared with many different modes of transport, and in large quantities in some 
cases. Cities need to be able to manage each mode of transport and facilitate a harmony between all. 
General observations from Amsterdam, where there are clear markings of where you can park 
bicycles, saw that people mostly follow the rules. Those that do not follow the rules run the risk of 
having bicycles confiscated, which imposes are monetary and time cost to the person in question. 
When designing these areas and lines of where you can and cannot park, the public and individuals 
should be consulted in a form of co-creation. This will help reshape cities so that they are designed 
from the bottom up, not the traditional top down approach. 
==PAGE:67==
55 
 
5.4.6 
Ubiquitous in the urban landscape 
Bicycle parking infrastructure should offer a feeling of homogeneousness across cities. In a sense the 
aesthetic should not have a strong connection to a specific city, rather it should have an aesthetical 
tie to urban landscapes in general. Cities use products to create a sense of identity, especially 
Copenhagen, as found in the interviews, but new products do not have to impose on this. Having a 
neutral design would allow products to infiltrate any city, with only some minor details to be changed 
to make it fit into the cities branding. Especially when a stronger sense of identity comes from the fact 
that there are bicycles everywhere in Copenhagen, not the design of the stand. 
Having bicycle parking products that are ubiquitous in the urban landscape could influence people’s 
perception and satisfaction of what the government is doing for them. People notice when small 
things are done to help them, whether it is a friend, family, or a government, people notice. Small 
features found in cities to benefit cyclists may not solve a problem as a whole, but they show that 
cyclists are thought of, and in doing so could increase satisfaction. As seen in Section 5.3.4, small 
features which do not necessarily rectify major problems still play a part in the feeling that someone 
experiences in a city. They also act as enablers of long-term behavioural change (Glanz and Bishop 
2010). 
5.4.7 
Distribution of parked bicycles 
The number of people, and subsequent bicycles, moving to Copenhagen is increasing. From the 
interviews, Kira mentioned that current infrastructure projects only consider the current figures for 
bicycles, not taking into account forecasts. This in part is reason for why bicycle stands are often 
overcrowded and frustrating to use. Currently there is only one formal piece of infrastructure for 
bicycles in Copenhagen (the Grid stand). In the future, Copenhagen should expand this to cater for 
different styles of parking. In doing so, fewer bicycles would use the Grid stands meaning that for 
those who want to, the Grid stands will have fewer users – making them easier to use. 
As with the construction of the new metro line in Copenhagen, the city projects that by building the 
new line more people will travel underground. With more people underground fewer bus routes will 
be needed. Fewer busses means that the roads will be better for those that need to use them, such 
as drivers, cyclists, and the remaining bus routes. This is an example of how a better distribution of 
travellers/commuters can have wider reaching benefits. This can and should be applied to bicycle 
parking infrastructure in the future by means of better management of mode of transport. 
5.5 PRODUCT INTERACTION AND QUALITIES 
Having an understanding of how the future of bicycle parking should look like is the backbone to 
coming up with new product. However, the product needs to have interaction qualities which embody 
the future context. Based on the future context, the following interaction and product qualities were 
thought of. 
5.5.1 
Simplicity 
Given that the facilitating conditions can vary drastically, the interaction should not be a complex feat 
of physical exertion. It should not be taxing on the thought process, more an intuitive thing to do. This 
interaction quality should be reflected in the product qualities too. To embody a simple interaction, 
the product itself should also be simple. 
5.5.2 
Obviousness 
When the cyclist arrives to their destination and sees the product, they should know exactly what to 
do. Their unconscious type 1 thought process should take over and instinct be the driver of behaviour. 
==PAGE:68==
56 
 
This should also be carried through into the product qualities. The product needs to be obvious as to 
its function. Little thought should be put into what the product is for or how to use it. 
5.5.3 
Frictionless 
It should not impede on the user’s journey. The interaction should not only be fast, but also not 
awkward. It should not disrupt their journey either in time or location and subsequent momentum. 
With regards to product qualities, the idea of frictionless mobility will come partly from the simplicity 
and obviousness stated in the previous points, but mostly from the location of the product. 
5.5.4 
Security 
To leave a bicycle unattended for any given amount of time can be a frightful experience. The 
interaction needs to leave the user feeling that their bicycle, not matter its worth, is safe and secure. 
This should be reflected in the product qualities through material selection, dimensioning and fixing. 
The product should leave a feeling of security through how it looks and feels. 
5.5.5 
Inclusivity 
This is with regards to bicycle lock types the product should cater for all (within reason). Lock type can 
vary just as much as bicycles do. This inclusivity means the product will not discriminate between 
different users. As with the idea of frictionless mobility, inclusivity in terms of product qualities will 
come from the sum of the above. 
5.6 CONCEPT GENERATION 
This ideation section will aim to conceptualise products which could be introduced to work alongside 
the current infrastructure. The aim is to also design for the ‘final inch’. This is in an attempt to cater 
for those people who will lock their bicycle as close to the final destination as possible, or simply not 
want to use the bicycle stands currently provided. Given the future context, interaction and product 
qualities previously defined, the concept generation phase will focus on a locking feature to be 
secured to a wall, lamppost, signpost and so on. 
5.6.1 
Exploring features 
The idea is to develop and feature which could be ubiquitous in the urban landscape. The feature 
should of course have the function of allowing cyclists to secure their bicycle to it. But it should also 
have an aesthetical presence which will not seem out of place in Copenhagen, or any other city. 
The reason the product revolves around a feature, is because this is the final part of the family which 
is not currently explored. In Copenhagen, and other cities across the world, bicycle parking 
infrastructure already comprises of grids at areas of large influxes of cyclists, such as train stations. U-
stands and railings in wide streets, still in a centralised manner, are also a common sight. But there 
are no bicycle specific features designed for narrow streets, where they cannot be fixed to the ground 
due to space constrictions. For this reason, a wall mounted feature is to be explored. Different profiles

5.7 CONCLUSIONS FROM SECTION 5 
The two exploratory sections reflect the simplicity of the concept. The bicycle stand should not be a 
complex product. The urban landscape is often cluttered due to the many users and stakeholders 
which share the environment. Any new product which wishes to enter the space with only a single 
function should not be complex in its form. 
By leaning on the ViP methodology, the design process focussed on designing the underlying idea 
before coming up with a product to embody it. Overall the key ideas behind radically rethinking the 
future of bicycle parking infrastructure are: 
1. The final inch 
Generally speaking, current infrastructure is designed in a centralised fashion. Grid stands are 
often located at the ends or middle of streets (if the street is wide enough). However, 
Copenhageners want to be able to park anywhere close to their final destinations. This is seen 
through the widespread fly-parking across the city and the use of car parking spaces for bicycle 
parking. With cycling enabling almost limitless mobility, the parking infrastructure should also 
reflect this. 
2. Adding to the family of products 
There is a need for banks of stands in places of high capacity, such as train stations, shopping 
centres and parks. Redesigning a Grid stand, Inverted U or any other style of common bicycle 
stand would be easy and has been done countless times. To try to come up with something new, 
it is more important to add to the family of bicycle parking. Spoiling cyclists with a variety of 
parking products is a way to show that their needs are thought of and considered. This aims to 
improve the situation of bicycle parking in Copenhagen and subsequent satisfaction. 
3. Distribution of bicycles 
With only one formal piece of bicycle parking infrastructure it is easy to see why it is so often 
overcrowded. To benefit all cyclists, parked bicycles should be better distributed. For those that 
are frustrated with the Grid stands, but use them as they are the only option, alternatives solutions 
should be provided. By distributing the parked bicycles better, those that want to use the Grid

stands will find them less crowded thus making them easier to use. Those that do not want to use 
them will have a parking facility or solution which caters to their needs. 
4. Importance of policy 
Though the focus of this thesis is to design a product, it is important to understand the role and 
impact that policy can have. A single product will not change the parking behaviours of a city, but 
policy can be used as a tool to impose a level of authority which forces people to change their 
behaviour. Introducing policy does not necessarily mean restricting where people can park, or 
imposing consequences for illegally parked bicycles. It could be a program of education to inform 
people of where is socially favourable to park bicycles. This would act as a slow but long-term 
solution to changing people’s behaviour (Glanz and Bishop 2010). 
The above points are all interconnected and must be considered together. They underpin the future 
context, as described in Section 5.4. To satisfy the future context the idea of a simple feature, which 
allows the cyclists to lock their bicycle to something, has been explored. This product would be found 
across cities, enabling widespread secure parking. A product which would satisfy the future context, 
and fill the identified gap in the market, should embody a feeling of simplicity, obviousness, 
frictionless, security and inclusivity.

6 THE CONCEPT 
6.1 INTRODUCTION 
In this section the product which was developed in accordance with the future context will be 
presented. It aims to bring something new to a heavily saturated market. By creating a simple feature 
for locking purposes, the product can be placed in close proximity to the final destination – enabling 
simple and secure bicycle parking. 
6.2 DESIGN VISION 
As previously mentioned in Section 5, this thesis sees the future of bicycle parking and cycling in cities 
to be one that provides a feeling of frictionless mobility. Meaning that cyclists and their bicycles should 
be allowed to go and park (almost) anywhere in a city. Currently in Copenhagen this is somewhat 
achieved, but not through the means of infrastructure. The O-lock and kickstand enable fly-parking 
anywhere. However, with the growing notion of cyclists wanting to be able to park their bicycle 
securely, there are a lack of products which can cater for those who want to lock their bicycle to 
something close to their final destination. This is exacerbated when considering narrow commercial 
streets. 
6.3 INTRODUCING: THE DROPLET 
Introducing The Droplet, a feature for the urban landscape which enables cyclists to park freely and 
securely. Empowering cyclists to freedom of mobility, frictionless transport and peace of mind when 
locking their bicycle. 
 
Figure 47: Renders of The Droplet 
The way The Droplet is to be used is simple from start to finish, especially as the feature makes use of 
existing behaviour. To use the feature the bicycle could be rested against the wall or supported using 
==PAGE:75==

a kickstand where The Droplet is located. Then using a chain, cable, folding or D-lock, the bicycle can 
be locked to the feature and left securely.

6.3.1 
The function  
Given the current state of bicycle parking infrastructure in Copenhagen, the idea behind The Droplet 
is to redistribute how and where people park their bicycles. The current overcrowding and misuse of 
the classic Copenhagen Grid stand means that people are frustrated, and have poor satisfaction of the 
infrastructure. What The Droplet offers is a safe and easy place for people who want to lock their 
bicycle to something. This will remove bicycles from the stands themselves, freeing up spaces for those 
who do not wish to lock their bicycle to something and are content using the current stands. 
This redistribution of where people lock their bicycles is a means to satisfy multiple groups of cyclists 
at the same time. This is similar to how car share initiatives help to remove cars from the roads, 
meaning that those who do not wish to partake in the car share also benefit from reduced traffic and 
==PAGE:76==
64 
 
more available spaces to park. The Droplet aims to free up spaces in the bicycle stands, whilst creating 
new specified places to securely park bicycles. 
The feature has been dimensioned and located so that any bicycle, regardless of size, handlebars, 
accessories, etc. can utilise it. The graphic on the sloping section, depicted in Figure 47, indicates its 
function to the user. Using the I BIKE CPH logo helps build the image of a family of products and 
projects specifically for Copenhagen cyclists. This graphic can easily be changed to fit the branding of 
any city. The simplicity of The Droplet means that it requires very low ability for cyclists to use it. It is 
designed to be located across cities and among their narrow streets, being located as close to 
commercial sites as possible to cater for ‘the final inch’. Being placed so close to final destinations 
aims to motivate people to use them.

6.3.2 
The aesthetic 
Not only does the shape hold aesthetical value, there is also a function to it. The size of it means that, 
for pedestrians, it can be easily seen and thus not be accidentally walked into. The sloping section also 
provides a surface for logos and graphics to suit the needs to whichever city it would be placed in. The 
raindrop aesthetic aims to give a slightly playful feel, but with urban relevance as rain and water is 
everywhere, particularly in Copenhagen. 
The Droplet envisages a simple and seamless aesthetic. The physically organic curves give a natural 
sensibility of water running down the side of a building. The idea is that this aesthetic is unanimous 
across most cities and should fit in to any urban landscape regardless of style. The simple design is 
subtle enough to blend into the urban landscape, yet eye-catching enough to be seen and recognised 
by cyclists and pedestrians alike. The playful styling also helps with drawing attention to what products 
in the urban landscape are specifically for cyclists. The blue colouring matches the blue bicycle lanes 
in Copenhagen, but also gives a bright and playful feeling. 
6.3.3 
The range 
The individual Droplet is a new feature to the bicycle parking infrastructure family. As previously 
mentioned, the product should belong to a family of products which would be ubiquitous throughout 
the urban landscape. For this reason, another product was conceptualised which would create a 
Droplet range. Similar products do exist to the following one, but this was designed to create a sense 
of a design language. It is The Droplet which is the new product on the market.

6.4 CONCEPT PRINCIPLES 
The principles that The Droplet and Shopfront feature tries to influence behaviour and decision 
making are detailed below. There will be a special focus on The Droplet rather than the Shopfront 
feature, as it is The Droplet which is a completely new product. 
1. Low ability and high motivation 
Fogg’s behavioural model states that there are three products of behaviour, namely the motivation, 
the ability and the trigger. These three products were all considered in the design of The Droplet. The 
motivation comes from The Droplet being placed close to commercial sites throughout streets and 
across cities. If people know that they are everywhere, and enable close secure bicycle parking, then 
people will be motivated to use them. This product of motivation also feeds into the ability. The 
simplicity of The Droplet, the dimensioning and positioning on walls will cater for fast and easy use. 
The feature provides a simple space to thread a lock through, regardless of lock type. The Droplet was 
designed to be as simple as possible in order to facilitate stress free use. Motivation is incorporated 
through people knowing that they are easy to use, and so will want to use them. If marketed correctly 
with advertising and branding showing that it is a new socially favourable place to park, then people 
will be motivated to use it through the idea of social acceptance (Fogg 2009). 
Visibility throughout the urban landscape of The Droplet will act as a trigger. It also plays on social 
norms and acceptance which can influence behaviour. If people see The Droplet throughout the city, 
it should influence their subconscious thoughts to use one. Moreover, this principle will be further 
promoted through people seeing others using it. In the same way that bad parking behaviour 
perpetuates bad parking behaviour. Then seeing people using The Droplet in a responsible way should 
promote good parking behaviour.

Currently, the bicycle parking infrastructure in Copenhagen does not reflect the complete freedom 
that the bicycle enables. Studies have seen that, even in Copenhagen, policies which move badly 
parked bicycles only work if they are continually imposed. For a city which does not want to introduce 
a long-term policy such as policing badly parked bicycles, then the only thing to do is allow for safe 
and secure parking to suit the behaviour of Copenhageners. In doing so the hope is to motivate them 
to think about where they park, rather than pick the nearest open space. 
2. Harmony between individual values and social concerns 
Cycling is promoted as a way to move freely within a city and enable mobility that does not 
discriminate. Why should cyclists be forced to park their bicycles in centralised banks of bicycle 
stands? Why should their bicycles be considered as a nuisance if fly-parked (within reason) in a way 
that benefits the individual? The short answer is that they should not.  
Fogg’s behavioural model presents belonging as a motivator for behaviour, with social acceptance and 
rejection as parameters. These two parameters work in a somewhat hypocritical yet symbiotic manner 
in Copenhagen. It is socially acceptable to fly-park, but rejection in the form of policing is not enough 
to change behaviour. The interview with Kira, and research on parking policies at train stations, shows 
that any behaviour change caused through policy is only sustained if it is enforced. The Droplet offers 
a more socially favourable place to park whilst not impeding on the individual values of cyclists. 
The Droplet feature empowers the cyclist to live up to everything that cycling embodies in the context 
of city cycling. It enables safe and secure parking throughout the city. However, there should still be a 
policy which dictates where cyclists can and cannot park. This need not infringe on the cyclist’s ability 
to park anywhere, and with The Droplet cyclists will have the ability to park safely and securely 
throughout any city. If The Droplet is located in socially favourable places, then when someone uses 
it their behaviour will have been subconsciously influenced to make a socially desirable decision. 
3. Changing perceptions of the environment 
==PAGE:82==
70 
 
Typically, people do not think about where or how they are going to park their bicycle until they are 
at their final location. People make unconscious decisions in the spur of the moment. The Droplet 
range aims to stand out and catch people’s eye. The colour and aesthetic should draw people’s 
attention to them. It will try to change the thought process in the moment they decide to park their 
bicycle. The subconscious thought process should be interrupted by the recognition of a new product 
in the urban landscape, one that signifies its bicycle parking function. This interruption aims to be 
enough to get people to think about where they could park their bicycle, rather than picking the first, 
closest or easiest place they see. Figure 52 shows how the new decision map and corresponding 
through processes could look like. 
4. Better distribution of parked bicycles 
Clusters form as people follow social norms and copy what others are doing. If one bicycle is parked 
in a car parking space or against a window, others often follow suit. Clusters also often form in and 
around of bicycle stands, causing overcrowding and frustration. The Droplet aims to cater for those 
who usually make poor use of bicycle stands, removing their bicycles from the stands. These people 
now have the option to lock their bicycles securely to The Droplet, thus creating more space in the 
stands for those who want to use them. In the narrow streets where there are no parking facilities, 
bicycles will not take up road space. Instead they will be securely locked to The Droplet in the 
designated spaces. Figure 53 displays how a better distribution of bicycles could look like. When 
comparing it to Figure 34, there are approximately 30 bicycles presented in both diagrams. However 
in the latter figure the distribution clearly shows a more tidy and coherent allocation of bicycles. 
6.5 THE BIGGER PICTURE 
A single product, or even product range, will not solve the problems associated with bicycle parking 
infrastructure alone. The Droplet and Shopfront feature offer a part of the solution. Their widespread 
placement will show cyclists that they are thought of, that their frustrations are heard, and their 
feelings on what cycling means as freedom of mobility are recognised. It will build into the picture of 
little things that make cycling easier, alongside the love handles, angled bins, and other small 
initiatives which aid cyclists. 
To really get to the bottom of the problem, a multifaceted approach needs to be taken. Such an 
approach should consider law making and policy, education, social change, marketing, branding, and 
products. The Droplet fits neatly into the product section, but for its benefits to be fully realised, it 
needs to be branded and marketed in a way that it promotes the social change it intends to enable. 
Its aesthetic and name help with branding, but in order for it to be fully realised policy will need to be 
involved. Laws regarding where you can and cannot park will need to be clear and respected. Consider 
Amsterdam, if a bicycle is parked in a location which is illegal it will be confiscated. The owner will 
then, at great lengths, go and pay to collect it – causing a physical and financial inconvenience. This 
law is widely accepted and respected, with very few bicycles being parked outside of the designated 
areas.

7 VALIDATION 
7.1 INTRODUCTION 
This section, The Droplet will be validated through means of an experimental psychological test. 
Though the type of behavioural change that is intended is large-scale, long-term and socially 
transformative, the test will be an abstraction of the big picture. This is done to determine principles 
and indicators to the change, which will be easier to test. 
7.2 PRINCIPLES 
The following section will detail the core principles of the Droplet, the ideas behind how this product 
would have an effect on behaviour in both an individual and social context. 
1. Socially responsible design theory  
Behaviour that benefits the individual does not always benefit society (Tromp, Hekkert, and 
Verbeek 2011). What this means in the context of bicycle parking is that bicycles are parked 
in places that may obstruct the pavement, block other modes of transport or be visually 
displeasing, all because it benefits the individual. What The Droplet aims to do, is provide a 
more socially favourable place to park a bicycle, which is obvious and visible. Thus influencing 
Copenhageners to pick the more socially favourable place to park. 
2. Dual process theory through nudging 
When presented with the environment of a final destination, type one process thinking drives 
people to park as close as possible. However, with the introduction of The Droplet cyclists will 
be presented with a new feature in the landscape. This new feature should catch their eye, 
and with the graphic detailing that it is for bicycle locking, the cyclist should turn to type two 
process thinking. They would then consciously think about where to park their bicycle, and be 
nudged to place it in the more socially acceptable place where The Droplet is located. 
3. Fogg’s behavioural model 
The Droplet offers an easy to use solution to secure bicycle parking. As shown in Figure 36, 
the solution space has been designed so that it is as easier than using the current 
infrastructure due to its simplicity. It also has a higher motivation to be used, which comes 
down to the proximity to final destinations and ability to easily secure the bicycle to the 
feature. 
The core principles are intertwined. They affect each other and work together to motivate, influence 
and change behaviour. Given the interlacing principles, a simple test needs to provide the subject with 
enough understanding of the product without giving away exactly what it is. A level of time pressure 
also needs to be added to encourage type one process thinking, but also allow a level of type two 
process thinking to understand the presented scenario. 
7.3 TEST DESIGN 
Given the nature of the transformative behaviour change, the following test is an abstraction of the 
complex problem. It has been designed to see if there are any indications for the product to do what 
it is intended to do. 
==PAGE:84==
72 
 
Hypothesis: In pictures with The Droplet feature, subjects will pick the more favourable places to park 
their bicycle. 
1. Take 16 pictures of street scenes in Copenhagen, see Section 11.4 (Appendix D) for all 
pictures. 
2. In some of the pictures The Droplet will be present. 
3. In the pictures there will be individually and socially favourable places to park. 
4. The subject will be informed that their final destination is either on screen or close by. 
5. The subject will be presented with each picture for a duration of 6 seconds, and instructed 
to click on the screen where they would park their bicycle. 
6. Should the subject not want to park their bicycle anywhere in the picture, they should 
click outside of the picture. 
7. Should the subject not click at all then no data will be collected. 
8. The location of where the subject clicks on the screen will be recorded. 
9. A total of 30 subjects were selected.

7.5 FINDINGS 
The results show that in all cases The Droplet is recognised as an acceptable place to park. In scenarios 
where there are visibly free slots in the bicycle stands, the feature is still chosen. This suggests that it 
is seen as an easier, hassle-free and more secure option to park in ‘the final inch’ of commercial spaces. 
In all scenarios with The Droplet, it is selected. Sometimes it is the most popular and others it is the 
least popular. What this shows is that the feature may not be suitable for all locations. This relates to 
the findings of Section 3.2.2, where location is an important factor when considering the use of the 
product. Typically, in the more spacious scenarios The Droplet is not that popular. Whereas in more 
spatially constrained scenarios it rises in popularity. This indicates that the feature is better suited to 
the narrow streets, typically where shops are found, which is in accordance with the specified 
environment of the thesis. 
The results from this test only show an indication to possible use. There are many factors which are 
overlooked in this simplified abstraction of reality. What the scenarios do not show is what the 
subjects would do if the bicycle stands, wall spaces, lampposts, or trees already had bicycles parked 
by them. For example if there were no free slots in the latter pictures, where would those respondents 
park their bicycles? In reality there are many permutations of each scenario. An almost infinite amount 
of possibilities could exist, ranging from changes in the weather, how long the bicycle would be left 
for, the cost of the bicycle, what the subject is doing in the given scenario, and many more. But what 
the experiment aims to do is see if the introduction of The Droplet could indicate a behaviour change. 
Overall the results show that The Droplet is noticed, and utilised. Though the number of subjects was 
small, the results do suggest that The Droplet can increase awareness of socially favourable places to 
park bicycles. It suggests that it can influence behaviour and get Copenhageners to choose more 
socially favourable places to park. Particularly in the latter scenarios involving full or almost full bicycle 
stands, The Droplet suggests that it could reduce the number of Copenhageners who would opt to 
overcrowd the bicycle stand. Considering all the data points, Figure 71 shows that 29.7% of subjects 
chose a socially unfavourable place to park their bicycle. However, after the implementation this value 
reduced to 20.4%. In Figure 72, of the 79.6% of subjects who picked a social favourable place to park, 
over half of them chose The Droplet.

7.6 FUTURE PILOT STUDY 
Based on the test conducted in this project, the indicators suggest that The Droplet can influence 
bicycle parking behaviour. It suggests there would be a shift from individual values coming first to 
socially responsible decision making. To confirm what the study suggests, a pilot study would need to 
be executed.  
Two studies should be completed. The first being of the status quo, measuring how many bicycles are 
parked in a socially acceptable and unacceptable ways at specified intervals. Sections of the street 
should also have qualitative observations of behaviour regarding attitudes to parking and subsequent 
satisfaction. The second study would be after the implementation of The Droplet. The same 
parameters would be measured to objectively see if there was a change in behaviour. Such a pilot 
study would also illuminate any unforeseen consequences that could arise from the implementation. 
The study should also aim to identify a clear link to socially favourable parking decisions and 
satisfaction of the infrastructure. 
7.7 CONCLUSIONS OF SECTION 7 
The results from the experiment indicate that The Droplet can influence behaviour, shifting individual 
values to more socially responsible decision making. The effects are realised more in locations which 
are less spacious and with already full or crowded bicycle stands. In these scenarios, subjects are more 
likely to continue overcrowding the stands if The Droplet is not in the picture. When the feature is 
present, fewer subjects chose to overcrowd the stand. However, not every single cyclist chose The 
Droplet which could indicate other factors (such as the O-lock habit and simply not recognising an 
alien product in the urban landscape) but could also be due to the way the scenarios were presented 
on the screen instead of reality. The static pictures are simplifications of dynamic streets, and also do 
not take into account many of the personal factors of the subject. Therefore, the results only indicate 
that the product could be successful. For a more definitive answer a pilot study should be carried out 
to see if The Droplet can influence behaviour on an entire street. Also, a pilot study would illuminate 
any unforeseen consequences. 
Regarding an improvement in satisfaction, the positive words spoken by subjects after the test 
indicate that The Droplet could improve satisfaction. It seems to be an obvious conclusion to make, 
but by The Droplet offering secure and easy to use bicycle parking options within ‘the final inch’ of 
commercial sites, it is likely that Copenhageners would be appreciative and subsequently increase 
their satisfaction.

8 DISCUSSION 
8.1 INTRODUCTION TO THE DISCUSSION 
This section of the thesis will discuss the development of The Droplet in relation to the process it 
followed. It will provide details of any contributions made by the thesis and reflect on any limitations 
from a practical and theoretical point of view. 
8.2 CONTRIBUTIONS OF THE PROJECT 
The contributions made by this project can be broken down into three categories. Firstly, there is the 
literature review and theory. This involves how a research framework was built to provide a rich 
understanding of the problem on a practical and theoretical level. Secondly, there is process. This 
covers how by blending two methodologies, behavioural design and ViP, a novel take on the problem 
could be taken. Finally, there is the product itself. The development of the product which aims to fill 
a gap in the market which was defined, observed and understood by the previous two points. 
8.2.1 
A holistic study through the lens of a framework 
The purpose of the literature review was to understand the narrative which is depicted in the current 
literature. Section 3.2 started with a broad stroke, understanding parameters surrounding bicycle 
parking infrastructure. It linked research and showed that there were four main components which 
should be considered when designing and implementing bicycle parking infrastructure. It also showed 
that almost all plans, projects and designs take a top down perspective, which is key to understanding 
why usage and subsequent satisfaction could be so low. Section 3.3 then utilised this understanding 
and combed through what Copenhagen has done and will do with regards to bicycle parking 
infrastructure. It went one step further into looking into specific behaviours of cyclists and how they 
interact with the infrastructure. Finally, Section 3.4 utilised the prior two sections findings and began 
to find behavioural models and theories which could be used to explain the behaviour of 
Copenhageners. 
By taking these steps in this order, a sound research framework could be built to provide details and 
produce a rich understanding of the problem. The literature review found that commonly, research 
was conducted on individual components of the problem without an understanding of the behaviour. 
What this thesis aimed to do was to take a holistic view of the problem. It aimed to consider how and 
why people interacted with their environment. The observations and data collection were all done in 
accordance to the research framework. It built a structure to operate within and, as a designer, 
indicates which avenues are important given the nature of the problem. For example, it was clear from 
the literature review that social factors, habits and facilitating conditions were big influences on 
individual behaviour. Combining this with social models helped link how government and policy 
created the system and environment that the individual experiences. 
Overall the thesis did exactly what aimed to do. It took a holistic view of the literature surrounding 
the problem, and sought out any gaps in it. Then by creating a research framework, it set out exactly 
how it would fill said gaps. The research framework was specifically designed to research the topic of 
behaviours associated with bicycle parking. However, the framework could be applied to almost any 
problem space which involves the behaviour of the people, the environment they are in, and the 
disconnect between the two. 
The research framework specifically for individual behaviour provided many different avenues to 
pursue. The Theory of Interpersonal Behaviour provided a complex model as the base of the 
==PAGE:97==
85 
 
framework. However, as a designer and based on the literature, some of the complexities could be 
overlooked so that the focus is on the core components. A simpler model, such as the Theory of 
Planned Behaviour could have been used. But as this model did not include the effect of habits, it 
seemed limiting in what it could have provided. The combination of the A-B-C Model and how habits 
work model seemed excessive due to their similarities. With regards to the observations the A-B-C 
Model was very useful in forming a narrative. Whereas the how habits work model needed a level of 
detail which could not be obtained through observations. If a more in-depth study had been 
conducted, the how habits work model could have been used to provide the level of depth. But from 
a naturalistic observational perspective it was not that useful. 
The research framework which focused on social models of behaviour worked as a strong framework 
which easily categorises specific areas. The social ecological model provides discrete integers of 
society. This was particularly useful when deciding on who to interview. In some ways the social 
ecological model was used in a similar way to the A-B-C Model, as it provided a narrative for other 
theories to be used. It was made useful with the combination of the socially responsible design theory 
and the social cognitive theory. These two models provided insights and inspiration for details and 
designs of the product which the social cognitive theory may not have illuminated. 
8.2.2 
A novel take on the problem enabled by the methodology 
With complex problems it is important to select an appropriate methodology which would yield the 
best results. It is equally important to be able to know when to deviate from methods and combine 
them. What this project has shown is that by taking a behavioural approach and combining it with the 
ViP, has enabled a future context which was supported and grounded in human behaviours. Section 4 
followed the behavioural design process, using field studies and data to map the behaviours of cyclists 
to get a rich understanding of their needs, requirements and problems they encounter. The results 
from this section formed the basis of Section 5, where a future context was designed. This future also 
incorporated personal experiences, trends, politics, culture and more. Though the thesis did not follow 
the ViP methodology to its core, the framework was adjusted based on the data that had been 
collected and other research undertaken. By using this methodology, the design process could 
objectively try not to redesign existing products. The bicycle parking infrastructure market is very 
saturated with redesigns and incremental innovations in material and shape. This methodology helped 
push the thesis in a new direction, turning its back on the existing products, to truly come up with 
something new. 
The ViP methodology allowed a jump to be made from the data collection, research and 
considerations into the future. This created a safe space for innovation and creative ideas which have 
a grounding in the actions of today. Key to this is deciding in an appropriate time frame. Picking a date 
10 years or more in the future would have created a much broader solution space, but it would have 
been more hypothetical and conceptual. As the problem that this thesis aimed to solve is very current, 
a more realistic timeframe was chosen. The future context revolved around simplicity. Urban 
environments are becoming increasingly connected and complex, so simple solutions can provide a 
level of elegance and sophistication. As mentioned in Section 5.3, it is the little things which can make 
a difference to the satisfaction of the people. But it was a subjective opinion to revolve around 
simplicity. The future could easily have been that of high technology, connected and interactive. 
However, this would not have fitted with the manual simplicity of bicycles. Though some bicycles are 
becoming electric, at their core they are mechanical machines which are beautiful in their simplicity. 
The future context was designed to reflect this.

8.2.3 
A comprehensive study from the bottom up 
The data collection and observations largely corroborated the findings from the literature review. 
Much of the reviewed literature implies that the reason bicycle parking infrastructure is not or poorly 
used is due to the habit of the O-lock and kickstand. It also suggests that social factors and facilitating 
conditions are key motivators to perpetuating bad parking habits. These behaviours were observed in 
Section 4.5.1. The questionnaire also confirmed some of the findings from the literature review, and 
went further to provide some details as to why these behaviours occurred. These details came from 
areas of questioning which were derived from the research framework. One interesting difference 
was the number of observed cyclists who did not lock their bicycle to something, compared to the 
80% of respondents who said they wanted to be able to lock their bicycle to something. This was one 
of the key differences which motivated the design of The Droplet. There was a clear need for those 
who wanted to lock their bicycle to something, as the existing infrastructure does not easily cater for 
this. 
However, this thesis likened poor parking behaviour to poor satisfaction. This was alluded to in the 
word cloud in Section 4.5.3, where respondents could share their thoughts and feelings towards the 
current infrastructure. A more pragmatic approach could have been to obtain a clear understanding 
of what it is that people are not satisfied about. But this is where the ViP methodology comes in. Based 
on personal experiences and subjective opinions, the link between satisfaction and poor parking 
behaviour could be justified. 
Many of the unexpected results came from the interviews, in particularly finding out that the City of 
Copenhagen were married to the design of the bicycle stand. Their thoughts that it was an identifier 
for Copenhagen had led them to be fixated on that specific design. It is understandable due to the 
cuts to their administration, political pressures and that it is simpler to use the existing design. 
However, this point seemed to counter the literature. Larsen states that a focus on infrastructure 
which does not take into account the behaviour of its use will not be able to understand why it fails. 
As it seems The City of Copenhagen pays no heed to the infrastructure or how Copenhageners interact 
with it when considering the facilitation of new parking infrastructure. 
Something that was found which can add to the literature is the willingness for cyclists to use bicycle 
stands if they are easily accessible and visibly free. The literature showed that bicycle stands in 
Copenhagen are very often overcrowded, but none explained how they got to that point. The results 
of this thesis showed that cyclists will use bicycle stands if it is easy to do so. The overcrowding 
problem starts once a bicycle stand is full, and because there are no other formal pieces of parking 
infrastructure. Moreover, this seems to have been a problem for so long that it is ingrained in habit 
and that bicycle stands just are overcrowded. Copenhageners simply deal with it rather than actively 
try to change it. But that is where this thesis comes in. By understanding this problem, it has aimed to 
design a supplementary product to try to change the current problems experienced. 
Another key point to make is the literatures confusion and use of the term illegal with regards to parkin 
behaviours in Copenhagen. Many of the articles clearly stated that there was an illegal way of parking. 
However, the interviews found that most of these ways of parking were not actually illegal. Only when 
a bicycle is obstructing operational flows or endangering others is it deemed illegal. The rest is far 
better described as socially unfavourable, as it causes inconveniences to society such as obstructing 
pathways. 
8.2.4 
A new product for bicycle parking 
What The Droplet offers is a new product to the family of bicycle parking infrastructure. Rather than 
following the traditional products, it tried to show that there is still space for something new. It also 
==PAGE:99==
87 
 
aims to show that more often than not, simpler is better. Though The Droplet does not support the 
bicycle itself in any way, it utilises the wall it is fixed to. Cyclists very often leave their bicycles resting 
against walls and windows as a fast form of parking. Now with The Droplet, they are able to do so 
securely in close proximity, and in more socially favourable locations. 
Overall the observations and data collection were an effort to put into practice what Larsen stated. 
The thesis put how cycling and parking is practiced at its core to understand the current situation and 
to develop a new product. The product in question was designed to enable safe and secure version of 
the behaviour that already exists, but in a better way. The form of The Droplet is one possibility of 
what it could look like. The product at its core is simply a feature for secure parking. Though products 
which enable similar behaviour do exist, they do not mirror the size of The Droplet as they are more 
similar to the Shopfront Droplet. The Droplet can fit into small spaces, between windows and against 
other pieces of urban furniture. Other products are more similar to the Shopfront Droplet which 
stretch across the fronts of buildings. With these products more bicycles can be securely parked, but 
at the cost of taking up a lot of space. The Droplet provides a unique space for an individual to park 
their bicycle securely within ‘the final inch’ of commercial spaces – particularly in narrow streets. 
With regards to materials, manufacturing and fixings, The Droplet is not yet a finished product. To 
make installation as easy as possible and reduce maintenance costs, The Droplet would likely be bolted 
to the wall. As it is a single formed piece of metal it would be easy to have spares to replace them 
when necessary. With regards to the finish, the features could be painted or enamelled. However, 
painting could cause some of the problems Stine mentioned regarding the replacement of scratched 
components. Whereas enamelling could incur unnecessary costs. An alternative would be to leave it 
in a metallic form, much like the NYC Wrap Rack from Section 5.3.4.4. However, in removing the colour 
the visibility and playfulness of the product may be taken away. 
Fixing the features to the walls of buildings may also cause problems with regards to ownership. The 
product is to be fixed to a building, but the building owners may not want to have them. This could be 
a problem as it could affect the location of the features. Overall, to rectify these points collaboration 
with manufactures, The City of Copenhagen and building owners should take place. 
8.2.5 
An indication that The Droplet will be a success 
The key result from the validation test is that The Droplet is recognised and used in all the scenarios. 
In the crucial scenarios which presented the subject with a full or overcrowded bicycle stand and 
alternative places to park a bicycle, the subjects were influenced by The Droplet to choose a more 
socially favourable place to park their bicycle. Without The Droplet some subjects opted to overcrowd 
the bicycle stand, or impede on pedestrian space by utilising lampposts and signposts. However, in 
the counterpart pictures with The Droplet subjects tended to choose the feature and not the socially 
unfavourable places to park. Though the test was a rudimentary abstraction of reality, it does indicate 
how the implementation of The Droplet can influence bicycle parking behaviour for the better. In 
doing so, more socially favourable bicycle parking decisions at the shops could be increased. How this 
would improve satisfaction is because it would begin to change Copenhageners’ behaviour over time. 
They would choose more socially favourable parking spaces, which would reduce the clutter of 
bicycles. Also, there would be a specific product designed for those who want to lock their bicycle to 
something in close proximity to their final destination.  
Limitations to the test come in the form of how simplified the experiment was. Within each picture 
there could be an almost infinite amount of permutations which could affect behaviour. Most notably 
from research framework in Section 3.5 are the impacts of facilitating conditions. The pictures display 
a static scenario, whereas in reality the entire street would be in motion. The presented scenarios do 
==PAGE:100==
88 
 
not include the effects of weather, time, what the subject is doing, and so on. All these factors could 
change the behaviour of the subject. Moreover, in the counterpart pictures with The Droplet only 
have one of the products in it. Whereas with widespread implementation, Copenhageners would be 
exposed to The Droplet on a regular basis, which could bolster their acceptance and usage. 
Overall, the results from the validation section are only an indication to the possibilities of The Droplet. 
They suggest that The Droplet can influence behaviour for the better. However, further testing is 
needed to objectively confirm that the behaviour change will be widespread, that the feature helps 
improve satisfaction of bicycle parking infrastructure, and expose any unintended consequences.  
8.3 CONCLUSIONS OF SECTION 8 
This section has discussed the results from this thesis in its entirety. The key points to note here are 
that based from the literature review a research framework was created. This framework can be used 
to observe and analyse behaviour in most social settings. It provides the grounds for a holistic view of 
the problem, ranging from the superseding principles of culture, down to the individual attitudes to a 
behaviour. It is then down to the designer to decide which avenues are more important than others. 
This is why it goes hand in hand with the ViP methodology, which also puts an emphasis on personal 
and subjective opinions and decisions made by the designer. 
The use of this framework to ground the data collection in observed behaviour, alongside the ViP 
methodology to project the findings into a realistic future, have enabled the thesis to come up with a 
new concept and product for bicycle parking infrastructure. The Droplet provides safe and secure 
bicycle parking close to the final destination. It came from a comprehensive and novel look on the 
problem of bicycle parking infrastructure. Though it was validated through rudimentary means, the 
results from the experiment do suggest that it could improve the problem of bicycle parking 
infrastructure and subsequent satisfaction. Though the test does not take into account the many 
permutations of possible scenarios, it does provide enough detail to suggest the outcome. 
Overall this thesis supplies a comprehensive look at bicycle parking infrastructure which considers 
different stakeholders, the environment that the infrastructure is in, and most importantly considering 
how people behave in the scenarios. It explores the relationships interlacing these factors and uses 
them to design a product which can fit into the future picture.

9 CONCLUSIONS 
The aim of this thesis was to utilise behavioural design methods to observe the current practice of 
Copenhageners parking and locking their bicycles. Then using this data exploring what the future of 
bicycle parking could be. Finally, with all the previously mentioned factors, designing a product which 
could influence behaviour so that Copenhageners made more socially favourable parking decisions. 
This in turn could increase satisfaction of bicycle parking infrastructure at the shops, as it provides a 
simple, safe and secure place to lock a bicycle with close proximity to the final destination. This idea 
of secure parking within close proximity of commercial areas is something new to Copenhagen, which 
the current infrastructure does not enable. 
The literature review identified key factors which need to be considered when introducing bicycle 
parking infrastructure: location, quantity, type and policy. It then went on to look at Copenhagen, 
specifically looking into the attitudes and behaviours associated with bicycle parking. It found that, in 
Copenhagen, the O-lock has produced a habit of rampant fly-parking. It also showed that there is a 
shift happening towards people wanting to be able to park their bicycle safely and securely. Moreover, 
cycling is something of second nature to Copenhageners, with behaviours acting in the unconscious 
decision-making process. With this knowledge and understanding of the problems that 
Copenhageners experience, different behavioural models and theories were used to build a research 
framework. This framework acted as a lens to observe and collect data on bicycle parking behaviours. 
The framework enabled a holistic view of the problem, which took the stance from prioritising the 
users at the bottom, rather than the traditional designed from above approach which cities so often 
take. 
The observations and data collection largely corroborated the findings from the literature review. 
Unsurprisingly cyclists want secure parking which is easy to use and as close to their final destination 
as possible. These are all things which the current infrastructure rarely accommodates. Interviewing 
members of the Technical and Environmental Administration, who focus on implementing new bicycle 
parking infrastructure, found that the City of Copenhagen is aware of the problems surrounding the 
current bicycle stand. However, due to a historically prestigious design scene and politics surrounding 
cycling, the City of Copenhagen is unlikely to change their current design. They made clear that they 
saw the current bicycle stand as a visual identifier for Copenhagen and that it was a means to keep 
bicycles parked in an orderly fashion. However, they contradicted the latter point by saying that it did 
not actually do this due to overcrowding and the non-tessellating properties of bicycles. Their main 
point was that they were married to the design of the stand and that would not change soon. 
Based on this, the decision was made to instead design a new piece of infrastructure which could 
alleviate some of the problems associated with the current bicycle stand. These problems were 
typically overcrowding, the stand being cumbersome to use, and the inability to easily lock a bicycle 
to the stand. Using the ViP methodology, a future context was imagined where bicycle parking 
infrastructure embodied simplicity, obviousness, frictionless, security and inclusivity. The product 
which was developed from this methodology was The Droplet.  A simple product which aimed to have 
a near zero ability due to its simplicity, and a high motivation due to its ubiquitous placing in the urban 
landscape. It aimed to play its part in solving the problem of low satisfaction of bicycle parking 
infrastructure by offering an alternative to those who typically use the bicycle stands poorly. One of 
the core ideas was that a formal alternative piece of bicycle parking infrastructure would help 
distribute bicycles in a better way. By distributing bicycles in a better way, all parties should enjoy 
easier parking as bicycle stands will be less crowded, and secure parking would be available for those 
who want to use it which is close to their final destination. 
==PAGE:102==
90 
 
A simple experiment was designed to see if The Droplet would be noticed and influence behaviour. 
The results indicate that The Droplet would be noticed and could influence behaviour. In some 
scenarios the results suggest the implementation of The Droplet can reduce the number of bicycles 
which would start to overcrowd bicycle stands. Moreover, The Droplet can influence subjects to not 
park in places which can impede on pedestrian space, and instead tuck their bicycles against the wall 
in more socially favourable places. The experiment showed a 9.3% drop in socially unfavourable 
parking decisions. Though the experiment was a simplified abstraction of reality, it is still an indication 
of The Droplet’s success. 
This thesis represents a behavioural design case study from start to finish. First finding a problem 
space based off motivation, then observing and collecting data to fully define the problem, 
transforming this data into a future context, designing a product to fit said context, and ultimately 
validating the product. Overall, the thesis has shown that with the correct research framework, data 
can be collected by appropriate means, incorporated into a process to come up with a product and 
tested. In the case of The Droplet, the behavioural design approach to redesigning bicycle parking 
infrastructure has produced a product which can influence behaviour to produce more socially 
desirable outcomes. However, the project is not without its limitations, Section 9.1 will present the 
limitations to the project. 
9.1 LIMITATIONS 
Though Copenhagen is an excellent example of a pro-cycling city, it is entrenched in tradition and old 
habits which are not found abroad. The biggest being the O-lock and kickstand enabling fly-parking. 
Also, Copenhagen has a clear lack of policy with regards to bicycle parking. In any other city it is 
common practice to lock bicycles to something, with fly-parking as a last resort. On top of this these 
cities then have parking infrastructure which enable secure locking. There are also clear laws regarding 
where bicycles can and cannot be parked. This limits the exportation of this thesis to other pro-cycling 
cities. However, cities trying to promote cycling can learn from this thesis in seeing some of the 
mistakes Copenhagen has made. This could also be true for pro-cycling cities, as the idea of wanting 
to park close to the final destination holds true for all cyclists. 
Other limitations come from the questionnaire and observations. Only a small population responded 
in the questionnaire and a similar amount observed. Though the observations were representative for 
Copenhagen as a whole, the questionnaire respondents could contain more bias. The cost of the 
respondent’s bicycles was approximately 50% under 2000 DKK and 50% over. This does relate to a 
shift in Copenhageners using alternative locks to the O-lock to secure more expensive bicycles. But it 
also shows that 50% of respondents have expensive bicycles. These people are more likely to want to 
lock their bicycle to something, than those with inexpensive bicycles who do not mind using the bicycle 
stands. Moreover, the observations took place in October and November. Though Copenhageners do 
cycle all year around, it is more popular in the summer months. This means that the snapshot taken 
in thesis may suffer from a smaller number of cyclists than the peak amounts experienced in summer. 
If so, the observations could be tainted as they do not represent the situation operating at full 
capacity. However, this is countered by the use of the ViP methodology as it includes personal 
experiences and trends accumulated over the previous five years. It is also in the personal experience 
where the author could quickly deduce the patterns of behaviour from a relatively small amount of 
data points. 
With regards to the validation results, the test itself was a rudimentary abstraction of reality. It aimed 
only to indicate if The Droplet could do what it is set out to do. The simplicity of the scenarios did not 
take into account permutations of facilitating conditions, socials factors or attitudes of the subject. 
==PAGE:103==
91 
 
Moreover, the scenarios were presented as a static image of what would be a dynamic street. 
Typically, the subject would be exposed to an entire street and they would choose where to park 
based on previous experiences and what they were presented in that given street. By showing them 
a static image of a small section of a street, it is hard to make an accurate choice which reflects 
individual behaviour. This is why the thesis only states that the results suggest and indicate that The 
Droplet could work in reality, not that it will. 
9.2 FUTURE WORK 
What was learnt from the validation experiment is something that could be implicated into a future 
study. To get a more concrete understanding of The Droplet’s possible success, statistical analysis on 
the experiment results should be carried out. For this the same test could be recreated, but with better 
imagery. The two images for each scenario must be identical, asides the implementation of The 
Droplet. No new places to park a bicycle should be introduced, as this would tamper with the ability 
to see if there is statistical significance. One way of achieving this would be to model each scenario to 
remove background noise and distractions. This way there would be more control over the 
behavioural choices made by the subjects. 
To truly see if The Droplet does exactly what it is supposed to do a pilot study should be conducted. 
Taking a sample street and collecting data on how many bicycles use it, how many are parked inside 
and out of the stands, where people park most, and so on, will provide a baseline to the study. Then 
the same data should be recorded after the implementation of The Droplet. The recordings should be 
taken at regular intervals throughout the day and into the night, this way a truly representative 
snapshot of Copenhagen could be achieved. This would provide objective data to support or nullify 
The Droplet. It would also illuminate any unforeseen consequences of widespread implementation. 
Given this is unlikely to happen, the next legitimate step for this thesis is its presentation at the DTU 
Transport Summit 2019. Here the project will be presented in the hope that it can start a conversation 
of way we look at problems such as bicycle parking, and how we try to solve them. 
9.3 FINAL WORDS 
This thesis has shown that with the correct framework, acting as a lens into how and why people do 
things, we can gain useful insights into problems that have tried to be solved many times over. The 
specific use of methodologies can also help provide new ways of understanding data and using it in 
unique and novel ways. The problem of bicycle parking infrastructure has created a saturated market 
of products which are more or less the same. Though functionally The Droplet developed in this thesis 
does the same as other products on the market, it aims to be a novel part of the solution, and not be 
the solution.

11.2 APPENDIX B: TRANSCRIBED INTERVIEWS 
11.2.1 Interview with Stine (Byens Fysik) 
 
1. For how long has the status quo been to use the VESKØ bicycle stand and for what reason? 
“Since before I have started, I have been here for 4 years. But to start in another place, I think it makes 
perfect sense to use the same kind of rack. If you look at the town or if you look at the things that we 
use in the town to identify. For example, in Fredericksburg they use different colours on their city 
equipment. So, they try to make themselves different from Copenhagen, so they use the green colour 
and we more or less have the blue colour. But on the racks we do not use colour anymore because it 
is too expensive to maintain them in terms of repainting damages. From time to time we do use 
coloured racks, it depends on the characteristic of the area. There is a design manual which 
determines if we use this colour, that colour or no colour. 
So the reason we use this standard equipment on 90% of the cases or city, is because we want and 
have these overall policies. We used to have a design manual and now we have a design guideline, 
that tells us what kind of equipment we should use. When we don’t use this standard equipment is 
when the characteristics of the place tells us to do something different. For example, at Nørreport 
there is a different type of rack, and by central station we have a new project which will use the same 
rack. But it would not make sense to use that rack not near a train station. So, we use the equipment 
to define how one place looks and feels from one place to another.” 
“So, the racks are quite a visual identifier for Copenhageners” 
“Yes. Visual in that sense that it is also the bike racks should be as anonymous as possible. The function 
is the importance of it. So for our bike rack the design is not so noisy. And that is the thought about 
bike racks in Copenhagen, the importance is the function, and the in some places and special areas 
we use a different type.” 
“So basically when we get a new project we use the standard rack. But every time we make a new 
project we look at the specific place and what makes sense here. We have a lot of project managers 
who are working on these projects, and in the beginning it is a very easy way to go around and decide 
what things should be in the area (using the guidelines). But if it makes sense in a place with special 
historical importance or very modern look then it makes to think about should we do something 
different. So it helps us make the city look the same, but we can also use the racks to make one place 
look different.” 
“Also with the function of the rack, it is supposed to be something that you just do. You shouldn’t have 
to think about how to use it. So that is why we think that it makes sense to use the same rack. We 
have an agreement with this company for four years, and every four years the contract is renewed. 
But if another company wins the contract they will still be making the same rack. We are not married 
with the company but we are married with the design.” 
“Do you compromise the use case functionality to prioritise aesthetics?” 
“No, we find a compromise between the two. For example, in really narrow streets we would rather 
(in our position working with the bike parking), we would rather put it out and occupy a car parking 
space. But we are not allowed to do that as we want to, we would have to get a political approval for 
that. But that would make it more visible to put it out like that, rather than hiding it by the building.” 
==PAGE:115==
103 
 
“Comparing to other cities such as Amsterdam and London, where inverted U racks are used, my 
research has found that most other cities prefer those style of racks. Copenhagen is the only one which 
applauds the grid rack.” 
“Maybe, our experience of using bike racks which you rest your bike against, it makes the look messy, 
and also the bikes get scratched by that rack. We have not worked with any racks like that. We work 
with making one rack which is good for all bikes. We added in this (butterfly) feature to protect bike 
lights which are attached to the wheel, as these have become very popular recently. So it is both like 
design and function. We also change the spacing of the angled racks, however we found the added 
space is not as useful, so we try to only use the 90°. But sometimes we have to use the angled ones as 
there is not a lot of space. But that is a case where we think of function over aesthetics. In some cases 
it is better to have bikes without racks.” 
“The Copenhagen style of rack likens bikes to cars, where they want to be parked in a uniform manner. 
However, bikes are promoted as a hyper flexible form of mobility, which contradicts the style of parking 
which the infrastructure can cater for. Do you agree?” 
“Yes, but I am also thinking about how these racks are maintained. The maintenance teams have 
different types of machinery, and when they have to clean and sweep up, they are very interested in 
the things that we put up. There was a case where in the beginning the racks were placed a bit too 
low, so the machine could not get in under the rack. So we had to elevate it a bit, and these issues are 
very important in a public administration. So when you are designing and producing a rack, you want 
to add all these possibilities for the biker, that is nice but not doable. Because we have to make it easy 
for the people who clean the streets as well. And if someone has broken one of these racks, then we 
have to replace it. As it is right now we have a lot of stock out here which we can use to replace and 
repair racks.” 
“We get contacted a lot with some really good ideas, but we simply cannot do them. Sometimes we 
get money from the politicians but it usually only for a trial. It is very difficult to get a trial to get into 
the guidelines. These guidelines tell us what to do.” 
2. Can you elaborate on what it means when you say, “it makes perfect sense to use the same type 
of bike parking rack”? 
 
3. What do you identify as the key problems with the VESKØ bike rack? And what “bad” behaviours 
do you associate with this? 
 
4. What constraints are imposed from the streets when implementing new bike racks in 
Copenhagen? 
“Our main problem is that 1 bike takes up approximately 1 m2. They have tried to make vertical bike 
racks or make one with a bench or flowers, but it doesn’t give us more space. And our basic problem 
in the inner city is that we do not have enough space. So what to do we have made an analysis of 
potentially using construction sites to for bike parking, it could be inside a building, underground or in 
a silo over ground. And the conclusion from that is that it is doable if we can find some free area in 
the air or underground. But it is very costly per bike. It is 100,000 kr for 1 bike if we do it underground. 
And the thing about main a silo in the city is that we don’t have space for it and it would ruin the 
aesthetic.” 
==PAGE:116==
104 
 
“We have a city architect who has an overview of how everything would look, and she would make it 
impossible to create a silo in the inner city.” 
“Would you consider introducing a new law to restrict where and how people can park their bikes?” 
“No, that would not happen. Politically people want to promote riding and parking anywhere. And 
bike parking is not a big issue. It is important that people can bike, so I don’t think that in this political 
climate they would say that this is something we should try to work on. First of all the law does not 
give us any movement in this direction.” 
5. How do you decide on how many bike spaces to create when implementing new bike racks? 
 
6. What kind of studies, if any, have been done to look at the behaviours of cyclists in the context 
of parking? 
“In Holland there are cases of train stations with places where you cannot park your bike, and there is 
a person who will move you bike if you park there. However, if there is no one to move your bike, 
people will park in that space. For cyclists to park 30m away from a train station is very far, but by car 
that would be amazing.” 
“People feel like they have the right to park wherever they want, and they do! And who is it a problem 
for that bikes are parked everywhere, people who are visually handicapped, people with trolleys for 
kids, and stuff like that. And then it is a visual problem, sometimes it doesn’t look very nice. Sometimes 
when we are in the inner city it looks so horrible, but we can’t fix it. There are just more and more 
bikes coming to Copenhagen.” 
“What would be useful would be for politicians to allow us to use car parking spaces for bikes.” 
7. What type of construction would constitute the design of a new rack? Is it purely aesthetical or 
functional? 
 
8. Are the flex parking or bike stand-less zones still in operation or were they just tests? If so what 
were the results from them? 
“According to the law for a flex parking space there has to be some kind of person to remove bikes 
from the space when the time is up. And that works with schools as they have some kind of janitor, 
but it is still only a trial. I think they are working okay, but you can’t use that kind of thing in the inner 
city as you need a person to work the spot. In some places there are just parking spaces, for any mode 
of transport, and these have usually been adopted by cyclists. But even if we could get rid of the cars 
in the inner city, we would then need to get the approval from the police. And they are not keen on 
us changing car places to bike spaces, as it restricts how they can get around. Also they are concerned 
that drivers will spend more time looking for a parking space, whereas we think that people won’t 
come into the city if they know there are no places to park.”

11.2.2 Interview with Kira (Cykelhåndtering) 
 
1. What problems do you face when handling abandoned bicycles? Does the VEKSØ rack in 
question play a role? 
“The main problem is that they take up space, which could be used for another bike or for something 
else. The fact that the bike is locked to something is not really a problem, because if a bike is 
abandoned then we just cut it.” 
“I don’t know if the rack itself influences abandoned bikes, but it does influence bad and good parking 
behaviour. This is because the rack doesn’t fit some bikes, and that is why it can be a case of annoyance 
and irritation. And it also has a lot of influence that also the fact that it looks uniform and ordered, but 
because of this it is really easy to put a bike in between two others. So it isn’t all negative but it isn’t 
all positive.” 
2. What kind of impacts, economics or otherwise, do you feel when handling abandoned 
bicycles? 
 
3. What do you consider wrongly parked bicycles? And are your operations limited to certain 
areas? 
“There are different ways to look at it. There illegal parked bikes, but the municipalities actually are 
not allowed to do anything about it, because that is the polices department and the law of traffic. 
Then we see the wrongly parked bikes, that could be illegal as well, but it could also just be clusters of 
bikes in the wrong places. In terms of pedestrian flows, security flows/areas around. It could also be 
regarding operational flows. If we have to sweep or clean in the street. But mainly, wrongly parked 
bikes are in areas like in the inner city, or around stations. So, where you have too many bikes and if 
first one parks their bike a certain way (which is the wrong way) people tend to do the same. It is 
exactly the same with littering. If one person litters, then others will.” 
“Many expensive bikes are being placed parallel to the road, blocking multiple spaces, because if you 
park the bike in the rack, the wheel could be stolen, or the frame could be stolen. And if people see a 
bike parked like that, then they tend to park messily around it. And it creates a visual mess.” 
“I was thinking about one thing that influences the way people in Copenhagen bike and park their 
bikes. I think it will only be stronger later on. I think that catering for Copenhageners as cyclists creates 
an influence between cyclist to cyclist whilst on a path. When on a bike lane there is a specific 
behaviour which is me against you. But when we are talking about taking over street or something like 
that, we are us versus the cars/drivers. We have seen it a few places where you have a street with 
shops and pavements, and you have bike parking next to a car parking. You will find bikers taking over 
the car parking space, because it is too difficult for the driver to move all the bikes. I think that because 
we have such a strong cyclist culture and we get locked into our head that it is much better than cars. 
And we are strong environmentalists because of cycling, then as individuals we tend to tell ourselves 
that it is okay to park badly as at least I don’t pollute/’I am picking the better/healthier option’. So not 
parking in the most socially responsible way is not that important as cycling is already doing something 
good in society. I think there will be more of people parking wherever they want, or cyclists taking 
over car parking spaces. So I think it is more important for politicians to advocate for turning car 
parking spaces into bike parking spaces, as the cyclists will take it anyway.”

4. What are the key design features of the current design which make it appealing for regular 
use? 
“The height from the bar that is running across needs to be a certain height (check the current design 
sketches). Also, if it is too low then it acts as a net to collect paper and litter. Already wheels act as a 
net for this stuff.” 
5. What kind of legislation are you trying to push to try and influence bicycle parking behaviour? 
“I think there is still a trial, but we had the possibility to move bikes within 300m if there is enough 
parking racks or room for parking within that area. And if we put up enough sings showing where the 
bikes are being moved to and which area the bikes can be moved from. The reason we wanted to try 
it at Nørreport was because of bikes being parked in security flows (fire escapes). But by moving bikes 
it just lead to more people parking their bikes in the wrong space as it was a free space. We found that 
it is really difficult to notice text heavy signs. Most cyclists do not have a driving licence so do not even 
know to look at these types of (road) sings. We found that it only works if you do it every day and 
don’t stop. Then people get used to having their bike moved if they don’t do it correctly. There was 
no success from this trial in terms of something we could do occasionally to keep bad bike parking 
down.” 
“Do you think that a policy like this could work in commercial streets?” 
“I think it would take a huge campaign and many years before people would know that this is 
something that is happening. And I think that that is better in Amsterdam where you have overall 
restrictions and then in some places there are exceptions. It would cost a lot of money to enforce and 
would it actually pay off? Probably not.” 
6. If a new rack design was being considered, what requirements or concerns would you have 
regarding it? 
“I think that the less material the better. We don’t clean the rack itself, but we repair and maintain 
them, so more material means more stuff. So, to use as few parts as possible is best. But also, how it 
is fixed to the ground/wall needs to be cheap/simple. For example, the new stands at Nørreport must 
cost a lot to fix them into the ground. But those are really designed in terms of operations, though 
they are placed in a basin, if they were on flat ground I think that it would be a very good solution.” 
7. Would the ability for a cyclist to secure their bicycle to a rack have an impact on the work your 
department does? 
“Well there have been so many bike thefts in Copenhagen, and it isn’t the expensive bikes getting 
stolen! And because so many people have had bikes stolen, people don’t tend to buy expensive bikes. 
And that make influence how people take care of them. Me personally, I have a chain, I don’t care 
how it looks I just don’t want my bike to be stolen because it is so annoying.” 
“I almost always use a rack because it is always windy, and I don’t want my bike being blown over and 
something broken on it. I think it is funny that they choose such a cumbersome rack. When you just 
have a bike wide bike it is really difficult to use. Especially if you have groceries or something, it is not 
a nice thing to use. You don’t feel ‘yay I’m going to park and go off’. It is always so irritating, you have 
to push other bikes out of the way, or lift your handlebars over. I think the reason we are so into 
minimising the space that we use for bike parking is because it is not only about bikes. Every time they 
have to find a space to park bikes, they have to fight other stakeholders. It could be a tree, or a café
