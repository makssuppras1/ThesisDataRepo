Abstract 
This study has investigated the added value of buried electrodes in direct current (DC) resistivity 
measurements to traditional surface setups using synthetic modelling. The focus has been on 
resolving clay blocks in sand covered by a thick clay layer. This corresponds to conditions at a 
selected field site in China, where arsenic contaminated groundwater is thought to originate from 
clay lenses enriched with organic matter. The synthetic modelling and inversion have been done in 
AarhusInv, using the least-squares method for the inversion. All modelling has been done with a 
view to using the conclusions in field work. 
   The tested subsurface models all have a top clay layer with a thickness of 15 meters and 
resistivity 10 m above a sand layer with resistivity 200 m. Clay blocks placed in the sand 
generally have a resistivity of 10 m, but a value of 50 m has also been tested. The tests have 
been based on the gradient array using a borehole-to-surface (B2S) setup with 32 electrodes in the 
borehole and 32 on the surface. A dipole-dipole array was initially tested, but was discarded due to 
low-quality results compared to the gradient array. 
   It has been found that the buried electrodes have an advantage over surface setups. When the 
clay block was placed 15 meters below the top clay layer, a traditional surface gradient array could 
not resolve it. At this depth, it was accurately resolved with the B2S gradient array when it was 
next to the borehole. However, moving the clay block further from the borehole quickly decreases 
the resolution. Even at 20 meters from the borehole, it could not be clearly distinguished from 
artefacts in the subsurface. Increasing the size of the clay block improved the resolution further 
from the borehole, but the area of investigation most likely has a maximum distance from the 
borehole of 20-30 meters. Increasing the resistivity of the block from 10 m to 50 m severely 
decreases the resolution of the clay block, even when it is close to the borehole. The upper 
boundary of the clay block is consistently placed too low in the inversion results, but the 
horizontal position is generally approximated, when the block is not placed too deep or far from 
the borehole. 
   The tests in this study have been made with a relative standard deviation (STD) on the data of 
2.5%. The noise level was found to have a large impact on the results. An STD of 0% significantly 
increased the resolution of structures further from the borehole, while increasing the STD to 5% 
and 10% reduced the resolution, even of structures close to the borehole. The depth of 
investigation was also strongly affected by the noise level.

Sammenfatning 
I denne opgave er det blevet undersøgt, hvordan elektroder i boringer forbedrer resultaterne 
sammenlignet med traditionelle overfladeopsætninger i direct current (DC) resistivitets-målinger. 
Dette er gjort ved hjælp af syntetisk modellering. Det primære fokus har været på at finde 
lerblokke i et sandlag, der er dækket af et tykt lerlag. Dette svarer til forholdene i et udvalgt 
feltområde i Kina, som er påvirket af arsenforurenet grundvand. Hypotesen er, at forureningen 
stammer fra lerblokke, der er beriget med organisk materiale. Den syntetiske modellering og 
inversion med de mindste kvadraters metode er blevet lavet i AarhusInv. Al modellering har været 
med henblik på at bruge konklusionerne i senere feltarbejde. 
   Forskellige modeller af undergrunden er blevet sat op. De har alle et 15 meter tykt lerlag med 
resistivitet på 10 m over sand med en resistivitet på 200 m. Lerblokke placeret i sandet har 
som udgangspunkt en resistivitet på 10 m, men en resistivitet på 50 m i blokkene er også 
blevet undersøgt. De syntetiske målinger er blevet udført med gradient arrayet i en boring-til-
overflade (BtO) konfiguration. I første omgang blev et dipole-dipole array også undersøgt, men 
dette gav dårligere resultater end gradient arrayet og blev derfor ikke undersøgt yderligere. Der er 
32 elektroder i boringen og 32 på overfladen. 
  Sammenlignet med traditionelle overfladearrays, kan det konkluderes, at resultaterne forbedres, 
når boringselektroder inkluderes. Når lerblokken befinder sig 15 meter under det overliggende 
lerlag, kan den ikke længere findes af et overfladebaseret gradientarray ikke kan finde den. Et BtO 
gradient array kan til gengæld finde den rimelig præcist med et BtO gradient array, når blokken 
befinder sig lige ved boringen. Flyttes den længere væk, forværres resultatet dog hurtigt. Blot 20 
meter fra boringen kan blokken ikke længere adskilles fra generelle ujævnheder i resultatet. Gøres 
blokken større, forbedres resultatet længere fra boringen, men det tyder på, at området, hvori 
lerblokke kan findes, er indenfor 20-30 meter fra boringen. Øges resistiviteten af lerblokken fra 10 
m til 50 m, forværres resultatet drastisk, selv hvis blokken befinder sig lige ved boringen. 
Lerblokkens øvre grænse placeres konsekvent for dybt i inversionsresultaterne, men dens 
horisontale position kan fastslås, når den ikke er placeret for dybt eller for langt fra boringen. 
   I dette studium er der blevet tilføjet støj i form af en relativ standardafvigelse (STD) på 2.5% til 
dataene. Støjen havde stor effekt på resultaterne, og en STD på 0% gav et væsentligt bedre resultat 
for lerblokke længere fra boringen. Omvendt gav en STD på 5% og 10% tydeligt dårligere 
resultater, selv for blokke tæt ved boringen. Måledybden var også stærkt afhængig af støjniveauet.

1 Introduction 
Many environmental applications depend on knowledge of the subsurface and its variations. In 
groundwater studies, information about the geometry and conductivity of aquifers is required. 
Traditionally, such information has been found using boreholes, but this is a costly and invasive 
approach and one that only gives information about a limited area. This is especially an issue as the 
groundwater dynamics are strongly dependent of the heterogeneity of the aquifers, which may vary 
even over a smale scale. Geophysics provide an alternative in the form of methods for mapping the 
subsurface without invasive technologies. They provide a cost-effective way of mapping large areas, 
while at the same time minimising the impact of the investigation. Geophysics have been used in 
mineral and petroleum industries since the middle of the previous century, but within the last decades 
they have been developed more and more for environmental purposes. Geophysical methods have 
several environmental applications, such as the assessment of water quality, delineation of 
contamination plumes, aquifer zonation, etc. This led to the rise of hydrogeophysics as a separate 
field in the 1990’s. Hydrogeoophysics is an umbrella term covering several different methods. 
Hubbard & Rubin (2005) define it as geophysical methods used to map subsurface features or 
determine properties and processes of the subsurface that are relevant for hydrological studies. These 
can include direct current (DC) resistivity measurements, induced polarisation (IP), the 
electromagnetic method (EM), and seismic methods, to mention a small selection (Binley, et al., 
2015).  
   Traditionally, hydrogeophysics have been put to much the same use as geophysics in the petroleum 
industries; determining lithological boundaries, thereby confirming and extending conclusions from 
boreholes. This is no doubt relevant as it can help define the geometry of aquifers, but current 
developments in hydrogeophysics attempt to get more information about the subsurface than just the 
geology. Some methods, such as DC, are sensitive to the movement of water, and the IP method is 
used to delineate contamination plumes. 
   There are attempts to link geophysical parameters directly to hydrological parameters – for 
instance, electrical conductivity and hydraulic conductivity. While such a relationship may be found, 
it is generally site-specific and uncertain and therefore cannot necessarily be applied on a greater 
scale. Another approach, which has had more success, is to link the geophysical observations to 
hydrological state variables, such as moisture content, and determine the hydrological parameters 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
2 
 
through calibration. Determining if a dependable link between geophysics and hydrology can be 
defined is one of the big challenges in hydrogeophysics. 
   Other issues in the field of hydrogeophysics include scale and resolution. While the scale of 
geophysical measurements is much larger than that of boreholes, most geophysical methods are still 
restricted to a scale smaller than that of many hydrological applications. Furthermore, there is often a 
trade-off between scale and resolution, with methods covering larger scales having less resolution. A 
recently developed method for covering a larger area is an airborne EM setup. This is possible 
because the EM method, as opposed to most other geophysical methods, does not require galvanic 
contact with the soil (Binley, et al., 2010). 
   An ongoing challenge in geophysics comes from the concept of non-uniqueness in the data 
processing. The subsurface model corresponding to the measured geophysical data is generally found 
through an iterative process known as inversion. Forward response is generated based on a model of 
the subsurface, which is subsequently updated based on the misfit between generated and measured 
data. Geophysical inversion is non-unique, meaning that several different models may fit the same 
data. The number of possible models can be limited by including prior knowledge, which can come 
from local boreholes, pumping tests, and other information about the subsurface. 
1.1 DC resistivity modelling    
This study will focus on the DC method with the aim of resolving subsurface structures. A DC 
measurement is done by generating electric current in the ground and it is used to determine the 
subsurface resistivity. Figure 1-1 shows two popular surface arrays. The C-electrodes are current 
electrodes, which generate an electric circuit in the ground, and the P-electrodes are potential 
electrodes which measure the voltage between two points. As the injected current is known and the 
potential is measured, the resistance of the ground can be calculated. If the geometry of the 
measurement setup is known, the resistivity can be calculated from the resistance. 
 
Figure 1-1: Two popular surface arrays. C1 and C2 are current electrodes, which generate an electric circuit in the ground, and P1 
and P2 are potential electrodes which measure the voltage. 
 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
3 
 
   Given that different soil types have different resistivities, the DC method is ideal for mapping 
subsurface structures. Mapping the resistivity of the subsurface and comparing with information from 
a borehole enables the ‘translation’ from resistivity to soil type. However, as the resistivity of a given 
soil type can fall within a wide range, a local borehole is necessary for such a conversion. The 
method is also sensitive to water flow and can be used to determine the depth of the water table. 
Because of the different resistivities of fresh and salt water, DC measurements are ideal for finding 
boundaries between these two types of groundwater.  
   There are several examples of application of DC investigations, both environmental and otherwise. 
Loke (2002) describes the use of DC measurements to find caves in a limestone environment. As the 
air is resistive to electrical current, the caves can be found as anomalies with high resistivity. He also 
gives an example of using the DC method for the delineation of a contamination plume from a 
contaminated site. The contamination is found as a low resistivity anomaly which can be delineated 
by geophysical methods. Doetsch, et al., (2015) have investigated the change in resistivity when the 
ground freezes in the Arctic. 
   As with other geophysical methods, the resolution of DC measurements decreases with depth. An 
option for increasing resolution in deeper parts of the subsurface is inserting electrodes on cables in 
boreholes. This is typically done as cross-borehole investigations, where electrodes are inserted into 
two boreholes and measurements made between them. Danielsen & Dahlin (2010) make both 
synthetic and field tests with cross-borehole in relation to a tunnel project and find that the method is 
able to distinguish between different rock types. Bing & Greenhalgh (2000) have investigated 
different setups for cross-borehole setups. There has been less work done on single borehole to 
surface measurements, but Tsourlos, et al., (2011) have done synthetic tests on a number of such 
setups. 
1.2 Study purpose  
In this study, synthetic data will be used to investigate the capability of the borehole DC resistivity 
method in resolving subsurface structures; in particular, the focus will be on resolving clay blocks in 
sand underneath a top clay layer. This corresponds to conditions at a selected field site, the Hetao 
Basin in China, where arsenic contaminated groundwater is thought to originate from clay lenses in 
the aquifers. 
   With surface measurements being the traditional method for resolving subsurface structures, there 
have been limited investigations using borehole electrodes. This study will investigate the possible 
added value of borehole electrodes compared to traditional surface measurements. Furthermore, the

2 Study area 
The synthetic data is based on conditions in the Hetao Basin in Inner Mongolia (China), which has 
inspired the subsurface models. The Hetao Basin is severely affected by arsenic contaminated 
groundwater. Studies have found concentrations up to 1000 μg/L in this area (Deng, et al., 2009). 
This value is significantly higher than the Chinese threshold value for drinking water of 10 μg/L, 
which is in compliance with the value set by the WHO (Guo, et al., 2008) (WHO, 2016). Studies of 
this area suggest that the arsenic is released in reducing environments caused by the biodegradation 
of organic carbon. A hypothesis is that the source of organic carbon is clay and silt lenses in the 
subsurface (Stolze, et al., n.d.). Many of the area’s clay lenses are enriched with organic matter, 
possibly as a result of parts of the basin being formed in lacustrine environments (Guo, et al., 2008) 
(Guo, et al., 2011). Therefore, a way to determine where such lenses are located could potentially be 
a tool to avoid drilling for groundwater in arsenic contaminated areas. This study investigates 
geophysics with a focus on borehole DC measurements as a possible method of finding the clay 
lenses.  
   The study site and its location are shown in Figure 2-1. It is a smaller part of the Hetao Basin which 
has been chosen as there are ongoing arsenic investigations in this area (e.g. Guo, et al. (2008), Guo, 
et al. (2011), and several others). DTU has a project in the area which aims to resolve clay blocks 
using geophysical methods and comparing the positions with arsenic concentrations (Stolze, et al., 
n.d.). They may utilise borehole-to-surface DC measurements in the future. This study was originally 
meant to be part of that project.  
   The entire Hetao Basin has an area of around 10,000 km2 and its elevation varies between 1007 m 
and 1060 m. It is bordered by the Yellow River to the south, the Langshan mountains to the north, 
and the Wuranbuh Desert to the west (Guo, et al., 2008). The annual average precipitation is 130-220 
mm, about 70% of which falls in the monsoon season of July to September. The annual evaporation 
is much higher, about 2000-2500 mm (Stolze, et al., n.d.) (Xu, et al., 2010). This indicates that most 
of the available water in the basin stems from groundwater flowing from the mountains. Some water 
is also drawn from the Yellow River. This is mainly for irrigation, and the main source of drinking 
water is groundwater (Deng, et al., 2009) (Guo, et al., 2012). 
   The Hetao Basin is a rift basin formed in the late Jurassic Cenozoic period (Xu, et al., 2010). The 
mountains are mainly formed by metamorphic rock such as slate, gneiss, and marble, which is folded 
and fractured (Guo, et al., 2008).

The groundwater aquifers can be divided into deep and shallow, where the deep aquifers are not 
relevant for the water supply. The shallow aquifers can be divided into alluvial-pluvial (formed by 
deposition of sediment from rivers and rainfall) and alluvial-lacustrine (deposition from rivers and 
lakes). Generally, the alluvial-pluvial aquifers are unconfined and found in the belt of alluvial fans 
and at river banks at depths of 5 to 20 m. The alluvial-lacustrine aquifers are leaky-confined and 
found at depths of 10-100 m. In the transition between these two the groundwater quality is bad, and 
here the highest arsenic concentrations are found (Guo, et al., 2008). The aquifer sediments generally 
consist of silt, silty sand, and fine sand. The natural groundwater flow is slow, but the flow is affected 
locally by irrigation and drainage channels (Guo, et al., 2011). 
   Groundwater is recharged through infiltrating precipitation and through fractured rock in the 
mountains. Some water also comes from infiltration from rivers and reinfiltration of irrigation water 
(Deng, et al., 2009). 
2.1 Geochemistry 
Drinking water is among the dominant pathways for arsenic (As) exposure to humans. Especially 
groundwater constitutes a problem as the interaction with minerals means more potential for 
releasing arsenic. Furthermore, the likelihood of finding the conditions conducive to arsenic release is 
larger in aquifers than in other sources. Arsenic is naturally occurring in many soils. It can be 
released by natural processes, such as weathering, but is also very often a problem occurring after 
anthropogenic interference. This human interference often takes the shape of mining or (too much) 
groundwater abstraction. In Northern China, including the Hetao Basin, 20 million people are 
exposed to arsenic contaminated drinking water. The groundwaters in these areas have high pH-
values, leading to desorption of arsenic, and there are strong reducing conditions in the aquifers. 
Under strong reducing condition the version of arsenic called As(III) is released, which is both more 
toxic and more mobile than As(V) (Onses, et al., 2015). 
   The groundwater in the Hetao Basin holds arsenic concentrations up to 1000 μg/L, and these high 
concentrations have been shown to be associated with the strong reducing conditions in the aquifers 
(Deng, et al., 2009). The highest concentrations are found in the alluvial lacustrine aquifers, where 
there is a reducing environment. Guo, et al., (2008) found that in Inner Mongolia alone, more than 
400,000 people were drinking water with arsenic concentrations above 50 μg/L, and as a result 3000 
patients of arsenic poisoning have been discovered, spread over 776 villages. In some parts of Inner 
Mongolia, arsenic has been released to the groundwater through mining, but in the Hetao Basin the 
==PAGE:22==

presence of arsenic is believed to have natural causes. Concentrations between 1.1 and 969 μg/L have 
been found in the shallow groundwaters of Hetao Basin. Up to 90% of this is present as As(III). 
   All aquifers with high As concentration have layers of the fine, dark grey sand and anaerobic water. 
It is assumed that these aquifers are the remains of lake estuaries where water flow gradually became 
stagnant during the Pleistocene period. In contrast, aquifers without As generally have lightbrown 
sands from a fluvial environment (Guo, et al., 2008). 
   Jia, et al., (2014) divide the study area into three redox zones; recharge-oxic zone (I), groundwater 
flow-moderate reducing zone (II), and groundwater flow-reducing zone (III). In zone I, which is at 
the foot of the mountains, there are generally low occurrences of arsenic, while the highest rate of 
arsenic occurrences are found in zone III, located in the flat plain. Zone II, located in between, has 
moderate arsenic occurrences. This supports the hypothesis that arsenic is released in reducing 
environments. The locations of the zones are shown in Figure 2-1.

3 Theoretical background of DC measurements 
This section describes the theory behind direct current (DC) measurements. DC measurements are 
basically a method for measuring the resistivity of the subsurface. The basic principles include four 
electrodes planted in the ground, known as a quadrupole. Two of these electrodes are current 
electrodes, which create a current loop in the subsurface, while the two potential electrodes measure 
the voltage difference between their two positions. As the voltage is measured and the injected 
current is known, the resistance can be calculated, and from this the apparent resistivity can be 
calculated. The difference between resistivity, apparent resistivity, and resistance must be stressed. 
Resistance, as given by Ohm’s Law, is voltage over current; this means that the value will change if 
the measurement geometry is changed, and it is therefore not an inherent property of the subsurface. 
Resistivity, on the other hand, can be defined as the resistance per unit volume, and is an inherent 
property of the soil type (Jones, 2007). 
   The actual resistivity of the different parts of the subsurface can only be found through inversion. 
This involves creating a subsurface resistivity model from which synthetic data is calculated. The 
model is updated iteratively, aiming to minimise the misfit between modelled and measured data. 
The inversion process is explained in more detail in section 5. However, the apparent resistivity can 
be found without inversion. The apparent resistivity is the resistivity that a homogeneous subsurface 
would have to give the same measured potential as observed in the study.

4 DC data acquisition 
Section 0 has described the principles behind a single quadrupole in a theoretical manner. A normal 
DC survey is done with hundreds or thousands of quadrupoles, collected into an array. In theory, the 
build-up of the array can be completely random, but there are some typical arrays that are used for 
their data acquisition capacities as well as time consumption. Which array to use is largely dependent 
on the geology to be mapped as well as the objective of the survey. Since the surface DC method is a 
well-established method, the capabilities of the typical surface arrays are well documented. This 
section starts by reviewing the knowledge of surface arrays, as this forms the basis for the further 
development of borehole-to-surface (B2S) arrays; following this is an overview of some of the 
studies that have been done in B2S and cross-borehole DC resistivity modelling. The measuring 
principles of a DC survey are described, followed by a description of the ABEM Terrameter LS, 
which will be used for potential future field investigations. Finally, a brief introduction to noise 
sources in a DC survey is given, as well as a description of how this is handled in the synthetic 
modelling. 
4.1 Arrays 
Generally, a larger electrode spacing gives a deeper measurement, but also a lower data quality. For 
this reason, it is important to incorporate quadrupoles with both a small and large spacing to get a 
good coverage of the subsurface. An example where the quadrupoles are defined according to the 
Wenner array is shown in Figure 4-1. For this array type, the relative spacing between the four 
electrodes is always equal, and the measurement runs through the different possible combinations: 
First all combinations with a spacing of a, then all combinations with a spacing of 2a, and so on.   
Using this method along the entire cable gives a triangular measuring area as shown in the figure as 
there are fewer possible electrode combinations for larger spacing and therefore fewer measurement 
points. The focus point is found at a 45o angle from the two current electrodes (denoted A and B in 
the figure). This point is used to plot the pseudosection, but it is important to keep in mind that the 
measurement does not give information about a single point, but rather about the entire potential line. 
Therefore the focus point is not the position of the measurement, but is used for visualisation 
purposes. Considering both this and the fact that the measurements show apparent resistivity and not 
actual resistivity, it is important to keep in mind that the pseudosection is not an accurate depiction of 
the subsurface (Loke, 1999).

measurements for each current injection. The Terrameter, which has 12 channels, can measure 11 
different potentials for each current injection (as one channel is used to inject the current). This 
reduces the acquisition time significantly, as it takes several seconds to do the measurements for each 
quadrupole. In comparison, the Wenner array has a fixed distance between all four electrodes, and 
therefore cannot benefit from this.

   Loke (1999) states that the dipole-dipole array is very sensitive to horizontal changes, but less 
sensitive to vertical variations. This means that it is good for mapping structures that have a large 
vertical extent. In a B2S survey there are electrodes in both the borehole and on the surface, which 
means that vertical variations should be as well resolved as horizontal variations. For this reason, it 
could be expected that a B2S dipole-dipole array would perform well for resolving clay blocks. 
According to Loke, the pole-dipole can be used in the same applications as the dipole-dipole. As he 
suggests to use the dipole-dipole array unless there is a limited number of electrodes, the pole-dipole 
array will not be considered further. 
   Non-conventional arrays have also been developed; one such is the multiple-gradient, or gradient, 
array, which is now widely used in DC surveys. This is also shown in Figure 4-2. Here, there is a 
fixed distance between the current electrodes, and the potential electrodes are moved progressively 
==PAGE:36==

between the two current electrodes while keeping a fixed internal distance. The capability of the 
gradient array has been found to equal and even exceed that of the traditional arrays although it may 
be more susceptible to background noise than some of the conventional arrays (Aizebeokhai & 
Oyeyemi, 2014). 
4.1.1 Borehole arrays 
As discussed previously, the resolution of the measurement will decrease with depth. This is 
especially an issue if the subsurface is covered by a low resistivity layer, such as the clay layer in the 
Hetao Basin. The current tends to flow in the low resistivity layer and information about the deeper 
subsurface will be very limited. A possible way to overcome or lessen the impact of these limitations 
is adding borehole electrodes, which will give a deeper depth of investigation (DOI) and can be 
placed underneath a potential low resistivity layer to get added information. 
   There is limited literature about B2S arrays, but generally, the arrays here seem to be based on and 
inspired by the traditional surface arrays. For instance, Tsourlos, et al. (2011) test several B2S arrays 
using synthetic data. Their arrays are based on the surface arrays and they find that the dipole-dipole 
and pole-dipole arrays are preferable to other conventional arrays. In their tests, the borehole is 
placed in the centre of the surface array. They observe a certain degree of symmetry around the 
borehole, where the subsurface structures are mirrored and appear as artefacts on the other side of the 
borehole. As current will flow freely in the ground around the borehole, this is not surprising, as the 
measurement does not ‘know’ which side the structure was actually found on.  
   Danielsen & Dahlin (2010) make synthetic tests for CB setups, and test CB dipole-dipole and pole-
tripole (three electrodes in one borehole and one in the other), as well dipole-pole and gradient in 
each separate borehole. Furthermore, they test combinations of these. They find that the best results 
are obtained by the gradient array and by combining the gradient and the dipole-dipole arrays. While 
these tests were for CB tests, the results may also be applicable for B2S measurements. 
4.2 Measuring principles with focus on the Terrameter 
Regardless of the chosen array, the measuring principles remain the same. The current is injected into 
the ground through the C1 electrode, thereby creating a current loop with C1 as the source and C2 as 
the sink. Some time should be given for the ground to charge up, before the actual measuring is done. 
The current is typically injected in a so-called 50 % duty cycle, shown in Figure 4-3, where the 
current is switched on for several seconds, off for several seconds, and then on for several seconds 
with reversed sign. The latter is done to avoid polarisation of the electrodes and the former to check 
==PAGE:37==
 
 
23 
 
for the so-called self-potentials (SP). This is a naturally occurring potential that is measured even 
though the current is switched off (Binley & Kemna, 2005). The concept is shown in Figure 4-3, 
including the SP effect. As seen, the voltage measurements are shifted upward by a value 
corresponding to VSP. Olsson, et al., (2015) suggest using a 100% duty cycle, where there is no off-
time. The background potential can then be found by considering data from the positive and negative 
current injections. This would have the advantage of reducing acquisition time by half, and according 
to the Olsson, et al., (2015), it gives a stronger signal. 
 
Figure 4-3 The 50 % duty cycle. Vsp is the naturally occurring self-potential (Binley & Kemna, 2005) 
The current injection pattern shown in Figure 4-3 is typically repeated a few times to get a stronger 
measurement. Each repetition is known as a stack. 
   When doing surface measurements, the roll-along method will typically be used, as illustrated in 
Figure 4-4. For the first measurement, three cables are rolled out in a straight line, one after the other, 
as shown in Figure 4-4A. Cable 1, the outer cable is not actually laid out, but is excluded in the 
instrument, which then knows not to use this (non-existent) cable. For the second measurement, four 
cables are rolled out. This setup can be repeated for as long as desired, simply moving the 
configuration one cable length in the x-direction after each measurement. In the final measurement, 
once more only three cables are rolled out. With the same principles as in A, cable 4 is now excluded. 
The measuring instrument must always be placed in the centre of the configuration. In the two outer 
measurements, the excluded cables are also part of the configuration and the centre is based on this. It 
is important that the cables are placed in a straight line, and are at least 20 meters from other 
electrical installations, metal fences, and other noise sources, as will be presented in section 4.4.

When doing 2D modelling, the cables should cut across known geological structures in order to 
model them. 
   When the cables have been rolled out, the electrodes are pushed into the ground and connected to 
the cables with cable jumpers. For the surface cables that are used with the Terrameter, the electrode 
take-outs are spaced with five meters and alternate between blue and red. For the two outer cables, 
only the red take-outs are used, meaning that the electrodes are spaced by ten meters, while on the 
two central cables both red and blue take-out are used. In situations A and C in Figure 4-4, there is

4.3 The Terrameter 
Understanding the Terrameter is essential when transferring the conclusions from synthetic data to 
field tests. Therefore an overview of the instrument, with a focus on the parts relevant for this thesis, 
will be given here. 
   The Terrameter works with spread- and protocol files. The protocol files hold the specific array 
information, that is, exactly how the electrodes are combined in the different quadrupoles. It holds 
==PAGE:39==
 
 
25 
 
the IDs of the electrodes, which are specified in the spread file, and thus the protocol file needs to be 
used together with the relevant spread file. The spread file holds the information on x-, y-, and z-
position of each electrode. Note that this is measured in electrode steps, starting at 0. That is, the first 
electrode on the surface will have x-position 0, the second x-position 1 and so on, regardless of the 
distance between the electrodes. The same holds in the vertical direction where z-position 0 is at the 
surface. The electrode spacing (constant in each direction) is then specified in the instrument itself, 
which calculates the electrode position in meters. This means that the same spread file can be used 
for cables with different electrode spacing. In the spread file, each electrode is also assigned an ID 
and a switch address. The switch addresses are especially important, as these are the numbers that tell 
the instrument which electrode is being called.  
   It is only possible to connect 32 electrodes on each side of the Terrameter (= 64 electrodes in total). 
As the surface cables have 21 electrodes each, two cables must be combined to resemble a single 32 
electrode cable. As described in section 4.2, only the red take-outs are used on the outer cable. The 
reason for this is the set-up of the cable connector between the two cables. Each cable is made with 
32 conductors, where 21 connect to the electrodes on the cable, and 11 run through the length of the 
cable. These conductors are the ones that are connected through the cable connector, which is made 
so that it connects to the red take-outs on the subsequent cable (Adcock, 2017). This also means that 
the 32 electrode cable cannot simply be imitated by using the first 11 electrodes on surface cable 
number 2. Instead, the cable must be folded so that the red electrode take-outs are spaced with 5 
meters, as shown in Figure 4-5a. It can also be done by folding the outer cable after the sixth 
electrode and laying them double, as shown in Figure 4-5b. A Matlab script to develop spread files 
based on the first of these methods is enclosed in Appendix 2 
   The Terrameter has spread files for several different surface setups, as well as protocol files for 
several different arrays, including the dipole-dipole and gradient array. There are no spread- or 
protocol files for B2S setups, so these have been developed in the course of this thesis.

4.3.1 Borehole cables 
The borehole cables are have a different configuration than the surface cables. Where each surface 
cable has a fixed distance between the electrodes of 5 meters, the borehole cables have electrodes 
spaced with 0.5 meters. There are 32 electrodes on a borehole cable, giving 15.5 m of cable with 
electrodes). In addition to this, there is a 50 m lead-in before the electrodes, which makes it possible 
to place the electrodes at deeper/different depths. As opposed to the surface electrodes, there is no 
direct contact between the borehole electrodes and the ground, and therefore the electrodes must be 
below the water table for the current to flow. 
   The borehole electrodes can be implemented in the measurements in three overall ways: 
1) quadrupoles with all four electrodes in the same borehole 
2) quadrupoles with some electrodes in the borehole and some on the surface (borehole-to-
surface) 
3) quadrupoles with electrodes in two separate boreholes (cross-borehole) 
The third option will normally give the best resolution of the subsurface (Loke, 2015),but is also the 
one that is most restricted. Both cables need to be connected to the Terrameter at the same time. 
Since each cable has lead-on of 50 meters, there is an absolute maximum distance between the two 
boreholes of 100 m. In addition to this, there must be enough cable length left for the electrodes to be 
below the water table, and possibly deeper if this is where the area of interest is. This means that the 
method requires two boreholes that are placed quite close to each other and are appropriate for DC 
measurements.

4.4 Noise 
Noise in a DC measurement can arise from electrode polarisation which is attempted to be avoided 
by changing the sign of the injected current. It can also come from metal in the ground, such as 
fences or pipes, which will act as current sinks. This restricts the types of boreholes that can be used 
for B2S surveys as discussed previously. High voltage power lines should also be avoided as they 
cause electromagnetic noise. Doing measurements parallel to structures such as streams or dykes may 
distort the signal. Considering all these restrictions, it is important to survey the study area before 
measuring to ensure that there are no obvious noise sources on or close to the survey line. The data 
also becomes more noisy is there is a high contact resistance between the electrodes and the soil. This 
==PAGE:43==
 
 
29 
 
limits the current that can be injected, and the electrode contact resistance should always be checked 
before starting the measurement (Gazoty, et al., 2013), (Anon., n.d.), (Møller, et al., 2006). 
   For the synthetic data modelling, an estimate of the noise is needed. Based on field studies, Gazoty, 
et al., (2013) suggest a relative standard deviation of 3% on DC data in synthetic modelling. Olsson, 
et al., (2015) are much in agreement, suggesting 2%. Based on these values, a relative standard 
deviation of 2.5% will be used as a reasonable estimate of the noise in the synthetic measurements. 
Different values will also be tested for comparison.

5 Inversion theory 
As explained previously, the DC method measures voltage, which can be converted into apparent 
resistivity, rather than the true properties of the subsurface; the same is true for other kinds of 
geophysical investigations. The actual properties of the subsurface must be determined through 
inversion. Inversion is actually the combination of forward modelling and inverse modelling. 
Forward modelling is the process of calculating results based on established equations with known 
model parameters. Inverse modelling is, as the name implies, the opposite, where known observed 
data is used to estimate the model parameters (Tarantola, 2005). Figure 5-1 gives a visual overview 
of the process. In this study, 2D inversion is done, where the subsurface is described by a grid with a 
given number of model cells/blocks. It is assumed that there is no variation in the y-direction. This 
section introduces the mathematics behind the iterative least-squares methods for 2D inversion. First 
the simple linear inverse problem is introduced to give a basic understanding. This is followed by the 
mathematical background of the inversion procedure used in AarhusInv, which is the basis of this 
study.

6 Synthetic data 
As explained in Section 4.1, different array configurations may be utilised for different purposes, and 
the same array is not necessarily the best to use in different geologies. In order to determine the best 
array configuration for the Hetao Basin, synthetic tests have been run. From literature and previous 
studies, the overall geology of the study area is known, as described in section 2. This makes it 
possible to create a model representing the expected geology. A forward modelling is done on this 
model, generating the expected measurement response for a given array as a synthetic data set. Noise 
is then added to the data set and it is inverted. The main purpose of the synthetic modelling is to 
determine an array (electrode setup) which will perform well in the geology of the Hetao Basin. Due 
to time constraints, the thesis has only focused on relatively simple geological models. 
   The inversion theory behind AarhusInv is presented in the previous section. This section deals with 
the practical aspects of using AarhusInv for synthetic data modelling, as well as some technical 
aspects of the software. All scripts and files for the synthetic modelling have been supplied by the 
Hydrogeophysics Group (2017). The synthetic modelling setup can be seen in Appendix 4.  
   Two input files are needed for the synthetic modelling: The cns-file, which holds all information on 
the geological model, constraints, etc., and the sequence file which describes the array. In order to 
understand how these files interact with AarhusInv, the files typically read and generated by the 
software will be introduced first. After this, the inputs of the cns-file will be explained, including 
their ‘translation’ into AarhusInv-files. 
6.1 Structure of AarhusInv

descriptions of the files can also be found, or observations made in the course of this study.

6.2 Synthetic modelling in AarhusInv 
This section introduces the input files for the synthetic modelling and how the parameters entered 
here relate to the mod-file introduced above. Some important settings in the Matlab scripts are also 
presented. 
 
cns-file 
The cns-file is the main input file and holds all inversion settings as well as the definition of the 
geological model. The cns-file is translated into the mod-file, which has been introduced above. This 
section introduces all relevant parameters from the cns-file. The cns-files in Appendix 7 have detailed 
comments on all parameters (by the Hydrogeophyics Group (2017)), so only the ones that are 
relevant in this investigation will be discussed here. 
   Two cns-files are developed for a synthetic modelling run. One file describes the subsurface model 
and is used as an input for the forward modelling. The other file holds information about the starting 
==PAGE:56==
 
 
42 
 
model, constraints, and other inversion settings. In both files, the settings are defined as described in 
the following. 
   The first setting is the number of layers, which is also apparent in the mod-file. Related to this is 
the number of vertical regions, which are sections that the model can be divided into vertically, and 
which can have different constraints and vertical discretisation. The maximum number of layers is 
100, and generally it has been attempted to stay well below this limit in order to minimise the 
computation time. However, when using buried electrodes, each electrode must be placed at a node 
point, so in the area with buried electrodes, the vertical discretisation must be 0.5 m. Different 
vertical regions can be defined, with only the area of the buried electrodes having a discretisation of 
0.5 m. This gives a lower number of layers, reducing computation time. Different constraints can also 
be used in each vertical region, but this presupposes a large knowledge of the subsurface which in 
most cases is not realistic. Finally, different starting models can also be used in each vertical region. 
    
Each vertical region is defined as shown in Figure 6-2. The first integers in lines 8 and 9 are the first 
and last depths of the region, thus giving the depths of the first and last layers in the region. The 
value in line 10 indicates how the thickness of the layers increases. Generally, a constant thickness is 
desired in the vertical regions, but this is handled by defining the value of the first layer in the 
subsequent vertical region, as explained further down. Line 12 defines the inputs for the parameter ρ, 
indicated by the second integer ‘1’.  
   The third input in the line is the starting value for this parameter, for each layer in the given vertical 
region. Three different options are used for this value in this study: any number, which will then be 
used as the starting value, or ‘average’, which means the average of the data. The input string ‘table’, 
as written in the figure, is used for the forward modelling and refers the table that defines the 
subsurface model (explained further down).  
   The reason for this is that the forward modelling is also part of the inversion, which consists of a 
forward run and then update of the model parameters based on the results from this run. Therefore, 
using the true values of the subsurface as starting values yields as result the data that would be 
measured from a geology with these values. The input ‘table’ can also be used to give a different 
starting value to each cell in the model.

6.2.1  Dummy arrays 
When placing the borehole on the edge of the model and using quadrupoles with two measuring 
electrodes in the borehole, some measurements are placed outside the mesh, giving problems when 
running the inversion. Because of this, and because the borehole measurements give information on 
both sides of the borehole and not just on the side where the surface electrodes are, it was decided to 
move the borehole further into the model. As explained previously, the horizontal model extent is 
defined by the electrode positions. Therefore, the borehole can only be placed further inside the 
model if there are surface electrodes on both sides of it. A very simple surface array was designed 
that extended 50 meters on both sides of the actual array. The real array was then displaced by 50 
meters. This is visualised in Figure 6-5. As the dummy array is very simple, it does not contribute 
with a lot of data and is assumed to have minimal effect on the measurement. However, since the 
models are horizontally constrained, it may have a small impact on the result. The dummy array in 
enclosed in Appendix 8.

6.3 Designing protocol files 
As explained previously, the protocol files are inspired by existing array types. Initially, two protocol 
files were developed: The gradient and the dipole-dipole. In all cases, the ability of the Terrameter to 
utilise multiple channels at the same time has been taken into account. This means that current 
electrode positions that make it impossible to place 11 potential electrode pairs have not been 
included. Similarly, only 11 potential measurements have been made for each current injection.  
   The arrays are first developed with electrode IDs only, which means that they could in theory be 
used for both surface and borehole surveys. The translator script holds information on which 
electrodes are buried and can thereby assign the correct position to each electrode ID. In order to 
==PAGE:62==
 
 
48 
 
enable multiple cables in the borehole for the borehole roll-along as explained in section 4.3.1, the 
scripts first generates the electrode array for only one cable. Subsequently, the IDs are modified so 
that surface electrodes get IDs that follow the total number of borehole electrodes. The array is then 
copied, and for each cable the buried electrode IDs are increased with the number of electrodes on a 
cable minus a possible overlap. Finally, the arrays are pasted together and repeated lines are 
removed. Doing it this way ensures that no electrode combinations are made between two borehole 
cables; in reality, of course, there is only one cable in the borehole at a time, so this is not possible. In 
order to explore the merits of borehole-to-surface measurements, the arrays are also developed for 
pure surface layouts, in order to test whether there is a benefit when including the borehole and if so, 
what and how much it is. 
   Matlab scripts have been developed to construct the different arrays with different settings. The 
outputs from these scripts are Excel-files containing the IDs of the electrodes. These can be translated 
directly into files readable by the Terrameter using the script in Appendix 9. For the synthetic data 
modelling, the Excel-file can be read by another script, which translates the IDs into x and z positions 
and writes a text file readable by AarhusInv. 
   When designing the arrays, it is important to take the time consumption into account. For the 
synthetic studies, it is of interest to have very big arrays, in order to investigate how and if this 
improves the result. However, most of the work should be done with the field work in mind, and here 
acquisition times of more than a few hours are problematic. They trade-off between data quality and 
acquisition time should be considered – if a given array 1 gives only slightly better results than 
another array 2, it may not be worth the possible extra acquisition time. Within each array type there 
are different settings that can be changed, giving several different versions with a very different 
number of measurements and thereby acquisition time.

7.1.3 Effects of different grids 
In this section, the effect of two different grid configurations is tested, using the gradient array on 
Model S1. One grid is the 17-layer grid described previously, where each layer has a thickness of 5 
meters. The result of with this grid configuration is shown in Figure 7-2. The second grid has 81 
layers, giving layers with a thickness of 1 meter between depths of 0 and 80 meters and a final 
infinite layer. Figure 7-4 shows the inversion result with the 81-layer grid. 
   None of the two figures show the correct geometry of the clay block, and in both cases, the block is 
not separated from the top clay layer. However, the block is much more well-defined for the 17-layer 
grid than for the 81-layer grid. For the 81-layer grid, the clay block is visible only as a slight 
irregularity along the boundary, and the inversion result resembles the result of Model S2 with 17 
layers, as shown in Figure 7-3. With the 81-layer grid, the block seems to be smoothed out. 
   The resistivity of the sand is also more accurately determined for the 17-layer grid than for the 81-
layer grid. In the 17-layer grid, most of the sand has a resistivity of 200 m as in the true model, 
while it is mostly around 100 m in the 81-layer grid.  
   As the 81-layer grid has smaller model cells, they are less sensitive and therefore may not be as 
accurately determined. At the same time, the strength of the vertical constraints will be smaller 
compared to the horizontal constraints when changing the vertical discretisation. As the constraints 
work from cell to cell, changing the vertical grid configuration means that 50% variation is now 
allowed for every meter, rather than for every five meters in the 17-layer grid. As the horizontal 
configuration remains unchanged, the horizontal constraints will be stronger relative to the vertical 
constraints than they were for a coarser vertical discretisation. 
   As a fine grid is necessary in some areas for the buried electrode tests, these results show that this 
may cause problems if features are smoothed out and the resistivity values are less accurately 
determined.

Conclusion on surface arrays: Limited efficiency with regard to finding clay blocks at depths 
From the results in the three previous sections, it is seen that the surface arrays do not perform well 
when the block is placed under the top clay layer. Even when the block is only 5 meters below the 
top clay layer, its geometry and position are not clearly determined, and it is not separated from the

Preliminary conclusion on the efficiency of buried electrodes and effect of constraints 
It has been seen that the constraints have a big impact on resolving the clay block, especially when it 
is positioned further from the borehole. The STDFs vary drastically when changing the constraints, 
but cannot necessarily be used to determine whether a constraint set or inversion result give an 
accurate representation of the true subsurface.

have an advantage over surface array when resolving clay blocks in this type of geology. However, 
there are clearly limitations, especially when the clay blocks are further from the borehole.

In the previous section, it was seen how buried electrodes led to a better resolution of a clay block 
when compared to the surface arrays discussed in section 0. This section splits the array into surface

that the surface quadrupoles contribute to a more well-determined subsurface, which is smoother and 
has a resistivity value closer to that of the true model. Although they only give information about the

doing the measurements without losing important information. The following section will investigate 
gradient array of different sizes to justify this claim.

7.2.6 Changing the depth of electrodes 
While the previous section did not find a significant improvement in results when lowering the 
borehole cable, it is still expected that different electrode depths will have an impact on the results. 
Lowering the electrodes increases the area in which the current can flow, and therefore it is expected 
that a lower electrode position will give a larger coverage. To test this within the limits that are 
possible with the standard borehole cable described in section 4.3.1, Model 1 (Figure 7-10) and 
Model 2 (Figure 7-11) have been tested with the electrodes starting at a depth of 30 meters below the 
surface. Note that this means that the grid has been changed, as the part with fine discretisation is 
now from 30 meters down to 46 meters. 
   Figure 7-26 shows the result for the gradient array with electrodes at this depth for Model 1. It can 
be seen that the DOI is now shallower than before, which was expected when the buried electrodes 
are now at a higher position. As seen, the clay block is also worse resolved, as was expected. There 
are a lot more artefacts in the subsurface, including one that is symmetrical to the clay block around 
the borehole. This could be an effect of the fine grid is this area, but may also be caused by the 
changed current patterns that result of the new electrode positions. The boundary between the top 
clay layer and the sand is also less well resolved.

Conclusion on the use of the method for clay blocks 
The B2S arrays have an advantage over surface arrays for resolving clay blocks underneath a top clay 
layer. However, the blocks need to have a certain size, and even so can only be resolved within a 
certain distance from the borehole.  
   If the contrast between the clay blocks and the surrounding medium is too low, the resolution of the 
clay blocks decreases significantly. This could cause problems in the field, where the contrast may be 
lower than the factor 20 generally used in this study. 
   It seems that two clay blocks cannot be separated from each other, but the overall position of the 
block can be determined, which is enough for the application in the Hetao Basin. When the blocks 
are placed too deep, they are not resolved properly. This is most likely because there is not enough 
data, but may also be an effect of the fine grid at the position of the buried electrodes.

surface electrodes when resolving clay blocks, the application is limited to areas close to the 
borehole. In the field, different noise levels may further impact the possibilities of B2S arrays. As 
described in section 4.4, a relative standard deviation (STD) of 2.5% was assumed to be a realistic

Conclusion on noise levels 
To conclude, the noise level clearly has a big impact on the results. If the noise level could be

8 Further Work 
There are several things that could be investigated further in this project. The effect of the starting 
model is not fully mapped. An option would be to do a Monte Carlo inversion where a large number 
of different starting models were tested. This would give a better picture of which arrays work well, 
as some results may be caught in a local minimum on the objective function with some starting 
models. 
   The L1 norm has not been tested, but it would be interesting to use this norm on the constraints and 
see if this improved the results. 
   More practically, different array configurations could be tested, including cross-borehole tests. 
While there are no possibilities for cross-borehole testing in the Hetao Basin study area, it would still 
be interesting to see what added value, if any, this would give. Given the limited extent of the B2S 
measurements, it is possible that cross-borehole measurements would not be a big improvement 
compared to doing similar B2S measurements from the second borehole. However, there is a 
possibility that including quadrupoles with electrodes distributed in two boreholes could give better 
resolution of the area between then boreholes. 
   Roll-along in the borehole could also be investigated, where the cable is lowered to several 
different depths within one measuring session. Before doing this however, the results from this thesis 
should be developed further, as it was seen that more measurements led to a worse model. It should 
be investigated whether this is through a fault in the array, or whether the starting model was not 
optimal for the result. Furthermore, it was also seen that placing the buried electrodes at a shallower 
depth led to more artefacts in the subsurface. Therefore, it should be investigated whether the roll-
along method could lead to less stable results. 
   More complex models should also be tested to see whether added complexity leads to worse 
results. As the reality is more complex than the simple subsurface models tested in this study, too 
much complexity may be difficult to resolve. 
   Finally, the conclusions made should be tried in the field. It would be very interesting to investigate 
the capability of borehole-to-surface DC measurements in resolving clay blocks. From an arsenic 
perspective, it would be interesting to investigate whether the position of possibly resolved clay 
blocks corresponded to areas with high arsenic concentrations.

9 Conclusion 
In this study the advantage of single borehole-to-surface arrays over traditional surface arrays in DC 
resistivity measurements has been investigated using synthetic modelling. The geology has been 
based on the Hetao Basin in China and is described by a top clay layer with a thickness of 15 meters 
and resistivity 10 m above sand with a resistivity of 200 m. The main objective was to resolve 
clay blocks in the sand. 
   The capabilities of the surface gradient array were tested with a clay block of 20m x 20m and a 
resistivity of 10 m. Placing it 5 meters below the top clay layer led to a resolution of the blocks as a 
part of the top clay layer, and the right dimensions and position are not apparent. Moving it further 
down decreased the resolution of the block, and at 15 meters below the top clay layer it was no 
longer clearly visible. 
   Arrays that include buried electrodes have shown an advantage over traditional surface layouts 
when resolving clay blocks, but the application is limited. A clay block 15 meters below the top clay 
layer with dimensions 20m x 20m and resistivity 10 m should be within 10-15 meters of the 
borehole for the array to resolve it. Even at these distances, the resolution of the block is severely 
decreased compared to a block placed right at the borehole. Increasing the size of the block gives a 
better resolution further from the borehole, but most likely the method can only be expected to yield 
good results with 20-30 meters of borehole, depending on the block size that is to be resolved. The 
upper boundary of the clay block is consistently placed too low in the inversion results, but the 
horizontal position of clay blocks can be approximated with borehole-to-surface arrays if they are not 
too deep or too far from the borehole. When placing two clay blocks in the subsurface, they are 
resolved, but not separated. Increasing the resistivity of the clay block to 50 m significantly 
decreased the resolution, and with this resistivity the capability of the methods is further limited. 
   In the borehole, a gradient and a dipole array were tested as they are popular surface arrays. The 
dipole array was shown to perform worse than the gradient array, even when having a comparable 
number of measurements in the two arrays. In addition to the array configuration, the number of 
measurements also affects the resolution and the depth of investigation. 
   For surface arrays, it was seen that a finer vertical discretisation gave smoother results with less 
well-defined clay blocks, and less accurate resistivity. This may cause problems in borehole-to-
surface arrays, as all electrodes must be placed on node points and a fine grid is therefore necessary. 
   Noise has a big impact on the results, with higher noise levels significantly decreasing both the 
depth of investigation and the resolution of the clay blocks. 
==PAGE:98==
 
 
84 
 
   When considering the standard deviation factor (STDFs), it is seen that they vary a lot with 
different sets of constraints, but less with different arrays or models. This indicates that the final 
result is very dependent on the constraints. Furthermore, the STDFs cannot be used to determine 
whether a result is good or not, as a constraint set with very low STDFs did not perform as well in 
resolving the true subsurface as a set with higher STDFs did. The STDFs vary with the noise level, 
with more noise leading to higher STDFs, but the change here is not as dramatic as when changing 
the constraints. 
   The overall conclusion is that buried electrodes give an added advantage when resolving structures 
underneath a low resistivity layer, but the application is limited to relatively large blocks close to the 
borehole and with a low resistivity compared to the surrounding medium. Further tests should be 
made with different inversion settings to gain a better understanding of the performance of the 
inversion. Different arrays and more complex models should also be tested. In the field, the noise 
should be kept as low as possible, as this strongly affects the capability of the measurements.

 Several of the following appendices are enclosed electronically as this provides a clearer overview 
than a paper version. For the emo-files and images thus enclosed, the names of are structured in the 
following way: 
  All files are named ‘[model]_[array]’. If nothing else is included, the settings will be as defined in 
Table 7-1. If some settings have been changed, this will be the next entry in the name.  
   ‘SM’ means the starting model has been changed, and the numbers refer to Table 7-3.

   ‘STD’ means a different noise level has been used, and the numbers are the relative standard 
deviation in percent. 
   ‘Surf’ is a surface array.

electrode in meters. For the surface arrays, a different grid may be used. 
   ‘Squads’ and ‘Bquads’ are the surface quadrupoles and the borehole-to-surface quadrupoles,

II. Appendix 2: Spread file script [electronic] 
Electronic appendix, please see attached flash drive. As the work done in this thesis may potentially 
be used as basis for field work, some scripts were made to translate the input files into some that 
can be read by the Terrameter. This script was developed for writing the spread files. Three 
different options are available in the script: Vertical roll-along, horizontal roll-along, or vertical 
roll-along with 32 surface electrodes on either side of the borehole. Developing the script took some 
technical knowledge of the Terrameter LS, which has been introduced in the previous section 4.3. 
For the option with 32 electrodes on both sides of the borehole, the IDs were mirrored, meaning that 
only one protocol file, developed for one side of the set-up, was necessary. 
   The roll-along was handled by defining all cables that would be used in the entire measurement, 
with consecutive IDs. For instance, in a vertical roll-along where the borehole cable is lowered 
three times in total (including the first measurement) and where there is no overlap, the total 
number of borehole electrodes in the spread file will be 96, which will also be the highest ID 
number for the borehole electrodes. The IDs of the surface electrodes follow the ones from the 
borehole. Overlapping electrodes have the same ID. It is important to exclude the cables that are not 
in use for the given part of the measurement on the Terrameter. 
   The spread file works with electrode spacings, where the values of the horizontal and vertical 
electrode spacings are defined on the Terrameter itself before the measurements start. To get the 
correct depth of the first buried electrode, this was defined as the start depth divided by the borehole 
electrode spacing – giving 20 for a start depth of 10 meters with an electrode spacing of 0.5 meters. 
For any start depth, a new spread file file will have to be written, so having a script that does it 
automatically simplifies the process a lot. 
   The script assumes that there is no variation in the y-direction. 
   It is also possible to create a pure borehole or surface spread file by simply defining the number of 
surface or borehole cables, respectively, as zero. 
   Roll-along is done in either the vertical or the horizontal direction – it is not possible to do both at 
once. The number of overlapping electrodes can also be changed. There are only overlapping 
electrodes in the direction where the roll-along is done – not between the two surface cables that 
comprise 32 electrodes together. This is also the case when doing surface roll-along, as the two 
cables are considered as a single 32 take-out cable. 
   As the Terrameter takes electrode spacings into account, the borehole will always be located in x-
position 0, with the first surface electrode at x-position 1. For the setup with a surface cable on 
==PAGE:110==

either side of the borehole, the borehole will be at an x-position corresponding to the number of 
electrodes on a cable (as the first surface electrode is in position 0).

the inputs. They have also been modified to save the dcp-files and mod-files from each run.
