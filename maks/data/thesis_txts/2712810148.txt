Abstract
In this thesis, the design of optical communication systems with reduced signal quality
per physical channel in favor of longer reach while keeping the capacity constant through
the use of space division multiplexing (SDM) has been investigated.
This speciﬁc SDM
scheme is called the dual ﬁber model, and we will focus speciﬁcally on applying the model
to unrepeatered systems.
Initially, the raw increase in link length that can be achieved
by the addition of more ﬁbers while keeping the total capacity of the system constant will
be discussed for three diﬀerent unrepeatered setups. This will then be built upon by the
addition of a cost model, and optimization of the cost eﬀectiveness of the three systems
using the dual ﬁber model is performed, and the evolution of the many diﬀerent design
parameters resulting in the optimal system is also followed. The cost analysis then ends
with a comparison of the cost eﬀectiveness of the three diﬀerent systems.
All of the signal quality estimation is performed using the Gaussian Noise (GN) model. This
model is derived for another type of optical system, the long, repeatered system consisting
of multiple in-line ampliﬁers. As such, numerical validation of the model is performed, and
a novel approach for correction of the GN model is proposed, and shown to work quite well
within certain, not yet fully understood, boundaries.

Chapter 1. Introduction
Chapter 1
Introduction
The exponential growth in the amount of data transmitted globally drives a need for new
methods of transmitting data more eﬃciently. For transmission of data over vast distances,
repeatered systems assisted by in-line ampliﬁers are needed, but these systems are expen-
sive and complex to build and maintain, so if the transmission distance allows for it, the
unrepeatered system can in some cases be the preferred method of data transmission. Ap-
plications can be connecting islands to mainlands, oﬀshore platforms, or when connecting
more challenging terrain such as mountains, deserts, and forest areas [1–3]. Increasing the
possible transmission distance of such systems or increasing the possible distance between
intermediate points could result in cheaper and more eﬃcient links.
However, it is very challenging to keep increasing the throughput/transmission distance of
unrepeatered links as, by design, light can only be launched at the transmitting and receiving
end. Because of the nonlinear phase noise and the risk of burning components, there is a limit
on how much power can be launched into a ﬁber link. In recent years, advanced setups for
pushing the performance of unrepeatered systems as far as possible have been investigated.
The performance of unrepeatered system can be pushed to its limit by utilization of Remote
Optically Pumped Ampliﬁers (ROPAs) placed at the transmitting and receiving ends where
each end is assisted by multi order Raman ampliﬁcation [1, 4]. Utilization of concatenated
ROPAs has also been demonstrated [5]. While it is possible to build these advanced, bleeding
edge systems, it is still very relevant to investigate and improve upon the simpler systems
used for unrepeatered transmission of data, as they are preferred when constructing systems
of shorter reach than those extreme cases from the aforementioned articles.
In the pursuit of increasing the possible distance between in-line ampliﬁers and the reach of
unrepeatered systems, a novel SDM method called the dual ﬁber model will be investigated
throughout this thesis, with the focus being on the unrepeatered case. The core principle
behind the model is to increase the bandwidth by splitting up a data stream in m ﬁbers, and
instead of using the increased bandwidth to increase the throughput, the total capacity is
kept constant, such that the reach of the system is increased due to the lower signal-to-noise
ratio (SNR) requirement in each ﬁber. If the equipment available is eﬃcient at transmitting

can maybe be increased eﬃciently using the dual ﬁber model. The economic viability of
any system is of course important, so a simple cost model [6] modiﬁed to ﬁt with the dual
ﬁber model will be implemented, and the cost eﬀectiveness of these systems will be analyzed.
The design philosophy of trading SNR for length with a constant capacity using SDM could
be even more interesting in the future when the number of ﬁbers could become less of a
constraint due to emerging technologies such as multi-core ﬁbers [7, 8] and ampliﬁers [9, 10].
All of the analysis will be based on SNR values calculated from a modiﬁed version of the

equation (NLSE) using the Split-step Fourier method (SSFM), but due to the size of the
parameter space, this is not a viable approach. The SSFM will instead be used as a tool to
investigate the accuracy of our GN model and to see if any corrections can be made.

Chapter 2. Theory
Chapter 2
Theory
Describing the propagation of pulsed light in optical ﬁbers is, even with numerous approx-
imations, not a trivial task. In this project, the quality of the transmitted signal will be
estimated using an analytical GN model based on the model introduced by Doran [11]. The
validity of the model will be tested by comparing it to the computationally demanding Split
Step Fourier Method (SSFM), which is a numerical method used for solving the Nonlinear
Schr¨odinger equation (NLSE). In the theory section of this report, we will seek to describe
these methods along with the basic physical properties of light propagation upon which the
NLSE is built and the diﬀerent ampliﬁcation schemes utilized in the investigated systems. A
short introduction to the nonlinear process known as Raman scattering will also be included,
as this process allows for eﬃcient distributed ampliﬁcation and can be useful in unrepeatered
systems. Finally, the dual ﬁber algorithm and the closed form SNR model used in this thesis
will be introduced.

2.2
Raman scattering
Raman scattering is another third order nonlinear eﬀect that occurs in an optical ﬁber. It
originates from the oscillations of the molecular structure, which happen at certain frequen-
cies. These oscillations are quantized as phonons. Light propagating in a silica ﬁber will
interact with the phonons and the induced oscillating dipole moment contains the sum and
diﬀerence frequency components between the optical ﬁeld and the phonons. The Raman
process can either generate a new lower frequency along with inducing a phonon in the ma-
terial, this is known as the Stokes process. A higher frequency component can be generated
through the absorption of a phonon, which is the anti-Stokes process. Since the anti-Stokes
process requires a phonon to be present, the anti-Stokes line has a weaker intensity [17]. The
Stokes process is illustrated simply in ﬁg. 2.3.
Figure 2.3: Sketch of Stokes process from the Raman eﬀect [17].
The Raman eﬀect can therefore be utilized as ampliﬁcation for a signal if a pump with the
correct frequency is chosen. This is known as Stimulated Raman Scattering (SRS). The
frequency of the phonons is of course material dependent, and for silica, the peak in Raman
gain is at ∼13 THz from the pump frequency, which is ∼100 nm at a signal wavelength of
1550 nm. In the next sections, the distributed Raman ampliﬁcation used for simulations in
the project will be described.
2.2.1

the performance of the system. It is especially useful in unrepeatered transmission links,
as only ampliﬁcation schemes that work with light coming from the beginning and/or end
of the transmission link can be used.

2.3
Closed form signal quality estimation
The SSFM is computationally demanding and we wish to investigate a large parameter space
for several diﬀerent unrepeatered setups. Therefore, a closed form estimation of the SNR
of the unrepeatered system is desired, and diﬀerent versions of such an estimation based on
the GN model based on the work of Poggiolini has been investigated [20][21]. If the aim
of the project was to optimize long-haul repeated systems, the model suggested by Downie
[22] could be useful as it also considers the electrical power limitations that one encounters
when EDFAs far from a power source are required. For this thesis, we ended up with the
model used by Doran [11] as a starting point, since the model suggests a way of estimating
the nonlinear noise based on physical parameters, whereas many others simply multiplies it
by some constant that is system speciﬁc, which would be a problem for us, as their models
are based on repeatered systems.
Even though the SNR model used by Doran is based on physical parameters, we still have
to be careful when using it in this thesis. The model is derived when assuming long systems
consisting of identical spans where the loss of each span is compensated for by optical ampli-
ﬁcation. The underlying assumption that the signal can be considered as Gaussian noise is
well veriﬁed for systems with large accumulated dispersion [23]. For an unrepeatered system,
this is almost certainly not the case, as most of the nonlinear interaction will occur before
a lot of dispersion has accumulated. Hence, some sort of validation and testing of how this
aﬀects the model will be important later.

the data signal in a measurable way, it would have to manifest itself as some sort of noise
power when considering the SNR.

they are attenuated during propagation just as the signal, and more noise is added whenever

simply the power of the signal divided by the total noise power, hence if we did not amplify

higher SNR value by ampliﬁcation of the signal, which would be unphysical. The method

nature of numerical root solvers, unless the parameter space was heavily restricted.

The dual ﬁber model presented here is used in the exact same way whether the SNR input
comes from any of the introduced systems, a more detailed simulation, or experimental data.
If derivations were made for this speciﬁc SNR model, a lot of the modularity of the dual
ﬁber algorithm would be lost. It would also mean that diﬀerent expressions would have to
be derived for the diﬀerent types of systems that we wish to consider, and if the SNR model
or the calculations of the diﬀerent components was changed or improved, new expressions
would have to be derived. Using a modular object-based numerical approach solves this
problem, as the diﬀerent components can just be stitched together in whatever order is
required, and upgrading or changing the components does not require changing the other
components. The calculations performed are also really fast and only need to be performed
once per system when the correct input parameters are used, so the gain in speed would not
be large.
Later in the report, we will be doing cost optimization on the dual ﬁber systems, and the
systems depend on four input parameters (m, Nch, Ps, L). Thus, we would have to perform
multi-variable calculus in four dimensions to do those optimizations analytically, so it is hard
to tell how much insight would even be gained by writing up expressions of that size that
would not be gained by just optimizing numerically and showing it graphically.

4.1
Correction of nonlinear noise term
The comparison between the numerical model and the GN model will be done by calculating
the SNR at diﬀerent propagation lengths while sweeping the total launch power for the two
systems mentioned in the introductory section. Doran states that their GN model does not
depend on the modulation format employed [11], which makes sense for a system consisting
of many ampliﬁed links. For long, repeatered links the bulk of the total nonlinear phase
noise will occur after a severe amount of dispersion has accumulated in the signal. During
that propagation, many symbols will overlap in each timeslot, which means that on average
there will be no resemblance of a modulation format in a given timeslot. However, this is
not the case in an unrepeatered system, as the bulk, if not all, of the nonlinear interaction
will happen before a lot of dispersion has accumulated.

Chapter 6
Cost analysis
When constructing any type of system, a very important thing to consider is the price.
Investigation of the price of designing systems using the dual ﬁber model will be performed,
and with the size of the parameter space that we have available, the optimal conﬁguration of
diﬀerent systems is not obvious. We will introduce a cost model to investigate and optimize
for cost eﬀectiveness in the diﬀerent systems based on the dual ﬁber model.
6.1
Transmission cost model
The overall model for calculating the cost of our system is introduced in this section. Our
starting point for calculating the price of a system comes from Bell labs [6]
ctot = (cD + cC)NspL + 2Nfp(cF NspL + cOANrep) + 2cT C/100.
(6.1)
This cost model was originally developed for repeatered submarine systems so some of the
parameters in the model are trivial for our system. Nsp is the number of spans (1 for an
unrepeatered system), Nrep is the number of repeaters. Usually Nrep = Nsp −1 for repeated
setups, but we do not discard it completely, as it can be used to estimate the cost of systems
which includes ROPAs. Finally, Nfp is the number of ﬁber pairs and C is the capacity in
one direction. Nfp = 1 as we do not include or investigate some limit to how many ﬁber
pairs can be used in a cable. We just need to keep this in mind later on, as it means that if
large optimal values of m are found, then multiple cables or eﬀective multi-core ﬁbers would
probably be needed in order for the solution to be viable. The cost parameters used in the
Bell labs paper are normalized to transceiver cost per 100 Gbit/s and shown in table 6.1.

7.1
Discussion & outlook
In this thesis, the impact provided by the dual ﬁber model as an additional tool for the design
of unrepeatered optical transmission links has been investigated. The dual ﬁber channel is in
isolation a relatively simple concept. Data is split into multiple ﬁbers, and instead of using
the additional bandwidth to increase the throughput of the system, the throughput is kept
constant such that the possible length of the system is instead increased.
We have demonstrated how the possible link length of three diﬀerent types of systems behaves
upon increasing the number of ﬁbers. It is clear that in some regions, a considerable link
length increase can be achieved simply by the addition of a few ﬁbers to the setup. An
example of how the price of these systems changes depending on the required length has also
been shown, and the addition of the extra dimension that m provides expands the number
of viable setups such that there is larger freedom in how one wants to design a system.
Developing a pipeline for constructing multiple diﬀerent unrepeatered setups with the as-
sociated costs is challenging for many diﬀerent reasons. In the pursuit of reaching a point
where relevant improvement by the use of the dual ﬁber channel in unrepeatered systems
could be presented in the time span of this project, some assumptions have been made in
order to create usable SNR and cost models, and as such, some of them could be made more
realistic in a multitude of ways.
Other than doing it in the interest of time, we do think that there is good reason behind
these assumptions, and we have also tested the validity and boundaries of the SNR model
inside the parameter space that is considered for this thesis. Another argument as to why
the assumptions are justiﬁed within this project is that the entire dual ﬁber algorithm and
cost analysis developed does not assume anything about the system that they are used on.
This means that the tools developed can easily be changed to work with any other SNR and
cost model, and as a showcase and investigation of how the dual ﬁber channel works and
how it can be used in the design of systems, it is deﬁnitely enough.
66
==PAGE:75==
Chapter 7. Discussion & concluding remarks
In order to proceed much further with the estimation of the base system performance, a
multitude of steps can be taken.
While some interesting connections were found when
comparing the SNR estimation of the GN model and the NLSE, the GN model and its limits
should still be tested further, or another, maybe similar, closed form SNR estimation could
maybe be derived with a focus on unrepeatered systems instead of the repeatered systems
that the GN model is currently based on. More detailed ROPA and Raman ampliﬁcation
models would also prove very useful, as tuning the parameters of the pumps would result in
further possible optimization and tuning of the diﬀerent setups, while also not having to rely
directly on the setups used in other applications to set up a system. Naturally, numerical
simulations are useful tools, but experimental investigation and validation of these systems
is also needed.
Having a more accurate cost model would of course be a necessity if the model was to be a
part of a tool used to design and optimize setups. As discussed throughout the report, the
speciﬁc numbers put into the model would probably be subject to change based on many
diﬀerent factors for each individual case. It would not necessarily have to be exact numbers,
but a good next step regarding the cost model could be an increase in complexity such that
the components would not always cost the same, and would change depending on the input
parameters. This could include changing the cable cost based on the number of ﬁbers used,
varying transceiver cost based on throughput, adding ampliﬁers to the transmitting and
receiving side, and having the cost of those being based on the amount of gain required.
While this project has focused speciﬁcally on the dual ﬁber model for unrepeatered systems,
the addition of other types of setups for further comparison would also bring an improvement
to the model. The addition of a repeatered system consisting of in-line ampliﬁers, which could
also have its own dual ﬁber algorithm built in, would make it possible to determine in which
cases such a system is preferred to the unrepeatered case.
7.2
Conclusion
Throughout this thesis, the novel transmission distance increasing dual ﬁber model has been
investigated. The concept behind the model is simple to understand. It trades signal quality
in the form of SNR for bandwidth using SDM, thereby increasing the possible transmission
distance. Speciﬁcally, the model works by distributing the capacity of a single ﬁber into m
ﬁbers while keeping the total capacity constant, and then the new possible length stemming
from the lowered SNR requirement due to the increased bandwidth of the system is found.
By increasing the possible transmission distance of diﬀerent systems, more possibilities be-
come available when optimizing the design of optical systems. This could result in unre-
peatered systems being viable over longer link lengths before having to build a complex,
repeatered system with in-line ampliﬁers. It also extends the viability of less complex sys-
tems, such that in some cases, one could avoid having to build and maintain more complex
setups simply by the addition of more ﬁbers. The transmission distance increase of a simpler
system comes at the cost of signal quality, so one also needs to make sure that the receiver
67
==PAGE:76==
Chapter 7. Discussion & concluding remarks
can decode the lower SNR eﬃciently. As mentioned earlier, other than just being able to
extend the reach of a system by simply adding more ﬁbers, the extra design parameter that
is provided through m has also been shown to provide a large increase in the number of
speciﬁc conﬁgurations that has the performance wanted from some optimization. This is
always good, as it allows the system designer to pick the one that suits a speciﬁc project the
best.
With the current knowledge that we have of both the cost model and the SNR estimation,
it still remains to be shown when this trade oﬀis actually worth it. If eﬃcient multi-core
ﬁbers and ampliﬁers supporting that technology, where the number of ﬁbers will be less of
a constraint, are introduced, the design philosophy of the dual ﬁber model will probably be
even more lucrative.
However, one of the major strengths of the model that has been presented throughout the
thesis is that the dual ﬁber model that has been developed is simply a tool. A tool that can
easily be adapted to and used in any system model. This means that if other SNR values
were presented for any given system, or if the GN model SNR estimation is improved, the
addition of the design parameter m is easily integrated in the same way as shown in this
project. This is of course also true for the cost model. The model presented in this thesis is
just an example where the exact numbers and dependencies of each of the cost parameters
could easily be changed, such that by changing essentially one line of code, the tool that
we have developed can calculate the new optimal dual ﬁber conﬁguration for any given cost
model based on whichever cost optimization is wanted.
Though a lot of testing is still needed, some interesting connections between the simple SNR
estimation of an unrepeatered system that the GN model oﬀers and the heavy, accurate
SSFM has been demonstrated. The error in the estimation of the nonlinear noise in the
region of optimal SNR from the GN model seems to be able to be corrected by some factor
that depends quadratically on the number of WDM channels, at least for the limited number
of WDM channels that we were able to test numerically, and most of the span lengths. This
is just one correction that we found upon investigation of a limited parameter space, and
maybe similar corrections exist in regions where this one does not hold. A more in-depth
understanding of the boundaries of when and why this correction holds and whether other
types of corrections exist would be valuable if one wishes to continue the route of estimating
the SNR of an unrepeatered system using this kind of GN model.

On the optimal SNR of our shorter distance ROPA system
This section exists in order to explain the sudden increase in the optimal power slope in
ﬁg. 5.9.
The explanation starts by taking a step back and considering the full SNR curves for link
lengths ∼400 km vs launch power, not just the optimal values. As this behaviour only occurs
for the system with a backward ROPA without Raman ampliﬁcation, it must be due to the
ROPA ampliﬁcation and the interplay between it and the rest of the system. When the
distance between the transmitter and ROPA is not very long, there will be some launch
power that causes saturation of the ROPA, thereby decreasing the total gain of the ROPA
due to the 4 dBm ceiling. So in ﬁg. B.1, the SNR at chosen lengths above and below the
large power slope in ﬁg. 5.9 is plotted along with the launch power that causes the ROPA
to saturate.

transition region that the optimal launch power in ﬁg. 5.9 has a sudden increase in slope.

m = 2, Nch needs to be large to come closer to that same total number of transceivers. This
explanation is further backed up by the fact that the 1% range at m = 2 only allows for
large values of Nch.

From 425 to 450 km, there is a large step down in optimal Nch. But considering the optimal
point at m = 3, there is a very large range of possible values of Nch that yield a cost/bit/s
that is at most 1% bigger. Even the m = 4 point is inside the 1% range, so for this speciﬁc
length for this speciﬁc system, there is a lot of freedom when designing a setup focused on
cost eﬀectiveness. Since Nch is so low at 450 km, it makes sense that it is possible for the
optimal value of Nch to stay the same between 450 and 475 km. However, at 475 km, this
number of WDM channels is pretty much at its highest possible value while still being inside
the 1% range, in contrast to 450 km, where it was possible to increase the multiplexing by a
considerable amount. But the capacity can still be increased at 475 km while staying in the
1% range, but m has to increase to 5, instead of increasing Nch. At a link length of 500 km,
there is no longer a large variation in Nch, but the line is much ﬂatter around the optimal
m, so the design freedom is instead in m, where it is possible to, for example, to go down to
m = 5 inside the 1% range, which would trade capacity for a better SNR.
