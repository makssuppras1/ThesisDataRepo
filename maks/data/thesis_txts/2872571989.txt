Abstract
Groundwater abstracted from limestone aquifers represents approximately one third of the drinking water
supply in Denmark. However, groundwater quality is threatened by the intrusion of contaminants into
the aquifers. In particular, chlorinated solvents – such as PCE and TCE – are one of the most prevalent
sources of groundwater contamination. The partial dechlorination of PCE and TCE often leads to the
accumulation of cDCE in contaminated plumes. Therefore, there is a need for remediation strategies
capable of degrading cDCE in limestone.
In this study, continuous-flow column experiments were designed and conducted to assess the potential for
enhanced biotic and abiotic degradation of cDCE in limestone. To date, very few experiments have been
conducted to investigate the degradation of chlorinated ethenes in limestone, making the experimental
design developed in this study novel. cDCE contaminated groundwater extracted from the plume of
Naverland site was used as the influent of the columns, with concentrations of approximately 80 µg/L. An
average porosity of 0.45 and a hydraulic residence time of 1.3 days were obtained in the columns packed
with bryozoan limestone grains.
Three different remediation strategies – one relying on enhanced abiotic degradation, one relying an
enhanced biotic degradation and one combining both – were compared. The abiotic column was amended
with a suspension of sulfidated micro ZVI, yielding a concentration of 11 gFe0/kg limestone. The biotic
column was biostimulated with lactate (63 mg lactate/L GW) and bioaugmented with KB-1® culture
(initial concentration of 1·109 cells/L GW). The combined column was amended with an EHC® Liquid
Reagent Mix solution – providing 33 g of ferrous gluconate into the column – and biostimulated with
lactate and bioaugmented with KB-1® as column B.
Degradation of cDCE was observed in all three columns in the 35 day time frame of the experiment. The
abiotic column amended with SmZVI exhibited the best performance regarding cDCE dechlorination.
Despite similar cDCE concentrations, higher VC levels were observed in the combined column than in
the biotic column, suggesting that the enhanced biotic strategy alone performed best than the combined
enhanced abiotic and biotic strategy. In addition, dechlorination of cDCE after biostimulation, but before
bioaugmentation, suggests that indigenous bacteria – including Dehalococcoides – might be present in
the plume. The results are subject to uncertainties caused by the short time frame of the experiment
and by the incomplete molar balance on cDCE and its degradation products. Indeed, ethene was likely
oxidized, as the redox conditions of the system were not methanogenic.
Improvement suggestions on the experimental design are proposed for further studies. Finally, mobility
and longevity of the amendments were also evaluated, and considerations on future perspectives and
potential for field application of the remediation strategies were developed.

Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
In Denmark, more than 99% of water use is based on groundwater, hence the drinking water supply
relies almost exclusively on abstraction from underground reservoirs (Stockmarr 2005; Jørgensen et al.
2009). In particular, the chalk and limestone aquifers contribute for about 35% of the total exploitable
groundwater resource (Nilsson et al. 2018). The Danish drinking water supply is based on the principle of
abstraction of pure groundwater or groundwater with very low impact from anthropogenic pressures. This
translates into a practice where Danish water supply companies are normally authorized to perform only
simple treatment to groundwater, and abstraction wells that are affected by pollution are often abandoned
in favor of newly drilled, usually deeper, wells (Jørgensen et al. 2009).
The Naverland site under investigation, situated west of Copenhagen in the Glostrup municipality, is an
example of contaminated limestone aquifer. Here, the presence of a PCE, TCE and TCA distribution
facility, in activity between 1965 and 1982, led to the leaching of large amounts of chlorinated solvents into
the underlying fractured limestone aquifer (Hedeselskabet 2002) and to the formation of an approximately
3 km long plume (COWI 2023). To prevent the contamination from further spreading uncontrollably
and as a mitigative solution, a pump and treat system has been implemented in the source area (COWI
2023). Further investigations are required to assess more precisely the extension of the contamination
down-gradient of the source area and to identify the most effective remediation strategy for the plume
zone, to re-establish the original high-quality conditions of the groundwater. Indeed, down-gradient of
the contaminated area, several abstraction wells extract large volumes of groundwater to provide drinking
water for the inhabitants of the area (HOFOR 2024).
The source area is heavily contaminated with a mixture of PCE and TCE, while the plume zone is mainly
polluted with cDCE, as the parent compounds are degraded over time (HOFOR 2024). Unfortunately, the
natural conditions at the Naverland site do not allow the chlorinated solvents to be further degraded, thus
leading to an accumulation of cDCE in the system (HOFOR 2024). However, the addition of chemical
and biological amendments to the groundwater is a common and established method to effectively
remediate aquifers contaminated with chlorinated solvents (Henry et al. 2002; McCarty 2010). Among the
amendments, those that are more frequently used are ZVI particles, electron donors and microbial cultures.
The introduction of ZVI leads to in situ chemical reduction (ISCR) of the contaminants, the amendment
of electron donors enhances the naturally occurring biodegradation processes (biostimulation), and the
addition of bacteria determines bioaugmentation (Henry et al. 2002; McCarty 2010).
Several laboratory investigations, including both batch and column experiments, have been conducted
on cDCE contaminated groundwater to evaluate biotic and abiotic processes involved in the degradation
of chlorinated solvents (Schaefer et al. 2009; Rangan et al. 2023; Azizian et al. 2010; Azizian et al.
2008; Mystrioti et al. 2020). While batch microcosms provide valuable data, column experiments offer
more significant insights into contaminant fate and transport process. This is due to their simulation
of continuous groundwater flow through a matrix, as well as the behavior and mobility of amendments
(Rangan et al. 2023). However, despite the promising results, the majority of these studies were performed
on sandy soils, which are not particularly representative of the fractured limestone characteristic of the
Naverland site. Consequently, this leaves a gap in the current understanding.

1.2
Aim
The scope of the thesis is to investigate the enhanced biotic and abiotic degradation of cDCE in limestone
through continuous-flow column experiments, with the final goal of doing a preliminary assessment of
the potential for in-situ remediation of a cDCE contaminated plume in a limestone aquifer.
More precisely, the thesis will focus on the chlorinated solvents plume originating from Naverland and
will evaluate biodegradation, carried out with amendments of electron donor and bacteria, and chemical
reduction of cDCE, the primary contaminant detected in the plume.
To achieve the objective, the following sub-objectives were defined:
• Design continuous-flow column experiments representative of site conditions, using limestone as
packing material.
• Evaluate the mobility and longevity of the amendments injected into the columns.
• Assess the potential for complete biotic and abiotic degradation of cDCE, comparing the outputs
of different strategies.
• Explore the potential for field application of the tested strategies to remediate cDCE contamination
in the fractured limestone aquifer at the Naverland site.

2
Chlorinated ethenes
Chlorinated solvents are a numerous group ofcompoundsthatincludesperchloroethene(PCE),trichloroethene
(TCE) and 1,1,1-trichloroethane (TCA) (McCarty 2010), which are identified as the parent contaminants
in the source area of the Naverland site. However, as further discussed in Section 5.2, the hot-spot is
mainly polluted with PCE and TCE, and the plume is characterized by high concentrations of cDCE, as
the parent contaminants are degraded as they are transported away from the source area (COWI 2023;
HOFOR 2024). For this reason, from now on the chlorinated compounds under investigation will be
often referred to as chlorinated ethenes, since the analysis focuses primarily on cDCE and its metabolites.
2.1
Uses, regulations, and environmental and health effects
Chlorinated solvents have been used predominantly as dry-cleaning solvents and metal degreasing agents,
especially because of their non-flammability and effectiveness, in the form of PCE, TCE and TCA
(Rodrigues et al. 2020; Doherty 2000a; Doherty 2000b). However, due to their environmental impacts
and the health risks caused by exposure, the worldwide production of chlorinated solvents significantly
decreased at the end of the 20th century (McCarty 2010).
Chlorinated solvents have been found to create negative environmental impacts and health risks for
humans and animals (Huang et al. 2014). Numerous studies have focused on the health issues caused by
exposure to these chemicals, proving the carcinogenicity of TCE for humans and other animals (Rusyn et
al. 2014; Zhu et al. 2024; ECHA 2025d), the potential carcinogenicity of PCE (Lynge et al. 1997; ECHA
2025b) and the neurotoxicity of PCE, even at low levels of exposure (Guyton et al. 2014). Moreover,
PCE and TCE can be degraded (Blázquez-Pallí et al. 2019; Maymó-Gatell et al. 1997), leading to the
production of metabolites, such as DCE and VC. Although there is not enough evidence to classify the
cis- and trans- isomers of DCE as carcinogenic (ECHA 2025c; PubChem 2025a), VC’s carcinogenicity is
supported by several long-term studies, which have demonstrated the recurrence of hepatic angiosarcomas
and hepatocellular carcinoma in humans (Bolt 2005; PubChem 2025c; ECHA 2025a).
These reasons led to a restriction on the production of chlorinated solvents and, ultimately, to the phase
out of some of them from the markets. The use of PCE and TCE was mainly restricted to reduce their
concentrations in groundwater (Doherty 2000a; Doherty 2000b). Nowadays, the production and use of
PCE and TCE is efficiently limited by the European Union regulation REACH (ECHA 2025b; ECHA
2025d; Slunge et al. 2022), but different bans and disincentives have been in place in Europe since the
’90s. For instance, Sweden banned any professional use of TCE starting from 1996, Norway implemented
high taxes from 2000, and Germany set tough technical requirements concerning emissions of both PCE
and TCE (Slunge et al. 2001). The Danish case is similar to the Norwegian one: in 1996, the "green
taxes" were imposed on some chlorinated solvents, among which PCE and TCE (Skatteministeriet 2020),
and in 1998 the Danish EPA included the two compounds in the list of undesirable substances due to their
persistency and potential carcinogenicity (Danish Environmental Protection Agency 1998).
Despite being strictly regulated, all three chlorinated solvents under investigation are frequently detected
in groundwater, mainly as a consequence of old spills, leaks and improper disposal, hence resulting
in contamination of potential sources of drinking water (Huang et al. 2014). In the Unites States, for
instance, PCE and TCE have been ranked among the most frequently detected VOCs in groundwater with
concentrations higher than the maximum contaminant level for drinking water (Moran et al. 2007). In

2.2
Properties
Chlorinated ethenes - including PCE, TCE, and their degradation products cDCE, tDCE, 1,1-DCE and
VC - are among the most prevalent groundwater contaminants (Moran et al. 2007; Johnson et al. 1992),
and they are typically released into the environment as pure organic liquids or as mixtures of several
organic liquids (Cwiertny et al. 2010b). Their behavior in the subsurface is controlled to a large extent by
their physical and chemical properties (Cwiertny et al. 2010b), some of which, based on data from Yaws
(1999), are presented in Table 1.
Chlorinated ethenes, with the exception of VC, have densities that are higher than the one of water (Table
1), and this is the reason why they are also known as "dense non-aqueous phase liquids" (DNAPLs)
(Pankow et al. 1996). DNAPLs are also sometimes referred to as "dense immiscible liquids", as they
exist as a stable, separate phase, despite having relatively high water solubilities (Pankow et al. 1996).
Relatively small releases of chlorinated ethenes can already produce significant and long-lasting sources
of groundwater contamination (Johnson et al. 1992). This is mainly due to their high densities and
low viscosities, which allow them to easily sink in the saturated zone, leading to formation of pools of
DNAPLs on top of less permeable layers, and to their relatively high water solubilities, which cause

Moreover, in the vadose zone, due to their high vapor pressures, chlorinated ethenes create vapor plumes

explained exclusively by infiltration of DNAPLs through the unsaturated zone and into the groundwater,
but also by contact of infiltrating water with the vapor plume (Pankow et al. 1996).

(logKow), which represents the hydrophobicity of the compounds, regulates sorption. Moreover, for
organic apolar compounds such as chlorinated solvents, sorption is mainly controlled by the content of
organic carbon (Hemdorff 2013). Further discussion on sorption is presented in Section 3.3.
Finally, the physical and chemical properties control the transport of the chlorinated ethenes in the
aquifers. This will be discussed in Section 3, with regards to fractured limestone aquifers.
2.3
Biodegradation
Chlorinated ethenes can be biodegraded in groundwater systems through several microbial mechanisms,
which have been observed mainly under anaerobic environments, although aerobic degradation has been
observed for some compounds, and include both metabolic and cometabolic processes (Bradley 2000;
Luo et al. 2024). During metabolic transformations, microorganisms use chlorinated ethenes as the
sole energy source, whereas in the cometabolic degradation, the chlorinated ethenes are transformed by
enzymes synthesized by the microorganisms for the metabolism of another carbon source (Bradley 2000;
Luo et al. 2024).
2.3.1
Degradation pathways
Aerobic biodegradation can occur either by direct oxidation or cometabolically, and the chlorinated
ethenes, that behave as electron donors, are mineralized to CO2, thus limiting the production of toxic

observed for cDCE and VC, which are used as primary substrate (Bradley 2000; Bradley et al. 2010).
Although aerobic biodegradation processes do not occur frequently in nature, due to the lack of polluted
oxic aquifers (Bradley et al. 2010), there is a potential for aerobic oxidation of chlorinated ethenes at the
fringe of the contaminated plume and at the point of plume discharge to surface water systems (Bradley
et al. 2010).
Anaerobic biodegradation can be observed as oxidation or reductive dechlorination (Bradley 2000).
Bradley et al. (1996) proved that VC can be oxidized to CO2 by microorganisms in anaerobic aquifers
if the redox conditions are sufficiently reduced - at least manganese- to iron-reducing. A later study
demonstrated that cDCE can also be oxidized under anaerobic conditions, provided that the aquifer
was characterized by manganese-reducing conditions (Bradley et al. 1998; Mattes et al. 2010). The
advantage of anaerobic oxidation is the limited degradation of cDCE to VC, in favor of the production
of CO2, thus preventing the accumulation of toxic by-products in the aquifers (Bradley 2000). However,
in aquifers under anaerobic conditions, the main naturally-occurring degradation pathway is reductive
dechlorination, also known as chlororespiration or dechlororespiration (Bradley et al. 2010; Löffler et al.
2013a; Fennell et al. 2001).
2.3.2
Reductive dechlorination
Reductive dechlorination is a hydrogenolysis reaction, which consists of the replacement of a chlorine
substituent with a hydrogen atom (Bradley 2000; Löffler et al. 2013a). Therefore, the occurrence of
reductive dechlorination is strictly related to the availability of H2 molecules as electron donor, making
it one of the limiting factors of chlorinated ethenes biodegradation (Bradley 2000). Figure 2.2 shows the
sequential dechlorination that starts with PCE and ends with ethene (Löffler et al. 2013a; Friis 2006).
Although the pathway is characterized by three isomers of dichloroethene, the most abundant compound
is cDCE, whereas tDCE and 1,1-DCE are only produced in small amounts, before being reduced to VC
(Maymó-Gatell et al. 1999).

During reductive dechlorination, chlorinated ethenes act as electron acceptors, with the more chlorinated
compounds being the stronger oxidants (Vogel et al. 1987), and hydrogen serves as electron donor.
PCE undergoes reductive dechlorination to TCE in the absence of oxygen, TCE is transformed into
cDCE under iron-reducing, or more strongly reduced, conditions, and cDCE reduction to VC, as well
as dechlorination of VC to ethene, requires at least sulfate-reducing conditions (Chapelle 1996; Bradley
2003). The decreasing reduction potential with decreasing number of chlorine substituents, shown in
Figure 2.4, often leads to incomplete reductive dechlorination of chlorinated ethenes and accumulation of
cDCE and VC in groundwater systems where the redox conditions are not sufficiently reduced (Bradley
2000). The stall of the reductive dechlorination transformations at cDCE or VC can also be explained
with the lack of specific degraders, since only Dhc and Dhg can reduce cDCE and VC to ethene (Stroo
et al. 2013b).

2.4
Chemical reduction
Chlorinated ethenes are also degraded through abiotic transformation pathways, among which there are
the reduction-oxidation (redox) reactions (Cwiertny et al. 2010a). Redox reactions are spontaneous if the
oxidizing agent has a higher reduction potential than the reducing agent (Rodrigues et al. 2020). The redox
ladder presented in Figure 2.4 is organized by reduction potentials (EH in volts), where high, positive EH
values correspond to species whose reduction is thermodynamically favorable, and species lower on the
ladder are capable of reducing species higher on the ladder (Cwiertny et al. 2010a). Therefore, numerous
iron and sulfur species are able to reduce a wide range of chlorinated ethenes at pH 7 (Cwiertny et al.
2010a).

2.4.2
In-situ chemical reduction and zero-valent iron
There are several chemical species that are capable of reducing chlorinated ethenes, but the most com-
monly used for in-situ chemical reduction (ISCR) is zero-valent iron (ZVI), thanks to its abundance on
the planet and its good efficiency. Indeed, it is a strong reductant, with a reduction potential of -0.44 volts,
which allows it to thermodynamically reduce all the chlorinated ethenes, by functioning as an electron
donor (Figure 2.4) (Cwiertny et al. 2010a; Rodrigues et al. 2020; Huang et al. 2014). Although ZVI
can degrade chlorinated ethenes using different mechanisms, the primary is the direct reduction on ZVI
surface, whose general reaction is shown in the following equation (Rodrigues et al. 2020):
Fe0 + RCl + H+ →Fe2+ + RH + Cl–
(4)
Multiple studies (Arnold et al. 2000; Roberts et al. 1996) proved that polychlorinated ethenes react
nearly exclusively via reductive β-elimination, thus resulting in formation of acetylene species that react
relatively quickly with Fe0 to produce fully dechlorinated products. At the same time, reductive α-
elimination has also been observed for 1,1-DCE (Arnold et al. 2000; Roberts et al. 1996). Figure 2.7
presents the proposed pathways for chemical reduction of PCE by ZVI.
Different types of ZVI particles are available on the market, and they are mainly divided into nanoscale
and microscale particles. An analysis of the main characteristics was done by Rodrigues et al. (2020).
Nano-ZVI particles have dimensions of 10-100 nm and they have large specific surface area (20-55 m2
g−1). For these reasons, nano-ZVI particles have high mobility and a great number of reactive sites on
the surface, but also a tendency to aggregate to form bigger particles and higher costs than microscale
ZVI. Micro-ZVI particles are smaller than 100 µm and specific surface area lower than 2 m2 g−1.
Despite having a longer reactive time frame than nano-ZVI, microscale particles are characterized by
sedimentation phenomena, resulting in rapid decrease in specific surface area and mobility.
Iron particles have low selectivity, and a rapid loss of ZVI reactivity is due to reductive reaction that
involve water and dissolved oxygen (Schöftner et al. 2015; Fan et al. 2016). Indeed, up to 80% of the
electrons from Fe0 oxidation are channeled to the production of H2 from H2O (Rangan et al. 2023).
However, sulfide species can be used for the sulfidation of the iron particles, improving selectivity and
longevity of ZVI by decreasing the reactivity with oxygen and water (Fan et al. 2016; Rajajayavel et al.
2015), other than the mobility of the ZVI particles (Fan et al. 2017). On the other hand, H2 produced by
oxidation of Fe0 can be used ad an electron donor for the reductive dechlorination of chlorinated ethenes
to ethene by species of organohalide-respiring bacteria (Rangan et al. 2023).

3.3
Transport in fractured limestone aquifers
Limestone aquifers often present a crushed or fractured part within the upper 10 meters of the limestone,
making them a complex medium to characterize (Jakobsen et al. 2021). The amount of fractures decreases
quikcly in the downward direction in the upper 10 meters, and continue to decrease slightly below the
top 10 meters (Jakobsen et al. 2021). Fractures are differentiated into three different types depending on
their origin and their degree of fracturing: vertical fractures usually originate from tectonic activities,
horizontal fractures are typically caused by the unloading of glaciers, and crushed limestone is also
caused by decompression from glacial activity and presents a degree of fracturing higher than in normal
fractured limestone (Jakobsen et al. 2021).
Fractured limestone aquifers can be described as a dual-porosity medium since the matrix and the
fractures have very different transport parameters. In bryozoan limestone, the matrix has a relatively high
porosity (4 to 45%) as shown in Table 2, but a very low permeability. On the contrary, fractures have
a very low porosity (0.016 to 1.6%), but a high hydraulic conductivity (Kidmose et al. 2022; Mosthaf
et al. 2018).
Therefore, the flow follows preferential pathways along the fractures where advective
transport dominates, with relatively high flow velocities (Kidmose et al. 2023). Mosthaf et al. (2018)
investigated the connectivity of different parts of a fractured limestone aquifer and found that the vertical
connectivity between the upper and lower parts of the aquifer was limited by the preferential pathways
along the horizontal fractures. Transport in crushed limestone does not show high flow velocities as in
the fractures, probably because the fractures in the crushed limestone might be filled with fine materials
(Mosthaf et al. 2018). The transport into and out of the porous matrix is dominated by diffusion and can
be significant when the matrix porosity is high (Feenstra et al. 1996).
Knowledge about sorption of chlorinated ethenes in limestone is scarce (Jakobsen et al. 2021; Wefer-
Roehl et al. 2001). PCE and TCE would present stronger sorption capacities than cDCE and VC (Pastore
et al. 2021). US EPA (2015) calculated a Koc value of 35.5 L/kg for cDCE. Considering an average
organic content of 0.24% in limestone (Gehman 1962), this gives a Kd value of 0.0852 L/kg. Sorption
of cDCE is therefore considered to be small in limestone aquifers, but it could become more significant
with higher organic content (Wefer-Roehl et al. 2001). Sorption would affect the transport mechanisms
due to retardation (Pastore et al. 2021).
Figure 3.2 shows a conceptual model of DNAPL transport in fractured porous media. In the early stages
following the DNAPL release, the contaminant migrates as DNAPL phase through the fractures. The
groundwater in the fractures then progressively dissolves the DNAPL, and the dissolved phase diffuses
into the porous matrix. The diffusion of the dissolved phase from the fractures to the matrix follows a
concentration gradient. When the concentration in the fractures is lower than the concentration in the
matrix, the dissolved phase diffuses out of the matrix in a process called back diffusion (Feenstra et al.
1996). Due to back diffusion, the matrix can act as a long-term secondary contamination source (Shapiro
et al. 2018), which makes the removal of contamination very challenging (Mosthaf et al. 2016; Pastore
et al. 2021).

Key findings from earlier laboratory studies
Few laboratory experiments have been conducted to investigate the degradation of chlorinated ethenes
in limestone media (Sørensen 2013; Rajic et al. 2015; Fallahpour et al. 2017). Among the limestone
column experiments reviewed, the primary focus has been either on the electrochemical reduction of
chlorinated ethenes (Rajic et al. 2015; Fallahpour et al. 2017) or on transport characterization (Bucur et
al. 2007; Wefer-Roehl et al. 2001; Mystrioti et al. 2020). Therefore, studies investigating the degradation
of chlorinated ethenes in non-limestone media were also considered relevant in this chapter.
4.1
Laboratory studies on abiotic degradation
Through batch and column experiments, Gillham et al. (1994) showed that degradation of chlorinated
ethenes by granular ZVI appears to follow pseudo-first-order kinetics. Batch tests were prepared by
adding 10 g of 100 mesh (0.15 mm) ZVI into 40 mL glass vials, that were then filled with an aqueous
solution of the specific individual compound (PCE, TCE, cDCE or VC). This set-up gave an iron surface
area to solution volume ratio of 0.078 m2/mL. Columns were packed with silica sand containing 10% by
weight of 100 mesh ZVI, yielding an area-to-volume ratio of approximately 0.14 m2/mL. Only PCE and
TCE were studied in the column tests.
Batch tests showed that the time required for 50% reduction of the initial concentration (t50) was longer
for cDCE and VC compared to PCE and TCE, with cDCE exhibiting a higher t50 than VC (Table 3). In
addition, Gillham et al. (1994) found that the t50 value is inversely proportional to the iron surface area
to solution volume ratio, provided that a sufficient amount of ZVI is present. The times required for a
50% loss were normalized to an area-to-volume ration of 1 m2/mL (t50−N) (Table 3). The normalized
t50−N values for PCE and TCE from the column experiments were consistent with those from the batch
tests. Due to the relatively rapid degradation rates of PCE and TCE, variations in flow velocity had little

4.2
Laboratory studies on biotic degradation
Azizian et al. (2008) conducted a continuous-flow column experiment to evaluate the degradation of PCE
through biostimulation with lactate and bioaugmentation using a Dehalococcoides-containing enrichment
culture. The column was packed with a sand and gravel mixture, resulting in a porosity of 0.33. A flow
rate of 0.1 mL/min was applied, yielding a hydraulic residence time of approximately 1.3 days. The
initial PCE concentration was 1 mmol/L. As shown in Figure 4.2(a), no PCE degradation was observed
following the addition of the microbial culture and lactate at a concentration of 0.34 mM. Increasing
the lactate concentration to 0.67 mM initiated sulfate reduction and the dechlorination of PCE, with
the formation of cDCE detected within one day. Despite continuous operation under these conditions
for 50 days, cDCE persisted in the system, indicating limited further degradation. Increase in lactate
concentration to 1.34 mM led to the reduction of cDCE to VC and ethene. Significant increases in VC
and ethene concentrations were observed approximately 15 and 40 days, respectively, after the lactate
concentration was increased. Results from Azizian et al. (2008) highlight the strong dependence of
dechlorination efficiency on electron donor availability. This is consistent with observations reported by
Lorah et al. (2008) that found that, despite the availability of dissolved organic carbon in the column,
PCE was not degraded until additional electron donor was added. In this study, the choice fell on ethanol
instead of lactate, but it led to similar conclusions. Indeed, biodegradation of PCE was not observed
before the amendment of ethanol, but removal rates rapidly increased after its addition, proving that the
dechlorination efficiency is strictly related to the supply of electron donor.

Laboratory and field studies on combined abiotic and biotic degradation
Peale et al. (2008) conducted column experiments to evaluate the remediation potential of the combination
of KB-1® culture with (1) emulsified soybean oil, (2) polylactate, and (3) EHC® Reagent – a blend of
hydrophilic carbon and ZVI. Columns were packed with soil from the investigated contaminated site and
groundwater was spiked with TCE at concentrations of approximately 10,000 µg/L representative of the
plume. No details on column set-up and concentrations of amendments were provided by Peale et al.
(2008). However, their conclusions were that the combination of EHC® and KB-1® showed the highest
remediation potential with removal of TCE and its degradation products. Complete dechlorination was
confirmed by an increase in ethene. Depending on the TCE spike concentrations and the duration of
the circulation period in the column, the degradation rate for TCE ranged between 0.2 and 0.6 day−1.
Pilot and field studies confirmed that the combination of EHC® and KB-1® is a promising strategy for
remediation of contaminated sites with chlorinated ethenes (Peale et al. 2008; Peale et al. 2010).

Site description
The V2-mapped site referred to as Naverland is one of the most severe chlorinated solvents contamination
in Denmark, and it is situated approximately 20 km west of Copenhagen (Clausen et al. 2010). The source
site is located at Naverland 26AB, where a storage facility was active between 1965 and 1982. Here, the
handling of perchloroethene (PCE), trichloroethene (TCE) and 1,1,1-trichloroethane (TCA) led to a very
significant release in the environment (Hedeselskabet 2002).
5.1
Geology and hydrology
The geology in the Naverland area is characterized by five dominant geological units: filling, clay till
with high clay content (ML1), clay till with low clay content (ML2), a mixture of meltwater sand and
crushed limestone, and finally limestone, as shown in Figure 5.1, which represents the main geological
layers at the site (Hemdorff 2013; Clausen et al. 2010). From the surface, top soil extends for 1-2 m bgs.,
and it is followed by 5-15 m of Quaternary clay deposits and then by meltwater sand and gravel. These
deposits lay above the Pre-quaternary, Bryozoan limestone formation, which is crushed in the first 0.5-1
m and fractured in the following 10 m, but extends for at least 30 m (Hemdorff 2013; Clausen et al. 2010).
Conversely to the glacier till which is predominantly dry, the limestone formation is mostly saturated
(COWI 2023).
The matrix porosity of the limestone in wells C1-C3, which are found in the proximity of the source
area, has been investigated, and it was found to vary between 13% and 48%, with an average of 33%
(Broholm et al. 2016). The fractures increase the complexity of the media by creating a dual porosity
media, where the flow follows preferential pathways along the fractures. Indeed, it is observed that
groundwater flows primarily in the pores of the crushed layer, whereas it is transported in the fractures in
underlying fractured limestone (COWI 2023). This reflects into a wide range of hydraulic conductivities
in the limestone aquifer. In the hot-spot area, for instance, hydraulic conductivity varies from 5×10−4 to

the redox conditions are presented in Appendix B.

5.4
Water extraction and risk
North of the hot-spot, several wells extract approximately 1.5 million cubic meters of groundwater
annually from the limestone aquifer for drinking water purposes (HOFOR 2024). In the plume, cDCE is
detected above regulatory limits, i.e. above 1 µg/L, in wells located 1.5 and 2.5 km from the source area,
and its concentration in well 200.4416 is expected to rise up to 20 µg/L in the next three decades (HOFOR
2024). Therefore, the contamination spreading from Naverland 26AB poses a threat to the drinking
water resource, and, according to model calculations that do not include degradation of the contaminants,
negative impacts on the groundwater reservoir could last for over 100 years (HOFOR 2024). HOFOR’s
strategy to mitigate the risk includes strategic pumping from well 200.4416 (Glostrup Forsyning) and
requires that the mitigation pumping from the source area is maintained (HOFOR 2024).

6.1
Collection and preparation of limestone
Limestone samples could not be obtained from the site. Therefore, limestone blocks were collected at
Faxe Quarry (Figure 6.1a) where the limestone is of the bryozoan and coral type (Jakobsen et al. 2021).
In the selected blocks, no flint inclusions were apparent and no flint was discovered inside the blocks
when crushing the limestone. Small blocks were collected to prepare the crushed limestone, and two
larger blocks were selected because they were large enough to drill 30 cm-long cores. Based on visual
observations, it seems that the limestone blocks had different characteristics. Indeed, the larger blocks
had visible fossils and seemed to have an induration of H3 whereas the small blocks seemed to have an
induration of H2. On the five-level scale, H2 is defined as weakly indurated (the material can be scratched
with a fingernail) and H3 is defined as indurated (the material cannot be scratched with a fingernail but
with a knife) (Jakobsen et al. 2021). It is possible that the larger blocks were of the coral type while the
smaller blocks were of the bryozoan type.

6.3
Experimental design
Saturated soil columns are usually used to represent the conditions found in the saturated zone of an aquifer.
The two main types of saturated soil columns are packed columns which use disturbed homogeneous soil,
and monolithic columns which use undisturbed soil such as cores (Lewis et al. 2010). The use of intact
cores could provide a better reproduction of field conditions, particularly for fractured aquifers. However,
intact core columns pose several problems: their reproducibility is much lower than that of columns with
homogeneous soil, as transport can easily differ from one core to another (Lewis et al. 2010). In addition,
it can be challenging to avoid lateral flow along the column walls (Jørgensen et al. 2019). As a result, it
can be difficult to compare the results of core columns if they exhibit different transport characteristics.
For these reasons, at the start of the thesis, it was decided to run three columns (A, B, and C) with packed
soil (crushed limestone) and one column (D) with a fractured limestone core. Columns A, B and C were
to receive different amendments and the results were to be compared. Column D with the fractured core
was to receive the same amendments as column C and was to be compared with column C in order to
study the difference in results between a homogeneous soil (crushed limestone) and a fractured core.
Due to time constraints during the thesis, the experiment was conducted without column D. The initial
reflections on the experimental set-up of the core column are presented in Appendix C. In the following,
only the experimental design for columns A, B and C is discussed.

The purpose of the tracer test is to characterize transport behavior by determining the effective porosity,
hydraulic residence time, and dispersion coefficient in each column at the beginning of the experiment.

6.5.1
Amendments in column A
Column A corresponds to the abiotic column. A suspension of sulfidated micro zero-valent iron (SmZVI),
supplied by Nanoiron, was added to this column to assess the potential for chemical reduction of cDCE
in limestone.
The product is a stabilized aqueous dispersion of spherical sulfidated ZVI microparticles, with an average
particle diameter of 10 µm (Figure 6.7a). The dispersion contains 20% microparticles by weight, and the
iron content in the particles is 90% Fe0, resulting in an overall Fe0 concentration of 18% by weight in the
dispersion. The density of the dispersion is 1.2 g/cm3 and Nanoiron indicated a pH comprised between
9.5 and 11.
Microscale particles were selected because of their mobility, which is lower than that of nanoscale
particles; it was hypothesized that microparticles would be more easily retained in the column and less

injection into the column, to simulate a push-injection approach similar to that used in field applications
(Lacinová et al. 2013).
Nanoiron recommended using concentrations of 2 to 5 gFe0/L GW, which aligns with the range of
typical ZVI concentrations for field applications. Indeed, Rangan et al. (2023) reported that typical
concentrations of Fe0 applied in the subsurface at contaminated sites are in the order of 0.2 to 10 gFe0/L

of chlorinated solvents (Table 2 in Rangan et al. 2023).

loss of particles retained in the syringe, a total of 48 mL of SmZVI suspension was injected, instead of
the 46 mL originally planned. However, it remains difficult to precisely estimate the amount of ZVI that
remained in the syringe, and therefore the exact mass of iron effectively injected into the column (Figure

Figure 7.4 shows the pH measured at the outlet of the three columns during every sampling event.

since corrosion of the iron particles occurs when they come in contact with water, releasing H2 and OH–

and isolated. On day 15, pH was as low as 6.3, but for the following 5 days it increased, until it became
relatively stable around the value of 7.

culture KB-1® for the entire time (Aziz et al. 2013).

Discussions

8.1
Evaluation of the experimental design and improvement suggestions
Designing and operating continuous-flow column experiments proved challenging; while certain aspects
of the set-up were successful, further improvements in the experimental design and protocols would
benefit future studies.
Limestone grains from bryozoan limestone blocks were obtained and columns were successfully packed
with these grains. Transport in the columns could be satisfactory described using the two-region non-
equilibrium model, yielding an average effective porosity of 0.45, which corresponds to the higher range
of porosities observed at the site (Broholm et al. 2016). A low flow of 0.17 mL/min, corresponding to a
pore water velocity of 90 m/year, realistic of aquifer conditions, was successfully applied throughout the
experiment. Using crushed limestone as a proxy for a fractured limestone aquifer cannot reflect perfectly
all transport phenomena. In particular, transport along preferential pathways through the fractures is a
critical aspect of transport in fractured limestone (Section 3.3), and could not be reproduced within the
experimental set-up. However, the grain size obtained – 1 to 2 mm – was still considered as a good
approximate to represent transport in fractured limestone (Bucur et al. 2007). Given the short time frame
of the experiment, the chosen column dimensions were adequate as an average hydraulic residence time
of 1.3 days was obtained, similar to the HRT used by Azizian et al. (2008) which was sufficient to observe
dechlorination (Section 4). Regarding the equipment, using plexiglas for the column was a good choice
as sorption of cDCE into plexiglas is very unlikely (Mendoza-Sanchez et al. 2010). Tedlar bags were easy
to handle and proved to be efficient at keeping the groundwater anaerobic. Contaminant loss from the
Tedlar bags during storage is considered to be low, as no decrease in inlet concentrations was observed
with increasing storage time. However, the difference in concentrations between samples collected at
the well and samples collected at the columns inlet indicates that contaminant loss occurred. Given that
the residence time in the tubing at the normal flow rate is approximately one hour, cDCE could have
sorbed into or diffused out of the Tygon® A-60-C tubing, despite its reported good chemical resistance.
Contaminant loss could also have occurred when filling the Tedlar bags during groundwater extraction.
Keeping the groundwater anaerobic was a challenge, as no anaerobic box was available. The system proved
to be diffusion-tight as no oxygen was detected in the groundwater at the outlet after saturation. Pumping
amendments into the columns proved complicated as column C temporarily clogged. Consequently,
amendments were added by injections through sampling ports. This resulted in an unequal distribution
of the ZVI particles in the column which could have enhanced aggregation of the particles, as a cluster of
particles was observed close to injection port (3) when unpacking the column. This could have reduced
the specific surface area of the particles, which could influence degradation rates (Dries et al. 2005).
Samples for chlorinated ethenes, anions, dissolved iron, VFAs, and gases were successfully collected
from the column. However, the sampling method could probably be optimized. Indeed, the sampling
protocol could have stressed the column by removing a significant amount of water during sampling
events where each port was sampled. In particular, when sampling for chlorinated ethenes at all ports, 30
mL of groundwater were withdrawn using a syringe – equivalent to approximately 9% of the column’s
pore volume (320 mL). This withdrawn volume increased to 46 mL (14% of the pore volume) when
55
==PAGE:63==
DTU
Master Thesis
June 2025
sampling for both gases and chlorinated ethenes. These percentages of withdrawn pore volume are higher
than the maximum of 2% recommended by Lorah et al. (2008). However, the flow rate was increased
to approximately 5 mL/min when sampling to limit draining of groundwater from the column. This
adjustment may have disrupted the groundwater distribution and introduced small gas bubbles into the
column. This could have resulted in partitioning of cDCE and VC between the groundwater and a gas
phase. The effect of such partitioning on the results is considered to be low given that an equilibrium
between the gas and aqueous phases would have been established and that no significant gas phase was
observed when unpacking the columns. Additionally, samples were collected sequentially — from the
outlet, then ports (3), (2), and finally (1). As a result, when sampling from port (1), located at 7.5 cm from
the inlet, some of the groundwater may have been newly drawn into the column to replace the volume
removed earlier by sampling the outlet, and ports (3) and (2). This could lead to artificially elevated
concentrations at port (1) if some of the sampled water had recently entered the column from the Tedlar
bag.
Improvement suggestions on the experimental set-up and protocols are as follow:
• Use limestone collected at the site. If limestone collected elsewhere, autoclave it prior to packing
column to avoid indigenous bacteria not present at the investigated site.
• Use nylon or stainless steel tubing to limit contaminant loss out of the system (Lorah et al. 2008).
Use Tygon® A-60-C tubing or other tubing suitable for peristaltic pumps only for the small portion
of tubing passing through the pump.
• Avoid variations in flow rate when sampling. Glass-on-glass luer-lock syringes could be used to
allow sampling at normal flow rate. The syringes are to be attached to the sampling ports, and are
supposed to fill naturally when closing the outlet of the column (Lorah et al. 2008).
• Measure contaminant concentrations at the inlet – port (0) – and directly from the Tedlar bag
regularly to monitor the stability of influent concentrations and to assess potential contaminant loss
in the tubing.
• Measure all degradation products – including ethene, ethane, acetylene – regularly to have a more
complete overview of degradation processes.
• Measure the dissolved sulfide concentrations to have a better overview of the redox conditions in
the system.
• Perform a bacterial analysis of the collected groundwater at the site to gain knowledge on bacteria
naturally present in the plume.
• Perform a bacterial analysis of limestone grains at the end of the experiment to gain knowledge
on the composition of the microbial community developed during the experiment and improve
robustness of results (Rangan et al. 2023; Azizian et al. 2008).
• Analyze samples quickly to have a real-time understanding of the system, enabling adjustments to
parameters such as amendment concentrations.
• Increase the column dimensions to increase the pore water volume and hydraulic residence time
inside the column; this would improve chances of observing degradation and would lower the
percentage of pore volume withdrawn during sampling events.
• Increase the duration of the experiment to enable adjustments, improve chances of observing
degradation, and increase robustness of results (Azizian et al. 2008; Lorah et al. 2008).
• Include a control column to facilitate results interpretation.

8.2
Evaluation of the mobility and longevity of the amendments
8.2.1
SmZVI
SmZVI particles appeared to react quickly, as Fe(II) was detected at column A outlet one day after the
product injection (Figure 7.5a). Stable to slightly decreasing concentrations of Fe(II) at the outlet until
the end of the experiment suggest maintained reactivity and good longevity of the particles in the column.
The effective longevity of SmZVI in the column could not be determined, as no significant decrease
in Fe(II) concentrations was observed due to the short duration of the experiment. Assuming that the
Fe(II) concentrations would have continued to decrease linearly at the rate observed during the last 10
days of the experiment, it can be estimated that the longevity of SmZVI in the column would have been
approximately 150 days.
From visual observations during the experiment and when unpacking the column, mobility of SmZVI
was assessed to be overall low as particles were only found close to the injection ports when unpacking
the column. A higher mobility of the iron particles in the column was expected due to the sulfide coating
(Fan et al. 2017). However, towards the end of the experiment, particles were observed in small quantities
in the effluent groundwater. In the last 10 centimeters of the column, iron precipitates were observed.
Therefore, mobility of SmZVI could be indirectly enhanced by the iron precipitates formed thanks to
release on iron (II) by ZVI oxidation.
8.2.2
EHC® Reagent Liquid Mix solution
The solution prepared from the dissolution of EHC® Reagent Liquid Mix powder was found to be highly
mobile. The solution was equally distributed in column C although it had been injected through the
lateral ports (Figure 7.3a). The very high iron (II) concentrations observed at the outlet one day after
the EHC® solution addition support the high mobility of the product (Figure 7.5b). From day 22 until
the last measurement on day 32, outlet iron (II) concentrations showed a linear decrease (coefficient of
determination R2 = 0.98). Assuming this linear trend had continued, it is estimated that only a limited
amount of EHC® would have remained in the column after day 39. However, the product led to formation
of precipitates that remained in the column.
The high mobility of the EHC® solution limits the evaluation of its longevity, as it was likely flushed out of
the column before it had ceased to be reactive. It should also be noted that the conventional formulation,
EHC® Liquid, consists of both the EHC® Reagent Liquid Mix powder and an ELS emulsion. Observations
of clogging suggest that the ELS emulsion significantly reduces the mobility of the product. Therefore, it
is likely that EHC® Liquid has a much lower mobility than the solution prepared from the powder alone.
8.2.3
Lactate
The mobility of lactate in the column was assessed by analyzing the peak in lactate concentrations detected
at the outlet of column B following the third injection (Figure 7.9b). The peak in lactate concentrations
appeared approximately 2.5 days after the pulse injection at the column inlet.
Using the hydraulic
residence time of 1.3 days estimated with the tracer test, a retardation factor of 1.9 can be determined
for lactate. This estimation relies on limited data. In literature, Koc values for lactic acid are reported to
be lower than 20.9 L/kg (PubChem 2025b). Using this Koc value, an foc value of 0.24% for limestone

column, a retardation of 1.2 for lactic acid was obtained. This calculated value is highly dependent on the

higher than the literature-based estimates, the retardation factor of 1.9 retrieved from experimental data

The low concentrations of lactate and other VFAs – acetate, propionate, butyrate – observed at the outlet
of the columns, compared to the mass of lactate injected, suggest that lactate was actively consumed
within the column and was therefore highly reactive (Figure 7.9). Consequently, longevity of lactate in
the column is considered to be short.
8.2.4
KB-1®
In column B, the consistent trend of cDCE concentrations decreasing at a high rate between ports
(1) and (2) suggests that bacterial activity was high in the bottom part of the column (Figures 7.12).
Bacterial activity could also be high in the upper part, but this is harder to assess since the contaminant
concentrations were low from the middle of the column. No bacterial analyses were performed, therefore
differentiation between indigenous bacteria and amended bacteria is complicated. In their experiment,
Azizian et al. (2008) observed an overall equal repartition of Dehalococcoides in the column, with
slighly higher concentrations towards the inlet. The consistent low values of cDCE outlet concentrations
over time in column B suggests that the bacteria were efficiently retained in the column (Figure 7.10b).
Therefore, the mobility of KB-1® culture is assessed to be low, and its longevity is assessed to be high as
long as good conditions for its growth are provided (Aziz et al. 2013).
8.3
Assessment of the potential for complete biotic and abiotic degradation of cDCE with
the selected amendments
The lack of complete information on by- and end-products (acetylene and ethene) did not create favorable
conditions for the estimation of mass balances of cDCE and its degradation products in the columns. In
the literature reviewed, the sum of the molar concentrations of cDCE and its degradation products was
overall constant at the outlet over time, as shown in Figure 4.2 (Azizian et al. 2008; Mendoza-Sanchez et
al. 2010) and Figure 4.1 (Qian et al. 2023). However, this was made impossible to verify in this study due
to missing data on acetylene and ethene. Moreover, ethene was not detected at the outlet of the columns
on day 35, likely due to microbial oxidation to CO2 (Fullerton et al. 2013). Indeed, under anaerobic
conditions, some microorganisms can oxidize ethene while reducing sulfate, whose concentration in the
effluent of all columns was found to be around 100 mg/L. Fullerton et al. (2013) emphasized that deficits
in mass balances of chlorinated ethenes and their degradation products in sulfate reducing zones might
be due to ethene oxidation. This is consistent with the deficit in mass balances observed by Damgaard
et al. (2013) in their remediation assessment.
The consistently lower cDCE concentrations measured at the outlet of column A compared to columns B
and C suggest that column A performed best in terms of cDCE degradation (Figure 7.10). The comparison
between column B and column C results is more difficult, since the concentration of cDCE at the outlet
were similar for both columns. However, in column C, the toxic by-product VC was consistently detected,

the experiment (Figure 7.10 b). This suggests that column B performed better than column C in terms of
cDCE dechlorination.
8.3.1
Column A
In column A, amended with ZVI, it was expected that cDCE would be mainly degraded into acetylene
by β-elimination, and that acetylene would further be degraded into ethene (Rodrigues et al. 2020).
Therefore, the low VC concentrations measured in the column are consistent with abiotic degradation
(Cwiertny et al. 2010a). Since acetylene was not measured and ethene was likely transformed to CO2
by biological oxidation, observing a complete mass balance at the outlet was not possible. However,
the abiotic dechlorination of cDCE can be considered almost complete, as only small amounts of cDCE
and VC were detected – always below 0.07 and 0.01 µmol/L, respectively – after day 19 (Figure 7.10a).
On days 16 and 18, the molar concentration of VC was, respectively, 3.8 and 3.3 times higher than the
molar concentration of cDCE. Such a higher concentration of VC suggests that indigenous bacteria from
the groundwater might have contributed to the dechlorination of cDCE in the column. The enhanced
bacterial activity was likely facilitated by hydrogen production in the column due to ZVI corrosion.
Iron (II) concentrations and visual observations showed that ZVI particles were oxidized (Figure 7.5a).
Rangan et al. (2023) reported that one of the major limitations of ZVI remediation is the loss of reactivity
toward chlorinated ethenes due to reaction with water.
However, since cDCE concentrations were
consistently very low after ZVI addition, it can be assumed that sulfidation of the particles enhanced
selectivity toward chlorinated ethenes (Qian et al. 2023).
In addition, chemical reduction by ZVI usually leads to an increase in pH, which was not observed in
column A (Figure 7.4). This could be explain by the buffering properties of limestone and by the relatively
low hydraulic residence time (29 hours) in the column. Indeed, as mentioned in Section 4, Dries et al.
(2005) did not observe an increase in pH in their column and partly attributed that to the HRT of 23 hours
only. Moreover, the sulfidation of the ZVI particles minimizes the corrosion of iron, thus limiting the
release of OH– and preventing an increase in pH values (Fan et al. 2016).
8.3.2
Column B
In column B, amended with lactate and KB-1®, it was expected that cDCE would be degraded into VC
by hydrogenolysis (Löffler et al. 2013a), and that VC would further be degraded into ethene and ethane
as shown in Figure 4.2b. However, under iron to sulfate reducing conditions, ethene is not transformed
into ethane, but is rather oxidized to CO2 (Fullerton et al. 2013). Therefore, a complete mass balance
cannot be performed; however biodegradation of cDCE to VC and further degradation products appears
likely (Figure 7.10b). As already discussed, throughout the experiment, several decreases observed in
cDCE concentrations can be linked to the injections of lactate in the column. The concentration profiles
of cDCE indicate that, starting from the first lactate injection, cDCE concentrations rapidly decreased
after the first injection port, located 7.5 cm from the inlet, and remained low in the upper part of the
column (Figures F.8 and F.9, Appendix F). Hence, it appears that, even before the addition of KB-1®, the
amendment of electron donors stimulated the biodegradation of cDCE. This suggests the potential for the
presence of indigenous bacteria – more specifically Dhc – in the groundwater collected at the Naverland

site, where a lack of electron donors could be the limiting factor for reductive dechlorination. However,
the addition of KB-1® seemed to have enhanced VC biodegradation, as no VC was detected after day 20.
This could indicate that the indigenous bacteria might be insufficient for complete removal of VC from
the system. The low concentrations of VFAs observed at the outlet support the assumption of significant
microbial activity with lactate being consumed in the column (Figure 7.9b).
8.3.3
Column C
In column C, amended with a EHC® Liquid Reagent Mix solution, lactate and KB-1®, it was expected to
observe both abiotic and biotic degradation of cDCE. The absence of acetylene and ethene measurements
could not allow to assess directly the contribution of biotic and abiotic dechlorination to the total
degradation of cDCE.
The cDCE concentration in the column significantly decreased after the injection of EHC® (day 13), but
almost no further change was observed until after the first injection of lactate (day 15). This is consistent
with the concentrations of VFAs – below detection limits until day 16 – suggesting that the organic carbon
contained in the EHC® product as gluconate was not immediately available to the microorganisms as
electron donor. Therefore, despite the probable presence of indigenous bacteria in the groundwater, the
unavailability of electron donors might have prevented biodegradation within those three days (Azizian
et al. 2008). Moreover, having the EHC® solution such a low pH (approximately 3.3), the microbial
community that had developed until then on the limestone could have been negatively affected after
the injection. Therefore, in the phase between days 13 and 18, the abiotic dechlorination is likely the
dominant process in the column. However, after lactate and KB-1® addition, VC was detected regularly
at the outlet, with concentrations ranging between 0.02 and 0.11 µmol/L, while cDCE concentration
fluctuates, but appears to be progressively decreasing (Figure 7.10c). The consistent detections of VC at
the outlet after day 19 suggest that biodegradation increased towards the end of the experiment.
The color of the column changed immediately after the injection of the EHC® (day 13), but over time
the color faded, until day 24–25, when the column had assumed his natural appearance. Looking at the
results of the VFAs analysis (Figure 7.9c), a parallelism can be seen with the visual aspect of the column.
Indeed, between days 16 and 24, a peak in concentrations of VFAs, as acetate and butyrate, was measured
at the outlet. The VFAs peak is also reflected on the chlorinated ethenes concentrations, with cDCE
that reached its lowest concentration (0.06 µmol/L) observed during the entire experiment, and VC that
maintained a concentration of 0.09 µmol/L over three consecutive days (Figure 7.10c). Over this period,
removal of cDCE was probably caused by a combination of biodegradation and chemical reduction, the
latter favored by the high concentrations of iron (Figure 7.5b). In the last part of the experiment, after day
25, the column turned gradually gray, probably due to iron precipitates. These compounds are capable
of reducing chlorinated ethenes (Cwiertny et al. 2010a), thus contributing to the abiotic degradation.
8.4
Potential for field application of the tested strategies and future perspectives
The results from the continuous-flow column experiments conducted in this thesis suggest that both
enhanced biotic and abiotic degradation of cDCE in limestone are possible with the tested strategies.
Column A, amended with SmZVI, showed the best performance regarding dechlorination of cDCE,
encouraging to consider ZVI as a remediation strategy for Naverland site.
However, extending the

duration of the experiment beyond 35 days would have likely benefited columns B and C in which biotic
degradation was ongoing (Azizian et al. 2008). Moreover, decrease in cDCE concentrations and increase
in VC concentrations in columns B and C after biostimulation, but before bioaugmentation with KB-1®,
suggest that Dehalococcoides might be present in the plume. This encourages to also consider enhanced
reductive dechlorination as a remediation strategy for the site.
Contrary to expectations, column C
– combining enhanced abiotic and biotic degradation – did not show the best performance regarding
cDCE dechlorination compared to columns A and B. Nevertheless, combination of abiotic and biotic
remediation strategies should not be ruled out. Indeed, the unconventional use of EHC® Liquid Reagent
Mix powder alone instead of its blend with ELS® Microemulsion could have affected its performance. In
addition, several field studies have shown encouraging results by combining biotic and abiotic remediation
strategies, such as EHC® with KB-1®, and lactate with nZVI (Peale et al. 2010; Lacinová et al. 2013).
In particular, Lacinová et al. (2013) observed superior performance of the combined lactate and nZVI
strategy compared to either the biotic or abiotic strategies alone. Therefore, it is recommended to conduct
further bench tests to increase the robustness of the results prior to implementing a pilot field study.
First, it is recommended to perform batch experiments to obtain an initial assessment of the remediation
potential of the selected amendment strategies.
Batch tests offer the advantages of being easier to
replicate and simpler to set up than column experiments.
This approach would allow the set-up of
multiple batches amended with the same products at varying concentrations, thereby ensuring greater
robustness of the results. Indeed, degradation can be highly sensitive to amendment concentrations
(Azizian et al. 2008). Consequently, it can be unfair to compare biotic and abiotic remediation strategies
based on only a single amendment concentration for each. Moreover, the closed-system nature of batch
tests would facilitate interpretation of results during the initial evaluation of amendments, compared to
the open-system conditions characteristic of continuous-flow columns.
Secondly, it is recommended to perform continuous-flow column experiments using the remediation
strategies identified as most promising from the batch experiments. Indeed, continuous-flow columns
present the advantages of better replicating aquifer conditions than batch tests due to the flow of ground-
water through a media. Schaefer et al. (2009) highlighted that degradation kinetics vary from batch to
column tests. Consequently, column experiments would provide a better understanding of the processes
that could occur in the aquifer. This would allow to help designing a pilot field study with the remediation
strategy evaluated as most promising after column experiments.
Finally, besides reactivity, longevity and mobility are important factors to consider when selecting
amendments.
Indeed, as mentioned in Section 3.3, in fractured limestone aquifers, the matrix acts
as a contaminant reservoir due to its high porosity (Shapiro et al. 2018). In addition, the matrix low
porosity makes remediation challenging as it is complicated to obtain the necessary contact between the
amendments and the contaminants (Kidmose et al. 2022; Damgaard et al. 2013; Shapiro et al. 2018).
Consequently, a remediation approach is to enhance degradation in the fractures, leading to concentration
gradients that promote back diffusion of the contaminants from the matrix to the fractures, therefore
decreasing concentrations in the matrix (Shapiro et al. 2018; Underwood et al. 2022). Diffusion of
amendments into the matrix would directly enhance degradation of the contaminants inside the matrix
(Damgaard et al. 2013). Therefore, it is desirable for the amendments to be mobile enough to distribute
through the fracture system and diffuse into the matrix. However, a too high mobility could limit the
contact time between the amendments and the contaminants. A high longevity of the amendments is

Conclusions
The aim of this thesis was to investigate the enhanced biotic and abiotic degradation of cDCE in limestone
using continuous-flow column experiments, with the final goal of providing a preliminary assessment
of the potential for in-situ remediation of the cDCE-contaminated plume in the limestone aquifer at
the Naverland site. Although numerous studies have focused on the remediation of chlorinated ethenes
through biological dechlorination and chemical reduction, none – to the knowledge of the authors – have
used continuous-flow columns packed with limestone grains with cDCE as the main influent contaminant.
Overall, the experiment design proved to be successful and provided many insights on degradation
processes and effectiveness of the amendments.
• SmZVI appeared to react rapidly after injection, and to have very limited mobility of the particles
and good longevity.
• EHC® Reagent Liquid Mix solution had relatively high reactivity with cDCE and very high mobility.
Longevity revealed challenging to quantify, since the product was being flushed out of the column.
• Lactate showed relatively high mobility and short longevity in the system.
• Abiotic dechlorination of cDCE was nearly complete in column A, with cDCE and VC outlet
concentrations always lower than 0.07 and 0.01 µmol/L, respectively, from day 19.
• Reductive dechlorination in column B led to efficient removal of cDCE, with little to none accu-
mulation of VC in the system. This column showed the potential for biostimulation as an effective
remediation strategy for the Naverland plume.
• The combination of biotic and abiotic dechlorination in column C caused relatively high removal
of chlorinated ethenes, with final outlet concentrations of 0.03 µmol/L for cDCE and 0.01 µmol/L
for VC.
• Overall, the best removal efficiency of chlorinated ethenes was observed for column A (abiotic
degradation), followed by column B (enhanced biodegradation) and then by column C (combination
of biotic and abiotic).
• Longer time frame, more frequent addition of lactate, and use of conventional formulation of EHC®
might have changed the ranking of the remediation strategies.
• Further testing is recommended before choosing the amendments for field application. Indeed, on
the long term, enhanced biodegradation or the combination of biotic and abiotic degradation could
be more efficient than chemical reduction alone.
• The presence of indigenous bacteria, potentially including Dehalococcoides genus, in the ground-
water creates the conditions for implementation of enhanced reductive dechlorination at the site as
a feasible and effective remediation strategy.
• High longevity of amendments is desired to mitigate effects of back diffusion from the limestone
matrix after stopping treatment.
Improvement suggestions to the experimental design were proposed, and it is recommended to conduct
further bench studies to gain a deeper understanding of the potential of the remediation strategies appli-
cable to the site. A longer experimental time frame should be considered to allow dynamic adjustments
to amendment concentrations and to improve the observation of degradation processes. Additionally,
changes could be implemented to better mimic the transport phenomena characteristic of fractured lime-
stone aquifer.

Figure B.1 shows the location of three V2-mapped contaminated sites with chlorinated ethenes that
contribute to the development of the plume generated from Naverland 26AB. The sites are Nordre Ringvej
67 (Glostrup Hospital), which is situated sourt-east of Naverland 26AB, Fabriksparken 26, near borehole

chlorinated ethenes above the drinking water quality criteria (Miljøministeriet 2021), the source strength
of the contamination is much greater in Naverland 26AB (HOFOR 2024). However, the hot-spot at
Glostrup Hospital presents high concentrations of PCE, TCE and cDCE, which are up to 3200, 220 and
54 µg/L, respectively (HOFOR 2024). Once the hydrological conditions in the area are considered, it
is clear that the contamination at Glostrup Hospital does not contribute to the main plume generated
from Naverland 26AB, since groundwater flow primarily southwards (HOFOR 2024). Conversely, the
hot-spots at Fabriksparken 26 and Herstedøstergade 46 affect the Naverland plume, due to north-eastern
movement of the contaminated groundwater (HOFOR 2024). It is worth noticing that the groundwater
used during the experiment was abstracted from well M1, which is located up-gradient of well F4 Fabrik,
and it is therefore not affected by the Fabriksparken 26 hot-spot.

Considerations on experimental set-up of a fractured core column
As mentioned in Section 6.3, the experimental design initially included a fractured core column, desig-
nated as Column D. However, due to time constraints related to the delivery of the columns, it was not
possible to implement this part of the setup. Nonetheless, the initial considerations for Column D are
presented in this appendix.
Figure C.1: Schematic representation of the experimental set-up planned for column D.
Figure C.1 shows a schematic representation of the experimental set-up planned for column D. The
column was to be made out of plexiglas and to have a length of 34.4 cm as columns A, B and C, but a
larger internal diameter of 10 cm. As with the other columns, glass beads and stainless steel meshes were
to be positioned at both ends of Column D to ensure uniform distribution of influent and effluent.
The longitudinally fractured core, prepared at DTU Construct Concrete lab, was to be placed in the center
of the column, as illustrated in Figure C.1. This core had a length of 29.4 cm and a diameter of 6.45 cm
81
==PAGE:89==
DTU
Master Thesis
June 2025
when the two halves were assembled. The fracture surfaces were roughened using sandpaper to create
asperities that would facilitate adhesion of the bacteria to the limestone. The intended aperture of the
fracture was 1 mm.
One of the main challenges with core columns is preventing side flow along the column walls, which
can bias transport processes in the column. To avoid such lateral flow, space between the column walls
and the fractured core was to be filled with expanding polyurethane foam (Jørgensen et al. 2019). To
minimize pressure buildup that could risk damaging the column, the foam was to be applied in small,
incremental rings, allowing each layer to fully expand before adding the next. Moreover, it was planned
to saturate the fractured core with contaminated groundwater prior to placing it in the column. This was
done to ensure that no oxygen remained in the limestone pores and to introduce chlorinated ethenes into
the matrix. The saturation was to be performed using the vacuum technique described by Hansen et al.
(2014). Specifically, the core was to be placed in a desiccator, from which the air would be evacuated
using an Alcatel 230 V vacuum pump for five hours. Then, contaminated groundwater from a Tedlar
bag would be drawn into the desiccator due to the resulting pressure differential. Additionally, to prevent
contaminant diffusion into the polyurethane foam, the saturated fractured core was to be wrapped in
Teflon tape before placement in the column (Wefer-Roehl et al. 2001). Finally, to ensure that groundwater
flow would be directed primarily through the core rather than the foam, stainless steel rings with an inner
diameter of 5.5 cm were to be installed at the top and bottom of the core.
