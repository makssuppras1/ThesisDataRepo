Abstract
Background Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) poses a major threat to human health, due to the high burden
of disease (BoD) it has. Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus is one of the leading pathogens of the
AMR burden. Other studies have modelled the transmission of MRSA but have not included new inter-
ventions such as monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) to investigate the effect of these on the burden.
Methods his thesis is based on a literature review and TRACE evaluation to asses already published mod-
els. With the goal of finding a baseline for the model of the transmission and BoD calculation. I used
a population-based, SCIS, compartment model to simulate the transmission of MRSA and from this pre-
dicted the total number of infected and deaths yearly. The models include both a model with antibiotic
treatment and a model with mAbs treatment.
Findings The literature review found seven relevant articles with a focus on MRSA transmission. The
TRACE evaluation showed that none of the articles fulfilled all eight elements of the TRACE guidelines, in
general only six out of eight elements were completely fulfilled. This model was able to simulate MRSA in
Denmark, with simulated cases matching the reported cases in Denmark, and a transmission mainly in the
community. Further, the model predicted the number of infected and deaths yearly. Leading to a burden
of disease for the model with antibiotics on 0.059 DALY/case and 0.055 DALY/case for one of the models
with mAbs.
Conclusion The usage of literature reviews and TRACE evaluations is a good way to assess models and
to get an idea of what to do and not to do in model development. Compartment models are useful for
the simulation of transmission and further for investigating the effects of interventions. Introducing new
interventions such as mAbs could potentially lower the burden of AMR.

Resume
Baggrund Antimikrobiel resistens (AMR) udgør en stor trussel mod den globale sundhed, på grund af den
høje sygdomsbyrde (BoD) den udgør. MRSA er en af de ledende patogene der udgør AMR byrden. Andre
studier har simuleret overførelsen af MRSA, men de har ikke inkluderet nye interventioner som monok-
lonale antistoffer (mAbs) for at undersøge effekten af disse på byrden af MRSA.
Metode Denne afhandling er baseret på et litteraturstudie og TRACE evaluering for at vurdere allerede
publiceret modeller. Med formålet om at finde en baseline til modellen for overførelsen af MRSA og BoD
udregningerne. Jeg har brugt en population-baseret, SCIS, compartment model til at simulere overførelsen
af MRSA og udfra dette forudsige det samlede antal inficerede og døde årligt. Modellerne omfatter en
model med antibiotikabehandling og en model med mAbs-behandling.
Fund Litteraturstudiet fandt syv relevante artikler med fokus på MRSA-overførsel. TRACE evaluering
viste at ingen af artiklerne opfyldte alle otte elementer fra TRACE, generelt var kun seks ud af otte ele-
menter fuldstændigt udfyldt. Modellen udarbejdet i denne afhandling er i stand til at simulere MRSA i
Danmark, med simulerede cases, der matchede de rapporterede cases i Danmark, og med en overførelse
som primært sker i samfundet. Desuden forudsagde modellen antallet af inficerede og døde årligt, som
giver til en sygdomsbyrde for modellen med antibiotika på 0,059 DALY/case og 0,055 DALY/case for én af
modellerne med mAbs.
Konklusion Brugen af literaturstudier og TRACE evalueringer er en god måde at vurdere modeller og
få en ide om hvad man skal gøre og ikke gøre i udviklingen af modellen. Compartment modeller er nyttige
til at simulere transmission og yderligere til at undersøge effekten af interventioner. Implementering af nye
interventioner såsom mAbs kan potentielt sænke byrden af AMR.

Introduction
Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is one the biggest public health threats and approximately 700,000 individ-
uals worldwide die each year due to resistant infection, and the number is estimated to exceed 10 million
by 2050 [1]. The global burden of deaths associated with bacterial AMR in 2019 was estimated to be 4.95
million [2]. Antibiotics are becoming increasingly ineffective as drug resistance spreads globally and the
clinical pipeline of new antibiotics is dry [3]. The emergence and spread of AMR are especially due to
the overuse and misuse of antimicrobials in humans and animals [4]. Due to overuse and misuse, many
countries have restrictions on usage, both in health care and livestock. WHO has made guidelines for the
usage of antibiotics and made examples of other antibiotics to use instead and demanded that the usage of
antibiotics critical for curing life-threatening diseases are used less to treat other diseases [5][6][7].
AMR makes infections harder to treat and increases the risk of disease spread, severe illness and death
[3]. AMR is due to changes in bacteria, as these evolve and adapt to new environments, and this causes
the drugs used to treat infections to become less effective [2]. AMR is a global threat as the emergence and
spread of drug-resistant bacteria means that the treatment of simple infections becomes more difficult and
new drugs must be developed. However, during the last decade, resistance has occurred simultaneously
with the development of new drugs [8]. As many bacteria are resistant towards antibiotics it is necessary
to look at other ways to treat or prevent diseases caused by these. New ways to do this are by the use of
vaccination or the use of monoclonal antibodies (mAbs).
Staphylococcus aureus (S. aureus) is one of the most important human pathogens worldwide. Its high antibi-
otic resistance profile reinforces the need for new interventions like vaccines and mAbs in addition to new
antibiotics [9]. Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) is one of the resistant bacteria that have
caused problems for decades. MRSA caused more than 100,000 deaths attributable to AMR in 2019 and is
one of the six pathogens leading the burden of AMR [2]. MRSA including outbreaks with this was seen
more and more at hospitals, and since 2009 an AMR surveillance program including MRSA has been used
in Europe. This program keeps track of multiple resistant bacteria and how the spread and emergence of
these evolve [10]. During the last decade, the transmission of MRSA has decreased but there is still seen an
increase in the cases associated with community and livestock.
The research goal of this project is to develop a mathematical model that can investigate the effect of in-
terventions towards MRSA infections. The model should be able to calculate the burden of disease (BoD)
and investigate how interventions such as vaccination and mAbs would affect the BoD. The BoD will be ex-
pressed in disability-adjusted life years (DALYs), which is an expression of healthy life lost due to disability.
This project contributes to a work package concerning a systematic review of AMR models including trans-
mission, interventions and secondary infections. This review covers several pathogens, where S. aureus and
MRSA are only one of them. The work package is a part of the EU project - Predicting the Impact of Mon-
oclonal Antibodies & Vaccines on Antimicrobial Resistance (PrIMAVeRa). The overall goal of PrIMAVeRa
is to "develop mathematical models and an epidemiological repository that will facilitate the assessment of

Background
2.1
Staphylococcus aureus
S. aureus is a gram-positive bacterium, which is usually arranged in grape-like clusters. It is a natural part
of the human bacterial flora and is often found in the nasal cavity, mucous membranes, and on the skin [12].
Approximately 50% of the human population carries S. aureus, where 20-30% have it as a permanent part
of their normal flora (referred to as permanent carriers) and 30-50% are intermittent carriers, which means
they only have it in their normal flora in periods [13].
S. aureus can cause infections but often a carrier will be an asymptomatic carrier and will not experience
sickness or discomfort [13]. Most often the infections caused by S. aureus is inflammation in cuts, wounds
and lesions in the skin, impetigo, and abscesses. However, if an immune-weakened person gets an infection
it can lead to more severe infections, such as bacteremia, pneumonia, osteomyelitis and septic arthritis [12].
The risk of getting these infections is higher when admitted to the hospital, as placement of catheters in
blood vessels, drains and operations increase the risk of getting an S. aureus infection [13]. S. aureus can
cause both a direct infection and in-direct infection by releasing toxins. The direct infections are the ones
mentioned above and the in-direct infections are Staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome and Toxic shock
syndrome, which are two infections caused by the toxins S. aureus release. As mentioned, bacteremia is one
of the infections S. aureus can cause. This is a state where bacteria are present in the blood, and it can lead to
bloodstream infections (BSI), sepsis, and death. In Denmark 2.512 cases of Staphylococcus aureus bacteremia
(SAB) were found in 2021 [14].

2.1.1
Transmission of S.aureus
S. aureus is often transmitted from contaminated hands. It can be transmitted from human to human both
direct and in-direct (devices), and animals to humans [12]. The transmission occurs when susceptible per-
sons meet either a permanent carrier or intermittent carrier, who has the possibility to transfer the bacteria
to another person. The transmission can also occur by in-direct contact, meaning that a susceptible person
touches a device, (e.g., a door handle) that has been used by a S. aureus carrier who has transferred bacteria
to the device. Figure 1 shows the routes of transmission. Here it is seen how a susceptible individual can
become a carrier or infected from three different routes. The number of bacteria on the skin determines the
infection risk. Staphylococci can bind to skin cells, which may end up in dust that can be stirred up into the
air. As many carriers have S. aureus in the nasal cavity and throat it can also be spread by coughing [15].
Figure 1: Transmission of S. aureus. Green indicates a susceptible individual, red indicates that it is a carrier or infected.
A susceptible individual can be infected directly by another person or animal or indirectly by a contaminated device,
which is represented as the door.
2.1.2
Treatment of S. aureus infections
The common treatment of S. aureus infections are the usage of antibiotics. The most prescribed antibiotics
include cefazolin, nafcillin, oxacillin, vancomycin, daptomycin and linezolid. To remove any pus or fluid
in skin infections a skin debridement is made around the infected tissue, and lastly, the area is thoroughly
cleaned. If the infection is due to a medical device, such as a catheter or artificial joint, the device will be
removed [16].
2.2
Methicillin-Resistant Staphylococcus aureus
MRSA is a multi-drug-resistant bacterium, meaning it is resistant to more than one antibiotic. MRSA has
resistance towards penicillin and drugs related to penicillin. Methicillin was the first drug developed to
fight S. aureus, it was introduced in 1959 and the first resistant bacteria were found in 1962 and 1968, in the
United Kingdom and the United States, respectively [17] [8]. Methicillin resistance is due to one of the two
genes: mecA and mecC. These genes are not a natural part of S. aureus but have been transferred from other
bacteria, making some strains of S. aureus resistant to Methicillin. As mentioned in section 2.1 S. aureus can
cause different types of infections, which for the most are harmless, however, if the patient is infected with

MRSA the treatment is more difficult and even a harmless infection can become lethal. MRSA can survive
for a long time (months) in the environment (e.g., in bed linen, on furniture, floors and objects), this is due

2.2.1
Transmission of MRSA
MRSA is primarily transmitted by close contact with people or animals and only to a smaller extent by
touching MRSA-contaminated objects. A single exposure to MRSA will not automatically make a person a
carrier. This means that the risk of becoming a carrier will increase when a person has close and frequent
contact with MRSA carriers, meaning living in the same household or working with MRSA-infected ani-
mals, while the risk of becoming a carrier through social contact is small [15]. Healthcare staff can transfer
MRSA from one patient to another, from contaminated objects to a patient or from themselves to a pa-
tient. Transfer of MRSA by hand contact is by far the most common infection route [15]. The transmission
of MRSA follows the same routes as seen in figure 1. As the transmission can occur in different places
the infections caused by MRSA are often referred to as, community-, hospital- or livestock acquired based
on where the transmission occurred. In Denmark, livestock-acquired MRSA refers to MRSA type CC398,
which is found in pigs/livestock. Table 1 shows the distribution of the 2021 cases of MRSA in Denmark.
2.2.2
Surveillance of MRSA
Denmark has a surveillance system towards MRSA. All clinician has a reporting duty when an infection
caused by MRSA is found. This means that data regarding MRSA infections are logged at Statens Serum
Institut, and it is possible to find information regarding age, gender, type and if the case is imported or not.
In 2006 MRSA was made notifiable and all new cases must be reported to the surveillance program. Since
2007 and until 2019 there was a rise in new cases of MRSA in Denmark. With approximately 3500 new cases
each year in the period from 2016-2019, however in 2020, there was a fall in new cases, which were said to
be due to Covid-19 and the restriction that made both travelling and social interaction harder. In 2021 2,714
cases were registered, where 2,460 were acquired in Denmark and 254 cases were acquired abroad. In 2022
2,963 cases were reported with 310 cases associated with travelling [19]. The cases acquired in Denmark
are split into Community-acquired with contact with the hospital or nursing home, Community-acquired
with no contact to the hospital or nursing home, Hospital-acquired and Livestock-acquired. Table 1 gives
an overview of the cases reported in 2021 in Denmark, where most of the MRSA transmission occurs in the
community with no contact with hospitals and nursing homes as this accounts for 44% of all cases. Further
Livestock-acquired MRSA plays a big role in the total amount of cases, this is due to the transmission from
animal to humans, which is often seen when working with livestock, such as pigs [20].

2.3
Interventions
To prevent the spread of resistant bacteria different interventions can be used. In Denmark the Danish
Health Authority made a guide on how to prevent the spread of MRSA, the newest version is from 2016.
This guidance has the purpose to maintain a low prevalence of disease caused by resistant bacteria, such as
MRSA [15]. In Denmark, the interventions towards MRSA spread are Screening, Hygiene and Isolation.
The Screening in Denmark looks at different risk groups and based on a questionnaire the doctor decides
if the patient should be screened for MRSA or not [22]. In Denmark, you can be tested for MRSA if you
fulfil the screening criteria even though you do not have a visible infection, in this case, you will be treated
as a carrier. By treating already in the carrier state it is possible to prevent further spread.
Hygiene is a standard intervention towards infections, in Denmark, we have National Infection Hygiene
Guidelines [23]. These guidelines are made to prevent disease spread, and the following are the six most im-
portant precautions: Hand hygiene; Work clothes; Personal protective equipment; Reprocessing of equip-
ment; Cleaning of rooms and inventory and Handling of patient secretions and sample material [23]. All
steps are made to prevent the spread of the bacteria.
Isolation is used at hospitals to prevent the spread of MRSA at the hospital. This means that if an admitted
patient gets a positive MRSA test, they will be moved to a single room with their own bath and toilet, and
the door will be marked with a sign indicating that there is MRSA in the room. The healthcare workers
will be extra careful regarding hygiene when visiting the patient in isolation, to make sure MRSA does not
spread [23].

There are different ways to target S.aureus and MRSA infections, these include antibiotic treatment, mAbs
and vaccines. In figure 2 a schematic view of different methods for targeting S.aureus is shown. The most
common way of targeting a bacterial infection is by using antibiotics, which work directly on the bacteria.
Both vaccines and mAbs are in the developing stages and are thought to be new ways to target or prevent
S. aureus and MRSA infections [9].

Treatment in Denmark is carried out when you are a carrier. If you have an infection this must be treated

mAbs are laboratory-made proteins, that act like the antibodies in the human body. Antibodies are a part
of the immune system and they bind to antigens to let the immune system know what to fight [25]. In

and research. mAbs are thought to be a new tool to prevent the spread of AMR. Here they are to be used as
treatment against infections. Looking at S. aureus different types of mAbs have been developed and are in
phase I or II [26]. In 2021 both AstraZeneca and X-Biotech had completed phase II of their clinical trial with
mAbs against Hla and SpA, respectively [9]. mAbs are thought to be used as treatment against S. aureus
and MRSA infections, as an alternative to antibiotics, as the resistance makes this way of treatment harder.
Vaccines can be used prophylaxis to prevent the spread and infections with MRSA. This is a way to prevent
the spread, whereas antibiotics and mAbs are used as a treatment when the spread and infection have
occurred. Development of vaccines against S. aureus have failed so far, and many do not proceed to clinical
trials [9]. One of the factors that hold back the development of vaccines, is the complexities of the host-
pathogen relationship between humans and S. aureus. Another problem they face is the lack of successful

2.5
Burden of Disease
The burden of disease (BoD) is a measure used to quantify the impact of living with illness and injury and
dying prematurely [27]. When calculating BoD the measure (DALYs) is used. DALYs is a measure of years
of life lost due to premature mortality (YLLs) and years of life lost due to disability (YLDs) [28]. To calculate
the BoD the following step-wise approach is used [29].
1. Study population definition - Age, gender, where and time-period
2. Disease model definition - Disease tree (Outcome-based, Hazard-based or Risk factor-based disease
models)
3. Data collection - Collection of the needed data regarding the study population, e.g. Total number
of males and females, per age group, of the selected area and time, the life-expectancy (LE) table,
number of cases in the different health states defined by the disease model, including death, and
disability weights (DW)
4. Data adjustment - Adjustment for potential bias in data
5. DALY calculation - When all data is acquired the DALY calculation can be done
Based on these steps an estimate of the BoD is obtained and this estimate can be used to interpret the specific
disease burden and compare diseases with different symptoms and severity.
2.6
Mathematical models
Mathematical models are an important tool in infectious disease epidemiology [30]. They are widely used
to examine, explain and predict the dynamics of infectious disease transmission, and further they play an
important role in developing public health strategies for control and prevention [31].
Using mathematical modelling of disease transmission always comes with a trade-off between simple mod-
els and detailed models. Simple models omit most details and are designed to highlight general qualitative
behaviour, whereas detailed models often are designed for specific situations and include short-term qual-
itative predictions [32].
Compartment models are often used to describe the transmission of disease in a population. Here the
population is divided into compartments and has assumptions about the nature and time rate of transfer
from one compartment to another. In compartment models, time is the independent variable and the rates
of transfer between compartments are expressed mathematically as derivatives with respect to time, and
the models are formulated initially as differential equations [32].
To model an epidemic, the population is divided into three compartments susceptible (S), infected (I) and
removed (R), and let S(t) denote the number of individuals who are susceptible to disease at time t, I(t)

Compartment models can either be deterministic, meaning the behaviour of the population is determined
completely by its history and by the rules which describe the model, or they can be stochastic in which
probabilistic concepts are used and in which there is a distribution of possible behaviours [32].
When making mathematical models it is necessary to test the sensitivity of the model. This is done using
sensitivity analysis (SA). It is used to see how the different parameters affect the model outcome. There
are different ways to conduct a SA and it can both be global and local. A global SA is an analysis of all
the parameters at once and can be used to reduce the number of parameters in the model, if the parameter
does not influence the outcome, it can be ruled irrelevant and be removed or assigned an arbitrary value
[33]. Local SA focus on a single parameter, while the rest remain the same. Local sensitivity is a great tool
once the model is calibrated. It can help determine which parameter should be modified for the system to
reproduce a desired outcome [33].

The model
Based on the described background and the findings in the literature review it was decided to base the

3.3.1
Model description
The model is based on Di Ruscio et al. "Quantifying the transmission dynamics of MRSA in the community
and healthcare settings in a low-prevalence country" [37] and Hogea et al. "A basic dynamic transmission
model of Staphylococcus aureus in the US population" [38]. Di Ruscio et al. [37], models the transmission
of MRSA in healthcare and community settings in Norway, using an individual-based model. Hogea et al.
[38], model the dynamics of the transmission of both MRSA and MSSA in the community of the United
States. Both models are compartment models described using differential equations, and these are used as
a base for the developed model.
The transmission model is a compartment model with four compartments. The population can be in four
different states; Susceptible (S), Carrier (C), Infected (I) or Death (D). The model has four sub-populations,
community, hospital, intensive care unit (ICU) and nursing homes. A susceptible individual will become a
carrier when meetings with other carriers occur, here a carrier refers to an individual in either C or I. As a
carrier, you can either progress to get an infection or decolonize by treatment or an immune response. In
the infected state, you can either become MRSA-free and go back to being susceptible or you can die, if the
infection is left untreated. A flow diagram of the model dynamics can be seen in figure 5. In appendix B a
modelling notebook of the entire model development is found, this elaborates on the development of the
model, starting with only the community and all the way to the final model in figure 5.

3.3.3
Model assumptions
To model the transmission of MRSA in the community, hospitals, ICU and nursing homes some simplifying
assumptions have been made.
• It is a closed system, with no birth or deaths of other causes than MRSA.
• The MRSA dead individuals go into a compartment - to count them, they do not leave the system.
The total population remains the same, N = S + C + I + D
• An individual can go from one sub-population to another in the infected state
• Infected individuals in the community can be admitted to the hospital but not the ICU
• Infected individuals at the hospital can be transferred to the ICU
• Infected individuals at the nursing homes are transferred directly to the ICU, assuming they are sicker
and need intensive care

• Infected individuals at hospitals that recover will go back to the susceptible in the community
• Infected individuals at ICU that recover will go back to the susceptible at the hospital
• Infected individuals in the community that recover will go back to the susceptible in the community
• Infected individuals at the nursing homes that recover will go back to the susceptible at the nursing
homes
• The decolonization rate is higher in the community and lowest at the ICU and follows this assump-

• There is no mortality in the community. It is assumed that only individuals at the hospital, ICU and

• An infected individual will no longer be infectious when the treatment period is over

is saved. The simulation is covering 10 years and it is simulated 50 times. The output is saved from the last
year as the model is stable here.
The output from the stochastic simulation is used to see again the course of spread and further to see how
the distribution of cases is when simulated multiple times. The output from the stochastic simulation is
used to calculate the BoD.
3.3.6
Sensitivity analysis
To test the sensitivity of the model a sensitivity analysis (SA) is made. This analysis will investigate how the
parameters affect the outcome and are done using scatter plots and partial rank correlations. Both analyses
will show if and how the parameters correlate with the outcome. The partial rank correlation is performed
using epi.prcc from the R package epiR, this will give an output with a gamma value between -1 and 1,
depending on the correlation between the variables [48]. The epi.prcc function takes a data frame with
the number of columns equal to the number of parameters plus one and the number of rows equal to the
number of replications of the model. The last column in the data frame, which creates the plus one, is the
model output that the parameters are tested against. Further, the function takes two inputs besides the data
frame, if it is a one- or two-sided test and the confidence level. To calculate the PRCC, the two-sided test
and a confidence level of 95% are chosen.
The scatter plots are made using the R function pairs, which creates a matrix of scatter plots plotting each
parameter against each other [49].
To perform the SA a new interval for each parameter is set. During the simulation of the model, all the
parameters used in each simulation are saved in a data frame, from this data frame the mean, min and max
of each parameter are found. These are found using the mean, min & max functions on each column in the
data frame. After finding these values for each parameter, 25% of the mean is either subtracted or added
to the minimum and maximum, making sure that the relative change in each parameter is the same. An
example of this is shown for the transmission rate between the hospital and ICU.
min_alpha_HI_old <- min(found_parameters$alpha_HI) # 0.002664127
max_alpha_HI_old <- max(found_parameters$alpha_HI) # 0.003238063
mean_alpha_HI <- mean(found_parameters$alpha_HI) # 0.002936973
min_alpha_HI <- min_alpha_HI_old - mean_alpha_HI*0.25 # 0.001929883
max_alpha_HI <- max_alpha_HI_old - mean_alpha_HI*0.25 #0.00250382
The SA is performed by running the simulation of the model 1000 times, and in each simulation, a new
parameter value is drawn from a uniform distribution containing the min and max value of the given
parameter. For each simulation, the sum of infected in each sub-population is saved and the last time
step is saved for the rest of the states, given 1000 different outcomes of the model. These values are then
used in the sensitivity analysis. As the model haves 16 states and 23 parameters the result is divided into
groups corresponding to the sub-populations, meaning all states and parameter related to the community
are saved as one output and all related to the hospital is saved as one output, etc. This gives in total four
different scatter plots and 4 different partial rank correlation plots.

Calculation of BoD based on the two models
To calculate the BoD based on the model outcome a few assumptions are made, as age and gender is used
when calculating the DALYs but not as part of the model, the distribution of the cases in 2021 is used to
divide the cases from the models into the different groups, meaning the modelled output will follow the
same distribution as the reported cases.
The output from the models is assumed to go to the different health states in the following way. Infected
individuals in the community are said to have a mild infection, infected individuals at hospitals and nursing
homes are said to have a moderate infection and the infected at ICU have a severe infection. Further, the
mortality cases are split into the last three age groups, such that individuals dying at the hospital are in the
age group 65-74 and individuals dying in ICU are in the age group 75-84 and the ones dying at nursing
homes are in 85+. This is a simplifying assumption as it has not been possible to find data regarding the
age distribution of the MRSA related deads in Denmark, however, from Statens Serum Institut data on SAB
it is seen that the cases mortality is higher in the elderly groups and this is used to back up the assumption
[51]. In appendix E the calculation of the BoD can be found. The calculation is done in Microsoft Office
Excel and all the information used is found in appendix E.

Problem Formulation
A model that investigates the epidemiological dynamics of MRSA in
low-prevalence countries. The model aims to understand the epidemi-
ological drivers of MRSA and the connection between healthcare and
society. Further, they investigate the effect of cases imported abroad.
Their model is a dynamic individual-based compartment model.
Model Description
Their model is a socio-demographic model and includes five settings,
and in each setting, an individual can be in either S, C or I. Their trans-
mission model is a SCIS model.
Data evaluation
The parameters used in this study are from other studies, and the pa-
rameters they were unable to find were calibrated using Latin Hyper-
cube Sampling. They have parameters both from studies, and National
registry data. The calibration of unknown parameters is thoroughly
described. The usage of national data means that the model and pa-
rameter are in best use in settings similar to the one in Norway, mean-
ing these values cannot be transferred to a model in a high-prevalence
country without further calibration.
Conceptual Model evaluation
They have simplifying assumptions that also include limitations to the
model, where the most important one is the exclusion of the hospital
referral network representing the movements of patients between hos-
pital institutions. Which in the end can mean that the model overes-
timates within-hospital transmission. They do not consider the emer-
gence of resistance caused by antimicrobial therapies. A limitation that
would affect the outcome as these cases appear in the population inde-
pendent of transmission.
Implementation verification
This is not described in the article nor the supplementary material.
Model output verification
This element is not completely fulfilled. They do make some validation
of the model results, on data not used in the calibration of parameters.
The validation results are mainly found in the supplementary material.
Model analysis
They did a local sensitivity analysis of firstly, the ratio of imported col-
onizations to infections and secondly, the importation of cases from
abroad. To test both the effect on the prevalence was tested, and did
not find significant differences in the epidemiological characteristics
and transmission dynamics of MRSA, compared to conclusions derived
from the main analysis.
Model output corroboration
Their model is able to predict the age-specific incidence of MRSA infec-
tion in different settings, on data not used in the calibration.

Discussion
In this project, models for estimating the disease burden of MRSA infections were investigated, using firstly
a literature review to assess already published articles using TRACE evaluation and lastly by developing a
new model to estimate the transmission and burden of MRSA.
In this section, a discussion of the results in section 4 is presented. Starting with the literature review and
TRACE, secondly, the models and lastly, the burden of MRSA will be discussed.
5.1
Literature review
The literature review was used to find and assess already published articles describing the transmission of
MRSA. In general, systematic reviews, which this literature review was a small part of, are used to identify,
appraise and synthesise all available and current evidence on a particular research question in order to
provide an objective assessment.
My literature review was used to assess where there is a need for change. In this literature review, the focus
was MRSA transmission and from the literature review, all seven studies were found to have important
information or model steps missing. A few notable things that were missing from some articles, were
programming software, flowchart and table with parameters, whether they did calibration or not and if
they had a validation of their model.
Doing a literature review shows the many ways a model can be developed and how different teams develop
in different ways, and also what they find important to have in their article.
It is a great tool to asses many articles, and a good way to extract data from many articles in a systematic
way, which is helpful when looking at many articles. It is important that the scope of the review is clear
from the beginning and also what is wanted from the output extracted.
A big part here was the actual data extraction, which is a good way to get information. Also being two
reviewers to extract ensures the quality of the extracted data, and gives the opportunity to discuss the
extracted data if any of the extracted outputs are different. With data extraction, it is easy to compare many
articles and see where they are similar and different, which in the end is helpful when developing a new

model. This review gave information about, the model types and settings used, and from these findings, it
was clear that a transmission model of MRSA in Denmark has not yet been made, and this emphasises the
need for a model investigating this.
Here the goal was to get information about transmission models regarding MRSA, and of the seven articles
not all gave useful information regarding the model. However, it has been helpful to read about different
modelling techniques and see how others have done their simulation. Using a literature review also ensures
that you do not develop an already existing model and make mistakes others have done before.
5.2
TRACE
Using TRACE as a part of the model development is a great way to ensure that other readers can replicate
your model. Using TRACE on already published models to assess their fulfilment of the guideline, shows
that authors focus on different things in their model descriptions and none covered all eight elements,
making it harder to replicate and test a model. Some of these elements are more important than others
when looking at replicating models. A thorough description of the model and model implementation are
needed as these two elements make the background of the model, and without a description, it is hard to
replicate the model, and further if they do not provide information about the implementation it is difficult
to know how they did their implementation.
TRACE is a great tool to use in the development of your model, and by following all eight steps, it is
ensured that the model can be used by others. In this project TRACE has first of all been used to assess
already published models, however, it has also been used in the development of the model. TRACE has
been a great tool to have, and looking at the eight elements when in doubt about what to do next, it has
been great to have TRACE at hand to see what steps were missing. So, when reading this thesis it should
be possible to mark all elements of the TRACE table as completed.
From the results in section 4.2 it was clear that none of the reviewed papers managed to fulfil all eight ele-
ments, and some even lacked the most, in my opinion, important ones: Model description, implementation
verification and flowchart and parameters, respectively. Without these fulfilled completely the replication
of the model is nearly impossible. And from the perspective of this project, these were especially needed if
the published model should be used in the development.
The finding of the TRACE evaluations corresponds to the systemic review conducted by Birkegaard et al,
[36], where they also found a lack of fulfilment in papers on mathematical models of AMR. These findings
indicate that the TRACE guidelines could be improved and used in a better way during model develop-
ment.
5.3
Model
The goal of this project was to use already published models to develop a new model, that could simulate
the transmission of MRSA, in more settings. The basis of one model was used for the development.
The model was developed to investigate the transmission of MRSA and to estimate the burden of this. To
do so the model should have an outcome that can be translated to the input needed in the BoD calculations.

This model is a population-based compartment model, and it is useful when investigating the transmission
of MRSA. Even though the model used in the development by Di Ruscio was an IBM [37], it was decided
to make a population-based model, as the IBM are more data-driven and requires a lot of data, which for
this project was hard to obtain.
As TRACE has been a part of this project, it has also been used in the development of the model. The
model has been validated by external users, to ensure no errors in the implementation. By doing this errors
were found and corrected to ensure that the model actually simulated the wanted transmission. Doing the
external validation as a part of the project has been helpful and a great way to have others’ input on the
model. This TRACE element and in general just verification by others is a great way to ensure the quality
and readability of the model.
It was decided only to include MRSA in the model, however, an add-on to future work could be to include
the Methicillin Sensitive S. aureus (MSSA). Including this would replicate the real-life more as this pathogen
is a natural part of the human bacterial flora for 30% of the human population. It was decided to look only
at MRSA as this pathogen is the one holding the burden in relation to antimicrobial resistance, however,
by including MSSA it would be possible to see how big a burden MRSA holds in the big picture and could
give an estimation of the excessive burden due to AMR.
5.3.1
Parameters
The parameters used in this project were limited to findings from two articles, and nearly no parameter
used could be used without further tuning of the parameter. In general, it was hard to find parameter
values that could be used to describe specific infections, and it was decided to focus on the transmission
and assume that the infection was setting dependent. This is discussed in the BoD section below.
5.3.2
Assumptions
To make the model that simulates the transmission of MRSA in Denmark, simplifying assumptions have
been made. In the following, each assumption is discussed.
The model is made as a closed system which is a simplification of real life, as individuals are born and die
each day. In this model, it is chosen to leave out both birth rates and mortality rates, except the MRSA-
specific mortality, which is used to count how many die. However, these do not leave the system, and the
entire system always has a total population of 5.8 million. This is a limitation of the model as it does not
replicate real life. Further, as a person can end up in the death state there is a small number of individuals
that leaves the SCIS model. If the model is simulated over a long period the entire population will end
in the death state, as no new individuals are added. Further, it is assumed that no one dies of an MRSA
infection in the community, to die you must be at the hospital, ICU or nursing homes. This assumption
is made, as it is unlikely that a simple infection in the community will lead to deaths, and further if the
infection gets more severe admission to the hospital will happen.
It is assumed that the main movement between the sub-populations happens in the infected state, and
when moving from infected back to being susceptible. This is also simplifying as in real life the movement
can happen in all states. It was decided to leave this movement out, to simplify the model. However,

34
==PAGE:44==

adding this would give a more accurate picture of the transmission, and also a way to see where the main
transmission occurs. In this model an individual only can be infected in their own sub-population and
not by meeting persons in the other sub-populations. This could be the next add-on to the model, to see
how the movement would affect the outcome. In the study by Di Ruscio they have movement between
the different settings, Households, Schools, Workplaces, Hospitals and Nursing homes, which makes it
possible for them the see what drives the transmission, even though the model in this thesis only has the
community, the finding is the same. In both studies, it is found that the community drives the transmission
[37].
To move the individuals a bit around it is assumed that infected at the hospital will move back to the
community when recovered, as it is assumed that there is no underlying disease that keeps them at the
hospital. For the individuals at the ICU, it is assumed that they move back to the hospital, as they are more
at risk for having underlying diseases that need further treatment before being moved out of the hospital.
For both infected in the community and nursing homes they move back to being susceptible in community
and nursing homes, respectively. This again is an assumption about the movement and it can be argued
that between all states in all four sub-populations, there should be movement both ways.
Lastly, it is assumed that the decolonization rate is different in all sub-populations, and this is done to reflect
that some individuals have a weaker immune system than others. This assumption is made to make the
model more true to life. By assuming this, the rate of moving from C to I is made setting dependent and it
was also a way to assume some sort of difference in the four settings.
It is assumed that an infected individual does not transmit bacteria after the treatment and the duration
until declared MRSA-free is done. Meaning when treated and the period of six months has passed you do
not transmit bacteria anymore. As the only parameter to affect here is the treatment length, it is preferred
to have a shorter treatment length than a longer one. This also means that treatment length is independent
of treatment success.
In the stochastic simulation, uninformed prior distributions (uniform) were chosen to ensure that the values
are evenly spread in the interval as they all have the same probability when drawing a random value from
the interval. This ensures that one parameter value is not overrepresented, which would be the case if a
normal distribution was chosen instead.
5.3.3
Model - with mAbs
It was not easy to implement mAbs into the model, as there is not much knowledge yet about the usage
regime, treatment length and treatment success. Due to this, the four scenarios were made, and these
showed that by changing the treatment length and success rate the burden of MRSA can be reduced. As
mAbs are discussed to have the potential to work as immunization, they could also be implemented in
other ways than treatment.
mAbs can be preferred used over antibiotics as the usage of this, can increase the resistant genes or new
resistance can occur. As mAbs replicate our own antibodies, they could be used as prevention instead of
treatment. One of the many difficulties with AMR is the overuse and misuse of antimicrobials, and if mAbs
can treat diseases as well as antibiotics this can be a new treatment strategy, which in the end could lower
the burden.

Burden of disease
The last part of this project was a burden study of MRSA infections. This was done on six different study
cases. Firstly, on data from Statens Serum Institut, to have a DALY/case to compare the model outcome
with. Secondly, the DALY/case was calculated for all five models, the one without mAbs and the four with
mAbs. To calculate these, assumptions about the case distribution and where the dead individuals were
placed were taken. The model outcome could not be given directly as input to the DALY calculations and
needed some adjustment to fit with the needed input.
Firstly, the DALY is calculated based on health state, and here the model only has a state called infected and
it was assumed that the setting of I decided which health state the case would go in. For a more accurate
DALY/case it would have been preferred if the model could have been modelled with more specific out-
comes corresponding to the input in DALYs, hence having more specific numbers on how many cases are
mild, moderate and severe, and further if you are hospitalized or not. This though would make the model
more complex, and here simplicity was chosen, and by assuming a distribution of the simulated cases it
was possible to calculate the burden. The assumptions, however, do effect the outcome and must be taken
into consideration as other assumptions would change the calculated burden.
Even though assumptions were made to calculate the burden, the results of the models are compared with
the DALY/case based on data. The results of the burden study indicate that a short treatment length and a
high success rate would decrease the burden of MRSA when compared to the antibiotic treatment used in
Denmark.
Again when looking at the burden of MRSA it could be argued that a model looking at all S. aureus in-
fections, MRSA and MSSA, would be preferred, to estimate the excessive burden of the resistant variant.

Conclusion
The aim of this project was to develop a mathematical model that could investigate the effect of interven-
tions towards MRSA infections. The model should be able to simulate the transmission of MRSA and the
number of infections caused by MRSA, with the goal of calculating the burden of MRSA infections. Further,
the goal was to use firstly a literature review and secondly perform TRACE evaluations to assess already
published models.
The literature review and TRACE evaluation were two great tools to assess already published models and
to identify data gaps. Based on the TRACE evaluation it could be concluded that most studies do not fulfil
the guidelines and there is a need for improvement in the usage of this in model development.
The model is based on already published models, which were found during the literature review and
TRACE evaluation. The model developed in this project is able to simulate the transmission of MRSA in
Denmark and give an estimate of the number of infected and deaths yearly.
The model is able to predict the total number of infected and deaths and from this, the burden of MRSA
infections could be calculated. It was found that the simulated model could predict the burden of MRSA,
but with a higher DALY/case than for the DALY/case of danish data.
Secondly, the goal was to implement interventions to see if these could affect the burden and overall reduce
the burden. Here mAbs were implemented as a treatment strategy and depending on the scenario the
DALY/case was both reduced and increased in comparison with the model with antibiotic treatment.
mAbs have the potential to be a new treatment strategy but further studies are needed to understand how
this intervention can be used.
7
Future perspective
As mentioned during the discussion in section 5 there are places where further development of the model
could be done. Firstly, the system could be an open system, that simulates the dynamics of a population
with birth and mortality rates. Secondly, more transmission routes could be implemented, meaning that an
individual can move between all states, e.g., a susceptible in the community can move to the hospital and
remain in the susceptible state. This would be added as rates going both ways between every state in the
entire mode. This would replicate the dynamics and movement of individuals more true to life.
It could be interesting to implement MSSA to the model, to investigate the dynamics between the two
pathogens, and further to assess the total burden of S. aureus infections, and by this also see how big the
MRSA-specific burden is.
Future studies could be to look at the implementation of mAbs in other states, it could be implemented in
the carrier state, to reduce the transmission of the bacteria and usage of antimicrobials and by this look at
the long-term effect of reduced antimicrobial usage.

They provide a brief description of the socio-demographic model and how each individual is characterized.
They have approx. 5 million persons and simulates their social interactions, by looking at households,
workplaces, schools, and healthcare settings. By doing this they can simulate the transmission of MRSA
among humans. The individuals are characterized by:
• age (with a minimum of 0 and a maximum of 100 years)
• ethnic background (Norwegian or foreign-born background)
• type of occupation (student, teacher, generic worker, healthcare worker or unemployed)
• hospitalization status (not hospitalized, hospitalized, hospitalized in ICU or long-term nursing home
resident)
• epidemiological status (susceptible, colonized, or infected)
Further, they distribute their population on a grid, to simulate the Norwegian territory, this is done using
data from Gridded Population of the World version 3 (GPW v3) produced by the Center for International
Earth Science Information Network (CIESIN) of the Earth Institute at Columbia University. The individuals
are distributed in different households of different sizes and based on their age distributed to either school
or a workplace. Further, they consider the average travel distance, when placing the individuals. This
account for the community setting of the model.
The healthcare setting is represented by hospitals and nursing homes. There are 58 hospitals included in the
model, these are divided into wards (using gaussian distribution), and in large hospitals, ICUs are included
(for every 300 beds there is an ICU). The nursing homes are generated based on the total number of beds
occupied by long-term patients is approx. 1% of the total population. The number of beds ranges between
20 and 152. The number of healthcare workers was set to respect a nurse-to-bed ratio of 0.2 (for hospitals)
and 0.3 (for nursing homes). The probability of being hospitalized is age-specific, as well as the length of
stay (LOS).

The seven free parameters were calibrated using Latin Hypercube Sampling to efficiently explore the pa-
rameter space by obtaining the same resolution of a random sampling procedure with fewer samplings.
For each sampled set of parameters, Pi, they ran a simulation over a period of 8 years and computed the
likelihood of the yearly time series of infections acquired in Norway in community, hospitals and nursing
homes, and of the prevalence in household contacts of MRSA carriers. The likelihood function is defined
by the product of three Poisson likelihoods and a binomial likelihood. The calibration procedure happened
in three steps. The outputs of the 5000 simulations, obtained from 50 repetitions of the 100 best parameter
sets, were pooled together and analyzed to produce the results. They provide the necessary information
regarding the data and how they have used the data to calibrate the free parameters.
The calibration of unknown parameters is thoroughly described in the supplementary material. The usage
of national data means that the model and parameter are in best use in settings similar to the one in Nor-
way, meaning these values can not be transferred to a model in a high-prevalence country without further
calibration.

They provide all the knowledge needed to understand the thoughts behind their model. This also includes
the assumptions they have made and these are:
• Complete adherence to national MRSA guidelines across all hospitals
• Baseline colonization-to-infection ratio of 10:1 (based on published estimates)
• Does not consider movements of patients between wards in hospitals. (backed by data on patient

• Screening at the entrance in hospitals: 70% of the patients who travelled abroad are screened at hos-

• Isolation: the patients remain isolated the whole reaming part of their hospitalization.
• Decolonization therapy: Infected individuals return susceptible after treatment.
Some of their assumptions are backed up by data and thereby making it a reasonable assumption. Further
limitations to the model include the exclusion of the hospital referral network representing the movements
of patients between hospital institutions. Which in the end can mean that the model overestimates within-
hospital transmission. Another limitation is that they did not consider the emergence of resistance in pa-
tients caused by antimicrobial therapies. These cases would behave in a way that is indistinguishable from
importation since they appear in the population independent of transmission.
