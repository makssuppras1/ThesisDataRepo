The project is a collaboration between Adept Water Technologies and 
DTU, on the development of a machine which processes tap-water into 
a disinfectant - saline solution with suspended hypochlorous acid. The 
machine is primarily intended for wound cleaning. This thesis addresses

Abstract  
This thesis investigates user needs, regulations and design constraints for a new device 
intended for hospital use, which filtrates tap-water and through added saline and electrolysis, 
produces isotonic saline with suspended hypochlorous acid which has been shown to be an 
excellent disinfectant with benign or no side effects. 
The device is intended for use in clinical environments, which means it will be governed by 
regulation and industry guidelines. Primarily EU Regulation 2017/745 on medical devices 
and ISO 14159 safety of machinery - hygienic requirements. These have been condensed 
into a pamphlet-guide to designing non-invasive class I or IIa devices for the medical 
industry. 
The development process encompasses an ongoing collaboration between Adept Water 
Technologies and DTU. Where a small group of students have developed the functional 
prototype under supervision and guidance by Lluís Vilar. This thesis addresses the external 
design of both the refined functional prototype and a proposed design, for manufacturing 
which can be finalized after insights have been gained from clinical trials of the prototype.

Preface 
This Master’s thesis was carried out during the first eight months of 2018 as a part of a wider 
collaboration between Adept Water Technologies (AWT) and Technical University of 
Denmark (DTU). The thesis deals primarily with exploring design options based on surveys 
of potential users, hygienic design requirements, construction of a functional prototype for 
further testing and creating a possible final design for the machine. 
The collaboration has been ongoing for longer than the author was involved with the project; 
in 2017 the first functional prototype was developed by Jacob Mortensen, who graduated 
that summer and stay on the project until spring 2018, and Lluís Ròmul Sala Vilar (Project 
Manager, DTU). After which Jakub Brzosko and Jesper Møller, both students at DTU, joined 
the project and worked on developing the refined prototype, which should suitable for testing 
in a hospital.

Chapter 1 - Introduction 
1.1 - Background 
Adept Water Technologies launched their BacTerminator Dental and BacTerminator Safe a 
few years ago. These products function by filtering tap water to sterile or near sterile 
conditions and in Safe through addition of salt followed by electrolysis adds between 70 and 
100 ppm hypochlorous acid (HOCL) to the water. HOCL has been proven to be a highly 
effective disinfectant agent, thus the water gains anti-bacterial and microbial properties. Both 
products are installed to practically eliminate the risk of contaminated pipes or the water 
from the mainlines cause e.g. a legionella outbreak. 
Due to the high reliability and low in-use cost of the machines, AWT has recently considered 
expanding their markets. The current focus is on hospital applications, where there has 
occasionally been outbreaks of bacteria, which pose an elevated risk to already weakened 
or immunocompromised patients. Where it is believed the filtration process and addition of 
70-100 ppm HOCL will eliminate this risk. 
Initially the product has been targeted wound irrigation use, where ordinary tap water is 
typically used in Danish hospitals. The water quality is monitored by both the water supply 
companies and occasionally on site by many hospitals, but this monitoring is not continuous, 
and many patients are at risk between the onset of an outbreak and until regular testing is 
performed and the outbreak identified. 
1.2 - Research Questions 
RQ1. What are the regulatory and ‘best practice’ requirements, when designing a 
machine for use in clinical environments? 
RQ2. Which general requirements and expectations do Danish nurses have to a new 
machine they will work with? 
RQ3. How can a new machines operation and interface be optimized to facilitate 
health personnel’s workflow, rather than impeding it? 
1.3 - Objectives 
The main objectives of this master’s project are: 
O1. 
Investigate and understand user needs for the design of the machine 
O2. 
Condense European medical regulations into clinical design guide for machines 
of this nature 
O3. 
Design and build a functional prototype. 
O4. 
Develop the external design for a near production ready version of the machine.

1.4 - Limitations 
The exploration of hygienic regulation for designing a new clinical machine, is based on work 
with a non-invasive CE class IIa device. Therefore, any guidelines and recommendations 
are only applicable for devices in the same class and by extension class I. Hygienic 
considerations and the developed design guide should not be consulted for any work 
regarding class IIb, III, active or invasive devices. 
1.5 - Approach 
To investigate user needs, a series of interviews will be conducted with potential end users, 
nurses in Danish hospitals working with wound cleaning in different areas. The interviews 
were structured around the questions seen in appendix 2.1 and were overall designed to 
first gain an understanding for the use of tap-water, saline and other solutions for wound 
cleaning and the procedures associated with this. Then going over renders of the four 
mockups for possible designs, seen in appendix 1, getting feedback on the design from the 
user perspective and their experience, and asking questions about how the construction and 
interface could be suited to their needs. 
Due to potential microbial growth and infection risks, associated with any object or machine 
placed in a clinical environment, new devices are strictly regulated. Due to AWT’s initially 
intended market being Denmark and its neighboring countries, this thesis will only explicitly 
address European regulation and standards in the field. The most important being EU 
regulation 2017/745 on medical devices [1], followed by ISO 14159 Safety of machinery - 
Hygiene requirements for the design of machinery [2] (in Denmark applied as DS/EN ISO 
14159). However, other guidelines such as EHDG DOC 8 Hygienic Design principles [3] and 
Foundation for meat & poultry research sanitary equipment design principles [4] were 
primarily consulted in the prototype development, but generally all four sources address the 
same principles. 
Design of the prototype and the final design proposal will follow Pahl and Beitz’s model of 
the design process. Where the prototype is the embodiment of the concept and preliminary 
layout, and the proposal, a further development of these. Not all steps will be addressed, 
since they have been part of an at times informal group process and decision making, and 
besides the outcome have not been adequately documented.

1.6 - What Remains to be Done? 
The prototype will need to be tested, to ensure it fulfills its requirements and can be used in 
the intended preliminary trial at Bispebjerg Hospital. The machine was delivered to AWT 
early in June, where the testing will be performed, and more extensive handling and care 
instructions will be written, for use during the trial. 
The proposed final design is based on experiences working with the prototype and 
interviews with potential users. During the clinical trial of the prototype nurses will use the 
machine in a real-world scenario; feedback from them combined with observations during 
use and usage data logged by the machine, will provide valuable insight into how the design, 
interface and overall usability of the machine can be improved. There is a high likelihood 
that this insight, will indicate that the design proposal can be improved in ways which did not 
come to light during the ‘hands off’ discussions based on models and renders. 
Lastly, the proposed design was created with some, but limited, supervision and 
recommendations from mechanical engineers and others. The design is based primarily on 
external design considerations and the internal structure, machinability and other cost 
saving measures can presumably be improved upon.

The functional requirements to the prototype, as put forth by AWT can be seen in appendix 
4.1. ISO 13485 encompasses quality management in the medical industry, including 
development of new products. By adhering to ISO 14159, hygiene requirements for 
design, compliance with ISO 1385 is possible when the necessary testing has been

2.1.1 - Clinical  
Regulatory requirements as put forth in Regulation (EU) 2017/745 on medical devices [1] 
and ISO 14159:2002 hygiene requirements for the design of machinery [2]. Throughout 
the development process, European Hygienic Engineering & Design Group’s DOC8 
Hygienic equipment design criteria second edition [6] and late in the process third edition 
[3], were used as the working guidelines for the design, due to its specificity. 
2.1.2 - Adept Water Technologies 
Functional performance requirements were specified, as mentioned in section 2.1, 
however design of the exterior construction was not specified. To paraphrase from a 
meeting with AWT: ‘outer appearance of this prototype is not so important as long as it is 
works’.

2.2 - Prototype Construction 
2.2.1 - Materials and Manufacturing 
It was preferable to limit the construction of the refined prototype, to the methods and 
materials available at DTU Skylab, due to time constraints.  
Acrylic (PMMA) was chosen due to its high resistance [7] to hypochlorous acid, and general 
detergents and alcohol as described in the national guidelines for cleaning equipment in the 
Danish health sector [8]. While also being highly machinable with the available means, i.e. 
laser cutting.  
Joining of parts was done primarily with cyanoacrylate glue, which is not considered 
resistant to highly concentrated chlorine [9], but hypochlorous acid in low doses from spills, 
is not expected to degrade the prototype considerably during testing and clinical trials. The 
drip tray was bonded using epoxy, due to favorable resistance to HOCL and also heat [10], 
meaning the drip tray can be cleaned in equipment washers or sterilization machines in 
hospitals.

2.3 - Hygiene Evaluation 
In an interview with Sanne Dragsted (appendix 2.5), hygiene specialist and nurse at Herlev 
and Gentofte hospital, the prototype was assessed as being adequately hygienic in design 
and construction for clinical testing. Design concerns about the assembly of frame and case 
with a rubber ‘weather-strip’ in between, creating a crevice, was fortunately deemed not to 
be an issue, due to the case can be lifted and the surfaces wiped down with disinfectants.

Chapter 3 - Design Proposal 
The proposed design, for a near production ready final version of the machine, was 
developed as a refinement of the prototype. Using stainless steel and industrial 
manufacturing methods, a much more compact and durable design can be achieved. A 
much more hygienic design can also be accomplished, with practically no sharp corners, 
gaps or cavities where bacteria and microbes could otherwise build up. 
The machine has a footprint of 520x350mm and a height of 400mm. It can be placed on a 
counter top or mounted on a wall. Further dimensions and details can be seen in appendix 
5.3 - Technical Drawings. 
3.1 - Design Guide 
The medical device sector is heavily regulated both by authorities, and guidelines, national 
and international. When designing only the exterior of a new device, not all of these are 
applicable, on top of which a simplified overview of e.g. EU regulation 2017/745 on medical 
devices, was apparently non-existent when starting the design project. Therefore a design 
guide has been created (see appendix 3), which in just one page (excluding front/cover) is 
believed to give an essential overview of regulations and guidelines, for designing a medical 
device under similar conditions. 
3.2 - Overview 
The proposed design is constructed 
nearly entirely out of AISI 316 stainless 
steel, due to its very high resistance to 
hypochlorous acid [7] and reasonably 
good

properties, 
in

The construction is comprised of

through an identical but mirrored panel on

3.3 - Requirements 
3.3.1 - Specification 
In appendix 5.1 the general product requirements are specified. The requirements are 
similar to the ones used in development of the prototype, except stricter. 
3.3.2 - Materials and Methods 
BacTerminator Medical will have nearly all outer surfaces constructed from AISI 316 
stainless steel, with only a tempered glass panel covering the display, the button and 
quarter turn locks on the access panels differing from this. All joining of metal parts will be 
done using continuously welded butts, to achieve a flush and sanitary surface. 
3.3.3 - User Requirements 
Through a series of interviews, summarized in appendix 2, the user needs were explored. 
The largest variation in considerations and concerns, the potential users had to the device 
and mockups (appendix 1), was found to be due to space constraints in their respective 
departments. With either a strong preference for a self-standing floor version or a 
countertop version. This stark contrast is believed to be addressed, by having developed a 
design which can be placed on a table/counter or hung directly on a wall. 
A consensus was found when it came to the tapping area and how the interface functions. 
All expressed concerns about the containers they use for water when performing wound 
irrigation being too bulky for the drip-tray dimensions in the tabletop mockup variations. 
This has been addressed by extending the depth of the drip-tray to 165mm, so a wash 
bowl of upwards of 30cm can be balanced fairly securely on it. In addition, there was a 
shared concern about needing to keep the button pressed throughout the duration of time 
it takes to fill the container in use. This concern is addressed by using a ‘push on, push off’ 
interface (with an adjustable standard volume dispensed before stopping automatically)

3.4 - Considerations  
The construction is designed as made of 2 mm steel, which results in a calculated weight of 
18.7 kg - with an addition of 3-5 kg for the weight of technical components. Since the device 
is meant to be stationary, this weight is acceptable, but for cost saving measures and ease 
of manufacturing, using 1.2 or 1.5 for most parts could be beneficial. 
As mentioned in section 3.2.4 the service access to the machine is limited by the access 
panel. It is possible that it will be necessary to enlarge the panel or make different design 
changes, for acceptable serviceability of components.

Chapter 4 - Conclusion 
As put forth in RQ1 the regulatory requirements governing development of a clinical device 
within the European Union is regulation 2017/745. ‘Best practice’ guidelines can be found 
in EHEDG Guidelines Doc 8 Hygienic design principles and ISO 14159 Safety of 
machinery - Hygiene requirements for the design of machinery. This thesis has only 
touched on limited aspects of the entire development process, for managing the complete 
process ISO 13485 A complete guide to Quality Management in the Medical Device 
Industry [12] is considered industry standard. 
In extension of this regulation 2017/745 and ISO 14159 have been condensed into a two 
page heavily condensed clinical design guide, which was Objective O2 of this thesis. In 
reflection the guide is possibly narrower in scope and details, than what would be 
beneficial to e.g. students and engineers unfamiliar with the field, designing a new medical 
device. 
The expectations and requirements from potential users, objective O1 and RQ2, have 
been explored through interviews (appendix 2) and distilled to the design changes 
expressed in section 3.3.3. To summarize, there was a shared concern for the dimensions 
of the device, since available space is often limited in hospitals. With strong preferences 
for both a floor standing and a tabletop version, a compromise has been found in a 
relatively compact device which is intended to be placed on a counter or tabletop, but can 
be mounted on a wall if this installation fits better into the given space. RQ3 was 
addressed in the same section, resulting in the requirement of an interface with minimal 
interactions, avoiding time wasted by pressing a button for extended periods of time. 
A functional prototype, Objective O2, has been developed by the team at DTU, chapter 2 
describes design of the frame and case, which is the part of the development process 
which this thesis dealt with. The prototype has been delivered to AWT and is undergoing 
testing to ensure the functional requirements are met and results are consistent.  
In chapter 3 a proposal for a final exterior design is developed and described, which was 
objective O4. The design proposal has been revised after consulting with a hygiene 
specialist, but should still only be considered a suggested design, primarily due to 
expectations of an unnecessarily high manufacturing cost. It should however be possible 
to further refine the design, for both cost concerns, and knowledge gained from the clinical 
trial using the prototype, after this is eventually completed.
