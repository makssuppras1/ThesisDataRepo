Abstract
Diffuser Augmented Wind Turbines(DAWTs) are promising in terms of energy cost
by increasing the energy output without changing the rotor size.
The project focuses on both numeric investigation of Generalized Actuator Disc
(GAD) theory and assessment of existing diffuser design concepts in the literature.
The goal of this project is after evaluating the existing designs, to design a new diffuser
by selecting one of the design concepts and optimizing that design.
The numeric investigation is carried on the Star-CCM+, a powerful software in Com-
putational Fluid Dynamics(CFD).
One of the challenges is preventing flow from separating in diffuser. Therefore,during
the optimization process, a precise convergence criteria is used.
At the end of the optimization, three possible diffuser designs are proposed.
A test
model is developed based on the design options to validate the numeric results by
experimental results. The test model is manufactured with 3-D printers. Due to the
technical challenges depending on the maximum nozzle size, only one model is man-
ufactured corresponding to the one diffuser design. After the surface improvement of
the test model, the experiments are conducted at the Green Wind Tunnel located
at DTU. An acceptable difference is observed between experimental and computa-
tional results due to the experiment conditions. Yet, it is shown that the diffuser
design works and increases the flow speed. After the validation of the diffuser, the
evaluation of three diffuser designs is done by using an edited version of BEM code
for DAWTs.
Following up two approaches, the assessment is made by comparing
the power production increase and the loading increase including the axial loading of
diffusers.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Lowering the cost of energy produced with wind turbines has been the most critical
point for the development of the technology and future designs. So far the industry
chose a path aiming more powerful wind turbines by improving the blade and tower
design. The improvement processes mostly consist of increasing the blade length and
parallel to this increasing the tower height.
As a result of bigger rotor designs with higher towers, the environmental and social
impacts of wind turbines have become an issue, Cîrstea (2015), despite the lower
levelized cost of energy.
Due to the self-awareness of people for environmental issues developed in recent years,
there is an increasing interest towards to the household use of power production
equipment based on renewable energy sources such as sun, biomass and of course
wind.
Small wind turbines have been satisfying this demand. However due to the length
of pay-back period, they do not appeal to the customers as a good investment. To
decrease pay-back period of such an investment, the wind turbines should provide
more power production meanwhile not costing more.
To increase the power production with same rotor size and tower height with the
same free wind speed, shrouded rotor concept is a promising method as it can even
exceed Betz-Jawceskesy limit of bare rotor,Tavares Dias Do Rio Vaz et al. (2014). As
K. Foreman (1981) stated, one of the main reasons to use DAWTs is to decrease the
pay-back time of the investment.
Since 1970s,Oman and K. M. Foreman (1973), the diffuser augmented wind turbines
have been discussed and investigated primarily based on power increase in respect of
economics. One of the first experimental investigation of DAWT design is made by
Igra (1981).
Despite its advantages, due to cost of diffuser structure the industry has focused
mainly on the conventional wind turbines. By the development of more powerful
computers and new computational fluid dynamics models and software, investigating
and developing new designs for DAWTs have become popular in academic world.
The industry stepped in to the use of DAWT concept with Vortec7 seen at Figure 1.2
on page 3 developed by Vortec Energy.
The company argued that a wind farm with 220 conventional wind turbines have the
same total power of 40 Vortecs,Limited (n.d.) Not as high as Vortec Energy states,
but Yuhi Ohya found from the field measurements of a diffuser augmented rotor that

With the help CFD tools, there have been many designs proposed yet a few of them

including the axial loading on diffuser structure is studied by using an extended BEM
code proposed by Tavares Dias Do Rio Vaz et al. (2014).

1.1
The Definitions
Generally, the definition of diffuser stands for the parts which have different inlet
and outlet section cross over areas to derive the air to the various directions. The
purpose of the diffusers emerging to the wind industry is to increase the flow headed
to the rotor section by accumulating the mass flow with a converging shape, in order
to increase the power production of turbine.
In the literature, augmentation parts with conical shape are commonly called as
diffusers, yet there are also shrouded rotors which are based on an airfoil geometry
having the same working principle. It is worth to state the difference here, to prevent
a confusion.
Depending on shape of cross section, a small extension part which is added upwards
to diffusers to increase the flow speed coming through rotor section, is called either
flange for diffusers with conical cross section or gurney flap for shrouded rotors.

Diffuser Design
The design process is carried out by using a CFD modelling software namely Star-
CCM+.
This chapter will discuss how the evaluation of diffuser designs is done
by covering both theoretical and computational analysis. The theoretical analysis
covers the derivation of Cs, a diffuser coefficient which has been stated by Hjort
and Larsen (2014). The diffuser coefficient is used in comparison of several airfoils’
performances when they are used as a base shape of diffuser design.
The computational analysis includes the selection of airfoil according to the Cs, the
modelling of the disc in 2-D and validation of it by investigation of bare rotor briefly.
Both of the analyses are explained in the following sections.
2.1
Theoretical analysis
Hjort and Larsen (2014) have derived a mathematical model to calculate a diffuser
coefficient(Cs) based on 1-D Momentum Theory. In the following section, the model
developed is summarized.

2.3.2
Methodology
The design steps are shown in the Figure 2.28 on the facing page.
The diffuser angles α and β are the key parameters due to their effect on the sepa-
ration of flow and power coefficient. To decrease the effect of starting point, the 1st
diffuser angle (α) is examined after determining the optimum rotor gap. A similar
approach is applied for the 2nd diffuser angle (β). After the configuration of the sec-
ond slot is determined, the β angle is changed with the new configuration to find out
if there are any better options. Finally, a last check part is added to the algorithm
to make sure that the first diffuser angle α, has its optimum value.
The previous studies, Aranake, Lakshminarayan, and Duraisamy (2015), Venters, He-
lenbrook, and Kenneth D. Visser (2017) have pointed out the significance of limiting
the separation of flow from the diffuser surface. To monitor whether the flow is sep-
arated or not, the order of convergence value has a vital role.
The angle studies
are done by monitoring both the airfoil surface and the converging behaviour of the
residuals. How this change affects the selection process is explained in the following
sections.
Convergence Criteria
The convergence criteria is selected as 10−6 for the error at
the continuity to limit the separation of flow along the trailing edge of the airfoil. Such
a precise convergence criteria is determined since the optimization process consists of
loaded cases to maximize the power coefficient. The loading of rotor section can effect
and limit the separation of flow as it is argued by Kanya and Kenneth D Visser (2017).
However, during the real life conditions of a diffuser, the rotor does not always operate
with a maximum load. To limit the separation also at the other operating conditions,
using a high sensitivity is found to be more appropriate.

2.3.3
The Diffuser Length/Rotor Diameter-Cp Analysis
The levelized cost of energy is another parameter in the wind industry, especially
in an optimization process. It is known that increasing the diameter of rotor is not
always logical choice due to the increase in the cost of wind turbines.
In this analysis, the cost is referred by the total weight of diffuser excluding the rotor
mass, since the only variable is selected as the length of diffuser. The ratio between
diffuser length and rotor diameter affects the total weight of the DAWTs significantly.
It also changes the power coefficient and varying this ratio may lead to a better point
with an optimum weight/Power Coefficient.
There are two ways to change the L/Drotor ratio.
• Scaling the Diffuser only in X direction
• Scaling the Rotor only in Y direction
Following the first option leads to changing the curvature of the diffusers. Since the
pressure difference between upper and lower surface of diffuser varies, the aerodynamic
features of it changes. Scaling down of the length in X direction might be an useful
idea as long as the flow remains attached to the body whereas, scaling up the diffuser
length is expected to result in a decrease in the power coefficient. Thus, the second
option is found more legitimate, and applied. The behaviour of power coefficient has
a direct proportion to the L/D since the distance between two diffusers affects the
flow speed largely and as it is seen from Figure 2.40 on the facing page and Figure 2.41
on the next page, the diffuser effect increases when the gap narrows.
However, it is also observed from the same results that the L/Drotor should not be
too low since it may create an extra resistance and limits the flow.

4.7
Evaluation of The Results
As it is seen from the results of this chapter and second chapter, the power coefficients
are obtained really differently. The reason for this differences is the methods used
for each chapter are different.
Inside the second chapter, the classical diffuser theory is used where it is assumed
that thrust is remained unchanged for a rotor with diffuser. It is also discussed inside
this chapter, and showed that is not true.
The edited version of BEM, does not require this assumption and indicates a relation
between maximum speed-up ratio,(γ) and speed-up ratio, (ϵ) based on the axial in-
duction factor. However, it should be noted that the γ value used inside the edited
BEM calculations have already obtained from a loaded case simulation in CFD.
The same blade optimization and power calculation methods are also carried out
based on the speed-up ratio obtained from free-loading CFD simulations. Due to the
airfoil characteristics and design angle selection, it is found that the blades go to the
stall region.
If the same process is repeated with a different design approach of rotor blades in
the future, it must be taken into account that at zero loading condition, the flow
separates along the trailing edges of the optimized diffuser designs. Thus, different
power values must be excepted when the BEM and 3-D CFD results are compared.
Basically, it can be concluded that due to use of values obtained from a loaded case,
limits the maximum power coefficient and power production. However, even with this
limitation, the use of diffuser results in a significant increase in power production for
each designs and each cases. For all cases with a power increase, the ratio of it is
found higher than the loading increase.
Based on this configuration, airfoil and design parameters, the second design option,

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
In this study, the diffuser augmented wind turbines, which can reduce the pay back
time of small wind turbines due to its higher efficiency, are scrutinized.
The investigation consists of evaluation of several design concepts following a litera-
ture review of the topic. The determination of design shape and the design lay-out
of diffuser leads to an optimization process.
The investigations are made by using a CFD model developed in fluid dynamics solver
software named as STAR −CCM+. The validation of model is done by comparing
its results with bare rotor theory and a previous study of Hansen, Sørensen, and
R. G. J. Flay (2000). The optimization process is done by creating an optimization
algorithm which reduces the effect of starting point while leading to higher power
coefficients(Cp). In addition to that a small robustness test is applied to validate the
design strategy of examining each parameter separately.
The flow separation problem is stated several times in the literature. To limit the
separation problem in the optimization process, the convergence criteria of simula-
tions is adjusted. During the optimization process, it is found that the rotor gap
ratio and the thrust coefficient affect the separation of flow at the trailing edge as it
is stated by Kanya and Kenneth D Visser (2017). And if the design parameters are
determined properly, it is possible to reach higher diffuser angles that higher bare
airfoil angles. Nevertheless, there is no correlation found between Cl/Cd ratio and
diffuser performance.
At the end of optimization process, three possible designs are proposed to test exper-
imentally.
A test model is designed with 3 parts representing design options.
The model is
decided to be produced by 3-D printers located at DTU since the rapid-prototyping
technology is more time efficient. However, due to the nozzle size of printers the sec-
ond and the third parts of model can not be produced. Therefore, only the first part
of model corresponding to the first design option is manufactured. Later, a surface
improvement process is carried out to increase the quality of diffuser surface.
The experimental tests are performed at Green Tunnel located at DTU, Lygngby Cam-
pus. The measurements are done using two different probes, a propeller anemometer
and a hot-wire anemometer.
The flow profile obtained by using both of anemometers matches with the CFD re-
sults. Yet, with hot-wire anemometer results in less uncertainty due to its higher
sensitivity than propeller anemometer. A small decrease in the exit flow speeds of

experiments and CFD results is observed due to the differences of exit environment.
And the flow separates as it is expected from the CFD model of bare rotor.
After validating the CFD model and the diffuser design, it is decided to carry out a
BEM-code analysis in order to evaluate the three design options in terms of power
and loading increases.
The design of blades are made by using an edited version of Glauert Optimization
Method proposed by for rotors with diffusers. The evaluation using BEM code con-
sists of two cases, using a diffuser with a re-designed blade and using a diffuser with
an existing blade design.
A significant power increase is observed from both of the cases together with a thrust
increase.
Yet the increase ratio of power is found to be higher than the loading
increase at rotor section. The axial loading of diffusers is also examined. And by
taking into account all, it is concluded that multi-slotted with 2 diffuser elements
with shapes of two airfoils is a more convenient option.
5.1
Further Recommendations
The design configuration obtained by applying a higher Re number is not included
as a design option due its unrealistic design wind speed, but as Re number is also
depending on the chord length of diffuser, the design wind speed can be lowered by
increasing the length of diffuser. However, in that case the rotor size will be increased
since the chord length is defined as a function of rotor length. Even though it results in
a higher power coefficient, it is probably way more heavier than the current design.An
analysis based on that specific case can be conducted and compared with the current
design with respect to weight increase and power coefficient increase.
The flange heights that used in the previous studies are higher than the optimized
one based on the steady flow conditions. If the process is repeated with an unsteady
condition, it is likelihood to obtain a higher length of flange. However as it is observed
from analysis and BEM code, the flap increases the loading of diffuser. Thus ,the
Gurney flap height with respect to loading increase on diffuser surface along the axial
direction can be investigated in the future.
The augmentation factors used in the BEM analysis, are obtained from loaded cases.
And when the procedure is repeated with the factors obtained from bare diffusers, due
to the selection of airfoil for the blade designs and design angle of attack, the wind
turbine blades go to the stall region. The design procedure should be repeated again
in the future with a different parameters for the blade design and the results must
be compared with 3-D simulations. A further work would be creating an unsteady
BEM code for investigations of diffusers after the validation of design with results of
3-D simulations. For the different wind speeds, the speed-up ratios can be obtained
and interpolated for the analysis of turbulent flows.
