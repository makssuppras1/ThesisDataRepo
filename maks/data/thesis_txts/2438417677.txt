Summary
As wind turbine blade grows in size, the distinction between the aerodynamic part
and the structural part of the blades is getting thinner. The project goes in the direc-
tion of a more integrated design of wind turbine blade sections where the conflicting
requirements of aerodynamic performance and structural merit can be traded off at
the airfoil level.
The projects scope will move from a thickness-based airfoil design (t/c=18%, 24%,
etc.) to a radial-based airfoil design (r/R=60%, 75%, etc.). In order to fulfill this
purpose, an airfoil optimization framework will be established in which for a given
blade section, the aerodynamic shape, the design points and the internal structure
will be optimized simultaneously. Other features might be added to the framework
covering the sensitivity of the airfoil outer (aerodynamic) and internal (structural)
design to airfoil performance in clean and contaminated conditions, flap-wise and
edgewise strengths, etc.
For the aerodynamic optimization process, different geometric parameterization exist
and can be used. The parametrization method implemented need to be stable, eco-
nomic and flexible, some recommended choices, Parsec, Bezier-Parsec, Shape function,
etc.. Structural optimization process, given the external shell and the direction of the
loads, an optimization of the airfoil external shell to maximizes the sectional stiffness
(moment of inertia) in the load direction. For the optimization strategy, possible
candidates, gradient-based, genetic algorithm or mixed methods.
A flow solver XFOIL will be coupled with the framework.
XFOIL, is a 2D po-
tential flow method coupled to an integral boundary layer method.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Project Background & Motivation
The wind turbine field is growing exponentially in complexity and so is the design
process. As such the relay on new numerical tools which automatically obtain most
efficient design, are day by day becoming more essential to assist in designing of
such complex system.
The main goal of a wind turbine, is to transform wind a
natural power resource into electrical power. However, simply producing power is
not enough of a reason for the wind turbine to be desired by the industry. The wind
turbine must have competitive presence (levelized cost of energy) to other power
resources to be considered as an efficient power source. As such, the wind turbine
must be optimized to extract the highest power with the lowest cost. Many parts
of a wind turbine can be optimized to achieve optimal performance.
The chosen
part to be optimized is the blade of the wind turbine where the main focus is to
optimize the cross sectional performance. The project goes in the direction of a more
integrated design of wind turbine blade sections where the conflicting requirements
of aerodynamic performances and structural merit can be traded at the airfoil level.
1.2
Project Scope
The project goes in the direction of a more integrated design of wind turbine blade sec-
tions where the conflicting requirements of aerodynamic performances and structural
merit can be traded at the airfoil level. The project will cover these goals:
• Move from a “thickness-based” airfoil design (t/c = 18%, 24%, etc.)
to a
“radial-based” airfoil design (r/R = 60%, 75%, etc.).
• Develop a framework which, for a given blade section, optimizes the aerody-
namic shape, the design point and the structure performance simultaneously.
• Grasp the sensitivity of airfoil outer (aerodynamic) and internal (structural)
design to airfoil performance in clean and contaminated conditions, flap-wise
and edgewise strengths, etc.

1 Introduction
1.3
Current Airfoil-Blade Design Process
A simple introduction on the current state-of-art wind turbine blade design process
is presented. The process starts by generating a preliminary design of the blade to
identify the lift levels and relative thickness for different radial positions. This step is
followed by the selection of airfoils where for the selected thickness over chord ratio,
the airfoils are optimized (or selected) with constraints on the lift levels and aerody-
namic objective function as a combination of the lift over drag coefficient where the
design point is the output of the process. Following this, the blade is aerodynami-
cally and structurally optimized. For the aerodynamics part, the blade plan-form in
terms of chord and twist angle is optimized with a Blade Element Momentum Method
(BEM) using the airfoil family to construct the final blade geometry. The blade will
be constructed internally in the structural part, where the shear web, the composite
materials and fibre orientations will be chosen. For the last step, the blade will be
subjected to heavy simulations where the aerodynamic and structural part will be
continually modified until the design satisfies the design standards with the intended
goal for levelized cost of energy.
Figure 1.1: Current Design process.
1.4
Project Objective and approach
The problem faced with, is to develop a framework in which based on radial-based
airfoil design (r/R = 60%, 75%, etc.) for a given blade section, the airfoil is directly
modified to optimize the aerodynamic shape, the design point and the structural
performance simultaneously. The project will involve solving a multi-objective op-
timization problem where the aerodynamic and structural parts are coupled with
a Blade Element Momentum Method (BEM) algorithm.
The problem is divided
into two parts, aerodynamic part and structural part. The aerodynamic part is con-
structed as a non-linear optimization problem having a single objective optimization
and is solved using a gradient-based method having sequential quadratic program-
ming (SQP) optimization algorithm. The aerodynamic part is discussed in details in

CHAPTER 2
Theory behind XFOIL
Xfoil is one of many existing 2D flow solver. The optimization problem in hand
requires a solid, reliable, economic and relatively fast solver for airfoil flow simulation.
The optimization problem will require multiple simulations for it to converge to an
optimal solution. This chapter will cover the theory behind Xfoil, some modification
on the source code and its implementation in the framework. Xfoil is an open source
software and can be found on its home page [9].

2 Theory behind XFOIL
For the complete description of the flow, additional equations are required. Consti-
tutive relations are employed expressing the fluid flow properties (viscous stress, heat
conduction) with reference to the velocity field and thermodynamic state variables.
The thermodynamic equations of state are supplemented to describe the thermody-
namic properties of the fluid, e.g. ρ = ρ(p, T) and e = e(ρ, T).
For a simple flow cases, an analytically solution can be found for the Navier-
strokes equation but in general this is not always true.
Therefore different CFD
methods are applied to obtain an approximate solution of the flow field of interest.
Many existing flow solvers are based on Reynolds Averaged Navier-Strokes equations
with a turbulence model. The output of the solvers contains a set of numbers which
must be interpreted carefully.
The use of such method (Reynolds Averaged Navier-Strokes equations) on a wind
turbine airfoil is computationally demanding. As such, it is preferable to use a less
expansive method (simplified model) with an acceptable and sufficient accuracy. A
method to simplify the flow model, is to take the airfoil surface viscous effects only
near the airfoil surface. For the model to be efficient, it is assumed that the complete
flow is inviscid and is coupled to a boundary-layer model to incorporate the viscous
effects near the airfoil surface. Xfoil is an example of this method, where it couples
panel method with a boundary layer integrated equation method [11].
For an optimization problem, Xfoil is ideal as it is computationally fast and
proven to have results equivalent to that of the wind tunnel. However, Xfoil tends to
overestimate the maximum lift coefficient and deviated in the post-stall region. The
inviscid flow model of Xfoil is a potential flow method. In 2D potential flow theory,
it is assumed that [12]:
• The flow is adiabatic, the effect of volumetric heat sources present in the flow
field is negligible.
• The flow is incompressible, the variations in density due to variation in pressure
are negligible. This assumption is generally made when the flow around the
streamlined surface has a mach number less than 0.3.
• The flow is non heat condition, in external low-speed aerodynamic the effect
due to the dissiptive process of heat are small and there for are neglected.
• The flow is subjected to a conservative external force field f(x, t) = −∇F(x, t).
• The flow is non viscous, inviscid flow model separation does not automatically
happens at the edge. To fix this non-physical behaviour, the Kutta condition is
imposed. This ensures the flow separation at the sharp edges, e.g. the trailing
edge of the airfoil.
• The flow is irrotational where ω(x, t) = ∇· u = 0, thus a velocity potential can
be intriduced by u(x, t) = ∇ϕ(x, t) which satisfies the irritational conditions.

2.3
Potential flow implemented in Xfoil
The flow model implemented in Xfoil is presented in figure (2.4). The surface of the
airfoil consists of strait line called panels. The airfoil is discretised into N number of
panel nodes and the wake into Nw number of panels nodes. The airfoil panels makes
use of a vortex distribution γj and a source distribution qj, while the wake makes
use only of a source distribution. Furthermore, a ¯s-coordinate is introduces such as it
starts at the trailing edge of the upper airfoil section, following counter-clockwise to
the trailing edge of the lower airfoil section. All relevant coordinates are scaled with
the chord length c.

2.5
Conclusion: Xfoil Theoretical Background and
limitations
The chapter discus the mathematical background of Xfoil. As presented above, Xfoil
is a potential flow solver that uses an integral boundry layer technique for the viscus
effect. The potential flow equation is obtained through the Navier-Strokes equation,
by having six assumptions. The six assumptions for the potential flow are: adiabatic,
incompressible, non heat condition, conservative force field, non viscus and irrota-
tional. The chapter also presents three basic solutions of the Laplace equation, how
they are combined and implemented to Xfoil solver through the panel method. The
solver can face a non-convergence issues as the connection between the potential flow
method and the boundary layer technique is an iteration process. This problem is
mainly seen at low-negative angles of attack and at and beyond the stall region. As
over all, the solver is good for simple use or estimation and for optimization prob-
lems due to its low computational time.
It should be considered, Xfoil tends to
overestimate the maximum lift coefficient and deviated in the post-stall region.

The NACA airfoil series was developed and introduced by the National Advisory
Committee for Aeronautics. The NACA airfoil series is a combinations of an explicit

length, the maximum camber in tenths of chord length and the position of the maxi-
mum camber in tenths of chord length. In order to describe more sophisticated airfoil
families, additional variables can be included. The construction of NACA airfoils [17]

3.3
Class Shape Transformation CST method
The class shape transformation method was developed by Boeing commercial aircraft
manufacturing company and is presented in detail in reference [20]. The method
represent the airfoil as a product of two functions, a class C(x) and a shape S(x)
function, summed with a trailing edge thickness function Z(x).
The airfoil is divided into two surfaces, upper and lower surface. Each surface
is represented separately, using an analytically function of the relative thickness as
function of the relative position. The function is coupled as well with parameters that
define the design. The class function handles the base airfoil shape and the shape
function is than used to perturb the class function, thereby defining the design. The
additional trailing edge function allows for the thick trailing edge to be represented
accurately.

3.4
Bezier-PARSEC method
A Bezier parameterization is determined by its control points which are physical
points in the design plane.
The first and last control points are the initial and
terminal point on the curve itself. However the other control points don’t have to be
on the curve even though they determine the shape of the curve. As such a Bezier
parameterization of an airfoil has control points that are only indirectly determined
by the underlying aerodynamics [21; 22; 23; 24].

ing edge radius (rle), trailing camber line angle (αte), trailing wedge angle (βte),
trailing edge vertical displacment (Zte), leading edge direction (γte), location of the

crest (xt, yt), curvature of the thickness crest (κt) and the half thickness of the trail-

four curves used to define the airfoil shape. The third degree Bezier curve is given

Mathematical optimization algorithms are widely used in all our life aspects and
comes in many different methods and conditions. The optimization solvers can solve
linear problems, nonlinear problems, constrained linear least squares, nonlinear least
squares and many more. The solvers goal is to maximize or minimize the objective
relative to a set of design parameters while satisfying a set of constraints. The solver
can either search for a local solution or a global solution depending on the problem
formulation and the optimizer method used. The optimization tools can generally
be divided into three mainly techniques, gradient-based, heuristic-based and hybrid-
based. For the presented problem, both the gradient-based solver and heuristic-based
solver (genetic algorithm) are used for the problem. Two open-source code (Nlopt
& Ipopt) and the Matlab Optimization Toolbox™are investigated where ultimately
one optimization method would be identified and used for optimization problem in
hand.
4.1
Nlopt open source optimization method
NLopt is a free open-source code for nonlinear optimization, providing a common
interface for a number of different free optimization routines available online as well
as original implementations of various other algorithms. The open source code is
callable from C, C++, Fortran, Matlab or GNU Octave, Python, GNU Guile, Julia,
GNU R, Lua, and OCaml. Furthermore, it supports large-scale optimization (some
algorithms scalable to millions of parameters and thousands of constraints).
The
open-source contain both global and local optimization algorithms.
==PAGE:50==
34
4 Optimization algorithms and techniques
Relevant NLopt Algorithms
For local optimization, NLopt open source code contains 11 algorithms in total, 6
local derivative-free optimization and 5 local gradient-based optimization methods.
Only the local gradient-based optimization methods will be presented.
Local gradient-based optimization
• MMA (Method of Moving Asymptotes) and CCSA
Globally-convergent method-of-moving-asymptotes (MMA) algorithm for gradient-
based local optimization, including nonlinear inequality constraints (but not
equality constraints). This is an improved CCSA (”conservative convex sep-
arable approximation”) variant of the original MMA algorithm published by
Svanberg in 1987 [25], which has become popular for topology optimization.
(Note: ”globally convergent” does not mean that this algorithm converges to
the global optimum; it means that it is guaranteed to converge to some local
minimum from any feasible starting point.)
• SLSQP
This is a sequential quadratic programming (SQP) algorithm for nonlinearly
constrained gradient-based optimization (supporting both inequality and equal-
ity constraints), based on the implementation by Dieter Kraft [26; 27]. The al-
gorithm optimizes successive second-order (quadratic/least-squares) approxima-
tions of the objective function (via Broyden–Fletcher–Goldfarb–Shanno (BFGS)
updates), with first-order approximations of the constraints.
• Low-storage Broyden–Fletcher–Goldfarb–Shanno (BFGS)
This algorithm is based on a Fortran implementation of the low-storage BFGS
algorithm written by Prof. Ladislav Luksan [28; 29]. The original L-BFGS
algorithm, based on variable-metric updates via Strang recurrences.
• Preconditioned truncated Newton
This algorithm in NLopt, is based on a Fortran implementation of a precondi-
tioned inexact truncated Newton algorithm written by Prof. Ladislav Luksan
[30].
• Shifted limited-memory variable-metric
This algorithm in NLopt, is based on a Fortran implementation of a shifted
limited-memory variable-metric algorithm by Prof. Ladislav Luksan [31].
For more information on the algorithms, refer to the original website in which the
above information where provided from. [32].

4.2
Ipopt open source optimization method
Ipopt is an open source software package developed by Wachter [1]. It also belongs
to the class of sequential quadratic programming methods. The QP sub-problem is
solved by applying the so-called barrier method. This interior point (IP) method
avoids the problem of identifying the active bound constraints during the solution
of the barrier problem. Ipopt solves a sequence of barrier problems and guarantees
convergence by means of line search approaches. The code can be supplied both the
Jacobian and Hessian matrix. When the second derivative information is not avail-
able, the Hessian is approximated by means of quasi-Newton approach. A detailed
description of Ipopt can be found under the work of Wachter [1].
4.3
Matlab Optimization Toolbox™
Optimization Toolbox™is a Matlab package constructed with a number of solvers
for linear programming (LP), mixed-integer linear programming (MILP), quadratic
programming (QP), nonlinear programming (NLP), constrained linear least squares,
nonlinear least squares, and nonlinear equations.
The solvers are functions con-
structed to find parameters that minimize or maximize the objectives while satis-
fying constraints. The solvers can find an optimal solutions to continues and discrete
problems, preform trade-off analyses and incorporate optimization methods into al-
gorithms and applications. The Optimization Toolbox™can perform design opti-
mization tasks, including parameter estimation, component selection, and parameter
tuning. It can be used to find optimal solutions in applications such as portfolio
optimization, resource allocation, and production planning and scheduling.
Four optimization algorithms are used from the Optimization Toolbox™, nonlin-
ear least-squares (nonlinear data-fitting) (lsqnonlin), local gradient-based (fmincon(SQP)),
local gradient-based (fmincon(interior-point)) and local multi-objective genetic al-
gorithm (gamultiobj).

The main optimization problem is to optimize a desirable 2D section of the blade
based on radial-based approach to achieve optimal performance aerodynamically and
structurally simultaneously. To insure the success and the efficiency of the end up
software, the problem was divided into two parts, aerodynamic part and structural

The approach used for the aerodynamic part is a direct approach where the pa-

aerodynamic part alone, the best suitable objective to optimize the airfoil is the lift
over drag coefficient with a set of nonlinear and boundary constraints. The problem
is treated as a single objective gradient-based optimization.
This chapter is organized into three sections. First, the formulation of the op-
timization problem with the best optimization method to solve the problem. The
coupling between the airfoil geometry optimization method, optimization method
and the open source 2D flow solver Xfoil. Second, the boundary constraints applied
to the problem. Last, the non-linear constraints applied to the problem.
5.1
Problem Formulation
In the wind turbine field, the most desirable output of any wind turbine is the levelized
cost of energy (LCoE) and thus optimizing the wind turbine blade based on the LCoE
is the rational approach. However, since the problem deals with localized 2D radial-
based airfoil section, optimizing the airfoil based on LCoE is unattainable. In LCoE,
the two main parameters are the Annual energy production AEP and the structural
mass.

The problem is a non-linear function having a single objective to be the lift over

an open-source software Xfoil, a 2D flow solver, is called to preform an aerodynami-

variables and a single objective, the best approach solving it is by a gradient-based
method using a sequential quadratic programming (SQP) for the optimization al-
gorithm. The sequential quadratic programming (SQP) is an iterative method for
constrained nonlinear optimization.
Having chosen the optimization method, the optimization problem can now be
formulated. The objective of the optimization problem is to maximize the lift over
drag coefficient, this can be achieved by defining the output of the problem func-
tion to be reversed in sign to negative in which it is minimized by the optimization
algorithm minf(x) => min −Cl/Cd(x), where x are the airfoil geometrical param-

is first transformed from geometrical parameters into x−& y−coordinates geometry.
Upon this, the open-source software Xfoil is called with the transformed airfoil to
generate the airfoil aerodynamic performance for a range of angle of attacks. Lastly,
the objective is identified from the generated data to be used by the optimization
algorithm. The parametrization method used for the airfoil optimization is the class
shape transformation CST method.
The problem requires a set of non-linear and boundary constraints to insure the
performance of the airfoil aerodynamically and structurally in which satisfies the
requirements of a wind turbine blade.

5.2
Constraints
The optimization problem is now formulated and the optimization method is decided.
The characteristic of a wind turbine airfoil consist of both aerodynamic and structural
requirements. Where at the root of the blade, are mainly dependent on structural
consideration, whereas at the tip of the blade, are mainly dependent on aerodynamic
consideration. The relative importance of these requirements vary based on the radial-
position considered. Generally, the framework will target the optimization of airfoils
having radial-based position between 30% & 90% where the power coefficient is the
highest. At these locations, the aerodynamic performance is more influential than the
structural performance. However, the structural part is not a matter taken lightly,
as the structural performance is a must for the stability of the over all of the blade.
The next two sections covers the aerodynamic and structural constraints.
5.2.1

constraints is needed to insure the stability of the framework with out sacrificing the
flexibility of the design space. The boundary constraints are chosen in which the

The first method based on figure (8.5) provided acceptable results, however, since
the objective is chosen to be the load over moment of inertia, the chord and twist
always seamed to be at the upper bound which means some other non-linear bound
must be added or the chord and twist must be predetermined as design inputs instead
of being design variables. It should be remarked, to control the thickness over chord
ratio, two approaches can be done. First, is a direct method, by simply specifying the
thickness as a non-linear constraint. Second, in an indirect method, by either using
the max lift over drag ratio where higher limit means less thickness and vise-versa or
by controlling it from the upper and lower boundary.

time is constrained for testing. 70 possible solution are presented by the solver before
filtering them using the constraints. 63 solutions out of the 70 solutions satisfied the
constraints. Due to the large size of data, the results are presented in Appendix C.

CHAPTER 10
Conclusion
10.1
Conclusion
Wind energy and wind turbines are a growing industry in there prime age. The wind
turbine field is getting more complex and more technically demanding. As such, new
methods and techniques are always at study to ultimately build and construct a wind
turbine that is ultimately balanced performance-wise and cost-wise. Generally, this
balance can be refereed to as the levelized cost of energy (LCoE). The presented
project, focuses around one of many areas of the wind turbine, the blade. Where a
study is made to optimize the aerodynamic and the structural performance of the
blade simultaneously. The project scope moves from a thickness-based airfoil design
(t/c=18%, 24%, etc.) to a radial-based airfoil design (r/R=60%, 75%, etc.). To ful-
fill this purpose, a framework is constructed in which the performance of the blade
section-wise is optimized covering the aerodynamic and structural aspects simulta-
neously. The framework evaluates the sensitivity of the airfoil shape aerodynamic
and structural performance and the general performance of the airfoil in clean and
contaminated conditions.
The project is divided to multiple parts, the geometric
parametrization method to construct the airfoil, the 2D flow solver considered, the
optimization method and algorithm optimal to the introduced problem, the aerody-
namic model (formulation of the problem), structural model, BEM model to couple
the aerodynamic and structural model, the constraints considered to achieve the op-
timal results and the final implementation of the problem to a usable framework.
A general formulation of the problem is preformed, the problem requires a number
of design parameters including the radial-based section for optimization. Having the
parameters, an initial airfoil is constructed using a geo parametrization method. The
airfoil aerodynamic performances is than simulated using an open-source software 2D
flow solver. Having the aerodynamic performance, the loads can than be computed
using a BEM approach. The structural model can be evaluated and the performance
of the airfoil are obtained both aerodynamic and structural. This problem is included
in an optimization method to optimize the airfoil shape for optimal aerodynamic and
structural performance. This problem can be described as a non-linear problem.
==PAGE:96==
80
10 Conclusion
Geo paramtrization
Few geo paramtrization method are investigated and compared. The method stud-
ied are, PARSEC, Class Shape Transformation CST and Bezer-PARSEC BP3333.
Based on the comparison preformed in section (3.5), the Class Shape Transforma-
tion function was deemed best for the presented problem having the least number of
parameters while maintaining a good and flexible performance.
2D flow solver
Many 2D flow solver for incompresible flow exist and can be used. For the presented
problem an open-sourced software Xfoil [9] is used. The software have a good com-
putational time while maintaining accurate results. A drawback of the software, the
software is very sensitive that it could face convergence problems. This is generally
seen at negative angle of attacks, in the stall regain and in simulating contaminated
conditions. The software also tends to over estimate the aerodynamic performance
at the stall regain.
Optimization solver
Many optimization techniques exist and can be used to address the presented problem.
Being the problem constraint non-linear, few methods are evaluated and compared
to ultimately choose the ideal available code.
The investigated methods are the
open-sourced code NLopt, the open-sourced code Ipopt and the Matlab Optimization
Toolbox™. The methods are compared by simulating the same three simple non-linear
problems using each methods. The results are presented in section (4.4). Based on the
results, the Matlab Optimization Toolbox™using the local single-objective gradient-
based function fmincon having Sequential quadratic programming (SQP) for the
algorithm proven to be ideal for the addressed problem. The decision is mainly based
on the least number of iteration to achieve convergence at the local minima.
Aerodynamic model
The aerodynamic model is formulated as a standard optimization problem having a
single-objective to be minimized. The objective is chosen to the lift over drag coeffi-
cient. The objective had to be reversed in sign to negative to be minimized by the
solver and thus be maximized. As mentioned above, the paramterization method is
chosen to be the CST function, the 2D flow solver to be Xfoil and the optimization
method is the local single-objective gradient-based function fmincon having Sequen-
tial quadratic programming (SQP) for the algorithm. The problem included a set on
non-linear constraints and boundaries to insure the optimized solution is suitable for
wind turbine blade application and for its corresponding radial-position.

BEM code
A BEM code is implemented to couple the aerodynamic and structural models in the
final framework. The BEM code is introduced as an optimization problem to insure
fast, accurate and guaranteed convergence. The code is tested and validated with
other BEM codes.
Structural model
The structural model is addressed as one of the objectives in the final framework.
Having the study based on 2D sectional airfoil, the best approach is decided to be
the load obtained from the BEM model as function of the moment of inertia in
the load direction as the objective. The model is directly implemented in the final
framework.
Final framework
The aerodynamic and structural model are coupled using the BEM code.
The fi-
nal framework problem formulation expanded from being single-objective to multi-
objective where the objectives are the lift over drag coefficient, power coefficient and
load as function of moment of inertia. The problem is addressed in three method.
First, as a single-objective and the rest of the objectives are constrained, this can be
solved using local gradient-method. Second, as a single weighted objective of all the
objectives, this can be solved using local gradient-method. Lastly, as multi-objective
where all the objectives are considered, this can be solve using multi-objective genetic
algorithm method. The three method are constructed and tested. The design pa-
rameters for the three methods are the CST parameters, chord and the twist. For
detailed formulation, refer to chapter 8.
The first proposed method using one objective and the rest of the objectives are
constraints provided some good results however the chord and twist optimal estima-
tion are always at the upper bound. This can be refereed to having the single-objective
to be the load as function of the moment of inertia. Using this method, it would be
best to have the chord and twist as design inputs rather than design parameters.
Having them as design inputs, the results duplicated the example considered from
the DTU 10MW wind turbine [6] presented in chapter 8.
The second proposed method where all the objectives are weighted is dismissed
as finding a combination of weighing coefficient α, β and γ proven to be difficult
to optimize the airfoil in which is suitable for a wind turbine application. In order
for a suitable combination is achieved, a large data of wind turbine blades is needed
to formulate somewhat an empirical relationship in which the coefficient are chosen
based on the radial position and the blade length.
The last proposed method using multi-objectives provided the best results as us-
ing the genetic algorithm, up to 70 potential solutions can be generated. This provide
==PAGE:98==

a much more flexible approach for choosing the optimal airfoil. This method com-
pared to the first proposed method requires less design inputs to generate the results
specially where the initial design parameters are generated randomly. Having all the
objectives in this method creates a large set of airfoil having many aspects. where an
airfoil could priorities the aerodynamic performance over structural performance and
vise-versa.
Out of the three proposed methods, the last proposed method using multi-objectives
is considered for the final framework approach. The framework is investigated further
where the DTU 10MW wind turbine blade [6] is considered as a casy study to build.
The same inputs and assumptions considered for the DTU blade are implemented
and simulated for radial-positions (30%,50%,70%,90%). The results and comparison
preformed is presented in chapter 9. Based on the results and assuming the DTU
blade is optimally constructed, the framework provided results similar to that of the
DTU blade. Thus the framework is validated to generate airfoils with minimal in-
puts based on radial-positions where the aerodynamic and structural performance is
simulated simultaneously.
10.2
Future work
For future work, the code can be expanded in many aspects:
• Instead of dealing with sectional analysis, the framework could be constructed
to target the entire blade studying simultaneously multiple sections. One ob-
jective can than be considered to be the levelized cost of energy LCoE. The
structural model can than be expanded to deal with topology optimization for
the internal part of the airfoil to increase the stiffness in the load direction and to
approximate ultimately the mass and the composite material used with its cor-
responding fiber orientation. Having the problem shifted from multi-objective
genetic algorithm to single-objective gradient-based, the optimization problem
could be solved more efficiently time-wise and accurate-wise.
• The 2D flow solver Xfoil could be reconstructed or modified to cover the con-
vergence problems and the overestimation problem or a different solver can be
used having a guaranteed convergence with better estimation and being more
efficient time-wise.
• Many features could be added to the framework, e.g., acoustic model for noise
estimation, buckling model for internal structure and many more.
