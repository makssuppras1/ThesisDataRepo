Abstract
This thesis deals with the eﬀect of extreme sea levels due to storm surges on the groundwater
level in inner Copenhagen. Due to climate changes, mean sea levels and the frequency
and magnitude of extreme sea levels are predicted to increase. Storm surges cause raised
groundwater levels at the coast, which may cause ﬂooding or soil failure at the bottom
of excavation sites. Extreme sea levels have been analysed to estimate their recurrence
interval at present and in the future. It is expected that a 100 year storm event will
cause a sea level increase of 1.57 m now and of 2.17 m in year 2100. Groundwater level
measurements along two transects have been analysed to describe the hydraulic contact
within the aquifers of Copenhagen and with the sea. The primary aquifer was determined
to be conﬁned or semi conﬁned. The groundwater level was found to be aﬀected by the sea
level according to the local geology. However, heavy rain events may also have an impact.
It was demonstrated that the sea level ﬂuctuations have a larger impact on the primary
aquifer at the Marble Church transect than at the Kgs. Nytorv transect. An analytical
model has been created to simulate the groundwater wave propagation inland and it was
calibrated to the transects indicating storativity values of 96.2 · 10−4 and 475.2 · 10−4
respectively with a ﬁxed transmissivity of 1.375 · 10−3m2/s. The model can be used as a
tool to estimate the groundwater level increase during storm surges at an excavation site
and to determine the minimum bed thickness in order to avoid ﬂooding and soil failure.
Resume
Dette speciale omhandler eﬀekten af ekstreme havvandstande i forbindelse med stormﬂod
på grundvandesniveauet i det indre København. Grundet klimaforandringerne forventes
middelhavvandstanden samt hyppigheden og størrelsen af ekstremvandstande at stige.
Stormﬂoder forhøjer grundvandet ved kysten, hvilket kan give oversvømmelse eller kollaps
af bunden af byggepladser ved kysten. Ekstremvandstande er blevet analyseret for at
kunne estimere deres hændelseshyppighed nu og i fremtiden. Det forventes, at en 100
års storm forårsager en havvandsstigning på 1.57 m nu og 2.17 m i år 2100. Målinger
af grundvandsniveau langs to tværsnit er blevet analyseret for at kunne beskrive den
hydrauliske kontakt inden for grundvandsmagasinerne i København og med havet. Det
primære magasin blev bestemt til at være artesisk eller semi-artesisk. Det blev påvist, at
grundvandsniveauet bliver påvirket af havvandsniveauet afhængigt af den lokale geologi.
Ekstrem nedbør har dog også en eﬀekt. Havvandstandsændringer viste sig at have en
større eﬀekt på det primære grundvandsmagasin ved Marmorkirke-snittet end ved Kgs.
Nytorv-snittet. En analytisk model er blevet udarbejdet til at simulere udbredelsen af
grundvandsbølgen ind i landet, og den blev kalibreret til tværsnittene, hvor den indikerer
et magasintal på hhv. 96.2 · 10−4 og 475.2 · 10−4 ved en transmissivitet på 1.375 · 10−3m2/s.
Modellen kan bruges som et værktøj til at forudsige grundvandsstigningen i forbindelse
med en stormﬂod ved en byggeplads samt ﬁnde den påkrævede bundtykkelse for at undgå
oversvømmelse og kollaps.

Chapter 1
Introduction and aim
1.1
Introduction
In recent years, the urban coastline and harbours have become attractive locations for
exclusive oﬃces and high-end apartment buildings. Where the coastline of Copenhagen was
previously dominated by industry and wastelands, new urban areas are now being formed,
for example Ørestaden, Nordhavn and Sydhavnen. As a result contractors are constructing
more buildings near the coast, where the hydraulic contact between the aquifer and the
seawater is high.
The eﬀect of changes in sea level on groundwater has been investigated for more than
a hundred years [Bredehoeft, 1967]. However, climate changes are expected to lead to
increased sea levels and increase the frequency of extreme storm events (IPCC, 2013).
Groundwater levels will thereby increase generally and will at the same time reach higher
peaks more often due to storms [Sonnenborg et al., 2009].
In the case of buildings, which need parking lots or cellars underground, construction sites
are excavated into or below the conﬁning clay layer of the aquifers of Copenhagen. The
excavations often take place in the vicinity of, or up to several meters under the water
table. This leads to a risk of ﬂooding of the building site by groundwater intrusion into
the excavation. It is important that the construction site is dry in order for e.g. building
machinery to function, for concrete foundations to dry and for the safety of the personnel.
Furthermore, when excavations are made within groundwater aquifers, there is a risk of
failure of the soil ﬂoor due to a hydrostatic uplift, which can delay projects, increase costs
or result in breakage of structures.
Concerning these coastal excavation sites, the water levels used for dimensioning are
based on coeﬃcients or routine estimations [Vejdirektoratet et al., 2013], which have not
been validated. This thesis will shed light on the eﬀect of storm surges on the groundwater
level in order to optimise the dimensioning of excavation sites and construction foundations.
In the process, a simple analytical model will be developed to quantify the groundwater
change.
The depth at which the problems of soil failure occur can be found by analysis of the
exact groundwater levels and the geology of the area. However, groundwater levels in
coastal areas are continuously aﬀected by the changing sea level. As the sea level rises,
the increased water level propagates inland in the groundwater aquifer [Bredehoeft, 1967].

1.2 Aims
The eﬀect of the increased sea level on the groundwater level is dependent on soil types,
man-made barriers, drainage systems and static diﬀerences in water levels. The sea level
varies due to periodic forcings like tide, seasonal changes and storms. In the future, global
warming is expected to cause an increase of the mean sea level and the level and duration
of extreme water levels. Therefore, it is important to ﬁnd the degree of change in the
groundwater level due to increased sea level. The issue, which is addressed in this thesis
concerns the risk of ﬂooding at building sites due to increased extreme sea level because of
global climate changes.
Previous works
The prediction of extreme sea levels in Denmark due to storm surges
has already been made by the Danish Coast Authority [Sørensen et al., 2013], [COWI,
2014] along with the estimation of the magnitude and risk of ﬂoods due to this[Vogensen
et al., 2013], [Sørensen and Piontkowitz, 2013]. However, the methods for this analysis have
not yet been used in a Danish context and new data may eﬀect the results. In this report,
the estimated recurrence intervals of extreme water levels in Copenhagen are re-evaluated
using the newest available data and alternative analysing methods.
The eﬀect of Earth tide on conﬁned aquifers has already been investigated [Narasimhan
et al., 1984], [Bredehoeft, 1967] along with other periodic forces [Crosbie, 2003]. Analytical
modelling solutions for periodic sea level changes have been developed, which need tidal
parameters [Kong et al., 2013], but so far, no model consider non-periodic forcings on
conﬁned aquifers. The eﬀect of long term climate changes on the groundwater has been
modelled numerically in Denmark [Sonnenborg et al., 2009], but not the eﬀects of short
term extreme events.
Numerical modelling of groundwater in Denmark has already been done to understand the
current water cycle [Jeppesen et al., 2011] and the eﬀect of the climate changes [Sonnenborg
et al., 2009]. None of these include the eﬀect of extreme sea events and therefore the results
of this thesis could be used as a component in such future groundwater models.
1.2
Aims
This thesis investigates the ﬂuctuations of groundwater due to extreme sea levels. The
main aims are:
• Determining the magnitude of extreme sea levels and their recurrence interval and
projecting the results into year 2100.
• Analysing sea and groundwater data in a near coastal region to determine impacts of
sea level increase due to storm surges.
• Developing an analytical model, which can simulate the groundwater response on
increased sea levels as a function of distance inland and time.
• Using the calibrated model to predict the impact of extreme sea levels on the
groundwater level in the future.
To be able to fulﬁl these aims, the necessary background knowledge will be reviewed.
This includes the geology of Copenhagen, sea level ﬂuctuations and statistical analysis,
groundwater level ﬂuctuations, ﬂood risk, groundwater management and the analytical

2.1
Sea water level
2.1.1
Drivers of sea level
The oceans of the world are part of a big dynamic system, which is variable in time and
space. The sea is governed by processes that can aﬀect the level on small or big scale and
locally or globally. The main drivers for the regulation of the sea level are listed below:
• Mean sea level The mean sea level ﬂuctuates over long periods of time due to
ﬂuctuating global temperatures, which causes thermal expansion, melting of ice
caps and glaciers and decreased land water storage [Field et al., 2014]. In the last
interglacial period mean sea levels were between 4 to 6 m above the present day level,
but the last 6,000 years there has only been a little net change in sea levels until the
start of the 19th century, where the levels have risen with increasing speed [Church,
2006]
• Tides The tidal variation in Denmark is calculated based on the harmonic constants
(O1, K1, M2 and S2). The sea level increases up to 2.1 m diurnally in Denmark, but
the coasts within Kattegat and the Baltic sea only get tidal changes of ± 0.1 m [DMI,
2014].
5
==PAGE:13==
2.1 Sea water level
• Spatial variation Short-term sea level changes due to wind or wave formations
can give considerably diﬀerent maximum levels within bays or ports as the water is
"pushed" up against the coast.
• Wind Wave formations and storm surges are created by strong wind headed towards
the coast.
• Periodic oscillations The global oceans are aﬀected by phenomena such as the
North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO) or the El Niño SouthernOscillation (ENSO) [Field
et al., 2014]. These ﬂuctuations are relevant for the big oceans and have a negligible
eﬀect in Denmark, which is why they will not be discussed further.
The mean sea level is traditionally used as a height reference. In Denmark, the DNN
(Dansk Normal Nul) datum point was deﬁned in 1891 based on tide gauge data. This
datum was superseded by the current datum DVR90 (Dansk Vertikal Reference 1990)
based on mean sea level in Denmark in the period 1982 – 1994. This datum shift was made
in order to accommodate land movement and changes in mean sea level in the intervening
period. In addition to the above, the Copenhagen area has its own datum, KN, deﬁned by
water level measurements in the early 19th century (1817 - 1832), and tide gauge stations
further have a local zero, e.g. in order to keep track of the relative changes in mean sea
level [Bahl, 2005]. Throughout this report, all heights are referenced to meters above
DVR90 (m.a.s.l) unless otherwise stated.
2.1.2
Expected climatic eﬀect on sea levels
As the global mean temperature rises, the sea level will also be aﬀected. As mentioned
before, the mean sea level increases as glaciers melt and water expands due to increased
temperature. Meanwhile, as seasonal changes in weather will become more pronounced,
more extreme wind is expected to cause more extreme water levels during storm surges at
many locations.
Projections of a future sea level rise still contain large uncertainties as well as regional varia-
tions [Field et al., 2014], [Olesen et al., 2014]. Observations show that the mean sea level has
increased 0.17 m - 0.22 m (±0.03) in Denmark during the last 100 years [Olesen et al., 2014].
On a global scale, the average rate of sea level rise is 3.4 mm/year (± 0.4) [Nerem
et al., 2010]. The IPCC expects the global mean sea level to increase 0.34 m (0.1 - 0.6) in
Denmark for the scenario RCP2.6, and for scenario RCP8.5 the expected mean sea level is
0.64 m (0.3 - 1.1) in 2100 [Field et al., 2014]. Naturstyrelsen predicts that the most likely
sea level rise in Copenhagen will be of 0.7 m within 2100, but that uncertainties about the
melting of the ice caps in Greenland and Antarctic could make this number much higher
[Naturstyrelsen, 2014].
The wind patterns in Denmark have been varying without a clear trend since 1890.
However, hurricanes and hurricane-like storms have been observed more frequently since
1965. Based on the international climate model ENSEMBLES, the average wind speed is
expected to decrease slightly, while storm and hurricane events will increase in intensity
[Olesen et al., 2014]. This will aﬀect the short term extreme sea levels in case of storm
surges. In some regions a climatic deterioration with more severe storms in the future,
extreme water levels may increase by more than accounted for by mean sea level rise,

2.1.3
Statistical analysis of extreme water levels
To be able to predict the future extreme sea levels to protect cities and coasts, a statistical
distribution can be applied to the extreme water levels. The distribution can be used
to predict water levels of a certain frequency so engineers can dimension buildings and
structures for an extreme event. Diﬀerent return periods are used depending on the purpose
of the structure ([COWI, 2014], [COWI and Arup Systra, 2008]). In this report, the 100
years recurrence interval will be used for the evaluation. Many methods and strategies are
available for this. An overview of the whole process from raw data to model construction
is shown below:
Figure 2.1: Map of the possible ways to conduct an extreme value analysis [Arns
et al., 2013]
To begin with, the water level data need to be de-trended by subtracting the trend in mean
sea level (MSL), in order for the dataset to be independent and stationary. A common way
to de-trend water level is to assume a linear regression on the initial dataset and subtract it
from all data. However, this method does not remove seasonal, decadal and secular trends
and this can lead to an underestimation of return water levels [Arns et al., 2013]. Next,
the method of selecting extreme values should be chosen. This can be done by the Block
Maxima method (BM), where a ﬁxed amount of storms is set per year and a truncation
value is found based on that. However, the Peaks Over Threshold (POT) method proves
to be much more robust and eﬃcient. The threshold value can be chosen based on physi-
cal criteria (such as water level that results in ﬂooding) or mathematically [Arns et al., 2013].
When the extreme values are chosen, a declustering technique can be used to separate
storms. This is done to avoid two storms being classiﬁed as one and means that a standard
storm surge length can be set (24-72 hours). This method is highly debatable as no single
storm length can be assumed [Arns et al., 2013].

The eﬀect of "Bodil"
In the beginning of December 2013, a severe storm hit Western Europe, which resulted in
the highest sea level in and around Denmark since 1888 and ﬂooding at many locations at
Sealand. Damages estimated by the insurance companies were at least 1.6 billion DKK
[Forsikring og Pension, 2014]. The storm was called "Bodil" and started the trend of
naming storms in Denmark. As the storm surge was so extreme in Copenhagen, it is liable
to inﬂuence the existing statistics on extreme water levels. This is due to the fact that
outliers in a statistical distribution aﬀect the ﬁt more than the average values, while it
also increases the standard deviation.
A technical report from COWI describing the statistical inﬂuence of "Bodil" concluded
that the inclusion of the storm increased the expected 100 year water level by 6.1 cm (from
150 to 156 cm) [COWI, 2014]. Extreme sea level values’ series used further in this report
include "Bodil".

Quaternary
Copenhagen has existed since the 1000’s and since then, the upper soil layers have been
altered to ﬁt human purposes. Trenches have been dug at strategic places surrounding the
old town to protect against enemies, while sea areas have been claimed by ﬁlling to use
the land for agriculture, urban expansion and as a dumping ground for waste [Frederiksen
et al., 2002]. Therefore, most ofthe upper soil of Copenhagen is covered with ﬁll containing
organic matter. This layer is usually less than 1 m thick, but can reach several meters
of thickness. Glacial quaternary deposits consist of melt water sand and clay and rarely
exceed 2 m of thickness. The quaternary glacial deposits are dominated by till, sand and
gravel from melt water and can extend from 5 to 35 m depth [COWI and Arup Systra, 2008].
The melt water sand and gravel are poorly sorted and may also contain silt and clay.
The lower melt water sand and gravel are very coarse and also contain fragments of
limestone and ﬂint. The till is not homogeneous either, varying from sandy and gravely
clay tills and clayey sand tills [COWI and Arup Systra, 2008].
The water level in the quaternary layers is signiﬁcantly aﬀected by three features: The
thickness of the clay/silt and tills layers with low permeability, the thickness of the middle
and lower melt water sand/gravel layers of high permeability and the buried town hall
valley.
Pre-Quaternary surface
The paleogene age is referred to as pre-quaternary. This age can be divided into two
stages called the Selandian and the Danian, whereof the Danian is again divided into early,
middle, and late Danian. The Selandian age is represented by the Lellinge Greensand
formation, while the late Danian age left the Copenhagen Limestone formation and the

middle Danian the Bryozoan formation. Lower layers than this have not been investigated
in the Copenhagen area historically or recently [Frederiksen et al., 2002].
The Lellinge Greensand mainly consists of marine calcareous deposits as sand and clay,
but also contains limestone beds. The deposits are named after the green colour caused by
a glauconite content. The layer is only continuous in some places, for example under the
Frederik’s Church, Enghave Plads and northeast of Copenhagen central station and rarely
exceeds 3 meters thickness.
The Copenhagen Limestone consists of sandy and silty limestone with layers of ﬂint.
The Upper layer is horizontally bedded and has been eroded by glacial processes. The
upper Limestone has a maximum thickness of around 15 m, but is thinner towards the
south of Copenhagen at Frederiksberg station, where is almost non-existing. The middle
Copenhagen limestone is between 18 - 22 m thick and is more irregularly bedded with
nodules ﬁlled with ﬂint. The lower Copenhagen limestone is regularly bedded and rich in
clay with sedimentary variations within short distances. The thickness reaches between 3
and 6 m. The Bryozoan limestone is the oldest formation, which has been investigated in
Copenhagen. The layer is 50 - 60 m thick and is composed of silty and sandy limestone
made up of skeletal fragments of bryozoans in lime mud [COWI and Arup Systra, 2008].
Below the glacially disturbed top layer of the upper Danian limestone, fractures are
observed. Usually the weaker limestone areas are more fractured than the harder. It should
be noted however,that the degree of fracturing is disturbed when boreholes are drilled and
therefore the observed amount of fractures might not correspond to reality. Layer-parallel
fracturing also occurs between layers of diﬀerent hardness. The fractures can act as highly
permeable zones [COWI and Arup Systra, 2008].
Aquifer conditions
A conﬁned aquifer is deﬁned by the parameters storativity and transmissivity. The stora-
tivity is a dimensionless coeﬃcient of the volume of water released from an aquifer per
meters decline in hydraulic head in the aquifer, per area of the aquifer. The transmissivity
is equal to the horizontal hydraulic conductivity multiplied by the conﬁned aquifer thickness.
When performing a pump test, a few assumptions are made [Einsiedl, 2011]:
• Hydraulic surface of the aquifer is horizontal before the start of the pumping test.
• The aquifer is homogeneous and isotropic
• All ﬂow is radial towards the well
• Groundwater ﬂow is horizontal
• Darcy’s law is valid
• The pumping well fully penetrates the aquifer
When these assumptions are made, the pump will create a drawdown in the aquifer and
it will extend radially from the well with time. There are diﬀerent methods to ﬁnd the
storativity and transmissivity values, and COWI and Arup Systra [2008] has used the
Cooper Jacob’s method for the drawdown calculations and Theis’s method for the recovery

The aquifers in Copenhagen consist of the primary aquifer and several secondary aquifers.

between the aquifers, while they are separated in other places. This can be due to leakage
through conﬁning layers or direct hydraulic contact between aquifers. Based on ﬂow

lower melt water sand/gravel layer to the Bryozoan limestone is conﬁned within most of

than the remaining limestone [Jakobsen and Klitten, 1999], and this layer is therefore
assumed to be the most permeable and water bearing unit. The secondary aquifers are
mainly inﬂuenced by precipitation and may rise over the ground level locally [COWI and
Arup Systra, 2008].
It should also be noted that the primary aquifer is neither homogeneous, nor isotropic
as assumed in the pumping test, which leads to diﬀerences in the test responses between
diﬀerent depths. Diﬀerences were also seen in the pressure head across the depth and some
of the wells showed test results that were signiﬁcantly diﬀerent to the surrounding ones.
Anisotropy was also clearly indicated, while it could not be quantiﬁed. Therefore, results
from models treating the geology should be treated with prudence.

2.3 Groundwater level
The early parts of pumping tests in Copenhagen show storativity values that correspond
to a conﬁned or semi conﬁned aquifer (0.8 −6.5 · 10−4). As the pumping tests proceed, the
S-values increase to much higher values (12 −400 · 10−4). This trend is expected to happen
due to the double porosity of the aquifer, but can also be due to unconﬁned conditions in
some areas [COWI and Arup Systra, 2008]. The theory of double porosity is a concept of
an aquifer consisting of two media: the fractures and the surrounding matrix, where both
have a porosity. The primary porosity in the matrix usually has a lower permeability and
high storage capacity, while the fractures have low storage capacity and high permeability
[Kruseman et al., 1994]. From a modelling perspective, the double porosity acts similarly
to a delayed yield in an unconﬁned aquifer and therefore the two diﬀerent processes were
not investigated further by COWI and Arup Systra [2008].
The double porosity causes high transmissivity values in the beginning of a test and
lower values towards the end. The long duration pumping tests show transmissivities of
the Copenhagen limestone of 0.25 −2.5 · 10−3m2/s and even up to 4 −5 · 10−3m2/s around
the Frederik’s Church. The total thickness of the Copenhagen limestone is found by COWI
and Arup Systra [2008] to be between 36 to 43 m, which is slightly higher than previous
investigations, placing the Copenhagen limestone from about -9 to -43 m.a.s.l [Frederiksen
et al., 2002].
2.3
Groundwater level
2.3.1
Drivers of groundwater level
Urban water is being carefully managed to ﬁt the human purposes and to avoid events
which compromise the safety and well-being of the inhabitants. Water is naturally recycled
and dispersed throughout the land, atmosphere and sea. A conceptual model of the most
important factors is shown in ﬁgure 2.4. When dealing with coastal cities the sea water
level and precipitation are closely linked to the groundwater level. The main drivers of the
groundwater level are listed below:
• Geology Geological parameters such as the hydraulic conductivity determine the
path and speed of water while the storativity and porosity put a measure to how
much water can be stored in the sediments. The localization of aquifers and aquitards
and their interconnectivity also play a role in the estimation of the extension and
pressure head.
• Gradient of water table The amount of water ﬂowing from the land towards the
coast determines the gradient in water head.
• Inﬁltration Inﬁltration of precipitation acts as a recharge to the aquifers. The
intensity, volume and frequency of precipitation are also important with respect to
how much and how fast the groundwater level increases. The inﬁltration can be
limited due to impermeable layers on the surface (such as roads and houses) creating
surface run-oﬀand can also be limited by the Lisse eﬀect by trapping air in the
unsaturated zone [Crosbie, 2003]
• Natural drainage Evapotranspiration and ﬂow downwards and laterally function
as natural drains and cause the groundwater level to decrease between rain events.
12
==PAGE:20==
2.3 Groundwater level
The closer the groundwater table is to the ground surface, the higher is the eﬀect
and the variability of the natural drainage, as plants have more access to the water.
As the evapotranspiration varies diurnally (due to light changes night/day), the
groundwater level also ﬂuctuates [Crosbie, 2003].
• Urban drainage Urban drainage consists of man-made channels, pumping and
sewer systems. The installations can cause both an increase and decrease of the water
table. As an example, the sewer pipes may be leaky causing groundwater to ﬂow in
when the pipes are dry and thereby drain the aquifer, while full pipes maybe leak
sewer water to the subsoil and increase the groundwater table.
• Private drainage Private houses may also have pumping systems, maybe even
automatic ones, which start in case of high water levels. These drainage systems are
unmapped and some of them might originate from many years ago.
• Water abstraction The groundwater level in the primary aquifer of Copenhagen
has been aﬀected by abstraction due to the increasing population during the 19th
and 20th century. The waterworks of Frederiksberg now abstract an average of 3
mill. m3/year .
• Periodic changes Other periodic forces that aﬀect the groundwater are ocean tides,
earth tides, barometric pressure changes and atmospheric tides. The tidal forcing
in Copenhagen is only in the range of ± 0.1 m [Olesen et al., 2014] and is therefore
not very signiﬁcant, and the barometric pressure change eﬀect is considered to aﬀect
conﬁned aquifers [Crosbie, 2003]. The remaining tidal eﬀects will be ignored, as they
are assumed to be negligible.
• Sea level As the sea is connected to the groundwater in coastal areas, increases
in sea level have an aﬀect on the groundwater. The extend of this eﬀect will be
discovered further throughout this report.

with the highest increase in winter time of 18.0 % (±12.0) and an decrease in summer of
16.6 % (±10.9) [Olesen et al., 2014].
Even though the precipitation is expected to increase globally, the need for fresh wa-
ter is also going to increase due to an increased population, energy consumption and
agricultural and industrial activities. The most recent IPCC report states that "As a result
of increased evaporative demand, climate change is likely to signiﬁcantly reduce water
availability from river abstraction and from groundwater resources (medium conﬁdence)"
in the European chapter. This topic is yet to be fully understood and in this report the
subject will not be further dealt with.
2.4
Flood risk
2.4.1
Drivers of ﬂood risk
Floods are strongly connected to the groundwater level and the sea level, but other factors
also come into play in coastal areas. The tectonic plates of the Earth are constantly
moving due to past and present stresses, and Denmark is still recovering from the pressure
which the ice cap exerted during the last ice age about 12,000 years ago. Copenhagen is
therefore expected to rise by 1.0 mm per year (± 0.2 mm) over the next 100 years due to
glacio-isostatic uplift [Knudsen et al., 2014]. In contrast to this, the soil in urban and rural
areas is settling due to pressure and drainage causing the soil to consolidate. The local
subsidence in Copenhagen is estimated at 0 cm/year (±0.5) [Jakobsen et al., 2013]. The soil
rise will decrease the eﬀect of the rising mean sea level, while local settlements will increase it.
Another factor to consider is the concurrence of events that increase the risk of ﬂooding.
This may be multiple high sea events or heavy rain showers that occur shortly after one
another, making it impossible to diﬀerentiate between them. This feature means that an
increased water level is acting on top of a raised water table and thereby increasing the
eﬀect. The duration of high sea levels also cause increased problems to urban areas, as
storage areas may be ﬁlled and unpressurised sewer systems fail to drain waste water to
the sea.
Finally, the use of the area which is susceptible to ﬂoods can deﬁne how severe and
elongated the event is. The impact on a ﬁeld is determined by the plant absorption,
evapotranspiration and inﬁltration speed into the subsoil. In a paved urban environment
however, water will collect in gutters and cellars from all paved surfaces thus intensifying
14

the ﬂood depth. The eﬃciency of the drainage system will be determining the degree of
ﬂooding in such areas.
The term ’ﬂood risk’ is deﬁned as "the combination of the probability of a ﬂood event
and of the potential adverse consequences for human health, the environment, cultural
heritage and economic activity associated with a ﬂood event." [European Council, 2007].
The analysis of the factors causing ﬂoods creates the basis for the deﬁnition of the risk
involved in connection with a building site close to the coast. At construction sites the risk
involves both security and costs. While the workers must be safe at all times, construction
work may be prolonged or expenses may increase. Following construction, buildings must
also be dimensioned for the case of ﬂoods without leakage or soil failure. The design
water level of a building must therefore have a long life span and consider adaptation and
mitigation to the future climate. More on this topic in chapter 2.5.
2.5
Groundwater management
2.5.1
Challenges of groundwater control
Groundwater management and control are important for construction work for many
reasons. First of all, it is necessary to ensure a dry and stable construction pit for security
and for the construction work. Therefore, water masses should be managed in volume
and pressure to avoid leakage, soil failure and the collapse of the pit. The management
of groundwater is important both on temporary and permanent scale, as the ﬁnished
constructions should be able to withstand the pressure of surrounding water in the subsoil
and the increase thereof in extreme cases.
Sediment is held together by the gravitational vertical stress, which is larger than the
pressure head in the chalk aquifer beneath it [Domenico and Schwartz, 1990]. However,
as the hydrostatic pressure increases with depth into the aquifer, the pore water pressure
may exceed the vertical stress from the clay layer and thereby push the sediments apart,
causing the soil to fail. Soil failure decreases the carrying capacity of the soil and that can
lead to the sinking of excavation equipment or lifting of foundations.
An underestimation or neglect of managing the water may result in construction de-
lays, additional costs and failure of the construction, which is why water management
should be included as soon as possible in the process to ensure the optimal project [Stæhr,
2014].
The challenges of dealing with water that may occur during and after a construction
work are generally:
• Sensitive old constructions Old buildings are usually built on wooden piles, which
are subject to rot if exposed to air. Furthermore, the soil under the buildings may
settle a lot in case of lowering of the water table or additional pressure from new
constructions.
• Protection of groundwater resources Groundwater is becoming more scarce and
is a vital resource. Therefore there are strict quantitive and qualitative limitations in
national law to assure the availability of fresh and clean water.

• Geological properties The better the knowledge of the subsoil is, the better a
project can be designed. In some cases, there are major geological structures, which
aﬀect the groundwater ﬂow. An example can be the Carlsberg Fault, a northwest-
/southeast trending fault zone in the chalk, which has a very hight transmissivity
[Frederiksen et al., 2002].
• Contamination The use and discard of chemicals from industry and waste have

the whole municipality of Copenhagen has been labelled as potentially contaminated

• Surface water and natural areas Wetlands and surface water may be aﬀected by
the removal or discharge of groundwater, which may disturb wild life and natural

Natural areas can be protected on EU level (e.g. Natura 2000), national level (e.g.

[Department of Nature, 2015].
• Climate changes Climatic changes will result in rising sea levels and groundwater

• Drainage systems Many sewer pipes under Copenhagen are leaky. This means
that groundwater is drained if the water table is above the sewers or will be ﬁlled

are pumps installed in many basements or drainage systems that activate in case of
rising groundwater levels.
• Rising groundwater Changes in the water catchment may aﬀect the groundwater

• Construction work During and after construction work, the groundwater ﬂow may
be disrupted by pumping, draining and inﬁltration and adjacent sites may aﬀect each

such as sheet piles or basements may change the course of the groundwater ﬂow.
Many of these considerations have been taken into account laws and guidelines set by the
authorities (municipality or government). General rule sets are created by the national
government for best practice. As an example, the Danish Road Directorate has issued a
national annex, which sets rules for implementing the Eurocode 7 for building work under
the building law or construction under the Road Directorate. This document sets national
values for selected parameters, best practice for the choice of methods in the eurocode and
additional guidelines [Vejdirektoratet et al., 2013]. Including groundwater management
early in the building process and carrying out cost-beneﬁt analysis is to be preferred to
optimise the process and minimise risks [Stæhr, 2014].

2.5.2
Authorities and regulations on groundwater control
The major permissions and regulations required when conducting major groundwater
lowering are as follows:
• Permission to discharge groundwater At construction works and digging of
clay, soil etc. permission is required from the municipality to remove or lower the
groundwater. The permission is not needed for B-wells, which implies that the works
are expected to be of less than two years duration, the discharge is below 100.000
m3/year and that there is no groundwater abstraction within 300 m of the site.
B-wells simply have to be announced to the municipality. All other wells, such as
monitoring wells, observation wells and permanent drains, are categorized as A-wells
and need a permission [Ministry of Environment, 2013].
• Permission to inﬁltrate water Compounds and materials that may contaminate
the groundwater and soil are not allowed to be discharged to the subsoil without
permission from the municipality [Environmental Ministry, 2010]. Seawater and
contaminated groundwater from the subsoil may be a contamination source to the
groundwater and must therefore be cleaned before inﬁltration.
• Permission to discharge groundwater The discharge of water to the sewer or
surface water must be permitted by the municipality to prevent the contamination
of surface waters or strain of waste water systems [Environmental Ministry, 2010].
• Excavation permission Change in public areas, such as excavations, must be
permitted by the municipality, e.g. in the case of digging in public roads. In case
of the need of urgent reparations on cables or pipes, this can be performed without
permission, if it is notiﬁed to the municipality immediately [Ministry of Transport,
2014].
• Landowner acceptance If wells are to be located on private property, the accep-
tance of the landowner is needed.
2.5.3
Setting a design water level
Based on the previously noted challenges, a control water level is calculated for the con-
struction period. First, a study of literature about the water levels and geological conditions
is carried out, to which further samples may be added if needed. On the basis of this
an estimation of the reference levels is made based on the mean of the measured water
levels. This is provided that there are no major inﬂuences on the groundwater level such as
signiﬁcant inﬁltration or pumping [COWI, 2006]. On the basis of the reference levels, the
control levels are set. These are decided by the municipality as a measure of, how much the
surrounding water table is allowed to change during a building project. In Copenhagen, no
groundwater disturbances are allowed, so the reference level and the control level are equal.
To get permission for a building project, the groundwater level outside of the excavation
site can not deviate from the control level.
The ﬁnished building must also be able to support the water level (as explained later on)
and therefore, a design water level is set according to the Eurocode 7. The deﬁnitions to
design the water level are Serviceability Limit State (SLS), Ultimate Limit State (ULS) and
Accidental Limit State (ALS). The SLS is the state at which the structure can still operate

2.5.5
Groundwater control
The challenges described above can be crucial for a building project as they can cost time
and money. Furthermore, the legislation on groundwater management needs to be fulﬁlled
and structures must be stable and safe to avoid claims and accidents. Groundwater control
can be seen as two diﬀerent aspects [Preene, 2012]:
• Exclusion techniques
• Dewatering techniques
During the construction phase exclusion techniques are used to prevent groundwater from
seeping into the construction pit. There are many diﬀerent types of exclusion techniques
including displacement barriers, excavated barriers, injection barriers, thermal barriers and
tunnelling methods [Preene, 2012]. The most commonly used methods in Copenhagen are
the displacement barriers that consist of sheet piles or vibrated beam walls and excavated
barriers [Albinus, 2014]. These are physically inserted or constructed in the ground against
the walls of the construction pit, usually to the depth of a conﬁning layer to create an
almost waterproof site. If necessary, exclusion can also be used on the pit ﬂoor to limit
inﬁltration of groundwater in cracks or along the wall barriers. In some cases, ﬁll material
can be injected at the bottom of the walls to eliminate this inﬁltration.
Dewatering techniques lower the groundwater level by pumping or by local drainage
systems. The choice of method depends on factors such as the extent of the drained
area, the nature of the conﬁnement, the distance of lowering required and the surrounding
geology including adjacent structures. Drainage ditches near the construction pit are
usually used to lower the secondary aquifer to prevent it from inﬁltrating or to take the
pressure oﬀthe vertical barriers. Outside and inside the site, pumps are used to control
the groundwater. The techniques the most frequently used in Copenhagen are deep wells
with submersible electrical pumps, shallow wells with suction pumps or passive bleed wells.
The latter are usually used at the bottom of the site to relieve short-term groundwater
rises (such as during storm surges or heavy rains). They work without electrical power and
are easy to implement. The overﬂowing water can subsequently be pumped away to dry

3.2 Analysis of sea water level
3.2
Analysis of sea water level
To estimate the risk of ﬂooding at building sites, it is important to investigate ﬂuctuations
and the statistical distribution in the sea water level. In this way, the frequency of a certain
high sea level at present time and in the future can be estimated.
3.2.1
Sea level analysis
Mean sea level
The change in mean sea level is, in general, based on the equation 3.1 modiﬁed from
Sørensen and Piontkowitz [2013]. Elements of this equation are familiar, as some of the
same elements are also included in the equation 2.1 for determining the design level of a
building from section 2.5.
∆WLext.,rel = (∆METext.,abs + ∆PHY Sext.,abs) + (SLRabs + ∆GIAabs + ∆LOWabs)
(3.1)
where the ∆WL is the location-speciﬁc relative change in extreme water level, ∆MET is
the meteorological extremes causing temporary sea level rise (rain and wind) , ∆PHY S
represents the physical changes in topography and bathymetry, ∆SLRabs is the change
in mean sea level, ∆GIAabs is the glacio-isostatic adjustment and ∆LOWabs is the local
settlements. The ﬁrst bracket containing the eﬀects of a climatic deterioration and of
physical changes is ignored in this study. The equation is a simpliﬁed explanation of reality,
including the most important features of extreme water levels only.
The absolute sea level was previously found to increase by 0.70 m in Copenhagen by
2100. The uplift of the land was found to be in the order of 0.09 m (1.0mm/year · 85years)
by the end of the century and the local settlements are so low in Copenhagen that they
can be neglected.
Extreme sea levels
Statistical calculations are performed using the X3M-program for S-Plus version 7 developed
by Insightful in 2005. The software provides robust estimates of the distribution parameters
and associated statistical test but has, however, limitations in the number of distributions
that may be applied.
Detrending
All extreme water levels have been made trend-free by removing the eﬀect
of the relative increase in mean sea level. In this way the extremes are evaluated as the
excursion from the mean in the year of occurrence. As the tide gauge measured the relative
change in mean water with contributions from an eustatic sea level rise and glacio-isostatic
uplift, the diﬀerence between the former reference system DNN and DVR90 has been
used (DVR90 - DNN = 7 cm/100 years). It is thus assumed that the mean sea level
has increased linearly from 1890 to 2015. Relating current data in DVR90 thus means a
reduction in these trend-free water levels from DVR90 as the sea level in the intervening
period (1990-2014) has risen more than compensated for by the local glacio-isostatic adjust-
ment. For a water level in 2015, 2 cm must thus be added (0.7mm/year·25years = 1.75cm).
As described earlier in section 2.1, the data preferably must be detrended also on a
seasonal, yearly and decadal basis, but as time series are not available for the entire period,

125 cm) and the corresponding standard deviations. To see the variation in robustness
with other recurrence intervals, the simulation was also run with 50 year and 500 year
events. Quantile plots were also examined to ﬁnd the best ﬁt and a selected range of
these can be found in appendix 7.2. The simulation was run in X3M with ﬁve diﬀerent
distributions (exponential, Gumbel, Frechet, Weibull and log-normal) to be evaluated on
these parameters. As a range of truncation values was tested, the change in 100 year water
levels and standard deviation could be observed. The truncation value was chosen at a
value, where neither of the two evaluation parameters diﬀered a lot from the previous
simulations to choose the most robust one.
The extremes have, as previously mentioned, been chosen with a minimum of 36 hours
between events where the water level in-between two consecutive events must be normalized.
It can be argued that a limit in excess of 36 hours is necessary to separate events based on
meteorological conditions and their eﬀects on the water level. Within this project it has
not been possible to revisit data preceding digital recordings to verify actual independence
between occurrences.
Parameter optimisation
The statistical analysis program X3M uses, according to the
recommendation of Arns et al. [2013], an MLE to estimate the location parameters [X3M].
The amount of estimated parameters varies in-between distributions from one to three
[X3M] and the standard variations on each of them are given as well.
Goodness of ﬁt
To evaluate the goodness of ﬁt for the extreme water levels, two dif-
ferent methods were used in accordance with DHI [2008], the Chi-squared test and the
Kolmogorov-Smirnov test. The Chi-squared test (χ2) makes a comparison of the number
of events and the expected amount of events and tests the hypothesis that the extreme
water levels are distributed similarly to a certain distribution. During the test, the extreme
values are divided into classes of at least 5 values each. Therefore, the test could not be
carried out when the truncation level was so high that few water levels came beyond it.
The equation is shown in appendix 7.3.
The Kolmogorov-Smirnov test ﬁnds the deviation between the empirical and theoreti-
cal distribution to see if it falls below a critical value. In this case, the distribution is
accepted if the diﬀerence between the two distributions fall below 10 %. All the distribu-
tions with a truncation level of 1.17 m were accepted in this test. The equation for the
test can also be found in appendix 7.3

Uncertainty on data
When handling data, the uncertainty is always an important analysis to carry out. The
sources of uncertainty concerning the sea level data were found to be the following:
Measuring devices
There are two main uncertainties on the collected data on water
levels, both in the groundwater and the sea. Firstly, the accuracy of the measuring equip-
ment should be considered. The water levels are measured with pressure sensors, which are
permanently submerged under the water level. The measured pressure is then related to a
datum and transformed into water depth. According to DMI, the sea level was measured
with a ﬂoat until the early 2000s, where a pressure sensor was installed in stead. The ﬂoat
is assumed to have an uncertainty of 1-2 cm, while the pressure sensor has an uncertainty
associated to the measurement and to the conversion calculation from pressure to water
level adding up to an uncertainty of 2-4 cm [DMI]. The sea levels measured by COWI with
pressure transducers have been validated occasionally with manual dip meters and have
also been corrected regularly to comply with the data from DMI due to faults in their
own data loggers. The total uncertainty on these data is estimated at 1 - 2 cm [Berger, 2015].
Sea level measures may also be aﬀected or defected during use. During winter, frost
may cause the sensors to get stuck and the installation can be damaged. This may intro-
duce errors in data.
Secondly, the set-up of a sensor/tide gauge may lead to uncertainties. The sea level
loggers of COWI are attached to the embankment. This may shift due to consolidation and
therefore aﬀect the measured water level. Extreme water level may not always be registered
eﬀectively either, e.g. when water levels above a certain threshold lead to inland ﬂooding
and higher extreme water levels may occur "behind" the measuring station due to set-up
eﬀects. More extreme water levels are experienced than measured by the device. Finally,
as the tide gauges measure a 10 minutes mean value (of a still water level) the actual
water level experienced may well be considerably higher due to waves and seiching. This is
mainly an issue when dealing with coastal ﬂooding, whereas in relation to groundwater,
this is not expected to be signiﬁcant.
Change of location
The switch between the diﬀerent measuring stations causes the sea
level data to have some uncertainty. It is however assumed that the mean sea level does
not diﬀer within the Copenhagen Harbour. The switch of DMI’s measuring stations was
investigated by COWI by logging at station 30336 and 30337 in parallel during July and
August. The mean sea level was then calculated for the period of 01-07-1999 to 05-09-1999
for both measuring stations, and the result showed that the new location measured 0.61 mm
lower than the previous one (mean of 119.41 mm opposed to 120.02 mm). This uncertainty
is smaller than the measuring uncertainties, which were informed by DMI, and therefore it
is conﬁrmed that the change of measuring station does not aﬀect the mean sea water level.
However, the location is very important for extreme sea levels. As mentioned in section
2.1, the sea levels can change a lot within coastal areas during storms.
Measuring frequency
The sea level can change quickly and therefore a high frequency
is necessary to capture the extreme events. The sea water levels are measured by COWI
every hour, while DMI measures every 10 minutes, giving a better image of the water level.
However, even within 10 minutes, the water level can change several centimetres and this

leads to an uncertainty. Furthermore, historical water levels may have an uncertainty, as it
is not necessarily noted by which frequency the water was measured.
Historical data
In additional to the unknown frequency of measurements, the historical
water levels also hold a substantial uncertainty. Some extreme levels may have been
detected by records of ﬂooding of certain buildings of areas, which were then estimated
into an extreme water level. This method may also contain a location uncertainty as the
extreme water level as mentioned above.
Detrending water levels
When detrending sea levels, it is assumed that the develop-
ment in mean sea level has been linear the last 125.4 years. This is not entirely correct, as
the mean varies over seasons, years and even decades. For the recorded sea water level, the
variation in mean has been determined for each of these cases. A table of the values and a
graph of the mean seasonal sea levels can be found in appendix 7.4. The assumption of
linearity is a rough estimate, which simpliﬁes the process.
Choice of distribution
As mentioned above, there are several options for choosing a
distribution for the measured data. It should be noted that the best ﬁt can vary according
to location (the Danish west coast as opposed to Copenhagen) due to weather patterns.
If this exercise were to be made in another location, it should be reexamined, which
distribution had the best ﬁt.

3.3.4
Correlating data
To ﬁnd a quantitative measure of how good the hydraulic contact is, a correlation analysis
was carried out on the seawater level and the groundwater level in the most coastal well.
A delay of the wave is expected inland due to the storativity and transmissivity acting
over a distance. It was attempted to ﬁnd this delay by comparing the time of a peak in
the sea level data and the peak in groundwater data in the coastal well. This proved to be
impossible for two reasons. Firstly, the recent groundwater measurements were carried
out with a one hour frequency, giving retardation times of either 0 or 1 hours. Therefore,
older data from 2005 were used where the frequency or measurement was 15 minutes. This
led to the discovery of the second problem. The retardation seemed to vary over time,
indicating that the timer on the data loggers were not consistent. Some examples of these
investigations can be found in appendix 7.6. For the correlation analysis, a delay of less than
an hour was assumed between the sea and the coastal wells. As the groundwater data was
collected hourly, this delay was not considered in the correlation analysis. The groundwater
levels within the wells inland were also correlated to see how good the aquifers are connected.

the data sets are sampled at diﬀerent frequencies and slightly diﬀerent times, a pairing of
the two data sets was needed. To achieve this, a simple program was created in Matlab
to pair a data point from each data set at corresponding times within ± 5 minutes. The
script for this program can be found in appendix 7.7.

correlation between two data sets [Legates and McCabe, 1999].

people might classify soil samples diﬀerently.

4.3.1
Simple model and sensitivity analysis
The analytical groundwater model was ﬁrst tested to be sure that both single event storms
and multiple events were modelled properly. As the model has two variables, it can be
illustrated with a constant distance, x0, and a variable time or the opposite. Metroselskabet
[2009] reports that the storativity in the Copenhagen subsoil varies between 0.8 −6.5 · 10−4
for the conﬁned aquifer, while the transmissivity lies within 0.25 −2.5 · 10−3m2/s with
occurrences of transmissivities up to 5 · 10−3m2/s at the Marble Church. To get an initial
guess of the geological parameters for the analytical model, the average of the two were
used to simulate the groundwater response. The average value of T = 1.375 · 10−3m2/s ﬁts
well with investigation made in the area of Copenhagen, where the transmissivity in the
limestone was found to be between 1.0 −2.5 · 10−3m2/s [Frederiksen et al., 2002].
When estimating parameters for a model it is important to know how sensitive these
values are, i.e. how much a variation in value aﬀects the result. Therefore a sensitivity
analysis was carried out on the model simulating a single surge event, where the sea level
rises 1 m during 1 h.
The analysis was carried out in two parts for each parameter. First, the water level
40 m inland was modelled (to correspond with the location of the coastal well in the Marble
Church transect) with the minimum, mean and maximum S and T values respectively and

Chapter 5
Discussion
In this chapter, the results and challenges of the report will be discussed.
Setting return rates for water levels
The data set used for the sea water level was tricky. A complete water level data set was
not available further back than 1992, which could have been useful in order to systematise
the selection of extreme water levels. As mentioned previously, lists of extreme water level
from the DCA and the DMI have been compared. They do comply with each other most
of the time, but at times there are discrepancies between the reference level of the water
level, the frequency of measurements and whether extreme water levels are based on a
cut-oﬀlevel or a ﬁxed amount of months per year. As the meta-data from these lists do
not exist, it is hard to verify any of them. As a way to facilitate the statistical process of
this increasingly important subject, publication of all the available water levels could be
implemented.
To predict the water level of a 100 year storm, the Weibull distribution was used with a
cut-oﬀlevel of 1.17 m. As explained previously, there are many diﬀerent distributions to
use and the most optimal cut-oﬀlevel can diﬀer within them. In this case, the exponential,
Gumbel, Frechet, Weibull or log-normal distribution will give 100 year water levels between
1.49 m and 1.62 m in year 2015 when ranging the truncation level between 1.0 and 1.25 m.
This shows a substantial deviation and therefore further analysis should be made on the
method of deﬁning the truncation level and the correct distribution.
The most appropriate distribution should be determined on the basis of visual com-
parison between empirical and theoretical distribution and by comparing the goodness
of ﬁt with the ξ2 test or the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test [Arns et al., 2013]. Ideally, two
distributions, which were not considered in this report should be applied, the Generalised
Extreme Value (GEV) distribution and the General Pareto Distribution (GPD). It has
been suggested by Arns et al. [2013] that the GEV is the best approximation for the block
maxima method, while the GDP considered the best ﬁt for the POT method, used in this
report. The GPD is not available in the used program and therefore it was not simulated,
but it is recommendable to do this as a veriﬁcation in the future.
As for the truncation level, an equation has been suggested by Arns et al. [2013], which
could be used if the data set was complete. In both cases it would be beneﬁcial to set
a standard method and truncation level for a certain location, so that results could be
59
==PAGE:67==
compared directly between institutions and companies.
It was shortly mentioned how the latest extreme high sea event "Bodil" modiﬁed the
statistics for future extreme events. It should be noted that as more frequent and extreme
storms are predicted in the future, the statistics will also have to be revised along the
way. "Bodil" decreased the slope of the water level recurrence interval graph [COWI, 2014],
and if future extreme storms follow the same trend, water levels in the far future may be
overestimated.
The duration of a high sea event is brieﬂy investigated in this report, but it is in fact an
important factor concerning not only groundwater levels, but also functionality of sewer
systems and drains. This topic could be further analysed in the future.
Comparing return rates
The DCA have carried out a statistical analysis of extreme water levels in 2012, where
a site-speciﬁc result for Copenhagen was given [Kystdirektoratet, 2015]. It this analysis,
slightly fewer years (123.9 years) of data were included in the analysis, not including
"Bodil". The truncation level used in these calculations was 1.19 m. The results of this
analysis gave a 100 year event of 1.50 m (±9cm) and a 50 year event of 141 cm (±6cm) .
These values are slightly lower than the ones found in this report. As mentioned previ-
ously, "Bodil" has increased the slope of the distribution function due to it’s extreme nature.
In the report about the signiﬁcance of "Bodil" [COWI, 2014], a 100 year event was
estimated at 1.56 m, which corresponds very well to the result in this thesis.
Conﬁnement of primary aquifer
In the course of this report, it has been assumed that the primary aquifer below Copenhagen
is conﬁned. However, there seems to be quite a good connection between the upper limestone
layer and the sand or gravel layers above. As mentioned in section 2.2, there is a correlation
between the higher and lower aquifer in all the examined wells, which indicates that the
conﬁnement is leaky or partial. The geological cross sections shown in ﬁgure 4.5 and 4.6
conﬁrm this by illustrating that the clay layer is discontinuous at times. The assumption
that the aquifer is entirely conﬁned simpliﬁes things signiﬁcantly, but it is not conservative,
as completely conﬁned aquifer will respond less than an unconﬁned one due to expansion.
The investigation from [COWI and Arup Systra, 2008] shows that the aquifer behaves
conﬁned with signs of semi-conﬁnement or leakage, which corresponds well with the results
of this report.
Hydraulic contact between the sea and the primary aquifer
As mentioned in the report, ﬁnding the delay of the groundwater wave from the coast
to the coastal well was not possible and the correlations were carried out, assuming that
there was no delay (or less than the measuring frequency). Theoretically, there must be a
delay, and therefore this assumption is wrong. However, if this was ﬁxed by pushing the
groundwater level one hour ahead, it is to expected that the correlations would be a bit
higher. The trend is not expected to change from this correction. The same goes for the
correlations in between wells inland.

==PAGE:68==
While investigating the eﬀect of a sea level rise on the groundwater table in the vicinity of
the coast, an attempt for obtaining a standardised method was made (explained in section
3.3). However, it is still diﬃcult to deﬁne the degree of response between the sea and the
aquifer without letting other processes like rain or human activities blur the image. In
section 4.3, ﬁgure 4.17b, all the increased water levels have been made comparable by
deﬁning the sea level rise as 100 %. This image shows a trend, but there seems to be a
high variation on the % of increased water level reaching the coastal well (48 - 78 %). The
result could be improved by expanding the period of analysis and including more high sea
events. However, there might always be disturbances in the groundwater level that are
caused by other factors than the sea level.
Evaluating data
It must be noted that the analysis of the increased water levels has a degree of uncertainty
due to subjectivity. It was attempted to standardise the method as described in section
2.3, but still, some subjectivity remained. The challenge was to separate the eﬀect of the
increased sea level and the heavy rainfall on the groundwater level. The way it was carried
out, by including only events that had high sea level increases and very little rain or vice
versa, limits the amount of storms to include. This narrows down the data basis and
thereby increases the uncertainty.
For determination of correlations as well, the period of comparison was chosen subjectively
and as mentioned, the values can vary over time.
Using the analytical model
The analytical model assumes a homogeneous soil, a conﬁned aquifer and an inﬁnite
distance to a hydraulic boundary. All of these assumptions are oversimpliﬁcations of the
Copenhagen subsoil. The model proved to be useful within the ﬁrst 30 - 40 m, and at this
distance the homogeneity and the conﬁnement may be applicable, but it should be taken
into account, that if it is used on further distances, variations may occur. However, the
water table under Copenhagen is so disrupted by urban water management that it is not
considered to be a problem.
The model was well calibrated to the Marble Church transect, where the simulations
of the storms in the fall of 2013 has a good ﬁt with the model and a low RMSE. At this
location, the limestone lies very highly in the subsoil and therefore has a good hydraulic
connection to the sea. The storativity was calibrated to 96.2 · 10−4, which lies within the
range given in technical reports [COWI and Arup Systra, 2008] of 12 −400 · 10−4 towards
the end of the pumping tests. As described in section 2.2, this behaviour could origin from
the double porosity of the soil.
The Kgs. Nytorv transect showed some problems with the calibration, probably due
to the small variations in groundwater table compared with the seawater table, which was
quantiﬁed in section 4.2. The visual calibration gave an even higher S value than in the
previous transect of 475.2 · 10−4. The reason for the high S value could be that both of the
calibrations have been run with the same T value of 1.375·10−3m2/s. However, the previous
data indicates that the transmissivity is slightly lower towards the south of the centre of
61
==PAGE:69==
Copenhagen. This could be adjusted with a more detailed study of the transmissivity values.
As a common feature of both of the calibrations is that as mentioned before soil is
assumed to be homogeneous. This means that geological features; more impermeable layers
in the limestone or clay layers blocking the hydraulic contact between the sea and the
groundwater are included in the general evaluation. Layers of low hydraulic conductivity
would result in lower T values and should be taken into consideration when evaluating the
analytical solution.
Concerning the assumption of a conﬁned aquifer, the model is not entirely accurate.
If the aquifer has unconﬁned areas, the transmissivity would be substituted by K · b, and b
would vary with time (as the water table ﬂuctuates). This inaccuracy may be modelled in
a numerical model, where b could be found at each time step in the simulation.
The analytical model showed that the eﬀect of a 100 year storm surge can eﬀect the
groundwater up to 1,000 m inland and last for days. However, when compared to the data
from Copenhagen, the signal was unrecognisable already a few hundred meters in. This
was assigned to the disturbances of the groundwater level beneath Copenhagen due to
water management. However, it could be highly interesting to test the model in a disturbed
area, where the groundwater level could be measured inland.
Equation 2.1 was presented in section 2.5 as a way of determining the design water
level of a building. The water levels found inland by the model can be used as the expres-
sion of the groundwater level increase due to extreme sea levels (XDV R90T ) of a T year
recurrence interval. The fact that the groundwater level increase due to heavy rain events
is not included in the equation is interesting as the results of the groundwater analysis
showed that there can be a big inﬂuence. The non-inclusion of this parameter could be due
to the assumption that all precipitation is collected by urban drainage systems, However,
more extreme rain fall events are expected in the future and thereby a risk of failure of the
sewers. The combination of this and the probable implementation of more reinﬁltration
installations could mean that this inﬂuence on the groundwater should be taken into
account.
Improving the model
The model was for this purpose developed analytically for easy and fast implementation. If
a more precise result was required including inhomogeneities in the soil, a numerical model
could be created. This would also be able to take point sources and sinks of water (like
abstraction and reinﬁltration) into account and thereby customize the model to the area in
question. By using the numerical model, the eﬀect of storm surges could be found inland,
but it would also be possible to model the impact of the long term mean sea level increase
inland. In the future, this model could be combined with holistic models, such as Jeppesen
et al. [2011] to predict and understand the dynamics of the complex groundwater system.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
Due to the climate changes, mean sea levels are predicted to increase, and extreme sea
levels are expected to increase in magnitude and frequency. The increased extreme events
will cause more ﬂooding in coastal cities due to waves exceeding the existing harbour
structures and to short-term increased groundwater levels close to the coast. A higher mean
sea level will amplify the extreme sea events in the future and will eﬀect the groundwater
level inland. The increased groundwater level may aﬀect building sites close to the coast
and result in soil failure or ﬂooding of excavation sites.
This thesis set out to investigate in collaboration with COWI, the connection between
extreme sea level events and the groundwater level. The purpose was to develop a tool, in
form of an analytical model, which could help determine design water levels for dimension-
ing excavation sites and ﬁnished buildings and structures. The exercises were carried out
for the Copenhagen area, where bore hole logs and recent water level logs for groundwater
and sea were made available by COWI and DMI.
The ﬂuctuations of sea levels were statistically analysed by ﬁnding extreme events and
ﬁtting several distributions to them. The Weibull distribution was chosen due to standard
practices of COWI and the DCA and a truncation value of 1.17 m was found to be the
most robust. Using this distribution, a 100 year event is estimated to be 1.57 m (±4.7 cm)
and a 500 year event is estimated to be 1.79 m (±16.2 cm). Expressed in another way, a
water level of 1.57 m has a probability of 1 % of happening during one year while the 1.79
m will be exceeded with the probability of 0.2 %.
The mean sea level was estimated to increase by 60 cm within the year 2100 compared to
current levels. It was found that a 100 year event of the present day will be regarded as a
yearly event in 2100 and other extreme water levels will be more frequent accordingly. An
analysis of the duration of extreme water levels historically and during the recent 100 year
event "Bodil" shows that while a water level of 1 m may last for 19 hours now, the same
water level may last for up to three days in year 2100.
To analyse the behaviour of the groundwater under Copenhagen, two transects going
from the harbour and inland were drawn and named the Marble Church transect and the
Kgs. Nytorv transect. Literature about the local underground was studied and geological
cross sections were drawn. It was determined that the primary aquifer lies in the upper
limestone layer and is conﬁned with occasional discontinuities in the conﬁned layer or
inﬁltration through it. The Marble Church transect has a high laying limestone layer
63
==PAGE:71==
towards the coast, which indicates a a good hydraulic connection to the sea. The Kgs.
Nytorv transect does not have the same feature and there may be less hydraulic connection
between the two water bodies here.
The correlation between sea level and the primary aquifer proved to be good with R2 values
of 0.8 and 0.7 at the coastal wells of the Marble Church and the Kgs. Nytorv transect
respectively during two chosen periods of comparison including extreme sea events. Data
showed a higher hydraulic contact between the sea level and groundwater level at the
Marble Church transect than at the Kgs. Nytorv transect in accordance with the geological
knowledge. Correlations carried out between the upper and lower aquifers of all wells
indicated a conﬁnement or semi conﬁnement as concluded in literature and from geological
cross sections. The correlation analysis in the primary aquifer between the inland wells
indicated dis-connectivity in the aquifer around Amalienborg and south west of Kgs Nytorv.
The water level in every well along the two transects was plotted with the sea level
and daily precipitation. As both rain and sea levels aﬀect the groundwater level, the two
processes were separated in order to ﬁnd the impact of each of them. It was shown that
the sea level is the dominant cause of increase of the groundwater level within a few 100
meters from the coast. The eﬀect of sea level changes was larger at the Marble Church
transect than at the Kgs. Nytorv transect. Most rain events did not aﬀect the groundwater
table, but one extreme event of 135 mm/day had a major impact inland (up to 0.7 m
increase), which indicates that the sewer systems could not handle the incoming waste water.
An analytical solution to the groundwater ﬂow equation was found and implemented
in a Matlab program. The model is able to model the groundwater response of sea level
changes over time and distance from the coast. Single events of sea level increase can be
modelled as well as a series of sea levels from measured data.
A sensitivity analysis was applied on the analytical model with average geological pa-
rameters from literature. The storativity and transmissivity were evaluated on two outputs
from the model: the peak height of the groundwater wave and the duration of the increased
groundwater level. The results showed that the two parameters are equally sensitive (as
they are proportional in the governing equation), but have opposite eﬀects on the result.
An increase of the storativity by 10 % results in a decrease of 1 cm in peak height and 1.5
minutes increase of the wave duration, while an increase of the transmissivity value results
in 1 cm increase a 1.5 minutes decrease respectively.
By inserting a time series of a month to the model with the default geological parameters,
it was demonstrated how the signal from ﬂuctuating sea levels could be traced up to 1480
m inland. It was also noted that combined high or low sea events gave larger responses
in the groundwater level, while the duration of the events also had an impact. When the
model was compared with measured data inland, it was concluded that in Copenhagen,
the groundwater was disturbed by pumping and drainage, so that the sea level pattern
was unrecognisable a few 100 meters from the coast.
Finally, the model was calibrated on the storativity parameter to each cross section
while keeping the transmissivity at an averaged 1.375 · 10−3s/m2, and the storativity was
found to be 96.2 · 10−4 for the Marble Church transect and 475.2 · 10−4 for the Kgs. Nytorv
transect. The calibrated model showed that a 100 year storm of 1.57 m would result in
64
==PAGE:72==
a 0.88 m groundwater increase 50 m from the coast at the Marble Church transect and
a 0.37 m increase on the Kgs. Nytorv transect. The model conﬁrmed that the response
would be attenuated quickly so that only about 10 % of the wave would be registered
200 m from the coast at the Marble Church transect and 100 m at the Kgs. Nytorv transect.
As a ﬁnal remark, the model was used in an example of an excavation pit to deﬁne
the thickness of the conﬁning clay bed that would be required to resist a 100 year storm
and to estimate the probability of soil failure at a given bed thickness. A graph was made
illustrating the necessary bed thickness needed to withstand sea levels ranging from a
yearly to a 500 year event.
To conclude the report, the analytical model is a good and simple tool that can estimate the
groundwater increase due to extreme sea level events (XDV R90T ) when determining the
design water level for an excavation pit and for the dimensioning of a building. Furthermore,
it may be interesting to evaluate the eﬀect of increasing rain fall on the groundwater level
to include this in the future.
