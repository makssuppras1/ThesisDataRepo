Abstract
This study examines the use of 2D optical aerial images and 3D LIDAR point clouds to classify
coniferous and deciduous trees using deep learning algorithms for the purpose of automating the
detection of trees that grow too close to power lines. The data survey was performed by Niras for
the N1 energy company.
Trees are detected and segmented in the point cloud data using a morphological filter method from
the lidR R package and labeled as coniferous or deciduous trees using the intensity values from the
LIDAR data to differentiate the two classes. Evaluating on a manually labeled dataset of 5400 trees
show a 88.12% labeling accuracy. Trees are detected in the 2D data using a pre trained neural
network approach and labeled using the labeled point clouds as reference.
Point cloud classification models are trained using a PointNet Python implementation, while 2D
tree crown classification models are trained using our own convolutional neural network. I achieve
a prediction accuracy of 78% using PointNet and 72.5% using the convolutional neural network.
Combining the outputs from both networks in a linear combination of outputs, I achieve a higher
prediction accuracy than either PointNet or the convolutional neural network is able to produce.
The experiments demonstrate the feasibility of using a combination of 2D and 3D data for tree
classification purposes, while the lack of properly labeled data limits the performance of the trained
models.

Visualization of tree segmentation. Describing classifying points to tree 1: Point A,
having highest elevation, is chosen as the seed point of the tree. Point B, having the
second highest elevation, is chosen as the first point to segment. Horizontal distance
from A to B is calculated, and as the disctance is above the pre defined threshold,
B is not part of tree 1. Next point C, the distance to both A and B is calculated.

the distance to B and C is calculated, since C is the closet point in tree 1. Here, the
distance to B is smallest, so D is not classified as part of tree 1. This process continues
for all points untill the tree is fully segmented, after which the tree is removed from
the segmentation and the next iterative step starts. source: Li et al: A New Method

Chapter 1
Introduction
Powerlines are an everpresent sight when driving through Denmark. They cover the landscape and
have to continuously be kept in good condition to ensure power access to all citizens. The temperate
danish climate gives ample conditions for trees of various species to grow in the Danish countryside.
Since power lines are erected at a certain distance from buildings and other human made objects,
trees are typically a common sight along powerlines. These trees can pose a threat to the power
lines, either from growing into the power lines themselves or from the damage they would inflict, if
they fell on the power lines during a storm. Due to this, Sikkerhedsstyrelsen had defined a ”nearby
zone” around the power lines, with trees inside this zone being classified as a potential treat[6] to
the power lines. The rules specify that trees must be pruned or cut down to ensure that they do not
reach the nearby zone (see figure 1.1) within three years of pruning.
Figure 1.1
Example of ”nearby zone” from Sikkerhedsstyrelsen
Traditionally, determining when to prune trees along the various powerlines has been done by workers
periodically driving around Denmark and noting which trees need to be pruned. This process is slow,
inefficient and expensive. In order to develop better techniques for monitoring tree growth along
power lines, Niras has used data collected from aircrafts in the form of 2D optical images and 3D
Lidar point clouds to determine the position and height of trees in tracks along the powerlines, as
well as marking the trees that fall within the nearby zone.
This covers only part of the problem, as it is also important to mark which trees are likely to reach
the nearby zone during the three years after the survey has been conducted. Using tree growth
6

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
models[7][8], it is possible to map the growth of trees from their size. These models are dependent
on the individual tree species. It is therefore of interest to map the species of the trees growing
along the powerlines for two reasons: Firstly, the tree species can be combined with growth models
to predict when each individual tree will reach the nearby zone. Secondly, when trees are growing
on private property, the owner of the tree is compensated finantially for each pruned tree. The size
of the compensation is dependent on the type of tree, making it possible to estimate the total cost
of compensations when pruning along a powerline track.
For this thesis project, I will attempt to classify tree types using the data collected by Niras using
various neural networks for machine learning classification.
Trees are divided into two categories, coniferous and deciduous trees. Both categories can be divided
further into subcategories depending on the growth rate of the trees. This project will attempt to
seperate trees into the two large categories, as further subcategorizing the trees is beyond the scope
of the project timeline.
As individual trees in the data are not segmented or labeled with either the coniferous or deciduous
tree class, part of the project will explore how trees can be detected in the two data types as well as
effective labeling techniques.
As two different data sources are available, in this project I will also explore the possibility for tree
classification using the two data sources separately and then jointly in an effort to determine the
strengths and weaknesses of using either data source, as well as whether combining the two data
types improves classification results.
In general, this project will focus on answering the following research questions.
How well can I segment and label trees in the two classes in the 2D and 3D data?
How well do neural networks perform when classifying trees from the 2D and 3D data
and what are their limitations?
How can the two data types be combined to form a single prediction output and how
does this combined method perform compared to using each data source individually?

2.1
Classification and Neural Networks
In machine learning, classification refers to the task of assigning data to classes based on learned
features.
A feature refers to a property of the data set.
The models use the features to make
predictions for the data.
Classification is a very common use case of machine learning due to the large number of use cases in
fields such as navigation, medicine, and security to name a few. Classification methods vary greatly
in their complexity, ease of implementation, and performance. In recent years, as the availability
of fast computational machines has increased rapidly, neural networks have emerged as the most
versatile tool for classification problems[?].
A neural network is a system of nodes connected by weights. The nodes, called neurons, are divided
into several layers, starting with an input layer where the features of the input data is inserted,
followed by a number of hidden layers and ending in an output layer.
As data is fed into the
network, each neuron in the hidden layers can activate based on a linear combination of inputs from
the previous layer. The following equation is an example of the linear combination of the first hidden
layer:

2.2
Convolutional Neural Networks
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) are a subcategory of neural networks optimized for 2D image
applications.
They are designed to work on matrices to extract features, and are thus ideal for
working with images. CNNs consist of a network of several different types of layers for extracting
features of the input image.
2.2.1
Convolution layer
Convolution layers perform the convolution operation by moving a kernal of a predefined size over
the image and in the process extracts features from the previous layer. The kernel size is defined
when building the network, but the weights of the kernel are learned when training the network. The
depth (number of channels) in the kernel is the same as the number of features in the input layer.
For an RGB image, the number of features is 3, but as the network expands through the layers, the
number of features increases.

With the convolution layers, the network learns by analyzing the pixel values themselves, in addition
to their neighbors. For classification purposes, the kernel weights are learned through training the
network; however, specific kernels can be implemented if certain features are desirable, an example
being edge detection. The kernel size is generally kept small, 3 × 3 and 5 × 5 kernels are common.
Increasing kernel size allows more information to be considered for each feature, but does increase
computation times.
As CNNs are already expensive in computational power and memory, it is
usually best practice to keep the kernel size small. Other parameters that one can consider when
building the network are the number of pixels the kernel moves each step (stride) and padding. A
low stride will retain more features, while a higher stride will decrease the computational time at the
cost of the loss of information. Padding refers to adding a number of uniformly valued pixels to the
borders of the image in order to retain image dimensions between layers and preserve information
at the borders of the image. As seen in figure 2.2, convolution without padding will result in a
reduction of the image size.
2.2.2
Activation layer
Just as with the traditional neural network described earlier, an activation function (here, activation
layer) is used to create threshold values for passing features to the next layer. In the traditional
neural network, this takes the form of a weighted sum of the outputs from the previous layer, while for
the CNN, this takes the form of a convolution operation. This is a one to one mapping of activation
values, and therefore the activation layer does not cause the input feature map to change dimensions.
Although several different activation functions exist, Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU), see figure 2.3,
has widely been adopted as the standard activation function due to both speed and accuracy. Most
CNNs will contain convolution layers followed by a ReLU layer, which is often not even shown in
network diagrams.

2.2.3
Pooling
Intermitting the convolution layers are often pooling layers. Their main function is to reduce the
size of the feature map in order to preserve memory and prevent overfitting by reducing the number
of parameters. Overfitting occurs when the neural network learns the data too well. The longer
the network trains, the better it will be able to fit the training data, but when applied to data the
network has not been trained on (the validation data), if it performs worse with additional training,
the network is overfitting the data.
Unlike the convolution layer, pooling is applied to each feature map individually and will not change
the depth of the map. Pooling is performed with a sliding window similar to the convolution kernel.
The size and operation of the window is predetermined when building the network and is not a
trainable parameter. Several pooling operations exist, with max pooling being the most common.
Here, the maximum feature value in the window is retained, so a larger window will retain fewer
features.
2.2.4
Fully Connected Network
The output of the convolution layers is a large number of features, somewhat reduced with pooling.
These are then run through a fully connected neural network to compute the final classification out-
put. This can take the form of one or several layers depending on the complexity of the classification
problem. In general, not only for the fully connected layer but then entire CNN, a deeper network
will not perform better on a simple problem than a simpler network.

4.1
Point Cloud Segmentation
The following describes the steps taken for segmenting the point clouds into individual trees. A
flowchart of the full process is shown in figure 4.3.
4.1.1
Ground Segmentation
The point cloud data consist of returns from the whole terrain along the track, including trees,
ground, buildings, power lines, etc. The first step after data cleaning is segmenting trees from non-
trees as much as possible. The majority of the points to be filtered are ground points, while other
objects in the landscape only account for a very small fraction of the overall points. Therefore, I
begin by filtering only the ground points. The algorithm used is called Progressive Morphological
Filter[10], a point cloud-based implementation of the raster-based method described in Zhang et
al.[2]. The algorithm is integrated, among others, in the lidR package[11]. It segments ground from
non-ground by running a morphological filter over the entire dataset, which are used for extracting
features from 2D images. It consists of two fundamental operations, dilation for enlarging features
and erosion for reducing the size of features. Morphological filters have typically been used to extract
features from grayscale images, but they can easily be used on 3D data when extracting features
from varying elevation. As the elevation changes in the data caused by non-ground objects will
change quite rapidly compared to the ground, a morphological filter that combines dilation and
erosion is able to separate ground from non-ground. Erosion will segment trees and other objects
smaller than the window size while dilation will restore ground objects larger than the window size,
see figure 4.1. The choice of window size is therefore important to get good results. For this project,
a single unchanging window of size 5 m and a threshold value of 3 m has been used, as this covers
the expected sizes of the trees in the study. It is possible to apply several windows of varying sizes
to the data. This would allow one to segment small non-ground objects while preserving smaller
ground features. However, since the terrain in this survey is quite flat and the spatial resolution is
very high, a window was deemed sufficient.

4.1.2
Individual Tree Segmentation
After ground segmentation, I should be left with only trees and a few additional objects. These are
only a few compared to the trees and should not pose any problems. The next step is the individual
tree segmentation, where I attempt to assign each point a point ID corresponding to a unique tree.
Identifying tree locations is a simple matter of finding local maxima. Assigning each point to a tree
is a much more complicated problem, as the trees I observe vary in size, shape, and density, so while
each point could be assigned to the closest tree, a more complicated algorithm is needed to account
for clusters of varying tree shapes and sizes.
Several individual tree segmentation algorithms have been developed, as they are useful for forest
management[12], biomass monitoring[13] and understanding the global carbon cycle[14]. A common
approach is raster-based algorithms that construct a canopy height model using LIDAR data, then
perform a local maxima analysis to detect individual trees[15][16]. These algorithms do introduce
uncertainties when interpolating during the convertion from point cloud data to the canopy model[3],
and for this reason I have chosen an algorithm that works directly on point cloud data. Li et al.[3]
introduce such an algorithm that I use for individual tree segmentation in this project. The algorithm
takes advantage of the spacing between trees: Although trees likely intersect close to the ground, it
can be assumed that there will be some spacing at the top of the trees.
Each tree is individually segmented in an iterative process. In each iteration, the point with the
highest elevation within a search radius (mean tree radius) is chosen as a tree top. Points are then
classified sequentially from highest elevation to lowest elevation using the following scheme (see figure
4.2 for visualization): The minimum distance to the closest point classified as the tree (seed point
A) dmin1 and the minimum distance to the closest point classified as not in the tree dmin2 are both
calculated. Then, if the point is closest to the tree and the dmin1 is below the set threshold, the
point is added to the tree. Otherwise, the point must be part of another tree. This scheme allows
trees to have varying diameters and density. When all points have been classified as either part of
the tree or not part of the tree, the tree is removed from the point cloud and the process is repeated
until all points are classified. Additional classification rules can be added as well, though for this
project, only the described rules have been used. Some examples of the segmented trees are shown
in figures 4.4 and 4.5.

4.2
Data Labeling
After each tree has been segmented, the next step is to label the trees as coniferous or deciduous.
The areas covered in this project are too large and contain too many trees to manually label each
tree. A more quantitative approach has to be taken to get enough training data for model training.
This section contains a detailed explanation of the different methods that have been used to label
the point cloud data, as well as an evaluation of their performance. As there is no true labels for
the covered areas, evaluating the labeling methods is not a straightforward process. To get some
estimate about the performance of the labeling techniques, a validation set of 5400 trees was created
and labeled manually by inspecting the general tree shape and taking into account the surrounding
trees, as the same species of tree generally grows close to one another. The validation set consists
of 3105 coniferous trees, 1325 deciduous trees and 970 ambiguous trees. The ambiguous trees are
either instances where the tree type is very hard to make out or instances where the cloud does not
contain a tree.
4.2.1
Manual Polygon Labeling
I assume that the same tree species will grow in clusters, therefore, coniferous and deciduous trees
can be expected to not grow close together. The trees can thus be labeled by drawing polygons
around clusters of similar trees. Figure 4.7 shows an example of labeling using this method. This
method does not scale well as the data size increases and requires some knowledge of how clusters of
coniferous and deciduous trees appear in the point cloud data. The overall accuracy of the manual
labeling method on the validation samples is 75.30%, Coniferous trees are labeled correctly in 72.47%
of the samples while deciduous trees are labeled correctly in 82.17% of the samples.
4.2.2
Mean Intensity Labeling
The LIDAR data contains intensity information for each point.
In theory, the leaf and crown
structure of deciduous trees will cause a higher intensity in the point cloud than coniferous trees.
The mean intensity is calculated for each tree and then each tree is labeled using threshold values.
Mean intensity values below 37000 are labeled as coniferous trees while values above 38500 are labeled
as deciduous trees. These thresholds were chosen to maximize the percentage of correctly labeled
trees, while keeping the number of trees high. The intermediate intensity values are not labeled and
used for training as there is alot of ambiguity between the classes in this region. Figure 4.8 shows an
example of labeling using this method. Evaluating on the validation set gives a total correct labeling
accuracy of 88.12%. Coniferous trees are labeled correctly in 93.79% of the samples while deciduous
trees are labeled correctly in 74.09% of the samples.
4.2.3
Mean Elevation Labeling
When the deciduous trees are in bloom in summer, their crowns will shadow the trunk and branches
below the leaves, while the thin coniferous needles allow for more returns from branches and the
tree trunk. The mean elevation of points in a deciduous tree is therefore expected to be larger than
that of points in a coniferous tree of the same size. For each tree, the ratio of the mean elevation
above ground reference (DVR90) to the total tree height above ground is calculated, then classified
as coniferous or decideous by threshold values as with the intensity labeling. With this technique,
the total correct labeled percentage is 81.12%. Coniferous trees are labeled correctly in 86.09% of the
samples while deciduous trees are labeled correctly in 70.26% of the samples. Higher accrucaries are
achievable with a wider threshold, but the number of trees are significantly reduced in that instance.

coniferous trees retain their needles while deciduous trees shed their leafs. With this assumption, it

Point Cloud Classification Methods
Classification of 2D images has been studied extensively, and the field has introduced a wide range of
efficient and robust methods[18]. In recent years, the focus has shifted to 3D scene classification as
several fields have begun using 3D point cloud to better represent the real world. As new methods
are constantly being introduced, building on old architecture, a broad catalog of methods exist.
Most methods are based on PointNet proposed by Charles et al.[4], a deep learning network made
for processing point clouds.
To get an overview of the methods available, I refer to Zhang et al.[18], in which they quickly
summarize the field of methods available at the time of writing (2023) and then test the performance
of each method on four different datasets of varying complexity. The methods are scored based on
their performance on a set of benchmark datasets. PointNet scores lower than the more advanced
methods on most parameters, but the simpler implementation and larger amount of documentation
and tutorials made PointNet a better starting choice when using the point cloud data, as the newer
methods proved to be challenging to run on our custom dataset.

4.3.1
PointNet
PointNet, first introduced in Qi et al: PointNet: Deep Learning on Point Sets for 3D Classification
and Segmentation[4], is a foundational method for deep learning classification on 3D point clouds.
Prior to PointNet, most machine learning using point clouds was performed by first transforming
the clouds into another data type such as regular voxel grids or a collection of 2D images. PointNet
pioneered the research into neural networks learning directly from the point clouds, reducing the
information loss from transforming the data and speeding up the training process.
PointNet consists of two networks, the classification network and the segmentation network. The
classification network assigns point clouds to classes from learned features in the clouds, while the
segmentation network is an extension of the classification network that subdivides point clouds into
seperate parts. Only the classification network is used for this project, as only classification of the
trees are needed.
Figure 4.10 shows an overview of the PointNet architecture.
The numbers in brackets are the
dimensions of the feature maps in the given layer and mlp stands for multi-layer perceptron. Point
clouds have two important properties for classification which the PointNet architecture is build to
support:
Invariance to input order
Unlike pixels in a 2D image, points in a point cloud are unordered and the network must be invariant
to the input order of the points in the network. PointNet achieves this by utilizing a symmetric
function which, when aggregating information from each point, takes n (number of points) input
vectors and outputs a single vector which is invariant to the order of the input vectors. For PointNet,
this is done with a number of multi-layer perceptrons followed by a single variable function and a
max-pooling layer. A multi-layer perceptron is a feed-forward network with several hidden layers.
With several different of these, different featues can be extracted from the data and feed into the
network.
Invariance to tranformations
As the point clouds represent objects in the real world, certain transformations such as rotation and
translation should not alter the structure of the point cloud for classification purposes. PointNet
achieves this with the T-Net block (see figure 4.10) which is an affine linear transformation in the
form of a mini-network which has most of the same modules as the full network (point independent
feature extraction, max pooling and fully connected layers). This T-Net is used to predicted the
affine linear transformation before feature extraction to make the data invariant to rotation and
translation.
In addition to the modules described here, PointNet utilizes batch normalization with ReLU activa-
tion. The network outputs a prediction score for each class for each point cloud.

For this project, a Python implementation of PointNet was used[19]. Before training using PointNet,
a few additional preprocessing steps were required. Firstly, as the PointNet software requires each
point cloud to have the same number of points (and has to be a power of 2), each tree was downsam-
pled using random sampling to 1024 points. This number of points was chosen to keep the number
of clouds large while still retaining enough points for the general tree shape to persist. While more
complex downsampling algorithms exist, random sampling still appears to retain the general tree
structure while being computationally efficient. Trees containing fewer than the requred 1024 points
are discarded. For track F1, this significantly reduces the number of trees. Pointnet does support
fewer points per cloud (512), but the number was kept higher in order to retain the tree stuctures.
After downsampling, each tree is normalized to a unit sphere (which is a requirement for PointNet),
then split into training and validation data, 80% and 20% respectively. Figure 4.11 shows the last
point cloud pre-processing steps after segmentation and before the clouds can be read by PointNet.

4.4.1
Detectree2
Detectree2[20] is a method for detecting tree crowns from 2D data using convolutional neural net-
works. The downloadable Python implementation1 provides both the means for training ones own
models, but also has a number of pre-trained models available for download2. For our tree segmen-
tation problem, 250312 flexi.pth has been used as it has been trained on a combination of closed
canopy systems as well as urban environments. As our data contains both closed canopy areas as
well as open fields and scattered buildings, this model should be better suited than others trained
only on large forests.
Detectree2 tiles the Orthofotos into separate 100x100 m tiles. Tile sizes can be chanes, however as
the pre-trained model was trained on these tile sizes, they have been kept as they are. Each tile is fed
separately into the network. Each tile is given a buffer area that overlaps with surrounding tiles as
this helps with tree detection on the edges of the tiles. After tiling, tree crowns can be detected using
the pre-trained model. The output of this training is a geospatial database file containing polygons
of crown outlines. Each polygon has an associated confidence score that indicates the probability
that the model has found a tree.

2D Classificaiton
The 2D classification task is performed with a convolutional neural network (CNN). In recent year,
much effort has been made in the field of CNNs resulting in complex and robust classification
methods[21][22] which are very well suited for classification tasks with a large (>1000) number of
classes[23], but these methods are in general unnecessarily complex and computationally expensive
for binary classification purposes. Instead, I implement a simple CNN to reduce the number of
trainable parameters.

4.6. 2D/3D COMBINED NETWORK
Each image is resized to [128 × 128 × 3] and normalized in order to have uniform input images. The
network architecture is shown in figure 4.17. The architecture consists of three sequential blocks
each containing a convolution layer and a max pooling layer. The blocks are followed by two fully
connected layers, which finally map the output to one of the two output classes. The network is
trained using random image batches of size 128. During training, it runs by default for 250 epochs
with a learning rate of 0.00001 and weight decay of 0.01. Adam optimizer[23] is utilized for network
training, and a split 80/20 train/test is used.

As both data types cover the same area, in addition to the two seperate classification methods, a

4.6.2
Multimodal Classification
Multimodal classification[5] refers to combining different data structures for a classification problem.
This is a challenging task, as the data structure can vary widely. Combining the 2D images and 3D
point clouds presents such a problem, as shown in the previous sections, with the widely different
neural network structures used for classification on either data type.
Data fusion for multimodal classification is generally separated into two types, early and late fusion,
describing when in the processing pipeline the data sources are combined. Figure 4.19 shows the
general pipeline differences between the two types.

Early fusion
In early fusion, features are extracted from each data source and then combined before the network
is trained. This can be done simply by stacking feature vectors if the data structure allows for it.
For 2D images and 3D point clouds, each data set is 3 dimensional (RGB and xyz), which would
allow for this method. It does however lose the benifits of each individual classification network, and
is therefore not used for this project.
Late fusion
In late fusion, the fusion occurs after each model has been trained. Here I retain the strenghts of
having individual networks optimized for the specific data type. The outputs from the networks can
either be learned features or simply class probabilities. These can then be used as inputs for a final
fully connected network, or in the case of the class probabilities be combined to a final classifier.
In Jensen et al.[24] they use a combination of 2D orthofotos and 3D airborne laser scanning point
clouds to classify forest biodiversity. Here, they employ a series of different methods for classification,
both using the data types seperately and combined. They employ three methods for multimodal
classification of increasing complexity: One using only the class probabilities for the classifier, one
where they extract features from each network and pass them to a fully connected network and
lastly one where they train and end-to-end network and update the entire network during the whole
training process. While more complicated, they do not achieve better results using the end-to-end
network. Indeed, the overall accuracies are very similar in all three methods (80.5%, 81.4% and 80.4%
respectively). For this reason, I opt to use the simplest of these three approaches as inspiration for
our multimodal classification network.

Multimodal Network Architecutre
The multimodal network consists of PointNet and the CNN being trained seperately until they each
converge. After both converge, class probabilities are extracted from both models and passed to a
final classifier that classifies tree type using a linear combination of the output probatilities:
P(class|input) = w2D · P2D(class|input) + w3D · P3D(class|input)
(4.2)
where P is the final probability of an input sample belonging to a certain class, P2D and P3D are the
output class probabilities from the CNN and PointNet respectively and w2D and w3D are the weights.
Optimal weights are determined by optimizing using the training data and then kept the same for
subsequent uses of the network. Figure 4.20 shows the work flow of the multimodal classification
steps.

6.1
Models Trained using PointNet
After data preprocessing and labeling, I am left with 8085 individual trees from the F1 track. The
data is skewed towards coniferous trees in an approximate 2:3 ratio, which appears to cause a skew
in the prediction ratio between the two classes as seen in the confusion matrix, figure 5.2. PointNet
is able to achieve a 89.2% prediction accuracy on the ModelNet40 dataset (Qi et al. p. 5[4]). I
can use this as an ideal baseline for how well a PointNet model can perform. I will look into some
steps taken for improving the prediction accuracy and then discuss the limitations of the current
implementations and data.
6.1.1
Increasing Data Size
I have access to a large amount of data. For classification tasks, the use of more data should improve
performance. Data from tracks F2 and F3 were preprocessed and prepared for training, increasing
the number of individual trees to 37985. As seen in figure 5.3, the predicion accuracy has slightly
improved to 78%, but the data is still skewed towards coniferous trees, and the class accuracy is very
skewed towards coniferous trees (84.3% prediction accuracy) vs. deciduous trees (67.2% prediction
accuracy). While adding more data has improved the classification results, the improvements are
small considering the amount of data added.
6.1.2
Using equal class sizes
In an attempt to equalize the in-class precisions, I reduced the number of coniferous trees in the
training data to equalize the class sizes, which reduced the dataset to 26990 trees. Figure 5.5 shows
the results of training PointNet on this smaller dataset. The overall prediction accuracy does not
significantly differ from the larger dataset, PointNet manages an accuracy of 77.5%.
The class
accuracies have changed however, observe the confusion matrix, figure 5.6. The model is now skewed
towards deciduous trees, with prediction accuracy being 70.6% for coniferous trees and 83.8%. Simply
equalizing the size of the training classes appears to not be sufficient for significantly improving the
class accuracies.
These three examples reveal a few things about the PointNet implementation: While increasing the
amount of data improves the prediction accuracy, as I would expect, the model seems to put too
much emphathis on one class over the other. It would be possible to heavily increase the training
data size further than what exists in tracks F1, F2 and F3, the theoretical gains would need to justify
the additional time it would take to process this large increase in data as well as the extra time it
would take to train models on the data. With additional time, the effects of adding more data could
be explored.

6.2
Models Trained using Custom CNN
The CNN implementation for training on 2D crown images was chosen in favor of more complicated

coniferous trees, either with coniferous trees having more prominent features or being better labeled.
Inspecting the data shows that even with the steps taken to minimize the number of mislabeled
crowns by using the point clouds as labels, a number of input images do not contain trees. Whether
a discrepancy between the two classes exist in this regard is not clear from a quick inspection and
would require further analysis.
6.2.1
Increasing Data Size
With data from tracks F2 and F3, the total tree samples is increased to 25469 split almost evenly
between the two classes (12672 coniferous trees and 12797 deciduous trees). The performance of a
model trained on the custom CNN for 750 epochs is shown in figure 5.9. The prediction accuracy
has only slightly improved with the addition of more data (peaking at 72.9%). This seems to be

closer (see confusion matrix, figure 5.10). The limitations of the CNN can likely be found in the
training data, which will be discussed in a later section.
6.3
Multimodal Classification
The multimodal classifier requires matching the 2D crown to 3D pointclouds in geographic space.
As two different segmentation algorithms have been used to segment the 2D and 3D data, 1:1 tree
matches are impossible. Instead, each point cloud can have multiple valid matching 2D crowns and
vice versa. Each valid match is used for the multimodal classification, however an argument exists
for having only one match for each tree in order to have each tree have the same influence on the
final result. On the other hand, this would greatly reduce the number of matches and therefore
data available for training and validation. In this case, having additional data was deemed more
necessary than letting each tree have the same influence on the classifier. For the tracks F1, F2 and
F3 this leafs us with 25814 valid matches. containing 7635 unique point clouds and 11824 unique
2D crowns.

6.4
Classification Method Limitations
PointNet and the custon CNN are, by the standards of modern neural networks, simple in design.
PointNet laid the groundwork for a wide range of methods[18] that attempt to improve the original
PointNet architecture.
Many of these methods manage to outperform PointNet on the baseline
ModelNet40 dataset1, and it can therefore be assumed that using one or more of these methods for
model training would improve the classification results. PointNet was chosen for this project due to
its relative simplicity compared to the later iterations, both in terms of architecture but also ease of
use. Due to PointNet’s position as the precursor method, useful documentation and troubleshooting
guides are more plentiful than for the majority of the more complicated methods. Correct data
formatting is a recuring hurdle one has to overcome when attempting to use these methods, as most
setup guides and documentation only describe training using ModelNet or other benchmark datasets.
It is therefore not clear what file types the programs read and how they should be formatted. With
time, it would be possible to go through the programs to extract the correct data formatting, which
the project time limit did not permit.
The simple custom build CNN does not perform particularly well, even when adding additional data.
The simple approach was chosen as this is a binary classification problem, so it is typically better
to utilize simpler networks, as more complicated networks only increase the computation time at
limited gains to the network performance. In this case, a deeper network might be able to learn
deeper hierarchical features, though a few tests with the addition of more layers did not significantly
increase performance. It is possible that using deeper layers such as ResNet[21] performs better on
the data, though the work and increase to training time were not deemed sufficiently worth it.
6.5
Data Limitations
While effort could be spent improving the training networks, the reason for the suboptimal perfor-
mance of the networks can likely be found in the data itself. Both point clouds and orthofotos are
of high resolution and plentiful data is available, so in theory the data should be great for training
models. The limitations with the data were in the labeling process. As discussed in the methods
chapter, the data had to be labeled after tree segmentation, which proved to be a difficult task.

6.5.2
Seasonal Differences
As deciduous trees shed their leafs in winter, if data from the same track were available from both
summer and winter, a difference analysis using either the point clouds or orthofotos should be
sufficient for labeling coniferous and deciduous trees at a much higher accuracy than using the

If the same track is flown either in winter, then before the growth season in
summer, or after the growth season in summer, but before the deciduous trees lose their leafs in
autumn and then again in winter, the trees should not have changed size significantly and the only
difference is the loss of leafs. This would of course require an additional survey, increasing the cost
of the project. If the survey covers a large enough track, a single track should yield sufficient data
to train a model.
6.5.3
Manual Labeling
To ensure perfectly labeled data, the only option is to send someone to the field and have them
note each tree’s location and type. This method would be very time consuming, as the number of
surveyed trees has to be sufficiently large to ensure that it is possible to train a good model. For
this reason, the other proposed methods for improving labeling are most likely preferable.
6.5.4
Using External Training Data
For this project, the only point cloud and orthofoto data used was from the Niras surveys. For better
results, it might be beneficial to look for open access datasets containing high quality labeled data.
Several such datasets are available online for research purposes[25][26][27], often containing both
RGB images and LIDAR point clouds. These datasets should provide more than enough data of
both coniferous and deciduous classes for model training, in addition to further subclasses. However,
no such datasets have been found from Denmark, and it is therefore not given that these datasets
represent a comprehensive representation of the Danish forests. Picking datasets gathered in similar
climate and latitude (German, Swedish, and Finish), the trees very well might be representative of
the Danish tree flora. In any case, the use of these datasets should provide useful information, either
from training or validation purposes. In addition, as the data is often subclasses into different tree
species, the data could be used for training more sophisticated models for classifying subclasses of
coniferous and deciduous trees, which would be useful for for providing analysis on tree growth along
the powerlines.
6.6
Forgoing the Neural Networks Entirely
Machine learning using deep learning is a powerful and popular tool these days for data analysis, but
for this problem it might not be the most effective or efficient classification method. Part of the goals
of this project was analysing the viability of using deep learning to classify trees from the 2D and
3D data, and while the results certainly show great potential, especially when combining both data
types, none of the machine learning methods have performed better than simply using the intensity
values from the LIDAR data. It is of course not possible to compare the prediction accuracy of the
mean intensity classification method to the various neural network classifiers as they have not been
validated on the same data, but more importantly, all neural networks have been trained using data
labeled using the intensity values. Therefore, I cannot conclude anything qualitatively about which
method performs better. Any errors from the labeling process is bound to create worse results in the
deep learning classification. If the goal of the project was simply to classify the trees with as high
accuracy as possible, the conclusion would be to just use the mean intensity of each tree to classify
coniferous trees from deciduous trees, but the study shows the potential of the neural networks in
this instance. If one of the proposed improved labeling techniques were used, possibly in combination
with higher performing classification neural networks, the combination of 2D and 3D data very well
might overperform the mean intensity method.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
The following is the answers to the research questions proposed in the introduction section.
How well can we segment and label trees in the two classes in both 2D and 3D data?
The point cloud data has been segmented using the LidR[11] R package, efficiently giving each
individual tree a unique ID and assigning each point to a tree.
The general tree structures are
captured by the segmentation, though clusters of trees growing close to each other do cause some
trees getting split between clouds. Each tree is labeled using their mean intensity from the Lidar
data, which on a validation set performs correct labeling in 88.12% of samples, and labeling is thus
possible using only the data itself, though as described in the discussion chapter, better methods for
labeling are preferable in order to obtain good training data for better classification results.
Trees are detected in the orthofotos using the Detectree2[20] Python library. Each tree is labeled
using the labeled point cloud locations, ensuring that the 2D and 3D trees in the same geographic
location has the same labels. In the same vein, bad tree crowns are removed when they do not
overlap with a significant number of points, ensuring better data quality.
How well do neural networks perform when classifying trees from the 2D and 3D data
and what are their limitations?
Using the two data sources individually, PointNet outperforms the custom CNN on total prediction
accuracy (approximately 78% vs approximately 72.5%).
For both cases, increasing the amount
of training data improved the prediction accuracy, with the PointNet model having the largest
improvements. The class prediction accuracy varies depending seemingly on several factors, as not
only did class inbalance have an inpact, but also the amount of additional data being used for training.
This possibly shows limitations with either the networks themselves or with the quality of the training
data. Both possibilities show the need for further research into applying more complicated neural
network structures to the data as well as better data labeling schemes.
How can the two data types be combined to form a single prediction output and how
does this combined method perform compared to using each data source individually?
Prediction probabilities for each tree is exported from the highest performing PointNet model and
CNN model, then the final prediction was performed with a weighted sum of the prediction proba-
bilities. Tests show better results with the combined method than what either network can achieve
on the data. Classification of both coniferous and deciduous trees is improved with the combined
method, providing evidence for the viability of combining the two data types to achieve the best
possible results. The results also revealed the need for a more uniform segmentation scheme, to
ensure 2D crowns and point clouds can easily be matched.
