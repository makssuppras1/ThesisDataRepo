Abstract
N-Nitrosamines (NAs) are N-nitroso compounds readily formed through the reaction of nitrite or nitrogen
oxides (NOx) with primary, secondary, or tertiary amines. Their acute toxicity has been recognized since
1937. Growing concern about NA presence in food products, particularly processed meats, led the European
Food Safety Authority (EFSA) to publish a report in 2023 highlighting ten target carcinogenic N-nitrosamines
(TCNAs). Among these, N-nitrosopiperidine (NPIP) remains under-characterized with respect to formation
kinetics and environmental dependency.
This thesis examines NPIP formation under food-relevant conditions, with a focus on temperature, pH, and
nitrite concentration. Aqueous piperidine models were used to evaluate nitrosation behavior and to develop
a predictive kinetic framework for NPIP formation. Experimental data were fitted to a bimolecular second-
order kinetic model, and Arrhenius parameters were estimated at nitrite concentrations of 5 mM, 10 mM, and
20 mM, yielding activation energies of 26.0, 13.4, and 12.3 kJ/mol, respectively.
To assess the applicability of the model in more complex systems, NPIP formation was studied in a protein-
liquid model (brain heart infusion broth) at RT, 50 °C, and 75 °C. Results showed rapid stabilization of NPIP
levels within the first two time points. Interestingly, NPIP formation was higher in the liquid-protein matrix at
RT than in the aqueous system, while the opposite was observed at elevated temperatures. These findings
indicate that while aqueous models can capture general temperature and pH trends, they do not fully reflect
the kinetic behavior in real food matrices, where competing pathways, enzymatic activity, and matrix effects
might play significant roles.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
According to the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA), chemical contaminants are “substances that are
unintentionally present in food or feed” 1.
The three main categories of chemical contaminants described by EFSA are: Naturally occurring toxins, such
as mycotoxins (produced by fungi) or alkaloids (produced by plants), Environmental contaminants, which
could be heavy metals or persistent chemicals (e.g, lead or per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS)),
and the last is Process contaminants, which are chemicals formed during food preparation or processing
(e.g., smoking, curing, or frying), such as N-Nitrosamine (NAs) [1–5].
Figure 1: Selected chemical contaminants relevant to food and feed safety. Mycotoxins (Aflatoxin B1) are reported by
EFSA [2024] [1]. Alkaloids (Acetylheliosupine) are assessed in EFSA [2017] [2]. Environmental contaminants (e.g.,
arsenic species and PFAS) are covered in EFSA’s [2020] and [2024] reports [3, 4]. Process contaminants, including
NAs (e.g., NDMA, NPIP, NTCA, NDEA), are described by Niklas et al. [2022] [5].
Process contaminants represent a significant challenge to food safety, as they can form or accumulate during
common cooking and industrial food processing conditions. Among these, NAs have received considerable
attention due to their carcinogenic potential and complex formation pathways, which may occur both during
food production and endogenously after ingestion [6]. NAs are typically formed through the nitrosation of
secondary amines in the presence of nitrite as a precursor to nitrosating agents. Since the discovery of the
carcinogenicity of NAs by Magee and Barnes [6], preserved and processed meats, particularly those cured
with sodium nitrite, have been a central focus in NA-related research.

INTRODUCTION
1.1
Aims of Master thesis
The European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) published a report in 2023, which reviewed available data
on the presence of 32 NAs in a range of food products. Among these, ten NAs are known to possess
carcinogenic properties; collectively, they have been termed TCNAs (NDMA, NDEA, NMEA, NDPA, NDBA,
NMA, NSAR, NMOR, NPYR, and NPIP). Although the toxic effects of NAs have been recognized for many
years, risk assessment is challenging due to the limited availability of formation data and the absence of
robust occurrence data for several key food matrices. This thesis aims to improve the existing understanding
of the formation of NAs through the extensive characterization of NPIP. Given NPIP’s significant exposure
potential and incomplete toxicological understanding, this compound was selected to be the focus of this
thesis. The formation of NPIP was first investigated in simplified aqueous solution models, where the effects
of temperature and pH, relevant to processed food, were examined. Based on the obtained results, a kinetic
model was developed and validated to characterize the formation of NPIP. Subsequently, the formation of
NPIP at different temperatures is analyzed in a real food system: a liquid protein model. The present study
seeks to address the following hypothesis:
Hypothesis
• Predictive models can estimate NPIP formation under varying experimental conditions, imitating the
conditions of preserved foods.
• While aqueous model systems can effectively capture general formation trends and temperature de-
pendence of NPIP, the absolute NPIP concentrations in a liquid-protein model are expected to be lower
due to matrix effects such as competing nitrosation paths.

2.1
What are N-Nitrosamines (NAs)?
2.1.1
Definiton of NAs
NAs are N-nitroso compounds readily formed through the reaction of nitrite or nitrogen oxides (NOx) with
primary, secondary, or tertiary amines. This process occurs via an electrophilic substitution involving the
nitrosonium ion (NO+), which is generated from nitrite or nitrate, and is favoured under acidic conditions [6,
7]. Although both nitrite and nitrate can be precursors, nitrosation primarily occurs at low pH values or at
high temperatures.
NAs have been detected in various sources, including preserved foods, beverages, personal care products,
tobacco products, and chlorinated drinking water [8]. Although NAs were discovered in the 1880s, it was not

until the 1950s that scientific interest in NAs began to arise [8]. This interest was intensified when Magee and
Barnes demonstrated the potent carcinogenicity of several NAs compounds in animal models, e.g., NDMA
and NPIP [9].
Shortly after this discovery, several disease outbreaks were reported on Norwegian animal farms. Between
1957 and the 1970s, a range of liver diseases and cancer cases emerged across multiple farms. These
incidents were ultimately linked to the use of preserved herring as animal feed, in which sodium nitrite was
the commonly used curing agent at the time [10]. Since then, preserved and processed meats have received
attention in NAs research, due to the formation of these compounds during curing with sodium nitrite.

2.2.2
Nitrite/nitrate metabolism
Nitrate is a naturally occurring compound in many foods and serves as a source of nitrite, which is a direct
precursor to nitrosating agents. In the bloodstream, however, nitrite is rapidly oxidized back to nitrate by
oxyhemoglobin [6, 18].
The endogenous nitrosation begins in the human oral cavity, as 93% of the nitrite exposure is converted
to nitrate [6, 18]. The nitrate is then digested and absorbed in the gastrointestinal tract, where it reaches
the bloodstream and is primarily excreted through the urine. However, a small portion of nitrate is carried
back to the salivary glands, concentrated, and then secreted back into the mouth. Oral bacteria then reduce
nitrate to nitrite, which is transferred to the acidic environment in the stomach. The low pH values present in
the stomach promote the formation of nitrous acid (HNO2), which can be converted to an active nitrosating
agent, dinitrogen trioxide (N2O3) [6, 18].
The endogenous formation path is estimated to account for the majority of human NAs exposure, with es-
timates ranging from 45% to 75% of the total intake [18]. Contributing factors include free amines naturally
present in food and those generated during processing.

3.1.1
Ionization techniques
Before the sample can be analyzed and the Mass Spectrometer can detect NPIP, NPIP needs to be ionized.
For this thesis, two different methods are being used: Electrospray Ionization (ESI) and Atmospheric Pres-
sure Chemical Ionization (APCI).
ESI is the most commonly used ionization technique for LC-MS, as it makes both positive and negative ions
detectable and applies to a broad range of compounds. It ionizes the analyte in the liquid state through the
Coulomb explosion, with bigger droplets becoming smaller and unstable droplets. However, ESI is less effi-
cient for ionizing low-molecular-weight, non-polar compounds, such as NPIP, making APCI a more suitable
option.
Unlike ESI, which is optimal for highly polar and larger biomolecules, APCI excels at ionizing smaller, less
polar compounds. Using a corona discharge needle, APCI generates gas-phase ions, making it particularly
useful for analytes with low to mid-polarity ranges that may not ionize efficiently via ESI (Figure 11).
That being said, NPIP is a relatively low-molecular-weight compound (114 Da) and is moderately nonpolar;
therefore, the most efficient ionization method would be APCI, as chosen in Herrmann et al [2014].
When ionizing, compounds tend to either lose or gain a proton, [M+H+] / [M–H+], which affects the com-
pound’s ability to become ionized. For NPIP, the double-bonded oxygen (––O), makes the compound easier
to gain a proton, rather than an alkyl group losing one. Additionally, the resonance effect seen in Scheme 2
Structure b, makes the negatively charged oxygen even more prone to accept a proton. This characteristic is
observed in many NAs, whereas many NVNAs possess a polar group capable of losing a proton. For NPIP
detection, running the mass spectrometer in positive mode is the most effective approach (Figure 11)5.

before re-entering the Orbitrap, allowing for high-resolution MS/MS and structural elucidation (Figure 13).
In this case, the Orbitrap serves as the detector, utilizing the Fourier transformation.
3.2
Previous studies of NAs
Understanding the kinetics and environmental conditions that influence NAs formation is critical when as-
sessing their risk and modeling their behaviour in food systems. Several studies have contributed to this
understanding.
Ashworth et al. [20] developed a conservative kinetic model to estimate the formation rates of VNAs from
secondary amines, under varying nitrite concentrations, pH, and temperature conditions. Their work fo-
cused on DBA and incorporated detailed reaction kinetics. In conclusion, their results showed a generated
prediction model consistently overestimating nitrosamine formation across pH ranges (3–9).
Building on this, Diab et al. extended the model and validated it through a robust automated experimen-
tal setup using LC-MS/MS analysis [21]. They also focused on DBA under various conditions such as pH,
chloride concentration, temperature, and competing amines. However, their result also showed a too con-
servative model, reaching approximately a 10-fold increase.
Diab et al. also evaluated quenching strategies and identified aniline hydrochloride as the most stable and
effective method for terminating nitrosation, sufficient for kinetics study experiments.

3.3.1
Introduction to Kinetics
Kinetic parameters such as the rate constant (k/kr) and activation energy (Ea) are essential for characterizing
chemical reactions, especially in modelling formation pathways and assessing reaction risk. Ashworth et al.
incorporated such parameters into a predictive model for NAs formation, sourcing them from experimental
determination, literature values, or general assumptions. One common assumption is that reaction rates
approximately double for every 10 °C increase in temperature, a principle often applied when activation
energies are unknown.

study. Since these constants are embedded within the Arrhenius equation, selecting an appropriate rate law

5.2.1
Conclusion
Aniline hydrochloride is an effective and stable quenching agent for nitrosation of piperidine across both
high and low nitrite concentrations. Furthermore, aniline is stable for at least 72 hours, and additional results
indicate that it remains reasonably stable for at least a week (Appendix B.2). Still, its effectiveness depends
on the relative concentrations of aniline and nitrite. Figure 25 demonstrates that aniline can not quench
concentrations of nitrite that exceed the aniline concentration.

Conclusion
The preliminary experiments investigating NPIP formation at 10°C and pH 2.7 demonstrated a clear depen-
dence on the concentration of added nitrite. In reactions with 10, 50, and 200 mM nitrite, a rapid increase in
NPIP concentration was observed within the first 10 hours, gradually approaching a stable condition around
40-54 hours. The concentrations were approximately 100 ng/mL, 1100 ng/mL, and 1400 ng/mL for 10 mM, 50
mM, and 200 mM nitrite, respectively. In contrast, the control reaction without added nitrite showed minimal
and fluctuating NPIP levels, likely due to instrument contamination.
When normalizing for nitrite input, the overall conversion efficiency to NPIP was low, with maximum con-
versions of 0.3%,0.6%, and 2.0% for the respective concentrations. These results exceeded the conversion
reported by Diab et al. under similar conditions, possibly due to residual NPIP concentrations in the instru-
ment from earlier high-concentration samples. To address this and prevent instrument saturation effects,
post-samples were performed with a tenfold dilution of reagents, resulting in an increased pH of 3.7; a con-
dition more relevant to food systems and shown by Diab et al. to yield higher nitrite conversion after the first
five hours.

5.4
Effect of Temperature on NPIP Formation
Having established NPIP formation behavior under varying nitrite concentrations at pH 2.7, the next step
was to investigate how temperature impacts formation kinetics at pH 3.7. This is especially relevant for
understanding NPIP formation in food-processing scenarios where thermal treatment is standard. Room
temperature (RT, 21 °C), 50 °C, and 75 °C are conditions under which this study examines the effect of vary-
ing nitrite concentrations on NPIP formation. Furthermore, this leads to determining the Arrhenius activation
energy, which is discussed in Section 5.6.4.
To facilitate kinetic studies, experiments were conducted in 50 mL Falcon tubes rather than autosampler
vials. The increased volume should minimize headspace exposure after sampling. Reagents were diluted
tenfold, raising the reaction pH to approximately 3.7, which aligns with more suitable conditions for processed
meat.
These parameters-temperature, nitrite concentration, and pH-mirror those used in Diab et al., allowing for
a meaningful comparison of nitrosation trends and conversion efficiencies across studies. However, it is
essential to note that while this thesis focuses on the NPIP formation, Diab et al. characterized NDBA
formation.
The total concentration of the reagents for the experiments conducted can be seen in Appendix B.4 Ta-
ble 23.

conducted in this thesis.
It is hypothesized that increased aniline concentration during quenching will improve data consistency and
reduce outliers previously seen at 5 and 10 mM nitrite. Furthermore, it investigates the hypothesis that the

5.6.3
Conclusion on choice for Arrhenius analysis
In summary, while both models yield comparable values for R2, B0, and RMSE, indicating acceptable sta-
tistical fit, the pseudo-second-order model differs significantly in its kinetic interpretation. Specifically, the
observed decrease of kr with an increase in temperature contradicts experimental findings, where NPIP
formation increased with higher temperatures. This trend, paired with broader 95% confidence intervals for
kr (often spanning multiple orders of magnitude and crossing zero), highlights the model’s uncertainty, par-
ticularly under conditions of low nitrite concentration and lower temperatures, where the NPIP formation is
minimal.
These limitations suggest that while the pseudo-second-order function can describe and fit the data, it lacks
the underlying reasoning and observed behavior that drive the kinetics. In contrast, the second-order model
for a bimolecular reaction not only fits well statistically but also aligns with known nitrosation behavior, where
increasing temperature and nitrite concentration enhance formation rates. This is consistent with prior studies
by Diab et al. [21] and Ashworth et al. [20], which explain nitrite-driven kinetics and temperature-accelerated
reactions in similar amine systems. Therefore, the second-order model is more appropriate for further kinetic
interpretation and use in Arrhenius-type analysis.
In theory, the excess piperidine assumption should verify a better model for the pseudo-second order nitro-
sation, due to the low impact that secondary amines have on the nitrosation reaction. Literature, such as M.J.
Hill and Williams, explains this; however, the mechanism of nitrosation is still challenging to describe and
may need to be revised as better analytical tools become available. It is possible that underlying reactions,
such as the formation of nitrosating agents and their interaction with secondary amines, may play a more
significant role than previously detected analytically.
Regarding the high uncertainty for the 1 mM addition of nitrite, indicated by the broad CI and low R2, it renders
the Arrhenius plot investigation irrelevant for this condition. Furthermore, for the same reason involved in
the first experiments of (k) and (j), the repeated experiment exhibited a better R2 and narrower CI, making
it the preferred choice in the Arrhenius investigation over the other.

5.7
Matrix effect
To test whether the kinetics observed in aqueous systems are transferable to real-world food contexts, this
section investigates NPIP formation in a liquid-protein model using Brain Heart Infusion (BHI) broth. BHI was
chosen due to its similarity to complex food matrices in terms of processed food and its previous use in NPIP
formation studies, such as the work by De Mey et al. [16]. The system was prepared by diluting piperidine
to a final concentration of 10 mM and adjusting the pH to 3.7. Each condition was tested with and without
the addition of 20 mM sodium nitrite. The elevated nitrite level accounts for potential suppression effects
caused by competing reactions or inhibitors present in the matrix, while the nitrite-free condition serves as a
control to detect any NPIP formation arising from matrix-driven catalysis.
In total, six experimental setups were conducted across three temperatures: RT, 50 °C, and 75 °C (see
Appendix B.7, Tables 31 and 32).
This experiment aims to assess whether the NPIP formation trends observed in simplified aqueous solutions
can reliably represent or predict behavior in more complex, food-relevant matrices. Specifically, the use of
a liquid-protein model enables evaluation of whether similar kinetic trends are retained despite the pres-
ence of competing nitrosation targets and potential inhibitors. If comparable kinetic behavior is observed,
the previously established model Eq. (6) may be applicable for predicting NPIP formation in food-relevant
conditions.
It is hypothesized that the total NPIP formation in the broth matrix will be significantly lower than in aqueous
solutions due to competing nitrosation targets and natural inhibitors (Section 2). However, it is hypothesized
that, if comparable kinetic behavior is observed, the previously established model could be extended to food-
relevant matrices.
Figure 40 compares NPIP formation in the liquid protein matrix (represented by striped lines) with earlier
results from aqueous systems (solid lines). Across all conditions, the broth samples exhibit a distinct pattern:
a rapid increase in NPIP formation, which quickly reaches a plateau, typically within the first two sampling
points.

17 hours, then stabilize between 57.5 and 63.3 ng/mL through the final time point at 49 hours. In Figure 40(lp),
the 50 °C condition, NPIP formation increases to 119.0 ng/mL in 17 hours and subsequently remains stable
between 111 and 119 ng/mL. For (rp), the 75 °C experiment, formation is extremely rapid, reaching 191 ng/mL
after 2 hours, and then stabilizing between 187 and 202 ng/mL over the next 31 hours.
These results indicate a strong temperature dependence on the NPIP formation rate, but also suggest early
saturation behavior in the broth matrix, possibly due to limited availability of nitrosating agents or competing

NPIP conversions observed in this study. Nevertheless, both studies report nitrosation efficiencies below 1%,
reinforcing the conclusion that NPIP formation is limited in matrix environments due to competing reactions
and the presence of inhibitors.
Conclusion
In conclusion, while the aqueous piperidine model provides valuable mechanistic insights, it cannot fully
predict the behavior of piperidine nitrosation in complex food matrices. The broth experiments at 50 °C
showed the most excellent alignment with aqueous results, but the differences in kinetic profiles, particularly
the early plateau in the broth, indicate distinct reaction dynamics. To better characterize the initial reaction
phase and verify the observed early plateau, additional measurements at shorter time intervals in the matrix
system are recommended. Such data would enhance modeling accuracy and support more realistic risk
assessments for NPIP formation in food-relevant conditions.

CONCLUSION
6
Conclusion
This study concludes that a bimolecular second-order rate expression (Eq. (6)) provides the best predictive
model for NPIP formation in an aqueous solution, offering more consistent and reliable parameter estimates.
This is evidenced by the narrow 95% confidence intervals for the rate constant kr, the logical temperature
dependence of kr, and high R2 values across datasets. The RMSE values ranged from as low as 0.4 ng/mL
(RT, 5 mM) to 31.4 ng/mL (75 °C, 20 mM), while R2 consistently exceeded 0.95, supporting a strong model fit.
The initial concentration of nitrosating agents (B0) was also within reasonable bounds, with even narrower
confidence intervals than kr.
Arrhenius analysis yielded Ea estimates of 26.0, 13.4, and 12.3 kJ/mol for 5 mM, 10 mM, and 20 mM nitrite
conditions, respectively. However, the reliability of these values varies: while the 5 mM condition showed
an excellent R2 of 0.99, the fits for 10 mM and 20 mM were less robust (R2 = 0.44 and 0.67, respectively),
indicating room for improvement. A suggestion lies in the inclusion of additional replicates to reduce the
influence of outliers.
The aim of this thesis was to enhance the current understanding of NPIP formation via the nitrosation of
piperidine under conditions relevant to food systems. Through systematic variation of nitrite concentration,
temperature, and pH, the study demonstrates that all three parameters affect NPIP formation and its kinetics.
Notably, increasing the temperature from 21 °C to 50 °C resulted in nearly a tenfold increase in nitrite con-
version within 24 hours. A further temperature increase to 75 °C produced a smaller 3-4-fold increase.
Conversely, increasing the pH from 3.7 to 5.1 and 6.6 resulted in a marked reduction in NPIP formation,
with final concentrations remaining below 2 ng/mL at pH 5.1 and even lower at pH 6.6 under 10 mM nitrite
conditions.
However, the experiments using a liquid protein matrix revealed formation behavior distinct from that ob-
served in aqueous systems. NPIP levels increased rapidly and reached early plateaus, stabilizing at ap-
proximately 63 ng/mL at RT, 114 ng/mL at 50 °C, and 197 ng/mL at 75 °C. These results suggest that NPIP
formation in the liquid protein model is comparatively higher than in aqueous solutions at RT, but lower at
elevated temperatures (50 °C and 75 °C). This is explained by matrix-specific effects, such as enzymatic
degradation, inhibition, or competing side reactions; however, it has not been tested or confirmed in this
thesis. Further investigation is needed to verify this.
Altogether, these findings contribute to a broader understanding of NPIP formation by extending the avail-
able kinetic data under food-relevant conditions. Furthermore, the results demonstrate that aqueous model
systems, while useful for controlled mechanistic studies, are insufficient on their own to fully characterize
NPIP formation within complex food matrices. Modeling based on aqueous systems must be adjusted when

Future work
Building on the findings of this thesis, future research should continue to investigate NPIP formation in the
liquid-protein system. Experimental results indicated that the system reached a stable concentration rapidly,
within the first two sampling points. As discussed, this behavior may be attributed to enzymatic processes
occurring in the protein-rich matrix. Therefore, applying an enzymatic kinetics framework could offer more
profound insight into the underlying mechanisms of NPIP formation in such systems.
Moreover, this study provides a foundation for exploring NPIP formation across a broader range of nitrite
concentrations under constant temperature conditions. This would enable the evaluation of how well the
estimated kinetic parameters can predict NPIP levels. Since the current model is based on a limited dataset
and may be susceptible to overfitting, collecting additional data could improve the robustness and predictive
accuracy of future models.
Given more time, the RT and 50 °C experiments for the liquid-protein system should be repeated with a
revised sampling strategy. Specifically, samples should be collected over a 0–8 hour period with 2-hour
quenching intervals to better resolve the early kinetics and determine whether stabilization occurs even
earlier than observed. Ideally, a gradual increase in NPIP concentration would be expected. The absence of
this trend suggests that the system may reach stable conditions faster than anticipated, potentially skewing
kinetic parameter estimation.
Future studies could also investigate NPIP formation at additional pH levels (e.g., 4.5, 5.5, and 6.0) to bet-
ter understand the influence of pH on reaction kinetics. These experiments should preferably use nitrite
concentrations around 20 mM to ensure reliable detection and quantification above instrumental limits.
