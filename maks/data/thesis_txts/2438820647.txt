Abstract 
Recently, a lot of research has been conducted to measure small pressure regimes (1-
10 kPa) with the purpose of detecting wrist pulse and develop human-machine interfaces. In 
this work, we report a highly sensitive and flexible piezo-capacitive pressure sensor based on 
a porous dielectric matrix containing multi-walled carbon nanotubes (MWNTs) as conductive 
fillers. Porous dielectric matrix containing conductive fillers has a high resolution because the 
conductive fillers increase the dielectric constant of the matrix. This difference leads to a high 
sensitivity. Although this sensor has good performances, it is vulnerable to environmental 
interferes such as the human hand and electromagnetic signals. Therefore, we applied 
shielding systems to the fabricated sensor. Additionally, temperatures and air humidity have 
little influence on the output signal of the sensor. 
Keywords  Flexible / Wearable sensor, Pressure sensor, Conductive filler, Porous dielectric

Chapter 1. Introduction 
 
1.1 Flexible Pressure Sensors and Their Importance in Future Applications 
 
Pressure is defined as the ratio of the force applied perpendicular to the surface of an object 
per unit area over which that force is distributed. Measuring pressure is important because the pressure 
involves the physical intermediation between objects. Therefore, a number of pressure sensors have 
been utilized as essential elements for measuring the pressure on various objects. However, most of the 
old-fashioned pressure sensors were developed based on the elastic property of bulky models and rigid 
spring. These sensors have limits of their applicability to different objects of arbitrary shapes. Nowadays, 
the sensor technology has become more specialized in personalized devices for various purposes. 
Moreover, the demand for flexible sensors has strongly increased in order to endure the high degrees of 
deformability and conformability. In particular, flexible pressure sensors are of much importance in 
these days because they are tightly related to various advanced applications such as healthcare 
applications [1], human-machine interfaces [2], and electronic textiles [3].

1.3. 
Existing Sensing Mechanisms of Previous Flexible Pressure Sensors 
 
Previously, many research reported successful pressure-sensing devices that operate on 
various sensing principles, such as piezoresistivity [16-19], capacitance [20-22], and 
piezoelectricity [23-26]. In this paper, we used the capacitance type sensor where capacitance is 
changed when pressure is applied. The capacitance type sensor has advantages of stable and precise 
responses. However, most of this type of sensors have low sensitivity compared to other types of 
sensors. In addition, they have critical interference problems when conducting objects, such as a 
human hand, are close to the sensor; this is because an additional parasitic capacitance is 
introduced.  
 
 
Figure 3. Sensing mechanisms of previous flexible pressure sensors [5].

1.4 
Literature Survey for Increasing Sensitivity of Capacitive Sensor 
 
Recently, many researches have tried to increase the sensitivity of capacitive type pressure 
sensors. Specifically, there are two different ways to increase sensitivity of capacitive type sensors. The 
first way is microstructuring of the sensing materials; this can increase sensitivity by changing the 
mechanical of bulk materials in the low-pressure regime. The second way that the sensitivity can be 
increased is by mixing filler to the sensing materials; this can enhance the relative change of capacitance 
significantly. 
 
Figure 4. Sensing mechanisms and strategy to increase the sensitivity of capacitive sensors[6]. 
 
In the case of the microstructured materials, these sensors have low initial capacitance 
value (< 200fF). In addition, the sensitivity of sensors is lower than 1, not enough to use these 
sensors to be applied in wearable or tactile pressure sensors. In the case of materials based on 
modification of electrical property, the dynamic range of the sensors are only 0-30Pa and have 
relatively low sensitivity (<0.01) due to material’s properties. Furthermore, still none of capacitive 
type sensors have solved the shielding problem that arises when these sensors are attached on the 
human body.

1.1 
motivation and research overview 
 
Previously, the porous elastomer using Ecoflex[7] was reported for successful pressure-
sensing. Although this sensor has good performance, it has still low sensitivity to use for wearable 
applications. For this application, it is necessary to use small size chip for measuring capacitance to 
attach on the human body. Therefore, sensor should have high sensitivity. Therefore, we propose 
novel flexible pressure sensors based on the mechanical and electrical characteristics of porous 
elastomer containing conductive filler to increase the sensitivity. 
First, we report a flexible piezocapacitive pressure sensor based on a porous elastomer 
containing 0.6wt% of CNT as conductive filler; this sensor is capable of highly sensitive and stable 
pressure sensing over the pressure range (~10 kPa). The sensor can provide perfectly reversible and 
elastic compressive behavior while pores are closed and opened without viscoelastic behavior, 
which is generally considered as the major cause of hysteresis. Based on the compressive behavior 
of the microporous structure under external pressure, we successfully achieved outstanding sensor 
performance with high sensitivity and high initial value; both of these values are ~4times higher 
than that of a sensor based on a porous elastomer that does not contain conductive filler.  
Second, we report solving the shielding problems. When the human hand is close to the 
liquid source, an additional parasitic capacitance is introduced into the model and the potential 
difference due to the human hand corresponds to disturbances. Using an out-of-phase electrical 
model, we can shield the signal from environmental interferers.

2.1 
Fabrication Procedure of Piezocapacitive Type Pressure Sensor 
 
 
 
 
A porous elastomer was formed by molding an Ecoflex elastomer inside a cylinder shape sugar 
template. Figure 6 shows the process to make the sugar template. For making sugar template, the 
cylinder shaped-mold was made by 3D printer. We mixed brown sugar after that it was applied pressure 
to put it in the cylinder shaped-mold. The mixed brown sugar power was made to become hard in an 
oven for 12 hours at 100°C. Finally, the cylinder shaped sugar template was made by detaching dried 
and gathered sugar power from the mold.  
 
 
Figure 6. Fabrication procedure of the sugar template using a 3D printed mold.  
 
 
==PAGE:16==
8 
 
Figure 7 shows the fabrication of the porous elastomer. There are two components: The 
Ecoflex (Ecoflex®  Shore hardness 00-30, Smooth-On, Inc., USA) prepolymer solutions which are a 
base and a curing agent. Each prepolymer solutions was mixed with same amount of concentration of 
CNT using planetary mixer. After that, mixed prepolymer solution (CNT-Ecoflex prepolymer) was 
prepared by mixing a base and a curing agent at a weight ratio of 1:1, after which the sugar template 
was immediately immersed. To infiltrate the solution into the sugar template, the solution with the sugar 
template was degassed within a vacuum chamber and cured in a convection oven at room temperature 
for 5 hours. The porous elastomer containing conductive filler (CNT) was made by dissolving the sugar 
portion of infiltrated-sugar template and drying in air. 
 
 
Figure 7. Fabrication procedure of the porous dielectric containing conductive filer (CNT).

2.2  
Proposed Design of Piezocapacitive Type Pressure Sensor 
 
Figure 8 shows the schematic of our pressure sensor. The pressure sensor consists of two 
elements: two parallel electrode layers (copper tape) and the porous elastomer containing CNT in  
between the electrodes. For the porous elastomer containing CNT, it had randomly distributed pores 
throughout the entire volume. The size of the micropores in pristine microporous Ecoflex cube was 
observed to be 288 ± 85 μm. In addition, the porosity was estimated by considering the mass ratio 
between solid and porous Ecoflex blocks with identical dimensions, and the porosity was calculated 
as 78.4 ± 2 %.

2.3 
Sensing Mechanism of Piezocapacitive Type Pressure Sensors Based on Porous 
Elastomer Containing Conductive filler 
 
Figure 9 describes the sensing mechanism of our pressure sensor named as the porous 
elastomer containing conductive filler (CNT) compared to the porous elastomer without filler. the 
parallel-plate capacitor is calculated by the dielectric constant (εr), the distance(d), and area (A).  
The sensitivity is factors of distance between the two electrodes and dielectric constant of 
material. Distance(d) change is the one of factor with related to change of capacitance upon loading. 
When the porous elastomer is mixed with conductive filler, the porous elastomer gets more stiff 
property. Therefore, compared to the porous elastomer without filler, the porous elastomer containing 
filler is deformed by a small amount owing to the increased stiffness. On the other hand, compared to 
the porous elastomer without filler, the capacitance change of the porous elastomer containing filler can 
be significantly boosted by great change of the effective dielectric constant. As a result, overall effects 
of the porous elastomer containing filler can increase of the sensitivity. 
 
 
 
Figure 9. Comparison of pressure-sensing mechanisms between the porous elastomer without 
filler and with filler.

2.3.2 
Electrical Model of Piezocapacitive Type Pressure Sensors. 
 
 
The elastomer without containing filler is considered as a dielectric material insulator. In fact, 
it is known that mixing conductive filler with the elastomer decreasing the resistance of the elastomer-
conductive filler composite. If the elastomer-filler composite contain high concentration of conductive 
filler, the measuring instrument such as LCR meter and CDC sensor cannot measure capacitance 
component of elastomer-conductive filler composite. Therefore, it is important to know the resistance 
and capacitance during compression. To measure the resistance and capacitance in AC mode of our 
sensor, we made simple electrical model such as resistor and capacitor parallel model as shown in 
Figure 13. In case of the porous elastomer without conductive filler, it is considered as dielectric that 
means the capacitance component is much more dominant so it represents 90° of phase. However, the 
porous elastomer containing conductive filler, phase is decreased when pressure is applied as shown in 
Figure 14. Therefore, it cannot be considered as a perfect dielectric. 
 
 
Figure 13. Electrical model of the porous elastomer: parallel resistor-capacitor circuit.  
 
==PAGE:23==
15 
 
 
Figure 14. Phase difference in AC circuit at different concentrations of CNT when pressure is 
applied up to 10kPa. 
 
If measuring instruments cannot measure the capacitance component of our sensor because of 
change of resistance, it cannot use for capacitive type sensor. To confirm this problem, we prepared 
commercial capacitor and resistor and set parallel electrical model to compare indicated capacitance 
value and capacitance value from the measuring instrument. In Table 1, if the resistance is higher than 
10kΩ, the measuring instrument measured the capacitance component within 0.1% error. Likewise, 
Figure 15 shows the change of resistance in DC mode. As shown in Figure 15, the minimum resistance 
is higher than 100kΩ. Therefore, the porous elastomer containing conductive filler can be considered 
as capacitive type sensor.

Static Response of Piezocapacitive Type Pressure Sensors

(AGS-X, SHIMADZU, Japan) was used to test the various performances of the sensors. The test

Agilent, USA) measured the capacitance of the sensor at a frequency of 100kHz.

3.3.1.1 Pressure-Response Curves 
 
Figure 20 displays the performances of the pressure sensor in the pressure range of 0kPa 
and 10kPa. This curves show the pressure input versus the relative changes of the capacitance for 
the porous elastomer; in this figure, the slope of the curve is defined as the sensitivity of the sensor 
and there are two different steps with different compressive behaviors. The first step up to a 
pressure level of around 2kPa with linear high sensitivity. It can be found that the sensitivity of the 
sensor using the porous elastomer containing 0.6wt% of CNT is nearly 4 times higher than the 
porous elastomer containing 0wt% of CNT. The reason for this high sensitivity in the first step can 
be due to the fact that the air in the porous elastomer is filled with the high dielectric constant 
elastomer during the compression. In the second step (pressure = 2-10 kPa), the sensitivity of the 
sensor response was decreased because there is little air in the porous elastomer to be filled with the 
high dielectric constant elastomer. On the other hand, mixing more CNT can increases the 
sensitivity in this step because CNTs are closed to each other and then it makes the porous 
elastomer close to the percolation threshold which means it increased dielectric constant of the 
porous elastomer.  
It should be noted in Table 2, the sensitivity and initial capacitance of the porous elastomer 
containing 0.6wt% of CNT is ~4 times and ~17times higher than that of the porous elastomer 
containing 0wt% of CNT over wide pressure span of 0-10kPa.

3.3.1.4 Effect of dust and mist on Pressure-Responses

4.1. 
Shielding in Capacitive Sensing 
 
 
Capacitance sensors has a lot of noise from environmental interferers such as human hand, 
electromagnetic signals. Using an out-of-phase electrical model, we can shield the sensor from 
environmental interferers. For shielding of the capacitive sensors,  a CDC (capacitance to digital 
converter, FDC1004, Texas instruments, USA) was employed to make all direction shielding. In 
this chip, we used module and composed sensor system in figure. Principle of shielding capacitive 
sensor is shown in Figure 29. There are four electrodes to shield the sides, top and bottom. To shield 
the top and bottom, electrode1, electrode2 and electorde3, electorde4 were provided by same phase 
voltage and then there is no current between electrodes so it cannot affect the measuring electrode 
such as electrode2 and electorde2. Moreover, electrode2 and electrode3 are 180 degrees out of 
phase with respect to each other. There is a virtual ground in the middle of sensor. When the hand is 
close to the sensor from the side, there is no current between the ground connected to hand and the 
virtual node. Therefore, we conducted all ditrection shielding. Figure 30 shows how the capacitive 
sensor can be fabricated using the shielding method as mentioned above.

Chapter 5. Concluding Remarks 
 
In summary, we have developed flexible pressure sensors based on porous elastomeric 
containing CNT. First, by utilizing the giant piezocapacitive effect of the porous elastomer, we 
employed a porous elastomer containing conductive filler (CNT) with high sensitivity and an initial 
capacitance value between 1pa and 10kpa.  The utilization of conductive filler increased the 
sensitivity and initial capacitance value of the flexible pressure sensor (made of porous Ecoflex 
containing 0.6wt% of CNT) by ~4 times and ~17times the values of 0wt%, respectively.  
 Second, we examined the mechanism of the porous elastomer based on its mechanical 
and electrical characteristics. By mixing more CNT with the porous elastomer, the gradual closure 
of pores under compression increases the dielectric constant. On the other hand, concentration of 
CNT less impact on stiffness of the porous elastomer. Therefore, overall sensitivity of the sensor 
was increased.  
Third, based on the mechanical and electrical characteristics of the porous elastomer, the 
pressure was developed with high performance in terms of dynamic responses and static responses 
under different environment conditions such as temperature variations, various dust levels, and 
humidity concentrations.  
Finally, to investigate its applicability to wearable devices, we have applied a shielding 
system on the capacitive sensor and we subsequently demonstrated that our sensor can be easily 
utilized as a wearable pressure-sensing device. This demonstration was done by incorporating the 
sensor on (i) a robot-arm system that moves a soft material with pressure feedback control, and on a 
(ii) wrist pulse detection system using tactile electrodes.
