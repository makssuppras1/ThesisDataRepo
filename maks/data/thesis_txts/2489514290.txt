Abstract
The 3212 inhabitants of Aasiaat, situated 230 km North of the Arctic Circle, makes the
town the ﬁfth largest in Greenland. Aasiaat is currently supplied by power and heat
from diesel generators, a waste incineration plant and oil boilers. The Government of
Greenland, Naalakkersuisut, states in the Sector Plan for Energy and Water Supply
from 2017 that “In 2030, the public energy supply must in the widest possible extent
come from renewable energy sources”. This thesis presents a prefeasibility study of
integrating wind and solar energy in Aasiaat, tapping into the debate about the future
of the town’s energy system. The overall objective is to model and optimise the energy
system by possibly integrating wind turbines, solar PV panels and battery energy storage,
without compromising power system stability and reliability in a cost eﬃcient way
to society. The potential of wind and solar energy in Aasiaat was assessed based on
meteorological data. System models were developed in the HOMER Pro and DIgSILENT
PowerFactory softwares. Load ﬂow analyses made with PowerFactory showed that up
to 5 wind turbines can be integrated in the grid without introducing a critical need for
transmission grid enhancements. No attempts were made to suggest control schemes or
analyse the dynamic behaviour of any of the power system components, as those aspects
are suggested as further work. An energy system optimisation model was created with
HOMER and the results of four modelling scenarios were compared based on economic,
technical and environmental parameters. The three modelling scenarios investing in new
RE generation were all more proﬁtable than both the fourth business as usual scenario
(thus oﬀering socio-economic beneﬁts) and a proposed hydropower plant. Of the four
scenarios investigated, results showed that the scenario investing in two Enercon E-53
wind turbines would be most proﬁtable and increase the total renewable energy share to
4.26%. This scenario did not compromise power system steady-state stability. However,
the hydropower plant achieves a much higher renewable energy share. The three RE
scenarios and the proposed hydropower plant could all aid in reaching the energy and
climate targets of Naalakkersuisut and Nukissiorﬁit. Further analyses should investigate
whether wind power and hydropower are mutually exclusive or might oﬀer beneﬁcial
synergies. Key discussion points included the thermal modelling limitations of HOMER,
the complexity of the heating systems in the town, and the importance of considering
diﬀerent indicators when comparing results.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The international call for climate action, and the ambitions to use more sustainable
resources for energy production, also resonates in Greenland. Since the 1980s Greenland
has invested in hydropower considerably increasing the renewable penetration in the
public power supply. Nonetheless, in 2019, Greenland still relies excessively on imported
oil and the interest for utilising renewable resources is growing. The hope is that the
ability to utilise wind and solar energy could reduce costs, increase the security of supply,
reduce the dependency on imported oil, and support the transition to a more sustainable
energy production.
1.1
Nukissiorﬁit and the energy system of Greenland
The 56,000 people living in Greenland inhabit 17 towns and 53 villages1 scattered along
the coast of the 2.2 million km2 island [1]. Nukissiorﬁit, the public utility of Greenland,
has a universal service obligation to supply all towns and villages with energy and water,
instated by Naalakkersuisut, the Government of Greenland [2]. This task is complicated
by the vast distances between towns and villages, and the strenuous arctic weather
conditions. All towns and villages are considered oﬀ-grid communities, Nuuk being
the largest with a population of almost 18,000 [1]. By the term oﬀ-grid is meant that,
with few exceptions, no interconnections between communities exist. In most of the
grids, power is supplied by sets of diesel generators and heat by oil boilers. Since 1993,
Greenland has commissioned 5 hydropower plants, supplying six towns with power and
electric heating to some degree. In 2018, hydropower made up 67% of the total electricity
sold by Nukissiorﬁit [3].
Even with the high hydropower penetration in the public power supply, the full
picture is a bit more nuanced. Greenland is still heavily dependent on imported oil,
since in practice not all consumers are supplied with heat by Nukissiorﬁit. One must
distinguish between the public energy supply, covered by Nukissiorﬁit, and the total
energy supply, including e.g. private individual heating of buildings not connected to
a district heating network or equipped with electric boilers. Figure 1.1 shows the total
primary energy supply (TPES) of Greenland as it has evolved since 1990. Hydropower
is gaining a larger role, but still remains a fraction of the TPES covering 15.8% of the
TPES in 2017 [4]. The fossil fuel share is made up of various oil products, primarily
arctic diesel.

1.1.1
Plans and strategies
Greenland has set targets for the future energy system, including renewable energy
rollout and modernisation of the energy system. Two key documents frame the plans
and strategies for the energy system of Greenland. In 2017, Naalakkersuisut presented
the Sector Plan for Energy and Water Supply [5], deﬁning strategies towards 2030 and
determining the work of Nukissiorﬁit for that time period. The sector plan sets out to
achieve three main goals:
1. Lower prices on electricity and water.
2. Green energy everywhere possible - by 2030, the public energy supply must be
based on renewable energy, to the widest possible extent.
3. Modernisation of the energy system.
The ﬁrst goal was achieved in January 2018 by introducing equal pricing all over Green-
land, regardless of the actual energy production costs in diﬀerent locations. The third
goal includes replacement of fossil fuels with renewable energy sources and a general
improvement of current energy production facilities.
Also in 2017, Nukissiorﬁit made a more short-term strategy covering the years 2018-
2022 [6]. The main goal on the subject of renewable energy is a minimum of 2% yearly
increase in energy production from renewable energy sources in the period 2018-2022.
The strategy speciﬁcally mentions prioritising wind measurements in many locations to
assess the feasibility of wind turbines.

1.2.2
Hydropower for Aasiaat and Qasigiannguit
Aasiaat is part of Qeqertalik Municipality covering the communities in the southern part
of the Disko Bay and on the Disko Island - four towns and eight villages in total. Aasiaat
and Qasigiannguit2 are the largest of the communities, located 68.7 km apart. The
opportunity of supplying Aasiaat, Qasigiannguit and the surrounding villages, Akunnaaq,
Ikamiut and Kitsissuarsuit, by hydropower, has been investigated over many years. The
hydropower potential is located about 20 km southeast of Qasigiannguit. The idea is to
use the lakes Kuusuup Tasia and Qinngap Ilulialeeraa as reservoir for a hydropower plant.
Figure 1.4 shows an overview of the location of Aasiaat, Qasigiannguit, the hydropower
potential and the suggested transmission lines.
The ﬁrst assessments of the hydropower potential in the area began in 1976. Through
the 1980s, several prefeasibility studies on a hydropower plant and transmission lines to
Aasiaat and Qasigiannguit led to a conceptual design [10],[11],[12],[13], yet the Aasiaat
and Qasigiannguit hydropower plant was set aside in order to prioritise more favourable
projects. In 1990, Inatsisartut decided to build Greenland’s ﬁrst hydropower plant near
Nuuk [14].
In 2005, an overview report of the hydropower resources across Greenland was
made [15], and in 2009 through 2012 further prefeasibility studies around Qasigiannguit
were initiated [16],[17],[18],[19], only to be cast aside again in 2015 due to high expected
investment costs. In March 2016, the investigations were reopened leading to the most
recent reports considered in this study:
1. An update of the hydropower plant conceptual design [20],
2. a more general consultancy report on renewable energy in Aasiaat and Qasigiann-
guit [21], and
3. a socioeconomic consequence evaluation of powering Aasiaat and Qasigiannguit
comparing the results of the ﬁrst and second mentioned reports [22].

1.3
Objectives and scope
Why study the integration of renewable energy, such as wind and solar, in Aasiaat, when
a political decision has just been made to focus solely on hydropower? First, the long
history of the potential hydropower plant for Aasiaat and Qasigiannguit indicates that
nothing is ﬁnal, even though current political interest favours hydropower. Further,
the Inatsisartut decision mentions speciﬁcally that the wind potential should also be
investigated [26].

But was the potential of renewable energy in Aasiaat and Qasigiannguit not already
investigated in 2016? Since the consultancy report on renewable energy in Aasiaat
and Qasigiannguit was made in 2016, political plans have changed. The waste strategy
from 2019 [9] means that Aasiaat will lose waste as a resource, as the waste will be

the waste resource makes several central conclusions in the 2016 consultancy report
obsolete. This aspect is elaborated upon in later chapters. Additionally, the 2016 con-

for wind energy assessment. In 2018, a new automatic climate station was set up on a
telecommunication mast at Top 90 with the main target of observing the local wind con-
ditions in this area for the purpose of preliminary investigations on the utilisation of wind
energy [27]. This masters thesis is the ﬁrst study to use data from the new climate station.
Regarding the current energy system in Aasiaat, two visits to the town during the
project period permitted the collection of the most exhaustive data set ever examined
in a single analysis of the energy system in Aasiaat. Data was collected not only from
Nukissiorﬁit, but also local stakeholders such as the waste incineration plant and the
local oil distributor. The data collection is elaborated upon in Chapter 3.
This study may be viewed as a prefeasibility study of integrating wind and solar
energy in Aasiaat tapping into the debate about the future of the energy system in
Aasiaat in case the hydropower plant is decided against. The objective is to model and
optimise the energy system by possibly integrating wind turbines, solar PV panels and
battery energy storage.
1.3.1
Overall research question
The overall research question addressed in this study is formulated as follows:
Can hybridising the energy system of Aasiaat, using wind energy, solar energy and
battery energy storage, optimise the energy system to oﬀer socio-economic beneﬁts, without
compromising power system stability and reliability?
The broad overall research question was broken down into four supporting questions
formulated as follows:
1. How can relevant data, covering the energy demand and generation, be systematic-
ally processed and analysed to establish the current energy supply and demand in
Aasiaat?
2. How can the wind and solar energy potentials be estimated from meteorological data,
and used to analyse the suitability of the diﬀerent renewable energy technologies in
the arctic environment of Aasiaat?

3. How can an optimised energy system conﬁguration for Aasiaat be determined,

1.3.2
Methodology and limitations
The methodology applied in the study can be outlined as:
1. Deﬁnition of problem, objectives and constraints, and mapping of those to technical
requirements
2. Information and literature survey
3. Collection of data
4. Generation and quantiﬁcation of scenarios and conﬁgurations
5. Development of a system model
6. Evaluation of conﬁgurations and the implications of diﬀerent solutions
7. Deciding and specifying recommendations for the ﬁnal optimal solution
As this study may be considered a prefeasibility study of wind and solar energy in
Aasiaat, the results can be used in the early stages of decision making when it comes
to the future of the energy system in Aasiaat. As much as possible, the study relies on
real data. In cases without suﬃcient data, assumptions and limitations has been decided
upon to give a best oﬀer. The remainder of this section lists the key limitations of the
study.
To compare results of scenarios with diﬀerent energy system conﬁgurations, a business
as usual (BAU) scenario is used as a baseline for comparison. Since the exact future of
the waste incineration plant is still not settled, the waste incineration plant is assumed to
be retroﬁtted with oil boilers similar to the boilers at the two other heating plants. Thus,
in the BAU scenario, the heating capacity of the waste incineration plant will remain
but use oil as a resource instead of waste.
The results of the energy system modelling presented in this study are also brieﬂy
compared with the existing hydropower proposals [23], [24], but this study does not
attempt to model the hydropower plant. The challenges of comparing results with
diﬀerent underlying technical and economic assumptions are acknowledged.

The preliminary power system stability analysis presented in this study was used for
setting initial modelling constraints and screening for problematic areas in the system
if the modelling results were to be implemented. For example, it was investigated how
much new generation can be included in a given part of the power system, before a need
for grid enhancements becomes critical. As another example, it was investigated where
the transmission system will be critically loaded in a scenario where the power generation
is no longer centralised at the CHP plant. The power system stability analysis consists
of a series of load ﬂow analyses of diﬀerent scenarios. The load ﬂow analyses assume a
balanced 3-phase, steady-state, non-faulted system and thus, this study does not attempt
to suggest control schemes or dynamic behaviour of any of the power system components.

2.1
Renewable resource assessment
The solar resource in Greenland was assessed by J. Dragsted in 2011 in the PhD thesis
titled Solar heating in Greenland - Resource assessment and potential [28]. The study
found a potential for solar heating in Greenland. While the study focused on solar
heating, the approach for solar resource assessment is similar for solar power. The
master’s thesis Feasibility of Photovoltaics in Greenlandic Power Systems by L. Algren
from 2012 showed that rooftop PV systems are economically feasible in Greenland [29].
In the present study, the solar resource was assessed using the deﬁnitions from Duﬃe
and Beckmann given in Solar Engineering of Thermal Processes [30] and by K. Mertens
in Photovoltaics - Fundamentals, Technology, and Practice [31].
The wind resource in Greenland was assessed in the PhD thesis from 2016 by K.
R. Jakobsen titled Renewable Energy Potential of Greenland with emphasis on wind
resource assessment [32]. The study found signiﬁcant wind potentials in the southern
part of Greenland. The PhD thesis also led to intensiﬁed wind resource assessment and
measuring campaigns. To assess the wind resource in this study, the Wind Resource
Assessment Handbook by the National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL) [33] was
consulted because of its approach to wind resource assessment given diﬀerent amounts of
information. The approach from the article Modern estimation of the parameters of the
Weibull wind speed distribution for wind energy analysis from 2000 by J. Seguro and T.
Lambert [34] was used when calculating the Weibull parameters needed for modelling
the wind conditions [34], [35].

favourable of the six conﬁgurations. The report concludes all conﬁgurations to have a
positive net present value (NPV) as opposed to the 2019 explanatory report [23], which
estimates the NPV of the hydropower plant to be -217 million DKK.
A report on waste heat utilisation from power production in Aasiaat from 2018 by

3.2
Energy system data processing
As any model is only as good as the input data fed to it, careful assessment, processing
and validation of data are necessary to achieve reliable results. Obtaining a data set
encompassing the full perspective on energy generation and consumption in Aasiaat
proved to be a diﬃcult task. Due to changes in daily inspection procedures and later
a renovation of the SCADA system at the CHP plant, most data related to power and
heat generation from the CHP plant from February 2018 to April 2019 has been lost. A
similar situation applies to HP1 and HP2 from January to May 2018. Therefore, 2017
was selected as reference year, since that was the most recent year with a complete data
set covering the CHP plant, HP1, HP2 and WP.
Naturally, a question arises: Is 2017 a representative year for the current energy use
in Aasiaat? Changes in demography, industrial activity, new construction and weather
conditions are examples of factors that could aﬀect the energy demand.
The demographic development in Aasiaat is illustrated in Figure 3.2. The overall
trend from 1977 to 2019 is slightly decreasing, but the population has been growing in the
last ten years. Since 2017, the population has grown by 100 individuals. In this regard,
the year 2017 is deemed to be representative for the current situation. The inﬂuence of
the future demographic development will be investigated as a sensitivity in Section 3.4.5.
Figure 3.2: Population in Aasiaat [1]. Aasiaat has seen an increasing trend in
population since 2008.
No construction or demolition has taken place since 2017 in a magnitude large enough
to change the energy demand signiﬁcantly. In 2013, the shrimp factory had a pilot
scheme involving the purchase of halibut, redﬁsh and catﬁsh, but the factory has not
changed production patterns since at least 2016.
The change in temperature from year to year can be compared using degree days. A
degree day is a measure of cooling or heating. The number of degree days is calculated
as the sum of days with an average outside-temperature below 19°C multiplied with the

Often, degree days are used to scale e.g. heat demands between diﬀerent years or
locations. In this case, since the power and heat generated from the CHP plant and
HP1 in DH net 1 are closely related, the heat demand has not been scaled by the degree

heat demand and not the power demand will blur the synergy between power and heat
generation in DH net 1.

load. The factory does not follow a daily schedule shutting down by night, but rather runs
both days and nights. Using HOMER to generate a synthetic electric load from monthly
averages would result in loosing this pattern from the load data. Appendix B includes
example screenshots from the SCADA system showing the factory start up and shut down.

3.2.2
Heat production & estimated individual heat demand
The CHP plant, the two heating plants, HP1 and HP2, and the waste incineration
plant, WP, all contribute to cover the heat demand in Aasiaat via three disconnected
district heating networks. Yet, there is no complete log of the total heat demand of the
Greenlandic towns and villages. The reason for this is that a large number of buildings
are not connected to a district heating network or in other ways supplied with heat by
Nukissiorﬁit. Instead, these consumers are supplied with heat from individual oil boilers.
No record exist on how many litres of oil a given household or building uses for heating
per year or the capacity and eﬃciency of the installed oil boilers.
Several locations in Greenland, particularly the towns with hydropower, have disrupt-
ible heating in the energy system. Typically, disruptible heating means that individual
oil heating systems have been retroﬁtted with electric heating. The advantage is that
the existing oil boiler can remain as backup in times when the electric heating is reach-
ing maximum capacity or when a hydropower plant, or other renewable generation, is
experiencing outages or production constraints. If a large amount of renewable energy is

to be implemented, it is of great importance to know the amount of disruptible heating
capacity that could possibly be installed, because the disruptible heating can act as a
dump load and increase the renewable share in the energy production.
Having collected data for the three district heating networks, the question of the
amount of heat provided by individual private oil boilers, and consequently part of the
potential disruptible heating capacity remains. As the data basis is limited, two diﬀerent
approaches for estimating the individual and total heating demand were examined.
3.2.2.1
Heat demand based on average speciﬁc consumption
The simplest approach for estimating the total heat demand in Aasiaat is to determine
the average speciﬁc heat demand per person per year. Using national statistics for all
of Greenland, one such estimate was found to be 13-14 MWh/person/year [44]. This
number includes the heating demand of households as well as industry and services,
i.e. the total heat demand for space heating and hot water in a town or village. With
the 3212 Aasiaat residents, this approach results in a total heat demand in Aasiaat of
41,756 - 44,986 MWh/year.
3.2.2.2
Heat demand based on oil import and sales
The more advanced approach used the oil import and sales in Aasiaat to estimate the
individual heat demand. In 2017, approximately 10,500,000 L of oil was imported to
Aasiaat [45]. Of those, 4,450,868 L was bought by Nukissiorﬁit for power and heat
production [46]. Of the remaining approximately 6 mio. L, Aasiaat Olie A/S stated that
about 2.5 mio. L of oil are sold to households for heating purposes every year [47].
Arctic diesel has an energy content of 36 MJ/L [48]. Assuming an average eﬃciency
for the individual oil boilers of 85%, the heat demand covered from individual boilers was
estimated. Combining the result with the known demand covered in the district heating
networks, the total heat demand in Aasiaat is 41,824 MWh/year. This result yields an
average speciﬁc heat consumption of 13 MWh/person/year, in agreement with the ﬁrst
approach. Aasiaat Olie A/S informed that some customers collect their oil at the gas
station and not directly from Aasiaat Olie A/S. Therefore, the actual number could be
slightly higher, but still agree with the ﬁrst approach. Note that the second approach
uses data for 2017 speciﬁcally. As ﬂuctuations between years exist, the importance of
sensitivity analyses on the heat demand is evident.
For the base case, to keep calculations simple and easily scalable with changes in
population, the ﬁrst approach was used conservatively assuming an average speciﬁc heat
demand of 14 MWh/person/year. The estimated total heat demand for 2017 is illustrated

3.3.2
Wind resource assessment
Climate stations in Greenland are often located in relation to airports, heliports, tele-
communication equipment or military installations. A quality assured wind data series,
covering the years 2004-2019, from a DMI operated measurement station by the airport
in Aasiaat, was used to get a ﬁrst impression of the wind conditions in the area. How-
ever, this measurement station was not sited for wind power assessment purposes. The
relatively ﬂat terrain around the airport and low measurement elevation (10 m above
ground) might not give a full insight in the wind conditions in the area.
A new automatic climate station was set up on a site referred to as ’Top 90’ in the
period between October 26th 2018 and April 13th 2019. The name refers to the site’s
location on top of a ridge 90 m above sea level. The climate station was set up with
the main target of observing the local wind conditions in this area for the purpose of
preliminary investigations on utilising wind energy. The measurement equipment is
placed on an existing telecommunication mast, so the location does not reﬂect an actual
planned wind turbine location [27]. Figure 3.9 presents an overview of the area around
Aasiaat with indications of climate stations and areas proposed for wind turbines.
The climate station at Top 90 measures wind speed and wind direction at three
elevations. The highest elevated sensor at 33 m was used here, to be closest to the
wind turbine hub height. Air temperature is also measured. However, other factors also
inﬂuence the wind potential such as the roughness of the terrain or nearby obstacles,
limiting the ability to reliably extrapolate the observations beyond the climate station’s
immediate vicinity. The NREL suggest the following guiding principles for wind resource
assessment [33]:
1. Preliminary area identiﬁcation
Screening of a relatively large region for suitable wind resource areas based on
information such as topography and wind data from e.g. an airport climate station
in the area.
2. Area wind resource evaluation
Characterising the wind resource in a deﬁned area where wind power development is
being considered based on wind measurement programs. Determining or verifying
if suﬃcient wind resources exist within the area to justify further site-speciﬁc
investigations.
Obtaining representative data for estimating the performance
and/or the economic viability of selected wind turbines. Screening for potential
wind turbine sites.

3.4
Energy system optimisation
The Hybrid Optimisation Model for Multiple Energy Resources6 software, abbreviated
HOMER, is a microgrid software for optimising microgrid design, for example village
power systems or island utilities. HOMER was originally developed by the NREL and
later distributed commercially by HOMER Energy, now comprising over 200,000 users
in more than 190 countries [54], making HOMER one of the most widely used tools for
hybrid energy system analysis [55].
HOMER has three core capabilities: Simulation, optimisation and sensitivity ana-
lysis. HOMER simulates the operation of an energy system by making energy balance
calculations in each time step (hour) of the project lifetime. For each time step, HOMER
compares the electric and thermal demand to the energy that the system can supply,
and calculates the ﬂow of energy among the system components. For systems that
include batteries or diesel generators, HOMER decides in each time step how to dispatch
the generators and whether to charge or discharge the batteries. If multiple system
conﬁgurations are considered, including e.g. diﬀerent numbers of given components,
the energy system balance calculations are performed for each, to determine in which
conﬁgurations the electric demand can be met under the speciﬁed conditions. If one
conﬁguration is predetermined, i.e. in a business as usual scenario, HOMER works
as a simulation tool, only optimising operations. For the feasible conﬁgurations, the
cost of installing and operating the system over the lifetime of the project is estimated [37].
6Alternatively referred to as Hybrid Optimization of Multiple Electric Renewables or Hybrid Optim-
ization of Multiple Energy Resources in the HOMER Pro user manual [37] and on the HOMER Energy
website [54], respectively.
==PAGE:47==
3.4 Energy system optimisation
27
The optimisation step follows after all simulations are done. HOMER searches for
the system with lowest total net present cost (NPC). The total NPC represents all the
costs that occur within the project lifetime as a single sum in the base year currency,
with future cash ﬂows discounted back to the base year using the discount rate [56].
Costs may include capital costs, replacement costs, operating and maintenance costs,
fuel costs and component salvage values. By default, HOMER calculates the salvage of
any component with remaining technical lifetime after the project lifetime had ended.
A linear depreciation of components is assumed, meaning that the salvage value of a
component is directly proportional to its remaining life [37]. The salvage values were
disregarded in this thesis, because the salvage values of existing diesel generators sug-
gested by HOMER were deemed much to high considering the age and condition of
the generators. The optimisation process is repeated for each speciﬁed sensitivity variable.
HOMER was chosen as the main tool for this project because of its ability to eﬀortlessly
handle base case simulation, optimisation with a wide range of system components and
sensitivity analyses. Another reason for choosing HOMER was the fact that Nukissiorﬁit
already uses HOMER for hybrid energy system simulation in prefeasibility studies.
Additionally, two earlier student projects with Nukissiorﬁit successfully used HOMER
for similar investigations [36], [57].
3.4.1
General modelling assumptions
A few general assumptions apply to all modelling scenarios in the base case, the base case
representing the normal or expected operation for comparison with sensitivity analyses.
The lifetime of the project was set to 20 years from 2020 to 2039. All investments happen
in year 1. Discounted reinvestments in system components occur when the technical
lifetime of a given component has ended. Any remaining lifetime of components was
disregarded after the end of the project lifetime. A 4% socio-economic discount rate
was used in the base case. System losses were assumed to be the same during the whole
project period.

3.4.2.4
Heating
Incorporating the various present and possible heating technologies in Aasiaat in a single
HOMER model was where HOMER got pushed to its limits and some of the main
drawbacks of the software emerged. In HOMER, a thermal load can be served by a
generator from which waste heat can be recovered, by a boiler, or by surplus electri-
city [37]. Thus, for modelling purposes, the heat supply in Aasiaat must be grouped into
the three categories i) waste heat from the CHP plant, ii) oil based heat generation, and
iii) disruptible electric boilers. Put simply, category i) was modelled by specifying the
heat recovery ratio of the diesel generators. Category ii) was an aggregation of HP1,
HP2, WP and all individual oil boilers, while category iii) was modelled as a thermal
load controller utilising excess electricity from renewable generation.
The aggregation of the category ii) elements was necessary as only one single boiler
can be included in a HOMER model. When simulating the energy system, HOMER
prioritises serving the electric load over serving the thermal load. When dispatching
generators to serve the electric load, HOMER uses any waste heat that can be recovered
==PAGE:49==
3.4 Energy system optimisation
29
from each generator, but a generator is never dispatched just to serve the thermal load.
HOMER assumes that the separate boiler can serve any thermal load not covered by
generator waste heat. In practice, the boiler is treated as a backup source of heat that
can serve any amount of thermal load whenever necessary.
The consequence of modelling HP1, HP2, the WP and all the individual oil boilers as
a single inﬁnite boiler has two disadvantages. First, since the boiler is treated as inﬁnite
and always available, the only parameters to be speciﬁed by the user are the eﬃciency
and the type of fuel. There is no way to set other operating constraints, OPEX or
reinvestment costs. Second, the eﬃciencies of all four sources of heat must be combined
into a single eﬃciency. The waste incineration plant was assumed to be retroﬁtted with
oil boilers, but otherwise remain as heat supply for DH net 3 with an eﬃciency of 92%
corresponding to the eﬃciency of the other oil based heat plants, HP1 and HP2. The
individual oil boilers were assumed to have an average eﬃciency of 85%. Since the
eﬃciency of the boiler is associated with great uncertainty, this particular parameter was
tweaked during the model validation to make the model reﬂect the actual operation as
closely as possible.
Another heat related issue is that HOMER is only able to model a single heat bus.
This means, that the three DH networks cannot be treated as isolated, as the thermal
load is considered as one accumulated load. Previous reports have recommended actions
to connect or expand the three district heating networks in Aasiaat [8], but this aspect
can not reliably be investigated with HOMER alone.
To counter and assess the eﬀects of the mentioned disadvantages, a base case validation
considering only the power grid and DH net 1 was used for a step wise validation of
model parameters. The results are presented in Chapter 4.
3.4.3
Resources
The fossil fuel resource used for the diesel generators and boiler was arctic diesel. Para-
meters such as density and lower heating value are given in Appendix A. The diesel

The measured 5 minute solar resource data, described in Section 3.3.1, was used as

index is a dimensionless number between zero and one, indicating the fraction of the
solar radiation making it through the atmosphere to the Earth’s surface [37].
The wind resource was synthesised in HOMER using the information in Table 3.3
in Section 3.3.2 and assuming that the wind distribution at Top 90 follows a Weibull

the breadth of a distribution of wind speeds. HOMER ﬁts a Weibull distribution to
the wind speed data, and the shape factor value refers to the shape of that distribution.
When ﬁtting a Weibull distribution to measured wind data, HOMER uses the maximum
likelihood method given by Stevens and Smulders [35]. The wind speed variation with
height is governed by the roughness length [51], which represents the height above ground
level, where the average wind speed is 0 m/s. This input was important to account for
the wind speed measurements being made at a diﬀerent height than the wind turbine
hub height. A roughness length of 0.003 m, corresponding to a snow surface, was used,
as this represents the surface in the months with highest average wind speeds.
3.4.4
Modelling scenarios
Based on initial test runs of the model, four key scenarios were selected for analysis and
comparison. The following subsections brieﬂy describe each scenario.
3.4.4.1
Scenario A: Business as usual
The business as usual (BAU) scenario represents the current energy system in Aasiaat and
thus the baseline for scenario comparison. No investments were made in new technologies,
and HOMER was used as a simulation tool, only optimising operations. A variation
of this scenario including only the power generation and DH net 1 was used for model
validation.
3.4.4.2
Scenario B: Renewable energy
Scenario B represents the current energy system in Aasiaat but now giving HOMER the
opportunity to invest in new solar PV, two diﬀerent wind turbines and battery energy
storage. In this scenario, it was assumed that power grid enhancements must be limited
as much as possible. PowerFactory analyses showed that up to 5 Enercon E-53 turbines
can be installed before the need for grid enhancements gets critical, as will be elaborated
in Section 3.5. Therefore, an upper bound of ﬁve Enercon E-53 turbines was used.
3.4.4.3
Scenario C: Renewable energy and introduction of disruptible
electrical heating
Scenario C gives HOMER the opportunity to invest in new solar PV, two diﬀerent wind
turbines, battery energy storage and up to 7825 kW disruptible electric heating capacity.
The limit for the disruptible electric heating capacity was based on the list of buildings
that could be equipped with electric boilers along with the sizes of the suggested boilers
from the 2016 conceptual design [20]. The upper bound of 5 Enercon E-53 turbines was
also used in this scenario.

3.5
Load ﬂow analysis
The overall research question stated that the energy system optimisation must be done
without compromising power system stability and reliability. In this domain, HOMER’s
capabilities fall short, as HOMER does not consider power system stability in any way.
In order to assess whether the current power system can accommodate the optimised
energy system conﬁgurations, supplementary methods and tools had to be selected.
Reliability generally represents the continuity of power supply to consumers, which
depends on both the availability of suﬃcient generation capacity to meet the demand and
on the ability of the transmission and distribution system to deliver the power [40]. As the
electricity demand is currently covered by the diesel generators at the CHP plant, there
is already suﬃcient generation to serve the current demand. In the HOMER scenarios,
the optimisation constraints were deﬁned such that no amount of unserved load was
accepted, which means that suﬃcient generation is available in all four scenarios. The
most typical interruptions of power are those associated with events in the distribution
system [40]. As such, it is necessary to investigate what will happen to the transmission
and distribution system in the event of renewable energy integration.
Power system stability is the ability of a power system to maintain a balanced operat-
ing state. A distinction is made between steady-state stability and transient or dynamic
stability. In the steady-state case, a system’s stability is evaluated under a ﬁxed set of
==PAGE:53==
3.5 Load ﬂow analysis
33
operating conditions, including constant loads and generator outputs. A central factor is
the length of transmission lines in relation to the amount of power they transmit [40].
Transient stability covers the system’s ability to handle sudden changes, such as faults,
loss of a transmission line, or failure of large generation, and promptly return from the
transient condition to a stable operating state [40]. Transient and dynamic stability
analyses are beyond the scope of this thesis.
Eﬃcient power system operation, under non-faulted, balanced three-phase, steady-
state conditions, requires that generation meets the demand (including losses), bus
voltage magnitudes remain close to rated values, generation operates within speciﬁed real
and reactive power limits, and transformers and transmission lines are not overloaded [39].
A load ﬂow analysis is a widely used procedure for examining these requirements. The
load ﬂow analysis determines the active and reactive power ﬂows for all system branches
and the voltage magnitude and angle at each bus. Input data consist of generation and
load data, transmission line data and transformer data. A load ﬂow analysis can be
performed to investigate both present and future operation of power systems [39], [65].
Under balanced steady state conditions all variables and parameters are held constant
during the period of observation [65].
DIgSILENT PowerFactory was the tool selected to perform the load ﬂow analysis
of the power system in Aasiaat.
PowerFactory is a leading power system analysis
software for analysing generation, transmission, distribution and industrial systems [38].
PowerFactory uses the Newton-Raphson load ﬂow, widely described in literature [39], [65].
The software was chosen for its large component library, ability to deﬁne grid variations
and case studies, and convenient graphical user interface.

change in oil price, while the scenarios are least sensitive to changes in CAPEX. The
two latter tendencies are partly explained by the minor fraction of the NPC made up by
CAPEX and the major fraction made up of fuel costs.
A higher discount rate means that future cost is low, making the overall project
cheaper, while a lower discount rate means that future cost is high, making the overall
project more costly. The diﬀerence between scenarios is explained by the amount of new
investments, i.e. on the CAPEX. When the CAPEX makes up a larger share of the total
NPC, fewer costs are discounted in the future, and the scenario will be less sensitive to
the discount rate.

in oil price expectantly aﬀects the scenarios with the highest oil consumption the most,

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
This chapter presents a general discussion of the methodology and results. First, the
methodology and methods applied are discussed, followed by an evaluation of the results
presented in Chapter 5. Some uncertainties, risks, and barriers to RE implementation in
Aasiaat are also mentioned and lastly the discussion touches upon the generalisability of
results.
6.1
Methodology
The project scope was quite wide in terms of methods and modelling, but at the same
time very narrow in terms of geography, considering only Aasiaat and its immediate
surroundings, and not e.g. wind power assessments across Greenland. The wide scope
might have limited the depth of analysis in certain parts of the project, however, consid-
ering the previous reports related to renewable energy in Aasiaat, it was clear that a
wider scope with a particularly strong focus on data collection and processing would be a
valuable addition in the decision making on the future of the energy system in Aasiaat.
6.1.1
Collection of data and data processing
As mentioned in Chapter 1, the models and analyses in this study were based on the
largest data set ever considered in a single report on renewable energy in Aasiaat. This
does not mean that the data set was perfect. A lot of assumptions were made, particularly
in relation to the heat demand supplied by individual private oil boilers. In Section
3.3.2 covering the wind resource assessment, a previous study [36] was boldly criticised
for assuming an average annual wind speed of 6 m/s. However, the estimates and
assumptions made in this study were based on a data set only marginally better than
the data base available for the previous study. Had it not been for the time constrained
nature of a master’s thesis, the wind resource assessment ought to have waited until at
least a full year of quality assured wind data was available. It would be interesting to
eventually compare wind data covering several years. Aasiaat is the northernmost ’open
water town’, i.e. the sea does not always freeze in winters. Some winters it does, and the
sea ice aﬀects whether Aasiaat experience an inland climate or a coastal climate. The
wind conditions might reﬂect this variation.
==PAGE:70==

6 Discussion
6.1.2
Appropriateness of tools
Overall, HOMER and PowerFactory proved to be very convenient tools, as long as
the results are considered the product of a prefeasibility study. The modelling tools
themselves introduced some limitations and uncertainties.
6.1.2.1
HOMER
At the time of selecting HOMER as the main modelling tool, it was favoured for its
ability to combine electric and thermal models into a joint hybrid energy system model.
During the study, the signiﬁcance of the multifaceted heating system and the limitations
of HOMER in thermal modelling became more evident. The limitations of HOMER were
reviewed in Chapter 3 and 4, but the most central limitations are repeated here:
1. The inability to model multiple heat buses,
2. the aggregation of oil based heating capacity as a single inﬁnite boiler, and
3. the lack of power system stability considerations.
Several recommendations to expand or optimise the DH systems in Aasiaat have been
put forward by previous reports. The previous reports [8], [21] recommended a number
of measures to be taken before implementing large scale RE. According to the reports,
the waste heat utilisation of the CHP plant needs to be optimised and further all the
three DH networks should be connected and expanded to increase heat utilisation. As
the heat demand is modelled as one combined thermal load in HOMER, it is not possible
to assess the inﬂuence of changes in the DH networks in HOMER. Neither does HOMER
capture the eﬀect on DH net 1 when some of the CHP plant power generation is replaced
by renewable generation and thus less cogeneration heat is produced, as was the case in
scenario B, C and D. HOMER is an excellent tool for modelling the electric side of the
energy system, and could easily be used to optimise the electric energy system, as this is
the core competency of the software. On the thermal side, a lot of nuances were lost.
A dedicated tool for heating systems, or another energy system analysis tool including
these aspects, should be used in further assessments. Regarding the lack of power system
stability considerations, PowerFactory was used to mitigate this limitation.
6.1.2.2
PowerFactory
PowerFactory was an ideal tool for the prefeasibility study of wind turbine grid integration
in Aasiaat. The load ﬂow analysis used only a fraction of the software’s wide functionality,
and the software could deﬁnitely be used to expand the power system analyses to include
the distribution grid (assuming availability of detailed load data). Further work should
include dynamic and transient stability analyses.

6.1.3
Scenario generation
Generation and quantiﬁcation of modelling scenarios and conﬁgurations implied selecting
suitable components and resources to include in the models. This task also meant
deciding which components not to include in the models.
A very central assumption in this thesis was the retroﬁtting of the waste incineration
plant to utilise oil boilers as a result of the plans to consolidate waste incineration in
Nuuk and Sisimiut. For that reason, waste has not been investigated as a resource in
this study. Very recently, on December 12th 2019, the Greenlandic newspaper Sermitsiaq
covered the story of the most northern municipality, Avannaata Kommunia, wanting to
leave the waste program altogether [67], [68]. Instead, they want to construct smaller
waste incineration plants locally in the villages within the municipality. Depending
on the eﬀect on the waste plans after one of ﬁve municipalities withdraw from the
deal, the future of the waste incineration plant in Aasiaat may soon be up for debate
once more, potentially making the exclusion of the waste resource a weakness in this study.
Other studies related to hybrid energy system optimisation in Greenland have in-
cluded low load generators as an investment option in HOMER models [36], [57]. In
this study it was decided to keep a strong focus on the renewable energy technologies
and therefore new diesel based generation was not considered. Not including new low
load generators might be a weakness in this study. However, according to the report
on waste heat utilisation in Aasiaat, the CHP plant was expanded in three phases with
MG5 in 1990, MG6 in 2006 and MG7 in 2010. This gradual expansion has resulted in
the CHP plant showing that there has not been an overall plan, which has resulted in
design errors and inconveniences [8]. It might be more eﬃcient to focus on optimising
the existing generation rather than risking continuing in this track by adding another
diesel generator.
Investigating the feasibility of heat pumps as an alternative or supplement to disrupt-
ible electric heating was also omitted. The operation of air to air heat pumps should
preferably be continuous over longer periods. The eﬃciency of the heat pump is reduced
if it runs discontinuously [21]. Therefore, air to air heat pumps might not be suitable as
dump load and therefore unable to support intermittent renewable energy integration.
In scenario B, C and D, wind turbines were favoured over solar PV panels. In all
scenarios, HOMER suggested other feasible but more costly scenarios including both
wind turbines and solar PV panels. This indicates that it might not be economically
attractive for Nukissiorﬁit to invest in solar PV panels in a large scare in Aasiaat, but on
the other hand, there is no reason that Nukissiorﬁit should prevent private individuals
from installing solar PV panels. Including a scenario where HOMER was allowed to
invest in solar PV, but not wind turbines, might have cast light over the competitiveness
of solar PV in Aasiaat.

6 Discussion
6.2
Results
In Chapter 1, the last step in the methodology outline was stated as: Deciding and
specifying recommendations for the ﬁnal optimal solution. But how can a ﬁnal optimal
solution be deducted from the results presented in Chapter 5? Depending on the eco-
nomic, environmental, or energy indicator in focus, diﬀerent scenarios could be declared
the optimal solution.
With a strictly economic focus, Scenario D has the lowest NPC and LCOE, while
Scenario B is the most proﬁtable in terms of the PI. Scenario B, C and D all proved
to be more proﬁtable than the BAU scenario as well as the proposed hydropower plant
regardless of the diﬀerent approaches to PI calculation described in Chapter 5. Given the
high uncertainty associated with scenario D, scenario B could be declared the best solu-
tion in this regard. From an environmental perspective, scenario C and D undoubtedly
beat scenario B in terms of fuel savings and avoided CO2 emissions. The same is true
for the energy indicators of the total RES and RES for light and power. Considering
those indicators might motivate further investigation in scenario D. However, none of
the investigated scenarios can compete with the hydropower plant total RES of 72% and
RES for light and power of 100%.
Turning the attention to the actual goals and strategies in place, the Sector Plan for
Energy and Water Supply stated the goal that by 2030, the public energy supply must be
based on renewable energy, to the widest possible extent [5]. What exactly is meant by
’the widest possible extent’ is unclear. If the widest possible extent is measured by the
RES, regardless of economic factors, there is no doubt that the hydropower plant is the
best solution.
The Nukissiorﬁit 2018-2020 strategy has a more quantiﬁed goal: A minimum of 2%
yearly increase in energy production from renewable sources in the period 2018-2022 [6].
From the time when the construction of the hydropower plant is decided, it is expected
to take at least ﬁve years before the hydropower plant is commissioned [20]. Therefore,
the potential hydropower plant does not aﬀect the current Nukissiorﬁit strategy. But
what about the wind power scenarios? Scenario B contributes with a 0.6% increase in
energy production from renewable sources, while scenario C and D contribute with 1.4%
and 3.6%, respectively. The three scenarios would all be ﬁne contributions to reach the
strategic goal, but only scenario D would be able to fulﬁl the goal on its own in the year
of commissioning.
Even though the Nukissiorﬁit strategy is convenient for quantifying the best solution,
it might not be wise to use only this indicator for decisions. Other actions than RE
implementation could e.g. reduce the fuel consumption. Connection and expansion of the
DH networks and other energy eﬃciency measures might be even more economically and
environmentally attractive, despite the fact that no additional RE generation is installed.
==PAGE:73==
6.3 Uncertainties, risks and barriers
53
So far the comparison between the investigated scenarios and the proposed hydropower
plant has considered solutions mutually exclusive. In 2016, it was estimated that it
would take at least ﬁve years before the hydropower plant was commissioned [20], but
since then constructions of new airports for Nuuk and Ilullisat have begun. Further,
according to the explanatory report from 2019 [23], the hydropower plant for Aasiaat and
Qasigiannguit is conditional on an expansion of the hydropower plant in Nuuk, pushing
the commissioning date farther into the future. To achieve the goals of both strategies,
the ﬁnal best solution could be to not consider wind power and hydropower mutually
exclusive, but rather implementing e.g. scenario B ﬁrst to utilise the wind resource until
a hydropower plant is in place. Selecting e.g. scenario C or D including disruptible
electric heating could also aid the energy system in a smoother transition to shifting more
oil based heating to electric heating. This shift is key to utilising the full potential of the
proposed hydropower plant [23]. Any synergies, or lack thereof, between the proposed
hydropower plant and wind turbines in Aasiaat should be investigated in further work.
6.3
Uncertainties, risks and barriers
So far, uncertainties in data, assumptions and methods have been reviewed. This section
touches upon some uncertainties and risks not easily measured or monetised.
In the event that a decision should be made to proceed and install wind turbines
in Aasiaat and not build the proposed hydropower plant, one piece will be missing
in the puzzle: What about Qasigiannguit? The proposed hydropower plant would be
able to supply both Aasiaat and Qasigiannguit, and Qasigiannguit has no known wind
potential. It it not unseen that investments in a small community can inﬂuence the
general perception of the community’s future perspectives and prospects. Qasigiann-
guit currently experiences a slow depopulation, but building a hydropower plant in
the area might even have an impact on the demographic trend by changing the incent-
ives to stay. These changes might provide other beneﬁts not related to the energy systems.
A highly uncertain factor in this study is the total heating demand in Aasiaat. In
this study, the total heating demand was estimated to fall between 41.7 GWh/year
and 45 GWh/year, but earlier works have estimated the heating demand as high as
78 GWh/year [23], [36]. Using degree days to scale the 2017 result to a normal year
results in a total heating demand of 40 GWh/year, but the number could be as low as
34 GWh/year, judging from the temperatures through the last 10 years.
Regarding the barriers to RE implementation in Aasiaat, the uncertainties related
to the total heating demand, potential for installation of disruptible electric heating,
and in turn the energy sales potential is viewed as the largest barrier to eﬃcient RE
implementation in the town. Regardless if the future energy system in Aasiaat will have
wind turbines or hydropower, it would be worth the eﬀort to map the actual heating
==PAGE:74==
54

demand, as this is an uncertainty present in all reports on the energy system in Aasiaat.
Another barrier would be increased maintenance costs due to lack of local expertise
and accessibility. Earlier works states those as the main challenges in the implementation
of RE technologies in remote areas [50]. On the other hand, investing in RE technologies
could lead Nukissiorﬁit to obtain more technical competencies and making RE technolo-
gies more mature in a Greenlandic context. This would be another not easily monetised
beneﬁt to future projects.
6.4
Generalisability
This study showed that by combining the HOMER and PowerFactory modelling tools,
valuable results can be generated for evidence-based decision making regarding renewable
energy integration in a Greenlandic context. In the case of Aasiaat, HOMER turned out
to have some constricting limitations, but the methodology and methods could easily
be generalised to the villages and possibly the smallest of towns with simpler heating
systems. The combination of HOMER and PowerFactory made the suggested solution
more robust, as PowerFactory provided a power system steady-state stability analysis in
addition to the energy system optimisation made with HOMER. For increased robustness,
the dynamic and transient system behaviour should be included in the investigation.
The results generated for Aasiaat are another matter. There is not much reason
to assume, that conditions in all corners of the world’s largest island would be the
same. From north to south, towns and villages experience diﬀerent outside temperatures,
weather conditions, topography and migration patterns, all of which aﬀect the energy
demand. Therefore, as the scope was very narrow in terms of geographical location, so
are the results. For example, it would be wrong to conclude from this study that wind
energy is generally more feasible than solar energy in Greenland. The results obtained
from following the methodology applied in this study are very case speciﬁc.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
This study investigated if hybridising the energy system of Aasiaat using wind energy,
solar energy and battery energy storage could optimise the energy system and oﬀer
socio-economic beneﬁts, without compromising power system stability and reliability.
The overall conclusion to this investigation is positive.
Information and data covering demand proﬁles, energy generation, and meteorological
conditions were systematically collected, processed and analysed to describe and analyse
the current energy system in Aasiaat. The largest uncertainties in the data were related
to the heating demand not supplied by Nukissiorﬁit and the wind conditions at Top 90.
The wind and solar energy potentials were assessed based on meteorological data, and the
assessments showed that there are potentials for both wind and solar utilisation in Aasiaat.
System models were developed in the HOMER Pro and DIgSILENT PowerFactory
softwares. Components and parameters were carefully selected for the models to analyse
the suitability of diﬀerent renewable energy technologies in Aasiaat. Load ﬂow analyses
made with PowerFactory showed that up to 5 wind turbines can be integrated in the grid
without introducing a critical need for transmission grid enhancements. Because of the
radial design of the transmission grid, care must be taken to connect the wind turbines
so as to avoid contingencies. Based on the load ﬂow analyses, transformer substation
N2 was suggested for wind turbine grid connection. A key limitation of this study was
that no attempts were made to suggest control schemes or analyse the dynamic beha-
viour of any of the power system components. Those aspects are suggested as further work.
An energy system optimisation model was created with HOMER. The results of four
modelling scenarios were compared based on economic, technical and environmental
parameters. Of the four scenarios investigated, results showed that the scenario investing
in two Enercon E-53 wind turbines would be most proﬁtable in terms of PI and increase
the total RES to 4.26%. It would be almost as proﬁtable to invest in nine wind turbines
and 7100 kW disruptible electric heating capacity thereby increasing the total RES to
43.91%. The latter scenario assumed that nine diﬀerent sites suitable for wind turbine
installation were found with a mean annual average wind speed of at least 5.15 m/s.
This scenario served as a perspective on the gains from a continued search for better
wind conditions, but the scenario itself is associated with high uncertainty.
The three modelling scenarios investing in new RE generation were all more proﬁtable
than both the BAU scenario (thus oﬀering socio-economic beneﬁts) and the proposed
==PAGE:76==
56
7 Conclusion
hydropower plant in terms of PI. The three RE scenarios all lower the LCOE compared
to the BAU scenario. However, the hydropower plant achieves much higher RES while a
large number of wind turbines is needed to signiﬁcantly displace oil based heating. There
is no doubt that the three RE scenarios and the proposed hydropower plant could all aid
in reaching the energy and climate targets of Naalakkersuisut and Nukissiorﬁit. Further
analyses should investigate whether wind power and hydropower are mutually exclusive
or might oﬀer beneﬁcial synergies.
Finally, the methodology, methods, data and results were critically assessed and
discussed. Key discussion point included the thermal modelling limitations of HOMER
and the importance of considering diﬀerent indicators when comparing results.
7.1
Recommendations for Nukissiorﬁit
1. Regardless if it is decided to proceed with the plans to build a hydropower plant, to
install any amount of wind turbines and/or disruptible electric heating, or to make
any other major change in the energy system, the value of mapping the actual
heating demand in Aasiaat can hardly be stressed enough.
2. In general, data collection and documentation should be improved. The new
SCADA system at the CHP plant is a great improvement, but should be expanded
to include the heat generation at HP2.
3. The climate station at Top 90 should be continually maintained and the collected
data used for further wind resource assessment when a larger data set is available.
4. It is not feasible for Nukissiorﬁit to invest in solar PV panels in a large scale,
however, there is nothing that indicates that local eﬀorts to install solar PV panels
should not be supported.
5. Regarding the use of tools,
• HOMER should be used with caution if modelling energy systems with complex
heating systems.
• PowerFactory is an excellent tool, but should only be adopted by Nukissiorﬁit,
if it would create value in the long term.
6. Possible synergies or lack thereof between hydropower and wind turbines should
be investigated. If it is decided to build the hydropower plant, consider erecting a
number of Enercon E-53 turbines and possibly installing an amount of disruptible
electric heating to make the energy system changes required to fully utilise the
hydropower plant more gradual.
7. If the hydropower plant is eventually decided against, consider erecting two Enercon
E-53 turbines in Aasiaat. Apart from the socio-economic and environmental beneﬁts,
the technical maturity of wind turbines in Greenland would be greatly improved.

The distribution of the power production between months vary greatly between years, as
exempliﬁed in Figure C.1. There is no clear pattern over a given year. The variation can
partly be explained by variation in weather, more speciﬁcally the spread of the sea ice.

2016, but not in 2017. Take the example of comparing with the ice charts in Figure C.4

around Aasiaat was open in mid-April 2016, while the water along almost the entire
coast of the Disko Bay was frozen hard in mid-April 2017. As the shrimp factory is
the largest power consumer in Aasiaat, the power production is directly aﬀected by the
ﬁshing conditions. The ﬁshing conditions are in turn aﬀected by the weather conditions
including, but not limited to, the spread of sea ice in winter.
