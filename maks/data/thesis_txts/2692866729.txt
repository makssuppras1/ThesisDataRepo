Development of γδT17 cells

ABSTRACT 
T cells that express a TCRγδ on their surface are functionally programmed during embryonic and 
neonatal development into two effector functions, that can either produce IL-17 or IFN-γ. The IL-17 
producers are characterized by the expression of the transcription factor RORγt while the IFN-γ 
producers express T-bet. They have a very important role within the intestinal lamina propria where 
some of them migrate to after birth. Upon entering the gut, IL-17 producing γδ T (γδT17) cells 
upregulate T-bet, to become RORγt+T-bet+. Their thymic dependence, temporal localization in the gut 
and potential plasticity are studied in this project. To study the thymic dependence of γδT17 cells,  
Nude mice were used and it was observed that their development and presence in the intestinal 
mucosa requires a thymus, as Nude mice do not have any γδT17 cells in the gut lamina propria. LP 
γδT17 cells could be identified at day 1 after birth and embryonic day 18.5, suggesting that these cells 
leave the thymus to localize in the gut before birth. This data also indicate the possibility that γδT17 
cells first colonize the small intestine before going to the colon. Finally, Rorc-CreRFP:RorcGFP mice 
were used to study the plasticity of these cells and it was found that, at steady-state, intestinal γδT17 
cells are not fully plastic as they do not become T-bet single positive.  Together, this data suggest that 
γδT17 cells can only develop  with a thymus and migrate to the gut already before birth, where they 
will upregulate T-bet without losing RORγt expression at steady-state.

INTRODUCTION 
γδ T CELL DEVELOPMENT 
There is still a lot that is unknown know about γδ T cells. They are innate-like, as they do not seem to 
require antigen processing and major-histocompatibility-complex (MHC) presentation of peptide 
epitopes. However, some of them recognize MHC class Ib molecules via their TCR (Murphy & Weaver, 
2017). They also have the ability to recognize stress antigens, enabling them to produce pro-
inflammatory cytokines that regulate pathogen clearance and keep tissue homeostasis in response to 
stress (Bonneville et al., 2010), making them very important in fighting a lot of immune diseases. Most 
of them produce the pro-inflammatory cytokines interleukin IL-17A or interferon IFN-γ, but some 
subsets may have a regulatory function (Murphy & Weaver, 2017). They can therefore have either a 
role of enhancement or suppression of inflammation. 
Origin, early development and migration to the thymus 
T cells are very important leukocytes of our immune system and play a central role in organizing 
immune responses, especially antigen-specific immunity. They can be easily distinguished from other 
cells by the presence of their surface T-cell receptor (TCR). They originate from hematopoietic stem 
cells (HSCs) in the fetal liver during embryonic life, and in the bone marrow after birth. These HSCs 
differentiate into multipotent progenitors (MPP) which can give rise to either myeloid cells or lymphoid 
cells. They differentiate into an early T cell precursor (ETP) which can give rise to T, B or NK cells, and 
migrate to the thymus for development (Murphy & Weaver, 2017). 
In the thymus, stages of T cell development are most often defined by the lack of the CD4 and CD8 
surface markers. Cells that are negative for both are termed double-negative (DN), cells that express 
both are called double-positive (DP), whereas cells that express either CD4 or CD8 are termed single-
positive (SP). During the DN stage, there are 4 developmental stages that are described in Figure 1.1. 
Owing to their lack of CD4 and CD8 expression, γδ T cells will never be DP or SP thymocytes.  ETPs 
arrive in the thymus as DN CD4−CD8−CD44+CD25−c-kit+. These cells will become DN1 after dividing and 
down-regulating c-kit. 
γδ T cell lineage commitment 
The DN1 cells upregulate CD25 and become DN2 cells (CD4−CD8−CD44+CD25+c-kit−). At the DN2 stage 
the cells downregulate CD44 to become DN3a cells. TCR β, γ, and δ gene rearrangement occurs 
between the DN2 and DN3 stages. There is still very little knowledge about the exact timing of the αβ 
versus γδ lineage commitment. Below, the three main hypotheses that help explain how this decision 
is brought about is discussed.

Instructive model: 
The instructive model explained by Kang et al. in 1997 was the first hypothesis trying to describe the 
lineage commitment between αβ and γδ T cells. Knowing that 67% of the time the TCR rearrangement 
is not successful (the V and J genes are rearranged in the incorrect translational reading frame), the 
hypothesis’s base is that the fate decision will be determined simply by the probability that the correct 
TCR genes are rearranged productively in a given cell. If a precursor DN thymocyte successfully 
rearranges its γ and δ genes and expresses a functional γδTCR, then it will adopt a γδ fate. On the other 
side, if it successfully rearranges its α and β chains first, it will adopt the αβ lineage fate (Joonsoo Kang 
& Raulet, 1997).  
The idea for this model comes from two observations and suggestions. The first one has been detailed 
by Allison and Lanier in 1987, suggesting that the precursor T cell would first attempt to rearrange its 
γ and δ genes to generate a γδ T cell and if it fails, then the β gene rearrangement is made (Allison & 
Lanier, 1987). Another hypothesis suggested that the three genes would be rearranged at the same 
time and that a competition between them would occur until a functional TCR is developed in order 
to determine the fate of the cell (Livak et al., 1995).  
A schematic understanding of this model is drawn in Figure 1.2.A. This model was the at very beginning 
of discoveries and research on lineage fate commitment and gave rise to other hypothesis few years 
after. 
Stochastic model: 
The stochastic model proposes that the lineage fate is determined prior to rearrangement of the TCR 
loci. A thymic progenitor generates two distinct lineage-committed populations at or before DN2 stage 
for either a γδ or αβ fate. It has been suggested (Porritt et al., 2004) that there are already genetic 
differences among the DN1 cells, creating different subsets of cells. These differences are seen as

different levels of expression of surface markers, showing that in some precursor cells, the initial 
lineage commitment is independent on TCR signaling. Indeed, a γδ selection has been shown to induce 
higher CD45, Egr and Runx3 expression compared to β selection, but also less proliferation (Taghon et 
al., 2006).  
It has also been shown that IL-7Rα-mediated signals are not only necessary for the survival of early 
progenitors but also have a critical role in γδ T cell development (J. Kang et al., 2001). IL-7 signaling is 
required for γδ T cell development, as IL-7R deficient mice lack all γδ T cells (He YW, 1996). Indeed, this 
receptor is involved in the synthesis of the TCRγ chain (and not β or δ). High levels of IL-7Rα favor γδ T 
cell commitment, whereas low levels tend to favor the αβ lineage (J. Kang et al., 2001).  
The transcription factor (TF) Sox13 also regulates the differentiation between γδ and αβ T 
lymphocytes, showing the lineage-fate decision at the molecular level (Melichar et al., 2007). Sox13 
was first identified as a γδ-specific gene that promotes γδ T cell development and is expressed in only 
a subset of the DN2 cells. Indeed, Sox13 inhibits αβ-lineage development and therefore its expression 
is turned off from DN3 stage in future αβ T cells (Melichar et al., 2007). The fact that Sox13 is expressed 
by only a subset of cells that will preferentially choose the γδ lineage before TCR expression shows that 
the choice of lineage does not necessarily depend on TCR. It has been confirmed by Spidale et al. in 
2019, wo renamed the Sox13-expressing cells “Soxpro”, for Sox13-progenitors that are pre-committed 
to the γδ lineage. So far, Sox13 is the only TF that has been identified to bias the lineage of the cell 
(Spidale et al., 2019). 
All the above suggests that there is a form of lineage commitment that occurs prior to or during TCR 
rearrangement. This commitment can be described by the expression of Sox13 or IL-7R for example, 
even if there might be some other markers that lead the lineage fate prior to TCR expression. 
Therefore, these cells become either αβ or γδ lineage-committed precursors.  
The stochastic model suggests that γδ lineage-committed precursors that express a functional TCRγδ 
will develop into a γδ population (whether or not they have expressed TCRβ chain). On the other side, 
αβ lineage-committed precursors that express a pre-TCR (composed of a surrogate α chain, pTα, 
associated with the TCRβ chain) proliferate and will become DP thymocytes. The αβ lineage-committed 
precursors that express a functional γδTCR instead will differentiate in DP cells but would eventually 
die (J. Kang et al., 2001). A schematic summary of this model is described in Figure 1.2.B. 
TCR strength hypothesis: 
The third hypothesis suggests that the lineage commitment depends on the strength of the TCR signal 
after binding to antigen-presenting cells in the thymus (Hayes, 2005).  
The TCR strength hypothesis model was first discussed when early experiments using TCRβ-/- mice 
showed rather efficient DP generation (Livak et al., 1995). This suggested that perhaps the γδTCR could 
support both γδ and αβ lineages. Similarly, mice expressing a transgenic αβTCR resulted in skewed and 
high levels of αβTCR, which would suggest that the αβTCR could support both lineages. This data 
combined put forward the idea that the TCR is instrumental in determining the lineage fate (Hayes et 
al., 2003). The suggestions that pre-TCR and γδTCR transduce distinct signals, and that both αβ and 
γδTCRs are capable of promoting the development of both αβ and γδ lineage cells are inconsistent 
with the instructive and the stochastic models.

To support this idea, Hayes et al. looked into the differences in the TCR structures that would explain 
the lineage commitment. The TCR is formed of either δ, γ, β, α, or pTα chains complexed with CD3 
chains (ε, δ, γ, ζ) and it was observed that a specific TCR expression depended on the expression of one 
or several CD3 chains (Hayes et al., 2003). This idea was confirmed more recently, as it has been shown 
that CD3 Immunoreceptor tyrosine-based activation motifs (ITAMs) sequence diversity is required for 
optimal signal transduction and could play a role in T cell lineage commitment (Bettini et al., 2017). 
They then looked into the surface level of the TCR. Knowing that a reduction of CD3ζ expression 
induces a reduced surface expression of γδTCR in immature DN thymocytes, they used TCRζ+− mice 
and observed an increase in DP thymocytes and a decrease in γδTCR+ DN thymocytes (Hayes et al., 
2005).  
The conclusion at this stage of research was then that TCR surface level of expression had an important 
role to play in the lineage commitment. 
Furthermore, the knowledge that TCR surface levels of expression influenced the signal strength of the 
TCR made researchers look into the strength of TCR signaling.  This strength has been manipulated by 
changing the number of ITAMs on the tail of CD3ζ. A strong TCR signal induces Extracellular-Regulated 
Kinase (ERK) phosphorylation, induction of the Early Growth Response (Egr) family of transcription 
factors and the DNA binding protein inhibitor Id3 (Haks et al., 2005)(Figure 1.5). A DN immature 
thymocyte with weakened γδTCR signal in the absence of TCRζ-ITAMs can no longer support γδ T 
lineage commitment and development, but can still direct the commitment to αβ lineage (Hayes et al., 
2005). 
The generation of DP thymocytes in γδTCR transgenic (Tg) mice is dependent on γδTCR and not pre-
TCR or αβTCR, as there are 25 times more DP thymocytes in γδTCR Tg than TCRβ−− mice (Hayes et al., 
2003). Also, γδTCR is expressed before productive TCRβ rearrangement, as there are few or any TCRβ 
on DN and DP γδTCR Tg thymocytes.  Low levels of γδTCR on DP thymocytes could be explained by a 
downregulation after the lineage fate selection (Hayes et al., 2003). Also, it has been shown that in the 
presence of a ligand, a γδTCR Tg mediates almost exclusive adoption of the γδ lineage, while in the 
absence of ligand the same γδTCR promotes αβ lineage development with an efficiency comparable 
to the pre-TCR (Haks et al., 2005). All this data combined suggests that γδTCR can be the receptor 
mediating the development of both αβ and γδ lineage cells (Hayes et al., 2003). 
These were the first experiments done that led to the conclusion that TCR signal strength has a link 
with cell fate commitment. More experiments have been done after, manipulating the strength of the 
signal by altering the number of peptide-occupied MHC molecules that engage TCR (Corse et al., 2011) 
or inducing mutations on the peptide sequences that TCR bind to (Corse et al., 2011). 
Therefore, all this data gave rise to the TCR signal strength model, suggesting that a weak signal from 
the pre-TCR would lead to an αβ lineage commitment, whereas a strong signal from the γδTCR would 
lead to a γδ lineage commitment. A γδTCR, however, can still give rise to an αβ-lineage committed cell 
when the TCR expresses a low signal, but without the strong proliferation burst that can be observed 
in the case of a weak signal on the pre-TRC (Ciofani & Zúñiga-Pflücker, 2010). 
A schematic understanding of the TCR strength model is described in Figure 1.2.C.

Pre-programming of γδ T cells 
γδ T cells are the first T cell lineage to develop in the thymus and take up residence in a wide variety 
of tissues where they can provide fast, innate-like sources of effector cytokines for barrier defense. 
Unlike αβ T cells which leave the thymus naïve, most of γδ T cells are pre-programmed for effector 
functions in the thymus before birth. 
In the mouse, γδT cells can be divided into different subsets depending on their variable γ region (Vγ) 
of the TCR. Indeed, it has been shown that the Vγ chain was to a certain extent correlated to the tissue 
residence of the cell, but also the cytokines produced by the cells (Papotto et al., 2017). The different 
subsets of murine γδ T cells are described in Table 1.1. Depending on the cytokine they produce, γδ T 
cells differentiate into IFN-γ-producers that can be Vγ5+, Vγ7+ or γδT1 cells (that are Vγ1+ or Vγ4+), or 
IL-17A-producers (γδT17 cells which are Vγ4+ or Vγ6+). The differentiation between these two subsets 
depends on a lot of mechanisms and will be seen later in this chapter.

More than being correlated with the type of cell, the Vγ chain is also correlated to the timing of 
development of the cell. There are two ways to differentiate γδ T cells. The first one is timewise, as 
they develop in the thymus in different timing waves. The second way is functional, depending on their 
effector function. It has been observed (Prinz et al., 2013) that the Vγ chain was correlated to both 
temporal and functional development of γδ T cells as each chain subset can be linked to a timing of 
development and effector cytokines produced (Table 1.1). To a less extent, the Vγ chain is also 
correlated to the tissue residence location of the cells. The correlation between the Vγ chain expressed 
and the effector function of the cell is described bellow. 
The first wave of γδ T cells will leave the thymus between embryonic days 13 and 16 (E-13 and E-16). 
They are Vγ5Vδ1+ and qualified as Dendritic Epidermal T cells (DETCs) progenitors. It has been shown 
that these cells undergo Skint-1 positive selection in the thymus (Barbee et al., 2011). Skint-1 is an 
immunoglobulin (Ig) superfamily gene that is expressed by medullary thymic epithelial cells. As mutant 
mice for Skint-1 do not develop canonical Vγ5Vδ1+ DETCs, Skint-1 interaction is necessary for the 
survival of the immature DETC in the thymus. After the selection, they will migrate to the epidermis,

where they will have an important role of immunosurveillance, homeostasis and immune response in 
case of skin infection (Li et al., 2018).  
The second wave is the development of γδT17 cells. They can be Vγ6Vδ1+, develop around E-14 and 
leave the thymus to colonize the epithelial layers of the female reproductive tract, skin, lungs, tongue, 
the intestinal mucosa, as well as lymphoid tissues. They can also be Vγ4+, which development occurs 
from E-16 onwards. Finally, a very small but significant number of IL-17 producing cells can be Vγ1+ 
and leave the thymus at the same moment as Vγ4+ cells (Papotto et al., 2017). Haas et al. showed that 
the development of IL-17 producing γδ T cells is restricted to a functional embryonic wave between 
embryonic days 15.5 and 18.5 as they are present in the thymus only during that period and very few 
in the newborn and adult thymi (Haas et al., 2012).  
A third functional wave is concerning the IFN-γ producing cells, also called γδT1. These cells are Vγ4+ 
or Vγ1+ (Haas et al., 2012). 
The fourth wave of γδ T cells development concerns the Vγ7+ cells that leave the thymus during 
neonatal life (in average 3 weeks after birth). They rearrange in the thymus and migrate directly to the 
intestine where they will become Intraepithelial Lymphocytes (IELs) and express the CD8αα 
glycoprotein. They will go through an extrathymic maturation process driven by butyrophilin-like 1 
(Btnl1) expressed by enterocytes (Prinz et al., 2013). These cells have a critical role for barrier 
maintenance during homeostasis but also in case of an infection as they are cytotoxic (Prinz et al., 
2013). 
Some of the cells leave the thymus as naïve γδ T cells. They have been defined as “inducible” in (Chien 
et al., 2013) as they can acquire their effector function when encountering an antigen in the tissues in 
an “adaptive-like” manner. The antigen specificity of γδ T cells has a role in determining their effector 
fate, as will be seen later. An algal protein, phycoerythrin, has been used as a tracker antigen to show 
that γδ T cells that do not have an antigen specificity for thymic ligands are activated in the tissue after 
encountering an antigen (they become CD44hiCD62Llow)(Zeng et al., 2012). They then start expressing 
retinoid related orphan receptor gamma (RORγt) and finally start producing IL-17. Cytokine receptors 
to IL-23 and IL-1 perpetuate the expression of IL-17 during inflammation. These cells migrate to the 
tissues as naïve and start producing IL-17 in case of antigen recognition during an infection.

γδT17 CELL DEVELOPMENT 
γδ T cell main functional groups and phenotype 
There are 3 different types of immune responses that can eliminate an infection. A type 1 response 
will target intracellular bacteria and start recruiting IFN-γ-producing lymphocytes that express the 
transcription factor (TF) T-bet. A type 2 immune response will target helminths and parasites and is led 
by IL-4, IL-5 and IL-13 producing lymphocytes expressing the TF GATA-3. Finally, a type 3 immune 
response targets fungi and extracellular bacterial infections, and it is driven by IL-17-producing 
lymphocytes. These cells express the transcription factor RORγt. Like Th cells or Innate Lymphoid Cells 
(ILCs), γδ T cells can be divided into effector subsets based on the expression of T-bet/IFN-γ (γδT1) and 
RORγt/IL-17 (γδT17)(Muñoz-Ruiz et al., 2017).  
As seen in the previous section, IFN-γ producing cells are Vγ5+DETCs, Vγ7+IELs and Vγ1+ but can also be 
Vγ4+. On the other hand, IL-17-producing γδ T cells are Vγ6+ but also Vγ4+ and a very small subset of 
Vγ1+ cells (Papotto et al., 2017).

CD27+ cells will highly proliferate at E15 when CD27- will expand at E18 (Ribot et al., 2009).  
Furthermore, IL-17 producing γδ T cells do not express the marker CD122 compared to IFN-γ producing 
cells that do (Shibata et al., 2008).  
Ribot et al. also showed that over 80% of the CD27− cells were NK1.1− when over than 80% of CD27+ 
cells expressed the marker NK1.1. This observation has been confirmed (Haas et al., 2009), adding that 
only CCR6+ cells produc IL-17.  
Sumaria et al. identified CD24- cells to be segregated in two subsets, IL-17 being CD44hi and 
CD45RBint when IFN-γ producers are CD45RBhiCD44+ (Sumaria et al., 2017). 
These markers develop at different stages during ontogeny, as can be seen in Figure 1.4. At first, the 
progenitor is CD24+CD25+. CD25 is downregulated, and some TCR-independent effector programs are 
initiated (that will be discussed in the next chapters).  
After a lineage differentiation, led by TCR signaling (as will be seen below), the different subsets 
acquire their thymic phenotype, with the differentiated expression of CD44, CD45RB (Sumaria et al., 
2017), CCR6 and all the other markers described above (Ribot et al., 2009).

It has been mentioned above that the TCR has a role in the choice of αβ against γδ lineage. A second 
differentiation depending on the strength of the TCR signaling occurs. Several studies (Sumaria et al., 
2017; Turchinovich & Hayday, 2011) described the mechanism to include TCR-independent effector 
programs that would occur prior to TCR-dependent signals (Figure 1.4). Turchinovitch et al. showed 
indeed that skint-1 determined the functional program of a subset of γδ T cells (Vγ5+) prior to TCR 
engagement. These cells go through a checkpoint that induces an Egr3-mediated pathway that 
suppresses the type 17 lineage factor by repressing Sox13 and RORγt transcription factors 
(Turchinovich & Hayday, 2011). Therefore, the effector programs would be pre-programmed in the 
thymus and a survival and proliferation signal would be given by the TCR depending on the strength of 
its signal. In the next chapter, both the mechanisms behind the TCR signaling and the TCR-independent 
effector programs that have been studied so far will be discussed. 
γδTCR signal strength hypothesis 
Some observations suggesting that TCRγδ signal strength had a role to play in the differentiation of the 
γδ T cell subsets were made. CD3 double haploinsufficient (CD3DH) mice have a reduced TCRγδ and 
CD3ε surface expression in γδ T cells and an impaired signal transduction downstream TCR, translated 
by the activation of the ERK kinases (Muñoz-Ruiz et al., 2016). It has therefore been used to study the

TCRγδ signal strength and role in γδ T cell subsets differentiation. Muñoz-Ruiz et al. observed reduction 
in IFN-γ producing γδ T cell frequency of CD3DH mice (Muñoz-Ruiz et al., 2016).  
The second observation is that both IFN-γ and IL-17 producing cells were present in lower frequencies 
in CD3DH than in wild-type mice, but the reduction of IFN-γ was maintained whereas the levels of IL-
17 were back to normal between week 1 and week 6 after birth. However, this back to normal came 
with a switch of phenotype from Vγ1- Vγ4- (embryonic day E-18 and neonates) to Vγ4+ (week 1 after 
birth). Knowing that Vγ6+ cells are exclusively generated before birth and that their low levels were 
also maintained, this data suggests that it exists a developmental window in γδT17 cell ontogeny when 
a distinct signal strength is required. Therefore, Vγ6+ cells are more affected by the lack of TCR signal 
than Vγ4+ cells. 
Instead of an experimental ablation of the TCRγδ, Wencker et al. used SKG mice that carry a mutation 
that reduces by 90% the function of the kinase domain of the TCR-proximal signaling kinase Zap70 to 
study the TCR strength model (Wencker et al., 2014). They observed a considerable depletion of CD27-
CCR6+ γδ cells in the dermis and LN, where they are usually a lot present. Both Vγ4+ and Vγ6+ cells were 
depleted, whereas no such substantial loss was observed in the CD27+ population, nor impairment in 
the ability to produce IFN-γ. They confirmed the previous idea that γδT17 cells require TCR signaling 
for their development. 
Haas et al. observed in TcrdH2BeGFP mice IL-17+ TCRδ- cells in embryonic thymi, showing that γδT17 
cells are able to produce IL-17 even before and during TCR rearrangement and prior to lineage 
commitment (Haas et al., 2012). This data would suggest that the IL-17 pathway would be the “default” 
pathway and that a strong signal downstream of the TCR would change it to an IFN-γ pathway.  
However, Turchinovich and Pennington suggested that this idea was wrong due to the fact that γδT17 
cells require TCR signaling for their development and survival as seen in the previous paragraph. They 
suggest that the IFN-γ secreting fate might be the default pathway, as a thymic-independent TCRγδ 
signaling may be sufficient to promote the type 1 fate (Turchinovich & Pennington, 2011). A strong 
TCR signal has been described by Sumaria et al. to restrict the development of all IL-17A secreting γδ 
T cells and promote drastically the development of IFN- γ secreting cells (Sumaria et al., 2017).  
This data suggested that although necessary for development, the TCR signal that γδT17 cells require 
may be a weak one. It has been proposed that the TCR might provide additional signals such as the 
access to skint-1 necessary for development of DETCs (Turchinovich & Hayday, 2011) in case of a strong 
signal, and an alternative signaling pathway with non-inhibition of Sox13 and RORγt that becomes 
available downstream of the TCR in case of a weak signal (Sumaria et al., 2019). I will describe different 
mechanisms that have a role on γδ T cell differentiation downstream of the TCR in the next paragraphs. 
TCR-dependent transcriptional networks 
The next mechanisms described are signaling pathways that occurs downstream of the TCR or in link 
with the TCR signal strength hypothesis.  
ERK signaling pathway 
After suggesting that the TCR strength could have a role to play in γδ T cell differentiation, studies have 
been conducted in order to describe what kind of signaling occurs. Transcription factors and signaling

No phosphorylated ERK was detected in CD27- γδ cells (Wencker et al., 2014). ERK-deficiency increased

Furthermore, after co-transfering increasing amounts of a plasmid encoding tagged-Egr3 along with

positively regulate RORγt expression, as a mutation of E protein boxes 3 and 4 substantially reduced 
the activity of RORγt (Xi et al., 2006).  
However, Id3 is critically required for the differentiation of IFN-γ producing effectors. Indeed, Rag2:Id3 
double deficient cells induced with Id3 started producing amounts of IFN-γ comparable to mature 
CD24low γδ cells (Lauritsen et al., 2009).

RORγt 
RORγt is a very important TF for a lot of type 3 lymphocytes, as it regulates the expression of IL-17A.  
Indeed, Rorc-/- mice have been shown to lack CCR6+CD27- T cell subset in both thymus and spleen and 
did not express IL-17A in total γδ T cells (Schmolka et al., 2013). However, the genetic deletion of Rorc 
did not impair the epigenetic marking of the gene Il17 (Schmolka et al., 2013). RORγt is not 
indispensable for the survival of γδ T cells, as some of the c-kitlowIL-7Rlow cells  remain in Rorc deficient 
mice, albeit their number may be reduced.  
In another study, the enhanced GFP reporter (EGFP) was used to report the RORγt expression in Rorc−− 
mice. It has been observed that a small fraction of γδ T cells in these mice still expressed EGFP in the 
intestinal mucosa, showing that they survived the RORγt deletion (Naito et al., 2008). This suggests 
that RORγt can be dispensable for the development of intestinal γδ T cells. 
c-Maf 
The transcription factor c-Maf has first been described as essential for the commitment of IL-17 
producing γδ T cells as it is selectively required for these cells and directly regulates the Rorc locus 
(Zuberbuehler et al., 2019). Indeed, a high expression of c-Maf has been observed to be restricted to 
RORγt+ γδ T cells when CD27+, T-bet+ and CD45RBhi cells lack expression of c-Maf. C-Maf and RORγt 
expression coincide timewise during ontogeny, suggesting a link between the two transcription factors 
(Zuberbuehler et al., 2019). 
Maffl/flIl7rCre mice were ablated from their γδT17 population when IFN-γ+ and T-bet+ populations were 
unaltered. Very few RORγt+ γδ cells in embryonic thymi were observed, suggesting an inefficient Rorc 
activation and a direct regulation from c-Maf to the Rorc locus. C-Maf has therefore been identified as 
selectively required for γδT17 cells differentiation (Zuberbuehler et al., 2019). 
Secondly, c-Maf has also been identified as necessary to maintain γδT17 identity during and after 
development, as the deletion of c-Maf in Rorc expressing cells in Maffl/flRorcCre mice showed an 
enhanced apoptosis of γδT17 cells. C-Maf is therefore a universal regulator of γδT17 cell differentiation 
and maintenance (Zuberbuehler et al., 2019). 
The signals that regulate c-Maf expression in developing thymi have been studied and hypothesized to 
be mediated by a weak TCRγδ signal. KN6, DTN40 and C1.21 TCRγδ Tg deliver respectively a strong, 
intermediate and weak TCRγδ signals. Zuberbuehler et al. observed an inverse relationship between 
the signal strength and the expression of c-Maf in these transgenic mice. C-Maf is therefore gradually

IL-7R and Notch signaling 
As seen above, IL-7R plays a critical role in expansion and TCR recombination during lymphocyte 
development by controlling the accessibility of the TCRγ locus with the recruitment of STAT5 and 
histidine acetylation. IL-7R it therefore very important for all γδ subsets as it is necessary for the correct 
development of TCRγδ. 
However, IL-7/IL-7R has also a role in the type 17 differentiation. Indeed, high levels of Il17 mRNA were 
detected among the IL-7RhiCD27− γδ T cells and only among this population (Michel et al., 2012). IL-7 
signaling favors the differentiation into IL-17A-producing γδ T cells as IL-7 has been shown to be 
enriched for CD27− γδ thymocytes in the LNs and the thymus (Michel et al., 2012). IL-7 promotes strong 
responses of IL-17 producing γδ T cells to TCR agonists (Michel et al., 2012). A higher decrease in IL-
17+ γδ population than IFN-γ γδ population was finally observed un IL-7R-/- TCRγ Tg mice (Nakamura et 
al., 2015). 
The IL-7R pathway is coupled with the Notch signaling pathway, which has 2 different ways to be 
integrated into the type 17 γδ T cell differentiation. Hes1 is a downstream signaling molecule of Notch 
receptors that has been shown to be required for the development of IL-17 producing γδ T cells

Blk 
Blk is the B lymphoid kinase, part of the B cell specific Src family. It is expressed in γδ but not αβ 
populations and its deficiency affects the early stages of development as Blk−− DN subsets undergo 
less proliferation than their WT counterparts, and a decreased number of ETPs was observed in Blk−− 
mice (Laird et al., 2010). 
Blk was preferentially expressed in IL-17 producing cells, as CCR6+ DN TCRγδ+ cells express high levels 
and CD27+ DN TCRγδ+ express low levels of Blk. Furthermore, a significant decrease in CCR6+ γδ T cells 
in TCRγδ Tg Blk−− mice shows that Blk is required for the development of IL-17 producing γδ T cell 
effectors (Laird et al., 2010). 
The regulation of the Blk gene has been studied. It has been shown (Zuberbuehler et al., 2019) that Blk 
was regulated by c-Maf as the expression of Blk was severely diminished in Maffl/flIl7rCre mice. Blk is 
also activated by some members of the High Mobility Group (HMG) box transcription factors, as will 
be seen in the next paragraph. 
High Mobility Group (HMG) box transcription factors 
The transcription factor Sox13 has been identified to program the γδT17 cell differentiation (Malhotra 
et al., 2013). Indeed, a Sox13 deficiency in Sox13−− mice showed a severely reduced number of γδT17 
cells identified with the RORγt+CCR6+CD27−CD44hiCD62L− phenotype, whereas the other subsets were 
largely intact. The remaining γδT17 cells did not synthetize IL-17 ex vivo. This data show that Sox13 is 
critical for the development of γδT17 cell differentiation and development. 
 Lack of Blk and RORγt expression has been observed in Sox13−− mice, but RorcGfp/+:Sox13−− mice 
showed a small but still significant expression of Rorc (Malhotra et al., 2013). However, Sox13 Tg mice 
showed increased proportions of Blk+TCRγδ+ cells, as well as increased proportions of BLK protein, 
independently of TCR expression. Sox13 is therefore a regulator of Blk (Malhotra et al., 2013). 
Sox4 has also been identified as critical for γδT17 cell development, as the thymic precursors lacking 
Sox4 in Sox4−− mice did not generate γδT17 cells, whereas the other subsets were present. The 
depletion was even more severe than in Sox13−− mice, and was correlated with the loss of Rorc 
expression in Rorcgfp/+:Sox4−− mice (Malhotra et al., 2013).

γδT17 cells in neonatal life 
Very little is known about the spatiotemporal and signaling requirements of γδT17 cells during 
neonatal life. In that regard, two main studies can be discussed. 
First, dermal Vγ4+ and Vγ6+ have been studied (Cai et al., 2014). Dermal γδ T cells are inexistent in 
embryonic day E-20 and day 1 after birth and only become obvious at day 3, and present adult 
frequencies at day 14. In comparison, Vγ6+ cells are apparent from E-20 in the lungs (Cai et al., 2014). 
Around day 3 to 5, dermal γδ T cells are almost exclusive to a Vγ6+ phenotype, and Vγ4+ appear around 
day 7.  
Furthermore, almost all Vγ6+ cells in neonatal thymus have a mature phenotype (CD24low) whereas 
most Vγ4+ were immature (CD24+) in both neonatal and adult thymi, but showed a mature phenotype 
in the BM. This suggests that Vγ4+ cells may require an extrathymic environment for maturation (Cai 
et al., 2014).  
To go further in the neonatal environment in the gut, Kadekar et al. showed that STAT5 is regulating 
the neonatal expansion of γδT17 cells. Indeed, RORγtCreSTAT5fl/fl showed severely reduced numbers of 
γδT17 cells in the LNs, skin and gut and an increase in IFN-γ+ and CD27+ γδ T cells (Kadekar et al., 2020).  
STAT5A and STAT5B have then been proved to have a regulation role in the differentiation between 
γδT17 and γδT1 cells, as hyperactive STAT5A mutant has higher numbers of γδT17 and a similar 
number of γδT1 than WT, and hyperactive STATB mutant has higher numbers of γδT1 and similar of 
γδT17 cells (Kadekar et al., 2020). 
Intestinal lamina propria γδT17 cells have been shown to co-express T-bet and RORγt in the gut after 
day 4 after birth (only single positive cells were observed before that) with both transcription factor 
staining and transgenic reporters (Kadekar et al., 2020). The cues that lead this co-expression have 
been studied in the same experiment. As RORγtCreSTAT3fl/fl mice did not show IL-17 and IL-22 
production by γδ T cells, it is therefore STAT3-dependent. On the other side, RORγtCreRARdnfl/fl mice 
that have lost their retinoic acid signaling capacities showed a reduced overall expression of IFN-γ. 
Therefore, lamina propria T-bet+ RORγt+ γδ T cells can coproduce IL-17, IL-22 and IFN-γ cytokines, which 
expression profiles are regulated by STAT3 and retinoic acid (Kadekar et al., 2020). 
Finally, Kadekar et al. showed that T-bet expression by γδT17 cells in the gut was regulated by STAT5, 
STAT5A downregulating T-bet in RORγt+ colon lamina propria cells whereas STAT5B is enhancing its 
expression.

γδT17 CELLS IN THE INTESTINES 
In this project, the focus is made on γδT17 cells in the murine intestinal mucosa. The importance of 
the intestinal mucosa in terms of immunology will be discussed in this paragraph (Peterson & Artis, 
2014). 
Mucosal tissues have a primary non-immunological function, the intestines for example are part of the 
gastrointestinal tract which takes care of nutrient absorption and waste removal. They represent a 
huge surface barrier against pathogens, 200 times more important than the skin surface in humans. 
They are also the location where adaptive immunity evolved, and a theory is that this is thanks to 
microbial colonization at mucosal surfaces (Murphy & Weaver, 2017). 
The gastrointestinal tract represents a unique challenge to our immune system. Its first challenge is 
the presence of food, necessary for the individual’s survival but bringing a need of tolerance to food 
proteins from the immune system. A second challenge is the presence of the microbiota, huge number 
of microorganisms present to ensure nutrient absorption for example. The immune system has to 
recognize self-microorganisms from pathogens. The third challenge is the need of immunity, as it is a 
huge barrier surface against pathogens. 
The immune system has several ways to cope with such challenges. It has a large number of specialized 
cells, that can be immune (leukocytes) but highly specialized and adapted to the environment, or non-
immune (such as epithelial and stromal cells) that have evolved immunological functions as 
represented in Figure 1.7.

The immune cells in the gastrointestinal tract are divided into the lamina propria and the epithelium. 
The ones in the epithelium are Intraepithelial lymphocytes (IELs) and can either be induced (migrate 
to the intestinal epithelium after encounter of an antigen) or natural (develop in the thymus and 
migrate to mature in the intestinal epithelium). These cells are part of the first line of defense against 
an infection together with the epithelial cells (Peterson & Artis, 2014). 
The ones in the lamina propria can be lymphocytes (B cells, T cells or innate lymphoid cells, phagocytes 
(dendritic cells or macrophages) or granulocytes (neutrophils, eosinophils, basophils and mast cells) 
that are immune cells providing an immune response in case of pathogen infection. 
γδ T cells are with other immune cells homing in the tissues in order to proceed to immunosurveillance 
and act very quickly in case of an infection as memory-like cells but with a pre-programmed effector 
function allowing them to sense in the environment cues of an infection without the need of TCR 
binding to a peptide antigen. They can directly start producing pro-inflammatory cytokines such as IL-
17 and be part of the innate cells to fight against the infection and recruit more adaptive lymphocytes 
(Murphy & Weaver, 2017).  
However, more than being in the tissues for immunosurveillance, they have been shown to have a very 
specific role in the intestines. Indeed, they are able to regulate the response to nutrient sensing with 
the epithelial cells (Sullivan et al., 2021). They are needed regulate the carbohydrate transcriptional 
program, as direct epithelial sensing is not sufficient. The way they do that is by controlling the levels 
of IL-22 secreted by the cells in the intestinal compartment (Sullivan et al., 2021).

THYMIC DEPENDENCE OF INTESTINAL γδT17 CELLS 
The first question of this project is whether γδT17 cells in the gut are thymically derived.   
Indeed, Sato in 1993 and Boll in 1995 have found that γδ T cells still develop in the gut of Nude mice 
(Boll & Reimann, 1995; Sato et al., 1993). Nude mice have a genetic mutation on the Foxn1 gene that 
causes an absence or deterioration of the thymus. As a result, they have very few T cells and are 
therefore immunodeficient. These mice also do not have hair.

γδ T cells found in the Nude gut would be preferentially (60-70%) IELs but CD4−CD8− γδ T cells have 
been observed (15-20%) (Boll & Reimann, 1995). The presence or not of the IL-17 producing subset of 
γδ T cells is discussed in this project.    
It has been shown (Haas et al., 2012) that the development of γδT17 cells requires an embryonic 
thymus. Indeed, γδT17 cells could not be regenerated in adult (Rag1)-targeted mice after Rag1-
induction (which induced T cell development). Therefore, the absence of γδT17 cells in Nude mouse 
guts would be expected. 
With this knowledge, the thymic dependence of these cells was studied in this project with the use of 
Nude mice. Intestinal lamina propria lymphocytes from Nude mice were prepared and stained for flow 
cytometry as described in the Materials and Methods section. Lymph nodes were prepared for control. 
Two experiments on 4 Nude mice were done: the two first were only stained for total γδ T cells and 
the two last for γδT17 cells. The LN from the 4 mice were used. 
There are very few γδT17 cells in the Nude LN 
In the skin draining LN, γδ T cells are identified as CD4−CD8−CD19−TCRβ−TCRγδ+. A reduction of the γδ 
T cell population is observed in Nude compared to WT mice (Figure 2.3.A). γδT17 cells are identified 
in this population as CD27−CD44+. Very few γδT17 cells are identified within the γδ subset (Figure 2.3.B) 
with less than 10% presence on average (Figure 2.3.C). 
There are very few γδT cells in the Nude gut LP 
For the first two mice, only a staining for total γδ T cells was done. In the gut LP, γδ T cells were 
identified in both WT and Nude mice. The proportion of γδ T cells within the T cell compartment is 
lower in the Nude than WT small intestinal and colonic lamina propria (Figure 2.4.A). A quantification 
as a percentage of CD45+ is shown in Figure 2.4.B. The decrease in numbers of γδ T cells is observed in 
both mice.

γδT17 CELL PLASTICITY 
In ILCs (Klose, 2013) and Th cells (Ghoreschi et al., 2010) a co-expression of T-bet and RORγt has been 
observed, which may be an evolutionarily conserved transcriptional program that originally developed 
as part of the innate defense against infections but that also confers an increased risk of immune-
mediated pathology to these cells. RORγt+CD4+ T cells and ILCs can differentiate into RORγt+T-bet+ and 
go through a second differentiation step to become RORγt−T-bet+. These cells are known as ex-Th17 
(Basdeo et al., 2017) and ex-ILC3s (Melo-Gonzalez & Hepworth, 2017). Since Kadekar et al. observed 
that RORγt+ γδ T cells in the gut became T-bet+ (Kadekar et al., 2020), the purpose of this part of the 
project was to check if, similarly to ILC3s and Th17, they fully differentiated into T-bet+ and lose RORγt 
expression, to be “ex-γδT17” cells. A scheme representing a flow cytometry analysis summarizing this 
part of the project is shown in Figure 2.9.

Firstly,the aim was to make sure that fate mapping lymphocyte lineages could indeed be done with

DISCUSSION  
In this project, we found that intestinal IL-17 producing γδ T cells (γδT17) required a thymus to develop 
as we could not find any in Nude mice that do not have a functional thymus. We also show that these 
cells are present in the gut at day 1 after birth and day E-18.5 of embryonic life. Finally, we show that 
after upregulating T-bet upon entry in the gut, they do not become single positive for T-bet at steady-
state. 
We first concluded that γδT17 cells are absent from Nude mice. As Nude mice do not have a functional 
thymus, we concluded that they need a thymus to develop and to exist. This is in agreement with (Haas 
et al., 2012) who stated that γδT17 cells needed a thymus to develop. This suggestion would mean 
that there are some developmental conditions that can only be found in the thymus. We know that 
DETCs that arise exclusively from fetal liver stem cell or progenitors and not from the adult BM cells 
and require fetal thymic environment to develop (Spidale et al., 2019). Here we suggest that it would 
also be the case for γδT17 cells that would need thymic molecular programs to be generated and 
develop preprogrammed effector functions. The fetal or neonatal environment dependence could be 
shown in future studies by grafting a fetal thymus to adult nude mice and observe or not the presence 
of γδT17 cells in the intestinal mucosa as did Pardoll et al. in 1988 to study the fetal thymic dependence 
of γδ T cells (Pardoll et al., 1988). 
After showing that γδT17 cells could not be in the gut without a thymus, we investigated the timing of 
their colonization within the intestinal lamina propria.  Another part of this project was to conclude 
whether γδT17 mice were already present before D2 in the gut lamina propria or not. My conclusion 
is that at day 1 after birth they are already there, in both small intestine and colon, with a higher 
proportion in the small intestine. Interestingly, we found γδT17 in the lamina propria of embryos. Our 
research would suggest that γδT17 cells that home to the gut already leave the thymus before birth. 
It has been shown in two different studies how much γδT17 cells played an important role in case of 
an infection during neonatal life. The first study was about Influenza infection in the lungs (Guo et al., 
2018). It has been observed that γδ deficient neonatal life could not survive influenza infection when 
WT mice could. They suggested that γδ T cells were required for recovery after neonatal influenza 
infection, with the secretion of IL-17 that is associated with tissue repair response and correlates wit 
improved disease outcome in influenza-infected children (Guo et al., 2018). A second study is in the 
intestinal tissue that is infected by Clostridium difficile bacteria (Chen et al., 2020). They showed that 
IL-17 producing γδ T cells played an essential role against the infection, as there were marked signs of 
inflammation in mice deficient in IL-17 or γδ T cells compared to WT neonatal mice which were 
resistant to the infection (Chen et al., 2020). 
These cells are therefore extremely important in very early infections that can occur during neonatal 
life. Our data suggests that they are present and ready to fight against a potential infection already 
before birth. It could be illustrated by the cells getting ready for a war that would start as soon as the 
baby is born. 
The observation that the small intestine has more γδT17 cells than the colon at either day 1 and E-18.5 
could be explained by a timing of migration different for the two organs.

Indeed, T cell migration towards the gut is regulated by several factors, including CCR9 and α4β7 
integrin that are secreted in a retinoic acid dependent manner by dendritic cells and stromal cells 
(Iwata et al., 2004). It has been shown (Iwata et al., 2004) that the differences in levels of expression 
of CCR9 and α4β7 could differentiate into migration to the small intestine or the colon. Indeed, Iwata 
et al. showed that CCR9 was needed for migration into the small intestine but not in the colon. Another 
chemoattractant receptor, GPR15 has been shown to regulate the migration of FoxP3+ regulatory cells 
to the colon (Dzutsev et al., 2017) and of DETCs to the skin (Lahl et al., 2014). Mechanisms including 
CCR9, α4β7 integrin or GPR15 could maybe play a role in the differential migration of γδT17 cells. 
Upon entry in the gut, we know from Kadekar et al. that γδT17 cells start upregulating T-bet after day 
4. It has been shown that several other subsets of lymphocytes can go through the same mechanism. 
ILC3s can we split into two subsets, either expressing the markers NKp44 or NKp46. ILC3s that express 
NKp46 can start expressing T-bet in a STAT4 dependent manner in the intestines (Mikami et al., 2018). 
Notch signaling allows the cell to co-express RORγt and T-bet, enabling these cells to produce IL-22 
and IFN-γ (but not IL-17)(Vonarbourg et al., 2010). On the other hand, Th17 cells have also been 
identified to be able to co-express T-bet in a STAT4 dependent manner in the gut (Murphy, 2010). The 
discovery by Kadekar et al. about the same capacities from γδT17 cells would hypothesize that these 
cells could resemble these subsets of cells. 
It has been shown that under steady-state conditions, ILC3s upregulated T-bet under the regulation of 
the microbiota and IL-23 (Klose et al., 2013). The transcriptional networks behind this are however 
unclear.  
ILC3s and Th17 are also known to transit from a type 3 lymphocyte to a type 1 lymphocyte in the 
intestines in case of an inflammation. For example, Salmonella enterica infection led to 
downregulation of RORγt in NKp46+ ILC3s and trans differentiation into ILC1s (Klose et al., 2013). The 
production of IFN-γ by these ex-ILC3s has been proven to be essential for the release of mucus-forming 
glycoproteins required to protect the epithelial barrier during Salmonella infection (Klose et al., 2013).  
Th17 in the same way are triggered by TCR simulation provided in specific conditions and convert into 
Th1 in case of infection (Murphy & Stockinger, 2010). These cells have been shown to have a great 
importance in immunoprotection in the gut.  
Type 3 cells stay in the intestinal mucosa for immunosurveillance and homeostasis in symbiosis with 
the microbiota.  After upregulation of T-bet they become very pathogenic and secrete both IL-17 and 
IFN-γ (Bal et al., 2020). As seen above, ex-Th17 and ex-ILC3s are cells that have lost RORγt expression 
to fully commit to the type 1 lineage (Basdeo et al., 2017; Melo-Gonzalez & Hepworth, 2017). Our data 
suggests that ex- γδT17 cells cannot be formed at steady-state, as they do not seem to lose RORγt 
expression to become T-bet single positive. However, there is a possibility that they could do so during 
an infection. 
Inducing an inflammation after an infection with bacteria or virus that would start a type 1 response 
in the gut in the transgenic mice that I worked with would allow us to see if the cells finally lose RORγt 
expression. Also, ex-ILC3s have been shown to reconvert into ILC3s after resolution of the 
inflammation (Bal et al., 2020) and it would be interesting to see if ex-γδT17 fully become again γδT17 
after an infection in the intestinal mucosa.

resuspended in 5 mL 40% Percoll, layered on 4 mL of 70% Percoll, and centrifuged at 20°C and 800 g 
for 20 minutes with deceleration set to 0. Cells from the interphase were collected, washed once, and 
then resuspended in supplemented RPMI. 
Lymphocytes isolation – neonates and embryos 
The dissection of the embryos was done at E-18.5 and the newborn was done at day 1. 
Both dissections of embryos and newborns must be done under a microscope to be able to see, but 
the mice are already big enough to be able to separate the colon from the small intestine. Special tools 
are also used to have more precision.  
The embryos are taken out of the placenta, their head is cut off. Their thorax is opened by two pairs of 
forceps and the intestines are pulled out of the body. As in an adult mouse, the intestines are cut on 
both sides of the cecum, the stomach, and the rectum. The organs are placed in HBSS/Hepes on a 24 
well plate. The thorax is then opened with scissors and the thymus is taken with forceps and placed in 
wash media. 
The same protocol is used for the newborns, except that there is no placenta. 
Small intestine and colon: no washes were needed for the embryonic samples, whereas one (10min) 
and two washes (10 and 15min) were respectively done for the new born small intestine and colon 
samples in 1mL of HBSS-EDTA at 37°C in a shaking incubator. 
Small intestine and colon cells were digested with 0.3mg Liberase TM (Roche) and 0.15 mg of DNase 
(Sigma-Aldrich) per preparation in respectively 2mL and 750μL of RPMI for 40 minutes on the magnetic 
stirrer at 37°C. The cells were filtered through a 70-μm cell strainer and were used directly for staining. 
Thymus lobes from embryonic day 18.5 fetuses and 1-day-old (Day of birth) pups were dissected and 
dissociated in supplemented RPMI using a dissection microscope to prepare single-cell suspension. 
The cell suspensions were filtered through a 40-μm cell strainer and stained for surface antigens under 
sterile conditions before FACS sorting.  
Staining 
Cells were harvested by centrifugation at 400 g for 5 minutes at 4°C followed by staining with fixable 
viability stain (BD Horizon FVS700) also called Live/dead in the rest of this report for 10 minutes on 
ice in PBS. Subsequently, surface antigens were stained in FACS buffer for 30 minutes on ice. 
All antibodies were used at a 1:200 dilution.
