ABSTRACT 
The aim of the current master thesis was to investigate the influence of cultivation methods on 
pigment production by Talaromyces atroroseus. The pigments, which exhibit a very intense red 
color, belong to the family of azaphilones and they are used extensively in food industry. 
The utilization of several extraction and analytical methods was necessary in order to achieve 
the separation, isolation, purification and characterization of known and unknown pigments. In 
order to investigate the diversity of the compounds produced in different cultivation media, 
both solid and liquid media were extracted using different extraction methods at different pH 
values and the analytical tool which measured the extraction results was the LC-DAD-MS. The 
result of this investigation verified that the production of known and unknown metabolites is 
dependent on the cultivation media and on the pH values. In more details, Liquid 
Chromatography in combination with Mass Spectrometry and a Diode Array Detector was used 
throughout all the experiments, since it is a very accurate separation and spectroscopic 
technique, and it provided valuable results which were the basis for every step of the procedure. 
 A further additional objective of the current master thesis was the isolation and purification of 
a novel red compound that derived from the cultivation of the fungus Talaromyces atroroseus 
in liquid media. Flash Chromatography was used for the fractionation of the compounds and 
Waters preparative HPLC was responsible for the isolation and purification of the red 
compounds of interest. In the end, NMR was used for the confirmation of the purity of the 
compound and a future perspective of this purification will be the identification of its structure. 
The red color of the compounds was confirmed by the DAD profile and the dereplication 
procedure was accomplished by using the BPCs. The compound with mass 540.210 Da, which 
is highly likely to be an azaphilone, didn’t show any hits in MPF/Antibase and for this reason 
it was considered novel.

Chapter 1: Background 
 
1.1. Natural Products 
 
Natural products have been reported since ancient years and nowadays they are considered a 
very promising source for bioactive compounds, vitamins, proteins, pigments and colorants. 
Natural products can be found in fungi, bacteria, plants, entophytic microorganisms, algae, 
lichens and in marine and terrestrial sources. Moreover, the development of many analytical 
techniques like NMR, MS and HPLC, as well as, the improvement of the isolation and 
purification methods has increased the progress in this field (K. Yoshida et al, 2009; D. A. Dias 
et al, 2012). 
Many natural products have been investigated during the centuries and they have been used for 
medicinal purposes. The fact that they exhibit enormous structural diversity, in combination 
with their tremendous biological activities (anticancer, antifungal, antiviral, cytotoxic, 
antibiotic, antidiabetic, anti-HIV, antimalarial), has render them very successful potential drug 
candidates (G. Strobel et al, 2004; S. Singh et al, 2005). As mentioned above, their use has been 
recorded since ancient years, because they were used as remedies, potions, oils and traditional 
medicines. The sources of these medicinal natural products were either some of the thousands 
species of plants in earth (250,000-500,000) or some of the numerous fungi (D.A. Dias et al, 
2012).   
As David J. Newman and Gordon M. Cragg (2007) claim in their review, there is a big variety 
of antibacterial, antifungal, antiviral, antiparasitic and anticancer drugs that come from natural 
products. For instance, one well known anticancer drug, Paclitaxel (Taxol), was isolated from 
the Pacific yew tree and it is used mostly for the treatment of breast cancer. (G. Strobel et al, 
2004; K.C. Nikolaou et al, 1994). 
Additionally, natural products have been used in cosmetics industry, since their extracts exhibit 
antiaging and regenerative properties and they are preferred from the consumers because of 
their purity and the fact they are not just synthesized in the laboratory but they exist in nature 
in their crude form (D.A. Dias et al, 2012). According to the historical review of Daniel A. Dias 
et al (2012), since the early Chinese and Egyptian civilizations, lichens have been used as the 
raw materials for perfumes and cosmetics, but today there are no approved products from 
lichens even though they were used extensively in folklore.   
It should be also mentioned, that antimicrobial compounds which derived from plants, have the 
ability to strengthen the plant defense mechanisms due to their insecticidal properties. 
Additionally, these compounds also affect plants’ odors, flavors and pigments (M. M. Cowan, 
1999).

1.1.1. Pigments 
Except for medicinal purposes, natural products have been also used as pigments and colorants 
since the ancient years. The roots of the plant Alcanna tinctoria were used since 300 BC as dyes 
(Papageorgiou et al, 2006) and the Parmelia omphalodes lichens were also known for their 
brown dyes in British Isles (D. A. Dias et al, 2012).  
Nowadays many efforts have been done for the production of pigments from natural products 
and the replacement of the synthetic ones. Natural pigments considered to be much more 
healthy and safe and the consumers have shown an increased preference for them even though 
they are more expensive and exhibit lower stability. Two of the most popular compounds which 
are used as dyes and pigments are anthraquinones and napthoquinones (N. Muangsin et al, 
2008; C.W.J. Chang, 1973).  
Anthraquinones and napthoquinones are polyketide pigments that produced in abundance by 
filamentous fungi and exhibit wide variety of colors. Anthraquinone is a building block of many 
dyes as K. Kavanagh (2005) claim and some of the fungi that are responsible for the production 
of anthraquinones are Eurotium spp., Fusarium spp., Helminthsporium oryzae, and Curvularia 
lunata (K. Kavanagh, 2005; G.T. Johnson & J.P. White, 1969). Yellow colored pigments like 
epurpurins, falconensins and falconensones are produced by Emericella spp., whereas orange, 
water-insoluble pigments, like monascorubrin and rubropunctatin, are produced by Monascus 
spp. and they are converted into soluble red pigments after their reaction with amino acids leads 
to monascorubramine and rubropunctamine. It is well known that these pigments are great 
colorants for foodstuffs and usually they replace food dyes as FD&C Red No. 2 & Red No.4 
(K. Kavanagh, 2005). 
Moreover, according to Kumi Yosgida et al (2008), blue pigment production from natural 
products, which was a very challenging procedure, has been accomplished from flower petals 
due to a flavonoid pigment, anthocyanin. Anthocyanins can be found in various tissues of higher 
plants like flowers, fruits, seed coats, leaves, stems, tubers and roots and they exhibit a wide 
variety of colors varying from red to purple and from blue to black. 
 
 
1.1.2. Azaphilones 
Azaphilones owe their name to their affinity for ammonia, as they react with primary amines 
found in amino acids, proteins and nucleic acids, for the production of vinylogous γ-pyridones 
(Park et al, 2005; Osmanova et al, 2010). They are considered to be a class of fungal secondary 
metabolites with an oxoisochromene ring system (Matsuzaki et al, 1998) and additionally many 
scientists categorize them as polyketides. Due to the fact that they are fungal polyketides, they 
exhibit an interesting diversity as 170 azaphilones have been isolated from 23 genera of fungi 
from 13 families, whereas the number of relative fungal metabolites is up to 373 compounds 
(Osmanova et al, 2010; Gao et al, 2013). Some of the most known azaphilones are listed in the 
table below (Table 1).

It is also worth mentioning that some azaphilones exhibit biological activities that are not so 
common among this category of compounds. Antimycobacterial activities for the treatment of

1.1.3. Biosynthesis 
Citrinin, which is a fungal mycotoxin, is a well-known azaphilone and it is derived from the 
genera of Penicillium, Monascus and Aspergillus (Gao et al, 2013). According to Bräse et al, 
citrinin was first isolated from Penicillium citrinum and it exhibited antibiotic activity against 
Gram positive bacteria, something that render it a possible insecticide, but since it is a very 
toxic secondary metabolite and exhibits nephrotoxic activities it cannot be considered yet a 
possible drug (Bräse et al, 2009). Moreover, it is appeared to be an infectious factor for several 
agricultural products (Zhang Dahai et al, 2007). 
As mentioned above citrinin is produced from three different genera (Penicillium, Monascus, 
Aspergillus) and this has as a result the development of two different biosynthetic pathways for 
citrinin production, regarding the genera that it is derived from (Gao et al, 2013). The two 
possible biosynthetic pathways of citrinin are presented below. 
A possible biosynthetic pathway for citrinin production from Penicillium or Aspergillus species 
starts from a pentaketide, which is a condensation product of one acetyl-CoA, four malonyl 
CoA molecules and then the pentaketide is methylated by adding three methyl units arising

The second possible biosynthetic pathway of citrinin from Monascus ruber was elucidated by 
using 13C labeling patterns. Compared to the biosynthetic pathway of citrinin from Penicillium 
or Aspergillus that produce citrinin from a pentaketide, Monascus ruber produced cirinin from 
a tetraketide. This was revealed by using 13C NMR analysis of carbon isotope distribution of 
13C-enriched citrinin after feeding the cultures with 13C-labeled acetate (Goa et al, 2013; Hajjaj 
et al, 1998). 
Figure 4 illustrates the biosynthesis of citrinin step by step. As seen from Figure 4, the precursor 
for citrinin production from M. ruber is a tetraketide, which is a condensation product of one 
acetyl-CoA and three malonyl-CoA molecules. Subsequently follows the intermediate (A´) 
production by adding one more acetyl-CoA unit to the tetraketide and then a sequence of other 
reactions like methylation, condensation, O-alkylation, reduction and dehydration lead to the 
final product.

1.2.  Fungi 
 
Fungi are organisms with an enormous biodiversity of approximately 70,000 identified species 
and around 1.5 million species in total. The fungal kingdom has been rapidly evolved and the 
products that derived from fungi have been increased due to various metabolic pathways (M.E. 
Zain et al, 2014). 
According to their morphology, fungi are divided in three categories: filamentous fungi, yeasts 
and primitive fungi (e.g. chytridomycetes). They are considered morphologically complex 
organisms, because they display huge diversity in structure at different times in their life cycle, 
they exhibit differences in form between surface and submerged growth and they also vary 
depending on the nature of the growth medium and the physical environment (M. Papagianni, 
2004). Filamentous fungi and unicellular yeasts are the most well-known, but in this report the 
main interest focuses on filamentous fungi (K. Kavanagh, 2011).  
Filamentous fungi have many important and interesting features which render them a very 
promising research field (D. Hoffmeister et al, 2006). They are well known for their ability to 
produce secondary metabolites (A.M. Calvo et al, 2002) and enzymes (primary metabolites) 
(V. Meyer, 2008) and they can grow on cheap substrates. They have many biologically active 
compounds that have been used for medical purposes over the centuries (N. Osmanova et al, 
2010). Specifically, fungi offer their special active compounds to scientists for the production 
of drugs such as antibiotics (e.g. penicillin, statins and antifungal agents), but also for industrial 
and agricultural purposes (A.M. Calvo et al, 2002; N. Osmanova et al, 2010). Nevertheless, it 
should mentioned that some of the natural products that derived from fungi are toxic and 
carcinogenic for all living organisms and they don’t exhibit advantageous properties (e.g. 
alfatoxins, mycotoxins, trichothecenes). On the contrary, there is another category known as 
“janus-faced metabolites”, which even though they are toxic, they exhibit pharmaceutically 
advantageous features like ergot alkaloids (M.E. Zain et al, 2014; D. Hoffmeister et al, 2006; 
A.M. Calvo et al, 2002). 
Moreover, according to N.Osmanova et al, fungi exhibit chemical diversity which is explained 
by the large number of chemical groups found in their products. Some of these chemical groups 
are listed below: 
 Organic acids 
 Polyynes 
 Polyketides ( quinones, anthraquinones) 
 Mono-, Di-, Triterpenes ( volatiles and steroids) 
 Polyscharides, Lipopolysacharides

1.2.1. Fungal metabolites 
Fungal metabolisms’ products can be classified in two categories: primary & secondary 
metabolites. The main difference between these two categories is that primary metabolites are 
substantial for all living organisms compared to secondary. According to Daniel A. Dias et al 
(2012), “the biosynthesis and break down of proteins, fats, nucleic acids and carbohydrates, 
which are essential to all living organisms, is known as primary metabolism with the 
compounds involved in the pathways known as primary metabolites. The mechanism by which 
an organism biosynthesizes compounds called secondary metabolites (natural products) is often 
found to be unique to an organism or is an expression of the individuality of a species and is 
referred to as secondary metabolism”. For instance, when filamentous fungi are used as cell 
factories, it is possible to delete some secondary metabolites in order to increase the yield of 
the production of the desirable product.  
According to K. Kavanagh (2011), primary metabolites usually are proteins, carbohydrates, 
nucleic acids and lipids and their role is crucial for normal growth. They are produced in the 
beginning of the growth phase of the organism and the highest rate of production is indicated 
at the end of this phase. Furthermore, they continue to be metabolized during the stationary 
phase and the most affluent products of primary metabolism are organic acids, enzymes, fats 
and alcohol. 
On the other hand, secondary metabolites aren’t crucial for normal growth of the organism. In 
fact they exhibit very little or not at all primary function. Unlike primary metabolites, their 
production occurs in the beginning of stationary phase only if the production rate of the starting 
phase has decreased. The special characteristics of secondary metabolites are sporulation, 
differentiation and their biological activities. Moreover, they can be found only in confined 
organisms and they may exhibit diverse features even in the strains of the same kind (K. 
Kavanagh, 2011).

1.2.3. Fungal Fermentation Systems 
The basic fermentation systems, which utilize different types of fungi (filamentous, yeast etc) 
according to the products that are required to produce and the production scale are: Solid 
(mushrooms), Batch (beer), Fed-Batch (baker’s yeast), Cell recycle batch (wine) and 
Continuous (mycoprotein). Sometimes the above systems are modified in order to be suitable 
for the production of specific fungal products, because the features of each product determine 
the characteristics of the fermentation system (K. Kavanagh, 2011). 
 Solid fermentation 
In solid fermentation the fungi grow on solid substrates with no water inside, which means that 
the fungi must be able to bear low water activity. Solid fermentations are usually used for the 
production of edible mushrooms and for the isolation of antibiotics and enzymes that come 
from filamentous fungi. Although solid fermentations have a wide range of applications, they 
may be more expensive and difficult to control compared with liquid fermentation                       
(K. Kavanagh, 2011). 
 Batch, Fed-Batch and Continuous fermentation 
Batch and Fed-Batch fermentations are the most important and common fermentation types. 
The majority of commercial fermentations use these systems, because it is easy to handle them 
and their size can be adjusted according to the production demands (K. Kavanagh, 2011). 
The batch system is commonly used for the production of alcoholic beverages and industrial 
solvents such as ethanol and acetone and it should mentioned, that in batch fermentation the 
medium is inoculated at a low density with fungal cells (K. Kavanagh, 2011). Moreover, batch 
cultivation is divided in three phases: the lag, the exponential and the stationary phase and each 
phase represents a different period during the cultivation procedure. The lag phase shows the 
adaptation procedure of the fungal cells and spores to a new environment. The exponential 
procedure indicates the increase in cell mass and the stationary phase represents the mass 
balance (M.Papagianni, 2003). 
In Fed-Batch, the nutrients are added periodically during the fermentation process compared to 
the Batch system (K. Kanavagh, 2011), whereas the growth rate in Fed-Batch isn’t constant 
compared to the Continuous fermentation system (M. Papagiannni, 2003). 
Finally, in the Continuous fermentation procedure the cells are cultivated under steady-state 
conditions and they are retained in a specific phase of the growth cycle. Moreover, in contrast 
with Batch fermentation the nutrients are added continuously with a constant rate and biomass 
is removed respectively with the same constant rate. It is more expensive compared to Batch 
fermentation but lately it is utilized for the production of antibiotics and mycoproteins (K. 
Kavanagh, 2011).

1.3. Analytical Chemistry 
 
1.3.1. Liquid Chromatography 
Liquid Chromatography is a separation technique. It consists of a mobile phase and a stationary 
phase. The stationary phase is a solid porous material or a liquid immobilized on a solid 
substrate, which is packed on a column, whereas the mobile phase is liquid. The transmission 
of the liquid mobile phase through the stationary phase is achieved either because of the gravity 
or due to the utilization of low or high pressure pumps. Low pressure pumps are used when the 
stationary phase is consisted of particles with large diameter and exhibit a small resistance (LC). 
On the other hand, high pressure pumps are used when the stationary phase is consisted of 
particles with small diameter, which they strongly resist to the separation (HPLC) 
(Chatziioannou & Couparis, 2005). 
Liquid chromatography is extremely useful for analysis and separation of molecular mixtures 
or ionic compounds with low vapor pressure. HPLC and UPLC (high and ultra-performance 
liquid chromatography) are used for the quantitative analysis of complex mixtures 
(Chatziioannou & Couparis, 2005). 
Figure 5 describes the instrument of a liquid chromatography system. The sample passes in the 
mobile phase through a column, which is packed with a stationary phase, and then follows the 
separation of the sample in its components that are eluted one by one from the end of the 
column. After that a specific detector (UV, DAD etc.) measures the eluted components and 
displays them as peaks on a chart (Premier Biosoft, 2015)

1.3.2. Reverse Phase Chromatography 
Reverse phase HPLC chromatography is one of the most popular and reliable analytical 
techniques and it is used for the separation of individual components that belong to one 
constituent class. It provides the best results, as far as reproducibility of retention time and 
separation of the most polar compounds, are concerned (A. Carrasco-Pancorbo et al, 2005). 
The stationary phase could be a C18 column, which consists of a non-polar octadecylsilane 
bonded phase, a C8 octyl column or a phenyl column, whereas the mobile phase is a polar 
solvent (eg. H2O/ACN (water/acetonitrile), H2O/MeOH). In RP the change in the ratio of 
H2O/ACN is necessary due to different adsorption rates of the species of the mixtures. In more 
details some of the mixture’s components have the ability to elute very quickly, whereas some 
others remain on the column during the experiment. This is solved by using gradient elution 
chromatography, where the concentration of an eluent constituent can change and enable the 
elution of all the mixture components (F. D. Antia & C. Horvath, 1991). 
1.3.3. Normal Phase Chromatography 
Normal phase liquid chromatography is the precursor of reverse phase and it has been used as 
an analytical tool for more than 100 years. In NPLC, the stationary phase is a polar phase 
whereas the mobile phase is nonpolar. In detail, the column could be packed with silica gel (or 
titanium, aluminium) or with a moderately polar bonded phase like diol: -(CH2)3-O-CH2-
CHOH-CH2-OH, cyanoproyl: -(CH2)3-CN and the mobile phase could be a mixture of two or 
more organic solvents (nonpolar solvent and strongly or weakly polar solvents) (P. Jandera, 
2005). 
 
1.3.4. Diode Array Detector 
The general concept of a diode array spectrometry regards the interaction between the 
electromagnetic radiation and the sample. This interaction comes out from the concurrent 
measurement of light intensity over small spectral channels, which have the same width (R.P.W 
Scott, 2000).  
According to R.P.W. Scott (2000), the Diode Array Detector is regarded as “the most versatile 
and useful detector for everyday use in liquid chromatography analyses”. 
Generally the detector is the second most important part of the liquid chromatograph (first is 
the column), due to its ability to define the accuracy and the precision of the LC analysis (R.P.W 
Scott, 2005).

spectrum. Then, in order the total light to pass through the detector flow cell on to a holographic 
grating, the light that comes out from this lamp is aligned by an achromatic lens. These lamps 
are able to produce light of all the wavelengths, which means that our sample is subjected to 
this light. After that, the scattered light from the holographic grating is permitted to fall onto

1.3.5. Mass Spectroscopy 
Mass spectrometry is an analytical technique that is commonly used in combination with LC 
for the investigation of molecules and complex mixtures. The MS fragments and adducts are 
responsible for the determination of the elemental composition of the molecule and the 
confirmation of the molecular structure (Byung-Man Kwak et al, 2014).  
The instrument consists of an ionization source, an analyzer and a detector. The ionization 
source produces the gas ions from the molecule. Then, these ions are placed into an analyzer 
and according to their mass-to-charge-ratio they are separated. Finally the detector records the 
relative abundance of the resolved ionic species and provides the mass spectrum of the 
molecules (Premier Biosoft, 2015). The schematic representation below (Fig.7) describes the 
appropriate steps for the analysis of a sample in an LC/MS instrument.  
It should also mentioned that the conjugation of LC and MS is done by means of a so-called 
interface. This interface is an instrument that is placed onto the MS to which the LC is 
connected. The most used interfaces are the ESI (electrospray ionization) (Fig. 8) and the APCI 
(atmospheric-pressure chemical ionization) techniques (W.M.A Niessen, 2005). The reason 
why the interface is necessary is because the mass spectrometer is under high vacuum and it is 
difficult for the liquid from the LC to be transferred into the ion source of the MS                  
(Daniel C. Harris, 2010).

1.3.6. Electrospray Ionization 
One of the most well-known and often used soft ionization methods in mass spectrometry is 
Electrospray Ionization (ESI). In ESI, the analytes are ionized and this ionization leads to the 
formation of either positive or negative gas-phase ions, which move toward the ion-sampling 
orifice (W.M.A Niessen, 2005). 
The principle of operation of ESI requires the use of nebulization. The pneumatically assisted 
electrospray is performed more often compared to electrospray nebulization, because the latter 
can be function only at low flow rates (up to 10 µL/min). In ESI, a strong electric potential 
between the needle and the counter-electrode is responsible for nebulization of the liquid flow 
into small charged droplets (Fig.8). Depending on the operation of the source, positive-ion 
mode or negative-ion mode, the droplets are positively or negatively charged respectively 
(W.M.A Niessen, 2005). The positive ions can be like [M+nH]n+, [M+nX]n+, where X can be 
Na, NH4, K etc. and the negative ions can be like [M-nH]n- (D.J. Harvey, 2005).

1.3.7. Quadrupole Time – Of - Flight  
Time Of Flight mass analyzers are responsible for ion separation by utilizing the different flight 
times of the ions and they are used almost 80 years for the determination of ion mass-to-charge 
ratios (m/z). More specifically the ions which exit their ion source are accelerated by applying 
different electric fields, and as a result they have equal kinetic energy. After that, they move 
towards a flight tube where they travel from the source to detector and lead to the calculation 
of m/z value. It is important to mention that the lower mass ions travel quicker through the tube 
and exhibit shorter flight times (M. Guilhaus, 2005; R. Sleeman & J.F. Carter, 2005). 
Quadrupole Time-Of-Flight mass analyzers exhibit better performance and high scan speeds. 
Moreover, as M. Guilhaus (2005) claim, the resolutions and the signal to noise ratio are very 
impressive and the quadrupole is able to transfer almost 100% of the ions to the TOF-MS. As 
it can be seen from Figure 9, the quadrupole mass filter contains four parallel rods and the mass 
selection is determined by the ion motion. Moreover quadrupoles exhibit high scan speeds, 
which are performed by changing the m/z value (R. Sleeman & J.F. Carter, 2005).

1.3.8. Dereplication 
Dereplication is a very important technique for the identification of novel and unknown 
compounds and it is used extensively in natural product chemistry, due to the complexity and 
diversity of the compounds that derived from natural products. This technique provides rapid 
identification of the compounds without using any standards. The application of dereplication 
techniques requires the utilization of chromatographic and spectroscopic methods along with 
database searching (e.g. Antibase) (K.F. Nielsen and T.O. Larsen, 2015; K.F. Nielsen et al, 
2011). 
The dereplication process will provide the necessary information for the compounds of interest. 
Base Peak Chromatograms are preferred in the dereplication process, as they exhibit high 
intensity peaks and less noise compared to Total Ion Chromatograms, which contain all the ions 
of the mass spectrum as well as a higher level of noise. Moreover, the DAD or UV profile of 
the compounds is a very important tool in the identification procedure as it separates the 
compounds according to their ability to absorb at different wavelengths (K.F. Nielsen and T.O. 
Larsen, 2015; K.F. Nielsen et al, 2011). 
The steps of the interpretation procedure depend on whether the compound is known or 
unknown.  In case of a known compound, where the formula and structure have already been 
identified, the first step is the calculation of the monoisotopic masses of the peaks with the 
==PAGE:26==
Chapter 1: Background                                                           Discovery Of Microbial Pigments                        
19 
 
highest intensity in the BPC (like [M+H]+, [M+Na]+, [2M+H]+, [M-H2O+H]+ and others) The 
second step is the search of these compounds in databases in order to confirm the compounds 
of interest by extracted ion chromatograms (EIC), which help in the differentiation of the 
compounds (deconvolution). On the other hand, in the totally unknown compound scenario, a 
series of different steps is followed. The adduct pattern analysis is the main step in order to 
move forward and indicates the existence of the unknown compound. The elucidation of the 
adduct pattern follows the search in well informed databases (e.g. Antibase), which will verify 
if the mass fits to any compounds (K.F. Nielsen and T.O. Larsen, 2015; K.F. Nielsen et al, 
2011). 
Nowadays high resolution mass spectrometry as well as new well informed databases has 
improved the dereplication process (K.F. Nielsen and T.O. Larsen, 2015) 
 
1.3.9. Nuclear Magnetic Resonance 
The nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy is a technique that is mostly used by chemists in 
order to study the structure and the dynamics of simple and more complex molecules. It is a 
nondestructive quantitative technique and it is also used extensively for medical purposes, MRI 
(magnetic resonance imaging). Depending on the complexity of the molecule, it is used either 
one-dimensional or two-dimensional NMR (J.B. Grutzner, 2005; A. Bainbridge, 2005)  
The NMR spectroscopy is based on the spectrum of different atomic nuclei that are excited 
from the application of a magnetic field and a radiofrequency transmitter. Moreover this method 
is capable for the determination of the 3D-structure of the molecules (J. B. Grutzner, 2005).

2.9. Fermentation procedure 
 
The fermentation of Talaromyces atroroseus in the three reactors was accomplished by the PhD 
student Kasper Bøwig Rasmussen as it is described below. 
Strain 
Talaromyces atroroseus IBT11181 was retrieved from the IBT collection at the Department of 
Systems Biology, Technical University of Denmark, Lyngby, Denmark. 
Media for liquid cultivations 
For batch cultivation a defined medium with the following composition was used:  
20 g L-1 sucrose; 1 g L-1 glucose;  10 g L-1 KNO3; 8 g L-1 K2PO4; 1 g L-1 NaCl; 2 g L-1 MgSO4; 
0.5 g L-1 KCl; 0.1 g L-1 CaCl·2H20; 1 mL  L-1trace metal solution (composed of: 0.4 g L-
1 CuSO4·5H2O; 0.04 g L-1 Na2B4O7· 10H2O; 0.8 g L-1 FeSO4· 7H2O; 0.8 g L-1 MnSO4· 2H2O; 
0.8 g L-1 Na2MoO4· 2H2O; and 0.8 g L-1 ZnSO4· 7H2O) 
Inoculum 
T. atroroseus IBT11181 was propagated on CYA for 7 days at 30 oC and spores were harvested 
with 0.9 % NaCl-solution and filtered through sterile Mira-cloth. The concentration of the 
resulting spore suspension was determined by hemocytometer counting. 
Batch cultivation 
Batch fermentations were carried out in 2L Sartorious Biostat B bioreactors with a working 
volume of 1.8 L. The bioreactors were equipped and monitored with a calibrated pH electrode     
and a temperature electrode. Agitation was provided by two Rushton impellers (flat bladed 
stirrers with 6 blades each). The cultivation was run at 30 oC and pH was automatically 
maintained at pH 5.0 by addition of 2M NaOH and H2SO4. The cultivations were run for 6 days 
after which the supernatant was collected by filtration, shielded against light and stored at 4oC 
until further processing. 
2.10. Inoculation procedure 
 
The inoculation procedure of Talaromyces atroroseus strain was accomplished by inoculating 
three spots in six different media. The semi solid fungal cultures were grown for 7 days at          
30 oC in CYA, YES, WATM, MEA-OX, MEA and PDA media in the incubator. After 7 days 
the plugs were extracted in order to look for secondary metabolites.

2.12. Crude fractionation on Isolera (Flash Chromatography) 
 
 
In order to start the fractionation procedure of the crude extract on Isolera, it was necessary to 
extract first the fat like molecules, because the goal of this experiment was to identify another 
family of compounds, the azaphilones. 
The separation procedure of fat like molecules started by pooling the extracts from reactors 2, 
3 and 5 in a blue cap bottle. The extracts from the three reactors dissolved with methanol, before 
they pooled all together in a blue cap bottle. The volume of methanol that was added in total 
was 50mL: Reactor 2: 20 mL, Reactor 3: 15mL and Reactor 5: 15mL. Subsequently, 50 mL 
heptane were added in the combined dissolved extract and the separation of the two phases was 
very clear. It should mentioned that 5 mL miliQ water were also added in the solution for better 
separation of the two phases. A quick separation was achieved by putting the mixture in a small 
separating funnel and after that collect the two phases in two different bottles. The heptane 
phase was extracted two times, by adding 10mL more methanol and separate again the two 
phases in the small separating funnel. The MeOH + H2O phase that was collected from the 
second extraction was added to the initial one and the final volume of methanol phase was 40 
mL.  
The next step of separation procedure was to evaporate the two solvents in order to find out the 
real amount of pigments from the initial fermentation broth (reactors 2, 3 and 5), after 
subtracting the fat like molecules that were collected in heptane phase. The evaporation of the 
heptane phase showed that the fatty acids and the lipids were approximately 15 mg, so the final 
amount of the pigments from the three reactors was: 
 0.2457 (R-2) + 0.2987 (R-3) + 0.3859 (R-5) – 0.015 = 0.9303g ≅ 930 mg

3.1. pH range 2 to 7 
 
The choice of pH was based solely on visual inspection of phases (Fig.22). The following BPCs 
show the amount of the compounds produced between different pH extractions but they cannot 
be the only criteria for pH selection, as there might be some uncertainties due to different 
volume of broth and organic phases. 
The BPCs along with the UV spectra presented in Figure 23, indicate that the highest amount 
of red compounds is produced in the more acidic pH range. This is also obvious from Fig.22, 
which shows the color gradation of the extracts and verifies that the reddest compounds are 
produced in the more acidic pH. The reddest compounds appear in pH 2, 2.5 and 3, whereas the 
least red compounds appear in pH 6, 6. 5 and 7. 
According to the BPCs of the extracts (Fig.23) at different pH values and the visual inspection 
of the gradation of the color (Fig.22), the most suitable pH for extraction of Talaromyces 
atroroseus appears in the range of 2-3. More specifically, for the experiments that took place 
in the current master thesis, the pH that was chosen was 3.  
According to M. Papagianni et al (2004), the pH of the medium is a very important process 
parameter that can be also affected by the composition of the medium. The optimum pH value 
differs between different fungi species and different cultivation procedures. 
It should be mentioned that the compounds which were extracted at different pH values, are 
known and unknown red compounds that are presented in the following sections and most of 
them are included in Table 16.

in this range. This is also confirmed by the DAD profile of some compounds that were extracted

acquire the mass spectrum of the compound, which is used as a base for the calculation of the

Chapter 4: Conclusion  
 
The main aspect of this project was to investigate the similarity of the compounds, produced by 
the fungus Talaromyces atroroseus, in different cultivation media, solid or liquid, at different 
pH values. The majority of the compounds weren’t the same and this led to the conclusion that 
the selection of the media as well as the regulation of the process parameters influences the 
production. Moreover, it should be also mentioned that the diversity of the compounds wasn’t 
obvious only between the solid and liquid media but also among different solid media. 
Another aspect of this master thesis was the isolation and the purification of a known and an 
unknown compound that derived from the cultivation of the fungus Talaromyces atroroseus in 
liquid media. The unknown compound with mass 540.210 Da was purified successfully, 
whereas the known compound pitholide B (386.173 Da) couldn’t be isolated, because either it 
was lost or it broke in more compounds during the preparative HPLC. The purified novel 
compound is highly likely belonging to the family of azaphilones, because of its very 
characteristic UV spectrum. 
The current master thesis showed that the isolation and the purification of a compound is a 
many steps process and requires the development of accurate extraction and analytical methods. 
The utilization of a very accurate analytical tool like LC-DAD-MS, is the key in order to 
achieve the purification of a novel or a known compound.
