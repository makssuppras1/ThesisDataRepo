Abstract
The aim of this work is to extend a two-stage stochastic programming formula-
tion to a multistage one for a Maritime fleet Renewal Problem and to evaluate
it. We have solved the multistage formulation for a small dummy network of
five ports and we have calculated the Value of Stochastic Solution, the Value
of Stochastic Solution Dynamic and the Expected value of Perfect Information
to assess its performance. We have implemented also a more efficient formu-
lation to calculate the bounds on the Stochastic Solution and on the Value of
the Stochastic Solution for a real network with twenty-three nodes. We have
concluded that our multistage stochastic programming formulation severely
suffers a curse of dimensionality and it is almost intractable for realistic net-
work. Nevertheless, the multistage stochastic programming looks appropriate
to address the Maritime fleet Renewal Problem for container liner shipping
companies, especially for instances favoring low profitability scenarios.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Introduction and motivation
The shipping industry is fundamental to international trade since it provides
a cost-effective means to transport large volume of goods. The demand for
shipping services stem from the world trade and its demand of goods. The
shipping service involves many activities to facilitate and support the trans-
port, such as fixed infrastructure (ports and terminal), vehicles (vessels) to
move cargoes and organizational system to plan effectively the usage of the
resources. Therefore the shipping industry requires intense strategic, tactical
and operational planning (Lun et al., 2010, pp.1-2). The strategic planning
addresses the fleet size problem, the fleet size mix problem and the network
optimization. The tactical planning provides fleet deployment scheduling and
short-term fleet adaption such as charter in and out, layoffs and buying or sell
on the second hand market. The strategic and tactical planning problems are
often tackle together since they are tightly related.
Considering the strategic and tactical level, the ŌĆØshipping cyclesŌĆØ cannot be
neglected. This is a pattern in the maritime shipping market affecting its rev-
enues. It is triggered by and excess of demand which rise the freight rates and
cause a short of vessels. The liner shipping companies react building more ships
until an excess of supply on the market hits lowering the freight rates. The
pattern of each cycle is difficult to predict and it derives from many economical
and political factors. Therefore, the liner companies have to make the best
estimation based on their experience of past cycles and the up to date informa-
tion on the global economical, political and market situations (Lun et al., 2010,
pp.27-30). Due its high uncertainty, the fleet renewal problem for maritime
shipping industry is suitable for the stochastic programming (SP) approach.
In fact, this decision support tool allows to consider different estimations for
the future demand and freight rates (Pantuso et al., 2014).
Therefore, we

aim to extend and further investigate a two-stage SP model already developed

1.2
Literature review
The previous work from which we started our multistage SP formulation and
investigation has found out that the stochastic programming approach has not
been deeply explored so far. Particularly, Pantuso et al. (2014) reviewed the
methodology used to model maritime fleet size renewal problem (MFRP) and
found out that only 10 out of 37 reviewed papers use a stochastic formulation,
nonetheless the high level of uncertainty involved into this strategic decisions.
Moreover, the same study also pointed out the lack of scientific research de-
veloping tools to support these strategic decisions for the maritime shipping
industry addressing the tactical and strategic level together.
The previous study started from a deep review of the main paper on the MFRP
and related tactical problem. We have reviewed the paper on which the two-
stage model had been developed and we have summarized their main features
in Table 1.1.
Since we started from a ready-made two-stage model, our work focuses mostly
on the extension of the model, its implementation and on the evaluation of
the relevance of the multistage SP. The review we have carried out on the
multistage formulation and the main methodologies to assess it are presented
in the Chapters 2 and 4.

1.3
Project structure
This project is organized as follows. Chapter 2 introduces the main features
and models related with SP. Then, it focuses on the difference between the two-
stage and the general multistage formulation. Chapter 3 presents the models
we have developed starting from the two-stage models. We present only the
extension (multistage) of the model we have developed, but we stress what we
have changed from the previous two-stage formulation. We also introduce a
slightly different formulation which makes the problem easier to be handled by

we have selected from the literature and a small example to evaluate our for-
mulations. In Chapter 5, we describe the numerical study we have conducted
using our multistage SP models. Eventually, Chapter 6 concludes the work
with a summary and outlooks.

Definition 4. A period is the single time-step in which the horizon is split.

last for the amount of periods needed to open the warehouse and the second
stage for all the rest of periods left to the end of the planning horizon. In case
that more than one single initial investments are allowed, then the correct way

Models formulation
In the Section 3.3, we introduce the multistage SP using the explicit formula-
tion for nonanticipativity constraint. Next, the Section 3.4 presents the same
model with small changes and embedding the nonanticipativity constraint into
its variable declaration.
3.1
Problem Description
We have introduced in Chapter 1 the need of support for a strategic and
delicate decision such as fleet renewal. So, after having introduced the SP and
its general case, i.e. the multistage. We have extended an already developed
two-stage model to support the aforementioned process. The model simulates a
complete maritime container shipping network with vessels routes at which the
container ships are assigned in each period and scenario. This routes does not
overlap completely with the container routes, hence a container may be picked
up at one port by one ship and being transshipped to another vessel to get to its
destination port. The containers are moved around to meet a random demand.
The developed model has variables to model the renewal of the fleet along
the horizon and others to depict the whole network evolution (deployment of
the fleet). The variables counting for the new vessels to buy are of the kind
here-and-now, while the other variables to adjust the fleet and deploy it are
wait-and-see. The model imposes also a minimum frequency on the routes and
the speed selection for the deployed vessels. Further, it also considers the need
of empty containers at the ports to ship goods. Therefore,those are balanced
as well in the model. Eventually, the life span of a vessel is longer than the
horizon considered in our numerical study, so also the fleetŌĆÖs book value is
accounted. All these details make the model closer to the real MFRP.
==PAGE:24==
3.2 General assumption
13
3.2
General assumption
The general assumptions stay the same as in the previous developed two-stage
model. Anyway, we are going to sum them up again for the reader who is not
familiar with the previous model.
Firstly, the frequency required on each port of a vessel route is weekly, i.e. we
can use the specific vessel route only if we have enough ships to ensure this
schedule. This complies with the minimum liner shipping standards ensuring
a minimum customer quality (Notteboom and Vernimmen, 2009).
Secondly, the fuel consumption is assumed to be constant regardless the total
weight of the ship, even though this is not realistic.
Thirdly, the maintenance costs are considered constant the same for group of
vessels. This is not completely correct because it should differ in proportion to
the age of the ships. We cannot implement it since we account for the groups
of vessels and not for the single ships.
Eventually, the time of loading and unloading the container is neglected as
found in other studies on fleet sizing with empty container repositioning (Dong
and Song, 2009). Moreover, the full container arriving at the port are imme-
diately considered available to ship new goods.
All these assumptions are needed to simplify the problem formulation for the
entire network. This is consistent with our goal of sketching a liner shipping
network on a strategic and aggregate level.

3.4
Path-based model formulation
We have developed a slightly different model in order to partially overcome
the curse of dimensionality suffered by the standard SP model formulation.
Nickel et al. (2012) developed a multistage model embedding into a path-based
formulation the nonanticipativity principle for a supply network design process.
We have applied the same path-based formulation to our SP multistage model.
Additionally, we have combined some small changes to the model with the
path-based formulation to bring about an overall enhanced performance of the
model without losing generality.
The main adjustments to the original formulation are the followings:
ŌĆó Implementation using dynamic arrays, which implicitly comprise the
nonanticipativity principle
ŌĆó Discard annuities values instead of accounting for the whole cost of the
vessels added to the fleet
ŌĆó Do not allow the model to sell or scrap any of the fleetŌĆÖs vessels
Particularly, we have decided to do not let the model sell or scrap any ves-
sel because in all the tests we have carried out, using the general multistage
formulation, those two options have never been used.

CHAPTER 4
Relevance of multistage
The stochastic programs suffers of an even worse course of dimensionality than
dynamic programming as mentioned in Section 2.1. So often the stochastic
programming is avoided and the deterministic approach using expected values
is preferred instead. Another well-known option is to solve the deterministic
problem associated with each scenario and then combining them using some
heuristic (Birge and Louveaux, 1997, pp.163). The problem is to assess when
a deterministic approach can accurately approximate the uncertainty involved
in the process. To evaluate that, there are different concepts that can be used.
The most common are designed for two-stage SP but can also be applied to
multistage cases. These concepts or KPIs involve solving some deterministic
or SP related to the one we are assessing but easier to compute. We will see
that also some bounds on the SP can be derived. Those could help in case the
stochastic model itself would be to large to solve optimally.
In this chapter we present the KPIs we have selected to evaluate the multistage
SP we introduced in Sections 3.3 and 3.4. In Section 4.1 are shown essential
models, known as Wait-and-See (WS), Expected Value (EV) and Expected result
of using Expected Value (EEV), which are needed to define the Expected Value
of Perfect Information (EVPI) and the Value of Stochastic Solution (VSS).
In the subsequent Sections 4.2 and 4.3 a dynamic version of VSS designed
for Multistage SP (VSSD) and a KPI to evaluate the Multistage over a two
stage program are explained.
Subsequently, some refined bounds to check
large instances are presented in Section 4.4. Eventually, in Section 4.5 a small
example is presented and commented.

Comment on the results
Overall the KPIs are higher on the small initial fleet, showing the higher rele-
vance of a SP and the need for higher quality forecast in case the initial fleet is
smaller. Moreover, the optimistic configurations have the lowest EVPI, VSS
and VSSD. This can be better visualized in Figure 4.4 - 4.5.
The higher EVPI denotes that knowing exactly the future realization leads to
non-single solution (see Table 4.17), hence larger profit when solving the WS
model.
We have also noticed that the EV solutions are almost the same as the RP
one in optimistic configurations (see Table 4.16 and Table 4.8-4.9). Therefore,
the solution of the EEV model leads to have almost the same optimal solution
of the RP model.
This is due to the fact that the here-and-now variables
are tightly upper limited, particularly for the 8000 TEU vessels which are the
most profitable on the tested network. Whereas, for the pessimistic instances,
the EV optimal solutions need less capacity than the RP one. This forces the
EEV models to use more wait-and-see variables to adjust the fleet than the
RP solution does, thus the EEV returns a lower objective.

5.2
Scenario tree generation
Due to the gaps we need an efficient way of structure the future uncertainty
into a scenario tree. We need to capture both the stochastic parameter we are
considering without increasing enormously the number of scenario. There are
many different scenario tree generation, such as binomial tree (Cox and Ross,
1979) and many reduction technique, such as algorithm to cluster nodes (Chen
and Yan (2018), Heitsch and R├Čmisch (2009)). However, those reduction tech-
niques usually require a not so easy algorithm to be implemented. Whereas,
Bakkehaug et al. (2014) propose to condense all the uncertainty into one single
parameter representing the market situation of the maritime shipping indus-
try. Therefore, it is possible to derive the demand and the freight rates only
knowing the market status given a perfect correlation among them.
This assumption allows to halve the number of scenario and describe the same
variety of future event since we do not need to combine two different tree, one
for each of the stochastic parameter. For this reason we have decided to follow
this methodology to generate the scenario tree used in our numerical study.
To model the market situation, a stochastic variable marketStatus is defined,
and it varies between 0 and 1, which respectively corresponds to the worst and
best possible market situations.
Then, to model the probability of the marketStatus having the value ms in
period z (P(m, z)), it is assumed P(m, z) to have a symmetric, bell shaped,

As we mentioned in the introduction, the maritime shipping industry suffer of a
problem called ŌĆØshipping cyclesŌĆØ regarding the supply of new ships. When the
market faces an overcapacity on the supply side together with a low demand,
==PAGE:67==
5.3 Small network study

then a recession phases hits in (stage of collapse). The equilibrium point is in
the left part of the ŌĆØJŌĆØ shaped supply curve. This makes the price of supply
of sea transport extremely elastic. This means that a small change of freight
rates would cause a large change in the quantity transported. This elasticity
effect is due to the fact that to increase the supply tonnage there is no need to
build new ships or travel faster. It is enough to reach slightly higher revenue
level to make more profitable to use the vessels instead of lay them up in the
harbour. On the contrary, if the shipping cycle is in its phase of rising freight
rates and shortage of supply (stage of peak), then the number of service can
be increased only rising sailing speeds and operation efficiency. Since these
options are expensive, the supply curve gets steeper and this results in an
inelastic price of the supply. Hence, to obtain a small increase of quantity
transported a large increase in the freight rates is needed (Lun et al., 2010,
pp.23-24).
Due to this characteristic of the maritime shipping market, we have decided to
test also three different configuration of price elasticity of the supply. Keeping
the direct proportionality between the variation of demand and market situa-
tion (Equation 5.7), we have decided to halve or double the change of fright
rates to simulate elastic and inelastic supply, respectively. We have tested it
for all the three initial marketStatus.

ing the freedom on the wait-and-see variable. We have tested three different
configuration, where we have raised the cost for the charter service and the
second-hand ships by 100%, 200% and 300%.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
Summary
Throughout this work we have focused on the extension of a previous work,
which developed a two-stage SP to support the fleet renewal decision process.
Firstly, we have introduced all the concepts related to multistage SP and we
have developed a multistage model from the two-stage one. Next, we have
tested different configuration to find out whether the multistage formulation
is worthy on our model. To assess our model we have used different concepts
from the SP literature.
From the study we have carried out can be concluded that the multistage model
we have developed suffers of an extreme curse of dimensionality (a common
problem in SP). However, we have observed some remarkable results for the
VSS and the EVPI, particularly for the instance favouring pessimistic scenarios
(low initial market status). This means that the stochastic approach is valuable
in periods of high uncertainty and low demand and revenue. The small study
we have conducted to compare the multistage with the two-stage approach
as shown a substantial superiority of the multistage approach, although it
suffers of an even increase curse of dimensionality. This makes the multistage
stochastic model almost intractable, as we have seen for the large network
study.
Overall, we believe that the multistage stochastic approach is worthy to be
applied on the decision process of fleet renewal for container shipping company.
Anyway, the model we have formulated might be further improved on the
modelling side as well as on the solution approach. We have tried to sum up
the possible improvement in the next Section.
==PAGE:78==
6 Conclusion
67
Outlook
The model could include some option which are not considered in our current
model. For instance the option to charter out some vessel of our fleet as it
is implemented in Meng et al. (2015). This implementation may result into
a worsening of the curse of dimensionality since we need to add variables
to the model. While, a not costly implementation could be to derive from
the marketStatus also the other parameter involved, such as the second-hand
vessels and chartering costs (Bakkehaug et al., 2014).
Additionally, we assume that in a short lead time (six month) we can receive
a new vessels and this is not realistic. Indeed, the average lead time of a new
vessels is ranging from one to four years (Lun et al., 2010, pp.27). We might
have extended the number of period of each second-stage decision but then the
increase of dimensionality of our problem would have made the multistage pro-
gram intractable. However, this problem could be overcome with a different
solutions approach and a more efficient scenario tree generation and reduc-
tion technique. For instance, applying some decomposition algorithm (Higle
et al., 2010) and apply some scenario tree reduction technique, such as the one
suggested in Kim (2006) for binomial tree.
We have seen in our numerical study that a large freedom of the model pro-
vided by the wait-and-see variables makes the here-and-now decisions more
insensitive to the scenario realizations. This is one of the main explanation for
the low VSS encountered, together with the narrow limit on the new vessels
available in each period. Indeed, the deterministic problem (EV) often pro-
vided almost the same solution as the SP. The freedom of the model could be
reduced changing the second-hand vessel variables from wait-and-see to here-
and-now variables. This assumption can still be considered realistic since also
in the study from Meng et al. (2015) and Bakkehaug et al. (2014) the second-
