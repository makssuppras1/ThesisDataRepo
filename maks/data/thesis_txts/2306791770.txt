Abstract (English)
The use of optimization techniques to solve unit commitment, and energy dispatch problems, is
a widely known and accepted method to simulate large scale power systems. These problems
are typically solved by techniques like Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP) optimization,
which rely on integers, and therefore are highly demanding in terms of computational power, and
solving time. Currently, there is a theoretical gap in relation with the use of quicker techniques
to solve these problems. Hence, the purpose of this research, is ﬁrst, to develop a faster Linear
Programming (LP) optimization model, and then to investigate diﬀerent options to utilize it to
solve unit commitment, and energy dispatch problems.
Based upon an already existent MILP, DispaSET 2.0 (developed at Joint Reseacrh Center),
a LP model was produced in this research.
The original model represents with a high level
of detail the short-term operation of the European power system, establishing with an hourly
resolution, the commitment status of the power plants of Belgium in 2012. The work carried out
for this thesis included the extension of the model to six additional countries: Belgium, France,
The Netherlands, Germany, Austria and Switzerland. Thus, the LP formulated, included six
countries, for the year 2015.
Advanced clustering of units is used in this project to produce a fast LP, from 444 generators,
after the clustering, only 58 units remain, producing a faster, and lighter LP, which was used
then to simulate the European power system. The LP was developed in response to the enormous
computation time required to solve the MILP model. The LP demonstrated to be able to attain
a result in a fraction of the time a MILP did, 4,595 seconds for LP compared to 59,400 seconds
for the MILP. Furthermore, the LP model developed was compared to the MILP, it proved to
be a tool able to estimate with great accuracy the electricity mix in the generation of a large
region, as well as the interconnections, and the total system costs.
LP models though, have a shortcoming when dealing with the commitment status of generators,
since they use continuous variables, it is not possible to describe with precision whether a unit is
on, or oﬀ. To tackle this issue, a hybrid approach was described and tested. By this approach,
the LP model is utilized to calculate the interconnections between countries, whereas the MILP is
used to determine commitment status, power output, and system costs. Finally, hybrid approach
and MILP are compared in their results. It is shown that the LP is able to reduce the computation
time drastically with respect to simply using MILP, moreover, the comparison showed negligible
diﬀerences in the results, compared to the MILP (the diﬀerence was lesser than 0.2 % for every
technology) demonstrating the suitability of hybrid approaches to solve unit commitment, and
energy dispatch problems.

Abstract (Danish)
Brugen af optimeringsteknikker i løsningen af ”unit commitment” og ”energy dispatch” proble-
mer er en velkendt og accepteret metode at simulere stor skala energi systemer. På nuværende
tidspunkt bliver problemer som disse løst ved brugen af såkaldte ”Mixed integer linear program-
ming (MILP)”. Der er en teoretisk kløft imellem brugen af hurtigere teknikker så som ”linear
programming optimization (LP)” til at løse problemer som disse. Formålet med dette studie er
at undersøge forskellige muligheder for brugen af ”linear programming optimization” til at løse
”unit commitment” og ”energy dispacth” problemer.
Baseret på en eksisterende MILP model, DispaSET 2.0 (udviklet hos Joint Reseacrh Center),
blev en LP model blev udviklet under dette studie. The oprindelige model repræsenterer med en
høj detaljeringsgrad den kortsigtetede drift af det Europæiske el netværk, beregnet på timebasis,
”commitment status” af de Belgiske kraftværker i 2012. Arbejdet der udført under dette speciale
inkluderer videreudvikling af modellen til at inkludere yderlige seks lande: Belgien, Frankrig,
Holland, Tyskland, Østrig og Schweiz. Dermed, er LP modellen udarbejdet med de førnævnte
seks lande for året 2015.
I dette projekt er der anvendt ”Advanced clustering” til at producere en hurtig LP model, der fra
444 generatorer til efter ”clustering” bliver reduceret til 58 enheder. Denne LP model er anvendt
til at simulere, både ren LP og en hybrid tilgang, tilslut sammenlignes der med den oprindelige
MILP. LP modellen blev udviklet i respons til den lange simulerings tid der er nødvendig for at
løse MILP modellen. LP modellen demonstrerede evnen til at opnå resultat i en brøkdel af MILP,
LP opnåede resultater på 4,595 seconds, hvorimod MILP krævede 59,400 seconds. Ydermere, når
den udviklede LP model blev sammenholdt med MILP vidste den sig som et eﬀektivt værktøj
der med stor nøjagtighed kunne estimere ”the electricity mix” i genereringen af en stor region
(med en negligibel afvigelse på MILP), samt koblinger og de totale system omkostninger (kun
værende 1 % fra MILP i forhold til omkostninger).
LP modellen alene, kommer til kort i håndteringen af ”commitment” status af generatorer, efter-
som brugen af kontinuerlige variabler umuliggør muligheden for at påvise at en enhed er tændt
eller slukket. For at efterkomme dette problem blev en hybrid model blev udviklet og efterprøvet.
Med denne tilgang, er LP modellen anvendt til at beregne de indbyrdes nationale forbindelser,
hvorimod MILP modellen blev benyttet til at fastsætte ”the commitment status”, elproduktionen
og system omkostningerne. Til slut, blev den hybride tilgangs resultater og MILP sammenlignet.
Det viste at LP muliggør en drastisk reduktion af simuleringstiden i forhold til MILP, ydermere
viste sammenligningen negligible forskellige i de opnåede resultater i forhold MILP, dermed viste
hybrid modellen sig egnet til at løse ”unit commitment” og ”energy dispatch problems”.

Chapter 1
Introduction
W
orldwide energy is a driving force toward the progress of societies. From powering the
transportation to its key role in agriculture and industry, it pushes the development of
economies and limits it. Meeting the energy demand at all times is no simple task. The elec-
tricity must be dispatched at the correct time, and in the correct amount, originating an energy
dispatch issue. This energy dispatch dilemma can be engaged from various angles. Diﬀerent
and rigid demand patterns, stochasticity of renewable energy resources, and scarcity of storage,
among others, cause meeting the demand at all times to be a challenging engineering issue. Fur-
thermore, the increasing electricity demand all over the globe, along with the growing inclusion
of intermittent and uncontrollable renewable resources, such as wind or solar energy, exacerbates
this market issue.
Focusing on the European case, and according to [1, Dispa-SET documentation], the new reg-
ulations regarding energy and climate change goals, impel the future of the European energy
system to promote and increase the use of renewable resources.
To cope with this problem, the scientiﬁc community is embarked in a number of strategies: en-
ergy eﬃciency, usage of energy and policies, demand side management, or large scale dispatch
models are examples of the topics most commonly addressed. All these approaches endeavor to
optimize production, minimizing costs and ultimately, lessening the impact of human activities

1.1
Thesis Motivation
This master thesis is conducted in response to the necessity of an improvement and extension
of Dispa-SET unit commitment model, tackling the aforementioned limitations of the original
model. Thus, from the former version of Dispa-SET, two paths of improvement may be consid-
ered:
Firstly, the incorporation of several nodes within the same model could be introduced, where
each node represents an undivided country. The relevance of this enhancement lies in the fact
that, if performed correctly, one single model is able to represent the entire power system of a
large region comprised of several countries. Being requested by the European Comission and
focused on the European Union (an increasingly united region), it is crucial that decisions are
adopted from a global perspective (opposing to individualized country-wise dispatch decisions).
A model that encompasses all the countries within the region is critical to successfully achieve
this goal.
The second path is intimately related with the ﬁrst, and deals with the optimization of solving
speed. Since a MILP model is hard to solve and considerably time demanding, introducing more
information into it may result in an enormous model, too large to solve in a reasonable time
frame. This means that, in order for the model to be usable in an intra-day schedule, there is a
necessity for it to run in a shorter time. With this issue in mind, a model that sacriﬁces accuracy
to deliver speed could be developed, and formulated as a linear programming model (shortened
as LP). The underlying premise is not to transform a mixed-integer model into a linear one, but
rather to attain a hybrid model, able to meet the speciﬁc needs of large-scale unit commitment
models, such the case of this thesis. Little or no studies combining both models currently exist.
In [5, Barry Rountree], the author establishes a list of previous studies regarding optimization
techniques developed over the past ﬁfty years; in his work it can be observed that no linear
programming optimizations have been used so far to solve large-scale unit commitment energy
dispatch problems.
Furthermore, in recent studies, [6, J. Warrington et al. - 2016], the authors report the importance
of undertaking studies that analyze in detail the eﬀects of relaxing binary unit commitment large
scale problems, in order to reduce the prohibitive computational times.

Theoretical Background

2.1
LP/MILP theory
As stated in the introduction, in this thesis, a LP model is created based upon an existent MILP,
this is done to produce a hybrid model (LP/MILP), that can simulate the European power
system. The motivation for building a LP instead of simply running the MILP, is the simulation
time. Whilst MILPs are hard and slow to solve, LP models can be solved in a shorter time frame,
this speed is delivered at the expense of accuracy.
This section explains the basics of LP and MILP models, pinpointing the diﬀerences between
them. Basic theory of the processes lying under the surface in CPLEX, when solving both types
of model, is provided next.
2.1.1
MILP - Branch-and-bound method
The main feature of MILPs is the fact that they use integers, (namely a number which is not
a fraction), in one or more decision variables. These models are required when fashioning a
mathematical optimization, in which some of the variables are restricted to be whole numbers.
The way a solver (CPLEX in this case) tackles these problems, is by the so-called method of
branch-and-bound. Some basic notions of this method are described following.
The branch-and-bound method is broadly extended to ﬁnd optimal solutions to MILP problems.
This algorithm searches the entire space of candidate solutions, sorting out portions of this space
==PAGE:28==
8
Theoretical Background
by using previous estimates. To solve a mixed integer linear program, the ﬁrst step is to relax
the integer variables within the bounds, to then solve the resulting LP. That is to say, that if
a variable bounds are 0 ≤x ≤3, the possible solutions of a MILP will be 0, 1, 2 and 3, and by
relaxing the variable, the possible range of solutions increases to [0,3]. Then, the solution of the
LP (for instance 1.2) serves to produce two problems, the ﬁrst one for 0 ≤x ≤1 and another for
2 ≤x ≤3; each of the two problems is solved relaxing the variables again as LP ([0,1] and [2,3])
and then the solver selects the best one, and continues the solving process generating a search
tree, each of the MILPs generated are the nodes of the tree.
It can be inferred then, that MILPs are problems extremely hard to solve. If for instance, a set
contains 5 elements and the integer is left as default (0 ≤x ≤100), there will be 1005 solutions,
that is 10,000,000,000 solutions the solver must check.
A special type of MILP is the binary MILP, in which the bounds for the integer variable are
0 ≤x ≤1, these variables are convenient to determine whether an action is carried out (1) or
not (0). In the case of unit commitment problems, there exists the need for the use of binary
variables. In the UC problem addressed in this report, the binary variables are reduced to the
power plants, assigning the units that are on (1) or oﬀ(0). The number of possible solutions of
a binary MILP are 2n, being n the number of binary variables. To exemplify it, let us say that
there is a binary variable, commitment status of power plants, if there are 333 power plants, this
means 2333 or 1.8 · 10100 possible solutions for the solver to check, for each time step (1 hour -
365 days), in addition to the rest of the constraints that might be involved in the optimization
problem. Thus, the computational time required to solve MILPs grows exponentially with the
amount of binary variables.
2.1.2
LP - Dual simplex algorithm
In LP models, all the variables are deﬁned as continuous, in the case of a binary variable in
MILP, it is transformed into a continuous variable [0,1] in LP. Meaning that it can be any value
from 0 to 1. The objective function, as in MILPs, needs to be linear, as well as the constraints
of the problem. When solving LP problems, CPLEX selects the best method to do so. In the
large majority of cases, the method chosen in dual simplex algorithm.
With the dual simplex approach, every linear problem (called primal problem), has a unique
dual linear problem. Then, the primal problem is provided with an upper bound to the optimal
value. Two ideas underlie: i) the objective value of the optimal solution of the primal and the dual
problem is the same (Strong Duality Theorem), and ii) the variables of the primal linear program
have identical values as the dual variables of the dual linear program [10, University Lecture,
DTU]. Thus, if the primal linear program is of minimization kind, the dual linear program will
be of maximization kind. The solver iteratively determines the solution for the dual and primal
[11, Stephen J. Wright]. Hence, satisfying both bounds. This method is considerably faster since
it does not need to consider each possible solution, as MILPs do.
2.1.3
LP vs MILP
From the above stated features of LPs and MILPs, it is apparent that, with the actual technology,
MILPs are not suitable to work in real time. Even for a small country, these models require a
large amount of computational power. Furthermore, to attain a solution within a reasonable

time frame, a minimum gap must be imposed in MILPs. This gap is the distance between the
best possible solution (relaxed solution) and the MILP solution. In the case of this thesis, the
gap is set at 4 % for all MILP simulations. Due to the necessary presence of the gap, the exact
solution of the MILP is unachievable. Moreover, reducing it merely by 1 % incurs in exponential
growth of the solving time.
LP models though, do not require to check for every solution independently. Rather, the LP
algorithm tries to ﬁnd the amount of each variable required to meet the speciﬁcations of the
problem formulated.
In the case of UC models, the decision variable committed represents
generators, thus, in a LP, the "amount" of each generator is meaningless. However, LPs might
be used, in the grander picture, to determine other features of the model. For instance, the power
a country must produce to meet a demand, or the interconnections required between countries,
can be calculated by means of LP models without incurring in large errors. Then, to specify how
this power is produced, LP models do not suﬃce since they cannot detail the status (on/oﬀ) of
a power plant.
All these motivations induced the generation of a linear programming formulation, which is
explained in the incoming chapters.

2.3
Literature Review
The idea of using Dynamic Programming to solve unit commitment problems ﬁrst appeared in
1966 [12, P.G. Lowery]. In this paper, the author broke down a typical week electricity consump-
tion into weekdays, Saturdays, and Sundays, diﬀerentiating between night and day patterns as
well. Lowery maintains that during the low demand hours, fewer steam-electric generators are
in service, this, according to Lowery results, complicates the modelling when the generator ﬂeet
is heterogeneous in its technical features, as it is typically the case in every country. The author
in the paper, explains how before Dynamic Programming existed, the simple rule of thumb to
decide which units to stop was to remove old, less eﬃcient generators. However, the investigator
==PAGE:30==
10
Theoretical Background
argued that diﬀerent fuel prices (per unit) and stand-by, start-up, or shut down costs, complicate
the dispatch problem, as a result the problem becomes unmanageable when trying to examine all
possible combinations. Lowery proposed a diﬀerent approach, by Dynamic Programming, build-
ing a mathematical model with a series of constraints, resulting in a problem which solution can
be reached within a reasonable time frame.
Having diﬀerent demand patterns makes the dispatch problem diﬃcult, as it is shown in several
investigation works such as [13, Benjamin F. Hobbs], [14, Subir Sen]. In [15, C.K. Pang - 1976],
the researchers proposed an unit commitment model solved by means of a truncated dynamic
programming method by minimizing costs. Although the commitment only covers a period of
48 hours, the paper establishes a solid base for the mathematical model including constraints
regarding: must run, must out, ﬁxed generation, generation limits and capacity, minimum up
and down times, and crew constraints. Most of these constraints are still used nowadays. This
author also observed spinning reserve requirements in the model.
Throughout the years, the scope of the scheduling problem has varied attending to diﬀerent
operating constraints or ﬂeet mix. According to [16, A.I. Cohen et al. - 1987], the scheduling
problem in electric power systems can be deﬁned as the way to determine which generating units
should be on-line and available for generation at each hour, and the associated nominal dispatch.
The authors assert that a reduction of 0.5% in fuel cost may result in savings of millions of dollars
every year, and introduced the concept of stochastic quantities in loads, hydro inﬂows, and unit
availabilities. The authors state that, if the actual values of this uncertainties diﬀer greatly from
the forecast, then it results economical to solve the scheduling problem. In their unit commit-
ment model, the investigators include some new constraints, such as cycling units and peakers
in addition to the already known must-run units. The resulting problem is a non-linear MILP
with 100 units that, back at that time was, not possible to solve. The authors point out a series
of deﬁciencies in models that need to be solved: introduction of interconnections, treatment of
short-term uncertainty of scheduling, and adding ramping rates among them.
In [17, Fred N. Lee], the author presents a method where focus is put on strong fuel constraints,
including gas contracts and gas delivery. Iteratively, Lee obtains a commitment status and then,
through a phase of adjustments, the model obtains hourly prices that are used to relaunch the
commitment phase, establishing a new commitment status. The proposed method results reli-
able and accurate.
It is important to note that the extraordinary computational power required to solve unit com-
mitment problems caused them (traditionally), to cover only from hours to a few weeks. It is
not until later, [18, K. Hussain - 1991] and [19, X. Guan - 1992], where the ﬁrst unit commit-
ment models which considered technical and economical constraints, over a horizon of one year,
appeared. In the ﬁrst article, a system with a load around 3,300 MW in Riyadh is modeled with
many constraints (fuel, ambient temperature, turbine degradation, transmission, and reactive
power/voltage control), the size of this problem leads to the impossibility of satisfactorily attain
a solution. Hussain proposes a series of techniques that allowed for the model to be solved with-
out developing a new software. Concerning the second paper mentioned, Guan emphasizes again
the importance of reducing even by a bare 1% the operating costs, which would in turn save
10 to 30 million USD per year. The author suggests a method to solve UC problems through
Lagrangian relaxation, relaxing the problem requirements, decomposing the problem into indi-
vidual relaxed UC problems without ramping constraints. In these two articles, the models were
large scale unit commitment problems covering up to a year, to be solved, approximations were
needed (diﬀerent techniques in the ﬁrst, and Lagrangian relaxation in the second). Hence, an
exact solution was not achieved.
A number of diﬀerent methodologies to solve the unit commitment energy dispatch problem have

been used ever since the problem was ﬁrst established in [12, P.G. Lowery - 1966]. Some of these
methods have already been mentioned, such as dynamic programming or Lagrangian relaxation.
In [20, N.P. Padhy - 2004], the author describes a basic mathematical approach for this problem,
furthermore in the paper, a list of the diﬀerent methods utilized in the literature to resolve this
problem are outlined:
• Exhaustive Enumeration: Where the UC problem is solved for all the possible combinations
and then the least costly is chosen as optimal. [21, K. Hara et al.] exempliﬁes this method.
• Priority Listing: The power plants are arranged attending to lowest costs, then the UC is
solved.
• Dynamic Programming: Focuses on minimizing costs of carrying a load between units.
Once a unit is used already, the rest must carry the remaining load. It is the ﬁrst and most
widely extended method. [12, P.G. Lowery] uses this method.
• Branch and Bound: Typically how a MILP solver works (for instance CPLEX). The prob-
lem is divided in subsets and evaluated independently.
• Integer: This is the method used by [1, Dispa-SET]. It consist of an improvement of the
branch and bound method. The integer programing solves by producing sub-problems,
rejecting those which are infeasible, thus reducing the solution search space. It can also be
non-linear integer.
• Linear Programming: This method is explored throughout this master thesis, the prob-
lem is divided in sub-problems, and solved through the revised simplex method, using
primal/dual solution.
• Lagrangian Relaxation: One or more constraints are relaxed attending to costs or units.
[19, X. Guan] uses this method to solve a large scale model.
While there are more methods described and utilized in literature, they are beyond the scope
of this thesis and therefore will not be further explained. Among these methods, the most im-
portant are: simulated annealing, neural networks, fuzzy algorithms, tabu algorithms, expert
systems, particle swarm or evolutionary algorithms. Worth mentioning are the genetic algo-
rithms, methods widely investigated and in which there has been a special interest during the
last 30 years. These algorithms are based on machine learning, which imitates the principles of
natural evolution, many papers have focused on this method to solve all sizes of UC problems.
[22, D. Dasgupta et al.] makes use of it.
In [20, N.P. Padhy], the author mentions hybrid models, according to this paper, there is a need
for further improvement that may be achieved through the use of two methods, one that is simple
but suboptimal and another that is complex but accurate. In order to attain a more advance
model, Padhy insists, one method must complement the other, making the approach superior.

State-of-the-art
In this section the latest advances are listed, including the newest papers with respect to unit
commitment energy dispatch models.
In [23, Bryan S. Palmintier - 2012], and [24, Bryan S. Palmintier et al. - 2013], the authors
describe the issue arisen from the increasing penetration of wind generation. According to this
==PAGE:32==
12

paper, addressing the challenges of a large fraction of the production being supplied by variable
sources, requires an embedded approximation to the UC problem. In their work, the investigators
propose the clustering of units as an eﬃcient method to simplify the UC problem resulting in a
tremendous improvement in solution time. By this method, the binary problem can be replaced
by a simpler integer problem, the start-up and shut down is controlled by the integer. If, for
instance, a cluster contains 50 units, th decision variable will be an integer (a natural number),
from 0 to 50, indicating the number of generators running.
A method by which unit commitment, energy dispatch, and reserve policies are considered, is
introduced in [6, J. Warrington et al. - 2016]. The authors propose a rolling decision making that
runs recursively with a look-ahead of several hours. The rolling window serves the purpose of
adjusting the dispatch, accounting for potential future (better) information regarding renewable
forecasts, thus focusing in unit commitment, taking into consideration forecast uncertainty. Fi-
nally, the paper suggests that relaxing the binary variables may lead to signiﬁcant computational
speed-ups, stating the need for future work characterizing the eﬀect of this relaxation.
The author of [5, Barry Rountree] lists nearly every novel optimization approach over the last ﬁfty
years, in this list, no linear programming approach is mentioned. Furthermore, the researcher
attempts to address the UC problem through linear programming optimization, and although
the results are not satisfactory, show that linear programming formulation might be an approach
worth careful observation.
The technical report developed by Artelys - optimization solutions, [25, METIS], proposes an
approach to the unit commitment problem with the use of linear programming formulation. In
it, the authors outline a standard model which size corresponds to the average European coun-
try. Afterwards, they perform a comparison, solving the unit commitment problem using MILP
and using LP. When solving as a MILP, the power plants are considered as independent units,
however, for the LP, the authors conduct a previous clustering of the plants. The clustering is
carried out attending to type of plant and age. Since one of the drawbacks of LP models is the
impossibility of accurately assigning start-up costs, the authors of this report propose that the
ramping of a cluster (in time t with respect to time t −1) can be used as an approximation to
account for start-up costs. Hence, a costs comparison of MILP and LP can be realized.
In [26, K. Poncelet et al. - 2014], the authors propose a method to solve MILP models without
using binary variables. In it, a procedure is suggested by which the power plants are clustered
in a similar fashion as [25, METIS]. Then, each cluster is assigned an integer variable with
limits {0,number of units in the cluster}. In light of this, the number of solutions the solver
must evaluate, is reduced from 2n (binary) to n (integer), being n the number of units in each
cluster. The authors assent that this method forfeits accuracy to yield a signiﬁcantly better
performance in computational time terms. Furthermore, in the paper it is advised that, in case
the computational time is still unreasonable after using the method proposed, the model could
be run relaxing the integer variables into continuous ones.

Conclusions
Based on the reviewed studies, it has been shown that modelling unit commitment including
energy dispatch problems, is highly relevant mainly due to the growingly inclusion of renewable
energy resources, which complicates the decision making process for an eﬃcient dispatch.
Clustering of units is a tool already used in the literature and that has proved to lighten UC

Whilst a number of diﬀerent approaches have been undertaken along the last half century, few

point at LP as the approach to investigate seeking the enhancement of UC models. Thus, backing
the decision undertaken in this master thesis of formulating a LP model to work along with the
MILP.

The ﬁrst step in the model is the collection of the data. In the original model, data were gathered

heavy processing of the data. The data gathered in the original model included, Belgium’s power
plants with their technical characteristics, demand of the country over 2012, and availability
factor of wind and solar energies.
3.1.2
Pre-processing
With the collected data, the information was passed to the next step, the pre-processing tool.
In this tool, the data were further processed prior to its insertion into the optimization program.
The pre-processing tool was written in Python [8, Python v. 2.7] and consisted of a single script
in which the data was treated, the sets were deﬁned, and the parameters were ﬁlled in. The script
thus produced (with the data processing embedded within it), was long, and ultimately diﬃcult

a GAMS readable ﬁle inputs.gdx, and a PYOMO readable ﬁle inputs.p are produced.
3.1.3
Optimization program
Finally, once the inputs.gdx and the inputs.p are generated, GAMS or PYOMO scripts can be

attending to the variables, constraints, and equations and then sends it to the solver, in this
thesis the solver selected is CPLEX. The model is thus a minimization problem, implemented in
GAMS or PYOMO and solved with CPLEX, the decision variables included in it and calculated
by the solver are deﬁned and listed in table 3.3. According to the documentation released, "the

The optimization program was originally formulated as a MILP. This original MILP is used (with

As explained in the introduction, the model is formulated as a tight and compact mixed integer
linear program. This is done to reduce the zone where the solver (CPLEX in this case) searches
for the optimal solution. Tightness is represented by the gap, namely the distance between the
integer solution and the relaxed solution whereas compactness refers to the amount of data to
be processed by the solver.
The model optimization program is formulated as a single script in GAMS (or PYOMO). To do
so, a list of sets, parameters, variables, and ﬁnally equations are used. They are presented next.

3.1.7.4

hydro storage units) must meet additional restrictions related to the amount of energy stored.
Storage units are considered to be subject to the same constraints as non-storage power plants.

units (i.e. a subset of all units). These restrictions include the storage capacity, inﬂow, outﬂow,
charging, charging capacity, charge/discharge eﬃciencies, etc. Discharging is considered as the
standard operation mode and is therefore linked to the Power variable, common to all units.

In this section, the collecting and treatment method for data concerning actual load, in MW, per

and prepared. Thus, all the methods described in this subsection concern both sets of data.
==PAGE:57==
4.3 Actual load and day-ahead
37
Actual load data is necessary to provide the model with a demand pattern to be met by the
generation (power output) of the power plants. The pattern must have a resolution of minimum
1 hour, since the model is designed to meet the demand hourly. Day-ahead forecast is necessary
to perform more advanced simulations in a later stage of the thesis, in principle only load is
required to run the program.
In this case, the information retrieved from Entso-e transparency platform was somewhat in-
complete. This did not cause the data to be turned down, but required for the processing to be
extensive. The main issues encountered were three:
• The date and time had to be readjusted to CET and UTM localized to Europe/Brussels.
• Not all the countries were complete. Moreover, those countries which were complete, had
gaps, that is, some hours scattered around the set of data were missing.
• Some countries data were provided in time steps of 15 minutes, and some others in time
steps of 1 hour.
4.3.1
Time Series:
This issue is the simplest of them all. The key to solve it was to understand how the data were
being displayed by Entso-e and how fetching them was working.
Entso-e transparency platform permits the user to choose the date-time format, the options are:
UTC, CET/CEST, WET/WEST and EET/EEST. For this thesis CET/CEST was selected, this
was done with the purpose of localizing it to Europe/Brussels and then transform it to UTC
keeping both formats: UTC for the index to query through, and CET/CEST as a local time to
better comprehend the information stored.
The main problem localizing the time series was that the data did not have a proper CET
format. For instance, the last Sunday of March should have duplicate hours that were missing.
Moreover, the last Sunday of October should have missing hours that were not missing. In plain
text, Entso-e provides time series not localized, and with the winter/summer adjustment already
done. This makes localizing them impossible, and without localization, it is not possible to select
a certain time period to simulate (correct date-stamps are required to slice the data to simulate).
To solve this issue, a complete new array of datetimes had to be produced (in fact two: one
with 8760 values in time steps of 1 hour, and another with 35040 values, in time steps of 15
minutes). This array was produced for the year 2015, and then localized at Europe/Brussels.
Since all simulated countries belonged in the same time zone, there was no need for localizing the
time series diﬀerently for each country. Once performed the localization, the UTC time series
were created from it. The UTC time series was then used as index for the data, whereas the
CET/CEST was included as additional information, facilitating the understanding.
4.3.2
Incomplete data sets:
In the case of load and day-ahead forecast, the information in Entso-e was available per country.
There was one country, Germany, where the data for 2015 were incomplete, January was absent.
==PAGE:58==
38
New Tool: Data
To ﬁll this month, (and only for Germany), January 2016 was used.
Solving the incompletion of the data required some investigation. When inspecting the data,
it was discovered that some values were missing, this values, labeled with NaN (not a number)
were distributed throughout the arrays without following any pattern. That is, the missing in-
formation did not correspond to a single event repeated regularly across the arrays, but rather
they were just missing hours scattered around the data sets.
Even though some libraries in Python (Pandas for instance), count with methods to ﬁll missing
values (since this is a typical issue in data mining), these methods only replicate values (the last
correct or the next correct for instance). Since the missing data corresponded to load, replicating
the value of the previous correct hour (perhaps 3 hours before), could lead to important errors.
Thus, it was decided to develop a new method which accounted for the particularities of electri-
cal load and day-ahead forecasts. In consideration of this, one function was developed that ﬁlls
empty values (or NaN), following a weekly pattern to do so.
According to [30, Electricity demand changes in predictable patterns], the electricity demand
patterns are generally predictable, having daily, weekly and seasonal patterns. Daily patterns
display a rise throughout the day, tending to be highest during a block of hours ("on-peak")
typically between 07.00 and 22.00. Hence, following a daily pattern to ﬁll the empty values by
copying the previous or the next value, corresponding to the previous or the next hour, was con-
sidered not to be a suitable option, since the variations from hour to hour may not be negligible.
When considering weekly patterns, and according to [30, Electricity demand changes in pre-
dictable patterns], the demand levels are generally lowest between 20.00 and 07.00 and on week-
ends ("oﬀ-peak"), establishing a diﬀerence between weekdays and weekends. Furthermore Luis
Hernandez et al. in [31, Clustering of Electricity Demand Patterns], after performing cluster-
ing by using the k-Means algorithm, obtained four diﬀerent patterns within the week (Mon-
day, Tuesday-Friday, Saturday and Sunday). Additionally, when presented with a similar issue,
A.Rosso in [32, Assessment of the contribution of storage and demand side management to power
system ﬂexibility], found that a year could be reasonably divided in weeks with similar demand.
Hence, following this ﬁndings, a k-means clustering was performed to the load data, in order to
explore the possibility of ﬁlling missing data with data from the previous and next weeks.

4.4
Availability factor
The way solar, wind oﬀshore, and wind onshore production are entered in the program, is
through the availability factor of certain unit at certain time.
Due to the intermittent and
uncontrollable nature of these renewable sources of energy, these power plants cannot be handled
as the dispatchable thermal plants. The solution implemented in the original DispaSET and
maintained in this thesis, is to add a factor, as a percentage of the nominal capacity of the
renewable power plant available at each time. This section deals with the way availability factors
were determined in this thesis.
The data collected in this section is the historical generation of solar and wind on/oﬀshore. With
this generation divided by total nominal capacity of solar or wind on/oﬀshore, per country, a
percentage of use can be calculated. This percentage can then be multiplied back again in the
actual simulation, yielding the generation of these RES in the optimization problem.
The availability factor, as well as the demand in the previous section, had to be determined
for all hours of the year. Again in this occasion, the time series provided by Entso-e was not
complete, indeed, as it occurred in the previous subsection, some countries data were available
in intervals of 15 minutes and some other in intervals of 1 hour. All the treatment utilized in the
previous section regarding these issue could be replicated (with some necessary changes, in the
availability factor case).
4.4.1
Historical renewable generation
The transparency platform of Entso-e, with regards to the historical production, can be accessed
by country, however the nominal capacity per production type has to be looked up by TSO.
For the German case, an aggregation of all TSOs was used.
Additionally, no data for The
Netherlands regarding historical generation was available, therefore, in order to determine its
availability factor for solar, wind oﬀshore and wind onshore, an average between Germany and

5.2.1
LP formulation
The procedure to build a LP model upon the old MILP is described in this sub-section. In the
LP model, instead of having binary variables assigning the commitment status of a power plant,
the variables are continuous. Thus, the committed variable in the LP model does not longer
provide information regarding the status of the power plants. The variable power is therefore the
sole variable that determines the power produced, and the commitment status of the generators.
This shortcoming of LP models, results in a faster solving speed (with respect to MILP models),
at the expense, notwithstanding, of accuracy in the results.
It is important to mention that, although the simplest way of creating a LP model would be
to formulate a new script, this was not the course of action. It was a premise of the thesis not
to produce several new scripts but rather, the ﬁnal tool had to be kept unique. Thus, instead
of producing a new tool to formulate the LP, it was introduced imbricated in the MILP script,
applying reversible changes in the script.
To apply reversible changes, GAMS allows for the creation of what is called ﬂags. This GAMS
internal variables enable, by deﬁning a symbol in them, to run only certain parts of the code.
Thus, when the ﬂag is provided with one symbol, and the user starts the simulation, only some
parts of the code run. If on the contrary, this symbol is not present, some other parts of the
code are active when performing the simulation. To illustrate this, an example is shown next.
Let us say that we want to run a code with the following line that brings the ramping up costs
to 0:
CostRampUpu = 0
But we want to apply it only in the cases where the code runs as MILP. Then, we could write a
ﬂag like this: Condition == True. This ﬂag should be written at the beginning of the script.
Then, in GAMS, the concerning line can be re-written as:
If Condition == True,
then CostRampUpu = 0
If Condition == False,
then CostRampUpu = RampUpCosts
Note that this is not written in GAMS language, rather is just a representation of what the code
does.
What this modiﬁcation does is: if the user desires to run as MILP, Condition == True, then the
ramping up costs would be 0. On the other hand, if the user wants to run a LP, Condition ==
False, this in return makes the ramping up costs to be the actual costs calculated in the pre-
processing tool.
This is the way all required changes were introduced in the MILP, turning the script into a
MILP/LP solver.
To formulate the LP I followed a three step process: i) removing binary
variables, ii) ﬁxing the mathematical model, and iii) creating a new solving statement.

The veriﬁcation method to disregard simulation issues, was to closely inspect the results of six

penalized by its use. This means that, whenever one of these variables has a value greater than

If any of these variables has a value greater than 0 at any time step, it indicates that some of
the constraints are nor being fulﬁlled at that time step. It was sought for the simulations not to
have any loss of load, however, certain tolerance exists depending on the simulation conducted.
For instance, in the case of LossLoadRampUp, for the ﬁrst day of a full year simulation, it is
considered acceptable to have some loss of load, especially if all the power plants are aggregated,
where there is less ﬂexibility.
Before obtaining correct simulations (i.e. without loss of load), there were a number of obstacles
to overcome. The main issues found are listed and explained next:
• Insuﬃcient nominal capacity in the power plants collected from Entso-e: since
the European association of TSOs only includes in their database certain power plants
(attending to their size, among other criteria), there is certain lack of capacity in the data
stored for all countries. This shortage of nominal capacity triggered LostLoadMaxPower.
Every country needed extra capacity in a larger or a lesser extent. At the least, all coun-
tries required the addition of renewable power plants. These units were generally missing
from Entso-e data set, making it necessary to use other sources to ﬁnd their capacity (as
explained in the data chapter). Moreover, whenever the total capacity per production type
and the sum of the capacities of the generators of that type did not match, a unit had to
added (or subtracted) forcing the two numbers to coincide.
• Excess of demand: in the case of Switzerland, it was discovered that the demand and day-
ahead in Entos-e were slightly larger than Switzerland TSO data (again LostLoadMaxPower
was abundant for that country). Therefore Entso-e data was replaced by SwissGrid TSO
information.
• Interconnections: the ﬂows between countries are limited by the day-ahead net transfer
capacities (NTC). However, apart from the NTC, there exists the TRM, transmission
reliability margin, in a way that NTC + TRM = TTC, total transfer capacity [39, elia].
This means that, aside from the NTC, there is an extra, unused capacity for electricity
transfer between two countries. In the case of Belgium for instance, there are 250 extra
MW of capacity to transfer in each border. This is done in order to supply emergencies
(unexpected unit break-downs for instance). This occasioned some inconsistencies since no

so far. Nonetheless, for the simulations conducted in this thesis, the NTC day-ahead was

6.1.2.1
Dividing the problem
All the measures, tuning of parameters (chieﬂy the physical features of power plants), and adjust-
ments in general, were not performed with the entire problem (6 countries + one year) running
at once. Due to the extremely large size of the problem, making minute adjustments and running
the whole simulation in order to perform debugging was a laborious task. The simulation of the
main program with the six new countries over a period of one entire year necessitated over 17
hours of execution. At the same time, simulating solely one country, aiming to decrease the
simulation time, was not an option since the interconnections between countries play a crucial
role in the simulation.
An obvious solution would be to run the simulations in a more powerful computer, however, be-
ing that not a possibility, another approach was followed, which enabled to try diﬀerent settings
for the simulations in a reasonable time frame. The yearly simulation was thus divided in four
reduced simulations, each one representing three months (Jan-Mar, Apr-Jun, Jul-Sep, Oct-Dec).
Then, each simulation was run separately, ﬁnding the one most prone to errors, which resulted
to be the period from July to September. Adjusting the settings for this simulation was then the
priority. Once Jul-Sep was free of errors, the rest of the simulations were launched, having some
minor problems only in Jan-Mar. Finally, when the four periods had been simulated without
any loss of load, a full simulation was run (6 countries + 1 year), obtaining results with no
errors. Then, the actual simulations, evaluating diﬀerent aspects, could begin. These analyses
are explained in the next section.

6.2.1
Costs evaluation
Two diﬀerent models are assessed throughout the whole thesis, LP and MILP. Thus, it was
considered relevant to frame each one in the appropriate economic plane. As already mentioned,
LP models sacriﬁce accuracy to deliver speed. This analysis is conducted trying to mitigate the
lesser accuracy in terms of total system costs. To do so, three options were explored: i) only
accounting for variable costs in both models. ii) Considering variable and ramping costs in the
LP, and variable, ramping and start-up costs in the MILP. Finally iii) considering variable and
ramping costs in the LP, and variable and start-up costs in the MILP. These options are detailed
next.
To conduct the cost analysis, a simulation of a single country was selected to act as reference.
Hence, Belgium was simulated over a period of one entire year for every option assessed.
6.2.1.1
Only variable costs in both models
The most consequential drawback of LP models is the impossibility of controlling with precision
which power plants u are started-up at time h. Therefore, computing these costs in the LP
model is beyond reach. For this reason, the ﬁrst option considered was to completely eliminate
the start-up costs, taking into account only variable costs for both models in order to compare
them. To do so, the start-up costs as well as the ramping costs (in case there was any) were set
to 0 in the pre-processing tool. The information was then passed to the optimization program,
to be run as LP and MILP. The results are presented and discussed in the following chapter.
6.2.1.2
Adding Ramping costs, part I
The second approach to compare both models is, instead of eliminating costs, to transform them.
Although it is not possible to determine the commitment status of the power plants in the LP, it
is feasible to produce ramping costs from the known start-up costs of the individual units. Then,
these recently determined ramping costs can be introduced in LP and MILP models. Thus, the
==PAGE:82==
62
Simulations Method and
Analyses Conducted
LP would consider variable costs and ramping costs (the last ones instead of start-up costs); the
MILP on the other hand would consider variable costs, start-up costs, and ramping costs.
To calculate the ramping costs, a modiﬁcation in the function that merges the power plants was
introduced: while merging similar plants (the process is extensively explained in the previous
chapter), the power of two merged plants is aggregated. Then, dividing the start-up costs by
this aggregated power, enables obtaining a linearized ramping cost as a function of the power
capacity and the start-up costs.
It is important to note that no exact manner of determining the ramping costs exists to the
knowledge of the author, at the time of conducting this thesis. The nature of these costs, causes
diﬃculties establishing a ﬁgure in [e /MW] that represents the costs originated from ramping
the unit. For instance, these costs should account for the hours of use of a shaft in a CCGT
before it breaks or is damaged, costs that cannot be calculated with certainty. For this reason
the linearization of theses costs (start-up / power capacity) was assumed as the best option in
this case.
Since there is no extensive literature with respect to how determining the ramping costs, an
investigation was conducted, trying to minimize the distance between MILP and LP costs. By
introducing an enhancement factor into the power capacity of the plant (only in the ramping
costs calculation), the error (LP costs divided by MILP costs) was measured for a range of factors
varying from 0.5 to 1; unfortunately the results did not depict any trend, making infeasible to
infer whether the ramping costs thus obtained, were too high or on the contrary, too low. Thus,
the default result was kept throughout the rest of the report. The results of this brief analysis
are presented in the next chapter.
Summing up, after the implementation of the ramping costs, if a power plant modiﬁed its output
from 0 to a positive value, some costs arise. They emerge in form of ramping costs in the LP
(instead of start-up costs) and in form of ramping costs + start-up costs in the MILP. The results
are shown in the next chapter.
6.2.1.3
Adding Ramping costs, part II
Finally, another analysis regarding the costs was performed. The premise for this last evaluation
was the next: since the ramping costs are calculated as a linear function dependent on start-up
costs, they should not be computed along with the last ones in the same simulation, for it incurs
in redundancy of costs.
For this reason, in the MILP problem, the ramping costs are neglected. The LP problem though,
is run with the same settings as in the previous cost analysis. The MILP considers then variable
and start-up costs, and the LP accounts for variable and ramping costs.
These simulations were run in order to deepen the understanding about how the costs are con-
sidered in the two diﬀerent optimization programs. It is worth noting that, if the ramping costs
were provided by the manufacturer, both start-up and ramping costs should be considered in
the MILP, since they would indicate diﬀerent costs. However, since the ramping costs calculated
emerge straight from the start-up costs, it is senseless to account them twice.
The results are presented in the next chapter along with some more insight about the conse-
quences of adding ramping costs + start-up costs.

With a functional LP model, the solver may reach a solution considerably faster. This solution,
though weaker in terms of accuracy, enables the model to be run recursively, improving minor
details in a faster manner. It also permits the exploration of diﬀerent possibilities quickly, always
bearing in mind the inherent inaccuracy of LP models.
Having two functional models (LP and MILP), both describing the same problem, unfolds an
array of new possibilities which combine them as a hybrid system. Two of these options are

Option A:
The optimization program can be run globally as an LP (including all countries). Since a con-
tinuous commitment variable cannot yield status (on/oﬀ) of power plants, it is pointless to have
disaggregated units. Thus, the clustering function (the same function used to cluster the units
in a MILP simulation) can be modiﬁed so that all plants are merged, provided that they are in
the same node (country) and their type is the same (Nuclear, Fossil Gas, Wind Onshore, etc).
In this manner, from 464 power plants, in a normal merging process 333 are obtained, however
with this new clustering, the total number of generators is reduced to 58. A linear program-
ming formulation with 58 power plants reduces dramatically the computational power required
to solve, therefore being faster.
Once the LP has been run and a solution has been achieved, the results can be used to improve the
MILP formulation. More precisely, the interconnections among countries can be regained (out-
ﬂows). Then, these interconnections can be sorted by country to/from which they enter/emerge,
and can be subtracted/added from/to the actual load of the country. Hence, the actual load of
the countries is augmented or diminished (depending on whether the country exports or imports
electricity respectively). Finally, simulations of the MILP can be run for each country as though
they were isolated (i.e. without neighbouring interconnections), with the interconnections sub-
tracted from the load, and with normal aggregation of units (i.e. using the regular clustering
function explained in the pre-processing tool chapter). The MILPs thus simulated result faster
than the MILPs without the isolation conditions.
To perform this analysis, LP58 and LP333 may be used, however, the underlying idea across
this master thesis is to produce agile models, quick in the solving, attempting to approach the
real time. Thus, for this hybrid approach, since the goal is to have a fast LP ﬁrst, to pre-solve
the problem, and then run MILPs to reﬁne the results, LP58 was used (the implications of using
LP58 instead of LP333 will be seen in the comparison between MILP, LP333 and LP58).
With this method, the globally launched LP calculates the ﬂows between two countries, whereas
the MILP reﬁnes, country-wise, the unit commitment energy dispatch. Both LP and MILP are
faster with this method than if the simulation was run normally.

A diﬀerent approach consists of running the MILP model ﬁrst, for all countries, being the demand
the day-ahead load. This simulation is signiﬁcantly slow, but yields the commitment status of
the generators for each time step of one year. With the results of this simulation, a subsequent
simulation may be run, with improved information, this time as LP. In this case, the information
regained is the binary variable committed. This variable is retrieved from the results and added
as a new parameter in the LP model. Furthermore a new equation is introduced.
Committedu,z = CommittedCalcu,z
∀u, z
(6.2)
Where Committedu,z is the continuous variable of the LP and CommittedCalcu,z is the new
parameter introduced (which holds the commitment status of the units according to the MILP).
It is worth noticing that this equation keeps track of the hours by means of the subset z instead of
h. The reason for using this subset is that the committed variable regained from the MILP, does
not cover the last day of the year (since the last 24h are trimmed oﬀby the rolling horizon). The
subset z is comprised precisely of all hours h except the ones discarded by the rolling horizon,
hence matching the information concerning commitment status of units.
The way this approach works is the following: ﬁrst, a MILP is run, with day-ahead forecast as
demand, and then, in the subsequent LP simulation, the demand considered is the actual load,
and the reserve requirements are neglected.
The aim while running MILP with day-ahead forecast, and then LP with actual load and without
reserves, is to assess whether the system can withstand slight variations in the demand (day-
ahead against actual load). This evaluation helps the model approach a real time functioning.
First, a MILP could be run, to determine the commitment status of the plants with day-ahead
forecast, and then, a faster LP could be used to minutely calculate the power output of each
power plant, with the actual load, in real time.
With this approach, a LP simulation can be run with all the advantages of continuous models
(namely the faster computational speed) and without their drawbacks, since the commitment
status is imposed from the MILP. This option attempts to achieve results that model the reality
with more certainty and at the same time, reduce the simulation time.
When dealing with unit commitment problems, the computational time is an issue. To enhance
the reach of the model, by using it to assign units and power output (namely, to use the model
in real situations), the results must be obtained within a limited frame time. Since currently the
technical limitations do not allow for CPLEX (or any other ﬁrst class solver for that matter)
to achieve a solution within a few minutes, the LP emerges as alternative. Since the LP is not
exact, this option suggests to add to LP precisely what is missing, information regarding the
commitment status of the plants.

7.2.3
Hybrid model - Option B
With respect to this approach, a global MILP simulation, using the day-ahead forecast as de-
mand of the model, was run. Then, with the results of this simulation, the commitment status of
the power plants was retrieved and inputed back to run a LP simulation. Thus, a LP simulation

The LP was simulated country by country, testing whether the reserves, previously calculated

Hence, checking the suitability of the manner in which reserves were computed (see equations
3.11 and 3.12).
The results showed that the reserves were to restrictive, not being suﬃcient to produce simula-
tions without loss of load. More precisely, loss of max load, and loss of min load was produced
in every simulation. The case of Belgium is illustrated as example.

Conclusions
Unit commitment, and energy dispatch models are widely used to simulate large power systems,
as for instance, the European power system. Traditionally, these systems have been simulated by
means of diﬀerent approaches, being MILP optimization problems among the most broadly used.
The main problem of MILP unit commitment models which rely on integer or binary variables
to simulate power systems is, by and large, the computational power required, and therefore the
simulation time, which is normally considerably large.
This thesis identiﬁed a theoretical gap in current research concerning unit commitment, and
power dispatch models, and focused on providing with meaningful evidence that helped ﬁlling
this gap. Thus, the switch from MILP to LP optimization problems is addressed in this master
thesis, since their use lead to signiﬁcantly reduce the computation time. Additionally, in order to
simulate a large system, an existent unit commitment, and energy dispatch model of Belgium, is
extended by the inclusion of six countries, Belgium, The Netherlands, France, Germany, Austria,
and Switzerland.
Data corresponding to those six countries, for the year 2015, is introduced. Then, and a posterior
development of a LP model, based upon the former MILP model is performed, and tested in the
results provided by the LP compared to the MILP. Furthermore, two types of LPs are considered,
with, and without extra aggregation of power plants (the extra aggregation means that only one
plant per type and country, with the combined nominal capacity of all of them, is simulated).
Additionally, a hybrid approach in which LP and MILP models are combined is proposed, in
this approach, the LP model is run globally, determining the interconnections between countries,
then, individual country-wise MILP models are run, calculating the commitment status of the
generators, and their power output.
It is found that LP optimization programs may, in a ﬁrst approximation, simulate large power
systems without incurring in signiﬁcant errors in terms of interconnections, power produced by
the generators of a country, and costs. However, there is still an issue with regards to the commit-
ment status of the plants, which cannot be resolved by means of this method. This issue can be
overcome though, by means of the hybrid approach described and tested in this thesis. With this
method, the power system of the whole area can be simulated by using LP, and later, inside each
==PAGE:114==
94
Conclusions
country, individual MILPs can be run, with the interconnections subtracted, increasing consider-
ably the solving speed. Thus, the global MILP, with six countries, necessitates 59,400 seconds to
attain a solution (roughly 16.5 hours), the LP without extra aggregation requires 23,400 seconds
(6.5 hours), and the LP with extra aggregation 4,595 seconds (1.3 hours).
The evaluation between the LP models created demonstrates the suitability of extra aggregation
of units when simulating large power systems. Only minute diﬀerences between both models are
found in the electricity mix of the generation. The costs though, show a diﬀerence between both
models, diﬀerence that, is not relevant in the simulation, since it is the ﬁnal MILP, the model
that calculates costs.
Respecting the costs, diﬀerent manners of computing costs when swifting from MILP to LP are
considered. It is found that calculating ramping costs, in a linear fashion, from start-up costs and
nominal capacity, and then applying these costs only to the LP model, while using star-up costs
in the MILP, yields the least distance between MILP and LP costs, 1.5 % of relative diﬀerence.
The other two cases studied, only including variable costs, and including ramping costs also in
MILP, yield worse results, 2 % and 6.5 % respectively.
In the devised hybrid approach, the interconnections between countries are calculated by means
of an extra aggregated LP. It is found that this simpliﬁcation provides with a good estimate of
the interconnections, being thus subtracted from the demand in the individual MILPs, which
are then run in isolation conditions. This approach delivers similar results as running simply a
global MILP, consisting the main diﬀerence in the computing time. Running individual MILPs
without interconnections enables a signiﬁcantly faster simulation for every country. The case of
Belgium for instance, from 3,300 seconds (55 minutes) of the regular MILP, after running without
interconnections, the results can be obtained in 1,200 seconds (20 minutes). Furthermore, since
the MILPs are run country-wise, several parallel simulations could be run, making use of the
diﬀerent cores and threads of a micro-processor, without jeopardizing signiﬁcantly the computa-
tion time.
The key role of interconnections is highlighted in this report, being a crucial part of large power
system simulations. Thus, the interconnections allow countries with large share of dispatchable,
non-ﬂexible power, to export whenever the electricity production surpasses the demand, as it is
the case of France, during the most of year. In the same way, essentially importer countries, as
Belgium, can meet their demand due to the presence of interconnections with the neighbouring
countries.
A ﬁnal remark of the work realized in this thesis is that, when evaluating large power systems,
there seems not to exist the need for detailing, to the highest extent, the power produced. This
means that, when focusing in the large picture, there is no need to specify which units are on or
oﬀ, but rather it is suﬃcient to determine the total power output of each technology. In light
of this, LP emerge as an alternative, which can perform correctly, provided that the analysis is
focused on the large picture. Then, by means of micro-managing, the results may be reﬁned,
using MILP for instance.
Further work is required. This model lacks outages, dams reﬁlling patterns, and accurate variable
costs that reﬂect with precision the economical circumstances of each country. Furthermore, it
is expected that the data resources improve in the coming years (i.e. the transparency platform
of Entso-e is rather new). Additionally, the analysis could be further improved by introducing a
varying eﬃciency of the power plants, extensive literature exists with that regard. Finally, the
analysis could be more meaningful by the addition of more countries, introducing at the same
time more nodes within each country.

Tools.Fill_NaN(countries, vector)
Function that ﬁlls the NaN values with: If it is given in steps of 15min: 1. If it is possible: the
average between the previous 15 min and the next 15 min. 2. If 1 fails and it is possible: the value
corresponding to an average between the same day the week before and the week after. 3. If 1 and 2
fail => it copies the previous correct value. If it is given in steps of 1h: 1. If it is possible: the value
corresponding to an average between the same day the week before and the week after. 2. If 1 fails,
it tries the average between the previous and the next hour. 3. If 1 and 2 fail => it copies the previous
correct value. IMPORTANT: Use this function with even vectors: 15min 35040 values. 1h: 8760
values.
Parameters
• countries (param) – The list of countries for which there is information.
• vector (param) – The information (in each country) which is desired to ﬁx (DA or Load).
As a list of dataframes.
Returns The vector with the information treated and ﬁxed is returned in a list of dataframes (one
per country).
