Preface
The thesis was written as the ﬁnal project to acquire a master degree in Electrical Engineering program
with the specialization in Acoustics. The project was carried out at Technical University of Denmark
(DTU) in collaboration with Brüel & Kjær (B&K) Sound and Vibration Measurement.
The project supervisor at the Technical University of Denmark is the Associate Professor: Jonas Brun-
skog (e-mail: jbr@elektro.dtu.dk), who also helped to ﬁnd this project. This master thesis was proposed
by a Brüel & Kjær Innovation Group employee: Dmitri Tcherniak (e-mail: Dmitri.Tcherniak@bksv.com).
The project investigates diﬀerent techniques that are used to identify source path contributions and
to solve Noise, Vibrations and Harshness (NVH) issues. This project was chosen in order to increase
the knowledge in the ﬁeld of structure-borne sound and to get familiar with experimental measurement
techniques.
Abstract
Transfer Path Analysis (TPA) technique has been widely used to identify individual path contributions
and to solve Noise, Vibration and Harshness (NVH) issues in diﬀerent industries: automotive, marine
and aerospace. This method helped to increase comfort and safety levels in vehicles, ships or planes, that
play a crucial part in everyone life. Even though the TPA provides qualitative results, its conventional
procedures are rather time-consuming. In order to determine individual contributions, the full system
must be separated into active and passive sub-systems to determine frequency responses. The separation
and frequency responses measurement procedure are often time-consuming and quite expensive, therefore
other techniques were introduced to overcome this limitation.
Operational Transfer Path Analysis (OTPA) is another approach which uses the transmissibility matrix
to estimate individual contributions. The main advantage of this technique, that it does not require
frequency responses measurement and hence the system separation into two, because the estimation is
based only on operational conditions. By overcoming TPA limitations, the OTPA method seemed to
be a good technique to determine path contributions. However, it was observed, that this technique
has its own limitations that sometimes highly aﬀect ﬁnal results, that lead to the wrong calculation
of individual contributions. For this reason, the results achieved with the OTPA approach are often
questionable, which suggested looking for alternative solutions.
One of these alternative solution was suggested in [1], where an OTPA - inspired technique was presented.
The analytical model showed, that this method, which is further referred to as Operational Transfer Path
Analysis - Diﬀerence (OTPA-D), solves one of the crucial problems regarding the OTPA technique. The
OTPA-D results in an analytical model perfectly matched the ones obtained with the TPA approach,
which was used as a baseline for method comparison. A perfect match between these two methods
suggested looking into it experimentally.
In order to replicate the analytical model in real life, a test bench that represents an actual vehicle, was
made. All the experimental measurements were done in a way as presented in [1]. The results obtained
by this approach did not exactly match TPA results, diﬀerently from [1] example. However, the outcome
obtained with OTPA-D method were closer to TPA than OTPA, thus showing an improvement towards
the TPA. For the author, it is hoped that this report can be used not only to get familiar with OTPA-
D technique, but also with other approaches, their procedures and what kind of results each method
provides.

Introduction
In a daily life, people are surrounded by the diﬀerent sounds that are produced by all kind of sources.
The sound is the transmitted energy, generated by a vibrating surface, which travels through the air
or any type of structure and being perceived by humans or animals. However, not all the sounds can
be perceived as pleasant ones, since they often distract the environment around us. These unwanted
sounds are often noise generated by a working structure, for example, the engine of the vehicle, which
aﬀects the comfort or safety levels on a daily basis. Many companies are working and focusing on noise
reduction in order to increase comfortability and safety in the vehicles, planes and ships.
Considering the marine or automotive industry, the main structure-borne noise source is the engine,
which produces unwanted sounds which propagate through the diﬀerent paths. Several methods were
suggested to characterize which paths contribute the most.
The most commonly used technique to
ﬁnd individual path contributions is the transfer path analysis (TPA) method. The conventional TPA
method produces a qualitative results regarding the path contributions and source strength. In addition
to that, separate transfer functions and operational forces data allow identiﬁcation of part of the vehicle
that produces most of the noise, which is a big advantage. However, TPA method requires to measure
frequency responses and also to separate the system into two sub-systems: active and passive, for
example a vehicle, where the engine has to be removed in order to measure the transfer function. The
active and passive parts separation can be rather time consuming and expensive procedure, which made
conventional TPA method somewhat unattractive for small industries.
The disadvantages of the conventional TPA method call for alternative methods that are less time
consuming. One of them is operational transfer path analysis (OTPA), which is based on transmissibility
matrix (TM) estimation. The OTPA method does not require any frequency responses measurement and
to separate the system into two, as it is based on operational measurements only. However, the OTPA
method has some disadvantages that are listed in [3], [4], [5]: eﬀect of neglected paths, cross-coupling
and the issues behind the TM estimation.
The authors who investigated the weaknesses of OTPA, developed another method which is eﬀective
and eﬃcient as TPA and OTPA methods respectively, that is called operational path analysis with
exogenous inputs (OPAX). This approach uses operational data and transfer function of a coupled
system to estimate the mount parameters, loads and individual contributions. According to [6] the
OPAX method solves the issues inherent for TPA and OTPA methods, which makes this approach very
attractive.
Also, in [1] another approach to determine individual path contributions was presented. Similarly as
OTPA method, this approach uses transmissibility matrix to estimate the individual path contributions,
therefore the procedure is considered to be signiﬁcantly faster than conventional TPA. This method
uses the response diﬀerence between the active and the passive sides and therefore it will be referred as
operational transfer path analysis - diﬀerence (OTPA-D).

1.1
Scope
In [1] an analytical model was presented, where the results from three operational data-based methods
were compared with TPA results. The outcome from OTPA-D technique showed a full consistency with
the exact contribution, which oﬀered an interesting topic to study it further.
The objective of this master project is to investigate the idea from [1] experimentally, by following the
similar procedure, as presented in [1], collecting data and achieving the ﬁnal conclusion based on the
experimental results. Since it is impossible to measure contributions directly, the OTPA-D results will
be validated by comparing them with TPA, which is considered to be a baseline. In addition to that,
a direct force measurement for a few paths will be compared with TPA results to verify them. Finally,
the results achieved with TPA, OTPA and OTPA-D methods will be presented and discussed.
Moreover, if at the end of this project some extra time will be available when the additional investigation
regarding the TPA and OTPA methods will be performed. It is highly hoped that this project is going
to be beneﬁcial for both the B&K company and the student.
1.1.1
Expectations
The OTPA-D method [1] was ﬁrstly presented in 2009, but since then, the idea was not touched further,
and therefore it is interesting to see how it can be applied in real life scenario. During this project,
the experimental setup, representing a highly simpliﬁed version of a real vehicle, is going to be made,
which later on, can be used as the learning material in the company as well as in the university, to show
how the diﬀerent methods work and the results they can produce. Moreover, if satisfying results are
obtained, the techniques used in determining the individual path contribution might be improved.
From a student perspective, during this project, it is expected to learn about diﬀerent techniques and
procedures that are used to determine the individual path contributions. Furthermore, this project will
help to increase the experimental skills and understanding of structure-borne measurement techniques
and principles that can be applied to the diﬀerent industries in the future career.

Theory
2.1
Terminology
Before presenting the theory behind each method, a few terms that are going to be used is this report,
have to be introduced. As it was mentioned previously, the engine is the main noise source in the vehicle,
see ﬁgure 1.
Figure 1: Vehicle representation
From the ﬁgure 1, two subsystems can be observed: active and passive. Active sub-system represents
the engine, which is the main noise source: {X}. The noise from the active subsystem to the passive
one propagates through the diﬀerent paths which, for structure-borne sound, are the engine mounts.
The noise on the passive side is measured at the positions of interest, that often are: steering wheel,
seat and driver ear level. These positions correspond to receivers: {R}, that can be accelerometers
or microphones, depending on the mounting point.
Also, at the passive subsystem, the additional
accelerometers to determine the forces are often mounted. In practice, these accelerometers are placed
close to the interface or the source and referred as indicators: {V }. The path between active and
passive subsystems is described with frequency response functions (FRF)s:
• Hyx - from the source to receivers
• Hvx - from the source to indicators
2.2
TPA method
Transfer Path Analysis (TPA) was ﬁrstly experimentally explored at around 1980s by Verheij, who was
investigating the noise transmission of the ship machinery through the mounts [7]. However, most of the
ideas were presented way before that, at the end of 19th century [8]. These explorations allowed us to
have the conventional TPA method as it is today, and it makes it possible to identify the most dominant
4
==PAGE:11==
Application of vibro-acoustic OTPA
paths that may produce disturbing sounds. By knowing which path contributes most to the noise, it
becomes possible to optimize noise and vibrations performance in the system. This method helps to
solve the issues concerning Noise, Vibration and Harshness (NVH) in many types of systems. In this
section a very brief description of the diﬀerences between the conventional TPA and operational TPA
is presented. The more detailed description, including diﬀerent experimental results and the limitations
of both methods, is provided in appendix I.
The individual path contribution using TPA method is identiﬁed from the Frequency Response Functions
(FRF)s, which describes the system of interest, and the operational forces acting in the system, as
presented in the equation 1
{Y } = [Hyf]{F}
(1)
Equation 1 represents the input-output relationship, where {F} is the vector of operational input forces,
[Hyf] is the FRF matrix and {Y } is the vector of responses. The FRF of the system is usually determined
experimentally, for example using modal hammer or exciter, where excitation force is measured. A crucial
parameter that is used to synthesize path contributions is the operational input forces: {F}. The forces
can be determined by one of the following techniques:
• Using a force Transducer - requires to mount a force transducer at the interface, between active
and passive subsystems, and directly measure the load. The drawback of this method, that it
requires to modify the structure in order to mount, which in practise is not feasible.
• Mount stiﬀness method - another method which uses mount deformations to estimate operational
forces by applying Hook’s law:
{F} = [K]{∆X}
where [K] is the diagonal matrix of mount stiﬀnesses, and {∆X} is the vector of mount defor-
mation. The drawback of this method is that the mount stiﬀness data is not always available.
The mount-stiﬀness can be estimated experimentally, however, it can become quite complicated
procedure, since it depends on various parameters.
• Matrix inversion method - the most used method, which uses the inverse relationship of equation
1. In order to get a better estimation, responses from addition accelerometers (indicators): {V }
are used:
{V } = [Hvf]{F}
and then by inverting [Hvf] matrix, operational forces can be found:
{F} = [Hvf]+{V }
The main issue with matrix inversion, that quite signiﬁcant amount of responses are needed in
order to avoid ill-conditioning. The rule of thumb is to have three times more responses than
number of paths.
In this project, all three force estimation methods will be studied.
From FRF matrix and operational forces data, the individual contributions can be synthesized. Consider

a very simple system with two paths, characterized by FRFs: H11 and H12, and one receiver, then the
individual contributions for the two paths are determined from equations 2 and 3.
C11 = H11F1
(2)

where C11 and C12 are synthesized individual contributions and F1, F2 are operational forces. By adding

Y1 = C11 + C12

In practise, the equation 4 is used to verify if all paths are included. If not all the paths are covered,
then the sum of individual contributions becomes diﬀerent from the measured responses [3].
The main drawback of the conventional TPA method is that it requires to measure the FRF matrix,
hence the active and the passive components have to be separated, which might be quite time-consuming
and expensive. These limitations suggested to look for alternative methods to investigate NVH issues.
The contributions estimated with the conventional TPA method will be used as a baseline for comparison
with other methods.
2.3
OTPA method
Operational Transfer Path Analysis (OTPA) is another method which uses a transmissibility matrix
(TM) technique to identify the individual path contribution. The transmissibility concept was ﬁrstly
introduced by Liu and Ewins [9], who showed the analytical representation of such a method. The
experimental work regarding the transmissibility was carried out by Ribeiro, Maia and Silva [10]. In
this section, only the main aspects concerning the OTPA method are presented.
First of all, the input-output relationship of OTPA method is presented in the equation 5.
{Y } = [Tyv]{V }
(5)
where vector {Y } is responses at the receivers and {V } is the vector of the indicators responses, that are
measured at the same time. The indicators must be mounted as close as possible to each path, while the
receivers remain the same. The [Tyv] represents the transmissibility matrix, which is determined from
operational measurements. In order to avoid the errors regarding the ill-conditioning [8], the number of
operational conditions: M have to be higher than the number of paths. The TM matrix is estimated
by moving the indicator responses to the other side of equation 5, which gives the relation:

2.4
OPAX method
It was noted that OTPA method has some major drawbacks and therefore other ways to determine
the individual contributions had to be invented.
The Operational Path Analysis with eXogeneous
inputs (OPAX) method was proposed in [6], where the diﬀerent approach to identify path contributions
was suggested. This approach models the parameters of operational forces, that are necessary to ﬁnd
individual contributions. In addition to that, OPAX method does not require to separate the system
into two, which is a big advantage comparing with conventional TPA.
Five step procedure is needed to determine the individual contributions:
• Operational measurement - similarly as for OTPA method, operational data have to be measured.
The number of operational conditions does not necessarily have to be higher than the number of
paths, however that increases the parameter’s accuracy.
• FRF measurement - similarly as for TPA, the OPAX requires FRF measurements, what are done
on a coupled system. The issue with this, that it might be complicated to excite at the interface
8
==PAGE:15==
Application of vibro-acoustic OTPA
position, therefore it is recommended to use the reciprocity method.
• Parametric load models - in this step, the parameters of the mount are modeled using the data
from previous steps. In [6] two models are presented: single degree of freedom and multiband, that
can be used to determine the load parameters.
• Operational forces - similar as for conventional TPA method, the operational forces are determined,
using parameters estimated previously.
• Individual contribution - after the operational forces are determined, the individual contributions
can be found.
It can be observed, that all the steps are similar to the TPA method, the diﬀerence is the modeling of the
load parameters. According to [6], the OPAX method does not face the drawbacks of OTPA method.
In addition to that, the procedure is rather fast, which gives the OPAX method a big advantage over
conventional TPA. In this project, the OPAX method is not going to be investigated, hence only a brief
description was presented.
2.5
OTPA-D method
Study [1] suggested another way to identify the individual path contribution, using a very similar proce-
dure as OTPA in terms that it also uses only operational measurements. This technique as the indicators
response uses the diﬀerences between the active and passive subsystems motions, therefore it is referred
to as Operational Transfer Path Analysis - Diﬀerence (OTPA-D).
The Hook’s law states that the force acting on the mounts can be found by multiplying the stiﬀness of
the mount with the mount deformation, as presented below:
{F} = [K]{∆X}
(14)
The equation 14 shows the force-stiﬀness relationship where {F} is the vector of operational forces, [K]
is the diagonal mount stiﬀness matrix and {∆X} is the operational mount deformation vector. In can
be noted that the same relationship is used to ﬁnd operational forces for the conventional TPA [8], if
mount stiﬀness method is used. Equation 14 can be inserted into the TPA input-output relationship
formula (equation 1) which yields:
{Y } = [Hyf]{F} = [Hyf][K]{∆X}
(15)
The equation 15 shows the casual relationship between input and output, identically as for TPA method.
The {∆X} corresponds to the diﬀerence between the indicators responses measured at the active and
the passive parts of the system (for example: active-engine, passive-chassis): ∆X = Xa −Xp.
In
addition to that, the multiplication between matrices [Hyf] and [K] links the receivers responses with
the deformation responses [1], which meets the transmissibility concept and can be expressed as the new

application does not require any knowledge regarding the mount stiﬀness or the transfer function of the

The analytical solution of this approach together with the conventional TPA and the OTPA methods
were presented in [1]. The results obtained by OTPA-D method, for a simple analytical system, were
compared with other methods where this technique showed an exact consistency with the TPA results,
meaning that the individual contributions estimated with the application were correct.
From the [1] one drawback concerning OTPA-D method was mentioned: double amount of indicators.
Furthermore, OTPA and OTPA-D methods are decomposition based ones, meaning that neglected paths
cannot be identiﬁed, because the sum of contributions is equal to measured receiver response. This tells,
that in order to determine the correct path contributions, all the paths have to be included.
Moreover, concerning the TM estimation, identically as for OTPA method, the OTPA-D also requires
uncorrelated forces for the best estimation. Highly correlated forces will make the TM rank deﬁcient,
which will give only an approximation. The correlation between the forces can be lessened with a big
number of operational conditions, similarly as for OTPA.
Furthermore, based on analytical results from [1], the OTPA-D method does not face the cross-coupling
issue, which gives a very big advantage over the OTPA approach.

Diﬀerent type of estimators can be used to determine the transmissibility matrices. In practice, each
estimator produces slightly diﬀerent results, and therefore it was decided to compare the outputs and
choose one, which produces the results closest to the baseline.

2.7
In-situ TPA
Operational forces, for the conventional TPA method, are determined by one of the following methods:
mount-stiﬀness, matrix inversion or using force transducer’s data, that often requires a long and complex
procedure. In this section, the innovative approach to estimate individual contributions is presented.
This new approach is based on combination of two methods: in-situ and blocked-forces [8]. This part is
an additional investigation concerning the conventional TPA method and does not really follow the main
topic of the project, however, it is interesting to investigate this method and compare it with others.
In-situ TPA measurement technique was presented by A.T.Moorhouse in [13]. In this study, it was both,
mathematically and experimentally, shown that blocked forces can be determined from the passive side
responses and FRFs of the full assembly. The relationship between responses and forces is presented in
equation 24 (full expression can be found in [13]).
{Vp} = [Hpi] {Fbl}
(24)
where Vp represents the responses vector, Fbl - blocked forces and Hpi - transfer function matrix. It can
be noted that equation 24 is very similar to equation 1 representing the input-output relationship of
conventional TPA method. The diﬀerence is that Hpi is now the transfer function of full setup, excited
at the interface i, between active and passive parts, and measured at the passive part p. However, in
practice it is rather complicated to excite the structure from interface position, therefore the reciprocity
principle can often be applied:
{Vp} = [Hip]T {Fbl}
(25)
where HT
ip is the FRF matrix excited at the passive part and measured at the interface. Furthermore, the
individual contributions are found using the same approach as for conventional TPA. It can be observed
that this method does not require to separate the system into two, which in practice helps to save a lot
amount of time.
According to [13], the estimated forces do not meet the operational ones, but this approach correctly
identiﬁes the individual contributions. The results from this method will be presented and compared
with other ones.

Experimental setup
The results between the conventional TPA and the OTPA-D in the analytical model were consistent,
which suggested to replicate the same procedure experimentally and to show the validity of this approach.
In this section, the experimental setup which was used is presented.
4.1
Design
The ﬁrst step was to design and built the experimental setup, that to be used to perform most of the
measurements. The experimental setup had to represent the simpliﬁed version of an engine attachment
in a real vehicle. In order to do so, four main parts representing the: engine, mounts, chassis and the
suspension had to be included into the design. By taking all four parts into account, the design of such
a setup was carried out and presented in ﬁgure 11.
Figure 11: Design of the experimental setup
The "engine" was designed to be made from plywood, with an empty cavity inside to mount the shakers.
The mass of the engine box was approximated using the material properties values from [14] together
with the dimensions used in the design. In addition to that, the mass of the shakers, engine mounts and
the holders (interface between the box and the engine mount) were included as well.
The frame was made from aluminum, as it is inexpensive, less stiﬀand not that heavy material comparing
with steel. The frame was designed to have an empty cavity as well, which could be used to mount
accelerometers below the engine mounts (mounting is presented in the next section). It is necessary to
22
==PAGE:29==
Application of vibro-acoustic OTPA
mount indicators as close as possible to each path, in order to minimize cross-coupling issue [4] as it was
discussed previously. The mass of the frame was approximated using the dimensions from design and
the material properties from [14].
As for suspension of the experimental setup, it was chosen to use simple springs (not the ones as it
is presented in ﬁgure 11). The suitable height of the springs was roughly estimated from the stiﬀness
of the spring and the force acting on them (note: that setup has 4 springs in total). This required to
determine the fundamental frequency of the full setup (the reason why the mass of the frame and the box
were discussed previously). The fundamental frequency was set to be fn ∼10 Hz, which is signiﬁcantly
lower compared with the frequency of structural modes. The estimated mass of the setup, without the
suspension, was approximated by adding all the masses together: mt ≈16 kg, which resulted to the
stiﬀness of s1 = 15 N/mm for one spring. Furthermore, by knowing the total mass and using the second
law of motion, it was possible to ﬁnd the force acting on the suspension: F ≈150 N. Lastly, by applying
the Hook’s law, the displacement of spring deformation was determined: ∆x = 1/4 cm. This suggested
that the spring height should be about 1cm in order to have some movement in the vertical direction.
It is worth mentioning, that spring could not be too high, because the movements on the horizontal axis
might become too dominant, and that would highly aﬀect the stability. The mass of the accelerometers,
force transducers and the wires were not included, meaning that real setup is somewhat heavier, which
should inﬂuence the resonance frequency and the displacement of each spring. The dimensions of the
experimental setup can be found in the appendix C.
4.2
Equipment
In order to implement and perform all the vibrational experiments, it was necessary to gather measure-
ment equipment.
To reproduce similar sounds as the real vehicle, it was necessary to implement an engine vibration
generator (EVG). The vibrations in the EVG were generated using three shakers, mounted on the
separate walls of the engine box, where each of them pointed to a diﬀerent axis: X, Y and Z. This
allowed exciting the vibrations in three directions.
Another important object in the setup was the mounts between the engine and the chassis. Similarly to
the vehicle, three engine mounts were used, where one of them was made to be easily removable (middle
one), to investigate the eﬀect of rotational DoFs.
To measure the responses at the receivers and engine mount positions (indicators), it was necessary to
have a certain amount of accelerometers. The OTPA-D method required two triaxial accelerometers
for one structure-borne path: six in total. In addition to that, the operational forces estimation using
matrix inverse method [8], required to add six additional triaxial accelerometers, since it is recommended
to have the number of indicators three times higher than the number of paths (rule of thumb).
For receiver responses, it was decided to use two more accelerometers, that were not necessarily triaxial
(preferably triaxial).
Since the idea of this project is to investigate the structure-borne paths, the
accelerometers were used as receivers instead of microphones.

7
Discussion & possible directions of future research
In the Results section 6, three parts were presented.
In the ﬁrst part the individual contributions
estimated with OTPA and OTPA-D methods were compared with conventional TPA results in two
frequency ranges. The results from the Case 1 showed that Method 2 & 3 coincided slightly better
with TPA results compared with Method 1. Furthermore, the results from Method 2 & 3 were
almost the same due to soft engine mounts, which was also presented analytically (section 3). By using
the more stiﬀengine mount it is expected that the Method 2 results will become worse, similarly as it
was illustrated in the analytical model.
In the Case 2 scenario, frequency range was increased at it was noted that Frequency Assurance Criteria
values decreased insigniﬁcantly for all three OTPA-based methods. The individual contributions plots
from OTPA-based methods seemed to be quite diﬀerent compared with TPA. The biggest diﬀerences
appeared in the range of 600 −1000 Hz, which was considered as not fully trusted. In addition to that,
neither OTPA nor OTPA-D methods correctly identiﬁed all the paths that contribute the most.
Having produced and analyzed results obtained during the project, it is learned that the chosen exper-
imental setup was not perfect for studying OTPA-based methods. The following drawbacks and the
ways to improve it in the future can be mentioned:
• Considering the TPA results, it was noted that all the paths contribute rather similarly. First of
all, all the forces were highly correlated, which could be reduced by having three separate sources
for each mount. By having three separate sources, it would be possible to excite three diﬀerent
frequency ranges, for example, mount 1: 0−500 Hz, mount 2: 500−1000 Hz and mount 3: 1−1.5
kHz, similarly as in [2]. This will allow investigating the cross-coupling eﬀect for OTPA-D method,
which ideally supposes to be zero.
• Secondly, in order to diﬀerentiate individual contributions between each path, the structure of the
frame should be changed. Considering a current frame, the FRFs between left and right mounts
are rather similar due to the same structure and since the forces are highly correlated, then the
contributions between left and right sides become quite alike. By changing the structure on one
side, or both, the FRFs will become more diﬀerent as well as contributions, which will give a better
identiﬁcation about most dominant paths determined by each method.
• The last thing is to install harder engine mounts, which will increase the cross-coupling eﬀect in
the active part. Due to stiﬀer interface, Method 2 results will regress, while for Method 3 - will
not, which will better represent the analytical model from [1].
Moreover, in the additional part, In-situ TPA method was implemented and its results were presented.
First of all, it was observed that blocked forces are similar to operational ones, but this only seems
reasonable for such a simple experimental setup, since in [13] the diﬀerences between both were found
signiﬁcant. The results from this method were comparable with OTPA-D in terms that both had similar
FRAC values. However, In-situ TPA method coincides worse with TPA results compared with OTPA-D,
which suggests that something went wrong and it requires additional investigation. As presented in [8],
the blocked forces method assumes that the active subsystem is rigidly ﬁxed, also the results accuracy
68
==PAGE:75==
Application of vibro-acoustic OTPA
highly depends on boundary stiﬀness. Considering that boundary stiﬀness is low, the results of blocked
forces become inaccurate and hence the individual contribution gives incorrect results.
In the last part of Results, the dynamic stiﬀness of the engine mount was presented. From the stiﬀness
plots it was noted, that actual engine mount behavior is rather complex. This complex behavior is not
taken into account while employing a single DoF model. However, it was shown that by using table
values, the single DoF model produces a very reasonable operational forces estimation, that is good
enough to estimate individual contributions. Furthermore, the dynamic stiﬀnesses of the mount were
multiplied with FRFs, which are the two parameters required to estimate TMs for OTPA-D method.
Even though both estimations matches perfectly in theory, the actual results were slightly diﬀerent,
which explains why TPA and OTPA-D results did not coincide perfectly.
This tells that OTPA-D
method has some drawbacks that were not accounted in the analytical model. Previously mentioned
changes regarding the experimental setup should help to investigate these disadvantages more deeply.
Overall, even though the OTPA-D method results did not perfectly coincide with TPA, it showed a
better match than OTPA and In-situ TPA techniques. In addition to that, the OTPA results can be
quite reliable if indicators are placed on the active side and the mounts are rather soft: Method 2.
Summarizing, to determine quite realistic individual path contributions, the OTPA-D method can be
applied, since it produces a rather trustworthy outcome and has a signiﬁcantly faster procedure than
conventional TPA. So if it is known that mounts are soft, the Method 2 can be used, but if the mounts
are stiﬀor no parameters are known, the OTPA-D method should be used.
8
Conclusion
In the ﬁrst part of the report, the brief theory of diﬀerent techniques for estimating source path contri-
butions were presented. These methods, except OPAX, were used in analytical and experimental setups,
therefore it was important to present their advantages and disadvantages.
In the next section, the analytical model from [1] was replicated in order to get familiar with TPA, OTPA
and OTPA-D techniques. In addition to that, the analytical model showed that by using indicators
diﬀerence to estimate TMs and individual contributions, the OTPA-D method produces the same results
as TPA.
After the analytical model was presented and investigated, the design of the experimental setup was
carried out. For the actual setup, it was decided to keep a simple design, representing a highly simpliﬁed
version of an engine mounted on a chassis. The experimental setup seemed to be a rather good replica
of the one presented in [1]. However, as it was discussed previously, modiﬁcations in the experimental
setup would be helpful to obtain a better investigation regarding the OTPA-D method.
In the next part, the actual procedure of how to determine individual contributions with all the methods
mentioned previously was presented. Concerning TPA approach, the FRFs and operational forces were
presented ﬁrst. From the operational forces plot, it was noted that Matrix inversion method gives
slightly diﬀerent results than the actual ones. These diﬀerences between the forces were most likely due
to rotational DoF, which cannot be measured with force transducer and for this reason, it was decided
69
==PAGE:76==
Application of vibro-acoustic OTPA
to investigate two frequency ranges. Regarding the OTPA and OTPA-D techniques, the transmissibility
matrices were estimated ﬁrst.
From the Results part, it was marked that Method 2 & 3 shows rather similar results, due to the
soft engine mount. Harder engine mount should better represent the analytical model from [1], where
results between Method 2 and Method 3 were quite diﬀerent. Furthermore, based on FRAC values it
was observed that OTPA-D approach showed the better match to TPA compared with Method 1. Also,
the results from OTPA-D approach surpassed the ones determined with in-situ blocked forces method.
Lastly, the dynamic stiﬀness of the mount was presented. It was noted that actual behavior is far more
complex from the ones estimated with the single DoF model. Nevertheless, the single DoF model can
produce reasonably correct forces if the mount parameters are close to the actual ones.
In the last part of this report, the outcome of the study was discussed, where addition suggestions
regarding the improvement of experimental setup were presented. It was suggested to use an OTPA-D
method over OTPA if the additional amount of accelerometers are available.
Even though OTPA-
D technique did not match TPA results, it gives a rather correct estimation regarding the individual
contributions.
In addition to that, the procedure of OTPA-D technique is signiﬁcantly faster than
conventional TPA, which is a big advantage for industries that are not willing to spend extra time to
conduct TPA measurements.
To sum up, during this project diﬀerent methods to determine individual path contributions were inves-
tigated. In the beginning phase of this project, many aspects were not considered, which caused some
issues regarding the procedure, that highly aﬀected the time schedule therefore not all the ideas were
investigated. Furthermore, in order to get a better understanding about the OTPA-D method, it would
be interesting to implement a ﬁnite element model, which will help to investigate the eﬀect of rotational
DoF and to determine why its results did not match TPA approach. From a student perspective, this
project was interesting and very useful to investigate the methods, especially TPA, that are highly used
in diﬀerent industries to determine individual path contributions.

Operational transfer path analysis (OTPA)
1
Introduction
Diﬀerent type of sounds surround us in a everyday life, where some of them are pleasant while others can
be described as annoying and disturbing ones. These distracting sounds can be generated by vibrating
sources, like a car engine, and transferred via structure or air into the chassis of the vehicle causing
uncomfortable environment. Transfer Path Analysis (TPA) is a tool which helps to investigate the noise
transmission from the source to receiver and to identify the issue.
The main purpose of this report is to learn all the main aspects regarding the Operational Transfer
Path Analysis (OTPA) method and its limitations. In addition to that, the report can be referred as an
introductory material to the master thesis which will focus on a certain application how to increase the
quality and precision of OTPA results.
In the ﬁrst part of the report, the brief description of the classical TPA method is going to be presented.
The classical TPA is one oldest techniques used to identify which path contributes the most and produces
the greatest amount of noise. However, the drawbacks of classical TPA made the method less attractive
and therefore other ways to identify most dominant noise transmission were investigated. Operational
TPA (OTPA), which is the main topic of this report, is another way to determine the transmission
from the source to receiver. The measurement technique of OTPA method is signiﬁcantly faster and
cheaper comparing with the classical TPA, which makes the OTPA method more compelling. However,
the OTPA method has its own drawbacks that aﬀect the measurement data and therefore it might give
faulty results, which might lead to the wrong engineering decisions.

2.1
Transfer path analysis
Before presenting the operational Transfer Path Analysis, it is crucial to get familiar with a classical
Transfer Path Analysis (TPA) method. Classical TPA is commonly used in diﬀerent industries to ﬁnd
the paths of structure-borne and air-borne noises in a system. Is it well known that both noise sources
cause diﬀerent problems and according to Reference [4], TPA method helps to reduce or solve the issues
and allows to improve the diﬀerent aspects of the system:
• Comfort - in automotive industry by reducing all the external noises, the comfort level in the car
increases, therefore the drive becomes more pleasant.
• Safety - in airplanes industry by cutting down all the active vibrations that appear during the
ﬂight, the plane becomes more stable and therefore the ﬂight becomes more safe.
• Secrecy - in marines industry by lowering engine noise the ships produce moderate level of sound,
therefore it becomes less noticeable and more secret.
The more detailed description about each TPA method is presented in the literature [4]. In the classical
TPA method, two parts of the system are considered: active and passive, which contain sources and
receivers respectively. In the literature, the interface between active and passive sides is described as a
Frequency Response Function (FRF) or a Noise Transfer Function (NTF), which basically characterizes
the system of interest, for example the car chassis. The such the system is presented in ﬁgure 1

2.1.1

In order to identify the individual contribution of each path, the transfer function of the system has

common way to estimate NTF is by applying hammer or a shaker force, which has a known excitation

car). Removal of the active component can be quite time consuming and expensive procedure, therefore
the classical TPA method is considered to be somewhat pricey. However, even though the procedure is
expensive, the ﬁnal results are excellent, which allows engineer to ﬁnd most dominant structure-borne
paths and optimize them.
Moreover, another unknown variables are the input forces, which have to be determined in operational
conditions. The operational forces determination is done by applying one of the following techniques.
One of the methods to measure the forces is by applying load cells [5] or a force transducer between
the active and passive parts of the system and measure it directly. The main drawback is that load
cells requires some space and appropriate surface to mount it. Furthermore, extra transducer might
require to change the mounting itself, which suggests that procedure becomes rather time consuming
and again expensive.
In addition to that, not all the customers will be happy about changing the
mounting structure of the system, therefore the alternative techniques are being used.

2.2
Operational TPA
Similarly as TPA method, operational TPA uses input and output quantities to estimate Transmissibil-
ity Matrix (TMM). These input and output quantities (excitation and response respectively) are also
referred as Degrees of Freedom (DoF). The main advantage of operational TPA is that this method does
not require to estimate NTF matrix, which determination involves the separation of the system into
two: active and passive. For the OTPA method, all the measurements are performed on the assembled
system and they are operational. Due to these reasons, OTPA is signiﬁcantly faster and cheaper method
to estimate individual paths contribution.
OTPA method estimates the individual path contribution from transmissibility matrix, which is entirely
diﬀerent concept comparing with NTF data, that is used in a classical TPA method. First of all lets
consider the system the same way as it was for classical TPA, where the relationship between input and
output can be represented as:
{X}[Hxy] = {Y }

are the same quantities as it is for a classical TPA. In practice not all the physical quantities have to be
presented.

2.2.4
Limitations
Even though the operational TPA method is signiﬁcantly faster and cheaper to perform comparing with
the classical TPA, it has some limitations which make the estimates determined by this method quite
questionable. According to [9] the Operational TPA has three critical issues which have to be taken into
account:
• Eﬀect of neglected parts
• Cross-Coupling between input accelerations
• Errors in transmissibility estimation
Neglected parts:
First of all the eﬀect of neglected parts is going to be investigated.
As it was
presented in classical TPA section, the measured total response is the sum of the contribution from each
path. In classical TPA each path is independent so by comparing the total response and the sum of
contributions, the error of neglected parts can be easily identiﬁed and taken into consideration. As for
OTPA, the neglected path might be correlated with other paths therefore its energy will spread and will
be taken into account unintentionally. In this case, the error of neglected path will not be recognized.
This issue was presented in [2] and the results from the paper will also be presented in this section

Discussion & Conclusion
In the ﬁrst part of the report, the classical TPA method was presented brieﬂy to show the idea about
the procedure. As it was presented, this method produces really good results which can be used to trou-
bleshoot the most signiﬁcant noise problems caused by structure-borne or air-borne sounds. However,
in order to determine the transfer function, this method requires to separate the active part from the
passive one. Considering complex system, the separation of two parts might be complicated and time
consuming issue. Furthermore, the separation requires experienced person to do it, which means that
not only time but also the cost of the procedure is quite high. These two reasons made classical TPA
method quite unattractive for the speciﬁc customers, which requires to perform the test quite fast with
the minimum amount of expenses. Due to this reason another approach to ﬁnd the individual path
contribution were proposed.
Operational TPA is an another method which was presented in this report. OTPA does not require
to divide active component from the passive, meaning that all the measurement are performed on
operational conditions, which is big beneﬁt comparing with classic TPA. However, as it was presented
earlier, the OTPA has its own diﬃculties, which aﬀects the data from the measurements and hence the
individual contribution estimation. First of all, the OTPA data requires incoherent input forces, which in
practice is hardly achievable, because all the paths are normally really close by and the sources produce
more or less coherent signals. As it was presented in the previous section, by having high coherence
between the forces, it becomes quite complicated to identify if the neglected path is dominant or not,
since the response and sum of individual contributions are almost the same.
Another issue presented previously concerns cross-coupling between diﬀerent paths. The results showed
that even though there is no force acting on a path, the energy from other paths will spread and therefore
the faulty individual contribution might be identiﬁed. This issue might be partly solved by having the
indicators mounted close to the speciﬁc path, which allows to reduce the bookkeeping parameter to the
minimum meaning that cross-coupling between the paths will be reduced as well.
The last ﬂaw regarding the OTPA method comes from transmissibility matrix estimation and in the
previous section two types of results concerning this issue were presented. The ﬁrst set of results were
achieved by having the number of paths lower than number of engine orders and it was observed that
total response and sum of individual contributions matched pretty well. In the second set, the number
of paths was ﬁx to be higher than the number of orders and it was observed that individual path
contribution became slightly diﬀerent. Since the number of orders was lower than the number of paths,
the APS matrix became rank deﬁcient and therefore only approximate solution was found. This issue
can be reduced by having higher number of operational conditions than the number of paths, which
however, sometimes might be quite complicated to achieve. In addition to that, as it was presented
previously, singular value decomposition helps to improve the TMM estimation as well, even though the
matrix is being slightly changed.
The more detailed explanation regarding the issues of OTPA method together with the experimental
results are presented in [2][3][9][10] literature.
To sum up, as it was mentioned previously, the main purpose of this course and the report is to learn
