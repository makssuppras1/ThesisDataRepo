1 ABSTRACT 
The adrenergic, dopaminergic and serotonergic effects of ergot alkaloids has resulted in a rich history 
of pharmaceutical value and application both neurological and psychological conditions, as well as 
aiding childbirth through stimulation of smooth muscle contraction. DLA, the precursor used for 
synthesis of the potent psychoactive drug LSD, is an alkaloid of particular interest in recent years with 
the growing demand for alternative therapies for mental illness such as anxiety, depression and PTSD, 
all of which are evidenced to be greatly ameliorated for long periods after treatment with a single or 
two doses of LSD in clinical trials. Conventional production relies on tedious strain development of 
environmentally sensitive Claviceps sp., with field production and submerged fermentation providing 
suboptimal yields to meet the growing demand for DLA. The use of heterologous EAS expression 
systems such as S. cerevisiae and A. nidulans adapted to large scale production and advanced genetic 
manipulation have emerged as promising alternatives for producing more coveted ergot alkaloids 
such as DLA. Keeping up this trend, EAS pathway construction was performed on an  A. nidulans strain, 
NID2742, heterologously expressing genes of the DLA biosynthetic pathway in A. homorphus. LC-MS 
analysis of NID2742 provided insight into why DLA production has not been successful with this strain 
and what can be done to fix it. Potentially non-functional EAS genes in the pathway were identified 
and swapped with various EAS enzyme homologue genes sourced from Cla. purpurea, Asp. leporis, 
Asp. hiratsukae, Asp. turcocus, Tri. arundinaceum, Tal. atroroseus and Pen. expansum. Homologues 
were selected based on sequence and structural homology, with specific focus on active site residues 
predicted with the use of alphafold structures generated with Colabfold and ligand bound crystal 
structures of the PDB database. 30 strains were developed in this project, with unique combinations 
of EasA, EasD and CloA homologue genes, with no apparent DLA production but a wealth of insights 
into how to achieve production in future experiments including the use of multiple copies of genes for 
mitigating bottlenecks, knocking out native A. nidulans genes that could be interfering with the 
pathway, and testing alternative promoters and terminators. Media optimization indicated that YES 
and CYA media may be optimal for ergot alkaloid production, confirming previous studies suggesting 
that sucrose and yeast-extract medias are best. Extraction solvent optimization gave mixed results, 
indicating higher solubility of some ergot alkaloids in IPA:EA(1:3)1%FA while other may be extracted 
more efficiently with MeOH.

4 INTRODUCTION 
Ergot alkaloids are most widely known for their toxic effect in the numerous victims of chronic ergot 
poisoning up until the 19th century. The resulting illness, ergotism, known as “Saint Anthony’s Fire” in 
the middle-ages, resulted in ghastly and severe symptoms such as gangrenous skin, rotting limbs and 
hallucinatory psychosis (van Dongen & de Groot, 1995). When harnessed appropriately, the same 
properties of ergot alkaloids that gave rise to these gruesome symptoms are also responsible for their 
immense pharmaceutical value. The structural similarity of the ergoline base structure of ergot 
alkaloids results and the neurotransmitters and hormones dopamine, serotonin and adrenalin result 
in an array of dopaminergic, serotonergic, adrenergic and smooth-muscle-contraction-inducing 
effects upon ingestion and have proved to be useful in treating neurological conditions such as 
migraine headaches, Parkinson’s disease, dementia and Alzheimer’s disease, as well as facilitating 
contractions and mitigating hemorrhaging during and after childbirth (J.-J. Chen et al., 2017; Dewick, 
2009). In fact, one such ergot alkaloid, ergometrine, is on the WHOs list of essential medicines for its 
vital use in inducing contractions during childbirth. Dihydroergotamine has shown potential for lung 
cancer treatment by inducing apoptosis and mitophagy in lung cancer cells in vitro (S.-H. Chang et al., 
2016). Ergot alkaloids can also be used as precursors for synthesis of the psychoactive drug LSD, which 
has gained significant attention in recent years after several clinical studies revealed its promise in the 
treatment of mental illnesses such as refractory depression, anxiety and PTSD (De Gregorio et al., 
2021; Liechti, 2017). The growing demand for these valuable compounds makes the development of 
filamentous fungal strains capable of high and efficient production of these clinically relevant alkaloids 
not only a lucrative venture but also of great benefit to human health and society.

5 BACKGROUND 
5.1 ERGOT ALKALOID BIOSYNTHESIS 
Fungal producers of ergot alkaloids include members of the genera Aspergillus, Penicillium, Rhizopus, 
Epichloe and Claviceps(Aniszewski, 2015; Cheng et al., 2010). The ergot alkaloid biosynthetic pathway 
in Claviceps purpurea starts with a condensation reaction between L-tryptophan and dimethylallyl 
diphosphate (DMAPP), giving 4-dimethylallyl-L-tryptophan (4-DMAT). The condensation reaction is 
catalyzed by the dimethylallyltryptophan synthase DmaW, in which DMAPP is dephosphorylated, and 
the resulting electron-deficient isoprene unit binds to L-tryptophan through nucleophilic attack from 
the C4 carbon of the aromatic ring. In the next step the enzyme EasF, a dimethylallyltryptophan N-
methyltransferase, catalyzes the transfer of the methyl group from S-adenosine methionine to the 
amine nitrogen of 4-DMAT, resulting in N-methyl-4-dimethylallyl-L-tryptophan (N-Me-DMAT)  (J.-J. 
Chen et al., 2017; Dewick, 2009). While the exact mechanisms remain unclear, the gene products of 
easC and easE, a catalase and FAD-dependent oxidoreductase, respectively, are known to both be 
critical for chanoclavine-I formation in the third step of the EAS pathway. Goetz et al. (2011) 
demonstrated that the deletion of either of genes encoding the homologues for EasC and EasC in A. 
fumigatus results in the accumulation of N-Me-DMAT, with restoration of chanoclavine-I production 
only when both genes were present in the BGC (Goetz et al., 2011). The alcohol dehydrogenase EasD 
then catalyzes the oxidation of chanoclavine into chanoclavine-I aldehyde via electron capture by 
cofactor NAD+ (Wallwey et al., 2010).  
Figure 1: Biosynthesis of chanoclavine-I aldehyde from DMAPP and L-tryptophan precursors. Homologous EAS enzymes 
from A. fumigatus in parenthesis. Reproduced from (J.-J. Chen et al., 2017). 
From chanoclavine-I aldehyde, the oxidoreductase EasA and dehydrogenase EasG catalyze the 
formation of the tetracyclic indole-containing ring structure known as ergoline, resulting in 
agroclavine, the precursor to d-lysergic acid (DLA), in C. Claviceps and festuclavine in A. fumigatus  (J.-
J. Chen et al., 2017; Young et al., 2015). This branch point in the EAS pathway is believed to be 
dependent on the particular isoform of EasA involved, with the isomerase version directing 
agroclavine synthesis and the reductase version directing festuclavine synthesis (J.-J. Chen et al., 
2017). Cheng et al. showed that the isomerase activity of the EasA homologue in Neotyphodium lolii 
is in part due to the phenylalanine residue on position 176, as a mutation to tyrosine, as found in the 
corresponding position in the reductase EasA isoform of A. fumigatus, resulted in the production of 
festuclavine. This is likely due to the fact that tyrosine can contribute to the reductase activity of 
EasARed as an electron donor. However, a Y176F mutation in A. fumigatus did not result in agroclavine 
production, indicating that other residues and factors may be involved (Cheng et al., 2010). In a later 
study, Wong et al. selected three C. purpurea easA homologues with phenylalanine in the

corresponding 176 position to functionally screen through co-expression with easG and easD in a yeast 
strain with genome-integrated dmaW, easF, easC, easE genes; the results revealed that all three 
homologues containing 176F in easA produced agroclavine, and no festuclavine (Wong et al., 2022). 
Therefore, while 176F is not the only residue involved in the isomerase activity of EasAIso, it appears 
to be key residue of the active site as well as a functional marker for EasA homologues with isomerase 
activity. The isomerase activity of EasAIso facilitates the cis-trans isomerization of the C8-C9 double 
bond to orient the aldehyde group in such a way that EasG can catalyze binding of the aldehyde carbon 
with the amine nitrogen, closing the fourth ring of the ergoline structure and resulting in an iminium 
intermediate. However, the higher reducing capacity of EasARed enables both cis-trans isomerization 
and reduction of the C8-C9 double bond, which is saturated in festuclavine and preserved in 
agroclavine and its derivatives (Figure 2). The C-N double bond of the resulting iminium intermediate 
is then reduced further by EasG, resulting in festuclavine (A. fumigatus) or agroclavine (C. Claviceps) 
(J.-J. Chen et al., 2017; Cheng et al., 2010). In an in vitro study, Matuschek et al. found that EasG from 
C. purpurea was able to catalyze the conversion of chanoclavine-I aldehyde to agroclavine in the 
absence of easA. This was only possible when both NADPH and GSH were present in the media with a 
C. claviceps EasG homologue and chanoclavine-I-aldehyde, resulting in a conversion rate to 
agroclavine of 59%. Their theory was that GSH provided the electron for the spontaneous 
isomerization of chanoclavine-I aldehyde into the iminium intermediate, which was then further 
reduced to agroclavine by EasG, using NADPH as the electron donor(Matuschek et al., 2011). 
Haarmann et al. discovered that the P450 monooxygenase, clavine oxidase (CloA) is necessary for 
production of DLA and its derivatives, as it’s inactivation in C. Claviceps resulted in an accumulation of 
chanoclavine-I, agroclavine, and elymoclavine, an intermediate between agroclavine and DLA 
(Haarmann et al., 2006). The accumulation of elymoclavine indicated the requirement of a second 
P450 monooxygenase for conversion of agroclavine to elymoclavine, and therefore Haarman et al. 
concluded that CloA only catalyzed the conversion of elymoclavine to paspalic acid, which can then 
undergo spontaneous isomerization to DLA (Haarmann et al., 2006). However, Wong et al. was able 
to produce D-lsyergic acid using only CloA from C. Claviceps heterogously expressed in a BGC in yeast 
along with dmaW, easF, easC, easE, easD, easA and easG, suggesting that CloA is capable of catalyzing 
the full conversion of agroclavine to paspalic acid and DLA in a two-step oxidation of the 9-carbon 
methyl group to a carboxyl group via the elymoclavine intermediate (Wong et al., 2022).  
DLA is not the end product in naturally occurring members of the family Clavicepitacea and genus 
Aspergillus, and subsequent modifications occur to the carboxyl group of DLA as a result of alkylation 
with amino acids catalyzed by various non-ribosomal peptide synthases (NRPS) contained within the 
EAS BGC. The products of these NRPS-catalyzed modifications to DLA are simple DLA amides or

ergopeptines such as the widely used pharmaceutically active compounds ergometrine or ergotamine, 
respectively, depending on the specific NRPSs involved. In C. purpurea, capable of producing both 
ergotamine and ergometrine, four NRPSs are contained in the EAS BGC: lysergyl peptide synthetase  
B (LpsB), LpsC and two variants of LpsA, LpsA1 and LpsA2 (J.-J. Chen et al., 2017; Dewick, 2009; Jones 
et al., 2021; Ortel & Keller, 2009).  
Each LPS contains one or more modules, and each module contains an adenylation domain (A), 
responsible for selecting and activating the substrate using ATP, a thiolation domain (T), which 
covalently binds the substrate via a thioester bond to its phosphopantetheine arm, and a 
condensation domain (C), needed for creating peptide bonds between the amino acids bound to 
neighboring modules (Figure 3). LpsA1, LpsA2 and LpsC also have a thioesterase domain (TE) on the 
C-terminal which releases the modified DLA ergopeptide product through cyclization of the tripeptide 
residue or hydrolysis of the thioester bond (Marahiel, 2016; Wallwey & Li, 2011).  
LpsB is responsible for activating DLA via recruitment of the alkaloid as a thioester, enabling 
subsequent modification by the other Lps enzymes to produce ergoamides or ergopeptines (J.-J. Chen 
et al., 2017; Ortel & Keller, 2009). LpsA1 and LpsA2 are two variants of LpsA found in C. claviceps, each 
consisting of three A-T-C modules that form a complex with LPSB and sequentially activate and 
transfer the amino acid residues to T-bound DLA. The resulting D-lysergyl tripeptide undergoes 
cyclization through binding of the nucleophilic amide nitrogen of the second residue to the carbonyl 
carbon of the C-terminus of the peptide chain, and the lysergyl peptide lactam is finally released 
(Dewick, 2009; Wallwey & Li, 2011). The P450 monooxygenase EasH then catalyzes hydroxylation of 
the alpha carbon of the first residue which then undergoes a spontaneous condensation reaction with 
the 3rd residue carbonyl group forming the cyclic ether ring of ergopeptines (Dewick, 2009; Havemann 
et al., 2014). A multitude of tripeptide ergopeptines can be produced depending on the substrate 
specificity of the LpsA homologue in the particular organism it is expressed in. LpsA1 in C. Claviceps 
strain 20.1 has shown specificity for alanine or valine for the 1st residue, proline for the 2nd and 
phenylalanine for the 3rd, resulting in formation of ergotamine, while the residue specificity of LpsA2 
in results in production of ergocryptine (Hicks et al., 2021). 
LPSB can also form a complex with LPSC, a monomodular enzyme with A-T-C domains as well a fourth 
reductase (R) domain. LPSC has specificity for alanine, which it activates, transfers and binds to its T-
domain and catalyzes amide bond formation to DLA. Using NADPH as cofactor, the R-domain catalyzes 
the reduction of the carbonyl group of alanine to a hydroxyl group, releasing the ergopeptide alcohol 
ergometrine (Hicks et al., 2021; Ortel & Keller, 2009). Claviceps paspali is primarily used for production 
of ergometrine, as it lacks LPSA, and therefore has higher ergometrine production than C. Claviceps 
(Qiao et al., 2022).

Two other genes in the EAS BGC, easO and easP, have been indicated in the production of the 
ergometrine derivative alpha-hydroxyethylamide (LAH), which can then undergo spontaneous 
conversion to lysergic acid amide (LAA). Neither have had pharmaceutical application but LAH has 
shown to have similar but weaker vasoconstrictive effects as ergotamine and contraction-inducing 
effects as ergometrine but with higher toxicity, and LAA has shown to have similar but weaker 
dopaminergic, serotonergic, and adrenergic activity as LSD, inducing hallucinations in higher doses but 
with potentially higher toxicity (W. Chen & de Wit-Bos, 2020; GLÄSSER, 1961; Schardl et al., 2013). 
However, a recent study by Qiao et al. demonstrated that the enzyme EasO, posited to be a 
monooxygenase, alone is responsible for converting ergometrine into LAH and LAA, as deletion of 
easO in C. paspari eliminated LAH and LAA production, while deletion of easP had no effect on 
biosynthesis of these ergometrine derivatives. This was confirmed by heterogously expressing lpsB, 
lpsC, easO and easP in A. nidulans, where the transformant expressing lpsB, lpsC and easO produced 
LAA when DLA was added to the media, while the transformant expressing lpsB, lpsC and easP 
accumulated ergometrine and no LAH or LAA. Interestingly, they found that although LAH and LAA 
production remained unchanged in the easP-deficient C. claviceps transformant, ergometrine 
production increased, and therefore both easP and easO were deleted in C. paspari in order to 
optimize ergometrine production. Also of note, they found that the growth of A. nidulans 
transformants in media was vastly improved when all four genes were present in the synthetic BGC 
construct, compared to when easP was absent, although the mechanism behind this remains 
unknown (Qiao et al., 2022).

5.2 ASPERGILLI AS CELL FACTORIES FOR SMS 
The terpenes, non-ribosomal peptides and polyketides encompass a vast diversity of secondary 
metabolites (SMs) produced by filamentous fungi  The enormous diversity of SMs has led to their 
application in hum an health with such drugs as the antibiotic penicillin and cholesterol lowering 
lovastin to food additives like (Campbell & Vederas, 2010; Gaynes, 2017). The continually expanding 
collection of full genome sequences of fungi has accelerated the development of genome-mining with 
the use of bioinformatics tools capable of predicting SM-related genes and gene clusters (Chavali & 
Rhee, 2018; Yaegashi et al., 2014). Although the growing amount of fungal SM-related genes and 
clusters identified in fungal genomes seems to be a resource of enormous potential, several challenges 
make it difficult to translate this to the lab. Fungal secondary metabolism is highly sensitive to a range 
of environmental queues such as light, temperature, acidity, nutrient availability and interaction with 
neighboring or host organisms and therefore reproduction in a lab environment in a native species 
can be an extremely challenging process (Avalos & Limón, 2021; Keller, 2019). As a result, 
heterologous expression of SM genes and BGCs in Aspergilli, with their comprehensive and evolving 
genetic toolboxes, including highly specific gene editing with CRISPR-CAS9 in NHEJ-deficient strains 
and simplified transformation with in vivo assembly of DNA fragments to circumvent the need for 
cloning steps in E. coli,  has become an ideal choice for discovery and production of novel SMs 
(Anyaogu & Mortensen, 2015; Jarczynska et al., 2022; Nødvig et al., 2018a) Other advantages of 
Aspergilli, which have superior SM biosynthesis than more extensively studied expression systems like 
S. cerevisiae, include a broader natural supply of necessary precursors and cofactors required for SM 
biosynthesis, specialization for SM production, and more efficient mRNA splicing due to intron 
similarity (Anyaogu & Mortensen, 2015). 
5.3 HETEROLOGOUS EXPRESSION OF THE ERGOT ALKALOID SYNTHESIS PATHWAY 
The total chemical synthesis of D-lysergic acid is an exhaustive process, with the highest yield from 
substrate to date of 12% requiring a 19-step synthesis with several toxic reagents (Kornfeld et al., 
1956; Umezaki et al., 2013). As a result, most of the world production of around 20 tons of lysergic 
acid and its derivatives are through field production of ergot-infected grains and submerged 
fermentation with various strains of Claviceps purpurea and Claviceps paspali (Hu et al., 2023; Hulvová 
et al., 2013; Qiao et al., 2022).  
There are still limitations to biotechnological methods of ergot alkaloid production. Field production 
of grains inoculated with C. purpurea is highly sensitive to climate, and suboptimal yields from 
submerged fermentation make the complicated downstream processing inefficient for meeting 
demand of these medically valuable compounds (Hu et al., 2023; Hulvová et al., 2013; Tudzynski et 
al., 2001). Furthermore, production of ergot alkaloids in Claviceps strains is dependent on formation 
of sclerotia during their life cycle, as natural alkaloid production does not occur in the sphacelial 
(conidial) stage. As a result, submerged fermentation ergot alkaloid production with Claviceps sp. 
requires a tedious selection of novel strains made through random mutagenesis capable of forming 
sclerotia-like cells under submerged fermentation. Tightly regulated environmental control including 
high osmotic pressure and high aeration rates are also necessary to facilitate the switch from the 
sphacelial stage to sclerotial cell development (Křen et al., 1994; Mai & Li, 2013; Miličić et al., 1989; 
TONOLO, 1966).   
DLA does not typically occur freely in ergot fungi in suitable concentrations for extraction, as various 
non-ribosomal peptide synthases quickly modify DLA into ergopeptine and lysergic acid amide 
derivatives. Therefore, DLA made through biotechnological methods is typically produced by alkaline 
hydrolysis of DLA amides or ergopeptines (Hoffmann, 2018; Hu et al., 2023; Tudzynski et al., 2001). 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
The use of semi-synthetic DLA derivates in medicine has led to a growing demand for a direct source 
of DLA rather than its derivatives, which has led to increased interest over the last decade in designing 
fungal cell factories capable of high production of DLAs (Anyaogu & Mortensen, 2015; Jakubczyk et 
al., 2015; Robinson & Panaccione, 2014; Ryan et al., 2013; Tudzynski et al., 2001; Wong et al., 2022; 
Yao et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2022).  
Various strategies have been employed for achieving partial and full heterologous expression of EAS 
genes and production of DLA and its precursors and derivatives in A. nidulans, A. fumigatus, S. 
cerevisiae (Robinson & Panaccione, 2014; Ryan et al., 2013; Wong et al., 2022a; Yao et al., 2022; Yu et 
al., 2022). A recent genome-mining of 5303 fungal genomes in search of EAS enzymes, led to the 
discovery of 111 EAS BGCs in the families Claviceptaceae, Aspergillaceae, Helotiaceae, 
Thermoascaceae and Arthrodermataceae, providing an abundance of EAS enzyme candidates for 
screening for optimal alkaloid production in heterologous expression systems (Yao et al., 2022). The 
first successful heterologous expression of EAS genes was performed in A. nidulans in 2013, showing 
that chanoclavine-I could be produced with the genome integration of dmaW, easF, easE and easC 
from A. fumigatus (Ryan et al., 2013). Yu et al. combined submerged fermentation of transgenic A. 
nidulans with cell-free systems (CFS) to produce the highest titer of agroclavine (1209mg/L) to date. 
Two copies of DmaW, EasF and EasE, the first three enzymes of the EAS pathway, were reconstituted 
in A. nidulans to produce prechanoclavine, which could then be fed to a CFS containing EasC, EasD, 
EasG and EasA purified from various fungal strains to produce the final product agroclavine. The 
advantage to using CFS for the final steps include the ability to easily identify bottlenecks and 
manipulate concentrations of the enzymes and cofactors involved to achieve optimal enzyme kinetics, 
as well as the elimination of the need to separate agroclavine from similar secondary metabolites 
produced by a cell factory in downstream processing (Lu, 2017; Yao et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2022).  
Although DLA production has been achieved in all heterologous EAS pathway expression systems 
tested to date, yield has been well below commercial level production, with the only recorded titer of 
1.7 mg/L achieved in S. cerevisiae (Wong et al., 2022a). Overexpression of enzymes responsible for 
precursors and cofactors such as tryptophan, DMAPP, SAM and FAD (check where abbreviations 
should come first) involved in the EAS pathway has shown to result in significant increases in DLA, but 
not as profoundly as the effect of introducing multiple copies of genes for EAS tailoring enzymes, 
which have increased some EA intermediates by nearly 800% (Wong et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2022; Yu 
et al., 2022). In S. cerevisiae expression systems, overexpression of host enzymes involved in protein 
folding has also enhanced yields of DLA and its precursors (Nielsen et al., 2014; Wong et al., 2022). 
Overexpression of heterologous and native cytochrome P450 reductase and cytochrome b5, 
responsible for CloA activation through reduction of the heme moiety of CloA, led to a 12-fold increase 
in elymoclavine, the precursor to DLA, in a recent study (Yao et al., 2022). However, DLA and DHLA 
production was extremely low, indicating that there are other factors affecting the complete 
conversion of agroclavine to DLA by CloA that need to be elucidated and optimized. Heterologous 
expression of EAS gene homologues from as many as seven different strains in a single expression 
system has also led to higher yields (Wong et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2022). 
What can be perceived as an added benefit of fungal heterologous expression systems is the potential 
of the host of secondary metabolite enzymes to modify ergot alkaloids, resulting in novel compounds. 
Robinson et al. 2014 was able to produce two novel agroclavine derivatives by expressing a foreign 
EasA isomerase in A. fumigatus, showing that the use of fungal expression systems, with their host of 
enzymes related to secondary metabolite production and modification, can be advantageous in the 
development of novel ergot alkaloids (Robinson & Panaccione, 2014).

6 PROJECT AIM 
A previous DTU masters student used in vivo assembly to insert a 7-gene construct containing native 
genes of the EAS BGC from Aspergillus homomorphus into Aspergillus nidulans. HPLC-MS analysis 
revealed several bottlenecks in the pathway and a failure to produce the final product, DLA. By 
employing various tools developed at DTU for the Aspergillus genetic toolbox, including CRISPR-Cas9 
gene targeting and in vivo assembly of DNA integration fragments, my aim is to identify which genes 
are responsible for the bottlenecks and either replace them with homologues from other EAS BGC 
containing fungi or introduce multiple copies in order to achieve and optimize DLA production. I also 
intend to optimize growth parameters and the extraction procedure in hopes of enhancing 
heterologous expression of the EAS construct in A. nidulans and extraction of ergot alkaloids 
produced.  
7.1 CRISPR CAS9 
Clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR) are a family of direct repeat DNA 
sequences found in most archaeal and many bacterial genomes . CRISPR repeats are interspaced by 
unique and identifiable viral and plasmid DNA sequences of 23 to 47 nucleotides in length, termed 
spacers, that form an archive of accessible information of foreign pathogens used for immunological 
recognition and response to invading viral and plasmid DNA (Horvath & Barrangou, 2010). CRISPR loci 
are typically flanking “CRISPR-associated” cas genes, which encode a diverse family of proteins capable 
of nuclease, polymerase, helicase and RNA-binding activity that enable each CAS protein to perform a 
specific function related to the CRISPR/Cas defense system (Hale et al., 2009; Horvath & Barrangou, 
2010; Jansen et al., 2002). These functions include the excision of foreign DNA sequences to be 
incorporated as new spacer sequences (protospacers) into a CRISPR locus, the cleavage of transcribed 
pre-crRNAs into crRNAs (CRISPR RNAs) and the crRNA-guided targeting and cleavage of 
complementary sequences in foreign plasmids and viral DNA (Hale et al., 2009; van der Oost et al., 
2009) 
 
There are three major CRISPR/Cas systemF types that detect, process and inactivate foreign DNA in 
different ways (Makarova et al., 2011). The endonuclease Cas9 used in this project belongs to Type II 
systems, in which Cas9 facilitates the binding of trans-encoded small RNAs (tracrRNAs) to the 
complementary repeat sequence of pre-crRNA, resulting in cleavage within the complementary 
sequences via RNase III in the presence of Cas9 and maturation of the crRNA (Deltcheva et al., 2011). 
The Cas9-tracrRNA-crRNA is guided by the mature crRNA to its complementary sequence in foreign 
DNA with high precision. The proto-spacer-adjacent motif (PAM), 2-5bp in length and unique to each 
Cas9 enzyme, is located in juxtaposition to the complementary target sequence, and its recognition 
by the Cas9 complex initiates blunt dsDNA cleavage upstream of the PAM sequence (Gleditzsch et al., 
2019; Jinek et al., 2012). The DSB is predominately repaired through the efficient error-prone non-
homologous end joining (NHEJ) pathway and can also be repaired with high accuracy but lower 
efficiency through the homologous recombination (HR) pathway if a homologous repair template is 
available (H. H. Y. Chang et al., 2017; Ding et al., 2019). 
 
Streptococcus pyogenes Cas9 was used in this project and has been optimized for strain 
engineering in A. nidulans and other Aspergillus species at DTU Bioengineering (Nødvig et al., 
2015, 2018a). Using a chimeric single guide RNA (sgRNA) consisting of a scaffold gRNA and a 
==PAGE:19==
18 
 
20-nucleotide protospacer sequence complementary to its target site, Cas9 can be directed 
with high specificity to induce DSBs in effectively any site of the fungal genome containing the 
PAM sequence NGG or NAG albeit with lower efficiency (Nødvig et al., 2015). 
 
All CRISPR vectors designed in this project were assembled with the pAC331 cloning vector 
backbone, designed at DTU bioengineering, containing a USER-cassette with restriction sites 
for linearization and assembly with PCR fragments. pAC331 also contains a ampR gene for 
selection of transformed E. coli in ampicillin-treated media, an E. coli origin of replication (ORI) 
for cloning purposes, an AMA1 sequence enabling plasmid replication in A. nidulans, argB 
allowing selection of  transformants auxotrophic for arginine and cas9 controlled by the 
strong TEF promoter (PTEF) and terminator (TTEF) (Aleksenko & Clutterbuck, 1997; Nødvig et 
al., 2018a)(Figure 5). The sgRNA-containing splicing cassette assembled with the pFC331 
backbone consists of sgRNA sequences flanked by identical glycine tRNA sequences and is 
controlled by the polymerase III U3 promoter and U3 terminator from A. fumigatus. The tRNA 
and sgRNA scaffold section form hairpin loops after transcription, thus stabilizing the pre-RNA 
transcript. Release of the sgRNA is controlled by cleavage of the tRNA-sgRNA junctions by 
endogenous RnaseP and RnaseZ (Figure 4 )(Nødvig et al., 2018b; Xie et al., 2015).  
 
 
For constructing dual protospacer CRISPR-CAS9 plasmids in this project for targeting two DSB sites, 
three fragments were amplified with PCR using pAC902 as template, which contains a single 
protospacer tRNA-based splicing cassette regulated by the A. fumigatus U3 Promoter and U3 
Terminator. The three amplified fragments consisting of a U3-promoter:tRNA fragment, tRNA:sgRNA 
fragment  and tRNA:sgRNA:U3-terminator fragment were amplified using primers with USER tails 
(Figure 5). The A.fumU3-promoter forward primer and A.fumU3-terminator reverse primer were 
available in the DTU Bioengineering collection, and their USER tails are complementary to the USER 
tails of the ends of predigested pFC331 plasmid. USER tails were designed were designed to form 20nt 
protospacer sequences upon USER tail hybridization and assembly of the three DNA fragments.

7.2 USER-CLONING 
Insertion of the tRNA-based sgRNA splicing-cassette into the AMA1-Cas9 plasmid backbone (pAC331) 
was performed with USER fusion. Uracil excision-based cloning, termed USER-cloning, utilizes Uracil-
Specific Excision Reagent (USER™) for the efficient assembly of several PCR fragments into a 
predigested USER-compatible cloning vector. To be USER cloning-compatible, a vector must have an 
inserted USER cassette, which contains a restriction site for the endonuclease PacI and two restriction 
sites for the nicking-based enzyme Nt.BvCI flanking each side (Figure 6). After linearization with PacI, 
additional digestion with Nt.BvCI results in the formation of 8 nucleotide single stranded 3’ overhangs 
of the linearized vector (Nour-Eldin et al., 2006, 2010). USER primer tails are designed to include a 
uracil base roughly 8 nucleotides from each 5’ end of the PCR fragments to be assembled into a pre-
digested vector (Salomonsen et al., 2014) These uracil bases are excised through the action of uracil 
DNA glycosidase and DNA glycosylase-lyase endo VIII contained in the USER™ enzyme mix, resulting 
in diffusion of the remaining 5’ tail and the creation of 8 nucleotide single stranded tails (Geu-Flores 
et al., 2007; Salomonsen et al., 2014). Hybridization of the complementary single stranded tails of the 
PCR fragments and pre-digested vector can occur during a short incubation period at 25°C, resulting 
in a circular plasmid that can be transformed into E. coli (Geu-Flores et al., 2007).

7.3 REPAIRING THE 7-GENE EAS BGC CONSTRUCT IN NID2742 
Previous LC-MS analysis of NID2742 by the DTU masters student that created the strain showed the 
presence of Me-DMAT, chanoclavine-I, festuclavine and the festuclavine derivatives dihydrolysergic 
acid (DHLA) and dihydroergosine, indicating that the EasA homologue from A. homomorphus, 
heterologously expressed in the 7-gene EAS construct of NID2742, is the reductase isoform and driving 
conversion of chanoclavine-I aldehyde to festuclavine rather than the wanted DLA precursor 
agroclavine. As discussed earlier, the 176F of the well characterized isomerase CpurEasA has shown 
to likely be a key residue for its isomerase function, whereas a substitution to 176Y, occurring in known 
reductase EasA isoforms such as that of A. fumigatus, is likely partly responsible for the switch to the 
reductase isoform. An MSA of aa-sequences between AhomEasA1 and CpurEasA revealed a 175F in 
the AhomEasA sequence corresponding to the 176F position of CpurEasA, indicating likely isomerase 
activity (Figure 7).

the abbreviated EAS enzyme name that the enzyme of note is homologous to.

7.4 CHOOSING STRAINS FOR HOMOLOGUES  
C. purpurea ECC93 was the only strain available from the DTU IBT strain collection, but no full genome 
sequence exists for this strain. Therefore, all BLASTp queries for C. purpurea EAS enzyme homologues 
were done with C. purpurea 20.1 amino acid sequences obtained from Uniprot.org. MSAs of EAS 
homologues from other C. purpurea strains showed very few mismatches between homologues for 
both DNA and protein sequences, and therefore PCR amplification of EAS genes from C. purpurea 
ECC93 was not expected to be problematic. Protein BLAST searches of C. purpurea protein sequences 
on Uniprot.org  for DmaW, EasF, EasE, EasC, EasD, EasA and CloA were performed to generate a list of 
20 candidate fungal strains that each contained several homologues in a range from low to high 
sequence identities. This list was given to the supervisor of this project Jakob Hoof and he provided 
seven strains available from the DTU IBT collection: Aspergillus leporis IBT 35243, Aspergillus 
hiratsukae IBT 14987, Aspergillus coremiiformis IBT 21944, Aspergillus turcosus IBT 27921, 
Trichoderma arundinaceum IBT 40842, Talaromyces atroroseus IBT 1118 and Penicillium expansum 
IBT 34017.  
7.5  SELECTING HOMOLOGUES 
Initially, a BLASTp search using the C. purpurea 20.1 EAS protein sequence of interest was performed 
on JGI Mycocosm against the full genome of each strain, which provided a host of candidate 
homologues with varying sequence identities. Several factors were taken into consideration and 
certain criteria upheld when selecting homologues. All chosen homologues should have the same 
predicted function by Interpro and subcellular localization predicted by DeepLoc2.0. Homologues 
should also have high secondary and tertiary structural homology as determined by the Pymol 
superposition of 3D Alphafold2 models generated with Colabfold, with a cut-off of 1Å for the Root-
==PAGE:24==
23 
 
Mean-Square-Deviation (RMSD) of superimposed residues. In most cases the top BLAST hit with the 
highest sequence identity was used to increase the likelihood that the homologue could accept the 
required substrate; however, lower sequence identity hits were chosen in some cases to investigate 
whether biological function could be maintained with high structural homology and low sequence 
identity and potentially lead to greater production of their respective reaction products or the 
production of novel ergot alkaloids. Additionally, the Dali Server was used to compare generated 
Alphafold2 models with crystal structures in the PDB database, to find structural homologues with 
similar ligands that could be used to predict substrate-binding active site residues of each homologue. 
Predicted substrate-binding residues were highlighted in MSAs generated with Clustal Omega, to get 
a clear picture of the similarity and differences between the active sites of each homologue and the 
C. purpurea 20.1 reference sequence.

homologue with the EasA reductase-affiliated tyrosine residue should lead to festuclavine synthesis. 
The obvious way to rigorously challenge this theory would be to perform a F176Y point mutation of 
the known EasA isomerase CpurEasA to see if it switches to reductase activity, but this project was 
focused on experimenting with homologues from other strains rather than manipulating sequences 
of the well characterized EAS BGC of Claviceps purpurea. Most of the active site residues had high 
conservation across selected EasA homologues, with 27T, 102Q, 171H, and 352Y of CpurEasA 
conserved in all homologues and 70F, 173A, 223K being the least conserved, indicating that these 
variable active site residues could have a greater impact on catalytic specificity and efficiency than the 
other predicted active site residues, aside from the intentionally conserved 176F. Due to the moderate 
sequence identity, predicted active side residue variability and very high structural homology (RMSD 
< 0.600) of the selected homologues, they were chosen to make seven unique EasA homologue strains 
with the NID2742 background.

7.7 SELECTING PROMOTERS 
The novel use of heterologous expression of bidirectional histone promoters (Ph4h3) from Aspergilli 
sp. was first published 2019 (Rendsvig et al., 2019), showing that Ph4h3 from phylogenetically distant 
sections of the genus Aspergillus had the same expression efficiency to native Ph4h3Anid promoters 
when heterologously expressed in A. nidulans, suggesting high conservation of Ph4h3 functionality 
when heterologously expressed across most members of Aspergillus. Two bidirectional histone 
promoters that have not been used in published literature were selected from A. turcocus and A. 
leporis, as these strains were part of the available strain library for EAS homologue amplification in 
this project. Promoter sequences were determined by a BLAST search of the h4 sequence from A. 
clavatus in JGI Mycocosm to locate the h4 and h3 genes in the genome browser. The Ph4h3 including 
5-UTRs of the h4 and h3 was identified as the untranslated sequence in between the start codons of

Colabfold, available open source through Github and google colab, provided structure Alphafold2 3D

combine it with structural information from PDB templates to generate 3D protein models with high 
confidence (Mirdita et al., 2022). Amino acid sequences were simply pasted to the query search, 
with template mode “on”, and run with default settings. Only models with high confidence were 
selected.

Pymol

project and predicting active site residues. Pymol was also used for taking high quality pictures of the 
protein structures with bound ligands.

Dali server 
The Dali server was used to compare Alphafold models with crystal structures of the PDB database 
with structural homology, in order to find crystal structures of homologues with bound ligand. 
Superimposition of the models allowed for prediction of the active site residues in the superimposed 
EAS homologue by selecting residues within 5Å of the bound ligand.

Agilent Masshunter Qualitative Analysis was used to analyze LC-MS data of plug extracts. Extracted

8.3 WET LAB METHODS 
8.3.1 
gDNA Extraction 
The extraction of genomic DNA (gDNA) was necessary in order to obtain PCR templates from different 
strains containing ergot alkaloid gene homologues as well as checking transformants for successful 
integration of a gene or an in-vivo-assembled construct. This protocol was optimized during this 
project in order to obtain the highest quality and concentration of gDNA possible. A good template is 
critical for achieving strong amplification of PCR products, which are required in large amounts for 
efficient in-vivo assembly. In order to extract gDNA, a small amount of tissue (1-2cm diameter) is 
harvested from a strain of interest grown on either solid or liquid media. If tissue is harvested from 
solid media, then one must be careful to keep the amount of agar collected along with the tissue to a 
minimum. YPD is a good choice for media, as it leads to robust growth with little to no pigmentation 
and sporulation, which can have an inhibitory effect on PCR reactions. Tissue from liquid cultures is 
separated from media in a miracloth filter funnel and washed with MQ, providing a very clean tissue 
sample, leading to higher quality gDNA extraction than with solid media. It is best to collect tissue 
samples after no more than 2-3 days of incubation, while fungi are in active growth phase (*check). 
Harvested tissue is placed in a 1.5ml screw-top FastPrep tube and 200µl of 0.5-1mm diameter glass 
beads are added along with 500µl of breaking buffer (2% Triton X-100, 1% SDS, 100mM NaCl, 1mM 
EDTA, 200mM LiAc). Samples are then placed in the FastPrep machine and run at speed 6 for 40 
seconds. Thereafter samples are centrifuged for 3 min at max speed, and 150ul of supernatant is 
transferred to a new 1.5ml Eppendorf tube. 15µl of 5M NaCl solution and 400µl of ice-cold 96% 
ethanol are added, and the tube is inverted several times to mix before putting in the -20°C freezer 
for at least 1hr or overnight for maximum DNA precipitation. Samples are then centrifuged at max 
speed for 20min (preferably at 4, after which supernatant is decanted and tube with precipitated DNA 
pellet is dried on a 55°C heating block for 5-10min. 200µl of MQ water is added and again incubated 
for min 10 minutes to allow DNA to redissolve. The DNA solution is then resuspended to fully dissolve 
and centrifuged at max speed for 3 min. 150µl of supernatant is transferred to a new 1.5µl Eppendorf 
tube. The final product is a highly concentrated DNA solution and therefore various dilutions are 
prepared to determine the optimal amount of DNA to add to a PCR reaction. Anywhere from 20-300ng 
of gDNA template was used per 50µl PCR reaction in order to achieve substantial amplifications.

8.3.2 
Polymerase chain reaction  
PCR was used extensively in the project in order to amplify DNA fragments for USER cloning, IVA, and 
checking transformants for successful integration. Reaction volumes of 15-50µl, with smaller volumes 
used for troubleshooting difficult PCRs. When extracted gDNA template or purified plasmid template 
were used, then 1min initial denaturation at 98°C was used, whereas tissue PCR required 10min initial 
denaturation at 98°C in order to liberate gDNA template from the cells. Annealing temperatures for 
primers were predicted using the IDT OligoAnalyzer™ tool. Typically touch-down PCR was used and 
through trial and error it was determined that a starting temperature of 10-12°C higher than the 
calculated primer melting temperature from the IDT OligoAnalyzer™ tool with a -0.5°C drop in 
annealing temperature per cycle was optimal for strong and specific bands. Gradient PCR of 8 replicate 
15µl reactions from 50-70°C was used to determine the ideal annealing temperature when 
amplification was not successful with touch-down PCR. PCR reactions were performed using the BIO-
RAD T-100™ and VWR® Doppio Gradient thermal cyclers.

Gel-electrophoresis 
Gel-electrophoresis was used to verify and/or purify successful amplification of DNA fragments from

SYBRSafe (1X) and with the appropriate well volume size and allowed to cool and solidify for 25min.

8.3.5 
USER-fusion and E. coli transformation 
All CRISPR plasmids were assembled with the USER-fusion method. The self-replicating AMA1 and 
argB-containing plasmid backbone pFC331 is first linearized by digestion with PacI restriction enzyme 
overnight at 37°C, followed by a second digestion with the nicking endouclease Nt.BbvCI and PacI for 
2 hours at 37°C and finally 30min at 80°C for enzyme inactivation (see figure X for reaction mixture). 
The DNA fragments with user tails are amplified with PCR using pAC902, which contains the template 
for the A. fumigatus U3-promoter/terminator, A.nidulans glycin tRNA and gRNA backbone. 0.5µl (10-
20ng) of the linearized pFC331 is added to the USER fusion reaction mixture together with gel-purified 
DNA fragments in a 5:1 DNA:vector molar ratio. Keeping the total reaction volume to 10µl, 0.5µl 
Cutsmart buffer and 1µl USER enzyme are added. A negative control is also made without DNA 
fragments to determine the efficiency of transformation. The USER-fusion reaction mixture and 
negative control are incubated at 37°C for 25-45min and then at room temp for 15-25min. Competent 
DH5α E. coli cells are taken from the -80°C freezer and allowed to thaw for 10min while incubation of 
the USER-fusion reaction proceeds. The USER-fusion product is then cooled on ice before adding to 
50µl DH5α cells in a 1.5ml Eppendorf tube and incubated on ice for 5-15min. The cells are then heat-
shocked at 42°C for 1min and subsequently placed on ice for 5-15min before plate-spreading on LB-
ampicillin agar plates. Transformed DH5α cells are incubated overnight at 37°C and checked the 
following day; a transformation plate with several colonies and a negative control with few to no 
colonies is indicative of a successful and efficient transformation. Colony PCR is performed to confirm 
4-8 colonies using the U3-promoter/terminator forward and reverse primers, of which 1-2 confirmed 
colonies are used to inoculate 5ml liquid LB-Amp cultures and incubated overnight at 37°C.

Restriction enzyme analysis of plasmids 
Before sequencing, assembled CRISPR plasmids were digested with BspEI restriction enzyme to check 
for insertion of the USER-fusion assembled sgRNA-tRNA construct. The pFC331 backbone plasmid 
used for CRISPR plasmid assembly was also digested as a control to compare with the assembled 
CRISPR plasmid. Keeping the total reaction volume to 10ul, 100-700ng plasmid DNA is combined with 
0.5µl BspEI, 1ul Buffer(NEB 3.1) and MQ. The digestion reaction mixture is incubated for 1 hour at 
37°C and then run on an agrose gel with loading buffer. The band pattern can then be compared with 
a reference to determine whether plasmid assembly was successful, comparing with the backbone 
plasmid for reference as well. 
8.3.9 
Protoplastation 
Protoplastation of background strains is a necessary step in order to prepare fungal cells that are 
capable of taking up extracellular DNA and plasmids, in a state termed “competency.” A strain of 
interest is inoculated on a MM plate with arginine supplement and given 5-7 days to cover the media 
surface and sporulate. In the meantime, 100ml sterile liquid YPD media with arginine supplement is 
prepared. Spores are harvested by pipetting 3ml of the sterile liquid YPD media onto the colony and 
dragging a Drigalski spatula (dipped in 96% ethanol and flame sterilized) over the surface to release 
spores into suspension. 2ml of the spore suspension is then transferred with a pipette to a 500ml 
shake flask containing the remaining liquid YPD media and incubated at 37°C at 150rpm for 24hrs. 
Cells are separated from media by filtering the culture through a funnel lined with Miracloth, washed 
with ADB and pressed with a flame-sterilized spoon to remove excess liquid. Roughly 5ml of biomass 
is transferred to a sterile 50ml Falcon tube and 20ml of 40mg/ml Glucanex/ADB solution is added. A 
single 100ml liquid culture of Aspergillus nidulans typically provides 10ml of biomass, and therefore 
two Falcon tubes are prepared for digestion. The lids are tightly closed, and the tubes are fixed with 
tape horizontally in the shake incubator at 30°C and 150rpm for 2.5-3hrs. The Glucanex digested cells 
are then passed through a new Miracloth-lined funnel into new sterile 50ml Falcon tubes, trapping 
unwanted cell debris in the filter and allowing protoplasts to pass through. ADB is then added to bring 
the volume up to 40ml and weighed to make sure that the tubes are within 0.3g in mass for later 
centrifugation and adjusted accordingly with ADB. Holding each tube at a slant, 5ml of 1:1 (v/v) 
ATB:MQ solution slowly pipetted down the inner side of the Falcon tube to form an overlay. A good 
overlay is visible as a clear transparent phase over the hazier ADB/protoplast solution. The tubes are 
transferred carefully to the centrifuge and spun at 3000g for 11min with acceleration 9 and 
deceleration 4. Working carefully to not disrupt the halo of protoplasts formed between the two 
phases, the tubes are carried back to the LAF bench for harvesting. Protoplasts are collected with a 
1000µl pipette and transferred to a new 50ml falcon tube and filled up to 40ml with ATB. Tubes are 
weighed again to make sure they are balanced properly for a subsequent centrifugation step at 3000g 
for 11min with acceleration 9 and deceleration 9. Supernatant is discarded and the pelleted 
protoplasts are resuspended in 1ml ATB, which is used to make 500µl protoplast aliquots in sterile 
1.5ml Eppendorf tubes and placed in the -80°C freezer.  
8.3.10 Transformation 
Protoplasts for transformation are taken from the -80°C freezer and put on ice for 10-15min to thaw. 
In the meantime, DNA fragments and CRISPR vector are added to 1.5ml tubes for each respective 
transformation construct and mixed gently by resuspension to improve assembly kinetics upon 
transformation. 50-75µl protoplasts are added and mixed gently with the DNA fragments and CRISPR 
vector. The volume of protoplasts added is adjusted in order to prevent the volume of DNA and CRISPR 
vector solutions under 25% of the total protoplast/DNA volume, and the quantity of CRISPR vector 
used and volume of PCT and ATB added in later steps is also adjusted proportionately. For a mixture 
==PAGE:38==
37 
 
of 50µl protoplasts and ≤ 16µl DNA-vector components, 750ng CRISPR vector is used and 150µl of PCT 
is added to the mixture and gently mixed by stirring. Additionally, a negative control with no plasmid 
or DNA repair templates, a positive control with 750ng of the argB-containing and self-replicating 
AMA1 pFC331 plasmid backbone and a TAPE (Tool to Assess Protospacer Efficiency) control with 
750ng CRISPR target vector. After incubation at room temperature for 5-10min, 250µl ATB is added 
and mixed gently by resuspension with the 1000µl tip and the total transformation mixture is 
transferred to a TM-agar plate. A Drigalski spatula is dipped in ethanol and flame-sterilized before 
spreading the transformation mixture to cover the entire media surface. Plates are incubated at 37°C 
for 3-7 days until sporulating colonies appear.  
8.3.11 Spore dissection 
Spore dissection is a technique used to isolate single spores that are used to cultivate and select 
colonies that are monokaryon for an introduced mutation, resulting in a “pure” mutant strain. 1-3 
colonies were selected for spore dissection from each transformation plate. Using a pipette with a 
200µl pipette tip, 20µl of MQ was used to resuspend and collect spores from each colony and 
transferred to a YPD plate with arginine supplement added. Using a straight line drawn across one 
third of the YPD plate as a guide, the spore suspension is dripped on one end of the plate and run 
along the line by tilting the plate in the appropriate direction. The plate is then inverted, lid removed, 
and placed on the loading dock of the Nikon Eclipse 50 spore dissection microscope. The 10X/0.25 
microscope lens is used to focus on the tip of the fitted fiberoptic needle as it makes contact with the 
surface of the agar media, bringing spores into focus as well. The needle tip is then maneuvered to 
pick up single spores through adhesive forces of the water molecules surrounding the spore and 
moved to the opposing 1/3 of the YPD surface in 12mm increments in a linear fashion (see figure x). 
A total of 5 spores are dissected and allowed to incubate for 2-3 days at 37 degrees Celsius until 
colonies of 10-15mm in diameter are formed, after which tissue from 1-2 colonies can be harvested 
for gDNA extraction or tissue-PCR. If there was not sufficient time for performing spore dissection, 
then a sterile toothpick was used to dab each transformant colony of interest and re-streaked on 
individual MM+Arg plates for each respective colony. After 2-3 days of incubation at 37°C, two 
colonies from each re-streaked plate were chosen to inoculate on a master MM+Arg plate. After 2-3 
days of incubation at 37°C and before sporulation occurs, colony PCR was performed from colonies 
on the master plate with either collected tissue or extracted gDNA to check for successful 
transformation.  
8.3.12 Sequencing 
Sanger sequencing by Eurofins Genomics was used to verify insertion of USER-cloned tRNA and guide 
RNA fragments in purified CRISPR plasmids and IVA constructs in transformed A. nidulans mutants. 
2.5µl of forward or reverse primer, 5µl of 50-100ng/µl purified plasmid or 10ng/µl DNA fragment, 
were added to the Mix2Seq sequencing tubes and filled up to 10µl with MQ. Tubes were sent to 
Eurofins Genomics and results were evaluated with Benchling multiple sequence alignment tool with 
respective reference sequences.  
8.3.13 Plug Extraction  
Plug extraction was performed on strains grown for 6 days at 37°C. 5 plugs (1 center, 2 peripheral, and 
2 between) were taken with plastic straws and added to 1.5ml FastPrep screw-cap tubes. 200µl 0.2-
0.5 mm small glass beads were added along with 800µl isopropanol-ethyl acetate (1:3, v:v and 1% 
formic acid) and 20µl chloramphenicol (500µg/ml in ethanol) as an internal standard and set in 
FastPrep machine at speed 6 for two 45sec cycles. Homogenized samples then underwent 
ultrasonication for 60minutes and subsequently spun down at max speed for 3 minutes to pellet the 
biomass/agar. 500µl of supernatant was transferred to a new 150µl Eppendorf tube and placed in the 
==PAGE:39==
38 
 
nitrogen evaporator at 40°C until samples have dried. After drying, 300µl 100% methanol is added and 
the sample is vortexed before a second round of ultrasonication for 20min to fully redissolve extracts. 
Samples are then spun down at max speed for 10minutes for purification, and 150µl of supernatant is 
carefully pipetted into V-shaped HPLC vials and sent to the DTU Metabolomics Department in Building 
221 for high resolution LC-MS analysis by the Gandalf Agilente QTOF machine in R115 using the 15-
minute method with positive ionization.

9.1 MEDIA AND EXTRACTION SOLVENT OPTIMIZATION 
Before attempting to repair the 7-gene construct of NID2742, a selection of growth medias were 
tested to optimize ergot alkaloid production and investigate whether ergot alkaloids previously 
undetected may be produced under a different nutrient composition, as previous LC-MS analysis of 
NID2742 was from cultures grown on MM cultures. Two extraction solvents were tested, 100% 
methanol (MeOH) and isopropanol:ethyl-acetate (1:3, v:v) with 1% formic acid (IPA:EA(1:3)1%FA), to 
see if the extraction solvent was to blame for the absence of peaks for chanoclavine-aldehyde, 
agroclavine DLA and other possible ergot alkaloids. MeOH was chosen for simplicity and due to 
successful extraction of DLA derivatives in the literature ((EURL-MP), 2020; Leadmon et al., 2020).  and 
IPA:EA(1:3)1%FA is the standard alkaloid extraction solvent used in DTU bioengineering. 6 well plates 
with 6 different complex agar media (MEAox, YES, DG18, OAT, CYA and PDA) in 5ml volumes were 
prepared for each strain which included: NID2741  containing a 6 gene EAS BGC construct in IS5,  
NID2742 containing the same six EAS genes of NID2741 with the addition of easCAhom, controlled by a 
PTEF promoter, HOM1, a wild type A. homomorphus strain from the IBT collection containing the EAS 
BGC from which genes of the synthetic EAS BGC constructs in NID2741 and NID2742 were sourced, 
and the parent strain of NID2741 and NID2742, NID2679 for reference. The six media types were 
chosen because they are used for screening fungal isolates for the SABS project at DTU bioengineering. 
There were no significant differences in coloration or morphology between NID strains after six days 
incubation at 37°C in six well plates and therefore only NID2742 and HOM1 plates are shown in the 
figure below (Figure 16Figure 1). Samples were not done in replicates as funding was not available due 
to the high cost of running LC-MS analysis, and therefore the findings of this experiment served as a 
rough estimate of which media and solvent type would be best for ergot alkaloid production and 
extraction. Extracted ion chromatogram (EIC) peaks for chloramphenicol were highly consistent across 
samples, allowing interpretation of the results by comparing EIC peak sizes.

9.2 EASA AND EASD HOMOLOGUE INSERTIONS 
9.2.1 
EasA homologue gene fragment prep  
Amplification of sufficient quantities of CpurEasA and the chosen homologues from their respective 
strains proved to be an exceedingly difficult task, and a major bottleneck in the progress of this project. 
This was likely in part due to primer dimer formation caused by the 30-45bp tails for homologous 
recombination and gene integration. As primer design was restricted to the start and end of each 
homologue coding sequence, there were also primers with low GC content (<40%) and repeating 
nucleotides. Best effort was made to ensure that the 3’ end of each primer had the highest GC content 
possible to improve annealing. In the end, what improved PCR amplification of genes from gDNA 
template the most was through optimizing the gDNA extraction for higher concentration and quality, 
as described in the Methods section. Generally high amounts of gDNA of ≥ 100ng per 50µl PCR reaction 
resulted in the strongest amplification. Gradient PCR was also helpful in some instances to determine 
the ideal starting temperature for touch-down PCR. After applying these strategies all homologue 
genes were successfully amplified purified in sufficient quantities (0.8 pmol) for transformation. 
Despite improvements in PCR amplification of DNA fragments from gDNA, amplification of easATaru 
continued to be challenging and inconsistent, and therefore only 0.5pmol was used for transformation 
with this homologue.

Insertion of easACpur was the first successful transformation, resulting in the strain NID2905. Due to 
the difficulty in amplifying the required quantities of gene PCR-fragments for transformation, column 
purification was tested to determine whether it was sufficient for transformation. Greater yields of 
purified PCR product can be obtained with column purification than with gel purification and requires 
fewer steps. Two transformations were performed, one with column-purified easACpur and one with 
gel-purified easACpur to compare transformation efficiency. The result of the transformation on 
arginine-deficient TM agar plates showed no colonies in the negative control with NID2742 protoplasts 
only, and only a single tiny weak colony showing up on the edge of the pAC2064-transformed NID2742 
control, thus confirming NID2742 arginine-auxotrophy and high efficiency of pAC2064 in making DSBs 
in the NHEJ-deficient nkuAΔ NID2742 (Figure 23). Both easACpur purification methods initially appeared 
successful, with many colonies on each transformation plate.  However, colony PCR of extracted gDNA 
from six spore-dissected colonies from each plate with check primers upstream and downstream of 
the insertion site revealed unusually short bands under 200bp in length for the column-purified-
easACpur transformants, indicating that primer heterodimers were used as repair template and 
outcompeted CpurEasA for HR repair of the DSBs (Figure 24). Three out of six gel-purified- easACpur 
transformant colonies showed successful integration with bands at the expected length of 1251bp, 
compared to the 1899bp length for easAAhom in the NID2742 wild-type control, and was confirmed by 
Sanger sequencing (Appx 13.2.2). The large difference in gene lengths was due to a large 669bp intron 
in easAAhom, and made checking successful integration of easACpur and homologues with colony PCR 
simple. In order to avoid potential heterodimer integration in the insertion site when using column-
purified DNA-fragments for transformation, gel-purification was used for preparing all future DNA-
fragments for transformation.

9.3.1 
EasD repeat 
For the new EasD insertions primers were ordered for amplifying Ph4h3Alep bioblocks that included the 
5’UTRs of h3 and h4, in hopes that the corrected promoter would enable full expression of the 
regulated genes. As a result of the limited time available to complete the planned transformations for 
this project, EasD transformations were performed with NID2905 as the parent strain, in which easA-
Ahom was substituted with easACpur. Although agroclavine or any other new ergot alkaloids were not 
identified in the LC-MS analysis of NID2905, the fact that CpurEasA is a well characterized isomerase 
and should increase the likelihood of making the desired switch from festuclavine synthesis to 
agroclavine synthesis made it a more promising strain to conduct further EAS pathway construction 
on. easDCpur was also inserted in NID2742, to compare the impact of easAAhom and easACpur in the EAS 
pathway containing easDCpur as well. 
As a result of time constraints, easDTaru was dropped from the second round of easD transformations, 
which required ordering new forward primers with tails homologous to the  5’UTR of Ph4h3Alep. This 
homologue was chosen to drop as it was the most difficult to amplify in sufficient quantities in the 
previous experiment. All EasD homologues were amplified successfully with the new forward primers, 
as well as the corrected Ph4h3Alep (Figure 31). To be more efficient with time, only one spore dissected 
colony from each of three selected colonies for each EasD homologue transformation were confirmed 
with check primers. Transformation efficiency was high, with PCR amplification of integration check 
amplicons from extracted gDNA revealing positive insertion of EasD homologue genes for all spore 
dissected colonies, with no heterokaryons. Insertions were validated by Sanger sequencing (Appx 
13.2.4), with results confirming integration in the correct locus and successful assembly with Ph4h3Alep, 
which showed no mismatches except for a single point mutation in the 5’UTR of Ph4h3Alep regulating 
easCAhom in the easDAtur mutant NID2922. No mismatches were found in easDAlep, easDAhir.  The same 
14 mismatches observed in the first easDCpur mutants made with 5’UTR-lacking Ph4h3 
(NID2921(easDCpur) and NID2915(easACpur + easDCpur)) were found in the sequencing results for the new 
easDCpur mutants on a NID2905 background (NID2927) and NID2742 background (NID2928). 172 
mismatches were found in easDAtur, of which 163 of which were identical to those found in the 
previous easDAtur mutant created (NID2922).

10 DISCUSSION 
The incubation time for HOM1 of 6 days proved sufficient, as several ergot alkaloids were identified 
with large EIC peaks indicating high production and confirmed previous findings that 6 days of growth 
on solid media can lead to ergometrine, LAA and LAH production in A. homomorphus (Jones et al., 
2021). The results of LC-MS analysis of the media and solvent optimization experiments gave 
conflicting results. Analysis of extracts from HOM1 grown on YES media resulted in much larger peaks 
for elymoclavine, ergometrine, agroclavine, N-Me-DMAT and fumigaclavine A while growth on CYA 
media lead to far greater peak sizes for DLA, LAA, LAH, and chanoclavine-I; although these differences 
in effects of media on alkaloid production were generally small when comparing samples extracted 
with IPA:EA(1:3)1%FA (except for agroclavine and N-Me-DMAT), the effect sizes were much larger 
when comparing solvent types, with the effect of CYA on increasing DLA,LAA, LAH and chanoclavine-I 
production only becoming clear when MeOH was used as an extraction solvent, and the effect of YES 
on increasing elymoclavine and ergometrine production becoming much greater when MeOH was 
used as an extraction solvent. The primary difference between CYA and YES media and the other 
media used is the sucrose and yeast extract concentration, both of which are much greater in YES 
media, and neither of which are present in OAT, PDA, MEAox or DG18. The generally larger peaks 
obtained for the identified ergot alkaloids in HOM1 when CYA and YES was used as growth substrate 
indicate that nitrogen and carbon in the form of yeast and sucrose may induce greater ergot alkaloid 
biosynthesis; this finding is supported by studies on carbon and nitrogen source optimization for ergot 
alkaloid production in the closely related fungi Penicillium commune and P. citrinum, in which highest 
total ergot alkaloid yields were achieved when yeast extract and sucrose were used as nitrogen and 
carbon sources (Shahid et al., 2015; Shahid & Nadeem, 2015). The high extraction of LAA and LAH with 
100% MeOH is supported by a recent study investigating ergot alkaloid production in several 
Metarhizium species, in which 100% methanol extracts of fungal biomass showed high abundance of 
LAA and LAH (Leadmon et al., 2020); however, no previous study has compared 100% MeOH with 
IPA:EA(1:3)1%FA, as MeOH and other solvents are typically diluted in water with the addition of an 
acid or base such as H3PO4 or NH3 to increase solubility of the basic ergot alkaloids (Holderied et al., 
2019; Oliveira Gonçalves et al., 2017; Ware et al., 2000). This shows that the use of MeOH alone can 
achieve high ergot alkaloid extraction in A. nidulans. The identification of several epimers for 
chanoclavine-I, LAH, and ergometrine is consistent with the literature, as ergot alkaloids are known to 
epimerise more easily when stored in protic solvents such as MeOH and IPA:EA, with greater 
epimerization in alkaline or acidic storage solutions (Hafner et al., 2008; Köppen et al., 2013; Smith & 
Shappell, 2002); extracts were often stored at -20 C for weeks before analysis due to high demand for 
or technical issues with available LC-MS analysis instruments, which could have increased the 
likelihood of epimerization. Although it was not possible to conclude whether the peak identified as 
festuclavine was one of its isomers costaclavine, pyroclavine or 6,7-secoagroclavine, the identification 
of DHLA and a fumigaclavine A-like compound makes it more likely that it is festuclavine, which is a 
precursor to both compounds. Festuclavine and its derivatives have not been identified in published 
literature in A. homomorphus, which is known to produce high amounts of the D-lysergic acid branch 
derivatives such as LAA and LAH, but it is possible that A. homomorphus contains both an isomerase 
and reductase isoform of easA, or that easAAhom has weak reductase activity under certain conditions 
resulting in a small production of festuclavine. The morning glory endophyte Periglandula sp. are 
known to contain both isoforms of easA and produce both agroclavine and festuclavine derivatives 
(Panaccione, 2023). 
Analysis of the results of growth media and extraction solvent optimization in NID2741 and NID2742 
confirmed many of the ergot alkaloid compounds found previously in these strains and also revealed 
 
==PAGE:60==
59 
 
a few alkaloids that had not been detected prior. The incubation time of 6 days for plug extraction 
proved sufficient with LC-MS results indicating heterologous production of several ergot alkaloids in 
NID2741 and NID2742, supporting previous findings that the high heterologous expression of genes 
associated with heterologous bidirectional histone promoters from Aspergilli sp. in A. nidulans can 
occur in little as 72hrs, as regulation of histone promoters during the S-phase of the cell cycle leads to 
expression of Ph4h3-regulated genes as soon DNA synthesis occurs (Ehinger et al., 1990; Rendsvig et 
al., 2019). Biosynthesis is also dependent the availability of precursors tryptophan and DMAP, 
products of the shikimate and mevalonate pathways, respectively, as well as cofactors NAPH, SAM 
which can be influenced by many factors. Compounds that had been identified by an earlier masters 
student in NID2741 comprised of DMAT, N-Me-DMAT and a festuclavine isomer compound, while 
newly identified compounds in NID2741 included DHLA, the fumigaclavine A-like compound found in 
HOM1 and elymoclavine, all of which had similar retention times and MS/MS fragmentation spectra 
as those identified in HOM1 and were undetected in the NID2679 parent strain . LC-MS results from 
NID2742, which contained the same six HOM1 EAS genes heterologously expressed in NID2741 plus 
the addition of easCAhom, revealed the same ergot alkaloids identified in NID2741 as well chanoclavine-
I and 4 new festuclavine-isomers. The identification of chanoclavine-I after introduction of easC in the 
EAS pathway supports previous evidence that heterologous biosynthesis chanoclavine-I in A. nidulans 
relies on the combined expression of easE and easC, with no chanoclavine-I production when easC is 
absent from the pathway (Ryan et al., 2013).Given that the 4 new festuclavine isomer EIC peaks were 
not found in NID2741 or HOM1, they are more likely not the result of solvent-induced epimerization 
and may be due to the production of chanoclavine-I in NID2742. Given that agroclavine, the precursor 
to elymoclavine, was not identified, it may also be possible that the peak identified as elymoclavine 
was in fact the isomer chanoclavine-I aldehyde, for which no fragmentation spectra were available; in 
either case, presence of elymoclavine or chanoclavine-I aldehyde indicated that an endogenous Anid 
EasD may be responsible for converting chanoclavine-I to chanoclavine-I aldehyde. This would also 
support the identification of festuclavine, the product of chanoclavine-I aldehyde reduction by easA 
reductase.  Although chanoclavine-I  or chanoclavine-I aldehyde was not detectable as a precursor ion 
in NID2741, the identification of the festuclavine isomer FI4, shared in HOM1 and NID2742, indicated 
that an endogenous Anid EasC homologue may have substituted for the role of easC in chanoclavine-
I synthesis. A BLASTp search of CpurEasC homologues against the Anid genome on JGI Mycocosm 
revealed a homologue with 53.4% sequence identity, suggesting that it may be able to substitute for 
the role. However, the EIC peaks for DHLA and elymoclavine in NID2741 and NID2742 were quite small, 
thus reducing their reliability as their fragmentation spectra are more easily influenced by background 
noise. Furthermore, biosynthesis of DHLA is dependent on a specialized CloA enzymes that to date 
have only been found in C. africans and C. gigantea, while fumigaclavine compounds are purported 
to most likely require unique p450 monooxygenases and acyltransferases to convert festuclavine to 
fumigaclavine A in A. fumigatus and certain Penicillium species including P. commune and P. 
clavigerum (Arnold & Panaccione, 2017; Bragg et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2009; Vinokurova et al., 2003; 
Wallwey & Li, 2011; Xu et al., 2014) . Identification of DHLA and fumigaclavine A in HOM1, NID2741 
and NID2742 would therefore indicate that both Aspergillus sp. contain endogenous homologous of 
these highly specialized ergot alkaloid tailoring enzymes, which seems unlikely. However, the 
consistency of the observed [m+z] values, isotopic patterns, and fragmentation spectra of the 
festuclavine isomers, DHLA, fumigaclavine-A in HOM1, NID2741 and NID2742 and absence in the 
parent strain NID2679 gives good indication that these compounds are ergot alkaloids and are 
synthesized directly by the heterologous EAS genes from HOM1 or indirectly by interaction with 
endogenous enzymes and/or secondary metabolites of A. nidulans. Absolute confirmation of the 
identity of these compounds would require the addition of standard compounds in NID2679 samples 
for LC-MS analysis to have a clear reference for EIC retention and MS/MS fragmentation spectra in the 
==PAGE:61==
60 
 
progenitor strain background. In the end, the larger EIC peak sizes for chanoclavine-I, N-Me-DMAT, 
fumigaclavine A and festuclavine isomers for samples grown on YES media and generally larger peak 
sizes for samples extracted with IPA:EA(1:3)1%FA led to the original conclusion that the 
YES/IPA:EA(1:3)1%FA combination was ideal for ergot alkaloid production and extraction in NID2741 
and NID2742, and was therefore utilized for the remainder of the project. The generally low alkaloid 
productions achieved in NID2741, NID2742 and HOM1 cultures grown on PDA and OAT indicated that 
starch or dextrose were poor carbon sources for ergot alkaloid production in these strains. It was not 
until revisiting the data late in the project timeline that elymoclavine and DHLA precursor ions were 
identified in NID2741 and NID2742, and only in extracts from cultures grown on CYA media and with 
larger EIC peaks from samples extracted with MeOH, suggesting that the CYA/MeOH combination may 
be the optimal combination for elymoclavine (or chanoclavine-I aldehyde) and DHLA production and 
extraction from NID2741 and NID2742. 
Preparation of EasA and EasD homologue genes by amplification from their native hosts proved to be 
the most challenging time-consuming aspect of the entire process of EAS pathway construction of NID 
strains. The discovery that optimization of extraction of high quality and concentration of gDNA 
template through extended ethanol precipitation at -20°C and centrifugation at max speed for 20 min 
would lead to much stronger PCR amplifications greatly increased the rate at which new transformants 
could be created. Furthermore, comparison of column purification and gel-purification of amplified 
easACpur PCR product for transformation indicated that primer heterodimers containing overhangs for 
homologous recombination with the regions flanking the insertion site can function as a repair 
template, and therefore gel-purification is likely the best way to ensure efficient integration of DNA 
insertion fragments, and to competition for insertion site recombination with primer heterodimers 
that can effectively function as knockout repair templates; although this is less likely to occur with IVA 
of multiple DNA insertion fragments, the presence of primer dimers and heterodimers with IVA 
assembly overhangs could compete with DNA fragments in IVA. 
The results of LC-MS analysis of the first EasA and EasD homologue gene insertions on the NID2742 
background provided similar results to those seen NID2741 and NID2742. The substitution of PTEF, 
regulating easCAhom in the 7-gene EAS construct of NID2742, with the bidirectional histone promoter 
amplified from Alep gDNA for bidirectional regulation of easCAhom and inserted EasA homologue genes 
resulted in abolishment of the chanoclavine-I peak in easD insertion mutants. It was discovered that 
the loss chanoclavine-I was likely due to the absence of the h4 and h3 5’UTRs in the Ph4h3Alep 
bioblocks, which presumably inhibited expression of easCAhom  and easD. As a result, easD insertion 
mutants had ergot alkaloid profiles similar to that of NID2741 grown on YES and extracted with 
IPA:EA(1:3)1%FA, with no significant differences in EIC peak sizes for DMAT, N-Me-DMAT, festuclavine 
or fumigaclavine A across EasD homologues.   
Despite the substitution of the potential reductase AhomEasA with the well characterized isomerase 
CpurEasA, there were no significant differences in the identified ergot alkaloid profiles of NID2905 and 
the other EasA homologue strains when compared to the parent strain NID2742. In fact, easACpur 
insertion resulted in even greater EIC peak sizes for festuclavine isomers FI1 and FI5, which would 
indicate reductase activity of CpurEasA. This raised the possibility that none of the easA homologues, 
including the easAhom, were expressed and that the festuclavine isomer peaks were the result of an 
endogenous Anid EasA homologue, which became more abundant with increased chanoclavine-I 
availability. Of the top hits for each EAS enzyme homologue found from the BLAST search against the 
Anid genome on JGI Mycocosm, the easAAnid homologue had the highest sequence identity of 55.5% 
and subject coverage of 88.9%; this homologue also contained a tyrosine in the aa-sequence position 
corresponding to 176F in Cpur, known to be a key residue in determining the isoform of EasA with 
==PAGE:62==
61 
 
characterized isomerases having a phenylalanine in this position and known reductases having a 
tyrosine in this position (Cheng et al., 2010). Lack of expression of  EasA homologues in NID2742 could 
be due to disfunction of the promoter, Ph4h3 of A. clavatus; its dual regulation of both easA and easG 
could also explain the absence of agroclavine precursor ions in the LC-MS analysis of NID2742, as easG 
is also responsible for agroclavine synthesis, and has been shown to convert chanoclavine-I aldehyde 
to agroclavine in the absence of easA from Cpur in vitro (Matuschek et al., 2011). However, Ph4h3Acla 
has been shown to function well when heterologously expressed in A.nidulans (Rendsvig et al., 2019). 
Another possibility is that the native terminator for easAAhom was not fully captured by adding 207bp 
of the downstream sequence from the stop codon; aside from their necessity for transcriptional 
termination, weak terminators can greatly reduce stability of the 3’ end of the mRNA transcript, 
resulting in degradation by endonucleases, and have been shown to greatly impact expression of 
genes regulated by bidirectional histone promoters in A. nidulans (Curran et al., 2015; Rendsvig et al., 
2019; Wefelmeier et al., 2022). Sequencing of EasA homologue insertion mutants revealed a G->A 
point mutation in the native easAAhom terminator across all sequencing amplicons for the easA 
insertions, which may have had an impact on terminator strength. Four point mutations were also 
found in easACpur which may have affected expression and folding. The same point mutations were 
seen across both sequenced easACpur colonies, indicating that the mutation may have occurred during 
PCR amplification with PfuX7; more likely, the 4 mismatches in the sequence may in fact be the correct 
nucleotides in Cpur ECC93, used for amplification of Cpur EAS genes in this project, and the 
mismatches may be the result of differences in the easA sequences of Cpur ECC93 and the reference 
sequence Cpur 20.1, as  a full genome sequence for Cpur ECC93 does not exist. Nevertheless, the 
variation in the FI1 and FI5 EIC peaks between the seven EasA homologue mutants in comparison to 
the conserved peak sizes of chanoclavine-I, FI2, DMAT, N-ME-DMAT and fumigaclavine A indicated 
that the EasA homologue genes may have had an effect on production of these festuclavine isomers, 
with largest FI1 and FI5 EIC peaks for the easAPexp and easACpur mutants, respectively. However, 
replicate samples would enable one to confirm whether variations in FI1 and FI5 production was due 
to random variation or were the direct result of expression of the EasA homologues.  
In the final stage of the project, easD insertions were repeated on a NID2905 (easACpur) background 
and assembled in vivo with corrected Ph4h3Alep containing the 5’UTRs of h4 and h3 exons, required for 
mRNA translation, in hopes that it would enable translation and full expression of the EasD 
homologues. EasA homologue gene insertions were repeated on a NID2928 background, containing 
easDCpur, to investigate whether lack of chanoclavine-I aldehyde availability was the reason behind the 
absence of agroclavine precursor ions in NID2742 and easA insertion mutants. And finally, CloA 
homologues were amplified from Cpur, Alep, Ahir and Pexp and assembled in vivo with Ph4h3Atur to 
regulate its expression upon insertion in the NID2905 parent strain; Ph4h3Alep and easACpur were 
included downstream of cloA in the IVA construct to enable the reuse of the pAC2077 CRISPR-Cas9 
plasmid targeting the easCAhom-regulating TEF promoter and provide a background with easDCpur and 
easACpur. A second cloACpur bioblock was also designed with a truncated ER-membrane-anchoring 
intermembrane sequence to investigate the impact of localizing cloA in the cytosol where the other 
EAS tailoring enzymes were predicted to be located, and a dual copy which are characteristic to p450 
enzymes that typically catalyze reactions within the ER; this was done as the other tailoring enzymes 
of the DLA biosynthetic pathway were predicted to be in the cytosol and to investigate whether it 
could improve DLA synthesis. A sixth 5-bioblock IVA construct containing two copies of cloACpur was 
also inserted in the NID2905 in hopes to improve DLA biosynthesis. LC-MS analysis of the new easA, 
easD, and CloA insertion mutants did not reveal the biosynthesis of any new ergot alkaloids, and ergot 
alkaloid profiles were highly similar across all mutants. Although initially discouraging, the results did 
provide insights into why the EAS pathway was not working fully and what could be done to fix it.

Despite the addition of 5’-UTRs to Ph4h3Alep, which showed successful assembly in Sanger sequencing 
results with all EasD homologue genes and integration in the target locus upstream of easCAhom, the 
loss of identifiable chanoclavine-I precursor ions in the LC-MS results of easA, easD and cloA insertion 
transformants, all containing Ph4h3Alep:easDCpur, indicated that Ph4h3Alep was still non-functional. The 
indication of poor promoter efficiency of Ph4h3Alep is contrary to previous findings by Rendsvig et al. 
showing high conservation of Aspergillus sp. Ph4h3 functionality when heterologously expressed in 
phylogenetically distant members of the genus Aspergillus (Rendsvig et al., 2019).The identification of 
all five festuclavine isomers in apparently chanoclavine-I deficient transformants was contrary to the 
LC-MS results of NID2741 and the first easD transformants assembled with Ph4h3Alep lacking 5’-UTRs, 
which only showed EIC peaks for the festuclavine isomer FI4 were identified. Therefore, it could not 
be reliably confirmed that chanoclavine-I production by EasCAhom was responsible for production of 
the four other festuclavine isomers. However, the use of two different HPLC-MS instruments could 
also be responsible for these contradicting results, and therefore a reliable comparison cannot be 
made until the samples are analyzed on the same instrument. Interestingly, the 14 mismatches found 
in the new easDCpur mutants (NID2927 and NID2928) were identical to those found in NID2915 and 
NID2921, which may also be the result of using easD from Cpur 20.1 as the reference sequence, and 
may in fact present in easD of Cpur ECC93. Similar findings were discovered in the sequencing results 
for easAAtur (NID2933) and easDAtur (NID2931), in which 43 of 45 mismatches in NID2933 were identical 
to the 43 mismatches found for the first easAtur mutant (NID2910), and 163 of the 172 mismatches 
found in NID2931 were also found in the first easDAtur  mutant (NID2922). Furthermore, the 29 identical 
mismatches were found in all sequenced Ph4h3Atur, which all indicates that the mismatches found in 
DNA fragments amplified from Atur IBT 27921 may be due to errors in the full genome sequence on 
JGI Mycocosm.  
The decision to incorporate Ph4h3Alep:easDCpur in the heterologous EAS pathway in all of the six cloA 
insertion mutants, and apparent loss of chanoclavine-I production, may have compromised the 
possibility of elymoclavine or DLA synthesis. However, there are a multitude of other possible reasons 
as to why DLA or elymoclavine synthesis by the CloA homologues may not have occurred. Any 
elymoclavine or DLA produced in the cloA mutants may also have been modified by endogenous A. 
nidulans tailoring enzymes to form novel ergot alkaloids undetected in the LC-MS analysis. Sanger 
sequencing results could not confirm the full assembly of cloA integration constructs, which could also 
be responsible for the failure to produce DLA. cloACpur disfunction could also have been caused by the 
43 mismatches found in sequencing, including 1 point deletion, which could shift the entire reading 
frame; however, it was likely again due to use of Cpur 20.1 as a source of Cpur reference sequences. 
However, previous studies attempting heterologous EAS pathway expression have shown that 
successful biosynthesis and identification of CloA products is extremely difficult, and is not just 
dependent on successful CloA expression; Yao et al. were able to significantly increase heterologous 
production of elymoclavine by introducing copies of cytochrome p450 reductase (CPR) and CYB5, 
responsible for transferring electrons to the heme moiety of CloA, into their A. nidulans EAS expression 
system (Yu et al., 2022).  
Lastly, the persistent and prominent EIC peak for N-Me-DMAT in all EICs analyzed in this project reveal 
a glaring necessity to mitigate this bottleneck, which could be done through introduction of multiple 
copies of easC. The greater availability of chanoclavine-I may lead to improved enzyme kinetics of the 
subsequent steps of the synthetic EAS pathway, and production of chanoclavine-I aldehyde, 
agroclavine, elymoclavine, DLA and any derivatives thereof.

11 FUTURE PERSPECTIVES 
Several strategies could be employed to potentially turn A. nidulans into an ergot alkaloid producing 
powerhouse The late discovery of elymoclavine and DHLA in the LC-MS results for NID2741 and 
NID2742 on CYA media, with higher extraction with MeOH, indicates that this media-extraction 
solvent combination should be used to analyze a handful of the mutants made in this project to see if 
any of the EAS gene insertions resulted in greater elymoclavine production. Aprotic solvents such as 
acetonitrile and chloroform should be considered for extraction and storage of extracts, which may 
help reduce epimerization of ergot alkaloids and improve the ease of compound identification with 
LC-MS. Ergot alkaloid identification could also be improved through the use of internal standards, and 
the supplementation of carbon-13 labelled L-tryptophan in the growth media, resulting in a +1 m/z 
shift of the fragmentation spectra of compounds based on the L-tryptophan scaffold. The endogenous 
EasA homologue (jgi1949) found in the BLAST search against the A. nidulans genome could be knocked 
out to see if it is the cause of the festuclavine isomer peaks observed in NID2741 and NID2742. The 
use of biological replicates would also allow one to determine whether the fluctuations in festuclavine 
isomers and fumigaclavine are the result of random variation or in fact due to the particular EAS gene 
homologue inserted. Native or synthetic terminators should be tested for the EasA homologues used 
in this project, as their expression may have been inhibited by poor strength of the easAAhom 
terminator.  
Although the equimolar quantity of 0.8 pmol per DNA bioblock proved to be highly efficient for 
insertion of a single gene DNA fragment and IVA of up to five bioblocks in this project, it would be 
valuable to investigate whether lower amounts of DNA bioblocks could be used without greatly 
reducing the efficiency of transformation. Longer incubation times of transformants should also be 
tested to see if ergot alkaloid production improves or if extension of the stationary phase when 
endogenous SM production increases and interactions between the host SMs and associated enzymes 
and the heterologous EAS pathway lead to novel ergot alkaloids. Given the large EIC peaks for LAA, 
LAH and ergometrine in the HOM1 LC-MS analysis, it would be worth considering optimizing this strain 
for ergot alkaloid production as well, and knocking out LpsB and LpsC to see if high DLA production 
could be achieved. Submerged cultures of A. nidulans and A. homomorphus  should also be 
experimented with to see if alkaloid production increases and investigate secretion of ergot alkaloids.
