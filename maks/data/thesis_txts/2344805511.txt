III 
Abstract 
 
In educational games, users are addressed both as a players and as learners. Many meth-
ods to design these kind of games have been proposed in academic literature, and have 
different approaches and addresses the structure of the design process on this kind of 
games. On the other hand, design methods of common use in engineering approaches 
the design task by decomposing the problem into functions, that can be fulfilled inde-
pendently. There are still some gaps between content, approach to the players, and a 
method to build a game from the games’ mechanics, as any of them approach this issues 
at the same time. In this report is developed a method that covers the three dimensions 
of the design of educational games: Normative background (what to teach), pedagogical 
approach (how to teach), and the gaming back ground (how to support the teaching), and 
the resulting method is used to design a game to teach innovation.

1 
Introduction 
This report describes the design process of a game to teach innovation. The game will 
be used in workshops, attended by people coming from companies. The target of the 
workshops are managers and project developers, and its objective is to make them un-
derstand innovation as a process. 
 
The report is divided in two parts: The first one is a literature review on game mechanics, 
innovation process models, and design methods. It is a theoretical approach to the game 
design that covers a brief introduction of gaming background and game mechanics, as 
the elements from which the game is built. Then, innovation, and innovation process mod-
els are introduced. In this section, models from different generations are compared in 
order to establish which are the key elements that should be addressed in the game. In 
other words, the scope of the game is stabilised in this section. Last, there is a presenta-
tion of design methods of general use in engineering, and methods to create educational 
games. They are used to create a method that takes the structure of engineering methods, 
but uses the game design approach of the ones to create educational games. 
 
The second part describes the design process of the innovation game, following a 
method inspired by general design methods of common use in engineering, as well as 
design methods proposed by academic literature to design educational games. The re-
sulting design process is based in Cross’ eight stage model, and complemented with key 
factors of the design of educational games. There are presented six alternatives, and the 
best one is selected to be developed as final concept. At the end are presented the dis-
cussions and reflections of the design process, and the project in general, as well as 
identification of the areas that should be addressed for further work.

1 Introduction to game mechanics 
A game is a system in which players engage in an artificial conflict, defined by rules, that 
result in a quantifiable outcome (Salen & Zimmerman 2004). Characteristics of games are 
the group of features that describe in a high level what is a game. Literature describes 
three basic characteristics that influences all the others. Two of them are presented in a 
direct way to players: Length of play and number of players. The third characteristic, the 
heuristics is more abstract. It refers to the things in the game that makes the players to 
play it, addressing the gaming experience, in a way of speaking (Elias et al. 2012).  
 
This section will focus in the game elements that are relevant to the design of the inno-
vation game as it is described in Section 4, and therefore, does not intend to be an ex-
tensive review of gaming theory. It is focused in the game mechanics, which enables the 
creation of a morphology based in them. 
 
1.1 Game mechanics 
The game mechanics are the set the rules, processes and data that define how the game 
progresses, what is going to happen and the winning and/or loosing conditions (Adams 
& Dormans 2012). Other definitions include the facilitation and encouragement of the user 
to explore and learn the properties of their possibility space through feedback mecha-
nisms (Cook 2006). Table 1.1 shows a group of 51 game mechanics commonly used in 
board games (BoardGameGeek.com n.d.).

1.2 Construction of morphology  
Game mechanics normally address parts of a game (i.e. the board, the type of decks, 
etc.). Analysing the mechanics described in Table 1.1 is possible to classify them into 19 
groups, according to their basic principles: 
 
1. Movement: It groups all the mechanics related to the movement in space of one 
or several elements at the same time 
2. Resources: It groups all the mechanics related to the management of resources, 
both financial and non-financial. Some basic examples are bull, sell, and trade. 
3. Cards: In this contexts, it groups all the mechanics that require cards to be devel-
oped, such as hand management, and deck/pool building, among others. 
4. Type of game: Refers to some game categories, according to their principal me-
chanics. It includes role games, cooperative play, and simulation games. 
5. Board: Groups the mechanics related to the shape and type of the board, as well 
as their grid pattern, if it applies.  
6. Communication: Covers the mechanics used to pass information within a game, 
from one player to other. It includes are acting, singing, drawing, and storytelling.

7. Players actions: Describes the actions a player can do in their turn, if the actions 
change the way the game is developed. Action point allowance system, ac-
tion/movement programming, and simultaneous action selection belong to this 
group. 
8. Winning mechanism: Refers to a mechanic from RPG games, in which is de-
scribed the attack conditions to defeat the opponent.  
9. Turn order: Refers to the mechanics that can change the turn order within a game. 
10. Pattern: It covers the mechanics related to the construction and recognition of 
patterns. 
 
11. Hidden information: Groups the mechanics that keep in secrecy some condition, 
objectives or answer, which shall be revealed by the end of the game. 
12. Route: Groups the mechanics to construct routes or paths. 
13. Player decision: Describes some attitudes and decisions the player could assume 
towards a game. In a multiplayer campaign, he or she may choses to forge alli-
ances with any other player, or in a luck game he may decide continue playing, or 
retire before he or she receives an adverse outcome. 
14. Players’ settings: Describes the differentiation of player abilities. Pandemic is a 
good example of this kind of mechanics.  
15. Drawing: Refers to the draw of lines to connect dots, like in dots and dashes. 
16. Randomize: Groups the ways of randomize and outcome. It does not in include 
shuffling cards, as this is a mechanic from card games. 
17. Player task: Describe a king of tasks that players are requested to do in order to 
win a game. 
18. Decision taking: Groups the mechanics that can be used to take an informed de-
cision. It does not include, for example, rolling the dices, as it is a random decision. 
19. Transport: Refers to the transporting of resources from one point of the board to 
another.

2 Innovation and innovation process models 
Innovation can be defined is the intentional introduction and application of ideas, pro-
cesses, products or procedures which are new to a job, work team or organization with 
the purpose to benefit them. Innovations may vary from those that are relatively minor, to 
those that are of great significance, and they can occur unplanned and by accident (West 
2002).  
 
Within innovation it is possible to distinguish two overall stages: Creativity, which covers 
the process related to the generation of new and valued ideas, followed by an Implemen-
tation of the innovation, which refers to the activities performed to develop and introduc-
tion of the developed ideas (West 2002). How to do innovation is described by the Inno-
vation process models. They can be defined as a sequence of phases or stages, which 
are connected to each other in order to develop a product or process from the idea to its 
implementation (Kozioł-Nadolna & Świadek 2011).  
 
This section is presented a sample of innovation process models, which are synthetized 
into a common reference back ground that defines the scope of the innovation game. 
This section focus in the models from first, second and third generation, as they are easier 
to gamify due to their linear structure, as it is discussed in Section 2.7. 
 
2.1 Ways of classifying Innovation Models 
There are many ways to classify innovation models, but there are two that stand out from 
the others: Classification according to their objectives, and classification according to an 
historical point of view. When classifying them according to objectives, there are two 
categories: Normative models and descriptive models. Normative models are derived 
from practical experience, case studies or quantitative studies analysing successful new 
product developments. They provide a basis for process clarification and systematization, 
and are intended to be management tools. On the other hand, descriptive models evolved 
from empirical studies and are intended to describe and evaluate the actual practice, and 
therefor they do not attend to advice managers (Verworn & Herstatt 2002).  
 
From an historical point of view, innovation models are classified them in five generations 
(Kozioł-Nadolna & Świadek 2011): 
==PAGE:26==
Chapter 2

1. First generation (1960’s): Technology-pushed innovation processes. Those were 
simple stage-gate-process models that focused in the research and development 
stages, without considering the introduction to the market (Section 2.2). 
2. Second generation models (1970’s): Market pulled models. Include stages such 
as Idea generation and Market introduction, in order to overcome the limitations 
of the first generation models (Section 2.3). 
3. Third generation models: Combines the technology-push and need-pull models 
by recognising the interactions between R&D and the marketing (Section 2.4).  
4. Fourth generation models (1980’s): Presents as a parallel process with cross-
functional developments in the company and with external partners. 
5. Fifth generation models: Combine the integration networking with innovation 
technology. This generation of processes usually has the same structure of the 
innovation process: R&D, production, customer and marketing, as the models 
from the first generation.

2.2 First generation of innovation process models 
First generation of innovation process models were originated during the 1960’s, and they 
are simple, linear, technology pushed. The Phase-review-process is a good example of a 
first generation model (Cooper 1994). First generation models focus only in the develop-
ing stage, without addressing the phases of idea generation nor the introduction to the 
market. 
 
2.2.1 Phase-review-process (Hughes and Chafin) 
In the phase-review-process, the life cycle of the development is broken into sequential 
phases, each one with defined inputs and outputs which are reviewed by the manage-
ment, who makes a go / no-go decision after each stage as shown in Figure 2.1 (Hughes 
& Chafin 1996). Phase-review-process models focus only in the development part, and 
not the complete innovation process, which includes from the idea generation to market 
launch (Verworn & Herstatt 2002). 
 
 
 
Figure 2.1: Phase-review-process 
The phase-review-process and the other stage-gate models addressed the core if the 
innovation process, and the necessity of having a more complete description of the in-
novation process encouraged the development of the second generation of models. 
 
2.3 Second generation of innovation process models 
The second generation of process models considers the complete process, overcoming 
the limitations of the first generation which only managed with the development part. With 
this new generation, two new stages were added to the innovation models: Idea genera-
tion and market introduction. In this section, some examples of second generation mod-
els will be introduced.

2.3.1 Second generation stage-gate-process (Cooper and Kleinschmidt) 
As in any stage-gate-process, the idea is evaluated along several gates through the de-
velopment process. Figure 2.2 describes a typical second stage-gate-process model, in 
which the idea has to meet the defied criteria for each one of the proposed gates. The 
initial screen in Gate 1 is a quick assessment of the idea in terms of strategic alignment 
and feasibility, followed by a quick assessment of the project in Stage 1. This takes into 
account soft financials, the market and technology which are evaluated in Gate 2. After 
the second screen, comes a more detailed research phase in Stage 2, that produces a 
Business case, evaluated in Gate 3. During Stage 3 the product and the marketing plan 
are developed, providing a prototype to be evaluated in Gate 4, which ensures that the 
product meets the specification defined in the previous stages of the process. Stage 4 
consist in the validation of the product by in-house and customer tests, as well as pro-
duction trials. Then, in Gate 5 is decided to start with the production ramp up and market 
launch, which are conducted in Stage 5 (Cooper & Kleinschmidt 1990; Verworn & Herstatt 
2002).  
 
 
Figure 2.2: Typical second-generation stage-gate-process 
 
In this model, all the activities between the Idea and Gate 2 conform the Idea Management 
stage, which refers to all the activities related to the development of a concept, covering 
from idea generation to the evaluation of the alternatives. It is performed just before the 
development, and attends to select the ideas that are worth developing. This model also 
introduces a Product Validation stage, in which the product is tested from the points of 
view of marketing, production and customers, allowing the product to be introduced into 
the market in the Commercialization stage.

2.3.2 Product Development Process (Ulrich and Expunger)  
This is another example of a second generation stage-gate-process model. It consists in 
six phases, as described in Figure 2.3 (Ulrich & Eppinger 2012). 
 
1. Planning phase: The output of this stage is the project’s mission statement (prob-
lem definition). 
2. Concept Development: In this phase, alternative product concepts are generated 
and evaluated, selecting one (or more) for further development and testing.  
3. System Level Design: The product architecture is defined, as well as the prelimi-
nary design of key components.  
4. Detailed design: In this phase is produced the complete specification of the prod-
uct, from the point of view of design and manufacturing. Also the components to 
be buy to suppliers are full specified in this phase.   
5. Testing and Refinement: In this phase high level prototypes (alpha) are con-
structed in order to evaluate multiple product configurations and more detailed 
prototypes (beta) are used for a deeper evaluation and user testing.  
6. Production Ramp-up: The product is made using the intended production system. 
It covers the training of the personnel responsible of the production process as 
well as the identification of remaining process.

2.4 Third generation of innovation process models 
The third generation models evolved from the second generation stage-gate-processes 
model, intending to be more flexible than them. They propose fuzzy gates, allowing con-
ditional GO decisions that depend on the situation and introducing stages that overlap 
each other. Third generation models also build in techniques to project prioritization and 
focus (Cooper 1994). Some examples are introduced in this section: 
 
2.4.1 Third generation stage-gate-process (Cooper) 
Third generation stage-gate models manage risk by gathering critical information in the 
parallel activities within a stage. Here, flexibility allows to speed up the products to market, 
as stages can overlap each other and long term activities can be brought to an early stage. 
Projects can proceed to the next stage without completing totally the previous one, and 
stages can be collapsed and combined. Figure 2.8 illustrates the generic stage-gate-
process model, featuring five overlapping stages (Cooper 1996): 
 
Stage 1: Preliminary investigation. It is the scoping of the project, where is done a pre-
liminary market, technical and business assessments. 
 
Stage 2: Detailed investigation. The business case is constructed in this stage, and in-
cludes detailed market, technical and manufacturing assessment, as well as detailed fi-
nancial and business analysis. At the end of this stage is delivered a defined product, 
business justifications and a detailed plan of action for the following stages. 
 
Stage 3: Development. In this stage the developing plan is implemented, providing a pro-
totype or a sample product. It also includes the in-house and customer testing. Manufac-
turing process and requirement are mapped out and the marketing plan is developed, 
defining also the test plans for the next stage. 
 
Stage 4: Testing and validation. It is the verification and validation of the proposed new 
product, including its marketing and production. In this stage is performed extensive 
product testing, customer trials on the field or on a market place. Includes also pilot pre-
production trials and in some cases sell trials. At the end of this stage is delivered a fully 
tested product and production process which is ready for commercialization

Stage 5: Full production and market launch. It is the start of full production and commer-
cial selling. In this stage the market launch plan is implemented, as well as the production 
plan and post-launch activities, which include monitoring and adjustments. Stages 4 and 
5 remain ongoing from commercialization and beyond.

2.5.2 Problem definition 
It refers to the statement of the problem to be addressed, and its analysis. In other words,

2.5.3 Strategy 
As such, it covers many support activities in the innovation process that are typically 
addressed by managers. Strategy formulation, Corporate strategy, Leadership, and Inno-

2.5.4 Idea management 
Covers all the cycle of development a concept idea. It starts with the generation of the

ies to support the selection of ideas. 
 
2.5.5 Research 
In this stage, more advanced investigation is performed in order to develop the ideas that 
were selected in the previous stage, and it is a requirement before starting the developing 
stage. It differs from the Information gathering stage as this no longer focus to capture or 
generate ideas, but to find the means to develop them. 
 
2.5.6 Development 
Refers to all the activities and use of the resources, whatever they may be, to transform 
the idea into their implementation. This is the core of the innovation process, and each 
model addresses it in different ways as it is shown in Table 2.2. 
 
2.5.7 Manufacturing 
Some models include manufacturing related activities as complement of the innovation 
process. In some models, it also includes the production ramp-up.

2.6 Evaluation of priorities for the innovation game 
As mentioned in Section 2.5, the common reference framework does not map completely  
the innovation processes introduced in the previous section, because the models may 
address different stake holders within the organization. For instance, normative models 
are intended to be management tools, and therefor focus more in the stages that involve 
the managers. Descriptive models may have other differences, as they describe how dif-
ferent organizations run innovation, forcing the models to make emphasis in different 
stages of the process. For example, the Second generation stage-gate-process (page 
15) divide the development phase in three sections, making emphasis in the definition 
and the business case decision; but does not address the strategy stage as other models. 
 
Using the common reference framework presented in Table 2.2, it is possible to make an 
assessment of the level of relevance of each one of the ten main stages of innovation 
presented in Figure 2.11. Although all stages are important to have a robust innovation 
process, it is necessary to prioritize them in terms of relevance for the innovation models, 
and applicability for an innovation game. The relevance can be assessed by how much 
the innovation processes focus on them. By inspecting Table 2.2, it is possible to see that 
there are three stages that stand out from the others: idea management, research, and 
development stages. They are not only addressed by all of the innovation models, but 
they are also analysed from multiple perspectives, allowing to have an overview that is 
more complete than any of the other stages. Table 2.3 provides a classification of all the 
stages, in three levels of relevance: high, medium and low. The assessment of their level 
of relevance for the game is based in the level of concordance of the innovation models 
around each stage, and how there are approached by the innovation models. For exam-
ple, not too many innovation models concord in stablishing the problem definition, strat-
egy, or process validation as a stage, so it is assessed with low relevance. Others, such 
as information gathering, manufacturing, and commercialization and market introduction 
are addressed by many methods, but they do not seem to approach in such structured 
way as the development stage, for example. For this reason, is assessed with a medium 
relevance.

2.7 Scope of the innovation game 
To define the scope of the innovation game, two things have to be taken into considera-
tion: The first one is the innovation process, and determining what is the most important 
things to be included in the game, from the point of view of the process. This was dis-
cussed in Section 2.6, and resumed in Table 2.3. The second one is related to the way of 
introducing the process to players, who may know very little about innovation. As inno-
vation processes have two generic parts, creativity, and innovation management, it could 
be possible to approach the players from the point of view of creativity, as people relate 
creativity and to be innovative, even creating confusion between the two (West 2002). In 
Table 2.2 it was shown that stages of idea management, research, and development ad-
dress creativity; and manufacturing, product and process validation, and commercialisa-

3 Design methods 
A design method is a goal oriented rationalization of engineering work in the form of 
standardized work description. In design, they are normally synthesis and clarification 
oriented and create a staging for creativity activities or structured research, classification, 
discussion, evaluation and decision making (Andreasen et al. 2015). 
 
In this chapter it is presented a literature review of design methods to design educational 
games and some other design methods of common use in engineering, presenting at the 
end a synthesis and the framework to be used in the design of the innovation game 
 
In this chapter, the methods are classified in two groups: General methods used in engi-
neering and Design methods for educational games. This classification serves to have 
easy way of presenting them, and does not claim the only way to classify design methods. 
The methods presented in this section were selected because they may be relevant for 
this design task, and do not claim to be the only ones. 
 
3.1 General methods used in engineering 
In this section are introduced five methods used in engineering. They are generic methods, 
in the sense that they can be used to address a broad range of design problems. Some 
of them, like Pahl and Beitz’s Conceptual Design, and TRIZ, are commonly used when 
solving design tasks, and other, like Tjalve’s Product Synthesis has a narrower scope, as 
it focuses in formal design.

This model integrates procedural aspects of design with the structural aspects of design 
problems. The procedural aspects are represented by the sequence of eight methods in 
the centre of the model, and the structural aspects are represented by the arrows show-
ing the relationship between problem and solution. They also show the hierarchical rela-
tionships between problems and between solutions. (Cross 2008). The Eight stage model 
is shown in Figure 3.1.

3.1.4 Theory of Inventive Problem Solving (TRIZ) 
TRIZ stands for Theory of Inventive Problem Solving (in Russian: Teoriya Resheniya Izo-
breatatelskikh Zadatch). It summarizes good inventive practices into a generic problem-
solving framework as shown in Figure 3.4. The role of the Design Engineers is to relate 
their specific problem with a generic TRIZ problem. Then they find a generic TRIZ solution 
by using the tools described in the methodology (Mann 2001).  
 
 
Figure 3.4: The TRIZ process 
 
TRIZ researchers argues that there are only 40 principle solutions to problems in all fields, 
and that the evolution of those fields follows the same trends. This is the working principle 
behind TRIZ, as it allows the researchers to elaborate, maintain and update the list of 
generic problems and generic solutions, with the certitude of covering all kind of prob-
lems. The transference a generic solution to a specific solution may require some crea-
tivity from the design engineer, as is not a trivial design decision.

be formulated in terms of the specific design case and then generalized in order to 
achieve a suitable biological analogy and is described in Figure 3.5 (Lenau et al. 2010).

2. Microadaptability: Regards to the adaptive interventions within a learning situation. 
Contraire to macroadapability, it deals with how the player interact within each 
learning scenario. 
3. Metacognition: Refers to the players’ own knowledge concerning their learning 
process. 
4. Motivation: It comprises all the relevant elements within the game that refers to 
fun and joy while playing. It also includes those elements who make the player 
want to keep learning. 
 
The 4Ms are the core of a design process that consists in 8 phases: 
 
Phase 1 – Identify instructional goals: The context in which the game will be used is de-
fined in this stage. Also the pedagogical approach. For example, it is defined if the game 
will be used in a high school classroom, or in a workshop with representatives from com-
panies. In this stage is also defined learning goals, target groups, topic and learning con-
tent. 
 
Phase 2 – Instructional analysis: In this stage, the learning content is transformed into a 
formal knowledge structure, called knowledge space. It is used to set the game environ-
ment to the learning needs of the player.  
 
Phase 3 – Analyse learners and context of learning: The learners and their context are 
analysed in depth in this stage. It focuses in the entry skills, learning problems, and pref-
erences and attitudes. During this stage, user of the game is studied both as a player and 
as a learner, and the difficulties they may have when approaching a given learning topic. 
  
Phase 4 – Write performance objectives and overall structure of the game: Performance 
objectives related to the learning process are defined in this stage, and they are con-
nected to the pedagogical structure of the game. It should include a general description 
of the story of the game, including the setting, characters and the plot. 
 
Phase 5 – Learning game design: It integrates the learning and gaming. In this stage is 
developed a detailed description of each situation of the game: learning situations, game 
situations, and storytelling situations. These three elements must work together in order 
==PAGE:56==
Chapter 3 
42 
to create a relevant gaming experience for the player. In this stage are addressed the 4Ms 
described previously. 
 
Phase 6 – Production and development: It groups the activities of creative teams and 
programmers. Creative teams are in charge of developing the story and the characters, 
accordingly to the thigs defined in previous steps. Programmers develop the technologies 
required for the game, and for the creative teams to develop the story. 
 
Phase 7 – Evaluation of learning: There are two ways in which the educational game: a 
formative evaluation and a summative evaluation. Formative evaluation is the testing stage, 
and is related to the developing and production (phase 6). On the other hand, summative 
evaluation refers to the general evaluation of the game as a whole, as well as the whole 
process, including the learning-game experience. 
 
Phase 8 – Revise instruction: In this stage, the outputs of the evaluations (phase 7) are 
used to provide recommendations and improvements to the learning game as a whole, 
addressing all phases in the process.

3.3 Synthesis and design framework 
In this section, the general design methods used in engineering (Section 3.1) and the 
design methods for educational games (Section 3.2) are compared, in order to have a 
generic design method that can be used properly to design the innovation game.  
 
3.3.1 A classification of Design Methods 
It has been mentioned before that design methods can be classified in several ways, but 
may be hard process as they cover several aspects at the same time. Typically, there are 
three schemes that are highlighted in literature to classify them, due to their practical 
relevance: (1) Staged-based vs Activity-based, (2) Solution-oriented vs Problem-oriented 
and (3) Abstract vs Procedural vs Analytical approaches (Wynn & Clarkson 2005). How-
ever, in this project is more relevant to compare them from a different perspective. Design 
methods introduced in Sections 3.1 and 3.2 approach the design task from different per-
spectives. Some of them give an overall structure of the process, and others seem to 
focus in presenting a procedure to be followed by designers. For this reason, a two-
dimensional space was created to compare them simultaneously, following to two crite-
ria: (1) How procedural are they meant to be and (2) How structural are they meant to be. 
A procedural method is focused in describing the sequence of steps or stages required 
to solve a given problem. TRIZ is an example of this approach, as its methodology lies 
on solving a given problem through a TRIZ generic problem and then a TRIZ generic 
solution, using the relevant tools. On the other hand, a structural design method is fo-
cused in providing an overall framework for designing. In other words, they do not de-
scribe detailed sequence of steps that will leave to the solution, but to describe a “way 
of thinking” or “philosophy” to solve problems. A good example of this kind of method is 
User Centred Game Design (UCGD), which only shows in a high level the sequence of 
stages and the expected outputs from each one. This Design Methods' Space is illus-
trated in Figure 3.9.

4 Design process 
In this chapter is described the design process of the innovation game, following the 
design process described in Section 3.3, presenting a concept of the innovation game at 
the end. The scope of the innovation game sticks to the framing stablished in Section 2.7, 
using the key game mechanics defined in Section 1.2.  
 
The design game will be used in the innovation workshops in order to introduce to the 
assistants the innovation process models. The people coming to the workshops are man-
agers and development responsible for companies around 100 employees. The time to 
play the game in this workshops is limited, as there are many other activities that are part 
of the program. The ideal time for playing is no longer than 1 hour, counting the deploy-
ment of the game, and teaching the game, so it must be fast to set and to learn. The 
game also must be for at least four players and should encourage discussions among 
them. It also has to be accessible to the facilitator of the workshop, in case he or she 
needs to make an intervention. 
 
4.1 Analyse context of learning 
The objective of this step is to identify the social interactions that support learning and 
documenting things such as what people were doing, the inventory of overall interactions 
between participants and facilitator, and the expected behaviours during the workshop.  
 
User observations were conducted in the first session of the Innovation Seminar at the 
Teknologisk Institut, on March the 17th, 2016. The workshop was facilitated by Erik W. 
Hallgren. There was no specific methodology associated to this stage, only documenting 
the observations and taking pictures without interfering with its developing. People at-
tending the workshops were coming from different companies around Denmark. There 
was a mix of managers and people who were involved in the development of products in 
a project basis (part of a project team, an “executor of innovation”). 
 
People assisting to the workshops come from different companies, with different back-
grounds and different areas of expertise. The game should not address anyone, or any 
special field directly, otherwise those people would have a more active role in the discus-
sions that people who may not be addressed.

4.4.1 Description of the relevant mechanics 
The alternatives are generated from the point of view of mechanics, since they can be 
linked both to concrete elements of the game (i.e. the board) and can improve the desired 
behaviours to facilitate the learning of innovation. The following mechanics follow the 
same classification described in Section 1.2: 
 
4.4.1.1 Movement 
1. Grid movement: Refers to the movement of a pawn on a grid in many directions. 
Grids can have many shapes. For example, Chess has square grid, and Abalone 
has hexagonal grid. Some examples are: Chess (square grid), Abalone (hexagonal 
grid). 
2. Point to point movement: Refers to the movement between two points on a map 
as long as they are connected with lines. Pandemic uses this mechanic to move 
people between cities.

4.4.1.2 Resources  
1. Auction/bidding: In this mechanic, players are requested to place a bid (money or 
goods) in order to enhance their position on the game. These goods may enable 
possible actions in the future, or improve a position. The auctions consist of taking 
turns and placing more bids until one winner is established. Then the winner takes 
the control of the item that has being bid. 
 
2. Commodity speculations: It allows players to bet in-game money to a given com-
modity, hoping that it will increase value as the game progresses. The value of the 
commodities may change throughout the game, so players bay and sell them to 
make money from their investments. 
3. Trading: Consist in the exchange of items between players. They can exchange 
types, or buy/sell them. An example of a game with this mechanic is Bohnanza. 
4. Worker placement: Players draft individual actions from a set that is available to 
all players. In a game with rounds, all the actions are refreshed so they are avail-
able again for drafting. Normally, there is a limit of times a single action can be 
drafted in the same way for the same price, and once this limit is reached, an 
action cannot be taken anymore until another round, or until the action space is 
no longer occupied by another player. 
 
4.4.1.3 Cards 
Cards may also refer to a type of games, in which the players manage their hands in 
order to win the game, or luck games in which random cards are drawn. Here is de-
scribed, however, the mechanics involving cards, and not card games as a category:  
1. Campaign/battle card driven: This mechanic focuses the players of actions in 
cards they have in their hand. Each card represents a possible action that can be 
done by the player, which is executed when the card is drawn.  
2. Card drifting: It consists in picking card from a set, such as a common pool, in 
order to have an advantage in the game, or to assemble a hand in order to meet 
objectives by the end of the game. This mechanics requires the play to make a 
decision, so games in which the player drawn random cards are not included. An 
example of game with this mechanic is Ticket to ride.

4.4.1.4 Type of game 
1. Cooperative play: IN cooperative play, players work together to complete/beat the 
game, with very little competition between them if any. Usually, players must com-
plete some objectives before certain events happen, in order to win the game. An 
example of this game Battlestar Galactica. 
2. Simulation: Intends to recreate some actual events or situations in real life. 
 
4.4.1.5 Communication 
1. Acting: Players are required to use mimicry or other way of non-verbal communi-
cation to communicate with other players. Verbal communication is often re-
stricted within the play. An example of a game of this mechanic is Charades. 
2. Drawing: Instead of conveying an idea through speaking or acting, the idea is 
communicated through drawing. In some games, such as Pictionary (draw and 
guess), the players are required to guess something (situation, concept, person) 
that is being drawn by someone else.  
3. Storytelling: Players are provided with elements such as graphic, conceptual or 
written input so they can be incorporated into a story of the players’ creation. 
Players can be presented with a narrative also follow the narrative of somebody 
else, and it can be change as consequence of players’ actions within the game. 
An example of a game with this mechanic is Dungeons and Dragons. 
 
4.4.1.6 Player actions 
1. Action point allowance system: In some games, players have a number of points 
per round, which can be spent on available actions, until the player does not have 
any remaining points to get any more actions. In Pandemic, players are given four 
points to execute an action in each round: movement, air travel, special action 
and special ability.  
 
4.4.1.7 Randomize 
1. Combination: In mathematics, a combination is a selection of elements form one 
or more sets. In games, this principle can be used to generate many possibilities 
from relatively few elements. An example of this mechanic is introduced in Section 
4.4.2.

4.4.1.8 Route 
1. Crayon Rail system: It consists in connecting lines on a board, in order to build a

4.4.1.9 Player task 
1. Set collection: Players are requested to collect a set of items within a game. An

4.4.1.10 Decision taking

4.4.3 Alternative 2: Collecting points 
In this game, 2 or more players compete to getting the higher amount of points from the 
point-cells on the board, using the movements described in the movement cards to build 
paths. The cells containing points also work as check points, from which the player can

start a new path towards another point-cells. The paths cannot be intersected or over-
lapped, so players can design strategies such as separate the other player from the other 
point-cells, or preventing him to continue building more paths. 
 
Game elements:  
1. 1 hexagonal board, with the point-cells marked on it – Variation 1 (Figure 4.8). 
2. 1 hexagonal board, with 12 golden coins and 6 check points on it – Variation 2 
(Figure 4.9). 
3. 1 hexagonal board, with 12 golden coins and no check points on it – Variation 3

4. 1 hexagonal board, with 12 golden coins and 4 red coins on it – Variation 4 (Figure

5. 21 movement cards (3 of each, Figure 4.12). 
6. 3 check point cards (for Alternatives 3 and 4). 
7. 1 markers of different colour for each player (i.e. red and blue). 
 
Setting the game: 
Players start from the start-cell, located in the middle of the board and each player takes 
3 movement cards before start. 
 
 
How to play:  
1. Players move across the board by drawing the movement cards on their hands. 
Once a movement card is drawn, the player shall take another one, so he/she 
always has 3 movement cards in his/her hand. While crossing the board, players 
should mark the path they have constructed. 
2. The number in the point-cells indicates how many paths can be connected to that 
cell, counting the arrival to the cell as one of the paths. So, a path will necessarily 
end in a cell with the number 1 as they cannot have more connections (Figure 
4.13). Cells with the number 2 can have only 1 path departing form them as the 
path to arrive counts as 1 path (Figure 4.14). Cells with the number 3 can have 2 
paths departing from them (Figure 4.15). Cells with numbers 2 and 3 serve as 
check points, as the player can chose to depart from them at any time, as long as 
they respect the number of available connections for that cell.

cannot continue building more paths. 
4. The winner is the player who collects more points, not necessarily the one who

4.4.6 Alternative 5: Simulation game 
This concept intends to simulate the innovation process, in which 3 players try to develop 
an idea together, investing resources such as people, money and time to move the idea 
through all the stages of the innovation process and profit from them at the end of the 
development. To do it, each player assumes one of the 3 available roles: developer, CFO 
and manager. In this concept, ideas are classified by how many resources they require

resources to solve the situation, or do nothing and assume the cost in the future. They 
crisis are introduced in a context deck, that serves to emulate the changing context of 
the organization. Some if the crisis may actually benefit the organization, as in real life 
changes in the context are not necessarily bad.

4.4.8 Evaluation and selection of alternatives  
In the previous section six game alternatives were presented, from which one of them 
has four variations of the same concept. The first two concepts are abstract games; it 
means that they don’t have a concrete theme. In this alternatives, two players are com-

of strategy to be more efficient than their opponent, or to prevent him/her of obtaining 
points. This racing mechanic in the back of the game also applies to the third alternative, 
in which dogs have to catch the cats before they arrive to the end of the lines. Although 
players can develop some basic strategy to have an advantage over their opponents, 
there are really limited by the set of mechanics that use these game concepts. From one

ingful strategy for players, and on the other hand they fail to address the innovation’s

game, and not by changing the basics of them. From these alternatives, Quest seems to

stages lies in the way the players have to solve the issues they may find while crossing

4.6 Summative evaluation 
In Table 4.2 it was presented an evaluation of the game alternatives. Some of them were 
selected to be prototyped, despites they didn’t address the complete innovation process, 
but had some interesting mechanics ‘worth to try’. But going further from this point, in 
Section 4.4.8 was highlighted that alternatives 1, 2, 3, and 4 were racing games, as the 
players had to arrive to their destination faster than their opponents. In alternatives 1, 2, 
and 3 the destination was the scoring cells, while in alternative 4 the destination was the 
end of the line (for the cats). The displacement of this racing games is based in the same 
mechanics: drawing movement cards in other to move a pawn, or to build a road. Alt-
hough this mechanics has the potential to generate a suspense feeling in players (who 
are hoping to get a specific card from the pile), it may not be the best way to address 
innovation. It is not evident a connection between these alternatives and the innovation 
process, and players failed to stablish a connection between them. In other words, met-
acognition was not developed (Section 3.2.3), and therefor only see that they were col-
lecting points. 
 
On the other hand, the only non-racing game prototyped and tested (save the kittens) 
had a different reception among players. The play experience was more joyful than the 
others, and players felt that the elements of the game allowed them to improve their in-
novative abilities. The elements in this concept address many of the stages of the inno-
vation process, as it was presented in Table 4.2. The core of the game is requesting 
players to solve a task, by using the limited resources. The type of resources that is pro-
vided to them will guide the kind of discussion they are expected to have. During the first 
game, players get two pieces of information: the kitten card, and 2 objects cards; which 
are replaced in the second game by 2 material cards. Although this is a given input, and 
not an enquired one, is used for the players to start defining the problem.  
 
Along the process, discussions support many stages of the innovation process. At the 
beginning they allow the definition of the problem, since the kitten cards do not describe 
a ‘problem’. The kitten card only describes a situation in which a kitten may be in distress, 
but such distress is not mentioned in the card itself. Then, once the problem is defied and 
the idea start to develop, it supports the idea management (what do propose), and the 
process validation (how good are we doing). The development stage is supported by the 
drawing, as both players have to agree in the solution they will present to the other team.

5.1 Reflections on the Design Methods 
Designing games is a task that is closely to handcraft. In many cases, it is the talent of a 
designer who allows him to produce best-seller games. Most of the times, designers 
come out with a theme, and start to do quick-and-dirty prototypes until they have a prod-
uct that can be tested in workshops, where they get have feedback form other designers. 
Nevertheless, designing an educational game is quite different. To start, educational 
games addresses the users from two perspectives: First, he is a learner, and then he is a 
player. Educational games must provide knowledge and joy at the same time, and it is 
design shall be addressed with a stronger methodology than regular games. 
 
While looking for methods to design educational games, it became evident that there was 
a gap between what theory was saying, and the means to produce a functional game. 
Design methods for educational games address only two of the three dimension de-
scribed in Section 3.3.3: Pedagogical approach and normative background. The peda-
gogical approach is described in all methods, as all require to establish a targeted be-
haviour in players to facilitate learning. Also, these methods lack procedural approach, as 
they focus mainly in the structural part, as shown in Figure 3.9. I don’t consider myself a 
gamer, so this kind of methods had some limitations for me, as I need to address the 
design process from a more analytical point of view. On the other hand, design methods 
in engineering can go form very structural to very procedural, so it was natural to com-
plement them in order to have a method that I could use to design the innovation game. 
Cross’ model was selected because it offers a good framework, and the rationale is quite 
simple. Also, the use of an engineering method together to the ones for educational 
games enables to address the third dimension: Gaming background. This third dimension 
groups the morphology used in the generation of alternatives. In this project, the mor-
phology was constituted from the game mechanics, and they were the corner stone to 
the generation of alternatives. The link between as the morphology and design method 
was not addressed in the design methods for serious games, as they were principally 
focus in the pedagogical approach. The Educational game’s space was introduced in 
Figure 3.13.

5.2 Reflections on Innovation processes and alternative generation 
In the early stages of the project, when I started to look for alternatives about the content 
of the game, it seems quite obvious to use the innovation model described in the Inno-
vation Management System. It was an innovation model that separates the activities from 
the innovation process itself, and those who supported innovation from the management. 
For this reason, the first alternatives were designed trying to follow that process, but as 
the project was maturating it became obvious that it was a perspective too narrow for the 
project. Comparing this model with the others in Sections 2.2, 2.3, and 2.4 led to the 
identification of the 10 stages described in the common reference framework (Table 2.2). 
 
Apart from this, I had a bias towards the innovation processes. When I was generating 
alternatives, all of them seemed to be creativity based games. This was not particular bad 
when I was starting to generate the alternatives. At the end of the day, creativity is an 
important part of the innovation process, as it was already discussed along Section 2, 
but as I tried to add more layers to the game, in order to cover all the other stages of the 
innovation process, it seemed that I still had a game that addressed only creativity. This 
may have two possible explanations: 
1. Lack of understanding of the innovation process: In that moment of the project, 
the alternatives were generated from the Management Innovation System model, 
which gave me a narrow perspective of the innovation process. Also as this pro-
cess addressed two different groups of take holders, one alternative that started 
to gain importance was the simulation game. A concept was generated, but it was 
not possible to develop more the idea as simulation games are considerably more 
complex than card games, for example. This led to the second issue: 
2. The chosen mechanics limited the possible alternatives: The design decision was 
to avoid simulation games, as they are considerably more complex than other kind 
of games, which were easier to develop. For this reason, the development focused 
more in card games mechanics, specially making a lot of experiments with com-
binations. This mechanic is very useful to generate a large quantity of possibilities 
from relatively low amount of elements. Nevertheless, the possibilities may be re-
duced to games that address creativity, as it was mainly used to train people to 
adapt to changing circumstances through generation of ideas.

5.3 Further work 
Along this project, there were identified some things that deserve to be developed. One 
of them is the simulation game, as in this report it was assessed its potential to teach 
innovation. It requires to change the morphology from board games to simulation me-
chanics. Also develop games that can support innovation models from fourth and fifth 
generation, as this project was focused only in the first three generations as they are 
easier to gamify due to their lineal structure. To finalize, the proposed design method can 
be developed more, as it was done only one iteration in the length of this project. The 
main issue is related to the process flow, which was not as expected as the resulting 
method adopted directly the key stages of the ones to design educational games. In next 
iterations, the method should be assessed to improve how these stages are connected 
to each other, assuring a smooth process flow.
