Solar Energy Buildings with a High Degree of Self-sufficiency
1
Abstract
The construction industry plays a crucial role in worldwide greenhouse gas emissions, highlighting the
need for the development of energy-efficient buildings and the integration of Renewable Energy Source
(RES) systems within homes to minimize the footprint environmental impact.
This master’s thesis aims to analyse the self-sufficiency of Denmark’s residential sector by designing
an advanced solar energy system tailored to meet the space heating and domestic hot water demand
of single-family buildings. Using TRNSYS software v.18 and SketchUp, the solar energy system and
building envelope elements are developed to comply with the latest Danish building regulations and
energy standards. The study focuses on designing the solar energy system configuration in TRNSYS,
with the building having natural ventilation of 0.3 l/s/m², achieving 31% total solar fraction energy
self-sufficiency through a system comprising 18 PV panels and 12 solar collectors.
However, the key findings highlight that maintaining 0.3 l/s/m² natural ventilation at all times and keeping
the building set-point temperature above 20°C results in a thermal consumption of 18,513 kWh/year from
the heat pump through floor heating. This is considered significantly high and does not meet the BR18
energy framework standards. This master thesis study reveals that by decreasing this ventilation rate
near 0 l/s/m2, it is estimated the heating demand could potentially be reduced to 11,398 KWh/year,
nearly meeting the energy framework threshold of BR18 standards. It is concluded that to meet the BR
standards, further optimization to the TRNSYS model is required, as described in this master’s thesis
study.
Keywords: Danish Building Regulation Code (BR18), zero-energy buildings: building class 2020, solar
RES heating system, single-family building energy self-sufficiency, TRNSYS software, IEA SHC Task 66

Introduction
4.1
Background and motivation
The urgency of addressing climate change is strongly emphasized by the European Commission´s 2030
Climate Target Plan [1] which aims to reduce GHG emissions by at least 55% by 2030 compared to 1990
levels. This commitment from all 27 EU Member States means they will invest 100% of emissions trading
revenues in climate and energy projects, and a new Social Climate Fund of €65 billion from the EU
budget to support vulnerable citizens and small businesses in the green transition, addressing inequality
and energy poverty. The European Green deal, which aims no net emissions of greenhouse gases by 2050,
economic growth decoupled from resource use and no person and no place left behind, is a crucial step
towards achieving climate neutrality by 2050 [2].
Globally, the activities of buildings contribute to 30% of total global final energy consumption and 26%
of worldwide energy-related CO2 emissions. This comprises 8% direct emissions from buildings and 18%
indirect emissions from the production of electricity and heat utilized for DHW and SH within buildings
[3]. To meet net zero emissions by 2050, it is imperative that all newly constructed buildings achieve net
zero status by 2030. Currently, less than 5% of new buildings constructed worldwide meet this criteria.
Buildings within the EU alone account for around 40% of the EU´s overall energy usage and 36% of both
direct and indirect greenhouse gas emissions energy related. By focusing on enhancing energy efficiency
and implementing renewable energy solutions in buildings, not only can their energy efficiency improve,
but it can also play a crucial role in reducing energy expenses for building residential owners and tackling
energy poverty [4].
The EPBD, one of the main policy areas of the Fit for 55 [5] is designed to revise the energy performance
of buildings directive to reduce the amount of carbon emissions produced by buildings. The EPBD pursues
an ambitious energy performance requirements for new and renovated buildings in the EU, encouraging
member states to actively pursue the renovation of their least energy-efficient existing buildings. Aiming
NZEB carbon emissions for all new public buildings from 2026 and all new buildings from 2028. [6]
Thereby, this master thesis project aims to explore and analyze the current state of RES technologies for
their potential employment, considering their environmental implications, cost-effectiveness, technological

4.2
State-of-the-Art Literature
Between 2000 and 2022, the global solar thermal capacity, comprising flat plate unglazed and glazed
solar water collectors, experienced significant growth from 62 GWth (equivalent to 89 million m2) to 542
GWth (equivalent to 774 million m2). This expansion translated into annual solar thermal energy yields
of 51 TWh in 2000 and 442 TWh in 2022. In 2022 alone, approximately 19 GWth or 27.1 million square
meters of collector area were installed. However, after consecutive annual declines in installed collector
area from 2014 to 2019, with a slight increase in 2021, 2022 experienced a decrease of 9.3%. Despite a
general upward trend in sales figures across most economic regions since 2021, this positive trajectory is
dampened by declines in China and India, attributed to factors such as COVID-19 lockdown measures in
China and changes in subsidies and strong competition from PVs in India [7].
Solar energy is recognized as a viable and cost-effective renewable energy source. Despite advancements
in PV and solar thermal technologies, challenges such as decreased efficiency at higher temperatures
and limited space for installation on building rooftops have hindered widespread adoption. However, in
relatively high altitudes in the northern hemisphere, colder weather conditions benefit the solar heating
system, enabling it to outperform the STC conditions of the collectors more effectively than in countries
closer to the equator. Although, PVT collectors systems do also exist and are employed across countries
close to the equator. This technology uses various cooling mediums including air, water, refrigerant as
fluid, with integrated features like HPs and energy storage. Achieving up to 81% efficiency, PVT systems
often involve trade-offs between thermal and electrical performance. [8]
According to increasing PV and solar collectors self-consumption literature [9] , energy transition and
transformation incentivises increasing solar collectors and PV self-consumption with heat pumps for
DHW and SH of buildings. The paper notes that electric energy tariffs for consumers are usually higher
than feed-in tariffs for PV electricity. Thus, maximizing self-consumption of domestic generated PV
electricity is economically beneficial while reducing grid energy consumption. However, self-consumption
measures should be balanced to avoid excessive system energy consumption, which could diminish PV
feed-in. Additionally, solar thermal systems primarily consist of solar collectors, with flat plate collectors
(FPC) and evacuated tube collectors (ETC) being the most prevalent types. In household solar hot water
systems (SHWS), FPCs are commonly preferred due to their affordability and minimal maintenance
requirements. Moreover, the outlet temperature of the collectors typically aligns with household hot
water needs. However, the performance of SHWS fluctuates depending on environmental factors such as
atmospheric temperature, solar radiation, and wind velocity. [10]

In addition, according to EU building stock, over 220 million building units wordlwide, representing 85%

A project developed by the European Commission called TABULA project in 2009 [12] established a
Danish typology for residential buildings, categorizing them into four main types: single family houses,
multi family houses, terraced houses, and apartment blocks. The project focuses three distinct category
types of buildings namely: the normal/standard building, the low-energy building, and the passive
building. While conventional buildings adhere to basic national construction standards, low-energy class
buildings emphasize energy efficiency to decrease consumption, and passive buildings increase the energy
efficiency further by minimizing dependence on active heating and cooling systems.
The Social and Housing Agency Building regulations outlines the standards mandated by the Building Act
and encompass specific energy requirements that construction projects must adhere to. In Denmark, the
latest energy Building Regulations are BR18, originally published in 2018 with the latest update released
in September 2022 by the Social and Housing Agency Building. [13] There are six distinct categories of
construction projects outlined within BR18 i.e. New buildings, change of use, extensions, conversions &
other alterations, replacements of building elements & installations, and reparations. Each category varies
in terms of energy requirements. To ensure compliance with these regulations, it’s essential to categorize
the project correctly and reference the corresponding sections of BR18. Sections § 250 to § 258 of BR18
apply to the first five categories, with additional specific paragraphs designated for each category.
Globally, building operations account for 40% of primary energy consumption and 25% of GHG emissions,
with Europe responsible for 40% of energy usage and 36% of CO2 emissions from buildings.
The
International Energy Agency Solar Heating and Cooling Technology Collaboration Programme (IEA
SHC), task 66: Solar Energy Buildings project focuses on solar energy supply for new and existing buildings.
The purpose is to achieve at least 85% of the heat demand and 60% of the electricity requirements of
single-family buildings in Denmark. A significant reduction in fossil energy demand can be achieved
by integrating solar thermal and photovoltaic energy systems into residential building operations. The
proposed IEA SHC task 66 aims to develop economically viable energy supply concepts with high solar
fractions for various types of buildings in Denmark, as the subject for this master´s thesis. The KPIs
objectives aim to optimize energy system integration, assess RES technology alternatives to provide
cost-effective solar energy solutions.[14]

The Aim of the Master Thesis Project
The aim of this master thesis project is to address the challenge of achieving energy self-sufficiency in
single-family buildings. Considering the framework provided by the IEA SHC Task 66 [14] , the research
focuses on designing and assessing energy systems that incorporate a diverse range of green technologies.
The main objective is to calculate the degree of self-sufficiency attainable for the selected buildings.
Furthermore, the study emphasizes the inclusion of cost considerations to evaluate and compare the
feasibility of various technology solutions, providing a comprehensive analysis of the economic viability
and sustainability of the proposed energy systems.
The study investigation is to be carried out using TRNSYS-18 simulation software. TRNSYS is a versatile
software used to simulate transient systems, primarily focusing on thermal and electrical energy systems.
It is widely used for processing inputs and solving systems, along with a library of around 150 components
modeling various parts of the system. It allows users to modify existing components or create new ones,
making it suitable for researchers and practitioners in the energy field to build up different energy systems
solutions and assess their plausibility. [15]
The focus of this study is based on three study cases: Building 1: New building - BR18 (building class
2020) Voluntary low energy class. Building 2: New building - BR18 Mandatory energy class. Building 3:
Conversions and other alterations (Existing building) - BR18 Mandatory energy class. The three buildings
are defined and considered to have similar construction characteristics to make them comparable.
The purpose of this study is to determine the best suitable solution for a solar heating system that can
efficiently meet the energy consumption demand of the household, including SH, mechanical ventilation
and DHW of a residential building. The KPI targets are aligning with IEA SHC Task 66 project objectives.
This study focuses on a low-energy and passive single-family house located in Denmark, where the
solar heating systems are equipped with a DHW tank which can be heated up using solar collectors.
Furthermore, PV panels are installed to supply electricity to the auxiliary heater of the DHW tank, the
household electricity demand by lighting and electrical equipment. The solar energy systems loop are
operated by integrating a smart controller. The DHW tank can be heated up using electricity from the
grid during off-peak hours, when electricity cost is lower than the average electricity spot market prices
in NordPool. [18]

study focuses on integrating solar energy into the heating system of an existing Danish single-family
building through the installation of a new roof. The building envelope design will be adjusted to comply
with ´Conversions and other alterations´ BR18 mandatory.

6.1
Change in BR for energy demand of single-family buildings 1961-2020
Figure 1: Single-family houses 1961-2020 BR change for energy demand
Between 1961 and 2020, single-family houses experienced significant changes in energy demand (measured
in kWh per square meter of living space), as shown in Figure 1, due to evolving BR aimed at enhancing
buildings’ energy efficiency. Initially, houses constructed before the 1970s typically had poor insulation
and inefficient heating systems, resulting in high energy consumption for heating during winter months.
As awareness of environmental issues grew, building regulations began to prioritize energy efficiency.
In the 1970s and 1980s, the energy crises urged governments to introduce stricter BR codes, leading to
improvements in insulation, heating systems, and overall energy efficiency. This period saw a gradual
decrease in energy demand for heating in newly constructed single-family houses. [19]
By the 1990s and early 2000s, building regulations became even more strict, focusing on energy performance
standards and the integration of RES. This resulted in further reductions in energy demand through
the adoption of technologies such as double-glazed windows, high-efficiency boilers, and increased
insulation.[20]
In recent years, Danish BR standards have continued to evolve, starting from the initial BR61 in the 1960s,
followed by BR72 in the 1970s, BR78 in the 1980s, BR98 in the 2000s, and subsequent updates including
BR06/08, BR10, BR15, up to the current standards of BR18&BR20. These progressive updates reflect
ongoing efforts to address climate change concerns and work towards achieving carbon neutrality goals.
Requirements for passive house standards, net-zero energy buildings, and renewable energy integration
have become more common, leading to even lower energy demand within buildings. [21]

The thermal conductivity of each building element is calculated by multiplying the surface area with the
U-value. This yields the thermal conductivity value, which is then converted into power in watts (W) by
multiplying it with Delta T (temperature difference between the mean interior air temperature and the
typical minimum exterior temperatures in Denmark). According to DS 418:2011 + Supplement 1:2020
[17], section 2 ’Dimensioning minimum Temperatures’ on page 13, the typical outside surface temperatures
for each building element can be found. Based on these assumptions for all building element surfaces, the
exterior surface temperature is typically -12°C, except for the external floor, which is 10°C. Delta T is
therefore calculated by subtracting the mean interior air temperature from the external temperature,
resulting in 32°C for all building elements except 10°C for the external floor. Subsequently, by multiplying
Delta T with the thermal conductivity, the power values are obtained.
Furthermore, the power values are converted to obtain the total heat transmission loss. This is accomplished
by dividing the total thermal conductivity with the total heated building area, resulting in a total heat
transmission loss of 17,396 W/m2 . An alternative method to verify the total heat transmission loss is
by using the BR-18 (voluntary) formula: 11 + 6/E + 300/A, where E = 1 storey and A = 101,444 m²
of heated surface area. According to BR-18 (voluntary) requirements, the total transmission loss factor
must not exceed 19.50 W/m², and 17,396 W/m² is compliant to this regulation.
Even though the U-value requirement is met, if we were to redesign the building to reduce investment
costs by allowing a U-value higher than 19.50 W/m²K, the employment of RES such as solar collectors
must be considered to meet the requirement.
Note that the linear losses around the building, windows and doors are considered. The linear heat
loss refers to the gradual dissipation of heat energy from the interior of the building to the surrounding
environment through all building construction elements. This loss occurs primarily due to thermal
conduction, where heat moves from areas of higher temperature to lower temperature along a linear path.
Factors such as insulation, building materials, and environmental conditions influence the rate of linear
heat loss. Therefore, efficient insulation and building design aim to minimize linear heat loss to improve
energy efficiency. The linear loss around the building provides a value of 0.044 W/m²K, and linear loss for
windows and doors, with a value of 0.013 W/m²K, are taken into account. Additionally, for the external
wall properties, a 0.200-meter insulation thickness of steel brick anchors (4 binders per m²) is considered,
resulting in a U correction factor value of 0.12 W/m²K.

As per the BR18 standards, in residential rooms as well as throughout the residential unit, there must
be a supply of outdoor air present at any time at a minimum rate of 0.30 liters per second per square
meter of heated floor area [l/s m2]. Internal coupling refers to the interrelation between different thermal
zones/rooms within the building facilitated by interior doors. This concept addresses how the 6 doors in
the building, as architectural elements, influence the flow of people, air, light, sound, and even energy
across the building. These values are assumed according to TRNSYS18 manual [15]. Furthermore,
the temperature inside the house, as previously mentioned, influences the thermal conductivity and
temperature differential between the exterior and interior of the house, thereby directly affecting heat
transmission losses. The greater the temperature difference between inside and outside, the higher the
rate of heat transfer, leading to increased heat transmission losses if not properly insulated.
In addition, internal heat gains from human occupancy peak, electrical lights peak, and electrical
equipment peak are considered. These values, assumed from the German passive house standard [24] and
DTU Construct department professors [16], are 1.5, 10, and 3.5 W/m2 respectively. Each internal heat
gain is composed of a radiative and convective portion expressed as a fraction. The radiative energy is
emitted by the direct emission of light from the source, while the convective portion provides indirect
contributions of light through interactions with surfaces and objects within the building. These values
are assumed according to TRNSYS18 manual [15] which complies with EN 13779:2007. Further details
regarding the monthly and annual energy losses are calculated as per the preliminary design of building
envelope elements as shown in ?? within the appendix section.
The thermal inertia capacity of the building refers to its ability to resist fluctuations in temperature
due to its inherent thermal mass, which can absorb and store heat energy. During the day, when
temperatures increase, the building elements and materials absorb heat preventing rapid increases in
indoor temperatures. However, during the night when the outdoor temperature decreases, these materials
release stored heat helping to maintain indoor temperatures.
All the mentioned building factors are input within TRNSYS18, and these are explained further later in

7.3
Material Selection for Building Element Layers
For windows, triple-pane windows are selected to ensure a U-value lower than 0.8 W/m2K , thus a U-value
for the entire window of 0.623 W/m2K, in compliance with the requirement specified in the building class
2020 regulation. The spacer bar length, window frame, and frame correction are considered and included
in calculating the U-value. The space between panes is 6 mm, and the thickness of each pane is 3 mm,
resulting in a total thickness between the interior and exterior of around 2.1 cm.
Internal doors, 6 doors in total, are simply made of wood with a thickness of 4 cm, providing a U value of
1.60 W/m2K. Wood is a traditional choice for interior doors, providing durability, sound insulation, and
a classic aesthetic appeal.
The the external door is selected to ensure a U-value lower than 1.50 W/m2k , thus a U-value of double
pane glazing of 1.163 W/m2K, in compliance with the requirement specified in the building class 2020
regulation. It is comprised of a multi-chambered PVC material, polymerizing vinyl chloride. This
design enhances its thermal performance ensuring minimal heat transfer through the door, maintaining
comfortable indoor temperatures and minimizing energy loss. This material is known for its effective
U-values typically ranging between 1.0 to 1.3 W/m²K [25].
The adjacent ceiling, or fake interior roof, is 0.05 meters thick and made of PVC material, providing
a U-value of 0.429 W/m2K. The fake interior roof provides a gap of 0.300 meters between the ceiling
and the interior of the house. This gap serves to provide space for electrical cabling, ventilation and
additional insulation between the rooms and external roof, enhancing the overall comfort and energy
efficiency of the building.
The external walls are constructed with five layers, starting from the outer surface: brick, steel brick
anchors for support, mineral wool insulation for thermal efficiency, gas/aerated concrete for structural
strength, and gypsum plaster for interior finishing. These layers provide a total insulation thickness
of 0.527 meters and a U-value of 0.103 W/m2K, which is lower than 0.30 W/m2K and therefore in
compliance with the requirement specified in the building class 2020 regulation.
The adjacent walls consist of a sandwich layer configuration, comprising three layers, which includes
double layers of gypsum plaster/board with mineral wool insulation and wood anchoring the wall for
support. This design offers great thermal insulation properties while also providing structural stability
and soundproofing benefits. With an overall insulation thickness of 0.150 meters, the walls achieve a
U-value of 0.267 W/m2K.
The external floor , also known as the ground floor, is composed of seven layers. Starting from the outer
surface, these layers include a capillary layer LECA, urethane foam, a concrete slab layer, wood (spruce
pine), mineral wool, plywood, and a parquet floor. This combination of materials provides structural
support, moisture protection, thermal insulation, and a finished surface suitable for walking on. With an
overall insulation thickness of 0.850 meters, the ground floor achieves a low U-value of 0.088 W/m2K,
which is lower than 0.20 W/m2K thus in compliance with the requirement specified in the building class
2020 regulation.
The external roof, also known as the loft, is constructed with three layers. These layers, starting from the
outer surface, consist of roof tiles and mineral wool, followed by mineral wool, wood with mineral wool,
and gypsum plaster. With an overall insulation thickness of 0.419 meters, the roof achieves an effective
U-value of 0.129 W/m2K which is lower than 0.20 W/m2K and therefore compliant with the building
class 2020 regulation.

Space heating, ventilation and DHW consumption
BR18 regulations establish consumption thresholds for residential space heating, ventilation, cooling, and
domestic hot water regarding the building typologies that are to be studied.
The first scenario , Building 1: New building - BR18 Voluntary (referred to as building class 2020) low
energy class, establishes both a higher energy-efficient classification and lower consumption levels than
BR18 mandatory. This regulation is voluntary, allowing building construction contractors to promote
buildings with reduced energy demand. The optional guideline, outlined in Chapter 25 of BR18, states
"Residential units, halls of residence, hotels, etc., may be classified as building class 2020 if the total
demand for energy supply for heating, ventilation, cooling, and domestic hot water per square meter of
heated floor area does not exceed 20.0 kWh/m2 per year" [21]. Thus, assuming an average single-family
house new building in Denmark with a heated floor area of 120 m2, the maximum yearly energy demand
for heating, ventilation, cooling, and DHW of 2400 kWh is obtained. This represents a significant reduction
of 47.83% compared to BR18 mandatory regulation, highlighting the substantial capacity these low-energy
class buildings offer. A summary breakdown of BR18 Voluntary (building class 2020) specifications are
defined later on under "Buildings construction elements" section of this report.
The second scenario, Building 2: New building - BR18 Mandatory energy class, is a mandatory
regulation that all newly constructed Danish buildings must comply with. The guideline outlined in
Chapter 11 within BR18 states: "For residential units, halls of residence, hotels, etc., the total energy
supply demand of the building for heating, ventilation, cooling, and domestic hot water per square meter
of heated floor area may not exceed 30.0 kWh/square meter per year plus 1,000 kWh per year divided by
the heated floor area" [21]. Similarly to the first scenario, assuming an average single-family house new
building in Denmark with a heated floor area of 120 m2, the maximum yearly energy demand for heating,
ventilation, cooling, and DHW of 4,600 kWh is obtained.
The third scenario, Building 3: Conversions and other alterations (Existing building) - BR18 Mandatory
energy class, the guideline outlined in Chapter 11 within BR18 states: "Renovation class 1 when the
total energy supply demand for heating, ventilation, cooling and domestic hot water per sq. metre heated
floor area does not exceed 52.5 kWh/sq. metre per year plus 1,650 kWh per year divided by the heated
floor area". Therefore, for a single-family house heated floor area of 120 m2, the maximum yearly energy
demand for heating, ventilation, cooling, and DHW of 7,950 kWh is obtained.

windows, furniture, etc. to evaluate the energy performance and efficiency of the building.

lighting during the day and throughout different seasons, ensuring energy efficiency and occupant
comfort. No daylight was considered for simplifying the study.

Figure 12 simulation studio of the solar heating system with solar collectors and PV panels consists of
various components interconnected to simulate the operation of the system. These components include:
• Solar Collectors: These represent the panels that capture solar radiation and convert it into heat
energy. Parameters such as collector type, orientation, area, and efficiency are defined within the
manufacturer specification sheet.
• Solar PV panels: convert sunlight into electricity using semiconductor materials. When sunlight
hits the panel, it excites electrons in the semiconductor, creating an electric current. This electricity
can be used directly by the building electricity demand.
• Storage Tanks: These components simulate the storage of DHW tank or heat transfer fluid generated
by the solar collectors. The DHW tank size, insulation properties, and heat losses are defined to
meet the energy demand.
• Pumps: these simulate the circulation of the heat transfer fluid between the solar collectors and the
DHW tank, ensuring optimal heat transfer.
• Diverter and mixer valves: flow diverter splits a single inlet liquid stream into two liquid outlet
streams according to a user-specified valve setting, while the mixer tee piece combines two inlet
liquid streams into a single liquid outlet stream.
• Weather Data (Copenhagen) Input: Users can input weather data such as solar radiation, ambient
temperature, and wind speed to simulate real-world conditions.
• Controller: manages the operation of the components such as pumps, valves, and sensors to ensure
optimal performance. It regulates parameters like flow rates, temperature set-points, and system
switching based on inputs from sensors and predefined algorithms.
• Load profile: represents the DHW demand profile over a specified time period. It characterizes the
varying heating or hot water requirements of a building throughout the day or year, influencing the
energy system’s operation and their size.
• Valve control: it regulates the flow of heat transfer fluid (such as water or glycol) through different
parts of the system. The valve control diverts the water flow between collectors, storage tanks, and
heat exchanger to optimize energy transfer, manage temperature differentials, and ensure efficient
operation of the system.
• Heat exchanger: enables efficient heat transfer between the two fluid circuits: one for the solar
fluid and the other for the domestic water, while maintaining physical separation. Inside the heat
exchanger, the solar fluid transfers its thermal energy to the heat exchanger’s and gradually cools
down.

by solar energy in this case, without relying on external sources such as electricity from the grid. It is
crucial to evaluate the dimensions of the energy system components, aiming to achieve as much energy
self-sufficiency as possible.
The formula used to calculate the total solar fraction is as follows:
fsol =
(EP V,tot −EP V,grid + QST,tot −QST,grid∗)
(EP V,tot −EP V,grid + Egrid + QST,tot −QST,grid∗+ Qh,c −Qh,el)
(6)

District Heating System. No district heating network system is considered, therefore 0 KWh is assummed.

the building’s thermal energy demand, reducing reliance on non-renewable heating sources and lowering
energy costs. While a low supply cover factor suggests limitations in the solar thermal system’s ability
to meet heating demand efficiently, resulting in higher energy consumption and operating costs. In this
study, as there is no consideration for a district heating network, thermal energy isn’t transferred into
district heating. Consequently, this KPI isn’t applicable to the scope of the master’s thesis but can be
further investigated as future work.

Conclusion
This study on Solar Energy Buildings with a High Degree of Self-sufficiency evaluated the 11 KPIs from
IEA SHC Task 66 to assess the effectiveness of the parameters defined and implemented in scenario 1 -
voluntary building class 2020 single-family building design. This investigation provides valuable insights
into the performance of the designed solar heating system and highlights the impact of having a relatively
high natural ventilation rate of 0.3 l/s/m2 in single-family buildings. It also demonstrates the potential
use of an advanced software like TRNSYS to develop and optimize various aspects of both the building
construction elements and the solar energy system.
TRNSYS simulation results demonstrate significant benefits for single-family buildings from Passive Solar
Gains (QSP,gain) through their windows, with the highest gains in the living room and kitchen (2,889
kWh). Rooms facing south with larger window areas receive the most sunlight, especially during summer,
contributing to natural heating and lighting. The lowest gains are in the technical room (89 kWh) due
to its north-facing orientation and smaller window area configuration. Furthermore, the overall annual
heat gains are dominated by solar gains (62.37%), followed by electrical equipment (20.08%), electrical
lighting (13.57%), and occupancy (3.98%). This indicates that the most significant impact on reducing
the building’s heating demand beside the solar energy gains are from the usage of electrical equipment
devices.
In addition, regarding the solar energy system designed for the designed building available roof area
achieves a maximum annual Total Solar Fraction (fsol) of 31%. This value, achieved with a solar system
including 18 PV panels and 12 solar collectors, can be increased significantly by reducing the thermal
and electrical consumption of the HP. This can be achieved by decreasing the natural ventilation of the
building from 0.3 l/s/m2 to 0 l/s/m2 , leading to a higher conservation temperatures inside the building.
In contrast, the maximum annual solar fraction thermal (fsol,th) achieved is 75%, indicating efficient use
of the solar thermal system for heating purposes. An additional storage tank could be added to maintain
a smoother thermal energy efficiency throughout the year, increasing the self-sufficiency, while allowing to
reduce the number of solar collectors in the system. This would avoid wasting the useful thermal energy
generated especially during colder months from the solar collectors while enhancing energy self-sufficiency.
Additionally, the solar fraction electrical (fsol,el) achieved is 38%, highlighting the need to decrease the
electrical consumption from the HP operation by reducing the building ventilation rate as previously
discussed. Alternatively, employing a battery energy storage system (ESS) sized to match the Egrid would
help to meet the system’s electricity demand and reduce reliance on the electricity grid, thus increasing
the overall degree of energy self-sufficiency up to 100%.
Even though the Supply Cover Factor for Solar Thermal Use (SCF,ST) was not fully applicable due to
the lack of a district heating network, this option could be investigated in future work. This suggests
that with an appropriate network infrastructure, the building could benefit significantly from the district
heating network through increased efficiency, use of space, maintenance savings, etc. Moreover, the Supply
Cover Factor for PV Solar Use (SCF,PV) resulted in 100% of the electricity been used by the building
and solar energy system.
In regards to the environmental aspect of Non-renewable Final Energy Saved shows a substantial amount
of non-RES by substituting coal and natural gas from the energy mix with solar energy, offering a total
replaced use of 122,915 KWh/a by the 18 PVs / 12 SCs system configuration. By replacing non-RES
with this solar energy system, the building offers the potential to avoid approximately 161 tons of CO2
emissions annually. This reduction in GHG emissions highlights the environmental benefits of solar energy
systems, highlighting the building’s positive impact on mitigating climate change.
However, simulation results show that as per the BR18 regulations compliance, scenario 1 provides 2,692
KWh/a which demonstrates it meets the BR18 standards Electricity demand threshold of 2700 KWh/a,
while the DHW, SH and ventilation it´s exceeded by a fairly large amount of 18,045 KWh of energy
beyond the threshold of 2,400 KWh/a for the low Energy Voluntary class. Although, by adding the
annual DHW demand of 1931 KWh/a this results in a total energy framework of 19,983 KWh/a, and
subtracting a maximum allowance of 3,125 KWh/a energy supply from RES this results in a total of

Further work
The scope of this thesis is limited by time constraints, which restrict the investigation of number
of system combinations that can be explored to optimize the solar heating system components and
identify the best optimal solution. However, there is potential for designing and evaluating additional
combinations/scenarios further that could lead to more optimized solar energy system solutions regarding
their energy performance and economic feasibility. This master thesis study helps as a starting point in
TRNSYS tool for further investigations focusing on new residential energy system configurations.
Potential areas to explore in future research could involve:
• To meet the energy framework for the voluntary class building, it is important that future work focuses
on decreasing the 0.3 l/s/m² natural ventilation near 0 l/s/m². Although, ventilation can be employed by
the family building occupants by opening the window when necessary to keep a pleasant temperature in
the building. KPI results and sensitivity analysis of this master study are based on the inclusion of 0.3
l/s/m² building ventilation at all times. Therefore, the ventilation should be modified to obtain updated
simulation results for SHC task 66 KPIs and assess the compliance to the BR18 standards. In addition,
to comply with the energy framework of scenario 1 building standards, a closer analysis of the building
envelope elements design should be conducted in TRNSYS software to further reduce the energy envelope
transmission losses.
• Optimization of the model by incorporating a battery Energy Storage System (ESS) for better
management of surplus electrical energy generated by PV panels and the grid to increase the overall
degree of energy self-sufficiency. Once the battery ESS is incorporated in TRNSYS system, size the
optimal number of PV panels and solar collectors to be installed in the solar heating system.
• Follow the same process been done for scenario 1 and define the building envelope elements within
TRNBuild according to their respective Building Regulations 2018 (BR18) for building scenarios 2 and 3.
Obtain simulation results for the same IEA Task 66 - KPIs to identify discrepancies among the three
buildings types, even including a return of investment financial analysis including electricity prices as
defined in one of the section of this report.
• Inclusion of more building factors in TRNBuild comprising comfort and daylight control typologies,
aiming to maximize energy gains and optimize the balance between natural and artificial lighting
throughout different seasons.
Overall, further research could contribute to the development of innovative systems that optimize the use
of solar energy, while increasing the level of self-sufficiency in the residential sector, not only useful in
Denmark but can be applicable in other countries worldwide.
