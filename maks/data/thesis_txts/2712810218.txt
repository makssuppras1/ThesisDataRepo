Abstract
Ironing of stainless steel is prone to galling on the backstroke due to sliding action under
high contact pressure. New ways to reduce galling are constantly being studied to prolong
tool life and reduce maintenance. The goal of this project was to develop a functional proto-
type of an ironing punch with adjustable diameter that is capable of considerably reducing
contact pressure and thereby galling on the backstroke. Also, the punch could potentially
be used to mitigate the relatively large part height variation caused by small variations in
sheet thickness. Numerical analysis was used to determine the diameter change required
to eliminate contact on the backstroke. It was found that the required diameter change is
proportional to punch diameter, blank thickness and thickness reduction, but the diameter
change relative to the punch diameter decreases when the punch diameter is increased. Ap-
proximately 0.3% of radial (and circumferential) strain is required to substantially reduce
the sliding length during backstroke for most studied cases. The designed punch prototype,
which features a retractable mandrel and internal slitting for controlling radial stiffness, was
tested using axisymmetric cups deep drawn of cold-rolled 1 mm thick EN 1.4404 austenitic
stainless steel. The adjustable punch was found to be well-comparable with a conventional
ironing punch if the mandrel is not retracted. In cases where the mandrel was retracted
during the backstroke, the work required to pull the adjustable punch out of the cup was
45 −55% lower compared to a conventional punch and the quality of ironed surfaces was
improved. The punch diameter and specimen inner diameter can safely be increased by
approximately 15−18 µm by applying a torque to the actuator screw. CMM measurements
of the ironed specimens showed that the repeatability of ironed geometries is comparable to
a conventional ironing punch when an adjustable punch is used, but the deformation during
the backstroke is reduced and that shall be taken into account when designing parts for
ironing with an adjustable punch.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Ironing is a demanding forming process due to high contact pressures between the punch
and the workpiece. The high pressures combined with sliding movement cause galling, which
leads to defects on the surface of the part. The sheet metal forming team at Grundfos has
observed that galling mostly occurs on the backstroke, where the axial punch force is much
lower compared to the forming stroke, during which the workpiece is deformed to a higher
degree.
The parts manufactured by Grundfos require multiple forming steps such as blanking,
deep drawing, redrawing and ironing, which are performed in progressive dies. The material
is fed into the press from a coil with a suitable width and the finished parts - mostly axisym-
metric cups - exit the press at the other end. However, the part height is heavily influenced
by small variations in the sheet material thickness. Currently, with a non-adjustable punch,
a blank thickness variation of ±45 µm leads to a part height variation of about ±1 mm. This
variation of height affects the part function more than the small variation in wall thickness
and gaining better control over it is desirable.
A punch with an adjustable diameter can be used to compensate for the variation in
blank thickness and produce parts with consistent length. Such solution would ultimately
require closed-loop control, where the coil is measured before entering the progressive die
and the tool diameter is changed accordingly at the right moment.
The solution space for solving these problems includes various options.
Firstly, the
force on the backstroke could be somewhat reduced by removing the part from the die
before pulling out the punch. This, however, can create problems with buckling in the parts,
especially in those with thin walls. Furthermore, this approach would create unnecessary
wear to the ironing die and would leave the problem of varying coil thickness unsolved.
Another option is to develop an ironing die with an adjustable diameter.
Such die
could potentially compensate for the coil thickness variation, but has limited effect on the
backstroke force, since springback in the part and punch would still generate contact pressure
between them.
The next logical option would be an ironing punch, the diameter of which is adjustable.

1.2. Deep Drawing and Ironing
This punch is the first and simplest solution that is theoretically capable of solving both
issues.
To solve the problems, the three discussed concepts can be combined, but it is not
desired due to higher complexity and here also due to limited time available. Therefore,
the most appealing option for solving the two problems is to develop an ironing punch with
adjustable diameter.
Grundfos in collaboration with Technical University of Denmark (DTU) decided to
launch a Master’s project with the aim of studying the feasibility of developing an ironing
punch with an adjustable diameter. For Grundfos, using such tool would lead to numerous
benefits. The adjustability would reduce wear of the contact surfaces, thereby prolonging
tool life and reducing maintenance costs and increasing the quality of the ironed surfaces.
It also has the potential to fulfill Grundfos’ wish of reducing dimensional variation and
establishing better control and a higher degree of automation in the process.
1.2
Deep Drawing and Ironing
Metal forming processes are usually divided into bulk and sheet forming operations [1,
p.3]. Bulk forming operations such as forging, extrusion, rolling and drawing are character-
ized by large plastic deformation of workpieces that initially have a low surface-to-volume
ratio. The plastic deformation is generally used to substantially change the cross-section
of the workpiece.
Another characteristic of bulk forming processes is the relatively low
magnitude of elastic recovery compared to the plastic deformation.
Sheet forming processes are characterized by three aspects [1, p.3]. Firstly, the starting
material is a sheet or a part manufactured from a sheet, which implies a high surface-
to-volume ratio. Secondly, the deformation is used to cause changes in shape, while the
resulting changes in cross-section are usually small and not desirable. Finally, in some cases,
the magnitudes of plastic deformation and elastic recovery (springback) are comparable.
Some examples of sheet metal forming processes are deep drawing, hemming, hydroforming,
bending and blanking [1, p.10].
This project focuses on developing and testing novel tooling for the ironing process.
During ironing, the wall of a sheet metal workpiece is thinned and smoothened, since the
clearance between the punch and the die is lower than the initial wall thickness [2, p.416].
Ironing is classified as a hybrid metal forming process, because it has characteristics of both
bulk and sheet forming [1, p.4, 23].
1.2.1
Deep Drawing of Axisymmetric Cups
Box-shaped and axisymmetric cups are some of the most common geometries manufac-
tured by deep drawing (Figure 1.1). For the latter, the process usually starts with a round
blank, which is held between the die and the blankholder. The punch is then moved into
the die thereby forming the blank. There are many important parameters in deep drawing
such as material properties (strength coefficient, strain-hardening coefficient, planar and nor-
mal anisotropy), friction conditions, punch speed (vP) and blankholder force (FBH). Also,

During deep drawing, the area near the outer edge of the blank experiences compressive
circumferential stresses and tends to wrinkle. Applying sufficiently high blankholder force
can prevent wrinkling and cause the flange to thicken instead due to conservation of volume.
However, increasing the blankholder force also increases the axial punch force and axial
stresses in the cup wall, ultimately causing a fracture in the cup wall near the bottom
rounded edge. The failure takes place near the bottom round, because the material in the
cup walls has been bent and unbent when flowing into the die and strain hardened as a
result, whereas the material near the bottom has been deformed less and is weaker. The
limit of wrinkling and the limit of fracture - both expressed by draw depth as a function of
the blankholder force - define the process window for deep drawing. An empirical expression
can be used to estimate the blankholder pressure, pBH, required to avoid wrinkling:

1.2. Deep Drawing and Ironing
The draw ratio (DR) is essentially a measure of the cup height. The limiting draw
ratio (LDR) is defined as the maximum DR that can be achieved in deep drawing without
fracture of the cup [1, p.108]. The theoretical LDR is e ≈2.72, but practically achievable
values lie in the range of 2.0 - 2.2 [3, pp.119-122]. LDR can be increased by decreasing
the friction coefficient between the blankholder-blank and blank-die pairs and increasing the
friction between the blank and the punch. Similar effects can be achieved by reducing the
blankholder force or increasing the punch or die corner radius [1, p.108][3, p.122].
The walls of deep drawn cups are not cylindrical and thinner near the bottom compared
to the opening. Ironing is often used to increase the efficiency of material usage by thinning
the cup walls and making the wall thickness more uniform. Ironing also reduces the residual
stresses present in the cup walls after deep drawing [2, p.416].
1.2.2

One of the biggest differences between ironing and deep drawing tools is the magnitude of
punch-die clearance, uD. In ironing, the punch-die gap is smaller than the sheet thickness,
so the cup wall is thinned during the process and the wall thickness becomes essentially
constant as a result [2, p.416]. Thickness reduction (TR) is hereby defined as follows:

Ironing can be performed either on the inside or the outside surface of the deep drawn
cup. During outer ironing, the thickness reduction is achieved using a specially shaped die,
whereas the punch is similar to the one used in deep drawing. On the contrary, when ironing
on the inside, the die is cylindrical and the punch features an ironing nose, which deforms
the cup. The two ironing methods are presented graphically in Figure 1.2. An increase in
cup height can be seen in both cases, because the volume of the blank remains constant
during the process.
Ironing on the outside requires a die with a geometry that gradually decreases the cup
wall thickness. This way of ironing is advantageous, because it does not require a blankholder
and it allows for multiple ironing dies to be placed in series to reach very high thickness
reductions [3, p.127] as is done with aluminium beverage cans, for example [2, p.418]. A
great advantage of this type of ironing is that there is no need for a backstroke, because the
straight cup can be driven through the die completely and ejected. Ejecting the part can
be performed by an ejection pin inside the punch or a ring around the punch. Cups with
flanges can only be ironed partly, leaving a section with a thicker wall near the cup opening,
which adds more constraints to the part design.
Ironing the inner surface with a punch is more complicated due to the need for a
blankholder and a flange. The blankholder force shall be sufficiently high to avoid drawing
the flange into the die and precise control over that force is not necessary. The punch stroke
is limited by the cup bottom and a thicker wall section is left near the bottom of the cup,
which is advantageous, since the material in this area is strain hardened less during deep

Inner ironing includes a backstroke, where the punch is in contact with the blank and the

Materials for Cold Forming Tools
Tools for high-precision forming, shaping and cutting operations at ordinary or elevated
temperatures are most often made of various tool steels. While there is no internationally
agreed definition of tool steels, they can all be characterized by three elements [4, pp.1-3].
Firstly, the properties of all tool steels can be greatly enhanced by heat treatments. Secondly,
the requirement for superior properties of tool steels necessitates special manufacturing pro-
cesses and conditions for these materials. Finally, tool steels are mainly used to make tools
for shaping, cutting, forming and blanking operations for both metallic and non-metallic
materials regardless of the temperature. Tool steels are often divided to groups by their

application: cold-work steels, hot-work steels, plastic mold steels and high speed cutting
steels.
Tools for cold forming operations must be able to avoid plastic deformation, resist both
adhesive and abrasive wear and cracking [4, pp.145-160]. Hardness of > 60 HRC is required
for forming high strength materials. High yield strength which is the basis for high hardness
is desirable for cold work tool steels, since these properties together ensure high resistance
to plastic deformation and wear. To avoid plastically deforming the tools, the yield strength
should be higher than the tensile strength of the workpiece material. To improve the wear
resistance even further, the presence of hard particles - either added as a powder or formed
in situ during solidification - is preferable.
Those particles should be sufficiently large,
stiff, strong and evenly distributed in the steel matrix to achieve the highest resistance to
wear. However, steels with high strength and hardness commonly have low toughness due
to the martensitic microstructure that forms during low temperature (200℃) tempering. To
mitigate this, tempering of martensite at approximately 500℃−600℃is used.
1.3
Galling
Galling is a wear phenomenon often encountered in sheet metal forming, where sliding
action at high contact pressures is present. The precise definition of galling is somewhat
ambiguous in literature. Dohda et al. [5] and Schedin [6] limit the definition of galling to
the transfer of the workpiece material to the tool surface. On the other hand, Olsson [7]
defines galling as the combination of pickup and scoring. Pickup - a form of adhesive wear
- is the adhesion of the workpiece material to the tool surface. Consequently, the picked-up
material hardens as a result of severe deformation and oxidation of virgin surfaces. Scoring
- a form of abrasive wear - occurs, when these hard particles scratch the workpiece ruining
the surface and affecting process conditions. Scoring can also occur as three-body abrasive
wear, when the hardened particles are broken loose from the tool during subsequent strokes.
Regardless of the definition of galling, a thorough understanding of the initiating mech-
anisms is necessary to reduce its effect. Archard’s model of adhesive wear [8] can be used to
estimate the volume of worn material, W:
W = b · FCL

Schedin [6] studied the galling mechanisms in sheet forming on zinc-coated deep-drawing
steels, austenitic stainless steels and brass. His findings stress the importance of tool surface
defects such as grinding scratches on the initiation of galling. These defects act as initiation
sites for galling, because they create a plastic wave in the workpiece material, which causes
accumulation of material in the tool surface asperities. The accumulated material creates a
transfer layer, that was found to have a critical thickness after which parts of it start cracking
off and causing abrasive wear. The growth of the transfer layer is generally a relatively long

and stable process, but after reaching its critical thickness, the processes become more rapid.
He concluded that completely avoiding galling is practically impossible for most commonly
used materials in sheet forming operations and emphasis should be put on slowing down
the growth of the transfer layer. In a separate study, Schedin and Lehtinen [9] showed that
applying lubricants is one possible method for slowing down the growth rate of the transfer
layer.
Archard’s wear model and Schedin’s study provide sufficient basis to describe the current
state-of-the-art of methods used to reduce galling. Since high quality of the tool surface is
essential, grinding and polishing the contact surfaces to Ra < 0.1 µm is standard practice
for Grundfos’ ironing tools and was also seen in [9].
The majority of ironing tools are
axisymmetric, so turning is the preferred method for manufacturing. That implies that the
polishing and grinding scratches are perpendicular to the sliding direction between the tool
and the workpiece. Polishing the contact surfaces parallel to the tool axis might reduce the
growth rate of the transfer layer and inhibit galling, because the created plastic wave would
be substantially reduced. However, creating this texture is also a more complex process,
especially if tight tolerances are required.
On the contrary, surface texture perpendicular to the sliding direction can be beneficial
for reducing friction since the micro-scale grooves act as lubricant reservoirs. Sulaiman et al.
[10] studied the influence of tool texture to the friction and lubrication using a strip reduction
test, which closely replicates the tribological conditions in ironing. They used die inserts with
varying distances between shallow (ca 7 µm) pockets filled with lubricant and found that
an optimum distance between the pockets exists, where the drawing load is the lowest. The
lower the distance between the pockets, the more the drawing load is increased by the plastic
wave created in the workpiece material. On the other hand, small distance between pockets
improves the lubrication, because the material drags some of the lubricant out of the grooves
when sliding and lubricates the flat areas. The influence of scratches parallel to the sliding
direction to galling has not been studied, but it is reasonable to suggest that if the surface
texture is parallel to the sliding direction the lubricant may escape the high-pressure zone
and cause metal-to-metal contact, which speeds up the adhesive wear.
Various surface treatment techniques are used to prevent galling in sheet metal forming.
Podgornik et al. [11] studied the effect of five different physical vapor deposition (PVD)
coatings - TiN, TiB2, V N, TaC and Diamond-Like Carbon (DLC) - using Vanadis 4
(Uddeholm) tool steel as the substrate and austenitic stainless steel, titanium and aluminium
alloys as workpiece materials. The effect of the coatings was compared to two Vanadis 4 tool
steel substrates - one hardened and the other nitrided. The findings show that carbon-based
coatings (TaC and DLC) give excellent protection against galling for austenitic stainless
steels.
Furthermore, low tool surface roughness was found to reduce the probability of
stainless steel transfer, which confirms Schedin’s findings discussed before.
Saketi and Olsson [12] studied the pickup of AISI 316L austenitic stainless steel on
cemented carbide substrates with a chemical vapor deposition (CVD) or a PVD coating.
They observed that the substrate surface quality has a notable effect on the amount of
picked-up material and suggested that polishing the substrate both before and after coating
would reduce the amount of material transfer.
In addition to PVD coatings, CVD and duplex coatings are also used in the industry. For

The main impact of coatings is the increase of tool surface hardness and reduction of
friction coefficient. However, the dimensionless constant in Equation 1.3 is also a function
of the lubrication conditions. Generally, viscous oils containing additives with polar groups
are used as lubricants [13, p.292]. Andreasen et al. [14] conducted a screening experiment
to study the performance of various lubricants using a strip reduction test with EN 1.4301
stainless steel. They studied chlorinated and non-chlorinated mineral oils and polymer-based
emulsions - all of which contained extreme pressure additives - and some experimental oils.
Of all the studied lubricants, chlorinated mineral oils showed the best performance. However,
chlorinated paraffin oils have been found to cause adverse effects for the environment [15] and
have been added to the candidate list of substances of very high concern for authorisation
in Europe [16], meaning that their industrial use is becoming more limited.
The viscocity of lubricants decreases with increasing temperature, reducing their per-
formance. According to Dohda et al. [5], heat is generated mainly from two sources during
ironing - friction and plastic deformation of the workpiece. They summarized that in ad-
dition to lubricant performance decrease, high temperatures also speed up diffusion, which
increases the rate of workpiece material pick-up.
The industrial partner has observed that galling often occurs on the backstroke, where
the deformation of the cup wall is relatively low. Üstünyagiz [17] studied this phenomenon
and reported that a significant portion of the total thickness reduction during inner iron-
ing takes place on the backstroke (20% on the forming stroke and 4% on the backstroke).
Furthermore, a numerical analysis of the two strokes revealed that the maximum contact
pressure is about twice as high on the backstroke (2050 MPa) compared to the forming
stroke (1050 MPa). The increased contact pressure was explained with the cup material be-
ing severely strain-hardened during the forming stroke, the contact area between the punch
and the blank being substantially smaller on the backstroke and the elastic contraction of
the die. The higher contact pressure and the fact that an insufficient amount of lubricant
is left on the ironed surface after the forming stroke are the main reasons why galling is
commonly observed during the backstroke.
Analysing Archard’s wear model once more from the perspective of the ironing process,
it turns out that the only option that is not widely applied to reduce galling is the reduction
of contact load. This project aims to study the feasibility of galling reduction through a
reduction in contact loads.

1.4. Flexible Metal Forming Tools
1.4
Flexible Metal Forming Tools
Flexibility and controllability of metal forming tools is desirable, because it has great
potential for reducing tooling costs and increasing productivity of metal forming processes.
While some sheet forming processes such as incremental forming, are inherently flexible [2,
p.388], they suffer from low production rate when compared to processes used in progressive
dies like drawing, stamping, ironing and coining, for example. Combining the advantages
of flexibility with the high production rate of progressive dies would reduce scrap rate and
considerably improve the efficiency of the manufacturing system.
Cao et al. [18] summarized the efforts towards controllability and flexibility of next
generation stamping dies in 2001. They mentioned the concept of a segmented die with local
adaptive controllers, which can be used to provide additional constraints to some areas of
the sheet during forming. For example, this improves forming of tailor-welded blanks, which
is of large interest in the automotive sector due to the possibility of combining materials
with different properties and thicknesses to one piece before forming [2, p.393].
The currently imperfect knowledge about the forming processes and uncertainties caused
by variation of process parameters as pointed out by Allwood et al. [19] demonstrate the
usefulness of flexibility of tool geometries. They also pointed out that closed-loop control of
metal forming tools is studied relatively little compared to other manufacturing processes
like electrical discharge machining (EDM), milling and others. As major problems for using
closed-loop control in metal forming they pointed out the non-linearity of the processes and
the difficulties of directly measuring important process parameters. The adjustability of
major process parameters inside progressive dies is currently very limited, so there is little
incentive to implement closed-loop control.
Yang et al.
[20] reviewed various studies demonstrating flexibility of metal forming
tools and pointed out the demand for increasing mass customization of products. Various
incremental forming methods were summarized that allow for altering the product geometry
without the need for dedicated tooling. For example, a tooling system for hot rolling of
I-beams with varying geometry was pointed out as a way to achieve products that are more
efficient in their material use. Digitized die forming was brought as an example of a flexible
manufacturing process that is the most suitable for a progressive die compared to other
reviewed methods. The tools for digitized die forming consist of matrices of punches, the
heights of which are controlled by a computer, so deviations from the nominal geometry can
be reduced in situ. However, the surface of this tool is discontinuous, which degrades the
part surface quality.
All in all, flexibility in metal forming is an active area of research and most of the
effort is put on improving processes with a relatively low production rate. Flexibility in
processes suitable for high-volume manufacturing in progressive dies has not been pursued
according to the current state-of-the-art, so the current work is among the first attempts to
considerably improve the flexibility and controllability of a metal forming process used in
progressive dies.

1.5. Problem Statement and Constraints
1.5
Problem Statement and Constraints
This project attempts to prove the feasiblity of solving two issues often present in
ironing:
• Galling on the backstroke during inner ironing of stainless steel cups causes costly
downtime in manufacturing and requires frequent tool maintenance;
• The random variation of blank thickness within its tolerance zone leads to a substan-
tial height variation of the ironed part. The part height is often functionally more
important than the material thickness and controlling it to a higher degree is desired.
The ability to adjust the diameter of the ironing punch can potentially solve both of the
problems and adjustable diameter ironing tools are not currently used in the industry. This
project is focused on developing a functional prototype that is capable of demonstrating that
the listed problems can be solved and provide basis for further research and development.
The requirements for the tool can be divided to two groups: short-term and long-term
requirements. The former can interpreted as the success criteria for this project, whereas
the latter are requirements that the solution(s) developed in this project must be adaptable
to.
The short-term requirements for this project are:
• The punch must be able to perform a 30% thickness reduction (calculated from nominal
blank thickness) in a deep drawn cup made of EN 1.4404 (AISI 316L) stainless steel;
• The punch must be able to demonstrate the decrease of the axial punch force on the
backstroke when compared to a non-adjustable punch as that is a measure of decreased
contact loads;
• The punch-blank contact interface geometry of the prototype must be continuous in
order to achieve a quality of ironed surfaces that is comparable with conventional
ironing tools;
• The prototype must be able to demonstrate the adjustability of punch diameter;
• The principle of diameter change must be feasible in an industrial environment;
The long-term goals that the developed solution must be adaptable to:
• The magnitude of diameter change must be sufficient to eliminate contact forces on
the backstroke;
• The punch shall be capable of changing diameter fast enough to accommodate 200 strokes
min ,
which makes the solution feasible for using in a progressive die;
• There must be room for designing of lubrication channels in the punch to ensure
sufficient lubrication of freshly ironed surfaces.

2.3. Numerical Analysis
2.3
Numerical Analysis
Both the die and the punch deform elastically during ironing. These deformations must
be taken into account to achieve high precision and control over the manufacturing process.
In addition to deformations in the tools, the workpiece springs back after forming, causing
its wall thickness to increase and cup diameter to decrease. One common way to investigate
all these phenomena is with Finite Element Analysis (FEA).
In this project, one of the most important design requirements for the ironing punch is
the magnitude of diameter adjustment required. It is therefore desirable to minimize that
property to simplify the punch design. The required diameter change is a function of both
elastic deformation in the tools and springback of the workpiece, so providing accurate es-
timates of it with analytical methods is difficult. Numerical simulations were used to study
the effect of two process parameters to the required diameter change of the punch. Die entry
radius and radial boundary condition applied to the outer perimeter of the ironing die were
selected for study. Radii 2 mm and 5 mm were selected, because these values are the ex-
tremes of existing deep drawing dies and the entry radius of the ironing die should be equal
to that value. The levels of the ironing die boundary conditions were Y - the movement of
bottom nodes constrained in the axial direction - and XY - in addition to Y-constraint, the
nodes along the outer vertical edge are constrained to translate radially (in the X-direction).
The study was conducted as a 22 full factorial experiment.
LS-DYNA was used for running finite element simulations of deep drawing and ironing
processes. LS-DYNA is a general purpose finite element program that is used in the auto-
motive, aerospace, manufacturing and construction industries, for example [21].

The different die and punch radii influence the useful length for ironing. The higher the

amount of data from the process.
Since radially constraining the die reduces the DAR required, its practical feasibility is
investigated by analyzing the fit between the ironing die and its mounting plate [23]. For
parts A and B, the nominal diameter of the mounting hole is 20 µm larger than the die
external diameter. Both dimensions are specified with a tolerance of ±10 µm. Therefore,
the diameter clearance between the die holder and the die is 0 −40 µm for parts A and B,
implying a clearance fit. Part C has identical nominal dimensions for both the die holder
hole and die outer surface with a tolerance of ±10 µm, leading to an allowed clearance of
±20 µm which is a transition fit. The die holder of part C has a chamfer on one side of
the hole, indicating that the die is press-fit into its socket and loaded as a result. Therefore,
prestressing the ironing die is recommended when an adjustable ironing punch is used.

A check for quadratic effects was performed to evaluate the significance of curvature in
the data. To estimate the quadratic curvature in classical factorial experiments where the
center runs are replicated, either ANOVA or a t-test can be used. A t-test is used to test

both sets of data (center runs and factorial runs) is needed to run the t-test and therefore
at least two center runs are required. In ANOVA, the mean square of curvature is needed to
estimate the significance of the quadratic effects. The mean square is calculated by dividing
the sum of squared differences from the mean by the degrees of freedom of the effect, which
is nC −1. It can be seen that if only a single center run is included, neither ANOVA nor the
t-test can be used to estimate the curvature. On the other hand, adding more center runs
would not be useful due to the deterministic nature of the simulations. [24, pp.71, 285–286]

Diameter Change Mechanisms
An ironing punch with an adjustable diameter can be broken down to three parts -
punch-press interface, punch-blank interface and the diameter adjustment system. The lat-
ter is a novel component for ironing punches, so different phenomena and mechanisms to
implement that will hereby be explored in depth.
Figure 3.1: Structure of potential diameter change mechanisms.
Possible diameter change mechanisms were identified by brainstorming. Later structur-
ing of the generated ideas revealed that there are two fundamentally different approaches
for changing the punch diameter - phenomena that cause volume change or shape change
through elastic deformation - whereas combinations of these are also possible. A structured
overview of the generated ideas is presented in Figure 3.1. Technically, elastic deformation
also results in volume change, but is treated separately here, because of the fundamental
difference in actuating mechanisms. The elastic deformation treats methods that use me-

==PAGE:45==

3.1. Volume Change Phenomena
chanical movement of solid bodies to cause deformation, whereas volume change phenomena
are created as electromagnetic, thermal or other non-dynamic methods.
In ironing, the punch stroke induces high radial compressive forces to act on the region
close to the punch-blank interface, causing elastic deformation in the punch. Such deforma-
tions are a sign of the punch elastic potential energy change. On the other hand, the punch
plastically deforms the workpiece, changing its energetic state, too. Therefore, the energy of
the punch motion is mainly transferred to the punch, the die and the blank in the form of
plastic (workpiece) and elastic deformations (punch and die), frictional heat and other less
significant phenomena. The change of energy of a system is defined as work [25, p.151]. In
thermodynamical systems work is defined as the product of the distance an object is moved
and the force that opposes that movement [26, p.237].
The press performs work by forcing the punch into the die and permanently changing
the energetic state of the blank. Similarly to the press, the change of punch diameter requires
work, since the change of diameter must be sustained with sufficient force to oppose the radial
compressive forces during ironing. Consequently, it is useful to evaluate potential diameter
change mechanisms and phenomena regarding their ability to perform work. The following
sections provide a general analysis of the principles with respect to the design constraints
presented in section 1.5 and work that the system is capable of performing.
3.1
Volume Change Phenomena
In thermodynamics, all systems can do two kinds of work - expansion and non-expansion
work. Non-expansion work includes systems where chemical reactions occur, batteries being
a good example. Expansion work is the kind of work that arises from the volume change of
a system and it is calculated as the product of the external pressure and change of system
volume [26, pp.237-238]. The term is conventionally used about systems containing gases,
because they expand substantially more than liquids and solids. In the present case, the
punch must obviously be made of solid materials and therefore considering expansion work
for solids is relevant.
3.1.1
Phase Transformation
Phase transformation is defined as the conversion of a substance from one phase to
another [26, p.333]. As different phases of the same substance usually have different densities,
phase transition may cause an abrupt change in volume. Obviously, gaseous and liquid phases
are not of interest for this project, since the stiffness of those phases is insufficient for ironing.
A phase diagram is a map, which presents the fields of stability for phases at different
pressures, temperatures and compositions [27, p.311]. As the stability of phases is influ-
enced by these state variables, phase transitions can be caused by changing the pressure,
temperature or composition of the substance. In metallurgy, the effect of pressure is usually
neglected, since metals and alloys are used in their liquid or solid forms, which are consid-
ered non-compressible in practical applications and are therefore sensitive to only extreme
pressures.

Since liquid and gasous phases are ruled out for the current application together with
pressure as a state variable, the only remaining option is solid-solid phase transitions caused
by the change in either temperature or composition. Various systems, where such phase
transitions are possible exist. For pure iron austenite is a phase with face-centered cubic
lattice stable at temperatures between 912℃−1394℃, whereas ferrite - stable at temper-

be used to tailor the phase transition temperature to be suitable for use in an adjustable
ironing punch.
However, even if such a material which can provide sufficient volume change to ad-

the temperature is necessary to drive phase transformation, which itself is a continuous
and non-instantaneous process. The speed of phase transitions is usually described with
time-transformation-temperature graphs that provide information about the duration of the
transformation at different temperatures.

All in all, the combination of demanding requirements for the punch material properties

unviable option, especially considering the timeline of the project.
3.1.2
Thermal Expansion
Thermal expansion is a phenomenon which causes objects to change their dimensions
due to a change of their temperature. Thermal expansion is observed on all substances and
for the vast majority of them increasing temperature leads to an increase in dimensions and
vice versa. Generally, the coefficient of thermal expansion is a function of temperature. [25,
pp.520-521]
The coefficient of thermal expansion (CTE) for steels is on the order of 10−5 K−1, so a
temperature change of 100℃creates a thermal strain of 0.1%. As was shown in section 2.5,
0.3% diameter reduction is sufficient to substantially reduce the sliding length in most cases.
To reach such thermal strain in ironing, temperature changes of about 200 K or more would
be required if steel tools are used. However, this comes with multiple disadvantages.

observed. The behaviour of piezoelectric materials is often described using the piezoelectric

In applications for piezoelectric actuators, two parameters are considered - free stroke

Piezoelectric materials react to external electric fields extremely quickly and are exten-

about 3 Hz is therefore not problematic.
Most piezoelectrics are crystalline materials where ions are asymmetrically distributed,
creating a non-zero dipole moment [13, p.430]. Mishra et al. [33] divided piezoelectrics to
four classes: ceramics, single crystals, polymers and polymer composites. Single crystals and
ceramics are often used in piezoelectric actuators as they have possess suitable properties for
that. State-of-the-art piezoelectric ceramics like lead zirconate titanate (PZT) show a strain
of approximately 0.1%, whereas single crystals of lead zinc niobate-lead titanate (PZN-PT)
can reach strains of up to 1% [34, p.3]. However, ceramics and single crystals are known for
their brittle behaviour and are therefore definitely not a reasonable choice for the contact
surfaces of an ironing punch.
Commercial-off-the-shelf piezoelectric actuators come in various shapes and sizes. To
achieve larger stroke lengths plate- or ring-shaped actuators are stacked or an amplified
actuator can be made similar to a car jack with a lead screw [35]. However, such solutions
are designed for uniaxial stroke, which is not desirable for an ironing punch that needs to be
evenly expanded in the radial direction.
Assuming that piezoelectrics are only used to deform an outer shell made of tool steel,
the space available inside the punch for placing the piezoelectric material is the limiting
factor. Considering all of the above, piezoelectric adjustment of ironing punch diameter is
worth looking into further for tools of larger diameters.
3.1.4
Electrostriction
Electrostriction is a phenomenon that is observed for all dielectrics, where the materials
undergo deformation when subjected to an external electric field. Unlike the piezoelectric
effect, electrostriction is not reversible, meaning that an external strain does not induce an
electric field in the material. Generally, the degree of deformation possible with electrostric-
tive materials is lower than with piezoelectrics. [36]
According to a literature summary by Santucci and Esposito [37], most electrostrictive
materials show expansion, but some materials (fluorites and polymers) can have the opposite
behaviour. Electrostriction also exhibits a contradicting phenomenon when considered for
metal forming tooling. Dielectrics with high stiffness (ceramics) show lower strains when
compared to polymers, for example. Low material stiffness means that the strain induced by
the electric field cannot be converted to stress effectively and the mechanical work possible
is low.

mechanism. The collet geometry can be adjusted so that the inner hole is slightly conical.

Inserting a mandrel into that hole would force the collet to expand slightly. However, as
the outer surface of the collet is not continuous, an extra layer of material must be added,
which increases the actuating force due to the thickness and stiffness of the sleeve material.
The collet is advantageous compared to the self-centering chuck, because it has more contact
points with the sleeve and the mandrel. In essence, the geometry of the collet can be designed
into the sleeve, reducing the number of components and interfaces, which experience wear.
A collapsible core like the one depicted in Figure 3.6c is a widespread component for
injection moulding, because it makes it possible to manufacture components with under-
cuts without ejection problems. However, when the core is collapsed it is not round, so a
continuous diameter adjustment - which is desired to solve both problems as described in
section 1.1 - is not possible with this principle. Whereas the quality and continuity of the
external surface can be extremely good, sharp edges are prone to chipping, especially when
made of relatively brittle, hardened tool steels. Adding an extra continuous sleeve would be
an option for this principle, but would increase the number of parts and interfaces, which
decreases the robustness and wear resistance of the tool.
Finally, a hydraulic tool holder chuck as seen in Figure 3.6d reveals a clamping technique
used to clamp tool bits into milling machines. That principle makes it possible to reach low
runout, high accuracy and high vibration dampening [44].
It uses a hermetically sealed
internal oil cavity, that lies close to the tool cavity. The axial dimension of the oil cavity is
higher than the radial dimension to allow the thin wall between tool cavity and oil cavity
to slightly bend into the tool cavity, clamping the tool. The oil pressure is regulated with a
screw, which can be driven with common hand tools.
This method can be adjusted to an ironing punch, where the oil cavity would be located
under the surface of the punch-blank interface. As oils are somewhat compressible under high
pressures, the cavity volume should be minimized. The adjustment screw can be positioned
close to the axis of the punch. This method makes continuous diameter adjustment possible
and is advantageous compared to the previous three methods, because it would not introduce
roundness errors if the geometry is accurately manufactured. Furthermore, it only consists
of two parts, one of which (the screw) is easily replaceable. This increases robustness and
eliminates multiple tolerance chains.
On the downside, creating the geometry with the oil cavity requires additive manufac-
turing, which is not yet widespread for tool steels. Also, the oil is easily displaced if the
radial compressive stress on the punch is asymmetric. This asymmetry can be caused either
by the uneven wall thickness of the blank along the circumference, anisotropy of the blank
material or positioning errors of the punch and the die, for example.
All in all, existing mechanisms use principles that can be adapted for use in an adjustable
ironing punch. While the hydraulic tool holder principle is robust and allows for continuous
an evenly distributed diameter adjustment, it is too risky for implementing in this project
due to the time-risk associated with the required manufacturing methods. The principle
of a collet is the best of the remaining ones as it uses a low number of components and is
well-proven. Considering the goals of the project, it is likely the most reasonable path for
the design of an adjustable diameter ironing punch.

Tool Design
The main requirements for the ironing operation are precise measurement of axial loads,
accurate alignment of the punch and the die, and a method for repeatably clamping the
flange with sufficient force to avoid draw-in during forming stroke. Precise measurement of
axial loads is necessary, because axial punch force is related to the contact pressure between
the punch and the blank and is therefore one of the key parameters for characterizing the
diameter change capability of an adjustable ironing punch that are easily measurable.
The servomechanical press with a ±150 kN load capacity described in subsection 2.2.1
was chosen for ironing due to a high degree of control over punch travel and velocity, and an
integrated data-collection system. The goal was to design the tools so that a subpress can
be used to improve the alignment of the punch and the die during ironing.
4.1
Punch Design
It was concluded in chapter 3 that the material requirements for active diameter ad-
justment are on the limit of the capabilities of currently existing tool materials. In addition,
the explored mechanisms either push the boundaries of existing manufacturing methods -
additive manufacturing of tool steels - or are overly complex, increasing risk of project failure.
Taking into account the time available for the project, a decision was made to pursue a
passive diameter reduction mechanism, instead. A passive mechanism uses the compressive
forces acting on the punch during ironing to decrease its diameter by controlling stiffness. In
principle, this solution reduces the contact pressure between the punch and the workpiece,
thereby reducing galling.
4.1.1

The punch-blank interface consists of the features of the punch that are in contact
with the blank during ironing. It must be a well-defined and high-quality hard surface that
in industrial applications is also coated to decrease friction, thereby reducing galling. In
the present project, the guidelines for designing the interface were given by the industrial
partner.

Elastic modulus is a material property closely related to the strength of atomic bonds
in the material and is therefore difficult to manipulate, but despite that two methods are
often used to do that [13, p.83]. To increase a material’s Young’s modulus, it can be mixed
with a material with a higher modulus, creating a composite. This is a widespread technique

example of this application, since they have open or closed cells filled with gas that can be
easily either compressed or pushed out of the material [13, p.60-64].
Neither of the methods mentioned above can be done in a fraction of a second nor
repeatably and easily, which suggests that directly manipulating the material stiffness is not
an option for the ironing punch. The only option left is to alter the size or shape of the
object.
A general understanding of the forces acting on the punch during ironing is necessary to
understand how the geometry should be altered. When the punch moves axially, the reaction
between the blank, punch and the die creates both compressive axial and compressive radial
(and hoop) stresses in the punch. With a fully solid punch, the only way to alter the stiffness
is to change the material, which is not viable in this application. However, when a concentric
hole is created through the whole punch, the majority of the forces are circumferential, which
means that given a constant force, a larger central hole leads to a smaller cross-section of
material for both circumferential and axial loads, higher strain and therefore a punch with
lower stiffness. To increase the stiffness rapidly, a mandrel can be used to support the sleeve
when necessary. The working principle of such punch assembly is illustrated in Figure 4.3.
Taking stiffness to its lower limit, the ironing sleeve has to be very thin leading to a cross-
section area perpendicular to the axis that is only sufficient to safely withstand axial loads.
If a maximum axial stress of 1000 MPa is assumed together with a peak axial load of 60 kN
(approximate value received from analyzes performed in section 2.3), the wall thickness of a
hollow punch with outer diameter of 33 mm would have to be 0.6 mm. While axial buckling
can be prevented by the extended mandrel during the forming stroke, buckling inwards may
become a problem when the mandrel is retracted under high compressive circumferential
loads, which are present after the forming stroke.

buckling resistance and low circumferential stiffness are reached.
To achieve a low radial (circumferential) stiffness in combination with a high axial
strength and buckling resistance, it was decided to create radial slits to the inner hole of
the punch. Such slitting concentrates the circumferential strain to narrow areas near the
tips of the slits along the perimeter of the ironing surface, leaving most of the material in a
relatively low stress state during the whole process. This approach was picked over optimizing
wall thickness of an axisymmetric hollow punch, because the parts can be created more
structurally robust and easier to handle, which was deemed desirable for the first prototype.

The punch-mandrel interface is the most important feature of the punch-mandrel as-

minimize wear at the interface and therefore both the contact pressure and sliding length
shall be minimized.

Effect of Internal Slitting to Punch-Mandrel Interface Stresses
The effect of adding straight slits to the punch-mandrel interface stresses was studied.
The remaining contact area after slitting is a function of the number of slits, their width, the
diameter of the punch inner hole, the cone angle and the shape and size of fillets between
the slit and conical surface. At this stage of the project, the punch design was frozen at 12
slits with a width of 2.1 mm and a cone angle of 7°.
Since the relationship between the interface area and the slit count and width is simple -
increasing the number or the width of slits decreases the interface area and increases stresses
- it will not be studied here in more detail. Instead, the effect of cone angle, punch hole
diameter and fillet geometry to the interface area is investigated. Interface area is hereby
defined as the total contact area between the punch and the mandrel and total area is the
hypothetical interface area if no slits were present. Stress concentration factor, X is the
ratio of total area over interface area and can be used as a multiplier for estimating stresses
at the punch-mandrel interface for slit punches, given that the stress profile at the interface
for non-slit punch (see Figure 4.14) is known.
Due to the conical shape of the interface, and the straight profile of the slits, the stress
concentration factor is a function of the distance from the bottom of the punch. In order to
take that into account, the stress concentration factor was calculated at different distances
from the bottom face using the ratio of total perimeter over the interface perimeter, instead
of the area ratio. The effect of the three chosen variables - cone angle, punch hole diameter
and fillet geometry - is studied using a one factor at a time approach. Cone angle 5° and
hole diameter 20 mm was used for the baseline.
The fillet geometry was shaped by the constraints of wire EDM, which is not capable of
creating undercuts. Figure 4.15a shows a profile, which rounds both the straight section of
the punch and partially also the conical part, whereas a more complete rounding is achieved
near the smaller diameter end of the cone, where the risk of chipping due to higher stresses
(see Figure 4.14) is higher.
The second analyzed profile visible in Figure 4.15b tries to
maximize the interface area by not creating fillets along the straight unloaded portion and
just rounding the slits near the smallest end of the cone, where the stresses are highest. This
profile was used as a baseline with the cone angle and hole diameter settings mentioned in
the previous paragraph.

4.1.4
Mandrel Actuating Mechanism
The mandrel actuating mechanism is used to retract the mandrel after the forming
stroke and extend it again after the backstroke. The selected actuating principle must be
capable of fulfilling the following requirements:
• Capable of moving the mandrel with sufficient force to overcome friction with the
punch. Force controlled motion is preferred on the forming stroke, while displacement
control is beneficial on the backstroke;
• Fast enough to accommodate 200 strokes
min ;
• Must be robust and easy to operate;
• Must have high stiffness to limit the displacement of the mandrel during the forming
stroke;
• Shall be as compact as possible to minimize the amount of modifications needed for
integrating it to existing progressive die tools.
A high level overview of some commonly used linear actuator types can be seen in
Table 4.4. For implementing the developed punch in an industrial setting, manual mechanical
actuator is clearly unsuitable.
To select the best possible actuating principle hydraulic,
pneumatic and electromechanical mechanisms are evaluated on the feasibility of fulfilling
the requirements listed above.
The fundamental limitations for operating speed for the
three mechanisms are guided by the power of the electric motor, hydraulic pump or air
compressor. Reaching the required speed with those actuator types is not problematic and
no mechanisms can be ruled out based on that criterion.
During the forming stroke, force controlled motion is preferred to ensure that the man-
drel does not damage the punch by extending too far. On the backstroke, displacement
control is preferred to ensure that the mandrel has retracted by a sufficient distance. Both
hydraulic and electromechanical systems can provide sufficient control, but the precise con-
trol of position is not feasible for pneumatic systems with the exceptions of hard stops.
Also, pneumatic systems do not offer sufficient stiffness due to high compressibility of gas
and would likely lead to unacceptable axial displacements of the mandrel.
Therefore, a
pneumatic system is not suitable for actuating the mandrel in an industrial setting.

detail, the axial force transferred to the mandrel during ironing must be considered (see
Figure 4.17). The peak compressive force at α = 7° interface angle is approximately 80 kN

machining process during manufacturing is usually not desirable for high-precision features
created during different operations, because it often requires mounting the unfinished part to
a new fixture and introduces a positioning error. The capabilities of available manufacturing
processes were considered for choosing the profile and depth of the slits. At this point it was
assumed that the aspect ratio of the slits must be high (>10) and milling is unfeasible.
Electrical Discharge Machining (EDM) techniques are some of the most capable pro-
cesses for machining cavities with high aspect ratios. In EDM, the workpiece material is
eroded away as a result of electrical discharges, so the forces acting on the electrode (the
tool) are very low compared to conventional techniques. This implies that in addition to
high achievable aspect ratios, small internal radii are also achievable. Wire EDM - a vari-
ation of the traditional EDM process - is comparable to cutting contours with a band saw.
The process uses a thin (typically 0.2 −0.3 mm) slowly moving wire, which cuts the
workpiece. The resulting geometries achievable with wire EDM are limited to straight cuts
through the whole workpiece. [2, pp.780-783]
Wire EDM was selected as the preferred manufacturing method for the internal slits in
the punch for four reasons:
• The wires used in wire EDM are standard, reducing manufacturing cost and eliminating
the need for manufacturing electrodes with specific shapes;
• The bottom of the slits is removed, eliminating internal corners and possible stress
concentrations;
• Through slits can be used to transport lubricant into the formed cup;
• Through slits can be used to constrain the rotation of the mandrel.
A straight slit profile (see Figure 4.4a) was selected, since it is simple to manufacture
and likely provides sufficient stiffness control for proving the concept. Based on the FEA

Blankholder Design
For this project, a bolted blankholder was selected for multiple reasons. Firstly, op-
erating a bolted blankholder does not require specific tools and expensive equipment, like
gas-springs. Secondly, there is no need to obtain a high degree of control over the blankholder
force during ironing, since the workpiece must be mounted with a blankholder force high
enough to avoid draw-in during the forming stroke. Using this approach, the force can be
determined with low accuracy by either tightening the bolts with a torque wrench or loading
the blankholder with a known force using a press and tightening the bolts with high torque
under the press. On the downside, operating a bolted blankholder is time-consuming, but
this is not an issue for experiments, where the number of ironed parts is low. Care must be
taken to preserve alignment between the top (punch assembly) and bottom (die assembly)
parts of the tooling system when the workpiece is changed.

Punch Fixator
Finally, the performance of the punch fixator - which undergoes three different loading
cases - was analyzed. In all three cases, the load is applied to the interfacing surface with
the punch flange, whereas the M8 screw counterbores are considered fixed.
The first loading case is when the fixator is fastened to the punch holder with the proof
load of the M8x20 screws, which is 35.5 kN per bolt if strength class 12.9 is assumed [50]).
In this case, the total load would be 4 · 35.5 kN = 142 kN. Secondly, during the forming
stroke when the mandrel is forced into the punch, the axial compressive loads must be
counteracted by the punch fixator. Assuming that all the load applied from the mandrel to
the punch holder (30 kN) must be counteracted by the fixator, the total load on it, including
the preload is 142 kN + 30 kN = 172 kN. During the backstroke, the tensile axial forces
combine with the preload applied by the M8 screws and the mandrel is retracted, so no load
is applied by that. Therefore the total load estimate is 142 kN + 20 kN = 162 kN.
The highest total load of 172 kN was selected for the structural analysis. The part was
meshed with en element size between 0.3 mm and 2 mm. The mesh size around internal
corners was set to 0.3 mm.

Chapter 7
Conclusions and Outlook
Sheet metal ironing - a mass-manufacturing process used for reducing residual stresses
in deep drawn components and making the walls more straight and uniform in thickness -
is an important process for Grundfos A/S for manufacturing pump components of stainless
steel. The focus in this project was on inner ironing, where the majority of thickness reduc-
tion in the part is achieved from the inside using a punch with a rounded nose. Galling -
a combination of adhesive and abrasive wear - is a common problem when ironing stainless
steel, and efforts have been made to reduce it, for example by increasing tool hardness, re-
ducing surface roughness or improving friction conditions by using more advanced lubricants
or tool coatings. Galling is usually considerably more severe during the backstroke, when the
contact pressure between the tool and the workpiece is higher due to prior strain-hardening
of the material and lower contact area compared to the forming stroke. Altering the contact
pressure which is a major factor for wear is not currently practiced in the industry.
Variation of blank thickness is also a problem. As an example, a deviation of ±45 µm
leads to a difference of ±1 mm in the length of the ironed part for some of Grundfos’ com-
ponents, which may cause the part length to be out of the specified tolerance range. An
ironing punch with adjustable diameter has the potential to solve both problems. The goal
of the project was to investigate the feasibility of an adjustable ironing punch and develop
a functional prototype, which is capable of demonstrating the working principle in a labora-
tory setting using stainless steel workpieces.
First, the approximate punch diameter was determined using parts provided as examples
by the industrial partner. The selection was narrowed further as the dimensions of existing
deep drawing tools were considered and 33.8 mm was selected as the ironing punch di-
ameter. Numerical methods were used to define the radii of the deep drawing die and the
punch by minimizing the required diameter change, which was described using a diameter
adjustment curve that relates the absolute diameter change and the resulting sliding length
on the backstroke.
Subsequently, a full factorial study was conducted using numerical simulations to deter-
mine the effect of punch diameter, blank thickness and thickness reduction in ironing to the
requirements for punch diameter change. The absolute diameter adjustment requirement
was found to be positively correlated to all of the three factors. In addition, the diameter
130
==PAGE:143==
Master’s Thesis - Kaarel Siimut
adjustment requirement relative to the punch diameter was analyzed and it turned out that
the relative measure - which is equivalent to the radial (and circumferential) strain needed
for reaching the absolute diameter change - is negatively correlated with the punch diameter,
but positively correlated with the blank thickness and thickness reduction. The data were
checked for curvature and it was determined that the effects are linear in the studied range.
A relative punch diameter adjustment of about 0.3% was found to be sufficient for consid-
erably reducing galling in most cases. To solve the problem of varying blank thickness, the
punch diameter needs to be above 60 mm to achieve enough diameter change for covering
the example blank thickness tolerance range of ±45 µm if the achievable usable strain is 0.3%.
Various volume change phenomena were analyzed with regards to feasibility of using
them as diameter change mechanisms. Phase transformation was found to be unsuitable
for the purpose mainly due to the requirement for accommodating 200 strokes
min . On the other
hand, thermal expansion requires high temperature change, since the materials suitable for
the punch have relatively low thermal expansion coefficients. The high required tempera-
tures are not compatible with most currently used lubricants and achieving sufficiently fast
temperature changes would require heating and cooling power on the order of 150 kW. In
addition, the punch would need to accommodate a large amount of cooling channels, which
reduce the stiffness and increase the required diameter change. Piezoelectric materials were
found to provide insufficient strain for the application and their brittle behaviour is not
suitable for an ironing punch. Using electrostriction as the diameter change mechanism is
not reasonable, since the phenomenon is only observed for dielectric materials, which are
too soft and weak in the case of polymers, or too brittle in the case of ceramics and single
crystals. Finally, magnetostriction was considered as a diameter change mechanism. The
largest known magnetostrain is on the order of 0.1 −0.2% and it decreases considerably
when an external compressive force is applied to the material. The magnetic field intensity
required to create the magnetostrain between 0.1 −0.2% is on the order of 100 kA
m .
Considering that the previously listed methods are not feasible, the limits of elastically
deforming materials suitable for an ironing punch were studied. The most important param-
eter for selecting the material for an ironing punch, the diameter of which can be adjusted by
elastic deformation, is the usable strain, which is the ratio of fatigue strength over Young’s
Modulus. Modern tool steels were found to have usable strain between 0.31 −0.37%, but
values up to 0.6% have been recorded for Uddeholm Vanadis 4 Extra. Utilizing the Poisson’s
ratio of materials was investigated as axial compression of the punch could lead to radial
expansion. Since Poisson’s ratio of most suitable materials is about 0.3, the material needs to
survive axial strains about three times higher than the required radial strain and a diameter
change of up to 0.2% would be possible with current state-of-the-art materials, which is not
sufficient.
An ironing punch prototype with a retractable mandrel was developed and designed.
The retraction of the mandrel after the forming stroke decreases the stiffness of the punch
and reduces contact pressure at the punch-blank interface. Internal slitting of the punch
was selected to reduce the radial stiffness of the punch. The effect of various slitting config-
urations to the punch stiffness and stresses in the punch was investigated using numerical
Page 131 of 139
==PAGE:144==
Master’s Thesis - Kaarel Siimut
simulations. It was found that the number of slits should be maximized and the distance
from the slit tip to the punch-blank interface (sleeve thickness) should be minimized to
reduce punch stiffness.
To reduce stresses in the punch, the number of slits, the sleeve
thickness and the slit width should be high. A conical interface geometry was selected for
the punch-mandrel interface. Using numerical analysis the stresses along the interface and
the stresses transferred to the mandrel during ironing were estimated. A screw was selected
for actuating the mandrel in this project mainly due to simplicity, robustness and low cost.
Numerical simulations were used to evaluate the structural strength and performance of the
designed parts and establish operating limits.

mandrel extension force was determined. That was used to study the relationship between
mandrel extension force and corresponding punch diameter expansion.

The designed tools were tested by ironing deep drawn cups prepared of EN 1.4404 and
EN 1.4307 sheets with a nominal thickness of 1.00 mm. During preliminary experimentation,
the process parameters were optimized to achieve repeatable results and EN 1.4404 cups deep
drawn of sheared blanks using a blankholder force of 10.55 kN were used for the rest of the
experiments. Six ironing punch configurations were tested to compare the designed punch
to a conventional ironing punch. Some of the ironed cups were measured with a CMM to
evaluate the differences in diameter, wall thickness, roundness and concentricity.
When the adjustable punch is used without retracting the mandrel during the back-
stroke, the axial punch force during the forming stroke is nearly identical to that of the
conventional punch. The latter shows a 8 −15% higher peak force during the backstroke,
but the work on the backstroke is similar for both punches. The CMM measurements showed
that the specimens ironed with the adjustable punch without retracting the mandrel have
about 5−10 µm higher average wall thickness. The majority of this increase in wall thickness
results from a decrease of inner diameter, since the change in outer diameter is smaller.
Two levels of torque were applied to the actuator screw of the adjustable punch depend-
ing on the tool configuration. When a high torque of 11 Nm was applied, the axial punch
force during the forming stroke was about 5% higher than the force observed when a low
torque setting of 4 Nm was used. The CMM data revealed that the wall thickness and inner
diameter of the specimens is correlated with the applied actuator torque, since a higher actu-
ator torque led to a larger punch diameter and larger inner diameter. As the outer diameter
of the specimens changed relatively little, the wall thickness reduction is mainly caused by
the increase of inner diameter. This correlation was observed on specimens ironed with both
the coated and uncoated punch.
When the mandrel is retracted before the backstroke, the work required to eject the
punch from the cup is reduced by 45 −55%. As a result of retracting the mandrel, the
roundness profile of the cups changed from quadrimodal (seen in specimens ironed with a

conventional punch) to bimodal (also seen in deep drawn cups), indicating that the degree
of deformation during the backstroke had reduced substantially. Furthermore, retracting
the mandrel was found to make the force during the backstroke more constant.
CMM

inner diameter near the cup opening is lower relative to the inner diameter measured deeper

Considering the goals of the project, it can be said that all the short-term objectives
stated in section 1.5 were met and the selected working principle can be used to pursue
the long-term objectives. The results of this project can be used as a basis for designing
next iterations of the ironing punch with an adjustable diameter that can demonstrate the
feasibility of the tool in an industrial setting and step closer to more efficient and intelligent
manufacturing at a large scale.
Outlook
As the project was carried out during five months and the manufacturing of the designed
parts took two months, the time available for developing ideas and finalizing the design
was only about seven weeks and the resulting design is not optimal, although sufficient for
demonstrating the working principle. Future researchers of this topic could consider the
following aspects to improve the tool:
1. Eliminate slits - Slitting creates small radii that concentrate the stresses, limiting the
maximum mandrel extension force that can be used to expand the punch. Furthermore,
the design presented in this report concentrates most of the circumferential strain to
the areas near the slit tips. To achieve a more uniform strain distribution around the
punch when the mandrel extension force is applied, eliminating slits completely would
be beneficial. In that case, the sleeve thickness does not have to be as low as in the
present design (2 mm near the shaft, 2.4 mm at the widest portion on the punch-
blank interface) to achieve an equivalent effect (see Equation 4.3 and the relationship
of stiffness with the height (x) and cross-section area (πR2)). Buckling of a slitless
design would have to be investigated further, but buckling to the inside is likely not
an issue, because the mandrel supports the punch whenever it is in compression.
2. Optimize punch sleeve thickness - The axial load during the backstroke (with
the mandrel retracted) decreases as the punch stiffness decreases. As the axial load
decreases, the required sleeve thickness is also reduced. At one point the axial stress in
the punch wall caused by the mandrel extension force becomes higher than the axial
stress during the backstroke. It would be beneficial to know the optimal wall thickness
of the punch in case slits are eliminated.
Page 133 of 139
==PAGE:146==
Master’s Thesis - Kaarel Siimut
3. Optimize punch-mandrel interface - In this project, the selection of a conical
interface and an angle of 7° was influenced by time limitations. The mechanics of the
round interfaces (Figure 4.10d and e) would be beneficial to study, because they might
prove advantageous for achieving more robust contact conditions at various mandrel
extension forces.
4. Fatigue strength of tool steels - The fatigue strength of tool steels has not been
widely investigated using classical methods (tensile fatigue test) due to lack of interest
in those properties. Tool steel manufacturers have developed tests that demonstrate
the fatigue performance in settings closer to the actual application of the material.
Measuring the tensile fatigue strength and estimating usable strain is becoming relevant
due to the loading cases on the adjustable ironing punch and more comprehensive
knowledge would be required for pushing the tool to its limit.
5. Automatic mandrel actuator - An automatic mandrel actuator should be designed
and tested to investigate the feasibility of reaching production speed with the designed
tool. The automatic actuator shall be capable of applying a well-defined force to the
punch before the forming stroke, keep the mandrel in place with high blocking force
during the forming stroke and quickly retract the mandrel before the backstroke.
6. Design of lubrication channels - Lubrication channels should be implemented on
the new design.
In case it is desired to eliminate the slits, which would be good
lubricant channels, the lubricant can be carried through the mandrel to the area above
the punch-mandrel interface and holes in the punch can be used to feed the lubricant
to the part.
7. Testing on a progressive die - Tests on an industrial progressive die should be
run to investigate controlling the diameter of the ironing punch and integrate it with
existing tools.

Appendix A
Effect of Blankholder Force to the
Diameter Adjustment Range
It was seen in Figure 2.7 that some material is drawn into the ironing die during the
forming stroke, increasing the DAR needed to completely eliminate punch-blank contact
during the backstroke. Minimizing such draw-in is desirable, since it loosens the requirements
for diameter change, so the effect of blankholder force to DAR was studied. The ironing
forming stroke was used for the analysis using the geometry with deep drawing punch and
die radii 2 mm to maximize ironing stroke length. The ironing die was constrained radially
to imitate prestressing, since it was shown in Figure 2.7 that this reduced the required
DAR. Blankholder forces 10.5 kN (1.67 kN
rad), 50 kN (7.96 kN
rad), 100 kN (15.9 kN
rad), 150 kN
(23.9 kN
rad) were selected, because these values are within the range of available presses. It
was assumed that the blankholder force during the backstroke (springback simulation, see
step 4 in Figure 2.4) is equal to the blankholder force during forming stroke.
Figure A.1: Cup wall profiles and corresponding diameter adjustment curves for different
blankholder forces applied to the cup flange during ironing.
I
==PAGE:153==
Master’s Thesis - Kaarel Siimut
The diameter adjustment curves for all blankholder forces are shown in Figure A.1. It
is apparent that the end of the ironing stroke (at low Y-coordinate values) is nearly identical
regardless of the blankholder force. However, the portion of the cup near the opening differs
considerably. As expected, increasing the blankholder force from 50 kN to 100 kN and
above decreases the height of the peak at low Y-coordinate values, because less material can
be drawn in. Unexpectedly, the maximum required DAR values are similar at blankholder
forces 10.5 kN and 150 kN and the cup wall profile at FBH = 10.5 kN differs slightly
from the other three blankholder force settings. Visual inspection of the simulation results
revealed that 10.5 kN is sufficient for keeping the cup from being drawn in completely, but
not enough to force the underside of the flange into contact with the ironing die. As indicated
in Figure A.2 with the red ellipses, 50 kN is enough to deform the flange and create contact
between the top surface of the die and the underside of the blank.
The reasons for the lower DAR at FBH = 10.5 kN can be found when analysing spring-
back of the blank, die and blankholder. The blank which is clamped with lower force slips
back between the blankholder and the die more than the cup that is clamped with higher
force. The high peak at low Y-coordinate values in Figure A.1 is likely a combination of
stretching in the round at the opening and slipping of the flange between the blankholder and
the die. As the blankholder force increases, the share of slipping during ironing decreases,
whereas the effect of stretching the unironed corner remains roughly constant, so the total
displacement decreases. The stresses induced during ironing that relax afterwards are high
enough to overcome the friction between the blank, blankholder and the die and push the
flange back between the tools if the blankholder force is insufficient.
Considering the above discussion it is reasonable to apply a high blankholder force to
the cup flange during ironing, because it creates repeatable contact conditions and lowers the
diameter change required to eliminate contact between the punch and the blank completely
on the backstroke.

Appendix G
Ironing Procedure
* - only applicable, if adjustable ironing punch is used.
** - Not applicable if the adjustable punch is used as solid.
1. Clean the deep drawn cup. Perform visual check for debris. Write cup ID and mark
texture direction.
2. Clean the ironing die. Perform visual check for debris.
3. Lubricate the die. Perform visual check for debris.
4. Lubricate the cup. Perform visual check for debris.
5. Place the cup into the ironing die, position blankholder and tighten screws until first
resistance. Apply 140 kN and tighten all screws to 30 Nm with a torque wrench.
Order: 1-5-3-7-2-6-4-8.
6. Extend mandrel (counter-clockwise) to specified torque using torque tool.*
7. Lubricate the ironing punch. Perform visual check for debris.
8. Inject lubricant on cup walls to cover the whole surface again.
9. Ensure that the punch is in raised position and mount ironing die assembly. Fasten
with four M5x16 (ISO 4762) screws.
10. Perform visual check of fasteners
11. Move punch to predetermined zero position (948.9 mm during the time of experimen-
tation)
12. Perform forming stroke: vp = 2 mm
s , end position: 932.9 mm during experimentation.
13. Inject 1-2 ml of lubricant between punch shaft and blankholder.
XXXVII
==PAGE:189==
Master’s Thesis - Kaarel Siimut
14. Retract mandrel using a wrench (do not use the same torque wrench as used for
extending, required torque is out of range). Turn the screw clockwise by at least two
full rotations. **
15. Wait at least 1 minute for the lubricant to settle.
16. Perform backstroke: vp = 2 mm
s , end position: 948.9 mm during experimentation.
17. Rename data files.
18. Dismantle punch assembly from subpress (four M5 x 16 screws).
19. Apply load to blankholder, loosen blankholder screws and remove blankholder. Remove
excess lubricant.
20. Flip die and place on elevated surface, where the cup path is unobstructed.
21. Place ejector into the die, ensure that it contacts the cup bottom.
22. Eject cup by actuating the ejector pin using a press.
