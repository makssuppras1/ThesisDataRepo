Abstract
Continual Learning (CL) is deﬁned as the ability of a system of invariably learning and
mastering new tasks while accumulating knowledge. It is regarded as one of the greatest
goals of machine learning and artiﬁcial intelligence in general. In this work, various CL
models are analyzed and evaluated on datasets including temporal dependencies, an anal-
ysis which is mostly absent in bibliography and resembles better real-world scenarios. Fur-
thermore, a deeper look at potential underlying dependencies between the performance of
a CL model and various characteristics of the problem (i.e., task deﬁnition, task sequence,
complexity, etc.) is taken. The experimental setup was based on diﬀerent time-series clas-
siﬁcation problems, modiﬁed to resemble a CL scenario. The obtained results revealed the
existence of dependencies between the task deﬁnition, the task arriving sequence and the
overall performance of a model. More speciﬁcally, frameworks establishing a shared mem-
ory cell across tasks showed to suﬀer more from dissimilar task deﬁnitions while beneﬁting
the most from similar ones expressing signiﬁcant knowledge transfer. Methods adopting
a parameter isolation schema for each task, also expressed increased performance in the
case of high task similarity, while being more robust in the opposite situation. Models
utilizing generative components for addressing a more complex CL scenario, where the
task identities were considered as latent variables, accumulated an extra error term due to
a potential wrong task inference phenomenon. This error was found to be signiﬁcant when
working with time-series contrary to the case of i.i.d. data. More importantly, two novel
CL methods were developed solely for the scope of this work. The ﬁrst method is referred
to as Continual Optimal Brain Damage (COBD). In detail, COBD fused a Bayesian prior
transfer regularization method with the phenomenon of over-parameterization in neural
networks, forming an eﬃcient way of overcoming catastrophic forgetting. Experimental
results on a CL baseline dataset showed that COBD achieves state-of-the-art performance
while utilizing signiﬁcantly less memory when compared to other rival frameworks. The
second method was speciﬁed as Continual Neural Dirichlet Process Mixture for Time
Series (CNDPM-TS) and was able to perform over a time-series CL problem in an un-
supervised way. Moreover, CNDPM-TS utilized diﬀerent expert networks for every task
while coupling each expert with a unique generative component.

Chapter 1
Introduction and motivation
You can’t connect the dots looking
forward; you can only connect them
looking backwards. So you have to
trust that the dots will somehow
connect in your future.
Steve Jobs
The human brain is capable of constantly learning and mastering new tasks, obtaining
new knowledge and developing new skills. The learning procedure of a new task is usually
highly dependent and related with an individual’s already accumulated experience and
prior knowledge.
For example, someone can think about the challenges that students
face in their freshman year at school compared to their senior year. The challenges the
students must face are of increasing diﬃculty and a successful accomplishment of them
is highly correlated with the abilities learned and mastered from previous years. A key
observation should be, that even though the human mind learns to adapt in various novel
tasks, it does not lose the capability of performing older ones. This exact behavior of
biological Continual Learning is one of the great goals of machine learning and artiﬁcial
intelligence in general. In the last years, major progress has been made towards machine
learning models that are able of semi-supervised and reinforcement learning [I. Goodfellow,
Bengio, and A. Courville 2016], but few steps have been done towards models that are
able to learn and perform continuously, expressing lifelong learning.
In the era of big data, real-world applications of machine learning and especially of deep
learning, require signiﬁcant amount of resources. Furthermore, the training procedure of
deep neural networks is often characterized by high time complexity. A usual phenomenon
while working with data applications, especially in a production setting, is that new aris-
ing tasks in need of performing are similar to the already completed ones, or their learning
procedure could beneﬁt from the already stored system information. What is more, some-
one could argue that creating a new model from scratch is not the best practice, both for
the amount of the required training time as well as the quantity of total resources. In
more detail, the needed resources are increasing linearly with the number of total tasks,
while the already accumulated knowledge of previous systems is disregarded. The use of
transfer learning for the novel model is capable of lowering the training complexity, but
is not able to remove the constraint of extra resources.
Continual Learning, oﬀers an
optimum setting in a changing world where new problems arise every day and extra re-
sources come with a cost. The aforementioned domain lies in the intersection of the three

contrast to these characteristics and advantages, Continual Learning as a ﬁeld of deep
learning is understudied for real-world scenarios.
In addition, as argued in [Farquhar
and Gal 2018] current evaluations of Continual Learning methods may lead to misleading
and not robust results and conclusions. For this reason, in this work various models are
analyzed and evaluated on datasets which include temporal dependencies, an analysis that
is mostly absent in bibliography, while resembling better a real-world case. Furthermore, a
deeper look on potential underlying dependencies between the performance of a Continual
Learning model and various characteristics of the problem deﬁnition (i.e. task deﬁnition,
task sequence, etc.) is taken. Overall, the introduced experiments aim to provide suﬃcient
evidence and results about the success of Continual Learning methods applied in the time-
series domain.
1.1
Thesis Objectives
This work followed multiple directions in order to answer questions existing in the ﬁeld of
Continual Learning. The main goals were set as:
• Provide an overview of the current status of Continual Learning and the existing
state-of-the-art methods.
• Create, implement and evaluate novel methods able to express lifelong learning.

• Research on the success of state-of-the-art and novel methods of Continual Learning

• Investigate on the deﬁnition of a Continual Learning problem and its underlying

1.2
Contribution to Knowledge
The main contribution to the current state of knowledge of this work can be summarized
in two main categories as:
• Development of novel methods for Continual Learning:
– A new framework called Continual Optimal Brain Damage (COBD) is devel-
oped and originally presented in this work. COBD can successfully perform
over complex Continual Learning scenarios. Moreover, it gives rise to a more
”natural” setting since the model is characterized by a ﬁxed capacity.
The
framework combines a Bayesian prior transfer regularization method with the
phenomenon of over-parameterization in neural networks, achieving state-of-
the-art performance.
– A second novel framework called Continual Neural Dirichlet Process Mixture for
Time-Series (CNDPM-TS) is proposed and presented in this work. CNDPM-TS
is the generalisation of Continual Neural Dirichlet Process Mixture (CNDPM)
to the domain of datasets including temporal dependencies, such as time-series.
CNDPM-TS is the ﬁrst model of its kind and it is able to address a time-series
Continual Learning problem in an online setting. Considering that completely
new tasks emerge during test time, this new method utilizes a Variational Re-
current Neural Network (VRNN) as a generative model. This allows CNDPM-
TS to capture task-shifts based on a likelihood metric in an unsupervised way.
• Research on the eﬀects that diﬀerent formulations of a Continual Learning problem
have to the total performance of a model:
– Contrary to other research papers, the performance over diﬀerent constructions
of a Continual Learning problem is studied explicitly in this work. An inves-
tigation over unique representations of the same problem, provides a better
understanding of how an ”optimal” Continual Learning framework can be ob-
tained. These representations introduce unique settings, with respect to the
deﬁnition and the arriving sequence of the tasks, which shape the Continual
Learning problem.

1.4
Structure of the thesis
Chapter 1 gives an overview of the motivation behind Continual Learning and discusses
the potential advantages and currently understudied cases of the ﬁeld.
Chapter 2 presents a brief review of the background studies and models used throughout
the thesis such as deep learning models, gating networks and Bayesian non-parametric
methods. Additionally, it lists and explores current applications of deep learning methods
on time-series. An investigation of gating networks is also conducted, since these models
oﬀer a nice setting for the majority of Continual Learning applications.
Chapter 3 reviews the foundation and deﬁnitions of Continual Learning, as well as pre-
senting current state-of-the-art models. Moreover, a novel method capable of expressing
lifelong learning is introduced and evaluated, called Continual Optimal Brain Damage.
Chapter 4 describes the experimental setup, the models and the modiﬁcations taken in
order to address Continual Learning problems deﬁned by sequential data such as time-
series.
This chapter introduces the novel framework called Continual Neural Dirichlet
Process Mixture for Time-Series. In the second part, the corresponding performance of
models and various other experimental results are displayed, followed by a brief commen-
tary on them.
Chapter 5 summarizes and discusses the results obtained throughout this work.
Chapter 6 contains the author’s conclusion based on the thesis results and introduces
propositions for future work and research.

Chapter 2
Background
2.1
A Brief Review of Deep Learning
Recent breakthrough results in many diverse ﬁelds, such as pattern recognition, signal
and image processing, control systems and ﬁnancial market predictions have made deep
learning a research topic receiving a lot of attention. The ﬁrst publication ever to in-
troduce a mathematical model consideration of a neuron appeared already from 1943 in
[Mcculloch and Pitts 1943], even though it did not consider a learning procedure for model
training. In the upcoming years, some breakthrough research papers followed such as the
famous Hebbian learning [Hebb 1949] where Hebb’s rule was introduced that “neurons
that ﬁre together, wire together”. Later, the Perceptron network [Rosenblatt 1957] was
created which acted as a binary classiﬁer via the introduction of a threshold function. The
ﬁrst feedforward multilayer Perceptron, which is regarded as the ﬁrst deep architecture
of a neural network was shown in [Ivakhnenko 1968]. Even though these ﬁrst modelling
attempts and research ideas were groundbreaking and promising, a potential training pro-
cedure of a deep neural network was characterized by high computational cost, especially
for the available resources of that era. One of the most important advances was the appli-
cation of the error BackPropagation (BP) algorithm [Rumelhart, Geoﬀrey E. Hinton, and
Williams 1986; P. Werbos and John 1974; P. J. Werbos 1981] to the training of artiﬁcial
neural networks. This method ﬁnally introduced a computational eﬃcient way of training
deep neural networks. Three years later, a Convolutional Neural Network was trained
via the BP algorithm to classify the handwritten digits of the famous MNIST dataset
[Y. LeCun et al. 1989], showing eﬀectively the power of deep networks combined with
an eﬃcient training method. During most recent years, various landmark models have
been introduced to the deep learning society such as: the Alexnet [Krizhevsky, Sutskever,
and Geoﬀrey E Hinton 2012] which brought a revolution in the ﬁeld of image process-
ing and utilized Graphical Process Units (GPU) for the training of neural networks, the
Variational Autoencoders [Kingma and Welling 2013] that are able to learn underlying
latent representations of data inputs and Generative Adversarial Networks [I. Goodfellow,
Pouget-Abadie, et al. 2014] which are capable of generating new data samples by intro-
ducing a zero-sum game between two sub-networks. These breakthroughs combined with
the increasing power of the available hardware, have made possible the application of deep
learning methods in a production level over various ﬁelds.
2.1.1
Artiﬁcial Neural Networks
Artiﬁcial Neural Networks (ANN) are computing systems capable of performing complex

a hidden state ht. The hidden state can be regarded as a memory storing information of
past computations while also giving feedback to the system. A typical RNN architecture

2.1.6
Deep Learning Applications in Sequential Data
Sequential data, such as time-series are present in many domains like economics, transport,
engineering, natural speech and image and signal processing, medicine and many more.
The aim of sequential data modelling is to decode the temporal dependencies and deﬁne
models that utilize this hidden temporal data structure in a predictive way. The main
tasks that one comes across while studying time-series and temporal data in general, can
be categorized into: Forecasting, Classiﬁcation, Anomaly Detection and Generative tasks.
An explicit overview of diﬀerent deep learning techniques, that have been proven to work
well in the above-named tasks can be found in works like [Fawaz et al. 2018; Gamboa
2017].

Forecasting Tasks
Forecasting is one of the most popular and researched topics in time-series domain. The
goal in this task is predicting future values of the input based on historic data and pos-
sibly some other external variables. Several deep learning methods can be found in the
literature which successfully address and perform forecasting tasks. The ﬁrst example
comes from the ﬁnancial time-series domain, where a mixture of Stacked Autoencoders
[Bengio, Lamblin, et al. 2007] and Long-Short Term Memory neural networks were used
for a successful forecasting method in [Bao, Yue, and Rao 2017]. More speciﬁcally, deep
and invariant features were generated via the architecture of stacked autoencoders, which
later were given as the input features to an LSTM network for forecasting the one-step-
ahead stock price values. In the traﬃc domain a Diﬀusion Convolutional Reccurent Neural
Network was introduced in [Y. Li et al. 2017]. The aforementioned model, was able to
capture both the spatial and temporal structure of the input traﬃc graph signals via the
combination of Convolutional and Recurrent neural networks. Another interesting deep
learning model application was displayed in [Kuremoto et al. 2014], where a combination
of Deep Belief Networks [Geoﬀrey E. Hinton and Osindero 2006] and Restricted Boltzman
Machines [Ackley, Geoﬀrey E. Hinton, and Sejnowski 1985] was studied. In detail, a three
layered Deep Belief network created from two stacked Restricted Boltzman Machines was
proposed, which utilized the feature learning capabilities of Restricted Boltzman Machines
in a predictive manner.
Classiﬁcation Tasks
Time series classiﬁcation ﬁnds application in various real-world domains, such as human
activity recognition, cyber security, medical exams, control systems and more. The ﬁrst
example comes from [Karim et al. 2017], where a time series classiﬁcation model containing
Convolutional Neural Networks with temporal convolutions that was also augmented with
an LSTM block was introduced. The added LSTM block was acting as an attention layer
[Bahdanau, Cho, and Bengio 2015] improving the total classiﬁcation performance.
In
[Che et al. 2016] a novel Neural Network addressed as GRU-D was proposed, which was
based on the Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) [Cho et al. 2014]. The model was focused
on exploiting the phenomenon of missing patterns inside some multivariate time-series
data. This phenomenon was later used as a way to increase the classiﬁcation performance.
Another example can been seen in [H. Lee et al. 2009] where Convolutional Deep Belief
Networks were used as a feature extraction schema of some time-series data, which later
were given as the input in several classiﬁcation models.
Anomaly Detection Tasks
One of the most common problems while working with time series, is the existence of
anomalies in the data distributions. This is a major phenomenon in mechanical devices
such sensors. In general, most of the time series that are aﬀected by special events usually
express data anomalies. A successful Anomaly Detection model was proposed in [Malhotra
et al. 2016], where an LSTM Encoder-Decoder network was used. This speciﬁc approach
is an error-based one, where the reconstruction error was used as the anomaly detection
metric. It was shown that by giving as an input only the normal data sequences of the
time series, the network learned to reconstruct only these sequences, returning higher
reconstruction error terms when a data anomaly was being present. Another example can
be found in [Chauhan and Vig 2015], where a deep LSTM architecture was created from
stacking multiple LSTMs together, in order to detect anomalies on Electrocardiography
(ECG) signals. This method relied on error metrics as well, in order to detect an anomaly
in the input. More speciﬁcally, a Gaussian distribution ﬁtted on the model error vectors
using a Maximum Likelihood estimation, then a threshold was deﬁned via an F-score
maximization procedure that was later used as the decision maker to the existence of an

Generative Tasks
Training and learning generative models of sequences is one of the most challenging tasks
of deep learning and machine learning in general. A successful and powerful model named
Variational Recurrent Neural Network was introduced in [Chung, Kastner, Dinh, Goel,
Aaron C Courville, et al. 2015b], which utilized Recurrent Neural Network and Variational
Autoencoder models in order to learn the distribution of highly structured sequential data
like time-series and natural speech data. Another interesting work was found in [Fabius and
Amersfoort 2014], where the Variational Recurrent Autoencoder (VRAE) was proposed.
More speciﬁcally, a Variational Autoencoder was modiﬁed to learn latent representations
over sequential data while using two RNNs as the encoder and decoder components. This
modiﬁcation introduced recurrent connections to the autoencoder with which the temporal
dependencies over the inputs were taken into account, allowing for more meaningful latent
representations.
2.2
Performing Multiple Tasks via Gating Networks
The background review continues with gating networks, which describe models that al-
locate the computation of a task (i.e. classiﬁcation, regression, . . . ) to a speciﬁc set of
the networks parameters or even to an independent sub-network belonging to the total
model. The allocation, or gate can be implemented using a neural network, deﬁning iso-
lated data-responsibilities to each expert or even deﬁning a mixture over the total decision
such as a weighted average across the diﬀerent expert outputs. In the simplest of cases
it can be implemented by a deterministic function. Gating networks have found many
applications throughout the years and are also utilized in this work. In what follows the
theory regarding models most relevant for this work is summarized.
2.2.1
Gating Networks for Conditional Computation
Conditional Computation [Bengio, Léonard, and Aaron C. Courville 2013], increases the
model capacity while avoiding proportional increase in the computation cost.
This is
possible by activating only a subset of the total model parameters per task example. In
[Shazeer et al. 2017] the Sparsely Gated Mixture of Experts Layer (MoE) is introduced
in order to create a model that is capable of exploiting conditional computation. MoE is
deﬁned by a total set of n experts networks referred as E1, . . . , En and a gating network G.
The gating network learns to output sparse representations of the experts, utilizing only
a few of them per data example. In the aforementioned paper each expert was deﬁned as
a simple FNN with the same characteristics. By representing for each datapoint x, the
output of the gating network and i-th expert as G(x) and Ei(x) respectively, the total
model output is expressed as:

2.2.2
Gating networks and Continual Learning
Gating networks is a ”natural” choice for Continual Learning. A gating network can be
regarded as a guide that assigns each task to their task-speciﬁc network or to a speciﬁc part
of the network. The gating network can be chosen via diﬀerent ways for diﬀerent purposes.
The following model deﬁned an autoencoder based gating network for Continual Learning
via diﬀerent experts [Aljundi, Chakravarty, and Tuytelaars 2016]. The total dataset is
denoted as D = [D1, D2, . . . , Dn] which includes n sequential arriving tasks, with the task
identities considered to be given in training time.
For each of the n tasks an expert
and a gating component are created. The expert is modelled by a FNN, while the gating
component is a simple autoencoder. This particular choice of the gate enables the model to
learn a non-linear representation of each corresponding task. Moreover, it is assumed that
each of these representations is quite diﬀerent for each task. Given that, at test time the
proper expert can be chosen based on the the reconstruction error of each corresponding
task-speciﬁc autoencoder. More speciﬁcally, the vector of the total reconstruction error is
given as an input to a softmax function which assigns the corresponding probabilities of
each expert. Whenever a new task arrives while in training phase, the model creates the
new expert by a ﬁne-tuning procedure of the most relevant past expert. The relatedness
of each expert is computed based on the task-speciﬁc autoencoder reconstruction errors.
2.2.3

3.1
Continual Learning Deﬁnitions
During the last years various studies have emerged, introducing models and methods
capable of lifelong learning. Despite the fact, that each of these approaches aims to solve
a CL problem, a large amount of variations exist in the corresponding CL architectures,
as well as in the methods used for maintaining past knowledge in the system. In addition,
diﬀerences are also present in the deﬁnition and structure of the problem, deﬁning diﬀerent
CL scenarios. In this chapter an overview of the CL deﬁnitions, methods and state-of-the-
art models is provided. More importantly, an original contribution to knowledge is oﬀered
by a novel CL model named Continual Optimal Brain Damage.
3.1.1
The three Scenarios of Continual Learning
Continual Learning is based on the idea of learning invariably over time while utilizing
previous stored information to adapt to new tasks.
One of the major challenges that
researchers have to overcome is the phenomenon of catastrophic forgetting, ﬁrst studied by
[McCloskey and Cohen 1989]. Catastrophic forgetting describes the phenomenon of critical
information loss and collapse of performance on tasks that have already been mastered,
when learning a new one. Various models and studies claim to address successfully the
problem (e.g., [Kirkpatrick et al. 2016; Rebuﬃ, Kolesnikov, and Lampert 2016; Swaroop
et al. 2019]) with each method having both advantages and disadvantages, while some of
those being domain speciﬁc, addressing catastrophic forgetting in speciﬁc conditions and
datasets. As in [Ven and Tolias 2019] most CL problems can be distinguished in three main
categories, each one of them deﬁning a scenario for CL with a corresponding complexity.
Any CL dataset which is characterized from clear and well-deﬁned task boundaries, can
be formulated into any of the three following scenarios.
Task Incremental Learning (Task-IL)
In the Task-IL scenario, which is the simplest one, the task boundaries are known be-
forehand and the models are told which task they need to perform both at training and
test phase. The most typical network architecture, as in [Swaroop et al. 2019] is that of

practical example while using again the split-MNIST dataset, can be the same one as the
example from the Task-IL case but without providing the task identities to the system at
the test phase.
3.1.2
Task Boundaries
All the previous scenarios were described by a common assumption, that the task bound-
aries are clear and well-deﬁned. When this assumption does not hold, current methods
struggle to express a lifelong learning ability. Few studies are focused around task agnostic
networks such as [Zeno et al. 2019]. A dataset with no clear task boundaries increases
complexity and new assumptions should take place in order to be addressed. The time-
series domain oﬀers an interesting setting for CL scenarios with no clear task boundaries,
due to the potential temporal similarities and correlation of the data samples.

3.2
Continual Learning Methods
The various methods introduced by diﬀerent researchers in order to develop models which
overcome the catastrophic forgetting phenomenon can be grouped in two main categories.
These are Regularization methods and Data Replay methods, while models utilizing a
combination of those exist as well. A more detailed review of these methods is provided
in the next sections.
3.2.1
Continual Learning based on Regularization Methods
One of the ﬁrst and most popular ways of overcoming catastrophic forgetting lies in regu-
larization methods. These methods add a regularization term in the network’s loss func-
tion, which acts like a guard responsible for preserving the predictive capabilities in older
tasks while the network is being trained on a novel one.
Regularization methods can
be further broken down to two sub-categories: task-distillation oriented approaches and
Bayesian prior transfer approaches. The ﬁrst category tries to solve a CL problem by
using knowledge distillation to transfer the old-task model’s knowledge to the new-task
one, in a compact way. In other words, task-distillation oriented approaches try to concen-
trate the old-task models into the new-task one as a sub-network. The second category of
regularization methods is a prior based one, where under the Bayesian framework, poste-
rior distributions over old tasks are assigned as the prior distributions over the new ones,
applying a soft regularization over the new-task posterior distributions.

Elastic Weight Consolidation
Another well known model, that has been proven to overcome catastrophic forgetting is
the Elastic Weight Consolidation model (EWC) [Kirkpatrick et al. 2016]. The aforemen-
tioned model is based on a Bayesian prior transfer regularization method. The motivation
behind it, lies in the synaptic consolidation eﬀect that takes place in the actual brain and
enables CL by reducing the plasticity (elasticity) of synapses that play a major role to
the previous tasks performance. In an ANN model, this phenomenon is adopted by con-
straining the most vital task-speciﬁc parameters to not move far away from their current
values. As it was explained in the paper, a deep neural network is deﬁned by a sequence
of linear projections with an element-wise non-linear function applied usually after each
projection. Training a neural network demands the estimation of the network parameters,
that minimize a cost function. This set of parameters though is not unique [Hecht-Nielsen
1988]. For example, whenever a network is asked to perform two sequential tasks A and
B, someone could seek to ﬁnd the set of parameters that performs well in the last seen
task B but also has maintained its predictive capabilities in the ﬁrst task A.
The previous, can be further explained based on the log expression of Bayes’ Rule for the
joint dataset of the two independent tasks A, B:
log p(θ|D) = log p(D|θ) + log p(θ) −log p(D).
(3.6)
It should also be highlighted that the log expression for the posterior probability density
function, of Equation (3.6), contains the log-likelihood term, which is deﬁned as the term
to be maximized during the training procedure. The two independent tasks in (3.6) are
splitted by using the product rule of the logarithmic function, (log(AB) = log A + log B),
and the log expressions for the likelihood and evidence terms. In addition, by using once
more the logarithmic version of Bayes’ Rule for the terms corresponding to task A, results
to:
log p(θ|D) = log p(DB|θ) + log p(θ|DA) −log p(DB).
(3.7)

dataset contains only the log likelihood of the second task, meaning that all information
regarding task A is accumulated into the corresponding posterior probability of the net-
work. The key ingredient of EWC lies in this posterior probability expression, since it holds
the information about which parameters are vital for the performance of task A. Since
the posterior probability is intractable, a Laplace approximation [MacKay 1992] is used,
where a Gaussian distribution is chosen with mean equal to the estimated parameters θ∗

3.2.2
Continual Learning based on Data Replay Strategies
In this case past information are shown to the model in a periodically manner. The purpose
of this replay procedure is to ensure that the network connections corresponding to past
task are strengthened rather than forgotten. The most common approach is storing part
of the previous training data of the corresponding tasks and replay the training process on
them. In addition, knowledge replay by using generated data samples from a generative
component (trained on the data history) makes the previous approach more eﬃcient, since
less memory resources need to be allocated.

3.3
A Novel Method For Continual Learning
In this section a novel model for Continual Learning is introduced, deﬁned as Continual
Optimal Brain Damage (COBD). The following model combines the ideas of CL via a
Bayesian prior transfer regularization method and a parameter isolation schema based on
a neural pruning procedure. The success of this model lies on the over-parameterization
of neural networks [Neyshabur et al. 2018].
3.3.1
Prior Work
A Continual Learning method based on pruning neural connections of a network was also
introduced in [Golkar, Kagan, and Cho 2019]. The model explained in the paper called
Continual Learning via Neural Pruning (CLNP), introduced a task parameter isolation
scheme via a mask obtained after a pruning procedure. CLNP deﬁned a threshold over the
average neuron activation value in order to achieve neural sparsiﬁcation. The parameters
allocated to each task were frozen when training on new tasks and more importantly the
task-ids were required both in the test and training phase no matter the CL problem. The
proposed model in this section takes advantage of the main idea described in EWC model,
sharing and updating the network parameters across the diﬀerent tasks taking active
advantage of knowledge transfer. Moreover, in speciﬁc CL problems the performance of
COBD does not depend on the inference of the task-ids during test phase, giving a more
realistic production setting.

speciﬁc task. The training of the new expert introduces the sleep phase of the model,
after which the STM is emptied. This speciﬁc procedure assumes that each one of the
unexplained samples stored in the STM belong to the same task.

Task Deﬁnition
The Synthetic Control dataset is translated into a CL problem by constructing three
binary classiﬁcation tasks. The way of deﬁning each task is not unique and it was studied
explicitly. The reason behind that, is to understand if diﬀerent task deﬁnitions, as well
as their arriving sequence, can lead to diﬀerent eﬀects in the model’s total performance.
Bearing this in mind, the class and task similarity metric is introduced.
Despite the
fact that various metrics exist and are able to measure the similarity between time series
(i.e., cross-correlation, dynamical window warping, etc.), most of them are limited into
discovering similarities based on a distance (usually euclidean) comparison or by searching
any potential linear dependency. In order to introduce a likelihood based measurement, in
this study the time-series similarity metric is chosen to be the reconstruction error term of
the negative ELBO loss function of a VRNN. More speciﬁcally, a unique VRNN is trained
to reconstruct each class/task independently and then accessed on the reconstruction
ability towards the rest of unseen classes/tasks. For example, the most similar task to
another is the one that is deﬁned by the minimum reconstruction error term when the
VRNN has been trained to reconstruct only one of them. This procedure is visualized
in Figure 4.6. Firstly, the similarity of the six diﬀerent classes of the Synthetic Control
dataset is studied. Figure 4.7, represents a confusion matrix where each entry deﬁnes the
negative log-likelihood (reconstruction loss) of the negative ELBO function for each one of
the diﬀerent classes. As expected, upward shift and increasing trend as well as downward
shift and decreasing trend are the most common couples since they follow an alike trend.
Normal pattern shares similarities with all the classes, while cyclic pattern is the most
unique one.

Chapter 5
Discussion
Continual Learning methods displayed high ability on performing over a sequence of classi-
ﬁcation tasks containing temporal dependencies, successfully overcoming the catastrophic
forgetting phenomenon. The achieved accuracy on each corresponding dataset was greatly
correlated with the CL scenario each model was asked to address. In this section the results
obtained throughout this work are summarized and discussed further.
A novel CL method named Continual Learning via Optimal Brain Damage (COBD) was
introduced. The approach combined neural pruning with regularization methods as an ef-
fective way of adopting lifelong learning. The model was ﬁrst evaluated on the Permuted
MNIST, a widely used and studied dataset in the CL ﬁeld. Further, COBD’s performance
on additional experiments (Table 5.1) was compared with various baselines. In detail,
Table 5.1a shows that COBD achieves state-of-the-art performance when compared to the
rival EWC, while utilizing signiﬁcantly less system memory (COBD requires to store a
unique regularization term independent of the total number of tasks, contrary to EWC
which requires a regularization term for each new arriving task). Moreover, a simple reg-
ularization model L2 failed to overcome the catastrophic phenomenon. The modiﬁcation
of EWC named EWC-SM which only stored a unique regularization term, decreased the
accuracy signiﬁcantly. The next conducted experiment shown in Table 5.1b, studied dif-
ferent pruning methods for the CODB model, in which the parameter salience returned
the best behavior as it is a combination of both the diagonal Hessian matrix and pa-
rameter magnitude. Disregarding the computation of the Hessian diagonal matrix would
eventually lower the model’s complexity. Independently of the latter, the obtained results
established that the Hessian matrix holds valuable information for an optimal performance.
The last experiment for the novel model given in Table 5.3 compared its achieved accuracy
with EWC’s in the latest arrived task, verifying that since COBD allocates a number of
unconstrained parameters to the current task, it is able to achieve better accuracy.
Overall, COBD expressed a state-of-the-art performance over the Permuted Mnist dataset
while having the characteristics of a ﬁxed capacity model.
The latter fact is a major
advantage since having a predeﬁned model complexity avoids scaling problems. These
defective phenomena are present in dynamically expandable networks like CNDPM and
other models such as [Rusu et al. 2016; J. Lee et al. 2017]. Moreover, COBD does not
require data replay of previous tasks in order to maintain past knowledge in the system,
such as the already described methods of VCL, Continual Learning with Deep Generative
Replay and others like Dynamic Combination models [Ren et al. 2017] and Gradient
Episodic Memory [Lopez-Paz and Ranzato 2017]. The latter fact, allows COBD to use
signiﬁcantly less memory (stores only the prune-surviving optimum network parameters)

component of CNDPM-TS. Reversing the order that each task arrives to the system,
aﬀected the models performance, as seen in Table 5.2c. In this case, the ﬁrst task fed to
the system was the most dissimilar one, lowering the performance of COBD-MH, CNDPM-
TS and CNDPM-TS-MI while increasing the accuracy of the Finetuned and CNDPM, but
not in a competitive level. The reason for this lower observed performance, is based on the
parameter sharing eﬀect between the diﬀerent networks. In more detail, the ﬁrst incoming
task creates the main environment of the network (in CNDPM-TS/MI models the ﬁrst
task is allocated with a bigger number of parameters than the following tasks). Based
on that, the results show that there is an optimum way of deﬁning the task sequence on
networks with shared connections and resources. That is to send ﬁrst the most similar task,
when compared to the total amount, in order to deﬁne the main body of the CL model
in the most task expressive way, taking advantage of the knowledge transfer phenomenon
between tasks at the higher possible way.

Conclusion
This work gave an overview of the state-of-the-art methods used in Continual Learning.
These methods aim to construct the mathematical expression describing biological lifelong
learning in an artiﬁcial way. More importantly, two novel frameworks able to learn contin-
uously were introduced. The ﬁrst deﬁned as Continual Optimal Brain Damage (COBD),
managed to achieve high performance via the combination of a Bayesian prior transfer
regularization and a neural pruning procedure. The second model of Continual Neural
Dirichlet Process Mixture for Time Series (CNDPM-TS), was able to perform over a time-
series CL problem in an unsupervised way. CNDPM-TS utilized diﬀerent expert networks
for every task while coupling each expert with a unique generative component. In ad-
dition, the absence of a CL model evaluation on more complicated datasets, lead to an
original investigation on the success of current and novel methods on learning sequentially,
while avoiding catastrophic forgetting in problems deﬁned by more structured data such
as time-series.
The experimental setup utilized two time-series classiﬁcation problems, modiﬁed to re-
semble a CL setting. Overall, the results showed that the studied CL approaches could
successfully address problems deﬁned by data including temporal dependencies. Moreover,
the total performance of a model was aﬀected both by the task deﬁnition and the task
arriving sequence. CL models which utilized networks with a shared main body (common
memory cell) across each task, showed to suﬀer more from dissimilar task deﬁnitions, while
beneﬁting the most from similar ones, expressing signiﬁcant knowledge transfer. Methods
described from a parameter isolation setting for each task, also beneﬁt from similar task
deﬁnitions while being more robust in the opposite case. In addition, it was found that
sending to the system ﬁrstly the task that resembled best the majority of the rest, boosted
the overall performance. Last but not least, models addressing a Class-IL scenario accu-
mulated an extra error term due to a potential Wrong Task Inference during test time.
This error term was found to be signiﬁcant when working with time-series in contrast
to the case of i.i.d. data [S. Lee et al. 2020]. This comes as a result of the higher sim-
ilarities, correlation and temporal dependencies that are present in time-series, deﬁning
a more complex setting for CL methods. The experiments regarding the evaluation of
COBD showed the ability of the model on achieving state-of-the-art performance, while
utilizing signiﬁcant less memory than other rival models. Moreover, pruning network con-
nections based on a parameter salience metric expressed the highest achieved accuracy
when compared to the other studied metrics.
Limitations of the current study lie in some of the key characteristics and assumptions
of the described methods, as well as in the nature of the CL problem. Models developed
64
Continual Learning Methods
==PAGE:76==
to address Domain-IL scenarios ﬁnd few applications in real world problems since the
assumption of observed task-ids especially during test time is not a common phenomenon.
Task-id inference of high correlated and similar data samples such as time-series, is limited
to the corresponding power of the generative component. Another limitation is that most
of the current methods structurally fail to support a time-series forecasting setting. More
concretely, when working with forecasting in a CL setting, someone needs to answer a
series of questions, such as ’what deﬁnes a task?’
Is it a variance shift over the data
distribution or a diﬀerent task should be deﬁned by a ﬁxed time window? In addition,
even if an optimal setting exists, for both the way a task is deﬁned and the sequence in
which it arrives, real-world applications usually do not oﬀer the luxury of choosing none
of the above.
The possibility of diﬀerent experiments and adaptations of the described methods might
prove an important area for future research. Foremost, in the case of COBD an optimal
pruning approach needs additional investigation. More speciﬁcally, the current version of
CODB allocates resources without taking into consideration the task complexity and as a
result does not utilize the total memory in the most eﬃcient way. Modifying COBD into
pruning the maximum number of network synapses per task while avoiding performance
collapse will lead to a coherent CL model, exploiting conditional computation at most. In
addition, further research should evaluate the performance of this novel method based on
more demanding datasets, such as the classic Atari 2600 task set [Bellemare et al. 2012]
describing a reinforcement learning context. In the time-series domain, supplementary
studies exploring more generative components for CNDPM-TS could be very much the
key component for a complete CL model performing a Class-IL scenario. Last but not
least, further work is certainly required to disentangle the complexities describing the
deﬁnition of a CL model addressing time-series forecasting tasks.

Appendix A
Experimental Details
A.1
Permuted MNIST
For the COBD framework, a two-hidden-layer MLP classiﬁer was used with 1000 units per
layer. For each permutation 10 epochs were used for training before a pruning procedure
and 5 epochs after a pruning procedure in order to ﬁnetune the model. The regularization
sensitivity was set as λ = 1 and for each task 20% of the total network parameters were
allocated.
For both frameworks of EWC and EWC-SM, a two-hidden-layer MLP classiﬁer was used
with 1000 units per layer. For each permutation 10 epochs were used for training and the
regularization sensitivity was set as λ = 1.
For the L2 framework, a two-hidden-layer MLP classiﬁer was used with 1000 units per
layer.
For each permutation 10 epochs were used for training and the regularization
sensitivity was set as λ = 0.01.
For all of the previous frameworks, the Adam optimizer was used with learning rate
lr = 0.001.
A.2
Synthetic Control
For the framework of COBD-MH, a two-hidden-layer MLP classiﬁer was used with 500
units per layer, while the weights connecting the second hidden layer to the output layer
were deﬁned separately for each task. For each task 20 training epochs were used for
training and 5 epochs for ﬁnetuning. The regularization sensitivity was set as λ = 1 and
for each task 33% of the ﬁrst hidden layer parameters were allocated. The Adam optimizer
was used with a learning rate lr = 0.001.
For the frameworks of CNDPM-TS, CNDPM and CNDPM-TS-MI, the classiﬁers in ex-
perts were deﬁned as a two-hidden-layer MLP classiﬁer, where for the ﬁrst expert the
number of hidden units per layer was set to 64 and for the second or later experts 16 new
units per layer, which were connected to the lower layers of the existing experts, were
introduced. For the VRNN component of CNDPM-TS the hidden state of the RNN was
set as h = 50 and the VAE network was set to have latent size z = 50. In the VRNN
component no lateral connections between experts were introduced. For both the classi-
ﬁer and generative components the optimizer was set to be Adam with learning rate as
lr = 0.0005.
