Abstract 
The study is focused on the problem area of textile waste and textile waste 
recycling with an emphasis on the context of countries within the European 
Union. It seeks to conceive concepts with a practical application prospect that 
would address some critical aspects of the textile waste issues.  
The analysis was conducted via the framework of Arenas of Development, which 
maps actors in the problem area and organises them accordingly into groups called 
actor-worlds. The analysis’s results yielded primary tasks and needs latter used 
for the concept synthesis performed according to Conceptual Design principles.  
The concept synthesis led to the conception of fourteen ideas. Five of the ideas 
that appeared to score best in self-evaluation of feasibility, attractivity, and 
tractability were further nurtured into the concepts described in further detail and 
placed in the context of the Arena of development modelled in the analysis. 
After evaluating feasibility, attractivity and tractability, two different concepts 
emerged to be the most sensible for pursuing further.  Concept ‘Shirts upcycled 
into boxers’ appeared as a feasible potential start-up while ‘Sustainability grading 
and labelling’ seems to be the most meaningful system measure.

1. Introduction  
The world has already been facing the challenge of climate change, however addressing it with 
concrete directed steps have been so far very slow process, despite the consequences of the 
human activity are starting to accelerate, and humanity is already paying tolls in form of 
droughts, floods, extinction of vast number of species and so forth. It is vital to pursue initiatives 
and activities on every level from corporate industries and states to every single individual in 
order to minimize the possible consequences. In that case it is equally important not to put 
everything on one bet while looking for the solution, since there is no single solution, not even 
single aspect that needs to be addressed – for instance focusing only on developing green 
technology while ignoring overconsumption as such will not solve the problem alone, since 
the issue is much more complex and deeply rooted on every level of society. Even though the 
technology, in a form of sustainable energy and recycling facilities have its role to play, it would 
be naive to think that no other change in the way we live is required. 
Textile industry is a perfect example for this line of thinking. Humans need clothes as external 
skin for the variety of different conditions that biological evolution did not provide them with, 
but at the same time it is much more than that. Mere protection from the environment is purely 
a technical problem, but clothing and people’s appearance is also an important marker in the 
society which accents the position of certain individuals within the social hierarchy. Among 
other things, this provides certain stability and transparency in the system of our society. 
Fashion industry is very well aware of this phenomenon and actively stimulates these forces 
by setting trends and continuously shifting attributes of what is ‘fashionable’ in order to 
maximize the cashflow, ending up with people buying more than they need while getting rid of 
perfectly wearable clothing still having the functional value, but often quickly losing its 
“societal value”, flooding the second-hand market. Used items are then competing with the 
brand-new clothes that are already very cheap due to the cheap labour in the less developed 
parts of the world. Furthermore, collecting and sorting is quite an expensive venture, which 
makes it even harder for the second-hand items to compete on the market.  
Reducing the overconsumption is a crucial aspect of the problem and it should be addressed 
with the change of people’s mindset, perhaps accompanied with some legal tools that would 
put the responsibility for the product’s end of life back to the producers. Nevertheless, once this 
is achieved and the amount of textile produced and circulating is significantly reduced, there is 
still a lot of textile at its end of life that must be tackled somehow via recycling. However, the 
author bears in mind that even perfect recycling technologies should not advocate for keeping 
‘the business as usual’ in the form of over-consumption and over-production. Thus, the 
conceptual solutions proposed in the last section of the thesis do not solely stick with recycling 
but aim to solve an issue from broader multidisciplinary perspective, using engineering 
approach. 
Last but not least, authors personal motivation is to step back a bit from very specific and deeply 
technical issues and learn how to look at things in a broader perspective in order not only to 
design the best technologies from the technical point of view but at the same time work on what 
is beneficial for the society as a whole and what can guarantee its sustainable development. 
Combining the technical expertise with the holistic approach, sense for sustainability and 
humane society is something he wishes to do after finishing the studies and launching the 
professional career.

1.2. General scope 
The final and the foremost motivation of this study is to contribute towards greater 
sustainability in area of textile. Therefore, all of the proposed concepts will be developed with 
sustainability as a primary criterion. 
This also implies that there is no particular scope in the sense of the specific type of apparel.

2. Problem formulation 
This section is an overview of the critical issues posed by the apparel industry. These issues 
have several layers, and some of them are interconnected so that one issue non-linearly induces 
the other and so forth. 
2.1. Environmental impacts of the fashion industry 
The apparel industry contributes by around 4% to the global greenhouse gas emissions (Berg 
et al., 2020). What is particularly alarming is that if the current pace of decarbonization of 
apparel industry is sustained, it will not meet the 1.5° goal set as the limit for global warming 
in this century, compared to and an average temperature in the pre-industrial times (Foran, 
2016), and in fact the emissions will be almost a double in contrast of needed reduction (Berg 
et al., 2020).  
==PAGE:11==
 
 
4 
 
 
Figure 3: Distribution of greenhouse gases emissions of apparel and footwear value chain in 2018 
source: (Berg et al., 2020) 
According to Berg et al. (2020) material production itself accounts for 38% of greenhouse 
gases emissions in the life cycle of textile (see Figure 3). Including recycled materials in the 
material mixes is one of the emphasized ways towards reducing these emissions. However, 
it is substantial that the recycling of textile is proceeded with lower impact than retrieving the 
virgin materials, which is unfortunately not that straight forward as might seem on the first 
sight.  
Apart from contribution to global warming, there are many other environmental impacts 
associated to the textile industry. It ranges from exploiting non-renewable resources, 
threatening biodiversity by extensive planting of single crops, over-consumption of fresh water, 
use of pesticides and herbicides, to extensive use of chemicals during the production (Laursen 
& Hansen, 1997). Furthermore, studies are increasingly showing that textile contributes to an 
issue of microplastics, because it emits microfibres into water during the washing  (Manshoven 
et al., 2019). 
2.2. Trends in consumer’s behaviour  
According to EllenMacArthurFoundation (2017) two interconnected alarming trends are 
showing that while worldwide textile utilization decreases, its consumption increases. The 
increase in the consumption is partly due to the decrease in utilization – people are wearing 
single item fewer times than they used to, and they are substituting it by other pieces – and 
partly by growing markets especially in the parts of the world that were known as developing 
in the recent past.

There are more reasons why people use clothes fewer times, but as usual in non-linear world, 
they are interlinked influencing each other in the feedback loops uneasy to unfold. The 
phenomena known as fast fashion make people buy more clothes, because they are fashionable 
at the moment, cheap and new. The fashion itself is very important factor, in the past there used 
to be up to 6 fashion cycles a year (Laursen & Hansen, 1997) nowadays fast-fashion companies 
like ZARA introduce new pieces as often as twice a week (Wicker, 2016). In simplified 
perspective this triggers a viscous cycle where the pressure on price forces producers to 
decrease the quality of the product, which also partly results in lower utilization, because people 
actually cannot wear the item for so many times.

Figure 6 shows however, that in the UK the quality of the material stands behind only 19% of

2019). 
2.3. Textile waste 
Textile as such is a material that consists of the network of fibres that can be both natural or 
artificial (Niinimäki et al., 2020). Its characteristics depend on the material it is made of, but 
also the way the fibres are assembled using weaving, knitting, crocheting, knotting, tatting, 
felting, or braiding.  
Combining different processes, textile structures, materials, chemicals used and so forth, results 
in different virtues of the final fabric. Ranging from insulating, dust filtering, moist absorption 
or rejection light reflection or absorption and so on.  
Furthermore, textiles are used across industries including construction, automotive, chemical 
industry and many more as new materials are being discovered and tested. 
The wide range of different virtues and properties outlined above is what makes the textile so 
valuable and widely used, but at the same time it is significant obstacle for recycling. 
Increasing production of apparel, outlined in Section 2.2, also results in an increasing generation 
of textile waste.

2.3.2. Textile waste categories 
Producers and consumers are the biggest groups generating the textile waste. Therefore, the 
textile waste is mostly labelled as the post-industrial waste generated by the industry, or the 
post-consumer waste generated by the consumer.  
 
Figure 9: Handling of textile waste in 2015 
Source: (EllenMacArthurFoundation, 2017) 
2.3.2.1. 
Post-industrial waste 
The post-industrial waste is generated along the textile value chain. Typical example of the 
post-industrial waste is the rest of the fabric that remains after cutting off the desired shapes.  
The post-industrial waste essentially presents financial loss for the producer and as such creates 
bold incentive for the producer to minimize it by optimizing its handling and if possible, 
further re-use the snippets for other purpose.  
Other reason that makes it easier to process is its known quality, exact composition and 
expected volumes. This is illustrated for example by the software start-up called Reverse 
Resources that provides matchmaking between fabric cut-offs and specific parts of the garments 
that are either hidden or their appearance is not of such importance (Reverse Resources, n.d.). 
Note that the post-industrial waste is sometimes also referred to as the pre-consumer waste. For 
the purpose of this study the term ‘post-industrial waste’ will be used in order to avoid confusion 
with ‘post-consumer waste’. 
2.3.2.2. 
Post-consumer waste 
The post-consumer waste is the apparel that has been purchased by the customer and then later 
disposed. 
Post-consumer waste represents a major challenge at variety of fields from the legislation 
through economics and societal behaviour to technologies, naming a few. The bottleneck of 
post-consumer waste handling is, however, its vast diversity, as discussed earlier (Roos et al., 
2019). 
==PAGE:16==

According to EllenMacArthurFoundation (2017) 73% of the textile end up landfilled or 
incinerated, which is compared to 12% of the loss during the production much greater problem. 
As shown in this section, the post-consumer waste presents a greater challenge regarding 
technological solutions than the post-industrial waste. Furthermore, it also presents a more 
extensive threat. Therefore, this study will primarily focus on post-consumer waste.

3. Methods  
This section contains a brief overview of the main theories providing the framework for the 
study. Whereas Section 1.1 Outlining the study, in general, illustrates a high-level line of 
thinking, this section describes methods used in greater detail and maps them on sections of the 
study. 
For the sake of this study, Arenas of Development are combined with the principles of 
Conceptual design. Simply put, arenas of development aim to map the problem area and define 
the needs based on stakeholder’s interaction. The conceptual design then takes the conclusions 
from the analysis and uses it to form and evaluate the concepts.

3.2. Arenas of development  
Arenas of development is a conceptual tool that aims to discover the forces that influence the 
product development process and possible directions they can lead to. It originally evolved from 
Actor-Network theory (Callon, 1986).  
One of the main virtues of the arena perception is its fluidness and constant change, which 
mimics the real world. Size and the scope of the arena changes with new actors entering the 
arena while changing its dynamics. Actors in this sense are both companies and individuals, but 
also customers, products, standards and laws influenced by the nature of the arena (Jørgensen 
& Sørensen, 1999). This broad range qualifies it as a perfect tool for mapping such a 
complicated issue as textile waste is.  
The analysis using Arenas of development is described in Section 4. European textile arena. 
Actors with their presumed virtues and incentives are discussed in Section 4.1 Actors in the 
arena. Based on virtues and incentives outlined there, they are put together into groups of 
actors, who share these concerns. These groups are called actor-worlds. The actor-worlds built 
around the entities that create them can be perceived as united and independent entities for itself 
(Callon, 1986). The actor-worlds are formed in the section 4.2.  
The core of the analysis is section 4.3 Controversies between actor-worlds, where the static 
virtues explained in 4.1 and 4.2 are put into relation to each other and thus unveiling dynamics 
of the Development arena, that are later used in the section 6. Goal formulation.  
==PAGE:19==

In order to understand forming the arena of development in depth,  graphical representations

Note that in theory there is an emphasis on geographical position of the actors (Jørgensen & 
Sørensen, 1999). This is important for the finely tuned arena of development, as it more 
accurately describes the dynamics of the arena – for instance, actors who are based in the same 
country or share the language might be in some cases more likely to form allies. In sake of 
simplicity, this factor was not included in the whole model of the arena of development, 
nevertheless is discussed within the concepts where relevant.  
To conclude the anthropology of technology is necessary to understand, because the relation 
between people and technology is non-linear and therefore one cannot be withdrawn from 
another (Akrich, 1992). Therefore, this paper was conducted to also form a bridge between 
technologies and social aspects of the issues discussed.

This section analyses the textile and textile waste in Europe by investigating its stakeholders, 
their incentives, and concerns. The analysis is carried out using Arenas of Development as 
explained in Section 3. Methods. 
Since the problem area of textile and textile waste is broad and includes many stakeholders, the 
analysis is very general. To compensate its general nature, specific examples of issues discussed 
are provided in the form of ‘infoboxes’. 
4.1. Actors in the arena 
The first step in mapping an arena of development is listing actors of relevancy. At first, actors 
were chosen according to its relation to the textile industry or textile waste processing, including 
both new and established players. Then the policymakers were added, followed by the 
researchers and environmental phenomena like global warming and biodiversity. At last, 
facilities responsible for treating general waste, like landfills and incineration plants. These 
actors were then placed into ‘messy map’ displayed in Figure 13.  
This section aims to unveil them broader, discussing their concerns and interests, in order to 
put them into relations of each other.  
Note that since this is first iteration of the textile waste development arena, some of the actors 
or their virtues discussed might later appear redundant for this study's overall goal and therefore 
not used.

4.1.1. European Union 
European union is far reaching political and economic entity. As such it is responsible for many 
policies regarding free trade and single market. It is very complex body of organs and associated 
institutes, but for the simplicity of the arena development model it is perceived as an entity, 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
14 
 
whose main interest is to thrive economically, socially, culturally, and all other direction 
aligned with values of liberal democracy. Also, it ideally aims to integrate its member states 
regarding the common values. These things together put EU into special position in the arena 
of development as it ideally seeks the paths to support especially those actors who have positive 
impact on its values.  
There are increasing efforts in EU to prioritise protection of environment and mitigating effects 
of global warming. Scattered initiatives being previously often eclipsed by emphasis on 
economic growth were now integrated under Green Deal initiative. 
Textile belongs into section "Mobilising industry for a clean and circular economy", which is 
one out of eight thematic areas described in the Green Deal initiative (European Commission, 
2019). Specific steps to be pursued are further described in Circular Economy Action Plan 
(European Commission, 2020). 
Apart legal tools and regulating the industry EU promotes sustainability by providing funds 
through various programs. Horizon 2020 and InterReg are perhaps the most remarkable ones 
worth mentioning. 
4.1.2. Governments 
In principle governments of member countries should play a similar role as the EU in the 
context of the modelled development arena, but emphasising the incentives of their country 
acting more locally. In practice however, the interests are much scattered, especially the 
question of Green Deal and active steps against global warming are perceived controversially 
in some countries. This is especially case for the newer members of the EU, like former 
members of soviet bloc. Many of these countries pursued heavy industrialization as a mean to 
economically chase more developed western countries and are now afraid that environmental 
efforts will hinder their growth. On the other hand, Nordic countries, whose economics has 
been growing already for decades, are emphasising sustainability as their main domain and 
export article. Specifically, Denmark, for example, invests lot of money and resources into 
education and innovation to fulfil the UN’s sustainable development goals (United Nations, 
2017). Furthermore, it also set an ambitious goal of cutting greenhouse gases by 70% in 2030 
compared to 1990 (Timperley, 2019).

4.1.3. Municipalities 
In regard to textile, municipalities are often responsible for tackling the waste in general. 
Different regions, sometimes even in the same country, have different approaches. This is more 
elaborated upon in section 3.3.6 Collectors and sorters. 
In general municipalities do not collect separated textile waste, but instead let private companies 
or charities place their containers on the public area. In some cases, they require fee for the 
bin’s placement (WRAP, 2012). 
Often, they are responsible for processing the general waste. This often also contains textile, 
but in most cases, it is treated as a general waste. 
Primary incentive of municipalities should be ideally well-being of its citizens aligned with 
governments’ directives and long-term strategies. However, it often has its own specific interest 
implied from its usual processes and assets. For example, some of municipalities own landfills 
or incineration facilities and it is in their interest to use them as optimally as possible. 
Nonetheless, this would again present too much of a detail, unbearable for the model on 
continental scale. Thus, in the case municipality acts as a collector who profits from the service 
(no matter if by selling used apparel or collecting fees from citizens), it is perceived as a 
collector in context of the model. Keep in mind though that if assessing specific cases, these 
nuances must be discerned. 
4.1.4. Producers 
Whole manufacturing process consists of long chain of suppliers, ranging from those 
responsible for getting the raw material, treating it (refining for synthetic materials, chemical 
and mechanical cleaning for natural ones), spinning yarn, weaving or knitting, trimming, 
==PAGE:23==
 
 
16 
 
sewing and so forth up to retail. This category of actors is therefore difficult to capture, 
especially in the focus on European context (many of the suppliers are not even European 
companies). But since the predominant incentive along the supply chain is more or less the 
same, producers are not further divided by the activity, but only according to a scale. 
4.1.4.1. 
Global Corporations 
Even though some of the big players, such as H&M, Adidas, Zara etc. have their historic origins 
in Europe, but over the years they developed into global corporation, spreading all over the 
world both with their production and markets they are selling to. Moreover, most of them have 
a vast network or suppliers, which can overlap with other brands as well. For these reasons, this 
category is not limited to European or European based companies, but to those operating on the 
European market. 
These companies are responsible for the current state of fashion industry. They are the patrons 
of the status quo and the ones who currently profit the most from it. Some of them are sensing 
however, that certain move towards the sustainability, might open them other markets, 
especially in western Europe, where most of the market has been pretty stable and saturated 
over the past years and current hunger for the sustainability, coming especially from the 
younger generation (Beltrami et al., 2020), is the first market disruption in the long time. Even 
despite several efforts balancing on the edge of greenwashing (Majláth, 2017) rather than 
having real measurable impact, it is a notable shift, especially because these companies have 
vast resources that they can invest and possibly accelerate transition towards more circular 
business. 
Some of the bold statements made in the industry include Zara pledging in 2020 to use 100% 
sustainable fabrics by 2025, following H&M that set 2030 as a deadline for using only recycled 
or sustainable materials. Adidas claims to exclude using virgin polyester by 2024 (Beltrami et 
al., 2020). 
The caveat in general lies in their natural tendency to support only those initiatives, that have a 
potential to bring them more profit, or alternatively secure them leading role in an emerging 
sector. Therefore, the assumption for the model is that they are generally reluctant to those 
paths, that are too risky for them despite their undoubtful value regarding sustainability. 
Due to their vast profits and position on the market, they are very influential. For instance, who 
they choose as their suppliers might have an impact on the whole arena of development. 
4.1.4.2. 
 
Small and medium enterprises (SMEs) 
Compared to corporations they do not operate with such vast resources to spend on 
development. On the other hand, their smaller size can provide them with greater momentum 
and flexibility in reaction to the trends.  
In Europe they represent 99% of all enterprises in the EU, which makes them vital for the 
development arena. For the same reason it is also primary focus of EU’s policies and subsidies 
(Eurostat, 2018). 
In order to gain more leverage, they cluster in associations and assemblies as described in 
Section 4.1.5.

4.1.6. Collectors and sorters 
Collectors and sorters are very diverse groups of actors and stakeholders and some of the EU 
countries solve this differently than others. Differences are not only between the countries, but 
sometimes even within a region. In one place the textile is collected by charity, while in other 
the same thing is performed by private company. Moreover, some of the entities are performing 
only collecting while others both collect and sort. 
Nevertheless, basic principles are the same and all stakeholders in this category hold the same 
incentive – yield maximum profit on the collected textile, mostly by selling it for re-use. 
Following paragraph further explains  
There is a common misconception that charities donate the given clothing to the ones in need. 
Some partly do so, but for many this is not economically feasible due to the expensive manual 
sorting, which also requires specific knowledge. Furthermore, people in need have specific 
requisites2 that are rarely met by the majority of textile waste3. Therefore, both charities and 
other collectors with no income from waste-service fees aim to turn the textile waste into profit 
by selling it further. Every batch collected either through the bins or handed over in local charity 
shops contains diverse textile pieces ranging from the expensive trendy easily sellable pieces 
to damaged or stained clothing of low or no value. In order to dig the “cream” (the best apparel 
in a batch) all of the gathered clothing must be manually sorted by trained workers (L. 
Harubcová, personal communication, July 22, 2020). Whether the clothes are sorted directly in 
the country where collected is mainly given by the economics of the whole venture, namely 
cost of demanding manual sorting on one hand and potential profit from sold clothes (Watson 
et al., 2016). Cheap fast fashion together with lower quality of the dumped textile, made the 
providers of second-hand textile optimize yield by utilizing the whole potential of the collected 
waste which is beneficial from the environmental perspective (Watson et al., 2016). 
On European level there is yet to be developed enhanced system for tracking the textile waste 
flows, as well as other materials, in order to determine the afterlife of the item more precisely. 
Currently available data on Eurostat only operate with the total textile waste, not taking account 
material flows between recycles (Roos et al., 2019). Some investigations in particular European 
regions are pursued by local stakeholders, especially in Scandinavian countries (Roos et al., 
2019) and Netherlands (N. Papu, personal communication, November 6, 2020).  
The interviews with collectors and sorters in the Czech Republic showed that the proportion of 
high value textiles in the textile waste depends on where it is collected. Whereas capital 
generates notable fraction of clothing in good shape and relevant to market, collections in 
villages and small town are dominated by the items at their end of life, mostly appliable only 
for energy recovery.

4.1.7. Reuse 
As other business, they earn profit by providing value to the customers. In this case second-
hand garment. However, unlike standard retailers they have limited ways of influencing the 
final offer. They might know through the experience what sources tend to yield the maximum 
profit, but there is always a degree of uncertainty. 
Second hands in EU countries used to be primarily budget option for low-income families and 
individuals, but recent years have noted the upward trend of people engaging second-hands as 
part of the life-style. Surprisingly enough, rather than concern about sustainability, social and 
nostalgic reason are reported driving this (Steffen, 2017). 
Apart from traditional second-hand retailers, there is number of apps and online markets. Some 
work as second-hand e-shops, while others are more peer-to-peer trades.

4.1.10. Recovery and disposal 
Stakeholders staying on the last two bars of waste treatment hierarchy on Figure 7 responsible 
for treating the waste which is no longer recyclable. The economics of the waste disposals 
differs according specific countries. In general, however they are paid per tones disposed by 
the municipalities (that gather money from citizens) or the companies. Their incentive is 
therefore to maintain or increase the flow that secures them income. The extent to which they 
existentially depend on the revenue varies whether they are publicly or privately owned. This 
makes them resilient to recycling technologies that might threaten their income.  
Moreover, the incineration plants are generating heat and electricity, which puts them into 
position of indispensable grid supplier, which might even further inhibit efforts to lower their 
production. 
Unlike incineration, landfills are considered to be the least sustainable way of waste treatment, 
leading to leak of the toxins and CO2 emissions. European Union pioneers reduction and 
eventual abandonment of this technology. In some countries, like Czech Republic for instance, 
there is strong lobby defending it, which keeps landfills to be economically the best possible 
option. (S. Jonášová, personal communication, March 11, 2020) 
Apart from classical incineration advanced methods of high temperature recovery are emerging 
lead by pyrolysis and its derivatives. Partly organic homogenous substance is gained, 
additionally to the heat, in form of oil, char, or gas. There are usually higher feedstock 
homogeneity requirements compared to incineration, but fewer limitations than when 
performing ‘traditional recycling’. 
4.1.11. Consumer 
There are many types of consumers depending on their shopping behaviour. They differ 
according to shopping frequency, total amount they spend on clothing per month on average, 
usual price range of the items, emotional approach (e.g., they find it rewarding), reaction to 
discounts and advertising, sustainability concerns and so forth.  
==PAGE:30==
 
 
23 
 
For the sake of simplicity, ‘modern type’ of consumer was chosen for the arena of development 
as presumed mainstream’s representative. This type of consumer is influenced by promotions 
and advertising, shops often, seeks for high shopping comfort, and looks for the best value to 
cost ratio (Trembošová et al., 2015). This implies that his attitude towards sustainability is not 
necessarily negative, but at the same time it is not his priority and stands behind factors like 
price and shopping comfort. 
Therefore, similar virtues will be applied also for consumer’s second role, potentially crucial 
for the concept’s development, that is responsibility for disposing the textile. That means, that 
he is willing to donate or separate the garment but is not willing to spend too much time and 
effort doing so. 
It is assumed that so called mainstream consumer differs in different countries and even at 
specific location, which might be taken into account with eventual next iterations of the model. 
Furthermore, studies are showing that younger generations are willing to spend more in sake of 
sustainability, as illustrated on Figure 16 (Beltrami et al., 2020).

4.1.12.1. 
Public research 
Organisations labelled as public are for the purpose of this thesis those institutes, that are

Academic 
The ones marked here as academic organisations are mostly Universities and other scientific

valuable articles regarding textile and textile waste issues. (RISE, 2020) 
4.1.12.3. 
Private researchers and think tanks 
Private researchers range from those with general interest to those who only tackle with textile 
issues. McKinsey consultancy is an example of the first one, company with broad scope across 
most of the industries, which is beside other things also generating reports on textile industry. 
Other examples, Ellen MacArthur Foundation, non-governmental organisation based in UK or 
Dutch Circle Economy both of which focus on the challenges of circular economy, lay 
somewhere between the general think tank and narrowly scoped institute. They publish papers 
regarding the sustainability and claim sustainable economy to be their main incentive, but at 
the same time, they have to generate income to cover their expanses, and this is where it gets 
complicated. In order to sustain their independency, they often apply for national and EU 
subsidies aimed for sustainable development, but this is often not enough, which forces them 
to collaborate with the companies within industry. 
Last category are organisations strictly focused on sustainability in textile industry, such as 
Global Fashion Agenda formerly called Danish Fashion Institute, or Sustainable Apparel 
Coalition based in California. 
As a caveat with forementioned researchers, one must always question what their incentives 
are and up to what extent are they connected to the industry, before taking any of their 
conclusions for granted.  
4.1.13. Environment 
Environment as an actor of non-human character stands for the most complicated and complex 
players in the arena, since it is composed of numerous processes, where some of them go against 
each other, influencing themselves in complex feedback loops that are impossible to unfold and 
precisely predict.  
It could be perceived both as an actor and an actor-world (explained in the following section), 
but such a division would not add any value for the model due to its complexity and non-linear 
==PAGE:32==
 
 
25 
 
nature. Moreover, the main interest of this study is sustainability and therefore environment as 
whole, not merely its part like climate change. Therefore, on all levels it is noted just as an 
Environment. Its constituents are to as actors, when addressing them individually. 
Unlike others, this system does not have any clear incentives and it often shakes with the cards 
on table. In sake of better understanding, some environmental actors are displayed below. 
4.1.13.1. 
 
Climate change 
One of the paradoxes of this actor is the fact that it actually was brought up to live by the humans 
themselves with the textile industry being one of the large contributors. 
Even though this actor was present for some time already, it gained attention in recent years 
when its presence got more tangible. There are lot of specific effects of climate change and 
other man induced environmental threads, where some of them even directly influence the 
textile industry itself (cotton crops for instance is part of the industry that relies on certain 
climate conditions). However, for the sake of this analysis climate change will be perceived as 
an actor that drives the industry towards sustainability without digging deeper into its specific 
aspects. More on this topic can be found in Section 2. 
4.1.13.2. 
 
Covid-19 
Global pandemic is very specific actor with both short-term and long-term effects on the 
European textile arena. Firstly, there is no doubt that the pandemic crisis will result in some 
sort of economic crisis with presumably long-lasting effects putting extra pressure on the 
business. These outcomes are difficult to anticipate – while consumers particularly hit by the 
crisis might incline towards cheap clothing supporting well settled fast-fashion business, the 
producers who are already slowly being dragged by the Green Deal and upcoming legislation 
might be even deeper motivated by the crisis to intensify those sustainable efforts that go 
together with cutting the costs. 
However, since there are still many variables and it is not directly related to the concepts4, it 
will not be further elaborated.  
4.1.14. Other actors  
Many actors with an impact on the development arena did not fit into this model within the 
timeframe of this study. Some of them might be even substantial for certain specific scenarios,

Firstly, there are plenty of projects, textile research hubs, accelerators and so forth who often 
lay somewhere in between the actors listed above. Some of them are closer to the public and 
research sector, while others are rather business driven. For example, Resyntex is EU funded 
project assembled to map the EU textile environment, but also responsible for developing 
concrete technical solutions and partner up with the industry (Boiten et al., 2017). As an 
example of project on other side of the spectrum can be Fashion for good, defining itself as a 
platform for sustainable innovation with partners from across the manufacturing industry

Actor-worlds are formed based on common discourses, goals and concerns (Callon, 1986). 
Their purpose is to make the arena clearer and more understandable, but it is to be noted that 
this simplification also bares a degree of inaccuracy as there are inner frictions in most of the 
actor-worlds.

stakeholders however, they hold together to reduce the barriers that stand between them and the

Research world is united by the intention of understanding the issues on as many levels as

Waste treatment world is responsible for tackling the waste, but since there are remarkable

Public world encompasses all of the publicly funded regulatory and executive bodies on all

Environment is not the world, but rather complex actor as explained in Section 4.1.13 that 
influences everyone.  
Consumer is also special category, that interacts with all of the worlds including Environment, 
which is why it is separated. Similarly, to an environment, it does not change between levels of 
actors and actor-worlds. It might in further iterations if more consumer categories were added. 
4.3. Controversies between actor-worlds 
This section aims to present some of the most substantial controversies between the actor-
worlds that are related to the sustainability and textile waste.  
Note that not all of the controversies presented are only between the actor-worlds. Some are

this study, consumerism is not controversy itself, but encapsulating term that covers Over-

4.3.1.1. 
Overconsumption 
The term over-consumption in this study5 refers to state when consumers are purchasing 
greater amounts of textile then they actually need in terms of covering and protecting 
their bodies. This was discussed in Section 2.2 and illustrated on study from UK, where 39% 
of respondents are disposing textile from other reasons than flaws or not fitting size. 
Producer world is in general benefitting from overconsumption gaining profit as consumers 
are craving for new things. Consumers are satisfying their needs, which are encouraged by the 
producer world (changing trends, promoting new things etc.). Environment is harmed because 
of pollution and resource exploitation. Position of Public world and Waste treatment world 
is a moot point. 
Waste treatment world should theoretically benefit from the surplus of waste, but it is mostly 
only living from profit it is generating and therefore dependent on demand for their products 
and services. Demand for recycled products are in general very low and the revenues are not 
high either (Watson et al., 2016). Second-hand market in Europe is in general over-saturated  
and gathered worn low quality pieces from fast fashion have to compete with new fast fashion 
(WRAP UK, 2016). If it had to be concluded, waste treatment world is in current setting in 
general rather suffering than benefiting from the over-consumption. 
Public world on one hand is the most remarkable advocate of Environment and some of its 
actors (like municipalities) are in charge of tackling disposed waste, which put it rather in the 
opposition to overproduction, but at the same time overall benefiting from healthy and growing 
economy. Being torn in this dilemma Public worlds is likely to promote recycling solutions, 
repairing and new business models (renting, sharing etc.) in order to keep economy running 
while mitigating impact on the environment and perhaps avoiding the fundamental question 
whether people actually need so many new clothes. 
4.3.1.2. 
Fast fashion 
The term fast fashion is generally understood to mean cheap and often low quality apparel sold 
in highest possible rate in order to adapt to the most recent trends (Vadicherla et al., 2017). This 
phenomenon contributes to the overconsumption in subsection above, but also generates issues 
its own – to maximize the net income companies are moving production sides to the countries 
with low wages, which is often associated with low working standards, low regulation on use 
of chemicals during the production, emissions on transportation. Furthermore, producers are 
using processes that are the cheapest, but not necessarily the most sustainable ones. Also, due 
to cutting costs he overall quality is not high and the goods are mostly not repairable which 
results in shorter lifespan of the products. 
Incentives and acceptance of fast fashion is very similar to the once drawn in sub-section above.

4.3.1.3. 
Overproduction 
Overproduction as referred to in this study is situation when a company produces more goods 
than it is capable to sell. They do this in chase of maximizing the profit and preventing the 
opposite situation – lack of stock. The deadstock is in some cases sold with great discounts, 
relocated to different stores or even country and in others recycled or incinerated. 
This is special controversy, because the ones causing it, some of the actors in Producer world, 
are not directly benefiting from it – they are benefiting from yielding the maximum of 
potential profit while losing money on deadstock. Waste treatment world theoretically profits 
from it as companies have to pay the disposal costs. Environment, as usually, suffers.

4.3.5. Use of new chemicals and new materials 
This controversy is in some respects tied to the overconsumption, fast-fashion and 
overregulation elaborated on above, but it is specific issue on its own and crucial regarding both 
sustainability and recyclability. 
Producer world is coming up with different new materials, chemicals used, and treatments 
applied in order to cut costs, attract new customers, and get ahead competition. These 
substances and processes are not necessarily sustainable. In some cases, they alone might be 
more sustainable in some respect than the old material or process but recycling for instance is 
not yet in competence of Waste Treatment World causing then more harm the good and posing 
a friction particularly between producer world and waste treatment world. Moreover, negative 
effects on the Environment are often discovered long after material’s deployment (for instance 
microfibres being released into water while washing the clothes). Public world’s role is ought 
to be regulative preventing negative effects whereas Research world often discovers them.

their stakeholders guard the position to maintain profit, sometimes even lobbying against new 
emerging technologies that seem to be more effective.

5.1. Sorting 
Most of the textile sorting in Europe is performed either entirely manually, or combined with 
technology, mostly using machinery to transport and physically sort the items, while trained 
workers are conducting the process. 
Sorting varies primarily based on the desired outcome. Some of the batches even undercome 
several rounds of sorting as the batch travels from the source country through the several stops 
as each sorting centre cherry-picks what is left valuable. Decision making on sorting for the 
second-hand market is always performed by skilled workers based on their training and 
experience. This is due to high range of parameters to be checked in every item and complexity 
of their combinations (for instance ripped jeans can be both designed and sold that way or worn 
out and thus unusable). Designing of such a machine is challenging, but what is perhaps even 
greater issue is the economical side of it, rather than the technical one – second-hand market is 
already low margin field and such a machine would require high investments.

5.2. Labelling 
All apparel sold in EU must be equipped with the label that provides consumer with an 
information about materials the piece consists of and how to treat it. However, due to several 
issues it is not yet able to assist during recycling process.  
The most importantly, as shown in latter sections, the technologies in general require high 
degree of purity and homogeneity in the intake. Accuracy is general problem due to lack of 
auditing, but also as a result of high accuracy margins. Also, fibres are being washed away in 
time, and in some cases, even deliberate manipulation with the fabric’s composition was 
reported (Circle Economy, 2019). Consequently, consumers are likely to be misled in about 
40% (Circle Economy, 2019). 
Additionally, the label itself can present a physical challenge for the recycling technology, as it 
often consists of a different material than the rest of the garment and thus needs to be physically 
separated from the fabric like buttons and zippers. Additionally, people tend to cut off the labels 
soon after the purchase to make it more comfortable to wear (Circle Economy, 2019).

5.5.1.2. 
Fibre recycling 
Process of fibre recycling is conducted on industrial lines. There are different machines that 
vary in nuances, but in principle they do the same – open the fabrics by applying mechanical 
force. This is mostly performed through several stages of cutting and shredding, where every 
stage presents advance in coarseness moving to finer shredders. Cutting mills or shredders are 
usually pairs of cylinders with different blades or needles (Haner & Bartl, n.d.).  
Before the shredding, it is necessary to eliminate all of the foreign materials elements like 
buttons and zippers that could damage shredders. All of this is in most cases performed 
manually.   
If it is required for the final recycled product, fibres can be after shredding mechanically 
separated using dynamic Separators like aerodynamic separator, a magnetic separator and an 
eddy current separator (Haner & Bartl, n.d.). 
Note that specific machinery always depends on the desired end product. The obtained fibres 
can be spun into new yarns, using a proportion of virgin cotton (the proportion depends on 
purpose of the fabric), glued into non-woven fabrics, pressed into pieces of thermal or acoustic 
insulation or used as fillings for upholstery (Lou et al., 2005). Fibres vary in length which 
predestines their possible applications – as a general rule of thumb the longer fibre the broader 
range of utilization. It was found, that small sized fabrics lead to an increase in short fibre ratio 
(Ütebay et al., 2019). Also structure of the fabrics might have an impact on the output quality 
of the fibres regarding mean fibre length and tenacity (Ütebay et al., 2019). 
In general, it is very well scalable and existing projects range from those which fit in ship-
container to the upscaled recycling plants.

for synthetic fibres since natural fibres do not melt (Harmsen & Bos, 2020). Moreover, it also 
requires very high purity of the feedstock, which is main reason why most of the materials 
suitable for this process can be remelted only few times, usually up to 3, or proportion of virgin

Cotton

As illustrated on Figure 30 there are currently three main processes of cotton chemical recovery 
(Le, 2018): 
1. Acid hydrolysis 
2. Lyocell process 
3. Dissolution in ionic liquids 
Hydrolysis of cellulose using hydrochloric acid or sulphuric acid at elevated temperature and 
pressure (or alternatively use cellulase enzymes that are capable of the same under lower 
temperature and atmospheric pressure). This yields glucose molecules that are unsuitable for 
reconstructing new cellulose but are used for production of new chemical building blocks 
(Harmsen & Bos, 2020). 
Lyocell process had been successfully used to produce textile from the cellulose utilizing 
sources like wood and pulp, which makes it suitable to be applied on cotton as it mainly consists 
of cellulose. However, this process requires 100% of cotton feedstock and consumes lot of 
water (Le, 2018). Most of the chemical recycling projects with focus on cotton reviewed for 
this paper are using lyocell process.  
Ionic liquids dissolution process is currently under development in academic environment on 
Aalto University and University of Helsinki. Its main advantage is possibility of using it for the 
mixed feedstock, but it has not been commercially applied yet (Le, 2018).

Polyester chemical recycling is in general very well mastered process, that can result either in 
the original monomers ethylene glycol (EG) and terephthalic acid (TPA) or larger molecules, 
known as dimers or trimers like dimethyl terephthalate (BHET) or bis(2-hydroxyethyl) 
terephthalate (BHET) (Harmsen & Bos, 2020). Since the building blocks obtained are fairly 
small, it is possible to remove contaminants and dyes, which enables polymerisation of 
monomers into virgin quality PET (Harmsen & Bos, 2020). 
IBM has been developing catalytic glycolysis process called ‘Volcat’ aimed for polyester 
recovery (Allen, 2019). In popular science magazines like for example (Smith, 2020) it is 
claimed to be capable of treating unsorted and unwashed PET bottles, which makes it suitable 
for plastic goods hard to recycle like clothing and toys. There is lack of data and scientific 
articles to assume anything based on it, but it shows that there is an ongoing research in the

5.5.4. Other technical possibilities 
Apart from recycling, some of the textiles are claimed to be biodegradable and therefore 
dissolve in soil, which would be in principle applicable for all of the natural fibres if no 
additional chemical treatment was applied (Roos et al., 2019).  
Besides that, pyrolysis is sometimes discussed as better alternative to traditional thermal 
recovery, but it was not further investigated in this paper. 
5.6. Sustainability of recycling technologies 
Making conclusions about the sustainability of recycling technologies is particularly tricky. It 
depends on many factors and such assessment would be enough for an entire study. 
Furthermore, the author of this study lacks solid expertise in chemical recycling that is 
indispensable for textile recycling and is recently gaining attention of the textile community. 
In general however, mechanical recycling and especially fabric recycling is perceived to be 
the most sustainable way of recycling, if certain conditions are met13 (Roos et al., 2019). This 
also corresponds with the fact, that most of the chemical or thermomechanical treatments 
require mechanical pre-treatments. So even in case non-harmful chemicals are used and 
generated as a by-product, the treatment requires more resources.  
On the other hand, there might special situations, where textile chemical recycling facility is 
in the synergy of other business that produces chemicals required for the textile recycling as 
by product. This is however mere speculation. 
Without doubt, chemical recycling provides outcomes of higher quality that last longer. 
Unfortunately, lasting longer is not virtue that current textile industry focuses on as shown in 
the analysis of the development arena. 
Mechanical recycling has been over the years well mastered discipline and it provides sufficient 
material for upholstery and construction, whose quality most likely raised its peak, probably 
not leaving too much space for the improvement in this field. However, since there are studies 
about what influences the resulting fibre length and tenacity, which are the main precursors for

longer fibres intended to completely substitute the virgin materials.  
Caveat of recycling in general is that they possibly induce so-called rebound effect. That stands 
for the situation when increased offer of cheaper recycled materials, in this case textile, leads 
to the increased demand (Gielen & Moriguchi, 2002).  In short it means that extensive use of 
recycling technologies does not necessarily mean reduction of virgin materials usage or amount 
of waste generated.  
The bottom line is that recycling has only limited ability to tackle the sustainability issues 
of the textile industry as concluded for instance in Berg et al. (2020) or Roos et al. (2019). 
6. Goal formulation 
This section concludes and synthesises findings identified during the initial analysis to define 
goal formulation for the concepts and document train of thoughts that led to their conception.  
The initial focus was set on recycling as proved by the title of this study.  However, the 
performed analysis showed that recycling is a partial problem, and in many cases, it is rather an 
effort to mitigate symptoms of a different cause. Therefore, to maintain sustainability as a 
primary focus, the solution space had been expanded from the focus merely on recycling to 
include more of the issues identified.  
 The analysis of different stakeholders in the arena of development determined consumerism 
as a root cause behind generating so much waste. Besides the waste, there is a good reason to 
believe that if the consumption, interlinked with a generation of waste decreased, most of the 
issues described in Section 2 would also have diminished.

Concept, as described in Andreasen et al. (2015), is “a design proposal that is detailed enough

realization and success” supposing that task and intention reflect both need satisfaction and the 
knowledge required (Andreasen et al., 2015). This differentiates it from ideas, which are 
somewhat „sudden initial thoughts, imaginations or visions“ (Andreasen et al., 2015). Apart 
from the concepts, the analysis also helped generate some ideas presented in Section 7.7. 
The concepts are explained using the following structure: 
• Targeted problems and task 
• Concept description 
• Arena placement 
• Limitations 
Where ‘Targeted problems and task’ explain what issues identified in the analysis it aims to

problems targeted are other associated issues identified during the analysis.  
‘Concept description’ unveils fundamental principles it is built on. In case the concept consists

regarding the actors inside the system.  
‘Arena placement’ puts the concept into the context of the stakeholders in the arena of

‘Limitations’ discuss the significant challenges that the concept may face complemented with

7.1.2. Concept description 
Many of the consumers are aware that the production of clothing, in general, is harming the 
environment and generates greenhouse gases contributing to the global warming. However, this 
general knowledge might not emerge in the moment of buying actually buying the garment.

7.5. Semi-Automatic sorting system using AI 
Proposed concept relies on machine learning to determine market potential of sorted item. 
Operators are only expected to place the item on the hanger, which does not require any special

7.5.1. Targeted problems 
Main tasks: 
• Stimulate the reuse sector. 
Related problems targeted: 
==PAGE:74==

• Yet non existing sufficient automatic sorting 
• Emissions produced due logistics for collecting/sorting/recycling/disposal

7.5.2. Concept description 
The machine’s staple activity is to sort the textile according to the requirements of subsequent 
processors, whose intention can be both recycling and reusing. Pursuing this goal, the system 
consists of 3 main organs namely Handling organ, Identification organ and sorting organ. The 
Figure 45 pinpoints 3 main activities that are aligned with the staple function of each main 
organ. 
7.5.2.1. 
Activity and organ domains 
The main activities illustrated on the Figure 45 are reflected in the organ domains pictured at

Figure 46: Fundamental organ domains. In the sketch operator is also providing oral feedback to the 
machine regarding the category he determined. Note, that this feature is not expected to be

Handling organ as a piece of hardware responsible for delivering textile to the sensors and 
latter to sorting boxes further divides into smaller organs like holder and propulsion, bearing in 
mind that more organs might be formed when planning the concept in greater detail. Holder 
organ must be capable of accommodating to different sizes of clothes to sustain the correct 
shape and when identified, drop it seamlessly on the sorting conveyor belt.  
Note, that main function of the holder organ is to support the apparel with the shape, for better 
scanning. It is assumed that apparel freely laying on the conveyor belt would be significantly 
more difficult to assess and identify. 
Identification organ is brain of the machine as it decides how to handle the clothing which is 
secured by cooperation of sensory organ that provides data to the controlling organ to evaluate 
them.  
Sensory organ contains optical sensors where the first one captures high resolution images 
from the front, back and the side and other scans it for flaws (especially holes) by using coarse 
3D laser-scanning and then comparing the data from these methods16. There is also an option 
of expanding the systems operational range by including NIR (Near infrared sensors) and use 
it for material analysis, as performed for example by FiberSort that is already available on the 
market. 
Controlling organ is responsible for decision making by processing data from the sensors and 
comparing the identified patterns with the neural networks. Quality of the output provided by 
AI depends on the data that were used to train it. As a general rule of thumb, the more data is 
fed to the neural networks, the more accurate results it is able to generate. This might be a caveat 
especially in the pilot phase due to the high number of parameters that vary and small nuances 
between them. Hence console for cooperation with human operator is implemented as a part of

8. Discussion 
The aim of the study was to perform an extensive and general analysis on the matter of post-
consumer textile waste leading to specific ideas and concepts, aiming on filling the niches 
exposed by the analysis. The study resulted in offering fourteen ideas. Five of those were after 
self-evaluation further nurtured into the concepts. Since the concepts were the foremost goal 
of the study, they will be discussed first, followed by the discussion of the analysis that was 
used as a tool to find them. 
Despite considering the feasibility and possible engagement of actors involved, more detailed 
studies of feasibility and other assessments might be required as discussed in the following 
paragraphs. 
EU-wide ‘Sustainability grading and labelling’ is particularly complicated and extensive 
concept, requiring participation of many stakeholders. Apart from aforementioned feasibility 
study, detailed research on customer’s behaviour is required to assess, wherever it has potential 
to affect consumer’s behaviour. In case the assumptions were verified, the concept would have 
a potential of far-reaching impacts on a drift of the development arena towards sustainability, 
for relatively low cost of implementing legislation and guidelines. 
Viability of ‘Self-sorting system’ highly depends on the ratio of consumers’ motivation (the 
ones disposing the garment) to barriers for doing so, as discussed in limitations of the concept. 
There are also possible variations in the practical implementation in order to maximize its 
potential. For instance, instead of requirement for the warehouse as initially designed, goods 
can be picked directly by the ‘buyer’ from re-use sector, wherever it is individual or second-
hand retailer. Deployment of AI and machine learning might also have the potential of 
stimulating its usability.   
Unlike other concepts ‘Shirts upcycled into boxers’ appears as relatively easy to test and deploy 
in the pilot version. A part of exploring the idea more profoundly was a market research aiming 
on finding similar projects adopting the similar idea. Although there are two similar projects, 
they were not exclusively focused on upcycled boxers. Either way it indicates the potential 
demand on the market. As there are not many apparent barriers, the author of this thesis would 
like to pursue this project further as a starting point for his future work in the field. Expected 
key aspect of this project is mastering marketing and approach to customers. 
‘Modular footwear as a service’ seems to be more complicated for launching the 
entrepreneurship as there are more variables. To start with, solid research must be conducted or 
reviewed verifying that the market is mature enough for this business model. On the other hand, 
the concept elaborated here might serve as a perception of an ideal and in practice start with its 
smaller chunks. For instance, instead of designing the shoes from scratch in the initial phase 
and investing a lot of money into development, it might be favourable to merge with existing 
shoemakers, whose products are in principle repairable. By pursuing this, lack of variety 
discussed in the concept’s limitations might be resolved.  In the scenario that the business case 
turned out to be feasible and had its niche in the market, more development might be pursued 
in the next steps. 
‘Semi-automatic system using AI’ is on the first sight challenging in technical implementation, 
but its potential contribution for the re-use sector would be remarkable as it could help with the 
utilization of items closer to its countries of origin. Meaning that with the deployment of this 
==PAGE:86==
 
 
79 
 
technology, the items would no longer have to be sent to the countries to the developing 
countries. The bottleneck of this concept is considered to be the recognition feature using 
machine learning. As with any machine learning technologies, this highly depends on the 
quality and relevance of the dataset used for training of neural networks. In that sense, it is 
rather a software project and therefore the supposed pilot phase might not in the beginning 
require high investments for developing and building the whole system as sketched in the 
concept. Alternatively, existing sorting facilities, that use operator’s voice commands to 
determine the sorting category, might be equipped with high resolution cameras pointed on the 
conveyor belt and matched with the determined category. This might serve as a training data 
set for the neural networks. Theoretically it could eventually turn out that the handling organ 
designed in the concept of supporting the garment (and thus provide it with standard shape) 
would not even be required.  
Apart from more elaborated concepts presented above, the exploration yielded five ‘other 
ideas’ with different levels of maturity ranging from ‘close to concept’ to ‘one sentence vision’. 
Even though some of them might turn out to be even more valuable than the proposed concepts, 
there was not enough space for bringing them further, as most of them appeared by the end of 
the project’s timeframe. Nevertheless, it is a useful reservoir for possible future work. To 
pinpoint some, ‘System for sharing information about the textile in EU’ might deserve special 
attention in the future, since data sharing among stakeholders is emphasised in many reports 
and potential contribution of such a platform would be remarkable. This would provide better 
fundamentals for decision making and attract new actors at the same time. However, it was 
found that very similar projects are being developed and since it is not author’s field of 
expertise, it was not further elaborated upon. The second important direction hinted in the ideas 
is optimalization of mechanical recycling technologies to drain all of its potential as it might 
be in general the most sustainable way of recycling. 
As mentioned at the beginning of this section, an analysis of the stakeholders using arenas of 
development was from the start meant to be primarily a mean for revealing the niches and 
possibly coming up with the concepts. Therefore, it must be perceived in this context rather 
than stand-alone academic research. It was naturally in the author’s interest to perform it in the 
highest quality possible to gain the concepts that are relevant, but as the creative process is 
mostly non-linear and ideas were emerging while working on analysis, some of the originally 
set directions were abandoned on behalf of the ideas and so forth. Furthermore, despite calls 
from some of the researchers20, there was practically no integral meta-level study on the 
stakeholders, that could serve as a solid starting point. 
Despite that, the author believes that further elaborating of the development arena might be 
very helpful for tackling the textile’s questionable issues and particularly useful for EU as the 
only central player on the ‘map’ with the emphasis on the protection of environment as its 
primary agenda. Identified needs and desires can be matched with needs regarding environment 
to efficiently shift towards sustainability.  
To further explore current development arena, it needs to get more specific. Next iterations 
might thus consist of adding new actors, including policies and legislation, forming finer actor

worlds while taking into consideration their geographic location, replacing some of the general 
placeholders by specific companies, institutes, and regions, adding relations and so forth.  
As expected, there is no single solution. More importantly, the solution might not even be of a 
technological nature, because new technology always brings new challenges and problems. No 
doubt, more of the unbiased public and academic research is needed, but after conducting the 
analysis, one thing appears to be certain – until there is a broad societal shift towards

has to be flexible enough to allow mistakes in initial judgement as well as regular iterations.  
The bottom line of the analysis is that there is no single villain responsible for the environmental 
crisis we are facing. We all are.  
9. Conclusion 
An analysis explored the current state of textile waste processing and its methods in Europe. 
The exploration was conducted via framework of Arenas of Development, that has evolved 
initially from the Actor-network theory. Based on the defined actors and their common 
concerns, actor-worlds were formed to simplify the model further. Controversies between the 
actor-worlds or particular stakeholders were described to understand the development arena's 
main dynamics and current state. This was followed by an overview of the development in the 
area of recycling technologies. Synthesising these findings and combining different 
stakeholders' needs with those regarding sustainability with the emphasis on waste led to 
forming the primary tasks. Together with unveiled needs, the tasks helped to form goal 
formulation to be used for concept synthesis. Out of the fourteen ideas conceived during the 
concept synthesis, five were further developed into socio-technic concepts.  
Four of those concepts are linked directly to the process of handling the waste. ‘Semi-automatic 
sorting using AI’ and ‘Self-sorting system’ are mainly designed to stimulate reuse, by 
providing cheaper ways of sorting with the aim to decrease the item's cost and increase its 
attractivity in the area, where it has initially been disposed. Both concepts are focused on sorting 
for second-hand shops, which had been to date an unoccupied niche with expected rise in the 
demand. ‘Shirts upcycled into boxers’ and ‘Modular footwear as a service’ are adopting new 
business models specifically invented for items that are particularly difficult to reuse, like 
underwear, or recycle, like shoes. The remaining concept ‘Sustainability grading and 
labelling’ is focused on shifting the consumers’ approach to the textile consumption, by 
exposing them to the accurate information regarding the garment’s origin and its environmental 
costs and thus preventing the waste generation in the first place. 
More detailed design of proposed concepts is required to assess more reliably their feasibility, 
attractivity and tractability but at this point it seems that ‘Shirts upcycled into boxers’ bare 
the least obvious hindrances for its realization, which qualified it for author’s deeper 
investigation beyond this study. Concept ‘Grading and labelling’ on the other hand seems to 
be very challenging in its implementation, nevertheless, it appears to have the furthest 
potential of reaching impact regarding sustainability compared to the rest of ideas.
