Learning objectives
Identify the issues in relation to cooling technologies and reflect on how to combat some of the technical issues by
implementing more sustainable solutions.
Identify and reflect on the issues regarding the indoor climate in schools and how the indoor climate affects the health
and cognitive performance of the students.
Knowledge regarding the use of questionnaires, quizzes and tests on young students and how to interpret the results
of such.
Understand the theory behind adiabatic evaporative cooling and how this theory is applied, on an adiabatic evaporative
cooling system that utilizes harvested rainwater.
A broader knowledge of leading international research in the field of sustainable cooling
Create and analyze models of the new adiabatic cooling system at Solbjerg Skolen and use it for solving some of the
issues and limitations of the cooling system.
Abstract in English
Schools in Denmark can’t normally afford to utilize cooling due to the high energy costs that are normally related.
However adiabatic evaporative cooling opens up an opportunity to combat the poor indoor climate at almost zero energy
cost in the danish schools. This thesis presents a new type of adiabatic evaporative cooler, installed on at school Solbjerg,
Denmark. Simulations done in IDA ICE showns that the system during normal operation almost can keep up with a tra-
ditional cooling coil, and by utilizing extra intake of supply air and mixing it with the return air, can increase its capacity
even further. The adiabatic evaporative system utilizes harvested rainwater to make the water resource more sustainable.
The study finds that it’s possible to cover all water demand by harvesting rain water during the danish summer at almost
zero risk for the need to utilize and by additional water. The study furthermore investigates if any effects on a better
indoor climate can be documented through a cross over design experiment conducted over 5 weeks in the summer
of 2018. Unfortunately several circumstances turned out to ruin the experimental design and the results after the 5
investigations weeks, couldn’t document any significant increase in cognitive performance during better indoor climate.

1 | Introduction
During the start of the 21st century an increasing focus on sustainability and climate change has hit the world. The
consequences of global warming have become obvious for almost everyone and steps all over the world are taken to
minimize the expected consequences and to secure a sustainable future for the coming generations. In Late 2015 the
Paris Agreement were adopted by the member countries of the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate
Change (UNFCCC)[1]. In short the Paris Agreement aims at limiting the temperature rise during this century to below
2 degrees Celsius above the pre-industrial level.
The European Environment Agency (EEA) reports on page 244 that the amount of heating degree days in northern
Europe has decreased over the last decade, while cooling degree days have increased[2]. In short a degree day is a
measure of how hot or cold a location is on a given day or period, and is therefore an indication of how many days
where either heating or cooling is needed[3]. The trend is predicted by The EEA to continue in the future, increasing the
need for cooling. This will especially affect the building sector due to an increase in the need for cooling in buildings.
In 2004 the building sectors consumption contributed to 37 % of the final energy consumption in EU which is higher
than other sectors such as the industry at 28 % and transportation at 32 %[4].
There are many factors that comes into play when looking into the energy consumption of buildings such as, Heat,
ventilation, air condition (HVAC), Domestic hot water, lightning, equipment, etc. Statistics over the energy consumption
by end use in the residential sector for 28 European countries in 2016, show that HVAC appliances in average is
accountable for 60 % of the total end use energy consumption[5]. The same tendency is seen for non-domestic
buildings where offices, hotels and retail all used more than 40 % of their final consumption on HVAC appliances[4].
Roughly 1 trillion kilowatt hours of electricity is spend annually all over the world on air condition, which in contrast is
more than twice the amount of electricity consumed by the continent of Africa, over all sectors[6] Not only is the energy
consumption for air condition a huge part of the energy consumption for a lot of countries, air condition is also related to
a lot of green house gases which are utilized as refrigerants in the equipment. Refrigerant such as chlorofluorocarbons
(CFCs) and hydrochlorofluorocarbons (HCFCs) have earlier been used but are both now disregarded due to their heavy
impact on ozone depletion[6]. They have been replaced by hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs) which isn’t directly associated
with the depletion of the ozone layer. However, HFCs are heavily contributing to global warming and are referred
too as high Global warming potential gases[7].
All these things combined gives a lot of incentives to investigate sustainable solutions for air condition systems to
reduce the energy consumption, while removing the use of harmful refrigerants. Cooling can basically be separated into
two categories, active and passive cooling. Active cooling can be described as a mechanical solution that can satisfy
the needed amount of cooling that nature itself can’t provide. These solution often requires a significant amount of
energy, from either heat or electricity. Active cooling technologies are, but not limited to, fans, heat pumps, evaporative
cooling. In comparison, passive cooling can be described as solutions that require no or minimal amounts of energy
to cool. Passive cooling techniques are often implemented in the design phase of a building and are therefore often
difficult to implement in already existing buildings. Some passive cooling techniques revolve around reducing the
heat gain, which can be archived with orientation and shape of the building or different kinds of external shading such
as overhangs, eaves, rolling shades or self-shading. A scientific review of 3 active cooling systems and 10 passive
cooling systems which can be applied to dwellings, evaluates the technologies based on their technical availability,
reliability and economic feasibility[8]. The findings of the review are such as the fact that passive cooling units in
some outdoor conditions can cool as much as active solutions. However, their economic feasibility are in most cases
still very low, especially in the residential sector
A lot of research is put into sustainable cooling methods one interesting method is by the use of a roof pond or spray.
This sustainable passive way of cooling utilizes a cool pond of water on the roof, which works as the warm air from
inside will transfer through the roof, cooling the room and improving the comfort inside. A study analyzing the use
of roof top ponds/sprays shows that it’s possible to drastically reduce the indoor temperatures during warm days by
having roof ponds. The study also concludes that roof ponds help towards a more stable indoor climate where rises
and falls in temperature are less frequent[9].
There are a lot of reasons why sustainable cooling methods are interesting to investigate, and even in countries like Den-
mark, where cooling might not be as necessary as other warmer places. It’s difficult to avoid the investment costs related

2 | Theory
2.1
Adiabatic evaporative cooling
An adiabatic process is in thermodynamics defined as a process where no heat is transferred into or out of a system
and its surroundings. This is seen in different ways. Either if the process happens so quickly that there is no time for
any significant heat transfer to happen or by making sure that the whole system is surrounded by enough insulation,
reducing the heat transfer to almost zero[21]. An adiabatic process follows the first law of thermodynamics which
states that the change in internal energy of a closed system (∆U) equals the difference between heat supplied to the
system from the surroundings (Q), minus the work done by the system on the surroundings (W), as seen on eq. 2.1.
U2−U1=∆U =Q−W
(2.1)
For the process in eq. 2.1 to be adiabatic, Q=0, as no heat supplied or extracted and the equation therefore becomes
eq.2.2
U2−U1=∆U =−W
(2.2)
For an ideal gas the work can be described as a product of the pressure p and the change in volume V. and can therefore
be defined as equation 2.3 which can be used in equation 2.2 to get equation 2.4. 2.3
W = p(V2−V1)
(2.3)
U2−U1=−p(V2−V1)
(2.4)
For the work to be positive the resulting internal energy will have to decrease. As the internal energy of the system
decreases the temperature will decrease as well. This process is called adiabatic expansion, and the result is a process
of adiabatic cooling. Adiabatic heating is on the other hand when an adiabatic compression happens. The work done
onto the the system results in a decrease in volume and the internal energy in the system will increase an a increase in
temperature will happen.
A process of either adiabatic heating or cooling can be drawn onto a Mollier’s IX diagram or a psychometric chart.
The two diagrams are basically the same just turned, through out the next section the psychometric chart will be used.

2.1.1
Evaporative cooler
Evaporative coolers utilizes the natural heat and mass transfer that occurs between cool water and warmer air. Evap-
orative coolers are known for being able to reach a very high efficiency, but with the negative side effect that the water
consumption of these systems also tends to be very high. Evaporative coolers can be split into two categories. The
Direct Evaporative Cooler (DEC) where the two fluids are in thermal contact while mixing of the two fluids happen.
The Indirect Evaporative Cooler (IEC) consist of a dry channel and a wet channel. Between the two channels a heat
transfer surface with a high heat transfer capacity makes sure that heat can be transferred effectively between the two
channels. The following section will clarify the principles of the most common types of evaporative coolers. The
section will include psychrometric chats of the different coolers. The charts are meant as a principle of the flows of
the return/supply air, the return air is only illustrated during the adiabatic evaporative phase. The return air heats up
this phase, as heat is transferred between the two channels in the heat exchanger, cooling the outdoor/supply air.
2.1.2
Direct evaporative cooling (DEC)
DEC can be described as the simplest form of evaporative cooling. The principle and flow can be seen on 2.2(a)
where the warm inlet air (1) is forced through a pad. Cold water at the wet-bulb temperature or lower of the inlet
air, is sprayed over the pad. During this phase, heat is transferred as sensible heat by the inlet air and will through
the pad be absorbed by the cold water as latent heat. Depending on the latent heat, an amount of the water will
evaporate into the warm air. While the inlet air is cooled, its water content is also increased. The cold outlet air
(2) has the same enthalpy as the inlet air due to the latent heat which is recovered as the moisture content of the air
increases[24].
The working process can be seen on 2.2(b) where warm supply air (1) is cooled at constant enthalpy until the outlet
(2-2*). The amount of cooling depends on the efficiency of the humidifier but can in theory proceed until saturation,
where the temperature of the primary air is equal to the wet-bulb temperature at inlet (2*).
DEC is broadly used due to its simple construction. However, the use is limited due to the high moisture content of
the outlet air which can be undesirable in for example residential- or office buildings[25].

2.1.3
Indirect evaporative cooling (IEC)
To circumvent the issues DEC has regarding the moisture content of the supply air, an IEC can be used. As drawn
on figure 2.3(a) the warm inlet air (1) runs in dry channels but is cooled by heat transfer through a heat transfer surface
to a wet channel. The wet channel consist of a secondary air (3) running through a channel that is sprayed with water.
The secondary air stream is cooled like the process of the DEC. As the secondary air is colder than the primary inlet
air, heat transfers between the two separated air streams through the high heat transfer capacity surface. By transferring
the heat through a surface, it’s now possible to avoid adding moisture to the primary air while still cooling it[26]. The
working process can be seen on figure 2.3(b) where the primary air (1-2) is cooled at a constant moisture content.
While the secondary air stream (3-4) is cooled at constant enthalpy by the evaporated water in the wet channel, just
like the DEC. Heat is transferred through the surface and is absorbed by the cool water as latent heat, while some
of the water is evaporated and embedded into the secondary air stream, increasing the moisture content. Depending
on the efficiency of the humidifier, the temperature of secondary air stream can be at three different temperatures at
outlet. 4a: The air stream isn’t saturated at outlet and the temperature is therefore lower than the wet-bulb temperature.
4b: Saturation is met at outlet and the temperature is equal to the wet-bulb temperature. 4c: If saturation is hit before
the outlet, heat will be transferred to the secondary air stream as sensible heat, increasing the enthalpy hereof while
increasing the temperature along the saturation curve above the wet-bulb temperature at inlet.
Compared to the DEC, IEC’s main advantage is that it’s possible to cool the primary air, without adding any additional
moisture to the air. This give the opportunity to use the cooling system in places where DEC can’t be used due to
restrictions on the moisture content of the indoor air. One disadvantage the IEC has, is that it’s still only able to cool
the primary air to the level of the secondary air wet-bulb temperature at inlet. This is the reason that these systems
are also known as wet bulb IEC.

2.1.4
Regenerative indirect evaporative cooling
Regenerative indirect evaporate cooling (R-IEC) is the result of trying to combat the main disadvantage of the IEC and
cool the primary air below the wet bulb temperature of the secondary air at inlet. As seen on figure 2.4(a) the primary air
(1) enters a dry channel and runs through the heat exchanger like on the IEC. After getting cooled, some of the primary air
at outlet (2) is extracted and used as secondary air. The process of the secondary air from (2) to (3) is the same as the sec-
ondary air of an IEC, with the exception of the secondary airs temperature in the R-IEC starting at a lower temperature.
The working process can be seen on figure 2.4(b) where the primary air (1-2) is cooled at constant moisture content.
Ultimately cooling the primary air lower than the wet-bulb temperature of the primary air at inlet, significantly lower
than a classic IEC. (1-2). The secondary air, which is extracted after being cooled, follows the same principle as the
IEC and will from the outlet 2 increase in moisture until fully saturated (3) at its wet-bulb temperature at inlet of
the secondary channel. By cooling the secondary air before entering the wet channel, the corresponding wet-bulb
temperature is also lowered compared to the wet-bulb temperature at inlet of the secondary air. Which means that
the temperature limit that the primary air at outlet can reach is lowered significantly. being able to reach temperatures
below the wet-bulb temperature is the main advantage of the R-IEC. However as regenerative extraction happens at
the outlet of the primary air, the flow rate is also lowered compared to the ordinary IEC[23].

2.4
Water use and management
One of the disadvantages of the IEC systems are the amount of water needed to properly run them. While there is
a costs associated with the use of clean water, it’s also considered a scarce resource in many parts of the world [28] and
can therefore not be considered as a sustainable way of cooling. A sustainable alternative is to use harvested rainwater.
Harvesting rainwater requires a large surface which works as a catchment area, such as a roof, where a run off is made to
gather and lead the water through pipes to a storage tank. The storage tank is used to decrease some of the uncertainties
there is regarding be reliant of precipitation. This way it’s possible to store excess water from rainy days and use it later
during periods with no precipitation. The storage tank should be sized in regards to the expected water consumption
and harvested rainwater. The amount of harvested rainwater can be described as the effective run-off as eq. 2.16[29].
ER=Rt·A·C
(2.16)
Where ER is the effective run-off given in m3, Rt is the precipitation in mm, A is the catchment area in m2 and C is
the run-off coefficient. The run-off coefficient is a value between 0-1, indicating the amount of rainwater caught by
the catchment area. The run off coefficient is both dependent on the surface of the catchment area, the rainfall intensity
and evaporation from the roof. The run off coefficient is typically around 0.7-0.95 for harder surfaces[30]. The amount
of water used to humidify the water in the adiabatic loop is based on two factors. First of, how much water is actually
humidified and therefore transported out of the system in the exhaust air. Secondly, all the water that isn’t humidified in
the process, is gathered in the bottom of the system and reused as long as it’s clean enough. Measuring the conductivity
is an easy way to see how clean the water is, as the conductivity of the water will increase as more and more particles
are gathered in the water. At some point the water will reach a conductivity level too high and the system will flush
out the water and pump new water into the system. During normal operations with rainwater, the system should be
able to run without flushing out any water during the day. The system will however flush out the water after a while,
if the adiabatic system haven’t been in use. This typically also happens at the end of everyday, and results in a water
consumption of approximately 0.3m3 per day in use. No measurements of this value is in place on the system, but
the value was approved by an operator of the system from SystemAir.
The effective run-off can be used to design the storage capacity, as this value states how much incoming water
is to be expected over a period of time. The tank should be designed to never run empty. As the water tank is a closed
envelope, it can be assumed that no evaporation happens and the following equation can be written
Storage level(t)=Max capacity−Wateruse(t)+ER(t)
(2.17)
Where Storage level (t) is the amount of water in storage at time t. Max" capacity" is the maximum capacity of the
storage. Water use is the amount of water used at time t. The max capacity should be at a level where storage level (t)
never hits zero, when simulating over a period of time with precipitation. However, as a storage tank of 10m3 is already
installed at Solbjerg skolen, eq 2.17 will mainly be used to investigate how the storage level acts towards different
levels of precipitation. To have a security in regards to the water resource, the IEC at Solbjerg skolen is installed with
a connection to the city water.
Hard Water
A factor that has to be taken into account when utilizing water as a resource, is the minerals the water contains. In
Denmark there is a high concentration of limestone in the underground which affects the water as it moves through
the ground. The water dissolves small amounts of the minerals in the underground, making the water hard affecting the
tap water which in Denmark is taken from underground. In Denmark the hardness of water is measured on a german
scale (Deutsche Hãrte) from 0-30. where one grade equals either 10 mg calcium oxide or 7.19 mg of magnesium oxide
per liter water[31]. As hard water isn’t considered as a health risk for human it’s not removed before being sent out
in the system as tap water. Hard water is a problem as the calcium can accumulate inside the pipes, water tank, filters,
etc. both decreasing the life time of the system and increasing the energy as the system would need to "work harder"
to provide the same amount of output. A way to avoid these problems in areas with hard tap water is to remove the
minerals from the water solution before use. One way to do this is by using a reverse osmosis system.

Reverse Osmosis Principle
The natural process of osmosis happens when water flows through a semipermeable membrane, that allows the flow of
the water and a few ions while being impermeable to dissolved solids such as calcium oxide. Take two solutions, one
with a high and one with a low concentration of the same dissolved solid. By adding a way for the water to flow between
the two solutions, through the before mentioned membrane, water will flow to the high concentration solution until the
concentration of both solutions equal each other. The difference in the water level is called the osmosis pressure. The
reverse osmosis principle is when adding a pressure, greater than the osmosis pressure, on top of the high concentration
solution. By doing so, water is forced into the low concentration solution, diluting the concentration of the solution. [32]

5 | Conclusion on the performance of adiabatic
evaporative cooling
The simulations done in IDA ICE shows that a standard adiabatic evaporative system, with the same properties as
the installed system on Solbjerg skolen, can manage to keep up with the cooling load in approximately 70% of the
occupied hours over the whole summer period. The simulations show that this is 11% worse than an AHU utilizing
cooling coils to stay at set point temperature at all times. The fact that the cooling coils can provide sufficient indoor
climate in 18.5% of the hours, indicates that the amount of air flow is too low in the model, to cool all classrooms.
Looking into reasons behind the lack in cooling of the supply air, the investigations show that the main limitations
of the adiabatic evaporative cooling system can be concluded to be the wet-bulb temperature of the return air and the
dew point temperature of the outdoor air.
The investigations shows that the wet-bulb temperature of the return air in 47% of the measurements are too high. The
dew point temperature is also seen to set a limit on the cooling of the outdoor air. The model shows that the dew point
temperature of the outdoor air at inlet is above the set point temperature in 7.7% of the time. Further investigations
showed that utilizing mixing between the cooler supply air and the return air to reduce the wet-bulb temperature here
of, increased its cooling capacity to be able to hit the set point of 16 °C from 47% to 63.3%.. Further investigations
showed that a temperature reduction of the return air potentially can have a very big effect on the cooling capacity.
From the presented results regarding the precipitation and harvesting here of, it can be concluded that it’s very unlikely
that the water storage at Solbjerg will dry out during a summer period. The large size of the water storage secures
a water supply in up to 49 days, at a daily consumption of 0.3m3 of water between Monday - Friday. Meanwhile
having a catchment area of 2200m2 that secures a effective run-off of 1.76m3 per mm of precipitation, with a run-off
coefficient of 0.8.

6 | Theory
6.1
Indoor climate parameters
The indoor climate in buildings can be separated into two main categories, thermal conditions and air quality. The
thermal conditions takes factors such as wind speed, clothing and activity of the users of the room into account when
estimating conditions. The quality of air is measured on the amount of bio effluents in the air. The indoor climate
in buildings are of great importance as humans spends most of their day indoor. Being exposed to a bad indoor
climate over a long period of time can potential have harmful health risks such as cancer, cardiovascular diseases,
asthma/allergy diseases[40]. Poor indoor climate is also shown to have a negative impact on work performance both
for adults and children in the area of 5-10% reduction in performance[40].
6.1.1
Thermal conditions
The most common way of measuring the thermal conditions of a building is based on the the predicted mean vote
(PMV) and the predicted percentage dissatisfied (PPD)[41]. The thermal comfort equation used to find the PMV, is also
known as Fangers comfort equation and was created by P.O Fanger in the late sixties/ early seventies[42][43]. PMV
is calculated by using the equation 6.1. The model is used to predict the mean value of votes of people in larger groups
and gives a result on a 7 point scale that predicts the groups’ thermal sensation, see below on table 6.1. PMV is also
used to calculate the before mentioned PPD. PMV predicts the mean vote of a large group whereas PPD is an index
that give a prediction of the percentage of thermally dissatisfied people. These people would be the once placed in the
categories on table 6.1 as hot, warm, cool or cold. From PMV the PPD can be calculated by following equation 6.3.

6.1.2
Air quality
Humans release bio effluents during the metabolic processes that are happening inside the human body. These
bioeffluents include alcohols, aldehyrdes, ammonia, CO2, hydrocarbons and ketones[45]. Bioeffluents contribute to
a indoor climate perceived as unpleasant. CO2 is a product of humans breathing by inhaling oxygen and exhaling
CO2. The carbon dioxide concentration normally found in the indoor climate does not affect humans in any way,
however, CO2 is used as an indicator of the concentration of all the other bioeffluents that usually are present in the
indoor climate. The concentration of bioeffluents in the indoor climate is interesting as studies have shown that higher
concentrations can lead to acute health symptoms and even have negative effects on the cognitive performance of the
humans exposed to the higher concentrations[46]. To avoid any nuisances from bioeffluents in the indoor climate, the
danish standard recommends a limit on the concentration of CO2 in classrooms below 800 ppm[47].

7 | Methodology
7.1
Experimental design
The school of Solbjerg were selected to undergo a field experiment to study the effect of a newly installed adiabatic evap-
orative cooling system. The cooling system were installed to work as the primary air handling unit for the selected build-
ing block D. The experiments are conducted in four different classroom while one more class is used as a control class.
The experiments were conducted to see the impact of classroom temperature and the impact of temperature changes on
the pupils’ well being and performance. During the weeks of investigation two thermal comfort levels are used. One
warm condition at 25-26 °C indoor air temperature and a cool condition with an air temperature at 22-23 °C Two 6th
grade classes with pupils around the age of 12, will be exposed to either long lasting warm conditions or long lasting cool
conditions. The intervention schedule were made as a cross over design, where the conditions of the two classes always
should be the opposite. The intervention schedule for 6.A and 6.C can be seen on figures 7.1 and 7.2. The last 6th grade
class (6.B) is used as a control class and will during the weeks of investigation be exposed to constant thermal conditions,
shown on figure 7.3, but will otherwise go through the exact same tests and questionnaires as the two other 6th grades.

Poor indoor air quality is known to have an effect of pupils performance and to eliminate this factor, it’s ensured
that the ventilation system supplied a high airflow to ensure that the CO2 level remained below the recommended
limit of 800 ppm. To provide the two different thermal conditions, the centralized cooling system were set to provide
a indoor condition matching the cool condition at 22-23 °C. In order to create the warmer conditions, radiators placed
under the windows in each class, were used to rise the temperature until the warm condition at 25-26 °C were hit. The
teachers were told to not open any windows or change the settings of the thermostats, no matter how the indoor climate
was perceived, during the weeks of investigation.

7.2
Performance
The investigation were performed over the course of five weeks, week 20-21-22 and 24-25, in May and June, 2018.
Two tests and a questionnaire were performed each week of the experiment. The two tests included a mathematical
test and a grammatical reasoning test, both described in the subsections below. No members of the project group
were present at the school during testing and questioning of the students and therefore the responsibility of performing
the test and questionnaires correctly were handed over to the teachers of each class. The teachers were handed an
instruction paper with all the necessary details, to make sure that all tests and questionnaires were handled the same
way between each of classes and weeks of testing, see appendix A.6
To increase the authenticity of the answers and replicating a ordinary school day, the teachers were instructed not to give
any information regarding the experiments to pupils. During the five weeks of experiments, the students will be exposed
to more tests than usual, and it’s therefore important for the teachers to not talk about the tests and questionnaires as
anything out of the ordinary. It’s important that the students perceive the tests as ordinary school work, to avoid the
pressure a normal test scenario can have on the students. The instructions were to perform the tests and questionnaire
at a specific time each week. The teachers were instructed to put notes in a logbook each week, containing the day
and start/stop times of the tests. The logbook should also be used to note if any unusual events or behaviours, such
as being more noisy or more tired than usual, had been noted during that week and during time of testing. The students
were to perform two tests and a questionnaire each week on a chosen day. The school uses different class schedules
on even/uneven weeks, see appendix A.7. It’s important that the test days are similar to lessen the variables between
each week. The days chosen for this experiment were Thursday for the sixth graders and Wednesday/Thursday for the
fourth graders. The tests were designed to appropriately fit the age of the pupils in the five classes while resembling the
normal teaching material. Both tests were designed so no one should be able to finish all questions in the given time
frame. However if this were to happen anyways, the teacher were told to stop the test and note the time used for the test.

7.2.3
Measurements of performance
The performance of the pupils are measured in both speed and the amount of errors both in relation to the total amount
of answers. Speed being calculated as the amount of answers per time unit, and errors being the percentage of errors
committed. The data from the tests are analyzed in two ways, first as a incomplete design using all data available. The
analyzed data is then looked at and tests from pupils that haven’t participated in all the tests are removed, leaving only
the tests from pupils that have participated under all conditions for the given test, a so called complete design. In a
study by Wargocki. P and Wyon an small increase in performance over the course of the five weeks were noted[48]
To avoid a too big difference due to the learning curve, a rehearsal week were implemented to smooth out the learning
curve. However the analysis might show a significant different between tests results between the weeks even while
disregarding the changes made to indoor climate for the experiment. This could happen due to different reasons such
as familiarizing with the tests, differences between the test versions or simple fatigue and tiredness of doing too many
tests. If this is the case an adjustment to the analysis is made by multiplying a factor as described in [48] to each of
the pupils performance on the given task. The factor is calculated by the average performance of the pupils class results
of the first week on the given task, divided by the average performance of the pupils during the specific week.

Analysis and discussion of results
The experimental design of the investigations conducted over the weeks on Solbjerg, was primarily focused on the
thermal conditions and their impact on the pupils cognitive performance. The main parameter of thermal condition
investigated, have been the temperature of the classrooms. The experiments were planned as a crossover experiment, but
as seen from the results, the temperatures didn’t follow the expected schedules in most of the classes. The data indicate
that it haven’t been possible to keep the temperatures down on the expected 22-23 °C. The temperatures were generally
high in all classes doing all weeks and the measurements show that the temperature difference between the days, only
in a few cases are more than 2 °C, which in the planned design would be the minimum difference between the warm
and cool weeks. Higher than usual temperatures were seen during week 21 and 22, which could have had an impact on
the temperatures inside, especially now that the wireless tags shows indication of a lot window movement over the days
of testing. Not only have the temperatures shown to be off, but the experimental design for the 4thgraders were also
scrapped after the first two weeks, as it wasn’t possible to make the quick adjust in temperatures which were originally
planned. Instead, the two 4th grade classes, changed to the same schedule as the 6th graders. The planned experimental

9 | Conclusion on the indoor climate investiga-
tion
The weeks of investigations turned out to be very warm compared to the average in May and June and this shows
as the installed system seems to not be able to handle the high heat load and through out the investigation weeks, it
became clear that the system wasn’t ready to run at full capacity yet. Even though there were several problems it
was chosen to continue with the investigations. From the presented results in 8 it can be concluded that the expected
experimental design of the 5 weeks of investigation didn’t go as planned. The temperatures in the classrooms didn’t
follow the planned schedules and only in a few cases there is actually a temperature difference between the weeks
of more than 2 °C. Small indications from the installed wireless tags also show that the windows have been opened
over the investigation period, which was supposed to not happen. The small difference in thermal conditions between
the weeks were still investigated, but no real correlation between the thermal conditions and the performance of the
pupils were documented. Through out the weeks high concentrations were measured during some hours and in hopes
of finding some results it was chosen to look for a correlation between the CO2 concentration and the results from
the weeks, but again the results turned out to be random and no correlation could be found.
It can be concluded that the results from the 5 weeks of investigations can document any thing in regards to the relation
between the indoor climate and the cognitive performance of the pupils.

10 | Final Conclusion
The first objective for this thesis were to investigate whether or not it’s possible to cover the cooling demand over
a danish summer with the use of a sustainable adiabatic evaporative cooling system and if it’s possible to cover the
demand for water by harvesting rain water.
The results showed that such a system can cover most of the cooling demand over a danish summer as the system
can keep a temperature at or below 23 °C during more than 70% of the occupied hours with a set point on the supply
temperature of 16 °C, which matches the set point used at Solbjerg skolen. The reason for the system not being able
to meet the required temperature during some hours over the summer is to be found in the properties of the outdoor
and indoor air.
The first limitation of the system is seen if the wet-bulb temperature of the return air from the classrooms are higher
than the set point. The results showcases that this is the case 47% of occupied hours. Further more is it seen that the dew
point temperature of the outdoor air limits cooling in 7.7% of the occupied hours. The system can’t do anything about
high dew point temperatures, but increasing its intake of outdoor air, the system can extract the extra supply air and mix
it with the return air, to lower the wet-bulb temperature here of. By doing so, the time in which the wet-bulb temperature
of the return air matches the set point temperature increases from 47% to 63.3%. The investigation showed that a small
decrease in return air temperature could potentially increase the effectiveness of the system by a significant amount.
The results regarding water use and precipitation showed no indication of problems in regards to utilizing harvested
rainwater as the only water resource for the adiabatic evaporative system. The 10m3 water storage tank, installed at
Solbjerg skolen, secures under normal operations between Monday and Friday, a water supply in up to 49 days. Due
to the large catchment area an effective run off of 1.76m3 per mm precipitation is available for storage. At this rate,
the analysis show that the storage system shouldn’t run dry at any point in the summer period. The analysis is made
for both a normal year and a exceptional case such as the summer of 2018, furthermore is the analysis is made for
a office scenario where the system runs through out July as well. In all scenarios there are no indications of not being
able to harvest sufficient amounts of water to run every day over the summer period.
The second part of the thesis aims at documenting an effect of an improved indoor climate in the school on the
cognitive performance of the pupils affected. The experiments conducted on the school of Solbjerg over 5 weeks in
the summer of 2018 turned out to not go as planned. During the five weeks of May and June, higher temperatures than
normal were measured. Further more indications pointed towards the adiabatic evaporative cooling system not being
completely ready for testing, which resulted in periods where it didn’t run properly over the 5 weeks. This resulted
in higher temperatures in the classrooms than expected, and indications did also point towards window openings which
weren’t allowed during the investigation period. All this resulted in the temperatures in the classrooms not following
the planned cross over design. The biggest temperature difference between the weeks were seen to be around 2 °C
in the same class. Furthermore did some of the classes not complete all of the planned tests and questionnaires, which
in the end resulted in only having test and questionnaire data for 3 weeks instead of 4. Only in some classes a small
indications of a relation between poor indoor climate and cognitive performance were noticed, but in none of the cases
consistent over all weeks. The questionnaires did however, show some indications towards the pupils being aware of
the thermal indoor climate. Overall the test results from either test didn’t show a clear and consistent relation between
the cognitive performance and the poor indoor climate and it was chosen not to investigate further.

11 | Future work / Outlook
There is no doubt that adiabatic evaporative cooling systems have their place in the future of cooling. The focus on
sustainability and energy savings are higher than ever and the adiabatic evaporative cooling systems fit right in. The
system shows a great potential as an almost zero energy cooling source.
In regards to the system at Solbjerg skolen, it would very exciting to further investigate if an increase in cooling capacity
can be achieved by utilizing full air flow from empty rooms. The only costs related to utilizing the full air flow is the
energy consumption of the fans which would be required to provide higher air flows during all hours. But if the increase
in energy cost is relative to the increased cooling effect, it will still make sense to actually utilize the empty rooms.
This thesis investigates the potential of the adibatic evaporative cooling system in a school environment, but the system
could potentially be applied in other settings such as office buildings or other large municipally buildings. The water
supply seems easily covered by storing rainwater as long as a large enough water storage and catchment area are
present. However, it will require some additional research. The cooling load can be expected to be a lot higher in
a office setting compared to a school as the main factors such as hours of operation are longer and the internal gain
presumably higher, due to more equipment and lighting. Furthermore will the set point traditionally be a bit lower
as office workers have more clothing and sits still all day, compared to pupils attending school.
Utilizing free energy and rainwater opens up the opportunity to investigate how such system could be implemented
in developing countries with limited access to water and electricity. The way the system works would also back this
up, as many developing countries are placed in parts of the tropics and subtropics where high temperatures and low
relative humidity are present during long parts of the year, which is perfect conditions for adiabatic evaporative system.
However, some of these areas tend to be either very dry or very wet and humid in long periods at a time, which
therefore would require additional research to investigate the possibilities and limits of storing rainwater in such areas.
The investigations conducted during this project at Solbjerg skolen didn’t go as planned. Over course of the five weeks
at Solbjerg, it was seen several times that the classes didn’t follow the plan laid out. Some of the findings were such
as the times of test differed over the weeks, windows were opened, which weren’t allowed or the answers of the tests
were not done correctly. Due to the great distance between the school and the university, it was simply not possible
to over watch the experiments at all times and it’s therefore needed to come up with a easier way of conducting such
experiments in the future, to increase the chance of successfully running such experiments at greater distances. In the
case of the experiments on Solbjerg, the pupils weren’t aware that their were being a part of an experiment. The tests
and questionnaires were only handled by the teachers, who by no chance can control what happens in the class during
all hours of the days. But what if the pupils actually were aware of the experiments, could this potentially impact the
results in a positive way. The idea is that the pupils could find it interesting to be a part of such an experiment which
potentially could increase their engagement and secure that rules are also followed during times where no teacher is
present.
A lot things happen both physically and mentally during the teenage years and even before, which potentially could
have an impact on the results. Investigating how the age of the pupils taking part in such an experiment, potentially
can affect the engagement or results of such experiments, can lead to better results in future test scenarios like the
one conducted at Solbjerg, by either excluding or including certain age groups, if any were found to be more or less
engaged or aware in regards to tests and the indoor climate compared to others.

1. Information til deltagende lærere 
Vi er et forskerhold fra Danmarks Tekniske Universitet (DTU), der gennemfører et 
forskningsprojekt om indeklimaet i klasseværelserne på Solbjergskolen i ugerne 20-
21-22 og 24-25. Projektet undersøger, hvordan indeklimaet i klasseværelset påvir-
ker elevernes opmærksomhed, ydeevne og sundhed.  
 
Forsøg venligst ikke at gætte, hvilke forhold, der er blevet ændret, da dette kan 
medføre en fejlkilde i form af, hvordan du forventer, eleverne vil opføre sig. 
 
Vi beder dig venligst om at udlevere opgaverne til klassen hver uge i forsøgsperio-
den, dvs. fra uge 20-21-22 og uge 24 til 25. Det vedlagte skema viser, hvornår i 
løbet af ugen vi beder dig om, at opgaverne udleveres til eleverne. 
 
På skolens kontor vil vi i begyndelsen af hver uge stille kasser med nye opgaverne. 
Kasserne vil være tydeligt mærket med klasse, fag, lærernavn, ugedag og tidspunkt. 
For klasser 6A, 6B og 6C vil der være to kasser med opgaver hver uge: Subtrakti-
ons og multiplikationsopgave til matematiktimerne og Grammatisk logik (san-
de/falske udsagn) opgave til dansktimerne. 
For klasser 4A og 4C vil der være én kasse med Grammatisk logik (sande/falske 
udsagn) opgave til dansk- eller matematiktimerne.  
Yderligere vil alle kasserne med Grammatisk logik opgave også indeholde spørge-
skemaer, som skal udleveres til eleverne efter at den grammatisk logik opgave er 
slut.  
I hver kasse findes også en mappe som indeholder: 
 Information til deltagende lærere med Vejledning til opgaverne 
(denne vejledning) 
Skema, der viser, hvornår i løbet af ugen vi beder dig om, at opgaverne ud-
leveres til eleverne. 
 Logbog, hvor du bedes indikere, hvornår de enkelte opgaver er startet og 
sluttet. Desuden bedes du notere usædvanlige begivenheder, og du må gerne 
sætte et par ord på elevernes adfærd i løbet af ugen, hvis de f.eks. har været 
mere trætte eller støjende end sædvanligt. 
 
Udfyldte opgaver og spørgeskemaer samt logbogen returneres i de samme kas-
ser til skolens kontor, hvor vi vil hente dem og aflevere et nyt sæt i begyndelsen af 
næste uge. Du skal ikke rette opgaverne - det sørger projektgruppen for. 
 
Vi beder om, at opgaverne første gang præsenteres i uge 20 med instruktion, så ele-
verne kan øve sig, således at de vil være bedre forberedt i uge 21, når eksperimentet 
for alvor går i gang.

2. Subtraktions og multiplikationsopgave (8 minutter) 
Lad eleverne arbejde i hver deres tempo og fortæl dem, at det ikke gælder om at la-
ve mange regnestykker, men rigtige regnestykker. Understreg at de helst ikke må 
springe regnestykker over, medmindre de slet ikke kan løse dem.  
 
Subtraktion: 
Eleverne skal trække to heltal fra hinanden.  
 
Multiplikation: 
Eleverne skal gange to tal, som vist nedenfor: 1-cifret tal med 2-cifret tal.  
   
 
Når du uddeler opgaverne, så skal det gøres med bagsiden opad. Dette sikrer at in-
gen af eleverne starter før de andre. Bed eleverne om at skrive deres navn og dato 
på bagsiden og vente med at vende papiret til du siger, at de må starte. Det er meget 
vigtigt, at vi ved, hvem der har løst opgaven. Ved tidens udløb, bed eleverne om at 
vende opgaven om og lægge blyanten.  
Det er ikke meningen, at en elev skal nå at løse alle opgaver, men sker dette, så bed 
de resterende elever om at stoppe og notér tiden. 
Vi vil bede om, at du bruger 8 minutter af undervisningstimen på subtraktions- og 
multiplikationsopgaven. Notér venligst, hvilken dag og start- og sluttidspunkt i log-
bogen.

3. Logbog 
I forbindelse med undersøgelserne udleveres en logbog. I denne er det meningen, at 
du skal notere dato og tidspunkt for, hvornår I laver øvelserne. Endvidere kan log-
bogen benyttes til at notere, hvis klassen virker mere ukoncentreret eller forstyrret 
end normalt samt eventuelle andre tanker, du gør dig igennem forløbet - også hvis 
der er usædvanlige begivenheder i løbet af ugen, som du synes kunne påvirke resul-
taterne af eksperimenterne. Vi vil også bede dig om at skrive et par ord om elever-
nes adfærd i løbet af ugen, hvis de f.eks. har været mere trætte eller støjende end 
sædvanligt. 
Logbogen vil ligge i mappen i kassen med ugens øvelser, og den bedes venligst re-
turneret til os sammen med ugens øvelser.

2. Grammatisk logik - sande/falske udsagn (5 minutter) 
Eleverne skal markere, hvorvidt en sætning er sand eller falsk ved at sætte en cirkel 
om S eller F som vist nedenfor. Sætningerne beskriver et par af geometriske figurer 
(cirkel/firkant/trekant) og kan enten være sand eller falsk.  
 
 
Bed eleverne om at tænke over hver enkelt sætning og ikke springe nogen over. 
Når du uddeler opgaverne, så skal det gøres med bagsiden opad. Dette sikrer at in-
gen af eleverne starter før de andre. 
Bed eleverne om at skrive deres navn og dato på bagsiden og vente med at vende 
papiret, til du siger, at de må starte. Det er meget vigtigt, at vi ved, hvem der har 
løst opgaven. Ved tidens udløb, bed eleverne om at vende opgaven om og lægge 
blyanten.  
Det er ikke meningen, at en elev skal nå at afslutte alle opgaverne, men sker dette, 
så bed de resterende om at stoppe og notér tiden. 
Vi vil bede om, at du bruger 5 minutter af undervisningstimen på opgaven. Noter 
venligst, hvilken dag og start- og sluttidspunkt.

3. Spørgeskema

maet i klassen (om det er for støjende, for varmt, osv.) og deres velbefindende (om 
de har det godt, om de gider at arbejde, osv.).
