Abstract (English)
This thesis explores personal informatics systems, with a focus on developing inter-
active visualizations that are both user-friendly and accessible. These visualizations
are intended to assist individuals in identifying patterns and triggers in the realm of
mental phenomena.
To evaluate the project, two fundamental questions were posed:
• Does the prototype fulfill its overarching goal of being user-friendly and acces-
sible?
• To what extent does the prototype effectively aid in the detection of patterns?
These inquiries were explored through a case study involving two participants, each
using a one-button tracker to monitor two distinct mental phenomena. The evaluation
employed user sessions, allowing for a detailed understanding of how these questions
were answered in practice. Evaluation with other participants were also used.
The prototype’s design allows users to view data through various visualizations, en-
riched with additional features such as annotation on data visualizations and integra-
tion of journal entries.
The outcomes were promising. Findings demonstrated that the prototype was both
user-friendly and accessible.
Particularly, the unique approach of having the two
participants track and evaluate their data provided more insightful feedback and
understanding than other participants in the evaluation. Findings from evaluations
helped develop methods that aided users in identifying patterns.
Additionally, it
was discovered that users generally preferred simple visualizations over complex ones.
However, the findings also emphasized the importance of adapting to different levels
of complexity in visualization, making the integration of personalization a crucial
element.
The conclusion of this thesis is that further exploration of this area of research can
prove to be a significant step towards more accessible and personalized tools within
mental phenomena.

Abstract (Danish)
Dette speciale udforsker personlige informatiksystemer med fokus på at udvikle inter-
aktive visualiseringer, der er både brugervenlige og tilgængelige. Disse visualiseringer
er beregnet til at hjælpe individer med at identificere mønstre og triggere inden for
mentale fænomener.
For at evaluere projektet blev der stillet to grundlæggende spørgsmål:
• Opfylder prototypen sit overordnede mål om at være brugervenlig og tilgæn-
gelig?
• I hvor høj grad hjælper prototypen effektivt med at opdage mønstre?
Disse forespørgsler blev udforsket gennem et casestudie, der involverede to deltagere,
der hver brugte en en-knaps tracker til at overvåge to forskellige mentale fænomener.
Evalueringen foregik som personlige brugersamtaler, der gav mulighed for en bedre
forståelse af, hvordan spørgsmålene blev besvaret I praksis. Evaluering med andre
deltagere blev også brugt.
Prototypens design giver brugerne mulighed for at se data gennem forskellige visualis-
eringer, beriget med yderligere funktioner såsom bemærkninger til datavisualiseringer
og integration af journalposter.
Resultaterne var lovende og viste, at prototypen var både brugervenlig og tilgængelig.
Især den unikke tilgang med at lade de to deltagere spore og evaluere deres egne
data gav mere indsigtsfuld feedback og forståelse end andre deltagere I evalueringen.
Resultater fra evalueringer hjalp med at udvikle metoder, der kan hjælpe brugerne
med at identificere mønstre. Derudover viste evalueringen, at brugere generelt fore-
trak simple visualiseringer frem for komplekse. Men resultaterne understregede også
vigtigheden af at tilpasse sig forskellige niveauer af kompleksitet I visualisering, hvilket
gør integrationen af personalisering til et afgørende element.
Konklusionen på denne afhandling er, at yderligere udforskning af dette område kan
vise sig at være et væsentligt skridt til mere tilgængelige og personaliserede værktøjer
inden for mentale fænomener.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Personal Informatics (PI) is the study of using technology to gather and examine
personal information. The objective of this is to offer individuals a more comprehen-
sive view of their habits and actions, leading to elevated self-awareness and a better
standard of living.
This field introduces new obstacles in human-computer interaction design and presents
opportunities in areas such as mental health, fitness, nutrition, and sustainability. As
people increasingly focus on well-being and self-enhancement, PI is poised to help peo-
ple live healthier, more fulfilling lives.
Mental health disorders, emotional states, psychological states, and physical and
behavioral phenomena are complex aspects that can be diﬀicult to grasp and analyze
effectively.
1.1
Goal of Thesis
The goal is to provide individuals with a prototype that simplifies the representation
of their mental phenomena through visualizations. This approach enables users, both
experts and non-experts, to easily interpret and gain insights from the data collected.
The motivation behind creating this prototype is to empower individuals to better
understand and manage their mental phenomena. Many people experience diﬀiculties
with these phenomena, and being able to visualize and identify patterns can greatly
assist in self-analysis and self-improvement.
By recognizing patterns and triggers,
individuals can develop strategies for coping and managing their mental well-being
more effectively.
Additionally, the prototype can be used in psychological therapies.
By providing
doctors and therapists with a comprehensive visualization tool, they can gain deeper
insights into their patients’ mental phenomena and tailor treatment plans accordingly.
This can lead to a more targeted and effective interventions, by enhancing the quality
of care provided to patients. Overall, the prototype’s creation stems from the desire to
bridge the gap between complex mental phenomena and individuals’ understanding,
enabling them to take proactive steps towards improving their mental well-being
and/or assisting healthcare professionals in delivering personalized treatments.

1.2
A Self-Tracking prototype for Mental Phenomena
The proposed solution involves creating a prototype named Visualine, which trans-
forms time series data from a one-button tracker into engaging visualizations. Visu-
aline aims to simplify the data into user-friendly, easy-to-interpret visual representa-
tions, accessible to both experts and non-experts for use in psychological therapies
or personal self-analysis. The development of Visualine emphasizes an optimal user
experience, with its focus on providing insightful visualizations and a seamless user
interface. The validity of Visualine will be confirmed by using data from participants
that struggle with anxiety disorders and subjective experiences like pain and other
participants.

CHAPTER 2
Related work
The following chapter provides a comprehensive overview of the existing literature
relevant to the proposed solution of transforming data from a mental phenomena
into engaging visualizations for psychological therapies and personal self-analysis. It
focuses on two primary areas: the tracking of mental phenomena and the visualization
of the data.
2.1
One-Button Tracking of Mental Phenomena
Larsen et al. [LEC17] pioneered the use of a one-button wearable device to track
mental phenomena, specifically anxiety. By translating subjective experiences into
quantifiable data, the device allows for analysis of anxiety patterns and trends, sig-
naling the one-button tracker as a promising tool for personalized mental health care.
Arendt et al. [Are+21] expanded on this concept by applying the one-button tracking
technology to explore the relationship between rumination and intrusions in PTSD
patients. Their study demonstrated the device’s potential applicability beyond anx-
iety, uncovering temporal relations between mental phenomena and highlighting its
potential for research and clinical treatment for PTSD.
Building on these findings, Van de Belt et al. [Bel+22] conducted a study that further
demonstrated the one-button tracker’s versatility and effectiveness in monitoring a
range of subjectively experienced health-related phenomena, such as creativity, snack-
ing, stress, hydration, drinking, wellness, and more. Conducted over 2 to 4 weeks with
13 employees, the study aﬀirmed the tracker’s ease of use, receiving favorable usability
scores. Van de Belt et al.’s work not only validated the device but also emphasized its
potential to support personal understanding and health behavior change across vari-
ous domains, solidifying its place as a valuable tool in mental and overall well-being
tracking.
The exploration and validation of one-button tracking technology, as discussed in this
section, provide a foundational understanding that informs the data collection and
analysis aspects of the present project.

2.2
Visualizing Mental Phenomena
Larsen et al.
introduced an innovative technique to depict anxiety patterns from
one-button tracker data over 24-hour and daily periods. By identifying peak times
and specific days when anxiety symptoms were most pronounced, this methodology
provided a detailed view of an individual’s emotional well-being, enabling targeted
therapeutic interventions.
Furthermore, Larsen et al.
developed an visualization
prototype on Observable, which includes interactive charts for both hourly and daily
data.
Their ’Calendar View’ combines a scatter chart with a bar chart, allowing
users to zoom and pan, thus offering an interactive and detailed perspective. Refer
to screenshot of calendar view, daily and hourly visualizations in fig. 2.1.

Young et al. [You+22] explored the use of passive mobile sensing to monitor behav-
iors in serious mental illnesses (SMI) patients. Through a mobile app, they collected
data like sensor readings and phone use over 9 months, refer to screenshot of mo-
bile application dashboard in fig. 2.2. This data aimed to predict behavior changes,
including anxiety. The study combined focus groups, testing, and interviews, sug-
gesting passive sensing as a potential tool for enhanced anxiety monitoring in SMI
contexts.

3.1
Personal Informatics
The widespread adoption of PI systems has enabled a new frontier for individuals to
explore personal data, fostering better self-understanding and facilitating informed
decisions. These systems, ranging from fitness trackers to mood journals, have shown
potential to transform personal health and well-being. Yet, their widespread use has
also unveiled a range of challenges and concerns.
In response to these challenges, a stage-based model was developed, as detailed by Li
et al. [LDF10]. This model not only delineates the various phases of interaction with
PI systems but also highlights potential pitfalls and opportunities within each stage.
It consists of five stages:
1. Preparation: Defines motivation and methods for recording personal informa-
tion.
2. Collection: Engages individuals in self-tracking various personal details.
3. Integration: Readies the data for user reflection, as depicted in fig. 3.1.
4. Reflection: Allows examination of personal information through lists or inter-
active visualizations.
5. Action: Facilitates decisions towards personal goals or behavioral alignment.
These stages reveal unique challenges and opportunities, characterized by essential
properties including interconnected design (Barriers Cascading), where issues in one
==PAGE:24==

stage affect subsequent stages, requiring a comprehensive approach. Inherent itera-
tion (Stages are Inherently Iterative) demands systems that are flexible and evolve
with the user’s needs, allowing for continuous adjustments. Balance between (User
and System Control) emphasizes finding the optimal mix between automation and
customization to enhance user experience. The decision making between (Uni-faceted
and Multi-faceted Designs) involves choosing between simplicity and a broader view,
affecting the system’s depth of insights. Together, these properties guide the creation
of systems that are adaptable, balanced, user-focused, and insightful.

3.4
Annotation Methods
Implementing annotation functionality within data visualization is an intricate task
that can be tackled through two principal methods: user-driven and system-driven
approaches.
• User-Driven Annotation: This approach involves human interaction and
control. It includes tasks such as manually selecting points, lines, or charts,
writing sticky notes, leaving comments, or using drawing tools, including free-
hand drawing or predefined shapes.
• System-Driven Annotation: This method relies on algorithms, rules, or
automated processes. It can be implemented using machine learning, rule-based
algorithms, or other computational methods to detect patterns, outliers, or
specific conditions within the data, and then apply the corresponding labels or
annotations.
User-driven methods confer greater flexibility and nuanced understanding but can
be laborious and susceptible to human error.
Conversely, system-driven methods

4.2.1
Design Thinking
Design thinking is a human-centered approach to problem-solving that involves empa-
thy, experimentation, and iteration. It seeks to understand users, challenge assump-
tions, redefine problems, and identify innovative solutions. It is most useful to tackle
problems that are ill-defined or unknown. Design thinking involves five phases [Sha]:
1. Empathize: Understand user needs through thorough research. Gaining this
empathetic perspective ensures genuine insight, sidelining biases and assump-
tions.
2. Define: Aggregate and analyze findings from the empathize phase to pinpoint
core issues. Problem statements are crafted, and personas can be developed to
maintain a human-centric approach.
3. Ideate: With a strong foundation of knowledge, innovative solutions to the
identified problem statements are brainstormed, challenging conventional view-
points.
4. Prototype: Experiment by developing scaled-down, cost-effective versions of
potential solutions, even if they’re as basic as paper prototypes.
5. Test: Rigorously assess prototypes. Despite being the last stage, design think-
ing remains cyclical. Results may lead to revisiting and refining earlier stages.
Each stage is a unique element within the comprehensive design project, beyond
mere sequential progression. The aim is to deeply understand user preferences and
identify the perfect solution.
By employing a iterative design thinking approach,
feedback and testing continually shape the process. This guarantees a user-focused
and exceptionally eﬀicient design [Fou23].
4.2.2
Minimum Viable Product
A Minimum Viable Product (MVP) is a concept in Lean Startup methodology, rep-
resenting the simplest version of a product that fulfills its core function. The MVP
includes only key features, allowing it to be released quickly to early adopters [Pro23].
The purpose of an MVP is to:
• Reduce Development Time and Cost: By focusing on core features.
• Gather User Feedback: To guide future development.
• Test Market Assumptions: To validate the product’s fit in the market.
==PAGE:38==
24
4 Design
• Facilitate Pivoting: Allows quick changes in direction based on real-world
insights.
In essence, an MVP is a functional, minimal product that enables quick learning and
adaptation, aligning closely with customer needs.
4.2.3
Build-Measure-Learn
The Build-Measure-Learn (BML) cycle is a core concept in Lean Startup methodology,
focusing on rapid development to align with customer needs [Czł23].
• Build: Create a MVP with essential features to test a hypothesis quickly.
• Measure: Collect quantitative data on user interaction and feedback to assess
the product’s performance.
• Learn: Interpret the results to understand what works and what does not,
possibly leading to further development or a change in direction.
The cycle is iterative, promoting continuous improvement and adaptability. It em-
phasizes eﬀiciency, minimizing waste, and focusing on real customer value, allowing
startups to respond quickly to market demands.

4.4.2
Typography
Serif and sans-serif fonts are two major categories of typefaces, or font families, that
are used in print and digital design. The distinction between the two lies in the small
decorative lines and details on the letters. Serif fonts have small decorative lines, or
’feet’, at the ends of the strokes in a letter. ’Sans-serif’ literally translates to ’without
serif’ - these fonts do not have the decorative lines at the ends of the letter strokes.
Iliinsky et al. [IS11] suggest that people prefer serif fonts for setting blocks of text,
while sans-serif fonts are better suited for titles, tags, and labels.
Given the absence of text blocks in the prototype, it solely uses the font known as
Poppins. Identified as a geometric sans-serif typeface, Poppins derives its geometric
label from the letters’ design, meticulously crafted to mirror geometric entities like
circles and squares.

5.1.1
JavaScript or TypeScript
React1 emerged as the preferred choice. React supports both JavaScript and Type-
Script. Notably, JavaScript was chosen for its combined benefits of sustainability,
as evidenced by its lower energy consumption in a paper by Pereira et al. [Per+21],
and its inherent adaptability. In the study, JavaScript consumed 4.45J compared to
TypeScript’s 21.50J, showcasing JavaScript’s eco-friendly edge.
Beyond sustainability, JavaScript is a dynamic-typed language, much-lauded for its
wide compatibility, flexibility, and rich ecosystem, which contribute to its adapt-
ability. However, it does have its challenges, such as potential runtime errors and
inconsistencies. Conversely, TypeScript, a superset of JavaScript, brings static typing
to the table, catching errors at compile-time. It offers advanced features and tooling
aimed at enhancing maintainability. While TypeScript introduces precision and ro-
bustness, it does come with a steeper learning curve, the need for transpilation, and
can occasionally feel verbose. Summarizing, JavaScript’s selection often pivots on its
adaptability and sustainability, whereas TypeScript is chosen for its meticulousness
and error-reduction capabilities.

React Context API
The React Context API provides a mechanism that facilitates data sharing across
the entire component hierarchy, eliminating the need to pass data explicitly through
props. This makes it particularly eﬀicient for managing global state and application
configurations.
One of the primary concepts to understand in this framework is the use of Provider
and Consumer components. The Provider wraps a part of the component tree and
supplies the data, while the Consumer or the useContext hook can be used by nested
components to access this data. This structure ensures a centralized and eﬀicient
data flow throughout an application.
The prototype manages data and annotations associated with charts through the
React Context API. Refer to fig. 5.3 for a visual representation of this implementation.
Below is a detailed breakdown:
1. Setup:
• A unique storage box named FileContext is created.
• The initialState serves as an inventory list specifying the type of data
(e.g., files, chart specifics) to be kept in this box.
2. Managing changes with fileReducer:
• Acts as a supervisor that monitors modifications to the data, such as choos-
ing data or annotating charts.
3. The FileProvider component:
• Functions as a central hub, deploying useReducer a tool for overseeing the
data and the actions to alter it.
• It shields various minor components, bestowing them access rights to view
or adjust the data in the FileContext.
4. Fetching the data with useFile hook:
• A utility facilitating sections of the program to retrieve the data contained
in FileContext.
• Ensures data is fetched only when within the protective confines of FileProvider.

many. Pain is a highly subjective and complex phenomenon, affecting individuals
differently and in various forms such as chronic pain, headaches, and muscle discom-

7.1.1
Challenges
Understanding the workings of the mind is a complex task. Unlike straightforward
physical measurements such as heart rate or steps taken, mental experiences are
personal, unique to each individual, and cannot be directly observed or quantified.
Moreover, mental health often comes with a stigma, leading to hesitation in tracking
or sharing related information.
Mental states are complex and can overlap, like
anxiety and depression symptoms, complicating their tracking. Interrupting activities
to reflect on one’s mental state, particularly in distressing moments, can also be
challenging.
One participant’s experience with tracking anxiety illustrated this complexity. After
engaging in self-monitoring for some time, he expressed concern that the very act
of pressing the button to record his anxiety seemed to intensify the feeling itself.
Fearing that tracking could exacerbate his anxiety, he sought his therapist’s advice
before proceeding further.
Fortunately, the therapist recognized the value in this
exercise and gave approval for its continuation.
Furthermore, interpreting the data from mental state tracking can be confusing. For
example, increased reports of anxiety might not indicate worsening symptoms, but
instead, a growing awareness of these feelings.
Despite these challenges, tracking
mental states remains valuable. It aids in self-understanding and offers crucial infor-
mation for mental health professionals. The diﬀiculties encountered emphasize the
importance of reliable tracking tools, particularly considering how widespread mental
health conditions are.
Recognizing these challenges, researchers have been actively exploring solutions to im-
prove the tracking of mental states. Van Kleunen et al. [VV19] examine the design of
self-tracking technologies for Serious Mental Illnesses, emphasizing control over data
disclosure, dynamic relationships, and flexible self-tracking variables.
They relate
these to predictive modeling and interventions in mental health systems, outlining
challenges in data disclosure, flexibility, and mood prediction for individuals with

specific needs like bipolar disorders.
The study proposes personalized, dynamic solutions that include custom variables
and scales, flexibility in tracking (including mobile sensors), and potential for predict-
ing symptoms like depression or mania. Despite limitations due to privacy concerns,
the system promises new avenues for intervention, such as crisis alerts. These techno-
logical advancements align with the need for more sophisticated tools to understand
and assist in mental health care, a need emphasized by the complexities outlined

7.2
Data Handling
When dealing with personal and, in particular, health data, adherence to the General
Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)1 is paramount. This concern was highlighted
as one of the challenges in the studies conducted by Van Kleunen et al., and it was
specifically a matter of concern for the individual whose data was being handled. Of
course, when handling data of this nature, it should always be considered and handled
mindfully. For this project, however, a database was not implemented, and informa-
tion was only stored temporarily within each session before being erased. GDPR
compliance was not a primary focus of this project; instead, the emphasis was placed
on creating a user-friendly prototype to visualize mental phenomena and support the
learning process for those engaging with the prototype.

7.6
Preferences and Adaptability
Findings from evaluations revealed diverse preferences for visualizations among par-
ticipants. One participant favored a complex calendar view, allowing detailed interac-
tion, such as clicking on specific days to observe related information. This approach
was preferred over having multiple visualizations, while another participant found
the calendar view overly complex. Despite both participants having experience in
interpreting data, they did not uniformly favor complex visualizations.
Opinions also differed regarding the line chart displaying progression over time. One
participant appreciated it, while another expressed distress at the fluctuating lines,
especially if prone to anxiety. The latter participant thought the home page was
too information-dense upon initially seeing it, containing the line chart at the top
and particularly disliked the line chart. In contrast, all users praised the bar chart’s
simplicity, allowing views of data in hourly, daily, or monthly format. During the eval-
uation, most participants first turned to the bar charts to solve tasks and consulted
other charts only if needed.
7.7
Web or Mobile Application
With today’s advanced mobile phones, many individuals, particularly among the
older population, find no need to own a computer. This presents a challenge for the
prototype, which is built with React and designed for web browsers. The prototype
has not been tested in a phone’s browser view, limiting its accessibility. Since the
==PAGE:82==

aim is to reach as many users within the target group as possible, it is vital that the
prototype can function on phones either through a web browser or as a mobile app.
Two solutions present themselves: converting the prototype to React Native2 for
mobile compatibility, or simply making it responsive for phone browsers. Developing
in React Native, although related to React, is time-consuming and requires building
the application from scratch. Making the existing prototype responsive is a more
feasible option since it is constructed with components, and only a few of them need
to be modified to ensure functionality.
The decision to use React for a web browser application rather than creating a mobile
app was deliberate. Had the prototype been designed for a mobile app, it might have
hindered the interpretation of visualizations during therapeutic sessions, due to the
smaller screen size. This would have made simultaneous viewing by both therapist
and patient more challenging. Creating a prototype for web browsers made more
sense, given that making components responsive for phone viewing is a simpler task.
In contrast, transitioning from a mobile application to a web browser would have
posed greater diﬀiculties.

Future work
The chapter delves into possible improvements that should be considered during fur-
ther development of the prototype.
8.1
Database and Sign-in Integration
Implementing a database is vital for the storage of data within the system. This pro-
totype comes with annotation and journaling features, which would lose its purpose
if the data vanished following a browser reload.
Integrating a database not only safeguards the data but also paves the way for sign-in
functionality. This is a crucial aspect, as it enables users to retain their data every
time they interact with the prototype. Consequently, annotations and journal entries
can be tied to individual user IDs, thereby enhancing user-friendliness.
During the design phase, a concept was developed for the sign-in functionality, as
illustrated in fig. E.2(a). This particular design permits users to log in using only their
username and password. A more advanced solution might have included the ability
for users to be identified by their personal cell phone number and authenticated with
a one-time passcode sent via SMS or email. This functionality not only enhances
user convenience but also improves security. By employing two separate forms of
identification, it creates a more robust defense against unauthorized access, making
it a preferable choice for safeguarding user information.
The addition of this functionality is poised to elevate the user experience, making it
more streamlined and accessible. Furthermore, optimizing the prototype for mobile
web browsers will cater to users who prefer mobile interaction. As the existing system
can be cumbersome when adding data via a mobile web browser, responsive design
would remedy this issue.
For a swift integration process, Firebase1 could be employed as the database solution.
As a NoSQL database, it offers ease of use, flexibility, and scalability, and is equipped
with a multitude of impressive features. This approach not only addresses immediate
needs but also sets the stage for future growth and adaptation.

8.2
Visualization Improvement
Currently, the annotation feature is incompatible with the calendar view. As outlined
in section 5.2, the calendar view is constructed using the Victory library, differing
from the other visualizations, which are created with the Recharts library. To es-
tablish a uniform user experience, implementing an annotation feature across all
visualizations—including the calendar view is a necessary improvement.
In addition to this, incorporating visual indicators within the calendar view could en-
hance readability. For example, vertical lines representing individual days to provide
a clearer reference for users. Making the weekends visually distinct with colored cells
could also aid in interpreting the data.
Further sophistication could be added to the calendar view to enable more in-depth
exploration. Allowing users to click on a specific day to reveal detailed information
about that day would cater to those desiring more intricate views. This enhancement
would not only make the view more engaging but also responsive to the diverse needs
and preferences of its users.
The line visualization illustrating the progression of time must be altered to encom-
pass all days including days with no observations, thereby creating a linear representa-
tion. This error was found during the usability evaluation by one of the participants.
In order to avoid overwhelming the user with excessive information upon opening the
prototype, the line visualization should be relocated to the bottom of the screen.
Another enhancement involves transforming the white boxes surrounding the visual-
izations into interactive widgets, empowering users to filter and rearrange them as
talked about in the design phase. This fosters greater adaptability and personaliza-
tion, placing more control in the user’s hands.
A potential enhancement to the data visualizations would have been the inclusion of
a long press. For instance, a user tracking various anxiety levels mild, moderate, and
high could use a one press, double press, and long press to represent each state. How-
ever, the current implementation only supports the visualization of one and double
presses. This limitation exists because the project focused solely on these methods,
reflecting the preferences of users in the case study, who only used one and double
presses for tracking.
8.3
Annotations Improvement
The current method for adding annotations involves clicking on the sidebar. While
this method functions, it isn’t as intuitive as some of the findings from the usability
evaluation suggest. Interestingly, half of the participants instinctively right-clicked the
data visualizations to add annotations. This was a solution not considered previously.
==PAGE:85==

71
This right-click approach is far more eﬀicient: it reduces the number of required
mouse clicks and streamlines the process. With the current method, users must click
on the sidebar, wait for a popup, click their desired annotation spot, and then interact
with yet another popup to rate or access annotation details. Conversely, right-clicking
followed by a simple rating prompt is faster and more user-friendly. It is recommended
to adopt this method in the prototype.
8.4
Integrating AI Insights
The high-fidelity prototype was not initially integrated with AI Insights, a feature
that could have enhanced the prototype by helping users interpret the data. In the
envisioned design, as shown in fig. 4.9(c), these insights would be displayed as widgets
on the right side of the data visualizations. These widgets would highlight trends or
patterns hidden in the data. Each widget would bear a color corresponding to either
a one or double press, offering a more intuitive understanding of the information.
Several AI methodologies are proficient at getting valuable insights from the captured
data. Some techniques that resonate well with the needs of this prototype encompass:
• Trend Analysis: Various analytical methods, encompassing simple moving
averages to more sophisticated models like ARIMA and state space models,
enable the detection of data trends. These AI-powered techniques can recognize
changes in anxiety levels over time. For example, a persistent upward trend
might signal escalating stress levels in a user. Conversely, a downward trend
could imply the effectiveness of employed coping strategies. Seasonal patterns
may also be discernible, identifying specific periods during the day, week, or
month when anxiety levels varies.
• Sentiment Analysis: The annotation system and journal feature in the pro-
totype enable users to capture their thoughts and emotions in written form.
By applying Natural Language Processing (NLP) techniques, a wealth of in-
sights can be extracted. For instance, NLP can detect emotions based on the
language used, categorize entries based on predefined anxiety levels, identify re-
curring themes or topics that might point towards anxiety triggers, and assign
sentiment scores to understand mood variations.
The AI methodologies, as mentioned above, can be embedded into the React appli-
cation using APIs or JavaScript libraries:
• APIs: Many cloud-based AI platforms, such as Google Cloud AI, Azure AI,
and IBM Watson, offer APIs for a variety of AI tasks like trend analysis and
sentiment analysis.
The React application can make API requests to these
platforms to obtain AI-based insights.

– TensorFlow2: The library supports training and deploying ML models

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
The goal of this thesis was to provide individuals with a prototype that could simplify
the representation of their mental phenomena through interactive visualizations.
In short, the prototype was intended to bridge the gap between complex mental
phenomena and individuals’ understanding, enabling them to take proactive steps
towards improving their mental well-being and/or assisting healthcare professionals
in delivering personalized treatments.
The validity of the prototype was to be confirmed by a case study, using data from
participants that struggle with anxiety disorders and subjective experiences like hip
pain and other participants included in the evaluations.
The overall project plan outlined four main phases: research & gather, design in
Figma & evaluate a low-fidelity prototype, develop application & evaluate a high-
fidelity prototype, and finally to finalize the thesis. The research phase consisted of
studying related work and relevant literature in order to get inspiration for the best
possible solution for tracking of mental phenomena and the visualization of the data.
To continuously refine the prototype, the case study participants were engaged in user
sessions and evaluation throughout the design and develop phases, and the project
plan was slightly revised after it was decided to provide two participants with trackers
for collecting own real-life data.
The usability evaluation taught that participants who have some connection to the
data or are in a field where the prototype may be useful are generally more engaged.
This connection leads to a greater willingness to participate in the evaluation, which
in turn can help gather more comprehensive and insightful feedback for enhancing
the usability of the prototype.
These observations emphasize the importance of including participants within the
product development phase, an idea prominently underscored in Alli’s research [All18].
Furthermore, the evaluations reveal that employing a case study approach not only
served as an effective method but also fostered a deeper engagement among users in
the process.
Findings indicate that users appreciate the functionalities implemented for the re-
==PAGE:88==
74
9 Conclusion
flection stage, including both the annotation method and the journal feature. These
results support the assertions made by Ahmadpour et al. [AC18], who stresses the
incorporation of features such as personalized data playback and journaling. Addi-
tionally, both the annotation method and journal are user-driven which aligns with
the insights provided by Cho et al. [Cho+22], highlighting the essential role of user-
driven methods in the reflection stage of the process.
Moreover, users generally favor simple visualizations, but opinions diverge as com-
plexity increases. In designing an effective visualization tool, it is crucial to adapt to
different levels of complexity. This may include providing visualizations ranging from
simple to complex, possibly with filtering options so users can select displays that suit
their needs. For users who favor simplicity, a well-designed interface could prevent
information overload by either moving more complex visualizations out of immediate
view or employing thoughtful prototype designs. Additional findings revealed that
some users would appreciate guidance from the prototype, including the provision of
helpful tips.
These findings line up with the study by Rapp et al. [Rap+18] on how to improve
user engagement. This approach focuses on the user, bringing together elements of
design, intuition, focus, and personalization.
The final conclusion of this thesis is that the overall goal has been reached: a proto-
type for interactive visualization of self-tracking data on mental phenoma has been
designed and developed, and the usability evaluation engaged a diverse panel of users,
aﬀirming the prototype’s intuitive interface and user-friendliness. Positive feedback
was complemented by constructive suggestions that helped refine the prototype even
further and even provided several great ideas for possible further development.
