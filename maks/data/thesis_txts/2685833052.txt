Abstract 
 
 
In low energy homes, the opacity and complexity of heating and mechanical ventilation installations 
are increasing. Current studies highlighted the importance of providing the occupants with information 
about the indoor climate and the technical systems as a fault detection tool, in order to increase their 
perception of control. 
This thesis seeks to reduce this growing complexity by designing and developing a data-driven model 
of an apartment’s thermal behavior based on several Resistance-Capacitance (RC) models and basic 
sensors. As a proof-of-concept we utilize the sensor data and physical boundaries of a 108 m2 single-
story apartment in Nordhavn (Copenhagen area). 
As a first step we design several RC model representations and their system of differential equations. 
These representations are modelled in detail inside a simulation software and converted into Functional 
Mock-up Units (FMUs) of the apartment in order to estimate the physical parameters. The result is a 
set of different models of increasing complexity, with which building characteristics are estimated, such 
as: heat capacity and resistance, ventilation, solar gains/window area and thermal conductivity. In 
addition, we develop a methodology to create the models, estimate their parameters and validate them. 
To conduct our experiments, the RC models are used as a grey box. The performance of the models is 
compared using the root-mean-square error (RMSE) obtained from the simulations against real data 
from the apartment in Nordhavn. To compare its accuracy, a black box is implemented using a 
Nonlinear Autoregressive Exogenous (NARX) model for the prediction of the data. The models can be 
used for different purposes, such as indoor climate control and energy consumption forecasting. 
The thesis summarizes and compares the accuracy of the results between grey and black box models 
for Fault Detection & Diagnosis (FDD). It proves that black-box models outperform grey-box models. 
It concludes with a discussion of the challenges and recommendations for future work, and possible 
implementations in practice. 
 
 
Keywords: Residential Buildings, HVAC Systems, RC Models, Genetic Algorithm, NARX, Energy 
Consumption, MPC, FDD, Python, Modelica, Dymola, ModestPy

1.1. Background and Project Motivation 
 
Field studies carried out among occupants of low-energy homes showed that many of them are 
concerned about the growing opacity and complexity of heating and mechanical ventilation 
installations. At times making it difficult for them to have the upper hand on the indoor climate and to 
recognize technical issues when they arise (Sarran, Hviid, & Rode, 2020). 
Residents identify significant issues in the reliability and usability of the energy technologies (Berry, 
Whaley, Davidson, & Saman, 2014), suggesting a growing need for a user-friendly system that provides 
better information (Mlecnik, 2012).  
Moreover, energy efficiency is becoming an important issue where around 50% of total energy in 
developed countries is consumed in the building sector (F. Belić & Slišković, 2016) and between 10% 
to 20% of total energy is consumed from HVAC systems in developed countries (Alghoul, 2017). 
Among different types of buildings, residential heating constitutes 60% of the total energy consumption 
(Ferrarini, Fathi, Disegna, & Rastegarpour, 2019). Therefore, there is a recent research trend to reduce 
the energy consumption of residential buildings, including the development of suitable energy models 
and the adoption of advanced control strategies for the heating, ventilation and air conditioning (HVAC) 
systems of the buildings.  In other words, there is a need to increase the energy efficiency in order to 
improve the control methods of HVAC systems, what requires a better quality of thermal models in 
order to recognize technical issues. Simulated methods could be used for this purpose, such as Fault 
Detection & Diagnosis (FDD) and Model Prediction Control (MPC). 
The high complexity and required knowledge to design thermal models is a handicap for the current 
need of agile and fast model design. This thesis investigates a better approach to estimate parameters, 
from basic data and simple RC thermal models. These data-driven buildings models are a way forward 
to detect faults in the system. It will reduce computation time and costs to simulate the thermal behavior 
of an apartment.

1.2. Problem Statement 
 
The problem statement is: “Is it possible to build a single-zone model of an apartment's thermal 
behavior based on easily accessible sensor data?” “Would it be accurate enough to detect faults on the 
building systems?”. 
 
The model would be able to predict the indoor temperature and energy heating consumption:  
- 
Predicting the indoor temperature, we could detect faults that could be derivative of an 
exceed/decrease of floor heating demand, ventilation power or electric suppliance power. Also, 
if the heating or ventilation are unexpectedly shutting off in specific periods.  
 
- 
Predicting the heating energy consumption, we could detect errors that cannot be verified 
looking at the indoor temperature. This is because the heating system compensates. These faults 
could be, for example, too much airflow coming from the ventilation system, or excessive 
window opening. 
 
 
1.3. Thesis Goals 
 
The project goals to be achieved are: 
• 
To develop a data-driven model of an apartment’s thermal behavior using basic sensors and 
agile thermal models to reduce complexity and costs. 
 
• 
To create models of different complexities and to investigate prediction accuracy. 
 
• 
To compare the performance of grey and black box models for FDD. 
 
• 
To investigate open-source tools to simplify parameter estimation. 
 
The developed model could be used for fault detection, in particular: 
• 
Detect faulty sensors by comparing the measured data against the predicted values.  
 
• 
Detect faulty or inefficient heating and ventilation systems by comparing measured against 
predicted data, such as air and water flows, temperatures and energy consumption.

2.1. Thermal Modelling 
 
Thermal modelling makes possible to simulate and predict how the indoor temperature in a building 
will vary in an apartment over time. In order to design the theoretical thermal modelling and mockups, 
a very popular approach to reduce the complexity is the thermal modelling of building with Resistance-
Capacitance (RC) method. 
The quality of thermal models produced with RC methods were investigated by (F. Belić & Slišković, 
2016) who found that models which were optimized with Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) obtained 
the most accurate results. Also, it was found that the structure of model was more important to model's 
accuracy than values of parameters. In particular, reduced models with an optimized design obtained 
better results in some scenarios. 
(Bacher & Madsen, 2011) proposed a procedure for identification of the most suitable models for the 
heat dynamics of a building. The proposed models were grey-box models where the results give reliable 
estimations. One of the model’s deficiencies pointed out was solar radiation entering the building. 
In order to design the thermal models, occupants and solar radiation have a high impact in thermal 
modelling and therefore are included as an input for the case study. These two variables introduce 
uncertainties as explained by (Mantesi, Hopfe, Cook, Glass, & Strachan, 2018). The authors found that 
modelling uncertainties account for up to 26% of the variation in the simulation predictions. Their 
impact might not be as high compared for example to uncertainties related to occupancy (up to 170%), 
however it is significant.  
Besides, occupant behavior (OB) is recognized as a leading source of uncertainty in building 
performance predictions. A stepwise approach for assessing the appropriate occupant behavior 
modelling in building performance simulation by (Gaetani, Hoes, & Hensen, 2020) is taken in 
consideration while developing our RC “full model” approach.

2.2. Black-, White- & Grey-box models 
 
- 
Black-box models 
Black-box models are built purely using measured data and do not require any physical 
understanding, where the processes are opaques. Therefore, there are a good option when the 
information about the modelled system is unknown. They are fast and requires minimal 
computing power, the counterpart is the lack of flexibility and its lack of any form of physical 
meaning, underlying dynamics involved in such system. This type of modelling is gaining 
popularity due to the advances in machine learning. Studies on this topic deal mainly with use 
of artificial neural networks and genetic evolutionary algorithms. 
 
- 
White-box models 
White-box models are the most detailed type of model and is as close as possible to a full 
description of the real building, with explicit equations of the behavior of the building. The 
models rely on a complete knowledge of the building operation and the underlying physical 
phenomena. All this mean that the models are extremely flexible because everything is 
modelled on a low level and very realistic. Conversely, that complexity make it harder to be 
developed and requires large computing processes. These models enable to explore the 
relationships between the performance of different systems, mostly use to quantify effect of 
faulty systems. White-box model are more difficult to develop than grey or black box models 
although once developed they can be more easily use for FDD. However, the calibration is 
difficult and requires iterations with human-in-the-loop. In consequence, practical application 
of white-box models in FDD is feasible only for simple buildings. 
 
- 
Grey-box models  
As measurements can be limited or a deep knowledge of the building’s thermal behavior can 
be expensive and time consuming, a good fit are grey-box models. This kind of models 
combines mathematical equations to describe building physics with real data to complete the 
model. Therefore, if the amount or quality of data is limited and only some basic information 
about modelled building is known, grey-box models can be a good alternative to black-box and 
white-box models.

2.3. Grey-box models 
 
Grey-box modelling is a powerful tool often used in building research, in particular for applications in 
model-predictive control and fault detection (Andriamamonjy, Klein, & Saelens, 2019). 
(Gunay, O’Brien, & Beausoleil-Morrison, 2016) put forward 12 gray-box models at varying sensory 
model inputs and parameters. The parameters of each model were recursively estimated using the 
extended Kalman. They found that it could robustly predict the indoor temperature at less than ±0.6°C 
mean absolute error over a 2-day horizon. 
(Hedegaard & Petersen, 2017) Thermal modelling using grey-box model parameter estimates are 
widely used for thermal characterizations of building. The authors found that their best performing 
models, using EnergyPlus and MATLAB, tended to overestimate the effective thermal mass by 10-32 
% compared to the Effective Thickness Method of ISO 13786. The results also indicated that identifying 
the distribution of the total heat loss into transmission and infiltration heat losses is unlikely to be 
achieved from typical building measurement data. 
(Ferrarini, Fathi, Disegna, & Rastegarpour, 2019) address the problem to develop a reliable energy 
model of a building to predict its future behavior where they found that gray-box models have a lower 
accuracy for future prediction than white-box and black-box, but it is a good fit for retrofitting actions. 
On this study (Saurav & Chandan, 2017) present and validate a grey-box methodology for thermal 
modelling of buildings to capture the dynamics of the buildings more effectively as compared to pure 
data driven approach. The authors present results of highly accurate building model with an accuracy 
of 99.7% for the internal temperature and 92.3% for the heat transfer. However, it requires significantly 
more instrumentation of the building since the proposed modelling paradigm requires a larger set of 
data (11 inputs). Also, the training period is not mentioned and the data sample for testing is only over 
one week. 
On this study, (Andriamamonjy, Klein, & Saelens, 2019) created a toolchain that uses BIM in order to 
automatically generate several grey-box structures. They found that it reduces the human involvement 
in the modelling process and can also produce more accurate models. Besides, this study shows that 
multi-objective optimization with datasets from two different seasons results in models that are valid 
for all seasons. 
Authors (De Coninck, Magnusson, Åkesson, & Helsen, 2016) presented an approach to obtain grey-
box models in a largely automated way and a toolbox that automates the parameter estimation for 
development and validation of grey-box building models for forecasting and control. They found that 
in single-zone building with an open-loop simulation over 20 days on the cross-validation dataset, their 
RMS error was 0.33. 
Authors (Arendt, Jradi, Shaker, & Veje, 2018)  presented a comparative analysis of white-, grey- and 
black-box models for thermal simulation of indoor environment. They showed that black-box models, 
using NARX models, outperform grey-box models. Besides, the authors pointed out that grey-box 
models with low-order RC models are not reliable for long-term prediction due to the error 
accumulation over the time. Moreover, longer training periods do not necessarily improve the 
performance of grey-box model. 
According to the studied literature, one of the most common use of grey-box models appears to be 
MPC. This method of constrained optimal control was first developed by Shell Oil engineers with its 
initial application in 1973. It is a set of strategies using a mathematical model of a building to minimize 
cost function subjected to some constraints. In our scope, we will generate estimations that could be 
used in such area although it will not be covered in this report.

2.4. 
Parameter Estimation 
 
In a grey-box model, the parameter estimation process uses sample data to estimate the internal 
parameters of a physical model. The parameter estimation algorithm looks for the set of parameters that 
yields the most accurate prediction of some output variables (usually indoor temperature), when 
compared with the measured values. 
For building models made in Modelica, as in our case, the Modestpy tool developed by (Arendt, Jradi, 
Wetter, & Veje, 2018) allows parameter estimation in Python, with the option of genetic algorithms. 
Other common tools to accomplish such task are MATLAB System Identification toolbox or R-based 
package CTSM (Bacher & Madsen, 2011). 
ModestPy is an open-source Python tool for parameter estimation in Functional Mock-up Units (FMU) 
(Arendt, Jradi, Wetter, & Veje, 2018). The tool uses genetic algorithm (GA) for optimization and search 
on a population of possible solutions, inspired on biological operators such as mutation, crossover and 
selection. The authors found that GA improved the model accuracy where it was used for calibrating 
gray-box models of buildings and HVAC systems for the use in MPC. 
We intent to apply this research in a real case scenario to generate the parameters estimation. 
Further details about GA will be provided in Section 5.5. 
 
 
2.5. Fault Diagnosis and Detection (FDD) 
 
The main idea of fault diagnosis is to determine the type, size and location of the fault as well as its 
time of detection, based on the available measurements of the system (Mouzakitis, 2013). A general 
scheme of model-based fault diagnosis is shown in Figure 2.2. 
 
Figure 2.2: Model-based fault diagnosis (Mouzakitis, 2013).  
 
A fault diagnosis is attained in a two-stage process. First, from the input and output measurement 
obtained from the system, a residual signal (possible error) is generated. When the system is fault free, 
then residual should be zero or close to zero, and otherwise a fault is present. Residual could be a single 
fault or multiples. The type of the residual generator varies from an analytical mathematical model to a 
black-box model of the system. The second stage is the decision-making process where residuals are 
==PAGE:21==
State of the Art (Related Work) 
 
9 
 
examined for the likelihood of faults. The type of the decision-making mechanism varies from a simple 
threshold to several sophisticated statistical approaches. 
 
(Turner, Staino, & Basu, 2017) presented a system identification approach, to simplify the fault 
detection using only the indoor and outdoor temperature. The authors introduced a new, data-driven 
automated building HVAC fault detection method that uses a recursive least-squares model approach. 
They found that model parameters converge to stable values during normal system operation. In 
contrast, faults can be detected when the model parameters deviate from their converged values.

3.1.  Description of the apartment  
 
Sundmolen is a building in Nordhavn (Copenhagen area) that was inaugurated in 2017. For this study 
we chose the apartment A33, located on the first floor, with a gross area of 108m2 and a net area of 
84.5m2. The apartment is composed by three bedrooms, one toilet, one bathroom and one kitchen/living 
room, plus balcony. The apartment was built in a low-energy home with estimated primary energy use 
of 30.1 kWh/m2 and space heating demand of 7.8kWh/m2 per year.

Chapter 4 
 
 Mathematical models 
 
A grey-box model is established using a combination of prior physical knowledge and data. The prior 
physical knowledge is formulated by a set of first-order stochastic differential equations, also called a 
stochastic linear state-space model in continuous time. 
Modelling the entire heat dynamics of a building using stochastic differential equations (Andersen, 
Madsen, & Hansen, 1998) would be a possibility although is out of the scope for this thesis and only 
the stochastic differential equations for the indoor temperature will be illustrated as an example. Instead, 
we will use a simulation environment, Dymola, which will generate the differential equations for us. 
Making more agile the process of modelling the thermal building, adding some abstraction. 
 
4.1. RC representations and Differential Equations 
 
A well-established resistance-capacitance (RC) model is used as an initial basic reference. It utilizes a 
representative network of electric resistors and capacitors to simulate the thermal behavior of such 
systems. The digits before 'R' and 'C' means the number of resistances and capacitances used for the 
model, respectively. 
The evolution of the RC model is presented in an incremental complexity progression further in this 
chapter. Each RC model is presented with an illustration and a stochastic formula. The formula is a 
representation of the heat flow, focusing on the indoor temperature. Furthermore, all models are made 
as one zone models, meaning that internal temperature represent average temperature for all rooms. 
 
The RC-network of the full model, developed for this building, is illustrated in Figure 4.6. The model 
individual parts are: 
• 
Occupants: The occupants are modelled by a variable heat source connected via a capacity and 
a thermal resistance to the Interior. 
 
• 
Electricity: The heat input from electric appliances is modelled by a standard gain multiplied 
with the power consumption. 
 
• 
Interior: The interior is the indoor air and it is modelled as a heat capacity connected to other 
parts by thermal resistances. 
==PAGE:36==
Mathematical models

• 
Medium: A thermal medium inside the building represents the interior walls, which is modelled 
with a heat capacity and a thermal resistance to the Interior. 
 
• 
Heater: The heaters are modelled by a heat source connected via a capacity and a thermal 
resistance to the Interior. 
 
• 
Solar: The heat input from solar radiation is modelled by the global irradiance multiplied with 
the effective window area and a standard gains value. 
 
• 
Envelope: The building envelope is modelled with a heat capacity and thermal resistances to 
the external and internal wall. 
 
• 
Ambient: The ambient is represented by the observed ambient air temperature.

5.0. General schema 
 
This is a brief summarization for the following Chapter. In order to apply this methodology, it is 
necessary to follow the next steps as presented: 
- 
Step 1: Use the RC models described in Chapter 4. 
 
- 
Step 2: Create the RC models in Dymola and generate the corresponding FMUs. 
 
- 
Step 3: Obtain the data measurements described in section 3.2 as inputs/output for ModestPy. 
In general, we use the indoor temperature as the output/target value and the other measurements 
as inputs. The number of inputs depends on the requirements for each model. 
 
- 
Step 4: Obtain the physical boundaries of the building, described in section 3.3, as parameters 
to be estimated in ModestPy. 
 
- 
Step 5: Validate the model with the Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) and predictions obtained 
from ModestPy. 
 
- 
Step 6: The model validation pass. Otherwise, revise the model and repeat the process. 
 
5.1. Choice of Software 
 
In general, there is very little research done by use of open-source platforms in grey-box modelling. 
For the purposes of this project, it was decided to investigate possibilities of numerical modelling in 
Python. Even though Python does not have same popularity for mathematical modelling as MATLAB 
or R, it is still a solid software with many packages including those for mathematical optimization as 
well as dynamic modelling. Also, PyTorch is used for machine learning, which is gaining popularity 
and is widely used in research, which offers an advantage with its dynamic nature of creating graphs.

5.5. Parameter Estimation (ModestPy) 
 
The main goal of parameter estimation is to get curve fit of modelled temperature and measured 
temperature. It is a process of estimating model parameters from data. This procedure is also commonly 
referred to as model calibration or training.  
The parameters were estimated using ModestPy (Arendt K. , 2020), a Python tool, with a Root Mean 
Square Error (RMSE) cost function and ModestGA (Arendt K. , 2021), which is an optimized and 
benchmarked parallel genetic algorithm (GA) with inequality constraints, and a scipy-like interface.  
 
The settings for the genetic algorithm used were the following: 
       modestga_opts = { 
            'generations': 20, 
# Max. number of generations 
            'pop_size': 60, 
 
# Population size 
            'trm_size': 7, 
     
# Tournament size 
            'tol': 1e-3, 
 
# Absolute tolerance 
            'workers': 3          
# Number of CPUs to use 
        } 
 
A briefly explanation of some of the setting options for Genetic Algorithms (GA): 
- 
The maximum number of generations (generations) as a termination criterion which sets the 
maximum number of chromosome populations that will be generated before the top scoring 
chromosome will be returned as the search answer. 
 
- 
The population size (pop_size) is the number of chromosomes in each generation. 
 
- 
Tournament selection is a selection strategy used for selecting the fittest candidates from the 
current generation in a Genetic Algorithm. The tournament size (trm_size) defines the number 
of individuals to run a tournament among them, where the winner of each tournament (the one 
with the best fitness) is selected for crossover. 
==PAGE:46==
Methodology

Figure 5.2: ModestPy - Representation of the parameter estimation process using GA 
 
In order to clarify a bit more for the reader how GA algorithms works, they could be summarized in 5 
steps as follows (Figure 5.2 illustrates these steps as part of the parameter estimation process): 
Step 1: Generate initial population 
GA requires a set of input parameters initialized to represent the GA population. Initialization 
is done by randomly to generate n parameters as string of numbers. 
Step 2: Evaluation 
Generated parameters firstly evaluated by means of performance. This performance evaluation 
has been done by a performance function. 
Step 3: Selection / Regeneration 
Specific numbers of members of the population for this generation are copied, reproduced and 
added to the next generation. 
Step 4: Crossover / Recombination 
Crossover combines the genetic information of two parents to generate new offspring, i.e., we 
get the average of the two real numbers for our particular case. 
Step 5: Mutation 
Mutation is the random changing of parameters in some of the surviving values.

The way the floor heating power it is calculated is the following: the indoor air volume (blue balloon) 
receives or loses heat from the different heat sources (occupants, internal gains, solar gains, external 
walls, internal mass), and exchanges air through window opening and ventilation. If there was no 
heating, the temperature would drop below its actual level. So, the heat delivered by the floor 
corresponds to the necessary heat to bring the temperature back to the measured level. In the model, 
this is done by connecting the indoor temperature to the indoor air volume and measuring the heat flow 
through that connection. Thanks to that heat flow, the temperature in the indoor air volume becomes 
Tint (measured indoor temperature).

we obtain the heat delivered from the floor.

period (in Joules instead of Watts). 
-        The calculated one just integrates the variable “heat_floor_calc” to give the total energy 
provided by the floor over the simulated period: that’s “energy_floor_calc”. 
 
-        The measured (reference) one is the one called “energy_floor_meas”: it directly integrates the 
measured heating power, to give the measured heating energy used during this period. 
 
 
6.3. Black-box model 
 
For this study, in order to compare the errors against grey-box model, we used a black-box with the 
implementation (Na, 2020) of a novel dual-stage attention-based recurrent neural network (DA-RNN) 
model for time series prediction (Qin, et al., 2017). 
The Dual-Stage Attention-Based RNN (a.k.a. DA-RNN) model belongs to the general class of 
Nonlinear Autoregressive Exogenous (NARX) models, which predict the current value of a time series 
based on historical values of this series plus the historical values of multiple exogenous time series.  
This model includes two Long-Short Term Memory (LSTM) networks with attention mechanism (an 
encoder and a decoder). In the encoder, they introduced a novel input attention mechanism that can 
adaptively select the relevant driving series. In the decoder, a temporal attention mechanism is used to 
automatically select relevant encoder hidden states across all time steps. The attention mechanism 
performs feature selection as the paper mentioned, where the model only keeps the most useful 
information at each temporal stage.

7.2. Black box model  
 
The Dual-Stage Attention-Based RNN (a.k.a. DA-RNN) model belongs to the general class of 
Nonlinear Autoregressive Exogenous (NARX) models which predicts the current value of a time series 
based on historical values of this series plus the historical values of multiple exogenous time series. 
The logic behind the DA-RNN is explained in more detail in Section 6.3. Briefly, we have an encoder 
and decoder where the encoder adaptively selects the relevant driving series, and the decoder uses a 
temporal attention mechanism to automatically select relevant hidden states across all time steps.  
With these two attention mechanisms, the DA-RNN can adaptively select the most relevant input 
features and capture the long-term temporal dependencies of a time series.

7.2.1. Results 
 
The approach followed to obtain the results is a 70% training – 30% test of the data. The measurements 
are the same used for the grey box model. The details of how the training and evaluation happens 
internally is described in Section 6.3.  
The training reveals a loss (RMSE) of ~0.0068, using 15 days of data. The same training was repeated 
using 4 months data with a loss of ~0.0054. Clearly there is an improvement in gathering more 
information when we are working with a BB models. 
We use minibatch stochastic gradient descent (SGD) together with the Adam optimizer to train the 
model. The size of the minibatch is 128. The proposed DA-RNN is smooth and differentiable, so the 
parameters can be learned by standard back propagation with mean squared error as the objective 
function.

training set; otherwise, the network is extremely slow to converge.

7.3. Grey box vs Black box 
 
The accuracy of the black-box model to predict the indoor temperature of an apartment was compared 
against the grey-box model, using 15 days data with 70% training - 30% test. The result was a RMSE 
(%) of 0.12 for the grey-box vs ~0.0068 for the black-box. 
In general, black-box model (BBM) seems to have a better performance than grey-box model (GBM) 
for MPC and FDD, since it has a lower complexity with a much higher accuracy. Even if seems like 
that, for FDD “in real life”, GBM has some advantageous situations, as it has knowledge of the physical 
building. In the case of training the model on faulty data, the GBM is giving us unrealistic estimates of 
the internal parameters, or a wrong prediction, while BBM is trying to adapt and, therefore, not so good 
as detecting the error. 
Summarizing black box model for MPC/FDD, when our target variable does not suffer dramatical 
changes on a large range of values, the model has a good performance after 50 epochs. In our case, it 
happens with targets such as: indoor temperature, ventilation power and return flow temperature. In 
contrast, our model performs badly with electric gains or heating power. 
A deeper investigation to detect faults in data using the black box model would be interesting because 
in theory the performance of the DA-RNN is very adaptative trying to be resilient against what would 
be considered wrong data, although for the purpose of this thesis we did not go further into 
investigations on this matter.

Chapter 8 
 Discussion 
 
This chapter discusses the results presented in Chapter 7 and reviews how they satisfy the thesis goals 
presented in Section 1.3. This chapter then discusses the implications and some of the limitations of the 
models. Finally, the possible extensions of the project are outlined. 
This also includes how to answer the thesis goal questions. The main problem statement was:  
“Is it possible to build a single-zone model of an apartment's thermal behavior based on easily 
accessible sensor data?”. “Would it be accurate enough to detect faults on the building systems?”. 
 
8.1. Goals and Results 
 
In Section 7.1, as a proof-of-concept, we evaluate the different grey-box models with real data. Our 
final model (4R4C) was proved to obtain the best performance with a RMSE of 0.12. This result is 
difficult to be compared with other studies due to the different characteristic. As a reference from the 
literature in the state-of-art, a study obtained an RMS error of 0.33 and other study a ±0.6°C mean 
absolute error. In comparison, our model is retrieving better accuracy although the predicted indoor 
temperature when the window is open does not perform well sometimes. It could be improve adjusting 
the air flow in the model simulation. 
By other hand, in Section 7.2, a black-box with a DA-RNN model was tested with the same data, 
obtaining approximately a RMSE of 0.0068. The accuracy of the prediction is incredibly precise even 
if we are aware that DA-RNN can outperform state-of-the-art methods for time series prediction. In 
order to prove its reliability, data from other buildings could be tested. 
In general, black-box model (BBM) seems to perform better than grey-box model (GBM) for MPC and 
FDD, since it has a lower complexity and a higher accuracy. 
The strength of the GB models is their knowledge of the physical building. This can be used to estimate 
the physical parameters of the system, where it cannot be done using our BB model. Also, in case we 
train our models with wrong data, GBM reveals unrealistic estimates unlike our BBM which tried to 
adapt. 
To detect faults on the building system, we did several tests using our best GB model, focusing on the 
indoor temperature. The model successfully detected faults coming from: an exceed in electric power, 
faulty data coming from a ventilation power sensor or an exceed in the heating power. These tests could 
give false positives in scenarios where the accumulated error is neglectable, or it ends matching the

We also created a reversed model, for FDD, in order to detect faults in other part of the system. This

Part of the goal was to use new open-source tools for parameter estimation, in our case ModestPy. It

8.1.1. Performance 
 
For MPC our black-box model with DA-RNN has great accuracy with a RMSE loss of 0.0068, and 
therefore is most likely to be used than our grey-box model with a loss of 0.12. For FDD, there are 
scenarios where our GB model is better since is aware of building physics and not only could detect the 
error, but it could also diagnose the faulty area; also, it could estimate the parameters of the system. 
Several tests were attempted using Dymola as white-box for FDD. We were able to detect several faults 
targeting the indoor temperature and the energy power from the heating floor. Although, errors can be 
hard to detect only visualizing the plots. It could be implemented an automatize a task to calculate the 
mean absolute error that triggers an alert using a threshold. 
For the parameter estimation, ModestPy was used in a Windows virtual machine, therefore the CPU 
execution times were slower than expected. A way to improve its performance would be adjusting the 
options of the genetic algorithm. 
Finally, we found that for our different RC models, higher complexity implies longer computation times 
to estimate parameters in ModestPy.   
 
8.1.2. Integration 
 
The integration among the tools is excellent and the integration process can be automatized and 
distributed using Python as the bridge for it.  
The file generation of the inputs/output for the model and ModestPy are written in Python, what bring 
wide possibilities to be integrated with other tools. 
Dymola models can be converted into standard Functional Mock-up Units (FMU), widely use, what 
ModestPy, for example, use for parameter estimation, model validation and prediction.  
For our BB, everything is written in Python using the library PyTorch for machine learning. Therefore, 
also easily able to be integrated as part of other systems as a tool. 
One important thing to point out is that experimental open-source libraries, like ModestPy, could be 
deprecated and therefore are dangerous to use in the long term for industrial projects. The best solution 
to maintain these projects is creating a community around them or investing time and resources.

8.1.3. Summary 
 
This section outlined how the results presented relate to the goals of this project. 
“Is it possible to build a single-zone model of an apartment's thermal behavior based on easily 
accessible sensor data?” “Would it be accurate enough to detect faults on the building systems?”. 
The answer is yes. As a proof-of-concept, we modelled a 4R4C model for one apartment and it was 
validated against real data obtaining a RMSE of 0.12 for GBM and 0.0068 for BBM. Those models 
successfully detected faults on the building system. 
Therefore, this methodology could be automatized and implemented as part of other systems.  The 
models could be reused due to their reduce complexity and basic sensors. 
On this study, we described the methodology for it and how it can be done, therefore achieving the main 
goal.

The project is build based on the data of a specific case study and lack feedback from other 
different scenarios. 
 
- 
A basic knowledge of the building is required in order to set the boundaries for the GBM.

The behavior of the system observed in the data obtained from 2020 is different from 2018, 
therefore, the results cannot be compared since they are unknow reasons for such different 
behavior.

Chapter 9 
 Conclusion 
 
This thesis presents a data-driven model of an apartment’s thermal behavior based on several 
Resistance-Capacitance (RC) models and basic sensors in order to detect faults in a building system. 
As a proof-of-concept we utilize the sensor data and physical boundaries of a single-story apartment in 
Nordhavn (Copenhagen area). 
The results from the different RC models shows that more complex models have a better performance. 
The best performance is obtained from our last design, a 4R4C model. We developed it in Dymola-
Modelica and tuned using ModestPy for parameter estimation as a grey-box. 
Tools like Dymola and Modestpy reduce cost and time required to create a thermal model of a building. 
Dymola provides agile modelling and ModestPy accurate parameter estimation with genetic algorithms. 
The methodology presented in this thesis, provides researchers and developers, without in-depth 
knowledge of building physics, a guideline to create their own systems. 
For this study, a black-box with a Dual-stage Attention-based Recurrent Neural Network (DA-RNN) 
model was implemented and compared against our 4R4C model as a grey-box. The results proves that 
black-box models outperform grey-box models for prediction. Nevertheless, grey-box models can be 
used to estimate the physical parameters of the system and identify faulty parts. 
Black-box using DA-RNN models are a powerful tool for predictive control systems due to their higher 
predictive accuracy. However, these models do not perform well outside their training data domain. 
Several test for Fault Detection and Diagnosis (FDD) were carried out using our 4R4C model as grey-
box and our black-box model. The tests found faults in the system successfully, proving the accuracy 
of the models. Last, it is demonstrated that we can reverse our 4R4C model for FDD. It allows to 
diagnose errors focusing on different parts of the system. The test for fault detection also succeeded.

Glossary 
 
 
 
Modelica is an object-oriented, declarative, multi-domain modeling language for component-oriented 
modeling of complex systems. 
Dymola is a commercial modeling and simulation environment based on the open Modelica modeling 
language. 
ModestPy is a python tool that facilitates parameter estimation in models compliant with Functional 
Mock-up Interface. 
Python is an interpreted, high-level and general-purpose programming language. 
PyTorch is an open-source machine learning library based on the Torch library. 
 
Machine Learning is the study of computer algorithms that improve automatically through experience. 
NARX Nonlinear Autoregressive Exogenous models, which predict the current value of a time series 
based on historical values of this series plus the historical values of multiple exogenous time 
series. 
DA-RNN belongs to the general class of NARX, is a novel Dual-stage Attention-based Recurrent 
Neural Network for time series prediction, can adaptively select the most relevant input features 
and capture the long-term temporal dependencies of a time series. 
LSTM Long-Short Term Memory networks are a type of recurrent neural network capable of learning 
order dependence in sequence prediction problems. 
 
Grey box model combines a partial theoretical structure with data to complete the model. 
Black box model combines data inputs and outputs where the processes between those are opaque, 
used in Machine Learning. 
White box model implies a fully understanding about how the model behave, produce predictions and 
influence the variables. Therefore, the processes are transparent.
