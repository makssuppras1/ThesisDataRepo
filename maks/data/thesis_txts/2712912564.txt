Abstract
A Flywheel Energy Storage System (FESS) is a storage technology that, differ-
ently from conventional batteries, is based on mechanical inertia. In the pres-
ence of an excess of power, this is used for accelerating a rotating mass em-
ploying an electrical motor. Its kinetic energy can then be converted back into
electrical energy by utilizing the electrical machine as a generator.
The mass keeps rotating at constant speed when no energy is stored or released
from the flywheel. Friction losses are minimized by containing the rotating mass
in a vacuum chamber and by levitating it on magnetic bearings. In this way, an
efficient storage capability is guaranteed.
The low carbon footprint, the elimination of hazardous materials in batteries,
and the possibility of achieving millions of full-depth discharge cycles, make this
technology an attractive green alternative to conventional batteries. The appli-
cations range from electrical transportation, electrical grid storage/services and
others.
A laboratory prototype was previously built in DTU Mechanical Engineering, with
the initial focus being on realizing the control system for the magnetic bearings
such that the rotor was levitating stably inside a non-vacuum chamber. The rotor
was being spun through of a small air turbine.
The air turbine itself does not allow to extract the power that is stored in the ro-
tating mass. Thus, the project aims to couple a motor-generator to the system,
such that the power flow between an electrical source and the FESS itself is con-
trolled, by controlling the speed of the electrical machine. This is possible using
a Variable Frequency Drive (VFD), composed of an electrical power conversion
system and the related control logic for achieving the expected result.
The first step consists in updating the mechanical infrastructure such that the
electrical motor is appropriately coupled properly with the existing system. With
the test rig being updated, the control system needs to be updated as well for
dealing with a system described by different dynamics. Finally, the VFD is in-
troduced and set such that the speed of the rotor is controlled correctly and
electrical power can flow in both directions.

1
Introduction
The widespread use of renewable energy sources leads to the necessity of pro-
viding electrical grids with energy storage systems, to harvest the maximum
amount of energy from them. In addition, getting rid of conventional power plants
leads to stability problems that can be contained by using the storage system as
an energy buffer. On a smaller scale, the electrical systems present in vehicles
also require the presence of a high-capacity energy storage system, as a con-
sequence of the significant electrical penetration in several means of transport.
The interest in developing systems with high energy density is dictated by the
limited amount of space and weight that the vehicle can offer and the necessity
of guaranteeing proper performances in terms of driving range and power.
The state of the art of energy storage is represented by electro-chemical batter-
ies, thanks to their high energy density and the well-known technology. Lithium-
ion batteries are the most commonly used, even if they are limited by their max-
imum power density and bad behavior at low temperatures. According to the
specifications imposed by each applications, several new battery technologies
are under development, such as redox flow batteries, Lithium-air batteries, etc.
An alternative energy storage technology investigated in this thesis is repre-
sented by Flywheel Energy Storage Systems (FESS). In principle, a FESS is
composed of a rotating mass that stores kinetic energy according to the equa-
tion
Ek = 1
2Jω2.
(1.1)
The bigger the inertia (higher mass under rotation) and the faster it spins, the
more energy can be stored inside these devices. The quadratic relationship with
the rotational speed is reflected in the tendency to maximize rotational speed,
by keeping the system compact in size and weight.
High rotational speeds can be reached by magnetically levitating the rotating
mass inside a vacuum chamber. In this fashion, any source of losses is removed.
The limitation in the rotational speed is given by the maximum tensile strength of
the shaft’s material. The development of high-strength fiber composite materials
allowed rotational speeds higher than 100 kRPM, reaching energy densities up
to 80 Wh/l. Li-ion batteries are still more performing under this aspect, reaching
values up to 400 Wh/l, but FESS present some other peculiarities.
1
==PAGE:12==
In terms of specific power instead, the performance of the two technologies is
similar, with values that reach 5000 W/l.[1]
FESS can rapidly change their speed, thanks to the electrical motor that drives
them. Batteries, on the other hand, are limited by the power they can deliver
and reduce their lifetime when high powers are required. Thus, FESS are more
suitable for applications with fast and wide power fluctuations.
Figure 1.1: ESS - Discharge Times.
Finally, the absent carbon footprint, the long life expectation, the absence of
toxic or hazardous materials, and the minimum amount of maintenance required
make this technology a good solution for a green future.
1.1
Motivation
The basis for a FESS has been built in the Mechanical Engineering Department
at DTU. The test rig consisted of a magnetically levitated shaft spun utilizing an
air turbine. The aluminum housing that contains the rotating shaft is fixed to a
gimbal mount, as the original location for which the system was designed was
an off-shore vessel.
2
==PAGE:13==
Figure 1.2: DTU Test Rig.
The project aimed to demonstrate that the designed magnetic bearing system
would have been able to keep the rotor levitating, even when the ship was sail-
ing. The control system’s effectiveness has been tested by simulating the move-
ment of the vessel using a series of pneumatic actuators.
The goal of this thesis is to transform the original concept into an actual FESS
with the addition of an electrical drive train. The energy stored inside the flywheel
was meant to function as an energy buffer and for peak shaving purposes, such
that the size of the combustion engine could be reduced. An adequate motor
and related power electronics are chosen and set up for fulfilling this scope.
Moreover, the test rig needs to be upgraded to ensure a mechanically-stable
motor coupling that guarantees a safe operation at high rotational speeds. The
stability of the renovated shaft is verified by combining experiments with mathe-
matical modeling, culminating in an updated model of the system that could be
utilized for implementing advanced control strategies for levitation.

The rotor is levitated inside the housing by means of a thrust Passive Magnetic
Bearing (PMB) and a couple of Active Magnetic Bearings (AMBs).
5

The first one is responsible for contrasting gravity by leveraging the magnetic
repulsion force between two magnets faced with the same polarity. It comprises
a part attached to the rotor and a similar one laying in the housing. Each of them
hosts two concentric rings of cubic rare-earth magnets, oriented with opposite
polarity. In particular, the inner circle shows the north polarity to the surface
(magnetic field ’exits’ from it), and the outer one shows its south polarity.
Figure 2.2: PMB.
The magnetic field generated by the PMB has a destabilizing effect on the shaft.
Hence, a pair of radial AMBs is needed to ensure a stable operation. The AMB
is composed of a stator core with several electromagnets’ coils wounded on it,
and a rotor. Both of them are made of iron because of its magnetic properties.
The working principle is the following: each of the EMs mounted on the stator
generates a magnetic flux that enters the rotor, attracting it like a magnet can
attract metallic objects. By having two EMs on the opposite sides of the shaft (2
poles) and feeding one of them with a higher current with respect to the other,
the shaft is experiencing a net force that makes it move. A conceptual scheme
of a 2 poles AMB can be seen in Figure 2.3a.
The AMBs installed in the system are characterized by an 8-pole heteropolar
configuration.

Each N-S couple highlighted in Figure 2.3b is supplied in series by the same

Thus, for controlling the movement in both directions in both the AMBs, 4 current
variables are necessary (i for both x and y in each bearing) instead of 8. On the
other hand, the constant presence of the bias current i0 is translated into ohmic
losses.
The magnetic flux produced by each couple of coils is not parallel with the x or
y-axis. As a consequence, the attractive force that is generated has an angle of
22.5◦. This has to be considered when modeling the system for implementing
advanced control techniques.
A couple of eddy-current sensors are installed for each AMB to measure the
shaft’s lateral movement. Two aluminum hubs are coupled to the rotor and facing
the sensors to ensure their correct operation.
The presence of backup bearings guarantees a safe operation at any time. They
are characterized by a clearance of 300µm with respect to the shaft. Conse-
quently, they are not involved in the dynamics of the shaft when the AMBs
are working correctly.
However, in the presence of disturbances or failures,
they intervene by preventing the shaft from damaging the housing. The backup
bearings installed on the system can work at temperatures above 150 degrees,
avoiding heating problems during the touchdown event.

surements of the quantities of interest, generate a control signal and feed the
coils with a physical voltage reflecting the outcome of the control algorithm.
In addition to the information on the position of the shaft obtained from the eddy-
current sensors, the currents supplied in the different coils are also detected,
thanks to 4 hall-effect sensors.

The control algorithm is implemented on a cRIO embedded controller from Na-
tional Instruments. It consists of two loops, as it is highlighted in Figure 2.4.
The inner control loop is responsible for controlling the current in the coils by
employing a PI controller. The reference for the current is set according to an
outer loop responsible for controlling the shaft’s position, keeping it steady in the
center. The original test rig used a PID controller for the scope.
8

Later, a new model-based controller was implemented by J. Skov Villefrance,
with the focus of minimizing the AMB energy losses[4]. One can switch from
one controller to the other or using a mixed signal, according to a controllable
parameter.
The interface used for programming the cRIO, as well as for monitoring the whole
system while in execution, is LabVIEW. In particular, two code files called Virtual
Instruments (VI) are generated. The FPGA VI is the one implementing the actual
control algorithm. It runs on the cRIO microcontroller as in this way, it can keep
low power consumption and minimize the execution time. The Real Time VI,
instead, allows the user to monitor the different parameters and interact with the
system while this one is running.
The main section of the user interface is presented in Figure 2.5. For example,
it is possible to turn the AMBs on and off, change controller type, reset con-
troller’s integrators, set references, record data, etc. This VI runs on the PC,
which can communicate with the cRIO through a LAN cable. For a more de-
tailed explanation of the general functioning of LabVIEW and the cRIO, see [4]
and the cRIO-9039 manual [5].

2.2
Controller Performance
Some experiments have been carried out to assess the performance of the dif-
ferent controllers that are implemented as position controllers. The experiments
were performed for each AMB in both the x and y directions. For simplicity, just
the results for the upper AMB in the x-direction are stated.
The first test involved a step response in the current, with an amplitude of 80
mA, as can be verified in Figure 2.9. The plots track the time behavior of the
current in the two opposing couple of windings in the x-direction: ix+ and ix−, as
described in Equation 2.1. The sum of the two currents was 600 mA, the value
of the bias current.
The response of the system running with the LQG showed a highly oscillatory
behavior, especially in the current. However, it was also faster in the response,
even if it tended to have bigger overshoots.

Electrical Drive Train
The process of transforming the original test rig into an actual FESS, starts by
choosing the proper components that will form the electrical drive train power-
ing it. The main components needed for the scope are the electrical motor-
generator, the power conversion system, and the encoder for controlling the
speed and, therefore, the energy stored in the flywheel.
To ensure that all these elements work correctly as a whole, the electronic sys-
tem in the test rig must to be updated to interface them with the elements that
are already present (AMBs, sensors, etc.).
3.1
Introduction to Electrical Drives
A drive is a system that can generate and control the motion of a mechanical
load using an actuator. An electrical drive (or drive train) is a drive characterized
by an electrical actuator, i.e., an electrical motor. A power conversion system
and a control algorithm are needed for controlling the motion of an electrical
machine. The generic scheme of an electrical drive is stated in Figure 3.1.

Figure 3.1: Electrical Drive Train - Basic scheme.
It is a crucial element for realizing a system able to store energy. In the presence
of an excess of electrical power, for example, when the FESS is coupled with a
wind or solar farm, the electrical machine accelerates the flywheel’s inertia and
converts electrical energy into kinetic energy.
17
==PAGE:28==
The electrical power coming from the source is usually a 3-phase alternate cur-
rent system (AC) with a frequency of 50 Hz. As a first step, the AC current has
to be transformed into a direct current (DC) before being converted back to AC
by employing a second power converter.
Introducing an intermediate DC stage, called DC-bus, is necessary because,
in general, the frequency at which the motor-generator needs to be supplied is
different from the one of the power source. In other words, the DC-bus acts by
decoupling the two AC systems. Usually, it also contains a high-voltage capac-
itor for smoothening the voltage supplied to the inverter.
Therefore, a rectifier is used for carrying out the AC/DC power conversion. In
the most simple case where the electrical machine is used only as a motor, the
rectifier only consists of diodes that are non-controllable components.
The second component in the chain is the inverter, which is responsible for the
DC/AC power conversion, as most electrical motors are supplied in AC. The in-
verter is composed of semiconducting switches that can be controlled to change
the amplitude and the frequency of the voltage that is set to the motor. In this
way, the speed is controlled. In general, the frequency of the electrical signals is
related to the rotational speed of the motor, according to the constructive spec-
ifications of the machine. Thus, the control algorithm is responsible for varying
the frequency of the voltage outputted by the inverter according to the requested
speed. Together with the frequency, the amplitude needs to be varied accord-
ingly, for ensuring an efficient utilization of the machine. The standard control
algorithm is the Flux Vector Control, relying on PID controllers for controlling the
machine’s torque, which is related to the magnetic flux.
The power converters, as well as the control algorithms, are standardized for
standard applications. Hence, there exist commercial products that contain both
the power converters and the control logic for controlling the motor by simply
plugging them into the grid and setting the motor’s parameters. These products
are denominated Variable Frequency Drives (VFD). An example of a commercial
VFD can be seen in Figure 3.2, which is also the product that has been chosen
for the test rig.
The system just described can store energy. However, the rectifier is not a bidi-
rectional power converter. This means that electrical power can only flow from
the electrical grid to the DC-bus. Besides, the electrical machine can be slowed
down when there is a need for energy, converting its kinetic energy back to
electricity, according to Equation 1.1. The inverter is a bidirectional component.
Therefore, the energy produced can reach the DC-bus but not the AC grid.
18
==PAGE:29==
Figure 3.2: Commercial VFD.
The only path possible for the current to flow is by charging the capacitor in the
DC stage. This will raise the DC voltage level until it reaches the limit value of
the capacitor, with possible safety problems. The simplest solution that can be
followed involves using a braking resistor. When the voltage reaches a certain
threshold, a switch is triggered, and a high-power resistor is connected in parallel
to the capacitor to discharge it and keep the voltage at a controlled level. The
solution is not efficient energetically, as the electrical power is lost into heat. It
is used to ensure a safe operation or in applications that are characterized by a
steady state operation of the motor.
For a FESS application and many others, there is the necessity to be able to
extract the energy produced during the generating mode. Thus, the rectifier
needs to be substituted with another inverter. By doing this, the power flow can
be inverted, and the current is able to flow back to the grid. Also in this case,
the inverter needs to be controlled, requiring a control infrastructure to be set
up. The complexity and the cost of the system increase. From a commercial
point of view, a second VFD, adequately designed for the purpose, is installed
by bypassing the rectifier and connecting directly to the DC-bus of the VFD used
for supplying the electrical machine.

3.2
Electrical Machine
Electrical machines utilized for FESS application are usually 3-phase machines
that can be classified into two main categories: asynchronous (or induction) ma-
chines and synchronous machines. The distinction is related to their rotational
speed, with respect to the speed of the rotating magnetic field generated by the
3-phase current with which they are supplied.
Asynchronous machines are characterized by having a rotational speed (n) that
differs from the one of the rotating field (n0), according to a slip factor
s = n0 −n
n0
.
(3.1)
This difference in the velocities is needed for the machine to work properly, allow-
ing the stator to induce an e.m.f. in the rotor. Consequently, the rotor generates
a magnetic field that interacts with the one of the stator, generating torque. In-
duction machines are then self-exciting machines, as there is no need to excite
the rotor with an external source for generating the rotoric magnetic field.
The rotational speed of synchronous electrical machines is instead the same as
the one of the magnetic field generated by the supplied currents. The absence of
the induction phenomenon between the stator and the rotor implies the necessity
of exciting the machine. This can be done trough sliding contacts for wounded
rotor machines, or by replacing the windings with permanent magnets in the so-
called brushless machines. Another type of synchronous machine is the variable
reluctance machine, which works according to a different physical principle.
When coming to FESS application, brushless machines seems to be the best
fit. Induction machines are cheap and rugged, but they are limited in the max-
imum speed that can be reached and require complex control strategies and
high maintenance. Variable reluctance machines have a low power density and
high torque ripples reflected in higher vibration amplitudes. Permanent magnet
motors, instead, are characterized by the highest power density by far and are
only limited in the speed by their tensile strength. The necessity of having a
compact system for obtaining an ESS with high energy density, as described
in chapter 1, is the reason behind the widespread use of permanent magnet
machines in FESS applications [6, 7].
Hence, the motor utilized in the test rig (Figure 3.3) is a permanent magnet
synchronous machine (PMSM), from e+a Motors. In this motor configuration, the
permanent magnets are mounted on the surface of the rotor, below the coating
layer, which prevents the magnets from coming off at high rotational speed.

3.4
Incremental Encoder
An incremental encoder is a device that allows measuring the shaft’s position
and speed. It delivers three different signals: two trains of pulses shifted by 90◦
and a single pulse for each revolution.
23
==PAGE:34==
Figure 3.5: Incremental Encoder Signals.
By counting, starting from the index pulse, the number of rising (or falling) edges
in channel A or B, as described in Figure 3.5, it is possible to know the absolute
position of the shaft with respect to the mechanical position the index signal is
triggered. Instead, the shaft’s velocity is derived by measuring the time interval
between two pulses. The phase shift between channels A and B determines the
direction of rotation.
The most common incremental encoder that can be selected is the optical en-
coder. In the left part of Figure 3.5 it is possible to appreciate the standard
structure of its rotating part. A pair of light source and receiver are mounted on
the two sides of the perforated disc. When the light passes though the hole, a
pulse is generated. When the light encounters the disc solid part gets blocked,
the related output signal becomes low.
The static and rotating parts of an optical encoder are usually part of the same
component. This represents a contact point between the rotating shaft and the
outside world. A magnetic encoder was chosen for the application to guarantee
that the shaft was not touching the housing at any point. The output signals that
are generated are the same, but they are triggered by a magnetic actuator (a
permanent magnet) rotating together with the shaft in proximity to the static part
of the encoder, which is connected to the housing. In Figure 3.6 a scheme of
the purchased item is presented, with the permanent magnet highlighted in red.
The manufacturer is RLS.

The final modification that has been performed on the system involved the bot-

5
Shaft’s Dynamics Analysis
The need to leverage mechanical coupling for creating the new shaft is reflected
in a less solid mechanical structure that can face stability problems when rotating
at high rotational speed. By crossing the first bending natural frequency, the
shaft would deal with large amplitude vibrations that could affect its integrity.
The dynamics of the free rotor has been analyzed to verify the rotational speed
at which this occurs. The aim was to determine the region of speeds where a
safe operation is guaranteed.
In particular, an Experimental Modal Analysis (EMA) has been performed to cal-
culate the natural frequencies of the shaft in a free condition and the related
mode shapes. Later, a Finite Elements Analysis (FEA) was deployed to gener-
ate a mathematical model that fitted with the experimental results. The Finite El-
ement (FE) model can be used to describe how the system’s natural frequencies
are affected by the rotational speed, including the information on the stiffness of
the magnetic bearings. The outcome of the analysis aimed to verify that the
shaft’s dynamics does not limit the speed range allowed by the newly designed
system.
5.1

The manufactured shaft and the setup of the experimental modal analysis are
presented in Figure 5.1. The EMA was performed by using a hammer (1), which
mounted a force transducer on its metallic tip. The system was excited while
being hung, and the responses in the different positions of the shaft (2) were
measured with the help of an accelerometer (3). By hammering the shaft in a
fixed position and moving the accelerometer along the shaft, the natural frequen-
cies and mode shapes could be derived.

the signal sent to a dSpace ds1104 (6) for properly collecting the data. The
amplifier is characterized by a gain and implements both a high-pass filter (HPF)
and a low-pass filter (LPF).

Finite Element Analysis
The FE model of the rotating machine is based on the presence of a flexible
shaft. The model for it has been developed by leveraging Timoshenko beam
theory, with four degrees of freedom per node: two for translation and two for
rotation (Figure 5.6).
Figure 5.6: Timoshenko Element.
Furthermore, several parts have been coupled on top of the main body of the
shaft, i.e., the iron and aluminum hubs, and the PMSM’s rotor. Hence, the full
model was obtained by including a mathematical description of these elements
as disks. The full equation of motion that represents the dynamical behavior of
a flexible rotating system is
M ¨q + [D + ΩG ] ˙q + Kq = Q,
(5.3)
where M is the inertia matrix, D is the damping matrix, G represents the gy-
roscopic effect, K is the stiffness matrix, and Ωrepresents the rotor speed. Q
includes all the forces acting on the system, i.e., the gravitational force and the
unbalance forces. q is the vector for displacements [13].
The shaft has been discretized by using 34 Timoshenko’s beam elements. The
disc elements have been included in the model by increasing the mass of the
elements with which they are coupled. This could be performed by increasing
their diameter while building the associated mass matrix M.

critical speed (CS). At this speed, the presence of an unbalance would generate
a resonant force that is reflected in high amplitude vibrations.
The presence of several discs coupled on the shaft enhances the gyroscopic
effect, i.e., the coupling between the lateral movements in the different direc-

axis tends to reduce as the energy is transferred to the other, which tends to
increase. The high number of mechanical coupling suggested running the ma-

damaging the shaft. The analysis of the shaft in a free-free condition led to a
limit of around 34 kRPM, corresponding to the first natural frequency of 581.33
Hz. The resulting value is higher than the 30 kRPM limit that the encoder is
imposing.

Performances
The scope of the project was to build a working FESS, with a magnetically levi-
tated shaft being spun by the PMSM that has been installed. The modifications
in the mechanical structure affected the performance of the control system that
was previously set1. Thus, the control system needed to be tuned once again

operation needed to be verified, as it is influenced by the final alignment utilized
and the way the VFD has been set.
6.1
Controllers Tuning
The control algorithm implements three different controllers. The PI controller for
the current should not be affected by the modifications that have been performed
in the system, as the current dynamics is only affected by the way the AMBs are
designed. This is not true for the position controllers, which are affected by the
dynamics of the shaft. The focus on having a working system as an outcome
of the thesis project and the limited time available for completing it led to the
decision to abandon the LQG controller for focusing on implementing a PID able
to replicate the dynamical performances of the benchmark shown in section 2.2.
As a starting point, the system has been levitated with the old PID parameters.
This has been possible thanks to the design choice of altering the system as
little as possible by inserting the motor in the middle instead of putting it at the
top. Consequently, the system maintained a good axial symmetry, and the PID
only needed to deal with the increased inertia that characterizes the new shaft.
The experiments that have been chosen for assessing the performance of the
different PID controllers were the same in section 2.2. The results for both the
step response and the backup recovery are stated in Figure 6.1. The compar-
ison with Figure 2.9 and Figure 2.10 displayed the poor dynamic response of
the old controller when applied to the new system. The position response was
characterized by a significant overshoot and a slow integral action when the
reference was set back to 0.

6.2
Shaft Spinning
The updated PID controller allowed to stably levitate the shaft. The vertical align-
ment ensured the proper positioning of the rotor inside the stator of the electrical
machine and the correct spacing between the encoder and the magnetic actua-
tor. Thus, with the VFD being set, as described in subsection 4.2.2, it has been
possible to spin the motor to verify its correct operation.
The best performing control strategy for controlling a PMSM is the flux vector
control. The closed-loop version, with the feedback from the encoder, is the
most suitable for a reliable control action over time. On the other hand, to ensure
that the encoder was working as expected, the shaft had to be spun, and the
measurement signals were verified to be outputted correctly. The VFD offers
the possibility of driving the motor with a VVC+ algorithm, an open-loop control
strategy that does not require any controller tuning to be set. However, this
control strategy is not efficient, absorbing very high currents even for spinning the
motor at a relatively low speed. Thus, it has only been used to verify the correct
operation of the encoder to drive the motor with the optimal control strategy.
The motor was initially put into rotation by slowly increasing the speed to test the
correct functioning of the control algorithm. The speed reference was increased
by steps of 100 RPM, and a speed of around 12.5 kRPM was reached before
the shaft touched a backup bearing. Thus, the speed reference was reduced,
but a stronger touchdown occurred, and the drive disconnected the motor. The
alarm was triggered when the velocity measured by the encoder differed from
the expected one, defined by the frequency of the currents flowing in the motor.

7
Conclusion
A functioning FESS has been successfully built during the project, starting from
the original test rig that was already provided with the magnetic system for lev-
itating the first version of the shaft responsible for the energy storage. This
has been possible by correctly installing an electrical drive train on the system,
including an electrical machine, a variable frequency drive and a magnetic en-
coder. The components have been chosen and ordered by direct contact with
the different companies. The integration on the system was possible thanks to
a mechanical update, which allowed to couple the motor and the encoder with
the original system, and an electronic update for interfacing the VFD with the
electronic system that was already present, such that it was able to drive the
electrical machine correctly. The mechanical stability of the system has been
studied through experiments and finite element modeling.
The new system showed some satisfying performances in terms of levitation,
with a new set of parameters being used for setting the related PID controller.
The performance as a FESS could not be fully assessed because of the limited
time that was available with the system running. However, the shaft was able to
rotate with relatively fast acceleration, in the momentary limited range of speed,
and brake with the consequent generation of power. The system could be op-
erated and monitored on the same platform that implements the control system
for the AMBs, i.e., LabVIEW.
The main outcome of the project was the realization of a working system repre-
senting a solid base for performing theoretical analysis and experiments, from
which it is possible to both improve the performance as a FESS and to deeply
investigate the dynamical behavior of a system with a high degree of complexity.
7.1
Future Aspects
From a mechanical point of view, there is room for improvements and several
studies to be carried out. The first area of interest is the shaft. The glued cou-
pling did not work as a solution for the manufacturing mistake and broke. A new
coupling needed to be performed, involving a new mechanical part. The time
remaining at that point of the project was limited (less than a couple of weeks).
Thus, it was impossible to perform a new modal analysis for the shaft, nor bal-
ance it, before the system was mounted back for performing the final tests.
64
==PAGE:75==
The shaft balancing, especially, is essential for reducing the amplitude of vibra-
tions during rotation. Hence, it would certainly improve the system’s stability for
rotating at higher speeds.
From a theoretical point of view, the new dynamics of the system can be studied
to deliver a new mathematical model for describing the dynamics of the shaft.
The model could be tuned by fitting the outcome of the simulations with the ex-
perimental data. The presence of the motor in the middle increased the complex-
ity by having to deal with radial forces that are difficult to be modeled, especially
in the case where the parameters are unknown.
With a reliable model being set, it would be possible to implement higher-level
controllers. Linear model-based controllers (LQG) can be the first step. It could
be interesting to compare its performance by including a model for the radial
forces with respect to a more straightforward controller that deals with them
as a disturbance. The strong non-linearity given by the PMB opens the door
for leveraging non-linear control and estimation theory for implementing high-
performance controllers. Parameter estimation could also be applied to derive
the electrical machine’s missing parameters.

B.1
VFD Troubleshooting
While the AMBs turned out to be very stable during the testing phase of the sys-
tem, the VFD was characterized by being more problematic during its operation
and setup.
The first issue involves the off-powering operation, by means of the related three-
phase switch. Sometimes it happens that the full electrical system of the lab-
oratory is shut down by shutting down the drive. Please be aware of this phe-
nomenon if other people are performing experiments simultaneously. A possible
solution could be installing an isolating transformer between the drive and the
grid to prevent the residual current circuit breaker (RCC) from being triggered.
The second concern involves the startup of the motor. When the drive is turned
on after inactivity, the closed-loop Flux control does not always work correctly.
When a velocity step is required, the system starts turning in the opposite direc-
tion with an uncontrolled speed (around 3 or 4 kRPM for a step of 200 RPM),
and an alarm is triggered. The cause of this phenomenon is still unknown. The
operations that have been used for finding the origin of the problems are:
• Check if the encoder is correctly outputting the pulsed 24V signals with an
oscilloscope
• Run the system with VVC+ at 100/200 RPM by selecting 4 for the param-
eter ’Set-up Operation’
• Check that there are no loose connection in the drive
• Perform an AMA (Parameter 1-29)
• Switch the system on and off
