Abstract
Fretting has historically been a problem in large diesel engines. It is a result of the
large cyclic forces in the combustion chamber, which makes it nearly impossible to
avoid relative movement between contacting components. MAN Diesel & Turbo has
through experience been able to prevent fretting between the piston crown and rod.
However recently, the design criteria has been unable to predict fretting wear in new
engine designs. This thesis will investigate the fretting phenomenon and hopefully
improve the current design criteria.
The three main methods used to investigate fretting are, literature study, nite
element analysis and an experimental test rig. The mindset of Robust Design is
used throughout the thesis to create a new design for the experimental fretting rig
and to minimize the eect of variation and ensure reproducible results.
The results of the nite element analysis and experiments show a close correla-
tion.
The fretting theory, based on the literature study, also closely match the
experimental results. A new design criteria, based on the frictional energy released
during the relative movement, shows promising results in predicting fretting wear.
The fretting rig is capable of producing the three types of fretting and the thesis
denes the boundary conditions for each type. This allows MAN Diesel & Turbo to
continue the thorough investigation of fretting.
Further testing using the fully operational fretting rig and the nite element anal-
ysis will allow MAN Diesel & Turbo an even better understanding of fretting. The
fretting rig can in the future be used by other researchers to test new materials and
boundary conditions for other components.
The objectives of creating a robust experimental test rig and nite element analysis
was achieved during a period of 6 months. Hopefully the new design criteria and
coecient of friction are gradually implemented as more tests are conducted using
the fretting test rig.

Introduction
Fretting was rst reported in a scientic paper in 1911 by Eden and Rose [1]. It
has caused mechanical failure for a century and engineers have still not fully un-
derstood the problem. Fretting is a complicated damaging phenomenon. It takes
place between two metal surfaces subjected to a normal load and a small oscillat-
ing relative movement due to cyclic loading. The consequence of fretting may be
a drastic reduction in component lifetime due to accelerated wear or unpredicted
cracking. When designing products, one must therefore take great care to avoid this
phenomenon.
Unfortunately fretting is highly complicated due to its dependency on a variety
of dierent parameters such as; surface shear stress, contact geometry, material
properties, normal force, temperature, relative movement size and many others.
Calculating and predicting fretting is therefore a dicult task.
At the time of writing, a common way of predicting fretting is by experimentally
creating fretting maps for each material of interest. This method was rst pub-
lished in 1988 by Olof Vingsbo and Staan Söderberg [2] and have since then been
improved by Z. Zhou to account for additional experimental parameters [3]. This
method however is expensive and requires huge amount of testing to cover all used
materials and load conditions.
Within the last few years the approach for solving the fretting problem has shifted
towards trying to formulate mathematical equations that can identify fretting fa-
tigue and wear. C. Ruiz and D. Nowell invented the initial analytical equations for
fretting fatigue in dovetail joints [4]. Further improvements have been developed by
Vidner, J. to accomedate for other geometries [5]. S. Fouvry [6] describes an energy
wear approach to quantify fretting wear for a Hertzian contact.
The methods developed for fretting wear and fatigue have proven successful when
used on simple designs such as a dovetail joint or a Hertzian contact. However,
nowadays methods are not being used commonly in the industry as they are not
very applicably in a everyday design phase, and requires a high amount of fretting
knowledge. This thesis will try to close the gap between available academic research
methods and usable industry tools.
A large diesel engine contains numerous clamped surfaces.
This combined with
the cyclic combustion creates ideal conditions for fretting. Engine designers try to
avoid this phenomenon, however due to insucient design criteria and prediction
tools it still occurs. Recently, fretting has occurred in the interface between the
piston crown and rod, see red circles in gure 1.

Currently MAN Diesel & Turbo (MDT) has dened a simple design criteria to avoid
fretting between clamped interfaces. It is based on the absolute relative movement
between the connecting parts, ±5µm. The design criteria is based on numerical
experience and has not been veried experimentally. It has recently been shown
to be insucient as fretting has occurred in new engine designs even though they
fullled the current design criteria. In other cases the design criteria increases engine
cost due to excessive use of material, expansive coating, requiring non-necessary high
tightening of bolts or tight tolerances. The temporary and costly solution to avoid
fretting wear in the piston assembly is by adding an anti-fretting disc. The disc is
placed in-between the piston crown and rod, which can be seen in the above gure.

Aim
The aim of this thesis is to increase the knowledge of fretting and improve the
current fretting design criteria used by MAN Diesel & Turbo. The main focus of the
investigation is on fretting wear; how does the wear occur, what are the eecting
parameters and can fretting wear be predicted eectively by numerical simulations?
Fretting fatigue and corrosion is secondary goals. Improved knowledge of fretting
will allow for a more robust engine design which will increase performance and reduce
time between overhauls of large 2-stroke diesel engines, see gure 2.
This thesis will investigate the critical interface parameters between the piston crown
and rod, and hereby try to improve the current design criteria for fretting. Hopefully
resulting in an industry applicable method capable of predicting fretting in advanced
designs, not restricted to engine design only. This will allow for an optimized design
which can reduce rst-cost, operational cost and increase competitiveness.
The investigation is based on the piston assembly as the current design criteria is
insucient in predicting fretting wear during engine operation.

Design of the study
To investigate the fretting phenomenon in the piston assembly three main methods
was used and compared for accuracy: An initial literature study, an experimental
test rig and numerical simulation.
An initial literature study on fretting theory was performed.
This provided the
fundamental knowledge about fretting and how other researches have approached
to subject. Afterwards a thorough study of the current in-house fretting reports
(≈400) was performed to gather as much information about the MDT's fretting
history. The in-house investigation proved to be inconclusive as each author has
a dierent view on the severity of fretting and frequently did not dier between
fretting fatigue, wear or corrosion. Tolerance analysis on engines with fretting also
proved unsuccessful as no-one at the engine manufactures had the complete overview
between built engines and the corresponding part tolerances.
Secondly 3D numerical simulations of the piston assembly was performed. The FEA
was created with the standard MAN Diesel FEA method (same contact conditions,
coecient of friction, loads, etc.). The critical outcome parameters are the relative
slip and contact conditions between piston crown and rod. These will act as the
baseline results and used as functional requirements for the test rig.
Thirdly, a test rig was constructed to imitate the contact and slip conditions as in
the piston assembly. The test rig is based on the "bridge" design by Fenner, A.J.
& Field, J.E [7]. However, due to the high contact pressure needed to imitate the
piston conditions changes were made. The changes were made before manufacturing
and based on the early stage FEA.
Physical experimental tests are performed and compared with the FEA of the test
rig. Similarities and disparities are investigated to improve the current FEA. The
test rig provides critical information to create the new and improved design criteria.

Fretting theory
Fretting has three sub-divisions; fretting wear, fretting fatigue and fretting corrosion
[2]. It is important to dier between the three types when discussing fretting, as the
resulting damage of each type varies. Fretting wear is considered the most moderate
type of fretting, causing gradual surface degradation. Fretting fatigue is worse as
micro cracks can evolve and cause rapid failure. Fretting corrosion can occur in
combination with the other types of fretting, which causes accelerated surface wear
or valley formation which can increase stress concentrations, see gure 3.
Figure 3: Illustration of the three fretting types
12.1
Fretting wear
Fretting wear describes the material removal between two surfaces in contact. The
normal load forces the surface asperities into contact. The cyclic relative movement
causes surface rubbing (abrasion), elastic and plastic deformations of the asperities.
This mechanical rubbing of the surfaces causes particles to break of. Due to the
high normal force and low relative displacement the particles are captured between
the surfaces, forcing them to accelerate the wear (3-part abrasion) [9]. For ferrous
materials the particles can deformation harden and/or oxidise becoming harder than
the surface from which they came.
Fretting wear in the current piston assembly may lead to loss of pre-tension in the
bolts. This can cause catastrophic engine damage if the piston crown becomes lose
in the cylinder. Figure 4 shows an example of fretting wear in the piston assembly.
Notice the changes in surface texture and color. Another costly eect of fretting wear
is when the ship crews inspect the piston conditions. If fretting wear is observed
(just the slightest and might not be critical) MDT is bound to assist and pay for
the reconditioning.

12.2
Fretting fatigue
Fretting fatigue describes the occurrence of surface cracks in mechanical components
subjected to a small relative movement and a stress well below the known pure
fatigue limit [10]. The crack nucleation is expected to be a result of the shifting
surface shear stress due to the relative movement. It is the tension in the surface
which opens the initial crack. The normal load can afterwards propagate the crack
downwards through the material. Fretting fatigue is considered the most critical
form of fretting as the component lifetime can be drastically reduced.

Fretting corrosion
Fretting corrosion happens when the ferrous particles comes into contact with an
oxygen lled environment, especially with moisture present. The fretting wear causes
a break down of the protective oxide layer. The virgin metal surface is exposed to the
"open" environment and a chemical attack can form. The heat generated during the
a slip will also speed up the process if the contact temperature increases. Corrosion
will make the surface material more brittle and larger chips can break o [11]. If the
oxidation debris cannot escape, it will lead to an increase in wear rate. It can also
lead to a formation of small local notch/valley (micro-pitting). If the local notch
reaches critical depth a crack can initiate. Figure 7 shows an anti-fretting disc where

MAN Diesel & Turbo fretting history
Fretting has historically been a related problem when designing diesel engines. The
engines contain several clamped surfaces and the cyclic combustion creates ideal
conditions for fretting. Fretting is especially a problem for the larger engines. The
reasoning being that larger engines consists of larger components and have a higher
thermal loading, which causes larger deformations. The larger deformations causes
larger ambiguities in the contact zone if the design lacks robustness to correctly to
compensate for this deformation. Figure 16 shows the thermal load in a small bore
engine S30 (Ø300 mm bore) and a large bore engine G95 (Ø950 mm bore). It can
be seen how the thermal loading is highest on the outer perimeter of the piston
crown, due to fuel injection angle and reduced cooling of this area. This causes
a non-uniform deformation of the piston crown. This kind of deformation causes
ambiguities in the contact zone between crown and rod, which directly aects the
contact area and pressure and therefore the slip.

Experimental investigation
The experimental test rig is created to investigate fretting, evaluate the accuracy of
the current numerical simulations and create fretting maps.
The test rig is designed to provide the following functional requirements:
 Achieve the three slip regimes
 Variable contact pressure
 Variable axial loading; magnitude and frequency
 Strain measurements
 Easy and cheap changeable components

The test rig consists of a servo hydraulic machine (SHM), a specially designed xture
and specimens. The servo hydraulic machine was provided by MAN Diesel & Turbo
and all tests were conducted at their Headquarters in Copenhagen. The xture and
specimen was designed and manufactured doing this thesis. It consists of two pads,
two rings and four screws. The specimen is xed in the center by the applied torque

The xture and specimen are placed in the claws of the servo hydraulic machine
which applies the axial specimen loading, see gure 46. The SHM applies sinusoid
tensile stress to the specimen from F0 to Fmax. Fmax is a variable parameter repre-

Partial conclusion
The numerical and experimental results show that increased contact pressure will
reduce the slip size. This can reduce fretting wear or completely remove it. How-
ever, it can also change the slip regime from gross-slip to mixed-slip and produce
fretting fatigue instead, which is worse. Increasing the axial loading will increase
the slip size. This will result in more fretting wear or worse fretting fatigue. The
experiments show larger cracks when the axial loading is increased. It can also cause
a change in slip regime, chancing the surface damage from fretting wear to fretting
fatigue. Generally the experimental fretting results have demonstrated surface dam-
age in good correlation to the stated theory. One thing to notice however, is that
the experiments have shown that the mixed-slip regime not only produces fretting
fatigue cracks but also some fretting wear.

Discussion
The purpose of the study was to investigate fretting wear in large diesel engines
and quantify whether or not the current design criteria is viable. To do this, the
phenomenon must rst be understood in its basic form, where complicated design
factors are removed. This was achieved by constructing an improved fretting test
rig, capable of producing similar contact pressures as in the diesel engine for the
studied contact pair. The fretting rig was designed with an iterative process, using
parametric control and simulated performance before the physical parts where pro-
duced. This allowed for an optimized structure and less sources of error.
The experimental results mapped out the forces required to achieve the three dier-
ent slip regimes and related surface damage for the piston material. The achieved
fretting wear, fatigue and corrosion damage is visually very similar to other re-
searchers results [23].
The experimental results showed how the current design
criteria of 10 µm is insucient in predicting fretting wear and fatigue as both fret-
ting wear and fatigue occurred at lower slip amplitudes. A fretting design criteria
can not be based merely on the slip size, as the damage is a result of the slip size
combined with the contact pressure.
The coecient of friction used in MDTs standard FEA should be changed as friction
experiments indicate a three times higher value. The comparison of experimental
and numerical results, with the new coecient of friction, conrmed that the friction-
and contact model is fairly precise in calculating the slip amplitude.
However, FEA should be improved by including static and kinetic friction coef-
cients, which allows for a gradual transition between static and kinetic friction
during the slip to more accurately calculate the slip, especially at higher contact
pressures where the FEA seems to underestimate the slip size.
The contact model is also limited at high contact pressures, where adhesive be-
haviour is not included. It will be very dicult/impossible to include this using
normal FEA.
An investigating of other contact elements could also be carried out, as only C3D8R
and C3D8 was tested (MDT standard elements for contact). Abaqus creates new
elements from time to time, and a new type might be more accurate.
The current FE-model is structurally limited by only including the pads and speci-
men, not the rings and screws. These components could be included to investigate
their eect, however during this thesis the added eect was considered not worth
the extra complexity and calculation time.
The sensitivity analysis created with FEA proved an ecient tool. It showed that
the most inuential of the four parameters was the external loading, which the ex-
perimental results also demonstrated. Surprisingly the coecient of friction only
contributed with ≈19% on the slip and ≈7% on the energy, which makes the new
design criteria less sensitive to friction variation compared to the slip value, mak-
ing the new design criteria more robust. This would be interesting to investigate
experimentally, especially when comparing the inuence of dierent materials. The
sensitivity analysis was limited to include four parameters. Additional parameters
could be added such as thermal loading, contact size or contact geometry.

Conclusion
The study has shown that it is possible to estimate fretting wear by calculating the
frictional energy based on the numerical results of a FEA. The results have been
veried by experiments which is the key to the success.
Moreover, this study has also shown that the current design criteria at MDT is
insucient and cannot accurately be used to predict fretting wear. The size of a
slip must be evaluated in combination with the contact pressure. It has also shown
that the coecient of friction used in MDTs numerical calculations is set too low
and should be increased to the experimental value. The experimental friction tests
also showed that the anti-fretting disc has the same coecient of friction. Indicating
that the benecial property of the anti-fretting disc is its ability to divide the slip
between components and not related to any material properties.
The expensive
coating and hardening of the disc could therefore be removed to reduce cost.
The objectives of the thesis was achieved and used to conrm the three stated
hypotheses.
Meaning, fretting wear can be determined by the frictional energy
dissipated, it will not occur under a specic value and the severity will increase with
increased energy.
A fully functional fretting test rig was developed and operated during this thesis,
and with it, a parameter based FEA. Choosing to model in full 3D has provided a
much deeper insight of how the surface slip occurs and is recommended for further
studies. 2D models will not provide information about how the middle of the surface
behaves dierently than the edges. A sensitivity analysis based on FEA results has
also proven a useful tool. It can indicate the importance of the included factors and
is a very cheap method compared to doing physical experiments. The friction energy
tool has shown promising results in cooperation with the experimental results, and
can over time be matured into the new design criteria. The friction energy tool
and fretting map would benet from more experimental results to verify the slip
sizes statistically and increase the operational area. The created fretting map as
is, will however already now indicate the boundary conditions of the three slip
regimes.
The energy calculation has shown promising results for approximating
fretting wear, however more investigation is needed to fully use it for fretting fatigue.
The theory used for the frictional energy tool is not limited to the contact in the
piston. However, due to the material dependency of fretting new experimental tests
should be performed for other materials.
The recommendations for MDT is to incorporate the frictional energy tool when
designing new piston components. Energy plot values above 0.0003J
0.1mm2 is a warning of
a problematic design surface (material specic). The fretting map can be used to
indicate which fretting type might occur once the design has proven problematic,
especially for contact pressures of 150 MPa and above. If possible MDT should
perform further testing using the fretting rig to explore the boundaries between the
slip regimes and the unknown "green" area of the fretting map.
In the following section, the knowledge from the investigation is compressed into
design guidelines to avoid fretting. The examples will be used on the piston assembly.

Design changes to avoid fretting
The following suggestions are design changes which can be used to avoid/minimize
fretting wear and fatigue between piston crown and rod in a S30ME-B9 engine. The
changes are rated from most viable (1) to least possible (7).
1. Move the crown leg outwards 17.575 mm to ensure equal force on each side.
Currently the outside area is 1.9 times larger than the inner. This will remove
the bending of the leg which causes the relative movement, see Design Change
1 (DC1) in gure 71.
2. Reduce the height of the crown leg (DC2), as this will reduce the slip ampli-
tude. The height from the top of the piston crown to the ring slots can not
be reduced as this protects the rings from heat. Therefore it is suggested to
make the rod contact the crown higher up inside the crown.
3. Increase the number of bolts to equalise the contact pressure (DC3), as there
currently is a large contact pressure dierence between the bolts.
4. Increase stiness of the leg (DC4), leading to a reduction in slip amplitude.
Downside is more material use and less cooling. Low mass is especially pre-
ferred in the end of the piston.
5. Reduce the rod stiness in the contact (DC5). This could allow for a small
elastic bending which would reduce the slip.
6. Increase contact pressure (DC6), which will reduce the slip. However avoid
changing from fretting wear to fatigue!
7. Reduce external loading (DC7). Very unlikely as high combustion pressure
equals high eciency and lower fuel consumption. However, between other
components this might be possible.

Future work
Investigating fretting has led to new knowledge and tools to avoid fretting. However,
understanding fretting fully has still not been accomplished and requires further
thorough investigation. The following presents ideas for future research:
 Include a more advanced friction model in the FEA, which will account for
the change from static to kinetic friction during the slip.
 Broader study of the inuence of other contact elements.
 Create a add-on tool for the FEA which can implement a random surface
height variation where asperities would be accounted for. The surface hight
variation should match a given machining process. Hereby including the much
higher contact pressures at the asperities instead of the lower contact pressure
on a at surface.
 Include a wear model based on the frictional energy.
 Investigate how sharp/gradual the transition is between the slip regimes or
whether duel zones exist.
 Investigate how fast the coecient of friction change during the cyclic loading
depending on load, material and frequency. Will a equilibrium be reached or
will the coecient keep changing?
 What is the inuence of the material hardness. Will harder materials reduce
fretting wear and fatigue?
 Create a fretting fatigue S-N curve and compare with a normal S-N curve to
estimate life-time reduction.
 Create a more robust contact geometry which can handle a larger thermal
variation.
 Teach the travelling MDT workers more about the types of fretting. An equal
understanding would create better engine reports and allow the design depart-
ments to respond faster.

Introduction
Since the invention of the electrical resistance strain gage 
more than a half century ago, the Wheatstone bridge has 
become the sensing circuit of choice in most commercially 
available strain gage instrumentation. This is due in large 
measure to its inherent ability to:
1)	 detect the small resistance changes produced in the 
strain gage as it follows even minute dimensional 
changes on the surface of a test part under load,
2)	 produce a zero output voltage when the test part is at 
rest, and
3)	 provide for compensation of temperature-induced 
resistance changes in the strain gage circuit.
To varying degrees, each of these factors is essential for 
accurate strain gage measurements.
In the majority of strain gage applications for the 
determination of the state of stress on a test-part surface, 
individual strain gage elements, whether from uniaxial 
or rosette strain gage configurations, are connected 
independently to the Wheatstone bridge in a quarter-
bridge arrangement. As discussed in the following sections, 
the wiring scheme chosen to connect the strain gage to the 
bridge circuit has a significant effect on the accuracy of 
measured strain data.
The Wheatstone Bridge
The Wheatstone bridge circuit in its simplest form (Figure 
1) consists of four resistive elements, or bridge arms (R1, 
R2, R3, R4), connected in a series-parallel arrangement, 
with an excitation voltage source (E). The connection 
points formed by (adjacent) pairs of bridge arms and the 
leadwires from the excitation voltage source are input 
corners of the bridge; and those formed by pairs of bridge 
arms and the signal (eo) measurement leads are output 
corners. It is worth noting for this discussion that each 
input corner is adjacent to each output corner, and each 
bridge arm is connected between two adjacent corners.
Also, if the bridge circuit is resistively symmetrical about 
an imaginary line drawn through both output corners, 
the output voltage eo will be exactly zero, regardless of the 
excitation voltage level, and the bridge will be “balanced”. 
Two-Wire Circuit
For an initially balanced bridge, if one of the resistors 
in Figure 1 is replaced with a strain gage of precisely the 
same resistance value and connected with two leadwires 
having negligible resistance, the bridge circuit remains 
at balance. But in practice the leadwires will have some 
measurable resistance RL as shown in Figure 2 on the next 
page. And because both leadwires are in series with the 
strain gage between adjacent corners of the bridge circuit, 
the bridge arm resistance becomes RG+RL1+RL2, causing a 
significant lack of symmetry and an unbalanced condition 
in the bridge,  resulting in a non-zero output voltage at eo. 
If the initial imbalance is modest, it may be mathematically 
subtracted from subsequent measured strain readings; but 
large imbalances may cause a more serious problem. As 
an example, a 20-ft (6 m) length of two-conductor AWG26 
(0.4 mm dia.) copper leadwire has a room-temperature 
resistance value of about 1.7 ohms. Wired in a two-wire 
connection with a 120-ohm strain gage, and connected to a 
measuring instrument with a gage factor setting of 2.0, this 
would produce an initial bridge imbalance corresponding 
to about 7000 με. This offset may significantly limit the 
available measurement range of the instrumentation, and 
also should be considered when correcting for Wheatstone 
bridge nonlinearity (see Tech Note TN-507).

Further, the leadwires are a parasitic resistance in the gage 
arm of the bridge, and effectively reduce or desensitize 
the gage factor of the strain gage, resulting in a reduced 
signal output. For modest values of leadwire resistance, 
the desensitization is approximately equal to the ratio 
of leadwire resistance to strain gage resistance. In the 
example given here, this results in about a 1.5% loss in 
sensitivity.
And finally, a more serious problem may result if 
the temperature of the leadwires changes during the 
measurement process. Copper leadwires change in 
resistance approximately 22% of their room-temperature 
resistance value for a 100°F (55°C) temperature change. 
For the 120-ohm gage circuit in Figure 2, this would result 
in an error equivalent to approximately 156 microstrain, 
or about 4700 psi (0.03 GPa) in steel, for a 10°F (5.5°C)
temperature change in the leadwire system.
All three of these effects increase in severity with increased 
leadwire resistance. It is worth noting that use of a 350-ohm 
strain gage circuit will reduce each of these effects, but 
cannot eliminate completely the associated measurement 
errors. But the three-wire circuit described in the following 
section will reduce the loss in sensitivity, and essentially 
eliminate the initial imbalance problem and the error that 
results from temperature changes in the leadwire system.

the adjacent arm. For an equal-arm bridge, if leadwires 
RL1 and RL2 are initially the same type and length, their 
resistances will be equal, and the two respective bridge 
arms will therefore be equal in resistance. The bridge is 
resistively symmetrical about a line through the bridge 
output corners, and the bridge is balanced. And regardless

instrument, is recommended to minimize the total length 
of the two-wire system.
The foregoing discussion applies primarily to measurement 
of static strains using a measuring instrument with 
dc-coupling between the bridge circuit and the amplifier 
input terminals. For measurement of purely dynamic 
strains when only the peak-to-peak amplitude of a time-
varying signal is of interest, the two-wire system may 
sometimes be used effectively by selecting a signal-
conditioning amplifier that provides for ac-coupling of the 
input signal, and blocks the effects of temperature-induced 
changes in leadwire resistance.
In summary, benefits of the three-wire circuit include 
intrinsic bridge balance, automatic compensation for the 
effects of leadwire temperature changes on bridge balance, 
and increased measurement sensitivity compared to the 
two-wire configuration. The three-wire hookup is the 
recommended configuration for quarter-bridge strain gage 
circuits for static strain measurement. The two-wire circuit 
can sometimes be used effectively for special situations 
such as dynamic-only measurements with ac-coupled 
instrumentation, or in static strain applications where the 
length of the two-wire system can be kept very short.
