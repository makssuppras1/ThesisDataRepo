Abstract 
Efficient and reproducible workflows for characterization of product variants is essential to ensure 
safety and efficacy of biopharmaceutical antibodies. Mass spectrometry (MS) has become an essential 
characterization tool in biopharmaceutical development, however, current MS methods, including 
peptide mapping, typically involve multiple manual, labor-intensive, and time-consuming steps. To 
combat this, the implementation of automated workflows is of great interest. In this study, the aim is 
to integrate automated liquid chromatography (LC) fractionation with automated digestion work-
flows, followed by analysis by nanoflow LC-MS/MS of digested antibodies. Here, automated cation-
exchange chromatography (CIEX) and size exclusion chromatography (SEC) fractionation was eval-
uated and found to be ideally combined with automated digestion using enzyme immobilized on 
magnetic beads. This workflow significantly reduces the hands-on work required for analysis, while 
simultaneously providing a robust, semi-automated workflow for product variants separated by SEC 
or CIEX. An automated workflow involving reversed-phase chromatography (RPC) fractionation has 
proved to be more challenging due to the requirement of buffer exchange to achieve optimal protease 
conditions. To address this, automated solid phase extraction (SPE) utilizing magnetic SPE beads 
followed by sample digestion was evaluated. It was concluded that automated SPE combined with 
subsequent in-solution digestion using free enzymes is a highly efficient and robust workflow. Case 
studies are presented for each of the established workflows, revealing increased levels of oxidation 
at multiple residues in aggregated monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) from the SEC fractionation work-
flow. Using the established CIEX workflow, it was shown that deamidation at Asn92 on the light 
chain is responsible for acidic peaks in the CIEX chromatogram. By applying the established RPC 
workflow, it was clearly demonstrated that under-conjugation of an antibody-drug conjugate (ADC) 
was caused by missing linker-payload conjugation at a specific site on the heavy chain. Furthermore, 
scrambling in the hinge region was evident using the RPC workflow and shown to correlate directly 
with the reduction process used for the naked mAbs prior to linker-payload conjugation. The results 
presented demonstrate the characterization power of the established workflows as well as the poten-
tial for development and optimization of additional workflows.

1 Introduction 
The first monoclonal antibody (mAb) was approved for pharmaceutical treatment almost 40 years 
ago, and the antibody industry has only expanded since1. As of June 2022, a total of 162 antibody 
therapies had been approved globally, with cancer treatments representing the biggest group, account-
ing for approximately 10% of all approved antibodies. Antibodies are of particular interest in the 
development of novel drugs due to their high specificity and binding affinity for antigens. This pro-
vides a highly targeted drug, which can potentially decrease adverse effects due to off-target interac-
tions. Furthermore, antibodies generally have a long half-life, providing long-term efficacy2. 
 
In more recent years, antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs) have emerged from mAbs. ADCs are com-
posed of a mAb linked to a small molecule drug with a linker. At present, ADCs are mainly used for 
cancer treatment or management as an alternative to chemotherapy, which frequently harms healthy 
tissue due to its poor specificity towards cancerous tissue3. 
 
To ensure safety and efficacy of biopharmaceutical drugs, mAbs and ADCs are characterized in order 
to confirm the identity and detect potential changes as well as to assess stability. Over time, various 
methods have been employed, many very time-consuming. As mass spectrometry (MS) has become 
increasingly more prevalent, it has proven to deliver high accuracy and fast results. Consequently, 
peptide mapping by MS is now widely used throughout the pharmaceutical industry for antibody 
characterization3. 
1.1 Antibodies 
Antibodies are part of the immune response and are triggered by the presence of antigens, which are 
foreign macromolecules such as proteins and carbohydrates. Antibodies are found on the surface of 
B cells4, from which they can recognize and bind to a specific site on the antigen called the epitope. 
An antigen can have multiple epitopes, each recognized by a different antibody5. 
Antibodies are also known as immunoglobulins (Ig). Human antibodies can be divided into five 
groups of immunoglobulins, IgA, IgD, IgE, IgG, and IgM, which mainly differ in glycosylation lev-
els, length of the heavy chain, and their ability to form multimers. IgGs are by far the most abundant 
antibody in human serum and can further be divided into four subclasses: IgG1, IgG2, IgG3, and 
IgG4 (see Figure 1). The main difference between the four subclasses is found in the length and 
flexibility of the hinge region6, while the functionality of the four subclasses also differs significantly. 
IgG1 is the most naturally abundant IgG. It primarily responds to soluble protein antigens and mem-
brane proteins, while IgG2 mainly responds to carbohydrate antigens. IgG3 also has an affinity for 
proteins, however, its half-life is only about seven days, compared to 21 days for the other three IgG 
subclasses, and its pharmaceutical use is therefore limited. IgG4 is commonly related to allergens7.

IgGs consist of four polypeptide chains, including two identical heavy chains (HC) and two identical 
light chains (LC), forming a “Y” shape (see Figure 2). The stem of the Y is referred to as the fragment 
crystallizable (Fc) region, which interacts with effector proteins6, while the two top fragments are 
antigen binding (Fab) fragments. The Fab and Fc regions are connected by a flexible region called 
the hinge region. The heavy chains consist of a variable (VH) and a constant domain (CH1) in the 
Fab region and two constant domains (CH2 and CH3) in the Fc region. The light chain consists of 
one variable (VL) and one constant (CL) domain in the Fab region. The variable region on both light 
and heavy chains each contain three complementarity determining regions (CDRs), which are hyper-
variable regions that together form the antigen recognition site9. Despite all IgGs exhibiting the same 
overall “Y” structure, the amino acid sequence can vary significantly. Differences are mainly found 
in the hypervariable region, which allows for the recognition of different antigens4. For all IgGs, the 
heavy and light chains are linked by one disulfide bond, while the heavy chains are linked through 
the hinge region by a varying number of disulfide bonds dependent on the IgG subclass9 (see Figure 
1). The heavy chains have a mass of approximately 50 kDa each, while the light chains are ~ 25 kDa 
each10.

Monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) are antibodies originating from a single clone of B cells, which pro-
duces chemically homogeneous antibodies. These antibodies recognize the exact same epitope on the 
antigen. Traditionally, mAbs for biopharmaceutical use were produced by hybridomas, which are B 
cells extracted from immunized mammals fused with immortal myeloma cells11. However, recombi-
nant antibody production by Chinese hamster ovary cells is currently the preferred method as it is 
highly suitable for large-scale production, cultured in suspension in serum-free media, resulting in 
high yields with little variation in product quality. Furthermore, the use recombinant antibodies pro-
vides high versatility through bioengineering for optimal growth and antibody production12. 
1.1.1 Antibody-drug conjugates 
ADCs represent a class of novel biopharmaceutical drugs that are currently developed for treating 
cancer.  ADCs are highly targeted drugs consisting of a monoclonal antibody, a linker, and a cytotoxic 
drug, which is commonly referred to as the payload13 (see Figure 3). 
 
Figure 3 Overview of the structure of an IgG1 ADC with four linker-payloads. Created in https://BioRender.com. 
ADCs are a fairly new approach for targeted drug delivery to tumor cells, with the first ADC approved 
by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) in 2000. As of 2024, only 13 ADCs had been 
approved, while more than 100 ADCs are in clinical trials13. 
The main advantage of ADCs as drugs lies in the combination of high specificity due to the mono-
clonal antibody and cytotoxicity from the small molecule drug linked to the mAb. The mAb allows 
for targeting an antigen that is highly expressed on the surface of the cancer cell without harming 
healthy tissue14. This also allows for a higher dose of the cytotoxic drug than what would otherwise 
be administered as it only targets cancerous tissue with minimal systemic exposure15. 
When synthesizing ADCs, the drug-to-antibody ratio (DAR) is very important as this can significantly 
affect the potency of the drug. A low DAR can result in reduced cytotoxicity against the cancer cells, 
while an increased drug loading may affect the pharmacokinetics of the drug as well as toxicity16. 
 
Conjugation of linker-payload to the antibody can be achieved through various methods. Among the 
most commonly used approaches is conjugation to a naturally occurring cysteine residue on the anti-
body. This is performed after reduction of interchain disulfide bonds, where the thiol group on cyste-
ine can be conjugated to a linker, usually to maleimide17. Similarly, lysine residues can function as 
the site of conjugation for activated esters (e.g. O-succinimide)18. Conjugation to naturally occurring 
lysines or cysteines result in heterogeneous ADCs due to non-site-specific conjugation, which may

negatively impact the functionality of the ADC. A way to address heterogeneity is to utilize engi-
neered or non-natural amino acids, resulting in site-specific conjugation. In engineered ADCs, func-
tional groups are introduced at specific sites on the antibody and can subsequently react with the 
linker. Engineered cysteines are commonly used for conjugation with maleimide linkers, while non-
natural amino acids allow for the introduction of a wide range of side chain functional groups that 
can be conjugated to a linker. Currently, a broad range of conjugation techniques are utilized in the 
development of ADCs as each has its own advantages and disadvantages, however, methods leading 
to homogeneous ADCs are generally preferred17. 
1.1.2 Post-translational modifications 
Post-translational modifications (PTMs) of antibodies can potentially alter the stability, specificity, 
and activity of the antibody. Characterization of PTMs is therefore crucial in the development of 
novel antibody drugs19. PTMs are modifications to amino acid side chains that occur during or after 
biosynthesis of the protein and can be either reversible or irreversible. These modifications can sig-
nificantly impact the structure and function of the antibody, e.g. by affecting the protein folding or 
antigen recognition and interaction20. Commonly observed PTMs include N-linked glycosylation, 
oxidation, deamidation, and cysteinylation19. 
 
N-linked glycosylation occurs mainly on asparagine in the CH2 domain of the Fc region. For anti-
bodies in this project, glycosylation is observed at position 303, however, the exact position can 
change slightly depending on the antibody21. Glycosylation involves a diverse mixture of saccharides, 
such as galactose, fucose, sialic acid, and mannose, which leads to a high degree of heterogeneity 
among glycoforms22. Different glycosylation patterns may result in differences in the quaternary 
structure of the Fc region, possibly affecting the effector function, stability9, efficacy, and immuno-
genicity of the antibody. It is therefore crucial to be able to identify glycoforms in order to predict 
and explain unusual antibody behavior22. 
 
Among the amino acid residues, tryptophan and methionine are the most susceptible to oxidation20. 
Oxidation of tryptophan is irreversible23, while methionine oxidation is reversible24. Oxidation in-
creases the hydrophilicity of the antibody, which can significantly impact multiple aspects of the 
antibody’s function and structure25. Oxidation on both methionine and tryptophan has been shown to 
increase aggregation, while also decreasing thermal stability. Aggregation may lead to immunogen-
icity and reduced potency, which give rise to considerable concern if seen in antibodies20. 
 
Deamidation occurs primarily on asparagine and to a much lower extent on glutamine 21. The con-
version of an amide into carboxylic acid can potentially affect the conformation and activity of the 
antibody due to the introduction of negative charges. It has been reported that deamidation in CDRs 
can result in a decrease in antigen binding affinity, while deamidation in the constant domains has not 
shown significant impact20. 
 
Free cysteines on the antibody can react with free cysteines in the cell culture media, resulting in 
cysteinylated residues. Cysteinylation may lead to structural changes, which can decrease thermal

stability and activity, while increasing aggregation20. For ADCs, cysteinylation can interfere with the 
conjugation of mAbs and linker-payloads if not properly removed during reduction, since cysteinyl-
ation functions as a capping mechanism of the cysteines intended for conjugation. This may result in 
under-conjugation of the ADC, which can affect potency26. 
1.2 LC-MS/MS 
In mass spectrometric analysis of intact proteins, PTMs that alter the total mass can be identified, but 
the location of these cannot be determined. To address this issue, peptide mapping by liquid chroma-
tography tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) has been introduced. This enables fast, efficient, 
and reliable characterization of proteins27. Characterization of biological drugs by peptide mapping 
is widely used in the pharmaceutical industry. This includes applications for proof of identity, detec-
tion and relative quantification of PTMs. For peptide mapping, sample preparation consists of protein 
digestion but may also include steps such as purification and buffer exchange. Peptides are then sep-
arated by LC and analyzed by28 MS/MS. 
1.2.1 Sample preparation 
Sample preparation for LC-MS/MS analysis of proteins involves digestion of the sample into its con-
stituent peptides. Trypsin is a commonly used protease for protein digestion as it is highly specific 
(see Section 1.2.1.1). However, other enzymes are occasionally used. This includes lysyl endopepti-
dase (LysC), which has a higher proteolytic activity than trypsin but forms larger peptides (see Section 
1.2.1.2). When choosing a protease for protein digestion, many things should be considered, including 
specificity, protease stability, cleavage site(s), and the size of peptides that are formed29. 
 
Protein digestion has traditionally been labor-intense, requiring many time-consuming steps. As a 
result, the use of automated digestion workflows with magnetic solid-phase extraction (SPE) has 
gained popularity since the recent introduction of the technology. The use of magnetic SPE allows 
for fast, unbiased sample preparation, requiring minimal manual labor. Magnetic SPE relies on mag-
netic as well as chemical interactions to prepare a sample. A magnetic material with a reactive surface 
(e.g. carboxylate) binds nonselectively to proteins. After binding, the beads along with protein can be 
extracted, or the supernatant can be removed, while retaining the magnetic material with an external 
magnet. This technique can be integrated into protein digestion workflows, where the use of SPE 
facilitate buffer exchange or removal of contaminants, while retaining high protein recovery30. 
1.2.1.1 Trypsin 
Trypsin is a serine protease produced in the pancreas31, where it is synthesized in the inactive form, 
trypsinogen, to prevent premature digestion. By cleavage of an N-terminal peptide of trypsinogen, 
trypsin is activated. This occurs in the intestine either by trypsin that has already been activated or by 
enterokinase. Serine proteases are enzymes with a serine at the active site that cleaves peptide bonds32. 
Trypsin is highly specific and hydrolyses peptide bonds C-terminal to the amino acid residues argi-
nine and lysine. However, it will not cleave these sites if they are followed by a proline33. Due to its 
high specificity, trypsin can act as both a digestive enzyme by breaking down proteins and as a regu-
latory enzyme by activating other proteins by specific cleavage31. 
==PAGE:13==
 
6 
Trypsin is commonly used for protein digestion for MS/MS analysis of peptides due to its high spec-
ificity and proteolytic activity. Furthermore, trypsin forms peptides with an average length of 6-30 
amino acid residues, which is highly suitable for mass spectrometric analysis34. The primary draw-
back of using trypsin for protein digestion is its inherent ability for autolysis. During the digestion 
process, trypsin autolysis causes a decline in trypsin activity, and the resulting tryptic peptides can 
interfere with the mass spectrometric analysis. To address this, chemically modified trypsin is typi-
cally used for protein digestion. Nevertheless, some tryptic autolysis is generally still observed34. 
1.2.1.2 LysC 
Lysyl endopeptidase (LysC) is a protease originally found in the bacterium Lysobacter Enzymo-
genes35. LysC hydrolyses peptide bonds at the carboxylic side of lysine residues and can, in contrast 
to trypsin, also cleave bonds C-terminal to a lysine followed by a proline. Due to the specificity of 
only one amino acid, protein digestion by LysC can result in rather large peptides, however, LysC 
still has some notable advantages over trypsin. The maximum specific activity of LysC is about an 
order of magnitude higher than that of trypsin36, and it exhibits a higher stability under strong dena-
turing conditions37. Additionally, LysC does not autolyze. For these reasons, LysC is occasionally 
employed prior to trypsin digestion under harsh solubilizing conditions38. A combination of LysC and 
trypsin has also shown great results by increasing the number of identified peptides and reducing the 
number of missed cleavages29. Nonetheless, trypsin remains the most used protease for peptide map-
ping due to the ease of use and the formation of peptides of optimal length, suitable for mass spectro-
metric analysis 34. 
1.2.2 Liquid chromatography 
LC is a method used for separation of molecules based on properties such as charge, size, and polarity. 
Ultra-High Performance Liquid Chromatography (UHPLC) is an area of LC, where high pressure is 
applied (usually >400 bar), which allows for a significantly lower flow rate and sample load, while 
simultaneously increasing resolution and sensitivity. This is highly advantageous as in addition to an 
increase in sensitivity a lower flow rate leads to reduced solvent consumption, thereby minimizing 
the financial and environmental impact. Additionally, a lower sample load enables analysis of samples 
only available in low quantities, while also preserving sample for other characterization methods. 
Furthermore, the use of high pressure to increase the velocity of the mobile phase reduces the sepa-
ration time greatly. Overall, the UHPLC system has demonstrated substantial improvements over the 
original LC system, making it the preferred choice throughout the pharmaceutical industry for char-
acterization of products39. 
 
The UHPLC system consists of solvents, a solvent delivery system, including pump and mixing 
chamber, a sample injection valve along with an autosampler, a column and column oven, a detector, 
and a data system (see Figure 4). Components are connected with tubing with diameters in the mi-
crometer range. The column particle size for UHPLC is significantly reduced compared to regular 
HPLC, which leads to a more uniform flow through the column and a larger surface area, allowing 
for better retention to the column and higher resolution. The particle size for UHPLC is typically 
below 2 µm, while for HPLC particle sizes in the order of 3-5 µm are most common. Various detectors

7 
can be integrated with the UHPLC system depending on the property to be examined. This includes 
detectors such as a UV detector or mass spectrometer, which can also be connected in sequence. Data 
is typically collected on a computer connected to the UHPLC system and the detector39. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
A number of different chromatographic separation techniques can be utilized with the UHPLC system 
by simply changing the column and solvents. These techniques include, but are not limited to, re-
versed-phase chromatography (RPC), size exclusion chromatography (SEC), and cation-exchange 
chromatography (CIEX)39. 
 
Molecules are separated by polarity in RPC, where the stationary phase is non-polar, while the mobile 
phase is polar. Gradient elution is applied, and the mobile phase becomes less polar and more similar 
to the stationary phase in polarity. This allows for the most polar molecules to elute first, while the 
less polar molecules are better retained on the column and elute later. The stationary phase typically 
consists of porous silica-gel particles to which hydrocarbons are bounds. Depending on the sample 
to be examined, hydrocarbons of different lengths, such as C4 and C18, can be used. Typically, a 
more non-polar compound will interact stronger with a longer hydrocarbon chain39. 
 
SEC is a non-interactive chromatography technique, where molecules are separated according to their 
hydrodynamic radius. Since separation is solely dependent on size, an isocratic gradient is applied. 
The stationary phase consists of small pores that allow small molecules to penetrate, while larger 
molecules are unable to enter the pores and will move through the column with no interaction with 
the stationary phase. This results in a longer flow path for small molecules, leading to a longer reten-
tion time. Consequently, larger molecules elute first due to a short flow path39. 
 
In CIEX, separation is based upon interactions between analyte ions and charged sites on the station-
ary phase. CIEX columns contain negatively charged sites, which interact with sample cations39. An-
alyte ions can be eluted by increasing either the pH or the ionic strength of the mobile phase. Sample 
ions and solvent ions are continuously competing for interactions with the anionic stationary phase. 
As the ionic strength of the mobile phase increases, solvent ions will preferentially bind to the sta-
tionary phase, and sample will elute40. As the pH increases, analyte ions are gradually deprotonated,

result, ions with a lower isoelectric point elute first, while more basic analyte ions with a higher

1.2.3 Mass spectrometry 
MS is used through every step of the drug development process and is crucial for the development of 
new pharmaceutical products. It is important to be able to confirm the structure and stability of an 
antibody during development, to monitor drug metabolism in clinical trials, and to examine the qual-
ity of pharmaceutical products before distribution to patients. This can be accomplished through the 
powerful analytical technique of mass spectrometry, which grants access to valuable information on 
the analyzed sample41. MS provides information on the mass of ions by measuring the mass-to-charge 
ratio (m/z). Exceptional mass accuracy is provided by the use of an ultra-high-resolution mass spec-
trometer, such as the Orbitrap mass spectrometer. This enables the distinction of masses in the ppm 
range of the m/z compared to low-resolution mass spectrometers, which only provides unit resolu-
tion39. 
 
In general terms, a mass spectrometer consists of an ion source, a mass analyzer, and a detector. Due 
to the low pressure required to run the instrument, a pumping system is connected to the MS as well 
as a computer to register and process data. An ion source is needed to create gas-phase ions from the 
liquid sample. Two general ionization methods are usually used for MS analysis, soft ionization and 
hard ionization. Hard ionization results in fragmentation of analyte ions leaving smaller ions of low 
charge, whereas when using soft ionization, lower-energy ionization techniques are applied, which 
results in minimal fragmentation. Soft ionization techniques, such as electrospray ionization (ESI), 
are commonly used for protein analysis as it leaves the proteins or peptides intact, which allows for 
identification and characterization of PTMs42. 
When employing ESI, liquid sample is sprayed from the tip of the source needle, where a high voltage 
is applied leading to the formation of charged analyte droplets (see Figure 5). Solvent starts evapo-
rating from the charged droplets until the coulombic repulsion forces of the charges inside the droplet 
overcomes the surface tension. This leads to the ejection of smaller charged droplets, which go 
through the process repeatedly until all solvent is evaporated, and gas-phase analyte ions are formed 
and enter the mass spectrometer43.

In addition to the clear benefit of employing soft ionization for molecules that should remain intact 
for MS analysis, other advantages include the formation of multiply charged ions and the ability to 
directly interface ESI with LC separation techniques. This can bring molecules of high mass, such as 
proteins, within the mass-to-charge range of the mass analyzer, enabling their analysis43. 
 
The Thermo ScientificTM OrbitrapTM Ascend TribridTM mass spectrometer used in this project consists 
of multiple parts that enhance the resolution and increase the ability to confidently analyze proteins 
(see Figure 6). The transfer tube in combination with the ion funnel directs the ion flow into the MS 
and towards the active ion beam guide. The active ion beam guide reduces noise by preventing neutral 
ions from entering the quadrupole, which works as a mass filter by isolating precursor ions of desired 
m/z. From the quadrupole, ions are led into the front ion-routing multipole (IRM), where higher-
energy collisional dissociation (HCD) fragmentation takes place, and ions are captured before being 
ejected into the C-trap. The C-trap traps and focuses ions before ejecting them into the Orbitrap, 
which consists of two outer electrodes and one inner electrode. Ions enter the Orbitrap, and an electric 
field causes the ions to oscillate around the central electrode. Ions of different m/z create currents of 
different frequencies, and by Fourier transformation a computer can decompose the m/z values from 
the currents. From the front IRM, ions can also be transferred into the back IRM, which works simi-
larly to the front IRM, or to the linear ion trap, where collision-induced dissociation (CID), electron-
transfer dissociation (ETD), and electron-transfer/higher-energy collisional dissociation (EThcD) are 
performed, and ions are detected45. 
Precursor ions can be fragmented by a multitude of different techniques, including ETD, HCD, and 
EThcD, which are all especially useful for fragmentation of peptides. Each of these fragmentation 
techniques results in a set of complimentary ions that can be used to determine the exact position of 
PTMs on the peptide46. 
 
EThcD is a hydrid fragmentation technique that integrates ETD and HCD. Precursor ions are initially 
fragmented by ETD, and subsequently, HCD is applied primarily to the unreacted and charge-reduced 
precursor ions47. This results in a mixture of b/y and c/z ions. The application of EThcD is limited to 
Figure 6 Overview of Thermo ScientificTM OrbitrapTM Ascend TribridTM mass spectrometer. Figure from instrument applica-
tion sheet, Thermo Fisher Scientific45. 
==PAGE:17==
 
10 
multiply charged ions and is therefore well-suited for fragmentation of peptides with two or more 
charges46. 
 
ETD is initiated by the generation of radical anions in the ion source. These radical anions are 
transferred to the linear ion trap along with the precursor ions, where they function as a source of 
low-energy electrons to be transferred to the positively charged peptide ions48. The transfer of an 
electron causes neutralization of a charge site and radicalization of the peptide, which induces 
fragmentation at the N-Cα bond of the peptide backbone. ETD is a rather gentle fragmentation 
technique that primarily forms c and z ions, which is advantageous for PTM analysis of peptides as 
it leaves labile amino acid side chains intact49. 
 
HCD is gaining increasing recognition in the study of peptides as an alternative to CID due to its 
ability to fragment precursor ions in the full mass range. When using CID, fragment ions less than 
one-third of the precursor ion m/z are lost, which is problematic for low-mass fragments50. 
As opposed to ETD, HCD is generally applied to precursor ions of low charge (+1, +2, +3), and HCD 
takes place in the ion routing multipole as opposed to in the ion trap. Precursor ions are excited by a 
voltage, increasing their kinetic energy, resulting in an acceleration of the precursor ions. The high-
energy precursor ions then collide with neutral collision gas molecules, which leads to fragmentation. 
HCD results in the amide bond of the peptide backbone breaking, forming b and y ions51. 
 
Advantages of HCD over ETD include that HCD is a rather fast fragmentation technique and can be 
applied to singly charged precursor ions51. Conversely, ETD is better suited for multiply charged 
precursor ions and as a milder fragmentation method better at retaining PTMs in peptides46.

2.2 LC fractionation 
For all RPC analyses, solvent A consisted of H2O and 0.1% DFA, while solvent B comprised of 95% 
ACN, 5% H2O, and 0.1% DFA. Gradients and solvents used for LC have previously been optimized 
at Symphogen and are used as the standard gradients and solvents52. 
 
RPC fractionation was performed using a 35-minute gradient with a flow of 300 µL/min at 55°C or 
80°C. The gradient was initiated at 30% B with a linear gradient to 45% B at 26 minutes. This was 
followed by a linear gradient to 95% B after 29 min, an isocratic hold for 1 minute, and decreased to 
30% B for 5 minutes for re-equilibration of the system (see Appendix 7.1.1 for details on gradient). 
 
For CIEX fractionation, solvent A comprised of 25 mM ammonium acetate (pH 5.4), while solvent 
B consisted of 10 mM ammonium hydroxide (pH 10.7). Fractionation was performed with a 30-
minute gradient with a flow of 200 µL/min at 25°C. The gradient increased linearly from 15% B to 
30% B over 20 minutes. This was followed by re-equilibration of the column consisting of an increase 
to 100% B over 1 minute, a 2-minute hold, decrease to 0% B over 1 minute with a 6-minute hold (see 
Appendix 7.1.2). 
 
SEC fractionation was performed with solvent A consisting of 86 mM ammonium acetate (pH 5.0). 
An isocratic gradient was used with a flow rate of 300 µL/min and 0% B at 25°C (see Appendix 
7.1.3).

2.3.1 KingFisher methods 
The KingFisher Duo system works by moving magnetic material (in the form of beads) from one well 
of a deep-well plate to another. The magnetic beads interact with protein and allow for extraction and 
transfer of protein from one lane to another. The automation of workflows results in efficient and 
consistent extraction with minimal hands-on time53. Furthermore, protease immobilized on magnetic 
beads can be applied for automated protein digestion, where protease is removed after digestion. In 
this project, the main use of the KingFisher Duo system is to extract protein from sample to allow for 
buffer exchange and facilitate digestion.

For the KingFisher Duo sample preparation workflows, a 96 deep-well plate was prepared with sam-
ple, buffer, protease, magnetic beads, water, TCEP, and GuHCl according to the protocols found in 
Appendix and explained below. The first digestion workflow (SMART trypsin digestion, Section 
2.3.1.1) is an already-established method30, while the second and third workflows were adapted for 
RPC fractionation and developed during this project, respectively. 
 
After digestion, 2 % v/v DFA was added to the GuHCl lane, content of this lane was moved to the 
digestion lane and mixed at 1350 rpm for 20 seconds on a thermomixer. The deep-well plate was 
centrifuged at 1000x G for 5 minutes to settle any remaining beads at the bottom, 100 µL was ex-
tracted from the digestion lane into a 96-well microtiter plate, which was centrifuged at 1000x G for 
1 minute in order to remove any air trapped in the wells.

Trypsin attached to magnetic beads, SMART trypsin, is used for digestion of protein. Double diges-
tion is applied with SMART trypsin from different lanes for each digestion in order to ensure dena-
turation of sample at high temperature followed by digestion at lower temperature52. Digestion con-
sists of 15 minutes at 75°C followed by 30 minutes at 40 °C (see Figure 7). This workflow does not 
contain SPE beads as it has been designed for workflows where buffer exchange is not needed. For 
full KingFisher Duo program, see Appendix 7.2.1. (see additional details in Kristensen et al.30).

LysC is utilized for digestion, and SPE beads are used for solid phase extraction of protein from the 
sample lane (Figure 8). The use of SPE beads facilitates buffer exchange to a buffer appropriate for 
digestion. In this method, protein is digested at 37°C for one hour. The full KingFisher Dup program 
can be seen in Appendix 7.2.2.

2.5 Data processing 
Data processing was performed in Byosphere Virtual Client by Protein Metrics. Workflows include a 
precursor mass tolerance of 3.00-10.00 ppm, antibody sequence, the modifications that are searched 
for, and information regarding the protease used including cleavage site(s), cleavage side, specificity, 
and number of missed cleavages allowed. 
 
For intact mass analysis, the mass of each peak was deconvoluted. The deconvoluted masses were 
compared to the suggested modifications from peptide mapping in order to determine the identity of 
each peak. 
 
When processing a sample, the modifications suggested by Byosphere were studied to determine if 
there was enough evidence to verify the presence of the modifications. This was done by first evalu-
ating the XIC plots and extracting the MS spectra at the apex of the XIC peaks. The MS spectra were 
used to confirm the identity of the XIC peaks by observing an appropriate charge envelope for the 
modified peptide in question. To further confirm the presence of the modified peptide, the MS/MS 
plot was evaluated in order to determine if multiple b, c, y, and z ions that included the modified 
amino acid were present. The presence of multiple fragment ions including both C and N-terminal 
ions is a clear indication of a reliable assignment. 
When all modifications had been correctly assigned, the XIC peaks were automatically integrated 
within a chosen time interval in order to determine the ratio between modified and wildtype peptide. 
If there were missing peptides for samples for any modification, in-silico peptides were added via 
existing peptides. This results in XIC plots for all samples independent of the XIC signal intensity, 
whereby the ratio of modified peptide can be determined for all fractions.

3.2 Denaturing fractionation by RPC 
A new workflow, SPE SMART trypsin digestion, was developed in an effort to create an easy and 
efficient method for sample digestion that would be beneficial for experiments where fractions are in 
solvents not compatible with other digestion workflows, such as RPC fractions. Hughes et al.54 
demonstrated lowered performance and increased variability in protein recovery for acidic samples 
and recommends using samples of neutral pH for tryptic digestion54. Due to the use of DFA in RPC 
fraction, neutralization of fractions is therefore required. Moreover, the presence of organic solvent 
in solvent B used for RPC inhibits the functionality of trypsin55, which further supports the im-
portance of buffer exchange. EtOH is added to ensure a sufficiently high ratio of organic solvent in 
the sample, which is required for optimal SPE, while TRIS is added to neutralize pH54. Additionally, 
the application of trypsin for digestion results in smaller peptides compared to LysC, which leads to 
easier and more accurate data analysis. 
 
The SPE SMART trypsin digestion workflow showed reduced signal intensity compared to that of 
antibody prepared by LysC SPE digestion (see Appendix 7.3.2). Furthermore, it was found that the 
HC/LC ratio, which is typically 2:1, was reversed in favor of LCs for the new workflow (see Appen-
dix 7.3.3). The LC signal intensity was significantly higher than that of the HCs. To ensure that a 
human error had not occurred and impacted the results, the workflow was repeated. However, this 
resulted in the same shift in HC/LC ratio (data not shown). A reason for this change may be that it 
more difficult for the HCs to release from the SPE beads compared to the LCs due to the larger size 
and therefore possibly interacting more with the beads. This results in a larger proportion of HCs that 
remain attached to the SPE beads, and as trypsin is also attached to beads, trypsin may be sterically 
hindered from proper digestion of the HCs. 
It was hypothesized that some heavy chains remained attached to the SPE beads after the first diges-
tion, and as all beads are removed from the digestion lane to prepare for the second digestion, a 
proportion of the heavy chains would therefore also be removed. This could possibly result in the 
shift in HC/LC ratio. The beads were originally removed after each digestion in order to reduce tryp-
sin autolysis, which could impact the results, especially for dilute samples. It was therefore decided 
to slightly alter the workflow so that all beads remained in the digestion lane throughout both diges-
tions to test the hypothesis. However, this showed the same results with a more intense LC signal 
compared to the HC signal (data not shown). Due to several issues with the new workflow, it was 
decided to discontinue any further work on it. 
 
In an effort to improve the solid-phase extraction, five new SPE beads were evaluated. Initially, the 
beads were evaluated based on the bead-to-sample ratio recommended by the manufacturer in the 
SPE SMART trypsin workflow. All beads provided full protein coverage and high signal intensity 
(see Appendix 7.3.4). However, it was found that the LC signal intensity was similar to that of HCs 
for all beads. This is consistent with the previous discovery of a shifted HC/LC intensity ratio for the 
SPE SMART trypsin digestion workflow. Furthermore, no clear correlation was observed between 
the amount of bead (50, 100, or 150% of the recommended amount) and the signal intensity for any 
bead type. Therefore, it was decided to increase the bead-to-sample ratio. This was also done partially

fractionation and sample preparation workflow had a positive impact on the results. However, the

reaching the upper pressure limit, the temperature of the column compartment of the Neo UHPLC 
was increased to 45°C. Additionally, the emitter became clogged and was changed during the run. 
Despite the issues, the TIC profiles were good. 
Due to both the emitter clogging and the pressure issues, it was theorized that the ADCs with their 
long, nonpolar linker-payloads stick to the reversed-phase column and are not fully eluted during the 
current gradient. Thereby, the column becomes partially clogged, which leads to an increase in pres-
sure. To manage this issue, it was decided to filter the fractions after digestion to evaluate if it was 
possible to filter out the residue that sticks to the column. When using the Agilent 0.22 µm PES 96-
well microplate filter, it was observed that no peptides eluted after approximately 45 minutes (see 
Appendix 7.3.15). The peptides that typically elute after this point in the gradient are the conjugated 
peptides. This suggests that the conjugated peptides were not able to pass through the filter and were 
therefore not present in the sample for MS/MS analysis. As we are focused mainly on the engineered 
cysteines of the antibody, the conjugated peptides are a necessity for the pepmap analysis. Therefore, 
another filter was examined. For fractions filtered on the Thermo Scientific SMART DIGEST FIL-

end of the gradient for all but the most concentrated fractions (see Appendix 7.3.16). This indicates 
that a contaminant is collected from the filter that can be seen on the TIC. At higher sample loads, 
which is used for the more dilute samples, the intensity increases, which is consistent with a contam-
inant collected from the filter. The m/z of the contaminant is consistent with that of PEG56, which is 
presumably derived from the filter. 
The use of both filters managed to retain the pressure at a somewhat stable level, though as both filters 
resulted in other issues, the implementation of filters in the preparation of fractions was halted.

4 Conclusion 
The SMART trypsin digestion workflow was found to be efficient for CIEX and SEC fractionation 
and has been established as the standard workflow for these LC fractionation methods. Case studies 
utilizing this workflow revealed that deamidation at position Asn92 on the light chain is the main 
factor in separation by CIEX, whereas aggregated forms separated by SEC were found to be enriched 
in oxidation at multiple residues for this particular reference sample. 
 
In an effort to improve peptide mapping of RPC fractioned samples, multiple experiments were con-
ducted to optimize a new workflow for these fractions, including the evaluation of dilution series, 
heating of samples, and various SPE beads. Ultimately, a workflow incorporating digestion by LysC 
protease combined with solid-phase extraction and buffer exchange of RPC fractions yielded the most 
favorable results. Consequently, this workflow has been selected as the standard sample preparation 
method for RPC fractions, including RPC fractionation of ADC samples. 
 
A mixture of LysC and trypsin was utilized for the LysC SPE digestion workflow, and this proved 
effective for the digestion of RPC fractions of ADCs. Case studies of two ADCs, ADC-1 and ADC-2 
revealed that fractions 3 and 4 of ADC-1 derived from two DAR3 forms originating from missing 
conjugation at the engineered cysteine residues, CysX and CysY, respectively. For fractions 1 and 2, 
DAR2 was correlated with under-conjugation at the CysX positions on both heavy chains. For ADC-
2, the presence of a half molecule in fraction 5 was attributed to an intrachain disulfide bond in the 
hinge region. Evidence indicated that the shoulder of the main peak originated from an isoform caused 
by disulfide scrambling in the hinge region. This scrambling was shown to correlate with the choice 
of reduction process for the naked mAb prior to conjugation. Further studies will be conducted to 
investigate the correlation between the reduction process and disulfide scrambling in the hinge region.

5 Future perspectives 
It would be of great interest to examine the SPE SMART trypsin digestion workflow further to fully 
understand the cause and effect of the shifted HC/LC ratio. This could be studied by changing param-
eters of the workflow one by one as this has not been possible in this project due to time constraints. 
Additionally, more research is needed to determine if the shifted HC/LC ratio has an impact on pep-
map results. Further evaluation of the workflow with various proteolytic magnetic resins could prove 
beneficial to ascertain if other proteases behave similarly to SMART trypsin, or if the expected 
HC/LC ratio is seen. 
 
In this project, it was shown that a combination of LysC and trypsin provided good results for diges-
tion of RPC fractioned samples. However, this mixture resulted in the formation of some large pep-
tides, which makes it more challenging to determine the exact position of PTMs. To mitigate this, 
evaluating other enzymes, either individually or in combination with trypsin or LysC, is suggested. 
By combining trypsin with an enzyme that hydrolyzes peptide bonds at other amino acids residues, 
smaller peptides may be formed, allowing for more accurate PTM identification. 
 
The shoulder of the main peak seen for ADC-2 will be evaluated in order to confirm the exact source 
of what appears to be an isoform of the main peak. Multiple versions of this ADC, each reduced under 
different conditions, are available at Symphogen. By examining each of these by peptide mapping, 
the cause and identity of the shoulder will likely be revealed. Additionally, further examination is 
required to optimize the reduction process for future development projects in order prevent the iso-
form from forming. 
 
Towards the end of this project, newly developed reversed-phase C18 μPAC™ Neo Trapping Col-
umns were received. It is of great interest to examine the potential outcomes of the application of 
these for pepmap workflows. Currently, pressure issues are encountered when analyzing ADCs, and 
the new trapping columns might resolve this if they can effectively trap what has destabilized the 
pressure over the separation column. However, the implementation of new trapping columns may 
require new instrument methods, loading settings, or other changes to the system, which need to be 
thoroughly studied in order to achieve the best results.
