Summary 
Companies play a key role in achieving global sustainability given the tremendous amount of resources 
that they possess. It has been shown however that this role depends on the country and regional 
approaches to corporate sustainability have been characterized. Despite the high performance of 
Scandinavia regarding sustainability, little research has been done to characterize the Scandinavian 
approach. The purpose of this master thesis is to explore the Scandinavian corporate sustainability 
approach and contrast it with the American corporate sustainability approach.  
It was found in the literature that the main difference between the two regions’ corporate 
sustainability approaches come back to the overall strategies of the companies. Scandinavian 
companies are characterized by their stakeholder approach whereas American companies put more 
focus on shareholders. The core of the stakeholder approach is cooperation. The firm cooperates with 
all its stakeholders in order to create value for everyone. Conversely, American companies’ emphasis 
is to generate profit for shareholders. Other stakeholders are often seen as competitors. Researchers 
have formalized these approaches into what they call Competitive and Cooperative Strategic Postures. 
Research question 1: To what extent do Scandinavian companies adopt a cooperative strategic posture 
as compared to American companies adopting a competitive strategic posture? 
Implications of these Strategic Postures on the corporate sustainability approaches are contended. 
Scandinavian companies would have an integrated approach to corporate sustainability in which non-
financial aspects are incorporated into the overall strategy of the company through relationships with 
stakeholders. American companies would have a residual approach to corporate sustainability in which 
social and environmental practices are implemented after profit has been generated. Given the 
importance of collaborations in achieving corporate environmental sustainability, it was chosen to 
focus on environmental collaborations. 
Research question 2: Can differences in environmental collaborations practices be observed between 
Scandinavian and American companies?  
To answer research question 1, CEO letters were analyzed. The competitiveness and cooperativeness 
of the language used by CEO of Scandinavian companies was compared to the language used by CEOs 
of American companies. The number of competitive and cooperative words were compared. The 
sample comprised 12 Scandinavian and 12 American companies from the Fast-Moving Consumer 
Goods sector. To answer research question 2, sustainability reports were analyzed. A categorization of 
the different environmental collaborations was performed and the collaborations undertaken by 
Scandinavian companies were compared to the American ones. The sample included 3 Scandinavian 
and 3 American companies from the Fast-Moving Consumer Goods sector. 
 
As expected, the total number of competitive words counted in the 12 American letters is higher than 
in the 12 Scandinavian letters. Conversely, the total number of cooperative words is higher in 
Scandinavia letters. However, the high variations in the number of competitive and cooperative words 
within each region’s companies do not allow to conclude on the significance of this result. More 
qualitative results on a smaller sample highlight evidence of the higher importance of cooperation in 
Scandinavian letters. It is recommended to confirm this tendency on a larger sample of companies, 
using the suggested method improvements and more complex research methods. 
Concerning environmental collaborations, no significant differences between the two regions were 
found. However, the amount of information available in sustainability reports on collaborations is

1. Introduction 
In this section, the term “corporate sustainability” as used in this report is defined, country differences 
regarding corporate sustainability are discussed and evidence of the high performance of Scandinavia 
regarding corporate sustainability measures is given.  
1.1. Corporate sustainability  
Decision-makers have not always thought of their long-term impact. The concept of sustainability 
became widely known on the politics scene by the publication of the Brundtland Report in 1974 (Reid, 
2013).  Companies have their role to play in sustainable development and none the least given the 
amount of resources that they possess and the extent of their impacts on the environment and 
communities. The concept of sustainable development applied to companies is known as corporate 
sustainability. There is no consensus on the definition of corporate sustainability but the common 
points between the different visions of corporate sustainability are the importance of meeting 
stakeholders’ needs and balancing the economic, environmental and social dimensions of corporate 
performance (Chang et al., 2017). In this study, it is chosen to use these common points to define 
corporate sustainability rather than focusing on the differences between the definitions. Therefore, 
corporate sustainability is used here as a general term and includes “corporate sustainability”, 
“corporate citizenship”, “Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)”, “corporate stewardship” and other 
related terms. All these terms are used as equivalents all along the report. 
1.2. CSR and country differences 
The word “Responsibility” in “Corporate Social Responsibility” implies subliminally that social and 
environmental practices depend on the companies’ will. However, a firm is dependent of its national 
context. In particular, in their highly cited article, Matten & Moon (2008) argue that differences in 
institutions between the United States and Europe explain differences in CSR. They contend that 
American companies have more freedom in defining their CSR policies whereas European companies’ 
CSR policies are often decided by larger institutional frameworks like laws and standards. Moreover, 
CSR reporting research offers evidence of the influence of cultures on CSR reporting. Depending on 
certain culture characteristics, companies are more or less likely to extensively report on their social 
and environmental practices (Gallego-Álvarez & Ortas, 2017; García-Sánchez, Rodríguez-Ariza, & Frías-
Aceituno, 2013; Orij, 2010). Overall, the 2009 Scandinavian Journal of Management’s special issue on 
corporate responsibility writes as one of its conclusion “the overarching argument of the special issue 
is that context matters….whether that context involves environmental conditions, or, social, economic 
and institutional arrangements” (Halme, Roome, & Dobers, 2009).  
It is important however not to exaggerate those differences. In their book reviewing corporate 
sustainability in several countries all around the world, Schaltegger et al. (2014) conclude that 
“similarities predominate for the overall pattern but some national differences are found when 
examining the results in detail”. 
1.3. The high performance of Scandinavia regarding sustainability 
Scandinavia is a region located in Northern Europe. It comprises Denmark, Norway, Sweden and 
sometimes Finland and Iceland. Scandinavia has the reputation of being a leading region in 
sustainability. On a national level, The World Economic Forum (2014) provides evidence for this claim 
with its Sustainability-Adjusted Global Competitiveness Index (GCI). Among 144 countries included in 
its 2014-2015 edition, Scandinavian countries reach the top of the ranking. Sweden comes out 10th, 
==PAGE:9==
2 
 
Norway 11th and Denmark in the 13th position. On a company level, the Dow Jones Sustainability Index 
(DJSI) shows similar result. Strand (2013), considering the total number of companies assessed by DJSI 
in the United-States and Scandinavia, found that Scandinavian-based corporations are three times 
more likely to be selected by the DJSI than U.S.-based corporations. Overall, Strand et al. (2015) show 
by looking at different measurement methods that Scandinavia performs well regarding sustainability 
on a national level and on a company level.  
Despite the high performance of Scandinavia regarding sustainability, the Scandinavian Journal of 
Management underlines in a recent call for papers “little to no research has been undertaken to 
understand what firm-level policies and practices may serve as contributing factors to the apparently 
strong sustainability performances of Scandinavian firms.” (Strand, Freeman, Marcus, Moon, & 
Morsing, 2016)  It is interesting to note as an example that in the large study comparing corporate 
sustainability internationally previously mentioned, Scandinavia is not studied (Schaltegger, Windolph, 
Harms, & Hörisch, 2014). The need for this research gap to be filled becomes even more clear as Strand 
highlights in his 2017 working paper the overall cooperation atmosphere in Scandinavia and the effect 
that it could have on its sustainability performance. He contrasts his arguments by pointing out to the 
very different atmosphere existing in the United-States.  
Purpose: Against this background, this thesis offers to explore Scandinavia’s specific business context 
and corporate sustainability approach. To better underline its specificity, these aspects will be 
compared to the US’s business context and corporate sustainability approach.

2. Theoretical framework 
In the following Scandinavian culture and institutions are described and compared to the American 
culture and institutions. Based on the underlined differences, it is argued that Scandinavia offers a 
cooperative business context as opposed to the competitive business context of the US. Important 
points of business research and teaching in Scandinavia are highlighted. Finally, the consequences on 
firm’s overall strategies and CSR approaches are discussed. A diagram summing up the overall 
reasoning of the theoretical framework is provided in Figure 1. Culture and institutions as well as 
business research and teaching influence Scandinavian companies’ strategy which in turn influences 
their CSR strategy. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
2.1. Scandinavian culture and institutions and comparison with the United-States 
It must be underlined here that Scandinavian countries such as Denmark, Sweden and Norway have 
distinct cultures and institutions. It was however chosen, just like for the corporate sustainability 
concept, to look at the common points rather than the differences and highlight overall tendencies. 
Moreover, it must be kept in mind that cultures and institutions are not two distinct features of a 
country. Culture influences institutions which in turn influence culture.  
2.1.1. Culture

between Scandinavia and the United-States. It is a robust theory, data-based and conducted in more 
than seventy countries over a forty-year time span. It must nevertheless be kept in mind reading the 
following that this theory is a deliberate oversimplification of cultures. It simplifies cultures to be able 
to put numbers on the cultural differences and by doing this, ignores, among other aspects, 
unquantifiable differences.

2.1.2. Institutions 
 
Institutions are the patterns of behavior that structure a society. It is basically the culture at a national 
level. Examples include family, religion as well as the economic system and the businesses. It will be 
given evidence here of how American and Scandinavian business institutions differ. 
 
Nature and role of the firm 
There are two main different ways of defining the nature and role of a firm within a society (Bradley, 
Schipani, Sundaram, & Walsh, 1999). In contractarian countries like the US (van der Laan Smith, 
Adhikari, & Tondkar, 2005), firms are an economic organization defined as an aggregation of 
individuals. The most important value is freedom and the role of law is the promotion of this freedom. 
The role of managers is to maximize shareholder wealth within the bounds of law. In communitarian 
countries like Denmark and Norway (van der Laan Smith et al., 2005), firms are a social, political and 
historical entity. The most important value is responsibility and the role of law is to promote fairness. 
Managers are responsible to all stakeholders (see Table 1 for a summary of the differences between 
contractarianism and communitarianism). Stakeholders would have more power and legitimacy in 
communitarian countries like Denmark and Norway as compared to contractarian countries like the 
US (van der Laan Smith et al., 2005).

Ownership structure  
There is a difference in the number of owners between American and Scandinavian companies. In the 
US, companies have a higher number of owners whereas in Scandinavia, ownership is more 
concentrated. This might influence the relationship between the management and the owners 
(shareholders). In Scandinavian countries where the number of owners is limited, managers would 
discuss more in detail with them about the functioning of the company whereas American managers 
might see the numerous shareholders as faceless entities for which to maximize profit. 
Moreover, not only is ownership more concentrated in Scandinavian countries but the region is 
especially unique in its amount of foundation and government ownership (van der Laan Smith et al., 
2005). Given that stakeholders’ influence on the company depends on the essential resources they 
control (Ullmann, 1985), these foundations and the government have more leverage in Scandinavian 
countries. 
It has been shown that a key cultural difference is Scandinavia’s focus on quality of life as opposed to 
the US’s emphasis on performance. In business institutions, the main difference found was the 
importance that companies grant to stakeholders. US companies are more focused on shareholders 
while Scandinavian companies pay more attention to other stakeholders. The following part comes 
back to the development of the stakeholder concept and its influence on Scandinavian companies. 
2.2. The stakeholder approach 
For a long time, business has been about maximizing profit. Because of its culture, institutions and 
historical aspects, Scandinavia offered a promising context to the development of a more cooperative 
way of managing a business which is best described by the Stakeholder theory. It is not a coincidence 
that Scandinavian scholars contributed so much to the development of this theory and concrete 
examples of its application by Scandinavian companies can be found.  
2.2.1. Basics of Stakeholder theory  
In 1970, famous American economist Milton Friedman wrote his well-known article entitled “The 
Social Responsibility of Business is to Increase its Profits” (Friedman, 1970). His reasoning is that profit 
maximization will, through market mechanisms, increase benefits, not only for shareholders, but also 
customers, employees and all the other stakeholders. Customers will benefit from having products at 
low prices, employees will benefit from keeping their job and getting raises and citizens in general will 
benefit from this profit through corporate taxes. Therefore, the focus of managers should be profit 
maximization.  Stakeholder theory does not agree with this view of business. Stakeholder theory 
asserts that “in order to maximize profits, companies need great products and services that customers 
want, solid relationships with suppliers that keep operations on the cutting edge, inspired employees 
who stand for the company mission and push the company to become better, and supportive 
communities that allow businesses to flourish” (Freeman, Harrison, Wicks, Parmar, & de Colle, 2010). 
Therefore, to make a business successful, the focus of managers should be on relationships with 
stakeholders. And by doing this, the company will not only generate profit for shareholders but also 
create value for all stakeholders (see Figure 3 for an illustration of a firm and its stakeholders).

An important aspect of Stakeholder theory is that it does not separate business and ethics (Freeman 
et al., 2010). Managers should not put their values aside when doing business. What they consider 
right and wrong should be part of managing a business and applied concretely in their relationships 
with stakeholders. It will be shown in the following how the integration of ethics in business depicts 
the Scandinavian way of doing business and how it affects corporate responsibility. 
2.2.2. Development and application in Scandinavia 
Stakeholder theory became well known in 1984 when American scholar R. Edward Freeman published 
his book Strategic Management: A stakeholder approach in which he summed up the methods 
managers can use to implement a stakeholder approach in their companies (Freeman, 1984). Strand 
& Freeman (2015) argue that Scandinavian scholars largely contributed to the development of this 
stakeholder approach. These scholars have first published and described the term “stakeholder” and 
developed of some of its fundamental concepts. The Swedish researcher Eric Rhenman, for example, 
in his book Industrial Democracy and Industrial Management published in 1968 (Swedish version in 
1964) wrote that the survival of a company ‘‘is a common goal of all stakeholders’’, one that ‘‘creates 
interdependence between the stakeholders.’’(p. 54) (Rhenman, 1968). This thinking breaks up with 
what Rhenman calls a “traditional view of business” in which management and stakeholders are seen 
as competitors. Figure 4 shows Rhenman’s illustration of the “traditional view of business”.  
 
==PAGE:15==
8 
 
 
Figure 4 - Management and stakeholders throwing rocks at each other (Rhenman, 1968) 
 
This formulation joins the fundamental concept of “Jointness of interest” core to the Stakeholder 
theory. Managers should not see stakeholders as enemies but focus instead on the common interest 
of the company and its stakeholders. For example, environmentalists should not be seen as nuisance 
but as guides that will help the company reduce its environmental impacts. The main role of managers 
should not be to maximize the company’s profit but to spot the win-win situations between the 
company and its stakeholders.  
Strand & Freeman (2015) also point out to the tight connections between academics and managers in 
Scandinavia and emphasize the importance of the stakeholder approach in Scandinavia’s management 
teaching. As an example, Näsi examined all the annual curriculum documents over the years 1966-
1980 at the University of Tampere, Finland, and found that books on stakeholder theory were 
compulsory part of basic studies for every management student from 1968 to 1980 (Carroll & Nasi, 
1997). Teaching is especially important since management science does not only observe management 
practices but influences them. In particular,  the choice of metaphors in business strategy could push 
managers into more competitive or cooperative behaviors (Strand, 2014). 
Concrete applications of the stakeholder approach can be found in Scandinavian companies. First, in a 
case study of Scandinavian Airlines (SAS), SAS’s employees argue that committing to CSR is the right 
thing to do (Lynes & Andrachuk, 2008). Moreover, the Deputy CEO highlights the financial effectiveness 
of the stakeholder approach while underlining that it is not its ultimate goal “The cost performance is 
not the driving force, but I sense that what is good for the environment is also paying off. If we do 
things in a way which makes people be more committed, more concerned about doing the right things, 
even from an environmental perspective, then we're probably also spending less resources and 
[money].” Other examples of stakeholder orientation can be found in collaborations along supply 
chains (Strand, 2009). The well-known Swedish furniture company IKEA partnered with the NGOs 
United Nations Children's Fund (UNICEF) and “Save the children” to tackle their child labor issues. 
These issues are complex. In a study UNICEF and “Save the children” found that companies pulling out 
from regions in which child labor occurs might cause great harm. The same children could turn to other 
ways of earning money such as prostitution. IKEA’s strong partnership with UNICEF and “Save the 
children” made the company aware of the complexity of the issue. IKEA therefore did not pull out from 
the concerned regions but made long-term commitments backed-up by its partners to improve 
children’s living conditions.

2.2.3. Competitive Strategic Posture and Cooperative Strategic Posture 
Strategic management is the management of an organization’s resources to achieve its goals and 
objectives (Investopedia, n.d.). Strategic management presents significant differences between the 
U.S. and Scandinavia. While US’s strategic management thinking has traditionally focused on 
competition, Scandinavia’s strategic management thinking is centered on cooperation (Strand, 2017; 
working paper). Drawing on this difference, Strand & Freeman (2015) introduce the concepts of 
Competitive and Cooperative Strategic Postures. 
A Strategic Posture is defined as “a company’s inclination to embrace strategies along a particular 
dimension” and the researchers introduce the “competitive-cooperative dimension as a useful 
description of a fundamental tenet of stakeholder theory to embrace cooperation between the 
company and its stakeholders”  (Strand & Freeman, 2015). Although aware that these concepts are 
oversimplifying companies’ complex strategies, the researchers emphasize their relevance to highlight 
general tendencies.  
For a special Issue on Sustainability in a Scandinavian Context, the Scandinavian Journal of 
Management called for “both case studies and large-scale surveys to compare whether firms with 
Scandinavian country of origin are more likely to […] exhibit a cooperative strategic approach than 
firms from elsewhere” (Strand et al., 2016). Drawing from the just mentioned differences in strategic 
management between the two regions and in general the differences in business context described 
previously, the following research question is set: 
Research question 1: To what extent do Scandinavian companies adopt a cooperative strategic 
posture as compared to American companies adopting a competitive strategic posture? 
 
2.3. Implications and reflections on Scandinavian and American CSR approaches 
 
Stakeholder theory has influenced companies’ practices worldwide. A good illustration can be found 
in The Global Reporting Initiative (GRI)2 guidelines. Companies reporting according to GRI are asked to 
report on their “stakeholder engagement” which is the way companies communicate and work with 
their different stakeholders. However, in reality, the amount of information given by companies on 
this aspect is limited (Manetti, 2011). Moreover, this engagement is often limited to CSR issues. The 
following explains how real stakeholder engagement goes beyond CSR. 
2.3.1. The integration of CSR into the overall strategy 
The implication of managers adopting a stakeholder point of view is that the classical division between 
the economic, social and environmental aspects of business no longer applies. By defining strategies 
that create value for all their stakeholders, managers always consider the three aspects at the same 
time.

and therefore an integrated approach to CSR (see Table 2). Conversely, the US would have a 
shareholder orientation and therefore a residual approach to CSR (see Table 2). These two different 
approaches imply two different CSR business model as shown in Table 2.  Scandinavian companies’

2.3.2. The importance of collaborations for corporate sustainability 
An important aspect of sustainability is collaboration. The United-Nations’ Sustainable Development 
Goals include “Partnerships for the goals” (United-Nations, 2016). This applies to companies. A first 
reason for the importance of collaboration is that products’ impacts are all along the supply chain. It 
is more efficient for companies to join forces and address together the impacts where they are the 
biggest. A second reason is that sustainability issues are complex and often need companies to pool 
their resources in order to solve them. It was chosen in the following to focus on the environmental 
aspect of sustainability because environmental impacts are more quantifiable which makes the 
collaborations more easily assessable. Environmental management literature calls for increasing 
collaboration to address environmental issues (Dekoninck et al., 2016; Kim, Woo, Rho, & Chung, 2016). 
Given the cooperation atmosphere argued for in Scandinavia and the integrated CSR contended, it is 
wondered whether it would foster environmental collaborations of Scandinavian companies as

2.3.3. Integration of the study into the Scandinavian cooperative advantage framework  
In his 2017 working paper, Strand proposed the Scandinavian Cooperative Advantage framework (see 
Figure 7) that links the cultural, institutional, sustainability performances and financial performances 
of Scandinavian companies. This framework offers a structure to organize future research on the 
Scandinavian cooperative advantage concept defined as “the general tendency for companies in a 
Scandinavian context to implement a value creating strategy based on cooperating with their 
stakeholders that results in superior value creation for the companies and their stakeholders” (Strand 
& Freeman, 2015). This research framework intends to encourage a shift in business from a 
Competitive Advantage to a Cooperative Advantage. The present master thesis fits in e) of the 
framework as it attempts to find evidence that “Scandinavian firms demonstrate strong cooperation”.

3. Method 
This part presents the methods used to answer the two research questions of the study: 
Research question 1: To what extent do Scandinavian companies adopt a cooperative strategic posture 
as compared to American companies adopting a competitive strategic posture? 
Research question 2: Can differences in environmental collaborations practices be observed between 
Scandinavian and American companies?  
Answers could have been found by interviewing corporate actors (e.g. managers and in particular 
CEOs) or by sending surveys to companies, however, it was chosen, given the limited resources and 
time constraints of this master thesis, to focus on easily accessible data. Corporate reports were a 
logical choice since they present a review of the companies’ financial and none-financial results in 
addition to being publicly available on the internet. They most of the times consist of two reports. The 
annual report presents the financial strategy of the company and the sustainability report present the 
social and environmental strategies and practices of the company. The sustainability report is 
sometimes also called “citizenship report” or “CSR report” or even “environmental report” when it 
only deals with the environmental aspects. It was chosen here to use the expression “sustainability 
report” as a general term encompassing the just mentioned terms as well as all reports dealing with 
the environmental and social practices of companies. Researchers have previously leveraged annual 
reports (Meek, Roberts, & Gray, 1995; Wallace & Naser, 1995) and sustainability reports (Albino, 
Dangelico, & Pontrandolfo, 2012; Montabon, Sroufe, & Narasimhan, 2007) to get information on 
companies’ financial and non-financial practices.  
There is a research gap in comparing sustainability reports from Scandinavian companies and U.S. 
companies. According to Fifka (2013)’s literature review on the determinants of sustainability 
reporting, there is only one article studying the differences in CSR reporting between the US and 
Scandinavian countries. This article counted the number of words and sentences in American and 
Scandinavian sustainability reports (van der Laan Smith et al., 2005). It found a higher number of words 
and sentences in Scandinavian reports than in American reports. This was interpreted as a sign of the 
stronger importance of stakeholders in Scandinavia. The findings of this article will be later compared 
to our results. 
Two different methods were used. The first one analyzes CEO letters in annual reports and the second 
one analyzes sustainability reports. They are presented in the following parts. 
3.1. Method of study I – Investigating the Strategic Posture of companies 
This part describes the method developed to find empirical evidence of the Cooperative Strategic 
Posture of Scandinavian companies and of the Competitive Strategic Posture of American companies. 
After an email conversation with Robert Strand, one of two the researchers who created the 
Competitive and Cooperative Strategic Posture concept, it was decided to focus on the CEO letters 
found at the beginning of annual reports. The strategy and results of the company are summed up in 
this letter. This was checked by reading a few of them. Moreover, previous research has been done on 
CEO letters (Conaway, 2010; Hutt, 2012) Conaway (2010) . Conaway (2010) compared letters written 
by the CEOs of US-based companies and Latin American-based companies. CEO letters would 
communicate more than factual information. By their writing style, they seek for support from 
shareholders. Hutt (2012) analyzed the extent and patterns of multi-stakeholder communications in 
annual report letters.  
==PAGE:21==

The up-coming Special Issue on Sustainability in a Scandinavian Context calls for “case studies and 
large-scale surveys to compare whether firms with Scandinavian country of origin are more likely to 
employ cooperative language” (Strand et al., 2016). Therefore, it was chosen to analyze the 
competitive and cooperative language of the letters. For that, a first method of content analysis was 
tried: Centering Resonance Analysis (CRA). It is an algorithm calculating the importance value of each 
noun of the text (see (Corman, 2002) for a detailed presentation). The algorithm was installed and run 
on three American and three Scandinavian CEO letters. Appendix 1 shows the most important words 
of each of the six letters according to this algorithm. Only words with a value of at least 0.01 are listed 
as advised by Tate (2010). However, no clear differences in competitive and cooperative words was 
spotted and it was therefore decided to drop this method. It must nevertheless be noted that CRA 
could be an interesting method to analyze the overall content of the letters. It was applied by Tate 
(2010) for example to study the content of sustainability reports.   
Another method of content analysis was used: word counting. Work counting has been used previously 
in the literature to analyze the content of texts. As examples, it was used to count circular economy 
keywords in corporate press release (Bocken, Ritala, & Huotari, 2017) or to search for financial and 
sustainable terms in sustainability reports (Hartman, Rubin, & Dhanda, 2007). 
The following presents the method steps. Being numerous, they are presented as a list of bullet points 
for clarity:

Description of how the lists of cooperative and competitive words were established:  
For each main type of words (noun, verb, adjective and adverb), what was thought as the two 
most relevant competitive words (respectively, cooperative words) were noted down (see 
Table 3 ). These words were searched for on the online synonym dictionary Thesaurus3, and 
the synonyms were all noted down. It resulted in two lists of about 200 words each (see 
Appendix 3 and Appendix 4). 
 
The use of an online dictionary enables the study to be more objective. The competitive and 
cooperative words were not selected by me, with all the subjectivity that it would imply, but 
are the result of the websites’ work of associating words with other words.  
 
The dictionary distinguished close synonyms from far synonyms. It was chosen to list all 
synonyms and not just the close in order to obtain extensive lexical fields and therefore be 
able to detect even small nuances of competitive and cooperative language.

Description of the lists’ pre-treatment in excel:  
The counting was done automatically in Excel. An important point is that Excel searches not 
for the exact word but for strings (in the algorithmic sense of the word: a set of characters). 
The advantage is that it searches for the listed words but also for all the words made of these 
words. For example, if “cooperat” is part of the list, “cooperation”, “cooperations”, 
“cooperative”, etc will be counted. The disadvantage is that it can also count words that are 
not supposed to be counted. For example, “about” will be counted if “bout” is present in the 
list. It must be noted that since the string search function used in excel is case sensitive, the 
excel sheet was made so that excel searches for the string without capital letters and the string 
with the first character in capital letter. For example, for the word “aggressive”, the sheet was 
made so that excel searches for the strings “aggressive” and “Aggressive”. 
o It had to be checked that no word in a list contain another. Otherwise, the word would 
be counted twice. For example, “challenger” contains “challenge” so “challenger” was 
deleted from the list. Otherwise Excel would have counted “challenger” twice. 
o For strings containing two or more words like “work with”, “is in the running” or “lock 
horn”, their conjugations had to be added to the list. For example, for “work with”, 
the strings “works with”, “working with” and “worked with” had to be added to the 
list. In the same way, for “is in the running”, it was added “be in the running”, “am in 
the running”, “are in the running”, “was in the running” and “were in the running”. 
o Strings of 3 letters had to be deleted since it was very probable that they were counted 
wrongly. For example, “try” could be counted wrongly if “industry” is present in the 
letter. Exception was made for “play” since it is an important competitive word. To 
avoid “display” to be counted wrongly, “display” was counted are subtracted from the 
counts of “play”.

3.2. Method of study II – Exploring the environmental collaborations 
Study I analyses the overall strategy of companies. This second part zooms on the sustainability 
strategy and more specifically on the environmental collaborations described in sustainability reports. 
By environmental collaborations is meant partnerships that aim at reducing environmental impacts. 
An analysis framework was established. It included the following dimensions:  
- 
Stakeholder: the kind of stakeholder with which the company has collaborated. Examples 
include supplier, competitor, NGO or research department. 
- 
Output: the output produced by the collaboration between the company and its collaborators. 
Examples include the design a more environmentally friendly product, water risk measures or 
an impact measurement methodology. 
- 
Resource exchange: resources (information or material) that the company gives to its 
stakeholder and that the stakeholder gives to the company in order to produce the desired 
output. Examples include environmental impact data or human resources. 
- 
Link to strategy: is the collaboration linked to an overall environmental strategy? How is the 
collaboration contributing to the achieving the goals of this strategy?  
- 
Duration: the duration of the collaboration. 
The environmental sections of the sustainability reports were read and each paragraph describing a 
collaboration was copy-pasted into an Excel sheet.  From the framework above, information about 
each collaboration was extracted. Among these dimensions only Stakeholder and Output were 
analyzed in the end since the reports did not contain enough detail to fill in the other dimensions.  
The following describes which collaborations were included and excluded from the analysis. We define 
here environmental collaboration as the action of a company to work with one of its stakeholders to 
produce knowledge, a product or tool that reduces or at least aims at reducing environmental impacts. 
Environmental impacts are here understood as the ones described in the field of Life Cycle Assessment 
(LCA) with the human toxicity impacts not being taken into account. The company has to join resources 
with its stakeholder in order to suit this definition. It therefore excludes, for example, just giving money 
to an organization (sponsorship) or just being a member of an association (membership).  
The collaborations not detailed enough were excluded. For example, when Molson-Coors says that the 
goal of the Molson Coors Grower’s Group is to “secure a sustainable supply of high-quality British 
malting barley from local and important barley growing regions of the UK, provide value to Molson 
Coors and the farmers, and implement best practice and innovation in the supply chain”, it is not 
precise enough to be taken into account. In that way, it was attempted to avoid the marketing lens of 
sustainability reports. 
Finally, the collaborations considered too anecdotal were excluded. For example, when public 
authorities help ConAgra acquire a grant for a can separator, it is too little collaboration to be included.

3.3. Sample of companies  
It was chosen to focus on only one sector to limit the number of variables.  Among the consumer goods, 
there are fast-moving products and durable products. Fast-moving consumer goods have a lower unit 
cost, are bought more often, and have a shorter service life than durable goods. It was chosen to focus 
on these fast-moving consumer goods as they cause high environmental impacts. The sector accounts 
for 35% of material inputs into the economy and 75% of solid municipal waste (Ellen MacArthur 
Foundation, 2013) 
Given that companies are increasingly globalized, it is not obvious to define a company as American or 
Scandinavian. The chosen criteria for a company to be considered as from a certain region was that 
the company has its headquarters in this region.  
Corporate Register4 is the largest database of corporate sustainability reports. It was used to list the 
Danish, Norwegian and Swedish companies in the fast-moving consumer goods sector having released 
a corporate sustainability report in 2016. The list contained 59 Scandinavian companies. Many of these 
companies were discarded because they did not publish CEO letters. For some of these companies it 
was because a CEO letter was written for the whole group to which they belonged and not just for the 
particular company. Another reason for excluding companies was that an English version of the 
sustainability report did not exist. Finally, cooperatives were excluded from the sample because of 
their high level of cooperation by nature which would introduce a bias in the sample. The final sample 
was selected among the remaining companies with the two following criteria: to have approximately 
the same number of companies coming from each Scandinavian country and approximately the same 
number of companies in each industry. The rational was to not introduce a country bias or industry 
bias. These criteria were fulfilled as much as possible given the limited amount of companies to choose 
from. The final sample of Scandinavian companies is presented in Table 5. A description of each 
company can be found in Appendix 5, Table 20 . 
Using again the sustainability report directory Corporate Register, a list of American companies 
belonging to the beverage, food, household goods and textiles and personal care industries was 
established. Scandinavian companies were matched one by one using the following criteria. The 
American company had to be from the same industry as the Scandinavian company, to have an activity 
as close as possible and to be approximately of the same size (using the number of employees). The 
resulting American sample is presented in Table 6. A description of each company can be found in 
Appendix 5, Table 21. 
It must be noted that due to the time constraints of a master thesis solely the following Scandinavian 
companies Carlsberg, Orkla and Cloetta and the following American companies Molson-Coors,

4.1.2. Discussion 
Here are suggested possible improvements for the word counting method. First, more words can be 
added to the lists of competitive and cooperative words. Even if the lists used are already extensive, 
some competitive and cooperative words could have been missed. In addition, despite the care that 
was given to avoid it, it is possible that some words are wrongly counted. Very small words were 
deleted because they could be contained in others. For example, “bout” being wrongly counted 
because contained in “about”. But to be more rigorous each counted word would have to be searched 
for in the corresponding CEO letter in order to check that it is rightly counted and not counted because 
it is contained in another word with a different meaning. Finally, During the normalization step the 
number of counted words was divided by the total number of words which includes small words like 
“the” and “a” that have no meaning. A pre-treatment of the letters deleting these small words with no 
meaning could be imagined in order to better compare the size of the texts. The algorithms doing this 
are called parsers.  
Beyond improvements of the word counting method, the method could be slightly modified and the 
results compared to this study’s results. It would be interesting to try word counting with more 
restricted lists of words. They would include the most competitive and cooperative words. For 
example, the competitive list could include words like “compete”, “aggressive”, “killer”, and the 
cooperative list could include words like “cooperate” and “collaborate” and other close related terms. 
It was chosen here to use more extensive lists because it was thought that a bigger difference would 
be seen but it is worth trying with shorter, more related lists. Moreover, it can be attempted to create 
lists manually by selecting competitive and cooperative words from business language. For instance, 
“industry peers” as opposed to “competitors”. 
Concerning the sample, only 24 companies were analyzed and from one sector only. It is advised to 
repeat this study with a larger number of companies and from different sectors in order to check the 
generalization of the results. Moreover, it was chosen to define a US company (respectively, a 
Scandinavian company) as a company that has its headquarters in the US (respectively, in Scandinavia). 
However, companies are increasingly globalized and it is not sure that their strategy is reflecting the 
country it originates from. For example, Molson Coors has its headquarters and operates in the US but 
the company also has large operations in Canada and Europe. Its strategy could be influenced by its 
operation outside the US. In addition, business education could have an influence on strategies of 
companies (Freeman, 2010a; p64), through, for example, the metaphors used to strategize (Strand, 
2014). Therefore, it would be interesting to see if, not only the region the company originates from 
but the region a CEO has been educated in, influences the use of competitive and cooperative 
language.  
A more qualitative analysis showed differences in the importance of cooperation in the CEO letters. 
Six of the CEO letters were read thoroughly and it was decided to copy-paste to a Word document all 
sentences mentioning a cooperation. Then the words in those sentences were counted and compared 
to the total number of words. Examples of sentences include “I would like to take this opportunity to 
pay tribute to all my colleagues in the Carlsberg Group for their collaboration, dedication and 
enthusiasm during the past year” for Carlsberg and “We are building stronger partnerships by 
providing beer expertise, customer-centric solutions and industry-leading service and results.” for 
Molson-Coors Table 11 and Table 12 show the results. They highlight a tendency of   Scandinavian 
companies to address cooperation in their CEO letters. 
It was also observed that American letters are more focused on detailing financial results. Drawing 
from the just mentioned results and Hutt (2012) who found that CEO’s of the ten largest US companies 
made little use of multi-stakeholder communication in their annual letters, it can be hypothesized that 
American annual letters are more focused shareholders who demand financial results.

4.1.3. Conclusion 
Even though differences in the normalized total amount of words counted could be seen, the results 
vary a lot from letter to letter, even for letters from the same region, and therefore no definitive 
conclusions can be made. A more qualitative analysis on a smaller sample revealed differences in the 
importance of cooperation between the two regions. It is suggested for future research to implement 
the recommended improvements on the method and confirming the results on a larger sample of 
companies. Studying a larger sample including companies from other sectors would enable to draw 
correlations with control variables such as the size of the company and the sector. Moreover, it would 
be interesting to compare our results with results from smaller sets of more relevant competitive and 
cooperative words. Finally, it can also be thought of using more complex text analysis algorithms such 
as machine learning algorithms. A collaboration between the departments DTU management and DTU 
compute could for that be undertaken. 
 
If the research hypothesizes were to be confirmed by these larger studies, the next step would be to 
prove that a competitive or cooperative language reflects a competitive or cooperative Strategic 
Posture. The language used could be the mere reflection of what the shareholders expect to see in the 
letter and not an image of the strategy. As Conaway (2010) believes: “corporate documents exist to 
communicate more than factual information to their constituencies. The purpose of corporate writers 
is to influence public opinion and attitudes, particularly among potential investors, in ways that create 
support for organizational practices or undermine opposition to them.”

4.2.2. Discussion  
As mentioned previously, according to Fifka (2013)’s literature review on the determinants of 
sustainability reporting, there is only one article studying the differences in CSR reporting between the 
US and Scandinavian countries. This article counted the number of words and sentences in American 
and Scandinavian sustainability reports (van der Laan Smith et al., 2005). It found a higher number of 
words and sentences in Scandinavian reports than in American reports. This was interpreted as a sign 
of the stronger importance of stakeholders in Scandinavia. This study is not able to confirm this result. 
The absence of significant differences between Scandinavian and American firms could be explained 
by a recent shift in American strategic management. Michael Porter is one of the most influential 
thinker of the field. He has focused for many years on how companies can gain competitive advantage 
as exemplified by his famous Five competitive forces model. However, in 2011, the same Michael 
Porter published, along with his colleague Mark Kramer, an article marking a fracture with its previous 
thoughts. Creating shared value (Porter & Kramer, 2011), instead of describing ways to deal with 
competition invites companies to collaborate. The article describes three ways that a company can 
create what it calls “shared value”, which is value not only for this company but value for, for example, 
several companies and society in general. Despite some differences, the Shared Value concept is close 
to the Jointness of Interest concept of Stakeholder theory (Strand & Freeman, 2015). This 
encouragement to cooperate by one of the U.S.’s most influential business thinker might be the cause 
of increasing collaborations among American organizations. 
The number of environmental collaborations of each company can be compared to the number of 
cooperative words in their CEO letters counted in Study I (see Table 16). It can be seen that for 
Scandinavian companies Carlsberg and Orkla have both high number of cooperative words and 
collaborations as compared to Cloetta. This result is however not observed for American companies.

It can be seen that Flowers Food has about the same number of cooperative words as Molson Coors 
but three times less collaborations. Only ConAgra Food has both a high number of cooperative words 
and a high number of collaborations. These results would need to be confirmed with a larger sample.

Collaborations are described very briefly in sustainability reports. The dimensions Resource Exchange, 
Link to strategy, Anecdotal and Duration could not be filled in for the majority of collaborations. Also, 
as mentioned in the method part, many collaborations were excluded because no concrete outputs 
were mentioned. Yet, Strand (2009) point out to the honesty and trust-based features of Scandinavian 
collaborations which enable successful, long-term partnerships. These characteristics can hardly be 
observed in sustainability reports and hence more in-depth research methods such as interviews are 
suggested for future research.   
The next step would be to link environmental collaborations to environmental performance. Evidence 
can already be found in the literature. De Marchi (2010) revealed that collaboration is positively 
correlated to environmental innovations among Spanish firms. In Spain again, Tachizawa et al. (2015) 
shows that collaborations with suppliers is positively correlated with environmental performance. 
Finally, Albino et al. (2015) showed that, among the largest publicly traded U.S. companies, inter-
organizational collaborations with actors both within (suppliers and customers) and outside the supply 
chain (government agencies and NGOs) are beneficial for a company’s overall environmental 
performance, the management of its environmental footprint and a company’s environmental 
reputation. 
As a side note, it was observed that, among the whole 24 companies of the sample, the Scandinavian 
companies Orkla and Cloetta as well as the US firm The Clorox Company have merged their annual and 
sustainability report into one and unique report called an integrated report. Royal Unibrew also 
publishes an integrated report but the sustainability part is so little that it is not considered as 
integrated. These integrated reports found in the sample can be seen as an indication of the integration 
of the sustainability strategy to the financial strategy. Garcia-Sanchez (2013) found that in feminine 
societies, companies are more likely to integrate their reports. 
4.2.3. Conclusion 
 
No significant differences between the two regions could be observed in the six sustainability reports 
analyzed. This could be explained by the recent shift in American strategic management. One of the 
most influential American business thinker made a U-turn on his previous work promoting

competition, by publishing an article encouraging cooperation in 2011 (Porter & Kramer, 2011). 
However, the lack of differences could also be due to the limited amount of information on 
collaborations available in sustainability reports. For example, the framework dimensions Resource

collaborations. Strand (2009) point out to the honesty and trust-based features of Scandinavian 
collaborations which enable successful, long-term partnerships. These characteristics can hardly be 
observed in sustainability reports and hence more in-depth research methods such as interviews are 
suggested for future research.  Concerning the overall environmental collaboration research field, the 
next step would be to study the link between environmental collaborations and environmental 
performance.

5. Conclusion and recommendations 
The aim of this master thesis was to explore the Scandinavian approach to corporate sustainability and 
compare it to the US approach. It was argued that, influenced by its culture and institutions, 
Scandinavian companies have not only a specific corporate sustainability approach but also a specific 
way of doing business in general. Scandinavian companies pay more attention to stakeholders than 
American companies who are more focused on shareholders. This stakeholder approach implies to 
stop putting maximizing profit at the heart of every move but instead to focus on the firms 
relationships with stakeholders. The role of managers is to spot the win-win situations between the 
company and its stakeholders and create value for everyone. It is argued this cooperation is the 
hallmark of Scandinavian companies and that it would be visible it their overall strategy (Strategic 
Postures) as well as their CSR practices.  
Both Strategic Postures and CSR practices were studied. The Strategic Postures were studied through 
a language analysis of CEO letters to shareholders. The aim was to reveal the competitiveness and 
cooperativeness of the language used by CEOs of Scandinavian and American companies. Competitive 
and cooperative words were counted in a sample of 12 Scandinavian letters and 12 American letters. 
The results showed more competitive words in the overall 12 American letters and more cooperative 
words in the overall 12 Scandinavian letters. However, the amount of competitive and cooperative 
words counted greatly varies between the companies of each region. Therefore, it is not possible to 
assert that the Scandinavian letters studied use more cooperative words and less competitive words 
but rather that there is a tendency that would need to be confirmed by further study. A more 
qualitative analysis on a smaller sample also revealed differences in the importance of cooperation 
between the two regions. Recommendations for further research include implementing improvements 
on the method and confirming the results on a larger sample of companies. It can also be thought of 
using more complex text analysis algorithms such as machine learning algorithms. A collaboration 
between the departments DTU management and DTU compute could for that be undertaken. The 
results would have to be compared to other studies studying the Competitive and Cooperative 
Strategic Postures of firms and using other kinds of data. It was underlined that the language of CEO 
letters could be the mere reflection of the demands of shareholders.  
The environmental collaborations analysis did not reveal any clear difference between the three 
Scandinavian and the three American companies studied. This could be explained by the recent shift 
in American strategic management. One of the most influential American business thinker made a U-
turn on his previous work promoting competition, by publishing an article encouraging cooperation in 
2011 (Porter & Kramer, 2011). However, the lack of differences could also be due to the limited amount 
of information on collaborations available in sustainability reports. Several dimensions of the pre-
defined framework could not be filled in. Strand (2009) point out to the honesty and trust-based 
features of Scandinavian collaborations which enable successful, long-term partnerships. It is therefore 
advised to use more in-depth research methods such as interviews in order to be able to observe these 
characteristics. Concerning the overall environmental collaboration research field, the next step would 
be to study the link between environmental collaborations and environmental performance. 
This study contributes to the Scandinavian cooperative advantage framework. This research 
framework aims at structuring research on the promising Scandinavian business context. It aims to 
prove the high cooperation level in Scandinavian business and its link with Scandinavia’s high 
sustainability and financial performances. If confirmed empirically, it would inspire other countries to 
encourage cooperation in business. In other words, it would foster a shift in business from the 
traditional Competitive Advantage to a Cooperative Advantage with all stakeholders.
