Quantiﬁcation and analysis of marine and
coastal litter on selected beaches in the Gulf
of Guinea using citizen science
Frederikke Ebba Haastrup-Vang 1
1 Technical University of Denmark, Aquatic Science and Technology
ABSTRACT
Litter pollution in the marine environment is a constantly increasing challenge, especially where plastic
pollution is considered a big threat. As litter in the ocean is difﬁcult to collect and quantify due to the mas-
sive surface areas and depths, multiple projects monitoring and quantifying litter is rely on data obtained
at shorelines. In recent times, citizen science projects have been used frequently in litter monitoring and
collection projects or experiments, as using multiple laymen allows access to more data covering larger
areas. This project used citizen science to collect litter and data on two beaches in Ghana, located in the
Gulf of Guinea. Ten volunteers from the University of Cape Coast participated in the project. The project
consisted of a collection program of six days spread out over three weeks, where the collection of data
and litter took place each Monday and Thursday, with the aim and objective of quantifying the amount
of litter at each beach each day and analysing the different sources of litter. The locations chosen were
a beach by Ampenyi and a beach by Cape Coast. In total 7,184 items of litter were found at the beach
by Ampenyi and 5,375 items of litter were found at the beach by Cape Coast. Of these items, 74.5%
of the items at the beach by Ampenyi were plastic items and 70.9% of the items found at the beach by
Cape Coast were of plastic. An overall decrease in the total amount of litter and total amount of plastic
can be seen at both locations during the six collection days, however a spike in both plastic items and
total litter items was found at the beach by Cape Coast on collection day 2, indicating unusual behaviour.
Data and information about the quantity of litter and the effect on the environment is scarce in the Gulf
of Guinea region, indicating the importance of research in this area. Using citizen science can be useful
in the future to ﬁll in the gaps in knowledge and to raise awareness of both the public and policymakers
to create a pathway for a cleaner and more sustainable environment.

INTRODUCTION
Littering refers to the incorrect discarding of a waste product in an inappropriate location and is a global
problem confronting the modern society [2]. Particularly, in underdeveloped countries and regions,
littering is, increasingly, a consequence of population growth, poor waste management and poor public
awareness [1]. Among the litter found in nature the majority consists of plastic, which is a continuingly
increasing challenge in the environment [18, 38, 29]. It is estimated that 4.8-12.7 million metric tonnes of
plastic enters the ocean every year [26], with single use plastic accounting for approximately 89% [42].
The rapid accumulation of plastic pollution in the environment is evidence of the anthropogenic disregard
for nature and is damaging the aquatic and terrestrial food web [10, 11], where physical entanglement
and ingestion is of high risk for animals [33]. The breakdown of mega- (>1000 mm), macro- (25-1000
mm) and meso-plastics (1-25 mm) in nature [23, 35], introduces micro- (0.001-1 mm) and nano-plastic
(<0.001 mm) particles to the environment leaving speculation about the damage that they can inﬂict.
1
==PAGE:3==
The ecological risks associated with micro- and nano-plastic particles are low, apart from some locations
in coastal waters and sediments [35]. However, if the emissions of plastic to the environment continues
at the pace currently experienced, the ecological risks might spread wider and more seriously, [36].
Global analysis has shown that the variables contributing to litter in nature are weather-related (wind-
force, water ﬂows, eg.), socioeconomic factors, population density and accessibility, where the primary
pathway for litter in the marine environment is human behaviour (littering) along rivers, creeks, streams
and storm water outlets. Areas with less access to waste management and infrastructure experience rapid
accumulation in the environment, such as the majority of countries in Africa [25]. Plastic has been used
in Africa since the 1950s, however data on waste management is challenging to come across. In Ghana
the production of solid waste ranges from 13,000 to 30,000 metric tonnes every day, and it is estimated
that 75% of this waste is discarded in dumps, in nature or is burned. It is estimated that more than one
million tonnes of plastic waste is generated in Ghana every year, however there is a lot of uncertainties
regarding this number [19]. In Ghana, and in the majority of the other countries in the Gulf of Guinea
region, the most frequently used plastics are [19];
• Low density polyethylene (LDPE, 25.3%): Carrier bags
• Polyethylene terephthalate (PET, 23.0%): Fizzy drink bottles and oven-ready meal trays
• High-density polyethylene (HDPE, 19.2%): Bottles for milk and washing-up liquids
• Polyvinyl chloride (PVC, 4.3%): Food trays, cling ﬁlm, bottles for squash, mineral water, and
shampoo
• Polystyrene (PS, 3.8%): Yoghurt pots, foam meat or ﬁsh trays, hamburger boxes and egg cartons,
vending cups, plastic cutlery, and protective packaging for electronic goods and toys
Many of the before mentioned plastic items are single-use plastics and will therefore be discarded after
only one or a few uses. Approximately 2-5% of all the plastic waste in Ghana is recycled [19].
The aforementioned challenges or possible futuristic complications with plastic litter in the environment
causes social awareness to increase, as well as scientiﬁc focus and political action, [24]. Citizen science
programs include and engage with citizens in the collection of litter in nature, and collect data to mon-
itor various nature sites, such as beaches [16]. Apart from educating and communicating to the public
about littering, citizen science projects collect knowledge to ﬁll the many gaps of research. Especially
in countries and areas with socio-economic and socio-political challenges, knowledge and information
about the amount of litter and the sources is scarce.
As a collaboration between the Technical University of Denmark and the University of Cape Coast in
connection with The HOTSPOT Project [39], a citizen science project was established with litter collec-
tion and monitoring of various locations in Ghana, in the Gulf of Guinea region. The project was initiated
to analyse and quantify litter on two beaches in the Gulf of Guinea, as well as to examine the advantages
and disadvantages of citizen science efforts. Initially the management of the project was meant to be
done on location in Ghana, but due to COVID-19, international travel was restricted and the aim of the
project had to be changed along the way [45]. The management of the citizen scientists was therefore
conducted remotely from Denmark, as was an evaluation of the remote management.
METHODOLOGY
CITIZEN SCIENCE MANAGEMENT
Due to the ongoing COVID-19 pandemic [45], the management of volunteer groups was done remotely
and online using the platform Zoom [47] with a distance from Denmark to Ghana. Ten volunteers from
the University of Cape Coast were found for the purpose of data collection and monitoring. The ten vol-
unteers were chosen due to their personal interests in, and motivations for the project and were instructed

in how to do the litter collection and monitoring, as well as being informed about the importance and
relevance of the study. This was done orally online by Power Point presentations [30] and discussions on
the Zoom platform. Furthermore, the volunteers expressed their opinion and experiences with the data
collection and the remote management. The complete list of the volunteers, as well as the educational
and motivational information about them can be found in Appendix 1. The volunteers were asked to ﬁll
out a survey after the collection days.
DATA COLLECTION
Collection dates
The data collection days were chosen to be Monday and the following Thursday three weeks in a row; the
1/3, 4/3, 8/3, 11/3, 15/3 and 18/3 in 2021. The selection of these exact days was based on the assumption
that the beaches would have more human activity during the weekend, therefore more litter could be
assumed to be evident at the beginning of the week, Monday. It was then assumed that the litter found
three days after, Thursday, would more or less represent the litter washed up on the shore from the ocean
as well as the litter travelling from rivers and streams towards the beaches. However, human activity on
the beaches from Monday to Thursday cannot be neglected entirely.
At all six days chosen, the data and litter collection began at low tide. The low tide schedule for the six
days can be found in Table 1 [40]. Weather information from the six collection days can also be found in
Table 1 [46]. The low tide time and weather data is presented for Cape Coast, this can be assumed to be
representable for Ampenyi as well, as the two location are no more than 25 kilometres from each other
[13].

Study sites
The study sites chosen prior to the data collection were a beach by the village of Ampenyi and a beach
near the larger city of Cape Coast, both located in Ghana in the Gulf of Guinea region [7] (Figure 1).
Ampenyi is a small village by the Gulf of Guinea with a population of 4,000 inhabitants. The main
occupation in Ampenyi is ﬁshing [27]. Cape Coast is located 25 kilometres east of Ampenyi [13] and is
a larger city with 143,000 inhabitants [6]. The locations were chosen to gain the perspective of a small
village with few inhabitants against a larger city with more inhabitants and activity. Before the data
and litter collection began, a 100-metre distance at the two locations were chosen. This exact distance
and location were then repeated the following data collection days. The length of 100-metres per beach
was necessary to apply with international guidelines for marine macro litter collection and monitoring
for an optimal comparative data collection [17, 8, 15]. The width of the beaches varied due to waves
and vegetation, but they are both estimated, based on the coordinates of the items collected and satellite
photos to be approximately 20-metres. Figure 2 is a graphic representation of the transects performed
during the litter and data collection.

Previous reports have calculated the amount of litter items per metre of beach covered [20, 21], however,
more recent reports have the tendency to display the amount of litter items found per area of beach
covered [12, 28]. This inconsistency in the data treatment leads to difﬁculty in data comparisons between
locations and projects. As the two locations in this project have the same width (approximately 20-
metres) a comparison by either length or area will be the same, if consistency is withheld. However, if
the beaches compared were 10-metres and 40-metres wide, respectively, yet contained the same amount
of litter, the beach of 10-metres width will appear to have four times as much litter as the beach of 40-
metre width, which can be misleading [35]. As a result, the data will be given in total amount of items,
items per metre and items per area.
Sampling protocol
The sampling protocol was given to the volunteers two weeks before the ﬁrst data and litter collection
day. The protocol included aims, locations, a schedule, appliances needed, instructions for the collection
and safety recommendations for the ﬁeld work. An online meeting with the volunteers was supposed to
clarify and discuss the protocol, however, due to poor internet connection only a few of the volunteers
participated in the meeting. As a result of this, the protocol was made as a video presentation. The
complete sampling protocol can be found in Appendix 2 and the video presentation of the protocol can
be found in Appendix 3.
4
==PAGE:6==
Included in the instruction for the data and litter collection, was information about the division of roles
on-site. Five volunteers collected data on the beach by Ampenyi and the other ﬁve volunteers went to
the beach by Cape Coast. Of these ﬁve, four were collecting the litter from the beach and one was
supervising the ﬁeld work, as well as overseeing the inputting of the data into a software application in
a smartphone. All the litter was placed into a bag and thrown out in nearby municipal waste skips at
the end of the data collection. Ensuring that the same litter collected would not be found the following
collection days.
Software application
For the data and litter collection two smartphones were used, one for each location to run the software
application Marine Debris Tracker [31]. The software application was chosen before the data and litter
collection on the basis of; availability, simplicity and conversion of data. In Appendix 4 a review of three
different marine litter tracker application can be found.
Marine Debris Tracker was developed by NOAA Marine Debris Program and is designed to help the
collection and tracking of litter by using citizen science [31]. Once an item of litter is found, the citizen
scientist will determine the material of the item and input it into the application. The application allows
the citizen scientist to choose from a list of items, including items of the materials; metal, glass, plastic,
rubber, paper and lumber, cloth and ”other”. The ”other items” category includes unidentiﬁed items, or
an item that is made of a material not listed. When the collection has ended, the application will create
an overall data set of the collected items as an Excel sheet. A guide of how to navigate and use Marine
Debris Tracker was given to the volunteers and can be found in Appendix 5.
RESULTS
DATA COLLECTED
All the items of litter collected can be found in data sets in Appendix 6 and Appendix 7.
A decrease in the total amount of litter found on the beach in Ampenyi from 2,926 items on collection
day 1, to under 339 items logged on the last day, on collection day 6 is presented in Figure 3a and Table
2. This is a decrease from 1.5 items m−2 (29.3 items m−1) to 0.2 items m−2 (3.4 items m−1). At the
beach by Ampenyi a decrease from each Monday and the corresponding Thursday is shown, with the
steepest difference from collection day 1 to collection day 2, where the items found were 2,926 and
1,158, respectively.
At the beach by Cape Coast, there was an overall decrease in the amount of items collected (Figure 3b
and Table 3). On collection day 1, 1,290 items were collected and just below 240 items where collected
the last day of collection, on collection day 6. This corresponds to an overall decrease from 0.6 items
m−2 (12.9 m−1) to 0.1 items m−2 (2.4 m−1). However, an increase from collection day 1 to collection
day 2 can be found from 1,305 to 2,025 items, respectively.

The total amount of each kind of litter; ”other”, metal, plastic, glass, cloth, paper and lumber and rubber

The total amount of items collected on the beach by Ampenyi was 7,184, 5,375 of this being plastic,

items, with a decrease after collection day 2 (Figure 4b).
Of the items presented in Figure 4c and 4d, metal, glass and cloth is the least of the items collected at

6 items collected between collection day 1 to collection day 2 (Figure 4c). At the beach by Cape Coast

during all three weeks of collection. As well as for plastic, the categories ”other”, metal, cloth and paper
and lumber increase from collection day 1 to collection day 2. After collection day 2, the categories all
decrease or remain steady from the Monday to the corresponding Thursday.

At the beach by Ampenyi ﬁlm fragments (category (8)), hard fragments (category (13)) and plastic bags
(category (16)) accounts for 26.9%, 20.5% and 10.6% of the plastic items collected, respectively, together

CITIZEN SCIENCE REMOTE MANAGEMENT
The remotely managed project was supposed to be supervised in-person, however the increasing chal-
lenges of COVID-19 and the international travel restrictions and difﬁculties forced an abrupt change of
plans. The change in plans resulted in all planning, contact with citizen scientists and execution of the
project taking place online. Contact with the volunteers was upheld on various platforms (Zoom [47], e-
mail [9] and WhatsApp [44]), and after the execution of the data and litter collection the volunteers were
asked to ﬁll out a survey with their opinions on the remotely held management, as well as the possible
difﬁculties and problems experienced during the execution of the data and litter collection. The answers
to the survey can be found in Appendix 8.
According to the answers, the volunteers seemed to be overall satisﬁed with the management and the
online communication. One volunteer expressed dissatisfaction with the provided information about dif-
ferent types of plastic and how to determine the categories. One volunteer had a comment regarding the
amount of litter, mentioning the amount of litter was too much to collect and that the ﬁeld work became
too tedious. The overall opinion about the software application were positive, with comments about
its simple navigation and that it was easy to understand, however one volunteer mentioned a difﬁculty
regarding the inability to delete data, once inputted. Only four out of all ten volunteers answered the
survey.
DISCUSSION
MARINE LITTER SOURCES
More litter in total was found at the beach by Ampenyi, where the concentration of litter ranged from 1.5
items m−2 (29.3 m−1) to 0.2 items m−2 (3.4 m−1). At Cape Coast the total amount of litter ranged from
0.6 items m−2 (12.9 m−1) to 0.1 items m−2 (2.4 m−1). Cape Coast is a much larger city, both in terms
of population and in size [6, 27], however, Cape Coast city and the beach by Cape Coast is divided by
a large road leading to less human activity. The location of collection were also a couple of kilometres
from the main beach area and the city centre. The beach by Ampenyi is located much closer to the village
of Ampenyi, where the main source of income is ﬁshing. This can give answers to the speculations of
9
==PAGE:11==
larger amounts of litter collected at the beach by Ampenyi.
The large increase in litter collected at Cape Coast on collection day 2 is not in line with the pattern of all
other collection days, where the total amount of litter decreases from the Monday to the corresponding
Thursday. An increase in the total amount of litter is found between collection day 1 and collection day
2 from 0.7 items m−2 (12.9 m−1) to 1.0 items m−2 (20.3 m−1). Looking at the weather data at collection
day 2, there is an increase in the wind and gust speed from collection day 1, that could cause stronger
waves carrying litter from the ocean to the shore. However, the increase in wind and gust speed does
not seem signiﬁcant enough for this high increase in litter at the beach. Supporting this insigniﬁcance is
that no increase in litter is experienced at the beach by Ampenyi, where wind and gust speed changed
similarly. A potential reason for an increase in litter could be some sort of event at the beach in the time
between Monday the 1st to Thursday the 4th of March, but no such event has been documented.
Of all six collection days at both locations plastic was the type of litter found most frequently, where
concentrations at the beach by Ampenyi ranged from 0.1 items m−2 (3.4 m−1) to 1.2 items m−2 (29.3
m−1), and concentration at the beach by Cape Coast ranged from 0.03 items m−2 (0.6 m−1) to 0.8 m−2
(16.0 m−1). With the suggestion that anthropogenic activity during the weekends increases the amount
of litter on the beaches, the main sources of litter found on the Mondays can be assumed to be directly
discarded by visitors. The overall decrease in amount of litter and amount of plastic items collected at
the two locations gives rise to speculation concerning the discard in nature. Logically, collection day 1
would contain more litter items as the items this day would have had the possibility of accumulating over
a long period of time, whilst the following collection days would then be a better representation of the
amount of litter items making their way to the beaches every half week. However, collection day 2 at the
beach by Cape Coast shows perplexing behaviour, as mentioned earlier.
On both beaches plastic bags were one of the three most found items, which corresponds to the knowl-
edge of the most frequently used plastic in Ghana [19] where carrier bags account for 25.3% of the
plastic used. Ghana, as well as many other countries in the Gulf of Guinea region, has a ban on plastic
bags below a certain thickness [25], which could indicate that plastic bags previously was of much larger
quantity in the environment, however it is an issue that still needs to be addressed, as plastic bags are still
found in nature. With Ampenyi village and Cape Coast city close to both beaches, it is assumed that the
plastic bags are still frequently used for shopping or storing in households. At the beach by Ampenyi,
ﬁlm fragments and hard plastic fragments, most likely being of the plastic types PVC and PET, respec-
tively, both of which are used for everyday purpose in Ghana; bottles, food trays, cling ﬁlm, food and
produce packaging, etc., all indicating rightfully that residential areas are close. Another plastic cate-
gory found of high quantity is foam fragments (category (10)), where a total of 552 items were found.
Polystyrene foam is often used by ﬁshermen as packaging and insulation for ﬁsh or as buoys and ﬂoats in
the water [37]. As Ampenyi is a village with the main occupation being ﬁshing [27], the high quantities
of foam fragments can be traced back to the activity regarding ﬁsheries in the village or the surrounding
villages.
With 143,000 inhabitants in Cape Coast [6], more litter was assumed to be found at the beach by Cape
Coast. However, a large road divide Cape Coast and the beach, and the location of which the collection
took place, where a couple of kilometres from the city centre and the popular areas by the beach. This
could indicate the lower quantities of litter found here compared to the beach by Ampenyi. At the beach
by Cape Coast buoys and ﬂoats were found of highest quantity, which could indicate a nearby populated
beach or harbour area; as buoys and ﬂoats often are used in areas of high marine trafﬁc; ﬁshing, boats,
ferries, swimming, etc. [5]. Bottle and container caps (category (3)) and beverage bottles (category (2))
were found at the beach by Cape Coast of third and fourth highest frequency, and are often made of
the plastic type PET. Even though drinking water in Ghana has improved during the last decade, many
Ghanaians still have to rely on bottled water, which could explain the high quantities of beverage bottles
and container lids for these bottles being found on the beach. The litter items found on the beach by
Cape Coast indicates urban residential close by, and as solid waste management in Cape Coast is poor
and difﬁcult to access, the environment becomes a dumping ground for the majority of the household and
everyday waste created [22]. Cape Coast is normally city with tourists [41], but COVID-19 restricted the
10
==PAGE:12==
majority of the tourist activity [45]. This can be assumed to have an impact on the amount of litter found,
where higher quantities would have been expected at peak tourist season and at normal circumstances.
Multiple beach cleaning and marine litter collection projects and initiatives have been executed in Eu-
rope [32, 38], as well as in Asia [28], New Zealand [20] and America [12, 21]. No documented data is
found for beaches in the Gulf of Guinea, and due to multiple geographical, hydrographical and societal
differences between the countries in the Gulf of Guinea region and Europe, Asia and Oceania, a compar-
ison between the data will be assumed to be somewhat inconclusive [14]. However, a project has been
performed in South Africa, where patterns of macro- and meso-plastic debris on beaches were analysed
in the years 1994, 2005 and 2015 covering a total of 82 beaches [35, 34]. As described in the reports,
the transect lengths of the beaches ranged from 5 to 80-metres with varying widths, and the report por-
trays the data per metre (m−1). The beach with the highest density of macro-plastic was an urban beach
with an average of 366 items m−1 of 66 bottle lids and caps, 52 earbuds, 39 straws and 124 pieces of
polystyrene food trays/cups [34]. Also portrayed in the study was two adjacent False Bay beaches, where
one of the beaches experienced frequent clean-ups, whereas the other beach did not. On average 35 items
of plastic m−1 were found at the uncleaned beach and 22 items of plastic were found at the beach which
frequent clean-ups. The study also showed, that 15 cm into the sand 414 items of plastic m−1 were
found at the uncleaned beach and 268 items of plastic m−1 were found at the frequently cleaned beach.
Of meso-plastic 700 items m−1 were found on average on the 82 beaches. The amount of items collected
ranged from 8 to 10,000 items m−1 on all beaches. In the items collected in this project at the beach
by Ampenyi and the beach by Cape Coast, no distinction between meso- and macro-plastic were made,
and therefore there is only a collective value of the items found. However, an average of 700 items m−1
of meso-plastic items is much higher than the amount of items m−1 on each collection day collected by
the beach by Ampenyi and the beach by Cape Coast covered in this project, were the largest density of
plastic items were on collection day 1 at the beach by Ampenyi, where 22.4 items m−1 were collected.
At the two adjacent False Bay beaches the amount of items found m−1 is more comparable to the items
found in this project, but buried plastic items were not considered in this project either. More inhabitants
live in South Africa (58.6 mio) than in Ghana (30.4 million), and the total plastic consumption in South
Africa is much larger than in Ghana [3]. According to [35] the main source of plastic litter in South
Africa was found to be from local sources, were more litter items were also found near urban sites. This
could explain the large quantities in plastic items found in the study in South Africa.
QUALITY OF DATA & LIMITATIONS
With the use of citizen science and citizen scientists there will be an elevated risk of errors and uncertain-
ties, as all individuals will have their own opinions and approaches to the project [14]. The errors and bias
occurring with the use of citizen science is determine in the difference in demographics, abilities, efforts
and motivations [4, 38]. All these categories are assumed similar or fairly similar in the citizen scientists
collecting data for this project due to age, origin and education. However, how items were identiﬁed and
collected is believed to have some variety, as the citizen scientists were not directly instructed in how to
characterise the items collected prior to the collection. The volunteers performing as citizen scientists
were divided into two groups each group collecting data and litter at a location. The two groups will
likely have different discussions and evaluations of the items found during collection, which will likely
differ from the group at the other location. With the use of citizen science, less participants will likely
cause more accurate data. However, the more participants and the larger an area enables a larger amount
of items to be collected. Therefore, the scope of the project will need to determine the amount of citizen
scientists, where knowledge and recognition of the possible errors is important when analysing the data.
As seen in the data, items of both fragments and items that appears whole, were collected throughout
the six sampling days, and a more thorough investigation of the data is needed to determine if the litter
was washed up on shore from the ocean, dumped directly on the beach by visitors or is coming upstream
from rivers and streams leading to the beaches. Items such as bottle or container caps (category (3)), food
wrappers (category (12)), plastic bags (category (16)) and straws (category (21)), that are not damaged or
11
==PAGE:13==
broken down, can be assumed to have been fairly new in the environment, where items such as fragments
(category (8), (10) and (13)) can be assumed to have been in nature for a longer period and by that have
been broken down in to fragments. Especially plastic in the ocean experience break down into smaller
pieces [23]. The litter collected from the two locations, can be of different stage and quality in terms of
how long they have been in nature. A bottle or container cap can be highly damaged, but still recognis-
able as a bottle or container cap. To determine how long every item has been in the nature, every piece
must be investigated. Therefore, a certain uncertainty is found with each item of litter collected. More
information and knowledge regarding slope of the beaches, ocean currents, waste patterns and sources
is needed to make an adequate estimate of how much litter is coming from the ocean and how much
has been thrown on-location [43]. This is a limitation to the analysis of this project, but can be used to
further approve projects in the future. If the volunteers has a more standardised identiﬁcation method,
the quality of the data would be more precise.
EVALUATION OF REMOTE MANAGEMENT
Due to the management of volunteers being quickly adjusted to be remote, the quality of the training
could probably have been better and more speciﬁc to the situation. It is assumed that each of the volun-
teers’ personal or academic interest in the project, litter management and protection of the environment
was a great motivation for the work performed in the ﬁeld. After conversations and discussions with the
volunteers, they expressed their own personal opinions and beliefs about the necessity of data and litter
collection and monitoring in the Gulf of Guinea and protection of the environment. One volunteer even
suggested doing the data and litter collection on a regular basis.
The online communication did raise trouble, as it was dependent on the internet connection, which at
times were faulty. However, the frustrations with bad internet connection resulted in other forms of
communication; such as written documents and videos. This form of ”one-sided” communication could
appear discouraging, but an addition to this was a group chat on WhatsApp [44]. This made communi-
cation much easier and less formal - resulting in more feedback and positive attitude on both ends.
A volunteer mentioned in the feedback that training about the characterising and determination of the
different plastic types and items could have been very useful in the ﬁeld. If the same baseline for the
identiﬁcation of litter, the quality of the data could be assumed better and more precise. If all ten volun-
teers identify the litter differently, multiple errors and inaccuracies can be expected.
Remote management of the citizen scientists as performed in this project opens the possibility of future
litter collection and monitoring programs being more accessible and possible to a larger scale, having the
possibility of being performed without much travelling and more inclusive of remote locations and re-
gions. In-person meetings with citizen scientists will likely highten the quality of the work they perform,
due to personal connection and motivation, but in situations where this is not possible, online communi-
cation and remote management can be done successfully, if the correct terms and conditions have been
considered and amended for the speciﬁc project.
The project did not have the objective of creating or communicating public awareness about litter, waste
management or the environmental implications of littering, but, it is likely that the volunteers were seen
on one or multiple of the collection days by citizens from Ampenyi or Cape Coast. One function of cit-
izen science is creating discussions amongst the involved, and will hopefully lead to a higher awareness
about the potential litter sources and impact to the environment, even if it only is amongst the participants
and a few bystanders.
CONCLUSIONS
The two locations in this project contained litter accumulated on every collection day, meaning that litter
had gathered within only three days (between Monday and Thursday, and then the following Monday),
assuming various sources such as directly thrown on the beach, travelling from creeks, rivers and streams
towards the beach and washed ashore from the ocean. To determine the precise amount of litter coming
12
==PAGE:14==
from each source, more detailed data and analysis is needed, as well as models regarding ocean currents
and waste ﬂows. At both locations more than 70% of the total amount of litter items were plastic items
distributed in 21 plastic categories. At the beach by Ampenyi ﬁlm fragments, hard plastic fragments,
plastic bags and foam fragments were found most frequently, whilst at the beach by Cape Coast bottle or
container caps, buoys and ﬂoats and plastic bags were found the most frequently. The amount and types
of plastic items found gives a good indication of the area, they were collected, as plastic bags, beverage
bottles and bottle and container caps correctly indicates nearby habitation, as well as foam fragments
at the beach by Ampenyi indicates ﬁshing activity, and buoys and ﬂoats indicate larger marine trafﬁc;
vessels, ﬁshing, ferries, etc., and large anthropogenic activity by the shore. More detailed analysis of
the litter items found is needed to determine the exact amount of litter transported from rivers, creeks
and streams, from the ocean or thrown directly at the beach, however, strong implications of local or
semi-local litter is suggested.
The management of the citizen science conducted in Denmark was challenging due to poor internet
connection and being distanced by a screen. However, the change in communication throughout the
project, as well as the interests from the volunteers, enabled a positive result and a respectable data set.
As learned from the project, the best data and most reliable data is obtained by remaining in contact
with the participants regularly and keeping them included in the process and progress of the project. A
”one-sided” communication approach will most likely make participants lose more interest, it is therefore
recommended based on the results of this project, to remain in contact with the citizen scientists, and to
make sure they have a personal interest or motivation for the participation, as well as feels included. The
information and instructions must be precise and to the point, as to not raise too much doubt or confusion.
As with any citizen science based project errors and uncertainties will occur due to personal bias, level
of education or opinions, however, citizen science is a method to use, if large areas need to be covered.
Citizen science can also be used to gain public awareness and contribution positively to a scientiﬁc and
social cause.
