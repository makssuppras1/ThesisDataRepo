Abstract 
Numerous studies have shown that especially poor indoor air quality is preva-
lent in Danish schools and that the indoor environmental quality [IEQ] does af-
fect the pupils’ learning ability as well as the well-being and the health of both 
pupils and teachers. It was therefore relevant to investigate possible solutions to 
the issues with poor IEQ. 
Other studies have shown that retrofit solutions targeting e.g. the sound condi-
tions in classrooms can influence the indoor air quality. It was therefore interes-
ting to investigate the association between indoor climate parameters in retro-
fitted classrooms as well. 
 
This thesis studied the IEQ and energy consumption of four retrofit solutions 
in classrooms located in Ravnsholtskolen in Allerød, north of Copenhagen. All 
retrofit solutions were relatively simple and inexpensive in order to comply 
with the limited funding for retrofitting in the municipalities.  
The retrofit solutions were different ventilation systems, aiming to increase the 
air exchange either with mechanical or hybrid ventilation systems or by 
behavioural change: 1) Decentralized ventilation with heat recovery, 2) Auto-
matic window control assisted with an exhaust fan, 3) Automatic window con-
trol assisted with heat recuperators and 4) Visual feedback of the CO2 concen-
tration in the room. The latter retrofit solution’s indoor air was exchanged 
solely dependent on manual opening of the windows, similarly to the reference 
classroom. 
 
The study of the association between indoor climate parameters was based on 
simple linear regression analysis and variance analysis of field measurements 
from the fall of 2014. 
Correlation was observed between CO2 concentration and temperature. The 
correlation between CO2 concentration and the noise level as well as between 
noise level and temperature was not convincing, however significant relations 
were observed between all three parameters with simple linear regression ana-
lysis. 
The association between CO2 concentration and noise level was investigated 
further with variance analysis and a significant association between noise level 
and CO2 concentration was shown, when other variables such as room, week-
day and lecture was taken into account. When the CO2 concentration was

ii 
above approximately 1300ppm in the classrooms, the noise level increased with 
increased CO2 concentration. The preconditions for these results as well as 
ideas for further development and use of the statistical model were discussed.  
 
The retrofit solutions were evaluated in terms of the indoor air quality, repre-
sented by CO2 concentration, and the thermal conditions, represented by air 
temperature as well as the energy consumption for space heating and ventila-
tion. The studies were based on field measurements from January 2015 and 
dynamic building simulations. 
The field measurements showed that the percentage of occupied time with CO2 
concentrations above 2000ppm was reduced in case of all the retrofit solutions 
compared to the reference classroom. Since the Working Environment Inspecto-
rate issue warnings if concentrations above 2000ppm are measured, the result 
was an improvement. 
The measured IEQ was significantly improved in the case of the automatic win-
dow control with an exhaust fan compared to the reference classroom.  
The decentralized ventilation improved only the indoor air quality significantly 
and not the thermal conditions. The thermal conditions could possibly be im-
proved with regulation of the system control.  
The automatic window control with heat recuperators and the visual feedback 
did not improve the measured IEQ significantly. 
The automatic window control with heat recuperators improved the simulated 
IEQ more compared to the automatic window control with an exhaust fan. The 
evaluation of the retrofit solutions was thus different dependent on whether it 
was based on measurements or simulations. 
In the case of the visual feedback, some changes in the behaviour were indica-
ted in the field measurements, but both the measurements and the simulated 
performance showed that the solution was not adequate in itself. Visual feed-
back could possibly be used as a supplement to other retrofit solutions. 
It was discussed whether the visual feedback should be redesigned in order to 
make it easier for occupants to distinguish acceptable CO2 concentrations from 
unacceptable. 
 
The simulated annual performance of the retrofit solutions was studied with 
different kinds of behavioural patterns. The results showed that the behaviour 
affected both the IEQ and the energy consumption.  
==PAGE:6==
 
iii 
Overall, the simulation results indicated that the levelling effect on the CO2 
concentration from systems with both supply and return airflow in the simu-
lation settings was overestimated, since the CO2 concentrations was close or 
equal to the setpoint during most of the occupied hours.  
The simulation results indicated that the driving forces were larger in the winter 
season and smaller in the summer season compared to the actual conditions: 
The simulations of the retrofit solutions, which relied mainly or entirely on na-
tural ventilation, was deviating more from the field measurements compared to 
the retrofit solution with decentralized ventilation. 
The retrofit solutions were simplified in the simulation model, and further de-
velopment of the simulation model could potentially estimate the performance 
of the retrofit solutions more accurately. 
 
The recordings of the actual energy consumption for space heating and ventila-
tion were unfortunately incomplete, but the available data indicated that the re-
corded energy consumption was generally lower than the simulated. 
The simulated energy consumption for space heating was at the same level as 
other schools of the same age and the consumption for ventilation was low 
compared to the average consumption in Danish offices. Since the classroom is 
the teacher’s working environment, the ventilation solutions were concluded to 
be energy conserving compared to other working environments. 
 
The evaluation of the retrofit solutions took the costs for the product, installa-
tion and maintenance into account as well as the noise level stated in the pro-
duct specifications. 
It was concluded that the automatic window control with an exhaust fan could 
be the recommended solution, since the decentralized ventilation and the auto-
matic window control with heat recuperators were both too noisy and the most 
expensive solutions. The observed improvements of both the indoor air quality 
and thermal conditions for these two solutions were not distinct enough to 
counterbalance the disadvantages.

Resumé  
Mange studier har vist at især dårlig luftkvalitet er udbredt i danske skoler, og at 
dårlig indeklimakvalitet har indflydelse på elevers indlæring, samt både elever og 
læreres sundhed og trivsel. Derfor var det relevant at undersøge mulige løsning-
er på indeklimaproblemerne i de danske skoler. 
Andre studier har vist at renoveringsløsninger, som har til formål at forbedre 
lydforholdene i klasseværelset, ligeledes kan have en effekt på luftkvaliteten. 
Det var derfor interessant også at undersøge sammenhængen mellem indekli-
maparametre i renoverede klasseværelser. 
 
Dette speciale undersøgte indeklimaet og energiforbruget for fire renoverings-
løsninger i klasseværelser på Ravnsholtskolen i Allerød, nord for København. 
Alle renoveringsløsningerne var relativt simple og billige, for at tage hensyn til 
kommunernes begrænsede midler til renovering af skoler. 
Renoveringsløsningerne bestod af forskellige ventilationssystemer, som havde 
til formål at øge luftskiftet ved hjælp af enten mekaniske eller hybrid ventila-
tionssystemer eller ved adfærdsændring: 1) Decentral ventilation med varme-
genvinding, 2) Automatisk styrede vinduer og udsugning, 3) Automatisk styrede 
vinduer og regenerative varmevekslere samt 4) Visuel feedback af CO2 koncen-
trationen i rummet. I den sidstnævnte løsning blev luftet udelukkende udskiftet 
ved, at vinduerne åbnedes manuelt, ligesom i reference-klasselokalet. 
 
Undersøgelserne af sammenhængen mellem indeklimaparametre var baseret på 
simpel lineær regressionsanalyse og variansanalyse af feltmålinger fra efteråret 
2014.  
Korrelation kunne observeres mellem CO2 koncentration og temperatur. Kor-
relationen mellem CO2 koncentration og støjniveau samt mellem støjniveau og 
temperatur var ikke overbevisende, men der var dog signifikante relationer 
mellem alle parametrene ved brug af lineær regressionsanalyse. 
Sammenhængen mellem CO2 koncentration og støjniveau blev undersøgt yder-
ligere med variansanalyse og denne viste en signifikant sammenhæng mellem 
CO2 koncentration og støjniveau, når andre variable såsom rum, ugedag og lek-
tion inkluderedes. Når CO2 koncentrationen var over 1300ppm i klasseværelset, 
steg støjniveauet med stigende CO2 koncentration.  
Betingelserne for disse resultater, samt ideer til yderligere udvikling og brug af 
den statistiske model, blev diskuteret. 
==PAGE:8==
 
v 
 
Renoveringsløsningerne blev evalueret i forhold til luftkvalitet, repræsenteret 
ved CO2 koncentrationen, og de termiske forhold, repræsenteret ved lufttempe-
raturen, samt i forhold til energiforbruget til rumopvarmning og ventilation. 
Undersøgelserne var baseret på målinger fra januar 2015 og dynamiske compu-
ter simuleringer. 
Feltmålingerne viste, at den procentvise andel af brugstiden med CO2 koncen-
tration over 2000ppm, var reduceret for alle renoveringsløsningerne sammen-
lignet med referencen. Da Arbejdstilsynet giver påbud, hvis der måles en kon-
centration over 2000ppm, var dette resultat en forbedring. 
Den målte indeklimakvalitet var signifikant forbedret for løsningen med auto-
matisk styrede vinduer og udsugning i forhold til reference-lokalet.  
Løsningen med decentral ventilation forbedrede luftkvaliteten signifikant, men 
dog ikke de termiske forhold. De termiske forhold kunne muligvis forbedres 
ved regulering af styringen i den decentrale ventilation.  
Løsningen med automatisk styrede vinduer og regenerative varmevekslere samt 
løsningen med visuel feedback forbedrede ikke den målte indeklimakvalitet 
signifikant. 
Løsningen med automatisk styrede vinduer og regenerative varmevekslere for-
bedrede den simulerede indeklimakvalitet i højere grad end løsningen med auto-
matisk styrede vinduer og udsugning.  
Evalueringen af renoveringsløsningerne afhang dermed af, om den var baseret 
på målinger eller simuleringer. 
For løsningen med visuel feedback tydede målingerne på nogen grad af ad-
færdsændring, men både målinger og simuleringer viste, at løsningen ikke var 
tilstrækkelig i sig selv. Visuel feedback kunne muligvis anvendes, som et supple-
ment til en anden renoveringsløsning. 
Det blev diskuteret, om den visuelle feedback burde redesignes, så det bliver 
tydeligere for brugerne at kende forskel på acceptabelt og uacceptabelt CO2 
koncentration. 
 
Renoveringsløsningerne blev simuleret for hele året med forskellig bruger- 
adfærd. Simuleringsresultaterne viste, at adfærden havde en indflydelse på både 
indeklimaet og energiforbruget.  
Resultaterne indikerede, at simuleringerne overdrev den udjævnende effekt på 
CO2 koncentrationen fra systemer med både indblæsning og udsugning i

vi
simuleringsindstillingerne, da CO2 koncentrationen var meget tæt på setpunktet 
i det meste af brugstiden. 
Simuleringsresultaterne indikerede, at drivkræfterne muligvis var større i op-
varmningssæsonen og mindre uden for opvarmningssæsonen i forhold til de 
faktiske forhold: Simuleringsresultaterne for løsninger, som var delvist eller 
udelukkende afhængig af naturlig ventilation, var mere forskellig fra målingerne 
end de var for løsningen med decentral ventilation. 
Renoveringsløsningerne blev simplificeret i simuleringsmodellen og yderligere 
optimering af simuleringsmodellen ville sandsynligvis gøre estimeringen af 
renoveringsløsningernes performance mere præcis. 
 
Målingerne af det faktiske energiforbrug til rumopvarmning og ventilation var 
desværre mangelfuld, men den brugbare data indikerede, at det faktiske energi-
forbrug generelt set var lavere end det simulerede. 
Det simulerede energiforbrug til rumopvarmning var på niveau med andre 
skoler af samme alder, og energiforbruget til ventilation var lavt sammenlignet 
med et gennemsnitligt forbrug for et dansk kontor. Da klasseværelset er lærer-
ens arbejdsmiljø, blev det konkluderet, at ventilationsløsningerne var energi-
effektive i forhold til andre arbejdspladser. 
 
Evalueringen af renoveringsløsningerne inkluderede omkostninger til produkt, 
installation og vedligeholdelse, samt det støjniveau, som var angivet i produkt--
specifikationen. 
Konklusionen var, at løsningen med automatisk styrede vinduer og udsugning 
kunne være den mest anbefalelsesværdige løsning, da den decentrale ventilation 
og løsningen med automatisk styrede vinduer og regenerative varmevekslere 
både var for støjende og de dyreste. De observerede forbedringer af luftkvali-
teten og de termiske forhold for disse to løsninger, var ikke udprægede nok til 
at opveje ulemperne.

  Introduction 
Children are more sensitive to poor indoor environmental quality than adults 
(Hillig 2013). Still, many pupils spend the first and important ten years of their 
education in an indoor environment, which would not be accepted by adults 
(Toftum et al. 2010).  
 
The Danish Working Environment Inspectorate [Arbejdstilsynet] visited 1625 of 
the 1918 primary schools in Denmark in 2014 and issued 1144 warnings. In 567 
schools, which is more than one third of the visited schools, more than one 
warning were issued (Munksgaard & Hein 2014). 
 
In 2009 a study of a broad section of the Danish public schools were carried 
out. More than 700 classes participated in the study, which therefore is one of 
the most comprehensive studies of the indoor environment in Danish schools. 
The study is referred to as the mass study. 
Momentary measurements of the CO2 concentration and temperature were 
carried out once a week at the end of a lesson (Clausen et al. 2014). 
The mass study showed insufficient air exchange in the classrooms. The CO2 
concentration was above the recommended maximum limit of 1000 ppm in 
66% of the classrooms. The proportion was 16% and 21% in similar mass 
studies in Sweden and Norway respectively (Menå & Larsen 2010). 
The mass study was repeated in 2014, and 60% of the classrooms still had too 
high CO2 concentration (Clausen et al. 2014). 
 
The poor Programme for International Student Assessment [PISA] results from 
Danish schools have been discussed broadly in the political arena as well as in 
==PAGE:19==
Background 
3
the media, especially because the results were remarkably poorer compared to 
Sweden, Norway and Finland.  
The poor PISA results and the prevalence of too high CO2 concentrations in 
Danish classrooms shown in the mass study indicated an influence on the 
learning ability from too high CO2 concentrations. 
The first mass study was followed by investigations of the association between 
indoor environmental quality [IEQ] and national test results in 17% of the 
Danish public schools (Kjeldsen et al. 2013).  
The study showed that the schools with mechanical ventilation had the best 
results. The next-best results came from schools with exhaust, whereas the 
schools with natural ventilation had the poorest results. The difference in the 
results between the schools with mechanical and natural ventilation was 
statistically significant (Kjeldsen et al. 2013). 
 
The aforementioned studies were partly responsible for public discussions re-
garding the poor IEQ in Danish schools, in particular in terms of the indoor air 
quality. 
The well-documented poor issues with IEQ in the schools became a topic in 
the Danish media to a degree, where it could not be ignored in the political 
discussions about the Danish education system’s quality and international 
prestige. 
In November 2014 a left-wing party Enhedslisten suggested a bill regarding 
stricter control of the indoor environment quality in schools, followed by a 
similar bill, which was suggested by a right-wing party Dansk Folkeparti a month 
later (Lauritsen 2014). Dansk Folkeparti’s bill were passing the first reading on 
March 26th 2015 (Folketinget 2015). 
1.1 
Background 
Most public schools are not able to allocate funding for renovation in a typical 
budget, and the available funding is often used on basic renovation – not on 
indoor environment and energy optimizing renovation (Munksgaard & Hein 
2014). 
 
A study from 2014 showed that the indoor environment was not improved 
sufficiently in newly renovated and even newly built schools (Wohlgemuth & 
Christensen 2014). 
==PAGE:20==

The study was carried out as an intervention study over a period of six weeks in 
multiple classrooms in three schools: an old naturally ventilated, an old but 
newly renovated school with mechanical ventilation and a newly built school 
with mechanical ventilation (Wohlgemuth & Christensen 2014). 
The CO2 concentration was above 1000ppm in a large proportion of time in 
the mechanically ventilated classrooms (Wohlgemuth & Christensen 2014). 
The findings of the study indicated that e.g. the integration of ventilation solu-
tions in both existing and newly built Danish schools might be poorly designed 
and executed. 
The reason for the unsuccessful renovations might partly come as a result of 
inadequate funding for the renovation projects. 
The buildings of many public schools are diverse in terms of age and typology 
(Wohlgemuth & Christensen 2014). This circumstance is another factor, which 
increases the complexity of public school renovation. 
Lastly, the unsuccessful renovations might be due to missing knowledge about 
efficient retrofitting of schools and how to overcome the challenges from the 
limited funding without compromising the quality of the renovation. 
 
This thesis aims to evaluate different ventilation retrofit solutions, which are 
simpler and less expensive than solutions such as large centralized ventilation 
systems.

1.1.1 Introduction to indoor environmental quality 
The indoor environment quality depends on the following quantitative para-
meters (Kjeldsen et al. 2013): 
==PAGE:21==
Background 
5
• 
Indoor air quality, which is described by e.g. the concentration of 
gasses, dust and particles 
• 
Thermal conditions, which is described by e.g. the air temperature, 
radiant temperature, air velocity and humidity 
• 
Lighting conditions, which is described by e.g. illuminance, colour, 
contrasts and reflections 
• 
Sound conditions, which is described with e.g. sound pressure level, 
impact sound level, reverberation time and speech intelligibility 
These parameters can be measured and compared to the requirements in force. 
In rooms with high person density, such as classrooms, the most significant 
pollution source is the occupants. In these types of rooms, the CO2 concentra-
tion is a good indicator of poor indoor air quality (Bidstrup 2014). 
 
Among the occupants in a room, poor indoor environmental quality is charac-
terized by discomfort and possibly symptoms such as irritated eyes, skin irrita-
tion, heavy head, cognitive difficulties and general indisposition (Toftum et al. 
2010). These symptoms are referred to as Sick Building Syndrom [SBS], but in 
general the perception of the indoor environmental quality is highly subjective 
(Kjeldsen et al. 2013). 
1.1.2 Influence from poor indoor environmental quality on pupils’ 
learning ability and well-being 
Many studies show that the indoor environment quality influences the pupils’ 
learning ability (Kjeldsen et al. 2013; Hygge 2003). 
 
In 2004 and 2005 two independent intervention studies in public school class-
rooms were carried out. The air supply to the classrooms was manipulated in 
terms of air exchange rate, speed and temperature for one week at a time in a 
blind crossover design (Wargocki & Wyon 2007). It was found that by decreas-
ing the air temperature in the classroom to approximately 20°C, the pupils 
solved both language-based and numerical tasks faster without affecting the 
error rate. The pupils moreover reported significantly less headache at the 
lower temperature (Wargocki & Wyon 2007).  
In 2010 it was concluded that pupils’ performance in tasks, which requires full 
concentration, increases with 2-4% for every 1°C decrease from 25°C in the air 
temperature (Toftum et al. 2010). 
 
==PAGE:22==
Introduction  
6 
The influence from high CO2 concentrations on pupils’ learning ability was 
investigated in 8 primary schools in England in 2011 (Bakó-Biró et al. 2011). 
More than 200 pupils participated in the intervention experiments, which lasted 
over a period of three weeks. The pupils were usually exposed to poor indoor 
air quality conditions with CO2 concentrations of up to 5000ppm. As a result 
of increased air exchange rate, the pupils’ became significantly faster and the 
error rate was significantly reduced compared to the results without increased 
air exchange rate. The pupils were tested in choice reaction, colour word vigi-
lance, picture memory and word recognition (Bakó-Biró et al. 2011).  
 
In terms of sound conditions it has been shown that pupils’ attention is re-
duced in noisy environments and that high noise levels therefore have a major 
impact on communication and the ability to remember (Mydlarz et al. 2012). A 
noisy teaching environment requires more cognitive resources than usual in 
order to understand the message from the teacher (Ljung et al. 2013).  
It is subjective, which noise level is acceptable in a teaching environment and 
for concentrating (Larsen 2015), but it is possible that sensitive and multi-
language children will have cognitive problems even when the recommenda-
tions are fulfilled. 
 
Besides the influence from poor IEQ on pupils’ learning ability, studies show 
that poor IEQ influences the pupils’ as well as the teachers’ well-being and 
health. 
One third of the Danish children suffer from allergy or asthma, and symptoms 
covered by chronic illnesses may be increased with poor indoor air quality and 
inadequate ventilation (Toftum et al. 2010). The improvement of the indoor air 
quality in Danish schools is therefore crucial. 
 
A study in California investigated 162 classrooms in primary schools with 
different levels of air exchange rate. The CO2 concentration and absence due to 
illness were measured and recorded over a period of two years (Mendell et al. 
2013).  
It was found that the absence was reduced significantly with a decreased CO2 
concentration and increased ventilation rate (Mendell et al. 2013). 
This finding was substantiated with studies of more than 17% of the Danish 
public schools, where the association between absence and indoor air quality 
was found to be statistically significant (Kjeldsen et al. 2013). The mechanically 
==PAGE:23==

ventilated schools and schools with exhaust had a lower absence compared to 
naturally ventilated schools. The difference between the naturally ventilated 
schools and the schools with mechanical ventilation or exhaust would corre-
spond to 80 and 160 less days of absence per year respectively (Kjeldsen et al. 
2013). 
 
Thus, the positive effect from reduced indoor air temperature, CO2 concentra-
tions, and noise level on pupils’ learning ability and well-being has been well-
documented in both national and international studies.  
1.1.3 Challenges and effects from ventilation retrofit solutions in 
classrooms 
Numerous aspects have to be taken into account, when ventilation retrofit so-
lutions are integrated in existing public schools. 
The retrofit solution has to be integrated in a way that is beneficial to the 
indoor environmental quality and supports expedient user behaviour. 
Moreover, there are practical aspects such as the space requirements as well as 
installation, operation and maintenance costs.  
 
In 2010 a study of 11 schools in Tallinn compared three ventilation systems to 
each other. The ventilation types were natural ventilation, decentralized ventila-
tion with heat recovery and centralized mechanical ventilation with heat reco-
very. It was found that decentralized mechanical ventilation with heat recovery 
was the most suitable solution for existing schools due to the flexibility, the 
minimal space requirements, the degree of improvement of the indoor air qua-
lity and the energy efficiency (Mikola et al. 2010). 
 
Integration of natural ventilation solutions, where the windows are manually 
opened, gives rise to different challenges, e.g. the efficiency depends on the 
behavioural patterns of the pupils and teachers. 
Studies show that humans are better at distinguishing temperature increase or 
decrease, whereas the build-up of CO2 concentration and human bio effluents 
are not noticed as easy by the occupants in a room. The visitors will however 
notice the poor air quality (Toftum et al. 2010; Bidstrup 2014). 
In a study from 2010 the pupils and teachers opened the windows, when the 
temperature increased, but not when the CO2 concentration increased (Wyon 
et al. 2010). It has therefore been shown that occupants tend to react on the 
thermal conditions rather than poor indoor air quality. 
==PAGE:24==
Introduction  
8 
This circumstance challenges the adequate user behaviour towards improve-
ment of the indoor air quality in naturally ventilated classrooms, unless habits 
of scheduled manual ventilation or other means are introduced. 
 
In the spring of 2014 an intervention study was carried out in three Danish 
public schools. The CO2 concentration and temperature reduced in the class-
rooms, where the occupants were instructed to open the windows in all breaks, 
thus the indoor climate quality was improved in these classrooms compared to 
the classrooms with typical user behaviour (Wohlgemuth & Christensen 2014). 
However, it is entirely up to the occupants to follow instructed behavioural 
patterns like the aforementioned, and if their habits are changed it is still up to 
them to assess the most adequate time for air exchange throughout the seasons 
of the year. 
In relation to noise issues, a retrofit solution, which is based on natural ventila-
tion, might conflict with the outdoor noise level if the public school is located 
in e.g. the city centre (Hillig 2013), since noise might prevent the occupants 
from opening the windows (Montazami et al. 2012). 
 
Retrofitting of existing schools gives rise to different challenges, besides the 
practical ones such as costs and space requirements, when the retrofitting in-
cludes mechanical ventilation solutions. Too high mechanical ventilation rate 
and poorly designed ventilation systems can cause noise issues from the 
installations.  
Mechanical ventilation systems might also conflict with the behaviour of the 
occupants e.g. in the case of heat recovery, since opening of windows can re-
duce the benefits from the heat recovery’s energy efficient property. 
Automatic control of the indoor environmental quality does narrow the occu-
pants’ behavioural options even though the control might improve the indoor 
IEQ. 
 
A study from 2013 of the indoor environment quality before and after renova-
tion of two schools in Århus in Denmark showed a significant association be-
tween the noise level and CO2 concentration. The noise level increased with in-
creased CO2 concentration. The renovation included acoustic treatment of the 
classrooms and thus reduced the noise level, but the renovation had a beneficial 
effect on the CO2 concentration as well (Toftum et al. 2014). 
 
==PAGE:25==
Background 
9
Another study from 2014 investigated the correlation between CO2 concentra-
tion and noise level in measurement data from a school in Allerød in Denmark. 
Different retrofit solutions, both natural, hybrid and mechanical ventilation 
systems were installed in the school (Larsen 2015). 
The aim of the study was to substantiate that the retrofit solutions reduced the 
CO2 concentration as well as the noise level. The hypothesis was that pupils’ 
become unfocused from high CO2 concentration and as a result of this 
becomes more noisy (Larsen 2015). 
The study did not show correlation between CO2 concentration and noise level, 
but it was suggested that other statistical methods such as multi-variable models 
could be applied to the measurement data. It was also suggested that the analy-
sis was made entirely on data from the occupied time (Larsen 2015). 
1.1.4 Recommended indoor environment in Danish schools 
The teaching environment law ensures legal rights for children in Danish 
schools. Unfortunately, the law is weakly formulated compared to the working 
environment laws, which apply for the teachers’ working environment (Larsen 
2015). The pupils are therefore mainly covered indirectly by the working 
environment laws (Hillig 2013).  
The work environment assessment [Arbejdspladsvurdering], which assesses 
whether the requirements of the working environment laws are met, is carried 
out every third year (Bidstrup 2014; Arbejdstilsynet 2015). CO2 concentration 
above 2000 ppm will result in warnings from the Danish Working Environ-
ment Inspectorate (Hillig 2013). 
The administration of the public school is responsible for the teaching and 
working environment to comply with the applicable requirements (DCUM 
Dansk Center for Undervisningsmiljø 2015). 
 
The building code “BR10” is not mandatory for existing buildings, but applies 
for newly built schools (Energistyrelsen 2014a). 
 
The standard DS3033 – Voluntary classification of the quality of indoor climate in 
residential houses, schools, children’s day-care centres and offices classifies the 
indoor environment in 5 quality classes, A++, A+, A, B and C, where C repre-
sents “the poor indoor environment” and A represents the minimum require-
ments necessary to fulfil the requirements in the building code (Dansk Standard 
2011).  As the standard’s title indicates, the classification is voluntary and it is

Generally for the statistical analyses 
The probability values were considered significant at p<5%. The variance ana-
lyses were performed on the measurement data from the occupied time only. 
 
The variance analysis of model 1 and 2 included interaction between the varia-
bles. Interaction refers to a situation, where one variable depends on another, 
which often is the case in field measurements. If the p-value of the interaction 
==PAGE:49==
Measurements 
33 
between two variables is below the significance level, there is no interaction 
(Fowler et al. 1998). 
 
All ANOVA models were inspected in order to ensure that the preconditions 
were met.  
Due to the Central Limit Theorem and the large number of observations in the 
measurement data, the data did not have to be normally distributed, but could 
be assumed to follow a normal distribution.  
The residuals should still be normally distributed (Bacher & Brockhoff 2014). 
 
Figure 17 – Principle of Q-Q plots: Residuals approaching the straight line 
The normality of the residuals were checked with a quantile-quantile plot, which 
shows the sample quantiles as a function of the theoretical quantiles; the quan-
tiles of the model. The residuals should approach a linear relation between the 
sample quantiles and the theoretical quantiles (Bacher & Brockhoff 2014). 
 
The residuals should also be randomly and symmetrically distributed around 0 
on the y-axis, when plotted as a function of the fitted values (Bacher & 
Brockhoff 2014). 
 
A way to double-check the absence of underlying dependencies in the model is 
to study the residuals as a function of the independent variables individually. 
The residuals should be symmetrically distributed around 0 in all variable 
groups and at least the medians should be close to 0 corresponding to small 
errors, generally referred to as variance homogeneity (Bacher & Brockhoff 
2014). 
 
The preconditions are thus: 
• 
Normally distributed residuals 
• 
Randomly distributed residuals as a function of the fitted values 
• 
Variance homogeneity

The third simulation will be referred to as the simulation of “worst-case” user 
behaviour, since the manually controlled windows were set to open only, when

  Discussion and Conclusion 
This chapter summarises and discusses the main findings. Perspectives for 
future studies are mentioned as well. 
 
Association between indoor climate parameters 
The initial statistical analyses were inadequate to show convincing associations 
between the indoor climate parameters and most importantly, to reveal the 
underlying variables and interactions influencing the associations.  
The variance analyses [ANOVA] were more suitable for studying of the field 
measurements, as the association between the indoor climate parameters was 
not a two-dimensional association, but rather a multi-dimensional association. 
 
The ANOVA model showed that the noise level was significantly dependent 
on the CO2 concentration, temperature, room, weekday and lecture, which e.g. 
indicated an association between the CO2 concentration and noise level in the 
classrooms (p<5∙10-4, n=7610). The model explained 38% of the variance.  
The analyses furthermore indicated that the CO2 concentration and noise level 
were simultaneously increasing, when the CO2 concentration was more than 
1300ppm. It was not possible to show that the CO2 concentration and noise 
level increased simultaneously when the CO2 concentration was lower than 
1300ppm. 
The reason might have been that the results were based on measurement data 
for all of the classrooms including the reference classroom. Since the aim was 
to show a positive effect on both the CO2 concentration and the noise level 
from retrofitting, the five classrooms could have been studied individually with 
more post-hoc comparisons. The analysis showed that e.g. the average noise 
==PAGE:105==
 
89 
level in at least on classroom was significantly different than the average noise 
level in the others, since the room variable was significant in the ANOVA 
model. 
Overall, the retrofit solutions could have been evaluated further based on the 
model. 
 
The ANOVA model only described the conditions in the specific classrooms in 
Ravnsholtskolen in the specific measurement period in 2014. The association 
between the CO2 concentration and noise level would not have been significant 
if e.g. the windows were open during most of the occupied time in a classroom 
in the city, since the CO2 concentration would in this case decrease and the 
noise level would increase.  
 
For future studies, the model could be developed by including variables for e.g. 
the outdoor climate, the activity in the classroom, the opening of windows and 
doors as well as the number of occupants in each lecture. In this way it would 
be possible to carry out a more subtle and comprehensive study of the influ-
ence on the indoor climate parameters from the retrofit solutions. 
If similar studies were to be performed in classrooms of deviating volumes, the 
volume should also be included in the model, since it affects both the CO2 con-
centration and the noise level. 
 
Evaluation of the retrofit solutions – Measurements 
The measurements of indoor environmental quality in January 2015 showed 
that the average CO2 concentration in the classroom with decentralized ventila-
tion (S3) was significantly lower than in the other rooms. The percentage of 
time within the recommended CO2 level was moreover the largest in the case 
of the decentralized ventilation. On the contrary, the temperature was too high. 
 
In the measurements, the automatic window control with an exhaust fan (S4) 
provided a significantly lower average CO2 concentration compared to the 
other retrofit solution with automatic window control (S5), which had heat 
recuperators. In the case of the latter retrofit solution, the average CO2 concen-
tration was not significantly lower than in the reference room (S7), and no 
significant improvements to the average level could be seen for the automatic 
window control with heat recuperators.  
==PAGE:106==
Discussion and Conclusion  
90 
The retrofit solutions with automatic window control (S4 and S5) had the best 
results in terms of the thermal conditions as the temperature was inside the 
recommended range during almost all of the occupied time. 
 
The retrofit solution with visual feedback (S8) had the poorest results in terms 
of both CO2 concentration and temperature, even though the percentage of the 
occupied time with CO2 concentration above 2000ppm was reduced.  
 
The measurements showed that the CO2 concentration and temperature was in 
general surprisingly low in the reference classroom (S7), which challenged the 
possibility of showing improving effects from any of the retrofit solutions.  
 
The analyses of the recordings of opening of the windows and doors proved to 
be quite complex, especially in the attempt to substantiate the reason for the 
opening and closing. It was however possible to observe some tendencies from 
the recordings:  
The façade windows in the classrooms without automatic window control were 
rarely open compared to the automatically controlled windows. This showed 
that the pupils and teachers did not open the windows as often as required in 
terms of the CO2 concentration.  
The recordings indicated that the automatic window control system (S4 and S5) 
in some cases by-passed the window opening during several hours when the 
CO2 concentration was above 1000ppm. Since the occupant bypass was 
affective in maximum 15 minutes, it was concluded that the system bypassed 
the opening of the windows due to the low outdoor temperatures.  
The recordings also indicated that the system was possibly faulty, since the win-
dows did not open in accordance with the time control of the system. It was 
observed that the windows opened at 7am even though the CO2 concentration 
was not above the setpoint of 800ppm, and in other cases the windows did not 
open when the CO2 concentration was above the setpoint in the beginning of 
the clock hour. 
For future studies it would be beneficial to analyse the system data for the auto-
matic window control system, especially in terms of differences between the 
measured CO2 concentration in the system compared to the measured CO2 
concentration from the HOBO loggers as well as the occupant bypass of the 
system. 
 
==PAGE:107==
 
91 
The recordings furthermore indicated that the windows were not open in 
accordance with the guideline for the visual feedback in classroom S8. Spot 
checks showed that the windows were closed during time with CO2 concentra-
tions above 1000ppm. The reason might have been due to prioritizing of the 
teacher or simply to the low outdoor temperatures.  
The study indicated that the external door might have been used for ventilation 
instead of the windows in the classroom with visual feedback.  
 
The openable window area in the classrooms with dedicated ventilation systems 
is larger than in the room with visual feedback, which made it even more 
challenging to achieve good results in the case of the visual feedback, where the 
air exchange was exclusively based on manual opening of windows and doors. 
Comparing the classroom with visual feedback to the reference room, some 
effect of the visual feedback was seen, as the percentage of time with open win-
dows and doors was larger than in the reference room.  
Overall, it was shown that the visual feedback was insufficient on its own in 
terms of improving the indoor environmental quality in the classroom.  
 
It was not possible to substantiate that the pupils and teachers were better at 
perceiving the indoor air temperature compared to the air quality, since the 
windows were not open during time with high temperatures and the spot 
checks did not reveal any tendencies.  
The occupants might not have considered temperatures above 23°C as un-
acceptable even though they are unacceptable according to the requirements. 
If the temperatures had been higher e.g. if the measurements were carried out 
during a warmer season, a more consistent tendency could possibly have been 
observed. 
 
The minimum measured CO2 concentration was as low as 250ppm during the 
occupied time, which indicated measurement errors. The spot checks of the 
CO2 concentration during random school days were in some cases very low, 
which indicated that the classrooms were occupied during time outside the 
class schedule and or unoccupied during time, where occupancy had been 
assumed. This meant that the definition of the occupied time did not necessa-
rily correspond to the actual time with presence of pupils and teachers in the 
classrooms. This is therefore a precaution in relation to the assessment of the 
measured indoor environmental quality.

Discussion and Conclusion  
92 
For future studies it is recommended to somehow record the number of occu-
pants in each lecture during the measurement period instead of defining the 
occupied time based on the class schedule. In this way the sensitivity to 
changed schedules can be avoided. 
 
The recordings of the energy consumption for space heating were discontinu-
ous or incomplete for most of the classrooms, and the consumption was not 
recorded for the reference classroom and classroom with visual feedback. 
Overall it was not possible to draw any sound conclusions from the recordings 
in terms of differences in the consumption for space heating. 
The energy consumption for ventilation was almost identical for the classrooms 
with dedicated ventilation systems in January 2015. 
 
Evaluation of the retrofit solutions – Calibrated simulation model 
The validation of the calibrated model showed both similarities and differences 
compared to the field measurements. 
 
The occupancy in the simulations was based on the class schedule, and since 
the actual occupancy was not necessarily following the class schedule during all 
of January, the simulated CO2 concentration deviated from the measurements 
in some cases. 
For future studies it would be recommended to assume some degree of occu-
pancy in the breaks between the lectures in order to create a more conservative 
estimation of the indoor air quality. 
 
The simulated CO2 concentration in the classroom with decentralized ventila-
tion (S3) were more or less 1000ppm corresponding to the setpoint during all 
of the occupied time. The percentage of time with CO2 concentrations below 
1000ppm was similar to the measurements.  
The simulated temperatures were generally lower than the measured, but the 
percentage of time with temperatures within the recommended range was al-
most the same as in the measurements.  
 
The simulated CO2 concentration was generally higher in the case of the class-
room with exhaust (S4) and lower in the case of the classroom with heat recu-
perators (S5) compared to the measurements. This resulted in an opposite eva-
luation of the two classrooms compared to the measurements: The automatic 
window control with exhaust (S4) has much less with CO2 concentrations be-
low 1000ppm and the automatic window control with heat recuperators (S5) 
==PAGE:109==
 
93 
provided almost double the time with CO2 concentrations below 1000ppm 
compared to the measurements.  
The temperatures in the classrooms with automatic window control (S4 and S5) 
were overall almost the same as in the measurements. 
 
Generally, the simulated CO2 concentration of the retrofit solutions with both 
supply and exhaust airflow in the simulations settings (S3 and S5), was varying 
less compared to the measurements, since the IQR was smaller than in the 
measurements. 
 
The classroom with visual feedback (S8) had better results compared to the 
measurements in terms of both the CO2 concentration and the temperature. 
It was not possible to recreate as good indoor environmental quality in the refe-
rence classroom (S7) as it was observed in the field measurements.  
 
During the occupied time, the temperature often decreased with up to 14°C in 
the classrooms without dedicated ventilation systems (S7 and S8), when the 
windows opened, which resulted in an increase of occupied time with tempe-
ratures below 20°C. 
 
The simulated energy consumption was larger than the recorded in the case of 
all the classrooms. In some cases the consumption for space heating was more 
than double the measured energy consumption.  
Since the recorded energy consumption for space heating was discontinuous or 
incomplete the comparison between the calibrated model and the recordings 
were difficult. However, the simulation generally overestimated the energy con-
sumption. 
 
Overall, it was difficult to simulate the retrofit solutions and the reference room 
in order to approximate the actual conditions in January 2015. It turned out to 
be easier to simulate the classrooms with dedicated ventilation systems com-
pared to the classrooms solely dependent on air exchange from manual ope-
ning of windows and doors, which indicated that the driving forces of natural 
ventilation were different in the simulation compared to the actual conditions.

Evaluation of the retrofit solutions – Annual simulations  
The annual simulations showed that the user behaviour had a large impact on 
the results and the performance of the different retrofit solutions. 
 
The CO2 concentration was acceptable during almost all of the occupied time 
in the case of the decentralized ventilation (S3). The only exception was for the 
simulation of the worst-case user behaviour for which the CO2 concentration 
was acceptable during approximately 2/3 of the occupied time. Thus, the de-
centralized ventilation could not compensate for the inadequate user behaviour, 
and that the automatic window control ensured that the CO2 concentration was 
more acceptable than in the rooms where the windows rarely opened. 
 
In terms of the automatic window control (S4 and S5), the benefits from this 
system on the CO2 concentration were more distinct in the case of the simula-
tion of the worst-case behaviour. However, the temperatures were too low in 
the simulations of both the best- and worst-case user behaviour. The tempera-
tures were more acceptable in the simulation with calibration settings except for 
June and August, where the temperature was above the recommended range. 
 
The indoor environmental quality was generally more acceptable in the case of 
the visual feedback (S8) compared to the reference room (S7).  
In the simulation of worst-case behaviour, the CO2 concentration in the room 
with visual feedback (S8) was higher during the unheated season compared to 
the heated season, which was surprising. 
The theory was that the driving forces for the natural ventilation in the simulat-
ions were underestimated in the summer season and overestimated in the win-
ter season, which was indicated in the rapid decrease in the indoor temperature 
observed in simulations of the month of January. 
 
In terms of the energy consumption, the consumption for space heating gene-
rally increased as the proportion of time with open windows increased, which 
was as expected.  
In the case of the simulation of worst-case user behaviour, the consumption for 
space heating was approximately halved in all classrooms except for the ones 
with automatic window control (S4 and S5) compared to the simulation with 
calibration settings.  
==PAGE:111==
 
95 
The consumption for space heating was in general smaller in the case of the de-
centralized ventilation (S3) compared to the other retrofit solutions, however, 
the temperature in this classroom were also too high. 
 
The energy consumption for ventilation was more or less the same in all the 
annual simulations in the case of the classrooms with an exhaust fan (S4) and 
heat recuperators (S5).  
The consumption for ventilation increased vaguely in the classroom with de-
centralized ventilation (S3) as the windows were less frequently open, since the 
ventilation attempted to compensate for the inadequate user behaviour. 
 
Compared to an average Danish office, all the retrofit solutions proved to be 
quite energy conserving ventilation solutions. The energy consumption for 
space heating was quite large, which was as expected, since the south-wing of 
Ravnsholtskolen was built in 1980. The energy consumption for space heating 
was at the same level as other schools of the same age.  
Overall, is was estimated that the total energy consumption could remain un-
changed or improve with two energy classes in the case of retrofitting with one 
of the studied retrofit solutions. This assessment was based on the assumption 
that the consumption for lighting was 25% of the total energy consumption 
including energy factors. 
 
Overall evaluation of the retrofit solutions 
The measurements and the simulations showed different performances of the 
retrofit solutions, both in terms of the indoor environmental quality and the 
energy consumption. 
 
Generally, the CO2 concentration varied less in the classroom with decentra-
lized ventilation (S3) compared to the other retrofit solutions. The decentra-
lized ventilation provided good indoor air quality both in the case of the 
measurements and simulations of January 2015. The indoor air quality was 
good as well, in the case of the annual simulation with calibration settings.  
The decentralized ventilation was generally not able to provide temperatures in 
the recommended range, since the temperatures were too high. The demand 
for space heating by the radiators and convectors were therefore generally low 
compared to the other solutions with dedicated ventilation system (S4 and S5). 
The thermal condition could possibly be improved with regulation of the 
control system and heat recovery. 
==PAGE:112==
Discussion and Conclusion  
96 
 
Compared to the decentralized ventilation the solutions with automatic window 
control (S4 and S5) were generally performing well in terms of the thermal con-
ditions, especially in the January measurements and simulation.  
The annual performance showed that if the automatically controlled windows 
were always open in accordance with the CO2 concentration setpoint, the tem-
peratures would become too low. The automatic window control was however 
simplified in the annual simulations, and in the actual control the outdoor tem-
perature would be taken into account. 
The automatic window control with an exhaust fan (S4) was generally perfor-
ming better in terms of the indoor air quality compared to the retrofit solution 
with heat recuperators (S5), except for the annual simulations with calibration 
settings.  
 
The visual feedback alone was not sufficient to improve the indoor air quality 
and thermal conditions compared to the reference classroom (S7). Even though 
some effect was vaguely indicated in the measurements, the retrofit solution 
was not improving the IEQ compared to the other retrofit solutions.  
The annual performance of the visual feedback (S8) was only improving the 
IEQ, since it was not possible to recreate the quite acceptable conditions in the 
reference classroom. 
 
It could be discussed whether the CO2 scale should be redesigned in order to 
make it easier for occupants to distinguish acceptable CO2 concentration from 
unacceptable. The current design has yellow diodes when the CO2 concentra-
tion is 1000-1600ppm, even though the maximum limit is 1000ppm according 
to the requirements and thus this range is unacceptable. Yellow was perhaps 
associated with “no need for action” or “nearly acceptable”, since the CO2 scale 
also had red diodes from 1600ppm and above. If e.g. the diodes were yellow 
for 800-1000ppm and red for 1000ppm and above, the occupants would possi-
bly act more expedient to the visual feedback. Also, it might not be an 
expedient solution to leave the classroom in the middle of the class, when the 
CO2 concentration is above 1600ppm as the current guideline suggests.  
For future studies it would be recommended to redesign the CO2 scale as study 
whether it has an effect on the user behaviour and CO2 concentration. 
 
==PAGE:113==
 
97 
Taking the costs for products, installation and maintenance into account, the 
automatic window control with either exhaust (S4) or heat recuperators (S5) 
could be the most adequate solution. 
However, the product cost for the solutions with heat recuperators was almost 
as high as for the decentralized ventilation (S3). 
The noise level of both the decentralized ventilation (S3) and the heat recupera-
tors (S5) moreover exceeded the working environment requirements according 
to the product specifications, which would make it difficult to recommend 
these solutions to any public school. In terms of the solution with exhaust (S4), 
the exhausts’ noise level was also too high according to the product specifica-
tion, but since the fan would be installed in the roof in a permanent solution, it 
could be assumed that the noise issues would be avoided in this retrofit solu-
tion.  
 
For future studies it would be interesting to install the automatic window con-
trol with an exhaust fan (S4) in a large number of classrooms in order to eva-
luate the system further.  
 
Overall, the evaluation of the retrofit solutions showed that the solutions had 
both advantages and disadvantages and that integration of retrofit solutions 
should always aim to comply with the requirements for both the indoor air 
quality, the thermal conditions and the sound conditions.
