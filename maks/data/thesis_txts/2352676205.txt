Summary
Cystic fibrosis is the most common autosomal recessive genetic disease among Cau-
casians. The disease affects the exocrine glands of the body by making their secrete
more viscous. Today treatment plans to circumvent the complications of pancreas
and malnutrition exist. This reduces cystic fibrosis to primarily being a lung disease.
The disease causes frequent lung infections that evolve over time to become a chronic
lung infection. In the end cystic fibrosis causes obstruction of the airways that leads
to an early death [1, 2].
In the lungs of a cystic fibrosis patient bacterias like Pseudomonas aeruginosa infil-
trate the mucus layer and create a biofilm. When bacterias are in a biofilm they
are more difficult to eradicate than their planktonic counterpart due to antibiotic
resistance. One theory is that dormant cells exist in the inner biofilm due to hypoxic
or nutrient starved zones.
Pseudomonas aeruginosa selfproduces an extracellular
polymeric substance matrix, which primarily is composed of polysaccharides. This
polysaccharide matrix can e.g. be resembled by a gel made of the polysaccharide
agarose.
To investigate if oxygen diffuses readily through an agarose gel, the oxygen concentra-
tion was measured over time in agarose gels of different polymeric concentration in
6 mm depth. The gels were prepared with 0.09% NaCl medium. The measurements
were performed with the oxygen sensors, OXR50-UHS, from PyroScience GmbH,
which are based on collisional quenching of the fluorescent REDFLASH indicator
at the sensor tip.
Due to the antiadvectional character of an agarose gel [3] and
the assumption of no reaction and that oxygen degradation is slow compared to the
timescale of the experiment, only diffusion is left in the mass transport expression.
It was found that the retardation scaling effect was no more than 1.6 by the use
of a similar diffusion model as Cao et al.’s [4] quasi-static approach. Whether the
retardation were due to sample variation or alteration of solvent viscosity were not
evident from this study. Redesign and consideration of changing the sensor should
be made to verify the cause of retardation.

Resumé (Dansk)
Cystisk fibrose er den mest almindelige autosomale recessive genetiske sygdom blandt
kaukasier.
Sygdommen påvirker de eksokrine kirtler i kroppen ved at gøre deres
sekreter mere viskøse. I dag findes behandlingsmetoder til at udbedre komplikation-
erne i pancreas og ved fejlernæring. Cystisk fibrose er derfor primært en lunge sygdom
i dag. Sygdommen resulterer i hyppige lungeinfektioner, der over tid udvikler sig til
en kronisk lungeinfektion. Sygdomsforløbet ender med obstruktion af luftvejene og
leder til en tidlig død [1, 2].
Bakterier, som Pseudomonas aeruginosa, infiltrerer mucuslaget i lungerne på cystisk
fibrose patienter og danner biofilm. Når bakterier lever i biofilm er de sværere at
dræbe sammenlignet med deres planktoniske modstykke på grund af antibiotikare-
sistens.
En teori er at inaktive celler eksisterer i den indre biofilme på grund af
hypoksiske eller næringsfattige zoner. Pseudomonas aeruginosa danner selv en ekstra-
cellulær polymer matrix, der primært består af polysaccharider. Denne polysaccharid
matrix kan f.eks. modelleres af en gel lavet af polysaccharidet agarose.
For at undersøge hvorvidt oxygen diffunderer frit gennem agarosegelen eller ej, blev
oxygenkoncentrationen målt over tid i agarosegeler med forskellig polymerkoncentra-
tion i en dybde på 6 mm. Gelerne var fremstillet med mediet 0.09% NaCl. Målingerne
blev foretaget med oxygen sensorerne, OXR50-UHS, fra PyroScience GmbH. Sensor-
erne er baseret på dynamisk quenching af den fluorescerende REDFLASH indikator på
sensorens spids. Massetransportligningen blev simplificeret til kun at være afhængig
af diffusion ved følgende antagelser. Agarosegeler har gode antiadvektionelle egensk-
aber [3], hvilket negligerer advektion, samt oxygennedbrydning kan negligeres da det
sker på en langsommere tidsskala end forsøgene. Ydermere antages det at oxygen
ikke reagerer med agarose eller vand.
Data er modelleret med en model, der ligner den quasistatiske diffusionsmodel af Cao
et al. [4]. Vi fandt, at hæmningen af oxygendiffusionen højst havde en skalerings-
faktor på 1.6. Om hæmningen skyldes prøvevariation eller ændring af opløsningsmi-
dlets viskositet fremgik ikke klart af dette studie. Redesign af forsøgsopstillingen og
overvejelser om at skifte sensoren skal foretages før baggrunden for hæmningen kan
verificeres.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Cystic fibrosis is the most common autosomal recessive genetic disease among Cau-
casians. The disease affects the exocrine glands of the body by making their secrete
more viscous. Today treatment plans to circumvent the complications of pancreas
and malnutrition exist. This reduces cystic fibrosis to primarily being a lung disease.
The disease causes frequent lung infections that evolve over time to become a chronic
lung infection. In the end cystic fibrosis causes obstruction of the airways that leads
to an early death [1, 2].
Throughout the introduction it will be established that one complication in CF is
bacterial infection in the mucus layer of the lung in the form of biofilm. Since biofilm
is very difficult to eradicate, it is important to investigate the reasons behind this.
Antibiotic killing on the surface of the biofilm with failure to kill the interior has been
shown numerous times [5–7]. Different hypotheses for this have been suggested such
as penetration time, stress response that signal the inner biofilm to protect them-
selves, or dormant bacterias in the inner biofilm.
Dormant bacterias are inactive
cellular organisms. Since antibiotics attack active cell processes, it is evident that
they will fail if this theory of dormant cells are true. Dormant populations are be-
lieved to exist due to hypoxic or nutrient starved zones in the mucus layer [8]. To
determine if oxygen diffuse freely into a polysaccharide matrix, like an agarose gel
that can resemble the self-produced extracellular polymeric substance matrix of a
biofilm is to be investigated. Kolpen et al. [6] have shown that hyperbaric oxygen
treatment simultaneous with antibiotic treatment can induce penetration of the gel.
To find why this works, it is important to establish that oxygen actually penetrate
the matrix in absence of bacterial growth and treatment.
In this chapter physiology of the normal and CF lung and disease development is
described, followed by a description about the bacteria Pseudomonas aeruginosa, a
common bacteria in especially chronic CF, in relation to biofilm. This is followed by
a section of chemical characteristics of agarose and oxygen. Next, comes a section
about mass transport with focus on diffusion and Henry’s law. This is followed by a
section about compartment modelling to model the diffusion in an agarose gel, and
finally the aim of the project is described.

1.1
Physiology
In this section the physiology of the normal and CF lung and disease development is
presented together with Pseudomonas aeruginosa’s and role in CF.
1.1.1
Normal lung
The normal airways consist of a tracheobronchial tree lined with ciliated epithelium.
The cilia is located within an approx. 7 µm thick fluid layer above the epithelium,
named the periciliary layer (PL), which is also the length of a fully extended cilium.
On top of the PL, there is a mucus layer, which is 7-70 µm thick. PL has a low viscosity
while the mucus has a high viscosity. The role of mucus is to trap microorganisms and
inhaled debris. The movement of the cilia moves the PL and thereby the mucus layer
out of the respiratory system (Figure 1.1). Thereby it acts as first line of defence in
the innate immune system, where it protects against respiratory tract infections. To
be protected properly, it is important that the PL has a height that leads to optimal
mucus movement [1].
Beside acting as a transport system for unwanted microorganisms and debris, mucus
is also a reservoir of water.
The water reservoir helps control the volume of the
PL by transferring water from mucus to the PL. The lower the height of the PL,
the more replenished the mucus layer becomes. Another mechanism to control the
volume of the PL is water movement across the epithelial cell membrane by regulation
of Na+ and Cl−, which are two opposing systems (Figure 1.1).
When the Na+
concentration dominates in the PL, the epithelial cells will absorb Na+.
Due to
osmosis and osmolarity Cl−and water will follow and lead to a PL of reduced volume.
On the other hand, if the Cl−concentration dominates in the PL, Cl−is secreted to
the PL and Na+ and water follow, and lead to an increased volume of the PL [1].
1.1.2
Cystic Fibrosis
CF were discovered in the clinic in the beginning of the 20th century, and today the
mechanism of CF is still not well understood. In 1938 a pathologist were the first to
give a comprehensive description of the symptoms and the changes produced in the
organs in relation to CF. At this time the focus were on the pancreas. A decade later
it were realized that ductal systems and other passageways also became clogged with
thick secretions. It was concluded that CF was a recessive condition probably caused
by mutation of a single gene. It has later been found that CF is a slowly developing
autosomal recessive genetic disease on chromosome 7 with more than 230 associated
gene defects [9].
In the mid-50’es a heat wave hit New York where it was observed that CF children
became dehydrated more readily than healthy children due to excessive loss of salt
through the sweat. A test for diagnosis were found by this observation. This also
lead to treatments as e.g. replacement of missing digestive enzymes and vitamins

1 Introduction
Research into CF have had a slower progression, but intensified in the 1980s. It was
observed that malfunction of epithelial tissue was at fault in every impaired organ and
that it was relatively impermeable to chloride too. Paul Quinton found that chloride
was not taken up efficiently in the glands, while R. Boucher and M. Knowles found
that movement from the epithelial into the airway lumen was diminished and the
sodium uptake was enhanced. Later it was found that deletion of three nucleotides,
namely phenylalanine at position 508 (Phe508) in the CFTR gene that encodes the
Cystic Fibrosis Transmembrane conductance Regulator protein (CFTR) were respon-
sible for 70% of CF cases.
CFTR was characterized as a channel that acts as a
conduit for chloride to move down an electrochemical gradient. The protein consists
of a transmembrane region, two nucleotide binding domains and a regulatory domain.
The channel gate is controlled by ATP binding to the nucleotide binding domain and
phosphorylation of the regulatory domain. Phe508 is in the nucleotide binding do-
main. CFTR is postprocessed in the endoplasmatic reticulum, where it is tagged for
degradation (70%) or the misfolded protein interferes with insertion into the plasma
membrane, and lead to reduced or no Cl−movement across the membrane (30%) [1,
12].
1 out of 27 Caucasians are carrier (heterozygotic) of the mutation in CFTR with no
or mild symptoms. 1 out of 2900 are homozygotic for a mutated CFTR gene that
cause CF to develop. CF is the most common lethal genetic disorder among Cau-
casians [1, 10, 11, 13]. The CF patients life are short lifed to the mid-thirties, but
with the right disease management survival into the forties are possible. 95% die due
to complications from lung infections [1, 2].
1.1.3
Developement of cystic fibrosis
CF is shortly after birth associated with excessive neutrophilic inflammatory response
that appear independent of infection. CF is throughout life associated with frequent,
serious respiratory infections, and irregular Cl−and Na+ ion and water movement
that cause the PL and mucus layer to grow thin (Figure 1.1) [1, 2, 10, 11]. Because
CFTR is malfunctioning, there will be no Na+-channel inhibition (Figure 1.1 (bot-
tom left)) Cl−can not pass the epithelium. This lead to increased Na+ absorption
and thereby Cl−and water by the epithelial cell and a replenished PL. To keep the
height of the periciliary layer, water is reabsorbed from the mucus, which become
continuously more viscous [14, 15]. The abnormal viscosity of mucus are also largely
increased due to the presence of polynuclear white blood cells and their degradation
products. Due to the reduced PL the cilia are hindered in rotating in their power
strokes which gives the cilia about 10 times lower velocity. Thereby an inefficient
system is created that enables bacterial infection and airway inflammation is created.
This can cause obstruction like alginate capsules leading to coughing and wheezing
and eventually death of the patient [1, 2, 10, 11]. Other symptoms seen in the new-
born CF patient is elevated blood levels of trypsinogen, a protoform of the digestive
enzyme trypsin, due to secretory obstruction in pancreas is seen [10]. Hyperglycemia

The bacterial colonization, which lead to biofilm, is beginning in infancy or early child-
hood by the bacterial species S. aureus and H. influenzae. In adolescence or early
adulthood most CF patients have become chronic and colonized with a non-mucoid P.
aeruginosa strain (Section 1.1.4). This shift in bacterial colonization seems to be asso-
ciated with the CF lung environment, which arises from the dysfunctional electrolyte
secretion and absorption. However, the mechanism for P. aeruginosa colonization
is unknown. Different hypotheses have been given. One is that attachment occurs
upon exposure of airway epithelium as PL grow thinner. That release inflammatory
mediators and leukocyte infiltration into the lung tissue. That again leads to contin-
uous inflammation and lung pathology. Another hypothesis state that the increased
viscosity of mucus acts as a matrix scaffold that decrease the clearance [14]. This hy-
pothesis are supported by Worlitzsch et al. [16] who has indicated that Pseudomonas
aeruginosa was present in mucopurulent oxygen depleted macrocolonies in the lumen
rather than in the epithelium. The same group has shown that motile Pseudomonas
aeruginosa penetrated hypoxic mucus layer and responded by increased alginate pro-
duction to the anaerobic signal. Worlitzsch et al. [16] conclude that colonization is
due to the CF lung environment that leads to exacerbation of the pathology. Thirdly,
Drenkard et al. [15] has identified a regulatory protein, PvrR, that seems to control
the phenotypic switch between antibiotic susceptible and resistant biofilm (Section
1.1.5) as well as biofilm formation. They propose that selection within the biofilm by
sublethal antibiotic treatment and environmental conditions in the biofilm and lung
(e.g. osmotic and oxidative stress) gives the resistant biofilm phenotype. So either
attachment, the lung environment or external factors could be reasons for coloniza-
tion.
After the initial manifestations chronic broncholitis and bronchitis develop that after
months to years lead to bronchiectasis. Due to the decline in respiratory function,
pulmonary inflammation develop. This may predispose the CF patient to chronic
infections by P. aeruginosa, B. cepacia, S. aureus and H. influenzae [2]. The CF lung
environment as well as a host inflammatory response lead P. aeruginosa to undergo
mucoid transformation, which is a sign of chronic CF. Walters et al. [17] has shown
that less than one percent is likely to reverse to the non-mucoid phenotype. The
mucoid variant overproduce a complex polysaccharide that creates a hypoxic environ-
ment that generates biofilm that protect against antimicrobial agents [2, 14, 15].
Antibiotics can lead to slower progression of the disease until the bacterias are ren-
dered resistant. First it is given to prevent colonization and later as maintenance
therapy and as aggressive therapy against acute exacerbations of pulmonary symp-
toms due to infections [1, 2]. Mucus-thinning drugs and bronchodilators to improve
mucus clearance and opening the airways are also given. If a CF patient become
antibiotic resistant or have too severe breathing problems, they are put on the list
for lung transplant, which lead to lifelong treatment with immunosuppressive drugs.
A transplant is a partial cure as the new lung will not have the CFTR defect. How-
ever, other exocrine glands in the body will still be affected.

1.1.4
Pseudomonas aeruginosa
P. aeruginosa is an opportunistic gram-negative bacteria. The virulence factors are
among others [10]
Flagellum that provides motility, dissemination and initiation of the innate immune
system.
Type IV pili that provides adherence and twitching motility.
LPS production that induces endotoxic shock, sepsis and serum resistance.
Alginate production that provide adherence and protection from dehydration and
the host immune system.
Pyocyanin is a redox-active compound that generates reactive oxygen species and
disrupt the action of cilia and phagocytic function.
LasR is a quorum sensing signal that plays a key role in controlling virulence factor
production, biofilm formation, swarming motility and expression of antibiotic
efflux pumps.
Rhamnolipids are non-protein toxins that are toxic to polynuclearleukocytes.
Rhl Plays a key role in controlling expression of virulence factors and biofilm forma-
tion.
In CF, LPS levels are increased as well as pyocyanin is produced in great amounts.
Pyocyanin induce interference with the normal redox cycle in mammalian cells and
lead to production of reactive forms of oxygen that can damage the cells. The cytokine
release is activated together with the complement cascade. Besides this the sequence
of virulence factors which act to develop CF is unknown.
P. aeruginosa is already resistant to some antibiotics before onset of infection in the
CF patient, but resistance evolve as infection progresses. Resistance to P. aeruginosa
comes from multiple mechanisms: antimicrobials are prevented entry by porins and
antibiotics and antibiotic-inactivating enzymes are pumped out by efflux systems [10].

1.1.5
Biofilm
Biofilm is a known factor in diseases like CF and inflammation of implants or burns.
The changed environment provided by the disease or the host response are believed
to act as virulence factors that induce biofilm formation [5, 14].
Biofilms are structural and dynamic complex biological systems evolved to accom-
modate one or more bacterial species in microcolonies interspersed by open water
channels. A selfproduced extracellular polymeric substance matrix (EPS) surrounds
the bacterias. The channels facilitate nutrient uptake and waste product exchange
both intra- and interspecies. Biochemically EPS is a hydrogel, a gel that can trap wa-
ter many times its own weight and have a viscoelastic mechanical behaviour, which
are beneficial in adaptation to seasonal changes in nature. The readily adaptable
structure and the homeostasis provided, this mode of living is thought to have played
an important role in the survival of bacterias throughout evolution [8, 14]. Bacterial
biofilms are populations of bacterias that are based mainly on surfaces. The slimy
EPS provides stability and acts as a component in surface adhesion for the biofilm.
The composition of EPS is species dependent, but is primarily based on polysaccha-
rides as well as proteins and nucleic acids. As an example, P. aeruginosa, produce
the exopolysaccharide alginate as a key component of its EPS matrix. Alginate is
believed to contribute to the antibiotic resistance of P. aeruginosa for antibiotics like
tobramycin by binding the antibiotic [14].
Gram-negative bacterias, like P. aeruginosa, has a five stage maturation process,
which takes 5-7 days (Figure 1.2). First, only transient association with a surface
exist. The bacterias are still susceptible to antibiotics at this stage. Next irreversible
adhesion render the bacterias more resistant to antibiotics. Then, the cells aggregate
in microcolonies, followed by growth and maturation where the EPS is produced. The
structure of the biofilm is defined by nutrient sources and genetics. Rearrangement
by elongated structures like type IV pilus can mediate a gliding motility by retraction
and pulling. The fifth stage is characterized by detachment. The biofilm is sloughed
or shed by a transient motility. This can either be motility-driven, shear-mediated
or driven by agents, which act active, passive or chemically. Throughout the devel-
opment cycle, fluid shear plays an important factor [14, 18].
The structure of biofilms, their growth can be limited by nutrition and oxygen avail-
ability, slow growth, increased mutation rates, persister cells, and quorum-sensing
activity, which all contribute to the antibiotic resistant phenotype [5]. The plank-
tonic mode of living, in contrary to the biofilm mode of living of the same species,
are single cells in suspension, for which antibiotic resistance normally is not a prob-
lem [14]. However, as the biofilm mode of living constitutes 99% of bacterial living,
antibiotic resistance is important to investigate. A huge concern is how to eradicate
the stable and somewhat antibiotic resistance biofilm.
Three protective mechanisms has been proposed for antibiotic resistance in biofilms,
and they probably variate due to the type of biofilm and environment. First, the EPS
matrix could dilute the antibiotic by neutralization or binding. This could create an

1.2.1
Agarose gel
Agarose is a linear neutral polysaccharide molecule isolated from seaweed as agar.
From agar the neutral agarose molecule is isolated, which consists of repeated units
of alternating 3,6-anhydro-L- and D-galactose. Albeit agarose is a neutral molecule
substitution of e.g. sulphate can introduce charges in the gel, which is also why the
sulphate concentration defines the degree of purity. The lower sulphate concentra-
tion, the purer agarose. The agarose gel is formed by suspending dry agarose in an
aqueous buffer and boiling until a clear solution forms (melting point: 62-65 ◦C). The
suspended solution is poured into a form and allowed to cool to room temperature
where it sets as a rigid gel. Depending on type and molecular weight of agarose, the
solidification temperature can vary. It is possible to reliquify the gel by heat. When
a gel is formed from agarose, it is a clear polysaccharide hydrogel [3].
Gel formation occur by the free polymer chains forming double helices which are
crosslinked followed by aggregation into bundles of helices which adds to the crosslinks
(Figure 1.3). The gelling properties are attributed to inter- and intramolecular hydro-
gen bonding, creating crosslinks, within and between the long agarose chains. This
is how the pores are created. The cross-links create an antiadvectional environment.
The gel has an open lattice structure, which minimize the diffusional distances. The
pore size is controlled by the initial concentration of agarose. The higher concentra-
tion, the smaller the pore size. A 1% agarose gel have pore sizes that are relatively
large to the size of proteins why they can move unhindered. DNA and RNA will also
be able to pass through a 1% gel. However, as their sizes are relative to the pore size
fractional effects will be seen as separation occur [3].
The gel is characterized by proportionality between stiffness and polymer concentra-
tion. Agarose gels have poor elasticity why the gel is mainly used as slabs. 2% gels will
separate double stranded DNA between 0.1 and 3 kbp (1 kbp has a Mr = 620000) [3,
20, 21]. In comparison oxygen has a relative moleculelar mass of 16 and is therefore
a much smaller molecule, which are expected to move unhindered through a 2% gel.
As described in Section 1.1.5, a biofilm is surrounded by a selfproduced EPS and due
to the polysaccharide nature of the agarose gel it can be used to resemble that.

Henry’s law
Henry’s law is an empirical relation that describes phase equilibrium between liquid
and gas. In equilibrium there exist no net exchange of mass, momentum or energy
with the surroundings. Consider a rigid container partially filled with liquid species A,
and all gas is removed above the liquid. The pressure in the space above is measured
and the container is maintained at constant temperature. Over time molecules will
vaporize from the liquid phase into the gaseous phase and the pressure in the gas
phase will rise. After some time equilibrium is established as the pressure has become
stable. This is known as the vapor pressure. If the temperature is raised, so is the
vapor pressure [11]. In the case of biological application except perhaps in very dilute
solutions the molecular structures of the components are dissimilar or dissociate into
ions in solution. This is due to aqueous solutions can contain many different species.
The use of the empirical relationship between mole fractions in the gas and liquid
phase is known as Henry’s law. It is also another way to describe the solubility in
the liquid and gaseous phase of a gaseous solute.

1.4.1
Fluorescence
Fluorescence is an emission phenomenon of light due to absorption of radiation. From
emission follows energy transition from a higher to a lower state by dissipating energy
as photons. The efficiency of the fluorescence process is quantified by the quantum
yield, Φ,
Φ = Number of photons emitted
Number of photons absorbed.
(1.12)
Even Φ = 0.1 is considered relatively fluorescent. Other non-radiative processes like
thermal degradation can induce the energy transition as well. Fluorescence ceases
shortly after the source of excitation is removed, opposite to phosphorescence. As
the fluorophore’s emission intensity is dependent on the solution temperature, tem-
perature control is required. The absorption of the solution should be less than 0.05
to be used for fluorescence experiments [3, 28].
A molecule is not static, but vibrate due to different forces. The molecule’s vibrational
levels of the ground state should not overlap that of the excited state for fluorescence
emission to occur (Figure 1.4). This is often the case, why few natural fluorophores
exist. A molecule can obtain many different potential energy levels, but it wants to
use as less energy as possible. This is why fluorescence is a spontaneous reaction. Af-
ter absorption of radiation the molecule is excited. The excited molecule collide with
surrounding molecules and gives up its energy to step down the ladder of vibrational

be able to accept all the energy from the excited molecule. If not, the molecule will

the ability of the solvent to accept the electronic and vibrational quanta. For exam-
ple water has widely spaced vibrational levels, why it can accept large quantums of

1.4.2
Quenching
Quenching is a non-radiating process that can eliminate fluorescence. An external
factor that can affect the fluorophore’s effect is called a quencher. Although, the
quencher does not alter the absorption and excitation process. The quencher only al-
ters the energy of the excited state by transferring energy onto the quenching molecule.
Quenching can be beneficial in case of energy or electron transfer or disadvantageous
in case of a decrease in the effect of a photochemical process. The efficacy of quench-
ing is dependent on the quencher molecule concentration [3, 28].
Two forms of quenching or a mixture of these exist: dynamic and static quenching.
Dynamic quenching occur due to collision between the excited fluorophore and the
quencher, whereas static quenching occur when the quencher forms a complex with
the fluorophore. This complex has another electronic structure than the fluorophore
alone and therefore returns to the ground state by non-radiating processes.
Mathematically the two types of quenching is described differently, but both are
based on the Stern-Volmer relationship (Equation 1.13 and 1.14). Dynamic quenching
is given by

1.5
Motivation
The aim of this thesis is to investigate if temporal effects exist when oxygen diffuse
into an agarose gel, which is a widely used medium for biofilm growth in the labora-
tory. As agarose gels are often made with Luria Bertani (LB) Broth, which contains
substances that could impede the diffusion of oxygen else than agarose itself. There-
fore, agarose gels were made with three different media to distinguish the effect of
agarose.
The three media were MilliQ water, 0.09% NaCl and LB. The diffusion
process should occur slower in the NaCl samples than in the MilliQ samples. And
since LB contains nutrients and salts, the diffusion coefficient is expected to be even
lower than for the other media.
Agarose was chosen do to its polysaccharide nature that resemble that of the EPS
matrix in the biofilm. All experiments were performed in absence of bacteria to de-
termine the sole effect of the matrix on diffusion of oxygen.
The original hypothesis were that only poor temporal effects would be seen for oxy-
gen diffusion in agarose, but unpublished results led to the test this hypothesis. If
binding exists, it was hypothesized that the higher the concentration of agarose, the
slower the diffusion process should occur. Binding effects were expected to exist if
retardation of oxygen occurred at a level similar to the recently published study about
the binding effects of tobramycin in alginate beads by Cao et al. [4].
The character of this thesis has changed markedly during the course of the work.
Initially, the main focus and work should have been on modelling with use of the
quasi-static model by Cao et al. [4] or a modified version. However, as the data did
not show the expected outcome, the main workload became establishing an usable
experimental protocol, while trying to keep the focus on the modelling.

The OXR5-UHS is a full range oxygen microsensor which can measure from 0% to
100% oxygen concentration in either fluid (aqueous solutions, ethanol, methanol and
isopropanol) or gas phase. Other organic solvents and gaseous chlorine induce inter-
ference with the sensor reading. UHS is an abbreviation for ultra high speed, which
corresponds to a response time of 90% signal change in <0.3 s in both liquids and gas.
The OXR50-UHS sensor can among others be used for depth-profile measurements of
oxygen concentration in semi-solid environmental samples like biofilm, and provide
high temporal and spatial resolution along microgradients [31].
Physically, the OXR50-UHS is a retractable needle-type sensors (Figure 2.7) that
consists of a fiber-optical cable connecting the oxygen meter and a metal housing
with a syringe needle (40 mm). The optical fiber has a sensing tip with immobilized
REDFLASH indicator. The fiber can be set in four positions: 6 mm inside the nee-
dle, at the tip of the needle (0 mm), +6 mm or +12 mm, which is position 0 to 3,
respectively. Each sensor has a specific sensor code named after type of sensor and
the phase shift given in the fabric calibration of 0% and 100% oxygen saturation in air.

conversion into oxygen units is based on the Stern-Volmer theory (Section 1.4.2) [31].

The modelling was inspired by the article of Cao et al. [4] as stated in Section 1.3.3.

than the error (In this work 1% was used) at all times in the timerange from 3.5 h

CHAPTER 4
Conclusion
In conclusion, it has been clear throughout this work that the retardation effect on
oxygen in agarose gels of different concentrations is not large. That is minor buffer-
effects and it is therefore shown that oxygen diffuses fairly readily through agarose
gels, consisting of up to 2% agarose, in absence of bacterias. This is however, a slow
process that takes more than 48 h to reach 90% bulk saturation. Whether the retar-
dation scaling factor of 1.6 is due to solvent viscosity or merely sample variation has
not been verified by this study. For this to be verified more experiments have to be
run. Since many bias are introduced by the experimental setup of this study, it is
recommended that redesign is made. Even though redesign would help immensely,
the reliability of the sensors have been the center of talk numerous times during this
work, it is difficult to say if the variability among data could be reduced by using a
different mode of calibration, or if the sensors simply do not fit the purpose although
PyroScience state that semi-solids like a gel is one of the work ranges of the sensor.
Nevertheless a more reliable setup is needed to verify the background for the re-
tardation in the agarose gel in absence of bacteria.This could be done by a similar
setup to the one by Hulst et al. [35]. This proposed new setup is drawn in Figure 4.1.
This setup would enable measurements of both the static diffusion coefficient and the
dynamic diffusion coefficient. As both values can be obtained an expression for the
dynamic diffusion coefficient alone is also possible to find by subtraction.
Anoxic agarose beads made with the desired solvent and concentration is placed in
a beaker with the pure solvent after gelification. To measure the dynamic diffusion
coefficient, it is important that the beads have the same radius as well as the total
volume of gel beads should be known. If only the static diffusion coefficient is to be
found, only the total volume of beads are necessary to be known. After preparation of
the beads the beaker is left in a anoxic chamber to deplete the contents of the beaker
for oxygen. This will also diminish that swelling occur during the experiment as the
deoxygenation time will be longer than that of swelling. Hereafter a known volume of
oxygen is added to a solution of the anaerobic pure solvent of known volume through
the inlet Figure 4.1). A magnet rotating slowly ensures that the solvent is well-mixed
and that the oxygen gradient over the agarose beads are similar independent of the
position in the beaker.
Since the solvent is well-mixed, but the concentration of
oxygen is changed as oxygen diffuses into the beads, the boundary condition for the
outer radius of the beads are described by this change in bulk concentration. In the
==PAGE:58==
44
4 Conclusion
center of the beads there will be a symmetric boundary condition that will describe
the reflective boundary in the center. A piston is positioned in the top of the beaker
to equilibrate the pressure, when the known volume is added. This ensures that the
entire chamber enclosed by the beaker and the piston is filled with liquid, and no
air contamination exists. This piston will also be able to change the pressure of the
beaker to some degree. A sensor oppositely placed to the inlet (Figure 4.1) measure
the oxygen level in the solvent. The setup is left to obtain equilibrium e.g. saturation
is obtained. The reduction in the measured oxygen level compared to the known inlet
volume must be retained in the beads e.g. by binding to either polymer or solvent.
It would be expected that the lowest point of oxygen measured would correspond
to the saturation concentration. In addition, the temperature should be known and
constant. This can be achieved either by an incubator room or a waterbath around
the sides of the beaker or sensors inserted through the beaker or piston.
This setup would enable testing diffusion of different levels of oxygen as well as differ-
ent polymer concentration, and when equilibrium occurs at these levels and concen-
trations. A control experiment without beads would define the level of oxygen bound
by the solvent alone. This setup could easily be expanded to the use of other gel
types or biofilm. When using the setup for biofilm, consumption of e.g. oxygen will
exist. Therefore, a higher pressure or more oxygen will be needed to reach the center
of the beads. Due to the well-mixed solvent, the problem of the precise position of
the sensor into the beaker is no longer relevant . This will also neglect the boundary
reflection. Due to equilibrium, the solvent should reflect the oxygen level in the beads
before adding the extra volume. Thereby, a control of total oxygen depletion can also
be made. Most importantly it is important to solve the challenge of calibration for
the sensors. Depending on the oxygen sensor used, the beaker might still need to be
shielded from light.

Figure 4.1: New experimental setup inspired by Hulst et al. [35]. Agarose beads of
a known volume made with the desired solvent is suspended in a fluid
of the pure solvent. Through the inlet a known volume of the solvent
with a known volume of oxygen can be transferred into the beaker. On
the opposite side of the inlet a sensor is placed to measure the oxygen
level in the solvent. Furthermore a piston is placed on top of the beaker
to equilibrate for the increased pressure, the added amount will bring.
A magnetic stirring ensure a well-mixed solvent and hereby the same
concentration gradient in all the beads.
The experiment should take
place in a room of constant and known temperature..
