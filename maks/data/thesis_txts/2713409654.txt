Abstract
Gaze information is one of the most important human behaviours that directly inflect hu­
man visual attention. So far, the most popular technique used in the lab scene is the
infrared eye tracker, which estimates the gaze position by emitting invisible near­infrared
light and then recording the reflection from the cornea. Recently, electroencephalogram
(EEG) has been considered another alternative to obtain eye movements, which records
the electrical activities from the brain.
EEGEyeNet is a new and rich dataset which contains simultaneous EEG and Eye­tracking
recordings. The benchmark of the dataset has shown that EEG data has the ability to es­
timate eye movement using deep learning frameworks. However, due to the black­box
property of deep learning models, it remains unclear how the model predicts the out­
comes. As with many other deep learning applications, user trust and the transparency of
the model are crucial and necessary. Furthermore, the model’s predictive capability could
be further improved by enhancing model understanding. Therefore, a new challenge has
emerged: how do we, as humans, comprehend deep learning’s prediction outcomes?
And by which means could the black box turn more transparent?
To address these issues, we explore the explainability of deep learning models for gaze
estimation using EEG data with two main approaches. In the first part of this work, we
employ post­hoc analysis like Layer­wise Relevance Propagation (LRP) and Local Inter­
pretable Model­Agnostic Explanations (LIME) and adapt the algorithms to fit better the
nature of the EEG data with the computational process of interpretation. In the second
phase of this work, we intend to explore how to construct an EEG­data­target interpretable
deep learning system. By finding that the recordings from some electrodes are completely
out of range, we develop an electrode­sensitive component that can explicitly estimate
the trustworthiness of an electrode and aggregate it over a network. Two models are pro­
posed, the Attention­CNN and Auto­Dropout­GCN, where the attention mechanism and
graph updating are implemented as the electrode­sensitive component.
The results from post­hoc analysis reflect that prefrontal electrodes contributed signifi­
cantly more to the prediction than the other electrodes. Furthermore, in the temporal di­
mension, the beginning and end of the clip matter more. The results from the interpretable
model show that the proposed models not only quantitatively surpass the benchmark, but
also demonstrate the dependability of the outcomes via visualization. By presenting the
location of problematic electrodes and the weights we obtained from attention blocks, it
could be found that the attention blocks are able to avoid the problematic area and focus
more on the trustworthy electrodes.

Introduction
The form of human­machine interaction has been pushing the boundaries of imagination.
In the past, we controlled devices through the mouse and keyboard, but now, the interac­
tion ways have been extended to touch screen, voice commands, and gesture recognition,
to name a few. Eye movement is progressively joining the game as an essential modality
of human attention. At the moment, gaze estimation has been applied in the fields of
virtual reality [1, 2], medical diagnosis [3, 4], and driver­assistance systems [5, 6]. Since
gaze reflects human interest directly, the future of human­computer interaction holds con­
siderable potential for gaze estimation applications, like deciphering gaze information for
recommendation systems or assisted control of smart gadgets [7].
At present, the infrared eye tracker, which works on the principle of corneal reflection,
is the prevalent way of gaze assessment [8]. Although this method is quite precise, the
equipment is prohibitively expensive and scene­dependent. Moreover, though it is a good
option for data collection in the laboratory, it is relatively impracticable for generalization
to the real world [9]. Another method that is widely under research is webcam­based gaze
estimation [10]. This technique is more economical and convenient than the previous one
but also less accurate [11]. Potential leakage of private information is one of the draw­
backs of using webcams for gaze estimation, as full­face images are typically required as
input. Meanwhile, researchers are looking into EEG­based eye­tracking as an alterna­
tive [12]. The Electroencephalogram (EEG) is a non­invasive method for detecting brain
activity that is reasonably affordable and provides the high temporal resolution [13].
By utilizing deep networks to discover new features, deep learning is advantageous to
numerous domains. Gaze estimation using EEG data is not an exception. Deep learning
architectures have been demonstrated to be the most efficient models in earlier research
[12]. In addition, deep learning models are used for an increasing number of EEG­based
tasks [14, 15]. However, with the benefit that deep learning delivers, new challenges
arise: how do we, as humans, comprehend deep learning’s prediction outcomes? And
by which means could the black box turn more transparent?
By answering these questions, not only the model’s predictive capability could be further
improved, users’ trust in the model will also be boosted, which is vital for future market
applications. Furthermore, the interpretability of the EEG­based DL task has not been
thoroughly explored. We also intend to uncover the commonalities in EEG data and con­
struct broad interpretable models, or provide inspiration for future EEG­based tasks, by
addressing this subject.
In this thesis, we aim to study the explainability of deep learning models for gaze estima­
tion using EEG data. In the first part of our study, we investigate how implemented basic
deep learning frameworks, such as CNNs, predict gaze estimations. Here, we examine
some popular post­hoc techniques like LRP and LIME and adapt the algorithms to fit better
the nature of the EEG data with the computational process of interpretation. In the second
phase of our research, we intend to explore how to construct an EEG­data­appropriate in­
terpretable deep learning system. We find that the noisy nature of EEG data causes noise
that cannot be removed entirely by preprocessing, and that noise is usually electrodes­
linked. For instance, when one electrode is loosened, the data recorded from this elec­
trode is typically vastly different from the normal range of EEG data. This fact inspired
us to develop an electrode­sensitive component that can explicitly estimate the trustwor­

deep learning frameworks: Attention­CNN and Auto­Dropout­GCN. In Attention­CNN, the

2) Bring up the idea of the electrode­sensitive component, which explicitly evaluates

3) Validate the efficiency of the proposed model qualitatively and quantitatively.
1.1
Background and Motivation
The collection of eye gaze information is widely used in cognitive science and psychol­
ogy. Moreover, many neuroscientific studies complement neuroimaging methods such as
functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) and electroencephalography (EEG) with
eye­tracking technology to identify variations in attention, arousal and the participant’s
compliance with the task demands. Another line of research is combining the behavioral
information gained from eye tracking with the high temporal resolution and neurophysio­
logical markers provided by EEG enables researchers to study the perceptual, attentional,
and cognitive processes [16].
Nonetheless, the additional cost for eye tracking hardware, setup requirements and com­
plexity (operator training, synchronization with auxiliary data and analysis of an additional
data type) of current eye tracking solutions precludes many researchers from accessing
eye gaze information [17]. To address limitations with conventional eye tracking sys­
tems, recent studies have focused on leveraging advanced machine­learning techniques
to compute gaze based on images from a webcam or images from the fMRI signal [18].
While using a webcam to specify the eye gaze position requires an additional system and
sophisticated synchronization with auxiliary measures (e.g. reaction times or EEG data)
from the actual experiment that is even more cumbersome as in traditional eye tracking
systems; fMRI data acquisition is costly and does not provide the temporal resolution at
the level that cognition actually takes place [13]. In contrast, EEG is a widely­used, safe
and cost­friendly method in cognitive neuroscience that directly measures the electrical
activity of the brain [19]. In addition, it enables measurement in clinical settings (e.g. long­
term recordings in the hospital bed). However, an eye­tracking approach that offers gaze
position based on concurrently measured EEG and electrooculography EOG (EEG­EOG)
is sporadic.
EEGEyeNet [12] is a new and rich dataset which contains simultaneous Electroencephalog­
raphy (EEG) and Eye­tracking recordings. The benchmark of the dataset has shown that
EEG data has the ability to estimate eye movement using deep learning frameworks.
Nevertheless, due to the fact that deep learning models are black boxes, it remains un­
clear how the model predicts the outcomes. Furthermore, the accuracy of the benchmark
is not competitive enough compared with other gaze estimation options. Exploring the
explainability could also assist the further improvement of the performance.

1.2
Research Questions
This work focuses on the explainability of deep learning models for EEG­based gaze
estimation. We are interested in understanding more about how deep learning models
comprehend the predictions of gaze estimation. This question is further split into two
sub­questions. First, as an extension of previous research [12], how do the basic deep
learning frameworks lead to good prediction results? In this phase, our investigation
is more data­centric. In other words, which electrodes or a part of the timeframe contribute
the most to the prediction outcomes, globally and locally?
The other sub­question is model­centric, compared with the first one. In [12], a compre­
hensive EEG dataset with complete data annotation from an eye tracker was gathered.
According to the benchmark result, CNN and related models are the most effective. How­
ever, unlike picture data, EEG data lack continuity throughout the electrode dimension.
In addition, as discussed in the Section 1.1 , the noise in EEG data is one of the problems
that hindered prior deep learning models. Therefore, our second research question is
how to create a generalized deep learning model for EEG data specifically in order
to address the issue of data noisiness in a explicit manner.
1.3
Research Objectives
According to the research questions raised in Section 1.2, the research objectives are
planned as follows:
1) For the first part of the research questions, classical post­hoc methods will be ap­
plied to analyse which electrodes/time­frames matter more in predicting gaze using
EEG data. At least two post­hoc methods should be implemented, and a compar­
ison between the results of different methods is needed. Moreover, if necessary,
the adjustment of the post­hoc methods for EEG data should be carried out.
2) For the second part of the research questions, we plan to create an interpretable
deep learning model which targets the issue of noisiness in EEG data. In order
to prove that the noisiness is handled to some extent, the proposed model should
outperform the benchmark. Furthermore, the explainability of the proposed model
should be able to display visually.

2.1
Deep Learning and EEG
2.1.1
Deep learning­based electroencephalography
In the last two decades, machine learning and deep learning (DL) have shown great
promise in helping make sense of EEG signals due to their ability to learn feature rep­
resentations from electroencephalography data [20]. To date, the best­known and most
comprehensive systematic reviews of the literature on deep learning applications to EEG
were proposed by [15] and [21]. The first review focused solely on EEG classification
tasks, and the second examined all papers that applied DL to EEG and were published be­
tween January 2010 and July 2018. Both reviews included studies that spanned different
application domains and addressed questions like which EEG tasks have been explored
with deep learning and what type of input formulations have been used for training the
deep networks? As reported in [21], about 40% of all included studies used convolutional
neural networks (CNNs), leaving far behind the second most famous architecture, recur­
rent neural networks (RNNs). Furthermore, some of the studies used GCN architectures
for EEG data. While this method seems promising, one must consider the substantial
limitation: the interpolation of missing/bad electrodes during data preprocessing. Thus,
the preprocessing structure of electrodes is affected quite significantly (with poorer quality
EEG data in particular).
It should be noted, however, that this review was published shortly after the introduction
of Transformer, based solely on attention mechanisms, dispensing with recurrence and
convolutions entirely. Over the last few years, there has been a substantial increase in
research employing this family of models [22, 23, 24]. Furthermore, many EEG studies
in the last years have begun to use attention modules that have significantly improved
previous state­of­the­art models’ results for different EEG domains [25, 26].
Despite the enormous popularity that the application of deep learning to EEG data gained
over the past decade, we can conclude that research is mainly focused on the following
topics: emotion recognition [27], epileptic seizures detection [28] and classification of
sleep stages [29]. Only very few studies have proposed more complex deep­learning­

2.2
Eye­Tracking and Gaze Estimation in General
2.2.1
Eye­Tracking
The complexity and uniqueness of eye­movement patterns have made them a topic of
intense interest in a wide array of research fields for several decades [46].
Early eye­tracking studies used mechanically attached suction cups or levers to transmit
the movement of the eyes on paper tapes [47]. Since then, the eye­tracking methods
have made significant technological improvements in accuracy and temporal resolution,
culminating in eye­coils, webcam­based, and infrared video­based systems [48].
A developing approach to eye­tracking is systems which use cameras built into laptops,
and mobile devices [49]. Webcam­based methods are attractive to researchers as they
do not restrict measurement to a laboratory setting, allowing more extensive and diverse
samples to be measured. However, this can result in a trade­off between sample size and
data quality. Furthermore, webcam­based methods are more sensitive to head move­
ments, and low light conditions [50].
Nowadays, the most popular approaches for eye­tracking are infrared video­based sys­
tems [47]. These eye­tracking devices are based on corneal reflection photography and
can be head­mounted [48]. An infrared camera is directed at the eye and computes the
pupil size and position. The approach is based on Purkinje images, the reflections of the
infrared light from the outer and the inner surface of the lens and the cornea. Most com­
monly featured in eye­tracking is the reflection of the outer surface of the cornea and the
reflection of the inner surface of the lens, the first and fourth Purkinje image, respectively.
The eye­tracking system uses the relative change of the Purkinje images to compute the
gaze position [51].
Although infrared video­based eye­tracking devices are the most commonly used sys­
tems, they suffer from various limitations [47]. The devices available on the market have
tremendous variability in performance and price and subsequently cause a high variability
of quality in eye­tracking studies [8]. Moreover, infrared video­based eye­trackers often
extend the experiment due to the intensive effort required for calibration that can be further
aggravated by glasses, contact lenses, or any other visual impairments like eye opera­
tions or drooping eyelids [52].
The limitations of the mentioned eye­tracking approaches drive the development of further
alternatives. Researchers are beginning to develop signal­based eye­tracking methods
that derive the eye movements from signals produced by or related to the eye­movement
process of eye­movement [53].
2.2.2

the horizontal and vertical coordinates on a screen that allows employing gaze points to

The successful gaze estimation is expected to be achieved by incorporating anatomi­

Gaze estimation using EEG data
Machine learning techniques allow extracting information from EEG recordings of brain

areas [66]. Additionally, new deep learning methods allow computational models to learn

that gaze position can be recovered using the combination of EEG and deep learning.
Inferring gaze position information from EEG­EOG data allows to extract a rich additional

of our knowledge, no explainable deep­learning­based approach that simulates a purely
EEG­based eye­tracker is available in daily research and clinical practice.
2.4

A thorough and influential review of EEG classification tasks using Deep Learning Mod­

a two­dimensional function of frequency and time [76]. Often, it could be used as a visual
tool to present the signal strength across time at different frequencies. There are also
some similar approaches using the Fourier Transform or other transformation methods
and using the 2D, or 3D grid as the input [14].

development of the Deep Learning framework has proved that the network can extract

3.1.1.3
Pre­processing, Synchronization and Segmentation
Eye­Tracking Preprocessing
There are three generic types of eye movements: saccades, fixations and blinks. Sac­
cades are rapid, conjugate eye movements that shift the centre of gaze from one point
to another. This thesis detects saccades using the eye­tracking software default set­
tings: acceleration larger than 8000°/s2, a velocity above 30°/s, and a deflection above
0.1°. Fixations are the maintaining of the gaze on a single location. Finally, blinks are
semi­autonomic rapid closings of the eyelid, defined by pupil diameter equals zero in
eye­tracking software.
EEG Preprocessing
Preprocessing EEG data is vital because EEG data are often noisy owing to environ­
mental or participant disturbance, such as humidity, temperature, pulse rate, or muscle
activity. The preprocessing involves identifying and interpolating broken electrodes, and
then filtering a 0.1 Hz high­pass filter to the data.
Synchronization
EEG and eye­tracking data were synchronized using the EYE­EEG toolkit (Dimigen et
al., 2011) to enable EEG analyses time­locked to fixations and saccades. First, the syn­
chronization algorithm detected ”shared” eye­tracking and EEG events (start­end trigger
of each experimental paradigm). Next, a linear function was fitted to shared event laten­
cies to refine start­ and end­event latency estimations. By comparing the trigger latencies
from the EEG and eye­tracker data, the quality of synchronization was evaluated. Con­
sequently, all errors with synchronization were less than 2 ms.

In the next step, we segmented the EEG data into fixation and saccade clips with the
length of 1 second (500 time­points). The segmentation are conducted based on the
event labels from eye tracker. Figure 3.3 shows a typical distribution of event labels from
eye tracker. For saccades, the clips are generated by setting the middle of a saccade
at 500 ms (the 250th time point) and cutting from 500 ms before the saccade to 500 ms
after it. For fixations, 1 second­long clips are obtained by cutting from the beginning of
the fixation. There are examinations to ensure that no saccade or blink occurs in this one
second.

Post­hoc Methods
This Section is the first half of the methodology, where we will describe the post­hoc
methods implemented in this thesis. Firstly, motivation for applying post­hoc methods
is elaborated, where a brief introduction of categories in explainability methods is given.
After that, Layer­wise Relevance Propagation (LRP, as an example of gradient­based

3.2.1
Motivation
While bringing in significant improvement in performance, advanced artificial intelligence
models also raise concerns about the unclear and nontransparent process in getting the
outcome, which mainly results from the black box design. Therefore, many Explainabil­
ity methods are developed to provide explanations and enhance the transparency of AI
models.
Explainability methods in Deep Learning could be divided into two broad groups based
on model independence: model­specific methods and model­agnostic methods [82, 83].
Model­specific methods disclose the explanations by the model itself, turning the black
box less ”black” and more ”glassy” by changing the model design. Therefore they are
also defined as Transparent Models, Opaque Models or Integrated Interpretable Models
[84]. On the other hand, model­agnostic methods could be applied to multiple tasks and
models. As most model­agnostic methods approximate the behaviour of a black­box by
post­train analysis, they are also called post­hoc methods.
This thesis investigates both groups of explainability methods in Deep Learning. While
post­hoc methods are introduced here, the designs of the interpretable Deep Learning
models, which aim at increasing model transparency and further model performance, will
be narrated in detail in Section 3.3.
There are many advantages to post­hoc methods in explaining model prediction. One
of the most important is the flexibility [85]. While most interpretable models that explain
themselves by design only target one task, post­hoc methods could be used in different
tasks and applied to other models. On top of that, post­hoc methods could be used when
comparing the explainability between different models.
According to the implementing details, post­hoc explainability methods could be cate­
gorised into Gradient­based methods and Surrogate models. In this thesis, we imple­
mented Layer­wise Relevance Propagation [86] for Gradient­based methods, and adopted
Local Interpretable Model­Agnostic Explanations(LIME) [38] for surrogate models.
3.2.2
Gradient­based methods
As the name suggests, gradient­based explainability methods justify the model prediction
by analyzing the deep learning model throughout the gradient. In Deep Learning, the
gradient is a vector that indicates the network optimization direction. For image­like data,
the more significant gradient for a pixel means, the more profound influence this pixel has
on the prediction. Therefore, we can discover the most predictive area in one image using
gradient­based methods.
There are several gradient­based methods used for image­like data in previous researches,
such as Gradient­based Saliency Map [87], Layer­Wise Relevance Propagation [86], Inte­
grated Gradients [88], DeepLIFT [89], Grad­CAM [90] and so on. In this work, we applied

the Layer­wise Relevance Propagation to probe how/why the benchmark models arrived
at their own predictions.

3.2.3
Surrogate models
A surrogate model is a technique in engineering that applies a simpler model to estimate
a complicated or incalculable process. Regarding explainability, surrogate models refer
to the method that uses a simple and interpretable model to approximate the prediction of
a black­box model. The idea behind surrogate models is converting the model decryption
into the input­prediction mapping decryption.
The most common machine learning models that are defined as self­interpretable or trans­
parent, and could be used as the simple models in surrogate methods, are linear regres­
sion, logistic regression, decision trees, naive Bayes classifier, and K­Nearest Neighbors
[85].
Surrogate models could be applied both globally and locally. However, unlike global sur­
rogate models, which use one simple model to approximate the holistic prediction, local
surrogate models train multiple local surrogate models to estimate individual predictions.
One of the well­known examples for surrogate models is Local Interpretable Model­Agnostic
Explanations(LIME) [38], which uses perturbations to train local surrogate models. Com­
bining the Shapely Values in game theory and local surrogate idea, SHAP [39] is another
surrogate method that is popularly used. Here in this work, we applied LIME as the sur­
rogate model to investigate how the DL models predict the outcomes.

The idea behind LIME is intuitive: it tries to re­learn a simple interpretable model around
an individual prediction by generating more ’fake’ data. More specifically, data generation
is realized by perturbing the original data. After feeding generated data into the black­box
model, the relationship between perturbations and predictions is discovered by fitting a
surrogate model like linear regression. The learned model would be a good estimation
locally but not globally.

into superpixels (see Figure 3.9b and Algorithm 2), the perceptual grouping of pixels, and
the permutation is applied by randomly selecting superpixels to be present or absent.

absence) of all superpixels, which could be formally represented as 𝑋′ = {0, 1}𝑝′, just as
text data. Figure 3.9 shows one example of explaining image classification using LIME.

interpretable by each column. However, the permutation method for tabular data varies
from text and image data. According to lime package [93], perturbation is done by sam­
pling from a normal distribution with a men­centring and scaling according to the training
data for numerical features and sampling based on the training distribution for categorical
features.

3.3.1
Motivation
3.3.1.1
The EEG Artefacts
Artefacts are signal distortions that contaminate recording and influence further analysis.
Though the ideal handling way is to prevent artefacts from happening during EEG record­
ing, this is too difficult to realize as some artefacts are avoidable due to the nature of the
EEG techniques [98].
EEG artefacts could be divided into three main groups based on their originates: envi­
ronment noise, experimental error and physiological artifacts [99]. Environment noise
could also be called as external noise, which comes from the outside environment, such
as electrical power and air humidity. Environment noise could be well processed by two
means: physical shield and filters. In this work, all data were recorded in an electromag­
netically shielded laboratory. Besides, as the frequency and amplitude of this kind of noise
are dissimilar with brain signals, it could be filtered easily [99] [100]. The pre­processing
step accomplished the elimination of environmental noise in this work.
Experimental error refers to the technical issues during EEG recording which are caused
by hardware. For example, some common experimental errors could be: broken wire
contacts, too much or dried gel, loose electrodes and cable movements [101]. Though
some of which can be took control of, e.g. examination on wires before testing could
ensures wire quality, some of them are hard to prevent, e.g. loose electrodes and dried
gel are frequently happened even though the gel will be re­fill every 30 minutes.
Physiological artifacts are also considered as internal noise since they are generated
from human body, e.g. eye blinks, heart beat, muscle activity. Those artifacts, unlike
environmental noise, have a similar frequency and amplitude compared with brain signal,
and are inevitable during recording. Generally, physiological artifacts are categorized as
three type: ocular artifacts (or EOG), muscle artifacts (or EMG) and cardiac artifacts (or
ECG) [99] [102]. In contrast to other EEG studies, in this specific task of eye movement
prediction, ocular activities are not considered as noise but information source. That’s
why we implemented minimal pre­processing in Section 3.1.1.3.

Motivation
In the first two architectures, the convolution operation was performed either along the

information that keeps being untouched is the Spatial Relationship between electrodes.

For example, in the light blue circle in Figure 3.16a, the neighbors of electrode №1 include

Graphical Nature of EEG Data
In the two above architectures, EEG data is regarded as image­like data, however, EEG
data is naturally graph­structured when considering each electrode as a node. According
to the mechanism of EEG [111], the recording from one electrode is the summation of the
synchronized activity of thousands of neurons on a small piece of scalp. Therefore, the
values from geographically close electrodes are highly correlated, and this relationship
between electrodes could be abstracted into links in the graph theory. Figure 3.16b is
one example of EEG graph, where the links are defined according to the distances. In
this example, only positive weighted links exist which are denoted with purple color.
Electrode Dropout
The Electrode Sensitive Component for denoising could be applied by dropping out the
problematic electrodes in the GCN scheme. Precisely, we could prevent the influence of
problematic electrodes during graph convolution by zeroing the weights of the links from
the problematic electrodes. As shown in Figure 3.16b, electrode №40 has 8 neighbors.
However, some of the electrodes are considered noise for this sample, including 2 of the
neighbors: electrode №39 and №45. Using the Electrode Dropout idea, the influence from
the problematic electrodes could be canceled. Thus, electrode №40 will only aggregate
the values from the trustworthy neighbors, as shown in Figure 3.16c.
In conclusion, Graph Convolutional Networks have advantages in the reuse of domain
knowledge and flexible electrode­based manipulation: it enables the utilization of spa­
tial information by re­forming the EEG data into the graph. The spatial knowledge has
not been used in the previously proposed architectures. In addition, the denoising ma­
nipulation could be performed by updating the adjacency matrix when executing graph
convolution.

Graph Initialization
The adjacency matrix in GCN could be trainable or fixed. In both manners, the initialization
of the adjacency matrix is essential. In previous work using the GCN model for EEG­based
tasks, there are two main strategies. The first one is to use predetermined and fixed graph
during the whole learning process [114, 115, 116], and the second one lets the network
to learn the graph where the graph is randomly initialized [117, 118]. In [114] and [116],
the comparisons between these two strategies are also conducted, and the conclusion
for both works is that the learnable graph has a better performance.
In this work, we try to combine both ideas by implementing a learnable graph which ini­
tialized with domain knowledge. To be more specific, we first initialize the graph using
EEG knowledge, then we let the network adaptively update the adjacency matrix during
the training. In this way, the intrinsic linkages between EEG electrodes could be discov­
ered by the learning process, while the expertise in the neuroscience field constrain the
electrode connections from the initialization step.
The problem now lies in how to initialize the graph based on EEG knowledge. In previous
studies which use predetermined graph in GCN model, the graphs are designed based on
a) spatial information [114, 116], i.e. a direct connection is determined if the two electrodes
are closely related in physical space; b) correlation between electrodes [115]; c) based
on other EEG characteristic, such as phase­locking value (PLV) [119], and so on.
As mentioned in Section 3.3.4.1, one motivation for using the GCN model is absorbing
the spatial information of EEG. Therefore, here we implement a similar strategy like [114]
and [116]. Nevertheless, instead of only assigning value to closely related electrodes, we
set a threshold for the shortest distance between ”connected” electrodes, i.e. as long as
the distance between two electrodes is short than the hyper­parameter threshold 𝑡, then
we regard an edge with weight 1 between the two electrodes:

4.3
A Philosophical Discussion on Explainability for Time
Series Data
Explanations should be composed of meaningful elements that could be represented by
machines and understood by humans at the same time. The element here is a minimized
unit that recalls unified human cognition. In image­based tasks, the element could be
superpixels. And for text­based tasks, the element could be the word in the sentences.
The difficulty lies in this task and many similar tasks in the field is that, we do not have
a consensus about what should be the element for time­series data. In the second part
of this work, we set the element as electrodes and the explanation over time is therefore
unavailable.
4.3.1
The Difference between Time Series Data and Other Data
The lack of consensus for element in time series data might be caused by the data nature.
The most commonly seen data types in the discussion of explainability are image, text,
and tabular data. There is one thing in common for all three: the data is inherently dis­
crete. Text data is a sequence of words and punctuation, tabular data is a combination of
several features (the data inside one feature could be continued, but for a single sample,
its components are not continuous), and image data, which might be often considered as
continues data in terms of the pixel extent, are actually the captures of the physical world
which consists of objects. In other words, all these data originated from discrete items.
Naturally, the minimized items are taken as the element in explanations (i.e. a piece of
superpixels, a word, and a value from one column).
By contrast, time series data, defined as a collection of observations obtained through
repeated measurements over time, is inherently continuous. Some typical time series
data could be: the daily closing value of a stock, physiological signals (EEG, EMG, etc.),
and rainfall measurements. It is very hard to naturally partition the time series data into
segments and assign a universal understanding for most of the humans.
4.3.2
The essentials for a meaningful explanation
Explainability is ultimately a mapping function from human cognition to machine represen­
tation. Hence, how to disassemble human cognition so that machines can represent it is
particularly critical for a good explanation. Take image data as an example; the contents
of Figure 4.16 include a border collie and a Christmas hat, both of which could be split
apart from the original image and represented by a machine as a collection of superpixels
(Figure 4.16b). The human cognition of recognizing the border collie could be disassem­
bled into deep levels with more details, e.g. a patch of fur colour which is white & black,
or even further, e.g. the striped black­white­black features on the forehead.
What we could learn from this case is that an image could be disassembled not only from
a machine’s perspective but also cognition­wisely. In addition, the decomposability of the
image is related to the nature of the image, a momentary capture of the combination of
objects in the physical world.
Accordingly, it could be concluded that a meaningful explanation relies on a cognition­
matched disassembling. The minimized unit in disassembling is denoted as element in
this section. To bring an explanation accepted by the majority, the element needs to have
a universal human understanding.
4.3.3
Discretization of Time Series
Now we go back to the question of how to disassemble time series data, or in other words,
what is the element for time series data. There are two main strategies of discretization
of time series data in the past studies. In the majority of the studies [124] [125], time

4.3.4
Beyond Human Cognition
It could also be doubted that there is no perfect discretization for time series data, as the
profound deconstruction of human cognition for time series data and image data might be
completely different. In terms of understandability, image and text data are much closer
to humans and, therefore, easier to understand, while time series data could be more ab­
stract. Unlike the decomposing strategy for image and text data, another way of explaining
time series data could be somehow reversed compared to the commonly used explain­
ability methods. Instead of passively matching human understanding, actively creating
and assisting human cognition could be another option in the future.
A traditional model for time series data, the Autoregressive–moving­average model (ARAM),
might provide some clues for extending human cognition with machines. By extracting a
time­dependent component, a constant and a noise from the original indivisible time se­

4.4
Limitations
This thesis, however, is subject to several limitations. Part of the limitations are caused
by the reliability of data (Section 4.4.1 and Section 4.4.2), while some of them are related
to the exploration for explainability (Section 4.4.3 and Section 4.4.4).
4.4.1
Calibration Failure from Eye Tracker
One of the limitations of this study had to do with the calibration failure of the eye tracker.
As mentioned in Section 4.2.3.4, even though the calibration process was carried out
before each procedure, sometimes it failed and brought inevitable errors in this task. Ev­
idence shows that the distances between the stimuli positions and where participants ac­
tually looked according to the infrared eye tracker are around 20 pixels. However, there
is no reliable method to decompose the calibration error and the error made by the par­
ticipants for our dataset. If the calibration error could be further reduced in the future by
more precise measurement, it would benefit the learning process and make the prediction
more reliable.
4.4.2
The Noisiness of EEG Data
The noisiness of the EEG data is another dataset­related limitation. Although much of this
thesis (the interpretable DL model part, Section 3.3) is devoted to handling the noisy elec­
trodes by building an explicit electrode­sensitive component in the deep learning frame­
work, the strategy highly relies on the model itself. While the electrode­sensitive compo­
nent could be pretty flexible to learn an optimized solution in the feasible domain space,
it lacks the necessary constraints, e.g. parameters modelling the noise of EEG data. In
other words, the model has very little prior knowledge during training, especially for the
CNN model with attention blocks. In addition, in this work, we consider the noises in EEG
data in an electrode­wise manner. Nevertheless, the noisiness could also happen just in
a part of the signals for one electrode. More significantly, managing the noisiness of EEG
data does benefit not only our task but also other EEG­based tasks. In short, denoising
EEG data before or during model training is essential and worth further work in the future.
4.4.3
The Explainability inside Models
In [85], interpretability has been classified into several levels: a) algorithm transparency,
b) global interpretability, c) global model interpretability on a modular level, d) local in­
terpretability for a single case or a group. We have explored most of the levels here;
however, algorithm transparency was only investigated by the electrode­sensitive com­
ponent. It remains unclear how the other parts of the DL model (e.g. layers) work when
predicting.
There are some representative researches about layer­wise visualization in Deep Learn­
ing. For example, the CNNVis [129] provides the visual analytics system for the CNN
model by formulating the CNN structure as Directed Acyclic Graph(DAG). LSTMVis [130]
is another work that visually analyses recurrent neural networks applied in Natural Lan­
guage Processing (NLP).
Although the layer­wise analysis could be visually insightful, it should be mentioned that
it is relatively difficult, even for image and text data where the DL techniques are more
mature than EEG data. Furthermore, such visualization tools are only applied for basic
deep learning frameworks like CNN and RNN, the advanced architecture like ResNet is
more challenging and has not yet been discovered.

Conclusion
This thesis aims to explore the interpretability of deep learning models for gaze estimation
using EEG data. Gaze information, as one of the most important behaviours that shows
human interest, was mainly estimated by infrared eye tracker, which is too costly to ap­
ply to daily life. Among all other alternatives, EEG is a safe and economical option that
records the brain’s electrical activity. In the preceding studies, a large dataset of EEG
and eye tracking data (EEGEyeNet) and the basic deep learning framework benchmarks
have proved that with enough EEG data, it can reliably train large neural models and get
complicated features in high­dimension space. To further improve the performance of
gaze estimation using EEG data, as well as enhance the user trust for future applications,
explainability becomes a necessary step and the new challenge.
According to the previously explored explainability methods for EEG data, two main strate­
gies are being used. One uses the post­hoc method to analyse the trained model and its
prediction, and the other creates an interpretable model and explains its own insight.
In this thesis, these two main approaches have been conducted. Two models have been
implemented when using the post­hoc analysis for existing deep learning models, LRP
and LIME, as examples of gradient­based methods and surrogate models, respectively.
To better adapt to the EEG data properties, we also proposed new perturbation techniques
in the LIME model. The results demonstrated that the prefrontal electrodes contributed
significantly more to the prediction than the other electrodes. Furthermore, in the tem­
poral dimension, the beginning and end of the clip matter more. This result adequately
demonstrates the significance of EOG data in gaze estimation and raises new research
problems regarding the optimal utilization of the visual function­regulating Occipital lobe.
Second, based on the goal of explicitly handling the noisiness caused by individual elec­
trodes in EEG data, we brought up the concept of the electrode­sensitive component and
implemented it in Attention­CNN and Auto­Dropout­GCN by creating the attention blocks
and updating the graph. The results illustrate that the proposed models not only quanti­
tatively surpass the benchmark, but also demonstrate the dependability of the outcomes
via visualization. By presenting the location of problematic electrodes and the weights
we obtained from attention blocks, it could be found that the attention blocks are able to
avoid the problematic area and focus more on the trustworthy electrodes. The visualiza­
tion of the learned graph demonstrated that domain knowledge assists the performance
by giving a regular and reasonable adjacency matrix, and it also learned the negative
connection between the left and right frontal areas during training. In addition, a variety
of graph initialization, information source, and dataset splits are discussed and analyzed,
where we compared the results from different settings. One of the crucial conclusions
here is that the calibration failure caused by the eye tracker hardware might be able to re­
move by predicting the end fixation directly from the saccade. It should also be noted that
due to limited combinations of dots sequence in the EEGEyeNet dataset, the reliability of
predicting absolute positions from saccades should be further examined.
By employing the post­hoc methods and creating interpretable DL models, this thesis
explores the explainability of Deep Learning in gaze estimation using EEG data. Non­
only are the proposed models and results applicable in this case study’s context, but they
also contribute to the cross fields of neuroscience and computer science. In particular,

Polarity of the Eye
Here we will introduce the polarity of the eye. This part is the reorganized information of
the video [131], which is provided as one episode of a series of videos for EEG learners.
Figure B.1 is a diagram of an eye from the lateral perspective. The polarity of an eye
means that the retina is relatively electronegative, and the cornea is relatively electropos­
itive. Figure B.2 illustrates eyes when looking at the head from the top down. When look­
ing to the left, the electrodes on the left side (e.g. F7 in Figure B.3) of the scalp will receive
a net positive charge as they are close to the positively charged cornea. Conversely, the
electrodes on the right side, like F8, are close to the negatively charged retina; therefore,
they will get a negative charge. In this case, we assume that other electrodes are far away
enough to experience any charge from the eyes, so we set those electrodes as zero. In
real­world cases, the influences from the eyes will be reduced by the length.
