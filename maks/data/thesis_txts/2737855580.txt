Abstract
This thesis explores the potential for reducing operational uncertainty in wind power plants
(WPPs) through hybridization with solar photovoltaic (PV) systems and energy storage
solutions. As renewable energy sources like wind and solar supply more of the global en-
ergy demand, their inherent variability presents challenges in maintaining grid stability and
meeting energy market commitments. This study specifically investigates the hybridiza-
tion of WPPs with solar PV and Energy storage systems (ESS) to enhance reliability in
the day-ahead (DA) energy market.
A comprehensive stochastic optimization framework is used to model the hybrid power
plant’s (HPP) participation in the energy market, incorporating forecast errors and intra-
hour generation fluctuations. The Energy Management System (EMS) is central to this
framework, tasked with optimizing bid strategies in the DA market to mitigate uncertain-
ties in power dispatch. A simulation algorithm was developed to simulate the resulting
operation of the power plant, when it uses the DA schedule.The methodology includes
extensive simulations to analyze different configurations of the HPP, focusing on param-
eters such as the size of the PV system and the capacity of the ESS.
Key findings reveal that overplanting with PV significantly enhances the dispatch reliability
of WPPs, though this benefit is somewhat overstated due to the exclusion of intra-hour
PV fluctuations in the model. Conversely, the contribution of ESS in the reducing devia-
tions due forecast error is limited, suggesting a need for high-capacity, long-term storage
solutions or market mechanisms to address these errors effectively.
The study concludes that hybridization can indeed reduce operational uncertainty for
WPPs, offering insights into optimal plant configurations and policy implications. Future
research should explore the economic aspects of hybridization, the role of empirical data,
and the impact of intra-hour PV fluctuations on the results. This research contributes
to the ongoing development of reliable renewable energy solutions, aligning with global
sustainability goals.

CHAPTER 1 Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
Renewable energy sources are becoming an increasingly significant part of the global
power system. In 2023 the records for the most amount newly installed capacity for both
wind power generation and photovoltaic (PV) power generation in year was broken. 116
GW of wind power [1] and 510 GW PV power [2] was added to grids all over the world.
It is projected that by 2026, 46% of all generation will come from renewable sources and
nuclear power[2]. This acceleration will only continue as countries around the world race
towards their net-zero goals that wsa agreed upon in the Paris Agreement[3].
Inflexibility and intermittency is one of the downsides of variable renewable energy (VRE)
generating. Hybrid power plants (HPP) combine the production of multiple energy sources
and usually integrate with an energy storage system (ESS). Combining multiple renew-
able sources has a positive synergies in terms of increasing reliability, profitability, and
utilization of the power plant’s infrastructure and land use[4]. Numerous power plant con-
figurations have been developed, and at the end of 2023, 240 HPPs with various con-
figurations of renewable energy sources were operating in the USA[5]. The International
Energy Agency (IEA) has proposed an IEA wind task specifying that hybrid power plants
are a technology that will help develop wind energy[6].
Furthermore, power plant developers and operators are balancing the need to make and
operate the power plant in a reliable and profitable manner. In most advanced economies,
power plants operate in an energy market. Lead times on energy bids differ in different
energy markets, but most require the power producer to submit how much energy the
power plant will generate with some lead time. In the Nord Pool day ahead (DA) market
where many danish energy producers sell their energy, power producers are required to
submit their bids for the coming day 12 hours in advance. After energy is bought and sold,
the power plant producers are legally obligated to supply the energy they have sold and
fines are associated with not delivering what energy they sold on the market. Because of
this, renewable energy producers have an incentive to make the most accurate possible
bid on the energy market.
VRE producers face additional challenges when operating in the energy market compared
to conventional energy producers. WP and PV generation must be forecasted based
on environmental prediction models. It is paramount for renewable energy generators
that these models can predict wind speeds and solar irradiance with good resolution for
the area where the power plant is located. However, even state-of-the-art models have
forecast errors.
1.2
Objectives and Research Questions
The objective of this project is to study the potential of reducing the uncertainty related to
bidding energy in the DA market for a wind power plant by hybridizing it with a solar power
plant and an energy storage system. The research questions that will be answered are:
• Can the uncertainty related to operating a wind power plant in the DA market, when

hybridizing it with solar PV and energy storage?
• What parameters of the hybridized power plant have the greatest effect on reducing

1.3
Assumptions and Limitations
The sources of uncertainty that are modelled in the project are the forecast errors and
the intra hour fluctuations in the VRE generation. These sources are not related to the
electrical dynamics of the power plants, and have variations over long periods. Simulation
electrical dynamics over longer periods would take large amounts of computational time.
Therefore it is assumed that the power plant will maintain a stable electrical power output
over a 5-minute period. Additionally, potential uncertainties from the grid or the plant’s
operation that could affect generation and dispatch are not considered. The main objec-
tive of the study is to investigate the operational reliability of the plant, not its profitability.
Therefore, electrical prices and load demand are not considered,.
Due to these assumptions, only bidding in the (DA) market is studied. The ancillary market
is primarily for grid stability, and bidding in the intra day market would not reduce the
operational uncertainty of bidding in the DA market.
1.4
Contribution
This project has the following contribution
• Development of a method for studying the reduction of uncertainty by hybridization
• Development of a simulation algorithm for studying the resulting operation of a power
plant when it tries to follow a DA schedule.
• Parameter study of power plant parameters that are related to the reduction of un-
certainty.
• An investigation into the HPP sizing that would reduce uncertainty to the most.

CHAPTER 2 Literature Review
This literature review aims to investigate the hybrid power plants concept, the European
energy markets, uncertainty sources in VRE power scheduling, stochastic problem mod-
elling methods, and power plant studies where the reductions of uncertainty is studied.
The intent of investigating these topics is too make a valid method for studying the project
problem. All information presented in the chapter is based on research articles.
2.1
Hybrid power plants
In reference [7], 3 general categories of hybrid power plants are defined: co-located,
virtual, and full hybrids.
Co-located hybrid power plants are placed in the same location, but the operation of each
power plant is primarily independent. A co-located power plant has multiple cost-saving
opportunities related to the balance of system costs and a shared interconnection.
Virtual power plants are not placed on the same location, but are just operationally linked.
The primary benefit of virtual power plants is that by coordinating the operation of multiple
power plants, grid services can be provided that a independent power plant wouldn’t be
able to provide. This gives the power plant an opportunity to participate in energy markets,
which the independent power plant might have limited access to. An additional benefit
over the co-located plant is that each of the power plants can be optimally sited.
Fully hybrid power plants are both operationally and locationally linked. To achieve this,
components and control strategies are shared between the different VRE and ESS in the
power plant. Fully hybrid power plants are the main focus of this project and will therefore
be discussed further. This type of HPP will henceforth be referenced as hybrid power
plant (HPP).
HPPs have several theoretical benefits. According to Wind Europe, these benefits in-
clude:[4]:
• Increased use of electrical infrastructure
• Higher yearly capacity factors and more stable power output over time
• Easier fulfillment of system-, site-, and owner-specific power production require-
ments
• Advantages when dispatching power in the market
• Reduced infrastructure cost, and more efficient land usage
• Reduced balancing cost and less energy curtailment
HPPs can also benefit from the anticorrelation between solar PV and wind power forecast
[8].

2.2
Energy markets
The Danish energy market has multiple markets where energy can be sold. Roughly, the
market can be split into energy markets and ancillary service markets[12]. In the energy
markets, energy can be sold and bought at different lead times before the actual produc-
tion happens. The bulk of the energy that is produced is sold on the spot market/day-
ahead market. In this market, energy producers bid the energy that they will produce the
following day at specific time slots. Consumers can place orders on the market or buy
specific bids. In the Nord Pool energy market, these bids must be submitted 12 hours be-
fore production. After this point, the market is cleared, and binding contracts are made for
the sold energy. Between the day-ahead market and real-time production, energy can be
bought and sold in the intra-day market. This can continue until 1 hour before real-time
production. Between the intra-hour market and actual production, there is a balancing
market, which is run by the TSO Energinet in Denmark.
In the balancing market, balancing service providers are responsible for regulating imbal-
ances in the system at the behest of Energinet. This includes both upward and downward

regulations. The last market is the ancillary service market. In this market, energy pro-
ducers and consumers sell services related to supporting the grid when faults occur. The
three main services are frequency support service, voltage support service, and black
start support service. Energinet can call upon these service providers when grid stability
needs support. These services are crucial, especially as intermittent renewable energy
becomes a larger share of the energy generation mix. The uncertainty related to renew-

2.3
Uncertainty sources
Forecasted wind and solar PV power are based on models that try to predict future weather
conditions. The lead time required for forecasts can vary depending on the energy market.
For the Nordic market, the lead time for submitting bids is 12 hours [13].
Different wind speed and density forecasting methods are used baed on time horizons
and lead times of the forecast[14]. Short-term forecasts are needed to bid in the spot mar-
ket. These short-term forecasts are based on either measured weather data, numerical
weather predictions (NWP), or a combination of both. NWP uses complex mathematical
models for climate systems and measurements from a multitude of sources to predict the
weather.
Forecast error can be seen as a nonlinear function with a multitude of inputs that each
affect the amount and distribution of the error. provides an extensive list of Factors that
impact forecast include time horizons, system size, and landscape[14] .
Solar irradiance forecasts with lead times of hours to days are commonly done using
numerical weather prediction (NWP) models [14]. Forecasting errors in solar irradiance
are mainly due to NWP models not being able to fully simulate small-scale clouds in their
models. Therefore, the movement of these clouds is simplified as parameterizations.

In [17], the IEA defines firm power as ”the capability for an electricity generating resource
to meet a given electrical load (e.g., the demand of a power grid) 24 hours a day and 365
days a year”. The articles then states that Achieving firm VRE generation can be accom-

It is degined in[17] that overbuilding/overplanting is when the installed capacity of some
VRE resources is greater than the rated power of the plant and exceeds the power injec-

stable power output by curtailing its generation.
In [17] Several case studies demonstrate how firm power could be achieved in power

The objective function of the entire stochastic problem of operating an HPP reliably in the
Northern European energy market over a given period can be defined as a minimization
problem of some measure of the deviation from the schedule. This problem is not tractable
for solving as a linear optimization problem since the schedule deviation is based on
simulations of the HPP. For this reason, the problem is solved as a sequence simulation
where the results from each simulation feed into the next sequence.

3.3
Data generation
In the stochastic frame work the information variable are the forecast for the power plant
generation. These forecast can be simulated using the tool correlations in Renewable
Energy Sources (CorRES) that is developed at DTU Wind[18]. CorRes can generate,
power generation timeseries for specific WPP and PVPP, and it can generate forecast
for the WPP that are correlated with its generation. For generating correlated forecast
for the PV generation, a Matlab script was provided that uses the clear sky method for
calculating the forecast. Both forecast have realistically calculate forecast errors, but only
the generated WPP generation has intrahour fluctions in the generation. to simulate wind
and solar generation time series . It is used in this project to generate the generation data
for the wind power plant and the solar power plant being studied. Additionally, it is used
to generate forecasted power and intra-hour fluctuations for the wind power.
3.3.1
WPP generation timeseries
CorRES uses the reanalysis atmospheric data from ERA5[19] as mesoscale data input.
This data is then downscaled with the mean value of wind speed at some specific height
from Global Wind Atlas 2[20] to generate the wind speed at the location of a given wind
power plant. The mesoscale downscaling method often underestimates the short-term
variability of wind[21]. Therefore, intra-hour fluctuations are added to the wind speeds.
These intra-hour fluctuations are modeled as a stochastic process to better simulate the
short-term variability of wind speed.
3.3.2
WPP generation forecast timeseries
CorRES can also generate forecasts for a given WPP. The error in forecasted wind speed
is modeled to have realistic temporal characteristics and is correlated between different
locations. This error is then added to the wind speed data [21].
3.3.3
PVPP generation time series
CorRES uses the same mesoscale downscaling method of the ERA5 atmospheric data
to generate the solar irradiance in a grid. From the solar irradiance, the generated so-
lar power is then calculated using the Python library pvlib-python. With a given location,
installed capacity, surface azimuth, and surface tilt, the generated power of a solar photo-
voltaic (solar PV) power plant can be simulated. The time series that CorRES generates
do not contain any intra-hour power fluctuations. It was deemed out of scope for this
project to apply intra-hour fluctuations to the dataset. This limitation is taken into account
when concluding the results of the project.
3.3.4
PVPP generation forecast
CorRES does not have the capability to generate forecasts. The forecasts and forecast
errors are created using the clear sky method.
3.3.5
Clear sky
Using the clear sky method described in references [22] and [23], different realizations of
PV forecasts can be generated. The method can create forecasts for an area using data
from the Weather Research and Forecasting (WRF) model and the ”clear sky model.”
The forecast error is then generated to capture the spatial and temporal characteristics of
cloud movements. This method creates forecasts on a regional level using empirical data
from a 10 km² grid rather than at a plant level. While this approach may be inaccurate for
a specific plant, it is deemed sufficient since it captures the essence of forecast errors.
Using CorRES, realistic power generation and forecasted generation time series can be
generated for specific PVPP and WPP. The generated data are validated in a number of
studies, the simulated results from CorRES [21][24][25] and the solar PV forecast method
have been validated in[22] [23].

3.4
Simulation of power plant operation/Transition function
In the stochastic framework, a function is used to calculate the state between periods.
To do this a simulation algorithm of the power plant operation is made, with the intent of
having a method for simulating if the power plant is able to follow the DA schedule. The
simulation calculates the transition to next period of the stochastic problem, which in this
problem context is the next day. For the ESS it will calculated what the state of charge
(SOC) will be in the next day. The SOC both used in the DA schedule and the simulation
in the next day.
3.4.1
Power plant simulation in real time (RT simulation)
To analyze how the power plant operates in real time, a simulation algorithm is devel-
oped for the operation of the power plant. The simulation is the transition function of the
stochastic problem. The objective of the algorithm is to simulate the hybrid power plant’s
operation when it has to follow the DA plan made by the EMS.
Inputs to the algorithm are the DA generation schedule and the simulated real-time (RT)
generation. Both the DA schedule and the RT generation data are power setpoints that
the EMS in theory would send to the power plant controller. Each value in the RT time
series represents a certain generation of the power plant in the next 5 minutes. From the
DA plan, the 5-minute setpoint for the power plant that results in the energy sold on the
market is given. In the first decision, three things can occur:
• If the DA schedule and the RT generation are the same, the ESS does not need to
take any action.
• If there is a positive difference between the RT generation and the DA plan, meaning
the RT generation is higher than the DA plan, two things can happen:
1. If the overgeneration can be absorbed by the ESS, it will be
2. If the ESS does not have sufficient capacity, the excess generation will be cur-
tailed. The ESS will first absorb what it can, and the remainder will be curtailed.
• If there is a negative difference between the RT generation and the DA plan, mean-
ing the RT generation is lower than the DA plan, two things can happen:
1. If the ESS has stored enough energy to dispatch at the level of the difference,
it will do so.
2. If the ESS cannot fully compensate, it will dispatch at its maximum level, and
the remaining difference will result in a power deviation.
In both cases, the power absorption and dispatch levels are also limited by the ESS. In
some scenarios, the ESS may have the capacity to regulate the difference but will be
limited by its power absorption and dispatch limits. When this happens, the ESS will
operate at its maximum limit for both dispatch and absorption.

might have. Three analysis will be done, a preliminary analysis, the hybridization analysis,
where the reduction of SDF will be analysed, and a parameter study on the effect of
storage capacity, the overplating with PV amount and the objective weight. As part of the
parameter study a analysis will be done on the combination of overplating and storage
capacity that lead to the lowest SDF and CuF will be done.

6.1
Introduction
The aim of the study was to develop a methodology to investigate whether hybridization
of a Wind Power Plant (WPP) would reduce uncertainty for the power plant participating in
the Day-Ahead (DA) energy market and to explore the effects of overplanting and energy
storage on reducing this uncertainty.
6.2
Key Findings and Interpretation
Hybridization
The hybridization study aligned well with the framework of a stochastic problem as defined
in[16]. This framework consists of five elements:
• State: Corresponding to the power plant model, the generation forecast, and the
distribution of the forecast.
• Actions: Corresponding to the actions the (EMS) can take. The actions are based
on two different policies. These the reults from these action is a DA generation
schedule.
• Exogenous Information: Corresponding to the actual generation of the power
plant and the resulting state of the Energy Storage System (ESS) after the oper-
ation of the power plant.
• Transition Function: Corresponding to the simulation of the power plant’s opera-
tion for each individual day.
• Objective Function: Corresponding to the function that aims to minimize the Sched-
ule Deviation Factor (SDF) and Capacity Utilization Factor (CuF) over the entire
study period.
To solve the objective function, the problem was addressed in sequences corresponding
to each individual day over a one-year study period.
The two policies were combined in the EMS using an objective weight to balance the
importance of each policy. The specific weight that resulted in the lowest SDF while bal-
ancing the CuF was determined through a separate sensitivity analysis of the previous
year. It was found that when the objective weight was tuned towards weighting the look
ahead policy the smallest SDF could be achieved. This resulted in the EMS making robust
DA generation schedules.
Two metrics, SDF and CuF, were used to evaluate the study. These metrics explained
the results and the trade-offs between reliability and utilization of the VRE resources.

6.3
Implications
Overplanting Implication on the Reduction of SDF
The parameter study shows that it is possible to achieve a small SDF with less overplant-
ing and more storage. This suggests that the ratio of overplanting to storage is important.
This is because, as overplanting increases, the amplitude of the forecast error also in-
creases. A larger forecast error leads to a greater difference between the scheduled
generation and the actual generation. Therefore, with less overplanting and a larger stor-
age capacity, the scheduling errors are smaller, and there is more energy available to
reduce these errors.
When comparing curtailment due to scheduling errors and not overplanting, the CuF in-
creases by only 1.2%. This result would likely be larger if intra-hour fluctuations were
considered. However, it indicates that forecast errors from the PVPP forecast have a
small impact during periods when PV generation is lower than the POC limit.
ESS Implication on the Reduction of SDF
Compared to the improvement overplanting has on the SDF, the ESS provides only mod-
est improvement. The duration curve of the SOC indicates that even though there is plenty
of energy that could be stored in the ESS, it can still fail to regulate differences due to a
lack of stored energy. This indicates that schedule deviations most likely occur in periods
where large forecast errors result in a schedule that is higher than generation for an ex-
tended period. An example of this can be seen in Figure 6.2 where only the scheduled
error and the schedule deviations are plotted. A negative schedule corresponds to a large
scheduled generation than actual generation.

From this it can be concluded that the best method for reducing the SDF from forecast
errors, is by using a robust policies when bidding in the energy market. This means a
ESS is ill-suited to reduce forecast errors.
Intra-Hour Fluctuations in PV Generation and ESS
It should be noted that the benefits of the ESS might be greater than shown in this analysis
since intra-hour fluctuations in PV generation are not simulated. These fluctuations would
increase the SDF and might also increase the CuF. The ESS would be able to decrease
these schedule deviations to some extent, in the same way it decreased intra-hour fluc-
tuations from the WPP. This would lead to a better result compared to only overplanting.
The duration graph of the SOC shows that the ESS is fully charged for long periods, hint-
ing at its ability to supply intra-hour fluctuations that might reduce the power output of the
PVPP. In this case, the deviation caused by the ESS power limitation would constitute a
larger percentage of the errors since PV generation intra-hour fluctuations have greater
variance and the PV rating is much greater than the WPP rating.
Alternative Solutions to Reducing Schedule Deviations Due to Forecast Errors
Another solution could be to import the energy deviation from the intraday market. The
likelihood of a forecast being wrong decreases with shorter lead times, making the intraday
market a viable option.
In the literature, much research has been done on using long-term storage solutions like
pumped hydro as an ESS. Pumped hydro has the advantage of being able to store a large
capacity for longer periods. Its longer ramp time would not be a problem considering that
periods with schedule deviations last from minutes to hours. Of course, an ESS with a
short ramp time would still be needed to reduce deviations due to intra-hour fluctuations.
Therefore, the best ESS option for an HPP, considering reducing schedule deviations
due to forecast errors, would likely be a hybrid ESS (HESS) combining long-term storage
with slow ramp times and short to medium-term ESS with fast ramp times. For the specific
region of DK1, pumped hydro would not be a viable choice due to the lack of geographical
locations that make pumped hydro a good solution.

Limitations
6.4.1
Capacity Factor difference
It was shown that there is a difference between the CF from the generated data from
CorRes and the actual generation data of the WPP used in the study. The CF based
on CorRes data is lower in all years, but the general shape over the whole period is the
same. The difference could be due to the specific power curve used to generate the data,
having too low air density, or due to inaccuracies in CorRes. When the CF for the WPP is
lower, the affect of the intra hour fluctuations and the forecast errors in the schedule error
are smaller. This would results in a different result for the WPP, and HPP without ESS.
Its hard to concluded if it the ESS would be able to handle an these bigger errors. But it
would change the overall results of the study, it might lead to a higher SDF and CuF.
6.4.2
SOC in the DA plan
In the analysis method the EMS makes the DA schedule using the last SOC at the end
of the period. In the energy market the HPP is operating in, it is assumed that the power
schedule is submitted to the market with a lead time of 12 hours. So the value for the
SOC that should be used in the DA schedule, should be the one from the middle of the
period. Using the SOC in scheduling creates a new uncertainty source since at the time
of submitting the energy bids the SOC will not be know. This source of uncertainty is
not considered in this study. If the SOC from the middle of the period was used without
accounting for its stochastic nature, it would likely increase the SDF. This is because,
in the middle of the period and day, PV generation is at its peak on most days. For an
overplanted HPP, this would typically result in the ESS begin fully charged. If the EMS
scheduled based on this assumption, it would bid more generation in the DA market.
PV Generation Model
The PV generation model in CorRes models generation for a PVPP at the regional level,
not the plant level. This means that the solar irradiance used for PV generation is based
on the irradiance hitting a larger region. With this resolution, it is assumed that the solar
irradiance hitting the PVPP is the same everywhere, which is not accurate since there
can be significant variance in irradiance across different parts of a PVPP. This reduces
the accuracy of the results and the specific ESS’s ability to reduce these variations and
fluctuations in power generation.

a ratio of overplanting and storage capacities that were close to 1.

Future Research Directions
There are many opportunities to expand this study.
6.5.1
Economic Viability of Optimal Combinations
The study has only examined the operational and utilization aspects of reducing the un-
certainty of a WPP. The results do not consider the economics of the power plant. A study
on the levelized cost of energy (LCOE) could have been conducted, based on CAPEX and
OPEX data for a PVPP and BESS, and by using historic energy prices in the DK1 market
area for the entire year. These results would indicate if the idea of hybridizing a WPP to
reduce its scheduling deviation would make economic sense.
6.5.2
Energy Surplus
For both the HPP with and without ESS, the percentage of energy curtailed due to gen-
eration being larger than the POC limit is around 19% of the maximum generation.

61
==PAGE:71==
Due to overplanting of the HPP, there are many periods with a significant energy surplus.
In the study, the energy surplus is simply curtailed, but it could be used alternatively in
a behind-the-meter method or stored in long-term storage ESS. Future research should
explore the best options for utilizing this otherwise wasted surplus energy. These options
could increase the revenue of the power plant, and compared to selling energy in the
spot market, the forecasts made based on these new revenue streams would have less
uncertainty since they would not be based on a forecast with a 12-hour lead time.
6.5.3
ESS degradation model
All types of assets in a power plant degenerate as it is begin used. It could be investigated
what would happen if ESS degenerate were modelled in the physical model and taken
into account when the schedules is made.
6.5.4
ESS Technology for Reducing the Schedule Deviation Due to Fore-
cast Errors
Many different ESS technologies exist; in this study, a general ESS model was used, but
as shown, it was ill-equipped to reduce schedule deviation due to forecast errors. The
method used in this study would be suitable for studying the appropriate technology for
this objective. The ESS model could be expanded to take more details of the ESS into
account, like ramp times and degradation of the ESS. It could also be investigated further.
6.5.5
Different Market Participation
It was shown that one of the main reasons for schedule deviations was forecast error. If
the EMS could participate in different markets, it could use different bidding strategies that
could lead to less deviation and more dispatch. This is due to the relationship between
lead time and forecast error, so as the time neared dispatch, energy could be sold in
the market using a less robust and conservative strategy. Deviations could be reduced
by being even more robust in the DA bid, minimizing the likelihood of a scheduling error
leading to a deviation. This should be balanced with optimizing the revenue of the power
plant.
6.5.6
Empirical Data
The data used for the forecast and generation of the WPP and PVPP is from the CorRes
real analysis data simulation. The wind speed data and solar irradiance are accurate to
a certain extent, but the generation data is calculated based on models of the WPP and
PVPP. These models are simplifications of the real power plant. This can also be seen
in the capacity factor calculated by CorRes compared to the empirical one taken from
Energy Styrelsen.
Using empirical data correlated with the forecast, where an accurate forecast error is
modeled, could provide a better indication of the most suitable ESS technology and the
sizing of capacity and overplanting.
6.5.7
Effect of Intra-Hour Fluctuations in PV Generation
As mentioned throughout the report, intra-hour fluctuations are not modeled in the gener-
ation time series of PV generation. Intra-hour fluctuations in PV generation have different
characteristics than those in WPP generation and would, therefore, affect the results dif-
ferently.
The study shows that the BESS can reduce intra-hour fluctuations, but it is uncertain if it
could do the same for PV generation intra-hour fluctuations.
Additionally, there might be dynamics between the two types of intra-hour fluctuations that
could result in unexpected outcomes for both technology choices and sizing, as well as
for the policies used by the EMS.

Generalization of Method
If this method could be generalized, it could be used as a tool for power plant develop-

component, it could also be part of feasibility studies for potentially hybridizing a WPP.
6.5.9
Different Optimization Methods for Schedule Planning
In the project, a stochastic optimization method was used to calculate the DA schedule
based only on the forecast. Historical results of the method were used to investigate and
tune the weight. This resulted in the stochastic optimization method being robustly solved.
If historical knowledge of forecast errors were used, different methods could be applied to
create the DA schedule. Conditional value at risk would be suitable, and distributionally
robust optimization would also be appropriate for making a schedule that could increase
the CF and decrease the SDF.

and that there is a non linear relationship between the CuF and the total generation of the
power plant.
The limitations of the CorRes model and the EMS model were discussed as sources of

Future research was discussed, and this study could be expanded to investigate different

the effect of power plant participation in more markets, the effect of using empirical data,

CHAPTER 7 Conclusion
This project researched if hybrid power plants using wind and solar energy together with
energy storage could reduce the errors between the sold energy on DA market and the
real energy generation of the power plant.
The existing literature on the hybridization of WPPs does not cover the potential improve-
ments in reliability when participating in the DA market that might be achieved through
hybridization. A method was conceived based on a stochastic problem framework. Using
the results of this method and considering its limitations, it was shown that hybridizing
a WPP can indeed reduce the uncertainty of operation in the North European energy
market.
The biggest reduction in uncertainty was due to overplanting the WPP with PV generation,
which resulted in long periods of high dispatch and high reliability. These results could be
misleadingly enhanced by the lack of intra-hour fluctuations in the PV generation, which
were not considered in the project. Conversely, the moderate improvements in reliability
and dispatchability from the ESS are worse due to this omission.
The specific BESS technology modeled is well-suited for reducing deviations due to intra-
hour fluctuations but poorly suited for decreasing deviations due to forecast errors. De-
viations due to forecast errors should be reduced by long-term high-capacity storage or
market mechanisms like importing energy in the intra-hour energy market.
The results of the project points at the following perspectives:
• Power plant developers should also try to diversify their energy usages and revenue
streams away from the DA marked if they aim to operate a reliable HPP. This is due
to the lead time of the energy bids, that cause schedule errors.
• The same is true in terms of the utilization of renewable energy resources, since
increasing reliability comes at the cost of curtailment and worse utilization of these
resources.
• ESS are best suited for reducing fluctuations in generation and not at decreasing
schedule errors due to forecast uncertainties.
• Contract types like Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs) are better-suited for en-
suring the reliability of renewable energy, as PPAs allow the cost of reliability to be
integrated into the contractual terms.
• As prices for PV panels and ESS technologies continue to decrease hybridization
of WPP as a tool to increase its reliability becomes more feasible
