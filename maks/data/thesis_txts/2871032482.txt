Abstract 
The effects of different regimes of feeding on the digestive organs of rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus 
mykiss) were examined, focusing on structural as well as metabolic changes. Three types of growth 
regimes were used to generate different patterns of growth: 28-day starvation, continuous intermediate 
feeding, and continuous ad libitum feeding. Intestine and pyloric caeca were then examined 
histologically and ultrastructurally through light microscopy and transmission electron microscopy. 
Metabolic capacity was measured through tissue respirometry (oxygen uptake) and citrate synthase 
activity assay. Starved trout expressed significant evidence of digestive tissue atrophy, with profoundly 
smaller organ mass as well as thinner mucosa of the intestine, whereas continuously fed trout had 
hypertrophic changes, such as thickened mucosa of the intestine and the pyloric caeca. Despite the 
rather dramatic structural differences, the indicators of metabolic capacity remained constant across 
treatments: mass-adjusted oxygen uptake as well as citrate synthase activity of the intestine and pyloric 
caeca remained not significantly different between starved, intermediate, and ad libitum-fed fish. 
Transmission electron microscopy corroborated the observation, revealing no substantial cellular 
degeneration, mitochondrial density, as well as morphological variations between starved and fed 
treatments. These findings suggest that short-term starvation induces morphological reduction of 
gastrointestinal tissues independently of a reduction of cellular metabolic capacity. In a practical sense, 
they predict that the digestive organs retained the ability to resume function rapidly as well as support 
growth immediately after resituating the feed, reflecting the resilient physiological adaptation with 
considerable significance for survivorship as well as aquaculture feed regulation.

1. Introduction 
1.1. Gastrointestinal system and digestion 
The gastrointestinal (GI) system of the fish plays a key role in the ingestion of food, nutrient 
assimilation, energy production, and overall growth in fish (Bakke et al., 2010). It also plays a role in 
immune function, health and pathology. Like all evolutionary systems, the digestive tract of a fish has 
modified over time to fit the functional demands that are placed on it, allowing different species to 
exploit various diet types based on the surrounding environmental conditions, food availability and 
physiological requirements, which are all essential for survival and ecological fitness (Buddington and 
Kuz’mina, 2000). The digestive tract of a fish can be classified into four main regions: first, the mouth 
and pharynx. Second, the foregut, which begins right after the posterior boundary of the gill cavity or 
the pharynx it includes the oesophagus and the stomach. Third, the midgut consists of the intestine and 
last, the hindgut, containing the distal part of the intestine and the rectum (Bone and Moore, 2008; 
Figure 1).  
Figure 1. The digestive system organs of a rainbow trout of approximately 250g.  
 
The ingestion of food begins at the oral cavity of the mouth after a food item has been successfully 
located and captured, after which it moves through the pharynx (Rust, 2003; Figure 1). Food can range 
from large items consumed individually down to small units such as zooplankton and phytoplankton, 
resulting in a diverse size and position of the mouth between species (Buddington and Kuz’mina, 2000). 
In aquaculture, the diet of the farmed fish consists of species-tailored feed pellets (Lee et al., 2016). The 
mouth of rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss) consists of conical dentition on the jaws, the tongue, 
and the pharyngeal bones, allowing for effective retention and capture of the prey (Rust, 2003).  
Following the pharynx, ingested food passes through the oesophagus, a mainly muscular tube that 
allows the passage of food from the oral cavity to the stomach, preventing regurgitation from taking 
place. This is brought about by synchronised peristaltic contractions with the secretion of mucus (Bone 
and Moore, 2008). The oesophagus of the rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss) is described as short, 
flexible, and lined with epithelial cells that are mucus-secreting, not only promoting the rapid passage 
of food but also protecting the underlying tissue against mechanical injury during feeding (Rust, 2003). 
After food enters the stomach via the oesophagus, it is stored and mechanically mixed before enzymatic 
breakdown begins. Hydrochloric acid (HCl) and pepsinogen are both secreted by oxynticopeptic cells, 
activating the proteolytic enzyme pepsin under acidic conditions.

HCl initiates digestion through lipid emulsification, cellular structure breakdown, protein and 
carbohydrate denaturation, and it also converts the pepsinogen into the active enzyme pepsin. The pH 
of the gastric fluids is around 2.5 (Bakke et al., 2010; Sahlmann et al., 2015). A protective mucus layer, 
secreted by goblet cells and neck cells, lines the mucosa to prevent epithelial damage from the acidic 
environment (Morrison and Wright, 1999). The food passes through the stomach with the peristaltic 
movements of its muscular wall. The pyloric region of the stomach regulates the gradual passage of 
chyme into the intestine via the pyloric sphincter (Bone and Moore, 2008).  
In rainbow trout, the proximal intestine is beset with finger-like appendages - the pyloric caeca aid in 
digestion by expanding the surface area for enzymatic mixing and early absorption (Buddington and 
Diamond, 1986; Einarsson and Davies, 1997). Their number and size show great variation among 
species and between individual specimens, depending on dietary habits, life stages, and environmental 
conditions (Bone and Moore, 2008; Buddington and Diamond, 1986) . In salmonids, they can make up 
to 50% of the overall mass of the gastrointestinal tract and are responsible for more than 70% of 
enzymatic activity, showing high levels of brush border enzymes, such as leucine aminopeptidase, 
maltase, and lactase (Krogdahl and Bakke-McKellep, 2005). Functionally, they are the equivalent of 
the anterior intestine, showing similar rates of amino acid and glucose absorption (Buddington and 
Diamond, 1986). The pyloric caeca in fish are sites of enzymatic digestion and nutrient absorption. In 
addition, a multiductal system from the diffuse pancreas allows the direct release of digestive enzymes 
into this region, thus enhancing the breakdown of nutrients as chyme passes through (Einarsson and 
Davies, 1997). During periods of compensatory growth, increased metabolic enzyme activity, as 
represented by citrate synthase in the pyloric caeca, indicates their role in meeting elevated energetic 
and digestive demands (Bélanger et al., 2002). 
In terms of histology, salmonid pyloric caeca feature distinct layers that are typical of the architecture 
of the gastrointestinal tract: serosa, muscularis, submucosa, mucosa and the lumen (Özel et al., 2019).  
Simple columnar epithelial cells with highly developed microvilli generate a brush border in the mucosa 
that is ideal for nutritional absorption (Anderson, 1986; Kalhoro et al., 2019, Figure 3). The mucosa is 
diffusely scattered with goblet cells that secrete mucus used to lubricate and protect the epithelial 
surface (Morrison and Wright, 1999). Other exocrine cells in the mucosa play a part in enzymatic 
secretions in targeted areas (Anderson, 1986). The mucosa’s lamina propria accommodates the 
capillaries and the immune cells to allow transport and protection from the immune system (Bakke et 
al., 2010; Özel et al., 2019). A relatively thin muscularis layer compared to the intestine indicates a 
stronger absorption focus than propulsion within the caeca (Bakke et al., 2010; Özel et al., 2019).  
The teleost fish intestine performs multiple functions related to the assimilation and digestion of 
nutrients and is generally divided into three major sections: the proximal, anterior, and posterior (or 
distal) intestines. The intestinal sections have morphological and functional specifications that depend 
on the food habits of the species. The intestines in carnivorous species are generally shorter and have 
to promote faster absorption of nutrients, while in herbivorous species have longer intestinal tracts with 
microbial fermentation and slow digestive activities (Bone and Moore, 2008). The intestine in 
salmonids is generally shorter in length (Figure 1). 
In both Atlantic salmon and rainbow trout, the anterior segment of the intestine starts directly after the 
proximal intestine and the pyloric caeca. It plays a crucial role in the enzymatic degradation and 
assimilation of nutrients. In this particular segment, food consumed combines with pancreatic secretions 
and bile, creating an environment that is mildly alkaline (pH around 7–8) and supports maximum 
enzymatic activity (Bakke et al., 2010). Macronutrient degradation is aided by several enzymes, 
including lipases, proteases, and amylases, while bile salts emulsify fats present in food, facilitating the 
absorption of micelles. Also, this region, especially the region immediately after the caeca, is the major 
site of absorption of glucose, amino acids, and peptides (Denstadli et al., 2004; Wood, 2001).

Anterior and distal parts of the intestine continue their function of water and nutrient absorption in the 
posterior parts with reduced enzymatic activity. The distal part has a role in the reabsorption of bile 
salts as well as osmoregulation maintenance. The area has a high population of goblet cells as well as 
lymphoid tissue, indicative of an added immunological role (Kooijman, 2009; Wood, 2001). Although 
with a limited ability to absorb fatty acids in the distal intestine, it continues with selective absorption 
as well as fermentation of remaining material depending on food composition (German et al., 2010; 
Røsjø et al., 2000).  
Visceral organs perform supportive roles. The liver secretes bile, stored within the gallbladder and then 
secreted into the intestine through the bile duct; the function results in emulsification and lipid uptake. 
The pancreas, whether localised or distributed along the intestinal tract, secretes enzymes including 
trypsin, chymotrypsin, and pancreatic lipase into the intestinal lumen (Einarsson and Davies, 1997).  
Fish feeding ecology, energy allocation, and physiological state can all be evaluated with the help of 
somatic indices. These indices provide information about diet quality, digestive development, and 
energy reserves by relating body proportions or organ size to total body weight or length. For example, 
the viscerosomatic index (VSI) and gastrosomatic index (GSI) show feeding intensity and gut 
development, and their temporal fluctuations reflect diet type or seasonal changes (Ahmed et al., 2022). 
Herbivorous fish generally have longer intestines than carnivores, according to Zihler's index (IWL) 
and relative gut length (RIL), which are used to infer trophic strategies (Duque-Correa et al., 2024; 
Zihler, 1981). Lipid storage is represented by the visceral fat index (VFI), whereas energy balance and 
reserves are indicated by the hepatosomatic index (HSI), which is the ratio of liver weight to body 
weight (Baudou et al., 2019). Finally, the condition factor (K) is a general health proxy indicating body 
robustness and is widely used in salmonid aquaculture to monitor nutritional status and welfare (Ahmad 
et al., 2019; Gao, 2024).

1.1.1. Appetite and GI Regulation via the Brain-Gut Axis 
Feeding and digestion in fish are controlled through a complex relationship between the brain, neural 
circuits, and gastrointestinal hormones. Information on gastrointestinal function is largely relayed by 
the vagus nerve together with a few other nerves; the brain, and particularly the hypothalamus, which 
combines this information and then releases the neuropeptides that will augment or inhibit the appetite 
(Olsson, 2010). The brain-gut axis controls the intake of food as well as the digestive mechanisms in 
accordance with the nutritional and bodily state of the individual (Volkoff, 2016). 
Regulation of the intestinal function in teleost fish, including the salmonids, involves the connections 
among central neuroendocrine messages and the local hormones in the intestine. Some principal 
hypothalamic neuropeptides, including the feeding-stimulatory orexigenic neuropeptides, i.e., 
neuropeptide Y (NPY), agouti-related peptide (AgRP), and orexins. On the other hand, anorexigenic 
peptides such as cholecystokinin (CCK), proopiomelanocortin (POMC), and cocaine-and-
amphetamine-regulated transcript (CART) peptides suppress feeding (Valen et al., 2011; Volkoff, 
2016).  
These neuropeptides act according to input from the metabolic indicators as well as by the feedback 
from the intestine, such as vagal afferent signals. The CCK released into the intestine after feeding acts 
via the vagus nerve and the pathways via the CRH-melanocortin in the brain in suppressing hunger 
(Kang et al., 2010). Orexin and ghrelin pathways are active not only in the brain but locally in the 
gastrointestinal system, i.e., the mucosa and the submucosa lining the stomach, pyloric caeca, as well 
as the intestine (Hevrøy et al., 2011; Varricchio et al., 2015). The first mammalian fat-linked hormone, 
leptin, which is synthesised by the liver and intestine in fishes, proves a long-term inhibitory message 
for feeding, tending normally to increase with feeding and decrease with starvation (Valen et al., 2011; 
Volkoff, 2016). 
Histologically, the structure of the gastrointestinal wall is also organised to allow for both digestive 
function and endocrine communication. The mucosal layer, rich in absorptive enterocytes (Figure 3) 
and enteroendocrine cells, provides the foremost site for the production of hormones like ghrelin, CCK, 
and leptin, which may be secreted into the circulation or have local actions (Hevrøy et al., 2011; 
Volkoff, 2016). Below this, the submucosa provides vascular and neural pathways that are crucial for 
the transport of hormones and vagal afferent communication, thus allowing for rapid feedback to central 
appetite centres. The muscularis layer is involved in motility and can impact the mechanical stretch 
signals that also affect gut hormone release (Olsson, 2009). Thus, the regulation within the fish 
gastrointestinal tract is highly integrated at both structural and hormonal levels, with different layers 
providing not just digestive function but also endocrine feedback mechanisms crucial for the 
maintenance of energy homeostasis. 
 
1.2 Energy production in fish 
The equilibrium between energy intake and the requirements of maintenance, activity, growth, and 
reproduction determines fish energy balance. Compared to endotherms, fish can devote more energy to 
growth because they are ectotherms and do not use energy for thermoregulation (Florkin, 1949). 
Following food consumption, the energy that has been assimilated moves into a reserve compartment, 
where it can be used for gamete production, tissue synthesis, standard metabolic rate (SMR), or 
movement. Additionally, the specific dynamic action (SDA) and the excretion of nitrogenous waste in 
the form of urea and ammonia cause energy losses during digestion (Rolland et al., 2015; Schreck and 
Tort, 2016;  Figure 3). Understanding the balance between these pathways is essential for 
comprehending the physiological state, feed conversion, and performance of fish. 
==PAGE:10==
10 
On the cellular level, energy production involves the catabolism of macromolecules—lipids, proteins, 
and carbohydrates—into acetyl-CoA that proceeds into the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle, also known 
as citric acid cycle or the Krebs cycle. Within the mitochondria, the TCA cycle is accountable for the 
regeneration of NADH and FADH₂ required in oxidative phosphorylation to lead to the production of 
ATP, the cell's overriding energy currency (Brand et al., 1991). Citrate synthase, an essential enzyme 
in the TCA cycle, is often used as an indicator of mitochondrial density and capacity for aerobic 
metabolism in tissues with high energy demands, including red muscle and liver (Seiliez et al., 2011).  
For rainbow trout, the relative contribution of energy substrates changes with physiological status: 
under periods of fasts, there is selective lipid oxidation; however, feeding causes a rise in amino acid 
catabolism; with high activity or hypoxia conditions, there can be anaerobic metabolism of 
carbohydrates through glycolysis (Van Raaij et al., 1995; Wood, 2001). Thus, energy metabolism in 
fish is highly flexible and is regulated with care to achieve an efficient ATP production in a vast range 
of environmental and physiological situations.

1.2.1 Energy metabolism 
The metabolism of energy is an underlying basis for teleost fish physiological processes, ranging from 
growth, maintenance, reproduction, and locomotion (Figure 4). Salmonids, known to be highly 
demanding cold-adapted carnivores, have characteristic profiles for energy partitioning and nutrient 
utilisation and actively participate in catabolic processes for lipids and proteins while displaying little 
capacity for metabolic utilisation of dietary carbohydrate. These macronutrients get metabolically 
converted to adenosine triphosphate (ATP) through well-conserved metabolic pathways—glycolysis, 
β-oxidation, tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle, and oxidative phosphorylation. The pathways are 
influenced by environmental, genetic, and dietary factors (Brett and Groves, 1979; Rolland et al., 2015). 
Protein is the major dietary component in salmonids, serving not only as an energy source but also as 
an amino acid pool for protein synthesis. Amino acids resulting from catabolism in the pyloric caeca
==PAGE:11==
11 
and gut are used for protein synthesis and absorption, while others are diverted towards deamination 
and generate carbon skeletons to feed into the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle as intermediates (Wilson, 
2003).  
Most of the ammonia is excreted through the gills without any significant energy expenditure (Brett 
and Groves, 1979), however, the rectum will also be used for osmoregulation and nitrogen excretion 
(Brijs et al., 2017; McLeese and Bergeron, 1990). While protein catabolism serves as an energy source 
during fasting and maximal exercise, it is an energetically costly process, causing loss of nitrogen. 
Significantly, aerobic training reduces protein oxidation dependency in rainbow trout, enhancing 
utilisation of lipids (Lauff and Wood, 1997). 
Lipids are the macronutrient of greatest energy density, about 9 kcal/g, and are used preferentially in 
sustained aerobic exercise. Lipid digestion takes place in the pyloric caeca and in the proximal part of 
the intestine, wherein they get emulsified by bile salts and are absorbed in the form of fatty acids. Later, 
fatty acids get catabolised by β-oxidation in mitochondria, yielding acetyl-CoA, NADH, and FADH₂, 
which participate integrally in the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle and contribute indirectly to adenosine 
triphosphate (ATP) synthesis by passing through the electron transport chain (Brett and Groves, 1979; 
Rolland et al., 2015). Trained fish have an optimised metabolic processing of lipids, as revealed by 
decreased nitrogen excretion and stable respiratory quotients for longer periods of swimming (Lauff 
and Wood, 1997). McKenzie et al. (2007) further revealed that trout lines bred for increased growth 
capacity have increased metabolic rate and lipid oxidation upon ingestion, indicating genetic variability 
in energetic efficiency. 
The capacity of salmonids to metabolise carbohydrates is limited by inefficient postprandial glucose 
clearance and a tendency toward extended hyperglycaemia (Wilson, 1994). Even if there is facilitation 
of gluconeogenic and glycolytic enzymes, the regulatory mechanisms in salmonids are still inefficient, 
leading to greater storage of dietary carbohydrate as hepatic glycogen with only a slight improvement 
in growth rate (Seiliez et al., 2011). This metabolic inflexibility can partially be attributed to a state of 
chronic insulin insensitivity experienced by the hepatocytes of these fish (Wilson, 1994). Nevertheless, 
glucose oxidation does contribute to ATP production during glycolysis and in the TCA cycle, 
particularly when digestible carbohydrates, like gelatinised starch, are available (Wilson, 1994). 
The catabolism of all macronutrients leads in turn to the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle, which serves 
as the major oxidative centre of metabolism. Oxidation of Acetyl-CoA, derived from glucose, fatty 
acids, or amino acids, generates NADH and FADH₂ responsible for ATP production by oxidative 
phosphorylation. This oxygen-requiring metabolic pathway has oxygen consumption (MO₂) serving as 
an accurate measure of metabolic rate. Moreover, proton leakage in the inner mitochondrial membrane, 
as recorded in mammalian models (Brand et al., 1991), can potentially impact the energetic efficiency 
of fish mitochondria as well; yet, information specific to different species is still limited. 
The energy stores in salmonids are largely in the form of glycogen in muscle and liver to satisfy short-
term demands, in addition to triglycerides within muscle tissues and internal organs to satisfy long-term 
demands. Energy deployment follows a prescribed hierarchy: maintenance takes precedence, followed 
by activity, growth, and finally reproduction (Brett and Groves, 1979). Under conditions of limiting 
nutrients or in circumstances of stressors in the environment, such as hypoxia or low temperatures, 
energy is diverted toward homeostasis at the expense of growth (McKenzie et al., 2007). 
The intestinal tract, and notably pyloric caeca, play an essential role in nutrient absorption and energy 
metabolism. In salmonids, they increase remarkably the surface area for nutrient uptake and represent 
key sites for amino acid, fatty acid, and monosaccharide absorption (Buddington and Diamond, 1986). 
Additionally, Rolland et al. (2015) highlighted that pyloric caeca harbour key nutrient carriers 
andmetabolic enzymes linking absorption processes to metabolic signal pathways, e.g., the TOR 
pathway, which regulates anabolism in relation to feeding (Seiliez et al., 2011).

1.2.2 Metabolic rate quantification 
Metabolic rates in fishes have traditionally been measured through oxygen consumption by 
respirometry, which provides an array of measures comprising standard metabolic rate (SMR), routine 
metabolic rate (RMR), and maximal metabolic rate (MMR). The SMR represents the minimum energy 
expenditure necessary for basic physiological processes during rest and fasting, analogously to basal 
metabolic rate in endothermic animals (Chabot et al., 2016). In contrast, RMR includes those 
components of energy expenditure due to movements without voluntary movement, while MMR 
represents the maximal oxygen consumption occurring during intense exercise. Combined, they 
represent aerobic scope (MMR - SMR), which shows significant variation, usually by almost an order 
of magnitude in highly active salmonids (Brett and Groves, 1979). 
In an attempt to increase resolution, scientists often complement whole-animal observations by using 
measurements of oxygen consumption at tissue and organ levels. Respiratory in vitro tissue rates (QO₂) 
are used to determine mass-specific metabolic activity in isolated tissues and reveal considerable 
disparity in energy demands among tissues. For example, brain, cardiac, and liver tissues have high 
QO₂ values, while white muscle and adipose tissues have relatively low metabolic activity (Oikawa and 
Itazawa, 2003). In addition, gut tissues, notably the intestine and hepatopancreas, have increased QO₂, 
notably in smaller actively feeding fish, and thereby highlight the importance of these in nutrient uptake 
and energy metabolism following ingestion (Itazawa and Oikawa, 1984; Oikawa and Itazawa, 2003).  
Empirical data demonstrate that as fish body size increases, tissue-specific metabolic rate reduces only 
slightly, while mass-specific metabolic rate for the whole body reduces to a greater degree. This 
difference mainly stems from ontogenetic changes in body composition, as larger fish have an increased 
proportion of low-metabolism tissue (Itazawa and Oikawa, 1986, 1984). When oxygen consumption by 
different tissues is totalled to get an aggregated figure reflecting in vivo oxygen uptake closely, this 
serves as quantitative support for data collected at the organ level (Oikawa and Itazawa, 2003). As such, 
tissue-level metabolic research is key in interpreting energy distribution among tissues, translating 
organ functions to whole-pet metabolic demands, and scaling these interactions in relation to body size 
changes—components necessary in understanding energy budgeting, scaling, and performance in fish 
(Chabot et al., 2016; Oikawa and Itazawa, 2003; Brett and Groves, 1979). 
 
1.3 Ultrastructural properties 
Mitochondria are the major site of cellular energy metabolism, often referred to as “the powerhouse of 
the cell” (Figure 5). They play a crucial role in the physiological performance and resilience to stress in 
ectothermic vertebrates like fish. As the site of ATP generation by oxidative phosphorylation, 
mitochondria enable vital biological processes in most tissues and organs that are characterised by high 
aerobic requirements, like the heart and the gastrointestinal tract. In the mostly temperature-sensitive 
internal regulation typical in ectothermic organisms and on the basis of external environmental 
conditions, mitochondrial function has significant plasticity and shows increased sensitivity to 
temperature and oxygen variations and the requirement for metabolites (Pichaud et al., 2020; Sokolova, 
2018). Consequently, mitochondrial attributes like respiration rate, the integrity of the membrane, and 
enzymatic activity are constantly adjusted to maximise cellular survival and promote overall individual 
proficiency under variable environmental conditions (Brijs et al., 2017; Gerber et al., 2020). 
The cardiac system in fish is heavily reliant on effective mitochondrial ATP synthesis to support 
prolonged pumping and to sustain aerobic function. As a result, cardiac mitochondria are highly 
adaptable to temperature fluctuations. In Atlantic salmonids, acclimatisation to 20 °C temperature 
improves the function of oxidative phosphorylation and mitochondrial membrane integrity during acute 
thermal stress episodes without increased reactive oxygen species production (Gerber et al., 2020). 
Furthermore, differences in enzyme function on the population level that are noted in European perch 
also demonstrate long-term adaptation since individuals from environments with chronically increased

temperature show alterations in the level of citrate synthase activity (CSA) and lactate dehydrogenase 
(LDH) (Pichaud et al., 2017). These findings emphasise the pivotal role played by mitochondrial 
adaptability to deal with fluctuations in environmental temperature. 
While the cardiac mitochondrion has received significant scholarly attention, it has been less explored 
in the digestive system. Intestine and pyloric caeca are high-oxidative capacity characters that show 
dynamic adaptation to fluctuations in the availability of nutrients. Citrate synthase activity, an 
indispensable enzyme in the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle and an excellent indicator of mitochondrial 
density, is used routinely to measure the intestinal tissue's capacity to oxidise (Brijs et al., 2017; Pelletier 
et al., 1994a). Notably, species like Atlantic cod and largemouth bass show considerable correlations 
between intestinal levels of citrate synthase and growth rate, implying that mitochondrial function in 
the intestine may limit the absorption rate of nutrients and general somatic growth (Blier et al., 2002; 
Goolish and Adelman, 1988). Both gut mass and citrate synthase levels are restored quickly through re-
feeding following fasting in cod species and reach peak levels two weeks afterwards (Bélanger et al., 
2002), thus highlighting the adaptability of the gut at the level of phenotype.

The endoplasmic reticulum (ER) plays an important role in the processing of nutrients and protein traffic 
within enterocytes. McLeese and Bergeron (1990), working with winter flounder, showed that 
prolonged periods of fasting provoked both morphological and functional changes in the ER of 
intestinal cells. In applying osmium impregnation methods, they demonstrated that whereas the total 
amount of the ER remained constant, its staining pattern—and therefore its state of function—
underwent considerable changes during fasting, particularly in the foregut and pyloric caeca. The 
dilation and under-impregnation of the ER tubules suggested an adaptation from transport-related roles 
to others like osmoregulation. Coincidental with these changes were the decrease in microvillar length 
and redistribution of the mitochondria, and these together indicated an orchestrated response of 
intracellular processes to fasting.

emphasising the importance of measuring several organelles against the background of intestinal 
physiology under dynamic feeding conditions.  
Among the salmonids like trout and salmon, mitochondrial adaptations within the gut are particularly 
important given their carnivorous nature, high growth rate, and anadromy life cycle. Salmonids exhibit 
rapid adaptive changes in gut tissue composition with environmental fluctuations in feeding regimes, 
mainly from fasting during upstream migration to re-feeding in rich food environments in seawater 
(Bélanger et al., 2002). In the phase of active feeding, pyloric caeca and intestine are subjected to 
hypertrophy and exhibit elevated activity with citrate synthase (CS) to enhance nutrient uptake and 
utilisation. Environmental changes like seawater entry further increase the metabolic demands by 
enhancing osmoregulatory functions in the gut that are temporary and elevate enzyme activities in the 
mitochondria (Brijs et al., 2017). The level of this kind of physiological adaptability is apparently the 
product of selection pressure on being flexible in life histories. Thus, the study of CS activity together 
with the structural adaptations in mitochondria and the endoplasmic reticulum informs us regarding the 
improvement in digestive efficacy in salmonids in accordance with variable food availability and 
environmental changes. 
 
1.4 Adaptations of the Gastrointestinal System to Food Availability 
The GI tract of fish is highly structurally as well as functionally adaptable to variations in food 
availability. This adaptability helps ensure an efficient distribution of energy by reducing the digestive 
structures during starvation, only to be rebuilt after refeeding. This plasticity can be widely seen over a 
great diversity of species as well as in different environments. 
During cases of nutritional deficiency, fish generally show loss of gut mass, mucosal surface, and 
enzymatic activity. For example, Krogdahl and Bakke-McKellep (2005) demonstrated that fasting for 
certain periods in Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar) led to tissue mass reduction, mainly apparent in the 
pyloric caeca, accompanied by substantial decreases in intestinal protein content as well as digestive 
enzyme activities. Notably, such changes were mostly reversible, with gut anatomy as well as enzyme 
function being able to return to basal values within one week of refeeding. Similar structural changes 
have, for example, been established for tambaqui (Colossoma macropomum), where restricted feeding 
caused reduced villus height as well as low enzyme activity, though refeeding caused compensatory 
improvements of both measures (Melo et al., 2024). 
Morphological changes seen within instances of feed deprivation include a reduction of mucosal fold 
length, reduction of enterocyte height, and reduction of length and density of microvilli. Associated 
with such changes is an increase in goblet cells as well as inflammatory infiltration (Baeverfjord and 
Krogdahl, 1996; Zaldúa and Naya, 2014). Ultrastructure changes such as the distension of the 
endoplasmic reticulum, as well as an attenuation of osmium staining intensity, have been described 
within the enterocytes of fasting winter flounder, reflecting an absorptive function downregulation 
rather than tissue regressive processes (McLeese and Bergeron, 1990). In some species, substantial gut 
surface reduction within the context of low-quality or zero-feeding does not necessarily correlate with 
a reduction of digestive enzyme activity, reflecting the priority of essential function over tissue 
structural integrity (German et al., 2010). 
The mucosal surface exhibits a high degree of sensitivity to nutritional intake. The onset of starvation 
leads to the thinning of epithelium and the regression of mucosal folds, which can be rapidly reversed 
by refeeding. 
==PAGE:15==
15 
Within a few days of the resumption of dietary intake, there are measurable increases in villus height 
and activities of enzymes, such as trypsin and alkaline phosphatase, that return intestinal function to 
pre-starvation levels (Melo et al., 2024; Krogdahl and Bakke-McKellep, 2005). This rapidrecovery has 
been observed across several species, including tambaqui (Colossoma macropomum), Atlantic salmon 
(Salmo salar), and brook char (Salvelinus fontinalis), and often entails the reactivation of protein 
synthesis mechanisms and anabolic growth signalling pathways (Middleton et al., 2024; Hevrøy et al., 
2011). 
Functionally active enzymes such as leucine aminopeptidase, as well as maltase, normally decline 
during fasting, but such enzymes return rapidly to their control levels after refeeding, further supporting 
the view that suppression of enzymes is reversible (Krogdahl and Bakke-McKellep, 2005; Zaldúa and 
Naya, 2014). In addition, even after prolonged starvation, structural integrity of the intestine as well as 
its digestive capabilities are well enough conserved for rapid growth on resumption of feeding (Melo et 
al., 2024; German et al., 2010). 
The findings of such research reinforce the role of gastrointestinal plasticity as a natural physiological 
adaptation for regulating variations in the availability of nutrients. In aquaculture production, utilising 
such plasticity by controlling feed restriction, followed by re-feeding mechanisms, can potentially 
improve food conversion efficiency, reduce waste, and enhance the health of the fish during production 
stages (McCue, 2010; Hevrøy et al., 2011). 
 
1.5 Aim and Hypotheses 
In the present study, rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss) were subjected to three different feeding 
regimes: prolonged fasting (starved), a moderate diet (intermediate), and ad libitum feeding with the 
intention of measuring their morphological and physiological responses to different states of nutrition. 
This experimental design made it possible to examine a variety of growth levels concomitantly with 
related changes in digestive and metabolic physiology.  
It was hypothesised that fish under starvation would show extensive atrophy of digestive tissues along 
with a decrease in metabolic processes, while trout given constant nutrition, especially on ad libitum 
terms, would maintain large, metabolically active intestines and pyloric caeca to accommodate high 
growth levels. It was also expected that trout given a moderate diet could show compensatory or 
optimised digestive adaptations, for example, relatively high digestive efficiency as a fraction of intake. 
The core hypothesis was that digestive tissues undergo coordinated adaptive change in accordance with 
nutritional status: under starvation phases, energy-conserving mechanisms are activated by a reduction 
in intestinal mass, enzymatic capacity, and mitochondrial density. In contrast, under phases of full 
feeding, it was expected that each of these parameters would rise to facilitate improved nutrient 
absorption. It was predicted that these physiological adjustments would be reflected in somatic traits, 
histology, and certain measurements of metabolic rate in different organs.  
In pursuit of these expectations, the study set out to examine the association between digestive expense, 
tissue flexibility, and energy metabolism in salmonids. Familiarity with these mechanisms is important 
not only to increase basic ichthyological knowledge but also to further the level of knowledge in 
aquaculture. Management of the feed strategy and periods of feed withdrawal in aquaculture requires 
detailed knowledge of how the biological components of the organism react to nutritional stress 
(Bélanger et al., 2002). In addition, in an environmental context, the capacity to restart growth after 
starvation epochs, which depends on speedy organ flexibility, may strongly affect survival and 
reproduction performance in varying environmental conditions.

2.3 Histology  
2.3.1 Sample preparation  
As soon as all of the measurements of the intestine and the pyloric caeca had been acquired, the tissues 
were fixed in 40mL of 4% paraformaldehyde in PBS 0.1M. After 72 hours, three ca. 4mm trimmings 
of the anterior intestine and five 4mm trimmings from multiple caeca from each fish were taken and 
placed in 10mL of 70% ethanol, where they would remain until all samples had been collected.  
Histology samples were prepared according to the instructions provided for the embedding medium 
(Technovit 7100, Heraeus Kulzer, Germany). After being stored in 70% ethanol for a minimum of 2 
weeks, all samples were transferred to 2mL of 95% ethanol for 2 hours, followed by three 2-hour-long 
periods in 2mL absolute ethanol (99.9%), replacing it each period. After dehydration, the samples were 
left overnight in a fridge at 4℃ to pre-infiltrate in a 1:1 mix of absolute ethanol and TechnoVit7100 
base liquid (Figure 11A).  
Infiltration was carried out by creating TechnoVit7100 preparation solution by first dissolving 1g of 
TechnoVit7100 hardener 1 in 100mL of base liquid (Solution 1). Each sample was placed in 2mL of the 
preparation solution and vacuum-infiltrated for 2 hours, after which the samples were stored in the 
fridge at 4℃ overnight (Figure 11B).  
Using a minimum of adhesive, infiltrated samples were glued (Loctite ®) to a 25x25x5mm mould 
(DISPOMOULD). two trimmings of pyloric caeca and one trimming of the anterior intestine were glued 
in each mould in a specific arrangement to enable orientation during slicing (Figure 11C). 
Polymerisation solution (Solution 2) was prepared using a 1:15 ratio of hardener 2 and solution 1. 3mL 
of the polymerisation solution was poured onto the tissues, filling the moulds. The moulds were covered 
with a plastic slip, and the air bubbles were removed if present (Figure 11D).  
The cured samples were removed from the moulds and any excess trimmed off. The cured samples were 
mounted onto a Histobloc using TechnoVit3040, which was prepared in a volume ratio of 2:1 
powder/liquid and poured into the recess at the back of the Histobloc and left to harden (Figure 11E). 
 
2.3.2 Sectioning 
The samples were sectioned at 5µm thickness using a “Leica RM2255” microtome. The slices were 
transferred to a water bath with fresh Milliq water to be stretched out by the surface tension of the water 
and then collected on a microscope slide (VWR® SuperFrost® Plus), with two sections per slide. Slides 
were subsequently placed on a hot plate at 60° C for 2 hours, and after for 12h at 40°C. Multiple slides 
were prepared for each sample to acquire the best one.

2.3.5 Quantitative stereological analysis 
The estimation of the volume densities, which can also be considered as volume densities of the separate 
layers of the anterior intestine and the pyloric caeca, was based on the general principle of Delesse 
(Nyengaard, 1999; Verdile et al., 2020).  
The volume density of four layers of the anterior intestine and the pyloric caeca: serosa and muscularis 
layer – they were combined and considered as the outer layer of the anterior intestine and the pyloric 
caeca; the submucosa, mucosa and the lumen were estimated from the fractional area of each layer and 
the total area of the reference compartment of the cross-section (e.g the intestinal wall, or pyloric caeca 
wall). The lumen was considered as the space without any epithelial cells present (Figure 12A).  
Measurements were performed by point counting. To estimate the volume densities of each layer, the 
regions of interest were defined in the histological sections. The total volume density of each layer 
within the section was sampled by layering a grid with equally distant points on top of the image with 
a width of 25 grid points across the horizontal axis using image processing and analysis software 
(ImageJ) (Figure 12B).

measurement phase.

2.7 Statistical Analysis 
All statistical analyses were performed using SigmaPlot (version 16.0, Systat Software Inc., Germany). 
Microsoft Excel was used for organising the data.  
One-way ANOVA was performed to assess whether there are significant differences between the 
treatments. Differences between treatments were considered significant when p ≤ 0.05. Normality of 
distribution was assessed using the Shapiro-Wilk test, and the Brown-Forsythe test was used to test 
equal variance. Holm-Sidak pairwise multiple comparison was carried out between each treatment, 
where normality and equal variance tests passed.  
If the normality or equal variance test failed, Kruskal-Wallis one-way analysis of variance based on 
ranks was performed. Pairwise multiple comparison (Dunn`s method) was carried out in the instances 
where ANOVA was based on ranks.  
Differences between treatments were considered significant when p ≤ 0.05.  
Data is presented as mean ± standard error (S.E).

Final body weight did not differ significantly among treatments. SGR differed significantly, however,

4. Discussion 
The present study investigated the structural and metabolic plasticity of two key gastrointestinal tissues 
of rainbow trout —the pyloric caeca and anterior intestine when exposed to varying feeding regimes 
designed to induce differential growth responses. Using three treatments—a period of starvation, 
intermediate and ad libitum feeding —a wide range of differing specific growth rates (SGR) were 
induced. From negative values in the starvation regime through to highly positive values in the ad 
libitum regime. Even though the differences in SGR values were significant between all treatments, the 
ad libitum group did not exhibit as high growth rates as expected (SGR > 2), and they were highly 
variable. This can be explained by the difference in the weight range of fish selected at the beginning 
of the study. From the quarantined stock, the smallest individuals were selected for the ad libitum 
treatment, which would have the highest expected growth and on the other hand, the fish which had the 
highest weight were selected for the starvation treatment. Essentially, this means that the fish with 
already lower growth rates were expected to grow the most. All animals were sustained at a similar 
body mass (~250 g), thereby allowing unconfounded measurements of physiological and morphological 
change.  
The results showed clear signs of tissue plasticity in response to nutrient intake, most notably at the 
structural level. The pyloric caeca and anterior intestinal diameter showed hypertrophy in line with the 
SGRs, a change maintained by structural alterations in the mucosal, sub-mucosal, muscularis and serosa 
layers. Such adaptations reflect a response to attain better digestive or absorptive efficiencies in fish 
showing higher growth rates. On the contrary, variables related to metabolic potential, such as citrate 
synthase activity (CSA) and oxygen consumption (QO₂), did not show clear variability across 
treatments, despite clear structural variety in the tissues. This implies that whereas the gastrointestinal 
system undergoes structural remodelling in response to food consumption, the mitochondrial metabolic 
performance per tissue unit might face less regulatory demands or might be constrained more. 
 
4.1 Somatic Indices 
Analysis of intestinal and somatic indices provides critical information on the physiological response 
of fish to variations in food availability. In the course of this study, a series of somatic indices, such as 
the condition factor (CF), viscerosomatic index (VSI), gastrosomatic index (GSI), hepatosomatic index 
(HSI), relative intestinal length (RIL), relative intestinal weight (RIW), relative pyloric caeca weight 
(RPCW) and Zihler's index (IWL), have been studied to determine energy distribution patterns as well 
as the adaptability of the intestine of rainbow trout reared under varying levels of food availability. 
The condition factor (CF) showed a high level of stability throughout the different treatments, with a 
slight reduction only seen in the starved group. This can be viewed as an indication that, for the most 
part, the body proportions were maintained, even without nutritional uptake, thus supporting the claims 
made by Simpkins et al. (2003) about the stability of the liver somatic index (HSI) values in juvenile 
rainbow trout after a 28-day starvation period. Additionally, a very distinct reduction in the 
gastrosomatic index (GSI) after starvation was observed. This is in support of the findings of Simpkins 
et al. (2003), who reported that GSI was one of the somatic indices that consistently decreased under 
restricted feeding conditions.  
Drastic reductions were registered in both the hepatosomatic index (HSI) of the group exposed to 
starvation. It is generally acknowledged that these indices are highly sensitive to energetic stress, as 
internal organs, such as the liver and gastrointestinal tract, generally reflect the first locations of mass 
loss under conditions of fasting (Krogdahl and Bakke-McKellep, 2005). The reduction in HSI is an 
indicator of reduced hepatic lipid content and glycogen stores (Mommsen, 2001). In addition, the 
hormonal changes brought about by starvation, as represented by elevated plasma ghrelin levels and
==PAGE:48==
48 
altered amino acid availability, have been related to hepatic tissue remodelling and a metabolic 
downregulation in Atlantic salmon (Hevrøy et al., 2011).  Considering that viscerosomatic index (VSI) 
and visceral fat index (VFI) did not show a significant decline after starvation, which means that the 
reductions in size were more localised to the energy reserve organs and tissues, as well as the 
gastrointestinal tissues.  
The relative intestinal length (RIL) and Zihler’s index (IWL), often linked to adaptations for dietary 
pattern and digestion efficiency, did not show any statistically significant difference between the 
different feeding treatments. The absence of such differences indicates that the intestinal length might 
be morphologically unvarying for rainbow trout under the conditions and the course of this study. This 
stability is supported by findings from Buddington et al. (1987), where little variation was observed in 
the intestinal length for piscivores like trout and salmon when fed with high-protein diets. However, 
other research indicates that intestinal length can decrease after long-term or severe starvation 
(Krogdahl and Bakke-McKellep, 2005), suggesting that such adaptability of the morphological 
characteristics might depend on the intensity as well as the time of food deficiency. 
Conversely, relative intestinal weight (RIW) and relative pyloric caeca weight (RPCW) showed a 
significant reduction under starvation conditions. These reductions confirm that, even though the overall 
length of the gut does not change, both the amount of digestive tissue and its functional capacity are 
reduced in the absence of feeding activity. This observation is consistent with the findings of (Zaldúa 
and Naya, 2014), which indicated that the intestine and pyloric caeca can lose 20–40% of their original 
mass during extended fasting, as manifested by mucosal atrophy, microvilli shortening, and epithelial 
remodelling. This structural flexibility is an energy-saving strategy, maintaining basic functions while 
reducing metabolic costs. 
The similarity between RIW and RPCW under the two different food regimes is evidence that both the 
moderate and the ad libitum regimes are sufficient for supporting intestinal tissue mass, thus making it 
clear that there is a threshold of food consumption necessary for gut maintenance. In addition, the 
observed findings are supported by larger-scale ecological patterns, as carnivorous fish species such as 
salmonids have short guts, suited for coping with protein-rich diets (Buddington et al., 1987; Kramer 
and Bryant, 1995), alongside their morphological variability under changes in food regimes.  
These changes revealed the ability of rainbow trout to adapt internal resource allocation according to 
changing dietary availability. While the overall body morphology remained constant, there occurred a 
substantial decrease in internal organs, especially of the liver, fat and gastrointestinal tract, with regard 
to tissue weight and volume during starvation. These findings support the effectiveness of somatic 
measurements as accurate physiological markers for evaluating nutritional status and, to an extent, 
energy distribution for salmonids. 
 
4.2. Histology 
The histological measurements offer a fine-scale inspection of how intestinal tissue layers were 
regulated in response to starvation and feeding. Stark differences in intestinal wall thickness and make-
up that complement the above organ-level trends were observed. Both the anterior intestine and pyloric 
caeca in starved trout had significantly narrower cross-sectional diameters than in fed trout – 
approximately 30–40% narrower on average, with intestinal diameters in starved fish in the vicinity of 
2800μm, whereas on the other hand, up to ~4600μm in fish fed ad libitum (Figures 20 & 21). This 
quantitatively confirms that the anterior intestine had been slimmed down due to the absence of food.  
In starved fish, the nutrient-absorptive lining (mucosa) had a far lower proportion of the intestinal cross-
section, while the supportive tissue groups (submucosa, muscularis) made up a higher proportion 
relative to fed fish. For example, in the anterior intestine in starved trout, the mucosa accounted for only 
~40% of the volume density, as opposed to ~50–53% in fed treatments. In contrast, in starved fish, the 
==PAGE:49==
49 
combined submucosa and muscle layer accounted for close to 43% of the volume density in starved 
intestines but only ~27–30% in fed fish. The same was observed in pyloric caeca: fasting resulted in 
thinner mucosa (57% vs ~68–71% in fed fish) as well as proportionally thicker in thickness in the 
muscle & serosa layer (23% vs ~15–16%). The differences observed were statistically significant in all 
cases. From a practical perspective, the starvation process caused atrophy of the intestinal epithelium, 
with a relative reduction in both the absorptive epithelium and villi; the structural elements, like 
connective tissue and muscle, however, showed lesser shrinkage, thus forming a greater percentage of 
the reduced organ. The observation supports established cellular reactions to fasting: the intestines of 
starved salmonids show a reduction in enterocyte height and cytoplasmic volume, along with significant 
disorganisation of nuclei. After a fasting period of 7 to 14 days, the intestines of Atlantic salmon show 
clear histological changes, such as thinned folds and a reduction in the normal vacuolisation of 
absorptive cells (Krogdahl and Bakke-McKellep, 2005). 
The 28-day-starved trout presumably underwent similar mucosal thinning and perhaps decreased cell 
proliferation, as shown by the reduced mucosal volume density. The moderate increase in the relative 
submucosa-to-muscle ratio could mean that these layers either atrophied less or became more distinct 
as the mucosa withdrew. Notably, significant differences were not observed in the lumen volume 
density among the various treatments, as the empty space within the gut cross-section still constituted 
roughly 2–7% of the total volume in each group. This suggests that mucosal atrophy did not lead to a 
larger open lumen; rather, the overall organ radius diminished in size. 
The intestinal mucosa of well-fed fish, especially those in the ad libitum group, was robust, occupying 
over half of the cross-sectional volume, and displayed well-developed villi and epithelial cells primed 
for efficient nutrient uptake. The histology of the intermediate group closely resembled that of the ad 
libitum fish, and for most layer metrics, no appreciable differences were found (both treatments shared 
the same letter groupings in our analysis). This suggests that feeding to satiation may not further 
increase mucosal proportion beyond a physiological maximum, and that even moderate feeding (1.4% 
body weight) was sufficient to maintain near-maximum mucosal development. 
In contrast to starvation, both intermediate and ad libitum fish maintained a thick mucosa. Their muscle 
and submucosa layers appeared comparatively thinner. For example, the pyloric caeca submucosa 
accounted for approximately 16% of the volume in starved trout but only 7–9% in fed fish. It appears 
that feeding primarily stimulates the mucosa—the absorptive layer—to expand, while fasting leads to 
its marked regression. Similar patterns were described by Zaldúa and Naya (2014), who reported 
reductions in the mucosal fold height, epithelial cell volume, and enterocyte structure during fasting. 
Additionally, changes in goblet cell density and microvilli have been observed. Such alterations point 
to a dynamic restructuring of the epithelial interface under nutritional stress, contributing to overall 
mucosal plasticity. 
Mucosal flexibility is instrumental in digestive function, as a greater enterocyte concentration 
corresponds with increased mucosal surface area and enhanced brush border functionality. Qualitative 
and quantitative histological analysis in our study indicated that the intestinal folds of fasted fish were 
considerably shorter and likely flatter than those of fed fish, as illustrated in stitched cross-sectional 
images. Fold complexity was highest in the ad libitum group, possibly reflecting greater surface area 
(Figure 20). Mucosal thinning and microvilli shortening are typical energy-conserving adaptations 
during starvation, with refeeding prompting swift structural restoration (McCue, 2010). Building on 
this pattern, it was observed by Middleton et al. (2024) that gut size and mucosal mass in brook char 
rapidly regrew during refeeding following starvation, even under cold conditions, underscoring the 
gut’s ability to rebound when nutrients are reintroduced.
==PAGE:50==
50 
Such diminution of intestinal structure is likely responsible, at least in part, for the reduced digestive 
capacity seen during fasting. Additionally, this trend is consistent with other vertebrate models. For 
instance, in the Burmese python (Python molurus), intestinal epithelium and microvilli are drastically 
reduced between meals, temporarily limiting absorptive function until feeding resumes (Secor et al., 
2000). 
 
4.3. Transmission Electron Microscopy 
Ultrastructural changes in the intestinal tissue were found to be negligible under different feeding 
conditions using transmission electron microscopy. Preliminary visual examination of the anterior 
intestine and pyloric caeca enterocytes demonstrated that the underlying architecture of the cells 
between trout under moderate feeding, full feeding, and starvation conditions did not display obvious 
differences. No ultrastructural difference was noticed in the morphology, density, and distribution of 
mitochondria between the treatment groups. No evidence of degeneration or proliferation of 
mitochondria due to 28-day fasting. There was predominant preservation of organelle localisation; in 
particular, there were no significant changes noticed in the relationship between mitochondria and other 
cellular elements. Only slight potential variations were noticed in the brush-border microvilli, such as 
elongation of microvillar length in the anterior intestine in the ad libitum fed condition; however, 
microvillar length and density seemed to be constant even among the fasted subjects. There was no 
extreme movement of cellular elements that could be expected after prolonged periods of nutritional 
deficiency. To summarise, there were at most minimal ultrastructural changes (if any) induced by short-
term starvation in the digestive cells of trout. 
This observation stands in contrast to the extensive structural plasticity observed in many fish species 
in response to food scarcity. The winter flounder (Pseudopleuronectes americanus) is a classic example: 
during prolonged fasting periods, flounder enterocytes can undergo dramatic ultrastructural changes 
during a 3-month fasting period, where osmium staining has shown dilation and changed impregnation 
of ER tubules. These changes were coupled with reductions in microvilli length and the reorganisation 
of mitochondria within enterocytes, reflecting cellular adaptation for energy conservation during 
prolonged food absence. Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar) also show clear morphological atrophy of the 
gastrointestinal tract even after relatively brief fasting periods; after only 1–2 weeks of starvation, 
salmonids show a thinning of intestinal mucosal folds and a reduction in the normal vacuolisation of 
absorptive cells (Baeverfjord and Krogdahl, 1996; Krogdahl and Bakke-McKellep, 2005). Likewise, 
the Dolly Varden trout (Salvelinus malma), which are exposed to predictable seasonal fluctuations in 
abundance and scarcity, show considerable flexibility at the organ level. In response to winter shortfalls, 
these fish drastically shrink the size of their digestive organs—namely, the stomach, pyloric caeca, 
intestine, and liver mass, each undergoing reduction — and then show recovery in gut capacity when 
seasonally available food resources, such as the arrival of salmon eggs, once again become available 
(Armstrong and Bond, 2013). 
In their study on the detritivorous Amazonian catfish (Pterygoplichthys disjunctivus), German et al. 
(2010) showed that extended fasting periods of 150 days, coupled with a low-quality diet of wood, led 
to dramatic changes in gastrointestinal morphology. The total intestinal surface area decreased by up to 
78%, and the microvilli surface area decreased by up to 52% in the starved fish compared to those that 
were fed a diet of high-quality algae. Some individuals of the fasted group showed a complete absence 
of microvilli in the distal intestine. These extreme morphological changes are in contrast to our fasted 
rainbow trout, which still had a recognisable microvilli structure, although after a more moderate 
starvation period of only 28 days.  
The Amazonian catfish are adapted to extreme seasonal fluctuations in food availability and may 
undergo programmed regression of the gut during dry-season famine. Their strategy appears to involve 
rapidly reducing tissue energy demands through physical remodelling.
==PAGE:51==
51 
Furthermore, while P. Disjunctivus was subjected to five months of complete starvation or nutritionally 
inadequate feeding; our trout experienced only four weeks of rationing, likely insufficient to trigger 
similar levels of gut structural reorganisation (German et al., 2010). 
Unlike other species that may extensively resorb and rebuild absorptive structures according to the 
quality and availability of food, trout appear to favour maintaining structural integrity, but a decrease 
in functional tissue mass. Such a strategy could allow for the rapid resumption of digestive function 
upon food availability, without the need for large-scale tissue regeneration. 
To summarise, the lack of obvious ultrastructural change in our rainbow trout intestines is noteworthy. 
It appears that rainbow trout did not resort to significant cellular atrophy of the gut during the weeks of 
reduced feeding. One explanation is that the starvation period was not sufficiently long or severe to 
necessitate morphological restructuring; indeed, the trout in this study lost weight (negative growth) but 
only fasted for about a month, whereas the flounder and char examples involve multi-month 
deprivations. Species-specific life history may also play a role. Rainbow trout may not regularly endure 
prolonged famine in nature to the same extent as winter flounder or Dolly Varden, and thus may be 
under less evolutionary pressure to cyclically dismantle and rebuild gut tissue. Maintaining gut structure 
could confer the ability to resume feeding and nutrient absorption at full capacity as soon as food 
appears – a clear advantage in opportunistic feeding scenarios. It should be noted that our TEM analysis 
was qualitative and based on single individuals per treatment, so subtle differences might have gone 
undetected. 
 
4.4 Citrate Synthase Activity 
In the present study, the activities of intestinal citrate synthase (CSA) did not show any statistically 
significant differences under the various feeding regimes. When compared, neither the pyloric caeca 
nor the anterior gut showed any statistically significant differences in CSA between starved, 
intermediate, and ad libitum-fed trout. The wide range of encountered CSA values prevented the 
determination of a correlation with specific growth rates. Therefore, as opposed to preliminary 
hypotheses, short-term starvation did not lead to any measurable reduction in the activities of this 
important mitochondrial enzyme. 
This lack of response in CSA is especially striking compared to other fish species that show increased 
metabolic adaptability. For Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua), there is a positive relationship between 
intestinal CSA and growth rate, with prolonged fasting periods causing extreme decreases in CSA that 
can be quickly reversed by refeeding (Bélanger et al., 2002; Pelletier et al., 1994). Cod can regenerate 
both the mass and metabolic enzyme levels of their gut within roughly two weeks of resumed feeding, 
reaching peak CSA and digestive enzyme activity after 14 days, following a 68-day fasting period (Blier 
et al., 2007). These findings demonstrate the remarkable phenotypic adaptability of the cod's digestive 
system, which can undergo close to an order of magnitude expansion of its functional capacity during 
refeeding. Salmonid fishes also commonly express metabolic adaptations to changes in feeding and 
environmental regimes. Rainbow trout acclimated to seawater showed an initial four-fold reduction of 
intestinal CSA (accompanied by a compensatory increase in glycolytic enzyme activity) within the first 
week post-transfer (Brijs et al., 2017). These trout returned CSA levels to those characteristics of 
freshwater conditions over a month, implicating a transient decrease in mitochondrial density to 
mitigate the energy costs of osmotic stress. 
Neither the 28-day fasting period nor the feeding intensity had a major effect on down-regulatory or 
up-regulatory effects on mitochondria and citrate synthase activity. Salmonids can compensate for 
transient energy deficits by mobilising muscle reserves and/or using the glycogen of the liver 
(Mommsen, 2001), rather than reducing gut capacity promptly. It could also be that the rainbow trout 
are maintaining oxidative capacity in the gut to be able to grow rapidly when food becomes available.
==PAGE:52==
52 
Inter-individual variation in enzyme concentrations may also have concealed more subtle effects of the 
treatment. 
The resultant homeostatic state suggests that, unlike more flexible species like cod and char, young 
rainbow trout show little change in their gut mitochondrial density after an acute starvation period. 
Instead, they appear to have a residual level of oxidative capacity in their GI tissues, presumably as a 
means of avoiding a reduction in digestive ability from too rapid down-regulation of gut metabolic 
function. The citrate synthase activity observations between starved and fed fish are supported by the 
observations from the transmission electron microscopy, where no degeneration or proliferation of 
mitochondria and no changes in the relationship between mitochondria and other cellular elements were 
found. 
 
4.5 Tissue Respirometry  
Tissue oxygen consumption (QO₂) measurements revealed only slight metabolic depression in starved 
trout, but the differences among the treatment groups were not widespread. In the anterior intestine, the 
mass-specific oxygen consumption (QO₂) in the ad libitum fish tended to be lower compared to the 
starved and intermediate treatments, although these differences were not of statistical significance (p = 
0.097). A greater pattern of metabolic suppression, however, became clear in the pyloric caeca of the 
starved fish. Starved trout showed a greatly diminished metabolic oxygen consumption (QO₂) in the 
pyloric caeca compared to both the intermediate and ad libitum groups (p = 0.002) and had an average 
40% decrease compared to the fed fish. The pyloric caeca's QO₂ measurements in the ad libitum and 
intermediate fed treatment groups were the same, suggesting that even moderate levels of feeding will 
be adequate to maintain near-maximal metabolic function in the respective organ. 
The results here confirm the hypothesis that, when deprived of nourishment, rainbow trout can reduce 
the metabolic activity of parts of their digestive system, such as the pyloric caeca, to lower their energy 
expenditure. The mechanism of saving energy is supported by a more than 50% decrease in relative 
intestinal weight (RIW) and an approximately 40% decrease in the relative pyloric caeca weight 
(RPCW) in the starved group. Although the mass-specific values of QO₂ in the anterior intestine did 
not exhibit a statistically reduced decrease, the total metabolic need for the maintenance of the intestines 
would be greatly reduced as a result of the reduction in organ mass. Pyloric caeca tend to show higher 
levels of QO₂ compared to the intestine and represent a major portion of the mass of digestive tissues. 
It can be deduced that the pyloric caeca act as the main site of digestion, and under conditions where 
feed availability is limited, the regulation of the pyloric caeca is crucial for restricting energy 
expenditure.   
Gut maintenance in fish is metabolically expensive. For example, in Dolly Varden, the gut can consume 
around 30% of resting energy expenditure (Armstrong and Bond, 2013). Suppression of digestive organ 
metabolism during starvation is therefore a key mechanism for energy conservation. However, the 
extent of metabolic suppression in our trout was modest compared to some highly specialised fasting 
species, potentially reflecting a “standby” rather than “shutdown” mode. This fits with the finding that 
intestinal tissues preserved their ultrastructure and enzyme function during starvation, indicating that 
while tissue volume is reduced, the remaining cells retain their functionality. 
This is reinforced by the fact that under starvation, the fish maintained their oxygen consumption rates 
in the anterior intestine, albeit with higher inter-individual variability. The anterior intestine may retain 
baseline metabolic function during fasting to maintain ion regulation and other homeostatic roles. In 
contrast, the pyloric caeca – more closely tied to digestive function – showed a more predictable drop 
in metabolic demand. 
Interestingly, within the fed groups, the fish with the highest QO₂ in the pyloric caeca also had the 
highest specific growth rate (SGR), suggesting a link between tissue-level aerobic metabolism and
==PAGE:53==
53 
whole-animal performance. This is consistent with Bélanger et al. (2002), who found that the pyloric 
caeca showed increased cross-sectional area during compensatory growth in Atlantic cod, positioning 
it as a metabolic hotspot. 
One additional factor that may have limited our ability to detect stronger treatment effects is the inherent 
limitation of in vitro respirometry. Respirometry of excised tissue typically yields lower oxygen 
consumption values than in vivo measures, since it excludes contributions from systemic physiological 
processes such as blood flow, peristalsis, and neuroendocrine regulation (Oikawa and Itazawa, 2003). 
In their study on porgy (Pagrus major), the authors found that total in vitro tissue metabolism was 
underestimated in vivo respiration, with intact animals consuming substantially more oxygen at the 
same body mass. These discrepancies arise from the loss of dynamic physiological drivers in isolated 
tissue. Consequently, the moderate QO₂ depression observed here may underestimate the full metabolic 
reduction that occurs in vivo during starvation. 
Overall, the patterns observed suggest that rainbow trout respond to food deprivation by shrinking gut 
tissue mass, especially in the pyloric caeca, while conserving baseline metabolic function in the 
remaining cells. Given that RIW and RPCW were halved in starved fish, the net reduction in intestinal 
energy expenditure was substantial, even if mass-specific QO₂ declined only modestly. This reduction, 
together with preserved cellular architecture and enzymatic capacity, supports a strategy of metabolic 
downscaling without irreversible degradation, allowing for rapid recovery upon refeeding. 
 
4.6 Limitations and Future Considerations  
Feed waste was not counted, and the individual FCR values were not calculated for each fish. This 
prevented the accurate knowledge of how much feed each fish consumed during rearing. It was assumed 
that each fish consumed an equal average of the feed distributed within the tank, however, that is rarely 
the case. As a result, fish were harvested at different times throughout the trial. The starved treatment 
had very similar SGR values, yet those of intermediate and ad libitum treatments were highly variable, 
indicating that the fish were consuming different amounts of feed each day. Additionally, the ad libitum 
treatment had high daily feed waste, meaning that they were not consuming the allocated 2.5% 
feed/body mass/day. It could be speculated that the ad libitum treatment was closer to the intermediate 
treatment. This is confirmed by the various parameters and indices, which showed insignificant 
differences between intermediate and ad libitum treatments.  
The size of the individuals allocated to the ad libitum treatment when introduced to the system was 
considerably lower than the individuals allocated to the other two treatments. Meaning that the fish 
which were already displaying signs of lower activity during feeding were being selected for the 
treatment, where the highest growth would be expected. This is a potential reason for the observed 
similarities between the intermediate and ad libitum treatments. In future experiments, this could be 
resolved by using a standardised weight range for all treatments when introduced to the system.  
There was a variable sample size for many of the experiments. Due to an error in tag reading before the 
starvation period, the SGR values of nine fish (n=9) from the starved treatment were missing. This 
affected the sample sizes for histology and CSA measurements.  
The absence of statistically significant variation in citrate synthase activity (CSA) among feeding 
conditions could be due in part to methodological limitations of the employed assay. The MAK193 
Citrate Synthase Activity Assay kit (Sigma-Aldrich) used for this study has since been discontinued. 
Inspection of its functioning suggests that it relies on deacetylation of acetyl-CoA, yielding free CoA—
a reaction not uniquely catalysed by citrate synthase. A range of enzymes found in fish tissue, such as 
fatty acid synthase (FAS), carnitine palmitoyltransferase I (CPT-I), acetyl-CoA acetyltransferase (also 
called thiolase), and HMG-CoA synthase, drive reactions that liberate CoA under a wide range of 
different metabolic states, such as fasting, feeding, or lipid (Brijs et al., 2017; Morash et al., 2008).
==PAGE:54==
54 
Considering such enzymatic redundancy, we should reasonably hypothesise that the assay tested a more 
generalised acetyl-CoA processing activity that exceeds the specificity related to citrate synthase. These 
enzymes may be responsible for the background activity of the assay, possibly masking treatment-
induced changes in citrate synthase activity. This methodological limitation likely generated variability 
and could be construed as a reason for the fact that the expected treatment effects associated with 
physiological variation were not observed in the present study. To resolve this source of variability, the 
CS0720 Assay kit should be applied in future studies. CS0720 includes a positive control (which 
MAK193 lacked). CS0720 provides the Acetyl Coenzyme A and the Oxaloacetic acid needed for citrate 
synthase to produce citrate (sigma-aldrich.com). 
It was originally intended to perform a Bradford assay to quantify total protein content in the tissue 
homogenates, enabling citrate synthase activity to be normalised per unit of protein. This would have 
allowed a more accurate comparison of enzymatic activity across treatments. However, unexpected 
leakage during the homogenisation process led to variable and, in some cases, insufficient amounts of 
remaining homogenate. As a result, not all of the samples had enough volume for the Bradford assay, 
and the analysis had to be stopped. Also, while preparation of an extra 10 mg subsample for the Bradford 
assay was contemplated, in the end, it was not appropriate since citrate synthase activity had already 
been tested on a different, unmatched subsample. Looking back, using a higher initial volume of 
homogenate would have afforded greater flexibility and ensured that enough material was available for 
measurement of both enzymatic activity and protein content. 
In terms of TEM, the images of the enterocytes in the anterior intestine and pyloric caeca were only 
inspected from one individual (n=1) per treatment, even though samples from the same individuals as 
histology were prepared. This was due to imaging being a time-demanding process, which prevented 
the capability of analysing more samples as well as more quantitative image analysis. In the future, a 
study investigating the differences in cellular composition through quantitative image analysis and 
pairing it with citrate synthase activity measurements with the more appropriate kit or by measuring or 
from the same tissues, could provide insights on the changes.  
In tissue respirometry, the sample sizes were varied between treatments as there were experimental 
errors related to calibration of oxygen probes, breakage of oxygen sensor spots, as well as oxygen 
bubbles under the respirometry chamber lid. In some cases, if the error could not be fixed or worked 
around, the individual was removed from the analyses. Additionally, the pyloric caeca from individuals 
used in other experiments were used to make up for the missing sample size.

5.  Conclusion 
In conclusion, rainbow trout exhibit remarkable structural flexibility within their digestive organs in 
response to changing levels of feeding, while generally maintaining metabolic processes at the cellular 
level. Trout subjected to 28 days of feed restriction had a clear reduction of both size and mucosal 
thickness of the anterior intestine and the pyloric caeca compared to trout fed at intermediate and ad 
libitum levels. Somatic and morphological parameters essential for examination, such as the 
viscerosomatic index, relative weight indices, as well as histological cross-sectional measurements, 
supported the atrophy of digestive tissues during starvation. In contrast, trout fed a steady and sufficient 
food supply had larger gut weight as well as larger mucosal folds, with trout given moderate levels of 
feeding generally being intermediate between the two. 
Whereas both the intestine and pyloric caeca underwent reductions in size, the nature and severity of 
their responses differed. The anterior intestine registered substantial reductions of epithelial thickness 
as well as mucosal density fraction under starvation, suggesting an adaptive response of reducing 
surface area to save energy. However, the pyloric caeca, whose main activity is increasing digestive 
surface area as well as enzymatic activity, retained a relatively higher level of structural integrity, 
suggesting that their activity during post-feeding digestion must be preserved even under conditions of 
nutritional deprivation.  
Most importantly, the structural changes that were observed did not reflect an absolute suppression of 
metabolic function. Analyses of tissue oxygen uptake (QO₂) and citrate synthetase activity 
demonstrated no substantial differences among the different treatment groups for any of the organs 
studied, indicating that metabolic machinery within pyloric caeca and intestinal enterocytes continued 
largely intact during starvation. Ultrastructural analysis supported this, as no obvious loss of 
mitochondrial density, morphology or arrangement was observed in both the anterior intestine and the 
pyloric caeca. Preservation of mitochondrial function could be critical for allowing for a rapid 
reengagement of metabolic function following a return to feeding. 
From the aquaculture point of view, such findings highlight a critical adaptation mechanism and have 
substantial practical significance for feed management. Rainbow trout's ability to survive short 
durations of feed abstinence through minimising digestive structure sizes while retaining the metabolic 
function of crucial tissues like the pyloric caeca and anterior intestine suggests that fasting can be used 
strategically without compromising long-term digestive efficiency. After refeeding, such organs appear 
well-placed for rapidly restoring their function, allowing for compensatory growth as well as efficient 
nutrient uptake. This finding would suggest that, used prudently, feed withdrawal periods can reduce 
feed costs and waste, while still maintaining fish welfare and performance. By learning of the 
differential response of the intestine and pyloric caeca, aquaculture operations can better tailor feeding 
programs that align with the physiological adaptability as well as operational readiness of the fish 
gastrointestinal tract.
