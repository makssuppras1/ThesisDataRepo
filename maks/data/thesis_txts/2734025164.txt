Abstract
This thesis project focuses on the development and implementation of novel fault de-
tection techniques for Wind Turbines, focusing in particular on the root bending moment
measurements to monitor the health condition of the blades.
The implemented fault detection strategies started with a straightforward alarm signal
computed over the root bending moments. Afterwards, the main implemented approaches
involved constructing a data-driven model of the system, first using a linear technique
through Principal Component Analysis, and then with a nonlinear technique, using Au-
toencoders.
After the training of a model of the system using real-life data from a healthy wind turbine,
it was employed to reconstruct new data from a turbine with unknown health condition. A
Fault Detection Algorithm was then applied to the distance between the measured data
and their reconstruction, initially utilizing a simple static threshold and subsequently em-
ploying a Generalized Likelihood Ratio Test (GLR Test). The implemented approaches
proved effective in detecting various failure modes in the root bending moment signals,
demonstrated through both simulated faulty signals and real-life data collected from wind
turbines experiencing blade faults.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The Wind Turbine market has experienced significant growth in recent years and is pro-
jected to continue expanding over the next few decades. This growth is driven by increas-
ing concerns about Global Warming, as evidenced by alarming studies and heightened
public awareness. Governments, such as the European Commission, are committing sub-
stantial resources to transition towards renewable energy sources, aiming for complete
carbon neutrality by 2050 [1].
As the number of wind turbines continues to rise (as shown in fig. 1.1) there is a growing
need to identify suitable locations for new wind farms. These sites must have consistent
and strong wind patterns to justify the considerable investment required. However, find-
ing such locations is becoming increasingly challenging due to limited availability. Con-
sequently, wind turbines may need to be installed in less optimal areas.
To address this challenge and still make the investment economically viable, a new de-
sign approach is emerging: constructing taller turbines with longer blades. These taller
turbines can capture wind from a wider area, maximizing power generation potential. This
trend towards taller turbines with larger blades is illustrated in fig. 1.2.
Figure 1.1: Projections of the expected increase in Wind Energy in the next years, and
needed increase to reach zero carbon emissions by 2050, as shown in [2].
Bigger blades, however, have the drawback of being more likely to be subject to faults.
Even with the current blade designs, as Chou et al. [2013] state, about 14.9% of the faults
on WT are blade failures [4]. This is why it has always been important to develop Fault
Detection (FD) strategies that are able to detect any faults preemptively before they can
spread to other parts of the plant and cause more considerable and costly damages.

1.2
Fault detection in WT
1.2.1
Importance of fault detection
Wind turbines are highly costly plants, and their construction is only justified if their lifespan
is long enough to allow the capture of enough power to cover the sustained costs.
In this regard, as stated beforehand, fault detection plays a key role in trying to prevent
additional and potentially heavy costs that could be necessary if the WT was subject to a
fault. This is why wind turbines are equipped with a vast number of sensors, measuring all
the variables on the plant that could be indicative of a fault happening. Moreover, on top
of being able to detect the fault, the designed FD strategies also have to react promptly
to the fault, to avoid its propagation, hence a higher repairing cost.
1.2.2
Most common causes of faults on the blades
A wind turbine blade is a complex structure, made up of different components assembled
together, as shown in fig. 1.3. As stated by M. Civera et al. [2023], blades are the structural
elements of the wind turbine most affected by damage. This is due to their high complexity
and prolonged exposition to strong dynamic loads.
Faults in the blades can be caused both by endogenous and exogenous factors. As stated
by Kong et al. [2023], the endogenous factors mainly consist of imperfections or invisible
defects caused by process problems occurring during the manufacturing of the blade,
while the exogenous factors come from the environment: rain, hail, frost or lightning that
could hit the blade and cause its failure.
Failures on the blades can be of all different kinds, but the main ones consist of the for-
mation of cracks on the length of the blade, erosion on the leading edge, ice formation

blade. A more complete overview of the main faults and fault causes happening on wind

1.4
Common approaches to FD on wind turbines: a literature
review
Hameed et al. [2009], gave an overview of the FD strategies that are most used for the
Wind Turbine systems [8], dividing them into four main categories. The first two, system
identification approach and observer-based approach, are both model-based, while the
last two, signal-based approaches and expert system and Artificial Intelligence (AI) ap-
proaches, are both data-driven, so they do not rely on an explicit model of the system. It
is worth delving into these two macro-categories separately.
1.4.1
Model-based methods
Model-based FD strategies are the ones that are traditionally used in the Wind Turbines
framework. They are all based on finding a linearized model of the system. The most
common faults that are detected using these approaches are sensor faults, which can be
easily modelled and simulated on the data.
These FD strategies typically consist of generating residuals by using KF and the collected
data, as described in [9]. Wei et al. in [10] and [11] provided a closed loop scheme
that describes the WT model, which was then used for residual generation using Kalman
Filters. At this point, a CUSUM algorithm and GLRT were implemented to detect whether
the blade load moments sensors were faulty or not.
In these last cases wind speed was taken as a reliable measurement for the KF design,
while as it is described in [12], it is extremely difficult to have an accurate local wind speed
measurement. This issue was dealt with by Enevoldsen et al. [2020], who estimated the
wind speed through the estimation of the aerodynamic torque [13].
1.4.2
Data-driven methods
Data-driven methods have the advantage of not requiring any modelling of the dynamical
system of the WT, or approximations given by the linearization of the equations. Villez et
al. [2009], developed a fault detection algorithm that monitors the data collected online
from a plant, whose model is built using Principal Component Analysis [14]. PCA was
later applied also to WT systems: Pozo et al. [2016] give an exhaustive description of
PCA implemented to detect sensors and actuator faults [15]. Later, this method was also
used by Wang et al. [2018], who after developing a Fault Detection algorithm, used PCA
also for Fault Diagnosis [16],.
Bakdi et al. [2019] proposed a way to deal with the linearity of the PCA approach, by per-
forming multiple model training stages, using, for each one, data collected from a different
Operating Region of the WT, and detecting simulated faults on the sensors and actuators
[17]. Later, Zhang et al. [2021], proposed a different way to handle this limitation: in [18],
an extension of PCA, Kernel PCA, is discussed.
Another data-driven FD approach, more advanced and less investigated, is Autoencoders:
building a nonlinear model using neural network architectures. In [19], A. Hallgrimsson
gives a broad overview of this method, describing the typical Neural Network (NN) archi-
tectures that can be used, and how to handle the data collection for the training of the
model, but the framework was not applied specifically to a WT.
Jiang et al. [2018], in [20], used this approach in the WT framework, in particular, to
detect faults happening on the gearbox and generator. In this case, to have more powerful
modelling, they both applied a dynamic extension of the dataset and added noise to the
training data to be reconstructed by the AE.

Zheng et al. [2020], propose a way to train an AE model with Long Short-Term Memory
layers to recognise when a blade is subject to a crack [21]. Other works have continued

such as Liu et al. [2022], [22], and Wang et al. [2023], [23], but in this case not dealing

Wind Turbine structure and modelling
2.1
System description
A Wind Turbine is a mechanical device designed to convert kinetic energy from the wind
into mechanical and then electrical energy. It can be firstly characterized according to the
position of its rotor: as can be seen in fig. 2.1, there can be vertical-axis or horizontal-axis
WTs. Currently, almost all produced wind turbines have a horizontal-axis, usually three-
blade, rotor. The main advantage of this configuration is its ability to capture more wind
energy since the rotor is mounted at the top of a high tower, where the winds are stronger
and less impacted by physical objects on the ground.
Figure 2.1: Vertical-axis wind turbine (a) and horizontal-axis wind turbine (b), as shown
in [12]
Horizontal-axis rotor wind turbines are typically made up of a number of different compo-
nents and subsystems, that can be modelled separately, but work together to assure the
most efficient energy conversion. The main components are, as shown in fig. 2.2:
• Tower subsystem: it is made up of the tower, which has the role of supporting the
nacelle with all of its components and has to be able to withstand the forces of the
wind.
• Aerodynamic subsystem: it is represented by the blades, which interact with the
wind creating aerodynamic forces and loads.
• Electrical subsystem: it consists of the generator, which converts the collected
mechanical energy from the mechanical subsystem into electrical energy.
• Pitch subsystem: it is the subsystem that adjusts the pitch angle of the blades,
to make sure that the interaction with the wind is optimal. In variable-pitch wind
turbines, it is the main actuation component to use when controlling the turbine.

• Mechanical subsystem: it includes the bearings, gears and shaft in the nacelle
that connect the aerodynamic subsystem (i.e. the blades) to the generator. It also
comprises a braking system.

2.1.1
Control strategy and Operating Regions of the WT
Usually, Variable speed and variable pitch Wind Turbines work in three different Operating
Regions, meaning they are applied four different control strategies, as shown in fig. 2.3,
according to the weather conditions they are working in.
The first one, called Below Cut-in Wind Speed Region, is applied when the wind speed
is lower than VCutIn, the cut-in wind speed. In this region, there is typically no generated
power, and when there is some generated power, the low wind speed prevents it from
being of any significance.
The second operating region in this framework is the Partial-load Region, and it is active
between VCutIn and V d, the rated wind speed. In this region, the pitch angles are kept to
βi = 0, i = 1, 2, 3, so that the rotor speed varies according to the wind speed to capture
the maximum amount of power that can be extracted from the wind. The generated power
varies according to the wind speed but stays below the rated value.
If the wind speed is between V d and VCutOut, the WT is working in the third operating
region, the Full load Region, where the pitch angles are controlled and adjusted so that
the rotor speed is kept constant at a certain value Ωrated, in order to keep the generated
power equal to the rated value P d
e , which is the maximum value of generated power that,
for safety and design reasons, can be allowed on the generator.
If the wind speed is greater than VCutOut (cut-out wind speed), then the WT enters the
Above Cut-out Wind Speed Region, where the blades are partly or fully pitched out so
that their rotation is slowed considerably for safety reasons, and the generated power
goes back to zero.

Introduction to data-driven FD methods
Fault Detection on wind turbines is traditionally performed using model-based approaches,
such as Kalman Filter based residuals: these methods give in many cases good results,
as stated in section 1.4, but they are working with relevant approximations, such as the
linearization of the aerodynamic subsystem, which shows many nonlinearities between
the variables, as shown in section 2.1. Another problem that may arise in the model-based
FD approaches is the wind speed estimation: Bianchi et al. [2007] explain how difficult
the proper estimation of the wind speed for the model equations is to reconstruct [12], and
many approaches have been proposed, e.g. in [13], to try to solve this issue.
A way to avoid all these problems in FD on wind turbines is to rely only on the mea-
surements provided by the sensors on the WT, using data-driven approaches. Such FD
methods have already been investigated in the literature, but not specifically focusing
on faults happening on the blades. Hence, this project aimed to implement data-driven
approaches to specifically evaluate the health conditions of the blades.
In particular, firstly a simple FD method based on the computation of an alarm threshold
on the root bending moment signals was developed, and then more advanced methods
were implemented, that could take into account together with the measurements of the
bending moments also other measured variables: one was based on Principal Compo-
nent Analysis and one on Autoencoders.

Conclusions
The FD method based on the computation of the alarm threshold is a quite simple and low-
effort method, that can detect spikes or jumps in the measurements of the root bending
moments, possibly caused by a dangerous and sudden fault in the blade. In particular,
this method is more effective when applied to the edge bending moments, which show
fewer changes in amplitude according to the grid power or the wind speed.
On the other hand, flap bending moments show more significant changes, being influ-
enced by the weather conditions and the operating region the WT is working in, making
this algorithm less effective. This happens because the FD method does not use any
additional information and correlations between the data, such as the grid power or the
wind speed, which could give a broader and more complete overview of the current be-
haviour of the system. In order to solve these issues, more advanced FD methods were
investigated, employing PCA and Autoencoders.
3.2
Introduction to PCA and Autoencoders
To have a more advanced FD, two methods were investigated. These methods rely on
the measurements provided by different WT sensors, such as, besides the root bending
moments, the rotor speed, the grid power, the pitch angles, etc... to develop a data-driven
model of the healthy system, by finding the main features between the collected data. This
first stage is called the Training Stage and is performed offline.
Once the model is trained, the Testing Stage is performed online: new data from the WT,
this time not knowing if faulty or not, are collected and then reconstructed by the trained
model. At this point, the main FD algorithm is applied, which consists of computing a
suitable statistic to compare the data reconstructed by the trained model and the real
data measured by the sensors (usually by means of SPE or MAE). This statistic is then
compared to the same statistic computed during the training stage with the data from the
healthy plant.
If the two statistics are similar, then the algorithm assumes that the new data are from a
healthy plant, while if they are showing strong differences, it detects a fault. Figure 3.11
shows the main steps shared by both methods that were implemented: Principal Com-

AE Extensions
LSTM layers
In dealing with WT systems, however, data show time-varying co-correlation, hence it was
chosen to expand the architecture, by using not just the standard non-recursive layers, but
also Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) layers. They are an example of a recursive neural
network (RNN) architecture that allows to deal with time series data. Its main advantage
is the ability to keep track of the output layer of the previous time steps, effectively being
able to detect and model timeseries data.
Convolutional layers
Another kind of neural network architecture that can be used in building the encoder and
decoder of the AE is the convolutional neural network. It is mainly used to find static
relationships in the data and is applied in many fields, but especially in autoencoders
dealing with image reconstruction.
Convolutional neural networks require, besides the number of layers, the Kernel size, that
is the dimension of the filter used during the convolution operation over the data. However,
increasing the number of parameters could potentially lead to overfitting the model, and
longer computational time.

3.3
Faulty signals for testing
After the design of the Fault Detection algorithms based on PCA and Autoencoders, they
had to be tested with data collected from faulty blades.
However, the data provided by Vestas Wind Systems A/S coming from the turbine that
was used to train the various models were all measured while showing healthy behaviour.
That’s why, in order to test the designed FD algorithms, the effect of faults on the data
was ”reconstructed” starting from the ones collected from the healthy wind turbine. Three
approaches were followed:
1. Two common failures that might affect the blade are icing, caused by the formation
of ice because of low temperatures, and the formation of cracks along the surface of
the blades. Both these events would entail a rather sudden change in the moment
of inertia of the blade, hence showing a sudden jump in the measurement of the root
bending moments, as stated in [28]. Hence, they were simulated on the available
data from the healthy turbine by adding an offset to the original bending moment
measurements, from a chosen time instant tf.
2. Another common fault could be the malfunction of a sensor, possibly caused by an
incorrect set-up on the blade, or by defects in the sensor itself. This kind of fault was
simulated by changing the measured bending moment signal to be constant after a
chosen time instant tf.
3. Finally, it was decided to use real-life data collected from a blade undergoing a
failure. For this reason, Vestas Wind Systems A/S provided other datasets, all from
different WTs, but all collected while a blade failure was happening.

depending on the specifications and necessities for the FD algorithm, T could be de-
creased, in order to have a faster response to the fault, but always keeping in mind that
a faster filter could also cause possible false alarms, due to the spikes in the testing SPE
that wouldn’t be entirely cut out.

SPE do not perfectly fit a normal distribution, they do not deviate too much from it, so the
assumption was kept.

computed on the testing dataset.
Faults simulated as a blocked output on a root bending moment sensor, on the other

of detection is only 3s.
For what concerns faults on the edge bending moments, they are not detected as well by
the GLR tests: in particular, fig. 4.10 shows how none of the designed GLRTs was able
to detect the blocked measurement, except partially in fig. 4.10f. Also, the offsets on the
edge bending moments are largely undetected by the designed FD algorithm. Figure 4.9
shows that the fault is only partially detected in operating region IV .
The inability of the PCA-based algorithm to detect faults showing on the edge bending
moments could be caused by the fact that edge bending moments tend not to change in
amplitude, regardless of the grid power or the wind speed, and so the principal compo-
nents chosen by the PCA may avoid taking into consideration the edge bending moments
and still provide a faithful model of the WT.

4.5
Limitations of the PCA approach
Even if PCA is a linear method, while the relationships between the variables measured
on the WT are highly nonlinear. Despite this issue, the applied extensions, performed by
both extending the datasets with the dynamic PCA approach and separating into different
linear models the five operating regions of the WT, managed to develop a FD algorithm
that could detect different faults happening on the blades. In particular, the designed
algorithm was efficient at detecting large-scale faults and mostly affecting the flap bending
moments. However, for what concerns faults that can be slowly forming and showing on
the edge bending moments, such as erosion on the blades, this linear approach proved
to be less effective.
48
Health Monitoring of Blades using Blade Load Sensors
==PAGE:70==
Another possible limitation to this approach would be in the online implementation of the
FD algorithm: while the data is collected, the correct model has to be constantly updated
according to the operating region the WT is working in so that no false alarms are detected.
This task, however, could prove to be burdensome and difficult to implement, especially
when the WT is changing through multiple operating regions in a short time.
To make up for both these limitations, that is to improve the fault detection effectiveness
also on faults showing on the edge bending moments, and not to have the need to train
multiple models of the system, a nonlinear approach was then designed and implemented,
using Autoencoders.

Autoencoders
Autoencoders were implemented as an improvement of the PCA approach, in order to
solve the main issues that it showed: first of all, the need to perform different training
stages according to the operating region the WT is working in, and its inability to detect
faults happening on the edge bending moments. The Autoencoder is a nonlinear model,
so it is able, from just one training dataset, to reconstruct a model of the healthy WT
that covers all five operating regions, provided that the data chosen for the training stage
covers all of them. For what concerns the second issue, it will be shown in this chapter
how FD performed using the AE model was able to detect faults using simulated faulty
signals in the testing stage that the model developed using PCA was not able to detect.
5.1
Training stage
First of all, the training dataset should be chosen so that it contains a broad range of the
possible weather conditions the WT can face, and covers all the operating regions (for
the sake of FD, the first operating region can be not taken into consideration, as it’s not
meaningful to detect faults when the blades are already still). For these reasons, the data
chosen for the training stage were the ones collected in the first 2.5 × 104 seconds of
the recording, and then other 0.5 × 104 seconds were added, collected between second
7.1×104 and 7.6×104 of the recording. The last 0.5×104 seconds were added because in

regions in a short time span, as can be seen in fig. 5.1: this case is a possible extreme
weather condition that the trained model should take into account.

GLR Test
Using the same setup used in section 3.2.2, the filtered MAEtest is used as the input of the
test: assuming that MAEtrain realization follows a normal distribution with a mean equal
to µ0 and a standard deviation σ (as it was done for the PCA approach), the designed
GLRT tests the hypothesis that also MAEtest follows the same distribution (hypothesis
H0(µ0, σ)), or instead, if there has been an unknown change in the mean (hypothesis
H1(µ1, σ). Also in this case the test statistic g(k) is computed at each time instant k
as eq. (3.14). In this case, parameters h and M were computed setting: PD = 0.99,
PF = 0.01 and µ1 = 2µ0.

Conclusions and Future Work
6.1
Conclusion
The wind turbine market is rapidly expanding due to global efforts to shift energy produc-
tion from fossil fuels to renewable sources, aiming to eliminate carbon emissions by 2050.
As a result, wind turbines are being designed with longer blades to maximize wind cap-
ture. Bigger blades are more costly and, most importantly, are more likely to be subject
to faults. That’s why fault detection algorithms are always going to be of great importance
in the development of wind turbines, to ensure preemptive maintenance and keep low re-
pairing costs: especially for what concerns offshore wind turbines, the costs for repairing
a damaged blade can be considerably high.
Traditionally, fault detection techniques implemented on WT have been model-based: af-
ter finding proper mathematical modelling and parameters of the plant, typically, residuals
are computed, comparing the expected values of the variables with the actually measured
ones, and then, with appropriate FD strategies, the behaviour of the plant would be clas-
sified as faulty or not. With the development of machine learning and deep learning in the
past years, which managed to broaden their use outside of computer science to all sorts
of data handling applications, also data-driven FD approaches have started to be applied
to fault detection strategies on wind turbines.
Data-driven approaches are able to extract the relationships between the measured vari-
ables on the WT and build a model of the system, without relying on pre-designed model
equations or structural parameters of the WT. This project aimed to enlarge this data-
driven approach to design FD algorithms to evaluate specifically the health conditions of
the blades: this was achieved by relying in particular on the measurements of the mo-
ments acting on the root of the blades (a flap bending moment and an edge bending
moment is measured on each of the blades).
The data-driven approaches that were investigated and implemented were, as a primary
approach, an alarm threshold method, computed according to safety indications and vary-
ing according to changes in the mean and variance of the data. This method was not
taking into account any relationships in the data and wasn’t effective, particularly with the
flap bending moment. Other more advanced approaches were then tested, both based
on machine learning, that would apply FD algorithms after building a data-driven model
of the system, the first through PCA and the second through autoencoders.
Both of them were tested with the same dataset, each of them containing a simulated
realization of a possible fault on the bending moment measurements, to be able to make
a better comparison between the performances. Interesting results were shown, with
the PCA based approach being able to detect anomalies on the flap bending moments,
but not on the edge bending moments, while the AE approach proved to be effective in
detecting faults on both flap and edge bending moments.
Moreover, the PCA approach has the main drawback of needing to perform different mod-
els of the system according to the Operating Region the WT is working in, on top of the
inability to detect faults showing on the edge bending moment measurements. It still
proved to be good at detecting faults showing on the flap bending moments, so much so
that when applied to real-life data from a faulty plant, it was able to detect the fault in a
reasonably short time.

6.2
Future work
Considering the advantages and disadvantages of the implemented Fault Detection meth-
ods, shown also in table 6.1, it is clear that the most promising one is the Autoencoder
based approach: it manages to reconstruct the whole model of the plant with one train-
ing stage (provided that the training data is covering as many cases as possible), and so
there is no need in switching between various models during the online implementation
of the Fault Detection.
Naturally, the more data are used, the more complete the model will be. Disposing of more
computational power would enable training a bigger model, even not having to resample
the collected data to reduce the dataset dimension (as it was done for the scope of this
project), hence obtaining a more reliable and precise model.
Another way to improve and broaden the range of faults detected by the FD algorithms
could be to design different GLRTs: the one designed in this work was meant to detect
changes in the mean of the error statistic, but it could be extended to detect also changes
in the variance, in order to capture a wider range of faults.
Moreover, the GLRTs that were designed for this project relied on the assumption that
the computed training losses followed a Normal Distribution. This assumption was based
on a quite relevant approximation, which means that another possible way to improve
the results of the FD algorithms would be to re-design the GLRTs after finding another
probabilistic distribution that can better approximate the realization of the training losses.
