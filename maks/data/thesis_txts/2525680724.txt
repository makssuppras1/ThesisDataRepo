Abstract
This work concerns the design of an integrated switched capacitor converter for a rectified
US mains input voltage and with a varying output voltage in the range from 8 V to 40 V.
The converter is a block in a larger system implementing a full AC/DC converter. The
input voltage is specified to vary from 30 V to 178 V. The converter is designed in a 200 V
silicon on insulator process, is estimated to take up an area of 6 (mm)2, and requires a
total of 8 external surface mounted capacitors for the power conversion stage. The design
uses an input multiplexer which is a switch array from the input to different nodes in a
ladder converter. This implementation achieves the different required voltage conversion
ratios for the varying input voltage. The power transistors in the input multiplexer are
optimized for conduction losses using the estimated input currents for a chosen chip area.
The input current waveform with a chosen switching scheme for the input multiplexer
are used for determining the ideal relative distribution of capacitance and transistor sizes
in the ladder converter, using a charge flow analysis of the ladder converter. External
capacitors deemed suitable for the application are chosen for the power conversion stage.
Most of the circuitry required for the full system implementation including level shifters,
gate drivers, supply generators, comparators for sensing and logic for controlling the
system have been designed and simulated. The designed level shifters have a typical
transistion delay of 2 ns, from logic input to high level logic output, and are estimated
to consume 92.5 µW for level shifting at 700 kHz. This is estimated for level shifting a
signal from 5 V referred to ground to a changing 5 V domain referred to 114 V to 152 V.
The designed gate driver, driving the largest transistor in the converter have a typical
rise time from 0 V to 3 V in less than 2.5 ns.
The designed linear regulators, which
convert a high voltage of 8 V to 40 V to a supply voltage of typically 4.4 V consumes at
most 16 µA bias current. Comparators with voltage hysteresis are designed for the feed
forward control on the input multiplexer. The required comparators combined consume
0.95 mW on average during a period of the input voltage.
The logic for dead time
generation of the switching signals in the converter are designed along with circuitry to
generate the short pulses required for the pulsed current level shifters. The total losses
for delivering 9.97 W at a variable output voltage have been estimated resulting in a
total system efficiency of 93 %.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The mains are the most widely available electrical power source for consumer electrical
power needs. Most electronics however only require a relatively small DC voltage to
operate. In order to utilize the power of the mains, voltage converters are required to
convert the higher voltage to the voltage required for the application. A physically small
converter is desired, which limits the capacitance of the rectified voltage resulting in a
large variation of the input voltage for the converter. A converter can be physically
small if omitting bulky inductors. The goal in this project is to design an integrated
switched capacitor converter in a high voltage integrated process. The converter is a
block in a larger system that would implement a complete AC/DC converter. As the
converter is a block in a larger system a considerable variation of the output voltage is
allowed. The converter designed must operate with the specified rectified mains input
voltage and deliver an output voltage within the specified limits. The input voltage is
specified to have a significant ripple due to low capacitance value following rectification.
The main target of the converter is to achieve as high efficiency as possible, as a low loss
interface from the mains voltage to the following converter’s input voltage.

A DC-DC converter is a power converter which converts a voltage to a different voltage,
ideally without losses. The simplest model of a DC-DC converter is the DC-transformer
and the output impedance. The output impedance models the losses in the converter,
which depends on the load. The model is seen in figure 2.1.

converters. Linear regulators do not have an inherent conversion ratio and only converts
the voltage down by adjusting the output impedance which directly increases losses in
order to achieve the desired step down voltage. If more efficient conversion or a step
up conversion is desired with a linear regulator another kind of voltage conversion must
be combined with it. If powered from the mains voltage traditionally a transformer
would be connected on the input to do conversion before a rectifier and then a linear
regulator. Switch mode power supplies are popular as with ideal components they are
lossless. However the componenets required at low switching frequencies are physically
large inductors and capacitors. To reduce the component size increased frequency can
be used, this however increases the losses associated with switching.
This is where
the resonant power converters, come into the picture. Operating at high frequency to
reduce the component sizes and incorporating resonant tanks in the circuit to minimize
the switching losses of switching transistors when a voltage is present across it. However
resonant power converters still require inductors in order to achieve resonance, and

The smallest converters are switched capacitor converters (SCC), as they can be

2.2
Switched Capacitor Converters
A switched capacitor converter is a type of DC-DC converter which consists of switches
and capacitors. They are switched periodically in different phases in order to convert
a voltage, by transferring charge from the input to the output through a network of
capacitors. Made of capacitors and switches SCCs do not inherently have any losses
apart from those associated with imperfect components and can achieve high efficiencies.
Furthermore capacitors have a much higher energy density than inductors so the power
density can also be very high [1].
The voltage conversion ratio (VCR) 1/M is the ratio between the output and input
voltage 2.1. In the DC-transformer model in figure 2.1 without a load, or with a relatively
small Rout the voltage drop across Rout is 0 and N = M. N is then the ideal VCR.

The ideal VCR ratio N in a SCC is not continuous or easily adjusted by duty cycle

amount of flying capacitors and topology. More specifically with k flying capacitors in
a SCC the ideal VCR is limited to a fraction X/Y where X and Y is limited to the
numbers of the k’th fibonacci sequence [2].
With a load, modelled as a resistance, the output voltage of the converter is based
on the N, but also Rout compared to Rload. Increasing Rout while having a fixed Rload
can decrease the VCR M of the converter 2.2. Adjusting the output voltage this way
is lossy, as the drop in output voltage is lost as power in Rout. This is analogous to a
linear regulator. An unregulated SCC minimizing Rout will perform a VCR as close as
possible to 1:N and therefore achieve VCR with lowest amount of losses.

Recent Development of Switched
Capacitor Converters
As the technology progresses and new process techniques allows for better performing
transistors for larger voltages, the possibility of using integrated SCCs also for higher
voltage power conversions are promoted. Switched capacitor converters are developed
in academia as the industry wants more efficient, cheaper and smaller power converters
for a range of applications such as LED drivers. A switched capacitor converter that
utilizes interleaving of phases to reduce capacitance required and different models of
resonant SCC is seen in [3]. For input voltages similar to mains to stepped down to
DC voltages for LED lighting applications in the power range 10 W-20 W [4] [5] [6] [7].
In [8] discrete implementation is shown of a resonant high voltage step down converter
that uses soft charging to reduce the RMS currents in the converter. The power level
is 53 W. The work of [9] presents a resonant SCC that resembles a modified ladder
converter operated in 4 phases. A higher power implementation of a SCC at 180 W is
seen in [10]. An interleaved ladder converter tested using either both coupled inductor
and a LC resonant tank as coupled inductance between the interleaved phases is used
to achieve resonance.
A SCC LED driver from a high voltage input is implemented in [7]. It uses a discrete
solution of a ladder converter with high voltage rated components on a PCB (printed
circuit board). The ladder does a step down conversion from a high voltage DC input
and a resonant output stage is implemented in order to regulate the VCR through the
switching frequency of the converter. A similar topology to the work of [7] is used in
[4], but this time it is an integrated solution. However the integrated process cannot
handle the input voltage and ICs are stacked to overcome the high voltage requirement.
The work of [6] is a further development of the previous work in [4] and multilevel
resonant output stage is added. The output stage is connected to many nodes of the
ladder not just one, the combined resonant circit achieves better performance than
previously, still through stacked ICs for high DC input voltages to drive a LED string
as load. In [5] an integrated converter with a PFC (power factor correction) rectifier
is implemented. The input is mains AC voltage which is rectified through the PFC
switch array which is controlled via sigma delta modulation. The rectified dc voltage
is then controlled by a rippled cancellation switch array also controlled via sigma delta
modulation to reduce the DC capacitor required. A SCC ladder steps the voltage down
and the output is tapped from a transformer which is added before the output to further
step down, and to add galvanic isolation of the output. Other interesting work with
switched capacitor converters include reconfiguring the available components to best
use the system resources. In [11] a low voltage, low power ladder converter is loaded
by up to 4 stacked loads, which is then sensed and the available capacitance is then
allocated optimally
To summarize a lot of the current work using SCCs for power conversion are utilizing
ladder converters and different resonant techniques to either achieve soft switching or

2.5
Ladder Converter
A ladder converter is a type of switched capacitor converter where flying capacitors are in
series, and bypass capacitors are in series. In the 2 periodic phases the flying capacitors
are alternating switched up and down in parallel with different bypass capacitors in the
ladder. The result is a DC-DC converter with a VCR based on the amount of capacitors
and switches stacked, and where the input and output is connected.
The voltage levels in steady state are ideally equal across all capacitors. This means
the voltage at each bypass capacitor stacked is a multiplum of the capacitor voltage. If
an output is connected in parallel with the bottom bypass capacitor and an input voltage
source connected in parallel with the stack of N bypass capacitors in series, the ideal
VCR must be N:1. However the N’th bypass capacitor is not included in the model for
transferring charge from the input to output as seen later in section 2.6, and is therefore
omitted in analysis. A ladder converter with a VCR of N:1 consists of 2·N switches, and

2.7
Key Process Properties
The process used is a 200 V (Bipolar, CMOS, DMOS) BCD on SOI process. The process
has many types of devices available, such as bipolar devices, CMOS, DMOS and zeners
diodes amongst others. Mainly 3 different kinds of high voltage MOSFETs are distin-
guished in the process and considered for applications. A high voltage super junction
IGBT (Insulated-Gate Bipolar Transistor) of N type is also available, however it is not
considered further,
Super junction (SJ) transistors, are made using super junction techniques. The SJ
transistors in the process achieves the highest voltage ratings of 200 V. They also exist
in smaller versions rated for 140 V and 100 V.
High voltage transistors are available in 2 different modules, both of them being
double diffused transistors (DMOS). One module utilizes drift implants to make high
voltage transistors. These are available in P and N version from 40 V to 125 V rated
drain-source voltage. These transistors were however late discovered to have a limited
drain to handle wafer voltage matching their rated drain to source voltage that no other
transistors in the process seem to have. This limits their usability for stacking them to
a high voltage like in a ladder converter, as most of the transistors will have a significant
==PAGE:21==
2.8 Thermal Model
13
source to handle wafer voltage, and are therefore not able to fully utilize the drain
to source voltage. This module of transistors was used for designing all the circuitry
requiring high voltage transistors rated bellow 125 V, as this module can be used with
the super junction transistor module. In the rest of the report a DMOS transistor refers
to a transistor in this module utilizing drift implants.
The second high voltage transistor module are drain enhanced transistors.
This
module utilizes drain enhancement implants in order to achieve a higher drain-source
voltage breakdown. These transistors exist in P and N versions at 40 V to 60 V rated
drain-source voltage. Above this voltage, only depletion NMOS versions exist up to a
maximum of 125 V rated drain-source voltage. This module is however exclusive with
the SJ module, which means one can not use transistors from both modules in a system.
For this reason the other DMOS transistor module is used.
For the lower voltage levels there are 1.8 V devices for a digital core, and 5 V devices
for eg. the voltages required to drive the gates of the higher voltage transistors which
are all rated for 5V gate source.
In figure 2.5 the concept of the SOI of the process is illustrated. A buried oxide (BOX)
is on top of the handle wafer. The oxide is not conductive, so what is on top of the BOX
is isolated from the handle wafer. Deep trench isolation (DTI) is then used as vertical
isolation, which means different domains can be fully isolated from each other and from
the handle wafer. As the process information is confidential an example of a pair of
transistors in CMOS transistors are shown, instead of the devices of the process. The
CMOS transistors shown are an NMOS with the substrate as bulk and a PMOS in an
N well. As the DTI and BOX completely isolates the different domains a high voltage
transistor could be made in domain 2 without affecting the N and P mos transistors
illustrated, and similarly in domain 3 an isolated domain is contained. The isolator also
contains the leakage current and prevent interference from noisy/leaking domains on the
chip. The isolation does have a parasitic capacitance from domain to domain based on
the size, and the DTI is not very wide, which is good for space utilization but increases
the capacitance, but double DTI can be made to reduce the capacitance at the cost of
area.

capacitor, which means all capacitor voltages will ideally be equal to the output voltage.

3.8
Timings
When switching between the two phases of the converter it is important not to turn
on both phases at the same time to avoid cross conduction. A deadtime tdead is added
between the different phases of the signals in order to allow the first signal to fully turn
off the corresponding gate signals before the gate signals of the second phase starts
rising. tdead must be longer than the delays td,off and td,on added with the fall time
tfall of the gate signal in order to avoid cross conduction. However adding too long
dead time can increase the losses in the converter if it is not operated in SSL, as the
effective duty cycle of the converter is reduced with increased deadtime, whichs results
in higher currents during the on-time, and therefore higher RMS currents and losses in
the switches. Simulations of a DC-DC 5:1 ladder converter operated close to FSL at

the time where ron of the switches are minimized.

4.5
Gate Drivers
The gate drivers are implemented using an inverter chain with increasing size. The last
inverter is sized large enough that the capacitive load of the gate.source capacitance of
the power transistor is charged and discharged sufficiently fast. The previous inverters
are then a scaling number α smaller, until small logic sized transistors can drive them.
The ideal α for propagation speed of the inverter chain is euler’s number, assuming
the current conducted and capacitance scales linearly with the size of the transistors.
However as lower power consumption is more important than maximizing propagation
speed an α of 5 is chosen as it is typically is more power efficient with a slightly larger α.
The inverter chain is sized to be able to drive the largest transistors in the power stage
fast. For smaller transistors another gate driver would be ideal, however due to time
constraints the large gate driver is used for all the transistors in the ladder converter.

4.12
External Capacitors
For discrete capacitors there are different technologies. For this project the type of ca-
pacitors considered are limited to film and ceramics. The capacitance of film capacitors
have a very low voltage dependency, meaning the capacitance does not vary much with
voltage applied. They are however typically not available for in high capacitance val-
ues in small SMD (Surface Mounted Component) packages. A larger package results
in more parasitic resistance and especially inductance which leads to a lower resonance
frequency. Ceramic capacitors have a temperature coefficient that describes how the
capacitance changes with varying temperature. This coefficient is also somewhat related
to the voltage dependency. The highest quality ceramic capacitors are described with
the lowest temperature coefficient C0G. These typically also have a low voltage depen-
dency, however the C0G type dielectrics do not have a large permitivity resulting in a
limited capacitance in a small package. Capacitors of C0G type ceramics in reasonable
SMD package sizes are usually limited to a few nF. For applications requiring several
micro farads in a small SMD package, X5R or X7R type of dielectrics are usually the
only options. Capacitors with these temperature coefficients, however, can experience
significant voltage dependency, apart from variations with temperature. This can be up
to 90% degradation of the specified capacitance with the rated voltage applied. This
needs to be taken into account when designing a circuit with a performance directly
affected by the capacitance. In a SCC with a DC input voltage, the voltage across the

5.1
Ladder Converter Optimization
The main system performance metric is the power loss of the converter.
A system
level simulation is prepared to determine to optimal capacitance values, transistor sizes
and switching frequency for minimizing the losses of the SCC. The simulation uses the
real transistor models provided by the fab. Supply generators, gate drivers and input
multiplexer are simulated with models. The input voltage is a voltage source with an
waveform as determined earlier and seen in figure 3.5. The model of the supply generator
and gate driver mimics the actual supply generator and gate drivers by charging the gate
voltage to 5 V from the high capacitor voltages, and discharging to the low capacitor
voltages. The model of the input multiplexer uses the equivalent on resistances calculated
in section 4.11, and the threshold voltages for gearshift from table 3.1. The load is a
constant power load with a feed back control adjusting the current to achieve a target
power. The gain and bandwidth of the power load is limited, and emulates a power
converter with a reference output power and feedback control. The models are described
further in appendix A.3. The capacitors M8 and M9 in 4.2 which are just used for
supplies, are chosen as at 100 pF each in the system level simulation. Furthermore with
no ripple requirement on the output, the output capacitor is chosen as a value equal
to the smallest capacitors, which are C1 and C2 in 4.2, in order to reduce the power
associated with gear switching. The relative transistor and capacitor sizing are set as
calculated in section 3.7. From the described setup transient simulations were made
sweeping the capacitor values, transistor sizes and switching frequency. The average
input power and output power of system level converter were measured across several
periods of the mains frequency. From the measured losses the efficiency of the converter
with different capacitor values, transistor sizes and switching frequency were compared
in order to chose the target converter. The total efficiency is measured in the many
transient simulations as the average output power supplied to the load divided by the
average input power of the voltage source and plotted in figure 5.1. The thicker lines
indicate the use of larger external capacitors. The transistor width in the legend is the
width of the smallest transistor in the ladder M1 and M2 4.2. The other transistors are
scaled according to the vector nr in section 3.7. The transistors are split into fingers with
a width of 50 µm width each, and the total transistor size is adjusted by increasing the
number of fingers. The finger size is limited at 50 µm to not have excessively long gates
which then can have a significant parasitic inductance or resistance, limiting transient
switching up the transistors. The capacitance in the legend in 5.1 refers to the smallest

is because the losses when switching gears are significant in comparison to the losses
of the Rout of the converter operating in a specific gear. Even while changing gears at
a very low frequency, the power lost is significant and limits the maximum efficiency.
Reducing the capacitance further results in higher Rout losses from the increased RSSL,
and increasing the capacitance results in more losses when changing gears. Increased
switching frequency also reduces the losses due to the hard switching of the drain source
capacitances, and losses for charging the gate using a linear regulator from a high voltage.
This makes the absolute capacitance values used in the power converter a very important
parameter, that must not vary too much from the target value. This also means if the
capacitances, of the external capacitors used, vary from the target value, additional
losses are introduced. This makes ceramic capacitors such with dielectrics such as X5R
and X7R badly suited for external capacitors as their capacitance values are greatly
dependant on the applied voltage which varies between 15 V to 38 V as seen in table 3.1.
The chosen design is at 700 kHz switching frequency, using a capacitance of 100 nF for
C1 and C2 and a total transistor width of 2.5 mm. This design achieves near maximum
efficiency of the system, while requiring relatively small external capacitors, which is
easier achieved.

The losses for the complete system are estimated based on the simulations so far. The
primary losses of the input multiplexer are accounted for in the system level simulation,
as the on resistances of the different transistors are included in the model. It is assump-

are significant, as the frequency for switching them are 120 Hz, the switching losses are

of the system. The losses for the 5 V to ground referred circuitry such as the pulse
generator in 4.2, is assumed to be generated from a lossless converter to 5 V, resulting

level shifter, and almost maximum voltage for the regulator. The losses are however

6.1
Required Changes
Before the system designed is fully functional some things need to be considered first,
as the system is not functional without. The following are the critical parts that need
to be solved before a fully functional system can be implemented.
6.1.1
Device Limitations
It was discovered that the used transistors in the implementation are seemingly not
suited for the job of being stacked at high voltage, relative to the handle wafer. Com-
munication with the fab is required to discover what causes the limitations as they seem
similar in their isolation to the handle wafer of other transistors, which do not have
these limitations, and some older documentation have different voltage ratings. If the
transistors used indeed cannot be used the other module with drain enhanced transistors
have to be used. However this requires a large redesign of most blocks as the module
is exclusive with the super junction module. This means that the levelshifters require a
new topology, and the supply generators for the input multiplexer require a new topol-
ogy, as the schemes proposed are not possible without the very high voltage transistors.
For 125 V which are used in the input mux only a depletion NMOS is available. This
requires a redesign of the supply generators and gate drivers for the input 2 and 5. Also
for 85 V transistors only an NMOS is available which again requires a redesign of the
inputs 3 and 4 to make sure.
6.1.2
Changing Gear
When changing from a conversion ratio N:1 to N-11:1 the voltage at the input side is a
voltage equal to the capacitor voltage higher than the voltage at the converter side. The
input will charge the series of capacitors to be equal to the input voltage. However as
the capacitors are not the same capacitance a voltage imbalance will happen if a small
resistance is in this path because the voltage balance of all the capacitors will not have
been established by the charge transfer of the switching converter.
When changing gear downwards; if a lower conductance is connected in first and the
resulting RC is much larger than the RC of the on resistance of the switches, the voltage
can equalize across the switches. This requires extra parallel transistors in the input
mux, that are much smaller, and timing and control signals for these.

6.1.3
Changing Gear up
When changing from a conversion ratio N:1 to N+1:1 initially the voltage on the con-
verter side is higher than the voltage on the input side by a voltage equal to the individual
capacitor voltages.
If this is connected with a low resistance through a large input transistor the converter
side capacitors will try to discharge through the series connected capacitors. As if the
capacitors are in the same order of magnitude they will not discharge completely, however
as the topmost capacitor is much smaller than the other bypass capacitors, as it is not
part of the power transfer, it will be discharged completely.
A solution similar to that of 6.1.2 would work. Where the input is for the first short
time connected with a lower conductance transistor, which in turn allow the converter
to achieve voltage balance across the capacitors by switching.
However conducts charge back in the source and makes for an inefficient transfer,
with unnecessary losses. In order to avoid this a different option is considered: The
input multiplexer can be disconnected for a certain time until all the capacitor voltages
of the ladder have discharged by the charge draw from the output. Then through sensing
it can be determined when the voltage at the converter side of the input should be equal
to the of the input source at which point the input switch is connected. It should be
easily implemented with a comparator for sensing and a combination of logic. Only
a way to remember past states are now required, such as flip flops or latches, as the
procedure for changing gear up and down are no longer the same.
6.1.4
Supply for M1
In figure 4.2 the transistor M1 has a special supply. As the voltage for the capacitor
cannot be bootstrapped by a diode, as this would exceed the maximum voltage to the
handle wafer of200 V the regulator must take a switched voltage as the only regulator
in the system. This switching behavior for the voltage supply has not been tested so far,
and should be done. The output voltage of the regulator is rectified to a supply capacitor
C9. If the same type of regulator is used here as for the rest of the switches the supply
voltage will be a diode drop lower at V ddh1 to V ssh1 than the rest of the switches.
While the system is likely to operate functionally at this voltage it could cause some
problems, if the voltage drops too much, that have not been investigated so far. Another
solution could be to have the regulator in 4.2 connected between V in5 and V ssh1 be a
modified regulator with a higher reference voltage for the pass transistor than just the
zener. Maybe 2 or 3 diodes can be placed in series with the zener to produce a slightly
higher output voltage. This voltage is then rectified by the diode to a voltage across C9
which is higher than a supply voltage. A second regulator matching the other regulators
could then be connected between C9 and V ddh1. This should work as seen in figure 5.8
the output voltage of the regulator works even with very low voltages across it. This
way the supply rail V ddh1 to V ssh1 would match the rest of the high supplies.

6.1.5
Startup
The startup of the converter is not trivial as the input mux is not rated for the max-
imum input voltage if the capacitors of the converter are discharged. The system is
only designed for steady state operation which means another circuit that charges all
the capacitors to values within steady state operation before the input and output are
connected. However as the 200 V super junction transistors in the process have a low
conductance per total area the series resistance would be raised a lot if implemented
on chip. A startup circuit with low conductance transistors could be implemented on
chip and charge the capacitors during startup and then off chip a high conductance high
voltage transistor could connect the input to the converter input. The output could
possibly be connected after startup by an on chip 40 V transistor, if the conductance
for the area allowed is satisfying.
6.1.6
Sensing for Input Multiplexer
The sensing scheme of resistors for the input voltage needs a second thought. It was
discovered that the high ohmic poly resistors used for sensing are specified to a maximum
of 100 V terminal to bulk, where the bulk is the underlying p-well on which the resistor
is made. As the input voltage is up to 180 V there is no node in the converter fits the
requirement of being within 100 V of the input and the ground at which the comparators
are placed, at all times. This means there is no node on which all the resistors the input
sensing chain can be made with good matching. No other poly resistor in the process can
tolerate the required terminal to bulk voltage. The only way a node could be meeting
the required voltage from terminals to bulk would be an auxiliary node at a voltage
between the input 2 and input 3 of the ladder. Alternatively the resistor R1 in 4.11,
blocking most of the input voltage would have to be made in 2 separate strips, one on
a bulk at high voltage, and one at a bulk of low voltage. This in turn would make the
matching of the low resistors R2, R3 and R4 very hard or impossible. It is possible that
the mismatch is small enough that changing the target voltage for gear switching to a
lower voltage. Alternatively a new sensing scheme must be implemented. Instead of
feed forward from the input a sensing scheme based on the output voltage could be a
possibility, as this is only sensing up to 40 V. The state decision is then however no
longer just based on the current voltage but also the current state, so memory would
be required, in the form of for example latches or flip flops. A solution for the initial
step voltage would also be required, as starting in a low gear could cause the switches
to break.
6.2
Possible Improvements
The following section mentions some things that could be investigated for a possible
improvement of the designed system. The changes are not crucial to a functional system,
but placed where that could be investigated in pursuit of higher system performance.
==PAGE:80==
72
6 Future Work
6.2.1
Voltage for switching gears
The current design was done with a decision to switch gears close to the maximum
voltage to achieve the highest output voltage on average, reducing the current draw, and
therefore limiting the losses due to the equivalent output impedance of the converter.
It was a design decision where the input multiplexer changes gear but it is very likely
that an optimal gear switching scheme exists that will minimize the sum of the losses
of the operating converter and gear switching converter. As the losses associated with
changing gear and the change in capacitor voltage are larger when trying to maximize
the output voltage. But as the current optimization was done based on the charge in the
different gears an analytic method seems unlikely. A numeric optimization procedure
seems more likely or alternatively making a few different designs and seeing if there is a
trend for how gear switching is best done.
6.2.2
Size of Ladder Converter
The current design of the ladder was done with minimizing the complexity and amount
of external components. Increasing the number of stages in the ladder from 5 to for
example 9 would mean more external components and potentially higher losses from
the equivalent rout of the converter due to more transistors and capacitors, however the
voltage change on the capacitors when changing gear would be much smaller reducing
the power lost on gear switch significantly.
The drawbacks are the complexity and
external components but pros are less capacitor voltage which means less power loss on
linear regulators and especially gear switch. Also the output voltage would ripple less,
making it more suited for a linear regulated load, if a second power converter following
is omitted.
6.2.3
Bypass Capacitor near Input
As it was seen in the waveform of the system level converter in 5.10 the bypass capacitors
connected to the input change the currents in the converter to higher rms currents. This
increase the conduction losses in the input multiplexer and the switches. Creating a way
of switching the topmost bypass capacitor out of the converter when it is not used for
charge transfer could be a way of reducing this undesired effect. Having small internal
bypass capacitors which could supply the gatedriver circuits like the capacitors C8 and
C9 in 4.2 and switching a transistor in series with the external capacitors off could be a
way to solve this. This is a switch event only happening once the gear changes, making
it possible to have a very large switch as switching losses are not a concern.
6.2.4
Low Power Load
For low power loads the input capacitor is naturally not discharged and the converter
only operates in 5:1 mode.
It is unknown how the performance in such a scenario
==PAGE:81==
6.2 Possible Improvements
73
plays out, but it should be investigated. If the performance is problematic, making a
way to reconfigure the converter to, for example, reduce the effective size of the power
transistors, or operate the converter in burst mode, to decrease the losses for low loads
should be investigated.
6.2.5
Parasitics
The simulations made of the converter did not include parasitics. Pad capacitances,
metallisation capacitances, bond wire inductances and resistances, and the paracitics
of the external capacitors should all be taken into account. The parasitics should be
estimated and added to in the simulations of the converter. Then the effects should be
evaluated on signals and losses of the converter. It is not unthinkable that the parasitics
of the large external capacitors and bondwidres results in a resonance are too close
to the switching frequency and limits the transients currents of the converter. Maybe
smaller SMDs of high quality dielectrics such as C0G is required in parallel with the
large film capacitors to allow the higher frequency harmonics of the currents, or maybe
the required capacitance is small enough that it can be implemented on chip, like C8
and C9 in figure 4.2. First the impact of the parasitics should be evaluated.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The target of this project was to design an integrated switched capacitor converter in
a 200 V silicon-on-insulator process. The converter steps down a varying voltage, from
a rectified US mains with large ripple, to an varying output voltage within a specified
voltage range. The output is allowed a significant voltage ripple as it is assumed that
the designed converter is part of a system, where the following converter operates effi-
ciently with a large input voltage range. For an input voltage between 30 V to 180 V a
converter resulting in an output voltage between 8 V to 40 V is made. This is done using
an input multiplexer, which is a switch array that connects the input to different nodes
in a ladder topology switched capacitor converter and in this way achieves different con-
version ratios from input to output without linear regulation. A feed forward control
approach is used where the input voltage is sensed through a sensing network and using
comparators. These control signals are then used to control the input multiplexer and
the resulting voltage conversion of the converter. The input voltage waveform was de-
termined for the given voltage specifications, calculating a minimum capacitance value
for the rectification. A design decision was made for the converter to operate maximiz-
ing the output voltage due to a constant power load resulting in reduced currents and
steady state losses. From this decision the converter component currents, over a period
of the determined input voltage, was calculated using a charge flow analysis of the con-
verter. With the component currents the relative sizing of the converter components
were designed. The input multiplexer was designed by distributing the area available
between the different transistors required for the input multiplexer and optimizing for
power loss using estimated input RMS currents. The circuitry required for an implemen-
tation of the converter was designed, examples being supply generators, level shifters
and gate drivers. The supply generators are implemented as linear regulators from the
high voltages available across the capacitors in the converter to a low supply voltage
for driving the gate, and supplying level shifters. The designed supply generators for
the ladder converter take up area less than the area of the high voltage transistor it
was designed for, use less than 16 µA bias current and work across a voltage range of
8 V to 40 V without additional current consumption or significant supply voltage drop.
The level shifters have a typical transition delay of 2 ns, from logic input to high level
logic output, and are estimated to consume 92.5 µW for level shifting a 700 kHz 5 V
signal from ground referred to a flying 5 V domain between 114 V to 152 V. The typ-
ical rise time of the gate driver driving the largest power transistor is less than 2.5 ns
from 0 V to 3 V. The level shifters, supply generators and gate drivers were simulated
across process corners to verify performance with no significant deviation in behavior,
other than slightly different supply voltages due to lack of feedback, and a few ns extra
==PAGE:84==
76
7 Conclusion
delay and rise time in the worst corners. A control circuit for the input multiplexer was
designed, including the sensing of the input voltage using comparators, and the logic
required to generate the control signals for the input multiplexer. The 3 comparators
designed to determine the state of the input multiplexer combined consume 0.95 mW on
average, for detecting a period of the mains input voltage. The designed circuitry was
simulated and evaluated in separate simulations due to convergence difficulties of the
complex simulations combining the designed circuitry. The system was simulated using
ideal models, regulators, gate drivers and input multiplexer. The simulated system load
was a constant power power load with a target instantaneous power and feedback with
a limited gain and bandwidth. The power load was modelled in verilog-A. The input
multiplexer was modelled in verilog-A using the on-resistances of the designed transis-
tors. Many system level simulations were made using the calculated optimal relative
capacitance and transistor sizes, and sweeping these along with frequency. The simu-
lations were made for multiple periods of the mains input voltage in order to measure
the average power loss. The losses were measured in order to find the optimal target
transistor sizes, capacitance and frequency. It was discovered that higher capacitance
was not equal to lower losses, as would be expected from the slow switching limit loss
calculations. External capacitors were chosen from the target capacitance determined
and the system losses were estimated using simplified models of the external capacitors
and the system circuitry. The total system power efficiency was estimated to be 93 %.
This did not meet the target goal, as it was discovered the gear switching introduced
significant losses from changing capacitor voltages. From the estimated losses while sup-
plying rated power, and a provided thermal model for a possible chip package and PCB
stack up, the chip temperature was estimated to 68.8 ◦C at an ambient temperature of
25 ◦C . Some limitations were discovered with the current implementation which were
discussed and possible solutions were suggested for future work.

A.1
Simulation Difficulties
During this process many simulations were made, both of the power system and test
benches of the different blocks required in the system.
Many times convergence issues were had during these attempted simulations, and
the troubleshooting output of cadence were not that helpful for determining what could
be done to solve the problem.
The first mistake is trying to simulate a system with high impedance nodes without
proper initial conditions.
For example simulating the power stage of switches and capacitors with a gatedriving
model but both phases off at t=0. This means from the gate to either the supply or the
source are high impedance and the simulator has a hard time figuring out what to do.
If the gate is not fully on or off at t=0, and the flying capacitors are not well defined too
refered to the bypass capacitors this too can lead to convergence issues as these nodes
are also high impedance if all the power transistors are off.
Sometimes initial conditions and a well defined starting point alone is not enough.
A dc solution still cannot be found. In this case it can be attempted to skip the initial
solution in the simulator, and try to reach a steady state from a fully off stystem. De-
laying the input voltage source, and injecting a current into the input and output node
from the start. This is done with a voltage pulse in series with a vccs condcuting current
only in one direction.
A.2
Considerations for Transistor Sizing
For most of the transistors operating in logic arrays minimum size is ideal for both
speed and power as long as the load is has to drive is also minimum size. However due
to the connection to the gate poly which has a minimum width it is beneficial to avoid
minimum width. Using transistor widths where the connection to the poly is wider than
the gate results in a dogbone shaped gate and connections area due to the resulting
==PAGE:86==
78
A Appendix
shape of the gate and connections. The width of the transistor should be at least the
width of the contact to the poly. This will avoid leakage due to distortion of the diffusion
shape, and possibly insufficient poly end coverage. Minimum length means variations
in threshold voltage. Doesn’t matter for power devices operated as switches,where as
high as possible conductance per area and parasitic capacitance is desired. Matters for
analogue devices used in saturation region where matching of thresholds matters.
When the load consists of several gates an up scaling of transistors near the output
is beneficial for the driving capabilities and speed of the system. The capacitance of
whatever the transistors have to drive should always be taken into account.
As NMOS have better material properties than PMOS which means they will have
the same conductance only if the PMOS has a high w/l ratio. If logic is sized with equal
n and p w/l ratio the result is different turning on and off slopes.

A.4
Modeling Real Transistor Models
The power transistors are operated as switches, and can be modeled with different passive
devices. With simulation difficulties using real transistors, one could characterize the
important behaviors of the transistors used and replace the real models with ideal models.
A transistors operated as a switch can be replaced with a switch from analogLib. The
switch from analog lib has 4 terminals. Control positive and negative and conducting
positive and negative. The negative control terminal is tied to the negative conducting
terminal. The other control pin then models te gate pin of an NMOs transistor. The
positive conducting terminal the drain node and the negative control and conducting
the source node. The on resistance of the switch is set equal to a resistance Rds. A
capacitance Cgs is placed between the source and the gate node, and a capacitance Cds
is placed between the drain and source node. The off resistance of the switch is set to
many mega ohms, to model a transistor being off.
The transistor parameters such as Cgs, Rds and Cds are characterized from a test-
bench of the actual transistors, with different with different transistor sizes.
