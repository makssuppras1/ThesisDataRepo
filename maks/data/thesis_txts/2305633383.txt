This report describes models and methods developed for offshore  
wind farm optimization. They are implemented in an application  
that interfaces two external programs, Colonel - for calculations  
connected with wind wakes, and IPOPT - for the use of optimization  
algorithm. Two different models for placement of wind turbines are  
proposed. A simple model where turbines have two Cartesian  
coordinates, and another layout model where turbines are arranged  
in a lattice. Formulations describing three different constraint types  
were proposed: external boundary, area of the wind farm  
and proximity of the wind turbines. Derived formulations  
and models are tested. First using simple wind climates and  
afterwards using an existing climate obtained from Horns Rev 1  
wind farm. Finally an implementation of a cost model that includes  
water depth is proposed.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation and main objectives
The main objective was to develop an experimental work ow for numerical optimization
of turbine positions inside a wind farm using Fuga as a wake model. The application
developed for the purpose of this project relies on already existing models and methods,
both for calculating wind resources, wakes and energy production as well as optimization.
The rst is realized with the help of WAsP [1] and Colonel - a program written by
Søren Ott implementing the Fuga wake model. WAsP is the industry-standard software
package for siting of wind turbines and wind farms.
Optimization is achieved using
IPOPT (Interior Point OPTimizer) [2]. It is a software implementation of primal-dual
interior point method which uses line searches based on lter methods. I developed a
Python interface for Colonel and used an existing one for IPOPT [3]. The following report
describes development of relevant problem formulations, layout models and constraints
to address the optimization of energy production, as well as cost of a wind farm.
The eld of wind farm optimization is not as developed as other elds of study con-
nected to wind power. This can be seen in the number of published research articles
and available books about this subject. Moreover there exist a very small number of
commercial and open source tools to deal with the problem of optimal wind turbine
placement. Additionally the software that oers wind farm optimization modules relies
on predetermined grid of possible turbine locations. This severely decreases the possible
set of optimal solutions. Furthermore some implemented algorithms, like in the case of
WindPro Optimize, are inherently slow because they are allowed to move only one tur-
bine at a time. This is the reason there exists a lot of room for development of computer
aided optimization tools when searching for the best wind turbine placement.

1.3
Optimization methods
There are two basic groups of optimization algorithms used in the wind farm layout
optimization. Those are the algorithms that use and don't use the information about
derivatives of objective and constraint functions. The most popular representatives of
==PAGE:12==
Chapter I. Introduction
3
the second group within the early research community were the optimization methods
based on genetic algorithm [4] [5] [6] [7]. The genetic algorithms do not require infor-
mation about derivatives to solve an optimization problem. The main advantage of this
approach is its simplicity and ease of understanding. It can also be almost eortlessly
used with existing simulations and models. However the family of genetic algorithms has
some inherent disadvantages. First of all it can be very slow to obtain a satisfying solu-
tion, moreover there is no absolute assurance that a genetic algorithm will nd a global
optimum. Another drawback is a slow convergence rate and signicant time caused by
numerous evaluations of the objective function (which in itself can be time consuming).
Gradient based methods, and especially interior point method algorithm used during
this project, are generally more complex and thus harder to understand than genetic
algorithms. Moreover the interior point methods can answer a question: how good is
the obtained solution? This done by the use of primal-dual approach which provides a
lower bound on the solution. A comparison between the use of genetic algorithms and
gradient based methods was presented in [8]. The general conclusion is that the gradient
based methods provide better convergence rate and can handle large scale problems.
1.4
Fuga wake model
Fuga wake model has been developed relatively recently - in 2011 [9]. It is based on
a linearized Reynolds-averaged Navier-Stokes equations based on simple turbulence clo-
sure. However unlike standard CFD methods there is no need to establish a grid system
representing considered geometry and usage of control volumes. The main advantage
of this particular wake model is the speed of calculations, said to be 10−5 to 10−8 of
the cost of a the full CFD model [10]. This particular feature makes it well suited for
the use in optimization, where it can be required to carry out a simulation hundreds or
even thousands of times before a satisfying result is obtained. Substantial computational
speed increases were also possible due to use of precalculated results stored in lookup
tables. They store velocity eld data behind a turbine. Wind wakes resulting form in-
teractions of multiple turbines are calculated as a sum of all velocity perturbations. The
annual energy production is estimated as a probability-weighted integral of power output
at all wind speeds and directions. Presentation the governing equations for Fuga wake
model is conducted in [9].
A main disadvantage of this wake model is inconsistency both with the behavior of CFD
models and the actual data in close distance to the turbine, downwind form the rotor.
Said distance was not described in any way and therefore it might be dicult to assess
the accuracy of calculations when turbines are placed close to each other. Moreover Fuga

model can be accurately applied to conditions where the terrain is relatively smooth and
uniform, such as oshore wind farms. Usage of more complex terrain topologies charac-
terized by horizontally varying surface roughness and wind prole can yield inaccurate
results.

the objective function. This in turn would prompt the optimizer to spread the turbines
as far away from each other as possible in order to reduce the wake eects. In Fuga this
phenomenon is modeled in a specic way, causing a ripple eect in the wind speed. It
can however be removed if a smoothing function is used.

Lattice layout
In case of the lattice layout the proximity constraints do not have to be used at all.
Instead the shear angle, corresponding to a minimum distance between turbines from
neighboring columns, can be precalculated before the optimization process begins. This
way we could obtain lower and upper bounds on the β variable and circumvent the need
to calculate the proximity constraints by the optimizer. Theoretically this method could
decrease the time needed to nd a solution, but it will also restrict the number of possi-
ble intermediate paths towards an extremum. A number of times I have experienced an
event where the optimizer would introduce infeasibility to the system by violating the
proximity constraints for a small number of iterations in order to place the wind farm
in a more desirable position. In the end this would yield higher value of the objective
function. By introducing bounds on a variable such operation might not be possible,
since the optimizer always complies with them. Thus the objective function could be
limited to a local extremum, which is further away from the global one than in the former
case.

Scaling and initial conditions
Scaling and initial conditions can have an enormous impact on the convergence rate of
the optimization algorithm. Badly scaled problem variables can result in ill-conditioned
matrices. Whereas initial conditions corresponding to exceedingly violated constraints
can add signicant computational time needed to reduce infeasibility and consequently
obtain a proper solution. Some variables in a problem can have entirely dierent mag-
nitudes, i.e. one parameter can be in the range < 106, 107 >, while another's values
can change between 1 and 2. Such a problem is said to be badly scaled and can cause
numerous issues. Some algorithms have stopping conditions dependent on the variable
step size between consecutive iteration. In the example above a step size of 1000 for the
rst variable would be orders of magnitude higher than the range of values the second
variable can assume. It is therefore necessary to use scaling factors to avoid such issues.
The concept of scaling in an optimization problem involves nondimensionalization of the
variables as well as adjusting their relative values so that a step increase in any one of
them would result in the same change in the value of the objective function.
In practice scaling parameters have to be applied on the variables and the relevant
expressions, such as objective function and constraints, as well as their derivatives, used
by the optimizer. The optimizer should only work on the scaled expressions.

Chapter 6
Cost models and bathymetry
6.1
Cost model
When dealing with wind farm development process it is crucial to properly position indi-
vidual turbines. Previous chapters presented results obtained using two layout models,
one where the turbine's location was described only using two cartesian coordinates and
another one where they were arranged in a lattice.
In the rst case the turbines could be moved around arbitrarily and in fact their position
was constrained by the external boundary polygon as well as changing AEP due to
wakes caused by proximity of another turbine. In the latter case position of the lattice
with respect to the datum could be largely arbitrary and the most important factors
inuencing the energy production were those describing shape of the lattice, as well as
its orientation towards the predominant wind direction. Adding an information about
the ground depth below water level can have an impact on the position of the lattice
within the boundary.
This chapter contains the information about inclusion of bathymetry to the optimization
problem, mainly in the form of combining water depth with energy production.
The chosen cost model represents a linear combination of cost and energy production. It
can represent the installation costs being oset by prots from electricity sales. Similar
cost models can be found in [28], [29].
While this cost model is simple, it enables demonstration of coupling between the annual
energy production and water depth. It is also possible to add the information about
water depth in a form of constraints, for example by requiring that the turbines should
be placed at a position where the depth is smaller than a specied value.

Chapter 7
Summary and conclusions
During the course of this project I developed an application to interface Colonel - a
program implementing Fuga wake model, with IPOPT - an optimization software based
on Interior Point Method. Formulations for two dierent layout models were derived to
represent the arrangement of turbines inside a wind farm. Constraints dening external
boundary based on convex polygon, wind farm area and turbine proximity were adopted
and tested in multiple cases. A series of numerical experiments was conducted to test the
viability of the developed application and models. First it was subjected to conditions
representing very simple wind climates, not present in reality. Two main cases were used
for testing - wind blowing predominantly from one direction and a climate where the
distribution of wind directions is uniform. The application yielded expected results by
placing turbines in a way that almost entirely avoids wake losses for the rst climate
group. For the second climate group it spread out the turbines from each other as far
as possible, while simultaneously minimizing wake losses. Consequently, the simulations
were carried out to attest the correctness of the implemented optimization methods when
exposed to real-life conditions. This was done by comparing the optimal layout designs
with an existing wind farm - Horns Rev 1. Two optimal layouts, obtained using dierent
bounds of the constraints, were produced. It was possible to increase the annual energy
production by 0.295% and 0.646% respectively for the wind climate used during the
simulations. Finally a cost model which combined water depth and energy production
was proposed.
While the models and methods used during the course of this project do not include all
the factors necessary during planning and development of wind farms, they are a good
starting point. The application itself is modular and additional extensions can be easily
added. Moreover the whole optimization process runs fast relatively to the available
wind farm layout optimization software.

7.1
Future work and possible extensions
The eld of of wind farm layout optimization is relatively sparsely researched and docu-
mented and therefore there exists a wide area for improvements. Furthermore the avail-
able commercial and open source software is mostly based on Jensen wake model [30] and
grid based optimization methods, which can make the whole process time-consuming.
The models and methods developed for the purpose of this project work well for sim-
ple wind farm layouts. If more elaborate designs are to be achieved one should modify
the models responsible for turbine positioning, for example by including a polynomial
function adding displacement of the columns in a lattice matrix. Moreover the current
model only considers external boundaries based on convex polygons. A modication of
this constraint could be added to included non convex polygons or a series of disjoint
polygons by introducing a penalty term to the objective function. The proximity con-
straints could be replaced and replicated more accurately by addition of an algorithm
implementing some aspects of multibody dynamics and calculating mechanical loads,
stress and fatigue of the blades.
Additionally the issues related to cable and infrastructure layout as well as operation
and maintenance could be integrated into the program to more accurately express the
cost model.
Currently the application used in this project is based on an gradient based optimizer.
This fact has inherent advantages and disadvantages. One drawback of this approach is
that only continuous formulations can be used. Another class of optimization algorithms
however can handle disjoint functions and could be utilized for specic problems which
IPOPT cannot solve.
Furthermore dierent wake models could be added to broaden the scope of possible
applications. Currently Fuga wake model should be used for relatively at and uniform
terrain, since the surface roughness and wind eld do not vary horizontally. This in
practice limits its use to oshore wind farms. Therefore a dierent wake model could be
used for onshore sites.
