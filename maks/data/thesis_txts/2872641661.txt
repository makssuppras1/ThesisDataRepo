Abstract
Anxiety is a prevalent challenge for children in pediatric emergency departments (PEDs), where
unfamiliar clinical environments, treatment uncertainty, and pain anticipation create distress. Un­
addressed, this anxiety can exacerbate pain perception, complicate treatments, and form nega­
tive healthcare memories. This thesis investigates how technology can mitigate pediatric anxiety
through two key objectives: (1) evaluating child­friendly methods for anxiety assessment and (2)
developing and testing a child friendly interactive intervention to reduce distress during waiting
periods.
Focusing on children aged 4–7, we developed a digital prototype aimed at detecting and reducing
anxiety. The solution consists of few distraction activities that identifies anxiety in children using
a combination of direct and underlying emotional symptoms of anxiety. It integrates detection
with intervention by offering familiar distraction techniques—such as puzzles, storytelling, and
sorting games immediately after signs of anxiety are recognized. Following interaction, the proto­
type evaluates changes in emotional state to determine whether the child’s negative emotions have
shifted toward more positive ones, indicating reduced anxiety. The intervention features colorful
visuals, sound effects, and dynamic elements, all designed using FIGMA to maximize appeal and
comprehension for young children.
The development of our solution was guided by a review of existing literature on pediatric anx­
iety, current emotional assessment tools, and evidence based intervention strategies designed to
reduce distress in children. To ground our design in practical, real­world needs, we conducted
field research at Herlev Hospital in Denmark. This involved in­depth observations of the pediatric
environment, child–nurse interactions, existing anxiety management practices, and the structure of
appointment and waiting room systems. Additionally, we carried out staff interviews, shadowed
(“ghosted”) clinical staff, and examined the roles of hospital clowns and magicians. Insights were
also gathered through surveys completed by parents, providing a broader perspective on anxiety­
related experiences within the hospital setting. Our prototype incorporates child­friendly anxiety
detection techniques, including adapted versions of validated tools such as the Wong­Baker FACES
Pain Rating Scale and the Venham Picture Test. Preliminary testing with children indicated high
engagement and a promising reduction in anxiety levels following the intervention.
This study evaluates the effectiveness of distraction techniques in the the form of a gamified in­
tervention designed to improve emotional states in children (ages 4–7) in a clinical setting. Out
of 8 participants, 7 initially exhibited negative moods (e.g., sadness, anxiety, sickness), while 1
required no intervention (baseline positive mood). Post­engagement, 6 out of 7 users (85.7%)
demonstrated a full shift to positive emotions (e.g., happiness, excitement, calmness), with a cu­
mulative 18 positive emotional markers recorded. The study contributes a practical framework for
implementing child­centered anxiety interventions in clinical settings, emphasizing the value of
familiar, playful interactions to transform stressful waiting experiences. While preliminary testing
with a small group of children at the hospital indicated that the prototype was engaging and had a
positive effect on reducing anxiety levels, further refinement is required for broader application.
Terminology Clarification
Throughout this report, the terms: “Distraction techniques” (primary term), “Non­pharmacological
interventions”, “Interactive activities” and ”therapeutic interventions”, all refer to the same core
solution: child­focused, medication­free engagements (e.g., games, puzzles) designed to alleviate
anxiety by distraction.
Keywords: pediatric anxiety, interactive intervention, distraction techniques, anxiety detection,
anxiety assessment in PED, feeling sick as an anxiety, Negative emotions as an anxiety, positive
emotions as non­anxiety, Somatic expressions of anxiety in kids, Emotional and physical pain.

Introduction
Multiple studies demonstrate that pediatric medical anxiety is widespread, with 60­75% of children
experiencing significant distress during healthcare encounters, for instance, [1]. It is often observed
that children feel uncomfortable and anxious in pediatric departments. Visits to the doctor can be
stressful for them, as the unfamiliar hospital environment can trigger feelings of unease and fear.
This discomfort is further exacerbated by the fact that children are often not physically well when
they visit the hospital, making them more vulnerable to the stresses of an unfamiliar or uncomfort­
able environment. This combination of physical discomfort and environmental stress can lead to
both physical and psychological negative impacts on children. If not addressed promptly, it can
also create significant challenges for healthcare providers, such as nurses and doctors, as well as
for parents.
When a child is uncomfortable in their surroundings, it becomes difficult to provide effective treat­
ment. This can result in increased time and resources required to manage the situation, further
straining the healthcare system. Anxiety can lead to behavioral issues such as resistance to treat­
ment, crying, or even refusal to cooperate, thereby increasing the complexity of pediatric care.
Healthcare providers may be forced to resort to more invasive measures, such as the use of seda­
tives or physical restraint, both of which can have their own set of consequences, including side
effects, increased stress for the child, and longer recovery times. This can contribute to a cycle of
fear and distress, which affects not only the child but also the parents and healthcare professionals
involved [2].
Recognizing these challenges, At Herlev Hospital, significant efforts have been made to create a
child­friendly environment. The waiting areas are equipped with toys, books, games, and engag­
ing activities such as treasure hunts (e.g., finding frogs or butterflies). Child­friendly decorations
and a welcoming atmosphere aim to make the environment more comfortable for young patients.
However, due to high patient volumes and the priority of providing medical care, there may be
limited time to address children’s anxiety adequately. If anxiety is not managed promptly, it can
lead to negative experiences for the child, potentially impacting their psychology and future visits
to the hospital. This can also create stress for parents and healthcare staff, sometimes resulting
in the use of sedatives or physical restraints to manage the child’s behavior. Therefore, detecting
anxiety early and implementing strategies to reduce it is crucial for ensuring a positive experience
for the child, parents, and hospital staff.
Research has shown that children who are not distracted during their wait are more likely to ex­
perience heightened anxiety. Long waiting periods, often lasting 6–8 hours, can be particularly
challenging for both children and their parents, exacerbating feelings of anxiety. Children be­
tween the ages of 4­12, while some are capable of conveying their emotions verbally while some
of them are capable of expressing their discomfort through behaviors such as crying, kicking, or
fleeing, cannot often fully understand the situation or communicate their feelings in non­physical
ways. This can lead to emotional outbursts, making it even more difficult to manage their anxiety
effectively. Such behavior can also contribute to negative experiences for parents, who may feel
helpless in managing their child’s distress, leading to additional stress and emotional burden [3].
However, the waiting period in the Pediatric Emergency Department (PED) also presents an im­
portant opportunity to assess children’s emotional states and intervene when necessary. During
this time, their level of anxiety can be monitored through child­friendly, user­centered platforms—
both digital and non­digital—that support easy detection of distress. Distraction techniques such
as interactive games, puzzles, storytelling, etc., can help divert children’s attention and reduce anx­

1.1
Initial Problem Statement
A study by [4] demonstrated that “the digital game­playing approach applied before and during the
suture procedure was effective in reducing children’s pain, fear, and anxiety levels.” The authors
also emphasized the need for further research into distraction methods across different medical
procedures, highlighting the potential for digital interventions. Despite the growing focus on man­
aging anxiety in children, many existing tools rely on verbal self­reporting or adult observation,
which may not always accurately reflect how young children feel—especially in clinical settings
where communication can be limited. This gap suggests a clear opportunity to develop more in­
tuitive, child­friendly approaches for detecting anxiety. A digital tool that uses simple emotional
cues—such as visual emotion cards—could make it easier for children to express their feelings,
while also helping clinicians recognize anxiety early and respond more effectively. Therefore this
thesis will investigate the following problem statement:
”How can we better detect and reduce anxiety in children facing unfamiliar healthcare envi­
ronments, using engaging and child­centered approaches?”
To address this systematically, we employ the Double Diamond methodology, which guides our
process through four key phases.

Methodology
Figure 2.1: The Design Process illustrated using Double Diamond Model
For our master’s thesis, we followed the Double Diamond process to guide the research and devel­
opment of a prototype aimed at detecting and reducing children’s anxiety in pediatric emergency
departments. This structured approach helped us systematically explore the problem, define key
challenges, develop potential solutions, and validate our final prototype solution. Moreover, due
to double­diamond’s iterative advantage, we can test and adapt solutions, ensuring they remain
engaging for children while meeting clinical feasibility constraints.
Before designing a solution, we needed to fully understand the factors contributing to children’s
anxiety in pediatric emergency departments. We conducted extensive research to gain insight into
both the emotional and environmental aspects that cause distress in children.
The discovery phase is the first step in solving a problem using the double­diamond process. At
this stage, the focus is on understanding the problem before jumping to solutions. This is done
by: researching the context, identifying user needs, and understanding the challenges that need
to be addressed. This was done through Desktop research by reviewing existing literature and
case studies related to our topic. Conducting interviews with key stakeholders, by observing and
analyzing behaviors, interactions, and environmental factors relevant to the problem, survey forms
by collecting insights from parents and workshops to group discussions to brainstorm and gather
different perspectives. The goal of this phase is to gather as much information as possible before
defining the exact problem. The detailed work for this phase is presented in Section 03 and 04 of
this thesis.
In the define phase, we focus on refining the problem by clustering related data to identify key pat­
terns. Through data synthesis, these insights are analyzed to clarify underlying issues. A design
specification is then created, outlining a clear problem statement and key objectives. This process
establishes a structured foundation for developing and evaluating potential solutions. The detailed
work for this phase is presented in section 05 of this thesis, where research insights were syn­

prototype was both user­friendly and helpful in reducing children’s anxiety in a real­world pediatric
emergency setting. Full details of this phase are presented in Section 07 of this thesis.

Background
This section provides a comprehensive overview of the key concepts and research relevant to this
study. We will begin by introducing Herlev Hospital, the site where the fieldwork and testing for
this research will be conducted. Furthermore, we will review previous studies related to pediatric
anxiety that have been carried out at this institution, laying the groundwork for understanding the
relevant context.
As part of this, it’s important for us to gain a clear understanding of what the hospital setting is like
at Herlev Hospital, and more generally, how hospitals function in Denmark. Since we’re not fully
familiar with the local healthcare system like how appointments are scheduled or how treatments
are carried out, it’s important for us to understand this before diving deeper. It’s also very important
to understand anxiety in kids, what causes it , what signs to look for (both physical and emotional),
and what solutions already exist to help them. By learning this, we can make sure our approach is
based on real evidence and actually works for children, making it more helpful and practical. At
the same time, we’ll be looking into anxiety in children, with a special focus on how it’s managed
in the pediatric department. This context will give us the foundation we need for the rest of the
project.
Following this introduction, we will define anxiety in children, outlining its potential causes and
symptoms. This discussion will also include an exploration of traditional methods used to assess
anxiety.
By establishing this foundation, this section seeks to equip the reader with a clear understanding
of the research context as well as the existing body of knowledge regarding pediatric anxiety. This
understanding will be instrumental in framing the objectives of the study and guiding subsequent
research inquiries. While some of the research solutions presented such as those proposed by fellow
students from DTU are not directly related to our proposed intervention, they have been included
to provide broader insight into how others have approached the same challenge. Collectively, these
perspectives help frame the objectives of the study and guide subsequent research inquiries.

3.1.1
Herlev Hospital’s Role in Pediatric Anxiety Research
Herlev Hospital has been actively involved in research focused on pediatric anxiety, particularly
in the context of children awaiting medical treatment. Previous studies conducted at the hospital
have explored a range of child­friendly interventions designed to reduce anxiety and improve the
hospital experience. These include the use of interactive play areas, medical clowns, and other
psychological strategies tailored to children’s needs. The hospital’s collaborations with institu­
tions like the University of Copenhagen, the University of Southern Denmark, and the Technical
University of Denmark (DTU) have further strengthened its role as a research hub for pediatric
anxiety and related studies [6].
Herlev Hospital’s Department of Children and Youth has been catered to cover the needs of children
and their families, being known for their predisposition to include parents in the child’s hospital stay
and procedures, constantly seeking feedback and collaboration.The Department of Children and
Youth has 5 main sections: 1 treatment for newborns, 1 center for social pediatrics, 2 clinics, and
the PED[7]. The PED comprises the Children and Youth Reception, “Børne og Ungemodtagelsen”
which takes care of children aged 0­2 years who are referred to the children and adolescent clinic
and children over 2 years and adolescents are referred to the Emergency Department with any ill­
ness (i.e. concussions, appendicitis, stomach pain, cystitis, and others), excluding any injuries or
lacerations to arms or legs [8].By maintaining a structured yet adaptable framework, the depart­
ment ensures that each child receives tailored care while minimizing distress. These efforts reflect
the hospital’s broader mission to merge clinical excellence with compassionate, anxiety­sensitive
pediatric care.

3.1.2
What is Anxiety?
Anxiety is a feeling of nervousness, unease, or worry that usually occurs even when there is no
immediate danger. It differs from fear, which is the body’s natural response to immediate danger.
Anxiety is part of the body’s natural reaction to stress, so it can be helpful at times, making you
more alert and ready for action.
Anxiety disorders and normal feelings of anxiousness are two different things. Many of us get
anxious when faced with particular situations we find stressful, but if those feelings don’t subside,
the anxiety could be more chronic. When feelings of fear or nervousness become excessive or
difficult to control or interfere with daily life, an anxiety disorder may be present.
Signs and Symptoms of Anxiety
Your heart beats fast, and your breathing speeds up. Your chest may feel tight, and you might start
to sweat. If you’ve ever felt it, you know that anxiety is just as much a physical state as a mental
state. That’s because there’s a very strong biological chain reaction that occurs when we encounter
a stressful event or begin to worry about potential stressors or dangers in the future. Other physical
symptoms include headaches and insomnia. Psychological symptoms may include feeling restless
or tense, having a feeling of dread, or experiencing ruminative or obsessive thoughts [9].
3.1.3
Children’s anxiety in the PED
In the pediatric department, it is common for children to experience fear and anxiety for various
reasons. However, when the situation escalates, it becomes a concern. The causes of anxiety in
children in the pediatric setting can include factors such as an unfamiliar environment, fear of being
separated from their parents, feeling a lack of control, pain, discomfort, long waiting time, parents’
anxiety, medical equipment and procedures, and past negative experiences [10]. Figure 3.3 depicts

Anxiety in children can often be detected through common symptoms such as crying, refusal to

to adults, as well as the child’s limited ability to communicate effectively and their insufficient
understanding of medical procedures.
3.1.4
Anxiety and Pain
The paper [11] explains that when kids have chronic pain, they can start feeling anxious or scared
about the pain coming back. This anxiety makes the pain feel even worse, and the more they worry,
the more intense the pain becomes.
For example, if a child has a painful experience, they might start fearing that the pain will happen
again in the future. This fear causes anxiety, which makes the pain feel stronger, even if the pain
isn’t as bad as before. So, pain and anxiety can feed off each other, creating a cycle that’s harder
to break.
The paper also talks about how kids might exaggerate the pain in their minds, which makes them
feel more anxious and helpless. The combination of pain and anxiety can make the situation even
more difficult for both the child and their caregivers.

3.3
Assesing Anxiety
Several studies have been conducted to explore different methods for assessing anxiety in children.
These methods include both biometric tools and behavioral/psychological assessment tools, each
designed to measure anxiety in specific contexts. In this section, we will focus on behavioral and
psychological assessment tools, as this is our area of interest.
3.3.1
Behavioral and Psychological Assessment Tools
Behavioral and psychological assessment tools rely on observations, self­reports, or caregiver re­
ports to measure anxiety. These tools are widely used in clinical and research settings and are
especially effective for assessing anxiety in children, who may not always be able to articulate
their feelings verbally.
Observational Tools
These tools assess anxiety by observing and documenting a child’s behavior in real­life situations.
Modified Yale Preoperative Anxiety Scale (m­YPAS):
The Modified Yale Preoperative Anxiety Scale (m­YPAS) is a widely used behavioral observation
tool designed to assess anxiety in children, particularly in medical or preoperative settings. It is an
adaptation of the original Yale Preoperative Anxiety Scale (YPAS) and is specifically tailored to
measure anxiety in children aged 2–12 years. The m­YPAS is highly regarded for its reliability and
validity in clinical and research contexts [14]. The m­YPAS is highly regarded for its reliability,
validity, and ease of use, making it a preferred tool for both clinicians and researchers.
How the m­YPAS Works
The m­YPAS assesses anxiety by observing and scoring a child’s behavior across five categories:
• Activity: Measures the child’s level of physical movement (e.g., calm vs. restless).
• Vocalizations: Evaluates the child’s verbal expressions (e.g., talking, crying, screaming).
• Emotional Expressivity: Assesses the child’s facial expressions and emotional state (e.g.,
smiling, frowning, crying).
• State of Arousal: Measures the child’s level of alertness or agitation (e.g., calm vs. hyper­
alert).
• Interaction with Parents:Evaluates the child’s behavior when interacting with their parents
(e.g., clinging, seeking comfort). Each category is scored on a scale from 1­4 (or 1­6 for
vocalization), with higher scores indicating higher levels of anxiety. The scores from all
categories are combined to provide a total anxiety score [14].
Observational Scale of Behavioral Distress (OSBD)
The Observational Scale of Behavioral Distress (OSBD) is a behavioral observation tool designed
to measure distress in children during medical procedures. Developed by Elliott et al., the OSBD
provides a standardized method for quantifying anxiety and discomfort in pediatric patients. It is
widely used in clinical and research settings due to its reliability, validity, and ease of use [15].
Methodology:
The OSBD involves continuous observation and recording of specific distress behaviors exhib­
ited by children during medical procedures. These behaviors include crying, screaming, verbal
resistance, physical resistance, and seeking comfort.
• Interval Recording: The distress behaviors are recorded in 15­second intervals, allowing
for precise measurement of the frequency and duration of each behavior.
• Severity Weights: Each distress behavior is assigned a severity weight, which helps in
quantifying the level of distress. For example, behaviors like screaming might be weighted
more heavily than mild verbal resistance.

The State­Trait Anxiety Inventory (STAI­C):
The State­Trait Anxiety Inventory (STAI) is a psychological inventory used to measure both trait
anxiety (a person’s general tendency to experience anxiety) and state anxiety (anxiety experienced

for diagnosis and differentiation from depressive syndromes [18]. STAI has been used in several

• State Anxiety Scale: Measures how a person feels at the moment (e.g., ”I feel nervous”).
• Trait Anxiety Scale: Measures how a person generally feels (e.g., ”I am a steady person”).

3.4
Reducing Anxiety
In the previous section, we explored the assessment tools used to measure anxiety. In this section,
we will discuss strategies to reduce anxiety in children, based on existing studies. These strategies
are divided into different categories as distraction techniques, parental involvement, environmental
modifications, non­pharmacological interventions, and play interventions. Each of these strategies
has been supported by specific research, as outlined below.
Distraction Techniques
Distraction techniques are effective in diverting children’s attention away from medical procedures,
thereby reducing anxiety. Heilbrunn et al.’s [22] comparative trial demonstrated that interactive
toys, games, and digital devices significantly lower anxiety levels in children. Similarly, Bid­
diss et al.’s [23]systematic review highlighted the effectiveness of interactive activities and visual
distractions in healthcare waiting spaces. These tools help create a more positive and engaging
environment for children, making medical procedures less intimidating [22].

Parental Involvement
Parental involvement plays a crucial role in calming children during stressful situations. Heilbrunn
et al. found that parents who engage in comforting behaviors, such as holding their child’s hand
or speaking reassuringly, help reduce their child’s anxiety. Segers et al.’s scoping review also
emphasized the importance of involving parents in the care process, as their presence provides
emotional support and helps children feel more secure. However, it is important to note that parents
themselves often experience heightened anxiety when their child is sick or undergoing medical
procedures. A study by Embong et al. used the State­Trait Anxiety Inventory to measure anxiety
levels among parents of sick children attending the emergency department. Their findings highlight
the need to address parental anxiety as well, as it can indirectly impact the child’s emotional state.
To support both children and their parents, healthcare providers can encourage parents to stay close
during procedures, guide how to comfort their children, and involve them in decision­making when
appropriate. Offering parents a brief orientation on how they can assist during their child’s visit,
such as using calming words or participating in distraction techniques, can also be beneficial. Addi­
tionally, providing resources or support to help parents manage their anxiety is crucial. Emerging
trends, such as parental coaching programs, where parents are trained in anxiety­reducing tech­
niques, are showing promise in improving outcomes for both children and their caregivers [19]
[24].
Environmental Modifications
Creating a child­friendly environment in the PED can significantly reduce anxiety. Heilbrunn
et al. and Biddiss et al. both highlighted the benefits of environmental modifications, such as
colorful decorations, play areas, and comfortable seating. These changes make the PED feel less
intimidating and more welcoming, helping children feel at ease during their visit [23] [22].
Non­Pharmacological Interventions
Non­pharmacological interventions, such as guided imagery and breathing exercises, have been
shown to reduce pain and anxiety in pediatric patients. Fein et al.’s research emphasized the effec­
tiveness of these techniques, particularly in emergency medical settings. Additionally, Segers et
al.’s scoping review identified child­centered approaches, such as clear communication and age­
appropriate explanations, as key to supporting children’s preferences during stressful medical pro­
cedures [25] [22].
Play Interventions
Play interventions, including therapeutic play and structured play activities, are effective in re­
ducing anxiety and negative emotions in hospitalized children. Li et al.’s study demonstrated that
play interventions not only distract children but also provide a sense of normalcy and comfort in
an otherwise stressful environment. These activities can be easily integrated into the PED to help
children cope with anxiety [26].
For instance, as shown in 3.11, preparation play uses tools like medical dolls and procedural books
to demystify treatments, empowering children by providing them with control and clarity. Medical
play, which involves real or toy equipment, allows children to express their fears creatively through
art or role­playing, while distraction play employs games and sensory toys to redirect focus away
from procedures. Developmental play, tailored to age and ability, ensures ongoing growth and pre­
vents regression. Together, these interventions address emotional, cognitive, and developmental
needs, making them a versatile and essential component of pediatric care.

Appointment System at Herlev Hospital
Registration Process
When children arrive at the emergency department, they are typically referred by their general
practitioner (GP) or another hospital if they fall within the area of Herlev Hospital or through acute
hotline number 1813. The department primarily handles acute cases where children are brought
in due to illnesses such as stomach pain, urinary tract infections, or complications following a
cold. These children often do not have a prior history of hospital visits but require immediate
medical attention for conditions like appendicitis or severe pain. For children aged 0–2 years, the
department also treats minor injuries, such as burns or scratches, which are common in this age
group.
Triage and Initial Assessment
Within the first 10 minutes of arrival, children are taken to the triage room for an initial assessment.
The triage nurse conducts a series of observational measurements, including heart rate, oxygen
saturation, respiration frequency, and blood pressure. Pain levels are assessed using the two Visual
Analog Scale (VAS) Pain Scale and Wong­baker Face pain scale for younger children who may

Evaluation of Pediatric Emotional States and Anxiety Levels in Clinical Settings
Currently, the hospital does not formally document a child’s emotional state or anxiety levels during

comfort level. Pain levels are recorded based on the child’s self­reporting. The child’s self­reported

estimate it as a 3 or 4. Both perspectives are important, as the child’s rating reflects their personal
experience, while the nurse’s assessment is based on clinical observation.
Gitte’s ongoing study aims to separate pain and fear by asking children, parents, and nurses to rate
both pain and fear levels independently. This approach seeks to provide a more comprehensive
understanding of the child’s emotional state and improve the accuracy of assessments.

The hospital employs various strategies to manage pain and anxiety in children. However, if not

4.4
Key Insights from the Research
Through our research, we have identified that interactive interventions—such as toys, books, parental
interaction, hospital clowns, and magicians—play a significant role in reducing anxiety among
children in the pediatric emergency department. These interventions help create a more engaging
and comforting environment, diverting children’s attention from the stress of medical procedures.
However, the VAS and other Scales, currently used to assess pain and anxiety, requires modifica­
tions to more accurately detect and measure anxiety levels in children.
The hospital environment, particularly the waiting area, emerges as a critical space for addressing
anxiety. With waiting times often extending to 6­8 hours, the waiting area becomes a key location
for implementing interventions. Providing informational knowledge, engaging activities, and dis­
traction tools (e.g., toys, games) in this space can significantly improve the overall experience for
children and their families. The waiting area, therefore, is not only a place to reduce anxiety but
also an ideal setting for detecting and addressing it early.
These findings suggest that focusing on interactive interventions, environmental modifications,
and improved anxiety assessment tools in the waiting area can effectively address pediatric anxiety.
We also observed that addressing anxiety in the pediatric emergency department is not only about
immediate comfort but also about long­term well­being. Negative experiences at an early age
can contribute to medical phobias and increased stress in future healthcare visits. Implementing
effective interventions early could foster a more positive perception of medical care for children as
they grow. This direction will guide the next phase of our work, where we will define the problem
more precisely and develop targeted solutions to enhance the experience for children, parents, and
healthcare providers.
4.5
Challenges and Future Directions for Improvement
Standardization of Anxiety Assessment in Pediatric Care: One of the main challenges is the
lack of a standardized system for documenting and addressing anxiety in children. While pain
levels are routinely assessed, fear and anxiety are not formally recorded, which can lead to in­
consistencies in care. Gitte’s research aims to address this gap by developing a more nuanced
approach to assessing and managing both pain and anxiety. However, developing a standard­
ized anxiety measurement system requires extensive clinical validation, integration with existing
healthcare workflows, and collaboration with medical professionals, which lies beyond the imme­
diate objectives of this study.
Integration of Digital Tools for Real­Time Emotional Monitoring: The integration of digital
tools, such as smart wearables, could provide real­time data on a child’s emotional state, but this
would require a shift in how healthcare providers interpret and respond to such data. Education
and training will be essential to ensure that nurses and doctors can effectively use these tools to
enhance patient care. While the integration of digital tools for real­time emotional monitoring holds
significant promise for enhancing pediatric care, implementing such technology presents practical
challenges. Real­time monitoring would require healthcare professionals to allocate additional
time and attention to interpret and respond to continuous data streams, potentially increasing their
workload in already demanding clinical environments.
Availability of Human­Driven Interventions: While hospital clowns, magicians, and other live
entertainment have proven to be effective in reducing anxiety, their availability is inconsistent due
to scheduling constraints, staffing limitations, and budgetary concerns. This unpredictability limits
the long­term sustainability of such interventions, making it necessary to explore supplementary

7.6
Complete Prototype Testing
After evaluating the core functionality and user interface of individual activities or games, we pro­
ceeded to test the complete prototype to assess its effectiveness in detecting and reducing anxiety
in children. The prototype is designed to track mood changes by prompting users to indicate how
they feel before and after engaging in the activities. Its effectiveness in identifying and alleviating
anxiety depends on comparing the user’s initial mood selection with their response post­activity.
For instance, if a child begins by selecting ”sad” and later reports feeling ”happy” or expresses a
desire to continue playing, this would indicate a positive effect. If the mood remains unchanged—
such as starting and ending with ”happy”—it is considered a neutral response. However, if a child
reports the same negative mood (e.g., ”sad”) after completing the activities, the prototype would
be deemed ineffective in that instance. This approach helps measure the prototype’s ability to
influence emotional states.

For this iteration of testing, we employed a naturalistic observational approach to evaluate the
prototype’s usability and effectiveness with children in a hospital setting. Our procedure began
with a brief introduction where we explained our role as researchers and presented the prototype
as an activity they could choose to try. After obtaining each child’s agreement to participate, we
observed their organic interactions with the system while carefully documenting several key data

maintained a non­interventional stance, allowing children to explore the prototype naturally with­

and expressing our appreciation for their participation. This methodology prioritized ecological
validity by preserving the authentic hospital environment and respecting children’s natural behav­

tative observations of engagement patterns. The approach balanced rigorous data collection with
ethical considerations for pediatric research, minimizing pressure on participants while gathering
meaningful insights about the prototype’s real­world application.

The initial testing of our prototype evaluation demonstrated strong effectiveness in engaging chil­

games, maintaining focus and frequently requesting to continue playing. Most significantly, every
child exhibited measurable mood improvements after completing the activities, with clear transi­

primarily for language­related comprehension. The children’s persistent desire to keep playing ­

interaction mechanics such as the drag functionality in the Color Sorting game with larger touch
targets and clearer visual feedback. These targeted improvements aim to preserve the prototype’s
demonstrated effectiveness while making it more intuitive and accessible for independent use by
children in clinical settings.

Discussion
This chapter evaluates the findings of the thesis in relation to the research and sub­questions:
”How can familiar distraction techniques and a playful, child­centered mechanism enhance
the detection and reduction of anxiety in children in the pediatric emergency waiting area?”
as this was broken down into two sub­questions:
a)How can a playful and accessible mechanism help children recognize and communicate

b)How effective are familiar distraction techniques in managing pediatric anxiety in children

Also, this chapter elaborates on key insights, limitations, and areas for further development, and
concludes with broader reflections for future work.
8.1
Was the problem statement answered?
How can a playful and accessible mechanism help children recognize and communicate their
anxiety level?
The prototype successfully incorporated a child­friendly anxiety detection tool inspired by the
Wong­Baker FACES Pain Rating Scale and Venham Picture Test. By using visual emotion cards,
children could intuitively express their feelings without relying solely on verbal communication.
Testing showed that children as young as 4 could engage with this system, though some required
parental assistance due to language barriers. The iterative design process refined the emotion se­
lection interface, making it more intuitive with clearer navigation and feedback.
How effective are familiar distraction techniques in managing pediatric anxiety in children
during hospital visits?
The distraction techniques (e.g., ”Find the Difference,” ”Spot Me,” ”Puzzle Pop,” and ”Color Sort­
ing”) demonstrated high engagement and effectiveness in reducing anxiety. Although the interac­
tive activity Bravebuddy was not frequently chosen by children during the testing iterations—and
initial engagement was low even when it was selected—children responded positively to its famil­
iar setting. They were able to recognize the hospital environment depicted in the story, understood
its purpose, and found comfort in the familiar visuals. Observations revealed that interactive games
held children’s attention and facilitated emotional shifts from negative to positive states. For exam­
ple, one child transitioned from ”sad” to ”happy” after playing ”Find the Difference.” The inclusion
of rewards, audio cues, and adaptive difficulty levels further enhanced engagement.

8.1.2
Limitations
1.Testing Size and Diversity:
Testing involved a small group of children (primarily ages 4–7), limiting generalizability. Future
work should include a larger, more diverse cohort to validate effectiveness across different ages
and cultural backgrounds.
2.Language and Cultural Barriers:
Some children required parental translation, highlighting the need for multilingual support or non­
verbal interfaces (e.g., icons, sounds).
3.Technical Constraints:
Prototype testing was conducted on touchscreen laptop instead of tablets (due to availability),
which may have affected interaction fluidity. Future iterations should optimize for mobile devices
commonly used in hospitals.
4.Long­Term Impact:
The study measured immediate emotional shifts but did not assess long­term effects on anxiety
reduction or future hospital visits. Longitudinal studies could explore sustained benefits.
5.Narrow Focus on Digital Distraction Modalities
This study successfully demonstrated the efficacy of digital distraction techniques as a child­
friendly approach to anxiety reduction in pediatric emergency departments. While our focused
exploration of game­based interventions yielded promising results ­ particularly with visual puzzles
and sorting activities ­ we recognize this represents just one pathway within a broader landscape
of pediatric anxiety management.
The deliberate choice to specialize in digital distraction games allowed for depth in prototyping
and testing, but inherently limited comparative analysis with other modalities such as:
Pharmacological approaches (e.g., sedatives mentioned by Nurse Gitte) Physical play therapy
(like Herlev’s frog hunt activity).Immersive technologies (VR/AR systems referenced in prior
work).Child life specialist techniques observed during hospital visits.This specialization means
our conclusions about effectiveness are necessarily contextual rather than comprehensive. The
prototype shows strong potential as a solution, but cannot claim to be the optimal solution across
all clinical scenarios.
6.Barriers to Testing With High­Anxiety Kids
A key limitation of our testing was the exclusion of children experiencing severe distress. Dur­
ing prototype evaluation at Herlev Hospital, we observed that those displaying the most acute
anxiety—who might have benefited most from intervention—were often unable to participate.
This occurred due to parental hesitancy to enroll visibly distressed children in testing.
7.Emotional cards display
During testing, we observed that many children selected from the initially shown emotions without
exploring other options, while some did use the navigation buttons to view all choices. This sug­
gests that displaying all emotion options simultaneously (as with physical cards) might encourage
more thorough consideration.

Conclusion
The study successfully demonstrated that child­centered digital interventions can effectively detect
and reduce anxiety in pediatric emergency departments. In our pilot testing with 8 participants, 7
initially exhibited negative moods (e.g., sadness, anxiety, sickness), while 1 required no interven­
tion (baseline positive mood). Post­engagement, 6 out of 7 users (85.7%) demonstrated a full shift
to positive emotions (e.g., happiness, excitement, calmness), with a cumulative 18 positive emo­
tional markers recorded. These findings validate that combining playful anxiety assessment with
engaging distraction techniques, the prototype offers a scalable, non­invasive solution to improve
children’s hospital experiences. While limitations exist, the findings provide a strong foundation
for future refinements and broader implementation. Addressing these gaps will further solidify the
prototype’s role in transforming pediatric care through technology.
The practical design of the solution allows for easy integration into existing pediatric workflows,
especially during stressful waiting periods. With enhancements such as multilingual support and
expanded emotional recognition, the prototype could serve a broader population and contribute
meaningfully to child­friendly care in diverse healthcare settings.
Furthermore, this research reinforces the importance of early detection of anxiety and intervention
through familiar, comforting mechanisms in unfamiliar environments. The use of emotion cards,
storytelling, cognitive games, and interactive play reflects a shift toward empathetic, child­centered
care models. By allowing children to recognize and express their emotional states in a non­verbal
and playful way, the solution supports both their emotional autonomy and clinical understanding
of their mental state.
Beyond waiting areas, this research’s principles can be effectively extended to private patient
rooms, where children often experience heightened anxiety due to isolation and anticipation of
procedures. Private rooms present a unique opportunity to integrate the solution more seamlessly
with clinical workflows while offering targeted support. The same anxiety detection and reduc­
tion techniques could be deployed on bedside tablets or interactive screens, creating a personalized
calming experience in a controlled setting.
Ultimately, the study contributes to a growing body of evidence that play is not merely a distrac­
tion in medical environments but a therapeutic necessity. By reframing anxiety management as
an interactive, child­led process, the prototype challenges traditional top­down approaches to pe­
diatric care. As healthcare systems increasingly prioritize patient­centered design, solutions like
this demonstrate how technology can be harnessed to create compassionate, effective, and scalable
interventions for our youngest patients.

Survey Form
The survey targeted parents of children aged 4 to 12 years old. Its purpose was to understand
how children feel in a hospital environment before, during, and after their visits. Additionally, it
aimed to gather parents’ perspectives on what causes anxiety in their children during these visits
and which strategies or mechanisms might help reduce that anxiety.
Among the respondents, 54.5% were parents of female children, while 45.5% were parents of male
children. Before hospital visits, 72.7% of parents reported their children feeling slightly anxious,
and 27.3% reported their children feeling not anxious.
Several causes of anxiety were identified, including medical equipment, doctors, nurses, strangers,
the hospital environment, anticipation, injections, and lack of eye contact. However, the most
highlighted cause was the waiting time.
When asked about their child’s behavior while waiting in the clinic, 54.5% of parents described
their children as restless, 18.2% as irritable, and 27.3% as calm. Regarding factors that seemed to
increase anxiety during waiting, the majority (25%) pointed to long waiting times.

children interacted with their parents, while 45.5% mentioned distractions as a coping method.
Regarding activities or distractions that could help children feel more comfortable while waiting,

Key observations: The activities successfully engaged children, holding their attention and en­
couraging interaction. Kids who could read and understand English navigated the games inde­
pendently without requiring assistance from facilitators or parents. However, additional time and
parental support could further enhance the experience, as some children expressed interest in con­
tinuing to play but were limited by time constraints.
While puzzles and other games captured their interest effectively, BraveBuddy did not engage
children as strongly though they found its familiar hospital infrastructure interesting. The Hero
Badge element was particularly well­received, making them happy and reinforcing the activity’s
core message. Despite varying engagement levels with BraveBuddy, children still grasped its in­
tended meaning, demonstrating that the concept resonated even if the execution could be further
optimized for higher interactivity.
