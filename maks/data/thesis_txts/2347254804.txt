Abstract 
This report contains investigations of the potentials of using 2-pipe low 
temperature heating and high temperature cooling systems in Danish office 
buildings. The temperatures in the 2-pipe system differ from the temperatures in 
the conventional 4-pipe systems since the 2-pipe system works with a temperature 
offset of only ± 2-4 ℃, where the conventional 4-pipe system has an offset of ± 10 
℃. The investigations are conducted with simulations for three main scenarios: 1) 
a 2008 reference building with a conventional 4-pipe solution, 2) a 2020 building 
with a conventional 4-pipe solution and 3) a 2020 building with a 2-pipe solution. 
Furthermore, this report contains investigations of the possibilities of making 
building management systems (BMS) redundant; two investigations were made, 
simulation 4 and 5.  
The investigations are conducted in an office building based on the INTEND building 
with indoor climate class A and analysed on an annual basis in the building 
simulation tool IDA ICE.  The office building has a North-South orientation and 
consists of 75 % offices and 25 % meeting rooms.   
The results show that the thermal indoor comfort is adequate in terms of the 
Danish Standard 474 which states a maximum of 100 hours above 26 ℃ and 25 
hours above 27 ℃. Additionally, the studies show a small energy saving going from 
the 2020 4-pipe solution to the 2020 2-pipe solution with BMS in both models. 
The results from the investigation without BMS showed a higher energy 
consumption due to the amount of heating required with a setpoint at 22.4 ℃ for 
heating.  
Sensitivity analyses are executed with different materials as acoustical ceiling and 
a lower heat transfer coefficient. The material investigation showed only a small 
difference when changing the material from gypsum to aluminium. The heat 
transfer coefficient investigation showed higher temperatures in the building, but 
a lower energy consumption. This leads to a final recommendation of a thorough 
investigating the heat transfer coefficient in IDA ICE.

1. Introduction 
1.1. Background 
The European Union’s energy roadmap for 2050 has the goal of reducing the 1990 
emission of greenhouse gas by 80-95 % in 2050 [1]. The energy today is based on 
fossil fuels and is not considering the resources and the climate changes. In 
accordance to the energy roadmap, renewable and sustainable resources should 
cover about two thirds of the total energy consumption in the EU in order to 
achieve a sustainable future and to achieve the goal of reducing greenhouse gasses. 
The building sector is responsible for 40 % of the total energy consumption [2], [3]. 
With this massive part of the energy consumption, the building sector has a larger 
incentive to reduce the energy and implement renewable and sustainable energy 
resources.  
Renewable and sustainable energy sources for heating and cooling of buildings in 
the future energy system are sources with a temperature close to room 
temperature. They have Low Temperatures for Heating and High Temperatures for 
Cooling (LTH-HTC). These sources provide energy with very limited convertibility 
potential, i.e. they are of low value or have low exergy content [4]. 
As the energy systems transition from fossil fuel sources to renewable sources, low-
valued energy becomes more abundant [5]. Consequently, it is strategically prudent 
to develop room heating and cooling solutions that can better exploit low-valued 
energy sources if a sustainable society is to be realized. Recent research efforts 
include the Rehva Guidebook No. 7 [6], Annex 37 [7] and Annex 49 [8] under the 
IEA ECBCS program where the applicability of new heating and cooling technologies 
for low-valued energy were investigated.  
Of these, hydronic systems that rely on pipes embedded in concrete slabs show the 
greatest potential for low-valued energy usage (from renewable sources) [9] 
because the large surfaces are capable of transferring sufficient heat even at small 
temperature differences between fluid and room air. In terms of thermal comfort, 
LTH-HTC systems outperform more orthodox solutions [7], [10]–[13].

1.2. Literature review 
1.2.1. Hydronic radiant ceiling systems 
LTH-HTC has changed the view on how to heat and cool a building. The most 
efficient way of heating a building is by floor heating due to the convection and it is 
more comfortable to walk on a warm floor than a cold floor.  Floor heating is very 
common in private houses, but because of the high internal heat gain from 
equipment and the number of people is rarely seen in office buildings. Cooling 
systems are most effective as a part of the ceiling since the cooling falls downwards 
and will thus mix the air.  
Having both floor heating and ceiling cooling is a very complex and expensive 
solution. Combining the heating and cooling system into one system makes the 
radiant ceiling systems interesting. Radiant ceiling systems can provide both 
heating and cooling to a building in order to maintain a good thermal environment 
and if there is ventilation in the room it is possible to create high comfort. In the 
following sections three different hydronic radiant ceiling systems (Figure 1-Figure 
3) will be described in order to get an understanding of how the systems function.

Suspended ceiling panels 
The suspended ceiling panel system traditionally uses pipes of copper or PEX placed 
on a metal plate with insulation above to direct the energy downwards. This system 
is primarily used in large areas, like factories, open plan offices or storage halls. The 
system has a high reaction time and is used in some buildings as a supplement to 
the concrete core system. Often the suspended ceiling panels are used with cooling 
alone [6], but it is also possible to have heating separately or heating and cooling 
combined.

Figure 4: Suspended ceiling panel. 
Concrete core system 
The concrete core system is often known as the Thermally Active Building Systems 
(TABS), which is, as the name describes, a system that activates the building. More 
specifically it actively uses the thermal mass through embedded pipes. The benefit 
of TABS in a building is a large capacity of thermal storage which reduces 
fluctuations in the building. This also means that TABS has a long reaction time, 
because of the thermal mass which in some perspectives is a disadvantage, e.g. 
meeting rooms. The cooling capacity is relatively low with TABS due to heat storage. 
A suspended ceiling is not recommended with TABS due to a decrease in energy  
transfer [6]. 
 
Figure 5: Visualization of a concrete slab with embedded pipes. 
TABS can be constructed in a variety of ways, because the pipes can be placed in 
the top, in the middle or in the bottom of the construction. The placement in the 
middle is mainly used in multi-storey buildings as it is then possible to provide 
energy to both the room above and below the pipes.  
Capillary tube ceiling 
The capillary tube ceiling is known by having pipes placed at the bottom of the 
construction. The capillary tube ceiling includes hydronic tubes placed in the screed 
at the ceiling. Unlike TABS, which has a long reaction time, the capillary tube ceiling

has, as the suspended ceiling panel, a short reaction time since the pipes are not 
embedded in the concrete, but is placed closer to the room.

1.2.2. Ventilation 
The purpose of ventilation is to create an acceptable indoor comfort and in order 
to do that the ventilation system needs to provide fresh air and remove heat and 
pollution produced by occupants in the rooms. With the indoor comfort is meant 
both the thermal environment and the indoor air quality which are important 
factors in a building. Currently, as a part of using less energy, the buildings are more 
air tight and the ventilation is reduced [14]. This has caused the so called “sick 
building syndrome” where the indoor air quality as well as the comfort is poor. To 
get around this “sick building syndrome” a focus on the indoor comfort as a whole 
needs to be considered.  
There are various types of ventilation methods introduced in the industry, the 
cheapest way of ventilating a room is by natural ventilation. With natural 
ventilation there is no control of the thermal environment, which during the 
summer can create too many warm hours inside, which can reduce the productivity 
of the employees. The most common mechanical types are mixing ventilation and 
displacement ventilation. An additional ventilation type, diffuse ventilation, is 
becoming more widespread. In this report, only mixing ventilation and diffuse 
ceiling ventilation will be described.  
Mixing ventilation 
Mixing ventilation aim to mix room air and supply air, as the term itself describes. 
The air will be supplied through a diffuser placed on the ceiling where the forces 
from the diffuser and buoyancy due to temperature differences will create the 
mixing of air and hereby create mixing ventilation [15]. 
With mixing ventilation, the placing of the diffusers is of high importance and the 
diffusers needs to be placed strategically in the room so the air distribution will be 
equal. This will also minimize the draught risk due to better mixing of the air. The 
ventilation should be constructed so the air velocity can be around 0.15 m/s in 
order to minimize the draught [16]. 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
5 
Diffuse ceiling ventilation 
The diffuse ventilation is characterized by supplying air through a relatively large 
surface with no fixed direction, thus the term diffuse. Typically, diffuse ventilation 
is supplied above the suspended ceiling where the air goes through a porous ceiling 
to supply air to the room. A great advantage of the diffuse ceiling ventilation is that 
draught risk is limited due to the system itself [17]. Furthermore, there is an energy 
saving due to a lower pressure loss in the system and a smaller fan power.  
1.2.3. Combining radiant ceiling with ventilation 
While embedded systems have significant advantages their applicability is (almost) 
limited to new concrete buildings. They are not flexible, the embedment impedes 
the heat transfer [18] and they require special acoustic solutions [19]–[21]. The new 
integrated heating, cooling, ventilating and acoustic (HCV+A) solutions (Figure 7) 
suspended from the upper slab are flexible, as they can be removed and replaced. 
Furthermore, their thermal performance is on par with embedded systems. This 
expands the potential of using radiant ceilings with very little temperature 
difference and introduces the advantage of self-regulating from embedded systems 
into suspended systems. In houses with low heating demand the self-regulating 
effect is up to 90% [22]. 
 
Figure 7: New heating, cooling, ventilation and acoustics solutions (HCV+A) integrates the self-
regulatory advantages of embedded systems with the flexibility of radiant ceiling panels and 
combines it with ventilation and acoustics to provide a holistic solution suitable for renewable 
energy sources. 
Combining ventilation with the radiant ceiling system increase the convective 
heating and cooling power from the ceiling; hereby the heat exchange will increase. 
In an experimental study from Onsberg & Eriksen [23] they first combine the 
capillary tube system with the suspended ceiling panel system; which means that 
the capillary tubes are placed just above a suspended ceiling. The suspended ceiling 
in the project is a high conductive gypsum plate. In Figure 8 below the mounting 
and connection of the capillary tube is shown.  
==PAGE:19==
6 
 
Figure 8: Mounting and connection of capillary ceilings. 
The study showed a great potential of combining ventilation with radiant ceiling 
system. The thesis states an increase of the heat exchange coefficient with mixing 
ventilation up to 9 % and with diffuse ventilation up to 84 % (Figure 9). Beside the 
large increase of the heat exchange coefficient in the diffuse case, the diffuse 
ventilation has other positive effects; higher inlet temperature is needed due to the 
cooling of the air through the ceiling which reduces the energy spend for cooling. A 
lower pressure loss and a lower amount of noise from the inlets are also some of 
the benefits with the diffuse ventilation.  
 
Figure 9: Overview of heat exchange and heat exchange to coefficient results [23]. 
Another way of combining heating, cooling and ventilation are by chilled beams. 
There are two types of chilled beams; they can either be active or passive. The 
passive chilled beam is mainly used for cooling a room even though it is possible to 
apply hot water for room heating as well. The passive chilled beam transfer energy 
by radiation and natural convection and the air supply is by diffusers separated 
from the beam. The active chilled beam has air supply through the beam which 
creates forced convection and radiation in order to supply energy to the room. In 
this system it is also possible to connect the hot water in order to combine the 
heating and cooling sources in the room [24]. 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
7 
 
Figure 10: Different operation types for chilled beams.  
The active chilled beam can be installed when the requirements for cooling are 
below 120 W/m2 and airflow rates between 1.5-3.0 l/s, m2 [24]. With this in mind 
the chilled beam has the highest efficiency in rooms with a lower internal load 
which means the chilled beam are less efficient in meeting rooms, conference halls, 
auditoriums ect. The active chilled beam has the disadvantage that it does not work 
without the ventilation which courses a higher energy use outside the office hours.  
Two investigations of the chilled beam have been carried out with a low 
temperature offset of ±3 ℃. The first attempt on this LTH-HTC system is from the 
Danish Building Research Institute and Aalborg University [25]; here a chilled beam 
has been investigated in an office building. The chilled beam has the combination 
of both heating, cooling and ventilation which allows free cooling in the building. A 
schematic overview of how the system function is shown in Figure 11. This first 
attempt lead to a total energy saving of 3 %, where the saving of the cooling 
demand was almost 6 % [25]. 
 
Figure 11: Schematic layout of the 2-pipe system when equal heating and cooling demand[25]. 
Another project exploring the small temperature offset has been made by Lindab 
A/S in collaboration with the Danish Research Institute [26]. In this investigation 
they use the same approach with temperatures between 20-23 °C for the 2-pipe 
system and the simulation is carried out in EnergyPlus. With this investigation it was 
found that the energy saving was approximately 5 % using the LTH-HTC system with 
offset of ±3 ℃ instead of the conventional ±10 ℃  [26].

1.2.4. Heating and cooling supply 
Conventionally the heating and cooling supply to a room in a LTH-HTC system is by 
having two pair of pipes; supply and return for both heating and cooling. With this 
conventional way of heating and cooling a building it is possible to have the 
maximum impact from the source considering radiant asymmetry. For the four- 
pipe system, the cooling pipes supply temperature is usually 16 ℃ and the heating 
pipes supply is around 35 ℃; which is a temperature offset from water to room of 
± 10 ℃. 
Depending on the heating and cooling source in the room, the connection of the 
pipes is different. For embedded systems the connection is either in a wall or as a 
part of the slab, where the space has been saved for the connection and is cast 
afterwards. A heated/chilled ceiling system that either lies just above a suspended 
ceiling or is mounted at the bottom of the ceiling will be connected to a 6-ways 
valve to control if there is heating or cooling in the room. Both ways are able to 
control the room temperature in each room separately, depending on the use of 
the room.  
Controlling the four-pipe system requires a great deal of Building Management 
Systems (BMS). The BMS controls when there is a need for heating, cooling, 
ventilation, a higher/lower flow and so on. In order to move toward a self-
regulating building, the systems must be reconsidered. As described above 
investigations have been made to lower the offset temperature from ±10 ℃ to        
±2-4 ℃ and thereby reduce the number of supply and return pipes from four to two 
and the same time reduce the amount of BMS controls. A reduction of the pipes 
and the temperatures will besides the energy savings also save construction and 
maintenance cost.  
1.3. Hypothesis 
With all of the above in mind this project is the beginning of the investigations of 
the HCV+A solutions where the focus will be on self-regulation and comfort. In this 
study, dynamic simulations of the heating and cooling effect, indoor environment 
and energy consumption in an office building will be examined. Two hypotheses 
have been developed: 
 
2-pipe LTH-HTC systems are able in 2020 to provide adequate indoor 
thermal climate in offices and meeting rooms with a temperature 
difference of ±2-4 ℃. 
 
2-pipe LTH-HTC systems provide self-regulation to the extend where 
building management systems become obsolete.  
 
2-pipe LTH-HTC systems are energy saving in comparison with 4-pipe 
systems. 
In order to answer the hypothesis, the following objectives are carried out 
throughout this project.

1. Construct a building model that can simulate both heat and mass flow in

2. Create a control system that can change the flow and temperature

2.3. Heat transfer 
Heat transfer is a broad term that includes both sensible and latent heat transfer. 
Sensible heat transfer includes advection, conduction, convection and radiation; 
and latent heat transfer is an evaporation or condensation that transforms to or 
from sensible heat. In this section, only terms used in the project will be described. 
2.3.1.  Conduction 
Heat conduction is a transportation of energy when vibrating atoms or particles 
collide. Thermal conduction is transferred between solids, gasses and/or liquids in 
contact with each other at different temperatures or inside the medium with 
varying temperatures. The material properties have high influence on the 
conduction as well as the thickness and temperature difference.

temperature differences, thickness and thermal conductivity. The thermal 
conductivity is the material property describing the ability to transfer heat through 
the material.  Fourier’s law is a stationary, one-dimensional calculation method

The program used for the simulation is IDA Indoor Climate and Energy (IDA ICE), 
which is a dynamic building simulation software that compute energy 
consumptions for the entire building and in zones in the building as well as 
calculating the indoor environments in the zones. Included in indoor environment 
are both indoor air quality, thermal environment and daylighting. IDA ICE works on 
two levels. The simple level modelling, which most users use, has an easy interface 
where it is possible to give all of the general inputs for the model and either draw 
or import a building model and create one or more zones in the building. In the 
advance level it is possible to change a great part of the systems and controls for 
the building. To work on the simplest level in IDA ICE it is needed to have an 
understanding of building physics and an even greater understanding of both the 
physics and the program in order to go into the advance level.  
In IDA ICE it is possible to visualize, analyse and report results. Here it is also possible 
to advance and go into any system and track any variable interesting for the specific 
case. In order to investigate and simulate assumptions have to be made in the 
program. 
In the transaction from reality to IDA ICE there are some assumptions, an 
understanding of the tools in the program, as well as a sense of what is to come out 
on the other side of the calculation. In the program there are many basics as 
constructions, occupancy schedule, internal loads etc. In this section some of the 
more complex inputs will be described, to create this understanding of how the 
simulations are conducted.

4.1.3. ESBO plant  
The ESBO plant (Advanced model) is introduced in order to get free cooling and is 
build up around 5 sections as shown in Figure 21. The whole plant ensures heating 
and cooling for both zones and AHU with free cooling.  
1. The first object is the free cooling area, that includes a fan and two buffer 
tanks. The fan transfers cooling from the outdoor air to the brine system; 
which is a fluid that transports energy. The brine goes through two buffer 
tanks and into the chiller. To make the system work correctly there has to 
be a constant flow though the exchanger in the chiller. Therefore, the 
buffer tanks are introduced to ensure this constant flow, without a need to 
have constant flow in the building. When it is possible e.g. the 
temperatures in the tanks are too high or too low the systems transport 
energy directly to the hot and cold water tanks: which creates the free 
cooling and in the program a small amount of free heating. The systems go 
as air to brine and brine to water.  
2. The boiler is kept as a simple boiler, as there will not be any changes in 
relation to introduce the free cooling system. 
3. The basic chiller has been replaced with a brine to water chiller. This is done 
due to the ambient heat exchanger, so the two components can work 
together. As stated in number 1, the brine to water chiller chills the water 
when it is not possible to get free cooling from the ambient heat exchanger.  
4. Is the hot and cold water tank. 
5. Indicates the control of the complete system, from the top; DHW-control, 
AHU-heating, zone heating, AHU-cooling and last the zone cooling. Here 
individual schedules are made to fulfil the different simulations.

4.2. Methodology 
The methodology of the simulations is first of all to create a base of information for 
the indoor comfort and energy consumption. This is done by first creating a 2008 
model with a 4-pipe solution as a reference, then improve the systems so they 
correspond to the building code of 2020. From here the 2-pipe investigation will be 
created. These three models will be the base of the investigations in order to see 
what are the possibilities with the different approaches.  
In the first 2-pipe model, the only thing changing from the 4-pipe is the temperature 
of the water. This goes from being variable to being constant. Initial investigations 
of the 2-pipe system are done; first a test of how the model reacts if the flow is 
constant, secondly the flow is kept constant, but the temperature of the water vary 
to seasons. An overview of this is created in Figure 27.  
From these simulations some sensitivity analysis is made. First a simulation where 
the heat transfer coefficient from water to fin is changed to the standard size from 
IDA ICE. Another test will be to see what happens if the acoustical ceiling is changed 
to a metal plate. An outline of the methodology is shown in Figure 27.

6. Discussion 
6.1. Indoor thermal comfort 
The simulation for the 2008 building showed to many warm hours during the 
wintertime, see Figure 32, but since there is the same setpoint all year the cooling 
does not start before the temperature gets beyond 25.5 ℃.  
The simulations of the 2-pipe system, with a constant temperature all year of 22 ℃ 
the temperatures during the summer is to low comparing with thermal climate 
class A [29], [30], which can be seen by the duration curves Figure 39 and Figure 44. 
These are almost removed by a supply temperature of 24 ℃ in the summer as 
simulated in simulation 5. Even with these differences from class A, the 2-pipe 
system is able to provide an adequate thermal climate with only a small 
temperature difference.  
Comparing the heat fluxes and temperatures from simulation 2-5 it can be seen 
that there is no need for supply temperature ± 10 ℃ from room temperature, but 
± 2-4 ℃ is sufficient. 10 ℃ is only negative, which it is means only provide cooling 
where ± 2-4 ℃ is fluctuating around 0 ℃ and hereby provide both heating and 
cooling.  
With the simulation 4 and 5, models were created to run without any control. Both 
models showed an adequate indoor thermal comfort, but with a higher 
temperature fluctuation. In section 5.6, heat flux compared Figure 57, the standard 
deviation for cooling heat flux is decreasing through the models as expected, but 
the heating heat flux is increasing which was unexpected.  
6.2. Energy 
Comparing simulation 2 and 3 it is seen that there is only a small energy saving, but 
when comparing simulation 2 with 4 and 5 the energy surprisingly rises with 36 % 
and 27 %. Even a comparison within the three simulations with the 2-pipe solution 
==PAGE:75==
62 
the energy rises from 27.7 kWh/m2 per year to 40.8 kWh/m2 per year which comply 
with a rise of 47 %. For the simulation 3 and 5 the rise is 37 %. 
Both simulation 4 and 5 has a constant flow, which means that the flow will not rise 
for either heating or cooling. With setpoints between 22.4-23 ℃, the heating is 
running up to 22.9 ℃ and this is 1.4 ℃ higher than simulation 2 and 3. Hereby the 
consequence is a higher energy consumption.  
Further investigations of the simulation technicalities should be conducted in order 
to see why the energy rises between 30-50 %.  
6.3. Sensitivity analysis 
The sensitivity analysis with the aluminium plate is due to suspicion of thermal mass 
in the gypsum plate. As it can be seen in section 5.7.1 the aluminium plate causes 
some more cold hours during the summer period than simulation 5. In Figure 60 
heat flux it can be seen that the surface temperature fluctuates just around the 
setpoint temperature; which is yet another indication of the large energy amount 
in the models with setpoints of 22.4 ℃. From the heat fluxes in the summer, there 
is a higher transport of energy, but the temperature drops faster outside of office 
hours, which is expected from the aluminium material characteristic. 
The other sensitivity analysis, with a heat transfer coefficient of 6 W/m2K from 
water to fin; the temperatures and fluctuations are similar, but the sensitivity 
analyses showed a bit more temperatures outside of the thermal comfort criteria. 
This is expected since the heating and cooling should have more difficulties coming 
from the water to the fin with a lower heat transfer coefficient.  
What cannot be described is that the sensitivity analysis, of the heat transfer 
coefficient, uses 6 % less energy than simulation 5; this simulation should use more 
energy taking the building physics into account. The resistance rises and at the 
same time the energy descends. This must be caused by the simulation 
technicalities and the mathematical method in the program. As described in section 
4.1.5 the surface resistance changes by the temperature. Hereby analysis should be 
performed on minute-basis; this is outside the timeframe for this project, therefore 
this should be a further investigation.

7. Conclusion 
From the investigations it was found that the 2-pipe LTH-HTC solution can provide 
adequate indoor thermal comfort. In terms of self-regulation, the 2-pipe LTH-HTC 
provides self-regulation to the room in the understanding of comfort; where in all 
cases the Danish Standard 474 is met. Though in terms of minimizing fluctuations 
during the day the 2-pipe LTH-HTC cannot provide self-regulation, due to lower 
effect in the zones.  
In the investigations it was found that BMS can become obsolete in terms of 
thermal comfort, however there is a challenge with the energy consumption. 
Where further investigations are required in order to see if it is possible to create 
model where a constant water flow and constant temperature in the rooms are 
held while save energy.  
It can be concluded that there is a small energy saving just by going from the 4-pipe 
system to the 2-pipe LTH-HTC system as well as less hours of overheating, if the 
BMS and setpoints are kept.  
The sensitivity analysis for aluminium showed there were only small variations 
between aluminium and gypsum, which makes it possible to choose material with 
the focus of acoustics and aesthetics.  
It can be concluded that further investigations of the heat transfer coefficient have 
to be conducted in order to find the connection between the heat transfer 
coefficient, the surface resistance and the calculation of the energy consumption. 
With the further investigation of the heat transfer coefficient it might make it 
possible to introduce the diffuse ventilation. Combining the investigations from this 
project with an investigation of diffuse ventilation, this could lead to lower energy 
consumption and a higher value of free heating and cooling in the zone. Which 
would give an even better thermal comfort in the zone.  
The solution of a 2020 building with the flexible 2-pipe solution, is one step further 
to net-zero-energy buildings although there is a need for further investigations.
